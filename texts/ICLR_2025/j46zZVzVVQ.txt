009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Practical reinforcement learning (RL) usually requires agents to be optimized for
012            multiple potentially conflicting criteria, e.g. speed vs. safety. Although Multi-
013            Objective RL (MORL) algorithms have been studied in previous works, their
014            trained agents often lack precise controllability of the delicate trade-off among
015            multiple objectives. Hence, the resulting agent is not versatile in aligning with cus-
016            tomized requests from different users. To bridge the gap, we develop “Preference
017            control (PC) RL”, which trains a meta-policy that takes user preference as input
018            controlling the generated trajectories within the preference region on the Pareto
019            frontier. To this end, we train a preference-conditioned meta-policy by our proposed
020
               preference-regularized MORL algorithm. The achieved meta-policy performs as
               a multi-objective optimizer that can directly generate user-desired Pareto solutions.
021
               The proposed algorithm is analyzed and its convergence and controllability are theo-
022
               retically justified. We evaluate PCRL on a discrete toy environment and challenging
023            high-dimensional robotic control tasks with up to six objectives. In these exper-
024            iments, PCRL-trained policies exhibit significantly better controllability than exist-
025            ing approaches and can generate Pareto solutions with better diversity and utilities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning (MORL) has attracted growing interests in applications of
032   training sequential decision-making agents that satisfy multiple objectives. In practice, optimizing for
033
      potentially multiple criteria often involves managing trade-offs between them. For example, speed vs.
      safety or distance vs. energy for robotic control tasks. Many previous MORL methods (Yang et al.,
034
      2019; Abels et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2023; Alegre et al., 2023) tried to address the
035
      trade-off issue by optimizing a linearly scalarized objective, which sums up multiple objectives with
036   preference weights. However, Linear Scalarization (LS) approach’s solutions do not always guarantee
037   to achieve desired trade-offs. For example, even with equal weights on two objectives, LS may heavily
038   optimize one objective over the other (as shown in Fig. 1). Another limitation is that some methods Xu
039   et al. (2020); Alegre et al. (2023) require learning multiple models to identify the Pareto front , which
040   is unscalable to increasing objectives or model sizes. By the design, each model only handles one spec-
041   ified preference, while a versatile RL agent should be adaptable to various unseen user preferences.
042   This inspires us to develop an innovative MORL scheme, “Preference Control (PC) RL”, to train
043   a meta-policy that takes user preference as input to control the trade-off between objectives. The
044   scheme enables us to apply methods (Lin et al., 2019; Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020) from the Multi-
045   Objective Optimization (MOO) domain to overcome the limitation of LS and find preference-specific
046   solutions on the Pareto front. Moreover, inspired by how some MOO methods (Désidéri, 2009; Liu
047   et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023) deal with conflicting gradients and stochastic gradients, we propose
048   a novel MORL-specific algorithm “PreCo” and conduct a theoretical analysis of it, which proves
049   that, with stochastic gradients, PCRL using PreCo can achieve Pareto stationary solutions precisely
050
      controlled by the input preference.
051   We conducted experiments in environments with conflicting objectives Felten et al. (2023) to em-
052   pirically demonstrate that (1) our PCRL scheme is compatible with various MOO methods; and (2)
053   PCRL with PreCo consistently achieves superior performance across multiple MORL environments.
      In particular, our method excels in cases with a large number of objectives or conflicting objectives.

056   Multiple Objective Reinforcement Learning In the multi-objective RL (MORL) setting, agent
057   needs to optimize possibly conflicting objectives with their separate reward function. MORL setting
058   can be modeled as Multi-Objective Markov Decision Process (MOMDP). Unlike the scalar reward
059   function in conventional MDP, the reward function in MOMDP is vector-valued. A MOMDP is
060   defined as M = (S, A, P, r, p0 , γ), with state space S and action space A, dynamics P (st+1 |, st , at ),
061   initial state distribution p0 (s0 ), and discount factor γ ∈ [0, 1). The vector-valued function r :
062   S × A → Rm is a multi-objective reward function with m objectives. A policy π : S → A is a
063   function mapping states to actions. The multi-objective value functions for a policy π are:

078
      Preference control Preference quantifies the trade-off among the multiple objectives. We define
079   the set of preferences P := {p ∈ Rm : pT 1 = 1, p ≻ 0}. The desired policy π for preference p
080   should have the value v π optimizing a similarity metric Ψ(p, v π ), which can be cosine similarity
081   or what we define in Definition 4.2. The optimal v π should be on the Pareto Front with a maximal
082   similarity to p. In other words, the ideal v π for preference p should be on the Pareto front and closest
083   to the intersection of between the Pareto front and the ray from the origin to the direction of p.
084
      Previous works (Yang et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; Alegre et al., 2023) consider maximizing a linear
085   scalarization of objectives pT v π . However, the solution of maxπ pT v π or maxθ pT v πθ can only
086   be in the convex part of the Pareto front (Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2004, Chapter 4.7) but not the
087   non-convex part. Even for some MORL cases where the Pareto front can be considered convex (Lu
088   et al., 2023), the solution is often limited to a Convex Coverage Set (CCS) that is a subset of Pareto
089   front. Even for strictly convex Pareto front, LS is still not guaranteed to be close to the direction of p
090   as shown by Fig. 1c.
091   Instead of learning a policy πp for each possible p ∈ P, our goal is to learn an agent with a conditional
092                                                                                          ′
      policy π(a|s, p) that achieves Pareto optimal values v π(·|·,p) ∈ arg maxπ′ Ψ(p, v π ) for any p ∈ P.
093
      For conciseness, we denote v π(·|·,p) as v πp in the following text. There are two requirements for the
094   agent. One is to explore the Pareto front as much as possible, and the other is to have a performance
095   trade-off close to the input preference. These two requirements can be evaluated for two metrics:
096   Hypervolume(HV) for exploration of Pareto front and Similarity Ψ(p, v π ) for controllability.
097
098   Multi-objective optimization methods Previous Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) methods
099   deal with how to manipulate gradients from multiple objectives so that updating with the manipulated
100   gradient can reach Pareto optimality. A typical method MGDA (Désidéri, 2009) can guarantee to
101   update in a common ascending direction and stops when the Pareto stationary points are reached.
102   Methods such as CAgrad (Liu et al., 2021) and SDMGrad (Xiao et al., 2023) can provide Pareto
103   optimal solutions by linear scalarization with preference as weights. However, as mentioned above,
104
      optimizing linearly scalarized objective with weight p can not guarantee a large similarity Ψ(p, v π ).
105   Methods such as PMTL (Lin et al., 2019) and EPO (Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020) apply similarity
106   constraints to reach the Pareto front with the desired preference, so they can be used for our purpose.
107   In the next section, we show how these methods can be used for learning π(a|s, p) and how we can
      make novel improvements to them for the MORL setting.

      3     L EARNING PREFERENCE CONTROLLABLE AGENT
128
      We propose “Preference control (PC) RL” scheme to address the trade-off between multiple conflicting
129
      objectives by training a single agent that can be conditioned on different performance preferences.
130
      Conditional preference p controls the agent’s emphasis on different objectives and corresponds to a
131   desired point on the Pareto front. We denote the policy conditioned on a preference π(·|·, p) as πp .
132   During training, we sample p ∈ P uniformly and collect rollout data to estimate v πp and evaluate
133   similarity Ψ(p, v πp ). Based on the evaluation, we obtain an update direction for πp . In PCRL
134   scheme, the update direction can be obtained using any methods that can incorporate preference
135   on the objectives, such as linear scalarization (optimizing maxπp pT v πp ), or other MOO methods
136   with extra optimization or regularization of the similarity (implementations in Appendix B). We also
137   propose a novel update method while these existing MOO methods will be tested as baselines. In
138   the following section, we first introduce how to estimate the v πp values then explain our proposed
139
      update method. We provide theoretical guarantee of our proposed update method in the next section.

150   3.1    O BJECTIVE ESTIMATION
151
      Preference control aims to achieve the desired trade-off on conflicting objectives. In the previous RL
152
      experiments of MOO methods like Yu et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Xiao et al. (2023), the loss of
153
      the value function is used as the objective for MOO, and equal weight is given to all value losses
154   to balance the multi-objectives. While this may be appropriate for RL tasks with minimal conflict,
155   in our setting for preference control, it is essential to align the objective with the preference, so the
156   objective to be aligned with the preference should be v πp itself rather than its approximation loss.
157   Here, we show how to estimate v πp for mainstream RL algorithms.
158
      When learning πp with value-based methods like DDPG (Lillicrap et al., 2016), TD3 (Fujimoto et al.,
159
      2018) and SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), we can estimate v πp by

174   3.2    U PDATING PROCEDURE
175
176
      After estimating objective values and similarity for preference control, we need to manipulate the
      gradients from different objectives and update the agent using the manipulated gradient. Our scheme
177
      has the following updating procedure:
178
179   1. Get the Jacobian matrix ∇πp v̂ πp :
180      Each row of the Jacobian matrix ∇πp v̂ πp is a gradient for one objective. The gradient can be
181      obtained by conventional RL methods, such as the policy gradient and the deterministic policy
182      gradient. An illustrative diagram (Fig. 9 in Appendix D) provides intuition and shows how to
183      estimate ∇πp v̂ πp for different RL algorithms.
184   2. Get similarity gradient ∇πp Ψ(p, v̂ πp ):
185      Ψ measures the similarity between preference p and the multi-objective vector J. For evaluation, co-
186      sine similarity is good enough to measure how close the value vector is to the preference. However,
187      when gradient ∇πp Ψ(p, v̂ πp ) is used for our updates, the resulting manipulated gradient update
188      is expected to not only keep the similarity large but also converge to the Pareto front. To this end,
189      we propose a novel design of the similarity function with theoretical analysis in the next section.
190   3. Manipulate the gradients and find the optimal update direction d∗ by solving:
191                          w∗ ∈ arg min ∥d∥, d ≜ ∇Tπp v̂ πp w + λ∇πp Ψ(p, v̂ πp )                       (6)
192                                    w
193                                                   d∗ = ∇Tπp v̂ πp w∗ + λ∇πp Ψ(p, v̂ πp )              (7)
194       This is a min-norm problem similar to MGDA (Désidéri, 2009) and SMGrad (Xiao et al., 2023),
195       but it adds a similarity gradient to every objective gradient, making the update not only ascent
196       in a common improving direction but also closing the value v πp to the preference p. We call
197       this update PREference COntrol(PreCo) update. Updating with d∗ converges to where ∥d∗ ∥ = 0,
198       indicating no common improving direction exists thus satisfying Pareto stationary. We will prove
199       the convergence of this gradient under our proposed similarity function.
200
      This is a general update procedure that can employ any RL algorithm for the calculation of the
201
      objective gradients ∇πp v̂ πp . In the third step, the gradient manipulation can also be performed by
202   various MOO methods. In the experiment, we examine our scheme with PreCo update against the
203   baselines with existing gradient manipulation methods such as EPO (Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020)
204   CAGrad (Liu et al., 2021). Computationally, the min-norm problem in the third step is solved at
205   the policy level with ∇πp v̂ πp instead of the parameter level with ∇θ v̂ πp . The size for a sample
206   of ∇πp v̂ πp is only m × B for a batch of B transitions, while ∇θ v̂ πp of size m × M could have a
207   parameter size M ≫ m. M can even be billions for large models. For those cases, solving the min-
208   norm problem at the parameter level could be memory-inefficient and computationally intractable. A
209   pseudo-code for the PCRL scheme with PreCo update and more details on the definitions of policy
210
      level gradients and parameter level gradients can be found in Appendix D.

337   5   E XPERIMENTS
338
339   In the experiment, we want to empirically answer the following questions:
340
341         1. Can the agent have non-dominated performance for different preferences?
342         2. How controllable is it for unseen preference trade-offs?
343         3. Is our method scalable for larger number of objectives?
344         4. Can our method be used for both discrete action and continuous action environments?

346   Benchmarking Environments To answer the questions, we need challenging environments with
347   conflicting objectives, so that there is a trade-off on the emphasis of different objectives. Also, we
348   need environments with more than just two objectives. In addition, we need environments with both
349   discrete action space and continuous action space. We use TD3 for continuous and PPO-clip for
350   discrete actions. We chose to conduct experiments on the following four MORL environments.
351
      • Fruit-Tree: A discrete environment. Every leaf contains a fruit with a 6-D reward for the nutrients

        Protein, Carbs, Fats, Vitamins, Minerals, and Water.

355
        x-velocity and y-velocity.
356   • MO-Hopper: A continuous robotic control environment with 2-D reward. The first dimension is

359     space. The reward is 4-D and is defined based on the distance of the tip of the arm and the four

      Baselines We compare PreCo with existing MOO gradient manipulation methods in PCRL scheme:
367
368   • Linear Scalarization (LS) Optimize the linearly scalarized objective and the manipulated gradient
369     will be simply the gradient linearly weighted by the preference (∇πp v̂ πp )T p. This is the most
370     common update approach in previous MORL methods. Works like Yang et al. (2019); Xu et al.
371     (2020); Alegre et al. (2023) can all be categorized as LS variations.
372   • Exact Pareto Optimal (EPO) Search MOO methods such as PMTL (Lin et al., 2019) and
373     EPO (Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020) apply similarity constraints and have two modes for situations
374     of low and high similarity. We implement it as updating with similarity gradient for low similarity
375     mode, MGDA (Désidéri, 2009) gradient for high similarity mode.
376   • Conflict-Averse Gradient (CAGrad) CAGrad tries to find a common ascent direction that is not
377     too far from the average gradient. In our setting, we modify it to be a common ascent direction not
        is not too far from the similarity gradient.

      • Stochastic Direction-oriented MO-Gradient (SDMGrad) Similar to PreCo, SDMGrad solves
379     a variant of the min-norm problem (6) for gradient manipulation. Instead of adding objective
380     gradients with similarity gradients like PreCO, they add a convex combination of objective gradients.
381     Therefore, SDMGrad can serve as a baseline to show whether our proposed similarity function Ψ
382     contributes to empirical performance for ablation study.

532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we proposed a PCRL scheme for preference control of the multi-objective trade-offs. To
535   achieve this, we proposed a novel MOO approach, PreCo, and provided a comprehensive convergence
536   analysis for stochastic optimization with non-convex smooth objective functions. Our experiments
537   across multiple RL environments show that our proposed method consistently outperforms baselines,
538   demonstrating that a meta-policy that adapts to user preferences is both feasible and promising for
539   potential applications in human-interactive AI agents. Future work for improving PCRL could be
      progressing training preference distribution for curriculum learning.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS

759   A.1    M ETA - POLICIES
760
761
      Existing MORL methods that learn a similar meta-policy conditioned on a weight or preference
      include Abels et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2023), of which Abels et al. (2019); Lu
762
      et al. (2023) are LS methods and they care more about discovering all Pareto optimal policies rather
763
      than the similarity between the weight input and the resulted value. Chen et al. (2019) employs a
764   setting most similar to our PCRL since they optimize a Tchebycheff Scalarized (TS) (Ehrgott, 2005)
765   objective for solutions aligned with the preference directions. However, Xu et al. (2020) reported that
766   Chen et al. (2019) has suboptimal performance in practice. This might be due to the oscillation and
767   stagnation issue inherent in the TS approach, as noted by Mahapatra and Rajan (2021). In particular,
768   for MORL, which is sensitive to stochasticity and conflicting gradients, the convergence of TS can be
769   problematic. Our baseline implementation of EPO with a small constraint threshold can be considered
770   as a version of TS tailored to the MORL setting, designed to mitigate oscillation when near preference
771   direction. More recent MORL algorithm (Basaklar et al., 2023) incorporates cosine similarity for
772
      preference alignment but it is designed specifically for off-policy value-based RL and its performance
      relies heavily on the HER (Andrychowicz et al., 2017) technique. In contrast, our PCRL with PreCo
773
      is a broader MORL framework compatible with both on-policy and off-policy RL, capable of learning
774
      quality policies without HER. Nonetheless, HER can still be integrated into PCRL with PreCo in
775   off-policy settings to enhance sample efficiency. While not targeting exact preference alignment,
776   Lu et al. (2023) addressed LS limitations by adding a concave augmentation term to the reward,
777   transforming the original Pareto front into a strictly convex one. However, this introduces information
778   loss, making the approach sensitive to the augmentation term’s magnitude. Their implementation is
779   limited to SAC, using policy entropy as the augmentation term. Compared to Lu et al. (2023), our
780   method possesses the theoretical advantages of no information loss and exact preference alignment,
781   which can be empirically demonstrated by our additional experiment in Appendix G.
782   Other RL paradigms employing meta-policies include Goal-Conditioned RL (GCRL) (Sekar et al.,
783   2020; Yang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) and skill-based RL (SBRL) (Nam et al., 2022; Lee
784   et al., 2019). GCRL is controlled by an additional input of a target state that it aims to reach.
785   SBRL is conditioned by a skill latent z that often has a lower dimension than the state for a
786   specific primitive skill. Similar to SBRL, the skill learning methods of Unsupervised Reinforcement
787   Learning (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Hansen et al., 2020) learns skills without external task rewards by
788   optimizing a Mutual Information Skill Learning (MISL) (Eysenbach et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024)
789
      objective I(s; z) = H(s) − H(s|z). Maximizing I(s; z) encourages the state space coverage to be
      high and the state distribution to be certain when controlled by a skill z. The concept of Preference
790
      Control (PC) has a resemblance to optimizing I(s; z) = H(s) − H(s|z). The purpose of PCRL
791
      can also be interpreted as optimizing I(v; p) = H(v) − H(v|p) to encourage diverse values on the
792   Pareto front and the distribution of the values needs to be controlled by preference p.
793
794
795   A.2    M ULTI - OBJECTIVE OPTIMIZATION
796
797   We have already introduced PMTL and EPO (Lin et al., 2019; Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020) that could
798   find preference-specific solutions and CAGrad, SDMGrad (Liu et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023) that
799   optimize for the average objective but can deal with conflicting gradients. Désidéri (2009); Xiao et al.
800
      (2023) has the most similarity to our proposed PreCo because they all solve a min norm problem
      like 6 for gradient manipulation. The advantage of our PreCo is not only like SDMGrad, which can
801
      provably deal with stochastic gradients, but also can follow a preference like EPO. Our theoretical
802
      analysis of PreCo is based on some results from Xiao et al. (2023), but incorporating the similarity
803   gradient from Ψ makes it more complicated and novel.

       Theorem 4.2 requires the similarity gradient to be both Lipschitz continuous and convex combinations
