                                                  A BSTRACT
009
               Open set recognition (OSR) requires deep learning models to identify unknown
010
               samples while recognizing known ones. Existing OSR studies focus on single-
011
               modal data but merely discuss how to handle multimodal data. In this paper, we
012            propose a new task multimodal open set recognition (MMOSR), extending OSR to
013            more practical scenarios. First, we analyze the necessity of MMOSR and provide
014            insights into the task. We find that simply combining OSR and multimodal fusion
015            methods faces the challenge of fusion degradation. The main reason is that the
016            OSR regularization constrains the fused representations to be excessively compact,
017            leading to deactivated and limited representations. We design the multimodal
018            representation reactivation network (MRN) to alleviate fusion degradation by reac-
019            tivating suppressed representations. MRN includes the mutually enhanced fusion
020
               for enhancing representations and performing cross-modal interaction, and the
               adaptive fusion for capturing multiple informative representations and outputting
021
               the adaptively fused prediction. Thus, the proposed method obtains effective and
022
               comprehensive multimodal representations and addresses the challenge of fusion
023            degradation. Finally, extensive experiments on various settings demonstrate that
024            the proposed method is superior to existing methods by up to 5.23% on OSCR.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep neural networks can accurately identify samples from learned classes during training but
030   struggle to recognize samples from unknown classes (Geng et al., 2020). Open set recognition (OSR)
031
      is proposed to enable models to classify known samples while rejecting unknown ones, which is the
      fundamental ability of models to ensure security and know what to learn (Scheirer et al., 2012).
032
033   Many researchers have dedicated to the OSR problem on various types of data, such as images, texts,
034   and time series (Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022). Existing OSR methods
035   can be roughly divided into two groups: generative methods that encode classes into various latent
036   distributions or synthesizing pseudo-unknown samples for constraining the space occupied by known
037
      classes (Katsumata et al., 2022; Kong & Ramanan, 2021; Yue et al., 2021) and discriminative methods
      that reserve space for unknown classes by incorporating placeholders or reciprocal points (Chen et al.,
038
      2020; 2022; Zhou et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023).
039
040   Despite the progress in OSR, existing studies work upon single-modal data but merely address
041   multimodal data. In fact, humanoid robots (Hirose & Ogawa, 2007) and unmanned systems typically
042   rely on multimodal sensors, e.g., image-text or vision-audio data, to perceive the environment, which
043
      challenges the mechanism of single-modal OSR methods that requires comprehensively capturing
      unknown factors. To address this limitation, we propose a new multimodal open set recognition
044
      (MMOSR) task, analyzing its necessity and challenge by discussing the following questions:
045
046   (1) Is it possible to solve MMOSR using existing multimodal fusion and single-modal OSR methods?
047   (2) If not, what is the main challenge of applying these existing methods to MMOSR?
048
049   (3) How to address the aforementioned challenge and design effective MMOSR methods?
050   To answer these questions, we empirically analyze three types of models: single-modal OSR,
051   multimodal fusion, and the combination of multimodal fusion and single-modal OSR. As illustrated
052   in Figure 1, we find that combining multimodal learning and single-modal OSR cannot perform
053   satisfactorily (Section 3.2). OSR regularization leads to the collapse of multimodal representations by
      suppressing the fused representations of each class to be excessively compact. Such an excessively

      To address the aforementioned challenge, we propose the multimodal representation reactivation
071
      network (MRN), which reactivates representations and learns comprehensive representations through
072   effective fusion across modalities. Concretely, to alleviate fusion degradation, we design an adaptive
073   fusion module using a mixture of experts to obtain comprehensive representations. To avoid the fusion
074   suppression of modality, we design a mutually enhanced fusion module to enable full interaction
075   between different modalities. With effective fusion modules in the model, the learned representations
076   are more comprehensive and informative. Extensive experiments show that our method achieves an
077   over 5.23% improvement on OSCR over existing methods, thereby verifying its effectiveness.
078   The contributions of the paper are three-fold:
079
080          • We propose a new multimodal open set recognition (MMOSR) task that extends the existing
081            OSR task to handling multimodal data.
082          • We provide insights into MMOSR and find that simply combining single-modal OSR and
083            multimodal fusion would lead to the challenge of fusion degradation.
084          • We design a multimodal representation reactivation network with mutually enhanced fusion
085            and adaptive fusion to enable comprehensive and informative representations.

      2     R ELATED WORK
089
      2.1   M ULTIMODAL FUSION
090
091   Multimodal fusion aims to integrate information from diverse sensory modalities (such as vision,
092   audio, and text) to achieve a more comprehensive and accurate understanding. Simple operations
093   like addition and concatenation enable the integration of representations from diverse sources (Noja-
094   vanasghari et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017). TMC (Han et al., 2023) leverages variational Dirichlet
095   distribution and Dempster-Shafer theory to improve accuracy and robustness by dynamically integrat-
096   ing views based on uncertainty. GQA (Ainslie et al., 2023) improves upon multi-query attention by
097   grouping query heads, achieving near multi-head attention quality with faster inference speed through
098
      minimal uptraining. MLA (Zhang et al., 2024) improves multimodal learning by alternating unimodal
      adaptations to reduce interference between modalities and optimize cross-modal interactions.
099
100   Moreover, large-scale pre-trained models excel in image-text classification. CLIP (Radford et al.,
101   2021) employs contrastive learning to align visual and textual representations in a shared embedding
102   space, CoOp (Zhou et al., 2021) extends CLIP by optimizing learnable prompts to adapt to new
103   downstream tasks, while MaPLe (Khattak et al., 2023) further enhances this by introducing multiple
104
      prompt vectors that enrich prompt diversity and enhance the connections between image and text.
105   Even with the recent developments in multimodal fusion (MM) methods, they neglect unknown
106   samples that may appear, leading to known classes dominating feature space and hindering the
107   detection of unknown samples. Intuitively, we investigate combining MM and OSR to handle the
      MMOSR task in Section 3, but the performance remains unsatisfactory.


                                                        2
108
      2.2   O PEN SET RECOGNITION
109
110   Early OSR methods used conventional machine learning techniques like support vector machines
111   and extreme value theory (Bendale & Boult, 2016; Scheirer et al., 2012). The maximum Softmax
112   probability served as the baseline for rejecting unknown samples, representing the highest probability
113   of a sample belonging to a known class (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016).
114   Recent OSR methods based on deep learning can be categorized into generative and discriminative
115   methods. Generative methods aim to generate pseudo-unknown samples, enabling models to handle
116   and distinguish unknown samples accurately (Katsumata et al., 2022; Kong & Ramanan, 2021;
117   Yue et al., 2021). Several generative methods incorporate an additional component that learns to
118   reconstruct inputs from features, using the reconstruction errors to estimate whether the test sample is
119   from known classes (Huang et al., 2023; Oza & Patel, 2019; Perera et al., 2020; Yoshihashi et al.,
120   2019). Discriminative methods use specific classification strategies for handling OSR tasks, such as
121   using prototype points as representatives for known classes or setting placeholders to reserve space
122
      for unknown samples (Chen et al., 2020; 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2021).
123   Despite recent advances, existing OSR methods are mainly for single-modal tasks. Extending OSR
124   to MMOSR using multimodal fusion models before applying OSR strategies is straightforward.
125   However, exploratory experiments show that simply combining MM and OSR does not achieve
126   satisfactory performance, highlighting the unique challenge in MMOSR.
127
128   3     MMOSR
129
130   In this section, we conducted experiments on the proposed MMOSR benchmark to illustrate the
131   challenge of handling the MMOSR task and the necessity of developing specific MMOSR methods.
132
133   3.1   P ROBLEM DEFINITION
134
                                                                                     |M|
135   In multimodal open set recognition (MMOSR) task, the i-th sample (x1i , ..., xi , yi ) comprises data
                                                                                               |M|
136   from |M| modalities and the corresponding label. The training set DK = {(x1i , ..., xi , yi )}ni=1
137   only contains samples from known classes, whereby yi ∈ {1, . . . , CK } and CK is the num-
138   ber of known classes. The traditional multimodal classification task aims to classify multi-
139   modal data under closed-set assumption. However, under the MMOSR setting, the test set
                          |M|
140   DT = {(x1j , ..., xj , yj )}tj=1 includes samples from both known classes and unknown classes,
141   where the label yj ∈ {1, ..., CK } ∪ {CK + 1, ..., CK + CU } and CU represents the number of
142   unknown classes that appear in testing scenario. Since we cannot obtain any prior information about
143   real scenarios, the model should assign all unknown samples to the unified unknown class as U .

      where basic fusion approaches reduce the distinctiveness of each modality’s representation. We

194   Weakened discrimination ability of the model on fused representations. Comparing Figure 2a,

201
      Over-compression of representations in OSR methods. To handle the risk of misclassifying

202
      for unknown samples. However, as seen in Figure 2c and Figure 2d, Fusion-OSR methods over-
203
      compress these representations. This excessive compression limits the model’s ability to represent
204   and differentiate unknown classes, causing unknown samples to closely resemble known clusters.

237   4.2     M ULTIMODAL REPRESENTATION REACTIVATION NETWORK
238
239   To explain our method clearly, we illustrate it using dual-modality data, where the training sample
240   is represented as (x1 , x2 , y). We first input modality 1 data x1 and modality 2 data x2 into the
241
      corresponding encoder for obtaining the representations separately: z1 = E1 (x1 ), z2 = E2 (x2 ).
      Then, we input the representations z1 , z2 into the following fusion modules to obtain effectively
242
      fused representations with deeply cross-modal interaction for prediction.
243
244
      4.2.1    M UTUALLY ENHANCED FUSION OF REPRESENTATIONS
245
246   Experiments in Section 3.2 show that existing methods suffer from fusion degradation, affecting
247   their ability to utilize comprehensive information to distinguish unknown samples from known
248   ones. It is necessary to ensure that the fused representations contain key information from multiple
249   modalities. In each modality, there is a sharing of class-irrelevant information across classes, which
250   is not advantageous for the OSR task. Thus, guiding the model to focus on class-relevant significant
251   information within each modality can enhance representations.
252   Inspired by the study of Katharopoulos et al. (2020) on attention mechanisms, we use cross-attention
253   to enhance representations of each modality mutually. We consider that the parts where single
254   modality representations highly correlate with representations from another modality represent the
255   class-relevant information that needs to be retained. Therefore, we use cross-attention modules C1
256   and C2 , each dedicated to extracting the crucial information from the corresponding modality, thereby
257   mutually reactivating suppressed representations and interacting between different modalities.
258   For module C1 , we use the modality 1 representations z1 as the query and the modality 2 representa-
259   tions z2 as the key and value. As in equation 1, the attention scores represent the relative importance
260   of each element in z2 concerning the query z1 . The normalized attention scores are then used to
261   compute a weighted sum of z2 and output zc1 , which captures the most relevant information from z2 .

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We discuss a new MMOSR task in this paper by analyzing its research necessity and main challenges.
489   We first find that introducing OSR to multimodal fusion would lead to fusion degradation, which
490   seriously affects the model’s ability to represent multimodal data by the feature suppression of
491   modality and limited representation capability of the model. To address the aforementioned problems,
492   we then propose a multimodal representation reactivation network that yields comprehensive and
493   informative multimodal representations via mutually enhanced fusion and adaptive fusion. The
494   mutually enhanced fusion module uses cross-attention to enable full modality interactions and
495   enhance the representations, while the adaptive fusion module uses the mixture-of-experts to obtain
496   comprehensive representations. We conducted various experiments and verified the effectiveness of
497
      the proposed MRN. Results show that, compared to single-modal OSR, multimodal fusion methods,
      and simple combinations of OSR and multimodal fusion methods, the proposed multimodal fusion
498
      method can significantly enhance the learned representations to facilitate OSR.

      E.1   R EVIEWS OF COMPARED BASELINES
898
899   In this section, we briefly introduce the baseline methods used for comparison.
900
901
      (1) Open set recognition methods
902
             • ARPL (Chen et al., 2022): The adversarial reciprocal point learning (ARPL) framework
903            aims to reduce the overlap between known and unknown data by modeling the extra space
904            associated with unknown classes and employing reciprocal points. Through adversarial
905            mechanisms, ARPL enhances the model’s ability to differentiate unknown classes.
906          • OpenAUC (Wang et al., 2022): OpenAUC evaluates samples using a concise pairwise
907            approach and checks if the open-set sample ranks higher than the close-set one. This method
908            aligns with Open-Set Recognition goals and mitigates threshold sensitivity for improved
909            open set performance.
910          • CSSR (Huang et al., 2023): The Class-Specific Semantic Reconstruction (CSSR) leverages
911            class-specific auto-encoders to reconstruct semantic features for each class, reducing open
912            space risk and improving recognition accuracy on both known and unknown classes.
913          • ASH (Djurisic et al., 2023): The Activation Shaping (ASH) method prunes a large portion of
914            activations (e.g., 90%) during inference and makes minor adjustments to the remaining acti-
915            vations. This approach requires no additional training or network modification, improving
916            OOD detection performance while preserving in-distribution accuracy.
917
      (2) Multimodal fusion methods


                                                        6
918
            • TMC (Han et al., 2023): The Trusted Multi-View Classification (TMC) integrates evidence
919           from multiple views using Dirichlet distributions and Dempster-Shafer theory to provide
920           uncertainty-aware and reliable predictions. TMC enhances classification robustness by
921           dynamically combining evidence.
922
            • GQA (Ainslie et al., 2023): As Multi-Query Attention (MQA) notably speeds up decoder
923           inference but may compromise quality, Grouped Query Attention (GQA) was proposed as a
924           streamlined alternative. By using an intermediate number of key-value heads, GQA achieves
925           a similar training speed to MQA while maintaining quality close to multi-head attention.
926
            • MLA (Zhang et al., 2024): MLA addresses the challenge of modality imbalance in mul-
927           timodal learning by alternating the optimization of unimodal encoders while maintaining
928           cross-modal interaction through a shared head.
929
930   (3) Large-scale pre-trained models
931
932         • CLIP (Radford et al., 2021): CLIP employs contrastive learning to jointly train a vision and
933
              language model on large-scale image-text pairs. By aligning visual representations with
              textual descriptions, CLIP enables zero-shot transfer to diverse downstream tasks, such as
934
              image classification and object recognition, without task-specific fine-tuning.
935
936         • CoOp (Zhou et al., 2021): CoOp introduces a prompt-learning framework designed to
937
              enhance vision-language models like CLIP. By optimizing task-specific prompts rather than
              using static templates, CoOp improves performance in few-shot learning scenarios, enabling
938
              more effective adaptation to various downstream tasks.
939
940         • MaPLe (Khattak et al., 2023): MaPLe advances prompt learning by simultaneously opti-
941
              mizing text and visual prompts across modalities. This method enhances the alignment of
              multimodal representations, leading to superior performance in tasks that require integrated
942
              understanding of both vision and language, such as visual question answering and image
943
              captioning.

947         • Area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC): The AUROC is a widely
948           used metric for evaluating the performance of binary classification models. It measures the
949           area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, which plots the true positive

951         • Average accuracy (ACC): ACC is the mean of the accuracies for each individual class. It
952           accounts for the performance of the classifier on each class separately and then averages
953           these performances.
954         • Open set classification rate (OSCR): OSCR measures the rate of correctly rejecting unknown
955           samples while correctly classifying the known classes. With τ denotes the threshold
956           for rejecting unknown samples and the test set of known samples is denoted as DTK =
