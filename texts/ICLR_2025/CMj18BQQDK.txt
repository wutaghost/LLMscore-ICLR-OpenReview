                                                                   A BSTRACT
034
                    High resolution panoramic video content is paramount for immersive experiences
035
                    in Virtual Reality, but is non-trivial to collect as it requires specialized equipment
036
                    and intricate camera setups. In this work, we introduce VideoPanda, a novel ap-
037                 proach for synthesizing 360◦ videos conditioned on text or single-view video data.
038                 VideoPanda leverages multi-view attention layers to augment a video diffusion
039                 model, enabling it to generate consistent multi-view videos that can be combined
040                 into immersive panoramic content. VideoPanda is trained jointly using two condi-
041                 tions: text-only and single-view video, and supports autoregressive generation of
042                 long-videos. To overcome the computational burden of multi-view video genera-
043                 tion, we randomly subsample the duration and camera views used during training
044                 and show that the model is able to gracefully generalize to generating more frames
045
                    during inference. Extensive evaluations on both real-world and synthetic video
                    datasets demonstrate that VideoPanda generates more realistic and coherent 360◦
046
                    panoramas across all input conditions compared to existing methods. Visit the
047
                    project website at https://mvpanovideo.github.io/VideoPanda/
048                 for results.

051   1       I NTRODUCTION
052
053   A key aspect of achieving true immersion in a virtual environment is allowing users to look around
      freely, by rotating their head and exploring their surroundings from all possible angles. To en-


                                                                               1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      able such experiences, it is essential to have access to high-quality and high-resolution panoramic
055   videos. However, recording such videos is both expensive and time-consuming, as it requires intri-
056   cate camera setups and specialized equipment. As a result, the available panoramic video content on
057   platforms such as YouTube or Vimeo remains limited compared to single-view videos. In this work,
058   we aim to address this issue, by developing a generative model capable of synthesizing panoramic
059   videos either from text prompts or by expanding single-view videos (either generated from models
060   like Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) or recorded) into panoramic format. We consider this an essential
061   step towards making immersive content more accessible and scalable.
062   Recently, diffusion models have shown remarkable success in generating images (Ho et al., 2022;
063   Blattmann et al., 2023a), 3D models (Shi et al., 2023b; Poole et al., 2022), and videos (Brooks et al.,
064   2024; Blattmann et al., 2023b) from text prompts. Despite their promising capabilities, generation of
065   panoramic videos using diffusion models presents significant challenges, mainly due to the scarcity
066   of high-quality panoramic video datasets. Furthermore, while substantial progress has been made
067   towards advancing standard video generation pipelines (Girdhar et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2022;
068   Chen et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024), very few works have attempted to apply these techniques to
069
      panoramic video generation. Existing methods are either limited to specific domains such as driving
      scenarios (Wen et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) or
070
      restricted to generating static scenes (Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). 360DVD (Wang et al.,
071
      2024a) directly generates equirectangular panorama video (with text condition), which presents a
072   large domain gap to base model pretrained on perspective view. We perform an extensive compari-
073   son to 360DVD in the text-conditional setting and demonstrate our improved visual quality.
074
075
      In this paper, we introduce VideoPanda, a novel approach capable of generating high-quality
      panoramic videos from text prompts and single-view video, as well as creating long video using
076
      auto-regression. Our approach builds on existing video diffusion models by adding multi-view at-
077
      tention layers to generate consistent multi-view outputs. Doing so ensures that the output domain
078   (perspective images) remains close to the original training distribution of the pretrained video model
079   (as opposed to directly generating equirectangular projections), which helps in maintaining video
080   quality while generating multiple views. The resulting views are then seamlessly stitched together to
081   create a cohesive panoramic video. We evaluate our model on a diverse set of data domains, includ-
082   ing both real and synthetic videos, and demonstrate its superior performance and quality compared
083   to previous approaches, both quantitatively and qualitatively. Additionally, a user study indicates
084   that the majority of participants prefer our generated videos over those from other baseline models.
085   In summary, we make the following contributions:
086   • We identify the value of panoramic video generation by allowing users to input single-view videos
087     as a condition – a widely available modality, and present a multi-view video architecture capable
088     of generating plausible panoramic videos.
089   • We demonstrate that our model can be jointly trained for text-conditioning, video-conditioning,
090     and autoregressive settings by randomizing the conditioning type, leading to improved results and
091     enabling the generation of long panoramic videos.
092
      • When extending the video model to multi-view, the number of generated image frames greatly
        increases. We overcome the inherent computational burden associated by randomly subselecting
093
        the number of views and frames and show that it gracefully generalizes to video with long duration
094
        and more views during inference.

      2.1   I MAGE AND V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS
100
101
      Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) have demonstrated remarkable success in generating high-quality
102   images (Karras et al., 2022; 2024; Pernias et al., 2023; Hoogeboom et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022) and
103   videos (Girdhar et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023a;b; Brooks et al., 2024; Guo
104   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023) from text prompts. To reduce the computational
105   cost of generating high-dimensional data such as images and videos, latent diffusion models (Rom-
106   bach et al., 2022) (LDMs) proposed to first encode the data into a compressed latent space using a
107   variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013a), and then conduct the diffusion in this lower dimen-
      sional space. These models have been proven highly effective for a wide range of downstream tasks


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      such as inpainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022), controllable generation (Zhang et al., 2023), customized
109   generation (Ruiz et al., 2023), and image/video editing (Kawar et al., 2023; Molad et al., 2023) etc.
110
111
      2.2   M ULTI -V IEW I MAGE G ENERATION
112
113   Building on the success of diffusion models for 2D image generation, they have been increasingly
114   adapted also for multi-view image generation. However, due to the limited availability of real-world
115   multi-view training data, several recent approaches (Shi et al., 2023b; Long et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
116   2023b;a) attempted to fine-tune pretrained image generation models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach
117   et al., 2022) to support multi-view generation. Such approaches can be roughly categorized into two
118   categories: object-centric and scene-centric approaches.
119   Object-centric models focus primarily on generating images of objects where all cameras are inward-
120   facing, looking at a single object from different viewing directions. Examples of such approaches
121   include (Kant et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023a;b; Tang et al., 2024; Voleti et al., 2024;
122   Wang & Shi, 2023). More recently, several object-centric generative models explored incorporating
123   custom attention mechanisms (Hu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023; Kant et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b)
124   to aggregate view-specific information across multiple views. Notable among these, CAT3D (Gao*
125
      et al., 2024) trains a model that generates novel views of an inward-focused scene from one or more
      input views, allowing for 3D reconstruction from a single image. However, these methods often
126
      focus on single-object scenes, which limits their applicability to more complex environments.
127
128   The second line of work seeks to generate realistic multi-view images of entire scenes, using
129   outward-facing cameras to capture different viewing directions and produce panoramas. For in-
130   stance, PanoDiffusion (Wu et al., 2023) is trained on equirectangular projections of 360◦ panoramic
131
      images, and relies on inpainting during inference to extend the input images into complete panora-
      mas. Building on this, PanFusion (Zhang et al., 2024) adds an additional branch to Stable Diffusion,
132
      enabling the simultaneous generation of panoramas and multi-view images. MVDiffusion (Tang
133
      et al., 2023) introduces correspondence-aware attention (CAA) layers, where each point attends only
134   to other points within its local neighborhood. More recently (Yuan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023)
135   proposed predicting the homography between input views and use a diffusion model to generate the
136   unseen regions of the panorama. Lastly, LayerPano3D (Yang et al., 2024) combines multi-view and
137   inpainting models to generate multi-layer panoramas, allowing for somewhat limited exploration
138   within the scene boundaries. Other notable works in this area include (Li et al., 2024a; Zhou et al.,
139   2024; Hara & Harada, 2024; Liu et al., 2024a).
140
141   2.3   M ULTI - VIEW AND PANORAMA V IDEO G ENERATION
142
143   The emergence of powerful open-source video diffusion models (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Chen
144
      et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) gave rise to the development of several approaches aimed at aug-
      menting them with multi-view capabilities (Watson et al., 2024) and extending them to generate
145
      360◦ panoramic videos. For example, 360DVD (Wang et al., 2024a) builds upon a pretrained text-
146
      to-video model (Guo et al., 2023) by adding a 360-adapter and fine-tuning it on equirectangular
147   projections of panoramic videos. This enables the creation of 360◦ videos from a text inputs, with
148   the option to condition on optical flow videos. Generative Camera Dolly (Van Hoorick et al., 2024)
149   extends the image-conditional Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023a) into a video-to-video
150   model. Given an input video of a scene, (Blattmann et al., 2023a) can generate a synchronized video
151   from a different camera trajectory. 4K4DGEN (Li et al., 2024c) draws inspiration from MultiDif-
152   fusion (Müller et al., 2024) and introduces a training-free method that denoises multiple views of
153   a spherical panorama simultaneously. Most similar to our method is Panacea (Wen et al., 2024),
154   which is inspired byVideoLDM (Blattmann et al., 2023a) and extends StableDiffusion by adding
155
      multi-view and temporal attention layers, trained on multi-view driving videos. Notably, Panacea
      relies on a dynamic birds’ eye view (BEV) representation as conditioning, which is most commonly
156
      available in the case of driving scenes, thus effectively limiting its applications to driving scenes.

      3.1   M ODEL D ESIGN
185
186
187   We train a multi-view video diffusion model that, given a text prompt and an optional set of con-
188   ditioning frames, is able to jointly generate multiple multi-view consistent videos of different view
189
      directions that together cover a full 360° panoramic video.
190   Our architecture builds on video latent diffusion models (VLDM) (Blattmann et al., 2023b) by in-
191   corporating multi-view attention layers inspired by MVDream (Shi et al., 2023b) and injecting view
192   direction embeddings into the model. Specifically, we add 3D multi-view self-attention layers that
193   perform self-attention across images from different views at each frame of the video. These layers
194   are combined with the existing 2D self-attention layers in a residual manner using zero-initialized
195
      convolutions, similar to ControlNets (Zhang et al., 2023). To provide the model with an understand-
      ing of viewing directions, we use ray direction representations that are the same height and width
196
      as the latent representations and encode the ray directions at each spatial location, following (Gao*
197
      et al., 2024). These rays are defined relative to the camera pose of the first view, and are invariant to
198   global 3D translations and rotations. The view embeddings are concatenated channel-wise with their
199   corresponding latents and are fed into the model at the first layer using zero-initialized convolutions.
200
201
      Given a set of target and optional conditioning frames of size 512 × 512 × 3, each image is encoded
      into a latent representation of size 64 × 64 × 4 using a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma,
202
      2013b). To enable conditioning on specific frames, we adopt the approach from CAT3D (Gao*
203
      et al., 2024). During training, the latents corresponding to the non-conditioned views are noised
204   according to the diffusion process, while the latents of the conditioning frames are kept mostly
205   clean. Following prior work (Ho et al., 2021), to improve robustness and prevent overfitting, we
206   use noise augmentation by adding a small amount of noise σ to the input conditioning latents and
207   pass this value σ to the model as well. A binary mask is concatenated channel-wise to distinguish
208   between the input conditioning latents and the target frames to be predicted. The diffusion model is
209   then trained to learn the joint distribution of these latent representations conditioned on the inputs.
210   We incorporate classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) by randomly dropping the
211   conditioning frames with a probability of 10% during training.
212   Finally, similar to prior works (Hoogeboom et al., 2023), we observe improved performance when
213   shifting the noise schedule towards higher noise levels, as our model generates more image frames
214   than the base video model. Please see Appendix A.2 for more details. We also find that using a v-
215   prediction objective (Salimans & Ho, 2022) leads to more stable training compared to ϵ-prediction,
      particularly with high-noise schedules.

      3.2     T RAINING S TRATEGY
235
236
      We initialize the model from a pretrained text-to-video diffusion model (Blattmann et al., 2023b)
237
      which has been trained on web-scale video data. Following prior works (Shi et al., 2023b), the
238   weights of the multi-view attention layers are initialized with the same weights as the existing 2D
239   self-attention layers to accelerate training.
240
241
      As we want to adjust the noise schedule (shifting toward higher noise levels) and change the
      model parametrization from ϵ-prediction to v-prediction without overfitting the model to our limited
242
      panorama videos, we train our model in two stages. In the first stage, we finetune the single-view
243
      text-to-video model from the existing checkpoint, adapting it to the new noise schedule and loss
244   objective. This stage is performed on a subset of the original pretraining data with standard cap-
245   tioned videos of 16 frames and requires minimal training time, as the model adapts quickly to these
246   changes. In the second stage, we freeze the spatial layers of the video model and finetune the rest
247   using multi-view video data.
248
      During training, we randomize both the number of views and video frames to enhance the model’s
249
      generalization and prevent overfitting to the limited 360◦ video data, effectively using this as a form
250   of data augmentation. The model is trained to generate multi-view video sequences represented as
251   view-frame matrices of varying sizes, such as 3 × 16, 4 × 12, 6 × 8, and 8 × 6, where the first
252   dimension refers to the number of views and the second to the number of frames. We refer to this
253   randomization as random matrix going forward. This allows the model to generalize to new view-
254   frame combinations, like 8 × 16 matrices, during inference—configurations that couldn’t fit in GPU
255   memory during training.
256   To handle multiple conditioning scenarios, we train a single general model that can generate multi-
257   view videos conditioned on text, video, or a combination of video and the first frame’s multi-view
258   images for autoregressive generation using a multi-task training strategy. Specifically, the binary
259   mask is randomized to reflect these different conditioning setups: all zeros (text conditioning), the
260   first column of ones with zeros elsewhere (video conditioning), or both the first row and first column
261   set to ones (autoregressive generation), with equal probability. See Fig. 3 for a visualization of the
262   different types of conditioning.
263
264   3.3     AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION OF L ONG V IDEOS
265
266   To generate long panoramic videos, we use an autoregressive approach (see Fig. 3). Initially, condi-
267   tioned on the first 16-frames of the input video, the model generates an 8 × 16 view-frame matrix.
268   For subsequent frames, the model is conditioned on the next 15 new frames of the video (a column)
269   and the last frame from all 8 views (a row) generated in the previous step. This iterative process
      allows us to generate long, coherent video sequences with smooth transitions and consistent motion.

      To facilitate fair evaluation, we use a common equirectangular format with resolution 512 × 1024
379   for a 16 frame long panoramic video and compose our multi-view results into it by warping each
380   of the images with bicubic interpolation. The pixel values in regions with overlap between views
381   are uniformly averaged. When evaluating our generations, we either directly evaluate the stitched
382   equirectangular video or following MVDiffusion, we crop 8 horizontal perspective view videos from
383   it, since some metrics are more naturally evaluated using perspective views as input.

      4.6   AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION
459
460   To demonstrate our model’s performance on long video generation, we run 4 iterations of autore-
461   gression, resulting in a total of 4 × 15 + 1 = 61 frames for the panorama videos. We observe that,
462   despite using noise augmentation, autoregressive errors gradually accumulate, causing the scene to
463   become blurry. To mitigate this, the noise-augmentation value can be increased during inference
464   to regenerate finer details, though this introduces slight flickering due to the newly added details.
465   Ideally, a dynamic system could be developed to increase the value when blurriness occurs and re-
466   duce it otherwise, minimizing flickering while keeping pixel quality high—an avenue we leave for
467   future work. We provide examples of extracted frames from our autoregressively generated videos
468
      in Fig. 1 and Fig. B1. Please see our website for best viewing of long video generations.

476   Random Matrix vs. Fixed Matrix. During training, we can fit a maximum of 6 time frames
477
      with 8 multi-views in memory. However, at inference we wish to generate 16 frames which is the
      native frame length for our base video model and aligns with 360DVD. To enable this we employ
478
      the randomized matrix strategy described in § 3.3. To evaluate the benefit of this strategy, we
479
      compare 8 × 16 video-conditional generations from a model that was trained with the “random-
480   matrix” strategy using an even mix of 8 × 6, 4 × 12 and 3 × 16 with one using a “fixed-matrix”
481   strategy trained with only the 8 × 6 setting. We include a quantitative comparison in Tab. 3 and
482   qualitative examples in Fig. 7. From the comparison, we see that the fixed-matrix trained model can
483   create more blurry regions in its generations which is also reflected in significantly higher FID and
484   FVD and somewhat lower clip score. Reconstruction metrics are very similar but slightly prefer the
485   fixed-matrix model. We hypothesize that focusing training more on the 8 view case could slightly
      improve the global color consistency at the cost of worse visual quality.

519   5     C ONCLUSION
520
521
      We present VideoPanda, a model for panoramaic video generation. VideoPanda augments a pre-
522   trained video diffusion model with the ability to generate consistent multiview videos that together
523   cover a full panoramic video. We train VideoPanda in a unified manner with flexible conditioning
524   supporting text and single-view video-conditioning and further support auto-regressive generation
525   of longer videos.
526
      Although VideoPanda demonstrates compelling results, there is still room for further improvement.
527
      The generation capabilities of our model are restricted by the performance of the base video model
528   and further improvements could be obtained by applying these techniques to more powerful video
529   diffusion models. Our model currently requires the field of view and elevation of the conditioning
530   input to be sufficiently close to the configuration used in training. This could be addressed by es-
531   timating these parameters as demonstrated by recent work in the image generation domain (Yuan
532   et al., 2024). Our autoregressive generation balances a trade-off between maintaining image qual-
533   ity over time and consistency between windows which motivates investigating methods that could
534   efficiently achieve both.
