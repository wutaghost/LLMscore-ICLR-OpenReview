009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Statistical watermarking is a technique used to embed a hidden signal in the prob-
012            ability distribution of text generated by large language models (LLMs), enabling
013            the attribution of the text to the originating model. We introduce the smooth-
014            ing attack and show that existing statistical watermarking methods are not robust
015
               against minor modifications of text. In particular, with the help of a weaker lan-
               guage model, an adversary can smooth out the distribution perturbation caused by
016
               watermarks. The resulting generated text achieves comparable quality to the orig-
017
               inal (unwatermarked) model while bypassing the watermark detector. Our attack
018            reveals a fundamental limitation of a wide range of watermarking techniques.
019
020
021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Large language models (LLMs) have made remarkable progress, posing the challenge of determin-
024   ing whether a text is written by a human or generated by AI (more specifically, by a given LLM).
025   One common solution to this challenge is watermarking the generated text (Aaronson, 2023; Christ
026   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), where the model provider subtly modifies the prob-
027
      ability distribution of the generated text (i.e., a sequence of tokens). For example, at each position
      of token generation, the likelihood of selecting from a subset of tokens (referred to as the “green
028
      list”) is slightly boosted, where the assignment of this subset is kept secret from the human users.
029
      This subtle statistical modification, while remaining largely unnoticeable to human users, can be
030   observed by the detector who knows the secret and attributed to the watermark (Kirchenbauer et al.,
031   2023a;b; Zhao et al., 2023a; Kuditipudi et al., 2023). Watermarking faces two main technical chal-
032   lenges, maintaining text quality and preventing easy removal of the watermark. In this work, we
033   focus on the second challenge and examine the resilience of statistical watermarking against attacks.
034
      Previous attacks typically rely on a stronger/larger reference model to erase watermarks, e.g., using
035
      a strong GPT model to paraphrase the watermarked texts generated from Llama2-7b (Kirchenbauer
036   et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2023a; Pan et al., 2024; Piet et al., 2023; Jovanović et al., 2024). However,
037   the assumption that the attacker has access to a stronger model undermines the realism of the attack
038   scenario, as such resources may not always be available in practice.
039
      In this paper, we attack statistical watermarking of LLMs under more practical conditions. In our
040
      setting, the attacker’s goal is not simply to remove the watermark at any cost, e.g., using a stronger
041
      model to paraphrase the watermarked text. Instead, we consider a more realistic attack scenario,
042   where the adversary only has access to a weaker reference language model. The central question
043   is: given access to a weaker model, can the attacker remove the watermark while maintaining the
044   quality of the text?
045
      We provide a positive answer in this paper, by presenting an attack algorithm, called the watermark
046
      smoothing attack. Our attack, which only queries the target watermarked model and a weaker ref-
047
      erence model through model APIs, is able to remove the watermark from the generated text while
048   maintaining its quality comparable to the original unwatermarked text. As suggested by its name,
049   the key component of our attack is to smooth the shift of token distributions caused by watermark-
050   ing. To do that, at each token position, we sample from a mixture of token distributions from the
051   reference model and the watermarked model, where the coefficient of the mixture depends on the
052   level of significance of the watermark. A higher level of significance means that the change caused
053   by the watermark is more noticeable; hence, we should assign a larger coefficient to the reference
      model to smooth out this change, thereby removing the watermark. Conversely, a lower level of sig-

      nificance means that the change caused by the watermark is not noticeable, in the first place; hence,
055   we should assign a larger coefficient to the watermarked model for better text quality.
056
057   The major advantage of our smoothing attack over existing ones, is that ours is watermark-agnostic
058
      as it effectively smooths out the distribution shift caused by watermarking, without knowing the
      exact set(s) of tokens that are more likely to get sampled and the exact level of increase in their like-
059
      lihoods. As a result, our method can be applied off-the-shelf to attack any statistical watermarking
060
      algorithm that relies on boosting the likelihood of sampling from specific tokens.
061
062   We conduct comprehensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our attack against eight ex-
063   isting representative watermark strategies on Llama2-7b (Touvron et al., 2023) and OPT-1.3b (Zhang
064
      et al., 2022). Notably, under certain setups, our attack removes the watermark completely, mean-
      ing that 0% of the generated text is detectable while maintaining high text quality. Meanwhile,
065
      under the same setup, the previous state-of-the-art attack (Piet et al., 2023) that uses the strong GPT-
066
      3.5-turbo (OpenAI, 2023a) to paraphrase the watermarked text fails to remove the watermarks in
067   48% of the text. We emphasize that our attack achieves this significant improvement with access
068   to only much weaker reference models such as TinyLlama-1.3b (Zhang et al., 2024b), and OPT-
069   125m (Zhang et al., 2022), confirming its practicality and effectiveness.

      2   P RELIMINARIES AND PROBLEM STATEMENT
073   Text generation of language models. We use M to denote a language model (LM) and V to denote
074   its associated vocabulary set (namely, the collection of all tokens). The LM generates a sequence of
075   tokens step by step, constituting the output text. During the generation, at each token position t, M
076   computes the logit scores of tokens at position t, written as a vector lt ∈ R|V| (where each dimension
077   corresponds to the logit of a specific token); after that M applies the softmax function (Bridle, 1989)
078   to lt , obtaining a probability distribution from which the t-th token is sampled. Two strategies are
079   often employed at this sampling step, top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018; Holtzman et al., 2018), which
080   samples a token from the k most probable candidates, and top-p/Nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al.,
081
      2019), which selects a token from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds
      a threshold p.
082
083   Statistical watermarking. The overall idea of statistical watermarking for LMs (Kirchenbauer
084   et al., 2023a;b; Zhao et al., 2023a) is to introduce a small shift to the probabilities for sampling a
085   specific set of tokens (which is not revealed to the users), in such a way that a detector algorithm
086   can identify this shift from a long text, whereas human users cannot. The canonical approach is as
087
      follows. At each token position t, a γ fraction of the vocabulary set is first selected as the green list
      (denoted as Gt ); next, the logit values for tokens in Gt are increased by δ while the logit values for
088
      the rest of the vocabulary remains unchanged.

103   Green token assignment. The assignment of the green token set Gt is random at each token position

111   Problem statement. We present an attack to analyze the robustness of statistical watermarking. We
112
      assume the attacker has API access to a reference model Mref , which is weaker compared to the
      original LM M ; since otherwise, he would have less motivation to attack the watermarked model,
113
      which rules out the paraphrasing attacks that leverage a strong LM (e.g., ChatGPT) to paraphrase
114
      the watermarked text (Zhang et al., 2024a). Regarding the knowledge of the watermarked model
115
      M̃ , we consider the realistic scenario that the adversary is unaware of the specific statistical wa-
116
      termarking strategies in use, e.g., the adversary does not know whether the assignment of Gt is
117
      context-dependent or not. The adversary can obtain the token sampled from the M̃ and Mref , as
118   well as the log probabilities of the most probable K tokens. This level of information is commonly
119   available, even when the model is closed-sourced, e.g., through OpenAI’s API 1 .

122   3       ATTACK FRAMEWORK
123
124   Recall Section 2 that the idea of statistical watermarking is to increase the sampling probability for a
125
      certain fraction of tokens (namely, the green tokens), by shifting their logit values. From a detector’s
      point of view, the trace of the watermark is said to be significant, when the actual occurrence of
126
      green tokens in the generated text from the watermarked model are significantly higher than any
127
      other unwatermarked model (which is roughly γT for texts of length T ).
128
129   Our attack exploits the above observation to bypass a detector. Consider the following extreme
130   scenario(s). When generating the token for position t, the unwatermarked model already assigns
131
      high logit values for some green tokens (alternatively, some red tokens), possibly due to the inherent
      characteristics of the model or just by chance. In such scenarios, we would not expect that adding δ
132
      to the logit values of the green tokens that are too high (or too low) will cause a significant difference
133
      in the actual occurrence of a green token at this position. In other words, this δ shift is not likely
134   to increase the z-score. Accordingly, as an attacker, we can just use the watermarked model for
135   this position without modifying the output distribution. Conversely, in the not-so-extreme scenarios
136   where the logit values for the green tokens are neither too high nor too low, the δ shift applied to
137   the green tokens could have a significant influence on the z-score. In such cases, we smooth out this
138   influence, by combining the token distributions of the watermarked model with those of a reference
139   model to generate the next token.
140   Overall, our attack runs as follows. When generating the token at position t, (i) we first estimate
141   the significance level of the watermark, which is defined as the relative increase in the probability of
142   generating green tokens from the watermarked model compared to the unwatermarked model; (ii)
143   if the significance level is high, then we combine the token distributions of the watermarked model
144   and the reference model to generate the t-th token; otherwise, we generate the token from the water-
145   marked model directly. Note that our attack is not designed for any statistical watermarking scheme
146   in specific. Instead, our attack is universally applicable to all statistical watermarking schemes, as
147   it directly aims at smoothing out the change in the actual occurrence of green tokens caused by the
148
      watermark. We describe our attack in more detail next.

305   Agnostic smoothing. Our attack is agnostic to how the statistical watermark is embedded, e.g., Un-
306   igram watermark (Zhao et al., 2023b) or KGW watermark (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a). Regardless
307   of the strategy for choosing the green token set Gt and internal details of the watermarked model,
308   we apply a universal smoothing over the watermarked model’s token distribution by combining it
309   with that of a reference model. Here the level of smoothness depends on the significance level of
310   the watermark, i.e., how much the watermark model agrees with the unwatermarked model, which,
311   again, can be estimated accurately without any knowledge of the model or watermarking strategy.
312   Practicality. Our attack is practical, as it leverages the reference model and watermarked model as
313   black boxes. Besides, the reference model is much weaker than the target watermarked model. Our
314   practical attack provides more insight into the robustness analysis of statistical watermarking–not
315   only is it vulnerable under the paraphrasing attack (Piet et al., 2023) that leverages a strong model,
316   but also not resilient against our smoothing attack where the attacker only gets help from a much
317   weaker model that contains an order of magnitude fewer parameters (as we will see next in the
318
      experiment). Our findings call for stronger watermark strategies for LLMs.

      Attacks. Attack baselines include Word-D, which randomly deletes a word at a specified ratio
342
      and Word-S, which randomly substitutes a word with its synonyms using WordNet (Miller, 1995).
343
      Following (Pan et al., 2024), we set the ratio to be 0.3 for Word-D and to be 0.5 for Word-S.
344   We also include the strong baseline P-GPT3.5 (Piet et al., 2023) that paraphrases the given text
345   based on the GPT-3.5-turbo using the prompt: “Please rewrite the following text:”. It is important
346   to note that this attack assumes a significantly stronger attacker than ours (Smoothing). We also
347   include the reference model (Reference) and the unwatermarked model (Unwatermarkd) in our
348   comparison. For our smoothing attack, we set the threshold τ to 0.5 by default, unless specified
349   otherwise. Namely, whenever the confidence level is smaller than 0.5, we always sample from the
350   reference model rather than the mixture of the reference and unwatermarked model.
351   Metrics. To measure the effectiveness of watermark removal, we compute the z-score (defined
352   in equation 2) on the generated text. Lower values indicate fewer traces of watermarks in the gener-
353   ated text and greater success for the attacker. We also report the positive prediction rate (PPR) when
354   using the default threshold on the z-score, which denotes the proportion of generated text identified
355   as watermarked. There are two types of texts, positive samples (those generated from the water-
356   marked model with/without attacks) and negative samples (those written by humans or generated by
357   unwatermarked text). PPR reflects the True Positive Rate when computing on positive samples and
358
      the False Positive Rate when computing on negative samples.
359   To measure the text quality, we follow prior work (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Pan et al., 2024) and
360   compute the perplexity using an oracle model. We use OPT-2.7b for texts generated by OPT models,
361   and Llama2-13b for those from Llama models. We also measure the negative log-likelihood (loss)
362   of the unwatermarked model on the generated text to assess how likely the unwatermarked model
363   would have produced it. Smaller perplexities and losses indicate better text quality. We also utilize
364
      GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b) as an evaluator of accuracy, consistency, and style, scoring on a scale of
      1 to 10, similar to the approach in (Jovanović et al., 2024), with higher scores indicating better
365
      performance. The prompt template used for evaluation is provided in Appendix A.4.

479
480   Early watermark solutions embed watermarks by altering text through synonyms (Topkara et al.,
481   2005; 2006b), changing sentence structure (Topkara et al., 2006a), or injecting invisible to-

485   altering the output token distributions (Zhao et al., 2023a; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Lee et al.,
      2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Wu et al.). On the contrary,

503   son, 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Dathathri et al., 2024; Hu et al.) and hence, are inherently more
504   vulnerable under our threat model, since the adversary can directly sample the next token from the
505   watermark-free top-K token probabilities.
506   We focus on evaluating the robustness of watermarking schemes via attacks. One canonical attack
507   is to disrupt the structure of embedded watermarks, making them undetectable by injecting certain
508   characters, homoglyphs, or emojis into the target text (Gabrilovich & Gontmakher, 2002; Helfrich
509   & Neff, 2012; Pajola & Conti, 2021; Boucher et al., 2022; Goodside, 2023).
510
      Another approach involves utilizing a separate large language model (LLM) to paraphrase the target
511   text (Sadasivan et al., 2023; Krishna et al., 2023; Piet et al., 2023), where the paraphrasing LLM is
512   significantly larger than the reference model targeted by our attack. Building on this, Jovanović et al.
513   (2024) and Zhang et al. (2024a) introduce advanced methods that incorporate additional models to
514   complement the paraphrasing LLM. Specifically, Jovanović et al. (2024) employs an auxiliary ref-
515   erence model to analyze how watermarks are embedded. Such analysis requires prior knowledge of
516   the watermarking algorithm (e.g., the choice of h) as well as access to a large set of texts generated
517   from the watermarked model. In contrast, our attack does not have such requirements. Zhang et al.
518   (2024a) enhances paraphrasing attack by integrating it with a reward model (termed as quality oracle
519
      model), which evaluates candidates generated by the paraphrasing LLM (termed as perturbation ora-
      cle model). In particular, multiple candidates are generated for each target text and the reward model
520
      selects the best one. In comparison, our attack generates only a single candidate. Despite these dif-
521
      ferences, our attack—using only TinyLLM with 1.3 billion parameters—outperforms existing para-
522   phrasing attacks that rely on much larger models, such as GPT-3.5 with 175 billion parameters, as
523   demonstrated in Table 1. Thus, our attack proves to be not only practical and cost-efficient but also
524   highly effective, illustrating that even a relatively low-resource adversary can bypass watermark-
525   ing. These findings underscore the critical need for the development of more resilient watermarking
526   strategies. Further details and comparisons are provided in Appendix A.5.

      6   C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this work, we present the smoothing attack to remove statistical watermarks that are embedded
531   into LLMs. The core idea is to smooth out the increased probability of sampling green tokens due
532   to the embedded watermark. Our method is agnostic to the specific watermarking algorithm used
533   and relies on more realistic assumptions on the attacker. Through comprehensive evaluations, we
534   demonstrated that our attack can successfully remove watermarks from a wide spectrum of wa-
535   termarking algorithms while preserving the quality of the generated text. Our findings highlight
536   vulnerabilities in existing statistical watermarking techniques.
537   Our attack removes watermarks at the cost of a quality drop in some cases, leaving room for future
538   improvement. Besides, designing more advanced watermarking techniques for LLMs that are robust
539   against our smoothing attack is also worth looking into.

739   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION OF THE WATERMARK ALGORITHMS
740
      We evaluate the smoothing attack on eight different watermarking algorithms, including
741
      KGW (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a), Unigram (Zhao et al., 2023a), SWEET (Lee et al., 2023),
742   UPV (Liu et al., 2023), EWD (Lu et al., 2024), SIR (Liu et al., 2024), X-SIR (He et al., 2024),
743   and DIP (Wu et al.). We use the implementations and default configurations provided by Mark-
744   LLM (Pan et al., 2024), which is included in the code submission. For completeness, we provide
745   details of these algorithms below.
746
747          • KGW (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a): The green set Gt at each position t is selected based on
748            the previous h tokens and a secret key known to the service provider. The hyperparameters
749            are set as follows: γ = 0.5, δ = 2.0, the threshold on the z-score is 4, and h = 1.
750          • Unigram (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a): The green set Gt is fixed for each token t and each
751            prefix, depending solely on the secret key known to the service provider. No dynamic
752            updates are performed based on previous tokens. The parameters are: γ = 0.5, δ = 2.0.
753          • SWEET (Lee et al., 2023): A shift is applied only when the entropy of the probability
754            distribution at position t is high, improving text quality, particularly for code generation
755            tasks. The parameters are set as: γ = 0.5, δ = 2.0, the threshold on the z-score is 4, the
               entropy threshold is 0.9, and h = 1.0.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
             • UPV (Liu et al., 2023): The green token selection process is similar to the previous ap-
757            proaches. However, this method requires training two additional models: a generator net-
758            work to separate red and green tokens and a detector network for classification based on
759            the input text. The watermarks are introduced using γ = 0.5, δ = 2.0, and h = 1.0. The
760            detector produces a binary prediction rather than a continuous score like a z-score.
761
             • EWD (Lu et al., 2024): Watermark introduction follows a similar process as the previous
762            methods. The hyperparameters are γ = 0.5, δ = 2.0, and h = 1.0. During detection,
763            tokens are assigned different weights based on their entropy, with higher entropy tokens
764            receiving greater weight to improve detectability in low-entropy scenarios.
765
             • SIR (Liu et al., 2024): This method trains a generator network to convert token embeddings
766            into context-aware biases, enhancing robustness against semantic invariant tampering. The
767            z-score threshold is set to 0.2.
768
             • X-SIR (He et al., 2024): Instead of operating at the token level, the red-green partition is
769
               applied at the level of semantic clusters, grouping similar words together and adding bias
770
               at the group level. This improves robustness against Cross-lingual Watermark Removal
771            Attacks (CWRA). The z-score threshold is set to 0.2.
772
             • DIP (Wu et al.): Similar to Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), this method selects green tokens
773
               but uses a distribution-preserving reweight function to adjust token probabilities. This
774
               increases the probability of green tokens while maintaining the overall distribution. The
775            reweighting is controlled by the parameter α. The hyperparameters are set as γ = 0.5,
776            h = 5, the z-score threshold is 1.53, and α = 0.45.

      A.5   D ETAILED C OMPARISON WITH Z HANG ET AL . (2024 A )
865
866   Compare with Jovanović et al. (2024). Jovanović et al. (2024) proposed a watermark-stealing
867   attack requiring knowledge of the watermarking algorithm, a large dataset from the watermarked
868   model, and access to a paraphrasing model and a reference model. In contrast, our smoothing at-
869   tack requires neither prior knowledge of the watermark nor extensive data and a strong reference
870   model. We utilize a small reference model compared to the target model. Their approach assumes
871   the adversary only has access to sampled tokens. Our attack leverages additional information typi-
872
      cally available from the API, namely the probabilities of the top-K tokens. Since our threat models
      are different, our algorithms are orthogonal and can be applied in different threat model settings
873
      accordingly.
874
875
      Compare with Zhang et al. (2024a). Zhang et al. (2024a) propose using reference models to
876
      remove the watermark. The main difference is in the capability of the adversary (our attack has
877   fewer constraints on the adversary’s capability) and the costs to run the attacks (our attack is less
878   costly to run). In addition, our attack is also more effective in removing watermarks.
879
880
      Adversary’s capability: In our attack, we use a weaker model of the same type as the target wa-
      termarked model. For example, we use TinyLlama-1.3B to attack Llama-7B, which captures the
881
      capability of an adversary in practice. The attack by Zhang et al. (2024a) demands a more capable
882
      adversary, who has access to a perturbation oracle model (which generates a candidate for the given
883   watermarked text) and a quality oracle model (which assigns a score for the candidate output by the
884   perturbation model). When attacking Llama-7B, Zhang et al. (2023) use T5-XL v1.1 of 2.8B as the
885   perturbation model and RoBERTa-v3 large of 335M as the oracle model.
886
      Cost: Our attack also requires fewer computation resources, as it makes fewer queries to the refer-
887
      ence model. In particular, we query the reference model (e.g., Llama-1.3B) only when the entropy
888
      for predicting the current token is high, and stops querying after producing the final token. The
889   outcome is a single candidate for the prompt. As a comparison, Zhang et al. (2024a)’s perturbation
890   oracle model (e.g., T5-XL v1.1 of 2.8B) generates multiple candidates (e.g., 200) for the prompt,
891   leading to much higher computational costs.
892
      We have benchmarked the attack costs on the KGW watermark using two NVIDIA TITAN RTX
893
      GPUs (24GB)—our attack takes around 30 seconds whereas Zhang et al. (2024a) takes around 800
894
      seconds.
895
896   Effectiveness: The attack algorithm in Zhang et al. (2024a) can be seen as a type of paraphrasing
897   attack. In our paper, we included a competitor that seems stronger than Zhang et al.’s attack—the
898
      paraphrase attack using GPT3.5 (175B parameters). Notably, our attack achieves lower z-scores
      than this paraphrasing attack (see Table 1), which, in turn, implies that our attack is better than
899
      Zhang et al. (2024a).
900
901   Overall, our attack is more practical, efficient, and effective. In other words, our attack reveals more
902   vulnerability to the existing statistical watermarks (we do not even need a strong adversary to break
903   the watermarks).

905   A.6   D ISTORTION - FREE WATERMARKS .
906
      Recent distortion-free watermarks Kuditipudi et al. (2023); Dathathri et al. (2024); Hu et al. em-
907
      bed the watermarks during the sampling process, which do not alter the token distributions of the
908
      original model. In our setting, as the adversary can observe the probability for the top-K tokens, he
909   can directly sample the next token based on the obtained probabilities, successfully removing the
910   watermark. In other words, attacking distortion-free watermarks is a trivial task under our threat
911   model (hence, we attack non-distortion-free schemes).

913   A.7   L IMITATION & P OSSIBLE D EFENSES
914
915   Our attack leverages the correlation between the significance level of the watermark and the un-
916   certainty in predicting the next token. To mitigate this risk, a service provider might limit access
917   to information about prediction uncertainty, such as only returning the most likely token without
      probability information.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      The intuition behind this defense lies in the design of our attack, which depends on estimating the
919   significance level of the watermark to generate the next token. When the number of tokens or associ-
920   ated probabilities is reduced, this estimation becomes less accurate. An inaccurate estimation of the
921   significance level would disrupt the attacker’s decision-making regarding when to use the reference
922   or watermarked model, ultimately degrading the quality of the generated text and/or leaving some
923   watermark traces.
924
      However, this defense may not be practical to implement. For instance, many existing LLM services
925   provide probabilities for the most likely tokens (e.g., OpenAI’s API returns the top-20 tokens and
926   their probabilities), which is already sufficient for running our attack. Additionally, restricting ac-
927   cess to such information could negatively impact user experience, as this data is often essential for
928   features like output customization, explainability, debugging, interpretability, evaluation, and mon-
929   itoring. Consequently, such information is typically available to users—and, by extension, to our
930   attack.
931   Our paper advocates for exploring new methods of designing watermarks that account for the inher-
932   ent uncertainty in model predictions.
