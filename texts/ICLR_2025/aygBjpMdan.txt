011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013             As Large Language Models (LLMs) scale up and gain powerful Chain-of-Thoughts
014             (CoTs) reasoning abilities, practical resource constraints drive efforts to distill these
015             capabilities into more compact Smaller Language Models (SLMs). We find that
016
                CoTs consist mainly of simple reasoning forms, with a small proportion (≈ 4.7%)
                of key reasoning steps that truly impact conclusions. However, previous distillation
017
                methods typically involve supervised fine-tuning student SLMs only on correct
018
                CoTs data produced by teacher LLMs, resulting in students struggling to learn the
019             key reasoning steps, instead imitating the teacher’s reasoning forms and making
020             errors or omissions on these steps. To address these issues, drawing an analogy
021             to human learning, where analyzing mistakes according to correct solutions often
022             reveals the crucial steps leading to successes or failures, we propose mistakE-
023             Driven key reasonIng step distillaTion (EDIT), a novel method that further aids
024             SLMs learning key reasoning steps rather than mere simple fine-tuning. Firstly, to
025             expose these crucial steps in CoTs, we design specific prompts to generate dual
026             CoTs data with similar reasoning paths but divergent conclusions. Then, we apply
027
                the minimum edit distance algorithm on the dual CoTs data to locate these key
                steps and optimize the likelihood of these steps. Extensive experiments validate
028
                the effectiveness of EDIT across both in-domain and out-of-domain benchmark
029
                reasoning datasets. Further analysis shows that EDIT can generate high-quality
030             CoTs with more correct key reasoning steps. Notably, we also explore how different
031             mistake patterns affect performance and find that EDIT benefits more from logical
032             errors than from knowledge or mathematical calculation errors in dual CoTs1 .

035   1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037   With the rapid growth in model size and pre-training data, LLMs have demonstrated impressive CoT
038   reasoning performance in natural language processing (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al.,
039   2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023b). However, due to the giant model architecture and
040   massive parameters (e.g. GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) with 175 billion parameters), the deployment of
041   LLMs in resource-constrained environments becomes challenging.
042   To address this, researchers (Xu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b) have explored distilling knowledge
043   from LLMs into smaller language models (SLMs) via instruction-tuning, as seen in LMs like Alpaca
044   (Taori et al., 2023) and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023). Despite progress, these distilled models often
045   struggle with complex causal reasoning. To enhance this capability, some studies (Magister et al.,
046   2023; Ho et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Zhou & Ai, 2024) explore distilling the CoT
047   reasoning ability from LLMs of over 100B parameters (Wei et al., 2022a;b) by fine-tuning on CoTs
048
      data annotated by teacher LLMs, known as standard CoTs distillation. Besides, other studies (Hsieh
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) propose distilling CoTs within a multi-task learning
049
      framework by incorporating additional objectives. However, CoTs usually consist mainly of simple
050
      reasoning forms, with a small proportion (≈ 4.7%2 ) of key reasoning steps that are pivotal moments

073   of the above methods is the simple Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) paradigm, where the student
074   model is trained solely on the teacher’s correct reasoning data. This paradigm may result in students
075   struggling to learn the key reasoning steps, instead imitating the teacher’s reasoning forms
076   and making errors or omissions on these steps, as illustrated in Figure 1. Drawing an analogy
077   to human learning, where analyzing mistakes according to correct solutions often reveals the key
078
      reasoning steps leading to successes or failures, we propose a novel mistakE-Driven key reasonIng
      step distillaTion (EDIT). This approach focuses on dual CoTs data, encompassing both positive
079
      and negative examples of teachers’ reasoning. By examining dual CoTs, students can identify and
080
      learn from the crucial reasoning steps, thereby improving their CoTs. Specifically, we first retain
081   all CoTs data annotated by the teacher, irrespective of correctness. Subsequently, we design two
082   comprehensive prompts to instruct teachers to produce dual CoTs that share similar intermediate
083   reasoning steps but lead to divergent conclusions. Finally, we utilize the minimum edit distance
084   algorithm to locate key reasoning steps in dual CoTs, as shown in Figure 3, and then utilize a
085   fine-grained loss function to optimize the likelihood of these steps.
086
      Extensive experiments show that the student models distilled by EDIT exhibits higher performance
087   and generalization than the baselines on both in-domain (IND) and out-of-domain (OOD) benchmark
088   reasoning datasets. Further analyses indicate that EDIT can generate higher-quality CoTs with more
089   correct key reasoning steps by auto evaluation and case studies. Notably, we also show EDIT can
090   benefit more from logical mistake patterns than knowledge or mathematical calculation errors in dual
091   CoTs, potentially paving the way for future research on the efficient use of mistakes.
092
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
093
094       1. We reveal a shortfall in the previous distillation methods, where the simple SFT paradigm may
095          result in students mimicking the teacher’s reasoning forms but making errors or omissions in
096          key reasoning steps, thus diminishing the versatility of CoTs.
097       2. We propose mistake-driven key reasoning step distillation, which allows students to learn key
098          reasoning steps from our specifically designed dual CoTs data, further improving reasoning.
099       3. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our method across both IND and OOD
100          datasets, showing that EDIT can reduce errors in key reasoning steps for students.
101
          4. We investigate how different mistake patterns impact EDIT and find that logical errors provide
102          the more significant benefits than knowledge or mathematical calculation errors.

105   2     R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   CoT Reasoning. The emergent ability appears in LLMs across a wide range of NLP tasks (Chowdhery
      et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022a). One such ability is CoT reasoning, which involves generating a


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      series of intermediate reasoning steps. This ability has been further explored recently with the
109   release of OpenAI’s o1 model (OpenAI, 2024). While CoT prompting techniques (Wei et al., 2022b)
110   significantly enhance the problem-solving capabilities of models (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
111   2023b; Huang et al., 2023), it has little effect on smaller models (Wei et al., 2022a). Chung et al.
112   (2022) suggest that CoT reasoning can be induced in SLMs via instruction tuning on CoTs data. Our
113   work show that the CoT capabilities of SLMs can be further improved by learning from key reasoning
114   steps in dual CoTs data.
115   Knowledge Distillation from LLMs. There has been a lot of work dedicated to distilling knowledge
116   (Hinton et al., 2015) from powerful proprietary LLMs, e.g. ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023a) in a black-box
117   setting. However, most of these works primarily focus on the general ability distillation by instruction
118   tuning on large and diverse datasets (Peng et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024). In
119   contrast, we aim to distill the CoT reasoning capabilities from LLMs same as the standard CoTs
120   distillation (Magister et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2023). Besides, some studies (Li et al., 2022; Hsieh
121   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) employ LLM’s rationale or self-evaluation output to enhance SLM’s
122   reasoning in a multi-task learning framework. Fu et al. (2023) fine-tune SLMs on four types of
123
      reasoning data to ensure out-of-distribution generalization. Wang et al. (2023c) distill SLMs by
      learning from self-reflection and feedback from LLMs in an interactive multi-round paradigm. Chen
124
      et al. (2024) maximize the mutual information between multi objectives for CoTs distillation. Ranaldi
125
      & Freitas (2024) use in-family and out-family teachers to generate more CoTs for fine-tuning students.
126   Different from the above works, we assist CoTs distillation with teachers’ mistakes to alleviate the
127   style imitation of teachers’ reasoning.
128
129
      Learning from Mistakes. Recent studies use mistake data to enhance the performance of LMs.
      Shinn et al. (2023) propose Reflexion that allows the LLM agent to self-reflect from its mistakes.
130
      Wang & Li (2023) introduce a study assistant that collects and retrieves LLMs’ training mistakes to
131
      guide future inferences. Li et al. (2023) propose CoK that corrects potential mistakes in the rationale
132   by retrieving knowledge to avoid error propagation. However, both of the above methods require
133   the models to be large enough to have basic CoT reasoning or instruction-following capabilities,
134   which is almost impossible to occur in vanilla SLMs. Wang et al. (2023a) propose fine-tuning on
135   counterfactual data to ensure the faithful reasoning of the student model. An et al. (2023) propose
136   LEMA that fine-tunes language models on corrected mistake data, where the mistakes are collected
137   from various LLMs e.g. LLaMA2-70B (Touvron et al., 2023), WizardLM-70B (Xu et al., 2023), and
138   corrected by GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b). Additionally, Sun et al. (2024) propose Retrieved In-Context
139   Principles, which retrieve mistakes to provide customized guidance and improve model performance
140
      during inference. In contrast, we collect the teachers’ mistakes to create a dual CoTs dataset for
      further key reasoning steps learning on model distillation.

      We present the overview of our proposed method in Figure 2. Concretely, (1) unlike prior works
146
      (Magister et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023) that only focus on correct CoTs annotated by teacher LLMs,
147
      we first retain all CoTs reasoning data, regardless of its correctness. (2) Then based on the previously
148   retained correct and wrong CoTs, we construct dual CoTs datasets consisting of positive-negative CoT
149   pairs that follow similar intermediate reasoning steps but lead to divergent conclusions. Specifically,
150   we design two comprehensive contextual prompts to instruct teacher LLMs to rectify the originally
151   wrong CoTs and corrupt originally correct CoTs. (3) Finally, we distill the student SLMs by training
152   on the teacher’s correct CoTs reasoning data and further Key Reasoning Steps Learning (KRSL) on
153   the dual CoTs datasets.

231

      In-domain (IND) Dataset: BIG-Bench Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2023) consists of 27 chal-
285
      lenging tasks that span arithmetic, symbolic reasoning, etc. This collection is mainly composed
286
      of multiple-choice questions, along with a minority of open-ended questions. To underscore the
287   superiority of our method, we divide the BBH dataset for each subtask into a training set (BBH-train)
288   for distillation and a test set (BBH-test) for in-domain evaluation, following a 4:1 ratio.
289
290   Out-of-domain (OOD) Dataset: (1) BIG-Bench Sub (BB-sub) is derived from the BIG-Bench
291   (BB) (Guo et al., 2023a), which includes 203 tasks covering linguistics, mathematics, common-sense
292   reasoning, etc. To simplify our evaluation, we refine the selection of tasks from BB by identifying
293   those associated with keywords such as "multiple-choice" and "reasoning."4 Additionally, we exclude
294   any tasks that are part of the BBH dataset, narrowing our pool to 61 distinct subtasks. For each
295   of these subtasks, we randomly sample up to 100 instances, culminating the BB-sub dataset. (2)
296
      AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023) is a benchmark that assesses LMs on reasoning capabilities using
      human exams across various fields, including English, Math, Law, and Logic. We focused on the
297
      English multiple-choice questions within this benchmark to evaluate our method’s effectiveness. (3)
298
      AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC) (Clark et al., 2018) comprises ARC-Easy and ARC-Challenge
299   from middle and high school science exams. ARC-E features simpler questions, while ARC-C
300   includes more challenging ones. We use their test sets for evaluation. Detailed statistics for all
301   mentioned benchmarks are provided in Appendix B.6.1. BigBench, AGIEval, and ARC are standard
302   benchmarks for evaluating LLMs reasoning performance. Specifically, BigBench and AGIEval have
303   been employed in related works (Fu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b), and ARC is frequently used in
304   technical reports for LLaMA3 (AI@Meta, 2024) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b).
305
306   Models & Implementation Details. We employ the widely-used open-source language model,
307   LLaMA2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), as our student SLM. For the teacher model, given its performance
308
      and cost-effectiveness, we employ OpenAI’s advanced black-box LLM, ChatGPT, specifically using
      the "gpt-3.5-turbo-0613" variant for extracting CoTs with the same manual prompt that is
309
      used in (Suzgun et al., 2023). We employ LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning
310
      of the student SLMs. We empirically set α in KRSL as 1.0 and β as 0.025. Our experiments leverage
311   a mixed-precision training strategy, carried out on 4 × A100 GPUs. We employ vLLM5 (Kwon et al.,
312   2023) to enhance inference speed, using a greedy decoding method for text generation on a single
313   A100 GPU. More training details and hyperparameter settings can be found in Appendix B.6.2.
314
315   Baselines. We compare EDIT with the following baselines: (1) Teacher & Vanilla Student under
316   various settings, e.g., Zero-shot (+ CoT) or Few-shot (+ CoT). (2) Std-CoT (Magister et al., 2023),
317   which is a standard CoTs distillation method that directly fine-tunes student SLMs on CoTs data. (3)
318   MT-CoT (Li et al., 2022) is a multi-task CoTs distillation strategy that aims to optimize both the
319   prediction of answers and the learning of CoTs concurrently. (4) SCOTT (Wang et al., 2023a) aims
320
      to bolster the reasoning consistency in the student SLMs by integrating counterfactual data into its
      training regimen.

      Can CoT distillation improve the performance of students? From the table, it is evident that the

      reasoning ability of LLMs can be effectively transferred to SLMs by distilling CoTs.

361   key steps learning, respectively? Ablation results in the table show that removing the rectified

366   improving the reasoning ability of the students. Additionally, we note that although KRSL and DPO

      5.5   M ISTAKE PATTERN M INING
508
509   In this subsection, we delve into the influence of various mistake patterns on the EDIT. Based on the
510   observation of mistake data, we utilize GPT-3.5 to categorize them into four types, including Logical
511   Errors (LEs), Knowledge Errors (KEs), Mathematical Calculation Errors (MCEs) and Other
512   Errors (OEs). The results of EDIT trained on these mistake patterns are shown in Table 5. We can
513   see that KRSL on DLEs consistently outperforms other mistake patterns, with KEs and MCEs having
514   a relatively smaller impact. This suggests that LEs provide a broader range of reasoning patterns that
515   are relevant for mathematical, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning. As for KEs and MCEs, since
516   these types of mistakes are more specific compared to LEs, it is not easy for the model to learn a
517
      general reasoning solution from these mistakes. Therefore, learning the key reasoning steps from
      logical reasoning errors is the most effective way among them.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we propose a novel mistake-driven key reasoning step distillation method to alleviate
532   student imitation of teachers’ reasoning forms. First, we preserve all CoTs data annotated by teacher
533   LLMs, irrespective of correctness. Using these data, we design two comprehensive prompts to guide
534   teacher LLMs in generating dual CoTs data. Finally, we utilize the minimum edit distance algorithm
535   to identify the key reasoning steps and employ a fine-grained loss function for guided learning.
536   Extensive experiments demonstrate EDIT’s effectiveness in enhancing student SLMs’ reasoning
537
      capabilities, outperforming baseline methods on both in-domain and out-of-domain benchmark
      datasets. We hope our work can make the community attach the importance of learning key reasoning
538
      steps in dual CoTs, collectively advancing the efficiency of CoT reasoning distillation.

849   B.5      KRSL V. S . DPO
850
851
      We note that the learning objectives of KRSL, utilizing both positive and negative examples, closely
      resemble preference alignment algorithms like RLHF and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023). Specifically,
852
      both KRSL and DPO are directly supervised learning paradigms. However, there are key differences:
853
854     1. KRSL requires the model to learn from highly similar positive and negative samples (dual CoTs)
855        for identifying key reasoning steps while DPO usually uses completely different positive and
856        negative samples from human preference data.
857
        2. In DPO, the loss function involves summing the negative log-likelihoods across all token
858        positions in the target text. This approach can struggle to differentiate rewards for texts with
859        high similarity since identical tokens dominate the sequence, and only a small portion of tokens
860        differ. In long sequences, the influence of these differing tokens on the overall loss is minimal,
861        potentially causing convergence issues.
862
863   In contrast, KRSL utilizes a minimum edit distance algorithm to pinpoint key texts in dual CoTs
      and precisely optimize the logits for these tokens, ignoring identical ones. This makes KRSL more

       B.6.2     H YPERPARAMETERS S ETTINGS
1001
1002   In our study, we ensure consistency in the hyperparameter settings across all baselines, including our
1003   proposed EDIT approach, to maintain the fairness of our comparative analysis. Here, we detail the
1004   hyperparameter configurations employed in our experiments.
1005
1006   Training Steps and Batch Size. The number of training steps is determined based on the size of
1007   the training dataset, the batch size, and the number of gradient accumulation steps required. We
1008   maintain a consistent batch size across all baselines to eliminate any performance discrepancies that
1009   could arise from varying batch sizes.
1010
1011   Learning Rate. Our initial exploratory experiments focused on the standard CoTs distillation
1012   method using the LLaMA-2 model. We found that while the batch size had minimal impact on
1013   performance, the learning rate was a critical factor. We tested learning rates of 1e-4, 2e-4, and 3e-4,
1014   observing optimal performance at 2e-4 across the standard CoT and other distillation baselines, as
1015   well as our EDIT approach. Consequently, we set the learning rate to 2e-4 for all methods involved in
1016   our study.
1017
1018   Epochs and Evaluation Strategy. Throughout our training process, we monitored the training loss
1019   curve and noted that it generally plateaued by the 15th epoch, indicating that the models had achieved
1020   convergence. Therefore, we set the number of epochs to 15 for 7B models. The process of determining
1021   the number of epochs for other model sizes followed a similar pattern. To mitigate the potential
1022
       risk of overfitting and to ensure our evaluation reflects the most effective model configuration, we
       systematically selected checkpoints from the epoch that demonstrated the best performance on the
1023
       IND task. These checkpoints were then used to evaluate performance on OOD tasks.
1024
1025   The hyperparameters in training and inference can be found in Table 18 and Table 15 respectively. In
       the KRSL, the second phase training in EDIT, the learning rate is empirically set as 5e-6.
