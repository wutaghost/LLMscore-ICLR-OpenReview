                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               We consider the problem of training an optical character recognition (OCR) model
012            to read short alphanumeric scene-text sequences, such as number plates or vehi-
013            cle type labels, in scenarios where labelled training images are limited in quantity
014            and sequence variety. OCR models may under-perform in these scenarios, so
015            we explore whether a diffusion model can be trained on the small set of labelled
016            images, to generate synthetic images with similar background statistics but new
017            character sequences. We find that a diffusion model struggles to generate charac-
018            ters in positions of the sequence where they did not appear during training. We
019            address this problem by introducing SADE: a scene-text autoregressive diffusion
020
               engine that generates multiple image parts one by one, conditioned on previously
               generated image parts for visual coherency. This approach reduces the effective
021
               number of possible positions for a character, and increases the diffusion model’s
022
               ability to generate characters in novel positions of the full sequence. Our results
023            indicate that SADE can indeed lead to substantial improvements in OCR accuracy
024            in data-scare scenarios, particularly on sequences with characters at positions not
025            encountered in the original training data.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Optical character recognition (OCR) is the computer vision task of extracting and identifying text
031   from an input image. For applications such as document digitization, there is often an abundance of
032   training data and the text might be relatively easy to extract and interpret. For scenarios involving
033
      scene-text captured in less constrained environments, and where training data is limited in quantity
      and variety, OCR performance can degrade substantially. In such scenarios, an OCR model may
034
      struggle to recognize characters appearing in positions that were infrequently or never encountered
035
      in the training data, posing limitations on the model’s ability to generalize to new sequences not seen
036   during training. A practical example is when a motor vehicle manufacturer uses OCR to validate the
037   type label on the rear of the vehicle. The introduction of a new vehicle type may render the model
038   unreliable until additional training data is collected. Off-the-shelf pretrained OCR models, while
039   effective for general OCR tasks involving natural language, might fail to meet the required perfor-
040   mance of specialized scenarios like vehicle type label recognition, where character-level accuracy is
041   vital.
042   A common trend in the literature to achieving state-of-the-art performance on OCR tasks is to take
043   a data-centric approach (Liao et al., 2019; Yim et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023), rather than improving
044   the OCR technology itself. In line with this perspective, we explore the use of a synthetic data
045   generator to bridge the performance gap of OCR for sequences seen during training and those with
046   characters in positions infrequently or never seen during training. An off-the-shelf generative model
047   is not suited for accurate generation of specialized scene-text character sequences, and a standard
048   diffusion model trained on a limited dataset is unable to generate characters in new positions of the
049   sequence. These findings lead us to introduce SADE: a scene-text autoregressive diffusion engine
050
      that produces realistic scene-text images of sequences both seen and unseen in training. SADE
      simplifies the generation process by progressively generating partial images of subsequences, each
051
      conditioned on a previously generated part for visual coherency in the full image.
052
053   To evaluate SADE’s ability to generate synthetic data that closely resembles the original training
      data, we experiment first with a mock number plate dataset of rendered images, where we can con-

061          • We show that our approach can generate realistic scene-text images, specifically of se-

065   2     R ELATED WORK
066
067   While there are ongoing efforts to enhance OCR model technology (Wang et al., 2024; Buoy et al.,
068   2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chi et al., 2024), there is also a focus on improving scene-text OCR
069   performance by developing datasets with sufficient variation in backgrounds, fonts, distortions, and
070   noise. Collecting and annotating real-world images is often costly and time-consuming, so a com-
071   mon approach is to generate fully annotated synthetic datasets.
072
073   2.1   RULE - BASED APPROACHES
074
075   Jaderberg et al. (2014) developed a widely used synthetic scene-text recognition dataset called MJ,
076   using a six-stage rule-based engine. A dataset commonly used alongside MJ is ST (Gupta et al.,
077
      2016), built from the SynthText engine. Zhan et al. (2018) expanded on SynthText by identifying
      regions where text would naturally appear. Yim et al. (2021) introduced two text selection strategies
078
      to address misrepresentations of text distributions, and developed a five-stage scene-text generation
079
      pipeline called SynthTIGER. The widespread use of these datasets in recent work on OCR (Buoy
080   et al., 2024; Fujitake, 2023; Du et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) highlights the value of synthetic data
081   but also reveals a possible need for more modern and realistic scene-text generation engines. To that
082   end, we investigate the efficacy of diffusion-based image generation.
083
      Generating scenes in virtual 3D space enables more accurate perspective transformations and light-
084
      ing, compared to 2D image editing. SynthText3D (Liao et al., 2019) embeds text into virtual en-
085
      vironments and renders images with varied illumination and camera angles. Long & Yao (2020)
086   addressed the scaling and diversity limitations of SynthText3D by leveraging 3D object properties,
087   such as meshes, in their three-stage pipeline called UnrealText. Despite the realism of this synthetic
088   data, 2D engines seem to remain the more popular choice for training and benchmarking OCR.
089
090   2.2   L EARNING - BASED APPROACHES
091
092   Rule-based methods can be efficient for generating large datasets, but they struggle to capture certain
093   natural image details. Learning-based approaches offer an alternative by automatically incorporating
094   more realistic variations. Storchan & Beauschene (2019) used a generative adversarial network
095
      (GAN) in an end-to-end OCR system, achieving strong results for reading damaged faxes and PDFs.
      Li et al. (2023) employed adversarial training to augment characters in tail classes, setting a new
096
      state-of-the-art in oracle bone script recognition. Similarly, Yeleussinov et al. (2023) demonstrated
097
      the effectiveness of GANs in boosting Kazakh handwritten text recognition accuracy.
098
099   The latest advancements in image generation leverage denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho
100   et al., 2020). Zhu et al. (2023) introduced conditional text image generation with diffusion mod-
101
      els (CTIG-DM), and showed improved performance of existing text recognizers and the generation
      of images of out-of-vocabulary words. Zhang et al. (2024a) addressed the challenge of generating
102
      multilingual scene-text images with their framework called Diff-Text, achieving improved accuracy
103
      and normalized edit distance from OCR tools. While these studies highlight the benefits of gener-
104   ative models for scene-text recognition, our work in this paper focuses on the recognition of short
105   alphanumeric text sequences where character-level accuracy is vital and training data is limited in
106   quantity and variety.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
109
110
      Our interest is in training an OCR model to read short alphanumeric sequences (like number plates,
111   or vehicle type labels) in images. We assume access to a labelled training set of images, cropped
112   roughly around the sequences, that might be limited in size and sequence variation. The idea is
113   to train a diffusion model to generate additional images with similar background statistics but new
114   character sequences, to assist the OCR model.
115
116   3.1   C HARACTER SEQUENCE CONDITIONING
117
118   Diffusion models are often conditioned on encodings from a large language model (LLM) (Rombach
119   et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024a), but because LLMs are designed to capture
120   the meaning of tokens by the presence or absence of other tokens, and are to some extent insensitive
121   to token order, such encodings are unsuitable in the case of nonsensical sequences where character-
122   level accuracy and order are important.
123   Our approach to condition a diffusion model on a sequence involves creating character-level embed-
124   dings and concatenating them to form a sequence embedding. This allows the diffusion model to
125   identify a character within a sub-space of the sequence embedding and infer its position.
126
127   3.2   G ENERATING CHARACTERS IN UNSEEN POSITIONS
128
129   For a diffusion model to correctly identify characters and their positions from the concatenated
130   character embeddings, it must see each character in each position during training. If the model does
131   not see a certain character in a particular position during training, it will not recognize the embedding
132
      vector at that position and fail to generate the character.

      3.3   OVERLAPPING IMAGE PARTS
144
145   One approach to increase the frequency of characters appearing in different positions is to split a
146   sequence, thereby decreasing the number of positions. For example, ABCD can be split into AB and
147   CD, so that characters in the first and third positions of the full sequence now both appear in the first
148   position of a subsequence. Images can be split vertically into parts, and a diffusion model can be
149   trained to generate such image parts individually. However, processing image parts independently
150   prevents the model from applying consistent orientation and background details across the parts. We
151   therefore include the previously generated part (from left to right) as an additional channel in the
152
      input image, thereby creating a sort of autoregressive process. In this way, the diffusion model has
      reference to the orientation and background that should be applied to the part being generated.
153
154   Generated image parts can be concatenated to form a final whole image, but we found that this can
155   easily lead to unwanted artefacts at part borders (see Appendix A for examples). Splitting an image
156   vertically can result in character pieces being cropped into the wrong image part. Since the model is
157   not conditioned on the embedding of such cropped characters, it interprets them as background. The
158
      solution we propose is to split an image with an overlap of at least one character. Figure 2 illustrates
      how an image (from our mock number plate set) is split into seven equal-width pieces and combined
159
      into two overlapping parts. While this approach does not guarantee that a piece always contains a
160
      single or whole character, it produces roughly the correct number of characters per image part.
161
      We call the method SADE: Scene-text Autoregressive Diffusion Engine.

205   3.5     E VALUATING A DIFFUSION MODEL WITH AN OCR MODEL
206
207
      While OCR is a downstream task that can benefit from additional training images, it can also serve
      as a tool for evaluating the performance of a diffusion model in generating scene-text images. The
208
      test performance of an OCR model can be viewed as a measure of how much information about the
209
      true data distribution is available to the OCR model in the training data, or to what extent the training
210   data explains the variation in the true distribution. From this perspective, the test performance of an
211   OCR model trained on original data (as opposed to synthetic data) can serve as a baseline metric for
212   a given dataset. Comparing this baseline to the performance of an OCR model trained on synthetic
213   data can thus illustrate whether the synthetic data captures more variation or deviates from the true
214   distribution. We will use three separately trained OCR models to evaluate the performance of a
215   single diffusion model: the first is trained on original data, the second on synthetic data from the
      diffusion model, and the third on a combination of original and synthetic data.

      4       E MPIRICAL PROCEDURE
217
218
      We conduct two sets of experiments to evaluate the ability of SADE to generate images of charac-
219
      ter sequences. The purpose of the first set is to establish some basic expectations and comparisons
220   between using synthetic data verse the original data for training an OCR model. For control over
221   variation within the data, we introduce a mock number plate dataset. In the second set of experi-
222   ments, we evaluate SADE on a more complex real-world dataset of vehicle type labels.
223
224   4.1      M OCK NUMBER PLATE DATA
225
226   Our mock dataset is loosely inspired by Venezuelan number plates1 . A few example images from
227   our set are shown in the top row of Figure 3. We form 7-character sequences from the set {A, L,
228   M, Q, X, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}, render such a sequence on a plate design, apply random noise, rotation and
229   translation perturbations, and pick a background shade at random. We also simulate poor image
230   quality by scaling down and up again. All images are rendered at a resolution of 112 × 112, to be
231
      easily divisible into seven character parts.
232   We construct six separate training sets. The first five contain 40, 50, 70, 80 and 100 sequences,
233   respectively, sampled randomly from the 107 possibilities. For each sequence, five images are ren-
234   dered with different perturbations. The sixth training set consists of sequences that do not have a 5
235   in the sixth position, to assess the ability of SADE to generate characters in positions not encoun-
236   tered during training. Starting with an initial set of 15,000 randomly sampled sequences, we remove
237
      1,508 that contain a 5 in the sixth position and reserve those for a test set (to be generated by SADE,
      after training). A separate test set of 5,000 randomly sampled sequences is also created.
238
239
240   4.2      R EAL - WORLD VEHICLE TYPE DATA
241
      We demonstrate a possible real-world use case for a motor vehicle manufacturer, by applying SADE
242
      to images of vehicle type labels. Starting with an initial set of 7,974 images of 31 unique vehicle type
243   labels, we augment the dataset by randomly occluding single characters. To occlude a character at
244   the beginning or end of a sequence, the image is cropped. When occluding a character in the middle
245   of a sequence, a background region of the image is pasted over the character, and a space character
246   replaces it in the corresponding sequence. These augmentations result in a dataset of 15,382 images
247   with 128 unique sequences.
248
      SADE is designed for fixed-length sequences, while the vehicle type labels vary in length. To
249   accommodate this, we add space characters randomly to the start or end of a sequence until it reaches
250   a desired length. Adding a space to the start has the added benefit of shifting characters, leading
251   to more diversity of characters at each position. Padding is added to the corresponding image to
252   account for the extra characters, with the colour of the left- or right-most pixel halfway up the
253   image. Images are resized to a resolution of 120 × 120 for divisibility by 3, 4 and 5 (the possible
254   sequence lengths). Examples of images from this dataset are shown in the bottom row of Figure 3.

      4.3     D IFFUSION MODEL IMPLEMENTATION
297
298   For each mock number plate set, SADE is trained for 12,000 iterations, using AdamW (Loshchilov
299   & Hutter, 2019) to optimize the ‘SNR+1’ weighting loss (Salimans & Ho, 2022), a learning rate of
300   0.0006, a weight decay of 0.002, a batch size of 24, a cosine learning rate scheduler that includes
301   warm-up of 200 iterations, and the DDIM noise scheduler with a cosine beta schedule (Nichol &
302   Dhariwal, 2021). The number of denoising timesteps is set to T = 1000, the character embed-
303   ding length is 64, and images are split into three parts. These parameters were selected based on
304   searches and early experimentation. The OCR models accept greyscale images, so for computa-
305
      tional efficiency, training images for SADE are also converted to greyscale. Our architecture and
      training scripts are publicly available2 and are based on the Diffusers library from Hugging Face3 .
306
      Hyperparameters used in the U-Net architecture are listed in Appendix B.
307
308   In the case of the vehicle type labels, the number of image parts and the chosen sequence length
309   are important hyperparameters that determine the number of characters per image part. It might
310   seem ideal for a model to see one character per image part, allowing it to learn all characters in
311
      a single position. However, due to the autoregressive nature of our approach, an accumulation of
      errors over many image parts can lead to distorted characters and poor image quality (see Appendix
312
      A for examples). To avoid this, the number of parts and the fixed sequence length must be tuned.
313
      For our vehicle type dataset, we found an optimal number of image parts and fixed sequence length
314   to be 2 and 6, respectively, where image parts have one character overlap. Details of the search can
315   be found in Appendix C. We train SADE on the vehicle type sets with a batch size of 16, for 10,000
316   iterations. All other hyperparameters are the same as those used for the mock data.
317
      In the case of the mock data, we sample 5,000 images in 20 timesteps with each trained model,
318
      using sequences randomly selected with replacement. In the case of the vehicle type data, we sample
319
      10,000 images with each trained model. Here we have only 128 possible sequences, meaning that
320   the model may occasionally generate an image of a test sequence. This highlights a key advantage
321   of using a generative model for synthesizing scene-text images for OCR model training.

      4.4    OCR MODEL IMPLEMENTATION
325
326   For each diffusion model we train three OCR models: on the original data, on synthetic data, and
327   on a combination of the original and synthetic data at a ratio of 1:3. The framework we use for
328   training and testing these models was introduced by Baek et al. (2019). Each OCR model is trained
329   for 5,000 iterations with a batch size of 128 and validation every 500 iterations. The checkpoint
330   with highest validation accuracy is saved as the final model. No additional data transformations are
331   done, VGG is used for feature extraction, a BiLSTM is employed for sequence modelling, and an
332
      attention-based model is used for prediction (Shi et al., 2016). All other training parameters are kept
      at their default values, and the model is trained end-to-end from scratch. We found more advanced
333
      features available in the framework to quickly overfit to our relatively small datasets.
334
335   Additionally, we compare our trained OCR models with the pretrained TPS-ResNet-BiLSTM-CTC
336   model provided with the OCR framework by Baek et al. (2019). This model was trained on MJ
337   (Jaderberg et al., 2014) and ST (Gupta et al., 2016), and serves as an off-the-shelf benchmark.

448   5.3   L IMITATIONS
449
450   While Figures 4 and 7 demonstrate SADE’s ability to generate realistic images, they also reveal that
451   high quality is not guaranteed. For example, in the third image of the bottom row in Figure 4, the 4
452   has an extra leg, and in the right-most image of the bottom row, the 5 looks more like a 6. In Figure
453   7, the second image of the top row displays two 5’s but the second should be a 3. Although OCR
454
      can still benefit from using this data, such deformations limit performance. A possible explanation
      for the artefacts is that the diffusion model relies too much on copying the right side of the previous
455
      image part into the next, rather than using the sequence embedding to guide character generation.
456
457   While SADE can generate characters in novel positions, its effectiveness is dependent on the training
458   data and certain situations can pose challenges. For example, if a character appears only in the
459   first position of a sequence during training, the sequence must be split with either one or very few
460
      characters per subsequence to enable the generation of that character in other positions. Depending
      on the total sequence length, the cumulative error from having many subsequences may hinder the
461
      generation of realistic images (as we demonstrate in Appendix A). Therefore, the number of image
462
      parts and the fixed sequence length should be tuned according to the characteristics of the dataset.
463

465   6     C ONCLUSION
466
467   We identified and addressed the challenge that OCR models face when training on images of short
468
      alphanumeric scene-text sequences, in data-scarce scenarios and where some characters do not occur
      in certain positions. Our goal was to create synthetic data with sufficient variation in order to improve
469
      the training of an OCR model. To achieve this, we developed SADE, a novel autoregressive diffusion
470
      engine capable of generating images of new sequences, including those with characters in positions
471   not seen in the original training data.
472
473   We experimented with a mock number plate dataset to showcase SADE’s ability to generate char-
474
      acters in new positions and produce data that resembles the original data. We also demonstrated
      SADE’s practical value on a real-world dataset of motor vehicle type labels, by investigating the
475
      benefits of synthetic data for training an OCR model to recognize vehicle type labels.
476
477   For future work we plan to explore pretraining an OCR model on synthetic data and fine-tuning it
478   on real data. We also aim to reduce unwanted artefacts by providing SADE with less visual context
479   from the previous image part and more textual context for the current one.
