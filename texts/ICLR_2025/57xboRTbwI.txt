009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The widespread usage of generative AI models raises concerns regarding fair-
012
               ness and potential discriminatory outcomes. In this work, we define the bias of
013
               an attribute (e.g., gender or race) as the difference between the probability of its
014            presence in the observed distribution and its expected proportion in an ideal ref-
015            erence distribution. Despite efforts to study social biases in these models, the
016            origin of biases in generation remains unclear. Many components in generative
017            AI models may contribute to biases. This study focuses on the inductive bias of
018            unconditional generative models, one of the core components, in image genera-
019            tion tasks. We propose a standardized bias evaluation framework to study bias
020            shift between training and generated data distributions. We train unconditional
021            image generative models on the training set and generate images unconditionally.
022
               To obtain attribute labels for generated images, we train a classifier using ground
               truth labels. We compare the bias of given attributes between generation and data
023
               distribution using classifier-predicted labels. This absolute difference is named
024
               bias shift. Our experiments reveal that biases are indeed shifted in image gener-
025            ative models. Different attributes exhibit varying bias shifts’ sensitivity towards
026            distribution shifts. We propose a taxonomy categorizing attributes as subjective
027            (high sensitivity) or non-subjective (low sensitivity), based on whether the clas-
028            sifier’s decision boundary falls within a high-density region. We demonstrate an
029            inconsistency between conventional image generation metrics and observed bias
030            shifts. Finally, we compare diffusion models of different sizes with Generative
031            Adversarial Networks (GANs), highlighting the superiority of diffusion models in
032            terms of reduced bias shifts.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Generative AI models have achieved realistic generation qualities for various modalities including
039
      text (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023), image (Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Esser
      et al., 2024), audio (Kreuk et al., 2023), and video (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2023). They
040
      are consequently employed for commercial uses and are available to every internet user across the
041
      world. The widespread use of these high-performing models, along with the potential social biases
042   embedded in their generation, increase the risk of discriminatory outcomes. Taking image genera-
043   tion as an example, Growcoot (2023) and Tiku et al. (2023) report racial and gender biases in popular
044   text-to-image (T2I) systems including DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021), Stable Diffusion (Rombach
045   et al., 2022) and Midjourney (https://www.midjourney.com).
046
      We define the bias of an attribute (e.g., gender or race) as the difference between the probability of its
047
      presence in the observed distribution and its expected proportion in an ideal reference distribution.
048   The ideal reference distribution may be based on social norms or population statistics, etc. A widely
049   studied problem is gender or racial bias with respect to occupations (Cho et al., 2023; Bianchi et al.,
050   2023; Luccioni et al., 2023; Friedrich et al., 2024). Depending on the context, previous works
051   use equality or U.S. labor statistics as the ideal reference distribution. In these cases, the typical
052   analysis protocol consists of generating facial images based on text prompts containing occupation
053   information, using pre-trained models to assign gender or racial labels for the generated images,
      followed by measuring and assessing the degree of biases in the images given a certain occupation.

071   Other studies have compared social biases between generated images and training datasets of gen-
072   erative AI models, with mixed findings. Friedrich et al. (2024) report that images generated by
073   Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) show cases of bias and even bias amplification compared to
074   the training data (LAION-5B) (Schuhmann et al., 2022). On the other hand, Seshadri et al. (2023)
075
      conduct similar experiments and discover that bias shift can be mainly attributed to discrepancies
      between training captions and model prompts.
076
077   Although analyzing biases empirically in publicly available generative AI models is of practical sig-
078   nificance, identifying the origin of these biases remains a challenge. Modern generative AI systems
079   are complex and generative biases can stem from various sources, such as biased datasets (Schuh-
080   mann et al., 2022; Karkkainen & Joo, 2021), the conditioning process (including textual prompts,
081
      and guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022)), pre-trained modules (including
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014)), and inductive bias of the gener-
082
      ative models (e.g., diffusion process (Ho et al., 2020), generative adversarial training (Goodfellow
083
      et al., 2014)). While biases in pre-trained models (Bommasani et al., 2021; Alabdulmohsin et al.,
084   2024) and datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022) have been widely studied, the impact of inductive biases
085   in generative models remains underexplored. Thus, in our experiments, we focus on unconditional
086   pixel-level image generative models without any guidance during training or inference.
087
      We propose a standardized evaluation framework that employs attribute classifiers to study bias
088
      shifts from training to generated data distributions in unconditional image generative models. Train-
089
      ing the classifiers requires ground-truth labels for the training and validation sets; hence, our frame-
090   work is applicable to any supervised learning dataset. We train unconditional image generative mod-
091   els using the training set and unconditionally generate images. We then use the trained classifiers to
092   predict attribute labels for each generated image. We compare the bias for each attribute between the
093   training and generated data distributions using classifier-predicted labels. We refer to this absolute
094   difference as the bias shift. If bias shift is close to zero, there is no systematic bias exhibited in
095   image generative models. We analyse the bias shifts on two real image datasets, CelebA (Liu et al.,
096   2015) and DeepFashion (Liu et al., 2016).
097   Our findings reveal that bias shifts vary in magnitude across different attributes, indicating varying
098   levels of sensitivity to distribution change between generation and training data. We categorize at-
099   tributes as subjective (high sensitivity) and non-subjective (low sensitivity) sets, based on the relative
100   sample density at the classifier’s decision boundary. If the classifier is confident in its predictions —
101   in other words, the decision boundary lies in a lower-density region (corresponding to non-subjective
102   attributes), bias shifts tend to be smaller, and vice versa. Fig. 1 shows translation distribution shift
103   as an example to introduce this idea.
104   Our bias analysis framework yields the following observations: 1) Biases of attributes shift between
105   training and generation distributions for unconditional image generative models. The magnitude
106   of bias shift is correlated with the subjectivity of the attribute. 2) Selecting the checkpoint based
107   on image generation metrics considering quality, diversity, and novelty (FID (Heusel et al., 2017),
      KID (Binkowski et al., 2018), and FLD (Jiralerspong et al., 2023)) does not guarantee the smallest

      bias shifts. Bias should be treated as an independent issue when evaluating generations. 3) BigGAN
109   models (Brock et al., 2019) have larger bias shifts compared to diffusion models, despite having
110   similar image generation metrics. 4) Bias shifts in smaller diffusion models tend to increase over
111   extended training steps, even though image generation metrics remain relatively stable.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
115
      Bias in Image Generation Previous studies focus on social biases in image generation, often
116
      concluding that these models are unfair (Friedrich et al., 2024; Cho et al., 2023) or fail to reflect
117   real-world biases as observed in U.S. labor statistics (Luccioni et al., 2023; Bianchi et al., 2023). A
118   commonly examined bias is gender or race to occupation. However, different studies select various
119   public models and develop their own evaluation benchmarks. For example, Cho et al. (2023) investi-
120   gate minDALL-E (Kim et al., 2021), Karlo (Lee et al., 2022), and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
121   2022) v1.4, while Luccioni et al. (2023) use Stable Diffusion v.1.4, v.2, and Dall-E 2 (Ramesh et al.,
122   2022). However, there are different components in T2I models that may contribute to generative
123   biases. We focus on the unconditional image generative model that directly outputs the generations,
124   without text conditioning or guidance.
125
126
      Bias Shift between Train and Generation Few studies attempt to compare bias between the
      generation and training distributions. These efforts often rely on publicly available Stable Diffusion
127
      models, comparing generated outputs with the LAION-5B training set (Schuhmann et al., 2022), a
128
      large-scale dataset lacking explicit attribute labels. Given a text prompt, Friedrich et al. (2024) select
129   a subset of LAION-5B based on pre-trained image-prompt similarity, then compare the bias between
130   this subset and the images generated using the same prompt. In contrast, Seshadri et al. (2023) select
131   subsets based on keywords in image captions, which may overlook relevant images. To avoid this
132   large-scale dataset search and subset comparison, we train generative models using datasets with
133   labeled attributes, ensuring reliable bias estimation across both the training and generation.
134
135   Bias-related Attribute Label Prediction To calculate bias in generation, the generated images
136   need to be assigned attribute labels, which is non-trivial in the case of unconditional generation.
137   Some studies (Bianchi et al., 2023) infer the labels in the representation space of self-supervised
138   learning models, for example, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Some methods use pre-trained vision
139
      language models and conduct zero-shot text generation. Cho et al. (2023) use BLIP-2 (Li et al.,
      2023) and get the label through visual question answering (VQA). Luccioni et al. (2023) use BLIP
140
      with VQA task and ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) with image captioning task. However, pre-trained
141
      models introduce their own biases (Bommasani et al., 2021; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2024), rendering
142   the predicted labels unreliable for accurate bias evaluation. Some approaches (Friedrich et al., 2024)
143   train an attribute classifier on other available supervised learning datasets. In our case, we train the
144   classifier on the same dataset used for bias analysis, resulting in more accurate predictions.

      3.1   B IAS D EFINITION
149
150   In this work, bias for an attribute is defined as the difference between the probability of its presence
151   in the observed distribution and its expected proportion in an ideal reference distribution.
152
      Considering a set of binary attributes1 C for which we want to study bias, each image in the dataset
153
      is annotated for every attribute. Given an attribute C ∈ C, we consider the positive case C = 1 in the
154   following. We can set an ideal probability P ideal (C = 1) for attribute C as the reference probability,
155   depending on the context. We denote the probability of this attribute in the data distribution as
156   P data (C = 1). We can use either P train (C = 1) or P val (C = 1) as an estimation for P data (C = 1)
157   and compare with the reference probability to determine degree of bias. For example, we define the
158   bias of the data distribution relative to P ideal (C = 1) as
159
                                  B data (C = 1) = P data (C = 1) − P ideal (C = 1).                           (1)

      3.2   B IAS E VALUATION F RAMEWORK
188
189   Fig. 2 illustrates our proposed bias evaluation framework. We train image generative models for
190   unconditional image generation using only images from the training set, without feeding ground
191   truth labels into the models. We generate 10,000 images for each checkpoint during training. To
192   calculate the proportion for each attribute in the generation distribution, we require attribute labels
193   for the generated images. We apply a trained classifier, developed using the training and validation
194   sets with ground truth labels, to the generated images to obtain classifier-predicted attribute labels.
195   The trained classifier inevitably introduces errors, meaning the predicted labels may not match the
196   ground truth labels for all images. To ensure consistent bias estimation across different sets, we
197   use the trained classifier to predict attribute labels for training and validation sets. In addition, we
198   use P val (C = 1) to estimate the probability of attribute C in the data distribution, as the classifier
199   may overfit to the training set. By adopting these techniques, we aim to minimize the potential bias
200   introduced by the classifier in our bias evaluation framework for generative models.
201   Given a binary attribute C ∈ C, we can therefore define bias shift between generation and training
202   data as

234   4.1                      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
235
236   Datasets We apply our proposed bias evaluation framework to two real datasets – CelebA (Liu
237
      et al., 2015) and DeepFashion (Liu et al., 2016). CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is a large-scale dataset
      with 200,000 celebrity facial images, each labeled with 40 binary attributes. It covers a wide range
238
      of facial features, from details (e.g., earrings, pointy nose) to outlines (e.g., hair color, gender, age).
239
      DeepFashion (Liu et al., 2016) is a clothes dataset with over 800,000 diverse fashion images. We
240   use a subset with 26 fine-grained attribute annotations to train the classifier. We then study the bias
241   shift over these fine-grained attributes. For both datasets, we follow the training/validation/test set
242   split from the official release. More details about these datasets are in Appendix A.
243
244
245
      Backbone models in the framework We follow the setup from Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) to train
      unconditional ablated diffusion models (ADMs)2 . We train models of varying sizes by adjusting the
246
      number of channels in the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) bottleneck layer (32 for tiny, 64 for
247
      small, and 256 for large), with proportional changes in each layer. In the following sections, we
248   report the results of the large diffusion model if the model is not otherwise specified. We gener-
249   ate 10,000 images per checkpoint using 100 inference steps across training. We use a ResNext50
250   (32x4d)3 based image classifier (Xie et al., 2017). We add a linear layer on top as the classification
251   head and fine-tune the last 6 layers of the ResNext50 model. For comparison with a GAN model,
252   we train a BigGAN (Brock et al., 2019) model4 using the recommended settings. Implementation
253   details are in Appendix B.
254
255
      Evaluation metrics for Image Generation We use some common metrics, e.g., FID (Fréchet
256   Inception Distance) (Heusel et al., 2017) and KID (Kernel Inception Distance) (Binkowski et al.,
257   2018), to evaluate the generated images. We use FLD (Feature Likelihood Divergence) and general-
258   ization gap (Jiralerspong et al., 2023) as two additional metrics to gauge the memorization level of
259   the generative models. FLD provides a comprehensive evaluation considering not only quality and
260   diversity, but also novelty (i.e., difference from the training samples) of generated samples. Positive
261   generalization gap shows no overfitting to the training set. We adopt the implementation5 of Jiraler-
262   spong et al. (2023) and follow their suggestion of using DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) as the feature
263   extractor to calculate FID, KID, and FLD. We also use a conventional FID implementation6 to give
264
      a comparable value of how well the trained models are.

310
311   Fig. 4 presents the average bias shift (ABS) throughout training. The overall ABS is still perceiv-
312   able when image generation metrics are small, indicating non-negligible bias shifts from the training
313   to generation distributions. Looking closer into bias shift for each attribute (Figs. 8 and 9 in Sec-
314
      tion 4.6), we can categorize all attributes into two categories: subjective and non-subjective.

316   tion judgements are consistent across populations, e.g., eyeglasses, wearing hat, bangs,
317   goatee, etc., while subjective ones are those where classification judgements differ signifi-
318   cantly from one person to another, e.g., heavy makeup, arched eyebrows, attractive,

      we will talk about the criteria for the attributes categorization.

392   4.4                  B IAS SHIFTS ’ SENSITIVITY RELATES TO DECISION BOUNDARY
393
394
      In this section, we analyze the classifier to explain why some attributes experience greater bias shifts
      than others, leading to the attribute taxonomy presented in Table 1.
395
396   Figs. 5 and 6 show the trained classifier’s pre-sigmoid logits distribution for some attributes of
397   CelebA and DeepFashion respectively. The distributions for all attributes are in Appendix B.2.
398   These plots provide visualizations of how the data points are distributed in a projected uni-
399   dimensional space. To estimate the empirical distributions, we use all the training images, 10,000
400
      images sampled from the validation set, and all the 10,000 images in the generation set.
401   The main difference between small bias shift and large bias shift attributes is the density at the
402   decision boundary. The distribution shifts for different attributes can manifest in various ways,
403   but the decision boundaries for large bias shift attributes consistently fall in higher density regions
404   compared to those for small bias shift ones. We thus use the density where the decision boundary
405   falls in the validation distribution to categorize the attributes. Those with density more than 0.01 are
406
      categorized as subjective, and vice versa.
407   Bias shifts of subjective attributes are more sensitive to distribution shifts compared to non-
408   subjective attributes. The distributions for non-subjective attributes still change between training
409   and generation sets, but their effects on bias shifts are small. Since the decision boundary falls in a
410   low-density region, it is more difficult to transport the density mass from one side of the boundary to
411   the other. We find empirically that the distribution shifts between the training and generation distri-
412
      butions generally have low earth mover’s distance (EM distance) (Rubner et al., 1998). Significant
      reweighting of well-separated modes would constitute a significant EM distance between training
413
      and generated distributions. For example, the distribution of male (Fig. 5e) shifts from training
414
      to generation, but the shifts are within each side of the decision boundary. This clear classification
415   margin leads to small ABS for non-subjective attributes.

431   according to FLD. This finding may be because the common understanding that GAN models suffer
      from mode collapse issues (Arjovsky et al., 2017).

519   discrepancy is that the classifier-predicted labels of subjective attributes are not always accurate.
520   Therefore, when conditioning on subjective attributes, classification errors propagate into the bias
521
      analysis pipeline, resulting in a distinct pattern of bias shifts.
522
523   5                     C ONCLUSION
524
525   This study focuses on bias shifts with regard to inductive biases of unconditional image gener-
526   ative models. We propose a standardized bias analysis framework applicable to any supervised
527   learning dataset. Our experimental results show that different attributes have varying bias shifts
528   in response to distribution changes. Attributes for which the classifier’s decision boundary falls in
529
      a low-density area tend to have small bias shifts. We thus categorize all attributes into subjective
      and non-subjective sets. Our analysis results in the following observations: 1) Biases shift between
530
      training and generation distributions for unconditional image generative models. 2) Selecting the
531
      checkpoint with the best image generation metrics does not guarantee the smallest bias shifts. 3)
532   BigGAN models and small diffusion models have larger bias shifts compared to large diffusion
533   models, despite having similar image generation metric values.
534
535
      We hope that our analysis for unconditional generative models can serve as a base framework al-
      lowing researchers to add other sources of bias such as conditioning (with ground-truth labels, text,
536
      etc.), guidance, pretrained modules, etc. in a gradual manner, and study their effects on bias shift in
537
      a systematic way.

      CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is a large-scale face attributes dataset with 200,000 celebrity images, each
705
      with 40 attribute annotations. The dataset includes 10,000 celebrities with 20 images for each.
706   These attribute annotations cover a wide variety of facial characteristics, ranging from details (e.g.,
707   earrings, pointy noise, etc.) to outlines (e.g., hair color, gender, age, etc.). We list all 40 attributes
708   in Table 2. Before feeding the training images to the model, we centre crop the images and re-
709   size them to 128x128 pixels. Because of the crop, some attributes, e.g., Wearing Necklace,
710   Wearing Necktie, are not visually grounded in the post-process images. Blurry is also an
711   attribute that we do not include since we want the image generation quality to be good. We excluded
712   these attributes in Table 1. We follow the Training/Validation/Test set split in the official release.
713   Training set includes the images of the first eight thousand identities (with 160 thousand images).
714
      Validation set contains the images of another one thousand identities (with twenty thousand images).
      The remaining one thousand identities (with twenty thousand images) go for Test set. In our bias
715
      analysis framework, we only use the Training set and the Validadtion set.
716
717   DeepFashion (Liu et al., 2016) is a clothes dataset with over 800,000 diverse fashion images, in-
718   cluding tops and bottoms. No footwears is in this dataset. Each image is associated with 1000
719   coarse attribute annotations about texture, fabric, shape, part, and style of the clothes. These at-
720
      tribute annotations are scrapped directly from meta-data of the images. They are thus very noisy
      and not reliable. Most of the attributes have less than 1% positive samples, making the classification
721
      problem very imbalanced. This dataset also provides a fine-grained annotation subset, where each
722
      image is associated with 26 find-grained attribute annotations. These attributes are presented in Ta-
723   ble 2. We train a classifier on this subset and apply this trained classifier to the whole dataset and
724   get classifier-predicted labels for each image. We follow the Training/Validation/Test set split in the
725   official release. Unlike CelebA dataset, the split of DeepFashion dataset is random.

789   B.2   R ESNET C LASSIFIERS
790
      We employ a pre-trained ResNeXt model as the base model. We add a linear layer to top as the
791
      classification layer. We then fine-tune the last 6 layers of the pre-trained model as well as the classi-
792
      fication layer using CelebA and DeepFashion dataset. We use AdamW optimizer and learning rate
793   at 0.001. We follow a standard training procedure for the classifier training. We train the classifier
794   on the train set (with ground truth labels) and choose the best classifier according to the average
795   performance across all the considered attributes on the valid set (with ground truth labels). We use
796   data augmentations to make the classifier more robust. The data augmentations include random hor-
797   izontal flip, scaling and resizing, etc. This can help the classifier become more reliable when applied
798   to the generation set. Previous work indicates that classifiers can amplify the discriminative biases
799   in the training set (Zhao et al., 2017; Hall et al., 2022). We use the positive and negative sample
800   ratio to reweigh the cross entropy loss terms. This acts as an upsampling of the minority samples
801
      and alleviates the label imbalance issue. We don’t see the discriminative biases being amplified for
      most attributes according to Figs. 15 and 14 comparing the training ground truth probability and
802
      the validation classifier-predicted probability. The classifiers’ performances for each attribute are
803
      listed in Tables 4 and 5. For both dataset, the accuracy for most attributes is over 80%. Figs. 12 and
804   13 show the pre-sigmoid logits distributions for each attribute in CelebA and DeepFashion datasets
805   respectively.
