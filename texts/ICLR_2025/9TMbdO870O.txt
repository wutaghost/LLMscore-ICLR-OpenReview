011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In the mixed cooperative-competitive scenario, the uncertain decisions of agents
014            on both sides not only render learning non-stationary but also pose a threat to each
015
               other’s security. Existing methods either predict policy beliefs based on opponents’
               interactive actions, goals, and rewards or predict trajectories and intents solely from
016
               local historical observations. However, the above private information is unavail-
017
               able and these methods neglect the underlying dynamics of the environment and
018            relationship between intentions, latent strategies, actions, and trajectories for both
019            sides. To address these challenges, we propose a Hierarchical Interactive Intent-
020            Latent-Strategy-Aware World Model based Opponent Model (H2IL-MBOM) and
021            the Mutual Self-Observed Adversary Reasoning PPO (MSOAR-PPO) to enables
022            both parties to dynamically and interactively predict multiple intentions and la-
023            tent strategies, along with their trajectories based on self observation. Concretely,
024            the high-level world model fuses related observations regarding opponents and
025            multi-learnable intention queries to anticipate future intentions and trajectories of
026
               opponents and incorporate anticipated intentions into the low-level world model to
               infer how opponents’ latent strategies react and their influence on the trajectories
027
               of cooperative agents. We validate the effectiveness of the method and demonstrate
028
               its superior performance through comparisons with state-of-the-art model-free re-
029            inforcement learning and opponent modeling methods in more challenging settings
030            involving multi-agent close-range air-combat environments with missiles.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In multi-agent environments, numerous agents interact and learn concurrently, influencing each
036   other’s transition dynamics and rendering the environments non-stationary. Additionally, adapting
037   to the ever-changing and unknown policies of other agents introduces non-stationary strategies,
038   posing challenges for policy improvement. Importantly, navigating interactions with these unknown
039
      opponent policies can jeopardize allies’ safety and limit performance, particularly in competitive or
      mixed cooperative-competitive scenarios. Therefore, modeling opponents behavior or reasoning their
040
      intentions is crucial for ensuring safety and achieving supremacy during decision-making processes.
041
042   Opponent modeling and intent reasoning are integral components of Theory of Mind (ToM), granting
043   agents the ability to infer opponents’ mental states, including preferences, styles, desires, beliefs,
044   goals, intentions, latent strategies, or behaviors. Some approaches reconstructed policy beliefs and
045
      predicted opponents’ actions based on the assumption that opponents’ behaviors are known from
      experience. Other works focused on extrapolating adversary strategies/intentions and trajectories from
046
      local observation experience without prior assumptions. However, the assumptions underlying the
047
      former method are unrealistic in the real world, as opponents typically do not disclose their actions,
048   strategies, or intentions. Furthermore, methods that directly estimate opponent latent strategies
049   based on their trajectories and predict future trajectories encounter challenges with a large state
050   space and overlook the environment’s underlying dynamics, failing to comprehend how intentions,
051   latent strategies, and actions dynamically interact for both sides. Specifically, they remain unclear
052   how estimated opponent intentions will influence their latent strategy, how ego agents will react to
053   opponents’ intentions and latent strategies, and consequently, how future intentions, latent strategies,
      and trajectories of both sides will be affected. In other words, they lack insight into how opponents’


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      latent strategies, influenced by time-varying intentions, will impact allies’ trajectories and struggle to
055   continuously reason about the opponents’ evolving intentions and strategies due to the influence of
056   future trajectories during the next iteration. Moreover, most opponent modeling methods only train
057   and test in matrix game, differential game, cooperative navigation, triangle game, and StarCraftII,
058   and so on, they have not yet trained in such high dynamic air-combat environments.
059
      Developing a human-like opponent model or intent reasoning model inevitably presents challenges.
060
      Maintaining a multi-hypothesis intention and strategy for opponents with advanced cognitive abilities
061   in dynamic and complex competitive-cooperative scenarios, adapting to a variable number of adver-
062   saries with changing intentions, and dealing with the resulting uncertainty in strategy estimation are
063   necessary.
064
      Main contributions: To address these challenges and fill the gap in opponent modeling without
065
      accessing any private information of opponents in the field of air-combat, drawing inspiration from
066
      the brain’s cognitive process, we propose a Hierarchical Interactive Intent-Latent-Strategy-Aware
067   World Model based Opponent Model (H2IL-MBOM), specially a Hypernetwork-based Hierarchical
068   Dynamic Dependence Transformer State Space Model (HyperHD2TSSM), and a Mutual Self-
069   Observed Adversary Reasoning PPO (MSOAR-PPO) for reasoning about opponents’ multi-intentions
070   and latent strategies without accessing any private information of opponents. Our contributions are
071   five-fold:
072
      1. We derive a hierarchical variance inference and construct a novel hierarchical world model based
073   opponent model for dynamically and interactively learning and inferring multi-intentions, latent
074   strategies, and trajectories of opponents and allies without accessing any private information of
075   opponents.
076
      2. The developed HyperHD2TSSM allows for the establishment of an interactive transition model
077
      for all agents without increasing parameters, enhancing adaptability and scalability. Moreover, the
078
      latent weights generated by the hypernetwork of each agent at each time step compress historical
079   information about opponents’ mental states reasoned by adjacent agents, achieving O(1) complexity
080   as in recurrent state space models (RSSM) (Hafner et al. (2019b)) while maintaining parallel training
081   as in transformer state space model (TSSM) (Chen et al. (2022)).
082
      3. A novel approach has been devised to more accurately predict the impact of opponents’ intentions
083
      on their strategies without clear intentions and latent strategies candidates, effectively extracting the
084
      distribution of opponents’ behavioral patterns, and thereby enhancing interpretability.
085
086   4. The developed MSORA-PPO equipped with the H2IL-MBOM can infer adversarial strategies and
087   intentions based on self-observation in real time, facilitating rapid adaptation to changes in multiple
088
      opponents’ intentions and strategies and addressing the non-stationarity issue caused by opponents’
      continuous learning.
089
090   5. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to integrate intent and latent strategy into the
091   world model as opponent modeling, validated in a multi-agent close air-combat game with missiles
092   in a gym-jsbsim environment, promoting theoretical development and application in the field of
093   air-combat. The results demonstrate superior performance as our method can capture changing
094
      behavior patterns of opponents and possesses generalization ability.

      Opponent modeling. Opponent modeling involves inferring opponents’ mental states, such as
      desires, goals, actions, beliefs, and intentions, to address non-stationarity and gain supremacy when
100
      facing unknown and changeable opponent policies. Previous methods like DPN-BPR+ (Zheng et al.

104   (2018)), SOM (Raileanu et al. (2018)), LeMOL (Davies et al. (2020)), and TDOM (Tian et al. (2023))
105   utilize opponents’ observations, actions, or rewards to infer their goals or beliefs, though lacking
106   access to opponents’ private information. PR2 (Wen et al. (2019)) and GR2 (Wen et al. (2021))
107   propose multi-agent probabilistic recursive inference but cannot simultaneously learn policies for
      agents. GrAMMI (Ye et al. (2023)) emphasizes multi-hypothesis belief over opponents and uses

109   et.al. (Wu et al. (2023)) and Xie et.al. (Xie et al. (2021)) predicted adversary’ incentive, strategies
110   or trajectories, they lack insight into how time-varying intentions affect opponent strategies and

121   & Van Hoof (2022)). Some extend single-agent models to multi-agent models, categorized as cen-

      Shpilman (2022)). However, these models become cumbersome as the number of agents increases
      and make independent predictions, and mainly focused on cooperative navigation in environments
124
      such as MPE or mazes. On the contrary, we focus on how to build an interactive multi-agent world
125
      model that can make interactive prediction and own different ways of information compression in
126   the multi-uav adversary environment. Specially, our model allocates diverse latent weights to each
127   agent, dynamically adjusting them based on neighboring agents’ preceding latent states, enabling
128   spatiotemporal sequence forecasting. This characteristic allows it to establish transition models and
129   interactive predictions for each agent without increasing parameters, enhancing adaptability and
130   scalability compared to centralized and decentralized models.

189   3.1   M UTUAL R EACTION AND I NFLUENCE B ETWEEN M ENTAL S TATES , ACTIONS , AND
190
            T RAJECTORIES
191
      During each interaction, the red and blue teams serve as each other’s opponents, and both teams of
192   agents progress through a series of stages. These stages encompass the influence of the opponents’
193   intentions on their strategies during the interaction; the influence of these intentions on the opponents’
194   trajectories, as well as the impact of the opponents’ strategies on the trajectories of the alliance
195   throughout the interaction; and the subsequent influence of these interactive trajectories on both
196   intentions and strategies in the repeated interactions.
197
      Stage one: the influence of the opponents’ intentions on their strategies during the interaction.
198   Following the t − 1 iteration, both teams of agents are capable of gathering their respective histories
199   of interaction observations. At this juncture, an intelligent agent ought to be able to discern the
200   intentions of its opponent, as well as the strategies that the opponent may employ in response to
201   varying intentions.
202
      Stage two: the influence of these intentions on the opponents’ trajectories and the opponents’
203
      strategies on the trajectories of the alliance throughout the interaction. From the perspective
204   of each intelligent agent, actions are taken in response to the predicted opponents’ intentions and
205   strategies, and the opponent’s mental states evolve in reaction to the behavior of the alliance agents.
206   This mutual response is contingent upon interactions with different agents, leading to uncertain
207   transition probabilities P(Oi,t |Oi,t−1 , ai,t−1 , zi,t−1 ). Moreover, the opponent’s evolving intention
208   directly reflects changes in the opponents’ trajectories, while their evolving strategy influences the
209   trajectories of the alliance agents.
210   Stage three: the subsequent influence of the interactive trajectories on both intentions and
211   strategies in the repeated interactions. After the t − th interaction, the cooperative agents up-
212   date their policies for the next moment based on the affected trajectory observations, historical
213   observations, and the opponents’ intentions and strategies inferred from these observations. At the
214   same time, the opponents also update their intentions and strategies to respond to the policies of
215   the cooperative agents. After the interaction of T timesteps, both agents experience trajectories
      {(Oi,t , ai,t , ri,t ), ..., (Oi,t+T , ai,t+T , ri,t+T )} with the goal of achieving long-term rewards.

      3.2    H IERARCHICAL VARIANCE I NFERENCE
217
218
219   The three stages illustrate that the opponent’s intentions and strategies evolve alongside the transfer
220   dynamics. Insights from stages one and two reveal that different opponent intentions result in distinct
221   strategies, and that intentions and strategies, in turn, reflect changes in the opponents’ trajectories
222
      and their impact on the trajectories of cooperative agents. Consequently, both agents can partition
      their local current and historical observations into those related to the opponents and those related to
223
      cooperative agents. They can then estimate the opponents’ intentions using their current and historical
224
      observations of the opponents. Additionally, the agents estimate the opponents’ strategies based on
225   their current and historical observations of the cooperative agents, combined with their estimates of
226   the opponents’ intentions.
227
228
      However, the actions, intentions, and strategies of the opponents cannot be directly observed. How
      can we predict these representations without explicit labels about the opponents’ private information?
229
      One straightforward approach is to use an autoencoder to directly learn representations of historical
230
      and current observations and predict future trajectory sequences, akin to the work in Qi & Zhu
231   (2018). Drawing from insights in stages two and three, the actions, intentions, and strategies of
232   both parties interact, jointly determining the future trajectory. This trajectory, in turn, influences the
233   evolving intentions, strategies, and actions of both parties. In other words, the future trajectory is
234   influenced not only by the opponent’s current mental state but also by their evolving intentions and
235   strategies. Therefore, capturing trajectory changes in dynamic adversarial environments using an
236   autoencoder-based approach is inherently challenging.
237   To implicitly capture the mutual reactions and impacts between the opponent’s intentions, strategies,
238   behaviors, and the trajectories of both sides, based on insights from the three stages, we recognize the
239   necessity of introducing transition models alongside actions. The teams of agents interactively predict
240   and capture the mutual reactions between the opponents’ intentions, strategies, and behaviors using
241   the transition model at each time step. As highlighted in stage two, the mutual reactions between
242   the opponents’ intentions and the actions of the team are reflected in trajectory transitions related
243   to observations for the opponents. Similarly, the mutual reactions between the opponents’ latent
244   strategies and the team’s actions result in transitions of trajectory sequences related to observations
245
      for the cooperative agents.
246   Therefore, we approximate higher-level and lower-level transition models using pψI and pψL , respec-
247   tively, and use them to continuously predict the changes in the opponent’s intentions and strategies,
248   as well as the hidden states (hI,i,t , hL,i,t ) that compress historical information about predictions
249   of the opponent’s intentions and strategies reasoned by themselves and other cooperative agents.
250   Notably, these hidden states differ from those in recurrent neural networks; they are deterministically
251
      generated by the hypernetwork for each agent at each time step, specifically the output of the deter-
      ministic model within the transition model, as detailed in Figure 5. As a result, each prediction at
252
      any given time has a complexity of O(1), avoiding the O(T ) complexity associated with explicitly
253
      requiring history up to time T as in TSSM. Additionally, in this process, we integrate the predicted
254   intentions and strategies of the opponent, along with the evolutionary history (hidden states) of
255   these mental representations, to reconstruct the affected trajectories of the opponent and cooperative
256   agents. Thus, the reconstruction probability is related to the prior estimates of the hidden states, i.e.,
257   pθI (Oopp,i,t |zI,i,t , hI,i,t ) and pθL (Oc,i,t |zL,i,t , hL,i,t ), and the future trajectories further influence
258   the actions and mental representations of both sides in the next time step. Overall, during the interac-
259   tion, the trajectories continually influence or induce changes and updates in the opponents’ mental
260   representations. Gradually, the agents on both sides maximize long-term rewards by learning the
261   evolving mental states of the respective opponents over multiple steps of interaction. The reasoning
262

      3.3    DYNAMIC F USION M ECHANISMS OF I NTENTIONS AND L ATENT S TRATEGIES IN
317
             O PPONENT M ODELING
318
319   Given the learning and reasoning capabilities of both sides, the historical trajectories exhibit variation
320   across different episodes. Moreover, the mental states of the opponents also evolve over time with
321   the dynamics of the game. Therefore, the H2IL-MBOM is designed to enable UAVs to dynamically
322   predict opponents’ evolving intentions and latent strategies over the most recent trajectories in a
323   mixed cooperative-competitive scenario. The HDIRF is responsible for predicting the intentions of
      opponents based on historical and current observations. This is achieved through the integration of


                                                                       6

324
      a H2TE and a MITD. The H2TE captures the historical context relevant to the opponents, while
325   the MITD decodes the intentions from the encoded historical context. This process involves the
326   updating of intention feature queries via a self-attention mechanism, followed by a cross-attention
327   mechanism that considers the assembled historical features of all cooperative agents’ observations
328   for the opponents. Additionally, A fusion module integrates the outputs of the mechanisms and the
329   inferred intentions embeddings generated by the hypernetwork to produce a unified representation of
330   the opponents’ intentions, taking into account the interactions of intentions across agents. The initial
331   intentions embeddings are generated from the initialized intentions through the hypernetwork, while
332   subsequent intentions embeddings are derived from the intentions inferred in the previous layer. For
333
      scenarios where different agents face the same adversaries, the module can capture the interaction
      and assign different threat weights to each opponent’s intention for each agent.
334
335   The LDLRF focuses on inferring latent strategies and understanding how these strategies respond
336   to the predictions of opponents’ intentions. It utilizes a LHTE and a MLTD. The LHTE encodes
337   the historical observations of cooperative agents, and the MLTD decodes the latent strategies by
338   considering the influence of opponents’ intentions. The process includes a hypernetwork-based
339
      intention self-cross-attention module, which captures the impact of opponents’ intentions, followed
      by a latent-strategy cross-attention module that aggregates historical features regarding all cooperative
340
      agents. Finally, a latent-strategy fusion module captures the interactions of latent strategies across
341
      agents. Similarly, the initial latent strategies embeddings are generated from the initialized latent
342   strategies via the hypernetwork, while subsequent latent strategies embeddings are derived from the
343   latent strategies inferred in the previous layer.
344
345
      Moreover, the mental states are automatically generated by the respective hypernetworks, thereby
      handling changes in the number of opponents and the number of opponents’ intentions and latent
346
      strategies.
347
348   By incorporating these hierarchical mechanisms, H2IL-MBOM facilitates a comprehensive under-
349   standing of the opponents’ behavior, enabling drones to make informed decisions based on a global
350   prediction of opponents’ intentions and a precise capture of evolving latent strategies. The detailed
351
      calculations of HDIRF that consists of H2TE-MITD, and LDLRF that is composed of LHTE-MLTD
      can be also found in the Appendix A.5, and A.6.

355
356
      Two teams engage in independent policy learning, value learning, and world model learning based
      on their local observations due to the limitation of imperfect game, which differs from MAPPO.
357
      The world model, when given a tuple (Ot , at , Ot+1 , Ht , r), outputs mental states zI , zL through a
358
      hierarchical model and reconstructs the trajectory states and rewards of teammates and opponents
359   in the next time step. In the reinforcement learning module, the policy network and value network
360   integrate their respective local observations and predicted mental states into the hypernetwork-based
361   embedded attention (HEA) model, and are updated based on PPO. Here, we also use the whole world
362   model that includes a reward model to generate three-step imaginations of trajectories, and then
363   combine imagining trajectories and real trajectories to compute Jπ , JV . Specifically, we calculate the
364   returns G1 and G2 for the actual trajectory and imaging trajectory respectively, and use the identical
365   value function to calculate the values V1 and V2 for these trajectories. Finally, we calculate the
366   advantages A1 and A2 of the trajectories by respective rewards and values. In policy learning, we
367
      update the policy using the advantages of these trajectories, and update the common value function
      using the returns G1 and G2 of the trajectories. It is worth noting that we do not learn another value
368
      function like Dreamer, which can ensure performance while reducing complexity. The pseudo code

524
525   This paper proposes a novel opponent modeling method that integrates multiple intentions and latent
526   strategies inference into the world model. We use a hierarchical architecture to study the impact
527   and importance of opponents’ intentions on their latent strategies and to predict the trajectories
528   of teammates and opponents. Additionally, we propose MSORA-PPO, which allows both teams
529   to learn their own H2IL-MBOM, infer adversarial strategies and intentions based on their own
530   historical observations, and integrate the opponents’ mental states inferred by H2IL-MBOM with
531   local observations to independently learn policies and make decisions. This enables both teams to
532
      quickly capture and adapt to changes in the intentions and strategies of multiple opponents, as well as
      address the non-stationarity issues brought about by their continuous learning.

       A.9     T ESTING R ESULTS
1059
1060   The win rate (WR) and survival rate (SR) are as evaluation metrics. We first confront the opponents
1061   who adopt the baseline strategy that includes straight fly, rectangular trajectory maneuver and evasion
1062   of missiles, and pursuing the tail of our aircraft. The results show that our SR is the highest and
1063   achieves a 100% WR in 4 vs. 4 scenarios as presented in Table 2. We then test the effectiveness of
1064   our method against our method and our method against MAPPO under different numbers of agents
1065   as presented in Table 3 and 4. We use SR to evaluate performance because a group with fewer agents
1066   may sacrifice less or equal to the other group. In most cases, both teams make equal sacrifices because
1067   of the same reasoning ability of both teams, and in a small number of cases (e.g., 4 vs. 6, 4 vs. 8, 6 vs.
1068
       8) where the quantity is at a disadvantage, the red team still destroys one more aircraft than the blue
       team. In situations where the red team has a numerical advantage, it can achieve 100% superiority
1069
       (e.g., 8 vs. 4, 10 vs. 4, 10 vs. 6). Additionally, the advantage ranges are further expanded when
1070
       our method against MAPPO. (e.g., 4 vs.4, 6, 8,10; 6 vs. 4, 8,10; 8 vs. 4; 10 vs. 4, 10 vs. 6). The
1071   results also demonstrate our method is endowed with good generalization ability. Due to the fixed
1072   dimensions of other MARLs, it is not possible to complete adversarial tasks in different quantities.
1073   As shown in Figure 3(c), the relevant opponent modeling methods are unable to complete this task,
1074   so there is no adversarial testing with these methods.

1107   A.10    E XPERIMENTS ABOUT HYPERPARAMETERS
1108
1109   We vary the dimensions of intentions from 4 to 64 and evaluate the impact of different dimensions
1110   on the performance of our method, as shown in Figure 8a. We observe that there is an optimal
1111   dimensions of intentions, 8, which maximizes the performance of the model. When the dimension of
1112
       intentions is below 8 or above 32, it takes twice the time to converge, and the convergence speed is
       significantly reduced. Based on our experiments, the optimal number of attention heads is 8. At this
1113
       optimal number, the model achieves the highest performance with lowest complexity.
1114
1115   Similarly, we vary the number of attention heads from 2 to 16 and measure the performance using the
1116   average rewards. As shown in Figure 1 8b, we observe that there is an optimal number of attention
1117   heads, 4, which maximizes the performance of the model. When the number of attention heads is
1118
       below 4 or above 8, it also takes twice the time to converge, and the convergence speed is significantly
       reduced. Based on our experiments, the optimal number of attention heads is 4. At this optimal
1119
       number, the model achieves the highest performance with lowest complexity.
1120
1121   In summary, the dimensions of the intention space and numbers of attention head are chosen based
1122   on the best balance between performance and computational efficiency.
