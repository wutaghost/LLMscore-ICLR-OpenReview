009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Inspired by the software industry’s practice of offering different editions or versions
012            of a product tailored to specific user groups or use cases, we propose a novel task,
013            namely, training-free editioning, for text-to-image models. Specifically, we aim
014            to create variations of a base text-to-image model without retraining, enabling the
015            model to cater to the diverse needs of different user groups or to offer distinct
016
               features and functionalities. To achieve this, we propose that different editions of a
               given text-to-image model can be formulated as concept subspaces in the latent
017
               space of its text encoder (e.g., CLIP). In such a concept subspace, all points satisfy a
018
               specific user need (e.g., generating images of a cat lying on the grass/ground/falling
019            leaves). Technically, we apply Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to obtain the
020            desired concept subspaces that correspond to specific user needs or requirements
021            from a representative text embedding. Projecting the text embedding of a given
022            prompt into these low-dimensional subspaces enables efficient model editioning
023            without retraining. Intuitively, our proposed editioning paradigm enables a service
024            provider to customize the base model into its “cat edition” (or other editions)
025            that restricts image generation to cats, regardless of the user’s prompt (e.g., dogs,
026            people, etc.). This introduces a new dimension for product differentiation, targeted
027
               functionality, and pricing strategies, unlocking novel business models for text-to-
               image generators. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the validity of our
028
               approach and its potential to enable a wide range of customized text-to-image
029
               model editions across various domains and applications.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent advances in text-to-image models (Zhang et al., 2023b; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh
035   et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Betker et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2022) have
036   revolutionized visual content creation, enabling users to create highly realistic images from natural
037   language descriptions. However, as these models become more widely adopted, service providers
038
      face challenges in monetizing them and tailoring offerings to diverse customer needs. In the software
      industry, providers overcome this by offering product editions or versions tailored to specific user
039
      segments, e.g., Home Edition, Professional Edition, Enterprise Edition. In this work, we propose a
040
      novel task, namely, training-free editioning (Fig. 1), and apply this strategy to text-to-image models.
041
042   While it may seem straightforward, editioning is a challenging task as it requires preventing users
043   from bypassing access controls. For example, as Fig. 2 shows, the naive solution of sensitive word
044
      filtering does not work as users can easily evade it using descriptive prompts that are difficult to filter.
045   Our core idea is creating model variations without retraining to cater to different customer needs
046   or offer distinct features, formulating editions as concept subspaces within the embedding space of
047   the model’s text encoder. Our concept subspace encapsulates points satisfying a user requirement
048   (i.e., concept), e.g., cat images with specific choices of backgrounds. Technically, we apply Principal
049   Component Analysis (PCA) to text embeddings corresponding to a given concept and retain principal
050
      components capturing key variations to obtain a low-dimensional subspace for that concept within
      the original embedding space. Then, we achieve training-free editioning of text-to-image models by
051
      projecting the embeddings of input prompts into these subspaces.
052
053   Crucially, our approach allows service providers to efficiently customize the base model into targeted
      “editions” satisfying diverse customer needs without costly retraining. This leverages the pre-trained

      networks (GANs) ushered in a new era of text-to-image synthesis techniques. Text-conditional

132
      Diffusion Models. Diffusion models are a class of deep generative models that have recently
133
      demonstrated remarkable performance in generating high-quality samples across various applications.
134   These models are parameterized Markov chains trained using variational inference to generate
135   samples that match the data distribution after a finite number of iterations (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
136   Ho et al., 2020). Diffusion implicit models (Song et al., 2020), which are based on a class of non-
137   Markovian diffusion processes, lead to the same training objective as traditional diffusion models, but
138   can produce high-quality samples more efficiently. A representative framework for training diffusion
139   models in the latent space is Stable Diffusion, a scaled-up version of the Latent Diffusion Model
140   (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022). Thanks to its flexibility allowing for multi-modal control signals
141   (including text), Stable Diffusion has captivated the imagination of many users and dominated the
142
      field, especially in the open-source community. For example, Gal et al. (2022) proposed a novel
      approach to create variations of a given “concept” by representing it with a single word embedding;
143
      Prompt-to-prompt (Hertz et al., 2022) focuses on manipulating the attention maps corresponding to
144
      the text embeddings for editing images in pre-trained text-conditioned diffusion models; Null-text
145   inversion (Mokady et al., 2023) proposed performing Denoising Diffusion Image Model (DDIM)
146   inversion on the input image with related prompts into the latent space of a text-guided diffusion
147   model, enabling intuitive text-based image editing. These efforts, while effective, have focused
148   primarily on extending the technical power and usability of the text-to-image (diffusion) model,
149   whose business model is still immature.
150
      To bridge this gap, we propose a novel task called training-free editioning for text-to-image models,
151   which aims to create customized variations or editions of a base model without expensive retraining.
152   This enables service providers to offer differentiated product editions, innovative pricing strategies,
153   and tailored solutions optimized for commercial deployment.

158
      a list of n concepts (textual) to be editioned on, p be an input prompt to M , we denote the image

      where I is restricted to only containing concepts in C.

      3.1   D IFFERENCES WITH I MAGE E DITING AND C ONCEPT E RASING
163
164   Editioning vs. Editing. Task-wise, text-to-image editing (Kawar et al., 2023; Rombach et al.,
165   2022; Hertz et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2023; Mokady et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Yang
166   et al., 2023; Couairon et al., 2022) works at the aspect/image-level, which typically refers to the
167   process of modifying or manipulating specific aspects of an existing image based on a text prompt or
168   instructions while leaving irrelevant aspects of it unchanged, e.g., inpainting, outpainting, or style
169   transfer. In contrast, the proposed text-to-image editioning task works at the model-level, which
170
      aims to customize the behavior of the text-to-image model itself to cater to specific user needs or
      functionalities. Methodology-wise, since editing works at the aspect/image-level, its key challenge is
171
      to disentangle the target aspect of an image that needs to be edited from the rest, e.g., by manipulating
172
      the attention maps of a given image (Xu et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022;
173   Mokady et al., 2023). On the contrary, our editioning task is performed at the model level, so its
174   main challenge lies in controlling the model’s behavior, e.g., by manipulating the model’s text/image
175   embedding space.
176
      Editioning vs. Concept Erasing. Task-wise, our editioning and concept erasing can be viewed as
177
      complementary tasks, where our editioning aims to retain concept(s) C from a model and concept
178
      erasing aims to remove C from the model. Methodology-wise, existing concept erasing meth-
179   ods (Gandikota et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
180   2023; Yildirim et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Kim et al., 2023) primarily focus on fine-tuning model
181   weights. In contrast, our approach does not involve any training and concentrates on manipulating
182   the model’s text/image embedding space directly. The choice of such distinct methodologies stems
183   from the observation that C typically constitutes a relatively small subset compared to the entire set
184   of concepts learned by the model. Consequently, for concept erasing, removing C can be achieved
185   through a minor perturbation of the model weights. However, for our editioning task, retaining C and
186   dropping all other concepts would necessitate a significant modification, akin to retraining the entire
187
      model from scratch.

191   Addressing Definition 1, we propose a novel approach, namely Concept Subspace Projection,
192   which achieves M (p | C) by projecting the embedding vector of p to a concept subspace SE (C)
193   defined by C. Specifically, let E and G be the text encoder and generator of M , respectively, i.e.,
194
      M (·) = G(E(·)), SE = Rd be the d-dimensional embedding space of E, we have:
195                                      E(p | C) = P RSE (C) (E(p))                                  (2)
196   where P Rx (y) denotes the projection of y on x, SE (C) = RdC ⊂ SE denotes the dC -dimensional
197   concept subspace specified by concepts C, dC < d. In this way, we create the C-edition of M as the
198   concept space SE (C) and have:
199

525   6       C ONCLUSION
526
527   Inspired by software editioning, we propose training-free “editioning” of text-to-image models by
528   identifying concept subspaces within the latent space of their text encoders (e.g., CLIP). These
529
      subspaces, obtained via applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on representative text embed-
      dings, correspond to specific concepts like “cat”, “dog”, “boy”. Projecting the text embedding of a
530
      given prompt into these low-dimensional subspaces enables efficient model customization without
531
      retraining. This unlocks novel business models, such as offering restricted “cat editions” that only
532   generate cat images regardless of subjects in input prompts, enabling new product differentiation and
533   pricing strategies.
