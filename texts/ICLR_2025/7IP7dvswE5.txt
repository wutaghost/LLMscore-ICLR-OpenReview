009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                In marked event streams, Marked Temporal Point Process (MTPP) is central to
012                predicting when and what mark the next event will occur based on the history. In
013                various real-world applications, the mark distribution is significantly imbalanced,
014                i.e., some marks are frequent, and others are rare. We unveil that such imbalance
015                can cause the rare mark missing issue when predicting the next event – frequent
016                marks are dominant, and rare marks often have no chance. However, rare marks can
017                be essential in some applications (e.g., the occurrence of a 7-magnitude earthquake),
018
                   and missing such rare marks in the next event prediction is risky. To address this
                   issue, we tackle a novel Rare-mark-aware Next Event Prediction problem (RM-
019
                   NEP), answering two questions for each mark m: “what is the probability that
020
                   the mark of the next event is m? and if m, when will the next event happen?”.
021                Solving RM-NEP gives rare marks equal opportunity as frequent marks in the
022                next event prediction. This guarantees that rare marks are always included in
023                the predicted results. Moreover, RM-NEP allows arbitrary number of rare marks
024                samples for time prediction without interference from frequent marks, ensuring
025                the time prediction is accurate. To solve RM-NEP effectively, we first unify the
026                improper integration of two different functions into one and then develop a novel
027                Integral-free Neural Marked Temporal Point Process (IFNMTPP) to approximate
028                the target integral directly. Extensive experiments on real-world and synthetic
029
                   datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our solution for RM-NEP against
                   various baselines.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
      Events have been generated continuously in human activities or observed from natural phenomena.
035
      The Temporal Point Process (TPP) models point sequences that represent the arrival time of events.
036
      TPPs are built upon rich theoretical foundations, with early work dating back to many decades ago,
037   where they were used to model the arrival of insurance claims and telephone traffic(Shchur et al.,
038   2020), till now widely applied in social network analysis(Farajtabar et al., 2017; Rizoiu et al., 2017;
039   Zeng & Gao, 2022), neural logic inference(Mei et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; 2022), and biological
040   activity modeling(Tagliazucchi et al., 2012).
041
      The Marked Temporal Point Process (MTPP) models scenarios where each event comes with a
042
      mark and its arrival time. The mark can be categorical, such as the small/large earthquakes,
043   mild/moderate/critical and symptoms of patients visiting an emergency department, sell/buy in
044   financial transactions; it can also be numerical, such as temperature in the weather forecast, the
045   longitude and latitude of observations in ecology. As often encountered in practice, the MTPP has
046   attracted much attention from the research community (See Shchur et al. (2021) for a comprehensive
047   review). Most existing studies assume that events in sequences are correlated, and therefore MTPPs
048   are conditioned on history, i.e., the events that occurred so far.
049   MTPP is central to modeling the sequence of events and predicting the mark and time of the next
050   event, based on the conditional joint Probability Distribution Function (PDF), denoted as p∗ (m, t) 1 ,
051   learned from history by encoding the interconnection between event mark m and inter-event time t.
052   Typically, a single mark and a single time are returned as the prediction. For the sake of description,

      we name it Next-event Prediction problem (NEP) in this study. Most existing MTPP studies first
055   predict when the next event will occur and then what mark the next event is at the time predicted(Mei
056   & Eisner, 2017; Zhang et al., 2020; Zuo et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022). Few studies first predict what
057   the mark of the next event is and then when it will occur(Waghmare et al., 2022). Some recent studies
058   solve NEP by directly generating the time and mark of the next event simultaneously(Yuan et al.,
059   2023; Lüdke et al., 2023).
060
      In various real-world scenarios, the mark distribution is significantly imbalanced, i.e., some marks
061   are highly frequent and others are rare. The distribution imbalance may significantly impact the
062   solution of NEP - the frequent marks are dominant so that rare marks have no chance in the next
063   event prediction. However, rare marks are often significant for real-world applications, and neglect of
064   such rare marks is risky. Let us consider the following scenario. Major earthquakes with significant
065   magnitude (for example, bigger than 7.0 on the Richter scale) are rare but considered devastating
066   as they can cause heavy damage to the city and lots of casualties2 . Therefore, with an MTPP-based
067   earthquake predictor for a region, people are interested in when it will occur if the next earthquake is
068   a major one, even rare but possible. However, the small earthquakes are frequent. Following the NEP,
069
      it predicts when the next earthquake will happen, denoted as t, and then what kind of earthquake it
      might be at t. As shown in Figure 1, small earthquakes will dominate the next event prediction and
070
      the major ones may be never predicted, making it impossible to inform the time of major earthquake
071
      if it will occur next. We name it the rare mark missing issue in NEP.
072
073   Our study shows that the rare mark missing issue is intrinsic in NEP and is hard to tackle if predicting
074   a single mark and a single time like NEP. To address the root cause of the rare mark missing issue, we
075
      propose the new Rare-mark-aware Next Event Prediction problem (RM-NEP). Different from NEP,
      RM-NEP gives rare marks an equal chance as frequent marks in the next prediction by answering two
076
      questions for each mark m “what is the probability that the mark of the next event is m? and “if m,
077
      when will it happen? This guarantees that rare marks are consistently included in the predicted results.
078   Furthermore, RM-NEP allows an arbitrary number of samples for rare marks in the next event time
079   prediction without the interference of frequent marks, so the time prediction for rare marks is accurate.
080   Solving RM-NEP faces the unique challenge of improper integration over the infinite time interval
081   for estimation of the probability of marks and their time. Classical numeric integration methods such
082   as Monte Carlo integration are computationally heavy and can only estimate integrals on a finite
083   interval. To attack the challenge, we first propose to unify two improper integral functions into one
084   and then develop a novel MTPP model specifically designed to efficiently solve improper integral
085   function, called IFNMTPP (Integral-free Neural Marked Temporal Point Process). Contributions of
086
      this study are threefold:
087
                 • This study identifies the rare mark missing issue in the Next-event Prediction problem (NEP)
088
                   and propose a novel Rare-mark-aware Next Event Prediction problem (RM-NEP) to give
089                rare marks equal chance as frequent marks in the next event prediction.
090
                 • For an efficient solution of RM-NEP, we unify the two improper integral functions involved
091
                   into one.
092
093              • To improve the improper integration, we develop a novel model IFNMTPP to directly
094
                   approximate the integration via a simple monotonically decreasing neural network.

140   2.2   NEP AND R ARE M ARK M ISSING

142
      With MTPP, NEP requires a single mark and a single time as the prediction of the next event. The
      frequent marks dominate the results. Let us consider two marks k1 and k2 where k1 is much more
143
      frequent than k2 in the observed event sequence. Suppose the next event is mark k2 . Because k1 is
144
      muchR more P   frequent than k2 , it is very likely that p∗ (k1 , t) > p∗ (k2 , t) for most time t, including
145          ∞
      t̄ = t=tl τ m∈M p∗ (m, τ )dτ . If so, k1 will be predicted as the next event. In the extreme case,
146
      if p∗ (k1 , t) > p∗ (k2 , t) for every time t, k1 will always be predicted as the mark of the next event,
147
      while k2 will never have the chance. This is true no matter the order that the time and mark of the
148   next event are predicted. We name this situation as rare mark missing in the results of NEP.
149
150   Figure 1 (a) demonstrates the mark frequency distribution in four datasets (see details in Section 4).
151

      all instances in the datasets and the line is the average of p∗ (m, t) across these instances. Figure 1

      (c) presents the percentage of each mark in the results of NEP. Using Retweet as an example, the
153
      frequent marks (mark 0 and 1 occupying around 50% and 45% of data) have a much higher p∗ (m, t)
154   than the rare mark (mark 2 occupying around 5%) at almost every time t for almost all instances as
155   shown in Figure 1 (b) (leftmost). So, marks 0 and 1 are predicted as the next event mark while mark
156   2 has no chance, as evidenced in Figure 1 (c) (leftmost). A similar situation can be observed on other

338   time of the event. More details of these baselines are available in Appendix C.4.

343   next event simultaneously by training a DDPM to enable a process to sample p∗ (m, t) from a normal
344   distribution, and does not work for the particular mark as needed in RM-NEP.
345   Evaluation Metrics For synthetic datasets, because the true distribution p̂∗ (m, t) is known, we can
346   compare the learned p∗ (m, t) against the real one to evaluate the fidelity of a MTPP model. Most
347   papers report the relative NLL loss, the average of the absolute difference between − log p̂∗ (m, t) and
348   − log p∗ (m, t) on the observed events(if markers are unavailable, − log p̂∗ (t) and − log p∗ (t)(Omi
349   et al., 2019; Shchur et al., 2020)). The lower relative NLL loss indicates a better performance.
350   However, such a metric only evaluates performance at discrete events, which cannot gauge the overall
351
      discrepancy between p̂∗ (m, t) and p∗ (m, t). Therefore, we select Spearman Coefficient, L1 distance
      and the relative NLL loss to measure the discrepancy between p̂∗ (m, t) and p∗ (m, t) over time.

      Details of these metrics are available in Appendix C.3.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      To solve NEP, most MTPP studies specify a separate Conditional Intensity Function (CIF) λ∗ (m, t)
489   for each categorical mark m, based on which p∗ (m, t) can be formulated(Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003;
490   Mei & Eisner, 2017; Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Enguehard et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022). All
491   these studies assume λ∗ (m, t) has a specific functional form which can be integrated to infer p∗ (m, t).
492   As pointed out by Shchur et al. (2020), requirement of integration is the intrinsic shortcomings of
493   CIF models due to the trade-off between efficiency and effectiveness. A more sophisticated intensity
494   function(Mei & Eisner, 2017; Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022) can better capture
495   the system dynamics but will require approximating the integral of λ∗ (m, t) using a numerical
496   method such as Monte Carlo. Recurrent Marked Temporal Point Process(RMTPP)(Du et al., 2016)
497
      eludes numerical integral approximation as the CIF and its integral have a closed form, which makes
      the log-likelihood easy to compute. However, RMTPP ignores the relation between mark and time
498
      because of factorizing p∗ (m, t) into two independent distribution p∗ (t) and p∗ (m). Moreover, the
499
      predefined closed-form CIF usually has limited expressiveness.
500
501   Recent studies move away from directly modeling CIF. Shchur et al. (2020) proposed an intensity-free
502   solution, called LogNormMix, to infer the density function p∗ (t) from a simple distribution such as
503
      the mixture of log-normal distributions. In the scenarios of multiple marks, the intensity-free solution
      factorizes p∗ (m, t) into a product of two independent distributions p∗ (t) and p∗ (m). Omi et al. (2019)
504
      proposed FullyNN to model the integral of CIF using a neural network where CIF can be derived by
505
      differentiation, an operation computationally much easier compared with integration. FullyNN was
506   proposed for TPP rather than MTPP, which does not consider event marks. Also, FullyNN cannot
507   guarantee essential mathematical restrictions(Shchur et al., 2020). The idea of FullyNN inspired
508   integral-based Spatio-temporal Point Process (STPP) models(Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou & Yu, 2023),
509   where the marks are locations in a continuous spatial space.
510
      All MTPP studies discussed so far predict the time of the next event first and then predict the mark.
511
      Recently, Waghmare et al. (2022) proposes to model p∗ (m) using a classifier to predict the mark of
512   the next event and modeling p∗ (t|m) to predict the time of the event based on LogNormMix. Besides
513   the classic MTPP approaches, some researchers explore approaches that directly generate the time
514   and mark of the next event simultaneously. Yuan et al. (2023) and Lüdke et al. (2023) suggest to
515   represent the conditional distribution by a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM)(Ho et al.,
516   2020). Lüdke et al. (2023) applied denoising diffusion to convert a long event sequence sampled from
517   a predefined Poisson distribution to any given distribution. Meanwhile, Yuan et al. (2023) proposed
518   DSTPP, which employs DDPM to represent STPP.
519   In summary, the existing studies in the current literature return a single mark and a single time as the
520   prediction of the next event, i.e., NEP. None of them consider the imbalanced distribution of marks.
521   The frequent marks dominate the results of NEP and rare marks are mostly missing in the next event
522   prediction as shown in Figure 1. This is unacceptable in many application scenarios as discussed in
523   Section 1. Our study aims to address this issue.
524
525   6    L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
526
527   Limitation One limitation is the experiment results on efficiency are measured by the total evaluation
528   time on the test datasets instead of the expected FLOP counting. However we carefully managed the
529   experiment environment and every run has the same computation resources, so we believe that the
530   evaluation time can properly demonstrate the efficiency of the models.
531
      Conclusion In the existing MTPP studies, the results of Next-event Prediction problem (NEP) are
532
      dominated by frequent marks, and the rare marks may be never present. This situation is unacceptable
533   in many applications if the rare mark is critical such as major earthquakes. To fill the gap, this
534   study tackles the novel Rare-mark-aware Next Event Prediction problem (RM-NEP) to answer two
535   questions for each mark m: “what is the probability that the mark of the next event is m? and “if m,
536   when will the next event happen?”. This study solves RM-NEP accurately and efficiently by unifying
537   two different improper integration functions into Γ∗ (m, t) and developing a novel Integral-free Neural
538   Marked Temporal Point Process (IFNMTPP) to directly calculate Γ∗ (m, t). Extensive experiments on
539   real-world and synthetic datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our solution for RM-NEP
      against various baselines.

982    probability distributions of TPP are non-linear, we select Spearman coefficient.

997
998    The details of baselines in Section 4 are introduced next.
999
1000          • Fully Neural Network(FullyNN)(Omi et al., 2019) uses a neural network to estimate the inte-
1001
                gral of λ∗ (t) for the history embedding h and inter-event time t. Then the density function
                is formulated to predict the time of the next event. FullyNN is designed for TPP without
1002
                the information of event marks. To work with MTPP, FullyNN can be simply extended but
1003
                it has a performance issue. Details are available in Appendix B.1. We rewrote FullyNN
1004            in PyTorch(Paszke et al., 2019) based on the official implementation available at https:
1005            //github.com/omitakahiro/NeuralNetworkPointProcess, which is pub-
1006            licly accessible without any license.
1007
              • Fully Event Neural Network(FENN) is an extension of FullyNN. FENN successfully over-
1008            comes the computation graph overlap issue yet still inherits FullyNN’s drawback of failing
1009            to comply with the mathematical restrictions. We believe sometimes such failure might be
1010            responsible for the inferior performance of FENN. Detailed information about FENN is
1011            available in Appendix B.2. The implementation of FENN is a direct modification of our
1012            FullyNN implementation.
1013          • Transformer Hawkes Process(THP)(Zuo et al., 2020) uses a Transformer-based encoder to
1014            represent history as a hidden state h. The softplus-based intensity function and the density
1015            function are modelled to predict the time of next event. We reproduce this model in PyTorch
1016            based on the paper.
1017          • Self-Attentive Hawkes Process(SAHP)(Zhang et al., 2020) is based on the same intuition as
1018            Continuous-time LSTM(CTLSTM)(Mei & Eisner, 2017), which generalizes the classical
1019            Hawkes process by parameterizing its intensity function with recurrent neural networks.
1020            CTLSTM is an interpolated version of the standard LSTM, allowing us to generate outputs
1021            in a continuous-time domain. SAHP further improves performance by replacing LSTM
1022            with Transformers. Because the only difference between SAHP and CTLSTM is the history
1023            encoder, and SAHP has reported achieving better performance than CTLSTM, we only
1024
                evaluate SAHP in this paper. We reproduce this model in PyTorch based on the paper.
1025          • Marked LogNormMix(Marked-LNM)(Waghmare et al., 2022) is an MTPP extension of
                the LogNormMix(Shchur et al., 2020). Marked-LNM also follows the MT paradigm by
