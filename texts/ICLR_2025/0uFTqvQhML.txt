009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                While controllable generative models for images and videos have achieved re-
012                markable success, high-quality models for 3D scenes, particularly in unbounded
013                scenarios like autonomous driving, remain underdeveloped due to high data ac-
014                quisition costs. In this paper, we introduce MagicDrive3D, a novel pipeline for
015                controllable 3D street scene generation that supports multi-condition control, in-
016                cluding BEV maps, 3D objects, and text descriptions. Unlike previous methods
017                that reconstruct before training the generative models, MagicDrive3D first trains
018
                   a video generation model and then reconstructs from the generated data. This
                   innovative approach enables easily controllable generation and static scene acqui-
019
                   sition, resulting in high-quality scene reconstruction. To address the minor errors
020
                   in generated content, we propose deformable Gaussian splatting with monocu-
021                lar depth initialization and appearance modeling to manage exposure discrepan-
022                cies across viewpoints. Validated on the nuScenes dataset, MagicDrive3D gen-
023                erates diverse, high-quality 3D driving scenes that support any-view rendering
024                and enhance downstream tasks like BEV segmentation. Our results demonstrate
025                the framework’s superior performance, showcasing its transformative potential for
026                autonomous driving simulation and beyond.

      1      I NTRODUCTION
048
049
      With the advancement of generative models, particularly diffusion models (Goodfellow et al., 2014;
      Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022), there has been increasing interest in gen-
050
      erating 3D assets (Poole et al., 2022; Vahdat et al., 2022; Bautista et al., 2022). While a significant
051
      amount of work has focused on object-centric generation (Poole et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024), gen-
052   erating open-ended 3D scenes remains relatively unexplored. This gap is even more critical because
053
            Project Page: https://magicdrive3d.github.io/.

054
      many downstream applications, such as Virtual Reality (VR) and autonomous driving simulation,
055   require controllable generation of 3D street scenes, which is an open challenge.
056
057   3D-aware view synthesis1 methods can be broadly categorized into two approaches: geometry-
058
      free view synthesis and geometry-focused scene generation (Rombach et al., 2021). Geometry-
      free methods directly generate 2D images (Chen et al., 2024) or videos (Gao et al., 2024; Wen
059
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) based on camera parameters, excelling in photo-realistic image
060
      generation. However, they often lack sufficient geometric consistency, limiting their ability to extend
061   to viewpoints beyond the dataset (Gao et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). On the
062   other hand, geometry-focused methods (e.g., GAUDI (Bautista et al., 2022) and NF-LDM (Kim
063   et al., 2023)) generate 3D representations (e.g., NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) or voxel grids) from
064   latent inputs, supporting multi-view rendering. Despite their broader applicability, these methods
065   require expensive data collection, necessitating static scenes and consistent sensor properties like
066   exposure and white balance. Street view datasets, such as nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020), often fail
067   to meet these requirements, making it extremely challenging to train geometry-focused 3D street
068   scene generation models using such datasets.
069   Recognizing the advancements in controllable generation by geometry-free view synthesis meth-
070   ods (Chen et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024), it is potential to use them as data engines. Their con-
071   trollability and photo-realism could address the challenges faced by geometry-focused methods.
072   However, the limited 3D consistency in synthetic views from geometry-free methods, such as tem-
073   poral inconsistency among frames and deformation of objects, poses crucial issues for integrating
074   two kinds of methods into a unified framework.
075   To address these challenges, we propose MagicDrive3D, a novel framework that combines
076   geometry-free view synthesis and geometry-focused reconstruction for controllable 3D street scene
077   generation. As illustrated in Figure 2, our approach begins with training a multi-view video gener-
078   ation model to synthesize multiple views of a static scene. This model is configured using controls
079   from object boxes, road maps, text prompts, and camera poses. To enhance inter-frame 3D con-
080   sistency, we incorporate coordinate embeddings that represent the relative transformation between
081
      LiDAR coordinates for accurate control of frame positions. Next, we improve the reconstruction
      quality of generated views by enhancing 3D Gaussian splatting from the perspectives of prior knowl-
082
      edge, modeling, and loss functions. Given the limited overlap between different camera views (Xie
083
      et al., 2023), we adopt a monocular depth prior and propose a dedicated algorithm for alignment
084   in sparse-view settings. Additionally, we introduce deformable Gaussian splatting and appearance
085   embedding maps to handle local dynamics and exposure discrepancies, respectively.
086
087
      Demonstrated by extensive experiments, our MagicDrive3D framework excels in generating highly
      realistic street scenes that align with road maps, 3D bounding boxes, and text descriptions, as ex-
088
      emplified in Figure 1. We show that the generated camera views can augment training for Bird’s
089
      Eye View (BEV) segmentation tasks, providing comprehensive controls for scene generation and
090   enabling the creation of novel street scenes for autonomous driving simulation. Notably, Magic-
091   Drive3D is the first to achieve controllable 3D street scene generation using a common driving
092   dataset (e.g., the nuScenes dataset (Caesar et al., 2020)), without requiring repeated data collection
093   from static scenes.
094
      We summarize our contributions as follows:
095
096   • We propose MagicDrive3D, the first framework to effectively integrate both geometry-free and
097     geometry-focused view synthesis for controllable 3D street scene generation. MagicDrive3D gen-
098     erates realistic 3D street scenes that support rendering from any camera view according to various
099     control signals.
100   • We introduce a relative pose embedding technique to generate videos with improved 3D consis-
101     tency. Additionally, we enhance the reconstruction quality with tailored techniques, including
102     deformable Gaussian splatting, to handle local dynamics and exposure discrepancies in the gener-
103     ated videos.
104
      • Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that MagicDrive3D generates high-quality street
105     scenes with multi-dimensional controllability. Our results also show that synthetic data improves
106     3D perception tasks, highlighting the practical benefits of our approach.

      3D Scene Generation. Numerous 3D-aware generative models can synthesize images with ex-
      plicit camera pose control (Zhao et al., 2024; Rombach et al., 2021) and potentially other scene
112
      properties (Tang et al., 2023), but only a few scale for open-ended 3D scene generation. GSN (De-
113
      Vries et al., 2021) and GAUDI (Bautista et al., 2022), representative of models generating indoor
114   scenes, utilize NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) with latent code input for “floorplan” or tri-plane fea-
115   ture. Their reliance on datasets covering different camera poses is incompatible with typical driving
116   datasets where camera configuration remains constant. NF-LDM (Kim et al., 2023) develops a hier-
117   archical latent diffusion model for scene feature voxel grid generation. However, their representation
118   and complex modeling hinder fine detail generation.
119
      Contrary to previous works focusing on scene generation using explicit geometry, often requir-
120   ing substantial data not suitable for typical street view datasets (e.g., nuScenes (Caesar et al.,
121   2020)) as discussed in Section 1, we propose merging geometry-free view synthesis with geometry-
122   focused scene representations for controllable street scene creation. Methodologically, Lucid-
123   Dreamer (Chung et al., 2023) is most similar to our approach, although it relies on a text-controlled
124   image generation model, which cannot qualify as a view synthesis model. In contrast, our video gen-
125   eration model is 3D-aware. Besides, we propose several improvements over 3DGS for better scene
126   generation quality. Besides, inpainting-based methods like LucidDreamer (Chung et al., 2023) and
127   WonderJourney (Yu et al., 2024) cannot complete our controllable street scene generation task. We
128
      showcase the differences in Appendix C.
129   Street View Video Generation. Diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020) have in-
130   fluenced a range of works on street view video generation, from single to multi-view videos (e.g.,
131   (Wang et al., 2023a; Gao et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b)). Despite cross-view
132   consistency being essential for multi-view video generation, their generalization ability on camera
133   poses is limited due to their data-centric nature (Gao et al., 2024). Furthermore, these models lack
134
      exact control over frame transformation (i.e., precise car trajectory), which is crucial for scene re-
      construction. Our work addresses this by enhancing control in video generation and proposing a
135
      dedicated deformable Gaussian splatting for geometric assurance.
136
137   Street Scene Reconstruction. Scene reconstruction and novel view rendering for street views are
138   useful in applications like driving simulation, data generation, and augmented and virtual reality.
139   For street scenes, attributes like scene dynamic and discrepancies from multi-camera data collection
140
      make typical large-scale reconstruction methods ineffective (e.g., Rematas et al. (2022); Martin-
      Brualla et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2024)). Hence, real data-based reconstruction methods like Xie
141
      et al. (2023); Yan et al. (2024) utilize LiDAR for depth prior, but their output only permits novel
142
      view rendering from the same scene. Unlike these methods, our approach synthesizes novel scenes
143   under multiple levels of conditional controls.

      Problem Formulation. In this paper, we focus on controllable street scene generation. Given
149
      scene description St , our task is to generate street scenes (represented with 3D Gaussians G) that
150   correspond to the description from a set of latent z ∼ N (0, I), i.e. G = G(St , z). To describe a
151   street scene, we adopt the most commonly used conditions as per Gao et al. (2024); Wang et al.
152   (2023b); Wen et al. (2023). Specifically, a frame of driving scene St = {Mt , Bt , Lt } is described
153   by road map Mt ∈ {0, 1}w×h×c (a binary map representing a w × h meter road area in BEV with
154   c semantic classes), 3D bounding boxes Bt = {(ci , bi )}N  i=1 (each object is described by box bi =
155   {(xj , yj , zj )}8j=1 ∈ R8×3 and class ci ∈ C), and text Lt describing additional information about
156   the scene (e.g., weather and time of day). In this paper, we parameterize all geometric information
157   according to the LiDAR coordinate of the ego car.
158   One direct application of scene generation is any-view rendering. Specifically, given any camera
159   pose P = [K, R, t] (i.e., intrinsics, rotation, and translation), the model G(·) should render photo-
160   realistic views with 3D consistency, I r = G(P), which is not applicable to previous street view
161   generation (e.g., Gao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023b); Wen et al. (2023)). Besides, we present
      more applications in Section 5.

      4.1     3D S TREET S CENE G ENERATION
197
      Direct modeling of controllable street scene generation faces two major challenges: scene dynamics
198   and discrepancy in data collection. Scene dynamics refer to the movements and deformation of
199   elements in the scene, while discrepancy in data collection refer to the discrepancy (e.g., exposure)
200   caused by data collection. These two challenges are even more severe due to the sparsity of cameras
201   for street views (e.g., typically only 6 surrounding perspective cameras). Therefore, reconstruction-
202   generation frameworks do not work well for street scene generation (Kim et al., 2023; Bautista et al.,
203   2022).
204   Figure 2 shows the overview of MagicDrive3D. Given scene descriptions S as input, MagicDrive3D
205   first extend the descriptions into sequence {St }, where t ∈ [0, T ] according to preset camera poses
206   {Pc,t }, and generate a sequence of successive multi-view images {Ic,t }, where c ∈ {1, . . . , N }
207   refers to N surrounding cameras, according to conditions {St , Pc,t } (detailed in Section 4.2). Then
208   we construct Gaussian representation of the scene with {Ic,t } and camera poses {Pc,t } as input.
209   This step contains an initializing procedure with a pre-trained monocular depth model and an op-
210   timizing process with deformable Gaussian splatting (detailed in Section 4.3). Consequently, the
211   generated street scene not only supports any-view rendering, but also accurately reflects different
212
      control signals.
213   MagicDrive3D integrate geometry-free view synthesis and geometry-focused reconstruction, where
214   control signals are tackled by a multi-view video generator, while reconstruction step guarantee the
215   generalization ability for any-view rendering. Such a video generator has two advantages: first,
      since multi-view video generation does not require generalization on novel views (Gao et al., 2024),

      it poses less data dependency for street scenes; second, through conditional training, the model is
217   capable of decomposition of control signals, and thus turns dynamic scenes into static scenes which
218   are more friendly for reconstruction. Besides, for the reconstruction step, strong prior from the
219   multi-view video reduces the burden for scene modeling with complex details.

221   4.2   R ELATIVE P OSE C ONTROL FOR V IDEO G ENERATION
222
223   Given scene descriptions and a sequence of camera poses {St , Pc,t }, our video generator is respon-
224   sible for multi-view video generation. Although many previous art for street view generation achieve
225   expressive visual effects, such as Gao et al. (2024); Wen et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b;a), their
226   formulations leave out a crucial requirement for 3D modeling. Specifically, the camera pose Pc,t is
227   typically relative to the LiDAR coordinate of each frame. Thus, there is no precise control signal
228
      related to the ego trajectory, which significantly determine the geometric relationship between views
      of different ts.
229
230   In our video generation model, we amend such precise control ability by adding the transformation
231   between each frame to the first frame, i.e., T0t . To properly encode such information, we adopt
232   Fourier embedding with Multi-Layer Perception (MLP), and concatenate the embedding with the
233   original embedding of Pc,t , similar to Gao et al. (2024). As a result, our video generator provides
234
      better 3D consistency across frames, most importantly, making the camera poses to each view avail-
      able in the same coordinate, i.e., [R0c,t , t0c,t ] = T0t [Rc,t , tc,t ].

526   6    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
527
528   This paper introduces MagicDrive3D, a unique 3D street scene generation framework that inte-
529   grates geometry-free view synthesis and geometry-focused 3D representations. MagicDrive3D sig-
530   nificantly reduces data requirements, enabling training on typical autonomous driving datasets, such
531   as nuScenes. Within the generation-reconstruction pipeline, MagicDrive3D employs a video gen-
532   eration model to enhance inter-frame consistency, while the enhanced deformable GS improves
533   reconstruction quality from generated views. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that Magic-
534   Drive3D can produce high-quality 3D street scenes with multi-level controls. Additionally, we show
535   that scene generation can serve as a data engine for perception tasks such as BEV segmentation.
536   Limitation and Future Work. As a data-centric method, MagicDrive3D sometimes struggles to
537   generate complex objects like pedestrians, whose appearances are intricate. Additionally, areas with
538   much texture detail (e.g., road fences) or small spatial features (e.g., light poles) are occasionally
539   poorly generated due to limitations in the reconstruction method. Future work may focus on ad-
      dressing these challenges and further improving the quality and robustness of generated 3D scenes.
