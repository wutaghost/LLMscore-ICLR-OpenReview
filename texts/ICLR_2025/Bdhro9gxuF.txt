                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               This paper primarily investigates large-scale finite-sum optimization problems,
015
               which are particularly prevalent in the big data era. In the field of zeroth-order
016
               optimization, stochastic optimization methods have become essential tools. Natu-
017            ral zeroth-order stochastic optimization methods are primarily based on stochastic
018            gradient descent (SGD). The method of preprocessing the stochastic gradient with
019            Gaussian vector is referred to as ZO-SGD-Gauss (ZSG), while estimating par-
020            tial derivatives along coordinate directions to compute the stochastic gradient is
021            known as ZO-SGD-Coordinate (ZSC). Compared to ZSC, ZSG often demon-
022            strates superior performance in practice. However, the underlying mechanisms
023            behind this phenomenon remain unclear in the academic community. To the best
024            of our knowledge, our work is the first to theoretically analyze the potential ad-
025
               vantages of ZSG compared to ZSC. Unlike the fundamental assumptions applied
               in general stochastic optimization analyses, the quadratic regularity assumption is
026
               proposed to generalize the smoothness and strong convexity to the Hessian matrix.
027
               This assumption allows us to incorporate Hessian information into the complexity
028            analysis. When the objective function is quadratic, the quadratic regularity as-
029            sumption reduces to the second-order Taylor expansion of the function, and we
030            focus on analyzing and proving the significant improvement of ZSG. For other
031            objective function classes, we also demonstrate the convergence of ZSG and its
032            potentially better query complexity than that of ZSC. Finally, experimental results
033            on both synthetic and real-world datasets substantiate the effectiveness of our the-
034            oretical analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Modern machine learning presents significant challenges for optimization due to the large scale of
      the problems involved. Contemporary datasets are both enormous and high-dimensional, often with
040
      millions of samples and features. Because evaluating the full objective or gradient even once is too
041
      slow to be useful, stochastic optimization methods have emerged in response.
042
043   Throughout the paper, we aim to solve finite-sum minimization problems of the form

      An optimization method that solves the problem (1) with function value access only is known as
048   zeroth-order optimization or black-box optimization (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Nesterov & Spokoiny,
049   2017). In recent years, zeroth-order optimization has attracted widespread attention from both the
050   machine learning community and the optimization community (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017; Ilyas
051   et al., 2018). One important application of the zeroth-order optimization is the black-box adversarial
052   attack on deep neural networks (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Bai et al.,
053   2023). In the black-box adversarial attack, only the inputs and outputs of the neural network are
      available and back propagation is often prohibited (Papernot et al., 2017). In the above situation,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the evaluation of gradient ∇f (x) is infeasible. So, applying zeroth-order optimization methods be-
055   comes a natural choice. Additional application scenarios in the field of artificial intelligence where
056   zeroth-order optimization algorithms demonstrate significant effectiveness are deep reinforcement
057   learning (Salimans et al., 2017; Mania et al., 2018; Zhang & Zavlanos, 2023; Jing et al., 2024),
058   hyper-parameter tuning (Snoek et al., 2012; Rapin & Teytaud, 2018), the problem of optimizing
059   functions with only ranking feedback (Tang et al., 2023), learning linear quadratic regulators (Ma-
060   lik et al., 2020; Mohammadi et al., 2020), and so on. Zeroth-order optimization has even played
061   a significant role in fine-tuning large language models (LLMs). Malladi et al. (2023) and Zhao
062   et al. (2024) use the zeroth-order optimization methods for fine-tuning, in addressing the signifi-
063
      cant memory overhead of first-order optimizers. Zeroth-order optimization achieves a substantial
      memory reduction and makes it possible to train and store LLMs on low-cost hardware.
064
065   Though ZO-SGD-Gauss (ZSG) and ZO-SGD-Coordinate (ZSC) share the same theoretical
066   convergence rate and their sample complexity is both linear to the dimension (Ghadimi & Lan,
067   2013), ZSG has wider application ranges and performs better than ZSC in practice. For exam-
068   ple, ZSG has been widely used in fine tuning LLMs (Malladi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024) and
069
      black-box attacks (Ilyas et al., 2018). The academic community is still unclear about the underly-
      ing mechanism why ZSG outperforms ZSC. For the gradient descent method, recent works by Yue
070
      et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2024) show that zeroth-order Gaussian gradient descent can outper-
071
      form coordinate descent if the Hessian has skewed eigenvalue distribution. An intriguing question
072   is whether the zeroth-order SGD algorithm possesses a similar property to the zeroth-order gradient
073   descent algorithm. Inspired by these works, we try to prove that ZSG can outperform ZSC under
074   similar conditions. We obtain a surprising result: compared to ZSC, ZSG possesses weak dimen-
075   sional dependence. Our work fills a theoretical gap in the field of zeroth-order optimization.

      1.1   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
079
080
081
      Here, we present a concise overview of stochastic optimization methods.
082   An optimization method that solves the problem (1) by accessing gradient information from a subset
083   of samples is called SGD. SGD and its variance reduction variants, which operate on only a small
084   mini-batch of data at each iteration, have become the preferred methods (Robbins & Monro, 1951;
085   Moulines & Bach, 2011; Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Allen-Zhu, 2018). However, stochastic optimizers
086   sacrifice stability in favor of speed. Parameters such as the learning rate are challenging to choose
087
      (Nemirovski et al., 2009), and for ill-conditioned large-scale machine learning problems, even find-
      ing the optimal learning rate can lead to very slow convergence. Second-order optimizers based on
088
      the Hessian, such as Newton’s method (Battiti, 1992) and quasi-Newton methods (Dennis & Moré,
089
      1977; Jin & Mokhtari, 2023), are the classic remedy for solving above challenges. Some researchers
090   have proposed using stochastic Hessian approximations while still utilizing the full gradient (Lacotte
091   et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2021). Then, Frangella et al. (2022) propose the SketchySGD algorithm
092   whose excellent performance suggests it could potentially replace SGD.
093
      When the gradient is difficult to calculate or cannot be obtained, researchers shift their attention from
094
      the study of SGD to stochastic zeroth-order optimization algorithms, estimating the gradient using
095
      function value differences (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Duchi et al., 2015; Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017).
096   Malladi et al. (2023) directly use zeroth-order optimizer (ZOO) for fine-tuning LLMs. However, the
097   zeroth-order optimization algorithms mentioned above overlook the use of higher-order information
098   about the objective, leading to less competitive convergence in practice. Similar to the development
099   of SGD, researchers have begun to introduce second-order Hessian information into zeroth-order
100   optimization algorithms. This idea holds promise for the design of efficient and competitive algo-
101   rithms. Chen et al. (2017) utilize the second-order Hessian information in a relatively coarsened
102   manner. Ye et al. (2018) take a first step to efficiently incorporate second-order Hessian information
103   of the objective function and propose a novel class of algorithms called the ZOHA algorithm. Zhao
104
      et al. (2024) propose HiZOO, which is the first work to leverage the diagonal Hessian to enhance
      ZOO for fine-tuning LLMs.
105
106   It is worth noting that Nesterov & Spokoiny (2017) conduct a theoretical analysis of the complex-
107   ity bounds for three random gradient-free oracles. However, they don’t find the conditions under
      which ZSG algorithm performs better than ZSC algorithm. The essential reason is that they do


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      not effectively utilize the information from the Hessian matrix in their theoretical analysis process.
109   Therefore, in essence, our work is different from that of (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017).

      1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
113
      The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
114
115          • Our work theoretically analyzes the conditions under which the ZSG algorithm outperforms
116            the ZSC algorithm. To the best of our knowledge, our conclusion is innovative and novel,
117            particularly in the context of quadratic functions. Through rigorous theoretical analysis,
118            we demonstrate that as long as tr(M) ≪ dλmax (M), where M is the Hessian matrix of
119            the objective function, the performance of the ZSG algorithm will surpass that of the ZSC
120            algorithm.
121          • Our theoretical analysis is based on the upper and lower quadratic regularity assumptions,
122            which generalize the L-smooth assumption and µ-strongly convexity assumption. Building
123            on a comprehensive theoretical examination of quadratic functions, we extend our com-
124            plexity analysis conclusions to a broader class of functions.
125
             • Our research indicates that ZSG also possesses weak dimensional dependence, similar to
126
               zeroth-order gradient descent. This fills a theoretical gap in the field of zeroth-order opti-
127            mization, and our analytical results provide significant theoretical insights.
128
129          • Extensive experiments confirm the reliability of theoretical analysis of our work. Either
130
               using synthetically designed data or real-world datasets, the performance of the ZSG algo-
               rithm outperforms that of the ZSC algorithm.
