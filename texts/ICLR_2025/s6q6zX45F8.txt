011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated learning (FL), aimed at leveraging vast distributed datasets, confronts a
014            crucial challenge: the heterogeneity of data across different silos. While previous
015            studies have explored discrete representations to enhance model generalization
016
               across minor distributional shifts, these approaches often struggle to adapt to new
               data silos with significantly divergent distributions. In response, we have identi-
017
               fied that models derived from FL exhibit markedly increased uncertainty when
018
               applied to data silos with unfamiliar distributions. Consequently, we propose
019            an innovative yet straightforward iterative framework, termed Uncertainty-Based
020            Extensible-Codebook Federated Learning (UEFL). This framework dynamically
021            maps latent features to trainable discrete vectors, assesses the uncertainty, and
022            specifically extends the discretization dictionary or codebook for silos exhibiting
023            high uncertainty. Our approach aims to simultaneously enhance accuracy and re-
024            duce uncertainty by explicitly addressing the diversity of data distributions, all
025            while maintaining minimal computational overhead in environments character-
026            ized by heterogeneous data silos. Through experiments conducted on various
027
               datasets, our method has demonstrated its superiority, achieving significant im-
               provements in accuracy (by 3%–22.1%) and uncertainty reduction (by 38.83%–
028
               96.24%), thereby outperforming contemporary state-of-the-art methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Federated Learning (FL), well known for its capacity to harness data from diverse devices and
034
      locations—termed data silos—while ensuring privacy, has become increasingly crucial in the digital
035
      era, particularly with the explosion of data from mobile sources. Despite its pivotal role in distributed
036   computing, FL confronts a formidable challenge: the heterogeneity of data across different silos.
037   Such diversity often results in a significant performance gap when integrating updates from local
038   models into the global model. In Fig. 1, we compare the mean accuracy of local FL models with
039   that of the global model after integration when addressing data silos with different distributions.
040   While local models may perform impressively within their own data domains, the aggregated global
041   model often struggles to achieve similar performance levels after synthesizing updates from these
042   varied data sources. This issue is especially pronounced in FL due to its reliance on varied data
043   sources.
044   Recent studies (Ghosh et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Kairouz et al., 2021a;
045   Zhang et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2022) have made significant advancements in addressing data het-
046   erogeneity within Federated Learning (FL), with one notable approach being the use of discrete
047   representations to enhance model robustness against minor data shifts. Nonetheless, this strategy
048   struggles to generalize models to data silos exhibiting significant distributional differences. Fur-
049   thermore, these methods face difficulties in adapting to unseen data distributions, as they typically
050
      require the entire model to be re-trained. Such constraints limit their flexibility in adapting to the
      dynamically changing data landscapes, posing challenges for their applicability in real-world sce-
051
      narios.
052
053   Moreover, we identify another critical issue impacting the model’s performance across diverse data
      silos: increased uncertainty, as shown in Fig. 1. The global model’s accuracy not only deteriorates,

074   challenges, we introduce Uncertainty-Based Extensible-codebook Federated Learning (UEFL), a
075   novel methodology that explicitly distinguishes between data distributions to improve both accuracy
076   and uncertainty.
077
      Specifically, our design features an advanced codebook comprising a predetermined number of latent
078   vectors (i.e. codewords), and employs a discretizer to assign encoded image features to their closest
079   codewords. These codewords, acting as latent representations, are passed to subsequent layers for
080   processing. The codewords are dynamically trained to align with the latent features generated by
081   the image encoder. To mitigate performance degradation when integrating local models from data
082   silos with varying distributions, we initialize a small, shared codebook for all clients. Additional
083   specific codewords are then introduced for individual client use, ensuring explicit differentiation be-
084   tween them. Since the initial codebook is small and requires only a few extensions, the final size
085   remains compact, minimizing the associated computational overhead. Given the privacy constraints
086
      in federated learning (FL), which restrict direct data access, we incorporate an uncertainty evalu-
      ator using Monte Carlo Dropout. This evaluator identifies data from diverse distributions, marked
087
      by high uncertainty. During training, our UEFL method systematically distinguishes between these
088
      varied distributions and dynamically adds new codewords to the codebook until all distributions are
089   sufficiently represented. In the initial training cycle, shared codewords are randomly initialized.
090   However, in subsequent cycles, the fully trained image encoder is leveraged to initialize new code-
091   words using K-means, aligning them more closely with the data distribution and facilitating faster
092   adaptation to various distributions. As a result, our UEFL model can accommodate data from pre-
093   viously unseen distributions with fewer communication rounds, making it applicable for enhancing
094   other FL algorithms. Furthermore, since uncertainty constantly decreases as training progresses, the
095   iterative process is guaranteed to conclude after a few iterations.
096   To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
097
098          • We identify a significant increase in model uncertainty across silos with diverse data dis-
099            tributions within the federated learning (FL) context, highlighting the challenge of data
100            heterogeneity.
101          • To address this heterogeneity, we introduce an extensible codebook approach that distin-
102            guishes between data distributions by stepwise mapping them to distinct, trainable latent
103            vectors (i.e. codewords). This methodology allows for efficient initialization of newly
104            added codewords using a K-means algorithm, closely aligning with the training data feature
105            distributions and enabling rapid convergence during codebook training.
106          • We propose a novel data-driven FL framework, named Uncertainty-Based Extensible-
107            codebook Federated Learning (UEFL), which merges the extensible codebook with an un-

119   2.1   F EDERATED L EARNING
120
121   Federated learning (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; Geyer et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Hard et al., 2018;
122
      Yang et al., 2019; Ghosh et al., 2020) represents a cutting-edge distributed learning paradigm, specif-
      ically designed to exploit data and computational resources across edge devices. The Federated
123
      Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm (McMahan et al., 2017), introduced to address the challenges of un-
124
      balanced and non-IID data, optimizes the trade-off between computation and communication costs
125   by reducing the necessary communication rounds for training deep networks. Federated Learning
126   (FL) faces numerous statistical challenges, with data heterogeneity being one of the most critical. In
127   real-world applications, data collected across different clients often varies significantly in terms of
128   distribution, feature space, and sample sizes.
129
      Several methodologies (Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018; 2019; Kalra et al., 2023) have been de-
130
      veloped to address this pivotal issue. PMFL (Zhang et al., 2022) approaches the heterogeneity chal-
131   lenge by drawing inspiration from meta-learning and continual learning, opting to integrate losses
132   from local models over the aggregation of gradients or parameters. DisTrans (Yuan et al., 2022)
133   enhances FL performance through train and test-time distributional transformations, coupled with
134   a novel double-input-channel model architecture. Meanwhile, FCCL (Huang et al., 2022) employs
135   knowledge distillation during local updates to facilitate the sharing of inter and intra domain insights
136   without compromising privacy, and utilizes unlabeled public data to foster a generalizable represen-
137   tation amidst domain shifts. Additionally, the discrete approach to addressing heterogeneity by Liu
138   et al. (2021), provide further inspiration and valuable perspectives for our research endeavors.
139
140   2.2   U NCERTAINTY
141
142
      Recently, the study of uncertainty modeling has gained significant prominence across various re-
      search fields, notably within the machine learning community (Chen et al., 2014; Blundell et al.,
143
      2015; Kendall & Gal, 2017; Louizos & Welling, 2017; Lahlou et al., 2021; Nado et al., 2021;
144
      Gawlikowski et al., 2021). This surge in interest is driven by the critical need to understand and
145   quantify the inherent ambiguity in complex datasets. Techniques such as Monte Carlo Dropout (Gal
146   & Ghahramani, 2016), which introduces variability in model outputs through the use of dropout lay-
147   ers, and Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017), which leverages multiple models with
148   randomly initialized weights trained on identical datasets to evaluate uncertainty, exemplify the ad-
149   vancements in this area. Furthermore, the application of uncertainty modeling has extended beyond
150   traditional domains, impacting fields such as healthcare (Dusenberry et al., 2020) and continual
151   learning (Ahn et al., 2019).

244   3.2   E XTENSIBLE C ODEBOOK
245
      To effectively manage heterogeneous data, we design an extensible codebook, beginning with a min-
246   imal set of codewords and progressively enlarging this set through a superior initialization strategy
247   that benefits from our UEFL framework. This strategy facilitates stepwise mapping of diverse data
248   distributions to distinct codewords. Starting with a larger codebook can introduce uncertainty in
249   codeword selection due to the concurrent training of multiple codewords.
250
      Similar to VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), we employ latent vectors as codewords, initializing
251                                                             d
      a compact shared codebook with n codewords c ∈ Rn× s , where n represents the size of the initial
252
      codebook. The codewords are initialized using a Gaussian distribution and shared across all data
253
      silos. After each iteration’s uncertainty assessment, we determine which silos require additional
254   codewords to improve prediction accuracy, and we extend the codebook accordingly for these silos
255   by adding n more codewords.. The newly added codewords are initialized using K-means, leverag-
256   ing the encoder’s improved latent features from the prior iteration to better align with the underlying
257   data distribution. To optimize codebook usage, data silos that demonstrated lower performance in
258   the previous iteration are allowed to select codewords from both the newly added codewords and the
259   original shared codebook. Typically, the codebook only requires 1-3 extensions until all clients reach
260   low uncertainty levels. The server updates the codebook by computing the average of codewords
261   across the clients that utilize those specific codewords.
262   For a given iteration, if the codebook size for the kth client is vk , the feature vector z is associated
263   with a codeword ci by the discretizer, which computes the distance between z and all available
264   codewords, selecting the nearest one as follows,
265

268   K-means Initialization. After the first iteration, the adapted encoder produces image features that
269   more accurately reflect the distribution of the training data. Instead of relying on random initializa-
      tion methods like Gaussian distribution, we initialize new codewords using the centroids of these

      features, obtained through K-means clustering. This approach expedites codebook training by pro-
271   viding a more informed starting point for the new codewords, allowing them to better align with the
272   underlying data structure. As a result, this initialization strategy facilitates faster convergence and
273   improves the model’s ability to adapt to varying data distributions across silos. This strategy hugely
274   reduces the number of training rounds required for model convergence (Details in Appendix I).

      Segmented Codebooks. For complex datasets, a finite set of discrete codewords might not fully
276
      capture the diversity of image features. To bolster the robustness of our methodology, we dissect
277   features into smaller segments to pair them with multiple codewords, thus covering the entirety of a
278   feature vector. This segmentation exponentially increases the codeword pool, ensuring a robust rep-
279   resentation capacity without necessitating a large-scale increase and permitting efficient K-means-
280   based initialization. This design minimizes runtime overhead associated with larger codebooks.

      5      C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we address the challenge of data heterogeneity among silos within federated learning
532
      setting by introducing an innovative solution: an extensible codebook designed to map distinct data
533
      distributions using varied codeword pools. Our proposed framework, Uncertainty-Based Extensible-
534   Codebook Federated Learning (UEFL), leverages this extensible codebook through an iterative pro-
535   cess that adeptly identifies data from unknown distributions via uncertainty evaluation and enriches
536   the codebook with newly initialized codewords tailored to these distributions. The iterative nature of
537   UEFL, coupled with efficient codeword initialization using K-means, ensures codewords are closely
538   matched with the actual data distribution, thereby expediting model convergence. This approach
539   allows UEFL to rapidly adjust to new and unseen data distributions, enhancing adaptability. Our
      comprehensive evaluation across various prominent datasets showcases UEFL’s effectiveness.

788   We conducted experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-100 based on the framework presented in (Pa-

790
      (i.e. training accuracy exceeds 99.9% for MNIST/CIFAR-10), the Terminal Phase of Training (TPT)
      begins, during which neural collapse (NC) emerges. To validate this, we first conducted local train-
791
      ing by training on all data together. Our results confirmed the paper’s claim that additional training
792
      beyond the zero-error point leads to improved performance. We then extended these experiments to
793   the federated learning setting. The detailed results are presented in Table 9.
794
795   From these experiments, we observed the following findings: 1. The dropout layer must be removed;
796
      otherwise, the training accuracy cannot exceed 99.9% (e.g., the final training accuracy for MNIST
      is limited to 96% with dropout). 2. Compared to local training, more training epochs are required to
797
      reach the TPT in federated learning. For example, on CIFAR-100 with data heterogeneity, federated
798
      learning requires 44 rounds of training (44 × 5 epochs), while local training achieves TPT in 38
799   epochs. 3. Although neural collapse yields improved performance, UEFL consistently outperforms
800   it, especially on more complex datasets like CIFAR-100. This is partly because removing dropout
801   layers for neural collapse increases the risk of overfitting.
