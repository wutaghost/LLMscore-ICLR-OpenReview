009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural PDE surrogates can improve on cost-accuracy tradeoffs of classical
012            solvers, but often generalize poorly to new initial conditions, accumulate errors
013            over time. To close the performance gap between training and long-term infer-
014            ence, we constrain neural surrogates with symmetry equivariance and physical
015
               conservation laws as hard constraints, using novel input and output layers that
               support scalar and vector fields on the staggered grids commonly used in compu-
016
               tational fluid dynamics. We systematically investigate how these constraints af-
017
               fect accuracy, individually and in combination, on two challenging tasks: shallow
018            water equations with closed boundaries and decaying incompressible turbulence.
019            Compared to a strong baseline, both types of constraints improve performance
020            consistently across autoregressive prediction steps, accuracy measures, and net-
021            work sizes. Symmetries are more effective but do not make physical constraints
022            redundant. Doubly-constrained surrogates were more accurate for the same net-
023            work and dataset sizes, and generalized better to initial conditions and durations
024            beyond the range of training data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recently, neural networks have shown promising results in predicting the time evolution of PDE
029   systems, often achieving cost-accuracy tradeoffs that outperform traditional numerical methods (Li
030   et al., 2020; Gupta & Brandstetter, 2022; Stachenfeld et al., 2021; Takamoto et al., 2022; Long
031   et al., 2019; Um et al., 2020; Kochkov et al., 2021). However, obtaining accurate and stable au-
032   toregressive ‘rollouts’ over long durations remains notoriously difficult. Several techniques have
033   been proposed to address this, including physical constraints, symmetry equivariance, time-unrolled
034   training, specialized architectures, data augmentation, addition of input noise and generative mod-
035   elling (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2024; Stachenfeld et al., 2021; Kohl et al., 2024;
036
      Brandstetter et al., 2022a; Fanaskov et al., 2023; Bergamin et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Hsieh et al.,
      2019; Tran et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Bonev et al., 2023). Nonetheless, the relative effectiveness of
037
      these strategies remains largely ambiguous, and transparent, systematic comparisons remain elusive.
038
039   Here we systematically investigate the utility of symmetry constraints and physical conservation
040   laws, alone and in combination. Across multiple tasks, accuracy measures and scenarios, we show
041   a clear, reproducible and robust benefit from these constraints, and find they can be combined syn-
042
      ergistically. In order to apply them broadly, we introduce novel input and output layers extending
      these inductive biases for the first time to staggered grids.

068   sional variable fields w(x), z(x) defined on Ω ⊂ Rd . Then for a group G of invertible transforma-
069   tions on R2 , f is equivariant if it commutes with the actions of G on w and z. That is, there should
070   exist transformations Tg , Tg′ operating on w, z respectively, such that

      software packages exist for equivariant convolutions (Cesa et al., 2022) and self-attention (Romero
080
      & Cordonnier, 2020), and have proven useful in image classification (Chidester et al., 2019) and
081

082   in some cases (Wang et al., 2020; Helwig et al., 2023; Smets et al., 2023; Huang & Greenberg,
083   2023; Ruhe et al., 2024). Numerical integration methods can also benefit from maintaining PDE
084   symmetries Rebelo & Valiquette (2011).

086

087
      standard convolutions and self-attention with relative encoding are already equivariant to translations
088   (up to boundary effects).

090   Staggered grids Fluid dynamical systems are often simulated using staggered grids (Fig. 1, left),
091   in which variables such as pressure, density, divergence or velocity along each axis are represented
092   at different locations. This approach can avoid grid-scale numerical artifacts in numerical integra-
093   tion, and is common in fluid dynamics (Holl & Thuerey, 2024; Kochkov et al., 2021; Jasak, 2009;
094   Stone et al., 2020) as well as atmospheric (Jungclaus et al., 2022; De Pondeca et al., 2011) and
095   ocean models (Korn et al., 2022; Madec et al., 2023). Unfortunately, existing equivariant network
096   layers (Cesa et al., 2022; Romero & Cordonnier, 2020) assume Tg can be described by a resampling
097
      operation followed by an independent transformation at each grid point as in Equation 5, but on
      staggered grids rotation and reflection do not take this form.
098
099
      Physical constraints Neural surrogates have frequently been applied to physical systems, many
100
      of which include known conservation laws. To improve accuracy, stability, and generalization ca-
101
      pabilities, these laws can be imposed through additional loss terms (Read et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
102   2020; Stachenfeld et al., 2021; Sorourifar et al., 2023). Taking the strategy of physics-derived loss
103   terms to its ultimate limit, on arrives at unsupervised training on PDE-derived losses for discretized
104   (Wandel et al., 2020; Michelis & Katzschmann, 2022) or continuous solutions (Raissi et al., 2019).
105   Alternatively, one can reparameterize network outputs to respect hard constraints (Mohan et al.,
106   2020; Beucler et al., 2021; Chalapathi et al., 2024; Cranmer et al., 2020; Greydanus et al., 2019).
107   Here we focus on discretized, supervised approaches, which have proven more competitive in larger
      and more complex PDE systems (Takamoto et al., 2022).

153   Input layers We consider input data on staggered Arakawa C-grids (Fig. 1 left, Fig. 7). This grid
154   consists of square cells, where variable fields can be defined at cell centers (typically scalar fields
155   like pressure, surface height, or divergence), at the midpoints of cell interfaces (such as velocity
156   components) at vertices (e.g. stream functions). For an n × n 2D grid of cells, there is an (n + 1) × n
157   grid of interfaces in the x1 direction (along rows, including boundaries), and an n × (n + 1) grid of
158   interfaces in the x2 direction (along columns).
159   We designed convolutional input layers to take scalar inputs at cell centers and/or vector fields with
160   components defined at interfaces. Inputs at interfaces are first processed with a bank of convolutional

199   3.1   BASE ARCHITECTURE
200
201   In order to measure the efficacy of symmetry constraints and conservation at the cutting edge of neu-
202   ral PDE surrogate research, it was essential to choose a flexible base architecture with efficient train-
203   ing and inference that has produced highly competitive results. To this end we selected the “modern
204   U-net” introduced in Gupta & Brandstetter (2022), which modifies the original U-net (Ronneberger
205   et al., 2015) for improved performance as a PDE surrogate. This architecture has shown strong re-
206   sults in Kohl et al. (2024), and a similar version performed well in Lippe et al. (2024). We used this
207   architecture without self-attention layers, which did not significantly affect our results.

      5.1   C LOSED S HALLOW WATER S YSTEM

      We first trained and evaluated neural surrogates for the SWE system. For this task, we followed a
296   hybrid learning strategy, based on the observation that the semiimplicit numerical integration scheme
297   calculates ζ t+1 slowly with an iterative solver, but then calculates [ut+1 , v t+1 ] given ζ t+1 quickly
298   and trivially through a mathematical formula. We therefore trained surrogates to predict only ζbt+1 ,
299   and calculated [b ut+1 , vbt+1 ] as in the numerical solver (Appendix G). Keeping parameter counts
300   constant, we compared networks trained equivariant to 3 symmetry groups: p1 (translation only), p4
301   (translation-rotation) and p4m (translation-rotation-reflection). We also compared mass conserving
302   networks (m) to those without physical constraints (∅). Table 1 lists all constraint combinations
303   used for training, which took 0.5 h for non-equivariant networks and 2h for equivariant networks on
304   an A100 GPU.
305   Figure 3a compares autoregressive rollouts from unconstrained (p1/∅) and maximally constrained
306   networks (p4m/M). p4m/M maintained accurate results for a much greater time interval, and in this
307   case was visually indistinguishable from the reference solution throughout the simulation (results
308   for all networks are shown in 13). Over 20 random held-out ICs in this test, p4m/M exhibited
309   lower normalized RMSE values and high correlations for predicted ζ values than other networks
310   (Figure 3b-c)). We also compared to unconstrained networks trained with input noise (p1m/∅ + ε,
311   details in 14), which improved long-rollout performance as previously proposed (Stachenfeld et al.,
312
      2021; Lippe et al., 2024), but was not as effective as the combination of symmetry constraints and
      conservation laws. Compared to other networks, p4m/M was able to train for more epochs before
313
      early stopping occurred, and achieved a validation lower loss (Fig. 3d). It also achieved lower values
314
      over a greater fraction of held-out ICs (Fig. 3e), maintained energy conservation (which was not
315   constrained by any archicture) for longer (Fig. 3f) and stayed correlated to the reference solution for
316   longer (Fig. 3g). Overall, we found that symmetry constraints were more effective than conservation
317   laws, but that the benefits provided by each combined synergistically, rather than redundantly.

      6     D ISCUSSION
458
459
      We enforced hard constraints on symmetries and conservation laws for neural PDE surrogates. We
460   extended the applicability of previous techniques to c-grids, and systematically tested performance
461   across tasks and constraints. Symmetries were more effective, but conservation laws were not re-
462   dundant. Double constraints best matched reference simulations, individually and statistically.
463
464   Limitations & Future work For large enough networks and datasets, constraints might be learned
465   from data (Stachenfeld et al., 2021; Watt-Meyer et al., 2023), but our results show the gap between
466   constrained and unconstrained surrogates grows with rollout length even for large networks and
467   datasets. Thus, constraints are likely relevant for longer time scales, e.g. for seasonal forecasts and
468   climate projections (Kochkov et al., 2024; Watt-Meyer et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023).
469   Our understanding of how these constraints limit error accumulation remains rudimentary. While
470   we lack a rigorous theory, empirical investigations of how error accumulation correlates with con-
471   straint violations over time and ICs could provide some clarity. It also remains unclear how these
472   improvements might transfer to other PDE types, such as hyperbolic equations.
473
      We considered mass and momentum conservation, and symmetries of square 2D grids. Future work
474   could pursue energy conservation (Cranmer et al., 2020), continuous symmetry groups (Cohen et al.,
475   2018; Esteves et al., 2018), alternative grids and meshes (Cohen et al., 2019; De Haan et al., 2020),
476   and other architectures and techniques, such as dilated Resnets, unrolled training, invariant measure
477   learning, transformers and denoising diffusions (Takamoto et al., 2022; Brandstetter et al., 2022b;
478   Schiff et al., 2024; List et al., 2024; Li et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2024). Nonetheless, we believe that
479   our results clearly demonstrate the potential of these inductive biases in improving rollout accuracy
480   and extensiib to longer time scales.
