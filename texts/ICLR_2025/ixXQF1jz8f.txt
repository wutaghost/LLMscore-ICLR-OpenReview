009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider one of the most relevant problems of distributed learning, i.e., the se-
012            lection of the learning nodes to include in the training process as well as the selec-
013            tion of the samples from each of the learning nodes’ local datasets, so as to make
014            learning sustainable. Traditional approaches rely on pursuing a balanced label
015            distribution, which requires label statistics from all datasets, including those not
016            selected for learning. This may be costly and may raise privacy concerns. To cope
017            with this issue, we aim at selecting few and small datasets. To this end, we propose
018
               a new metric, called loneliness, which is defined on unlabelled training samples.
               First, through both a theoretical and an experimental analysis, we show that lone-
019
               liness is strongly linked with learning performance (i.e., test accuracy). Then, we
020
               propose a new node- and data-selection procedure, called Goldilocks, that uses
021            loneliness to make its decisions. Our performance evaluation, including three
022            state-of-the-art datasets and both centralized and federated learning, demonstrates
023            that Goldilocks outperforms approaches based upon a balanced label distribution
024            by providing over 70% accuracy improvement, in spite of using information that
025            is both less sensitive privacy-wise and less onerous to obtain.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Node and data selection are two of the foremost issues in distributed machine learning, e.g., fed-
031   erated learning (FL), requiring to strike a difficult balance between having enough data to properly
032   learn, and limiting the resource consumption and cost of the overall learning process. Such issues are
033
      especially relevant in scenarios where dependable, sustainable learning is required, e.g., for safety-
      critical applications at the network edge. In these scenarios, it is important to guarantee a given
034
      level of learning quality (e.g., test accuracy) while being able to involve in the training process het-
035
      erogeneous nodes with different memory and computing capabilities. It follows that selecting only
036   few, small local datasets becomes crucial, since including additional nodes in the model training—
037   or using more data from the already-included nodes—may increase resource consumption whilst
038   resulting in longer training times, poorer learning performance, or both. Avoiding these issues—
039   intuitively, identifying the nodes and data that are worth the additional expense and effort—is the
040   overarching goal of all node and data selection strategies.
041
      The problem is compounded by the fact that evaluating the quality of the local dataset of a potential
042   learning node is a very hard problem. Consider for simplicity a classification problem. The vast
043   majority of current approaches (Wu & Wang, 2022; Li et al., 2021; 2022) use as discriminant the
044   labels of data. Specifically, they associate a higher quality to balanced datasets in which all classes
045   are equally represented, and they combine local datasets from different nodes in such a way that the
046   resulting global training data is as close to balanced (often also referred to i.i.d.) as possible. How-
047   ever, sharing label information impinges the learning nodes’ privacy, which might be problematic
048   in the case of highly sensitive data. Additionally, in practical scenarios, labels can be expensive to
049   obtain. While this is unavoidable for datasets that will eventually be used for training, going through
050
      the expense and hassle of labeling a dataset only to find it unsuited for the current learning task
      would again result in a waste of time and resources.
051
052   In this paper, we address the above issues by proposing a new metric, called loneliness, that can
053   be effectively used to evaluate the quality of unlabelled datasets, hence, how suited they are to the
      learning task being carried out, while preserving data privacy. Loneliness is linked to how far away

081
      Through a set of experiments, we show that (i) loneliness exhibits a stronger correlation than label
      balance with the testing performance of a learning task, and that (ii) the best performance is asso-
082
      ciated with intermediate loneliness values. As a theoretical explanation of this second finding, we
083
      provide a probably-approximately correct (PAC) Bayes bound, based on adaptive subspace com-
084   pression (Lotfi et al., 2022). Through this bound, we illustrate that while the training error increases
085   monotonically with the loneliness, the number of compressed bits to represent the weights of the
086   deep neural network (DNN) (which we use as complexity term in the PAC-Bayes bound) decreases
087   with the loneliness.
088
      Inspired by our theoretical and experimental findings, we propose a node- and data-selection pro-
089
      cedure, called Goldilocks, that exploits loneliness to make the inter-related decisions of (i) which
090   nodes to include in the process, and (ii) which of their local data to use for the model training. By
091   making such decisions jointly, Goldilocks is able to explore a wide range of high-quality trade-offs
092   between the effectiveness of the learning process and the resources this necessitates. Furthermore,
093   Goldilocks does not require the use of label information, but it can leverage it if available. Finally,
094   and very remarkably, it can identify the most promising datasets for which it is worth it to obtain
095   labels.
096   A pictorial sketch of how our approach and the Goldilocks procedure improve over the state of
097   the art is provided in Fig. 1. The traditional approach, represented on the left, is geared towards
098   scenarios where we must select tens or hundreds of learning nodes and makes decisions based on
099   how balanced labels are. While it is true that better-balanced datasets yield better performance,
100   there is still a significant variability within the accuracy yielded by similarly-balanced datasets. Our
101   approach, represented on the right, leverages the loneliness metric (along with label information,
102   if available), resulting in a much more accurate knowledge of which datasets yield the best test
103   accuracy, especially when datasets are small and only a small number thereof can be selected.
104   We evaluate the performance of Goldilocks in both centralized and federated scenarios, using state-
105   of-the-art DNN models and datasets, and find it to consistently outperform approaches only consid-
106   ering label information. Importantly, the performance metrics we consider go beyond mere classifi-
107   cation accuracy, and include the number of learning nodes to involve in the learning process and the
      quantity of data therein to exploit.

280   To shed lights on the impact of the loneliness on the PAC-Bayes bound (4), we experimentally
281

284
      on Z. Training is performed in two steps, according to the adaptive subspace compression algorithm
285
      introduced in Lotfi et al. (2022): (i) first, we learn a low-dimensional linear embedding of the model
286   weights; then (ii) we quantize the embedded weights to a fixed number of levels. The training loss
287   is computed using the 0–1 loss function. The KL term for the selected posterior and prior is upper-
288   bounded by an expression containing the length of the shortest program needed to reproduce W ⋆ .
289   This is computed as the number of bits required to represent the quantized model weights extracted
290   from step (ii) of the training procedure, using an arithmetic code.

293

297
      We see that for all dataset sizes m considered in the figure, the training loss appears to increase as a
298
      function of the loneliness value. Intuitively, as the samples in the dataset become more dissimilar, the
299
      training process becomes slower, and the training error achieved after a fixed number of iterations,
300   increases. On the contrary, the KL term appears to decrease monotonically with the loneliness. This
301   suggests that the model complexity decreases. Intuitively, a dataset with a higher loneliness can be
302   classified using a more compressible neural network.
303
      As a result of these two opposite trends, intermediate loneliness values are preferable. Unfortunately,
304
      this cannot be demonstrated by evaluating the PAC-Bayes bound in (4) directly, since the training
305

307   no trends can be inferred. Indeed, this is the reason why the slope of the linear regression curve in
308   Fig. 4 decreases when m increases.

311   5   N ODE AND DATASET S ELECTION : G OLDILOCKS
312
313   In this section, we describe our Goldilocks node- and data-selection procedure. For the sake of
314   simplicity, we first focus on the node selection part (i.e., we assume that all local data of selected
315   nodes will be used), and then we will discuss data selection.
316
317   Node selection: The node selection problem can be stated as follows: given a set N curr ⊂N of
318   currently-selected learning nodes, we want to identify a new node n∗ ∈N \N curr to add to the training
319   process, so as to optimize the learning outcome, e.g., maximize the test accuracy. Such outcome   S    is
320   estimated through a proxy metric µ̂(Q), taking as input a set Q of selected datasets, i.e., Q= q X q ;
321   we are also given a target value τ̂ for the metric µ̂. The metric µ̂ itself can correspond to any
322   metric taking as an input a set of datasets, including the loneliness ℓ̂ defined in (2) and the unbalance
323   factor α. In all cases, the value of the metric of a set of datasets Q corresponds to the value of that
      metric computed over the union of all datasets therein.

      It is worth emphasizing that the Goldilocks procedure just outlined can be performed for arbitrary
      metrics, including the unbalance factor α introduced in Sec. 3, the loneliness level λ(X k ) defined
354
      in (3), as well as other metrics defined in the literature, e.g., the normalized entropy used in (Bansal
355
      et al., 2023). Furthermore, Goldilocks supports arbitrary target values, i.e., it can be applied to
356   metrics that do not need to be maximized or minimized: this is fundamental in situations like those
357   depicted in Fig. 3(right), where the best performance is associated with intermediate loneliness
358   values. In these cases, Goldilocks is able to select the nodes that are just right (hence the name of
359   the procedure) for the task at hand.
360
361   Data selection: Goldilocks follows a similar approach when selecting samples x within a given
362   dataset X k . Specifically, given a number T of samples to select, and assuming a per-sample ver-
363   sion µ(i, k) of the metric µ̂ and the corresponding target value τ , Goldilocks proceeds as follows:
364
                        1. Associate with each sample xi ∈ X k a score s(i, k) = |µ(i, k) − τ |;
365
366                     2. Take the T samples with the lowest score.
367

368
      samples, i.e., if µ(i, k) does exist. This is the case of loneliness (as per (1)), but not, as an example,
369
      for the label balance. We can thus remark again that, by combining the loneliness metric and the
370   Goldilocks procedure, we are able to make fine-grained, joint node and data selection decisions.
371

479   7        R ELATED W ORK
480
481   The problem of node selection in distributed learning and of dataset selection in conventional learn-
482   ing is well investigated. We provide below some relevant works while highlighting the difference
483   with the present contribution.
484
485   Node selection: The problem of node selection in cross-device FL is particularly relevant in the
      presence of data heterogeneity, which causes FedAvg to suffer from client drift (Karimireddy et al.,


                                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2020). Data heterogeneity, combined with sporadic client participation, causes also a participation
487   gap, on top of the usual generalization gap in statistical learning (Yuan et al., 2022). To estimate
488   this participation gap, Yuan et al. (2022) suggest to let each client share some held-out data with the
489   aggregator. The availability of held-out data at the aggregator enables the implementation of sophis-
490   ticated client-selection strategies, which, as shown in Singhal et al. (2024), improve communication
491   efficiency. However, sharing such data seems to defy one of the main purposes of FL, which is to
492   maintain data privacy at the clients. Cho et al. (2022) propose a different approach, in which client
493   selection at the aggregator is performed on the basis of the local training loss computed at each
494   client, which does not require sharing held-out data. Note that, differently from our much-simpler
495
      loneliness based approach, both solutions require one to label all datasets, including the ones that
      will not be used.
496
497
      Data selection: This is often referred to as data pruning in the literature (Sachdeva & McAuley,
498
      2023), and involves pruning low-quality data, typically during the training process. An extensive
499
      review of data pruning methods can be found in Guo et al. (2022). Among the existing solutions,
500   Ghorbani & Zou (2019) uses the Shapely-value of a subset of data to estimate their utility. Toneva
501   et al. (2019) remove from the training set unforgettable examples, i.e., examples whose predicted
502   label is correct and does not change over the training process. Paul et al. (2021), instead, retains
503   “hard” examples, i.e., examples that have large ℓ2 -norm scores on trained models. A different
504   approach is used in CRAIG (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020), which selects a subset of the training data
505   that closely approximates the full gradient. Unlike these approaches, the much simpler loneliness-
506   based method proposed in this paper does not require knowledge of the sample labels and/or of the
507   learning algorithm.
508
509   8   C ONCLUSION
510
511   We have considered the problems of node and data selection in cooperative learning scenarios where
512   only a small number of small datasets can be used. In this scenario, it is especially advantageous
513   to select the datasets (and, hence, the nodes) without using label information, for both efficiency
514   and privacy reasons. To this end, we proposed a metric called loneliness, which, using unlabelled
515   data, computes the distance between samples of the same dataset. We integrated loneliness with a
516   node- and data-selection strategy called Goldilocks, and found – across multiple datasets and in both
517
      centralized and federated schemes – that using loneliness consistently results in higher improvement
      of training accuracy, by over 70%, in spite of requiring no label information.
