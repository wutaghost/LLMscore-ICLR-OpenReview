011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            A mechanism of effective communication is integral to human existence. An es-
014            sential aspect of a functional communication scheme among a rational human
015            population involves an efficient, adaptive, and coherent apparatus to convey one’s
016
               goal to others. Such an effective macro characteristic can emerge in a finite popu-
               lation through adaptive learning via trial and error at the individual (micro) level,
017
               with nearly consistent individual learning faculty and experience across the pop-
018
               ulation. In this paper, we study and hypothesize pertinent aspects of glossoge-
019            netics, specifically primal human communication mechanisms, through compu-
020            tational modeling. In particular, we model the process as a language “guessing”
021            game within the fabric of a decentralized, multi-agent deep reinforcement learn-
022            ing setting, where the agents with local learning and neural cognitive faculties
023            interact through a series of dialogues. Our homogeneous agents seek to achieve
024            the principle of least effort and overcome the poverty of stimulus through efficient
025            concept selection, guided feedback and mirror learning. In our examinations, we
026            observe the emergence of successful and structured communication among static
027
               and dynamic agent populations through guided, reinforced and continual learning.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Effective communication via signals is the key to success in a cooperative world, where the goal is
031   to complete the desired tasks by efficiently coordinating among themselves. A functional communi-
032   cation language should be nearly unambiguous, efficient, easily acquirable (culturally transmitted),
033   and rooted in the environment. Language Chomsky (2006); Montague et al. (1970); de Saussure
034   (2011) is an autonomous, culturally transmitted, complex adaptive system realised through multi-
035   ple modalities—either vocal or gestures—that translate mental representations, which are internal
036   structures, to utterances that represent the surface structure. In cooperative AI and cognitive science,
037   language games Wittgenstein (1954); David (1969); Arrington (1954); Steels (1997; 2003); Wag-
038   ner et al. (2003) which was motivated by the picture theory of language Wittgenstein (1954) and
039
      operant conditioning theory Skinner (1986) are empirical computational models developed to study
      the origin, evolution, and acquisition of human languages. The game setting involves a bottom-up
040
      simulation model which usually consists of multiple artificial agents (neural or statistical) equipped
041
      with sufficient cognitive/inductive reasoning abilities and sometimes sensory-motor systems inter-
042   acting in a shared environment through vocal or non-vocal means and subsequently learning from
043   the outcomes of the interactions. The language structures that emerge in these settings never entirely
044   mirror human languages, since human languages are refined through millions of years of cultural
045   evolution. However, language games can provide deep insights into the emergence of various as-
046   pects of human language, such as syntactic structures, compositionality, word order, generalization,
047   brevity, stability, statistical regularity, complexity, coherence, and linguistic divergence.
048   With the recent advancement in the field of deep learning Mnih et al. (2013) with respect to compu-
049   tational tractability, one could observe rigorous applications of deep learning and deep reinforce-
050   ment learning involving multiple agents in language games Lazaridou & Baroni (2020); Dafoe
051   et al. (2020), especially Lewis signalling/referential/discrimination games Lazaridou et al. (2017);
052   Havrylov & Titov (2017), reconstruction games Kharitonov et al. (2020), navigation/action games
053   Kajić et al. (2020); Mordatch & Abbeel (2018) and visual communication games Qiu et al. (2022)
      without customized tweaking. The central feature of all the existing work is that it belongs to the

      ”Naming game” category, where the semantic aspect used to distinguish the topic from other con-
055   text elements is clear and free from ambiguity. A few of these focus on the emergence of coherent

057   (2016); Foerster et al. (2016); Havrylov & Titov (2017); Lazaridou et al. (2017; 2018). A few others
058   target the pertinent linguistic universals of natural languages, such as the symbolic grounding Mor-
059   datch & Abbeel (2018); Kottur et al. (2017); Lin et al. (2021), compositionality Mordatch & Abbeel

      (2020), language structures through ease-of-teaching pressure Li & Bowling (2019) and networked
      communication Gupta et al. (2020). Some of the recent works also provide deeper analysis pertain-

      and language constructs. Kottur et al. (2017); Resnick et al. (2020); Tucker et al. (2022) delve into

069   motivation required for the emergence of a coherent language. Another direction in which language
070   emergence is being evaluated is along the dimension of scale Chaabouni et al. (2022); Rita et al.
071   (2021), where the correlation between language characteristics and system complexity and popula-
072   tion dynamics is examined, while Lazaridou et al. (2020) incorporates pre-trained general language
073   models to develop task-specific language models. Choi et al. (2018) explores the obverter technique
074
      Batali (1998) which explores emergent communication among pseudo-homogeneous agents, where
      the speaker is assumed to be always true, while the listener calibrates its parameters to align itself
075
      with the speaker. Chaabouni et al. (2019) studies the existence of inverse correlation of word length
076
      and input frequency that exists in natural human language.
077
      Our contribution: In this paper, we develop a decentralized, uncorrelated, multi-agent “Guessing
078
      game’ with significant combinatorial complexity compared to the Naming games, primarily due to
079
      the ambiguity in inferring the topic from the context, which, in turn, significantly hinders vocab-
080   ulary alignment and learning. Our unique approach includes the integration of an explicit agent
081   ontology to distinguish between referents and concepts, as well as the provision of tangible feed-
082   back, which differs from existing works that share feedback directly, mimicking “telepathy”. In
083   this unique guessing game framework, we investigate the emergence of certain coherent properties
084   of languages such as interchangeability, and compositionality. The interchangeability property of
085   languages enables the agents to act as either listener or speaker (contrary to dedicated roles in the
086   existing works), where they synchronise through mirror learning. This ensures continual learning,
087   which resembles human language acquisition. Additionally, we integrate the principle of least effort
088
      which encourages agents to convey information in a way that minimizes cognitive effort. The goal
      of the paper is to explore the dynamics of these components within the guessing game setup and to
089
      analyze and hypothesize the emergent behaviors that arise from their interaction.

093   In this paper, our objective is to enable the emergence of coherent symbolic structures among a popu-
094
      lation of deep neural agents through decentralized learning and self-organization in guessing games.
      Our setting consists of N deep neural agents populated on a graph world G = (V, E) which is em-
095
      bedded on a bounded 2D plane (flat earth), where V is the set of vertices and E is the set of edges.
096
      All the nodes are similar in shape. However, they possess two relevant features, location and color
097   which distinguish them from each other. The location is unique for a node, although they can have
098   the same color. Our agents are homogeneous in nature, where they can perform both comprehension
099   and reproduction of language. This property is referred to as homogeneity/interchangeability which
100   is one of the core properties of human language. Our language game (Figure 3) is as follows: At
101   each instant in the game, one agent is paired against another to initiate a semiotic cycle of dialogue
102   consisting of D conversations. In dialogue, one random agent takes the role of speaker, while the
103   other agent is the listener (step 1 in Figure 3). In a conversation, the speaker randomly selects a
104   topic node unknown to the listener and communicates it using a suitable conceptualization (using its
105   ontology) and utterance via a noise-free, discrete channel (steps 2 to 5 ). The listener attempts to
106   decipher the meaning of the temporal sequence of utterances by correctly identifying the topic node.
107   The interaction is subsequently rewarded according to the interpretation outcome which is shared
      among the participants (steps 6 to 8 ). In case of failed communication, the speaker discloses the

      topic node to the listener (step 9 ). Learning occurs through the induction of hypotheses (the innate
109   linguistic structure that is characterized by neural networks) based on payoffs, and disclosures.

146   The ontology Guarino & Giaretta (1995); Mark et al. (2003) C of the agent is the concept space
147   through which an agent perceives the space. In this paper, C = H ∪ W ∪ B ∪ {⊥} which consists of a
148   finite collection of segments H, sectors W, colors B and the NULL concept ⊥. We let H̄ = H∪{⊥},
149   W̄ = W ∪ {⊥} and B̄ = B ∪ {⊥}. A segment is a strip of region in the 2D plane encompassed
150   by outer and inner concentric circles (Figure 1 (a)) centered at a certain point. A sector is defined
151   to be a part of a disc made of the arc of the disc centered at a certain point along with its two
152   radii extending to the boundary of the world. These are spatial deixis which are generally perceived
153
      relative to the location of the central point. The segments and sectors provide a conceptualization of
      space that is grounded in the sensory and physical interactions of the agents with the world and one
154
      can relate it to the concepts of cardinal directions in the human discourse. Each node possesses the
155
      intensive property of color and we assume that the agents possess the sensory mechanism to capture
156   the hue range of colors. Hence, we also consider colors as concepts. Roughly, C represents the
157   hierarchical deep structure of concepts (semantic entities) where one can be either specific (finer)
158   or general (coarser), or disjoint than the other. The concept space C is equipped with an operation
159   < ·, · >: C × C → C ′ , where C ′ is the set of derived concepts, which are concepts which can be
160   derived from the basis concepts Andreas (2018); Montague et al. (1970). In our setting, the operation
161   <> is set intersection since our concept space consists of regions and colors. An illustration is
      provided in Figure 1 (b). We also maintain a pre-defined encoder Γ : C → Z which maps the

173   possibly be associated to a novel word and the listener cannot, with only one exposure, determine

175   is limited or insufficient for the listener to comprehend. This complicates the construction of a
176   shared vocabulary, as aligning meanings through communication becomes more challenging. This
177   is Quine’s “Gavagai” problem also referred to as Poverty of stimulus Quine (1960).

      4   G ROUNDED VOCABULARY L EARNING
183   The lexis (Ψ) of a language is a finite catalog of all q-letter words available a priori to an agent. A
184   vocabulary bidirectionally maps lexis (phonological entities) to meanings (semantic entities), where
185   one is able to evoke the other Ren et al. (2020). This symbolic association is referred to as the prop-
186   erty of groundedness. For a population of agents to successfully communicate, there should exist a
187   shared, coherent vocabulary among the population. This implies that the vocabulary possessed by
188   the agents should hold the same meaning for everyone to successfully communicate verbally among
189
      themselves. Ideally, the mapping should be isomorphic. Apparently, in every realistic scenario, this
      is not the case, which transpires into various language characteristics like homonyms and synonyms.
190
      Initially, there is no ex-ante meaning associated with the words, and hence no coherence among the
191
      agents exists and we aim to foster common grounding among agents incrementally, which is fully
192   shaped by past linguistic experience. This is referred to as the symbol grounding problem Steels
193   (2012). We achieve this through verbal interactions between them, where they extract and rein-
194   force similarities across multiple episodes incrementally through evidence of understanding which
195   can be either positive or negative. This trial and error based calibration process shapes, reshapes,
196   and enforces the mental mapping, where the phonological expressions become more efficient and
197   established through repeated use Bisk et al. (2020); Arrington (1954), and eventually drives the
198   system to a dissipative structure Prigogine (1987) which enables common ground for expressing
199   concepts. The speaker exhibits an inherent categorical bias, to structure messages in a specific or-
200
      der (M(segment), M(sector), M(color)) reflecting the agent’s conceptualization process.
      However, the listener module does not share this bias and instead treats each word as potentially
201
      belonging to any category. This discrepancy in how the speaker and listener process information
202
      makes it more challenging to establish a shared language, as the listener does not rely on the same
203   categorical ordering as the speaker.
204
      Assumption: We assume that all the agents possess the same lexis.

263   6.2 P RINCIPLE OF L EAST E FFORT
264   According to the principle of least effort Zipf (2016); Cancho & Solé (2003), language evolves as
265   speakers of the language tend to simplify their speech in various ways to achieve a trade-off between
266   understanding and effort. When choosing how to express themselves in a language, speakers take
267   into account both their current and future communication requirements. This drives the speakers to
268   consider linguistic constructs that are effective in meeting their communication goals and efficient in
269   optimizing their labour. A similar hypothesis connecting the overarching fairness between cognitive
      load and language exposition is the principle of the economy of thought Mach (1898). It suggests


                                                                    5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      that the human mind, with its limited cognitive resources, seeks to represent the infinite complexities
271   of the world in a way that is efficient and economical. Hence, we believe that languages tend to
272   evolve in ways that promote the economy of least thought and linguistic effort, where the language
273   users communicate using messages that are relatively easy to produce and comprehend. Hence, in
274   the post-transient phase of language evolution, message length tends to decrease Futrell et al. (2015).

386   Hypothesis I: A measure of tangible guided feedback supplemented with reinforcement learning is
387   required for a shared language to emerge among a population through decentralized learning.
388   In our experiments, we observe that our decentralized guessing games are converging very often.
389   The vocabulary-concept mappings developed by the individual agents during the transient phase
390   are random which enables sufficient exploration to drive the evolution towards coherence in a fi-
391
      nite number of dialogues. Coherence implies a more consistent and meaningful use of language,
      where words and expressions convey clear, grounded and shared meanings. This is corroborated
392
      by the convergence of loss functions and the maximization of average reward (average of the re-
393
      wards of the conversations in a dialogue) as illustrated in Figures 13b and 16b. Positive rewards,
394   attributed to successful communication events, are considered rare. When such events occur, posi-
395   tive rewards are reinforced by policy gradient-based calibration operations. This is further boosted
396   by the guided feedback mechanism, where the listener uses the topic vertex information conveyed
397   by the speaker to calibrate his parameters to match the conceptualization of the topic vertex (pos-

399   schemes. The success ratio is defined as the frequency of conversations in a dialogue, where the

495   7.1 E MERGENT L ANGUAGE C HARACTERISTICS
496   Emergence of Compositionality: The economy of thought gradually transpires into composition-
497   ality in the emergent language since language encapsulates thought. Hence one could observe the
498   emergence of efficient communication strategies, where shorter phrases are preferred over longer
499   ones. In our setting, the language that emerged is two word compositional, where it follows a word
500   order which involves segment followed by color. More complex scenarios can also arise. To
501   illustrate compositionality more vividly, we consider a smaller graph with N = 3 vertices (Figure
502   10) and two homogeneous agents with the concept space as before (Figure 6). The outcomes are
503   depicted in Figures 10 and 11. Notably, no specific word order emerges in this scenario; instead, a
504
      blend of word combinations dominates the discourse. It is noteworthy that, in this setup, the number
      of available colors in the agents’ concept space is 4, which is more than the number of vertices. This
505
      implies that the agents can communicate the topic vertices by just referring to the color alone. The
506
      same holds true for sectors. However, the observed trend reveals that in the majority of conversations
507   (50%), the agents opt for the color alone (< ⊥, ⊥, color>), and in 14% of instances, it relies on
508   the sector alone (< segment, ⊥, ⊥ >). Interestingly, in (20%) of conversations, the agents prefer
509   a combination of segment and color (<segment, ⊥, color >). The above mixture (dominated by
510   an optimal concept selection) is a sub-optimal limiting behaviour with respect to concept selection
511   and this scenario arises due to the non-convex nature of the objective function operating over a de-
512   centralized setting, as shown by the convergence to sub-optimal values in Figure 10). However, the
513   success ratio (Figure 10) Despite this, the success ratio is nearly 100% (Figure 10), and all agents
514   have nearly identical vocabularies (Figure 12), indicating the emergence of an effective semi non-
515
      compositional language with 65% of the conversations being non-compositional and 24% two-word
      compositional. Similar sub-optimal behaviors can be expected in human scenarios. Nevertheless,
516
      what stands out is that the agents employ one, sometimes two, and rarely three words or stay silent
517
      during the dialogue, mirroring patterns observed in human interactions.
518
519   Adherence to Zipf’s Law: Zipf’s law is a linguistic phenomenon that describes the distribution of
520   word frequencies within a language. It states that the frequency of any word is inversely proportional
521
      to its rank. In other words, the most common word occurs approximately twice as often as the second
      most common word, three times as often as the third most common word, and so on. We see in the
522
      Figure 15 (world setting from Figure 10), the occurrence of the word decreases exponentially for
523
      the lower rank words in the language emerged among agents. This aligns with the characteristic
524   pattern described by Zipf’s law. To further understand this relationship, we studied the impact
525   of the principle of least effort on the Zipfian characteristic of emerged languages. Specifically,
526   we looked at scenarios where the weight (represented by κ2 ) associated with the loss function that
527   quantifies the principle of least effort varied (Figure 8). When κ2 had higher values (1.1 in one case),
528   the discrepancy between the observed word usage frequency and the expected Zipf distribution is
529   minimal. This indicates that the sensitivity of the Zipfian characteristic to the weight of the principle
530   of least effort. In other words, higher weights lead to word frequencies that more closely follow an
531   inverse relationship to their ranks, as predicted by Zipf’s law.
532   8 C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we develop a computational language game framework to model the factors influenc-
534   ing language dynamics involving a finite number of homogeneous deep neural agents in a guessing
535   game setting. We integrate the following: explicit ontology, guided feedback mechanism to over-
536   come poverty of stimulus, mirror learning for interchangeability and factoring silence as a symbol.
537   From our observations, we conclude the following: “The emergence of a shared, grounded, struc-
538   tured, cognitively local optimal and Zipfian language among a population arising from ambiguous
539   interactions is attributed to diverse phenomena weaving together in the intricate tapestry of decen-
      tralized, guided and reinforced learning”.

      A.1   D EFINITIONS
759
760   Emergent vocabulary: An emergent vocabulary M is a shared mapping (not necessarily bijective)
761   function between lexis Ψ and the concept space C, i.e., M : Ψ ↔ C collectively agreed upon by
                                                                                  |Ψ|
762   all the agents in the population de Saussure (2011). Note that there are |C| possible vocabularies
763   for all the agents to agree upon, which makes it unlikely for all agents to converge on the same
764   vocabulary without some mechanism for coordination and consensus.
765
      Compositionality: A language is compositional Andreas (2018); Montague et al. (1970) if the
766   utterance of each derived concept is determined by the utterances of its basis concepts. Formally,
767   for the derived concept c =< g, h, q >, we have M(c) = M(g)M(h)M(q), with the implicit
768   operation of concatenation connecting them.

852   B.1   R EWARD F UNCTION
853
854   We follow a reward mechanism that balances exploration, cooperation, synchronization, accuracy,
855
      and efficiency in communication. Agents are trained using a shared reward mechanism by which
      they learn to cooperate by forming a shared language. To encourage agent exploration, we offer
856
      partial and complete rewards, motivating the agent to try different approaches and adapt themselves
857
      to make informed decisions during training. Both agents receive a partial reward if the listener
858   infers the right region where the topic vertex is located but fails to identify the topic vertex. This
859   acknowledges the successful transmission of relevant information without complete understanding.
860   A full reward is given if the listener can accurately and unambiguously infer the exact topic vertex
861   from the communicated information. This indicates a high level of successful communication and
862   concept selection. A penalty is given if communication fails in order to discourage the respective
863   concept-vocabulary mapping and to prevent incorrect or ineffective communication choices. The
      balance between partial and complete rewards, along with the penalty for communication failure,
