009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across
012            a wide range of tasks, sparkling an increasing interest for their application in sci-
013            ence. However, in scientific domains, their utility is often limited by hallucinations
014            that violate established relationships between concepts or ignore their meaning;
015            problems that are not entirely eliminated with Retrieval Augmented Generation
016            (RAG) techniques. A key feature of science is the use of niche concepts, abbrevia-
017            tions and implicit relationships, which may deem RAG approaches less powerful
018
               due to the lack of understanding of such concepts, especially in emerging and less
               known fields. Ontologies, as structured frameworks for organizing knowledge
019
               and establishing relationships between concepts, offer a potential solution to this
020
               challenge. In this work we introduce OntoRAG, a novel approach that enhances
021            RAG by retrieving taxonomical knowledge from ontologies. We evaluate the per-
022            formance of this method on three common biomedical benchmarks. To extend the
023            value of OntoRAG to emerging fields, where ontologies have not yet been devel-
024            oped, we also present OntoGen, a methodology for generating ontologies from a
025            set of documents. We apply the combined OntoGen+OntoRAG pipeline to a novel
026            benchmark of scientific discovery in the emerging field of single-atom catalysis.
027            Our results demonstrate the promise of this method for improving reasoning and
028            suppressing hallucinations in LLMs, potentially accelerating scientific discovery
029
               across various domains. The code for reproducing the results is available under
               https://figshare.com/s/4f898ef092ae5898c1b7.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Scientific discovery stands at the forefront of human progress, driving innovations across fields from
035
      medicine to materials science (Daston, 2017). In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs)
036   have emerged as promising tools to accelerate this process, offering unprecedented capabilities in
037   natural language understanding and generation (Vaswani et al., 2017). In view of these capabilities,
038   a growing volume of work has been devoted towards applying LLMs and derived technologies
039   into a number of scientific domains (Birhane et al., 2023). Despite these efforts, the application of
040   LLMs to complex scientific domains remains challenging, often hampered by the models’ tendency
041   to generate plausible but factually incorrect information, a phenomenon collectively referred to as
042   "hallucinations" (Verspoor, 2024).
043   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) has been proposed as a solution to ground LLM outputs in
044   factual information by providing relevant documents during inference (Lewis et al., 2020), leveraging
045   the in-context learning (ICL) capabilities of these models (Olsson et al., 2022). While RAG has
046   shown promise in general knowledge domains (Shuster et al., 2021; Lewis et al., 2020), its application
047   to specialized scientific fields often falls short (Soong et al., 2024). The complexity and interconnect-
048   edness of scientific concepts (Bizon et al., 2019), coupled with the rapid evolution of knowledge in
049   cutting-edge fields (Agrawal & Choudhary, 2016), pose significant challenges to traditional RAG
050
      approaches (Wu et al., 2024).
051   In this paper, we introduce OntoRAG, a novel methodology that combines the power of ontologies
052   with RAG to enhance applications in scientific discovery. Ontologies, as formal representations of
053   domain knowledge, offer a structured framework of concepts and relationships (Keet, 2018) that can
      guide LLM reasoning. By integrating ontological knowledge into the retrieval and generation process,

076   OntoRAG aims to produce more accurate, logically consistent, and scientifically grounded outputs.
077   This approach is particularly advantageous in emerging scientific fields where the terminology,
078   definitions, and conceptual relationships are often still evolving and may not be well-represented
079   in the training data of general-purpose LLMs. This capability addresses a significant limitation
080   of standard RAG approaches, which may underperform when dealing with cutting-edge scientific
081   concepts and terminology.
082   A significant bottleneck in ontology-based systems is the time-intensive and expert-dependent process
083   of ontology creation (Keet, 2018). Tools have been proposed recently to accelerate this process
084   by inserting new terms into an already existing ontology Toro et al. (2023); Funk et al. (2023), or
085   automating tasks such as term typing, taxonomy discovery, etc, under the frame of Ontology Learning
086   Ciatto et al. (2024); Toro et al. (2023); Babaei Giglou et al. (2023). However, none of these works
087   have attempted to generate full ontologies. To address this we propose OntoGen, an LLM-based
088   method for automatic end-to-end generation of ontologies. Our pipeline iteratively constructs domain-
089   specific ontologies from collections of relevant scientific documents, making the OntoRAG approach
090
      adaptable to new and rapidly evolving fields of study. This enables the application of ontology-based
      methods to a wider range of scientific domains, and in particular fast-developing fields and those
091
      where no ontologies have yet been defined. The full pipeline (Figure 1) relies entirely on a predefined
092
      corpus of papers that determines the scope of the field and produces both a RAG application and an
093   ontology, which may have many other use-cases (Jablonka et al., 2024).
094
095   To demonstrate the efficacy of OntoRAG, we apply the pipeline to a novel benchmark in the emerging
096
      field of Single-Atom Catalysis (SAC). This cutting-edge area of materials science serves as an ideal
      case study due to its novelty, complexity, and the critical role of synthesis procedures in advancing the
097
      field. While SAC holds promise for more efficient and selective catalytic processes Wang et al. (2018),
098
      the challenge of predicting effective synthesis methods often bottlenecks progress Grzybowski et al.
099   (2023). Additionally, we perform control evaluations on three standard biomedical benchmarks for
100   Q&A (MedMCQA Pal et al. (2022), MedQA Zhang et al. (2018), and MMLU-Medical (Hendrycks
101   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023b)), where we observe no decrease in accuracy relative to baselines.
102
      Our contributions are threefold:
103
104
                 • We present a novel ontology generation pipeline that automatically constructs domain-
105
                   specific ontologies from scientific literature (OntoGen).
106
107              • We introduce OntoRAG, a methodology for ontology-based Retrieval Augmented Generation
                   that enhances the scientific accuracy of LLM outputs.

111   The results indicate not only the potential that LLMs offer for ontology engineering, in the sense of
112   rapid prototyping and in niche domains, but also the usefulness that ontologies provide to LLM-based
113   applications as a source of structured and human interpretable information about said domains. Our

121   2.1   O NTOLOGIES
122
      From a practical engineering perspective, ontologies are a specification of conceptualization using
123   description logic (Iqbal et al., 2013). They model relationships between concepts and the words
124   used to represent them. In their most basic form, an ontology can be thought of as a series of
125   "isA" relationships formed in a hierarchical tree structure called a taxonomy (Keet, 2018). This
126   taxonomy serves as the backbone of an ontology and can be engineered to include other relationships,
127   instances, properties, and axioms. For further reading on the definition of an ontology, the reader
128   should reference "What is an Ontology" Guarino et al. (2009) and "An Introduction to Ontology
129   Engineering" Keet (2018), however for the purpose of this work, we use the definition from Guarino
130   et al. (2009): "an ontology is an explicit conceptualization of knowledge." When engineering
131
      ontologies for the sake of our workflow, we utilize the following definition.
132   Definition 2.1 An ontology is a tuple {C, R, I, P} where:
133
134          • C is a set of classes {C1 , C2 , ..., Cn } present in the ontology.
135
             • R is a set of relationships present in the ontology.
136            R = {(Ci , rs , Cj )|rs ∈ Rs }, where Rs is the set of all possible relations (object proper-
137            ties).
138
139          • I is the set of all instances of classes present in the ontology
140            I = {i1 , i2 , ..., im }; i : C, C ∈ C.
141          • P is the set of all possible properties in an ontology.
142            P = {p1 , p2 , ..., pl } and p : I →
                                                  − V or p : C →
                                                               − V; where V is the set of all possible values
143            for a property (data properties).
144
145   The creation of ontologies is a complex process that typically involves domain experts, knowledge
146
      engineers, and extensive evaluations (Iqbal et al., 2013). The development process often relies
      on domain analysis and knowledge acquisition, identification of key concepts and relationships,
147
      followed by formal representation of concepts, relationships, and axioms (Keet, 2018; Contreras
148
      et al., 2019). Ontologies then require extensive validation and refinement, as well as documentation
149   and maintenance (Yang et al., 2019).
150
151   In scientific domains, ontologies play a crucial role in organizing and standardizing knowledge. Some
152
      notable examples include the Gene Ontology (GO) (Aleksander et al., 2023), Chemical Entities
      of Biological Interest (ChEBI) (Degtyarenko et al., 2007), and NASA’s Semantic Web for Earth
153
      and Environmental Terminology (SWEET) (Raskin & Pan, 2005). Beyond organizing knowledge,
154
      ontologies have found numerous practical applications including data integration (Confalonieri &
155   Guizzardi, 2024), information retrieval (Vallet et al., 2005), natural language processing (Abburu &
156   Golla, 2017), building decision support systems (Bhattacharyya, 2015), among others (Jain & Kihara,
157   2019).
158
159   2.2   R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED G ENERATION
160
161   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) is a hybrid approach that combines the strengths of retrieval-
      based and generative models in natural language processing (Lewis et al., 2021). It enhances

191   retrieval methods. Dense retrieval methods (Lewis et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2020) rely on neural
192
      encoder models to map both queries and documents into a shared embedding space, enabling semantic
      matching. These approaches have seen significant advancements in domain-specific applications,
193
      such as RXNFP (Schwaller et al., 2021) for chemical reactions and MatBERT (Trewartha et al., 2022)
194
      for materials science. On the other hand, sparse retrieval methods like BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009)
195   and TF-IDF (Salton & Buckley, 1988) are based on statistical features of the data (Robertson et al.,
196   2009). These approaches often excel in scenarios requiring exact matching or when dealing with
197   out-of-distribution queries (Luan et al., 2021).
198
      RAG has found applications in various domains, including question answering (Q&A) (Izacard &
199
      Grave, 2021), content generation (Edge et al., 2024), and code generation (Bhattarai et al., 2024)
200
      among others (Wang et al., 2024). In science, RAG techniques have been explored for scientific Q&A
201   (Lála et al., 2023), explainable AI (Shahul et al., 2023), and literature review assistance (Edge et al.,
202   2024). Challenges remain in applying RAG to scientific domains, including ensuring the reliability
203   of retrieved information and handling domain-specific terminology (Barnett et al., 2024). Moreover,
204   the evaluation of RAG systems, particularly in specialized scientific contexts, remains a significant
205   challenge, necessitating the development of robust and domain-specific evaluation methodologies.
206
207   2.3   E VALUATION OF RAG S YSTEMS
208
209   The evaluation of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) systems presents unique challenges,
210   particularly in scientific domains where accuracy and reliability are paramount (Soong et al., 2024).
211   Traditional evaluation metrics for natural language processing tasks often fall short in capturing
212
      the nuanced performance of RAG systems, especially when domain-specific knowledge is involved
      (Barnett et al., 2024).
213
214   Evaluating RAG methods typically involves assessing both the retrieval and generation components
215   Shahul et al. (2023). Metrics such as Precision, Recall, and Mean Average Precision (MAP) are
      commonly used to assess the quality of retrieved documents (Robertson et al., 2009). Automated


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie,
217   2005) are often employed to evaluate the quality of the generated text, however, these often fall short
218   to assess the semantic quality of a text given a question. The more recent BERTScore (Zhang et al.,
219   2019) aims to capture semantic similarity beyond surface-level matches.
220
221   Evaluation of Scientific RAG In scientific contexts, additional complexities appear, as now answers
222   need not only to be semantically similar or use similar concepts as a ground truth answer, but they
223   should also make use of those concepts in a logical and scientifically accurate manner. Accurate
224   evaluation thus often requires domain experts, making large-scale automated evaluation challenging
225   (Chang et al., 2024). Assessing the factual correctness of generated content is crucial in scientific
226   applications but is difficult to automate (Petroni et al., 2020). Furthermore, from the perspective of
227   scientific discovery, evaluating the novelty and impact of new hypotheses is hard to quantify (Hope
228
      et al., 2020).
229   Some benchmarks exist that aim to tackle these challenges. KILT (Petroni et al., 2020) is a com-
230   prehensive benchmark for knowledge-intensive language tasks, including some scientific domains,
231   SciFact is a dataset for evaluating scientific claim verification, which can be used to assess the
232   factual accuracy of RAG systems in scientific contexts (Wadden et al., 2022). Other works default to
233   expert-in-the-loop evaluation by combining automated metrics with expert assessment (Chang et al.,
234
      2024).
235   Despite these advancements, the evaluation of RAG systems in scientific domains remains an active
236   area of research (Barnett et al., 2024). Challenges persist in developing evaluation methodologies
237   that can accurately assess the reliability, relevance, and potential impact of RAG-generated content
238   across diverse scientific fields (Li, 2024).

247   coherence between different pieces of retrieved information, especially when dealing with complex

291
      a high-quality, domain-specific ontology. Creating such ontologies has been traditionally a long and
292
      expensive process requiring committees of experts to decide on concepts, relationships, and axioms,
293   see Section 2.1. This limitation can restrict the applicability of OntoRAG to only fields for which
294   ontologies have been already created, excluding new and fast-developing fields—precisely those
295   where such technology could have the highest impact.
296

      4.1   VOCABULARY E XTRACTION
325
326   Vocabulary extraction is a critical first step in our ontology generation process. Our approach
327   leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to extract domain-specific terms comprehensively while
328   minimizing spurious entries. The process consists of three main steps:
329
330         1. Terms Extraction: Using LLMs for zero-shot identification of domain-specific terms from
331            sentence-split text, with a grounding check to filter hallucinations.
332
            2. Acronym Extraction: Employing a similar LLM-based procedure to identify and pair
333            acronyms with their full terms.
334
335         3. Lemmatization: Applying lemmatization to group variations of the same term, reducing
336            redundancies in the vocabulary.
337
338   Throughout these steps, comprehensiveness is prioritized while maintaining accuracy. Traditional
339
      Named Entity Recognition models often struggle with domain generalization (Behr et al., 2023),
      making our LLM-based approach particularly valuable for emerging or specialized fields. Each
340
      extraction step includes a verification process (Appendix A.4) to ensure fidelity to the source text,
341
      addressing the challenge of LLM hallucinations. This multi-step process results in a vocabulary that
342   accurately represents the domain knowledge present in the input documents.
343
344
345
      4.2   C ATEGORY E XTRACTION
346
      The first level of the taxonomic hierarchy consists of the main concepts that the ontology will cover.
347   Extracting these high-level categories poses a unique challenge, as individual scientific papers often
348   focus on specific areas within a domain, necessitating a broader perspective across multiple papers.
349
350
      To address this challenge, we propose the two-step process illustrated in Figure 2b):
351
352
            1. Generation: Conventional LLMs struggle with this task due to limited context windows.
               We leverage Long-Context LLMs (LCLLMs) (Chen et al., 2023a; Peng et al., 2023; Han
353
               et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023) to overcome this limitation. A random sample of papers is
354
               presented to an LCLLM, which is prompted to extract main categories across the given
355            papers. To mitigate the known sensitivity of LCLLMs to input order (Liu et al., 2024a),
356            multiple answers are generated, each with a randomly shuffled order of papers.
357
358         2. Refinement: Using the answers from the generation step, an LLM is prompted to curate a
359
               list of categories based on frequency of occurrence. Self-consistency (SC) techniques (Wang
               et al., 2022) are applied in this step to ensure categorical consistency across outputs, see
360
               Appendix A.4.
361
362
      The outcome of this process is a seed list of categories that forms the foundation for the rest of the
363
      ontology. However, it’s important to note that the ultimate correctness and utility of the ontology
364   depend on its intended downstream application. For practical use, some manual effort may be applied
365   at the conclusion of this step in a human-involved alignment phase (see Appendix A.4).
366
367
      4.3   TAXONOMY E XTRACTION
368
369   After vocabulary extraction and category generation, the next step is organizing terms into a hier-
370   archical structure using isA relationships. We propose a top-down, incremental approach to build a
371   global taxonomy from individual papers.
372
      Given a corpus of N papers P = {P1 , P2 , ..., PN }, with corresponding vocabularies V =
373
374
      {V1 , V2 , ..., VN }, the aim is to construct a taxonomy T (k) = {(s1 , t1 ), (s2 , t2 ), ..., (sM , tM )} at
      iteration k, where each pair (si , ti ) : si , ti ∈ Vl ∈ V, represents an isA relationship.
375
376   Our iterative and incremental top-down taxonomy generation process is outlined in Algorithm A.5
377   and illustrated in Figure 2c. Note that the iterative nature of this process allows for terms that couldn’t
      be placed in earlier iterations to find their proper position in later stages of the taxonomy construction.

413   a corpus of 500 recent research papers on SACs as our data source, see Appendix A.5.1, and they
414   were processed as illustrated in Section 4 using two different LLMs: Claude-3.5-sonnet (Anthropic
415   (2023)) and Llama-3.1-70B (Touvron et al. (2023)), in order to assess the model-dependence of the
416   generated ontologies.
417
      Displayed in Figure 3 are different descriptors for the ontologies, for the two LLMs and across 5
418   cycles of OntoGen. The results show a clear difference between the ontologies generated by each
419   LLM, in terms of every variable measured. In general, Claude seems to not modify it’s ontology
420   beyond step 2, while Llama continuously adds nodes and relationships until iteration 4. In terms of
421   topology, Claude’s ontology seems to be more flat or linear, with branching factors lying close to 1
422   while Llama’s are almost twice in average.

      These results are not conclusive of any particular advantage of any model over the other, and more

      6   D ISCUSSION
499
      We present OntoRAG, a method for performing RAG using context from field-specific ontologies.
500
      The method is complemented by OntoGen, a method for automatic generation of ontologies from
501
      a corpus of papers, which allows the use of OntoRAG even for fields where no ontology has been
502   defined. For evaluation we select the task of planning synthesis procedures for Single-Atom Catalysts,
503   and present a novel, diverse and challenging benchmark.
504
505
      Our experimentation with the pipeline on both general domain benchmarks and focused, narrow
      benchmarks indicate the variability in performance depending on the questions asked, the ontologies
506
      that are utilized, and the language model employed for principle tasks. This variability represents
507
      the importance of the knowledge used to generate the ontology, and should be carefully selected by
508   humans to ensure maximum performance. Furthermore, the success of OntoRAG depends on the
509   quality and relevance of the ontology utilized.
510
511
      Our contributions highlight the potential for using LLMs as not only a method of extracting knowledge
      for downstream applications, but also as a means of leveraging text-based knowledge for the structured
512
      extraction and formalization of domain specific concepts in the form of an ontology. We hope that the
513
      ideas, benchmarks and analyses presented in this paper will contribute to the construction of more
514   reliable, accurate and interpretable AI systems for accelerating scientific discovery across domains.
515
516
517   7   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
518
519   The results of our work demonstrate some fundamental limitations. The principal limitation is
520
      the computational cost of generating the ontology, and the variability that arises from different
      knowledge sources and language model. The variability in the OntoGen pipeline highlights the
521
      ongoing importance of human expertise in ontology development. We also recognize that, while
522
      the integration of ontological information may improve QA performance on question answering
523   tasks in particular domains, there is no direct improvement over the conventional RAG system on
524   a benchmark-wide scale. We attribute this to limitations in the ontologies utilized rather than the
525   pipeline itself. More studies are needed to decide conclusively on the effect that relevant ontological
526   information has on the reasoning process and question answering accuracy of an LLM.
527
      In future work, we aim to further integrate ontologies into the RAG pipeline. The core focus of
528
      future work will be direct comparison of performance on bench marks between one shot LLM,
529   LLM- RAG incorporation, and LLM-RAG-ontology integration. Furthermore, we future studies will
530   integrate open-ended question answering so that the reasoning of each system can be more thoroughly
531   evaluated. We also interned to utilize more specific and human made ontologies of niche domains
532   (such as single-atom catalysis) for direct analysis of ontologies generated by the OntoGen pipeline,
533   in addition to the downstream integration into the OntoRAG pipeline.
