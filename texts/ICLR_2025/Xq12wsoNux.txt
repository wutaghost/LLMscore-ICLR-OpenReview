                                                  A BSTRACT
009
010
               Deep learning using large models have achieved great success in a wide range
011
               of domains. However, training these models on billions of parameters is very
012            challenging in terms of the training speed, memory cost, and communication ef-
013            ficiency, especially under the privacy-preserving regime with differential privacy
014            (DP). On the one hand, DP optimization has comparable efficiency to the standard
015            non-private optimization on a single GPU, but existing DP distributed learning
016            (such as pipeline parallelism) has suffered from significantly worse efficiency on
017            multiple GPUs. On the other hand, the Zero Redundancy Optimizer (ZeRO) is a
018            state-of-the-art solution to the standard distributed learning, exhibiting excellent
019            training efficiency on large models, but to work compatibly with DP is technically
020
               complicated. In this work, we develop a new systematic solution, DP-ZeRO, (I)
               to scale up the trainable DP model size, e.g. to GPT-100B, (II) to obtain the same
021
               computation and communication efficiency as the standard ZeRO, and (III) to en-
022
               able mixed-precision DP training. Our DP-ZeRO, like the standard ZeRO, has the
023            potential to train models with arbitrary size and is evaluated on the world’s largest
024            DP models in terms of the number of trainable parameters. 1

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advances in differentially private (DP) deep learning have witnessed the power of large pre-
030   trained models, achieving comparable accuracy to state-of-the-art (SOTA) non-private models across
031   computer vision De et al. (2022); Bu et al. (2022a); Mehta et al. (2022); Xie et al. (2018), natural
032   language processing Yu et al. (2021a); Li et al. (2021); Bu et al. (2023a), and many other tasks.
033   Similar to their non-DP counter-parts, it has been observed that larger DP models tend to have
034   better performance. For example, the DP accuracy increases from 83% using RoBERTa-base (123M
035
      parameters) to 86% using RoBERTa-large (354M parameters) on GLUE datasets Li et al. (2021);
      Bu et al. (2023a); Yu et al. (2021a); the DP BLEU score increases from 61 using GPT2-small (124M
036
      parameters) to 64 using GPT2-large (800M parameters) on E2E dataset Li et al. (2021); Bu et al.
037
      (2023a); a similar trend is also observed using ViT (Base/Large/Huge) up to 600M parameters to
038   achieve state-of-the-art DP accuracy on ImageNet, around 81% at ϵ = 8 Mehta et al. (2022).
039
040   Driven by this success and the surge of computational power, it is high time to enable DP deep
041
      learning at the same scale of the standard non-DP one, e.g., GPT3-175B (Brown et al., 2020) and
      LLaMA-63B (Touvron et al., 2023a;b). Specifically, such a DP training system must have high
042
      speed and memory efficiency, low communication cost, and the compatibility with general model
043
      architectures.
044
045   For small to moderately large models (e.g. with less than a billion parameters) that fit within the
046   memory of a single GPU, a range of DP algorithms are feasible, producing the same result at differ-
047
      ent efficiency. Examples include TensorFlow-privacy Subramani et al. (2021), Opacus Yousefpour
      et al. (2021); Bu et al. (2022b), ghost clipping (GhostClip) Goodfellow (2015); Li et al. (2021);
048
      Bu et al. (2022a), and Book-Keeping (BK) Bu et al. (2023b), among which the BK algorithm has
049
      allowed DP optimization to be almost as efficient as the standard one. To be specific, the time/space
050   complexity of BK algorithm is 1.08 × /1.05× of the standard optimization on ViT-Large (300M
051   parameters, 147 layers) and 1.03 × /1.01× on GPT2-large (800M parameters, 220 layers).

061   To enable the DP distributed learning of these not-too-large models, one can directly use DDP
062
      (distributed data parallelism) (Li et al.), where each mini-batch of data is partitioned to smaller
      micro-batches and each GPU computes one micro-batch with a full copy of the DP model. A line
063
      of researches (Yousefpour et al., 2021; De et al., 2022; Kurakin et al., 2022) have reported that
064
      DDP with DP usually either incurs huge memory cost due to caching the per-sample gradients, or
065   suffers from 2 − 9× slower training speed than non-DP optimization De et al. (2022); Bu et al.
066   (2021). While the efficiency issues can be addressed through a better DP algorithm, such as BK, the
067   feasibility issue remains insurmountable because DDP cannot train models that exceed the capacity
068   of one GPU. Notably, the efficiency of BK algorithm is enhanced by two key techniques: mixed
069   ghost norm (computing per-sample gradient norms almost for free) and book-keeping trick (only
070   using one round of full back-propagation, not two rounds as in Li et al. (2021); Bu et al. (2022a)),
071   which are detailed in Appendix A and will also be leveraged in our DP-ZeRO solution.
072   As the model size further increases beyond a reasonable bound for one GPU (e.g. 32GB memory,
073   which roughly translates to 2B model training with Adam), the model must be partitioned in addition
074   to the data, e.g. using pipeline parallelism and model parallelism, so that each GPU only holds
075   a partial shard of the model (see Figure 2). In He et al. (2022), DP is combined with pipeline
076   parallelism to fine-tune about 0.1% of GPT3-175B. Yet, the pipeline parallelism can be inefficient
077   due to a non-DP-related issue – the pipeline bubble, where GPUs are idle while waiting for data to
078   process.
079   Generally speaking, more advanced distributed methods such as Zero Redundancy Optimizer Rajb-
080   handari et al. (2020) (ZeRO) and mixed-precision training have not be paired with DP due to the
081   lack of algorithmic advances. In this work, we develop DP-ZeRO, equipping state-of-the-art dis-
082   tributed learning solution with DP (see comparison in Table 1), without altering the mathematics of
083   DP optimization. We summarize our contributions as follows2 .
084

             1. We propose the zero redundancy distributed learning with differential privacy (DP-ZeRO),
                demonstrating the same level of communication efficiency, computation efficiency
086
                (speed and memory), and scalability (to GPT3 level and hundreds of GPUs) as the stan-
087
                dard ZeRO.
088

             2. We enable the mixed-precision training with DP by addressing the issue of loss scaling.
                This solution allows us to reduce the memory cost by roughly 50% and allow significantly

094          4. We will open-source our codebase as the first DP distributed learning library, that automat-

096             general network architectures (e.g. ResNet, ViT, GPT), and general distributed solutions

                (including DeepSpeed and FSDP).

140   Mathematical gradient partition In equation 1, the trainable parameters and their gradients are
141   mathematically partitioned into M groups, e.g. in all-layer clipping, all parameters form one group
142   (M = 1) Abadi et al. (2016); in layer-wise clipping McMahan et al. (2018); He et al. (2022),
143   each layer’s parameters form a group (M = number of layers). Empirical evidence and theoretical
144   analysis show that different partitions have the same training speed, though a finer partition (e.g.
145   layer-wise) has lighter memory footprint3 .
146
147   Per-sample gradient clipping In equation 1, a number of clipping functions Ci = C(∥gi ∥; R) are
148   available. Most works Abadi et al. (2016); Li et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021a) use the vanilla clipping
149   Ci = min(R/∥gi ∥, 1). Recently, Bu et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2022) advocate the automatic
150   clipping Ci = 1/(∥gi ∥ + 0.01) which is hyperparameter-free and comparably accurate. Note if
151   Ci ≡ 1, then the clipped gradient reduces to the standard gradient. The main overhead of DP
152   optimization is the computation of per-sample gradient norms. On a single GPU, the mixed ghost
153   clipping Bu et al. (2023b) has reduced the time complexity to < 10% on large models like GPT2.
154
155   Privacy accounting In equation 1, adding Gaussian noise to the clipped gradient protects the
156   privacy that is quantifiable by the privacy accounting theory Abadi et al. (2016); Bu et al. (2020);
157   Dong et al. (2019); Zhu et al. (2022); Gopi et al. (2021); Koskela et al. (2020). The privacy guarantee
158   is increasing in the noise level σDP , independent of Rm , learning rate, clipping function and model
159   architectures, with σDP = 0 leading to ϵ = ∞ (non-private).

      2.2     Z ERO R EDUNDANCY O PTIMIZER (Z E RO)

164   2.2.1    PARALLEL COMPUTING
165
166   Parallel computing is necessary to train large-scale models and is critical to the optimization ef-
167   ficiency. For models that fit in a single GPU, data parallelism (DataP) can be used to speed
168   up the training by partition the mini-batch of samples into multiple micro-batches. Then, each
169
      GPU (holding a full copy of parameters) executes the forward and backward propagation of one
      micro-batch, from which the parameter gradients are generated and averaged across GPUs to up-
170
      date the trainable parameters. However, for models that do not fit in a single GPU, the model
171
      parameters need to be sharded by alternative solutions such as ZeRO Rajbhandari et al. (2020),
172   model parallelism (ModelP) and pipeline parallelism (PipeP).
173
174   ModelP partitions a model vertically, e.g. using 3 GPUs to store the parameters of one layer. As
175
      a consequence, ModelP does not scale efficiently beyond a single node due to fine-grained com-
      putation and expensive communication between layers. Implementation-wise, ModelP frameworks
176
      usually require heavy code integration that may not be generalizable in model architectures. In con-
177
      trast, PipeP partitions a model horizontally across layers, e.g. storing 3 layers in each GPU. Each
178   GPU deals with all micro-batches sequentially, though PipeP can be inefficient due to the pipeline
179   bubble, which is overcome by ZeRO Rajbhandari et al. (2020). ZeRO is an advanced data parallel
180   method that eliminates memory redundancies during the training, and improves the training speed
181   and communication volume proportionally to the number of GPUs. Unlike basic DataP, ZeRO par-
182   titions a model’s states across GPUs and gather/reduce in a just-in-time manner, thus sustaining the
183   high efficiency of very large model training. Notice that ZeRO can work compatibly with ModelP
184   and optionally offload the model states to CPUs Ren et al. (2021); Rajbhandari et al. (2021).
185

      2.3     M IXED - PRECISION TRAINING
220
221   Mixed-precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2018) performs the forward and backward propagation
222   on the half precision (fp16 or bf16) parameters, activations, and gradients, while performing the
223   model update in full precision (fp32). Compared to the full-precision training, it is capable of saving
224   the memory by ≈ 50% and accelerating the computation by ≈ 20%. We note that fp16 has better
225   precision but a limited range: its representable numbers are among 10−8 ∼ 105 , and vice versa for
226   bf16. Therefore, loss scaling is necessary when using fp16 to prevent small gradients from being
227   rounded to zero, thereby preserving the model’s accuracy (see Appendix C for details). In contrast,
228
      bf16 usually does not need loss scaling since it has the same range as fp32. However, bf16 is not as
      widely supported as fp16, e.g. only available on NVIDIA Ampere GPUs or above (Nvidia).

      3.2.1    N UMBER OF COMPUTATION DEVICES
325
326   When scaling from one GPU (zero communication) to one node (multiple GPUs) and to mul-
327   tiple nodes, the communication efficiency decreases sub-linearly (↑communication). On a sin-
328   gle node, multiple GPUs can communicate using the high-speed intra-node connections such as
329   NVLink/NVSwitch (Foley & Danskin, 2017; Ishii et al., 2018). On multiple nodes, which are nec-
330   essary for large models, the inter-node connections are 3 ∼ 24× slower than the intra-node connec-
331
      tions (Li et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022). In short, DP-ZeRO can be as fast as ZeRO by equation 3
      when multiple nodes are employed.
332
333
334   3.2.2    M EMORY- EFFICIENT DISTRIBUTED LEARNING
335
      The communication volume is specific to different distributed algorithms, most of which trade the
336   communication or speed for memory, in order to feasibly train very large models. For example,
337   ZeRO3 (but not ZeRO1/2) needs to all-gather the sharded parameters at each iteration, hence suffer-
338   ing from 50% extra communication volume (↑communication). Another example is the activation
339   check-pointing (also known as gradient check-pointint Chen et al. (2016)), where a second for-
340   ward propagation re-computes the expensive activations during back-propagation, though at a 33%
341   slower speed (↑forward propagation). These techniques improve the relative speed of DP-ZeRO but
342   worsens the absolute speed.
343
344   3.2.3    PARAMETER EFFICIENT FINE - TUNING
345
      Parameter efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), Adapter (Houlsby et al.,
346
      2019), and BiTFiT (Zaken et al., 2022), optimizes a small fraction (e.g. Ψtrain = 0.1%Ψmodel )
347
      of model parameters and thus boosts the efficiency of back-propagation and communication (↓DP
348   back-propagation ↓back-propagation ↓communication). Consequently, (I) the communication vol-
349   ume of the gradient can be reduced possibly by 1000×; (II) the local computation can accelerate by
350   50% (Hu et al., 2021; Bu et al., 2022b), which can be seen by treating Ψtrain in Table 2 as almost
351   zero; (III) the memory cost is saved on the non-trainable layers, which translates to larger batch size
352   and faster computation. Hence, both relative and absolute speed of DP-ZeRO improve using PEFT.
353
354   3.3     M EMORY EFFICIENCY OF DP-Z E RO
355
356   We claim that DP-ZeRO is as memory efficient as the standard ZeRO, similar to the single GPU

410   4      E MPIRICAL PERFORMANCE OF DP-Z E RO
411
412   We evaluate DP-ZeRO on five aspects : model architectures, efficiency, scalability, compatibility
413   with various distributed learning and DP techniques. We use DP-ZeRO to train ResNet (He et al.,
414   2016), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022) and GPT (Radford et al., 2019; Brown
415   et al., 2020), which are workhorses in computer vision and language tasks. We report two accuracy
416   experiments in Appendix E.4 for ViT and GPT to show that the performance is consistent across
417   systems and to verify our implementation. We do not aim for higher accuracy with larger models,
418   because the accuracy depends on many traning hyperparameters like learning rate and number of
419
      epochs that are not relevant to the system designs. In fact, this is the norm of system papers including
      the standard non-DP ZeRO Rajbhandari et al. (2020) and FSDP Zhao et al., where the focus is on
420
      the system efficiency.
421
422   We measure the time and memory efficiency of DP-ZeRO under settings such as PEFT and multiple
423   precision formats (fp32 or fp16/bf16). We evaluate the scalability of DP-ZeRO in terms of number
424   of GPUs and number of model parameters. Our experiments scale from single node (8 GPUs) to
425
      multiple nodes, up to 256 GPUs, and train models up to 100B trainable parameters. Moreover, DP-
      ZeRO is compatible with mainstream implementations of ZeRO8 and with different clipping styles,
426
      clipping functions, privacy accountants, and so on. We leave the experimental details in Appendix D.

      By default, we use AdamW, mixed-precision training, layer-wise clipping style, B = 4, and A100

531   5             D ISCUSSION
532
533   In this work, we develope DP-ZeRO that enables the optimization of models up to 100B trainable
534   parameters, thus allowing DP distributed learning to be as efficient and scalable as the standard one,
535   for as few as 2 and as many as hundreds of GPUs. We believe this is a significant milestone to pave
536
      the path towards DP foundation models, especially for its open-source nature (link to be released).
537   We emphasize that, since DP only modifies the back-propagation, our DP-ZeRO is orthogonal to
538   any large-scale training techniques that are not tied to back-propagation: for example, activation
539   check-pointing, CPU offloading, weight/activation quantization (Dettmers et al., 2023; Xiao et al.,
      2023), and tensor parallelism (Narayanan et al., 2021) in 3D parallelism (e.g. Megatron).

778   Forward propagation: The matrix multiplication during forward propagation results in 2BT Ψmodel
779
      complexity (see Bu et al. (2022a)). Notice that, if the activation check-pointing is used, essentially
      two rounds of forward propagation take place in one iteration. Hence the time complexity doubles
780
      and becomes 4BT Ψmodel .
781
782   Back-propagation: This contains two sub-processes: the output gradients are computed at all lay-
783   ers, taking 2BT Ψmodel complexity; the parameter gradients are computed only at trainable layers (a
784   few if doing PEFT), taking 2BT Ψtrain complexity. Clearly, in full parameter training, the total is
785
      4BT Ψmodel , and in PEFT, about 2BT Ψmodel .
786   Attention: The time complexity of attention is O(BT 2 ) in Vaswani et al. (2017), where T is the se-
787   quence length (a.k.a. token length). When T is large, e.g. training with long context like T = 8192,
788   this cost is prohibitively high. In this regard, a line of researches have proposed linear complexity
789   attention, including but not limited to Wang et al. (2020); Katharopoulos et al. (2020); Shen et al.
790   (2021).
791   Communication: For algorithms that don’t shard the model, such as data parallelism and ZeRO1/2,
792   the communication is only used to send gradients and optimizer states. Hence the communication
793   volume is proportional to the number of trainable parameters O(Ψtrain ). Otherwise, for algorithms
794   such as ZeRO3 and tensor parallelism, the communication volume is proportional to the number of
795   total parameters O(Ψmodel ), because the forward propagation needs to gather the parameters from
796   many GPUs. This makes a big difference in PEFT when Ψtrain ≪ Ψmodel .

832   decisions and does not prevent overflow when using fp16 (due to step 2, see Table 3).
833
      Another walk-around is to delete step 5 and let per-sample gradient clipping do its job, i.e. 1 →
834   2 → 3 → 4 → 6. However, this approach is harder to implement because in the standard process
835   step 2 and 5 are simultaneously enabled or disabled. Also we cannot prevent overflow when using
836   fp16 as we still use step 2.
837

839

876   E.1   W ITH FORWARD & BACKWARD HOOKS
877
878   Hooks10 are important functions to enrich the deep learning optimization. To be specific, there are
879
880         1. forward modular hook (nn.register forward hook),
881         2. backward modular hook (nn.register backward hook),
882
883         3. backward tensor hook (tensor.register hook).
884
      DP libraries including Opacus Yousefpour et al. (2021), Private-transformers Li et al. (2021),
885
      Private-Vision Bu et al. (2022a), FastDP Bu et al. (2023b;a), FastGradClip Lee & Kifer (2021)
886
      and so on, use modular hooks to modify the standard optimization. However, ZeRO libraries in-
887   cluding DeepSpeed and FSDP use tensor hooks. This difference in the types of hooks and many
888   other differences (e.g. both ZeRO libraries and DP libraries modify the optimizer’s step function)
889   cause non-trivial problems when combining DP with ZeRO. For example, to keep DP optimization
890   as efficient as the standard, it is necessary to not waste time on computing the non-private gradient.
891   However, if we skip such computation, then ZeRO’s tensor hook will not be triggered and the corre-
892   sponding distributed-learning-related operations cannot carry on. For another example, because DP
893   and ZeRO add different types of hooks, the number of hooks is larger than either optimization and
894   they slows down the training: consider an 100-layer network, each layer with weight and bias (2 ten-
895
      sors), then DP-ZeRO in this subsection needs 100 modular hooks and 200 tensor hooks, adding to a
      total of 300 hooks. In addition, the Book-Keeping algorithm (in FastDP) in its original form cannot
896
      be implemented together with ZeRO3, because all model states are partitioned including the output
897
      gradients which are meant to be book-kept. To work around this requires rewriting the distributed so-
898   lution’s communication mechanism, and if successful, still requiring additional communication cost
899   during the second back-propagation. Similar problems are present for Opacus and FastGradClip,
900   which instantiates per-sample gradients that will be partitioned in ZeRO2/3 and requires additional
901   communication cost when gathered to create the privatized gradient.
902
      As a consequence, the hooks are fully supported on DP-ZeRO1 and partially supported on DP-
903
      ZeRO2/3 under the layer-wise clipping.
