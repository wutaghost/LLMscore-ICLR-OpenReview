009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge graph (KG) embedding methods learn geometric representations of
012            entities and relations to predict plausible missing knowledge. These representa-
013            tions are typically assumed to capture rule-like inference patterns. However, our
014            theoretical understanding of the kinds of inference patterns that can be captured
015            in this way remains limited. Ideally, KG embedding methods should be expres-
016            sive enough such that for any set of rules, there exists an embedding that exactly
017            captures these rules. This principle has been studied within the framework of
018
               region-based embeddings, but existing models are severely limited in the kinds of
               rule bases that can be captured. We argue that this stems from the use of repre-
019
               sentations that correspond to the Cartesian product of two-dimensional regions.
020
               As an alternative, we propose R ESHUFFL E, a simple model based on ordering
021            constraints that can faithfully capture a much larger class of rule bases than ex-
022            isting approaches. Moreover, the embeddings in our framework can be learned
023            by a Graph Neural Network (GNN), which effectively acts as a differentiable rule
024            base. This has some practical advantages, e.g. ensuring that embeddings can be
025            easily updated as new knowledge is added to the KG. At the same time, since the
026            resulting representations can be used similarly to standard KG embeddings, our
027            approach is significantly more efficient than existing approaches to differentiable
028            reasoning. The GNN-based formulation also allows us to study how bounded
029
               inference can be captured. We show in particular that bounded reasoning with
               arbitrary sets of closed path rules can be captured in this way.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Knowledge graph (KG) embeddings (Bordes et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2015; Trouillon et al., 2016;
035
      Sun et al., 2019) are geometric representations of knowledge graphs. Such representations are typi-
036
      cally used to infer plausible knowledge that is not explicitly stated in the KG. An important research
037   question is concerned with the kinds of regularities that can be captured by different kinds of mod-
038   els. While standard approaches are often difficult to analyse from this perspective, region-based
039   approaches make these regularities more explicit (Gutiérrez-Basulto & Schockaert, 2018; Abboud
040   et al., 2020; Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023; Charpenay & Schockaert, 2024). Essentially, in such ap-
041   proaches, each entity e is represented by an embedding e ∈ Rd and each relation r is represented
042   by a geometric region Xr ⊆ R2d . We say that the triple (e, r, f ) is captured by the embedding iff
043   e⊕f ∈ Xr , where we write ⊕ for vector concatenation. In this way, we can naturally associate a KG
044   with a given embedding. The key advantage of region-based models is that we can also associate
045
      a rule base with the embedding, where the rules reflect the spatial configuration of the regions Xr .
      However, not all rule bases can be captured in this way. As a simple example, models based on
046
      TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) cannot distinguish between the rules r1 (X, Y ) ∧ r2 (Y, Z) → r3 (X, Z)
047
      and r2 (X, Y ) ∧ r1 (Y, Z) → r3 (X, Z). This particular limitation can be avoided by using more so-
048   phisticated region-based models (Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023; Charpenay & Schockaert, 2024), but
049   even these models remain limited in terms of which rule bases they can capture. This appears to be
050   related to the fact that these models use regions which are the Cartesian product of d two-dimensional
051   regions, i.e. Xr = Ar1 × ... × Ard , with Ari ⊆ R2 . To check whether (e, r, f ) is captured, we then
052   check whether (ei , fi ) ∈ Ari for each i ∈ {1, ..., d}, with e = (e1 , ..., ed ) and f = (f1 , ..., fd ).
053   We will refer to such approaches as coordinate-wise models. Existing models primarily differ in
      how these two-dimensional regions are defined, e.g. ExpressivE (Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023) uses


                                                          1

054
      parallelograms for this purpose, while Charpenay & Schockaert (2024) used octagons. Using more
055   flexible region-based representations typically leads to overfitting. In this paper, we go beyond
056   coordinate-wise models but aim to avoid overfitting by otherwise keeping the model as simple as
057   possible, by learning regions which are defined in terms of ordering constraints of the form ei ≤ fj .
058
      We show that this model, which we term R ESHUFFL E, can capture a larger class of rule bases than
059
      existing region-based models. For instance, to the best of our knowledge, R ESHUFFL E is the first
060
      that can capture (some) rule bases with cyclic dependencies. Furthermore, we show that entity em-
061   beddings in our framework can be learned using a monotonic Graph Neural Network (GNN) with
062   randomly initialised node embeddings. This GNN effectively serves as a differentiable approxima-
063   tion of a rule base, acting on the initial representations of the entities to ensure that they capture the
064   consequences that can be inferred from the KG. An important practical consequence is that our KG
065   embeddings can be efficiently updated when new knowledge becomes available. Thus, our model is
066   particularly well suited for KG completion in the inductive setting, where we need to predict links
067   between entities that were not seen during training. From a theoretical point of view, the GNN-based
068   formulation allows us to study bounded inference, where the number of layers of the GNN can be re-
069
      lated to the number of inference steps. In particular, we show that our model is capable of faithfully
      capturing bounded inference with arbitrary sets of closed path rules. Finally, while the main focus
070
      of this paper is on advancing our theoretical understanding of the expressivity of knowledge graph
071
      embeddings, we also empirically evaluate R ESHUFFL E on the task of inductive KG completion,
072   where we find that it outperforms existing differentiable rule learning strategies.

075   2    R ELATED WORK
076
077   Region-based models Despite the vast amount of work on KG embeddings in the last decade, the
078   reasoning abilities of most existing models are poorly understood. The main exception comes from
079   a line of work that has focused on region-based representations (Gutiérrez-Basulto & Schockaert,
080   2018; Abboud et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Leemhuis et al., 2022; Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023;
081   Charpenay & Schockaert, 2024). Essentially, the region-based view makes explicit which triples
082   and rules are captured by a given embedding. This allows us to study what kinds of semantic
083
      dependencies a given model is capable of capturing, which is important for ensuring that models
      have the right inductive bias, especially for settings where reasoning is important. Existing work
084
      has uncovered various limitations of existing models. For instance, Gutiérrez-Basulto & Schockaert
085
      (2018) revealed that bilinear models such as RESCAL (Nickel et al., 2011), DistMult (Yang et al.,
086   2015), TuckER (Balazevic et al., 2019) and ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016) cannot capture relation
087   hierarchies in a faithful way. Gutiérrez-Basulto & Schockaert (2018) studied the expressivity of
088   models where regions can be represented using arbitrary convex polytopes, finding that arbitrary
089   sets of closed path rules can be faithfully captured by such representations. However, learning
090   arbitrary polytopes is not feasible in practice for high-dimensional spaces, hence more recent works
091   has focused on finding regions that are easier to learn while still retaining some of the theoretical
092   advantages, such as Cartesian products of boxes (Abboud et al., 2020), cones (Zhang et al., 2021;
093   Leemhuis et al., 2022), parallelograms (Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023) and octagons (Charpenay &
094
      Schockaert, 2024). However, all these models are significantly more limited in the kinds of rules
      that they can capture. For instance, while the use of parallelograms and octagons makes it possible
095
      to capture some closed path rules, in practice we want to capture sets of such rules. This is only
096
      known to be possible under rather restrictive conditions (see Section 3).
097
098
099
      Inductive KG completion Standard benchmarks for KG completion only test the reasoning abil-
      ities of models to a limited extent. For instance, BoxE (Abboud et al., 2020) achieves strong re-
100
      sults on these benchmarks, despite provably being incapable of modelling simple rules such as
101
      r1 (X, Y ) ∧ r2 (Y, Z) → r3 (X, Z). In our experiments, we will therefore focus on the problem
102   of inductive KG completion (Teru et al., 2020). In the inductive setting, we need to predict links
103   between entities that are different from those that were seen during training. To perform this task,
104   models need to learn semantic dependencies between the relations, and then exploit this knowledge
105   when making predictions. This can be achieved in different ways. A natural strategy is to learn rules
106   from the training KG, either explicitly using models such as AnyBURL (Meilicke et al., 2019) and
107   RNNLogic (Qu et al., 2021) or implicitly using differentiable rule learners such as Neural-LP (Yang
      et al., 2017), DRUM (Sadeghian et al., 2019) and NCRL (Cheng et al., 2023). In practice, better


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      results have been obtained using GNNs. For instance, some approaches (Teru et al., 2020) reduce
109   the problem of link prediction to a graph classification problem. They first construct a subgraph
110   containing paths connecting the head entity with some candidate tail entity, and then use a GNN to
111   predict a score from this subgraph. Such approaches suffer from limited scalability, as answering a
112   link prediction query requires constructing and processing such a subgraph for each candidate tail
113   entity. NBFNet (Zhu et al., 2021) alleviates this limitation, by using a single GNN that processes
114   the entire graph. The resulting node embeddings can then be used to score the different candidate
115   tail entities. However, the node embeddings are query-specific, meaning that this model still re-
116   quires a new forward pass of the GNN for each query, which is considerably less efficient than using
117
      KG embeddings. While we use a GNN for computing entity embeddings, once these embeddings
      have been learned, we can use them to answer arbitrary link prediction queries. R ESHUFFL E is thus
118
      considerably more efficient than the aforementioned GNN-based models for inductive KG comple-
119
      tion. ReFactor GNN (Chen et al., 2022) also uses a GNN to learn entity embeddings, by simulating
120   the training dynamic of traditional KG embedding methods such as TransE (Bordes et al., 2013).
121   However, their method has the disadvantage that all embeddings have to be recomputed when new
122   triples are added to the KG. Moreover, their model inherits the limitations of traditional embedding
123   models when it comes to faithfully modelling rules. Conceptually, our method has more in common
124   with differentiable rule learning methods than with subgraph classification strategies. Indeed, each
125   layer of the GNN updates the entity embeddings by essentially simulating the application of rules.
126   Moreover, our model can simulate the deductive chaining of rules, which makes it fundamentally
127   different from Neural-LP and DRUM, which focus on one-off rule application. Finally, while we
128
      focus on methods that learn by analysing the structure of a given KG, in some domains it is also
      possible to exploit prior knowledge, for instance by leveraging LLMs (Zhu et al., 2024).

131   3    P ROBLEM SETTING
132
133   Let R be a set of relations, E a set of entities, and G ⊆ E × R × E a knowledge graph. Similar
134   to standard KG embedding models, our aim is to learn a vector e ∈ Rd for every entity e ∈ E
135   and a scoring function sr for every relation r ∈ R such that sr (e, f ) reflects the plausibility of
136   the triple (e, r, f ). In region-based models, this scoring function is defined in terms of a geometric
137   region Xr ⊆ Rd . Specifically, the triple (e, r, f ) is then considered to be captured by the embedding
138   iff e ⊕ f ∈ Xr , where we write e ⊕ f to denote vector concatenation. The scoring function sr
139   then reflects how close e ⊕ f is to the region Xr (which is formalised in different ways by different
140
      models). A key advantage of region-based models is that they offer a mechanism for modelling rules.
      Let us write η to denote a given region-based embedding, i.e. η(e) ∈ Rd denotes the embedding of
141
      the entity e ∈ E and η(r) ⊆ R2d denotes the region representing the relation r ∈ R. Similar to
142
      existing (differentiable) rule-based methods for KG completion (Yang et al., 2017; Meilicke et al.,
143   2019; Sadeghian et al., 2019; Qu et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023), we focus on so-called closed path
144   rules, which are rules ρ of the following form:
145
146                     r1 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r2 (X2 , X3 ) ∧ ... ∧ rp (Xp , Xp+1 ) → r(X1 , Xp+1 )               (1)
147
      We refer to r1 (X1 , X2 )∧r2 (X2 , X3 )∧...∧rp (Xp , Xp+1 ) as the body of the rule and to r(X1 , Xp+1 )
148
      as the head. We say that η captures this rule if for all vectors x1 , ..., xp+1 ∈ Rn we have:
149
150                (x1 ⊕ x2 ∈ η(r1 )) ∧ .... ∧ (xp ⊕ xp+1 ∈ η(rp )) ⇒ (x1 ⊕ xp+1 ∈ η(r))                    (2)
151
152   Apart from their practical significance, our focus on closed path rules is also motivated by the
153
      observation that existing region-based models have particular limitations when it comes to cap-
      turing this kind of rules. Some approaches, such as BoxE (Abboud et al., 2020) are not capable
154
      of capturing such rules at all. More recent approaches (Pavlovic & Sallinger, 2023; Charpenay
155
      & Schockaert, 2024) are capable of capturing closed path rules, but with significant limitations.
156   Specifically, given a set of closed path rules P, we ideally want an embedding η that captures
157   every rule in P while not capturing any rules that are not entailed by P. Charpenay & Schock-
158   aert (2024) showed this to be possible, provided that every non-trivial rule entailed from P is
159   a closed path rule in which r1 , ..., rp , r are all distinct relations. For instance, rules of the form
160   r1 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r1 (X2 , X3 ) → r(X1 , X3 ) and r1 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r2 (X2 , X3 ) → r1 (X1 , X3 ) were not al-
161   lowed in their construction. Similarly, they cannot capture rule bases with cyclic dependencies such
      as P = {r1 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r2 (X2 , X3 ) → r3 (X1 , X3 ), r3 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r4 (X2 , X3 ) → r1 (X1 , X3 )}.

      In the following, we write P ∪ G |= (e, r, f ) to denote that the triple (e, r, f ) can be entailed from
163   the rule base P and the knowledge graph G. More precisely, we have P ∪ G |= (e, r, f ) iff either
164   (e, r, f ) ∈ G or P contains a rule of the form (1) such that P ∪ G |= (e, r1 , e2 ), P ∪ G |= (e2 , r2 , e3 ),
165   ..., P ∪ G |= (ep , rp , f ) for some entities e2 , ..., ep . We furthermore write P |= ρ for a rule ρ of
166   the form (1) to denote that P entails ρ w.r.t. the standard notion of entailment from propositional
167   logic (when interpreting rules in terms of material implication). Note that while we consider both
168   a knowledge graph G and a rule base P in our analysis, in practice only the knowledge graph G is
169   given. We study whether our model is capable of capturing rule bases because this is a necessary
170   condition to allow it to learn semantic dependencies in the form of rules.

172   4    M ODELING RELATIONS USING ORDERING CONSTRAINTS
173
174   Our aim is to introduce a knowledge graph embedding model which is more general than existing
175   coordinate-wise region-based embeddings, but which remains simple enough to make the represen-
176   tations learnable in practice. The central idea is to rely on ordering constraints. Specifically, we
177   model each relation r using a region Xr of the following form:
178                             Xr = {(e1 , ..., ed , f1 , ..., fd ) | ∀i ∈ Ir . eσr (i) ≤ fi }                  (3)
179
180   where the representation of a region r is parameterised by a set of coordinates Ir ⊆ {1, ..., d} and
181   a mapping σr : Ir → {1, ..., d}. We thus need a maximum of 2d parameters to completely specify
182   the embedding of a given relation. Note that in the special case where Ir = ∅, we have Xr = Rd .
183   Example 1. Let e = (0, 0, 0), f = (0, 0, 1) and g = (2, 2, 0) be the embeddings of entities e, f, g.
184   Let the relations r1 , r2 , r3 be represented as follows: Ir1 = {3}, Ir2 = {1, 2}, Ir3 = {1}, σr1 (3) =
185   2, σr2 (1) = σr2 (2) = 3 and σr3 (1) = 2. Then we find that e ⊕ f ∈ Xr1 , meaning that the triple
186   (e, r1 , f ) is captured. Indeed, for e⊕f ∈ Xr1 to hold, we need eσr1 (3) = e2 ≤ f3 , which is satisfied.
187   We similarly find that (f, r2 , g) is captured, because fσr2 (1) = f3 ≤ g1 and fσr2 (2) = f3 ≤ g2 .
188
189
      The following example illustrates how the use of ordering constraints allows us to capture rules.
190   Example 2. Consider a rule of the form r1 (X, Y ) ∧ r2 (Y, Z) → r3 (X, Z). This rule is captured
191   by an embedding of the form (3) if for each i ∈ Ir3 we have that i ∈ Ir2 , σr2 (i) ∈ Ir1 and
192   σr1 (σr2 (i)) = σr3 (i). For instance, the relations r1 , r2 , r3 from Example 1 satisfy these conditions.
193
      In general, if these conditions are satisfied and we have (e, r1 , f ) and (f, r2 , g) in G, then for each
      i ∈ Ir3 we have: eσr1 (σr2 (i)) ≤ fσr2 (i) ≤ gi . Since we assumed σr1 (σr2 (i)) = σr3 (i) it follows that
194
195
      eσr3 (i) ≤ gi for every i ∈ Ir and thus that the embedding captures the triple (e, r3 , f ).

241   5    C ONSTRUCTING GNN S FROM RULE GRAPHS
242
243   In equation (8) we already showed how a given closed path rule can be captured in R ESHUFFL E.
244   However, our main question of interest is whether it is possible to faithfully capture a set of closed
245
      path rules P. More precisely, in this paper we study the following question: can parameters be found
      for the matrices Br such that all rules entailed by P are captured, and only those rules. Rather than
246
      constructing the matrices Br directly, we first introduce the notion of a rule graph, which will serve
247
      as a convenient abstraction for studying this problem. We then explain how we can construct the
248   matrices Br from a given rule graph. Throughout this paper, we will assume that G contains the
249   triple (e, eq, e) for every e ∈ E, with eq a relation which does not appear in the rule base P. This
250   assumption corresponds to the common practice of adding self-loops GNN models.
251
252   Rule graphs We associate with the rule base P a labelled multi-graph H, i.e. a set of triples
253   (n1 , r, n2 ). Note that this graph is formally equivalent to a knowledge graph, but the nodes in
254   this case do not correspond to entities. Rather, as we will see, they correspond to the different
255   rows/columns of the matrices Br . A path in H from n1 to np+1 is a sequence of triples of the
256   form (n1 , r1 , n2 ), (n2 , r2 , n3 ), ..., (np , rp , np+1 ). The type of this path is given by the sequence of
257
      relations r1 ; r2 ; ...; rp . The eq-reduced type of the path is obtained by removing all occurrences eq
      in r1 ; r2 ; ...; rp . For instance, for a path of type r1 ; eq; eq; r2 ; eq, the eq-reduced type is r1 ; r2 .
258
259   Definition 1. A rule graph H for a given rule base P is a labelled multi-graph, where the labels are
260   taken from R, such that the following properties are satisfied:
261
      (R1) For every relation r ∈ R, there is some edge in H labelled with r.
262
263   (R2) For every node n in H and every r ∈ R, it holds that n has at most one incoming r-edge.

             r1 (X1 , X2 ) ∧ r2 (X2 , X3 ) ∧ ... ∧ rp (Xp , Xp+1 ) → r(X1 , Xp+1 ). Then there is a path in
266
             H from n1 to n2 whose eq-reduced type is r1 ; ...; rp .

269          whose eq-reduced type belongs to {(r11 ; ...; r1p1 ), ..., (rq1 ; ...; rqpq )}. Then there is some

332   6   C ONSTRUCTING RULE GRAPHS
333
334   We now return to the central question of this paper: given a rule base P, is it possible to construct a
335   R ESHUFFL E model which captures the rules entailed by P and only those rules? Thanks to Propo-
336   sitions 1 and 2 we know that this is the case when a rule graph for P exists. The key question thus
337   becomes whether it is always possible to construct such a rule graph. As the following result shows,
338
      if there are no cyclic dependencies in P, a rule graph always exits.
339   Proposition 3. Let P be a rule base. Assume that we can rank the relations in R as r1 , ..., r|R| ,
340   such that for every rule in P with ri in the body and rj in the head, it holds that i < j. There exists
341   a rule graph for P.
342
      It follows in particular that the class of rule bases that can be captured with R ESHUFFL E models is
343
      strictly larger than the class of rule bases that has been considered in previous work (Charpenay &
344
      Schockaert, 2024). Unfortunately, it turns out that there exist rule bases with cyclic dependencies
345   for which no valid rule graph can be found. This is illustrated in the next example.
346
347
      Example 4. Let P contain the following rule:
348                                   r1 (X, Y ) ∧ r2 (Y, Z) ∧ r1 (Z, U ) → r2 (X, U )
349
350   To see why there is no rule graph for P, consider the following knowledge graph G:
351
352
      G={(x1 , r1 , x2 ), (x2 , r1 , x3 ), ..., (xl−1 , r1 , rl ), (xl , r2 , xl+1 ), (xl+1 , r1 , xl+2 ), ..., (xk , r1 , xk+1 )}
353   We have that P ∪ G |= (x1 , r2 , xk+1 ) only if the number of repetitions of r1 at the start of the
354   sequence matches the number of repetitions at the end, but rule graphs cannot encode this.
355
356   The argument from the previous example can be formalised as follows. Let P be a set of closed
357   path rules. Let R1 be the set of relations from R that appear in the head of some rule in P. For any
358   r ∈ R1 , we can consider a context-free grammar with two types of production rules:
359
360
             • For each rule of the form (1), there is a production rule r ⇒ r1 r2 ...rp .
361          • For each r ∈ R1 , there is a production rule r ⇒ r.
362
363   The elements of (R\R1 )∪{r | r ∈ R1 } are treated as terminal symbols, those in R1 as non-terminal
364   symbols, and r is the starting symbol. Let us write Lr for the corresponding language.
365   Proposition 4. Let P be a set of closed path rules and suppose that there exists a rule graph H
366   for P. Let R1 be the set of relations that appear in the head of some rule in P. It holds that the
367   language Lr is regular for every r ∈ R1 .
368
369   This result shows that we cannot capture arbitrary rule bases using rule graphs. For instance, for the
                                                         (l)   (l)
370   rule base from Example 4, we have Lr2 = {r1 r2 r1 | l ∈ N \ {0}}, where we write x(l) for the
371   string that consists of l repetitions of x. It is well-known that the language Lr2 is not regular, hence
372   it follows from Proposition 4 that no rule graph exists for this rule base.
373   Following the negative result that arises from Proposition 4, we now establish two important positive
374   results. First, in Section 6.1, inspired by regular grammars, we introduce a special class of rule bases
375   with cyclic dependencies for which a rule graph is guaranteed to exist. Second, in Section 6.2, we
376   focus on the practically important setting of bounded inference: since GNNs use a fixed number of
377   layers in practice, what mostly matters is what can be derived in a bounded number of steps. It turns
      out that if we only care about such inferences, we can capture arbitrary sets of closed path rules.

      7     E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS
425
426   Thus far, we have shown that R ESHUFFL E is capable of capturing bounded reasoning for arbitrary
427   sets of closed path rules, as well as complete reasoning for several important special cases. We
428   now complement this theoretical analysis with an empirical evaluation, to show that suitable model
429   parameters can be effectively learned in practice, and to compare the performance of R ESHUFFL E
430   with existing differentiable rule learning strategies. For this evaluation, we focus on the task of
431   inductive KG completion, as the need to capture reasoning patterns is intuitively more important for
      this setting compared to the traditional (i.e. transductive) setting.

455   Datasets We evaluate R ESHUFFL E on the three standard benchmarks for inductive knowledge
456   graph completion (KGC) that were derived by Teru et al. (2020) from three datasets: FB15k-237,
457   WN18RR, and NELL-995. Each of these inductive benchmarks contains four different dataset vari-
458   ants, named v1 to v4, and each of these variants consists of two graphs, a training and a testing graph,
459   which are sampled from the original dataset as follows. The training graph GTrain was obtained by
460   randomly sampling different numbers of entities and selecting their k-hop neighbourhoods. Next,
461   to construct a disjoint testing graph GTest , the entities of GTrain were removed from the initial graph,
462
      and the same sampling procedure was repeated. Each of these graphs was split into a train set
      (80%), validation set (10%), and test set (10%). Thus, the three inductive benchmarks consist in
463
      total of twelve datasets: FB15k-237 v1-4, WN18RR v1-4, and NELL-995 v1-4. Furthermore, each
464
      of these datasets consists of six graphs: the train, validation, and test splits of GTrain and GTest . The
465   supplementary materials provide additional information about these benchmarks.

      Experimental setup Following Teru et al. (2020), we train R ESHUFFL E on the train split of GTrain ,
468   tune our model’s hyper-parameters on the validation split of GTrain , and finally evaluate the best
469   model on the test split of GTest . As discussed by Anil et al. (2024), some approaches in the literature
470   have been evaluated in different ways, e.g. by tuning hyper-parameters on the validation split of
471   GTest , and their reported results are thus not directly comparable. To account for small performance
472   fluctuations, we repeat our experiments three times and report R ESHUFFL E’s average performance.1
473   For the final evaluation, we select the hyper-parameter configuration with the highest Hits@10 score
474   on the validation split of GTrain . In accordance with Teru et al. (2020), we evaluate R ESHUFFL E’s
475   test performance on 50 negatively sampled entities per triple of the test split of GTest and report the
476
      Hits@10 scores. We list further details about the experimental setup in the supplementary materials.
      To facilitate R ESHUFFL E’s reuse by our community, we will provide its source code in a public
477
      GitHub repository upon acceptance of our paper.

      Results Table 1 reports the performance of R ESHUFFL E on the inductive benchmarks. The results
487   of R ESHUFFL E were obtained by us; AnyBURL and NBFNet results are from Anil et al. (2024);
488   Neural-LP, DRUM, RuleN, and GraIL results are from Teru et al. (2020); and CoMPILE results are
489   from Mai et al. (2021). Table 1 reveals that R ESHUFFL E consistently outperforms the differentiable
490   rule learners DRUM and Neural-LP, often by a significant margin (with WN18RR-v1 the only ex-
491   ception). Compared to the traditional rule learners, R ESHUFFL E performs clearly better on FB15k-
492   237 and NELL-995 (apart from v1) but underperforms on the WN18RR benchmarks. Anil et al.
493   (2024) found that the kind of rules which are needed for WN18RR are much noisier compared to
494   those than those which are needed for FB15k-237 and NELL-995. Our use of ordering constraints
495
      may be less suitable in such cases. Finally, compared to the GNN-based methods, R ESHUFFL E
      outperforms CoMPILE and GraIL on FB15k-237 and NELL-995 v1 and v4 while again (mostly)
496
      underperforming on WN18RR. R ESHUFFL E consistently underperforms the state-of-the-art method
497
      NBFNet. Recall, however, that our approach is significantly more efficient than such GNN-based
498   approaches, as R ESHUFFL E can score the plausibility of a given triple almost instantaneously. In
499   contrast, NBFNet (Zhu et al., 2021) requires one forward pass of the GNN for every query, whereas
500   methods such as GraIL (Teru et al., 2020) even need one forward pass for each candidate link for
501   every query. Moreover, thanks to the use of max-pooling in the GNN, our embeddings can straight-
502   forwardly be updated when new knowledge becomes available. Finally, as the analysis by Anil et al.
503   (2024) revealed, the performance of rule based methods can be significantly improved by combining
504   them with other methods. The main issue is that for many queries, no strong evidence is available
505   for any of the answer candidates, which rule based methods struggle with. To outperform methods
506
      such as NBFNet, rule based approach thus need to be combined with some kind of fallback model.
      A detailed analysis of this is outside the scope of this work.
507
508   Finally, we empirically investigate R ESHUFFL E’s components. We consider two variants for this
509   ablation study, namely: (i) R ESHUFFL EnL , which does not add a self-loop relation to the KG (i.e.
510   triples of the form (e, eq, e)); and (ii) R ESHUFFL E2 , which allows for more general Br matrices. In
511   particular, different from R ESHUFFL E, which applies the softmax function on the rows of Br (see
512
      Section 4), R ESHUFFL E2 squares the Br matrices component-wise, thereby allowing them to con-
      tain arbitrary positive values. For a fair comparison, we train each of R ESHUFFL E’s versions with
513
      the same hyper-parameter values, experimental setup, and evaluation protocol (see supplementary
514
      materials). Table 2 depicts the outcome of this study. It reveals that R ESHUFFL E performs compara-
515   ble to or better than R ESHUFFL EnL and dramatically outperforms R ESHUFFL E2 on all benchmarks.
516   The similar performance of R ESHUFFL E and R ESHUFFL EnL on most datasets suggests that the
517   self-loop relation only matters in specific cases, which may not occur frequently in some datasets.
518   The poor performance of R ESHUFFL E2 is as expected since allowing arbitrary positive parameters
519   makes overfitting the training data more likely.

521   8    C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   The region-based view of KG embeddings makes it possible to formally analyse which kinds of
524   inference patterns are captured by a given embedding. An important question, which was left unan-
525   swered by previous work, is whether a region-based embedding model can be found which is capable
526   of capturing arbitrary sets of closed path rules, while still ensuring that embeddings can be learned
527   effectively in practice. In this context, we proposed a novel approach based on ordering constraints
528   between reshuffled entity embeddings. This model, called R ESHUFFL E, was chosen because it al-
529   lows us to escape the limitations of coordinate-wise approaches while otherwise remaining as simple
530   as possible. We found that R ESHUFFL E has several interesting properties. Most significantly, we
531
      showed that bounded reasoning with arbitrary sets of closed path rules can be faithfully captured.
      We also revealed two special cases where exact reasoning is possible, which go significantly beyond
532
      what is (known to be) possible with existing region based models. From a practical point of view,
533
      our GNN formulation enables an efficient approach to inductive KG completion, where the result-
534   ing entity embeddings can moreover be efficiently updated as new knowledge is added to the KG.
535   Empirically, we found our approach to outperform existing differentiable rule learners, while under-
536   performing the state-of-the-art more generally. This latter result reflects the fact that (differentiable)
537   rule based methods are less suitable when we need to weigh different pieces of weak evidence. In
538   such cases, when further evidence becomes available, we may want to revise earlier assumptions,
539   which is not possible with R ESHUFFL E. Developing effective models that can provably simulate
      non-monotonic (or probabilistic) reasoning thus remains as an important challenge for future work.

1263   C.2    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1264
1265   R ESHUFFL E is trained on an NVIDIA Tesla V100 PCIe 32 GB GPU. We train R ESHUFFL E for up
1266   to 1000 epochs, minimizing the margin ranking loss (see Equation 11) with the Adam optimiser
1267   (Kingma & Ba, 2015). If the Hits@10 score on the validation split of GTrain does not increase by at
1268   least 1% within 100 epochs, we stop the training early.
1269   R ESHUFFL E was implemented using the Python library PyKEEN 1.10.1 (Ali et al., 2021). PyKEEN
1270   employs the MIT license and offers numerous benchmarks for KGC, facilitating the comfortable
1271   reuse of R ESHUFFL E’s code for upcoming applications and comparisons. Upon acceptance of our
1272   paper, we will provide R ESHUFFL E’s source code in a public GitHub repository to further facilitate
1273   the reuse of R ESHUFFL E by our community.
