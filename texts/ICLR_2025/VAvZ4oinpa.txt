                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Long-term stochastic video generation remains challenging, especially with moving
               cameras. This scenario introduces complex interactions between camera movement
011
               and observed pixels, resulting in intricate spatio-temporal dynamics and partial
012
               observability issues. Current approaches often focus on pixel-level image recon-
013            struction, neglecting explicit modeling of camera motion dynamics. Our proposed
014            solution incorporates camera motion or action as an extended part of the observed
015            image state, employing a multi-modal learning framework to simultaneously model
016            both image and action. We introduce three models: (i) Video Generation with
017            Learning Action Prior (VG-LeAP) that treats the image-action pair as an augmented
018            state generated from a single latent stochastic process and uses variational inference
019            to learn the image-action latent prior; (ii) Causal-LeAP, which establishes a causal
020            relationship between action and the observed image frame, and learns a seperate
021
               action prior, conditioned on the observed image states along with the image prior;
               and (iii) RAFI, which integrates the augmented image-action state concept with a
022
               conditional flow matching framework, demonstrating that this action-conditioned
023
               image generation concept can be extended to other transformer-based architectures.
024            Through comprehensive empirical studies on robotic video dataset, RoAM, we
025            highlight the importance of multi-modal training in addressing partially observable
026            video generation problems.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Video prediction is a valuable tool for extracting essential information about the environment, utilized
032   in various applications such as motion planning algorithms Hafner et al. (2019), and autonomous
033   navigation and traffic management Claussmann et al. (2020); Bhattacharyya et al. (2018). However,
034   the complex interactions among different moving objects in a scene present significant challenges for
035   long-term video prediction Finn et al. (2016); Finn & Levine (2017); Mathieu et al. (2016); Villegas
036   et al. (2017); Gao et al. (2019b); Villegas et al. (2019); Ebert et al. (2017); Sarkar et al. (2021). Recent
037   approaches include recurrent deep architectures Srivastava et al. (2015); Oh et al. (2015); Vondrick
038   et al. (2016); Finn et al. (2016); Mathieu et al. (2016); Villegas et al. (2017); Wichers et al. (2018);
039
      Oprea et al. (2022); Liang et al. (2017); Ebert et al. (2017) and latent variational models Denton &
      Fergus (2018); Babaeizadeh et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2018) on human action datasets such as KTH
040
      Schuldt et al. (2004), Human3.6M Ionescu et al. (2014) and robotic datasets such as BAIR Robot
041
      Push Ebert et al. (2017). However, these typically involve static cameras and do not capture the
042   complexities of moving camera scenarios. Recently visual transformers Dosovitskiy et al. (2021);
043   Ye & Bilodeau (2022); Gao et al. (2022a), diffusion and flow based models Ho et al. (2022); Mei
044   & Patel (2023); Davtyan et al. (2023); Harvey et al. (2022); Höppe et al. (2022b) have shown great
045   promise in generating long-term, high-fidelity predictions.
046
      In scenarios where the camera is moving, video frames are influenced by both the inherent scene
047
      dynamics and the motion of the recording platform. This interplay introduces significant challenges,
048   particularly in partially observable settings, which are common in domains such as autonomous
049   vehicles and mobile robotics. Previous works by Villegas et al. (2019); Gao et al. (2019a; 2022b);
050   Zhong et al. (2024) highlight the complexity of modeling interactions between scene dynamics and
051   camera motion in partially observable video prediction problem and tried to address it with novel
052   network architecture designs Gao et al. (2022b); Zhong et al. (2024) or larger latent space Villegas
053   et al. (2019). Existing datasets such as KITTI Geiger et al. (2013), KITTI-360 Liao et al. (2021),
      A2D2 et. al (2020), and Caltech’s pedestrian dataset Dollar et al. (2011) emphasize this issue in

054
      outdoor autonomous driving scenarios. For indoor robotics, the RoAM dataset Sarkar et al. (2023) has
055   demonstrated the importance of modeling such interactions by including synchronized image-action
056   pairs, enabling a more comprehensive exploration of partially observable video prediction tasks.

058
      Prior studies on action-conditioned video, introducing Atari reinforcement learning Oh et al. (2015)
      and Introspective Variational Autoencoders Valencia et al. (2021) incorporated actions as extended
059
      video generative model states. However, these approaches assumed the availibility of future actions
060
      and learned image priors independent of the camera actions. Similarly, Ma et al. (2022), Finn
061   et al. (2016), Nazari et al. (2022), and Nunes et al. (2020) established video prediction frameworks
062   for object manipulation, predominantly focusing on stationary camera setups with pre-computed
063   manipulator end-effector trajectories.
064
      Recently text token based video diffusion Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2022) models
065
      like AnimateDiff Guo et al. (2023), Videocomposer Wang et al. (2024a), Motionctrl Wang et al.
066
      (2024b) and Direct-a-video Yang et al. (2024b) uses textual instructions and in some cases the camera
067   parameters like pan and zoom Yang et al. (2024b) to generate high fidality videos. However these
068   models also assume the avaiability of the desired camera movement beforehand. This assumption
069   may work in controlled environments like stationary robotic manipulators with pre-computed end-
070   effector trajectories, but fails in more dynamic scenarios such as moving cameras in unpredictable
071   environments like busy roads or crowded spaces. In these complex, stochastic settings, an ideal
072   approach requires the ability to learn and predict platform actions based on past and predicted image
073   frames, and vice versa.
074   In this work, we take a step forward by introducing the two following theoretical frameworks that not
075   only incorporate actions into video prediction but simultaneously predict future actions:
076
      Conditional Independence: Under the conditional independence assumption, we model image-
077
      action pair as an extended system state and simultaneously predict the next image-action from a
078
      shared latent stochastic process. This assumption implies that image and action are independent when
079   the generative latent prior is known. Leveraging this principle, we propose two models: VG-LeAP,
080   a variational generative framework, and RAFI, built on sparsely conditioned flow matching. By
081   introducing RAFI alongside VG-LeAP, we demonstrate the versatility of incorporating conditional
082   independence into contemporary normalized flow matching Behrmann et al. (2019) frameworks.
083
      Causal Dependence: In our causal framework we assume the action is taken after observing the
084
      image state and then action leads the system to a new state. Thus images and actions are modeled
085   as causally interlinked nodes, reflecting the real-world scenario where a robot or vehicle takes an
086   action based on the current state and observes the next state as a consequence. This model learns
087   separate latent priors for image and action, with a conditional dependency between them. Following
088   this framework we introduce a new model Causal-LeAP, a variational generative frameworks.
089
      All the three proposed models: VG-LeAP, Causal-LeAP and RAFI, not only condition the predicted
090
      images on the camera actions, but also model and predict the future camera movement. This aspect has
091   been missing in the video predictive frameworks and paves the way for advancements in autonomous
092   navigation, robotic planning, and beyond.

095   2    P RIOR WORKS
096
097   Over the past decade, numerous mathematical frameworks have been proposed to model the current
098   image frame xt from a sequence of frames x1:T −1 from video data of dimension d = [ih × iw × 3].
099
      In their seminal work, Denton & Fergus (2018) introduced the stochastic learned prior model
      (SVG-lp) . This framework posits that a sequence of image frames from a video is generated
100
      from a latent Gaussian distribution. The latent distribution is learned through a variational training
101
      and inference paradigm using a set of observed image sequences. The current image frame is
102   predicted as x̃t conditioned on the past observed frames x1:t−1 and a latent variable zt . Given that
103   at the time of prediction p(zt ) is unknown, it is learnt with a posterior distribution pθ (zt |x1:t ) =
104   N (µθ (x1:t ), σ(x1:t )) approximated by a recurrent network parameterised by θ. The sampled variable
105   zt is then used to generate the current image frame xt conditioned on the past observed frames x1:t−1 .
106   Denton & Fergus (2018) proposed two methods for learning pθ (zt |x1:t ): (i) with a fixed Gaussian
107   prior and (ii) with a companion prior model pϕ (zt |x1:t−1 ) and minimising the KL divergence loss
      between the two. This learned prior model has subsequently been utilized in various video generation

144   3     ACTION CONDITIONED VIDEO GENERATION
145
      We introduce three distinct action-conditioned video generation models. The first two Learned Action
146
      Prior or LeAP models: VG-LeAP and Causal-LeAP are variational video generation frameworks
147
      in which the image and camera actions are learned through latent Gaussian distributions. However,
148   VG-LeAP is founded on the idea of conditional independence and Causal-LeAP assums that image
149   and camera actions are linked via causality. With the third model, we introduce RAFI, the Random
150   Action-Frame Conditioned Flow Integrating video generation model, based on RIVER by Davtyan
151   et al. (2023) which uses conditional flow matching. Conditional independence based RAFI shows
152   how camera action conditioning and prediction can be seemlessly integrated into Flow Matching by
153   Lipman et al. (2023) for enhanced video prediction quality.
154   In this paper, we denote the action of the robot or the platform on which the camera is mounted at
155   timestep t by at ∈ Rn , where n is the dimension of the action or actuation space of the robot/platform.
156   We also assume actions are normalised, that is, at ∈ [0, 1].
157
158   3.1   V IDEO GENERATION WITH LEARNT ACTION PRIOR (VG-L E AP)
159
160   Video generation with Learnt Action Prior, or VG-LeAP, is built on the principles of stochastic
161   video generation in Denton & Fergus (2018). However, unlike Denton & Fergus (2018) where only
      images were considered as the observed state of the stochastic process, we introduce the notion of

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      image-action pair (xt , at ) as an augmented state of the extended stochastic process that models the
163   image frames as well as the action of the robot. In scenarios where the camera is moving, the observed
164   image frames are influenced by the past actions or movements of the camera. Additionally, in many
165   cases, the future actions of a robotic agent or a car (on which the camera is mounted) depend on the
166   images observed, particularly when obstacle avoidance modules are integrated into the platform’s
167   motion planner. This interdependence between the image and action is also referred to as the partial
168   observability problem in video prediction literature Villegas et al. (2017); Sarkar et al. (2021). Thus
169   modelling this process with the notion of system or robot action as a part of an extended state of the
170   process provides a clear way of encapsulating these interdependent dynamics.
171   We assume that the extended image-action pair χt = (xt , at ) is generated from a latent unknown
172   process p(zt ) of variable zt whose posterior is approximated with a recurrent neural architecture

174   ploy a variational architecture similar to that of SVG-lp. However, in our case, we use the no-
175   tion of an extended image-action state instead of just the images. We use the reparameterization
176   trick from variational inference Kingma & Welling (2014), to approximate pθ (zt |x1:t , a1:t ) as a

      3.3     R ANDOM ACTION -F RAME C ONDITIONED F LOW I NTEGRATING VIDEO GENERATOR
300
              (RAFI)
301
302   The Random Action-Frame Conditioned Flow Integrating video generator or RAFI is based on the
303   sparsely conditioned flow matching model of RIVER by Davtyan et al. (2023). Like RIVER, we also
304   encode our image states in the latent space of a pre-trained VQGAN Esser et al. (2021). However,
305   unlike RIVER, we join the latent image state zt from the VQGAN network with the action vectors to
306   generate the extended image-action state z̃t as shown in the fourth step in Algo. 1. Specifically, zt has
307   a shape of [C, H, W ], where C is the number of channels in the latent space, and H and W are the
308
      height and width of the latent representation, respectively. The action vector at , initially of shape [A]
      where A is the dimensionality of the action space, is broadcast to [A, H, W ] and then concatenated
309
      to zt along the channel dimension. This results in z̃t having a shape of [C + A, H, W ], effectively
310
      integrating action information into every spatial location of the latent representation. Following the
311   creation of z̃t , we follow steps similar to RIVER to train the flow vector regressor Lipman et al.
312   (2023) using gradient descent. The step-by-step algorithm for RAFI is given in Algo. 1. During
313   inference, after applying the flow-matching process, we obtain z̃t1 , which maintains the shape of
314   [C + A, H, W ]. To predict action values, we extract the last [A, H, W ] maps from z̃t1 and compute
315   their average across the [H, W ] spatial dimensions. This operation results in a vector of predicted
316   action values with shape [A], corresponding to the dimensionality of the action space.

352   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
353
354   Out of the 25 frames in each sequence, we randomly select 5 consecutive frames to condition our
355   networks VG-LeAP, Causal-LeAP, SVG, RIVER and RAFI on the past data. All the 5 models generate
356   the next 10 frames in the future during training conditioned on the observed 5 frames. In order to test
357   the networks, we created 1024 randomly generated video sequences of length 40 from the original 5
358
      test sequences in RoAM and tested all the 5 networks against the quantitative performance metrics
      such as: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), VGG16 Cosine Similarity Simonyan & Zisserman
359
      (2015), and Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) Unterthiner et al. (2018) and Learned Perceptual Image
360
      Patch Similarity or LPIPS metricZhang et al. (2018). Among these metrics, FVD is based on the
361   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) that is commonly used for evaluating the quality of sequence of
362   images or videos from generative frameworks and measures the similarty between ground truth and
363   the learnt data distributions. We also use VGG16 cosine similarity index, LPIPS and PSNR for
364   frame-wise qunatitative evaluation. The VGG16 cosine similarity index uses the pre-trained VGG16
365   network Simonyan & Zisserman (2015) to measure the cosine similarity between the generated and
366   ground truth video frames. Recently perceptual similarity metric LPIPS Zhang et al. (2018) which
367   uses pretrained AlexNet as its image feature generator, has emerged as a popular measure Franceschi
368   et al. (2020) for its human-like perception of similarity between two image frames. In case of VGG16
369
      Cosine Similarity and PSNR values, closer resemblance to the ground truth images is indicated by
      higher values whereas in LPIPS and FVD scores, superior performance is associated with lower
370
      values. Each stochastic frameworks is sampled 20 times for each of the 1024 test video snippets.

477
      We have presented three new stochastic video generative frameworks based on the mathematical
478
      premise of incorporating action into the video generation process. We have also established a causal
479
      relationship between the image and camera actions in the partially observable scenarios where the
480   camera is moving with our Causal-LeAP model and have shown with our detailed empirical studies
481   that not only image-action models improve the efficacy of the prediction framework but also provides
482   a way to learn and model the system dynamics by simply observing and modelling the interaction
483   between the image-action pair. The causal model learned an action prior conditioned on the latent
484   image state pφ (ut |a1:t−1 , z1:t ) which can have direct applications to the field of robotics and RL.
485   The model RAFI also shows how easily one can extend the concepts of image-action state pair to
      existing flow matching approaches leading to useful results and avenues for future research.
