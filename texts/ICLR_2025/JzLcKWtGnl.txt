011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            New era has unlocked exciting possibilities for extending Large Language Models
014            (LLMs) to tackle 3D vision-language tasks. However, most existing 3D Multimodal
015
               LLMs (MLLMs) rely on holistic 3D scene information or specifically designated
               regions for 3D vision-language tasks, failing to capture multi-level location-based
016
               information. Addressing these concerns, we present Spatial 3D-LLM, a 3D MLLM
017
               specifically designed to enhance spatial perception and reasoning for 3D vision-
018            language tasks by enriching the spatial embeddings of 3D scenes. Spatial 3D-LLM
019            incorporates an LLM backbone and a meticulously designed progressive spatial
020            awareness scheme that captures spatial information as the perception field expands,
021            generating location-enriched 3D scene embeddings that serve as visual prompt.
022            Additionally, we introduce two novel tasks, namely 3D object distance measure-
023            ment and 3D layout editing, and construct a 3D instruction dataset MODEL, to
024            inspire more profound 3D spatial perception capabilities. Experimental results
025            demonstrate that Spatial 3D-LLM achieves state-of-the-art performance across
026
               a wide range of 3D vision-language tasks, revealing the improvements stemmed
               from our progressive spatial awareness scheme of mining more profound spatial
027
               information and the proposed dataset.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, Vision-Language Models (VLMs)(Hong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
033   2024; Wang et al., 2024) have rapidly advanced, with 2D Multimodal Large Language Models
034   (MLLMs) demonstrating remarkable capabilities in understanding complex visual scenes(Li et al.,
035   2022; Han et al., 2023). Concurrently, much success of developing 3D MLLMs has been achieved
036   on 3D scene understanding(Hong et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). 3D spatial
037   awareness encompasses the perception of spatial states, including locations and distances, as well
038   as spatial reasoning and generation derived from this perception, such as embodied planning and
039
      spatial layout editing. While diving into 3D world, 3D spatial awareness is one of the keys for 3D
      MLLMs to perform downstream tasks, such as robotics(Gao et al., 2024), virtual and augmented
040
      reality(Konenkov et al., 2024) and interior design(Yang et al., 2024b).
041
042   To enable VLMs to perceive and comprehend the 3D world, most existing 3D MLLM architectures
043   incorporate a 3D vision encoder to extract 3D features and align them with an LLM(Hong et al., 2023;
044   Wang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). However, current methods(Chen et al., 2023b;
045
      Huang et al., 2023; 2024) primarily focus on object attributes, overlooking strategies for precise 3D
      location perception. Approaches like Hong et al. (2023), Zhu et al. (2024) and Chen et al. (2024b)
046
      utilize the Q-former(Li et al., 2022) module to extract instruction-related information from 3D scene
047
      embeddings to form 3D MLLM input. The extracted input embeddings are too correlated with the
048   instructions to fully capture the spatial concepts of 3D scenes. Existing works (Wang et al., 2023;
049   Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b) still lack effective perception of 3D spatial
050   relations and precise location generation, which is a fundamental capability for spatial reasoning and
051   generation tasks. In 3D scenes, spatial information naturally exists at different levels, including that
052   of individual objects, object groupings, and entire architectures etc. Consequently, the majority of
053   existing 3D MLLMs depend on holistic 3D scene information or specifically designated regions for
      3D vision-language(3D VL) tasks, failing to capture multi-level location-based information.

079   Considering spatial intelligence from the perspective of tasks and datasets, several works(Cheng et al.,
080   2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Cai et al., 2024) have improved image-based spatial reasoning capabilities
081   by generating large-scale spatially-aware training data. They hypothesize that VLMs’ limited spatial
082   reasoning capability is due to the lack of 3D spatial knowledge in training data(Cheng et al., 2024;
083   Chen et al., 2024a). Those generated question answering datasets are mainly related to estimating
084   object pair relationships and metric measurements. Existing 3D instruction following datasets(Li
085   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Lyu et al., 2024) support a variety of spatial tasks, including visual
086   question answering, visual grounding, and spatial relationships estimation. However, these datasets
087
      mainly concentrate on perceiving coarse-grained and abstract object relationships while leaving
      fine-grained measurement unexplored. Moreover, they typically focus on local object interactions,
088
      neglecting the utilization of commonsense knowledge of object-scene spatial information.
089
090   In light of the mentioned deficiencies in existing 3D instruction datasets, we propose two novel
091   tasks, namely 3D object distance measurement and 3D layout editing in 3D scenes, to evaluate
092   the spatial perception capabilities of 3D MLLMs. We construct a 3D instruction dataset called
093
      Measure Object Distance and Layout Editing (MODLE) that is furnished with 263K vision-language
      annotations specifically targeted towards these tasks. Inferring precise distances between objects
094
      enhances fine-grained spatial perception, while performing object placement and movement in a 3D
095
      scene fosters a deeper understanding of object-scene spatial information, accumulating commonsense
096   knowledge for downstream tasks. The agent will gain wider and deeper spatial awareness and be
097   better equipped to interact within complex 3D environments. By successfully completing these two
098   tasks, the spatial intelligence of the agents can be significantly enhanced.
099
      Given the aforementioned concerns regarding the inadequate exploitation of spatial information in
100
      existing 3D MLLMs, we propose Spatial 3D-LLM, a 3D MLLM aimed at improving capabilities
101
      of spatial perception and reasoning for 3D VL tasks by enriching the spatial embeddings of 3D
102   scenes, as depicted in Figure 1. Spatial 3D-LLM incorporates a frozen 3D scene encoder, an LLM
103   backbone, and a meticulously designed progressive spatial awareness scheme that includes intra-
104   referent clustering and abstraction, inter-referent message passing, and contextual referent-scene
105   interactions. This spatial awareness visual referent evolution begins with relation-based clustering. It
106   then continues with inter-referent message passing to model spatial distribution based on the distances
107   between different referents. Finally, it encompasses broader contextual information by considering the
      interactions between referents and the surrounding environment. This stepwise scheme progressively

      2.1   S PATIAL I NTELLIGENCE IN 3D V ISION -L ANGUAGE TASKS
131
132   Diverse 3D VL tasks pose disparate demands on a model’s capability of spatial perception and
133   reasoning within 3D environments. For instance, 3D Visual Question Answering (3D-VQA) (Azuma
134   et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022) primarily rely on understanding the
135   holistic scene to provide answers or descriptions, without delving deeply into object-to-object spatial
136   configurations. 3D Visual Grounding(3D-VG) (Achlioptas et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020) and 3D
137   Object Detection (Qi et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2013) demand precise spatial localization, focusing on
138   identifying and locating specific objects or regions within the 3D space. Additionally, 3D Dense
139   Captioning (Chen et al., 2023a; 2021) involves generating detailed descriptions for various regions
140
      or objects in a 3D scene, requiring a strong grasp of how objects are positioned and interact within
      their spatial context.
141
142   Existing 3D VL tasks primarily focus on perceiving coarse-grained and abstract object relationships,
143   coupled with concentrating on local object interactions. Our newly proposed tasks, namely 3D object
144   distance measurement and 3D layout editing, enhance fine-grained spatial perception and accumulate
145   commonsense knowledge for downstream tasks, advancing the capability of spatial intelligence.
146
147   2.2   S PATIAL L EARNING IN 3D M ULTIMODAL LLM S
148
      Recent advancements in 3D MLLMs(Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b; Huang et al.,
149
      2023; Chen et al., 2024b) have explored a variety of spatial learning paradigms. These architectures
150   typically comprise 3D vision perceptrons, projectors, and LLM backbones. 3DLLM(Hong et al.,
151   2023) introduced location special tokens to better capture 3D spatial information, enabling models
152   to output 3D coordinates. LL3DA(Chen et al., 2024b) used clicks and boxes as visual prompts to
153   interact with 3D embeddings and generate spatial queries. SpatialRGPT(Cheng et al., 2024) enhanced
154   region-level spatial reasoning in VLMs by improving regional information representation and spatial
155   knowledge acquisition. Chat-3D v2(Huang et al., 2023) segmented scenes into objects, mapped
156   each with an index, and used special tokens to capture 3D attributes and spatial relations. Grounded
157   3DLLM(Chen et al., 2024d) introduced special noun phrase tokens to reference 3D scenes and let
158
      models process 3D-textual data sequences. Most existing 3D MLLMs rely on holistic 3D scene
      information or specifically designated regions, missing multi-level location-based information.
159
160   Distinguished from current approaches, our method explores a progressive spatial awareness scheme
161   that incorporates intra-referent clustering and abstraction, inter-referent message passing, and contex-
      tual referent-scene interactions, injecting richer spatial knowledge into the 3D scene embeddings.

      3.1     3D O BJECT D ISTANCE M EASUREMENT TASK
180
181   This task focuses on inferring 3D spatial distance between two objects within 3D scenes. We create
182   173K text-location pairs. Questions are made with manually defined templates, generating synthetic
183   data by filling in object descriptions sourced from ScanRefer(Chen et al., 2020) dataset. Answers are
184   derived from the actual 3D bounding box coordinates of the objects. We introduce Interaction Tokens
185   to distinguish between coordinate information and distance values in the output. Coordinates are put
186   within <loc></loc> tokens, and distances are marked with <gap></gap> tokens.
187
188   3.2     3D L AYOUT E DITING TASK
189
      This task demands the model have 3D layout editing capabilities. We design two subtasks: object
190
      movement and placement. Unlike the 3D-VG task that grounds an object in the scene, 3D layout
191
      editing requires a precise understanding of 3D spatial positions for predicting new object positions.
192
193   For the object movement task, the model is required to relocate an object in the scene based on its
194   description and an editing instruction. We define a template including the object description and
195
      movement instructions to construct the dataset with 45K text-location pairs. The object descriptions
      come from the ScanRefer dataset, and the movement instructions are randomly generated. In the
196
      object placement task, the model needs to understand the holistic scene and accurately place an object
197
      of a specified size within the scene layout. We created 33K sub-scenes from the ScanNet(Dai et al.,
198   2017) dataset, each with 3 to 8 objects. During training and evaluation, we randomly mask one object
199   from each sub-scene and require the model to predict a reasonable spatial position.

203
204
      We propose Spatial 3D-LLM, a 3D MLLM for comprehensive 3D scene understanding, 3D visual
      grounding, 3D spatial measurement, and 3D scene layout editing. The main pipeline of Spatial
205
      3D-LLM is illustrated in Figure 2. Spatial 3D-LLM incorporates a frozen 3D scene encoder, an
206
      LLM backbone, and a meticulously designed progressive spatial awareness scheme that includes
207   intra-referent clustering and abstraction, inter-referent message passing, and contextual referent-scene
208   interactions. Next, we will explain the details of each part.

210   4.1     S CENE E NCODER
211

214   unordered set of point features. To represent the input 3D scene, the scene encoder outputs 1,024
215   point tokens, Fenc = [penc , fenc ] ∈ R1,024×(3+256) , containing scene features fenc for 256 dimensions
      and coordinates penc for 3 dimensions.

237   4.2     P ROGRESSIVE S PATIAL AWARENESS S CHEME
238
239   Considering that the majority of existing 3D MLLMs struggle to effectively capture multi-level
240   location-based information, we propose Progressive Spatial Awareness Scheme that encompasses
241   intra-referent clustering and abstraction, inter-referent message passing, and contextual referent-scene
242   interaction. This approach aims to enhance spatial information retrieval as the perception field
243
      expands, thereby infusing location-enriched spatial knowledge into the 3D scene embeddings.
244
245
      4.2.1     I NTRA -R EFERENT
246   To capture point-to-point relational information within the scene, we propose the Intra-referent
247   module, which comprises Feedforward Neural Network (FFN) layers and a Cluster&Abstraction
248   module. Specifically, we sample 256 points from the encoded set of 1024 scene points using Farthest
249   Point Sampling (FPS), resulting in seed points Fseed = [pseed , fseed ] ∈ R256×(3+256) , following
250   VoteNet(Qi et al., 2019). Next, 3D spatial offset ∆pvote is predicted from the seed point feature
251   fseed by means of FFN layers. With the aim of adjusting seed point location to align with the centers
252   of object, as indicated by:

281   4.2.3    C ONTEXTUAL I NTERACTIONS
282
      To achieve contextual interactions among visual referents and the global scene, we introduce the
283
      Context Interactions module, which employs multiple blocks of self-attention, cross-attention, and
284   Refine-Location Module to obtain spatially aware representations of referents. In detail, visual
285   referent representation processed by Inter-referent Module Fvr and the scene features fenc undergo self-
286   attention and cross-attention layers. The updated Fvr can be claimed as a scene-aware visual referent
287   representation, effectively incorporating object-specific features and spatial positional information,
288   thereby enhancing its comprehensiveness and achieving spatial scene awareness.
289
290   Refine-Location Module. To further improve the precision of visual referent location predictions,
291   we introduce the Refine-Location Module, designed to refine the spatial positioning of referents by
292   minimizing the relative distance to their ground truth coordinates.
293

315
316   For representing 3D point coordinates occuring the text, following 3D-VLA(Zhen et al., 2024) and
317   LL3DA(Chen et al., 2024b), we normalize the point cloud coordinates into discrete unsigned integers

319   which help differentiate the spatial coordinates from other data.
320   To integrate the visual prompt, denoted as Fvisual , which contains both visual referent features
321   fvr and location representation pvr into the large language model (LLM). We introduce a trainable
322   projector consist multi-layers of FFN to align Fvisual = [fvr , pvr ] as Visual Prompt within the
323   language space of LLM, allowing the model to process 3D spatial information alongside natural
      language input.

      5.1     DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
339
340   Datasets. To evaluate the performance of our model, we require 3D scene point clouds along
341   with a visual-language task dataset. For the 3D scene input, we utilize ScanNet(Dai et al., 2017),
342   a real 3D indoor scene dataset that includes 1,201 training scenes and 312 testing scenes. For
343   visual-language data, we incorporate Scan2Cap(Chen et al., 2021), ScanQA(Azuma et al., 2022),
344   SQA3D(Ma et al., 2022), and embodiedQA(Hong et al., 2023) from 3D-LLM for training and
345   evaluation of visual-language understanding tasks. Additionally, we use ScanRefer(Chen et al., 2020)
346
      and Multi3DRefer(Zhang et al., 2023) for single- and multi-object grounding, and leverage proposed
      distance measurements, object movement, and object placement tasks for precise spatial position
347
      perception and generation.
348
349   Implementation Details. We initialize the weights of the 3D scene encoder using the pre-trained
350   Vote2Cap-DETR(Chen et al., 2024c). The large language model utilizes the pre-trained Vicuna-
351   7B and implement LoRA for instruction-tuning. During the training process, we jointly train the

      progressive spatial awareness scheme and the LoRA parameters across all task datasets. We employ

522   6        C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we have presented Spatial 3D-LLM, a multi-modal LLM for 3D scene understanding
525   and spatial perception, which could fully exploit the spatial information within 3D scenes. By
526   carefully designing a progressive spatial awareness scheme within our framework, Spatial 3D-LLM
527   achieves superior performance across a spectrum of 3D VL tasks. Moreover, two novel tasks including
528
      3D object distance measurement and layout editing are proposed to enhance fine-grained spatial
      perception and commonsense knowledge. The experimental results verify our Spatial 3D-LLM’s
529
      capability in 3D scene understanding and spatial perception. Our future work includes expanding
530
      the diversity of the training datasets to encompass more complex and varied 3D scenes, which
531   would enhance the model’s generalizability. Additionally, we aim to investigate methods to improve
532   real-time performance without compromising accuracy for practical applications such as robotics and
533   augmented reality.

723   A.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS OF OUR CONSTRUCTED TASKS
724
725   3D object distance measurement task To assess the accuracy of distance predictions, we draw
726   inspiration from the evaluation of 3D-VG, focusing on the localization accuracy of Objects A and
727   B. We follow the approach used in Chen et al. (2010), employing absolute relative error (ARE)
728   to evaluate distance prediction accuracy. Our primary metric, mARE@kIOU, measures the mean
729   absolute relative error for predictions on the X, Y, and Z axes, providing a detailed assessment of the
730
      model’s spatial reasoning capabilities.
731
      3D layout editing task To evaluate the accuracy of object editing in the scene, we follow the
732
      metrics used in the 3D-VG task, calculating the Intersection over Union (IoU) between the predicted
733
      bounding box and the ground truth to assess the rationality of the predicted positions.
