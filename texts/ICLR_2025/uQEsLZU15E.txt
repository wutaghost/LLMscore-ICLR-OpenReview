011                                                                                                                           A BSTRACT
012
013                                    We present the Modality Integration Rate (MIR), an effective, robust, and general-
014                                    ized metric to indicate the multi-modal pre-training quality of Large Vision Lan-
015                                    guage Models (LVLMs). Large-scale pre-training plays a critical role in building
016                                    capable LVLMs, while evaluating its training quality without the costly super-
017                                    vised fine-tuning stage is under-explored. Loss, perplexity, and in-context evalu-
018                                    ation results are commonly used pre-training metrics for Large Language Models
019
                                       (LLMs), while we observed that these metrics are less indicative when aligning
                                       a well-trained LLM with a new modality. Due to the lack of proper metrics, the
020
                                       research of LVLMs in the critical pre-training stage is hindered greatly, including
021
                                       the training data choice, efficient module design, etc. In this paper, we propose
022                                    evaluating the pre-training quality from the inter-modal distribution distance per-
023                                    spective and present MIR, the Modality Integration Rate, which is 1) Effective
024                                    to represent the pre-training quality and show a positive relation with the bench-
025                                    mark performance after supervised fine-tuning. 2) Robust toward different train-
026                                    ing/evaluation data. 3) Generalize across training configurations and architecture
027                                    choices. We conduct a series of pre-training experiments to explore the effective-
028                                    ness of MIR and observe satisfactory results that MIR is indicative about training
029                                    data selection, training strategy schedule, and model architecture design to get
030
                                       better pre-training results. We hope MIR could be a helpful metric for building
                                       capable LVLMs and inspire the following research about modality alignment in
031
                                       different areas.

077   1                   I NTRODUCTION
078
079
080   In the past two years, the exploration of LVLMs (Liu et al. (2024c); Zhu et al. (2023); Dai et al.
081
      (2023)) has exploded from multiple aspects, showcasing amazing usages in many areas. Putting
      aside the diversity among these explorations, most LVLMs follow a dual-stage paradigm that uses a
082
      pre-training stage to align the modality, followed by a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage to enable
083
      diverse multi-modal capabilities. With the progress of LVLMs, the pre-training stage evolved from
084   lightweight alignment with few image-caption pairs toward deep modality integration with large-
085   scale diverse multi-modal data, playing a more critical role in building capable LVLMs.
086
087
      Despite the rapid evolution, how to qualify the LVLMs after pre-training is still an under-explored
      problem, which hinders the pre-training study from being more controllable and reliable. Bench-
088
      mark results after supervised fine-tuning is the commonly used solution in recent studies, while the
089
      SFT stage introduces non-neglectable computation overhead and complex the process.
090
091   Another naive idea is to borrow the metrics from the Large Language Models (LLMs) pre-training
092   (Zhao et al. (2023)) and use the loss value, perplexity, or in-context evaluation results to evaluate
093
      it. However, as shown in Figure 1, we empirically find that these metrics are neither stable nor
      reliable in the multi-modal area (Yin et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024d); McKinzie
094
      et al. (2024)). Specifically, we conduct a controllable experiment about pre-training data quantity to
095
      study the relation between these metrics and the benchmark results after supervised fine-tuning. The
096   training loss and perplexity keep decreasing with training data quantity increasing, while the model
097   performance is already saturated with less than 1M data. When it comes to in-context evaluation, its
098   performance shows irregular jitter with varing training data, leading to meaningless indication.
099
      We argue the in-effectiveness of the aforementioned metrics is based on the pre-training target differ-
100
      ence between LLMs and LVLMs. The former learns to model the language while the latter focuses
101
      on closing the domain gap between two modalities. We sample images and texts from multiple
102   sources and visualize their distribution by the embedding layer feature of the LLaVA-v1.5 (Liu et al.
103   (2024a)). As shown in Figure 2(a), despite the content diversity within images or texts, they show a
104   relatively uniform distribution within each modality and a clear distribution gap between modalities.
105   We further calculate the layer-wise modality gap on several leading LVLMs (Lu et al. (2024); Wang
106   et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024b); Tong et al. (2024)) in Figure 2(b), and we have
107   a consistent observation that the gap is closed with the layer increasing, indicating that the LVLMs
      learn to align the distributions to understand the newly introduced image modality.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Inspired by this, we introduce the Modality Integration Rate (MIR), which measures the pre-training
109   quality of LVLMs from the distribution distance perspective. The MIR enjoys several advantages:
110
111   1) Effective in representing LVLM pre-training quality, exhibiting a strong correlation with post-
112
      SFT benchmark performance. MIR naturally converges during pre-training, offering a useful indi-
      cator to aid in identifying critical points during pre-training, such as performance saturation. For
113
      instance, as shown in Figure 1 and 6, Table 1, it can effectively identify the saturation point when
114
      improving the pre-training data scale or detailedness, making it a reliable tool for early stopping,
115   especially when training on large-scale but homogeneous data.
116
117   2) Robust across diverse types of image-text pairings used in the evaluation. Benefiting from its dis-
118
      tribution perspective design, MIR reflects the distribution distance and be robust toward the specific
      evaluation data sample. We experimentally find MIR is stable regardless of the input type, including
119
      different vision and text contents (e.g., natural or document images, news or mail text), and even
120
      irrelevant image-text pairs. This robustness extends to its stability in handling different conversation
121   templates, and ensuring reliability even in the face of overfitting during training.
122
123   3) Generalized across various training recipes, strategies, and module designs, offering flexibility
124
      in evaluating diverse settings or configurations of LVLM pre-training. Whether adjusting training
      recipes, altering architectural choices, or unlocking specific model modules, MIR helps provide
125
      valuable insights into how these variations impact on the quality of cross-modal alignment. For
126
      example, MIR offers insight into unlocking strategies in LVLM pre-training, particularly highlight-
127   ing that unlocking both the projector and language model simultaneously can boost the cross-modal
128   alignment and prevent early saturation when scaling the pre-training data.
129
130
      Ultimately, MIR proves to be a versatile and consistent metric, facilitating broader LVLM pre-
      training optimizations. Based on the design of MIR, we further propose MoCa, a lightweight and
131
      learnable calibration module for each layer’s vision tokens, designed to enhance their alignment
132
      with text tokens. MoCa achieves the obviously low MIRs on both LLaVA-v1.5 and Mini-Gemini,
133   with +1.5% and +0.9% average gains on post-SFT benchmark performance.

      its basic properties including input-agnostic, training convergence, and robustness to overfitting.

142
      Problem Definition. We aim to establish an effective metric for quantifying the cross-modal align-
143   ment quality of LVLM pretraining. This metric should accurately reflect the impact of various
144   pretraining configurations (such as data, strategies, recipes, and architectural choices) on model per-
145   formance, without requiring subsequent supervised fine-tuning evaluation on benchmarks. Also, it
146   should be applicable across different LVLM architectures.
147

      Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al. (2017)) compute the domain divergence between
163   these token representations.
164
165   Text-Centric Normalization. FID is sensitive to the absolute values of features, which is problem-
166
      atic for directly applying FID to evaluate modality gaps across layers, since deeper layers tend to
      exhibit larger token magnitudes (as indicated by increasing ℓ2 -norm values) in LVLMs.
167
168   A naive solution would be to normalize all tokens to a common scale and compute the distance like
169   cosine similarity. However, it overlooks the significant absolute value disparity between vision and
170   text tokens, which is particularly obvious in the shallower layers. While RMSNorm within each
171   transformer block significantly reduces this disparity, skip connections partially retain its influence,
172
      altering the direction of token representations. To address this, we adopt a text-centric normalization
      that preserves the absolute value differences between vision and text tokens, while neutralizing
173
      the effect and enabling cross-layer FID comparison reasonable. Specifically, we first perform ℓ2
174
      normalization for text tokens of each layer, deriving a scaling factor α as (Ablation is in appendix):
175
176                                                                                                                                        \alpha _k = \frac {s}{\sum _{j=1}^s ||f_{k,j}^t||_2}.                                                                                                                                        (2)

      By multiplying text tokens with the scaling factor αk , the average ℓ2 -norm of the text tokens is
179
      normalized to 1. Thereby, we equally scale both vision and text tokens with the factor αk , to maintain
180
      the absolute value differences between vision and text tokens and facilitate more accurate cross-
181   layer comparisons. During implementation, we observe occasional outliers in both vision and text
182   tokens, characterized by unusually high ℓ2 -norm values. To address this, we apply an outlier removal
183   function ω(·) based on “3σ” principle, focusing on the majority of token representations.

205   2.2         I NVARIANCE TO D IVERSE I NPUTS
206
207   As a metric that requires some image-text pair data as inputs, MIR is generally input-agnostic for
208   different types of inputs, no matter what types of the images or question-answer pairs, what conver-
209   sation templates are used, whether the model has seen the data during pretraining, and whether the
210   text is relevant with the image. This property is quite necessary for the practical application of MIR
211   since we can easily or randomly choose some samples for validation to compute the MIR without
212
      any bias from data types or sources. This property also proves the proposed MIR exactly reflects
      the domain gap between the vision and text representations for a particular model, i.e., it should be
213
      specific to a particular model but not sensitive to different types of image-text inputs.
214
215   Here we present several kinds of scenarios to show MIR’s invariance to diverse inputs, with the
      pretrained model in LLaVA-v1.5 7B:

      correlate with a significant improvement in model performance, indicating that the loss metric may
312   not accurately reflect the pre-training quality at this stage.

314   To explore the robustness of MIR in face of such model overfitting, we conduct the evaluation on
315
      the stronger baseline ShareGPT4V, which curates around 1.2M detailed image captions for pre-
      training and unlocks both latter half vision encoder and the whole LLM for better comprehending
316
      the detailed semantics. Two epochs of pre-training are performed, and both MIR, training loss,
317
      and post-SFT model performance (averaged across 7 benchmarks) are recorded at each step. From
318   Figure 5, it is evident that while the training loss drops significantly at the start of the second epoch,
319   the model performance remains stable after the convergence reached during the first epoch. This lack
320   of correlation highlights how training loss fails to serve as a reliable indicator of performance during
321   overfitting scenarios. In contrast, MIR more closely aligns with model performance, maintaining
322   convergence from the end of the first epoch without drastic fluctuations. This result indicates the
323   robustness of MIR in face of the model overfitting, exhibiting a more reliable indicator than training
      loss for monitoring the training states of LVLMs.

464
465
      Vision-language connector. The architectural design of vision-language connector in LVLMs is
      critical, since it plays a role in projecting vision features into the language space and narrowing the
466
      modality gap. Previous LVLMs typically adopt two kinds of classical visual-language connectors,
467
      i.e., MLP and Q-Former (Li et al. (2023b)). The MLP utilizes several linear layers to map visual
468   tokens into the text space, while the Q-Former leverages cross-attention to absorb instruction-aware
469   information from the visual tokens. Here we leverage the proposed MIR to quantify the effectiveness
470   of different types of the vision-language connector on LVLM training. Following the LLaVA-v1.5
471   setup, we adopt CLIP-ViT-L/336 (Radford et al. (2021)) as the vision encoder and Vicuna v1.5 as the
472   LLM by default. Using the pre-training and SFT data of LLaVA-v1.5, we first pre-train each type of
473   the vision-language connector with the learning rate of 1e-3, keeping the vision encoder and LLM
474   frozen. Afterward, we unlock the LLM to allow joint training with the vision-language connector
475   during instruction tuning. For a fair comparison between MLP and Q-Former, we initialize Q-
476
      Former using a BERT-Base checkpoint, without employing any additional warm-up stages to pre-
      align Q-Former with the vision encoder like BLIP-2.
477

479   Former. The 2-layer MLP projector proves to be the optimal choice, achieving the lowest MIR and
480   the highest post-SFT performance. The lower MIR score suggests that MLP facilitates better cross-
481   modal alignment than the Q-Former, allowing it to more effectively comprehend visual information.

      tors indicates that MIR is a reliable metric for selecting optimal module designs in LVLM training

      without relying on SFT.

509   unsuited for promoting cross-modal alignment. One such design is the use of identical normalization
510
      for both vision and text tokens at each LLM layer. Since the normalization is pre-trained on language

513   LLM normalization modules.
514
515   To this end, we propose MoCa, a simple yet effective calibration method specially designed for
516
      vision tokens, to help LVLMs automatically adjust the distribution of vision tokens to align more
      closely with the distribution of text tokens. Specifically, given vision tokens fkv and text tokens fkt
517
      in the hidden states of k th LVLM layer output, we apply a learnable scaling vector u ∈ R1×d to fkv
518
      and add it with a learnable shifting vector v ∈ R1×d before passing it to the next layer, i.e.,
519

522   LLM layer. The vector u, v are initialized as an all-ones vector and an all-zeros vector respectively.
523

524

525
      supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stages. From Table 5, MoCa achieves significantly lower MIR scores
526   on both models, with average post-SFT performance gains of 1.5% for LLaVA-v1.5 and 0.9% for
527   Mini-Gemini. It provides the significant gains on MMStar, demonstrating the learnable vectors
528   effectively bring vision features closer to the distribution of text tokens, ultimately enhancing the
529   model’s ability to better comprehend and process visual inputs.

      4   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper introduces Modality Integration Rate (MIR), a novel and effective metric for evaluating
534   cross-modal alignment during the pre-training of LVLMs. By capturing domain differences between
535   vision and language features across all layers of the language model, MIR provides an effective
536   and reliable measure of pre-training quality compared to traditional metrics like loss or perplexity.
537   It demonstrates its good robustness, generalization ability, and the strong correlation with post-
538   SFT performance, offering valuable insights for optimizing architecture designs and training setup.
539   Complementing MIR, we propose MoCa, a lightweight, learnable calibration module that enhances
      the alignment of vision and text tokens, ultimately driving better multi-modal comprehension.

      A.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE F OUNDATION M ODEL .
759
760
      Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have emerged as a significant advancement in nowadays’ multi-
761
      modal learning, capable of understanding and generating human-like responses based on visual and
762   textual inputs. Early models like CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training) (Radford et al.
763   (2021)) marks a pivotal moment by aligning images and text in a shared embedding space, enabling
764   the strong cross-modal understanding. Following CLIP, models like BLIP (Bootstrapping Language-
765   Image Pre-training) (Li et al. (2022; 2023b)) extends this foundation, enhancing the fusion of vision
766   and language modalities by leveraging more complex pre-training objectives. As the capabilities of
767   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Zhao et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023)) progressed, their inte-
768   gration with vision models gave rise to more powerful instruction-following Large Vision-Language
769   Models (LVLMs) (Liu et al. (2024c;a;b); Zhu et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023); Zhang
770
      et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024c); Qiao et al. (2024)). Early models such as Flamingo (Alayrac et al.
      (2022)) and PaLM-E (Driess et al. (2023)), and more recent ones like LLaVA (Liu et al. (2024c))
771
      and Qwen-VL (Bai et al. (2023)), exemplify this trend.
772
773   Most LVLMs share three essential components: the vision encoder, the vision-language connec-
774   tor, and the language decoder. The vision encoder is responsible for extracting precise features
775   from images, capturing both detailed and abstract visual information. Popular choices include CLIP
776
      (Radford et al. (2021)), OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al. (2021)), EVA-CLIP (Sun et al. (2023)), SigLIP
      (Zhai et al. (2023)) and DINO series (Oquab et al. (2023)), which are designed to provide both
777
      coarse-grained and fine-grained visual guidance. The vision-language connector plays a critical role
778
      in mapping the encoded visual features into a format that can be interpreted by the language model.
779   Common designs include simple MLP projectors and the Q-Former used in BLIP-2, while more
780   advanced solutions, such as the vision abstractor in mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al. (2023)) and QLLaMA in
781   Intern-VL (Chen et al. (2024e)), push the boundaries of cross-modal alignment. The language de-
782   coder is typically a pre-trained LLM designed to handle large-scale language data, ensuring that the
783   model has robust instruction-following and conversational abilities. However, the central challenge
784   in building a strong LVLM lies in bridging the modality gap between vision and language. The goal
785   is to ensure that the language decoder can process visual tokens as naturally as it does language to-
786   kens, enabling smooth and meaningful conversations with multi-modal inputs. This crucial process
787
      is typically addressed during the pre-training stage of LVLM development. In this paper, we focus
      on evaluating and improving cross-modal alignment during the pre-training of LVLMs, a critical
788
      step in enhancing their overall performance and ensuring seamless interaction between visual and
789
      textual modalities.
790
791
792   A.2   C ROSS -M ODAL A LIGNMENT IN LVLM S .
793
794   Cross-modal alignment plays a pivotal role in building a strong LVLM that can well support users
795
      to input images/videos and the model can understand the multi-modal contents. For the connector
      module of cross-modal alignment, there are typically three types widely used in current LVLMs:
796
      1) Flamingo-style (Alayrac et al. (2022)). The perceiver resampler projects the vision features into
797
      the fixed number of vision tokens, and the language decoder captures the vision information by
798   introducing cross-attention in Gated XATTN-DENSE layer. 2) BLIP-2-style (Li et al. (2023b)).
799   A Q-Former to extract the instruction-aware information from vision tokens through cross-attention
800   and pass the extracted tokens to the language decoder. 3) LLaVA-style (Liu et al. (2024c)). A simple
801   MLP projector directly map the vision tokens into the text embedding space.
802
      Current Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) typically undergo a pre-training stage specifi-
803
      cally designed for cross-modal alignment. As a result, the quality of the pre-training data and the
804   strategies employed are critical for enhancing this alignment. Early datasets, such as COCO (Lin
805   et al. (2014)), Flickr30k (Plummer et al. (2015)), and LAION-400M (Schuhmann et al. (2021)),
806   focus on short captions describing visual content. More recent datasets like ShareGPT4V (Chen
807   et al. (2023)) and ALLaVA (Chen et al. (2024a)) feature longer captions, aiming to provide richer
808   descriptions to encourage the model to fully utilize the dense information of vision tokens. Be-
809   sides, some works have shown that incorporating grounding information (Peng et al. (2023)) or
      dense priors Krishna et al. (2017) in the captions further enhances LVLMs’ ability to comprehend

810
      visual inputs. High-quality data plays a key role in improving the cross-modal alignment in LVLMs,
811   driving advancements in multi-modal understanding.
