011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Inference of latent human mental processes, such as belief, intention, or desire,
014            is crucial for developing AI with human-like intelligence, enabling more effec-
015            tive and timely collaboration. In this paper, we introduce a versatile encoder-
016            decoder model designed to infer evolving mental processes based on irregularly
017            observed action events and predict future occurrences. The primary challenges
018            arise from two factors: both actions and mental processes are irregular events,
019            and the observed action data is often limited. To address the irregularity of these
020
               events, we leverage a temporal point process model within the encoder-decoder
               framework, effectively capturing the dynamics of both action and mental events.
021
               Additionally, we implement a backtracking mechanism in the decoder to enhance
022
               the accuracy of predicting future actions and evolving mental states. To tackle
023            the issue of limited data, our model incorporates logic rules as priors, enabling
024            accurate inferences from just a few observed samples. These logic rules can be
025            refined and updated as needed, providing flexibility to the model. Overall, our
026            approach enhances the understanding of human behavior by predicting when ac-
027            tions will occur and how mental processes evolve. Experiments on both synthetic
028            and real-world datasets demonstrate the strong performance of our model in infer-
029            ring mental states and predicting future actions, contributing to the development
030            of more human-centric AI systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      In the rapidly advancing field of artificial intelligence, there is a growing interest in the development
036   of autonomous agents capable of collaborating with humans across various tasks (Carroll et al.,
037   2019; Puig et al., 2020; Strouse et al., 2021). Effective collaboration relies on these agents’ capacity
038   to understand human actions and accurately infer underlying intentions, enabling timely and
039   appropriate assistance.
040
      Human daily activities consist of intricate sequences of actions, where the successful execution of
041
      complex tasks is contingent upon the precise ordering of these actions, driven by specific intentions.
042   For instance (as illustrated in Fig. 1), when preparing oatmeal, an individual typically follows a
043   discernible sequence of actions based on intentions: entering the kitchen, retrieving a cup, taking
044   oatmeal, pouring it, and subsequently cleaning the table (Damen et al., 2018). This example un-
045   derscores how intentions can guide actions, illustrating the need for AI systems to comprehend the
046   underlying intentions of human behavior.
047
      Although prior research has focused on forecasting future actions based on observed se-
048   quences (Abu Farha et al., 2018; Cramer et al., 2021; Darvish et al., 2020; Furnari & Farinella,
049   2020), the inherent irregularity of these actions poses significant challenges. The time intervals
050   between actions convey crucial information regarding the underlying intentions and dynamics of
051   human behavior; neglecting these intervals may result in AI systems overlooking vital contextual
052   cues. Furthermore, differing intentions can lead to deviations in subsequent actions, complicating
053   AI agents’ ability to fully comprehend human behavior and accurately predict future actions (Hu &
      Clune, 2023; Roy & Fernando, 2022; Zolotas & Demiris, 2022).

054
      It is noteworthy that while human behaviors may appear complex, the underlying logic governing
055   these actions and mental states is often straightforward, clear, and generalizable (Northrop,
056   1947). Logic rules serve as compact representations of knowledge that delineate likely actions
057   based on specific conditions. By incorporating intuitive logic rules as prior knowledge, AI agents
058   can significantly enhance their capacity to infer human mental states, predict forthcoming actions
059   and tackle with limited data challenges.
060
      In this paper, we propose a novel model based on a well-structured encoder-decoder architecture
061   designed to infer unobserved human intentions at fine-grained time resolutions from continuously
062   observed irregular action events. To tackle the challenges of irregularity, our encoder utilizes a self-
063   attention mechanism to map these irregular actions and their timestamps onto a discretized timeline.
064   The latent mental state is modeled as a discrete-time renewal process, with the encoder using action
065   sequence embeddings to estimate occurrence probabilities for each grid. This inference process
066   dynamically integrates historical and future information, facilitating a comprehensive representation
067   of hidden mental state evolution.
068   Upon obtaining the inferred mental states, we employ a rule-informed decoder to generate actions
069   using the temporal point process (TPP) models. The logic rules can be predefined or refined
070   through our model, which incorporates a rule generator utilizing an efficient column generation
071   algorithm (Barnhart et al., 1998; Li et al., 2021) to uncover latent rules. This generator mitigates
072   inaccuracies associated with hand-crafted knowledge and facilitates the exploration of potentially
073   overlooked rules. The decoder utilizes the mined rules, along with observed actions and sampled
074   mental events, to derive conditional intensity functions that enable action generation.
075   The overall learning objective function is grounded in the variational lower bound, with the encoder-
076   decoder and rule generator trained jointly. To enhance the accuracy of real-time action predictions,
077   we introduce a novel backtracking action sampling mechanism. This mechanism iteratively refines
078   predictions by revisiting and adjusting previously inferred mental states in response to newly ob-
079   served actions and contextual information, thereby increasing the model’s adaptability to real-time
080   fluctuations in human thoughts and behaviors.
081   Contributions: The contributions of this work are three-folds: i) We introduce a well-designed
082   encoder-decoder architecture that infers unobserved human intentions in fine-grained time resolu-
083   tions, effectively addressing irregular action events. ii) The model employs a rule generator that
084   uncovers latent rules, enhancing adaptability and accuracy by integrating both predefined and re-
085   fined logic rules, tackling with limited data. iii) A novel backtracking action sampling mechanism
086   iteratively refines predictions, improving responsiveness to real-time fluctuations in human behavior.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK

090   Neural Temporal Point Process (TPP) TPP provides an elegant model for irregular events in
091
      continuous time, which are characterized by the event intensity function. Over the past decades,
      researchers have focused on enhancing the flexibility of this intensity function. Some approaches
092
      utilize recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, such as
093
      (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Xiao et al., 2017; Omi et al., 2019; Shchur et al., 2019; Mei
094   et al., 2020; Boyd et al., 2020). Others leverage Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017),
095   including (Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Enguehard et al., 2020; Sharma et al., 2021; Zhu
096   et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021). Recently, Xue et al. (2022) combined the base TPP with an energy
097   function to overcome the cascading errors of auto-regressive models in making predictions, which
098   is also what our model aims to avoid. The work by Lüdke et al. (2023) employed a probabilistic
099   denoising diffusion model for TPP, addressing similar issues. In our framework, we leverage TPP
100   to model the interleaved observed action events and latent mental events, aiding the design of the
101   encoder and decoder.
102   Enhance Interpretability via Logic Rules Logic rules effectively represent domain knowledge
103   and hypotheses, offering explanations for real-world event data. Previous studies have utilized pre-
104   defined logic rules as prior knowledge to enhance model performance. For example, (Liu et al.,
105   2023) formalized traffic laws using linear temporal logic derived from government regulations and
106   expert insights. Similarly, Li et al. (2020) incorporated first-order logic rules, summarized by human
107   experts, to model event dynamics via intensity functions. Other works, such as (Zhang et al., 2021),
      has also employed temporal logic encoded prior knowledge. Recently, some research has focused on

      learning rules from data. For instance, Li et al. (2021) introduced a temporal rule learning algorithm
109   based on column generation, while Yan et al. (2023) developed a differentiable neuro-symbolic
110   framework for modeling TPPs by learning weighted clock logic formulas. Cao et al. (2024) presents
111   a model for learning spatial-temporal logic rules to explain human actions that are closely related to
112   ours; however, it does not infer fine-grained latent mental events in real time. Our model strikes a
113   balance by leveraging rules as prior knowledge while maintaining the ability to refine these rules.
114   Latent Variable Inference Latent variable inference presents a fundamental challenge. The
115   Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977) provides a robust iterative
116   method for learning latent variable models, particularly when posterior distributions are tractable.
117   In the context of human intentions as latent variables, Wei et al. (2017) developed an EM-based ap-
118   proach for their inference. Additionally, spectral algorithms (Hsu et al., 2012; Kulesza et al., 2014)
119   are recognized for their computational efficiency and provable guarantees, enhancing the feasibility
120   of inference tasks. Variational inference methods (Kingma & Welling, 2013) and wake-sleep algo-
121   rithms (Bornschein & Bengio, 2014) are commonly employed to approximate intractable posterior
122
      distributions. The Variational Autoencoder (VAE) framework (Kingma & Welling, 2013) encodes
      input data into latent variables, subsequently reconstructing the data from these latent representa-
123
      tions. Notably, Mehrasa et al. (2019) leveraged the VAE architecture to synthesize human trajecto-
124
      ries, albeit without addressing the interpretability of the latent variables. In a similar vein, Zolotas &
125   Demiris (2022) explored VAE-based models for inferring human intentions but were constrained to
126   discrete intent variables. Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) have also been employed for modeling
127   sequential data and inferring hidden states. For instance, Foti et al. (2014) proposed a stochastic
128   variational inference algorithm for estimating HMM parameters, while Jeong et al. (2021) inte-
129   grated HMMs with VAEs to infer human activity sequences. In contrast, our model aim to infer the
130   evolving latent mental processes in continuous time when the observed action events are irregular.

      3      P RELIMINARIES
144   We assume both actions and mental processes are temporal point processes (TPPs). To address
145   the sporadic nature of mental processes and facilitate subsequent inference, we represent them as
146   discrete-time renewal processes (DT-RPs). It enables us to approximate the intensity function of the
147   latent mental process by stitching together conditional hazard functions. Furthermore, the discrete
148   conditional hazard rate, can be computed using instantaneous event probability and survival rate
149   functions. As the discrete interval approaches infinitesimal, it approximates the continuous condi-
150
      tional hazard rate function, thus approximating the continuous conditional intensity function of the
      mental processes. Building on this foundation, we will infer latent mental states in discrete time
151
      using a DT-RP encoder, while action events will be generated by the decoder in continuous time,
152
      modeled by TPPs.

      With reliable expert knowledge and well-derived logic rules, we can steer the reconstruction process.
303

304
      difficult, we need a more flexible approach to acquire prior knowledge. Therefore, we introduce
305   a plug-in rule generator that utilizes predefined logic templates to generate temporal logic rules,
306   employing the column generation algorithm (Barnhart et al., 1998; Dash et al., 2018; Wei et al.,
307   2019). In this framework, the rule generating problem is solved via two alternating procedures: the
308   master-problem and the sub-problem (Li et al., 2021), where the master-problem aims to reweighting
309   current rules, and the sub-problem is to search and construct a new temporal logic rule based on the
310   given template, or generate rules from scratch (Please refer to Appendix. B for further details).
311   In practice, we can employ two complementary strategies tailored to different problem settings:
312   autonomous rule learning for data-rich domains and template-guided learning for scenarios with
313
      limited data but abundant prior knowledge. Every time we obtain the inferred latent mental events,
      the rule generator is trained on this data together with observed action events until stable logic rules
314
      emerge. These rules then guide the decoder’s reconstruction process. Upon completion, rule bases
315
      and weights will update again synchronously with other model parameters.

356   4.4    BACKTRACKING M ECHANISM
357
358
      In real-life scenarios, human behavior evolves with changing thoughts. These shifts in thinking are
      common and noteworthy, as humans often reflect on past thoughts before reaching final decisions.
359
      Based on this human cognitive and behavior logic, we need to prevent the omission of the crucial yet
360
      sparse mental events. Inspired by (Upadhyay et al., 2018), we propose a backtracking mechanism,
361   which is detailed illustrated in Appendix. A, Alg. 1. If any mental event occurred during the time
362   period between last action and the newly next action, first mental event within that time range should
363   be considered, and the action will be re-generated accordingly, until no new mental event being
364   adopted. By introducing this backtracking mechanism, our algorithm can consistently generate
365   actions with relatively accurate samples of latent mental events. After the model is well-trained, this
366   backtracking mechanism also plays a significant role in predicting future events.

      6      C ONCLUSION
536   We propose a novel model for asynchronous action sequence modeling and inferring latent mental
537   events utilizing a flexible encoder-decoder architecture that incorporates predefined temporal logic
538   rule templates as prior knowledge and introduces a rule generator to refine them. The introduc-
539   tion of the backtracking mechanism improved the stability of the model’s predictions. Our method
      demonstrates promising results in both synthetic and real-world datasets.

996
997
       In this paper, we primarily focus on baselines from three different fields: neural Temporal Point
       Process model, Logic-Based model, and generative model. Below, we will provide a detailed intro-
998
       duction to these baselines.
999
1000
             • Neural Temporal Point Process Model
1001
1002              – RMTPP (Du et al., 2016): The approach considers the intensity function of a temporal
1003
                    point process as a nonlinear function that depends on the history. It utilizes a recurrent
                    neural network to automatically learn a representation of the influences from the event
1004
                    history, which includes past events and time intervals, thereby fitting the intensity
1005
                    function of the temporal point process.
1006
                  – THP (Zuo et al., 2020): The model employs a concurrent self-attention module to
1007
                    embed historical events and generate hidden representations for discrete time stamps.
1008
                    These hidden representations are then used to model the interpolated continuous time
1009                intensity function. THP can also incorporate additional structural knowledge. Im-
1010                portantly, THP surpasses RNN-based approaches in terms of computational efficiency
1011                and the ability to capture long-term dependencies.
1012              – PromptTPP (Xue et al., 2023): The model incorporates a continuous-time retrieval
1013                prompt pool into the base TPP, enabling sequential learning of event streams with-
1014                out the need for buffering past examples or task-specific attributes. Specifically, this
1015                approach consists of a base TPP model, a pool of continuous-time retrieval prompts,
1016                and a prompt-event interaction layer. By addressing the challenges associated with
1017                modeling streaming event sequences, this mode enhances the model’s performance.
1018              – HYPRO (Xue et al., 2022): The hybridly normalized probabilistic (HYPRO) model is
1019                capable of making long-horizon predictions for event sequences. This model consists
1020                of two modules: the first module is an auto-regressive base TPP model that gener-
1021                ates prediction proposals, while the second module is an energy function that assigns
1022
                    weights to the proposals, prioritizing more realistic predictions with higher probabil-
                    ities. This design effectively mitigates the cascading errors commonly experienced
1023
                    by auto-regressive TPP models in prediction tasks, thereby improving the model’s
1024
                    accuracy in long-term forecasting.

        K            L IMITATION AND B ROADER I MPACTS
1362
1363
       Our work has vast potential applications in the field of human-robot collaboration. Our approach
1364   enables timely and accurate inference of human mental events, as well as precise prediction of
1365   future human behavior. This will assist robots in providing timely, accurate, and useful assistance.
1366   For instance, it can aid elderly individuals with limited mobility in managing daily activities or help
1367   self-driving vehicles navigate roads more safely and smoothly.
1368
       One limitation of our current approach is the reliance on hand-crafted logic rule templates in the
1369
       decoder. While these rules provide interpretability, they may introduce biases and limit the model’s
1370   flexibility. To mitigate this limitation, we could explore techniques for automatically learning rules
1371   from data without prior knowledge logic rule templates. By leveraging advanced machine learning
1372   algorithms, such as reinforcement learning or differentiable logic programming, we can train the
1373   model to discover and refine rules directly from the observed data. This approach would enhance
1374   the model’s flexibility by adapting to the nuances of the data and reduce the risk associated with
1375   manually introduced biases.
1376   Additionally, the discretization of the timeline might introduce some noise when sampling the latent
1377   mental events. In real-world scenarios, establishing a well-defined and fine-grained discrete time
1378   grid can necessitate conducting numerous experiments. It’s worth noting that choosing the interval
1379   for discretization is a tunable hyperparameter. We can explore methods to automate hyperparameter
1380   tuning to streamline this process and ensure optimal performance without the need for extensive
1381   manual experimentation.

1383    L            I LLUSTRATION OF ATTENTION W EIGHT FOR O BSERVED ACTIONS
1384
1385   In our proposed model, the temporal point processes involve triggering between latent mental and
1386   action events, where historical actions can influence latent events and vice versa. Therefore, we
1387   resort to the attention mechanism to map the information of the entire action sequence on the span-
1388   ning time horizon on each discrete time grid. But the attention in our model cannot capture how the
1389   mental state influences actions. The influence of mental process on action process is reflected on the
1390   intensity function after the inference of latent mental process.
1391   In Fig.9, we provided an example of attention weights for action sequence of Syn Data-1 on different
1392   discrete time grids, which visualizes attention patterns of different attention heads. Pixel (i, ξ) in
1393   each figure signifies the attention weight of the event (tai , kia ) attending to the discrete time grid
1394   tξ . We can see that each attention head employs a different pattern to capture dependencies. For
1395   each attention head, the impact of one event is different on each discrete time grid, reflected by
1396   various attention weights. The impact of the entire action sequence on a discrete time grid can
1397   be conceptualized as a weighted combination of the entire action sequence, with weights derived
1398
       through attention mechanisms. The attention mechanism effectively captures the potential influence
       of historical events at specific time points on the intensity of future events.
