009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit neural representation and explicit 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) for novel
012            view synthesis have achieved remarkable progress with frame-based camera (e.g.
013            RGB and RGB-D cameras) recently. Compared to frame-based camera, a novel
014            type of bio-inspired visual sensor, i.e. event camera, has demonstrated advantages
015            in high temporal resolution, high dynamic range, low power consumption and
016            low latency. Due to its unique asynchronous and irregular data capturing process,
017            limited work has been proposed to apply neural representation or 3D Gaussian
018            splatting for an event camera. In this work, we present IncEventGS, an incremental
019
               3D Gaussian Splatting reconstruction algorithm with a single event camera. To
               recover the 3D scene representation incrementally, we exploit the tracking and
020
               mapping paradigm of conventional SLAM pipelines for IncEventGS. Given the
021
               incoming event stream, the tracker firstly estimates an initial camera motion based
022            on prior reconstructed 3D-GS scene representation. The mapper then jointly refines
023            both the 3D scene representation and camera motion based on the previously
024            estimated motion trajectory from the tracker. The experimental results demonstrate
025            that IncEventGS delivers superior performance compared to prior NeRF-based
026            methods and other related baselines, even we do not have the ground-truth camera
027            poses. Furthermore, our method can also deliver better performance compared
028            to state-of-the-art event visual odometry methods in terms of camera motion
029            estimation.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Acquiring accurate 3D scene representations from 2D images has long been a challenging prob-
035   lem in computer vision. Serving as a fundamental component in various applications such as
036   virtual/augmented reality and robotics navigation etc., substantial efforts have been dedicated to
037   addressing this challenge over the last few decades. Among those pioneering works, Neural Radiance
038   Fields (NeRF) Mildenhall et al. (2021) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) Kerbl et al. (2023), stand
039
      out for its utilization of differentiable rendering technique, and have garnered significant attention
      due to its capability to recover high-quality 3D scene representation from 2D images. Commonly
040
      used sensors for 3D scene reconstruction are usually frame-based cameras, such as the RGB and
041
      RGB-D cameras. They usually capture full-brightness intensity images within a short exposure time
042   at a regular frequency. Due to the characteristic of the data capturing process, they often suffer
043   from motion blur or fail to capture accurate and informative intensity information under extreme
044   brightness or darkness in the environment, which would further affect the performance of downstream
045   applications.
046
      The event camera, a bio-inspired sensor, has gained significant attention in recent years for its potential
047
      to address the limitations of frame-based cameras under challenging conditions. Unlike conventional
048   cameras, event cameras record brightness changes asynchronously at each pixel, emitting events
049   when a predefined threshold is surpassed. This unique operation offers several advantages over con-
050   ventional cameras, in terms of high temporal resolution, high dynamic range, low latency and power
051   consumption. Although event cameras have attractive characteristics for challenging environments,
052   they cannot be directly integrated into existing frame-based 3D reconstruction algorithms that rely on
053   processing dense 2D brightness intensity images, due to its time continuous, sparse, asynchronous
      and irregular data capturing characteristics.

054
      Several pioneering works have been proposed to exploit event stream Kim et al. (2016); Rebecq
055   et al. (2017); Gallego et al. (2018) to recover the motion trajectory and scene representation. While
056   existing methods deliver impressive performance, they usually exploit 2.5D semi-dense depth maps
057   to represent the 3D scene, and bundle adjustment (BA) is hardly being performed, due to the
058   asynchronous and sparse characteristics of event data stream. Klenk et al. (2024) recently proposes
059   to convert event stream into event voxel grids, and then adapt a previous frame-based deep visual
060   odometry pipeline Teed et al. (2023) for accurate camera motion estimation. As neural radiance fields
061   (NeRF) exhibits impressive scene representation capability recently, Klenk et al. (2022), Hwang et al.
062   (2023), Rudnev et al. (2023a), Low & Lee (2023) and Low & Lee (2023) explore to recover the
063
      underlying dense 3D scene NeRF representation from event stream, by assuming ground truth poses
      are available.
064
065   In contrast to those works, we propose IncEventGS, an incremental dense 3D scene reconstruction
066   algorithm from a single event camera, by exploiting Gaussian Splatting as the underlying scene
067   representation. Different from prior event-based NeRF reconstruction methods, IncEventGS does not
068   require any ground truth camera poses, which is more challenging and provides more flexibility for
069
      real-world application scenarios. To overcome the challenges brought by unknown poses, IncEventGS
      adopts the tracking and mapping paradigm of conventional SLAM pipelines Mur-Artal & Tardós
070
      (2017). In particular, IncEventGS exploits prior explored and reconstructed 3D scene for camera
071
      motion estimation of incoming event stream during the tracking stage. Both the 3D-GS scene
072   representation and camera motions are then jointly optimized (i.e. event-based bundle adjustment)
073   during the mapping stage, for more accurate scene representation and motion estimation. The 3D
074   scene is progressively expanded and densified. Both synthetic and real datasets are used to evaluate our
075   method. The experimental results demonstrate that IncEventGS is able to recover the underlying 3D
076   scene representation and camera motion trajectory accurately. In particular, IncEventGS outperforms
077   prior NeRF-based methods and other related baselines in terms of scene representation recovery,
078   even IncEventGS does not have the ground-truth poses. Furthermore, our method also delivers better
079   camera motion estimation accuracy than a most recent state-of-the-art visual odometry algorithm, in
080
      terms of both the Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE) metric. The recovered 3D scene representation
      can be further used to render novel brightness images. Our main contributions can be summarized as
081
      follows:
082
083          • We present an incremental 3D Gaussian Splatting reconstruction algorithm from a single
084            event camera, without requiring the ground truth camera poses;
085
             • The experimental results on both the synthetic and real datasets demonstrate superior
086
               performance of our method over prior NeRF based methods and related baselines in terms
087            of novel view synthesis, and better performance over state-of-the-art event-based visual
088            odometry algorithm in terms of camera motion estimation;
089
             • Compared to prior methods, we are able to efficiently render high-quality brightness images
090            thanks to the powerful representation capability of 3D Gaussian Splatting.

093   2    R ELATED W ORKS
094
095   We review two main areas of prior works: event-based neural radiance fields and 3D Gaussian
096   Splatting, which are the most related to our work.
097
098   Event-based neural radiance fields. Prior works from Klenk et al. (2022), Hwang et al. (2023)
099
      and Rudnev et al. (2023a) propose to exploit event stream to recover the neural radiance fields with
      known camera motion trajectory. Low & Lee (2023) further improves the reconstruction algorithm to
100
      handle the situation with sparse and noisy events under non-uniform motion. The recovered neural
101
      radiance fields can then be used to render novel view brightness images. The ground truth poses are
102   usually computed from corresponding brightness images via COLMAP Schonberger & Frahm (2016)
103   or provided by indoor motion capturing system. Recently, Qu et al. (2024) proposed to integrate event
104   measurements into an RGB-D implicit neural SLAM framework and achieve robust performance
105   under the situation with motion blur. Li et al. (2024) also propose to exploit event measurements and
106   a single blurry image to recover the underlying neural 3D scene representation. In contrast to those
107   works, IncEventGS conduct incremental 3D scene reconstruction without requiring any prior ground
      truth poses, which is more challenging and provides more flexibility for practical application scenarios.

108
      The method further exploits 3D Gaussian Splatting as the underlying scene representation, which
109   demonstrates better image rendering quality and efficiency, compared to NeRF-based representation.
110
111   3D Gaussian Splatting. With the recent success of NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) and its further
112   developments Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022); Müller et al. (2022), novel view synthesis utilizing 3D
113   representations like MLPs, voxel grids, or hash tables has advanced significantly. While these
114   NeRF-inspired models perform admirably, they frequently require long training and rendering times
115
      for individual scenes. The introduction of 3D Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023) proposes a novel
      explicit 3D representation to further improve both the training and rendering efficiency. Due to its
116
      impressive efficient scene representation capability, several pioneering work have been proposed to
117
      exploit 3D-GS for incremental 3D reconstruction. For example, Keetha et al. propose an RGBD-based
118   3D-GS SLAM Keetha et al. (2024), employing an online tracking and mapping system tailored to the
119   underlying Gaussian representation. Yan et al. implement a coarse-to-fine camera tracking approach
120   based on the sparse selection of Gaussians Yan et al. (2024). Matsuki et al. propose to apply 3D
121   Gaussian Splatting to do incremental 3D reconstruction using a single moving monocular or RGB-D
122   camera Matsuki et al. (2024). Huang et al. exploits ORB-SLAM3 to compute accurate camera poses
123   and feeds it into a 3D-GS algorithm for dense mapping Huang et al. (2024). Fu et al. uses monocular
124   depth estimation with 3D-GS Fu et al. (2023). Yugay et al. combine DROID-SLAM Teed & Deng
125   (2021) based camera tracking with active and inactive 3D-GS sub-maps Yugay et al. (2023). Hu et
126
      al. propose a novel depth uncertainty model to ensure the selection of valuable Gaussian primitives
      during optimization Hu et al. (2024). While those methods deliver impressive performance in terms
127
      of 3D scene recovery and motion estimation, they usually assume the usage of frame-based images
128
      (i.e. either RGB or RGB-D date). In the contrary, we propose to exploit pure event measurements for
129   incremental 3D-GS reconstruction. Two concurrent work have also tried to exploit 3D Gaussians
130   for event-based reconstruction recently, i.e. EvGGS Wang et al. (2024) and Event3DGS Xiong
131   et al. (2024). However, both of them rely on ground-truth poses for training, and EvGGS further
132   constrained to object reconstruction from 360-degree surrounding views, while ours assumes the
133   camera poses are not available and is more challenging.

      The overview of our IncEventGS is shown in Fig. 1. Given only a single event camera, IncEventGS
138
      incrementally performs tracking and dense mapping under the framework of 3D Gaussian Splatting,
139
      to recover both the camera motion trajectory and 3D scene representation simultaneously. The main
140   insight of IncEventGS is to accumulate incoming event data into chunks and treat each chunk as a
141   special “image”. We associate each chunk with a continuous time trajectory parameterization in the
142   se3 space. Two close consecutive timestamps (i.e., tk and tk+∆t , where ∆t is a small time interval)
143   can be randomly sampled within the chunk and the corresponding event stream can then be integrated
144   into an image E(x). Based on the parameterized trajectory, the corresponding camera poses (i.e.,
145   Tk , Tk+∆t ) can be computed and the images (i.e., Îk , Îk+∆t ) can be further rendered from the
146   3D Gaussian Splatting. The synthesized image Ê(x) can be computed for event loss computation.
147   During tracking, we only optimize for the camera motion trajectory of the newly accumulated event
148   chunk and exploit the recovered trajectory to initialize the dense bundle adjustment (BA) algorithm
149   for the mapping stage. During mapping stage, we continuously densify 3D Gaussians for newly
150   explored areas and prune transparent 3D Gaussians. For computational efficiency consideration, we
151   exploit a sliding window of the latest chunks and perform BA only within this window for both
152
      3D-GS reconstruction and motion trajectory estimation. We will detail each component as follows.
153

221   3.2   E VENT DATA F ORMATION M ODEL
222
223   An event camera records changes of the brightness as a stream of events asynchronously. Every
224
      time a pixel brightness change reaches a contrast threshold (i.e. |L(x, ti + δt) − L(x, ti )| ≥ C),
      the camera will trigger an event ei = (x, ti , pi ), where pi ∈ (−1, +1) is the polarity of the event,
225
      L(x, ti ) = log(I(x, ti )) is the brightness logarithm of pixel x at timestamp ti , C is the fixed contrast
226
      threshold.
227
228   To relate 3D-GS representation with the event stream, we sample two close consecutive timestamps
229   (i.e., tk and tk+∆t , where ∆t is a small time interval), and accumulate the real measured events
230
      within ∆t to an image E(x). The accumulation is defined as:
231                                    E(x) = C{ei (x, ti , pi )}tk <ti <tk +∆t ,                            (7)
232
233
      where e(x, ti , pi ) is the ith event within the defined time interval corresponding to pixel x. The
      corresponding camera poses Tk and Tk+∆t can be interpolated from the camera motion trajectory
234
235
      parameterization, allowing us to render two grayscale images (i.e. Îk and Îk+∆t ) from the previously
236   recovered 3D-GS. The synthesized accumulated event image Ê can then be computed as:
237                                   Ê(x) = log(Îk+∆t (x)) − log(Îk (x)),                                (8)
238
239   where Ê(x) depends on the parameters of both the motion trajectory parameters and 3D-GS, and is
240   differentiable with respect to them.
241   Both in tracking and mapping, inspired by the work of Rudnev et al. (2023b), we segment the current
242   event chunks into nseg equal segments according to the number of events, obtaining nseg timestamps
243   that correspond to the end of each segment. We then randomly select one timestamp from these
244   nseg timestamps to serve as tk+∆t , and we randomly sample an integer nwin between the integer
245   bounds nlow and nup . The index of tk is equal to the index of tk+∆t subtract nwin . nseg , nlow and
246   nup are hyperparameters. This sampling strategy enables the model to capture both local and global
247   information.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS .
317
318   Implementation Details. All experiments were conducted on a desktop PC equipped with a
319   5.73GHz AMD Ryzen 9 7900x CPU and an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. The first m = 3 event chunks
320   were used for initialization. During the mapping stage, a sliding window size of nw = 20 was
321   employed for the bundle adjustment algorithm. The hyperparameters were set as follows: λ = 0.05,
322   λV = 0.8, and nseg = 100. For the synthetic dataset, nlow = 400k and nup = 500k, while for
323   the real dataset, nlow = 60k and nup = 80k. Each event chunk had a time interval of 50 ms. The
      learning rate of the camera poses is set to 1e-4 and that for the attributes of 3D-GS are set the same as


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      the original 3D-GS work. The number of optimization steps for initialization is 4500, and that for
325   tracking and mapping are set to 200 and 1500 respectively. The contrast threshold C of the event
326   camera is set to 0.1 for synthetic datasets and 0.2 for real datasets empirically.
327
328   Baselines and Evaluation Metrics. IncEventGS performs incremental dense 3D-GS reconstruction
329   and motion estimation using only a single event camera. To the best of our knowledge, there are no
330   existing event-only NeRF or 3D-GS SLAM methods that do not rely on ground-truth poses, making
331
      direct comparisons challenging. Therefore, we conduct a thorough comparison of our method with
      several event-based NeRF approaches, including E-NeRF Klenk et al. (2022), EventNeRF Rudnev
332
      et al. (2023a), and Robust e-NeRF Low & Lee (2023), as well as two-stage methods such as E2VID
333
      Rebecq et al. (2019) + COLMAP Schönberger & Frahm (2016) + 3DGS Kerbl et al. (2023), and
334   E2VID Rebecq et al. (2019) + DEVO Klenk et al. (2024) + 3DGS Kerbl et al. (2023). E-NeRF,
335   EventNeRF, and Robust e-NeRF leverage implicit neural radiance fields for 3D scene representation,
336   requiring ground-truth camera poses for accurate NeRF reconstruction. The two-stage methods we
337   examine include E2VID + COLMAP + 3DGS and E2VID + DEVO + 3DGS. In those approaches,
338   event data is first converted into grayscale images using E2VID. The subsequent steps differ: in the
339   E2VID + COLMAP + 3DGS method, camera poses are estimated from these images using COLMAP,
340   while in the E2VID + DEVO + 3DGS method, poses are estimated using DEVO. Finally, 3D-GS is
341   trained with the generated images and poses in both methods. Both the quantitative and qualitative
342
      comparisons are performed on the synthetic dataset. Since there are no paired ground truth images
      for the real dataset, we only perform qualitative comparisons on the real dataset. In terms of motion
343
      trajectory evaluations, we use the publicly available state-of-the-art event-only visual odometry
344
      method, i.e. DEVO Klenk et al. (2024), for comparison, both quantitatively and qualitatively.
345
346   The metrics used for novel view synthesis (NVS) include the commonly employed PSNR, SSIM, and
347   LPIPS. For motion trajectory evaluations, we utilize Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE). To ensure fair
348
      comparisons, we employ the evaluation code provided by EventNeRF to compute the NVS metrics,

477   5               C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We present the first incremental 3D dense reconstruction algorithm, i.e. IncEventGS, with a single
480   event camera under the framework of 3D Gaussian Splatting. We adopt the tracking and mapping
481   paradigm in conventional SLAM pipeline to do incremental motion estimation and 3D scene re-
482   construction simultaneously. To handle the continuous and asynchronous characteristics of event
483   stream, we exploit a continuous trajectory model to model the event data formation process. The
484   experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the superior performance of
485   IncEventGS over prior state-of-the-art methods in terms of high-quality novel image synthesis and
      camera pose estimation.
