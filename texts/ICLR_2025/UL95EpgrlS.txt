011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Vision and Language Models have enabled significant advances in fully
014            supervised and zero-shot visual tasks. These large architectures serve as the base-
015            line to what is currently known as Instruction Tuning Large Vision and Language
016            models (IT-LVLMs). IT-LVLMs are general-purpose multi-modal assistants whose
017            responses are modulated by natural language instructions and visual data. Despite
018
               this versatility, IT-LVLM effectiveness in fundamental computer vision problems re-
               mains unclear, primarily due to the absence of a standardized evaluation benchmark.
019
               This paper introduces a Multi-modal Evaluation Benchmark named MERLIM, a
020
               scalable test-bed to assess the capabilities of IT-LVLMs on fundamental computer
021            vision tasks. MERLIM contains over 300K image-question pairs and has a strong
022            focus on detecting cross-modal “hallucination” events in IT-LVLMs. Our results
023            bring important insights on the performance of state-of-the-art IT-LVLMLs1AUQ in-
024            cluding limitations at identifying fine-grained visual concepts, object hallucinations
025            across tasks, and biases towards the language query. Our findings also suggest that
026            these models have weak visual grounding, but manage to make adequate guesses
027            from global visual patterns or language biases contained in the LLM component.
028            We name this phenomena of correct answers with no visual grounding as hidden
029            hallucinations4cSE

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large-scale transformer networks have significantly advanced the field of Natural Language Pro-
035   cessing (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020). Large
036   Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable zero-shot performance in a wide range of
037   NLP tasks (Lin et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023), and their success has resulted in a fundamental shift
038   for NLP research, moving from task-specific pre-training to task-agnostic representation learning.
039
      Similarly, in the image domain, vision transformers trained on large-scale image collections have
      achieved state-of-the-art performance on multiple fully supervised tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020;
040
      Fan et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2021). The integration of a language stream has also demonstrated
041
      empirical benefits in fundamental computer vision tasks such as recognition (Alayrac et al., 2020; Jia
042   et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021) and detection (Chen et al., 2021; Du et al., 2022). Ultimately, these
043   advances in computer vision and NLP fields have converged in the development of Large Vision and
044   Language models (LVLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a; Nichol et al.,
045   2021; Radford et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022), which have achieved state-of-the-art performance on
046   several downstream visual tasks while enabling significant improvements in the zero-shot setups.
047
      Following the success of these large-scale multi-modal transformers, a new trend has emerged which
048   involves integrating frozen LLMs and vision transformers by means of a smaller neural network (Bai
049   et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023;
050   Peng et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024; Tsimpoukelli et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023).
051   This straightforward modification only requires the training of a fusion module to interface between
052   the frozen modality encoders. The resulting model requires significantly less computational effort to
053   train, and enables querying image data with natural language, thus providing a contextualized natural
      language response through the frozen language decoder (Tsimpoukelli et al., 2021).

072
      Such models are known as Instruction Tuning Large Vision and Language models (IT-LVLM), as
073
      their zero-shot capabilities are mediated by a natural language input that instructs the model about
074
      the expected output (Huang et al., 2023b). Although IT-LVLMs are intended to be general-purpose
075   instructional assistants, there is still no standard test-bed to assess their zero-shot effectiveness in
076   fundamental computer vision tasks. The presence of an LLM introduces additional challenges, among
077   them the inherited tendency to Hallucinate (Huang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023), resulting in the
078   emergence of language outputs that can not be grounded to the visual input.
079
      In this paper we take an in depth look at the capabilities of IT-LVLMs for fundamental computer
080
      vision tasks, while assessing the impact of hallucination events in the model’s performance. Our
081   proposal has two key elements: First, instead of presenting the model with a fixed set of possible
082   answers, we allow the IT-LVLM to reply in natural language and then parse the open-set IT-LVLM
083   prediction. Second, to better analyze the hallucination issue, we propose an inpainting procedure
084   that subtly manipulates both the image and its corresponding ground-truth. This technique enables
085   us to identify two types of hallucinations: (1) Regular Hallucinations, which are outputs that do
086   not correspond to the image, and (2) Hidden Hallucinations, which appear to be true positives but
087   lack visual grounding. We illustrate these two phenomena in Figure 1, where an ostensibly correct
088   prediction, such as ”Tennis Racquet,” lacks visual grounding in the edited image, thereby categorizing
089
      it as a ”hidden hallucination”.
090   We compile our image edits, corresponding ground-truths, and parsing strategy into MERLIM
091   (Multi-modal Evaluation benchmaRk for Large Image-language Models), a novel test-bed aimed at
092   empirically evaluating IT-LVLMs on core computer vision tasks including object recognition, instance
093   counting and identifying object-to-object relationships. Our benchmark serves as a straightforward
094   and extensible evaluation strategy of core computer vision tasks on well-established image datasets
095
      like MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014), LVIS (Gupta et al., 2019), and Visual Genome.
096   Our paper brings the following contributions: (i) A standardized test-bed to assess the zero-shot
097   effectiveness of IT-LVLMs on fundamental computer vision tasks, including Object Recognition,
098   Object Counting, and Inter-object Relationship Understanding. (ii) Through extensive empirical
099   evaluation, we identify and quantify ”hidden” hallucination errors which are out of the scope of other
100   benchmarks, we observe this hallucination unfairly increase the accuracy of IT-LVMLMs. (iii) We
101
      conducted a thorough analysis of language biases through extensive experiments using a diverse set
      of carefully selected question prompts. Our observations reveal that IT-LVLMs exhibit a strong bias
102
      toward the language tokens of both the question and answer. MERLIM’s code is available here

107   The widespread adoption of LLMs and their zero-shot capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; Chiang et al.,
      2023; Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020) has led to an increased interest in the


                                                         2

108
      development of LVLMs (Jia et al., 2021; Pham et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Zhai
109   et al., 2022). LVLMs typically build upon metric learning, estimating a similarity measure across
110   modalities, making these architectures effective in inherently multi-modal tasks such as VQA and
111   text-image retrieval.
112
113   Visual Instruction Tuning The effectiveness of LVLMs, coupled with the unprecedented availabil-
114   ity of large-scale data in the visual domain, has led to the development of IT-LVLMs. Unlike LVLMs,
115
      IT-LVLMs allow to prompt a joint language and visual representation and then generate results with
      a frozen language decoder. This procedure preserves the multi-modal capabilities of the LVLM, and
116
      enables the use of natural language queries as an instruction set that modulates the expected output
117
      (Alayrac et al., 2022; Tsimpoukelli et al., 2021).
118
119   A pioneer work exploring the few-shot learning abilities of frozen language models and vision was
120   presented by Tsimpoukelli et al. (2021) where the image input is encoded into a feature set and
121
      inserted as a prefix in the text stream, thereby conditioning the responses of the language network.
      Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) introduced an auto-regressive text model conditioned on visual input,
122
      by using gated cross-attention. The tokens from the perceiver architecture (Jaegle et al., 2021) are fed
123
      into a frozen language model. This Gated cross-attention was extended by BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a),
124   linking the T5 language model and its visual stream (Raffel et al., 2020) with the Q-Former module,
125   thus improving the modality alignment at training time. Recent work has continued to explore the
126   visual-to-language alignments on IT-LVLMS, either by improving the training and annotation data
127   (Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023), or by introducing large-scale data on the training of the IT-LVLM
128   (Bai et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023).
129
130
      VQA Benchmarks & Datasets Many existing benchmarks and datasets for IT-LVLMs are designed
      for the standard VQA tasks, including a pre-defined set of questions that can be answered by looking
131
      at image or video data (Antol et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2015; Fu et al., 2023; Goyal et al., 2017; Gurari
132
      et al., 2018; Hudson & Manning, 2019; Zhao et al., 2022). Although these benchmarks provide a
133   direct evaluation of the multi-modal capabilities of LVLMs, they do not assess if the reply originated
134   from the global visual context of the image or from the local visual patterns that effectively ground
135   the prompted question. In contrast, MERLIM focuses on querying and attributing the response of
136   the IT-LVLM to a specific set of object instances in the image by means of small image alterations
137   and direct verification of the same query on the modified visual data. Moreover, we explore the
138   connection between these replies (original image and edited image) and the potential hallucinations
139   from the LLMs component, as some responses can only be attributed to the LLM hallucinating
140   objects.
141   Perhaps the most similar benchmarks to ours are POPE (Li et al., 2023b) and NExT-GQA (Xiao
142   et al., 2023), as both these benchmarks aim at finding direct visual groundings related to the language
143   query. While POPE proposes to prompt for the presence/absence of a single instance to evaluate
144   hallucinations (in the form of a false positive), we directly modify the visual ground-truth and can
145   verify if the predictions have an effective visual grounding. NExT-GQA approximates the grounding
146   of a prediction by the accurate temporal localization of the event. Despite the straightforward proposal,
147
      contemporary research (Otani et al., 2020) has shown that temporal action localization commonly
      suffers from biased predictions, in particular, this bias emerges directly from the language stream.

150   3   MERLIM
151
152   MERLIM’s main goal is to assess the effectiveness of Instruction-Tuning Large Visual Language
153   Models (IT-LVLM) in fundamental computer vision tasks, while simultaneously evaluating if the
154
      responses have an effective visual grounding. In practice, we design an evaluation scheme that
      queries the IT-LVLM with a set of predefined language questions. Then, we parse the text responses,
155
      transforming the natural language output into a suitable prediction format for each task. This scheme
156
      enables us to measure the performance of the IT-LVLM using standard image evaluation metrics,
157   such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 metrics. Across all tasks, MERLIM contains a total of
158   300,6641AUQ unique image-question pairs. Table 1 outlines the sizes of the query set and the image
159   data for all the selected tasks.

213   We evaluate the object recognition capabilities of IT-LVLMs on the MS-COCO dataset by adapting
214   its ground-truth for the multi-label classification task. If at least one instance of class c is grounded in
215   image i, that indicates the image contains the class c, and is therefore expected in r. In this task, we
      design a query set for the IT-LVLM requesting a list of all the visible objects in the image.

      Prompt Construction. To further analyze the bias towards a particular language prompt, we
217   formulate 5 similar prompts that instruct the IT-LVLM to list the objects present in the images. Our
218   set P for the object recognition task is detailed in the following listing:
219
220     1. “Itemize the things seen in the image”.       2. “Identify the objects within the image”.
221     3. “Detail the items shown in the picture”.      4. “List the objects that appear in the image”.
222     5. “Enumerate the items visible in the picture”.
223
224   Evaluation. We identify all the nouns in r and compare them against the ground truth labels of
225   MS-COCO. To do this, we split the sentence by words and determine if it is a noun or not using the
226   spaCy library (Honnibal & Montani, 2017). We discard words that refer to places or areas like room,
227   street, square, etc. We consider compound nouns as one, for instance, ”the back of the car” and ”ice
228   cream”. We designate a prediction as a True Positive if the predicted word matches or is a synonym
229   of a ground truth element. Likewise, a prediction that does not match any ground truth label or any of
230
      its synonyms is labeled as a False Positive. Finally, any ground truth class that can not be found in
      the prediction (including their synonyms) is regarded as a False Negative. These definitions enable us
231
      to calculate precision, recall, and F1 metrics for all IT-LVLMs.
232
233   To better manage the extensive vocabulary output of IT-LVLMs, we also utilize the WordNet hierarchy
234   (Miller, 1995), and the API provided by ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023) to identify potential synonyms for
235   elements in r. We pre-compute a corpus of noun-to-noun relations totaling 334608 tuples and stored
236
      them, therefore future changes in ChatGPT model/API will not affect our benchmark predictions. On
      occasions, we discover unsuitable outputs, e.g., “There are many objects” or “It is a beautiful scene”,
237
      the recall and precision for such responses are defined as 0. For completeness, we also report the
238
      amount of such instances in the dTrJ Supplementary Material.

247
      this task, we only formulate questions with a yes or no response. We also verify if the described
248   inter-object relationship has a visual grounding or not. For instance, for the images in Figure 1, we
249   could query the model about the relationship between the tennis racquet and the child: “Does the
250   child have a tennis racquet?”. Since Visual Genome does not provide, questions with this exact
251   structure, we use the ChatGPT API (OpenAI, 2023) to build a natural language question with a
252   Yes/No answer:

      3.3   O BJECT C OUNTING
297
      Finally, we also evaluate the counting capabilities of IT-LVLMs. Although this task is common in
298   VQA benchmarks, even state-of-the-art methods struggle to provide accurate results. In particular, the
299   counting task requires the models to have strong object recognition and instance detection capabilities
300   that enable them to count in a discrete fashion (Trott et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018).
301
302   Prompt Construction. We instruct the model to count the objects in the original and the edited
303   images and require its response to be strictly a number. Unlike other tasks, we share a single prompt
304
      for the entire set 1AUQ “How many object category are there? Just answer the number.”. As a
      second step, we check the consistency of the answers. We formulate yes or no questions based on
305
      previous answers over the very same image. For instance, if the model responds “There are five
306
      books”, on the second step we prompt: “Are there five books?” using the very same image.
307
308   Although the task is very challenging, we focus on the consistency exhibited by the IT-LVLM on the
309   follow-up questions, and the responses obtained from the edited images (which should have exactly
310
      1 less object), even if the actual number of objects does not match the ground-truth, it would be
      expected for the secondary query to be consistent with the initial one. This query scheme helps us
311
      understand if the IT-LVLM leverages the same visual patterns despite being queried in syntactically
312
      different ways.
313
314   Evaluation Scheme. As we instruct the model to deliver a numeric answer, we compute the mean
315   and standard deviation of the absolute error from the original and edited sets. Additionally, we
316   calculate the accuracy metric for secondary questions designed to return Yes/No responses.

      (Accneg ).
417   Performance in the edited images set. Table 2 shows that all tested IT-LVLMs exhibit an average
418   performance drop of 7.49% when transitioning from the original to the edited set. This performance
419   gap, is nearly the same gap in the object recognition task, suggesting that IT-LVLMs may also be
420   hallucinating certain visual relationships due to the global visual context and the inherent biases
421   within the language models. MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) (with Vicuna-7B or 13B) attains the
422   lowest accuracy overall, although it compensates with the smallest variability between the original

      for MiniGPT-4) and a relatively small fusion module (θh ), consisting of a single linear layer. It

448
449   In Figure 4, we report the mean absolute error (MAE) and its standard deviation for all tested
450   IT-LVLMs on both the edited (blue) and original (orange) in MERLIM. All IT-LVLMs exhibit a
451   significant increase in error on the edited set. This indicates that the models’ intrinsic error in the
452   counting task is not correlated with the visual grounding, but rather a lack of visual grounding in their
453   responses, leading to hallucinations.
454
      Answer Consistency. To further investigate language biases, we also compute the accuracy metric
455
      for the yes/no questions, which validate the answers given by the IT-LVLM in the initial prediction
456   (i.e. Are there N objects?). We highlight that all methods still perform better in the original image
457   set, especially the largest IT-LVLMs, like InstructBLIP over Vicuna-13B. However, the drop in
458   performance remains significant in the edited set. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) exhibits the highest
459   consistency, with over 80% of the replies confirming the initial assessment (regardless if the count
460   was accurate or not) for both the edited and original sets. Similarly, the LLaVA IT-LVLM also
461   has the smallest average error and the smallest standard deviation on the initial counting, clearly
462   outperforming every other IT-LVLM in this particular task.

      Limitations. MERLIM comprises a total of 300664 unique image-question pairs across all tasks,
497
      which may limit its usability for evaluating and prototyping new models. Therefore, we recommend
498   using the subset of MERLIM designed for the Object Recognition Task, where image inpainting
499   removes an entire category. This subset, used in Figure 3, contains 5608 edited and 3037 original
500   images.
501
502   Conclusion. This paper introduces MERLIM, a novel multi-modal Benchmark for evaluating
503   IT-LVLMs in fundamental computer vision tasks. Despite the versatility of current IT-LVLMs, we
504
      observe an overall poor performance in these fundamental tasks, with multiple failure scenarios
      associated with weak or non-existent visual grounding of the language predictions. We think
505
      MERLIM can be the initial effort to better diagnose Hallucination events, and to create stronger IT-
506
      LVLMs where ensuring a solid visual grounding could improve the overall quality of the predictions.

922   A.4    G RADIENT A NALYSIS
923
924   To further investigate the visual grounding of IT-LVLMs, we take inspiration from Selvaraju et al.
925   (2017) and compute the proportion of the total gradient attributable to each specific type of input
926   (Image, Question, Answer) for each predicted token. We analyze the model’s output logits before
927   token selection and propagate the gradient to the input by identifying the maximum activation value
928   per output token. Specifically, for each output token, we determine the gradient contribution from
929
      each input type and then average these contributions across all answers. To simplify our analysis,
      we focus on architectures with autoregressive LLMs, such as Vicuna, which explicitly use previous
930
      output tokens as inputs for predicting subsequent tokens.
931
932   As shown in Figure 5, the gradient of the visual input is significantly lower than that of the language
933   inputs (Question and Answer). This indicates that LLMs prioritize language tokens over visual
934   ones when predicting answers, leading to hallucinations based on language biases. Additionally, we
935
      observe that only a few tokens account for most of the visual gradients. For instance, in InstructBLIP
      with Vicuna-7B, just 10 out of 30 tokens represent nearly 73% of the visual gradients, making it
936
      challenging for specific and strong visual information to be adequately considered. These findings
937
      support the results of our previous experiments.
