                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            The normal distribution plays a central role in information theory – it is at the
011            same time the best-case signal and worst-case noise distribution, has the greatest
012
               representational capacity of any distribution, and offers an equivalence between
               uncorrelatedness and independence for joint distributions. Accounting for the
013
               mean and variance of activations throughout the layers of deep neural networks
014
               has had a significant effect on facilitating their effective training, but seldom has
015            a prescription for precisely what distribution these activations should take, and
016            how this might be achieved, been offered. Motivated by the information-theoretic
017            properties of the normal distribution, we address this question and concurrently
018            present normality normalization: a novel normalization layer which encourages nor-
019            mality in the feature representations of neural networks using the power transform
020            and employs additive Gaussian noise during training. Our experiments compre-
021            hensively demonstrate the effectiveness of normality normalization, in regards
022            to its generalization performance on an array of widely used model and dataset
023
               combinations, its strong performance across various common factors of variation
               such as model width, depth, and training minibatch size, its suitability for usage
024
               wherever existing normalization layers are conventionally used, and as a means to
025
               improving model robustness to random perturbations.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The normal distribution is unique – information theory shows that among all distributions with
032   the same mean and variance, a signal following this distribution encodes the maximal amount of
033   information (Shannon, 1948). This can be viewed as a desirable property in learning systems such as
034   neural networks, where the activations of successive layers equivocates to successive representations
035   of the data.
036   Moreover, a signal following the normal distribution is maximally robust to random perturbations
037   (Cover & Thomas, 2006), and thus presents a desirable property for the representations of learning
038   systems; especially deep neural networks, which are susceptible to random (Ford et al., 2019)
039   and adversarial (Szegedy et al., 2014) perturbations. Concomitantly, the normal distribution is
040   information-theoretically the worst-case perturbative noise distribution (Cover & Thomas, 2006),
041   which suggests models gaining robustness to Gaussian noise should be robust to any other form of
042   random perturbations.
043   Furthermore, normality in the representations of deep neural networks imbues them with other useful
044   properties, such as producing probabilistic predictions with calibrated uncertainty estimates (Guo
045   et al., 2017), and rendering them amenable to a Bayesian interpretation (Lee et al., 2017). This
046   suggests that developing a method for enforcing and maintaining normality throughout model training
047   is of general value.
048   We show that encouraging deep learning models to encode their activations using the normal distribu-
049   tion in conjunction with applying additive Gaussian noise during training, helps improve generaliza-
050   tion. We do so by means of a novel layer – normality normalization – so-named because it applies
051   the power transform, a technique used to gaussianize data (Box & Cox, 1964; Yeo & Johnson, 2000),
052   and because it can be viewed as an augmentation of existing normalization techniques such as batch
053   (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), layer (Ba et al., 2016), instance (Ulyanov et al., 2016), and group (Wu &
      He, 2018) normalization.

057   when and why it is effective, its suitability for usage wherever existing normalization layers are
058   conventionally used, and its effect on improving model robustness under random perturbations.

074
075   2.1.1    OVERVIEW OF THE F RAMEWORK
076
077   The normal distribution is at the same time the best possible signal distribution, and the worst possible
078
      noise distribution; a result which can be studied in the context of the Gaussian channel (Shannon,
      1948), and through the lens of the mutual information game (Cover & Thomas, 2006). In this
079
      framework, X and Z denote two independent random variables, representing the input signal and
080
      noise, and Y = X + Z is the output. The mutual information between X and Y is denoted by
081   I (X; Y ); X tries to maximize this term, while Z tries to minimize it. Both X and Z can encode
082   their signal using any probability distribution, so that their respective objectives are optimized for.
083
084
      Information theory answers the question of what distribution X should choose to maximize I (X; Y ).
      It also answers the question of what distribution Z should choose to minimize I (X; Y ). As shown by
085
      the following theorem, remarkably the answer to both questions is the same – the normal distribution.
086
087   Theorem 2.1. (Cover & Thomas, 2006) Mutual Information Game. Let X, Z be independent,
088
      continuous random variables with non-zero        support over the entire real line,  and satisfying the
      moment conditions E {X} = µx , E X 2 = µ2x + σx2 and E {Z} = µz , E Z 2 = µ2z + σz2 .
089
      Further let X ∗ , Z ∗ be normally distributed random variables satisfying the same moment conditions,
090
      respectively. Then the following series of inequalities holds
091
092                         I (X ∗ ; X ∗ + Z) ≥ I (X ∗ ; X ∗ + Z ∗ ) ≥ I (X; X + Z ∗ ) .                   (1)
093
094   Proof. Without loss of generality let µx = 0 and µz = 0. The first inequality hinges on the entropy
095   power inequality. The second inequality hinges on the maximum entropy of the normal distribution
096   given first and second moment constraints. See (Cover & Thomas, 2006) for details.
097
098   This leads to the following minimax formulation of the game
099
                             min max I (X; X + Z) = max min I (X; X + Z) ,                                 (2)
100                            Z   X                         X   Z
101
      which implies that any deviation from normality, for X or Z, is suboptimal from that player’s
102   perspective.
103
104   2.1.2    R ELATION TO L EARNING
105
106   How might this framework relate to the learning setting? First, previous works have shown that
107   adding noise to the inputs (Bishop, 1995) or to the intermediate activations (Srivastava et al., 2014) of
      neural networks can be an effective form of regularization, leading to better generalization. Moreover,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the mutual information game shows that, among encoding distributions, the normal distribution is
109   maximally robust to random perturbations. Taken together these suggest that encoding activations
110   using the normal distribution is the most effective way of using noise as a regularizer, because a
111   greater degree of regularizing noise in the activations can be tolerated for the same level of corruption.
112
      Second, the mutual information game suggests gaining robustness to Gaussian noise is optimal
113
      because it is the worst-case noise distribution. This suggests adding Gaussian noise – specifically – to
114
      activations during training should have the strongest regularizing effect. Moreover, gaining robustness
115   to noise has previously been demonstrated to imply better generalization (Arora et al., 2018).
116
117   2.2   M AXIMAL R EPRESENTATION C APACITY AND M AXIMALLY C OMPACT R EPRESENTATIONS
118
      The entropy of a random variable is a measure of the number of bits it can encode (Shannon, 1948),
119   and therefore of its representational capacity (Cover & Thomas, 2006). The normal distribution
120   is the maximum entropy distribution for specified mean and variance. This suggests that a unit
121   which encodes features using the normal distribution has maximal representation capacity given a
122   fixed variance budget, and therefore encodes information as compactly as possible. This may then
123   suggest that it is efficient for a unit (and by extension layer) to encode its activations using the normal
124   distribution.
125
      2.3   M AXIMALLY I NDEPENDENT R EPRESENTATIONS
126
127   Previous work has explored the beneficial effects of decorrelating features in neural networks (Huang
128   et al., 2018; 2019; Pan et al., 2019). Furthermore, other works have shown that preventing feature
129   co-adaptation is beneficial for training deep neural networks (Hinton et al., 2012).
130   For any set of random variables, for example representing the pre-activation values of various
131   units in a neural network layer, uncorrelatedness does not imply independence in general. But for
132   random variables whose marginals are normally distributed, then as shown by Appendix Lemma E.1,
133   uncorrelatedness does imply independence when they are furthermore jointly normally distributed.
134   We use these results to motivate the following argument: encouraging normality in the feature
135   representations of units by using normality normalization, together with uncorrelatedness in these
136   features, would lead to the desirable property of maximal independence; in the setting where increased
137   unit-wise normality also lends itself to increased joint normality.

      We next discuss a few facets of the method.
201
202   Justification for the Second Order Method                 The justification for using the Newton-Raphson
203   method for computing λ̂ is as follows:
204
205       • A first-order gradient-based method would require iterative refinements to its estimates of λ̂ in
206         order to find the minima, which would significantly affect runtime. In contrast, the Newton-
207         Raphson method is guaranteed to find the minima of the quadratic loss in one step.
208       • A first-order gradient-based method for computing λ̂ would require an additional hyperparameter
209         for the step size. Due to the quadratic nature of the loss, the Newton-Raphson method necessarily
210
            does not require any such additional hyperparameter.
          • The minibatch statistics µ̂ and σ̂ 2 are the MLEs for their respective optimization problems, and
211
            are available in closed-form. It is therefore natural to seek a closed-form expression for the MLE
212
            of λ̂, which is facilitated by using the Newton-Raphson method.
213

      4.2      A DDITIVE G AUSSIAN NOISE WITH S CALING
239
240   Normality normalization applies additive random noise to the output of the power transform; a step
241   which is motivated using information-theoretic principles in Subsection 2.1.
242
      For each input indexed by i ∈ {1, . . . , N }, during training3 we have yi = xi + zi · ξ · s, where
243
      xi is the i-th input’s post-power transform value, zi ∼ N (0, 1), ξ ≥ 0 is the noise factor, and
244
      s = N1 ∥x − x̄∥1 represents the zero-centered norm of the post-power transform values, normalized
245   by the sample size N .
246
247   Importantly, scaling each of the sampled noise values zi for a given channel’s minibatch4 by the
248
      channel-specific scaling factor s leads to an appropriate degree of additive noise for each of the
      channel’s constituent terms xi . This is significant because for a given minibatch, each channel’s norm
249
      will differ from the norms of other channels.
250
251   Furthermore, we treat s as a constant, so that its constituent terms are not incorporated during
252   backpropagation.5 This is significant because the purpose of s is to scale the additive random noise
253   by the minibatch’s statistics, and not for it to contribute to learning directly by affecting the gradients
254
      of the constituent terms.
255   Note that we employ the ℓ1 -norm for x rather than the ℓ2 -norm because it lends itself to a more
256   robust measure of dispersion (Pham-Gia & Hung, 2001).

465   It is worth noting that many power transforms were developed with the aim of improving the validity

469   Gaussianization Non-parametric techniques, for example those using quantile functions (Gilchrist,
470   2000), offer an alternative approach to gaussianizing but are not easily amenable to the deep learning
471   setting where models are trained using backpropagation and gradient descent. Employing iterative
472   gaussianization techniques (Chen & Gopinath, 2000; Laparra et al., 2011) offers an interesting
473   direction for future work.
474
475   Normalization Layers The properties of normality normalization explored in the present work can
476   be utilized to extend normalization layers studied in the context of specific architectures (Shen et al.,
477   2020), and those tailored for general manifolds (Brooks et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024). Furthermore,
478   as a result of normality normalization’s gaussianizing effect, the analysis of works which have sought
479   to better understand the effects of existing normalization layers, and to motivative new ones, may
480   be facilitated (Bjorck et al., 2018; Santurkar et al., 2018; Hoffer et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019; Luo
481   et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Daneshmand et al., 2020; 2021; Joudaki et al., 2023).
482
483   Adversarial Robustness It would be interesting to tie the present work with those suggesting
484   robustness to ℓ2 -norm constrained adversarial perturbations increases when training with Gaussian
485   noise (Cohen et al., 2019; Salman et al., 2019). Furthermore, it has been suggested that adversarial
      examples and images corrupted with Gaussian noise may be related (Ford et al., 2019). This might


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      indicate gaining robustness to Gaussian noise not only in the inputs, but throughout the model, can
487   lead to greater adversarial robustness.
488
489   However, gaussianizing activations, and training with Gaussian noise, may only be a defense in the
490
      distributional sense; exact knowledge of the weights (and consequently of the activation values), as is
      often assumed in the adversarial robustness setting, is not captured by the noise-based robustness
491
      framework, which is only concerned with distributional assumptions over the activation values.
492
      Nevertheless it does suggest that, on average, greater robustness may be attainable.
493
494
      Neural Networks as Gaussian Processes Neal (1996) showed that in the limit of infinite width,
495
      a single layer neural network at initialization approximates a Gaussian process. This result has
496   been extended to the multi-layer setting by (Lee et al., 2017), and Jacot et al. (2018); Lee et al.
497   (2019) suggest the Gaussian process approximation may remain valid beyond network initialization.
498   However, these analyses still necessitate the infinite width limit assumption.
499
      Recent work has shown that batch normalization lends itself to a non-asymptotic approximation
500
      to normality throughout the layers of neural networks at initialization (Daneshmand et al., 2021).
501
      Given its gaussianizing effect, layers trained with normality normalization may be amenable to a
502   non-asymptotic approximation to Gaussian processes – throughout training. This could help to further
503   address the disparity in the analysis of neural networks in the infinite width limit, for example as in
504   mean-field theory, with the finite width setting (Joudaki et al., 2023).

      7    C ONCLUSION
508
509   Among the methodological developments that have spurred the advent of deep learning, their success
510
      has often been attributed to their effect on the model’s ability to learn and encode representations
      effectively, whether in the activations or in the weights. This can be seen, for example, by considering
511
      the importance of initializing model weights suitably, or by the effect different activation functions
512
      have on learning dynamics.
513
514   Seldom has a prescription for precisely what distribution a deep learning model should use to
515   effectively encode its activations, and exactly how this can be achieved, been investigated. The
516
      present work addresses this – first by motivating the normal distribution as the probability distribution
      of choice, and subsequently by materializing this choice through normality normalization.
517
518   It is perhaps nowhere clearer what representational benefit normality normalization provides, than
519   when considering that no additional learnable parameters, relative to existing normalization layers,
520   were introduced. This highlights – and precisely controls for the effect of – the importance of
521   encouraging models to encode their representations effectively.
522   We presented normality normalization: a novel, principledly motivated, normalization layer.
523   Our experiments and analysis comprehensively demonstrated the effectiveness of normality
524   normalization, in regards to its generalization performance on an array of widely used model and
525   dataset combinations, its consistently strong performance across various common factors of variation
526   such as model width, depth, and training minibatch size, its suitability for usage wherever existing
527   normalization layers are conventionally used, and through its effect on improving model robustness
528
      to random perturbations.

       D.8    U NCORRELATEDNESS , J OINT N ORMALITY, AND I NDEPENDENCE B ETWEEN F EATURES
1156
1157
       Following the motivation we presented in Subsection 2.3, here we sought to explore the potential
1158   effect normality normalization may have on decorrelating features, the extent to which it may increase
1159   joint normality in the features, and the extent to which it may increase the independence between
1160   features.
1161
       We use the following experimental setup. For each layer of a ResNet34/STL10 model trained
1162
       to convergence using either BatchNormalNorm or BatchNorm, we compute the correlation, joint
1163
       normality, and mutual information over 20 pairs of channels, and across 10 validation minibatches.
1164
1165   We evaluate joint normality using the negative of the HZ-statistic (Henze & Zirkler, 1990) (higher val-
1166   ues indicate greater joint normality), and evaluate independence using the adjusted mutual information
1167
       (AMI) metric (lower values indicate a greater degree of independence) Vinh et al. (2010)9
1168   We evaluate joint normality across pairs of channels rather than across all of the channels in a layer,
1169   because measures of joint normality are sensitive to small deviations in sample statistics for finite
1170   sample sizes (Zhou & Shao, 2014; Ebner & Henze, 2020). Wherever we measure AMI, we use the
1171   square root of the number of sampled features as the number of bins (a generally accepted rule of
1172   thumb) when discretizing the features, and we use uniform binning, which is appropriate for (close
1173
       to) normally distributed data.
1174   Figure 14 demonstrates that models trained with BatchNormalNorm have lower correlation between
1175   unit features, higher joint normality, and have greater independence, across the model’s layers. This
1176   is of value in context of the benefits feature independence is though to provide, which we explored in
1177   Subsection 2.3.
