009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used in a variety of important
012            applications, yet their safety and reliability remain major concerns. Various ad-
013            versarial and jailbreak attacks have been proposed to bypass the safety alignment
014            and cause the model to produce harmful responses. We introduce Defensive Self-
015            supervised Prompt INjection (D-SPIN) which can detect and reverse both prompt
016            injection and jailbreak attacks on LLMs. Just by injecting an adaptive defense
017            prompt at inference-time, our method is simple, effective, and compatible with
018
               existing safety-aligned models. Our benchmarks demonstrate that our system can
               reduce the attack success rate by up to 87.9%, while maintaining the performance
019
               on benign user requests. In addition, we discuss the situation of an adaptive at-
020
               tacker and show that our method is still resilient against attackers who are aware
021            of our defense.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Large-Language Models (LLMs) have achieved great success in code generation (Rozière et al.,
043   2023), question answering (Touvron et al., 2023), and task planning (Surı́s et al., 2023). However,
044   they can be jailbroken by adversarial attacks and prompts (Zou et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Yuan
045   et al., 2023), leading them to exhibit behaviors of manipulation (Carroll et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024),
046   deception (Park et al., 2023; Hazell, 2023), and explicit content (Kang et al., 2023). This poses an
047   inherent risk as LLMs are widely deployed in many critical applications, like programming.
048   To align LLMs for safety, prior work focuses on training to make the model more robust. For ex-
049   ample, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) trains reward
050   models based on human ratings, and is used to judge LLM outputs. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023)
051   trains model on direct preference optimization. To make train-time defenses robust against attacks,
052   it is often required to train the model on the generated attacks, which can take significant resources
053   to gather responses (Casper et al., 2023). In addition, training-time defenses have fundamental dif-
      ficulties defending against unforeseen attacks and jailbroken prompt injection, due to a mismatched

086   In this paper we introduce a method to defend against these attacks by self-supervised prompt in-
087   jection, dubbed D-SPIN allowing us to achieve a defense that adapts to each attack online. Just
088   as an attacker finds the right prompt injection to break the LLM, our approach will find the right
089   prompt injection to detect and repair the input. Figure 1 shows our prompt injection defending an
090   adversarial prompt injection.
091   Our key insight is that the prompts which override the natural guidelines of the LLM also degrade
092   other capabilities of the model. By constructing various self-supervised tasks, we can detect attacks
093   without needing to know the true labels of the input requests or examples. Our constructed self-
094   supervised tasks also allow us to repair attacked user prompts that slip through detection. This is
095   achieved by adding tokens to the start of the input and taking gradient steps to reduce the perplexity
096   of the resulting string. By using self-supervised prompt injection at test time, we can defend against
097   unseen attacks and even adaptive attacks given the online adaptive property. Figure 2 shows the
098
      results of the different tasks rejecting malicious attacks while letting benign requests through.
099   Our method is model agnostic and can be compatible with existing defense systems. Our self-
100   supervised criteria do not depend on malicious or benign labels, thus can be used online at inference
101   time on demand.
102   Visualizations, experiments, and qualitative results demonstrate that our self-supervised prompt in-
103   jection significantly improves the LLM safety upon existing alignment defenses. On Advbench with
104   Universal Adversarial Triggers (Zou et al., 2023), we are able to reduce Attack Success Rates (ASR)
105   to 12.11% and 0% on Vicuna and Llama-2 respectively. Our method also can detect natural language
106   based jailbreak attacks (Wei et al., 2023), adversarial instructions (Wei et al., 2023), and role-play
107   (Shen et al., 2024), reducing ASR to 0% on both models, fully protecting them. Our method also is
      shown to be resilient against attackers who are aware of our defense.

131
132
133   Large-Language Models: LLMs are pre-trained models on a huge corpus of textual data. A mix
134   of these will have open source weights (Touvron et al., 2023), while others are closed off and are
135
      only accessible through APIs (Liu et al., 2023b). Studies have shown that various capabilities are
      learned at the pre-training stage (Zhou et al., 2023). Performance of these models is correlated
136
      with parameter size and train time (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020), however, larger
137
      models also exhibit more problematic behavior (Perez et al., 2022; Elazar et al., 2023) and even the
138   fine-tuning of these large models on benign datasets can compromise their safety (Qi et al., 2023).
139
140   Alignment: Alignment is an inbuilt system in recent LLMs. Originally used for text summarization
141
      (Stiennon et al., 2022), the main motivation recently has been to align the goals of AI models with
      human values and morals (Leike et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2022). It can be done through
142
      reinforcement learning through human feedback (RLHF) or even through other LLMs as judges
143
      (Ziegler et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023). However, as long as alignment does not remove the harmful
144   behavior entirely, there will exist prompts that can elicit that behavior (Wolf et al., 2023). Wei et al.
145   (2023) showed that two main issues of alignment come from conflicting objectives, and mismatched
146   generalization.
147
      Adversarial Attacks: Specific modifications to inputs can cause models to output incorrect or ma-
148
      licious responses. This first gained prominence with Szegedy et al. (2014)’s paper attacking image
149
      classification models. For vision tasks, these attacks can also be repaired through natural super-
150   vision as well (Mao et al., 2021). For Large-language models, research has been done compiling
151   jailbreak prompts that can cause harmful responses (Gehman et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023a). These
152   refer to user-created prompts, and usually involve a scenario that encourages the LLM to break away
153   from the preset safety guidelines (Wei et al., 2023). Recent developments such as AutoDAN (Liu
154   et al., 2024) and CodeChameleon (Lv et al., 2024) incorporate LLMs to generalize and automatically
155   generate these malicious prompts for a variety of requests.
156   Universal Adversarial Triggers (UATs) (Wallace et al., 2021) has been one method to attack these
157   models which embeds a sequence of tokens into the user prompt that will cause it to bypass align-
158   ment and safety guidelines. In particular, gradient-guided search (Guo et al., 2021) has proven
159   remarkably effective in adversarially misleading the model. Zou et al. (2023) show that these at-
160   tacks are also transferable across models, allowing harmful generation even in closed-box models.
161   In response, various defense methods have been suggested, however, many of them (Kumar et al.,
      2023; Li et al., 2023; Phute et al., 2023) rely on external LLMs to verify the safety of the request.

      Defense Methods exist for guarding against certain forms of Adversarial attacks. One example is
163   Jain et al. (2023) showing a perplexity filter is able to reject certain token attacks that cross a given
164   score. Another approach have been defenses (Cao et al., 2023; Robey et al., 2024) that randomly
165   drop or modify tokens within the input to try and trigger alignment. For shorter prompts, such
166   methods risk dropping important nouns or context, which can classify benign prompts as malicious.
167   These approaches are similar to ones that preprocess the inputs in a way that adds a secret passphrase
168   (Liu et al., 2024) or strictly structures the query Chen et al. (2024). As a layer of defense, some
169   researchers (Inan et al., 2023) proposed adding another LLM to either judge or classify an input as
170   benign before returning the answer.

172   3     M ETHOD
173
174   We begin by describing a series of defenses that use self-supervision to defend against both hand-
175   crafted jailbreak attacks and optimization-based adversarial trigger attacks (Zou et al., 2023). We
176   provide two layers of defense, detection and reversal, that complement each other. Both methods
177   rely on self-supervised signals that we construct for language tasks. These signals are shown in
178   Figure 3 being able to accurately distinguish between benign and malicious prompts. Lastly, we
179   consider the scenarios where the attacker can adapt their attack in response to the defense, and
180   empirically show that our methods are still robust.

182   3.1   JAILBROKEN L ANGUAGE M ODELS
183
184   In a given user interaction, the user inputs the request x, and the chatbot responds with response y.
185
      The LLM generates text outputs by predicting the next token, sampling from a probability distribu-
      tion conditioned on the input such that y ∼ F (·|x).
186
187   For attacks, the attacker wants to minimize the difference between the response y and their desired
188   goal t. In this case, this would be malicious instructions prefaced by an agreement such as “Sure,
189   here’s how”. To do this, they can add a set of tokens a which causes the model to respond with their
190   desired output. This results in an attack optimization goal of:
191                                       a = min Lattack (F (x + a), t)                                   (1)
192                                               a

193   Note here that ’+’ denotes the concatenation of two strings, and that the attack is appended to the end
194   of the user request. For GCG attack (Zou et al., 2023), this optimization is through gradient descent.
195   For natural language jailbreaks (Wei et al., 2023), this optimization is through human construction.

249   3.3                               R EVERSALS
250
      As a second layer for defending against adversarial attacks, we can even reverse attacks that slip
251
      through detection. To do so, we append additional tokens in front of the request x′ , and aim to find a
252   series of tokens that will restore the natural alignment of the LLM. This would allow it to work with
253   existing defenses, and further strengthens the models against attacks. Our proposed method, the
254   introduction of defense tokens into the prefix of the user prompt also leaves the core content of the
255   request unchanged. One observation we made with the GCG adversarial triggers (Zou et al., 2023)
256   was that their inclusion would drastically increase the total perplexity of the input. By defining our
257   Lautoreg as the perplexity of the entire user input, we get the following objective:
258                                         d = arg min Lautoreg (d + x′ )                                (4)
                                                                                                     d
259
260
      After every p stages of optimization, we run the completion of the new input request ŷ = F (d + x′ )
      and record if the response began with any of the common alignment refusals. To pass through, it
261
      must run through all n steps without triggering any of the common denial prefixes. Once the optimal
262
      d is found, the final output of the model is ŷ = F (d + x′ ).

      3.4                               T OTAL S YSTEM
265
266   For an attack to be successful, it must pass through all the detection stages, and the generated re-
267   sponse will have the defense tokens as the prefix. The multi-layered approach allows each defense
268   system to plug up holes in the others, and adds to the variables that an attack must consider. Due to
269   the compositionality of our self-supervised defense, users can easily remove some tasks to speed up
      the system.

      3.5      A DAPTIVE ATTACKER
271
272   Another key advantage of our defense is that it is also robust to adaptive attackers. If the attack
273   ignores our defense strategy, then we will defend it. If the attackers consider our defense strategy and
274   then attack, then they need to optimize to also respect the self-supervised tasks, therefore make their
275   optimization harder due to the additional constraints. Formally, an adaptive attacker will alternate
276   between attacking and defending the same prompt until it converges:
277                                             a = arg min Lattack (F (x + a), t)                                             (5)
                                                             a
278
279                                             d = arg min Lautoreg (d + x + a)                                               (6)
                                                             d
280
      This iterative process is computationally intensive, and has the equivalent result as a constrained
281
      optimization problem. We use the Lagrangian penalty method by adding a new penalty to the score
282
      function. For each new defense system, we can include it as a new multi-target optimization the
283   attack must consider. With all the detection layers and reversal implemented, the adaptive attack
284   would have a multi-target optimization problem as follows:
285
286
          Ladapt (x′ , t, λr , λi , λs , λp ) = Lattack (x′ , t) + λr Lrepeater (x′ ) + λi Linterject (x′ ) + λp Lautoreg (x′ ) (7)
287   By allocating more optimization budget to satisfying these constraints, the resulting attack is also
288   less efficient. There is a trade-off that the attacker must consider: by optimizing to bypass the
289   detection layers, it also increases the coherence of the input and constraint the input the be benign. If
290   the attacker does not optimize to bypass the detection layers, our defense algorithm will be activated
291   due to the violation of our self-supervised constraints. Thus, adding our self-supervised constraint
292
      will result in a lose-lose situation for the attacker, and we will straightly increase the safety.

368
      Detection. For our Repeater layer, we inject the string “Repeat only the following sentences exactly
      without changes: ” into the model with the entire input surrounded by quotation marks. We use the
369
      best threshold of 0.89 to filter out inputs that failed to correctly repeat.
370
371   For our Interjection layer, we chose the question to be “But first, answer only what is the capital of
372   France?”. This gets appended to the end of the model, which leaves the system prompt unchanged.
373   On benign cases, the model was able to answer correctly more than 99% of the time, making it a
374
      reasonable question to ask. It passes the layer if the generation returns the answer within the first 10
      tokens, and in practice, this gave similar results to using the loss threshold.
375
376   The ROC curves in figure 4 show that there is a clear delineation in loss between malicious and
377   benign requests when asked to repeat back the entire string. With only the malicious requests, the
      LLM will identify the harmful content and refuse to repeat the sentence. When the adversarial

      As for the interjection task, it makes sense that the benign and malicious prompts without the attack

      Reversal. For each request, we append 5 tokens “! ! ! ! !” to the start of the user message. For
389
      25 steps of optimization, at each stage the reversal calculates the top-256 tokens that best decrease
390
      the perplexity of the overall sentence. Then for our batch size of 50, we uniformly pick from one
391   of the 256 tokens and substitute it in. The sequence with the lowest loss is then chosen. At every 5
392   iterations it checks if the generated output matches one of the common denials for Vicuna or Llama.
393   In both models, the reversal alone is able to reduce ASR by nearly half.

      Jailbreaks present an interesting scenario. Because these are handcrafted prompts, they usually
395
      involve role-play and bypass guidelines by misleading the priorities of the model. The sentence
396
      structure is coherent, and as a result, they have the same perplexity as benign inputs. This means
397   that our reversal is less effective because minor perturbations do not affect the overall understanding
398   by the model. Most of these prompts also tell the model to ‘Ignore all the instructions you got
399   before’, which is needed to weaken alignment or safety reminders in the system prompt. In the case
400   of the repeat task, this actually causes the attack to fail that detection layer, because the model no
401   longer repeats the input, and the Levenshtein distance will be above the threshold. As seen in Figure
402   5, the repeat condition is strong enough for D-SPIN to prevent all attacks.
403   Performance on TriviaQA Figure 5 shows that under the benign cases, the overall performance is
404   not significantly affected. Because of how we chose the thresholds for D-SPIN detection, the benign
405   cases passed through without any issues. The addition of tokens that lower the perplexity does not
406   impact how the model answers the question.
407
408   4.5   D EFENSE AWARE A DAPTIVE ATTACK
409
410   We present the adaptive attack success rates in Figure 6. The same defense is run, but depending on
411   the λ penalty chosen, the attacker can optimize the attack for certain layers. However, this usually
412   comes with a lower ASR in other layers. This is because the attacker has less of a budget to tackle
413   the original goal of breaking alignment. Our defense system still reduces the attack success rate by
414
      up to 76%, even under an adaptive attacker, proving that the method is resilient.

452    5                      C ONCLUSION
453
454    In this paper, we show that self-supervised metrics are sufficient in detecting adversarial attacks, and
455
       that the attacks are also repairable by targeting perplexity. We introduce an inference time defense
       system to detect and repair input generation through prompt injection. Our defense works with ex-
456
       isting models and alignment, while not requiring any additional training or fine-tuning. This ensures
457
       that our defense system can react to new types of attacks that were not present in the alignment
458    training set. The creation of these self-supervised defenses allow for multiple variations that can
459    detect attacks. The layered nature of the method also shows resilience against adaptive attackers.

       6                      E THICS S TATEMENT
463
464
       This paper presents work on Large-Language Model safety, in hopes of presenting results that will
       make future conversational models safer to use. We acknowledge that our discussion does contain
465
       mentions of attacks and harmful content, but it is done to illustrate how our techniques specifically
466
       address those problems. These attacks have already been published, and we believe that there is
467    value in bringing up discussions of new defense methods, even if it might bring increased promi-
468    nence to these attacks. Whether researchers should publicly share defense methods because it might
469    help attackers is a point up for discussion, but we do present the scenario of an adaptive attacker to
470                                                        4
       help alleviate those concerns. There is already substantial discussion about the safety and prevalence
471    of conversational AI, and the models we tested on are already pub- licly available for use. Our meth-
472    ods are designed with practicality in mind, but they cannot account for every scenario in real-life use
473    cases. As with any other safety system, the methods we present must be constantly monitored, and
474    updated if necessary as attackers discover new avenues. This is to ensure that the work this paper
475
       produces does not lead to eventual harm.
