009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While scaling Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) has demonstrated
012            promising performance across various tasks, it also introduces redundant archi-
013            tectures, posing efficiency challenges for real-world deployment. Despite some
014            recognition of redundancy in LLMs, the variability of redundancy across different
015            architectures in transformers, such as MLP and Attention layers, is under-explored.
016
               In this work, we investigate redundancy across different modules within Trans-
               formers, including Blocks, MLP, and Attention layers, using a similarity-based
017
               metric. Surprisingly, despite the critical role of attention layers in distinguishing
018
               transformers from other architectures, we found that a large portion of these layers
019            exhibit excessively high similarity and can be pruned without degrading perfor-
020            mance. For instance, Llama-2-70B achieved a 48.4% speedup with only a 2.4%
021            performance drop by pruning half of the attention layers. Furthermore, by tracing
022            model checkpoints throughout the training process, we observed that attention
023            layer redundancy is inherent and consistent across training stages. Additionally,
024            we further propose a method that jointly drops Attention and MLP layers, allowing
025            us to more aggressively drop additional layers. For instance, when dropping 31
026            layers (Attention + MLP), Llama-2-13B still retains 90% of the performance on the
027
               MMLU task. Our work provides valuable insights for future network architecture
               design. The code will be released upon acceptance.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) have significantly advanced AI research, achieving
033   remarkable performance across various domains OpenAI (2024); Team (2024). However, scaling
034   these models also introduces redundant architectures, namely overarching, leading to inefficiencies
035   that complicate their real-world deployment Frantar et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023), e.g., inflating
036   deployment costs and resource demands. For instance, while the deployment cost of Llama-2-70B
037   exceeds 128GB in FP16 precision—surpassing the capacity of a single A100 GPU—it can be reduced
038
      in depth without significantly impacting performance Gromov et al. (2024).
039   Although several previous works have been proposed to promote LLM efficiency via removing
040   redundant parameters or architectures Frantar et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023), these approaches often
041   employ universal techniques that overlook the unique characteristics of transformer architectures.
042   Specifically, transformer Vaswani (2017) architectures are composed of multiple stacked blocks,
043   each containing an MLP layer and an Attention layer, which serve distinct functions and exhibit
044
      corresponding different levels of redundancy Wang et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023) This motivates a
      deeper investigation into the specific redundancies within Transformers, with the goal of identifying
045
      and addressing the most critical modules.
046
047   In this work, we systematically explore the redundancy in three key Transformer components: Block,
048   MLP, and Attention. Using a similarity-based metric Gromov et al. (2024); Men et al. (2024) , we
049   evaluate the importance of each component and progressively drop those identified as redundant.
050
      We first apply a “Block Drop” approach but observe that removing entire blocks leads to significant
      performance degradation. This suggests a need for a more fine-grained strategy.
051
052   Upon further examination, we explore the separate pruning of MLP and Attention layers. Our
053   findings reveal that while dropping MLP layers negatively affects performance, a substantial portion
      of Attention layers, i.e., the core of Transformer architectures which distinguish it from other

054
      mainstream architectures (e.g., RWKV Peng et al. (2023) and Mamba Gu & Dao (2024)), can be
055   pruned without degrading the model’s performance. For instance, dropping 50% of the Attention
056   layers in Llama-2-70B Touvron et al. (2023) results in comparable performance to the full model,
057   indicating a high degree of redundancy in these layers.
058
      Building on these insights, we propose a more flexible approach, “Joint Layer Drop”, which targets
059
      both MLP layers and Attention layers. By combining the importance scores of these layers, we find
060
      that jointly dropping low-importance Attention and MLP layers yields better performance under high
061   sparsity conditions compared to pruning only one type of layer.
062
063   Our work demonstrates that Attention layer redundancy is not only significant but also consistent
064
      across different training stages, indicating that this redundancy is an inherent property of Trans-
      former architectures. These findings open the door to more efficient Transformer designs, reducing
065
      both memory (e.g., KV-Cache) and computational costs (e.g., inference speed), while maintaining
066
      performance.
067
068   In summary, our key contributions are as follows:
069
070          • Through an in-depth analysis of redundancy in three key Transformer components—Block,
071            MLP, and Attention—we uncover a surprising level of redundancy within the Attention.
072          • We propose “Attention Drop”, a simple yet effective algorithm for efficiently removing
073            redundant Attention layers in a training-free manner. Additionally, we introduce "Joint
074            Layer Drop", which further improves the performance at high dropping ratios by jointly
075            targeting both Attention and MLP layers.
076
             • Our extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of dropping Attention, for instance,
077            removing 50% of the attention layers in Llama-2-70B results in only a 2.4% performance
078            reduction while achieving up to a 48.4% speedup.
079
080          • We further show that the attention layers remain consistently high redundancy throughout
081
               the training process, indicating it as an inherent property and providing valuable insights for
               future architecture design.

084   2   R ELATED W ORKS
085
086   Large Language Models Although Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) have
087   demonstrated promising performance across various tasks, their deployment costs still remain a
088   significant challenge for practical usage Sun et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2024); Gromov et al. (2024).
089   Transformer Vaswani (2017) models consist of multiple blocks, which include Attention layers and
090   MLP layers. Attention layers compute the contextual information between input tokens with quadratic
091   complexity concerning the input sequence length Li et al. (2020). KV-Cache Pope et al. (2022)
092   mitigates the computational issue but results in excessive memory costs Zhang et al. (2023). MLP
093   layers Liu et al. (2021); Mai et al. (2022) transform each token independently, using an up-projection
094
      followed by a down-projection, and contribute most of the model parameters. Recent works have
      revealed that not all blocks or layers are equally important Men et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024),
095
      which urges us to reflect on the structured redundancy within LLMs and the potential design of more
096
      compact architectures.
097
098
099
      Model Compression LLMs can be compressed to promote their efficiency in memory and compu-
      tation. Quantization Frantar et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2024) and Pruning Sun et al. (2023); Frantar &
100
      Alistarh (2023) are the most widely used techniques to compress LLMs. Specifically, quantization
101
      transforms the data type into low-bit but remains potentially redundant architecture and parameters.
102   Pruning can be categorized into unstructured pruning Kusupati et al. (2020); Sanh et al. (2020) and
103   structured pruning Zhuang et al. (2020); Kwon et al. (2020). While unstructured pruning maintains
104   better performance than structured pruning, it cannot be effectively applied to hardware, limiting
105   its practical usage. Our methods, Block Drop and Layer Drop, focus on removing structured mod-
106   ules rather than fine-grained parameters, creating hardware-friendly efficient architectures while
107   maintaining comparable performance. Additionally, Block Drop and Layer Drop are orthogonal to
      quantization, and their integration with quantization significantly enhances efficiency.

      3       M ETHODOLOGY
109
110
      In this section, we present the methodology for identifying and removing redundant modules in
111   LLMs. We begin by introducing a similarity-based metric to assess redundancy across both Attention
112   and MLP layers. Based on the insights gained from this analysis, we develop two targeted techniques,
113   i.e., MLP Drop and Attention Drop, to efficiently eliminate redundant components while preserving
114   model performance.
115
116   3.1       P RELIMINARIES
117
118   Similarity-based Drop To assess the redundancy of modules in LLMs, we employ a similarity-
119   based metric that evaluates the importance of each module by measuring the similarity between its
120
      input and output Gromov et al. (2024). The underlying hypothesis is that redundant modules produce
      outputs that are similar to their inputs, implying minimal transformation. In contrast, important
121
      modules are expected to significantly alter their inputs and thus should be preserved. The similarity
122
      between the hidden states of the input X and output Y of a module is quantified using cosine
123   similarity. The importance score S of the module is computed as:
124
125                                               S = 1 − Cosine(X, Y ).                                                   (1)
126   Modules with higher cosine similarity exhibit lower importance scores, indicating redundancy. We
127   identify and prune the modules with the lowest importance scores according to a predefined prunting
128   ratio. A complete evaluation of the metric’s effectiveness is provided in Appendix B.

232   4    I NVESTIGATION OF D ROPPING D IFFERENT TARGET M ODULES
233
234   In this section, we conduct a comprehensive investigation into the effects of dropping different target
235   modules. To quantify the trade-off between performance degradation and speedup, we introduce a
236   new metric, i.e., Speedup Degradation Ratio (SDR), defined as:
237
                                                       ∆Avg.
238                                              γ=            ,                                          (7)
                                                      ∆Speedup
239
240   where ∆Avg. represents the percentage change in average performance across the evaluated tasks,
241   and ∆Speedup denotes the corresponding percentage of speedup achieved by each method. Therefore,
242   γ measures the amount of performance degradation incurred for each 1% increase in speedup. A
243   lower γ value indicates that the model achieves speedup with minimal performance loss, making it
244   more efficient. In contrast, a higher γ value suggests that the performance loss is substantial relative
245   to the speedup gained, implying a less favorable trade-off.
246   Table 1 and Figure 5 summarize the results of dropping different target modules, such as Block, MLP,
247   and Attention layers. Specifically, Table 1 shows the performance impact of dropping a fixed number
248   of modules (e.g., 4 and 8 layers), while Figure 5 extends this analysis by evaluating a broader range
249   of dropping ratios (0% to 100%).
250
251   Block and MLP Drop: Significant Performance Degradation with Moderate Speedup Block
252   Drop and MLP Drop both lead to notable performance declines across both models, despite achieving
253   moderate speedups. For example, Dropping 4 blocks results in a 2.4% average performance decline
254   (from 68.2 to 65.8) for Llama-2-13B, with a speedup of 1.11×, corresponding to a γ of 0.22. However,
255   dropping 8 blocks causes a 7.5% performance drop (down to 60.7), with only a modest speedup of
256
      1.24× and a higher γ of 0.31. Similarly, MLP Drop exhibits a comparable trend, with a small decline
      at 4 layers (1.3%, γ = 0.32), but a much larger drop at 8 layers (6.3%, γ = 0.79). These results
257
      suggest that while Block and MLP Drop provide moderate speedup, they do so at the cost of
258
      significant performance degradation, especially at higher drop ratios.
259
260
      Attention Drop: Minimal Performance Impact with High Efficiency Surprisingly, despite the
261
      critical role of attention layers in Transformer architectures, dropping attention layers is highly
262
      effective. Both Llama-2-13B and Mistral-7B maintain over 99% of their original performance even
263   after dropping 8 attention layers, as shown in Figure 5(c). For example, Attention Drop maintains
264   near-baseline performance even after dropping 8 layers (69.8 vs. 70.3), with a speedup of 1.23× and
265   a low γ of 0.02. Dropping 12 attention layers results in only a slight performance decline (67.3), with
266   a significant speedup of 1.40× and a γ of 0.08. The superior performance of Attention Drop persists
267   when compared to other compression techniques B. These results demonstrate that attention
268   layers are highly redundant, and their removal has minimal impact on model accuracy, making
269   Attention Drop a highly efficient pruning strategy.

366
      In this section, we evaluate the efficiency of Attention Drop in terms of both memory usage and
367
      inference speed. Specifically, we examine the reduction in memory overhead due to the key-value
368
      (KV) cache and measure the speed-up during the entire generation phase. The results demonstrate
369   that Attention Drop provides substantial improvements in both efficiency metrics while maintaining
370   high performance.
371
372   KV-cache Memory Reduction Given the auto-regressive nature of attention, where outputs are
373   generated token by token, the KV-cache is used to store intermediate representations of input
374   sequences. This cache helps accelerate inference by preventing redundant computations but comes
375   with a significant memory cost, especially with longer sequence lengths or larger batch sizes. Our
376   proposed Attention Drop method efficiently removes unimportant attention layers, reducing the
377   corresponding KV-cache. Table 4 provides a comparison of 16-bit precision KV-cache memory
      usage before and after Attention Drop for various models, where we use 8 Nvidia RTX A6000

379   shown, Attention Drop results in substantial memory savings across all tested models. For instance,
380   in Llama-2-13B, the KV-cache is reduced from 52GB to 26GB, a 50% reduction. This memory
381   reduction is even more pronounced in larger models like Llama-2-70B, where the KV-cache decreases
382   from 20GB to 10GB. Note the reported results are based on resource-constrained scenarios. In
383   resource-sufficient cases, where larger batch sizes and longer sequence lengths can be applied, the
384   memory usage savings from Attention Drop become even more significant. These reductions are
385   beneficial for both memory-constrained and memory-sufficient environments, allowing for more
386   efficient model deployment.

466   7                      J OINT L AYER D ROP F URTHER E NHANCES THE P ERFORMANCE
467
468   While a significant proportion of attention layers exhibit high redundancy, our findings also show that
469   some MLP layers have low importance. To further optimize model efficiency, we introduce Joint
470   Layer Drop, which combines both Attention Drop and MLP Drop strategies. This approach leverages
471   the redundancy in both attention and MLP layers to enhance the overall performance of the model.
472
473   Methodology: Combining Attention and MLP Drop The Joint Layer Drop method is imple-
                                                                                    l                    l
474   mented by first calculating the importance scores for both attention layers (SA ) and MLP layers (SM )
475
      individually. These scores are computed based on a similarity-based metric that identifies redundant
      layers, as previously discussed. Once the importance scores are obtained for each type of layer, we
476                                                         l    l
      concatenate the scores into a single array: S = [SA     , SM ]. From this combined set of importance
477
      scores, we drop the layers with the lowest values, regardless of whether they are attention or MLP
478   layers. This joint approach allows us to remove the most redundant components from both layer
479   types simultaneously, enhancing the model’s efficiency while maintaining performance.
480
481   Superior Performance with Joint Layer Drop As demonstrated in Figure 8, Joint Layer Drop
482   consistently achieves better performance than either Attention Drop or MLP Drop alone. The process
483   begins by exclusively dropping attention layers, which are typically more redundant than MLP layers.
484   This continues until the number of dropped attention layers exceeds 14 for Mistral-7B and 18 for
485   Llama-2-13B. As a result, in the initial stages of pruning, the performance of Joint Layer Drop
      overlaps with that of Attention Drop.

487   layers start to become the next most redundant components. At this point, Joint Layer Drop begins

514   8      C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSIONS
515
516   Insights for Future Network Architecture Design Despite the success of scaling up LLMs, our
517   work provides valuable insights for scaling down models to achieve more efficient architectures. One
518   key insight is the high redundancy in attention layers, particularly in deeper layers, which suggests
519   that future works could reduce the number of attention layers without sacrificing performance, rather
520   than maintaining parity with MLP layers. Moreover, unlike MLP layers, attention layers exhibit
521   consistent redundancy as training progresses. This consistency may pose a bottleneck in training
522   large models. To address this, future research could explore replacing attention layers with alternative
523   mechanisms or develop new training techniques that capitalize on this redundancy to further enhance
524
      language model capacity.
525
526   Limitations While our proposed dropping techniques improve efficiency in the models we evalu-
527
      ated, there are limitations. A key area for future work is testing the applicability of these techniques
      across a broader range of models, such as vision transformers and vision-language models. Fur-
528
      thermore, our methods focus on post-training dropping without involving retraining, which could
529
      potentially recover or even improve performance after pruning. Retraining these models could unlock
530   even greater efficiency in more compact architectures.
531
532
      Conclusion In this work, we systematically revisited transformer architectures by investigating the
533
      effects of dropping three types of structures: Blocks, MLP layers, and Attention layers. Our findings
534   reveal that attention layers display significant redundancy and can be removed in large proportions
535   without compromising performance. To build on this, we introduced Joint Layer Drop, a method that
536   further increases both dropping ratios and performance by targeting redundant layers across both MLP
537   and Attention layers. This study empirically demonstrates the potential for creating more compact
538   and efficient transformer models, providing valuable insights for future network design within the
539   NLP community. By exploring structured redundancy, we open up new avenues for designing more
      efficient, scalable models that maintain high performance even under resource constraints.
