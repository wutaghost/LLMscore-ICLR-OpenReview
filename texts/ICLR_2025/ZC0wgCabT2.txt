009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating realistic graphs presents challenges in estimating accurate distribu-
012            tion of graphs in an embedding space while preserving structural characteristics
013            such as topology. However, existing graph generation methods primarily focus
014            on approximating the joint distribution of graph nodes and edges, overlooking
015            topology-wise similarity hindering accurate representation of global graph struc-
016
               tures such as connected components and loops. To address this issue, we propose
               a topology-aware diffusion-based graph generation method that aims to closely
017
               resemble the structural characteristics of the original graph by leveraging persistent
018
               homology from topological data analysis (TDA). Specifically, we suggest a novel
019            loss function, Persistence Diagram Matching (PDM) loss, which ensures the gener-
020            ated graphs to closely match the topology of the original graphs, enhancing their
021            fidelity and preserving essential homological properties. Also, we introduce a novel
022            topology-aware attention to enhance the self-attention module in the denoising
023            network. Through comprehensive experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness
024            of our approach not only by exhibiting high generation performance across various
025            metrics, but also by demonstrating a closer alignment with the distribution of
026            topological features observed in the original graphs. In addition, application to
027
               real brain network data showcases its versatility and potential for complex and real
               graph application.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The major goal of graph generation is to achieve high resemblance between generated graphs and
033
      their reference counterparts. To achieve this goal, various graph generation approaches have been
      taken based on conventional generative models, e.g., recurrent neural networks (You et al., 2018b),
034
      variational autoencoders (Simonovsky & Komodakis, 2018) and diffusion models (Jo et al., 2022;
035
      Vignac et al., 2023), and each exhibited promising results. Despite the achievement in the context of
036   the quantitative measures, e.g., similarity in distributions of graph characteristics such as degree and
037   clustering coefficients, there remains a limitation on generating graphs coherent to the graph structure
038   via the lens of graph topology.
039
      A brain network is perhaps a suitable example that demonstrates the challenges above. A brain
040
      network is typically represented as a graph that characterizes intricate wiring system of the brain,
041
      which is comprised of anatomical regions of interest (ROIs) defining its nodes and the connectivity
042   between different ROIs serving as edges (Farahani et al., 2019; Bullmore & Sporns, 2009). It is often
043   large and dense, but its topological properties are well-known as critical biomarkers (Sizemore et al.,
044   2019; Saggar et al., 2018). Moreover, brain networks are expensive; acquiring diffusion magnetic
045   resonance images (dMRI) and processing them via tractography (Sporns et al., 2005) to obtain
046   structural brain connectome data is costly in both cost and labor. In this regime, generating realistic
047   graphs (e.g., brain networks) that preserve their inherent connectivity as well as global structures is
048   highly demanding, however, existing methods often fall short in capturing the essential topological
049   features crucial for modeling interconnected brain regions with high fidelity.
050   In recent years, diffusion methods have been heavily studied for graph generation (Liu et al., 2023).
051   The methods in Niu et al. (2020) and Jo et al. (2022) proposed score-based diffusion methods in a
052   continuous time domain, originally defined for images (Song et al., 2020). However, the continuous
053   diffusion methods suffer from high computational cost, as the forward and reverse diffusion process
      is performed on infinitesimal continuous time point. Moreover, the uniformly added Gaussian noise

054
      results in a noisy and complete graph, which causes the loss of structural information and destroys
055   the sparsity of a graph. Later, Vignac et al. (2023) proposed a discrete diffusion method and applied
056   additive noise to each nodes and edges independently for graphs, nevertheless, existing methods
057   overlook the topologically invariant characteristics, e.g., geometric shape and connectivity, limiting
058   the generation.
059
      To overcome such issues, we propose a novel Topology-Aware Graph Generation (TAGG) method,
060
      from which the sampled graphs resemble not only in the distributions of the original graphs in
061   the embedding space but also in the homological features of the original graphs. Conventionally,
062   topological data analysis (TDA) from algebraic topology have been studied in various graph analyses
063   (Carlsson, 2020; Bukkuri et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021) to investigate topological features, and we
064   bridge the gap between TDA and graph diffusion model to generate topologically realistic graphs.
065   We define Persistence Diagram Matching (PDM) loss with persistence homology, which regularize
066   the homological features of the reference graphs to be incorporated in graph generation process via
067   1-Wasserstein distance. Furthermore, we introduce a topology-aware attention module, utilizing the
068   homological features derived from the persistence landscape (Bubenik, 2015) of a given graph, to
069
      foster the denoising network with global structural information.
070   Contributions. To this end, our main contributions are as follows: 1) We propose a novel topology-
071   aware graph generation method that yields homologically similar graphs with high fidelity. 2) We
072   propose PDM loss, utilizing persistent homology to encode the graph topology. 3) We propose
073   a topology-aware attention module that leverages persistence landscape to enhance the denoising
074   network in capturing graph topology.
075   Our model demonstrates superior performance on real and synthetic graph generation tasks, with
076   intuitive visualizations for topological comparisons. Especially with the application on brain network
077   generation from AlzheimerвҖҷs Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), our method demonstrates its
078   adaptability to diverse real-world graph generation tasks.

080   2   R ELATED WORK
081
082   Graph Generation. Graph generation has been developed in two major branches; autoregressive
083   and one-shot. Auto-regressive methods (You et al., 2018a;b; Simonovsky & Komodakis, 2018;
084
      Jin et al., 2020; Kong et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al., 2024) recursively capture the intricate graph
      dependencies, and sequentially generate the graph structure conditioned on the current incomplete
085
      graph. In spite of their impressive performance, auto-regressive approaches exhibit considerable
086
      computational demands due to the increasing number of generation steps along with the graph size.
087   Also, they face a challenge stemming from the absence of an inherent node generation order.
088
089   Conversely, one-shot methods (Ma et al., 2018; De Cao & Kipf, 2018; Madhawa et al., 2019; Zang
090
      & Wang, 2020) generate the whole graph, i.e., every nodes and edges, at once. By doing so, they
      reduce the computational requirements while facing performance degradation as the dataset scale
091
      grows. Recently, diffusion-based methods (Jo et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al.,
092
      2024) showed promising capability in graph generation, by defining the forward and reverse diffusion
093   processes and training a neural network that mimic the reverse process to reconstruct the graphs.
094
095   Persistent Homology. Persistent homology from computational topology studies the topological
096
      features of given objects, such as the number of holes (Edelsbrunner & Harer, 2022). It provides
      a way to capture and quantify the shapes and global structures by computing homological features
097
      of objects. By treating a graph as a topological object, the concept of persistent homology can be
098
      utilized to analyze the global structure of graphs, which leads improvements for graph classification
099   (Hofer et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Horn et al., 2022) and link prediction (Yan et al., 2021).
100   Furthermore, persistent homology has been successfully applied in biology (Chen & VolicМҒ, 2021; Qiu
101   & Wei, 2023; Bukkuri et al., 2021), signal processing (Xu et al., 2021), and point cloud (Nishikawa
102   et al., 2024), demonstrating its versatility and robustness.

275   4.3 T RAINING OBJECTIVE FOR TOPOLOGY PRESERVING GRAPH GENERATION
276   To produce accurate estimation of the probability vector pМӮG0 , we optimize the denoising network П•Оё
277   with two loss terms: 1) Persistence Diagram Matching loss, which aligns the homological features of

279

328   Benchmark Graph Datasets. To obtain a coherent analysis of graph generation performance, we

330   200 synthetic graphs with 12 вүӨ |V | вүӨ 20 generated from a stochastic block model with two

      from the BRENDA database (Schomburg et al., 2004).
334   ADNI. To validate practicability of TAGG, we use brain connectivity from AlzheimerвҖҷs Disease
335   Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI). In house tractography pipeline was applied to Diffusion Weighted
336   Imaging (DWI) of healthy subjects from ADNI adhering to the Destreiux atlas (Destrieux et al., 2010)
337   with 160 regions of interest (ROIs) comprising 148 cortical and 12 sub-cortical regions. The dataset is
338   composed of N = 844 undirected weighted graphs, i.e., structural brain connectivity, where the edge
339
      weights represent the number of fiber tracts connecting different ROIs. The edges were thresholded
      by removing those below 5%p of the maximum edge weights to obtain sparsity.
340
341   Baselines and Quantitative Metrics. We used the following one-shot deep generative methods as
342   baselines: EDP-GNN (Niu et al., 2020), GDSS (Jo et al., 2022), DiGress (Vignac et al., 2023), and the
343   one-shot version of LocalPPGN (Bergmeister et al., 2024). In addition, a conventional auto-regressive
344   generation method, i.e., GraphRNN (You et al., 2018b), is used for comparison. The (dis)similarity
345
      between distributions of graph statistics on the same number of generated and test graphs were
      computed using the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2012). Specifically, we
346
      compared the distributions of degree, clustering coefficient, and the number of occurrences of orbits
347
      with 4 nodes, as in Niu et al. (2020) and Jo et al. (2022). For consistency, we adhered to the train/test
348   split reference from Jo et al. (2022) and performed three replicate experiments to report averaged
349   performance on all models.
350
351
      Visualization of Homological Assessment. We utilized two distinct measures to assess the homo-
      logical (dis)similarity between the test and generated graphs: 1) Automatic Topologically-Oriented
352
      Learning (ATOL) (Royer et al., 2021), and 2) Mean Landscape (Bubenik, 2015). These methods
353
      directly encode the homological features from the persistence diagrams into vectors of desired dimen-

      6    C ONCLUSION
534   In this study, we proposed a novel graph generation framework preserving the intricate topology of
535   the network. Through the proposed topology-aware attention module and the Persistence Diagram
536   Matching Loss, we achieve high generation performance while maintaining the essential topological
537   features of the original graphs. This approach improves the fidelity of generated graphs and provides
538   valuable insights into their structure for both synthetic and real graph datasets. Our research addresses
539   a critical challenge in complex real-world graph generation, particularly in the context of brain
      networks, and pave the way for practical graph generation with topological consistency.

993      D.2   C HOICE OF FILTER FUNCTION f .
994
995      The degree function was employed as the filter function in TAGG, as it is one of the most
996      simple but common and effective ways to define the filtration of a graph (Hofer et al., 2017;
997      2020; Carriere et al., 2020). While the degree-based filter function may yield relatively smaller
998
         improvements in clustering and orbit metrics compared to the degree metric itself, the overall
         results of TAGG across all datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over other baselines
999
         in the quantitative comparison and ablation studies. Additionally, further experiments were
1000
         conducted using alternative filter functions, such as the clustering coefficient and betweenness
1001     centrality. The clustering coefficient Ci measures the local density of connections, defined as
1002     Ci = 2ei /ki (ki вҲ’ 1), where ki is the number of neighbors of node i, and ei is the number of
1003     edges between those neighbors. Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on the
1004     shortest path between other nodes, reflecting how crucial a node is in bridging different parts of
1005     a graph. However, due to the computational complexity and differentiability, we approximate
1006     the betweenness centrality using
                                       P random walks. The betweenness centrality with random walks
1007     Crw is defined as Crw (k) = iМё=kМё=j Pi,j (k)/Pi,j , where Pi,j is the total number of random
1008     walks from node i to node j, and Pi,j (k) is the number of random walks from node i to node
1009     j that pass through node k. As presented in the Tab. 5, changing the filter function affect the
1010     optimization process; for example, using the clustering coefficient enhances its corresponding
1011
         MMD score. However, the overall impact on the generation performance remains minimal, as
         the new averaged MMD metrics continue to outperform the baseline methods without significant
1012
         degradation.
