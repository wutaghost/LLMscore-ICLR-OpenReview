009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Langevin Dynamics framework, which aims to generate samples from the
012            score function of a probability distribution, is widely used for analyzing and in-
013            terpreting score-based generative modeling. While the convergence behavior of
014            Langevin Dynamics under unimodal distributions has been extensively studied in
015            the literature, in practice the data distribution could consist of multiple distinct
016
               modes. In this work, we investigate Langevin Dynamics in producing samples
               from multimodal distributions and theoretically study its mode-seeking properties.
017
               We prove that under a variety of sub-Gaussian mixtures, Langevin Dynamics is
018
               unlikely to find all mixture components within a sub-exponential number of steps
019            in the data dimension. To reduce the mode-seeking tendencies of Langevin Dy-
020            namics, we propose Chained Langevin Dynamics, which divides the data vector
021            into patches of constant size and generates every patch sequentially conditioned on
022            the previous patches. We perform a theoretical analysis of Chained Langevin Dy-
023            namics by reducing it to sampling from a constant-dimensional distribution. We
024            present the results of several numerical experiments on synthetic and real image
025            datasets, supporting our theoretical results on the iteration complexities of sam-
026            ple generation from mixture distributions using the chained and vanilla Langevin
027
               Dynamics.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Langevin dynamics, a.k.a. Langevin Monte Carlo, is a well-known Markov Chain Monte Carlo
032   (MCMC) sampling methodology that has been widely used to implement and interpret score-based
033   generative modeling. It can produce samples from the (Stein) score function of a probability density,
034   i.e., the gradient of the log probability density function with respect to data. It has been widely rec-
035   ognized that a pitfall of Langevin dynamics is its slow mixing rate (Wooddard et al., 2009; Raginsky
036   et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2018). Specifically, Song & Ermon (2019) shows that under a multi-modal
037   data distribution, the samples from Langevin dynamics may have an incorrect relative density across
038   the modes. Based on this finding, Song & Ermon (2019) proposes anneal Langevin dynamics, which
039
      injects different levels of Gaussian noise into the data distribution and samples with Langevin dy-
      namics on the perturbed distribution. While outputting the correct relative density across modes can
040
      be challenging for Langevin dynamics, a natural question is whether Langevin dynamics would be
041
      able to find all the modes of a multi-modal distribution.
042
043   In this work, we study this question by analyzing the mode-seeking properties of Langevin dynam-
044   ics. The notion of mode-seekingness (Bishop, 2006; Ke et al., 2021; Li & Farnia, 2023; Li et al.,
045
      2024a) refers to the property that a generative model captures only a subset of the modes of a multi-
      modal distribution. We note that a similar problem, known as metastability, has been studied in
046
      the context of Langevin diffusion, a continuous-time version of Langevin dynamics described by
047
      stochastic differential equation (SDE) (Bovier et al., 2002; 2004; Gayrard et al., 2005). Specifi-
048   cally, Bovier et al. (2002) gave a sharp bound on the mean hitting time of Langevin diffusion and
049   proved that it may require exponential (in the space dimensionality d) time for transition between
050   modes. Regarding discrete Langevin dynamics, Lee et al. (2018) constructed a probability distri-
051   bution whose density is close to a mixture of two well-separated isotropic Gaussians, and proved
052   that Langevin dynamics could not find one of the two modes within an exponential number of steps.
053   However, further exploration of the mode-seeking tendencies of Langevin dynamics and its variants
      for general distributions is still lacking in the literature.

073   In this work, we study Langevin dynamics under multi-modal distributions in a slightly different
074
      setting from the standard theory literature on sampling. As illustrated in Figure 1, the existing
      theoretical literature commonly considers a mixture of well-separated modes with bounded variance.
075
      On the other hand, in our analysis, we consider a low-density high-variance mode (referred to as
076
      Mode 0 or P (0) ) surrounding the other modes and filling up the space between the modes. Note
077
      that Langevin dynamics relies on the score function (i.e., the gradient of log-pdf) to search for the
078   modes. For a mixture model with no in-between modes (Figure 1.a), Langevin dynamics could
079   carry information about the direction towards the closest mode, even if they are far from all the
080   modes. However, assuming an in-between mode P (0) with high variance (Figure 1.b), the gradient
081   information outside the support set of the low-variance modes will be dominated by P (0) , despite the
082   minor overall mass of P (0) . As a result, one can expect that the dynamics would randomly explore
083   a large volume in Rd until finding a low-variance mode, which can take a significant time.
084
      To theoretically formulate and demonstrate the potential mode-seeking tendency of Langevin dy-
085
      namics, we begin by analyzing the convergence for mixture distributions of Gaussian modes, under
086
      which Langevin dynamics could fail to visit all the mixture components within sub-exponential
087   steps (in the data dimension). Subsequently, we generalize this result to mixture distributions of
088   sub-Gaussian modes. This generalization extends our earlier result on Gaussian mixtures to a sig-
089   nificantly larger family of mixture models, as the sub-Gaussian family includes any distribution over
090   an ℓ2 -norm-bounded support set.
091
      To reduce Langevin dynamics’ large iteration complexity shown under a high-dimensional input
092
      vector, we propose Chained Langevin Dynamics (Chained-LD). Since Langevin dynamics could
093   suffer from the curse of dimensionality, we decompose the sample x ∈ Rd into d/Q patches
094
      x(1) , · · · , x(d/Q) , each of constant size Q, and sequentially generate every patch x(q) for all
095
      q ∈ [d/Q] statistically conditioned on previous patches, i.e., P (x(q) | x(0) , · · · x(q−1) ). The com-
096   bination of all patches generated from the conditional distribution faithfully follows the probability
097   density P (x) due to the chain rule, while drawing samples from each patch requires less cost due
098   to the reduced dimension. We also provide a theoretical analysis of Chained-LD by reducing the
099   convergence of a d-dimensional sample to the convergence of each patch.
100
      Finally, we present the results of several numerical experiments to validate our theoretical find-
101
      ings. For synthetic experiments, we consider moderately high-dimensional Gaussian mixture mod-
102   els, where the vanilla Langevin dynamics could not find all the components within a million steps,
103   while Chained-LD could capture all the components with correct frequencies in O(104 ) steps. For
104   experiments on real image datasets, we consider a mixture of two modes by using the original im-
105   ages from MNIST/Fashion-MNIST training dataset (black background and white digits/objects) as
106   the first mode and constructing the second mode by i.i.d. flipping the images (white background and
107   black digits/objects) with probability 0.5. Following from Song & Ermon (2019), we trained a Noise
      Conditional Score Network (NCSN) to estimate the score function. Our numerical results indicate

122   2   R ELATED W ORKS
123
124   Langevin Dynamics: The convergence guarantees for Langevin diffusion, a continuous version of
125   Langevin dynamics, are classical results extensively studied in the literature (Bhattacharya, 1978;
126   Roberts & Tweedie, 1996; Bakry & Émery, 1983; Bakry et al., 2008). Langevin dynamics, also
127   known as Langevin Monte Carlo, is a discretization of Langevin diffusion typically modeled as a
128   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (Welling & Teh, 2011). For unimodal distributions, e.g., the probability
129   density function that is log-concave or satisfies log-Sobolev inequality, the convergence of Langevin
130
      dynamics is provably fast (Dalalyan, 2017; Durmus & Moulines, 2017; Vempala & Wibisono, 2019).
      However, for multimodal distributions, the non-asymptotic convergence analysis is much more chal-
131
      lenging (Cheng et al., 2018). Raginsky et al. (2017) gave an upper bound on the convergence time of
132
      Langevin dynamics for arbitrary non-log-concave distributions with certain regularity assumptions,
133   which, however, could be exponentially large without imposing more restrictive assumptions. Lee
134   et al. (2018) studied the special case of a mixture of Gaussians of equal variance and provided an
135   analysis of sampling from general non-log-concave distributions.
136
      Mode-Seekingness of Langevin Dynamics: The investigation of the mode-seekingness of gener-
137
      ative models starts with different generative adversarial network (GAN) (Goodfellow et al., 2014)
138
      model formulations and divergence measures, from both the practical (Goodfellow, 2016; Poole
139   et al., 2016) and theoretical (Shannon et al., 2020; Li & Farnia, 2023; Li et al., 2024a) perspectives.
140   In the context of Langevin dynamics, mode-seekingness is closely related to a lower bound on the
141   transition time between two modes, e.g., two local maximums. Bovier et al. (2002; 2004); Gayrard
142   et al. (2005) studied the mean hitting time of the continuous Langevin diffusion. Lee et al. (2018)
143   proved the existence of a mixture of two Gaussian distributions whose covariance matrices differ by
144   a constant factor, Langevin dynamics cannot find both modes in polynomial time.
145   Score-based Generative Modeling: A central task in unsupervised learning involves learning the
146   underlying probability distribution of training data and efficiently generating new samples from the
147   distribution. Since Song et al. (2020a) proposed sliced score matching which can train deep models
148   to learn the score functions of implicit probability distributions on high-dimensional data, score-
149   based generative modeling (SGM) has been going through a spurt of growth. Annealed Langevin
150   dynamics (Song & Ermon, 2019) estimates the noise score of the probability density perturbed by
151   Gaussian noise and utilizes Langevin dynamics to generate samples from a sequence of decreasing
152   noise scales. Song & Ermon (2020) conducted an analysis of the effect of noise levels on the
153
      performance of annealed Langevin dynamics. Denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) (Ho
      et al., 2020) incorporates a step-by-step introduction of random noise into data, followed by learning
154
      to reverse this diffusion process in order to generate desired data samples from the noise. Song
155
      et al. (2020b) unified anneal Langevin dynamics and DDPM via a stochastic differential equation.
156   A recent line of work focuses on the non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for SGM with an
157   imperfect score estimation under various assumptions on the data distribution (Block et al., 2020;
158   De Bortoli et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Benton et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
159   2024b). Conforti et al. (2023) also investigated the KL convergence guarantees for score-based
160   diffusion models. We highlight that a key difference between SGM and our theoretical analysis is
161   that we assume the sampler has direct access to the true score function, whereas SGM typically
      focuses on learning the score function from training data.

306
307   To reduce the mode-seeking tendencies of vanilla Langevin dynamics, we propose Chained
308   Langevin Dynamics (Chained-LD) in Algorithm 1. While vanilla Langevin dynamics apply gra-
309   dient updates to all coordinates of the sample in every step, we decompose the sample into patches
310
      of constant size and generate each patch sequentially to alleviate the exponential dependency on the
311
      dimensionality. More precisely, we divide a sample x into d/Q patches x(1) , · · · x(d/Q) of some
312
      constant size Q, and apply Langevin dynamics to sample each patch x(q) (for q ∈ [d/Q]) from
313
      the conditional distribution P (x(q) | x(1) , · · · x(q−1) ). Intuitively, vanilla Langevin dynamics needs
      to explore the entire space (of volume exponentially large in d) to find the missing modes, while
314
      Chained-LD could significantly lower the volume by dimensionality reduction.
315
316   In practice, we can also apply annealed Langevin dynamics (Song & Ermon, 2019) to facilitate the
317   sampling of each patch, by perturbing it with a series of noise levels {σt }t∈[T Q/d] . Specifically,
318   we refer chained vanilla Langevin dynamics (Chained-VLD) to Algorithm 1 without noise injection
319   (i.e., σt = 0 for all t ∈ [T Q/d]), and chained annealed Langevin dynamics (Chained-ALD) oth-

1747
1748   Our implementation and hyperparameter selection are based on (Song & Ermon, 2019) and (Song
1749   & Ermon, 2020). During training, we i.i.d. randomly flip an image with probability 0.5 to construct
1750   the two modes (i.e., original and flipped images). All models are optimized by Adam with learning
1751   rate 0.001 and batch size 128 for a total of 200000 training steps, and we use the model at the last
1752   iteration to generate the samples. We perform experiments on MNIST (LeCun, 1998) (CC BY-SA
1753   3.0 License) and Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) (MIT License) datasets and we set the patch
1754   size as Q = 14.
1755   For the score networks of chained annealed Langevin dynamics (Chained-ALD), we use the official
1756   PyTorch implementation of an LSTM network (Sak et al., 2014) followed by a linear layer. For
1757   MNIST and Fashion-MNIST datasets, we set the input size of the LSTM as Q = 14, the number of
1758   features in the hidden state as 1024, and the number of recurrent layers as 2. The inputs of LSTM
1759   include inputting tensor, hidden state, and cell state, and the outputs of LSTM include the next
1760   hidden state and cell state, which can be fed to the next input. To estimate the noisy score function,
1761   we first input the noise level σ (repeated for Q times to match the input size of LSTM) and all-0
1762
       hidden and cell states to obtain an initialization of the hidden and cell states. Then, we divide a
       sample into d/Q patches and input the sequence of patches to the LSTM. For every output hidden
1763
       state corresponding to one patch, we apply a linear layer of size 1024×Q to estimate the noisy score
1764
       function of the patch.
1765
1766   To generate samples, we use T ∈ {10000, 30000, 100000} iterations for annealed Langevin dynam-
1767   ics (ALD) and Chained-ALD. The initial samples are chosen as either original or flipped images
1768
       from the dataset, and the results for MNIST and Fashion-MNIST datasets are presented in Figures
       11, 3, 4, and 12 respectively. The two subfigures above the dashed line illustrate the samples from the
1769
       initial distribution and target distribution, and the subfigures below the dashed line are the samples
1770
       generated by different algorithms.
1771
1772   All experiments were run with one RTX3090 GPU. It is worth noting that the training and infer-
1773   ence time of chained Langevin dynamics using LSTM is considerably faster than vanilla/annealed
1774
       Langevin dynamics using RefineNet. For a course of 200000 training steps on MNIST/Fashion-
       MNIST, due to the different network architectures, LSTM takes around 2.3 hours while RefineNet
1775
       takes around 9.2 hours. Concerning image generation, chained Langevin dynamics is significantly
1776
       faster than vanilla/annealed Langevin dynamics since every iteration of chained Langevin dynamics
1777   only updates a patch of constant size, while every iteration of vanilla/annealed Langevin dynamics
1778   requires computing all coordinates of the sample. One iteration of chained Langevin dynamics us-
1779   ing LSTM takes around 1.97 ms, while one iteration of vanilla/annealed Langevin dynamics using
1780   RefineNet takes around 43.7 ms.
