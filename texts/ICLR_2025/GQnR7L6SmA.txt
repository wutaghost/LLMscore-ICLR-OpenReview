009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have been well-investigated for solving ill-posed inverse prob-
012            lems to yield excellent performance. However, their application to highly ill-posed
013            inverse problems remains challenging. In this work, we propose zero-shot diffu-
014            sion model for large and complex kernels, dubbed Dilack, incorporating novel
015            data fidelity terms. Based on our analyses on the ill-posedness for challenging
016            inverse problems, we propose regularized fidelity called pseudo-inverse anchor for
017
               constraining (PiAC) fidelity loss. Inspired by locally acting classical regularizers,
               we also propose to incorporate masked fidelity within PiAC loss that can interact
018
               with globally acting diffusion models, which adaptively enforces spatially and step-
019
               wisely local fidelity via masks. Our proposed scheme effectively reduces erratic
020            behavior and inherent artifacts in diffusion models, thereby improving restoration
021            quality including perceptual aspects and outperforming prior arts on both synthetic
022            and real-world datasets for modern lensless imaging and large motion deblurring.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Image restoration (IR) is a fundamental low-level vision task that solves ill-posed inverse prob-
028   lems (Lim et al., 2017; Kupyn et al., 2019; Larsson et al., 2016; Song et al., 2021; Sriram et al.,
029   2020). IR attempts to reconstruct the original image x ∈ Rn from the degraded and/or undersampled
030   measurement y ∈ Rm by exploiting the forward model for imaging:
031                                               y = Ax + n                                              (1)
032
033
      where n denotes a measurement noise and A is an m × n measurement matrix. This operator A,
      often a kernel matrix, encapsulates the effects of forward imaging processes in various low-level
034
      vision tasks (Liang et al., 2021b; Luo et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2017; Quan et al., 2021). A typical
035
      formulation to solve the inverse problem based on Eq. (1) or to recover x from y involves minimizing
036   a cost function expressed as:
037                                      x̂ = arg min L(x; y) + λR(x)                                   (2)
038                                               x
039   where L(x; y) represents a data-fidelity term, R(x) denotes a prior term for regularization, λ is a
040   parameter balancing regularization with fidelity, and x̂ is the estimated image.
041
      There are a number of approaches to formulate and solve Eq. (2). A classical approach uses
042   regularizers such as sparsity or low-rankness for R(x) (Krishnan & Fergus, 2009; Dong et al., 2018;
043   Tirer & Giryes, 2020). This approach works well with diverse imaging problems by modifying A
044   and n while using the same R(x), but it lacks perceptual priors (Boyd et al., 2011; Beck & Teboulle,
045   2009). Eq. (2) usually yields an iterative algorithm, but often leads to a closed form solution for some
046   cases. Deep learning-based approach solves Eq. (2) by training deep neural networks (DNNs) to
047   directly map from the measurement to the estimated image x̂ = D(y) (Lee et al., 2023; Zeng & Lam,
048   2021; Khan et al., 2019; Wan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Zhong et al., 2023). While this approach
049   has yielded excellent image quality, it often struggles due to their reliance on scarce, high-quality
050
      datasets and limited generalizability to new data variations (Song et al., 2021).
051   One promising approach to formulate and solve Eq. (2) is a hybrid form to use both the data fidelity
052   term in the classical approach and the regularization term R(x) by exploiting DNNs so that DNNs
053   can be decoupled with the forward model. This zero-shot (ZS) approach for IR can utilize pre-trained
      models such as generic denoisers (Metzler et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Ryu et al., 2019; Zhang

082   deblurring unlike classical methods. Here we argue that generative models such as DMs interact with
083
      data fidelity term globally so that they may not be as effective as locally acting classical regularizers
      for highly ill-posed inverse problems. In fact, Bora et al. (2017) showed that using both generative
084
      model and classical regularizer helped to improve performance for less measurements (i.e., more
085

086
087   Here we investigate the behavior of DMs interacting with data fidelity and then we propose ZS DM
088   with masked fidelity that adaptively enforces spatially and step-wisely local fidelity for highly ill-posed
089
      restoration problems such as modern lensless imaging and large motion deblurring. Surprisingly, for
      these challenging problems, we demonstrate that ZS DMs often yielded poor results over classical
090
      methods (Boyd et al., 2011; Beck & Teboulle, 2009) even with model mismatch and calibration
091
      errors (Monakhova et al., 2019; Rego et al., 2021; Poudel & Nakarmi, 2024). Our masked data
092   fidelity term enforces spatially and step-wisely local interactions with DMs by minimizing outlier
093   generations in DMs for stability while maintaining enough global interaction.
094
095
      Our contributions: (1) Analyzing the behaviors of current ZS DMs for solving highly ill-posed
      inverse problems with large and complex kernels. (2) Proposing a novel zero-shot Diffusion model
096
      for large and complex kernels, dubbed Dilack, with our novel fidelity that utilizes region-of-interest
097
      (ROI)-based spatially masked fidelity to dynamically toggle for emphasizing local consistency as
098   well as step-wisely masked fidelity over iterations. Moreover, our regularized data fidelity term, PiAC
099   (Pseudo-inverse Anchor for Constraining) loss, replaces the pseudo-inverse (Pi) with a more robust
100   approximation tailored for highly ill-posed tasks. (3) Demonstrating that Dilack outperforms existing
101   ZS DMs and classical methods in highly ill-posed IR tasks encountered in modern imaging systems
102   with large and complex kernel degradations, across both synthetic and real-world datasets.

      4.1   R EGULARIZED F IDELITY: P SEUDO - INVERSE A NCHOR FOR C ONSTRAINING (P I AC)
283
      Consider LP i in Eq. 10, ∥A† y − A† Ax̂0|t ∥22 , where A† y represents an analytic solution like Wiener
284
      deconvolution, and A† A projects onto the subspace spanned by A† y. For well-posed problems, A†
285
      is equivalent to the exact inverse A−1 , making A† y the true estimate x and A† A the identity matrix.
286   However, for highly ill-posed problems, these terms fail to preserve fidelity effectively. To address
287   this, we propose a new fidelity term to approximate LP i under such conditions.
288
289
      Pseudo-inverse anchor. The first term, A† y, serves as an anchor to enforce fidelity to the measure-
      ment y. However, for highly ill-posed problems, the pseudo-inverse solution A† y is insufficient. We
290
      replace it with a regularized solution x̃∗ , selecting a total variation (TV)-regularized solution (Rudin
291
      et al., 1992) obtained via Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) (Beck & Teboulle,
292   2009; Boyd et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2013) (Appendix C). This leads to the approximate fidelity
293   term ∥x̃∗ − A† Ax̂0|t ∥22 . The optimization starts with an initial point of 0 for the first x̃∗ , updated
294   G − 1 times using normalized intermediate sampling outputs, where G is the number of initializa-
295   tions. The TV regularizer weight λt decreases over re-initialization steps, starting at λT −1 = 10−7
296   (Appendix G.6). If G = 1, the initial x̃∗ is reused without updates.
297
      For well-posed problems, A† A acts as an exact identity, and for mildly ill-posed problems, it
298   approximates the identity. In highly ill-posed cases, however, A† A loses significant information,
299   making it unsuitable as a fidelity term. To address this, we approximate A† A as the identity, leading
300   to the simplified fidelity term ∥x̃∗ − x̂∥22 in posterior sampling, where x becomes xt . This enables
301   applying DPS Theorem 1, resulting in ||x̃∗ − x̂0|t ||2 .
302
303
      The difference between LP i and LP iAC lies in two aspects: LP i uses A† y as a minimum norm
      solution, while LP iAC employs a TV-regularized solution. Additionally, LP i directly applies A† A,
304
      whereas LP iAC approximates it as the identity. As a result, LP i evaluates fidelity within the projected
305
      space, while LP iAC accounts for the entire space, including the null space filled by TV regularization.
306   Our Dilack sacrifices the aspect of measuring in the projected spaces for highly ill-posed inverse
307   problems, but instead we approximate it by comparing the values in the null space that was filled by
308   TV regularization.

343   Dynamic ROI mask design. Model-based optimization approaches in PiAC fidelity (Boyd et al.,
344   2011; Beck & Teboulle, 2009) enhance or reduce fidelity point-wise across regions. Integrating
345
      diffusion priors adds complexity, as their global application during sampling may overfit noise and
      artifacts in x̃∗ , degrading realism. This occurs because x̃∗ is sparsely updated G − 1 times or remains
346
      static when G = 1. To address this, we introduced ROI masking to selectively control these effects,
347
      balancing fidelity and perceptual quality while leveraging the generative strengths of the diffusion
348   model. To compute the ROI and adaptively mitigate the local effects of LP iAC , we construct a
349   2D binary mask Mt , which is dynamically updated at each iteration t. Let the TV-regularized
350   solution x̃∗ and the current sampled image x̂0|t , each with dimensions H × W , be divided into ψ × ψ
351   non-overlapping patches. In our implementation, we set ψ = 32, resulting in N = H × W /ψ 2 = 64
352   patches for H = W = 256. Define the indices of the patches on the image grid as i, j, where
353   i, j ∈ {1, . . . , H/ψ}. Let P (i,j) denote the patch located at the (i, j)-th position of the image
354                                                                           (i,j)

      Limitation. Dilack employs a model-based algorithm iteration and ROI mask calculation, resulting in
525   slightly slower processing times (DPS: 340 seconds vs. Dilack: 390 seconds), despite its demonstrated
526   effectiveness (Appendix F.1). Nonetheless, we challenge the assumption that the latest diffusion
527   models are universally optimal for most image inverse problems. In the context of modern imaging
528   systems, their limitations are evident. As the first study to explore zero-shot diffusion models in this
529   domain, our work offers a fresh perspective. Addressing efficiency concerns will be a focus of future
530   work. Next-generation ultra-high-definition camera technology, capable of operating without a lens
531   or under large motion blur effects, remains largely unexplored. Our research serves as a foundational
532   study, laying the groundwork for future advancements in this field.

534   6     C ONCLUSION
535
536   Existing zero-shot diffusion models for IR struggle with large and complex kernel degradations. To
537   address these challenges, we propose Dilack, which revisits classical optimization and introduces
538   a novel masked data fidelity with skip step-guided, ROI masked PiAC loss. This approach ensures
539   localized regularization, improves local fidelity in each iteration, and thus delivers robust, realistic
      image restoration results in modern lensless imaging and large motion deblurring.

      The Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) breaks down complex optimization
      problems into simpler subproblems, speeding up solutions and enhancing flexibility. By incorporating
853

854

932   Mask-based lensless camera. A lensless camera is a new class of compact and low-cost imaging
933   devices based on computational image reconstruction. Instead of using a lens, a lensless camera
934   uses a phase mask placed in front of an image sensor, achieving ultra-thin designs by reducing
935   the thickness of the lens and the focal length of the imaging system. Because the mask randomly
936   modulates the incident light from the scene, the encoded intensity information of the scene should be
937
      recovered through computational processing of the raw measurement that is otherwise unidentifiable.
      Additionally, lensless imaging with 2D PSFs decouples the one-to-one mapping between each position
938
      in the scene and the sensor pixels, enabling single-shot multiplexed measurements without using
939
      superpixels. Along with their miniaturization and multiplexing capabilities, various applications
940   based on lensless imaging have been widely explored recently, including depth imaging(Antipa
941   et al., 2018; Adams et al., 2022), hyperspectral imaging(Monakhova et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2023),
942   high-speed imaging(Chan et al., 2023), polarization imaging (Baek et al., 2022), and wavefront
943   sensing(Wu et al., 2024).
944
      Advantages: lens camera vs. lensless camera. Lensless cameras are typically smaller and lighter,
945
      making them suitable for applications where space and weight are critical. Without the need for
946   expensive lens assemblies, lensless cameras can be more cost-effective to produce. They can naturally
947   have a very wide field of view without the distortion issues often associated with wide-angle lenses in
948   traditional cameras. With fewer moving parts and no glass lenses, lensless cameras are more robust
949   and less susceptible to damage. Furthermore, lensless cameras can utilize advanced computational
950   algorithms to reconstruct images, potentially leading to new imaging capabilities and applications.
951   They are crucial in the development of next-generation technologies, such as ultra-thin sensors and
952   integration into compact electronic devices (Zeng & Lam, 2021; Boominathan et al., 2022; Lee et al.,
953   2023).

1525

1533   images slightly deteriorated, likely due to saturation in the TV-regularized optimization process
1534

1535
       induced noise. As our work is the first to address such highly ill-posed kernel degradation problems
1536
       using zero-shot diffusion models, we believe this study represents an important first step. Specifically,
1537   in Case 3, increasing the re-initializing count G reduced fidelity in real-world problems, unlike in
1538   synthetic conditions. This indicates that while adjusting G may be beneficial in Sec. G.6 on the
1539   synthetic lensless imaging dataset, it poses challenges in more complex, real-world scenarios.

1595   G.15     R EGARDING THE POTENTIAL APPLICABILITY TO OTHER TASKS
1596
       For super-resolution (SR), the A matrix consists of a combination of blur kernels and downsampling
1597
       operations. As the degree of downsampling increases, the condition number becomes larger, present-
1598
       ing a challenging scenario where the strengths of our proposed method are expected to be effective.
1599   However, highly ill-posed SR has not been well investigated in the field yet, so we believe that we
1600   need to carefully validate one by one. Especially, the impact of downsampling operator is worth
1601   investigating. Thus, at this moment, we can say that our Dilack can work for SR as compared to other
1602   prior works, but there is still room for improvement due to the reasons mentioned. The qualitative
1603   results of toy experiments for SR on the FFHQ dataset can be found in Fig. S14, which are the results
1604   of our additional experiments with SR x4, x8.
1605   For gaussian denoising, it may be difficult to expect the applicability of our Dilack since highly
1606   ill-posed cases with high noise levels have different ill-posedness from other tasks with kernels. Full
1607   investigation will be needed for these cases. Nonetheless, under Gaussian noise levels (σ = 0.05
1608   to 0.1), our method performs comparable to DPS, though a more comprehensive investigation is
1609   required for these cases as a future work. The qualitative results of toy experiments for denoising on
1610   the FFHQ dataset can be found in Fig. S15, which are the results of our additional experiments.
