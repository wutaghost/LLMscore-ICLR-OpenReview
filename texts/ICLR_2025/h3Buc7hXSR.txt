009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent symmetry-based methods on variational autoencoders have advanced disen-
012            tanglement learning and combinatorial generalization, yet the appropriate symmetry
013            representation for both tasks is under-clarified. We identify that existing methods
014            struggle with maintaining the consistent symmetries when representing identical
015            changes of latent factors of variation, and they cause issues in achieving equivari-
016
               ance. We theoretically prove the limitations of three frequently used group settings:
               matrix multiplication with General Linear groups, defining group action with set of
017
               vectors and vector addition, and cyclic groups modeled through surjective functions.
018
               To overcome these issues, we introduce a novel method of conformal mapping
019            of latent vectors into a complex number space, ensuring consistent symmetries
020            and cyclic semantics. Through empirical validation with ground truth of factors
021            variation for transparent analysis, this study fills two significant gaps in the litera-
022            ture: 1) the inductive bias to enhance disentanglement learning and combinatorial
023            generalization simultaneously, and 2) well-represented symmetries ensure signif-
024            icantly high disentanglement performance without a trade-off in reconstruction
025            error, compared to current unsupervised methods. Additionally, we introduce less
026            guidance-dependent validation results, extending our findings to more practical use.
027
               Our research highlights the significant impact of verifying consistent symmetry
               and suggests required future research for advancing combinatorial generalization
028
               and disentanglement learning.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Combinatorial Generalization (Montero et al., 2021) and disentanglement learning (Bengio et al.,
034   2013) have been studied for a common objective of constructing inductive bias for latent factors
035   of variation, mainly discussed on variational autoencoders (VAEs) in Montero et al. (2022; 2021);
036   Schott et al. (2022) and Kingma & Welling (2013); Chen et al. (2018); Kim & Mnih (2018); Keller
037   & Welling (2021a); Jeong & Song (2019); Keller & Welling (2021b); Shao et al. (2020; 2022),
038   respectively with dimension-wise disentangled representation (Wang et al., 2023). The importance of
039
      incorporating symmetries in latent vectors, grounded in group theory, has been highlighted in Higgins
      et al. (2018; 2022); Huh et al. (2023). Moreover, the development of symmetry-based methods has
040
      significantly enhanced performance, as demonstrated in Zhu et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2021); Keurti
041
      et al. (2023); Tonnaer et al. (2022b); Jung et al. (2024); Hwang et al. (2023).
042
043   These studies have primarily focused on incorporating diverse symmetries into models. For example,
044   in disentanglement learning, the Lie group-based works (Zhu et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2024; Keurti
045
      et al., 2023) represent the group as a matrix exponential that acts on the set of latent vectors with
      matrix multiplication. Another approach implements symmetries with the pre-defined groups such as
046
      SO(n), SE(n), and O(n) (Winter et al., 2022). Other branches based on cyclic group representations
047
      employ a mapping function that projects real number vectors onto a unit circle using a surjective
048   function (Yang et al., 2021; Tonnaer et al., 2022b; Cha & Thiyagalingam, 2023). In combinatorial
049   generalization, MAGANet (Hwang et al., 2023) derives symmetry representations through vector
050   subtraction. However, these works often overlook the critical task of verifying their equivariance,
051   which is essential for capturing all symmetries in latent space. In particular, the equivariance, which
052   manifests as consistent symmetries, is insufficiently induced for identical change of latent factors
053   of the variation that implies the same semantic change, as empirically demonstrated in Hwang et al.
      (2023).

054
      In this regard, we address limitations in representing the consistent symmetries for the same variations.
055   First, we identify and prove the limitations caused by three conditions of group settings: 1) the use
056   of the General Linear group with matrix exponential for matrix multiplication, 2) vector addition is
057   used to define a group action for cyclic semantics, and 3) surjective mapping from latent vector to
058   unit circle. Subsequently, we propose conformal mapping of latent vectors to discretized and cyclic
059   representations that guarantee consistent transformations for all pairs of adjacent points. To guarantee
060   consistency among the mapped representations, we arrange them on a shared, fixed grid (codebook),
061   selecting their nearest codes and performing translations from the canonical point. In the final step,
062   the two points on the grid corresponding to two samples are adjusted to maintain symmetry for the
063
      given latent factors of variation in the data.
064   Through empirical analysis of the consistent symmetries for the ground truths of latent factors of
065   variation in the given data, we aim to answer two important questions that have not yet been clarified
066   in the literature. Firstly, this study queries whether the inductive bias associated with combinatorial
067   generalization and disentanglement learning enhances each performance simultaneously. Secondly,
068   it examines the efficacy of inductive biases achieved through refined symmetry expressions in
069
      disentanglement learning. These studies highlight the impact of correctly expressing symmetries as
      an inductive bias, suggesting a necessary direction for this field. In addition, we also explore the
070
      potential of these approaches under more practical conditions, reducing the reliance on ground truth.
071
072   Our main contributions are:
073
            1. identifying the difficulty of representing consistent symmetries for the identical change of
074
               latent factors of variation in current disentanglement learning and combinatorial generaliza-
075
               tion,
076
077
            2. proposing an expression method using conformal mapping to a space that guarantees
               consistency of general symmetries and even for cyclic semantics,
078
079         3. providing a learning method for inducing equivariance while maintaining the consistent
080            symmetries,
081         4. empirically validating that disentangled representations and combinatorial generalization
082            are improved simultaneously, and investigating achievable disentanglement learning perfor-
083            mance without a trade-off of reconstruction error through learning appropriate symmetry
084            representation.

132   2.2   D IFFICULTY OF C ONSISTENT S YMMETRY FOR D ISENTANGLED R EPRESENTATION
133
134   The purpose of disentanglement learning with symmetries (gz ∈ Gz ) is to change a single dimension
135   value of latent vectors as symmetries act on the latent vectors. However, there is a lack of sufficient
136   theoretical discourse on which symmetries are suitable for disentangled representations. In this
137   section, we show the difficulty of previous works for consistent symmetry such that 1) only the
138
      identity element represents consistent symmetries for cyclic semantics of the dataset with disentangled
      representations (cases 1 and 2), and 2) symmetry information is not preserved (case 3).
139
140
      Case 1: A Limitation of GL(n) General Linear group GL(n), used in prior works (Kuzina et al.,
141
      2022; Miyato et al., 2022; Marchetti et al., 2023a), is limited in representing the consistent symmetries
142   for disentangled representation. We first show the property of disentangled representation with matrix
143   exponential. Then we show the limitation of GL(n).
144

145
      Linear group that implemented with matrix exponential, where GL′ (n) = {eM |M ∈ Rn×n }, g k is
146
      an element of GL′ (n), and g = k g k . Then eg z = eIgz + v ′ .

516   Disentanglement Learning The initially proposed methods, such as Higgins et al. (2017); Chen
517   et al. (2018); Kim & Mnih (2018), partition each dimension to ensure mutual exclusivity, employing
518   measures like mutual information or total correlation. However, these approaches do not account
519   for the symmetry structure of the dataset space. Defining the symmetry group and group action as
520   a General Linear group and matrix multiplication (Zhu et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2024; Marchetti
521
      et al., 2023b) enhances disentanglement performance. Nevertheless, we theoretically demonstrate
      the limitations of the General Linear group for cyclic semantics in the disentangled space. Other
522
      works commonly define the symmetry group acting on the latent vector space as a cyclic group with
523
      surjective functions (Yang et al., 2021; Keurti et al., 2023; Falorsi et al., 2018). Differently, our focus
524   is on employing isomorphism to represent the cyclic group rather than a homomorphism.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we address the limitations in expressing consistent symmetry for identical semantic
531
      changes, demonstrating three conditions of group settings causing them in existing methods of
      disentanglement learning and combinatorial generalization with VAEs. We propose a conformal
532
      mapping of latent vectors to a space specifically designed to guarantee consistency, along with its
533
      learning methodology. This work aims to fill theoretical gaps in the literature by conducting empirical
534   analyses based on given ground truths concerning latent factors of variation. To the best of our
535   knowledge, we are the first to propose a method that enhances combinatorial generalization and
536   disentanglement learning simultaneously. Moreover, the consistent symmetry significantly enhances
537   disentanglement scores for VAEs without compromising reconstruction error, thereby identifying
538   achievable performance for unsupervised methods in case accurate estimation of factor variation
539   is supported. We believe this work indicates a promising direction for constructing more effective
      inductive biases for disentangled representation in practical generative models.

979
       Definition A.15. Let (G, ·), (H, ◦) be two groups. If mapping function Γ : G → H, s.t. Γ(gi · gj ) =
980    Γ(gi ) ◦ Γ(gj ), then Γ is called homomorphism.
981
982
       Definition A.16. Let Γ is surjective then Γ is called endomorphism.
983    Definition A.17. Let a special case of homomorphism where Γ is bijective is called isomorphism.
984    Corollary A.18. By the equivariant and surjective function b : Z → Y, the capacity of Z and Y
985    is |Z| ≥ |Y| then Γ is an endomorphism because |GZ | ≥ |GY |. On the other hand, isomorphism
986    identically maps the two spaces (|GZ | = |GY |).
987
988    Therefore, if b is surjective and not injective, then there exists at least one case where Γ′ (gi ) = Γ′ (gj ).
989    It implies that loss of symmetry structure occurs with a surjective function.
