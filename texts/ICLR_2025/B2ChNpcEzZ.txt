009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing approaches leveraging large pretrained vision-language models (VLMs)
012            like CLIP for zero-shot text-image classification often focus on generating fine-
013            grained class-specific descriptors, leaving higher-order semantic relations between
014            classes underutilized. We address this gap by proposing DefiNed Taxonomic
015            Stratification (DefNTaxS), a novel and malleable framework that supplements
016            per-class descriptors with inter-class taxonomies to enrich semantic resolution
017
               in zero-shot classification tasks. Using large language models (LLMs), DefN-
               TaxS automatically generates subcategories that group similar classes and appends
018
               context-specific prompt elements for each dataset/subcategory, reducing inter-
019
               class competition and providing deeper semantic insight. This process is fully
020            automated, requiring no manual modifications or further training for any of the
021            models involved. We demonstrate that DefNTaxS yields consistent performance
022            gains across a number of datasets often used to benchmark these frameworks, en-
023            hancing accuracy and semantic interpretability in zero-shot classification tasks of
024            varying scale, granularity, and type.

052   The rise of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has transformed
053   zero-shot text-image classification by learning shared representations between visual content and
      textual descriptions. These models effectively align multimodal data, enabling quick classification of

054
      images based on text prompts without additional training. However, their performance heavily relies
055   on the specificity of these prompts, often making it challenging to distinguish between semantically
056   similar classes.
057
058
      To address this, recent approaches like (Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Pratt et al., 2023; Novack et al.,
      2023) have used Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate detailed class-specific descriptors, en-
059
      hancing text-image alignment. WaffleCLIP Roth et al. (2023) achieves similar accuracy to D-CLIP
060
      by replacing LLM-generated descriptors with random words, highlighting that high-level semantic
061   concepts from LLMs enhance classification more effectively than fine-grained details. CuPL (Pratt
062   et al., 2023) also uses LLMs for generating descriptors, but while D-CLIP enforces a structured list
063   of identifying features to improve explainability, it may reduce classification performance; CuPL, in
064   contrast, employs multiple free-form prompts to capture nuanced category information, resulting in
065   improved accuracy. CHiLS (Novack et al., 2023) takes a different approach by refining class labels
066   into finer-grained subclasses, using either existing label hierarchies or LLMs like GPT-3 to generate
067   linguistic hyponyms, whereas our work clusters related classes into broader taxonomic groups to
068   streamline classification and reduce competition among similar classes. MPVR (Mirza et al., 2024)
069
      leverages LLMs to automate the creation of diverse, category-specific prompts for zero-shot image
      recognition based on minimal input such as task descriptions and class labels. While effective to
070
      some extent, these methods face limitations: (1) They overly focus on fine-grained details, neglect-
071
      ing medium- and coarse-grained semantics that provide crucial context. (2) Fine-grained descriptors
072   can introduce noise and ambiguity, reducing interpretability and leading to misclassifications. (3) A
073   lack of structured semantic hierarchy amplifies competition between similar classes, particularly in
074   datasets with high intra-class similarity.
075
      Motivated by viewing zero-shot classification through the lens of competition among classes, we
076
      argue that the goal is not to find the “best” descriptor for a class, but rather the “most distinctive”
077
      one. This perspective aligns with the idea that classes should not directly compete with one another
078   in a complex, high-dimensional space. Instead, effective differentiation can be achieved by grouping
079   classes within a structured hierarchy, leveraging taxonomic relationships to enhance clarity. By
080   working together within this framework and establishing distinctions at multiple levels of resolution,
081   classes can reduce inter-class competition and improve classification accuracy.
082
      To this end, we propose Defined Taxonomic Stratification (DefNTaxS), a novel approach designed
083
      to overcome the limitations of existing methods by incorporating taxonomy classes directly into
084   the text prompts of CLIP. Our method uses LLMs to analyze the classes within a dataset and pro-
085   pose taxonomic groupings based on shared semantic relationships, creating a hierarchical classifica-
086   tion framework. DefNTaxS consistently outperforms existing methods across all evaluated datasets,
087   showcasing its effectiveness in zero-shot classification tasks. Additionally, it reveals and organizes
088   the underlying structure of the CLIP embedding space, offering a semantically structured view that
089   clarifies how classes are organized and differentiated within the hierarchy.

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   Zero-shot Image Classification using VLMs. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) (Jia et al., 2021;
095   Kim et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Cho et al.,
096   2021; Li et al., 2023; Naeem et al., 2023) learn a joint representation that aligns visual content with
097   associated textual descriptions in a shared embedding space. This learned alignment allows VLMs
098   to perform effectively on zero-shot image classification tasks, where they rely on textual cues, such
099
      as class labels, to classify novel image categories without prior exposure during training. Notably,
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has emerged as a prominent approach for learning multimodal repre-
100
      sentations that align visual and textual information within a shared embedding space. The model
101
      utilizes a dual-encoder architecture, with separate encoders for image and text modalities, trained
102   through contrastive learning to maximize the similarity between matching image-text pairs and min-
103   imize it for non-matching pairs. Each encoder can have a different backbone. At inference, CLIP
104   uses prompts like “a photo of a [class name]” providing context for classification and enabling
105   zero-shot transfer to various tasks without task-specific fine-tuning. Subsequent works, such as
106   FLAVA (Singh et al., 2022), Florence (Yuan et al., 2021), and BLIP (Li et al., 2022), have built
107   upon the CLIP paradigm and advanced multimodal representation learning. Florence enhances this
      learning by leveraging a significantly larger and more diverse pre-training dataset. FLAVA focuses


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on novel training objectives beyond contrastive learning, such as masked image modeling combined
109   with contrastive loss, to improve multimodal understanding. Meanwhile, BLIP incorporates a re-
110   fined model architecture that better integrates visual and linguistic features for more effective joint
111   representation. VLM research follows two main pipelines: visual prompting and text prompting.
112   Visual prompting enhances performance by processing or aligning visual inputs with textual repre-
113   sentations, while text prompting focuses on refining textual descriptors Li et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
114   (2024). Our work adopts an exclusively text-based approach, leaving the images, model weights,
115   and embeddings unaltered.
116   Training-free textual prompting in VLMs. While CLIP demonstrates strong zero-shot capabil-
117   ities, its performance in downstream tasks is significantly affected by prompt choice, as noted by
118   (Radford et al., 2021) and (Zhou et al., 2022). (Zhou et al., 2022) specifically point out that finding
119   the optimal prompt is a complex and time-consuming process, often requiring prompt tuning. How-
120   ever, with the rise of large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3 (Brown, 2020), new approaches
121   (Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Pratt et al., 2023) have emerged to enhance CLIP’s zero-shot generaliza-
122   tion by leveraging LLMs. Rather than relying on handcrafted templates to generate class features,
123
      these methods utilize LLMs to create high-level concepts, class descriptions resulting in enriched
      text features and improved performance. D-CLIP (Menon & Vondrick, 2023) demonstrated that
124
      leveraging the knowledge embedded in LLMs to automatically generate class-specific descriptions
125
      that focus on the discriminating features of image categories can enhance zero-shot classification.
126   WaffleCLIP (Roth et al., 2023) achieves the same accuracy as D-CLIP by replacing LLM-generated
127   descriptors with random words. It highlights that high-level semantic concepts from LLMs improve
128   classification more effectively than fine-grained details. CuPL (Pratt et al., 2023) also uses LLMs
129   for generating descriptors, but D-CLIP enforces a structured list of identifying features, enhanc-
130   ing explainability but potentially reducing classification performance. In contrast, CuPL generates
131   multiple, free-form prompts to better capture the nuances of each category, resulting in improved
132   accuracy. CHiLS (Novack et al., 2023) refines class labels into finer-grained subclasses by lever-
133   aging either existing label hierarchies or large language models like GPT-3 to generate linguistic
134
      hyponyms for each class. In our work, we also consider the taxonomy of classes but take the oppo-
      site approach—by clustering related classes into broader taxonomic groups to reduce competition
135
      among similar classes and streamline classification. MPVR (Mirza et al., 2024) automates the cre-
136
      ation of category-specific prompts for zero-shot image recognition by leveraging LLMs to generate
137   diverse prompts based on minimal input, such as a task description and class labels. Another study
138   Ren et al. (2024) addresses zero-shot classification by constructing a class hierarchy through iter-
139   ative k-means clustering and LLM-generated descriptions; in contrast, our work avoids clustering
140   and iterative refinement, offering a more efficient, single-stage framework with enhanced semantic
141   interpretability through directly leveraging inter-class taxonomies.

149

150
      essary competition amongst classes. This problem arises when each class is considered in direct
151
      competition with all other classes, which can result in misclassification, particularly for classes with
152   overlapping semantics. To address this, our process begins by using the LLM to analyze the classes
153   in the dataset and propose a set of taxonomic classes. These taxonomic classes are designed to
154   cluster classes based on shared semantic relationships, thereby minimizing the competition between
155   classes with similar characteristics. For instance, classes such as “forks,” “knives,” and “spoons”
156   might all be grouped under a broader taxonomic class like “kitchen utensils.”

      3.2   D ESCRIPTORS WITH SEMANTIC CONTEXT
217
218   Once the list of potential taxonomic classes Tc is finalized, we iteratively use the LLM to allocate
219   each individual class c ∈ C to one of the taxonomic classes ti ∈ Tc . This allocation provides
220   a semantic context for each class based on its shared relationships with other classes within the
221   subcategory. Consequently, each class c is not only associated with its specific textual descriptors
222   Dc = {d1 , d2 , ..., d|Dc | }, but also with the broader context of its taxonomic class t.
223   To effectively encode this semantic information into a text prompt, we construct a structured text
224   representation for each class c. Inspired by CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which classifies a query
225   image x by finding the category c ∈ C that maximizes the cosine similarity between its image
226   embedding ϕI (x) and its textual prompt embedding ϕL (f (c)), where f (c) = “A photo of a {c}”,
227   our approach enhances this structure. Specifically, D-CLIP (Menon & Vondrick, 2023) introduces
228   a richer set of descriptors Dc , using prompts of the form f (c, d) = ”{c} which is/has/etc {d}” to
229   better capture the visual characteristics of each category. The classification score of D-CLIP is
230
      computed by averaging the similarity between the image embedding and all descriptor embeddings:

236   the corresponding taxonomic class ti ∈ Tc is included in the textual prompt to provide a broader

376   nario, we conduct an ablation study where the taxonomic refinement process is limited to a single
377   iteration, resulting in subcategories with 100 or more classes. We also conducted various studies
      of gradually increasing the number of taxonomic subcategories, but as the numbers purely act as

461
462   In our initial investigation, we analyzed the prompt structure of D-CLIP (Menon & Vondrick, 2023),
463   which follows the format “ci which has/is di ,” where ci represents the class and di the descriptor.
464
      We explored how the arrangement of these elements influences classification performance.
465   We first tested reversing the positions of the class and descriptor, using the structure “di , which is
466   a description of a ci .” This modification aimed to prioritize the descriptors over the class, based on
467   findings that initial tokens in a prompt have greater weight in embedding space (Han et al., 2024;
468   Kazemnejad et al., 2024). However, this change resulted in reduced accuracy, showing that the
469   model performs better when the class is positioned at the start of the prompt.
470   Next, we added prefixes such as “An image of ...” before the class label, restructuring the prompt as
471   “An image of a ci , which has/is di .” This modification also decreased accuracy, as the filler content
472   shifted focus away from the class. The model consistently performed better when the class was
473   positioned at the start of the prompt without additional prefixes. However, one notable exception
474   was observed with domain-specific templates, such as the BirdSnap template. Using a structure
475   like “a photo of a class label, a type of bird,” tailored for bird classification, significantly improved
476
      accuracy, even surpassing the baseline D-CLIP performance. This indicates that carefully designed,
      domain-specific templates can be beneficial despite generally negative effects of filler content.
477
478   We also experimented with simplifying class names to focus on broad categories. For instance,
479   “Red-winged Blackbird” was simplified to “Blackbird,” relying on the descriptor to distinguish
480   between similar classes. This approach significantly reduced accuracy, as it removed distinctive
481   features from the class name and increased dependence on the descriptors, which were often insuf-
482
      ficiently detailed for fine-grained distinctions.
483   In an extreme experiment, we eliminated the class name entirely, constructing prompts solely with
484   descriptors. This approach caused a sharp decline in accuracy, highlighting the critical role of class
485   labels in guiding the model to differentiate between categories effectively. Without class names, the
      model struggled to perform reliable classification, even with detailed descriptors.
