009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The training and fine-tuning of large language models (LLMs) heavily rely on a
012
               large corpus of high-quality data. Nevertheless, the internet’s extensive data is of-
013
               ten of varying quality, and collecting high-quality data is exceedingly expensive.
014            To facilitate data engineering and trading, the quantification of data value, also
015            known as data valuation, is emerging as a critical topic. Traditional approaches
016            for data valuation typically depend on model retraining. However, with the in-
017            creasing model sizes and expansive data volumes of LLMs, these conventional
018            methods are encountering significant declines in valuation precision, efficiency,
019            and transferability. To alleviate these problems, we propose NESTLE, which is
020            an efficient and robust framework for data valuation of LLMs. To accurately es-
021            timate the data value distribution across different target domains, we develop a
022
               training-free mechanism based on gradient tracing to simulate the data influences.
               To further tackle the dynamical value adjustment when multiple data providers co-
023
               exist, we draw inspiration from the Shapley value theory and devise an accelerated
024
               strategy for estimating marginal contributions of data through gradient additivity.
025            Extensive experiments demonstrate that our proposed framework NESTLE is ca-
026            pable of accurately and robustly providing accurate estimates of data value with a
027            minuscule cost across a wide range of real-world scenarios.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   With the ongoing evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) Touvron et al. (2023); Achiam
034   et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023), it has become a common paradigm to finetune
035   these LLMs with domain-specific data to align and enhance their downstream performances Zhou
036   et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023), which largely hinges on large-scale, high-quality training data.
037   However, the quality of publicly available data on the internet varies significantly, and the collection
038   and curation of standard-compliant data are extremely time-consuming and labor-intensive Ghorbani
039
      & Zou (2019). To this end, data valuation Schoch et al. (2022); Ghorbani et al. (2020), which aims to
      estimate the worth of data from different sources, has gained considerable focus from the community
040
      and has been broadly utilized in real-world applications like model adaptation Jiang et al. (2023a)
041
      and data trading Agarwal et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2023a).
042
043   Traditional data valuation approaches can be roughly grouped into marginal-contribution-based
044   Ghorbani & Zou (2019); Jia et al. (2019) and influence-based Park et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023a);
045
      Pruthi et al. (2020). The former marginal-contribution-based ones typically assess the worth of
      data by quantifying the data contributions for different marginal subsets Schoch et al. (2022). The
046
      Leave-One-Out (LOO) Jia et al. (2019) strategy is achieved by tracking performance variations once
047
      different data sources are removed. Among these methods, the most prevalent paradigm is driven
048   by the Shapley Value (SV) theory Shapley (1953); Schoch et al. (2022), which suggests arranging
049   all possible subsets to assess the contribution of a specific data source (i.e., the SV for provider i
050   can be expressed as ES⊆N \i [U (S ∪ i) − U (S)] where S represents the collection of all possible
051   subsets). Despite the success, these approaches can be computationally intensive, especially for
052   large model sizes and expansive data volumes in the era of LLMs. These methods involve an atomic
053   procedure of cumbersome retraining and testing for every data sources, and the SV manners even
      require (2N − 1) groups of such complicated atomic experiments for N data providers.

054
      On the other hand, another line of research relies on estimating the data influence Jiang et al.
055   (2023a); Park et al. (2023) to alleviate the valuation cost for the marginal-contribution-based ones.
056   These methods expedite the estimation of Shapley values by tracing gradient calculationsJiang et al.
057   (2023a) or by assessing client contributions Tastan et al. (2024) during federated training processes.
058   Nevertheless, these approaches either necessitate high-cost federated training across various data
059   providers Koh & Liang (2017) or yield inaccurate estimates Jiang et al. (2023b), particularly in the
060   presence of coexisting multiple data sources. Hence, such influnce-based approaches are not readily
061   transferable to the data valuation of LLMs in multi-domain, multi-data source scenarios. It remains
062   a challenge how to systematically estimate the value of different data sources for LLMs.
063   Motivated by this, we propose aN Efficient and robuSt daTa vaLuation framEwork, NESTLE, for
064   LLMs in this work. Our primary target is to accurately and efficiently estimate the data value distri-
065   bution of different data providers across different target domains. To achieve this, we first draw in-
066   spiration from traditional influence-based methods and then develop a training-free valuation mech-
067   anism that simulates the data influences in different domains based on gradient tracing. Specifically,
068   we first maintain a customized support set for each of the target domains, which functions as the
069
      standard for data value estimation. Our core intuition is that the data value can be reflected by the
      decrease in the loss on the support set after the LLM is trained on the to-be-valuated data. We
070
      theoretically find that such a data influence can be further represented and formalized as the inner
071
      product of the gradient of the valuated data and that of the support set. Based on this theoretical
072   foundation, we design our fundamental valuation framework, which consists of two steps: support
073   data gradient caching and valuation data gradient querying. We further incorporate optimization
074   strategies of gradient projection and gradient calibration to reduce the valuation cost and bolster its
075   theoretical credibility. Empirically, such a framework can successfully and accurately estimate the
076   value distribution of a single data source across different target domains.
077
      Despite the success, such a framework fails to dynamically adjust the value distribution when mul-
078   tiple data providers coexist, for example, two data providers with highly similar data need to be
079   penalized. To address this, we aim to harness the traditional Shapley value theory’s intuition of
080   marginal contribution to assess the interplay of multiple data providers by evaluating the marginal
081   contributions of different subsets. Nevertheless, traditional Shapley value methods adopt perfor-
082   mance metrics as the utility function and necessitate training for each marginal subset, resulting in
083   exponential time complexity as the number of data providers increases. In contrast, our approach
084   naturally evaluates value based on gradient sums rather than metrics. The additive nature of gradi-
085   ents compared to performance metrics can significantly reduce computational costs. By leveraging
086
      the additivity of gradients as a utility function in combination with the Shapley value, we can main-
      tain the original linear time complexity without incurring exponential time costs. Extensive exper-
087
      iments demonstrate that our accelerated NESTLE framework can accurately adjust the data value
088
      distribution when multiple data providers coexist, while the time consumption is only 1.25% of the
089   traditional ground-truth Shapley value. The contributions of this work are as follows.
090
091          • We delineate the task formulation, core requirements, and challenges of data value estima-
092            tion for LLMs in multi-domain, multi-source scenarios. Then we proposed a training-free
093            framework NESTLE based on gradient tracking to address these issues.
094          • Our framework estimates data value by tracking gradient inner product of support and valu-
095            ation data. We further designed an accelerated Shapley-based valuation strategy, leveraging
096            the additivity of gradients to handle dynamic value adjustments in multi-source scenarios.
097          • We conduct extensive experiments and demonstrate that our proposed framework can effi-
098            ciently and accurately perform data value estimation across different task scenarios.

102   Traditional Data Valuation. Measuring the value of training data is a pivotal theme in machine
103   learning Sim et al. (2022); Ghorbani et al. (2020); Schoch et al. (2022), which focuses on assess-
104   ing the worth of different data sources. It is broadly utilized in the applications of data trading
105   Agarwal et al. (2019) and pricing Jiang et al. (2023a). Existing data valuation methods in machine
106   learning can be mainly categorized into two types, marginal-contribution-based Ghorbani & Zou
107   (2019); Jia et al. (2019) and influence-based Park et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023a); Pruthi et al.
      (2020). The former marginal-contribution-based approaches estimate the data worth by computing

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      its marginal performance improvement when the to-be-valuated data is incorporated across different
109   subsets Schoch et al. (2022). This can be achieved through computational paradigms such as the
110   leave-one-out (LOO) Jia et al. (2019) and Shapley value Shapley (1953); Schoch et al. (2022). The
111   latter method relies on data influence Koh & Liang (2017); Jiang et al. (2023a); Park et al. (2023)
112   and aims to assess the impact of adding or removing specific data segments on model training Koh
113   & Liang (2017). However, these approaches can lead to exceedingly unaffordable costs of training.
114
115   Federated Client Contribution Estimation Another relevant setting is the estimation of client
116   contributions in federated learning Tastan et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2020), which
117   focuses on designing principled mechanisms to assess the contributions of individual participants
118   from the standpoint of machine learning fairness Oneto & Chiappa (2019); Ghani et al. (2023).
119   These methods predominantly build on Shapley value Koh & Liang (2017) and aim to develop more
120
      efficient estimation mechanisms Wang et al. (2020) of that.
121
122
      Data Selection for Large Language Models The emergence of large language models (LLMs)
      has prompted initial research into exploring the significance of data quality during model fine-tuning
123
      Zhou et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024c); Chen et al. (2024); Xia et al. (2024). To enhance the quality
124
      of training data, the topic of data selection is receiving increasing attention Li et al. (2024b); Du
125   et al. (2023); Ge et al. (2024). Some preliminary approaches are designed to filter high-quality
126   training data through different perspectives such as difficulty Li et al. (2024b), quality Li et al.
127   (2024a), diversity Ge et al. (2024), and necessity Du et al. (2023). The enhanced quality of the
128   selected data then facilitates model training with improved performance and reduced training cost
129   Xia et al. (2024). Some work additionally incorporates a validation set from downstream tasks as
130   criteria to select training data that meet personalized requirements Li et al. (2024d); Xia et al. (2024).
131   However, these data selection methods are primarily tailored to enhance the training efficiency, but
132   fail to finely assess the value distribution of different data sources in various target areas. Thus, it
133
      remains a challenge how to systematically estimate the value of different data sources for LLMs.

206   them, thus proposing a dynamic adjustment mechanism based on Shapley-based approximation.

      Efficiency Analysis The SV method, which relies on performance indicators, requires calculating
262
      the performance of all possible combinations of datasets from n data owners. This involves training a
263
      model on each combination and subsequently evaluating its performance on a validation set. Hence,
264   the time complexity escalates exponentially with the addition of more data owners (i.e., O(2n )).
265   In comparison, our NESTLE first calculates the gradients of different providers, and the gradients
266   between data points are independent. When computing combinations of datasets Dk and Dj , we can
267   treat it as extending dataset Dk by directly summing the representations of datasets Dj and Dk to
268   form the representation of dataset Dkj , thus avoiding redundant gradient calculations for each data
269   point in datasets Dk and Dj . In summary, our method only requires calculating the gradient for each
      data owner, resulting in a time complexity of O(n), which is crucial for the practical application.

295   Evaluation Protocols. As mentioned in Section 3.3, a valuation framework needs to estimate
296   the multi-domain value distribution for a single data provider and dynamically adjust values for
297   coexisting multiple providers. Hence, our evaluation scenarios are categorized into single-source
298   and multi-source scenarios. (i)- Single-source evaluation primarily assesses whether the framework
299   accurately estimates the value distribution for a single data provider across different target domains.
300   (ii)- Multi-source evaluation primarily assesses whether the framework can perform unbiased and
301   fair dynamic adjustments for multiple coexisting data providers within a specific domain.
302
303   Datasets and LLMs. Our proposed framework is evaluated on a variety of datasets. For the
304   single-source setting, the evaluation is conducted at two different cross-domain granularities:
305   coarse-grained and fine-grained. (i)-For coarse-grained cross-domain data, we adopt the fields of
306   Finance, Healthcare, and Law. (ii)-For fine-grained cross-domain data, we adopt three sub-
307   fields under the healthcare domain: Consult Zhu (2023), Medicine, and TCM (traditional Chi-
308
      nese medicine). For the multi-source setting, the evaluation is focused on the consult domains, with
      multiple data providers possessing different to-be-valuated data in this consult field. For each of
309
      these domains and subdomains, we randomly divide 1000 samples to form the support set Dsup ,
310
      and the to-be-valuated data Dvalu are sampled from the remaining samples. Our framework is
311   architecture-agnostic and compatible with any open-source LLMs, hence we mainly select some rep-
312   resentative ones for evaluation, including Llama2-7B, Qwen1.5-7B, and ChatGLM3-6B. We
313   also included explorations on larger models of the Qwen series LLMs, including Qwen1.5-14B
314   and Qwen1.5-32B. The complete statistics can be found in Appendix.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we propose a gradient-based data valuation framework, NESTLE, for different down-
478
      stream fine-tuning tasks of LLMs. We first find that traditional data valuation methods typically
479
      rely on cumbersome re-training and can be distorted. Thus we present our training-free valuation
480   algorithm based on gradient tracing to accurately and efficiently estimate the data value distribution
481   across different target domains. To further address the potential dynamic adjustments in multi-
482   source scenarios, we combine our grading tracing mechanisms with Shapley value (SV) theory with
483   the additivity of gradients, effectively evaluating the contribution of each data provider. Extensive
484   experiments demonstrate that our accelerated Shapley-based gradient estimation can accurately ad-
485   just the data value distribution, while requiring very little calculation cost. Future endeavors could
      explore the connection between value estimation and data scaling law.
