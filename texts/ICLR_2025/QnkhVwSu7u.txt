011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning (RL) has demonstrated compelling performance in
014            robotic tasks, but its success often hinges on the design of complex, ad hoc re-
015            ward functions. Researchers have explored how Large Language Models (LLMs)
016            could enable non-expert users to specify reward functions more easily. However,
017            LLMs struggle to balance the importance of different features, generalize poorly
018
               to out-of-distribution robotic tasks, and cannot represent the problem properly
               with only text-based descriptions. To address these challenges, we propose EL-
019
               EMENTAL (intEractive LEarning froM dEmoNstraTion And Language), a novel
020
               framework that combines natural language guidance with visual user demonstra-
021            tions to align robot behavior with user intentions better. By incorporating visual
022            inputs, ELEMENTAL overcomes the limitations of text-only task specifications,
023            while leveraging inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) to balance feature weights
024            and match the demonstrated behaviors optimally. ELEMENTAL also introduces
025            an iterative feedback-loop through self-reflection to improve feature, reward, and
026            policy learning. Our experiment results demonstrate that ELEMENTAL outper-
027            forms prior work by 42.3% on task success, and achieves 41.3% better general-
028            ization in out-of-distribution tasks, highlighting its robustness in LfD.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Reinforcement Learning (RL) has been shown to be a powerful tool for enabling robots to perform
034   complex tasks across a wide range of domains, from manipulation (Kober et al., 2013; Levine et al.,
035   2016) to navigation (Tai et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2017). However, the effectiveness of RL hinges
036   on the availability of a carefully designed reward function that accurately encapsulates the desired
037   behavior. Without sophisticated reward functions, RL agents often struggle to learn competent poli-
038   cies (Matignon et al., 2006); worse yet, poorly designed reward functions can lead RL agents to
039
      achieve undesirable outcomes (Gupta et al., 2024). Booth et al. (2023) shows reward specification is
      non-trivial even for experts, and designing reward functions that align with end-users’ expectations
040
      is particularly challenging due to the varied and latent user preferences (Abouelazm et al., 2024).
041
042   Considering recent advancements in large models (e.g., large language models (LLMs) and vision-
043   language models (VLMs)) on text understanding (Bommasani et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023)
044   and emergent abilities (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022), researchers have explored utilizing
045
      LLMs for reward engineering. For instance, the EUREKA framework takes as input a text de-
      scription of the task, queries LMs for a draft of the reward function, and trains a policy with the
046
      reward function (Ma et al., 2023). This paradigm, while promising, presents several limitations.
047
      First, describing complex robotic tasks purely through language is imprecise: humans often have
048   latent, unspoken preferences that are difficult to articulate fully, leading to incomplete or ambiguous
049   descriptions of their objectives (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977; Ericsson & Simon, 1980; Hoffman et al.,
050   1995; Feldon, 2007). Second, even if all objective function components are accurately conveyed,
051   determining the relative importance or weights of these components poses another significant chal-
052   lenge: assigning these weights involves subtle mathematical trade-offs, something that LLMs are not
053   particularly equipped to handle. As a result of these limitations, methods like EUREKA struggle to
      generalize well to out-of-distribution tasks.

054
      Given these limitations, a more natural and effective approach is for users to provide demonstra-
055   tions of the desired behavior to supplement a general task description. Demonstrations offer rich,
056   illustrative information that can capture not only the task objectives but also the nuanced, latent pref-
057   erences that may be difficult to express verbally. Learning from Demonstration (LfD) approaches
058   seek to leverage human-provided demonstrations to reverse-engineer the underlying objective and
059   optimize a policy accordingly (Chen et al., 2020; 2022; Suay et al., 2016; Ravichandar et al., 2020).
060   However, a key challenge in LfD is the ambiguity in interpreting demonstrations – there can be an
061   infinite number of possible reward functions that could explain the same set of demonstrations, a
062   problem commonly referred to as the reward ambiguity problem in Inverse Reinforcement Learning
063
      (IRL) (Abbeel & Ng, 2004). Prior methods have sought to address this ambiguity by pre-designing
      features to constrain the space of possible reward functions, but this often limits the flexibility and
064
      generalizability of the learned rewards and policies (Zhu & Hu, 2018; Arora & Doshi, 2021). Our
065
      key insight is that language models are well-suited to contextualize demonstrations and infer task
066   features, narrowing down the possible interpretations and enabling robots to learn more robustly.
067
068   We propose to integrate the strengths of language models and LfD methods, leveraging (1) the
069
      emergent reasoning capabilities of language models to identify robust and relevant objective func-
      tion components, and (2) the demonstration-matching capabilities of LfD to determine the opti-
070
      mal weighing of these components. Crucially, we incorporate visual demonstrations into Vision-
071
      Language Models (VLMs), facilitating a more comprehensive understanding of human objectives.
072   Additionally, we introduce a self-reflection mechanism that enables VLM and LfD to iteratively im-
073   prove both the feature extraction and the reward & policy learning. This fusion of LfD and VLMs
074   offers a novel pathway for more effective robotic learning from demonstrations. This paradigm also
075   mirrors how humans naturally learn from others. When observing a demonstration, humans typi-
076   cally (1) identify the key aspects of the task, (2) formulate a policy to match the demonstration based
077   on those salient features, and (3) reflect on the discrepancies between their own behavior and the
078   demonstration to refine their understanding and execution (Locke, 1987; Di Stefano et al., 2014). By
079   iterating through these steps, humans progressively improve their performance. This iterative cycle
080
      of observation, reflection, and refinement is not only fundamental to human learning but also serves
      as an ideal framework for robotic learning (Chernova & Thomaz, 2014).
081
082   To develop this novel integration between VLM and LfD, we introduce ELEMENTAL (intEractive
083   LEarning froM dEmoNstraTion And Language). ELEMENTAL enables robots to identify key task
084   features from human demonstrations, learn rewards and policies that align on these features, and
085   iteratively reflect on their performance to improve over time. Our key contributions are three-folds:
086   1. We propose a novel, general framework that integrates VLMs and LfD, introducing an iterative
087       self-reflecting mechanism for continuous improvement. ELEMENTAL is the first to incorporate
088       visual demonstration inputs into language models to accomplish LfD, which facilitates more
089       accurate behavior understanding within demonstrations.
090   2. We evaluate ELEMENTAL on a set of challenging, standard robotic benchmarks in IsaacGym,
091       demonstrating its superior performance over previous state-of-the-art (SOTA) reward design and
092       LfD methods by 42.3%, showcasing its effectiveness.
093   3. We further assess ELEMENTAL’s generalization capabilities by designing novel variants of the
094       standard benchmarks. Our results show that ELEMENTAL achieves 41.3% better generalization
095       than existing methods, underscoring the importance of combining VLMs with LfD.

097   2   R ELATED W ORK
098
099
      Learning from Demonstration (LfD) – LfD approaches, such as Behavior Cloning (BC) and
100   IRL, have long been used to enable robots to learn from human-provided demonstrations. BC (Ross
101   et al., 2011), a supervised learning approach, is effective for relatively simple tasks, but it is prone
102   to compounding errors (known as covariate shift). IRL (Ng & Russell, 2000; Abbeel & Ng, 2004;
103   Ziebart et al., 2008; Ziebart, 2010) seeks to infer the underlying reward function that explains the
104   demonstrated behavior. However, reward ambiguity poses a significant challenge – an infinite num-
105   ber of reward functions could explain the same behavior. This challenge becomes exacerbated in
106   complex domains with limited, hetergeneous demonstrations Chen et al. (2020); Peng et al. (2024a).
107   ELEMENTAL addresses this key limitation by integrating VLMs to inject emergent reasoning capa-
      bilities into the learning process. VLMs reduce ambiguity by providing semantic context that allows
      robots to better understand relevant task features.

      Language Models as Reward Engineers – Recent works, such as EUREKA (Ma et al., 2023)
109   and L2R (Yu et al., 2023), leverage LLMs to convert language descriptions into reward functions,
110   offering a promising alternative to manual reward engineering. However, these methods are limited
111   by their reliance solely on brief task descriptions, restricting their ability to capture the full com-
112   plexity of robotic tasks and the subtle preferences of users. Additionally, determining the appropri-
113   ate weighting of different objective function components is particularly challenging, as the reward
114   design process is disconnected from policy training, resulting in poor out-of-distribution generaliza-
115   tion. ELEMENTAL addresses these limitations by integrating IRL with VLMs and supplementing
116   task descriptions with demonstrations. In ELEMENTAL, the responsibility of assigning reward
117
      component weights is shifted from the VLM to IRL, which matches the reward components to the
      demonstrated behaviors. This allows the VLM to focus on its strength—semantic understanding and
118
      task feature identification.
119
120   A closely related line of research is that by Peng et al. (2024b), which also integrates feature design
121   using LLMs with IRL. While this work demonstrates promising results, it operates under several
122   limiting assumptions. First, it assumes that demonstrations can be provided as input exclusively as
123
      text-based state-vector sequences. Due to this constraint, the work is only applied to simpler tasks
      (e.g., problem horizons of fewer than five steps) and where only one or two features are missing (with
124
      other ground-truth features given a priori). Additionally, as seen in other works (Yu et al., 2023;
125
      Kwon et al., 2023), this method relies on specially designed prompts tailored to each experimental
126   domain. These limitations hinder its scalability to more complex, high-dimensional tasks typically
127   encountered in real-world robotic applications. In contrast, ELEMENTAL leverages visual modality
128   for demonstration inputs, making it well-suited for more complex tasks where textual descriptions
129   alone are insufficient. In addition, to account for the complex domains, we introduce an upgraded
130   MaxEnt-IRL approach, detailed in Section 4.2. By incorporating visual modality, using a general
131   prompt across domains, and developing enhanced IRL, ELEMENTAL provides a more scalable
132   solution, as demonstrated in our results on standard robotic benchmarks without requiring any prior
133   knowledge of task features.
134   LM-Assisted Robot Learning – Several recent works have sought to leverage LMs to assist in
135   robotic learning, such as RL-VLM-F (Wang et al., 2024), RoboCLIP (Sontakke et al., 2024), and Du
136   et al. (2023). While promising, these methods depend on a surrogate reward derived from the LM’s
137
      understanding of the task-state alignment, which can introduce inaccuracies. Furthermore, these
      approaches lack interactivity with users, a key component shown to be helpful in gaining human
138
      trust (Chi & Malle, 2024). In contrast, ELEMENTAL potentially allows engineers and users to
139
      interactively refine the robot’s behavior, ensuring that the robot is user-aligned.
140
141   Other works, such as Wang et al. (2023) and Mahadevan et al. (2024), directly query LMs to output
142   robot actions or primitives. These approaches rely heavily on the LLM’s ability to plan and optimize
143
      actions, but LLMs are not inherently designed for mathematical optimization required for robot
      control. ELEMENTAL addresses these limitations by using VLMs to understand task features and
144
      by deferring the demonstration-to-policy alignment to IRL algorithms, which are better suited to
145
      optimize behavior in complex environments.

153   (MDP), defined by the tuple (S, A, T, R, γ). S is the set of states the agent can occupy. A is the set

155   where T (s, a, s′ ) gives the probability of transitioning from state s to state s′ after taking action
156   a. R : S → R is the reward function that assigns a scalar reward to each state. γ ∈ [0, 1) is the
157   temporal discount factor. The goal of Reinforcement Learning (RL) is to learnP∞ a policy π : S → A
158
      that maximizes the expected cumulative reward, given by: J(π) = Eτ ∼π [ t=0 γ t R(st )].

      In this section, we describe our algorithm, ELEMENTAL, which consists of three interconnected
210
      phases, as shown in Figure 1. The first phase involves constructing an initial feature function through
211
      a VLM based on visual human demonstrations and environment specifications, detailed in Sec-
212   tion 4.1. In the second phase, this feature function is integrated with IRL to learn a reward function
213   and policy that best align with the demonstrated behaviors (Section 4.2). Finally, the third phase
214   introduces a self-reflection mechanism that automatically compares the learned behavior with the
215   demonstrations, enabling iterative refinement of the feature function, the reward function, and the
      policy (Section 4.3).

241   4.1   P HASE 1: I NITIAL P ROMPT
242
243   The first phase begins with an initial prompt that includes three inputs: (1) the MDP environment
244   code, which specifies the environment’s state space S, (2) a text description of the task, and (3) a
245
      visual human demonstration of the desired task. The form of the visual demonstration depends on
      the task domain (Figure 2): for tasks where superimposition is meaningful (e.g., locomotion tasks
246
      such as the moving-forward ant or humanoid), we compose a superimposed image that displays
247
      the sequence of motions from demonstration (darker shades indicate temporal progression). For
248   manipulation tasks, where superimposition can result in cluttered robot poses (e.g., the FrankaPanda
249   Cabinet), four key frames are provided to illustrate stages of the task execution. By incorporating
250   visual demonstrations alongside language-based task descriptions, ELEMENTAL allows the VLM
251   to generate feature functions that more accurately reflect the user’s latent goals.
252
      These inputs are then processed by the VLM, which leverages its emerging reasoning capabilities to
253
      infer the task-relevant features. The goal is not simply to describe the state but to capture important
254   factors that align with the user’s intent for the task. The output of this phase is a feature function
255   ϕ : S → Rn , where ϕ(s) = (ϕ1 (s), ϕ2 (s), . . . , ϕn (s)) represents a vector of features describing the
256   key aspects of the task and n is the number of features. Given VLM’s coding capabilities (Piccolo
257   et al., 2023), we ask the VLM to output feature functions as Python codes, providing a structured rep-
258   resentation. If the returned code is not executable (e.g., wrong function signature), we re-prompt the
259   VLM with the trackback information up to three times. A successfully executable feature function
260   serves as the foundation for the reward learning and policy optimization in the subsequent phase.

345
346   In this section, we evaluate the performance of ELEMENTAL on challenging robotic tasks from
347   IsaacGym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021). We design experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of
348   ELEMENTAL in both benchmarking against SOTA baselines (Section 5.1) and generalization to
349
      task variants (Section 5.2).
350   Environments and Tasks In benchmark experiments, we test ELEMENTAL and baseline algo-
351   rithms on nine challenging IsaacGym Robotics tasks, using GPU-accelerated training to enable effi-
352   cient experiments. These tasks span various domains, including locomotion and manipulation, and
353   are recognized for their complexity in the robot learning community (Makoviychuk et al., 2021). For
354   all methods that utilize a LLM/VLM, we use the OpenAI GPT-4o model unless otherwise noted. We
355   use five demonstrations for each task collected with RL-trained policies.
356   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first successful application of IRL to IsaacGym – with
357   or without large models – due to the realistic high-dimensional state and action spaces. The per-
358   formance of ELEMENTAL in these tasks demonstrates its robustness and scalability, making it a
359   suitable framework for solving complex real-world robotic problems using IRL.

      powered reward engineering approaches:

      5.2     G ENERALIZATION R ESULTS
468
469   In the second set of experiments, we ask the question “is LLM/VLM just remembering the reward
470   function, as the knowledge cut-off date is Oct 2023, after IsaacGym is public?” To answer this ques-
471   tion, we test the generalization capability of ELEMENTAL by applying it to modified versions of
472   the IsaacGym environments, particularly variants of the Ant task. For these modified environments,
473   we change certain properties, such as state vector order, physics property (e.g., gravity coefficient),
474   and the task, to evaluate whether ELEMENTAL’s VLM-driven feature inference combined with IRL
475   can adapt to new, unseen environments better than EUREKA. We test on four Ant variants:
476
477   1. Ant Original: The standard Ant task without modifications, serving as the baseline environment.
478   2. Ant with Reversed Observations: The order of the state vector is reversed, testing the algorithms’
479      ability to adapt to changes in the structure of the input data.
480
481
      3. Ant with Lighter Gravity: The gravity coefficient is reduced from 9.81 to 5.00 and requiring the
         feature and the policy to adjust for a different dynamics. A performance drop is expected as the
482
         ant moves with less friction.
483
484   4. Ant Running Backward: The task is modified to require the Ant to move backward rather than
485      forward, assessing how well the approaches generalize to a different task objective in the same
         environment.

494   properties and the task’s nature.

496   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
497
498   We introduced ELEMENTAL, a novel framework that integrates VLMs with LfD, supplements task
499   descriptions with visual demonstrations, and introduces an iterative self-reflection mechanism for
500   robust learning from demonstration. Evaluations showed that ELEMENTAL outperforms previous
501   state-of-the-art methods by 42.3% on standard benchmarks and 41.3% on generalization capabilities
502   in adapting to novel tasks.
503
      While ELEMENTAL demonstrates strong improvements in LfD with VLMs, several avenues for
504   future work remain. First, testing ELEMENTAL in real-world human-robot systems is a critical
505   next step. Real-world scenarios may require addressing the heterogeneity and suboptimality of
506   human demonstrations, and the training wall-clock time may become a bottleneck in human-robot
507   interactions. To address this, more efficient IRL approaches could be explored. Additionally, given
508   the demonstrated utility of visual inputs, future research could investigate alternative methods for
509   providing visual demonstrations, beyond superimposition and key frames.
