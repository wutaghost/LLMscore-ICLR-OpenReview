009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Deep Learning techniques have excelled at generating embedding spaces that cap-
012            ture semantic similarities between items. Often these representations are paired,
013            enabling experiments with analogies (pairs within the same domain) and cross-
014            modality (pairs across domains). These experiments are based on specific assump-
015            tions about the geometry of embedding spaces, which allow finding paired items by
016            extrapolating the positional relationships between embedding pairs in the training
017            dataset, allowing for tasks such as finding new analogies, and multimodal zero-shot
018            classification. In this work, we propose a metric to evaluate the similarity between
019            paired item representations. Our proposal is built from the structural similarity
020
               between the nearest-neighbors induced graphs of each representation, and can be
               configured to compare spaces based on different distance metrics and on different
021
               neighborhood sizes. We demonstrate that our proposal can be used to identify simi-
022
               lar structures at different scales, which is hard to achieve with kernel methods such
023            as Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA). We further illustrate our method with two
024            case studies: an analogy task using GloVe embeddings, and zero-shot classification
025            in using CLIP and BLIP-2 embeddings. Our results show that accuracy in both
026            analogy and zero-shot classification tasks correlates with the embedding similarity.
027            These findings can help explain performance differences in these tasks, and may
028            lead to improved design of paired-embedding models in the future.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Several tasks in machine learning rely on obtaining pairs of corresponding representations from
034   the same item in the same or different domains, such as word pairs in an analogy task (where the
035   underlying item is the analogy itself) or items from different media or text modalities, such as text-to-
036   image cross-modal representations. Each item representation is usually converted to an embedding
037   vector in a space associated with its respective domain. Higher performances in these paired-item
038   tasks are often linked to the preservation of proximity between embeddings related to the same item
039   as we cross from one space of embeddings to the other. Thus, the study of how proximity is preserved
040   between spaces may provide deeper insights into the reasons underlying performance differences in
041
      these tasks.
042   Contributions1 In this work, we introduce a method, namely Nearest Neighbor Graph Similarity
043   (NNGS), to measure the similarity between paired embeddings of items based on the estimated
044   Jaccard similarity between the neighborhoods of induced graphs (Donnat and Holmes, 2018). We
045   show that the neighborhood size can be adjusted so that it reflects the locality of changes observed in
046   the similarity measure. In two case studies, we show that the proposed metric can provide insight
047
      on the reasons underlying performance in both the analogy and the zero-shot classification tasks.
      In special, it can be used to identify categories of words that are not represented adequately for
048
      the analogy task, and to identify prompt templates that can lead to higher accuracy in zero-shot
049
      classification.

      2.1   E MBEDDINGS
057
058   Semantic Word Embeddings Prior research involving embedding spaces has found usefulness in
059   particular topological characteristics, such as the one spanned by GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014). In
060   GloVe, each word from a vocabulary is represented by a vector eword in a so-called semantic space.
061   Within a semantic space, a change of meaning is represented by a translation vector, that is, stating
062   that the relationship between “a” and “b” is the same as that between “c” and “d” means that their
063   embeddings have the relationship:
064                                eb − ea = ed − ec = ecategory A→category B .                           (1)
065
066   In Equation 1, ecategory A→category B is a vector that maps elements from one category to another (e.g.,
067   countries to capitals, adjectives to corresponding superlatives, etc.) (Ethayarajh et al., 2018).
068
069
      Cross-modal Embeddings More recently, multimodal embedding spaces have been used to represent
      items according to their content in different modalities. In CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), for example,
070
      the image corresponding to a photo of a car is represented by a vector ei , the text “a photo of a
071
      car” is represented by et and, ideally, ei ≈ et . In BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), the multimodal models
072   are obtained by training unimodal language models that map onto each other using an attention
073   mechanism. These ideas allow zero-shot classification by calculating the embeddings of an unknown
074   image and comparing it to the embeddings of several candidate texts, and then choosing the most
075   similar text as the class for that image.
076
      Assessing embeddings Traditionally, the properties of embedding spaces have been evaluated
077
      using accuracy in task-specific benchmarks (Faruqui et al., 2016). This is important, as it allows a
078   straightforward comparison between systems and a direct measure of its utility, but leaves gaps that
079   were only addressed later, like the requirement for exponential data (Udandarao et al., 2024) or the
080   tendency to behave like a simple bag-of-words (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023) in cross-modal zero-shot
081   learning and the propensity to biasing embedding spaces according to term frequencies (Faruqui et al.,
082   2016). Importantly, in subsequent work, these embeddings have been analyzed to find properties that
083   could help explain their performances, such as studies on bounds for linear analogies (Ethayarajh
084   et al., 2018) in GloVe and the properties of partial orthogonality (Jiang et al., 2023) and linear
085   compositionality in CLIP (Trager et al., 2023).

087   2.2   S IMILARITY BETWEEN REPRESENTATIONS
088
      Items from a dataset can have different representations. The similarity between representations can
089
      be measured with a similarity index s(X, Y ), which compares different representations X ∈ Rn×p1
090
      and Y ∈ Rn×p2 (with possibly different dimensionalities p1 and p2 , with p1 ≤ p2 without loss of
091   generality) of the same set of n items (Kornblith et al., 2019).
092
093   Arguably, a similarity index should be invariant to orthonormal linear transformations and isotropic
094
      non-zero scaling and translation (Kornblith et al., 2019). More generally, a similarity index could
      be invariant to general invertible linear transformations (Raghu et al., 2017), but this property will
095
      not be adopted in the present work as it implies that all datasets have the same similarity index if
096
      p1 > n (Kornblith et al., 2019).
097
098   An important measure for similarity between representation is the Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)
099   measure (Kornblith et al., 2019), calculated by:
100                                                    tr(Kxc (X c )Kyc (Y c ))
101                       CKA(X, Y ) = q                                                        ,         (2)
102                                          tr(Kxc (X c )Kxc (X c ))tr(Kyc (Y c )Kyc (Y c ))
103
104
      where tr(.) is the trace operator, Kx and Ky are kernel functions applied to X and Y , and the
      c
        operation consists of centering a cloud point by subtracting the dimension-wise mean from all
105
      elements.
106
107   CKA draws from the idea of assessing how distances between items change from one representation
      to another. With a linear kernel, a changes in the distance between data blobs have a larger impact

      2.3   S TRUCTURAL SIMILARITY BETWEEN GRAPH NODES
145
146
      Given a point cloud V = {vi }, vi ∈ Rd , a directed graph Gk = (V, Ek ) can be induced by connecting
147   each point to its k closest neighbors (Eppstein et al., 1997), k ∈ [1, n − 1], thus forming the set of
148   directed edges Ek . A point is not allowed to have itself as a neighbor, thus Gk is a directed simple
149   graph. We say that Gk is induced by k-neighborhood in V . The set of neighbor indices of a vertex
150   vi is Nk (vi ) = {j|(i, j) ∈ Ek }. Two graph vertices vi and vj , i ̸= j, can be said to be structurally
151   equivalent if they share the same set of neighbors, that is, N (vi ) = N (vj ) (Newman, 2018). If
152   they only share some neighbors, we can use the Jaccard similarity J(N (vi ), N (vj )) (Equation 4) to
153   measure the degree of structural similarity between vi and vj (Donnat and Holmes, 2018):
154
155
                                                             |Nk (vi ) ∩ Nk (vj )|
                                   J(Nk (vi ), Nk (vj )) =                         .                       (4)
156                                                          |Nk (vi ) ∪ Nk (vj )|
157
158   A soft approach to graph structural similarity has shown to lead to minor accuracy increases in
159   downstream classifiers based on image-text pre-trained embeddings (Sobal et al., 2024). In this work,
160   we further assess how this idea can relate to zero-shot learning tasks in this same scenario. Also, we
161   bring evidence that this type of approach can relate to task performance in other tasks such as an
      embedding-space analogy task.

      In this section, we propose Nearest Neighborhood Graph Similarity (NNGS). We discuss some some
165   theoretical perspectives that explain how to interpret its results.

      3.1       S IMILARITY BETWEEN CORRESPONDING POINT CLOUDS
168
169   The notion of structural similarity between nodes (Equation 4) can be adapted to compare point
170   clouds where there is a one-to-one correspondence between points in both clouds2 . For such, let
171   X = {xi } and Y = {yi }, i ∈ {1, 2, · · · , n}, be point clouds where xi ∈ Rp1 and yi ∈ Rp2 are
172   corresponding points. Two graphs, GX,k = (X, EX,k ) and GY,k = (Y, EY,k ), can be induced by
173   k-neighborhood on X and Y as described previously.
174   Let NX,k (xi ) be the set of indexes of the k points closest to xi in X, and define NY,k (yi ) analogously.
175   Importantly, the distance measure used for this operation can be selected according to the problem.
176   The structural similarity between corresponding points xi and yi is defined as:
177
                                                                       |NX,k (xi ) ∩ NY,k (yi )|
178                                 J(NX,k (xi ), NY,k (yi )) =                                  .           (5)
                                                                       |NX,k (xi ) ∪ NY,k (yi )|
179
180
      Note that, different from Equation 4, points xi and yi do not belong to the same graph, hence the
181   need for comparing their sets of neighbor indices, and not the neighboring points themselves.
182
183   From Equation 5, we define the Nearest Neighborhood Graph Similarity (NNGS) between point
184
      clouds X and Y as the average structural similarity between corresponding points:
185                                                            n

188
      Note that the structural similarity between point clouds depends only on their neighborhood structure:
189
      the precise definitions of distance in each domain, or the value of point coordinates only impact the
190
      structural similarity if they change the ranks of closest points among the cloud. For instance, if the
191   point clouds are equal, that is, X = Y , then NNGS(X, Y, k) = 1, ∀k ∈ [1, n − 1]. Furthermore,
192   NNGS(X, Y, k) = NNGS(X, Y ′ , k) if Y ′ is constructed from Y by applying only isotropic scaling,
193   translations, or orthonormal transformations, as they not change the ranks of neighborhood distances
194   between points.

      related to the number n of points in the evaluated set and their dimensionality (p1 and p2 ). These
      properties are discussed next. In the following experiments, we used the Cosine similarity to define
201
      neighborhoods.

320   NNGS has two additional parameters when compared to CKA: the neighborhood size k, and the
321   distance metric used to induce the neighborhood graph. GULP has one additional parameter: the
322   regularization term λ. In datasets with more than one cluster, these parameters can be manipulated to
323   find similarities at different scales and in different situations. The experiments below were conducted
      with toy datasets crafted especially to demonstrate these differences, as follows.

      In a cross-modal zero-shot classification task, the text embeddings are calculated from a set of
438   captions that are constructed from the class labels and template phrases. In CLIP, these templates
439   are variations of the phrase “a [description] [photo/image] of X”5 , where “X” is substituted by the
440   desired label.
441
      We perform experiments to draw insight about the relationship between the phrasing of text captions,
442
      the zero-shot classification performance, and the similarity of cross-modal embeddings using the
443
      pre-trained CLIP model available on HuggingFace6 . In these experiments, we assess the similarity
444   between embeddings in each modality in the CIFAR-100 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009; Krizhevsky
445   et al.)7 and the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009) using NNGS(ei , et , k). For such, we calculate
446   the mean image embedding for each class (ei ). Then, we use the corresponding text embeddings et
447   to estimate NNGS(ei , et , k).
448
      We also experiment with two methods to make new templates. The first is to change the original
449
      templates to their negations, that is, we use variations of “not a [description] [photo/image] of X”.
450   The second method is to make templates from phrases unrelated to images or photos using variations
451   of famous film and music phrases. The complete list of prompts can be found in Appendix F.
452
453
      In this situation, there are 100 points, but there are no blobs. In this experiment, we are interested in
      low neighborhood sizes, as they already relate to inter-class positions. For this reason, we use k = 3.
454
455   Next, we evaluate the zero-shot classification accuracies using each single template, and compare
456   them with its NNGS(X, Y, k) for k = 3, as shown in Figures 6 and 7. We present the measures
457   ρ1 , which is the Pearson’s correlation between NNGS and the zero-shot accuracy for each prompt
458   template considering only the original templates, and ρ2 , which considers the added templates as
459
      well. We also present results of similar experiments using CKA and GULP.
460   Interestingly, results indicate that using negative templates instead of positive templates leads to
461   accuracy differences of less than 0.02. However, the film/music templates had a lower accuracy.
462

463   Figures 6 and 7. Importantly, the values of ρ2 can be seen as skewed because the added prompts
464   seem to form a series of poorly-performing elements. When only the original prompts are considered,
465   the obtained values are lower, but still significant. However, we highglight that the same experiments,
466   when performed with CKA or GULP, leads to a lower correlation, which indicates that NNGS was
467   able to identify similarities that are more related to the zero-shot classification performance.
468

469   to the zero-shot classification accuracy. However, we note that this trend cannot be observed if

471   This behavior could be due to reaching an accuracy higher limit, similarly to the observations in

495   5   C ONCLUSION
496
497   Our study introduces a novel approach, namely Nearest Neighborhood Graph Similarity (NNGS) to
498   evaluating the similarity of paired embedding spaces using GloVe, CLIP, and BLIP-2 models as case
499
      studies. The similarity is measured using the average Jaccard similarity between the corresponding
      nodes of k-neighborhood-induced graphs. The resulting measure informs how well these models
500
      preserve the underlying structural properties of the data. Our findings demonstrate a strong corre-
501
      lation between similarity and task-specific accuracy, indicating that maintaining structure within
502   corresponding embedding spaces is a possible route for achieving high performance in tasks such as
503   analogy calculation and cross-modal zero-shot classification.
504
505
      We have compared our method with two previous approaches (CKA and GULP), showing that
      NNGS is invariant to dimensionality changes, robust to changes in the hyperparameter, and can be
506
      made invariant to point cloud sizes by using a relative neighborhood size instead of an absolute one.
507
      Also, we have shown that changing the value of k can make NNGS focus on local or on global
508   transformations over data, and finding a value for k can be done by investigating the clusters found
509   within the dataset. We have shown that a well-tuned NNGS presents a greater correlation with
510   task-specific performances than CKA and GULP.
511
      Importantly, the results shown here do not mean that NNGS is a superior similarity measure in all
512
      possible cases. Rather, we note that each representation similarity measure draws from different
513
      assumptions, hence is more adequate for a different situation. Consequently, different similarity
514   metrics can be used harmoniously to provide different viewpoints about embedding spaces.
515
516   Looking forward, our findings raise important questions about other practical applications of similarity
517
      metrics. We surmise that they could serve as an effective stopping criteria in early-stopping strategies,
      aiding in the prevention of overfitting in cross-modal learning. Also, they could offer insights into
518
      identifying groups of words where analogy tasks excel, guiding future model development. Last,
519
      similarity could inform broader discussions on prompt engineering, helping to provide explanations
520   regarding the underlying agents behind the performance of specific prompt templates.

787   C     P ROOF THAT NEIGHBORHOOD CHOICE IS NOT INDEPENDENT
788
789   Let x1 , x2 , x3 ∈ Rd be 3 points in a point cloud. Using a distance measure γ(xi , xj ), we induce a
790
      k-neighbor graph with k = 1. Suppose we find the edges: (x1 , x2 ) and (x2 , x3 ). What is the edge
      starting at x3 ?
791
792   Finding the aforementioned edges implies that γ(x1 , x2 ) < γ(x1 , x3 ) and γ(x2 , x3 ) < γ(x1 , x2 ).
793   Because distances are symmetric, γ(x1 , x2 ) < γ(x3 , x1 ) and γ(x3 , x2 ) < γ(x1 , x2 ).
794   Thus, γ(x3 , x2 ) < γ(x1 , x2 ) < γ(x3 , x1 ). Henceforth the third edge of the graph must be (x3 , x2 ).
795
796   This counter-example shows that neighborhoods are not entirely independent. Rather, there are
797
      less possible choices than the total number of vertices in the graph as distance properties must be
      respected.
