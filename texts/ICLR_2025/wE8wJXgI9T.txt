009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning jointly from images and texts using contrastive pre-training has emerged
012            as an effective method to train large-scale models with a strong grasp of seman-
013            tic image concepts. For instance, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), pre-trained on a
014            large corpus of web data, excels in tasks like zero-shot image classification, ob-
015            ject detection, geolocalization, and more. These contrastive models embed input
016
               images and texts into a shared representational space. Recently, it was claimed
               that models like CLIP show a modality gap, where image and text embeddings
017
               occupy disjoint areas in the representational space. Previous studies attribute this
018
               gap to factors like data artifacts (mismatched pairs), model architecture artifacts
019            (the cone effect), and the nature of the loss landscape (getting stuck in local min-
020            ima). We demonstrate that, even after accounting for these factors, and even when
021            using the same modality, the contrastive loss actually creates a gap during train-
022            ing. As a result, we propose renaming this phenomenon the contrastive gap. We
023            show that the contrastive gap is exacerbated by training with small batch sizes in
024            high-dimensional spaces, causing embeddings of each modality to occupy small
025            disjoint portions of the latent space. Our experiments show that minimizing the
026            contrastive gap via the addition of uniformity and alignment terms optimizes the
027
               representational space and conveys better performance on downstream tasks such
               as image-text retrieval, zero-shot image classification, and multi-modal arithmetic.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Multi-modal models map inputs from different modalities into a unified representational space, such
033   that semantically similar inputs from different modalities map to nearby points in the representa-
034   tional space. This can be practically useful because it allows transfer between modalities, but it is
035   also more consistent with the human sensory experience, which collects information across many
036   modalities to better understand the world. In the context of learning from paired images and text,
037   CLIP (Contrastive Language Image Pre-training) (Radford et al., 2021) establishes a strong proof-
038   of-concept for this reasoning. CLIP’s multi-modal contrastive loss allows the model to predict the
039
      text associated with an image and vice versa. CLIP scales this approach to a very large dataset of
      400M image-caption pairs, learning embeddings that cover a wide variety of visual concepts appli-
040
      cable to many downstream tasks.
041
042   But, while CLIP is powerful, it suffers from a modality gap (Liang et al., 2022), wherein image em-
043   beddings reside in a space disjoint from that of the text embeddings. This phenomenon is also seen
044   in multi-modal contrastive models in other domains such as medical images (Zhang et al., 2021),
045
      videos (Xu et al., 2021), amino acid sequencing (https://github.com/MicPie/clasp)
      and brain decoding (Luo et al., 2024). Prior work has shown that performance on downstream tasks
046
      improves when we minimize this gap, which can be achieved by simply shifting one modality’s
047
      embeddings in CLIP space to change the size of the gap (Liang et al., 2022) through projection
048   (transforming the embeddings through some projection operation) (Zhou et al., 2023), or through
049   fine-tuning (Oh et al., 2023). Recent work has additionally shown that learning latent spaces of
050   visual embeddings via alignment with human similarity judgments improves downstream task per-
051   formance (Muttenthaler et al., 2023). Similarly, work relating CLIP embeddings to human brain
052   data found that representations that had reduced the gap between modalities also led to improved
053   downstream model performance (Luo et al., 2024). Therefore, analyzing and closing this gap is a
      promising direction to improve upon the strong representational capacity of CLIP and its variants.

      In this paper, we conducted a comprehensive study of what causes the modality gap and simple ways
055   to close it. We make the following contributions:
056
057          • It’s not a modality gap. After summarizing the common purported causes of the modality
058
               gap, we perform comprehensive experiments that show that accounting for these factors
               does not close the gap, suggesting that the present understanding of modality gap may be
059
               flawed.
060
             • It’s a contrastive gap. We present experiments that demonstrate that the gap is a byproduct
061            of a high dimensional CLIP space, combined with contrastive loss that encourages CLIP
062            embeddings to occupy a lower dimensional manifold relative to the latent space.
063          • The contrastive gap can be closed. We show that simply fine-tuning CLIP by adding
064            a factor for uniformity and alignment can reduce the size of the gap by distributing the
065            embeddings more uniformly throughout CLIP’s latent space.
066          • Closing the contrastive gap improves downstream performance. Finally, we present
067            experiments to show that closing the contrastive gap, and thereby creating more aligned
068            and uniformly distributed representations, creates a representational space that is better
069
               for downstream tasks, including image-text retrieval, zero-shot image classification, and
               multi-modal embedding arithmetic.

072   2   BACKGROUND
073
074   A multi-modal contrastive model learns a representational space in which semantically paired sam-
075   ples from different modalities (positive pairs, e.g., an image and its associated caption) are closer
076   together in the latent space compared to other randomly chosen pairs (negative pairs, e.g., an image
077   and the caption associated with another randomly chosen image). Contrastive learning was origi-
078   nally developed for the unimodal setting to learn self-supervised representations. One such popular
079   model is SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), which uses the NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled
080   Cross entropy) loss to efficiently learn robust image representations. The NT-Xent loss is differ-
081
      ent from previous contrastive learning methods as it does not need explicit negative samples. The
      NT-Xent loss works by treating samples other than the positive pair in a training mini-batch as soft-
082
      negatives, pushing them away from the positives. Unlike previous contrastive methods, the NT-Xent
083
      loss also normalizes the embeddings to lie on a unit hypersphere in the representational space.
084
085   Wang & Isola (2020) introduced uniformity and alignment factors as desirable properties of the
086   representational space and showed that the NT-Xent loss optimizes the uniformity and alignment
087
      properties in the limit of infinite negative samples, which is equivalent to infinite batch size for
      NT-Xent loss.
088
089   Many models have been recently proposed that generalize the contrastive loss to the multi-modal
090   setting (Zhang et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021). Before these, several works
091   have explored the integration of different modalities using contrastive and triplet losses. Wang
092   et al. (2018) uses a two-branch neural network architecture with a bidirectional retrieval ranking
093
      loss to learn joint embeddings for images and text. Wang et al. (2018) also included neighborhood-
      preserving constraints, sampling mini-batches such that they contained multiple positive matches for
094
      a given target, in order to properly capture the many-to-many relationships between images and texts
095
      (i.e multiple images matching one text sequence or vice versa). Mahajan et al. (2019) employs joint
096   Wasserstein autoencoders to align multi-modal embeddings in a shared latent space. Further, Lee
097   et al. (2018) proposes a stacked cross-attention mechanism to discover fine-grained correspondences
098   between image regions and words for more accurate image-text matching. However, CLIP stands
099   out from these approaches due to its simplicity and scalability. While prior work requires complex
100   architectures and sampling strategies, CLIP uses a simple contrastive loss and random mini-batch
101   sampling from a vast dataset (400M image-text pairs). CLIP’s approach circumvents the requirement
102   for complex architectures and additional loss terms, yet achieves strong zero-shot results on many
103   standard image benchmarks. CLIP’s efficiency and ability to generalize across tasks have led to its
104
      widespread adoption across a wide range of applications.
105   While CLIP has demonstrated remarkable advances in zero-shot learning and cross-modal tasks,
106   Liang et al. (2022) observed that a modality gap appears in many models that use multi-modal con-
107   trastive learning. There have since been several attempts to close the gap while monitoring its effects
      on downstream task performance. Liang et al. (2022) show that altering the gap by simply trans-

      lating the embeddings of one modality onto the other can positively impact zero-shot classification
109   performance. Further, Oh et al. (2023) attribute the modality gap to the absence of hard-negatives in

111   showing that training with the hard-negatives improves representational quality. In this work, we
112   frame the gap between the multi-modal embeddings as a problem of low uniformity and show that
113   by simply optimizing for more alignment and uniformity (properties known to be desirable in uni-
114   modal contrastive learning), we can significantly reduce the size of the gap, while increasing the
115   quality of the representations learned.

144   3     T HE G AP IN M ULTI - MODAL C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
145
146   Though CLIP effectively associates images with related texts, it also creates representational spaces
147   with a modality gap, a phenomenon that has generated some interest. Liang et al. (2022) attributes
148   this modality gap to two independent factors. First, they describe the cone effect of deep neural
149   networks, in which different random initializations cause the embeddings of two encoders to occupy
150   two non-overlapping narrow cones in CLIP space. Second, they note the existence of mismatched
151   pairs in the dataset, where the pairing of images and captions is incorrect for some data points.
152   Shi et al. (2023) studied the gap in three dimensions and attributed it to conflicting uniformity and
153
      alignment terms in the contrastive loss, claiming that this leads to the existence of local minima that
      encourage the gap. Further, (Oh et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2022) show that the modality gap persists
154
      even after fine-tuning. In our first experiment in Section 3.2, we will show that these factors cannot
155
      fully account for the modality gap by simulating an ideal scenario where all of the above factors are
156   controlled for, but the gap still exists at the end of training. The goal of this work is to suggest that the
157   modality gap is not caused by trying to align different modalities in a unified representational space,
158   but instead arises as a consequence of the contrastive training that is used to align both modalities.
159   We therefore suggest that the term contrastive gap better describes the underlying phenomenon.
160   We define the contrastive gap as the separation between the embeddings produced by two encoders
161   trained using a multimodal contrastive loss (Equation 1). This gap occurs because the embeddings
      from the two encoders arrange as two disjoint clusters within the shared representational space.

      3.1   M EASURING THE GAP
177
      To show that we have closed the gap between the embeddings of the two encoders, we must first find
178
      a way to quantify the gap. We introduce the following two metrics to measure the size and severity
179   of the gap:
180
181   Distance between modality centroids (from Liang et al. (2022)) Given N images and N captions,
                                                                         PN
182   we denote the centroid of the image embeddings as C I = 1/N j=1 EjI , and similarly for the
183   centroid of the text embeddings. We compute the distance between centroids as ∥C I − C T ∥2 : this
184   distance can vary from 0 to 2, with 0 distance meaning there is no gap between the embeddings.
185   Linear Separability (from Shi et al. (2023)) is the percentage of image and text embeddings that
186   can be distinguished by a linear classifier operating in CLIP space. We used 80% of the dataset to
187   train a linear model to classify CLIP embeddings as originating from either ”image” or ”text” input.
188   We then tested the performance of the classifier on the remaining 20% of the dataset and reported
189   the accuracy. If a set of embeddings are 100% linearly separable, this means that the space occupied
190   by each modality is completely disjoint, with a clear gap between the embeddings. Conversely, 50%
191   linear separability means that the image and text embeddings are overlapping in CLIP space; i.e.
192   there is no gap between the embeddings.

196   We now systematically remove the factors commonly known to contribute to the modality gap. We
197   started with the default CLIP architecture and used the MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014) dataset. We
198   created an idealized scenario where:
199         1. There is only one modality. We replaced the text encoder in CLIP with another copy of the
200            image encoder and trained the model on pairs of images instead of text-image pairs. Thus,
201            for this experiment, the CLIP encoders are identical image encoders with different random
202            initializations.
203         2. Embeddings from the two image encoders occupy the same cone at initialization. In our
204            setup, after we initialize the two image encoders, we computed a fixed transformation ma-
205            trix that translates the embeddings of the second image encoder to overlap with those of
206
               the first image encoder (following Liang et al. (2022)). Thus, the second encoder’s embed-
               dings are translated to occupy the same narrow cone as the first encoder’s embeddings at
207
               initialization. This way, there is no modality gap at initialization.
208
            3. There are no mismatched pairs. The positive pairs in our constructed dataset are actually
209            identical images. This eliminated the possibility that there would be mismatched pairs in
210            the dataset.

250   3.4     W HY DOES THE CONTRASTIVE LOSS INDUCE A GAP ?
251
252   In Section 3.2, we observed that the contrastive gap persists even when training CLIP on idealized
253   conditions. We speculate that this is because of the small value of the learned temperature parameter
254   τ = 0.01 in CLIP. As shown by Wang & Liu (2021b), when the temperature approaches zero, the
255
      contrastive loss becomes a triplet loss with a margin of zero. This means that the loss only focuses on
      the nearest negative sample within the batch: Once the similarity between a positive pair is greater
256
      than that between the positive and the nearest negative sample, the loss stops pushing the positive
257
      pair closer together. Wang & Liu (2021b) uses the following approximation to illustrate this point:

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
525
526
527   We studied the representations of multi-modal contrastive learning and the contrastive gap. Our
528   analysis showed that controlling for the reasons thought to cause the gap does not close it. Thus, this
529   is not a modality gap. We instead proposed the term contrastive gap to describe this phenomenon.
530   We investigated the relationship between dimensionality and batch size, concluding that the con-
531   trastive gap is exacerbated by small batch sizes in high dimensional CLIP space. To reduce the gap,
532   we proposed adding uniformity and alignment terms to the CLIP loss. Our results demonstrated
533   that directly optimizing for uniformity and alignment in the latent space significantly reduces the
534   gap. Moreover, we found that closing the gap through fine-tuning with LCUAXU improved average
535   image-text retrieval and zero-shot image classification performances, suggesting that reducing the
536
      contrastive gap could lead CLIP to develop a more accurate understanding of concepts in images.
      Further, the new losses also improved multi-modal arithmetic performance, suggesting that the new
537
      representational space could have more consistent geometric structure.
538
539   While our experiments focused on fine-tuning on MS COCO, we hope to expand our scope and train
      CLIP models from scratch to better understand the impact of uniformity and alignment at scale.

675   A.1   M OTIVATION BEHIND TRAINING CLIP IN 3D
676
      We train CLIP in 3D in Section 3.3 to better visualize the persistence of the gap on the 3D hy-
677
      persphere. Our study builds on the work in Shi et al. (2023), where the authors also studied the
678   behaviour of CLIP loss by visualizing training on a 3D sphere. The authors devised this proof of
679   concept experiment to show that the CLIP loss induces a gap even in low dimensional space without
680   external factors coming from the dataset or neural network architecture. They randomly generated
681   1,000 points on the 3D sphere and trained with a batch size of 10, learning rate of 0.01, using the
682   SGD optimization algorithm. The parameter space for the SGD optimization algorithm was the eu-
683   clidean coordinate space of the generated points. This allowed the algorithm to directly optimize the
684   positions of the points to reduce the CLIP loss.
685   Their experiment highlights that the modality gap can arise even in low-dimensional spaces and
686   without any external factors from the dataset or neural network architecture, but can eventually be
687   closed after a large number of training steps.
688
      Shi et al. (2023), we studied the behaviour of CLIP loss when we reduce the CLIP dimensionality
689
      from 512D to 3D. We extended the proof-of-concept experiment done by Shi et al. (2023) by
690
691          • Using Real Data: We sampled 1,000 image-text pairs from the MS-COCO dataset rather
692            than using synthetic points. This way, we can better capture the nuances of a real-world
693            data distribution.
694
             • Optimizing with CLIP architecture: In the original experiment by Shi et al. (2023), the
695            authors bypassed the neural network entirely by directly optimizing the positions of points
696            in a 3D Euclidean space. However, in our modified experiment, we utilized the CLIP model
697            to project image and text representations into a 3D space (by adjusting the output projection
698            layer from 512D to 3D). By doing so, we could examine how the CLIP model and its loss
699            function behave together, allowing us to assess the impact of both the neural network and
700            the loss function in a more realistic setting.
701
      We visualize the results of our 3D experiment in Section 3.3 of the main text.
