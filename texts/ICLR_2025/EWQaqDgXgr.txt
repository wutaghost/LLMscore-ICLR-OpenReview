009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           The increased adoption of diffusion models in text-to-image generation has trig-
012           gered concerns on their reliability. Such models are now closely scrutinized under
013           the lens of various metrics, notably calibration, fairness, or compute efficiency.
014           We focus in this work on two issues that arise when deploying these models: a
015           lack of diversity when prompting images, and a tendency to recreate images from
016           the training set. To solve both problems, we propose a method that coaxes the
017           sampled trajectories of pretrained diffusion models to land on images that fall out-
018
              side of a reference set. We achieve this by adding repellency terms to the dif-
              fusion SDE throughout the generation trajectory, which are triggered whenever
019
              the path is expected to land too closely to an image in the shielded reference set.
020
              Our method is sparse in the sense that these repellency terms are zero and inac-
021           tive most of the time, and even more so towards the end of the generation trajec-
022           tory. Our method, named SPELL for sparse repellency, can be used either with
023           a static reference set that contains protected images, or dynamically, by updating
024           the set at each timestep with the expected images concurrently generated within
025           a batch. We show that adding SPELL to popular diffusion models improves their
026           diversity while impacting their FID only marginally, and performs comparatively
027           better than other recent training-free diversity methods. We also demonstrate how
028           SPELL can ensure a shielded generation away from a very large set of protected
029
              images by considering all 1.2M images from ImageNet as the protected set.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056   Diffusion models (Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020) are by now widely used for engineering and
057   scientific tasks, to generate realistic signals (Esser et al., 2024) or structured data (Jo et al., 2022;
058   Chamberlain et al., 2021). Diffusion models build upon a strong theoretical foundation used to
059   guide parameter tuning (Kingma & Gao, 2023) and network architectures (Rombach et al., 2022),
060   and are widely adopted thanks to cutting-edge open-source implementations. As these models gain
061   applicability to a wide range of problems, their deployment reveals important challenges. In the
062   specific area of text-to-image diffusion (Nichol et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022), these challenges
063   can range from an expensive compute budget (Salimans & Ho, 2022) to a lack of diversity (Ho &
064
      Salimans, 2022; Shipard et al., 2023) and/or fairness (Cho et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024).
065   Controllable Generation. We focus on the problem of ensuring that images obtained from a model
066   are sufficiently different from a reference set. This covers two important use-cases: (i) the purveyor
067   of the model wants images generated with its model to fall outside of a reference set of protected im-
068   ages; (ii) the end-user wants high diversity when generating multiple images with the same prompt,
069   in which case the reference set could consist of all previously generated images, or even other im-
070
      ages generated concurrently in a batch. While the problem of avoiding generating images in a pro-
      tected training set (Carlini et al., 2023) originates naturally when deploying products, that of achiev-
071
      ing diversity within a batch of generated images with the same prompt should not arise, in theory,
072
      if diffusion models were perfectly trained. However, as shown for instance by Sadat et al. (2024),
073   state-of-the-art models that incorporate classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022, CFG) do a
074   very good job at outputting a first picture when provided with a prompt, but will typically resort to
075   slight variations of that same image when re-prompted multiple times. This phenomenon is illus-
076   trated in Figure 1 for three popular diffusion models, Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024), Simple
077   Diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2023) and MDTv2 (Gao et al., 2023).
078
      Contributions. We propose a guidance mechanism coined sparse repellency (SPELL), which repels
079   the backward diffusion at generation time away from a reference set of images.
080
081   • The SPELL mechanism adjusts diffusion trajectories when the generation of an image is expected
082
        to land close to a shielded region (defined as balls around the latent representation of images in the
        reference set). These regions can be static or updated dynamically, when used to obtain diversity,
083
        by using the expectation of other generation trajectories within the same batch.
084
085   • SPELL interventions are sparse in that they only consider by design very few active shielded
086
        images (typically one) in the reference set at each time t, and make these changes rarely throughout
        diffusion trajectory. Because they are driven by geometric considerations on the expected final
087
        generation output at time 0, these interventions happen mostly early in the backward diffusion.
088
089
      • We show that applying SPELL to numerous state-of-the-art open-sourced diffusion models leads
        to images that better reflect the diversity of the true data (see Figure 1) with a marginal or even no
090
        increase in the Fréchet inception distance (FID) between generated images and training dataset.
091
092   • SPELL is simply parameterized by r, the shields’ radius. We show that increasing r increases
093
        accordingly the output’s diversity, with a better diversity-precision trade-off than other recently
        proposed methods (Sadat et al., 2024; Corso et al., 2024; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2024).
094
095   • We showcase that SPELL can be scaled to a static reference set of millions of images, thanks to
096
        fast nearest-neigbor search tools. We use the whole ImageNet-1k dataset as the protected reference
        set, and generate images that are novel, without ever requiring to filter and/or regenerate images.

      4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
292
293   We add SPELL to both class-to-image and text-to-image diffusion models. In the class-to-image
294   setup, we use Masked Diffusion Transformers (MDTv2) (Gao et al., 2023), EDMv2 (Karras et al.,
295   2024), and Stable Diffusion 3 Medium (SD3) (Esser et al., 2024). Since SD3 is a text-to-image
296   model, we follow their template “a photo of a class name”. We use the pretrained model check-
297   points to generate 50,000 256x256 images of ImageNet-1k classes(Deng et al., 2009) without and

405   4.4                               S PARSITY A NALYSIS
406
407   We focus in this section on the dynamics of SPELL interventions and investigate when and how
408   SPELL corrective terms arise. Figure 5a tracks the magnitude of the SPELL correction vector ∥∆∥2
409
      normalized by that of the diffusion score vector ∥∇ log pt (xt |y)∥2 . We track this relative magnitude
      throughout 452 backward trajectories for 50 prompts of CC12M with both intra- and inter-batch
410
      repellency (Equations (5) and (8)). Appendix H adds further setups. First, we find that repellency
411
      only adds a small correction term in most cases. Its magnitude is most often less than 5% of the
412   magnitude of the diffusion score and never exceeds 35%. This explains why our repellency does
413   not reduce image quality or introduce artifacts. A second reason for this is that SPELL corrections
414   happen mostly in the early stages of the backward diffusion, which literature claims to be when the
415   rough image is outlined, rather than in late steps, where the image is refined (Biroli et al., 2024;
416   Kynkäänniemi et al., 2024). Recall that the backwards diffusion starts at t = 1 and outputs the final
417   image at t = 0. Figure 5b shows that at t = 0.8, only 40% of the trajectories have a non-zero
418   repellency term anymore, further declining to 21% at t = 0.6. If we impose a higher repellency
419   radius, the repellency acts for longer. Especially in this cases, adding overcompensation helps. As
420
      intended, the repellency strength is increased and in return stops earlier. These stops are often final:
      The repellency stays zero for the remainder of the generation, verifying that the trajectories do not
421
      bounce back into the repellency radii, as shown in Appendix H, and reaffirming SPELL’s sparsity.

      5     D ISCUSSION
527
528
529   This work introduces sparse repellency (SPELL), a training-free post-hoc method to guide diffusion
530   models away from a certain set of images. This both prevents repeating images that were already
531
      generated, thereby increasing diversity, and allows protecting a certain set of reference images, with
      applications like machine unlearning. SPELL can be applied to any diffusion model, whether it is
532
      class-to-image or text-to-image, and whether it is unconditional or classifier(-free) guided. We see
533
      two main ways in how future work can extend SPELL. First, SPELL can currently be proven to
534   guarantee to generate images outside the shields if all shields are disjoint. If there are overlapping
535   shields, there can be cases where the guarantee is not strict anymore, and the algorithm could be
536   improved to find a direction that points out of the convex hull of all points, at the cost of decreased
537   scalability. Second, we currently apply SPELL with respect to the L2 distance inside the diffusion
538   model’s latent spaces, which lead to visually different outputs. Using a distance inside a semantically
539   structured space could lead to generating more semantically different images. This has the potential
      to further increase metrics like FID and recall, which evaluate generated images in semantic spaces.

       def backward_step(x_t, t, protection_set, r, lambda, repel_within_batch):
973        """
974        A generation step from t to t-1 of a diffusion with repellency.
975        x_t: Matrix of size [batch, dimensions] containing the current latents
976        t: float, current time

977         we want to repel from or a database that will output closest neighbors in this format

979        repel_within_batch: Boolean, whether to apply intra-batch repellency
980        """
981        # Predict x_0 using the diffusion model (using diffusion without repellency)
982        x_0_hat = diffusion_score.predict(x_t, t)
983        # Repel from protection set
984        repellency_term = 0
           if protection_set is not None:
985            if isinstance(protection_set, database):
986                protection_set = protection_set.find_neighbors_within_radius(x_0_hat, radius)
               diff_vec = x_0_hat.unsqueeze(1) - mu.unsqueeze(0)
987            # diff_vec has size [batch, num_protection_images, dimensions]
988            weight = (diff_vec**2).sum(dim=2).sqrt()
               trunc_weight = ReLU(radius / diff - 1)
989            repellency_term += (diff_vec * trunc_weight).sum(dim=1)
990
           # Repel within batch
991        if repel_within_batch:
992            diff_vec = x_0_hat.unsqueeze(1) - x_0_hat.unsqueeze(0)
               # diff_vec has size [batch, batch, dimensions]
993            weight = (diff_vec**2).sum(dim=2).sqrt()
994            trunc_weight = ReLU(radius / diff - 1)
               diag(trunc_weight) = 0 # Don’t repel from the image itself
995            repellency_term += (diff_vec * trunc_weight).sum(dim=1)
996

997        x_0_hat = x_0_hat + lambda * repellency_term
998

999        x_t_minus_1 = calculate_update(x_0_hat, x_t, t)
1000       if t > 0:
               x_t_minus_1 += generate_noise(t)

1013
       their metadata. To turn this into our soft-label subset of CC12M, where each caption has a set of
1014   multiple possible images related to it, we first group all images in CC12M by their caption and keep
1015   only captions with at least four and at most 128 images.
1016
1017
       Some of these images are falsely grouped together. For example, there are photo albums whose
       images were assigned the same generic title in their metadata. A useful heuristic to filter out such
1018
       cases is to analyze the top-level domains of the images. We filter out sets where the most frequent
1019
       top-level domain belongs to 75% or more of the image urls. Second, we filter out automatically
1020   generated captions by removing captions that include the strings ’Display larger image’, ’This image
1021   may contain’, ’This is the product title’, or ’Image result for’. Last, due to privacy guidelines, we
1022   filter out any caption whose image may include individuals. This is done by filtering out caption
1023   that include ’<PERSON>’, which is a placeholder that the CC12M dataset overwrote any possible
1024   person name with. After these filtering steps, we arrive at 5554 captions. We randomly split them
1025   into a validation set of 554 captions and a test set of 5000 captions. Table 3 shows how many images
       belong to each caption.

1029   P (X|c) to something different from the training distributions. In other words, a model that learned
1030   the train distribution ideally is expected to have a stronger mode at near-duplicate images but testing

1220      I                             I MAGE P ROTECTION ON L ARGE DATASETS
1221
1222     Image protection involves computing the repellence between the current batch xt being generated
1223     with a large dataset D of size N , with N ≫ 105 . This dataset will be typically too large to fit
1224     entirely in GPU memory. Furthermore, computing the repellence term of each element of the batch
1225     with every element of the dataset would be prohibitive. However, since the repellence term is zero
1226     for vectors that are far-away, this opens the possibility of an optimization: first, the closest images
1227     from the batch are retrieved using a vector similarity index (stored in RAM), and only then these
1228     images are moved into GPU memory for the actual computation of the repellence term. An efficient
1229     implementation of this technique is provided by the Faiss library (Douze et al., 2024). We use
1230
         the IndexIVFFlat object, that rely on Voronoi cells to cluster vectors and speed-up search. We
         chose a number of Voronoi cells equal to the square root of dataset size, i.e 1131 cells containing
1231
         typically 1132 examples each. During generation, we probe only the two voronoi cells closest to the
1232
         current expected outputs. The behavior of the repellence term ensures that false positive are rarely a
1233     problem. False negatives (if any) are typically “far-away” which means that their contribution to the
1234     sum of all ReLU repellency terms would have been small. In Table 2, we show that one Voronoi cell
1235     is often enough. Searching the ten closest cells gives an even higher protection rate, though at the
1236     cost of higher searching costs. This shows that advances in efficient search algorithms will directly
1237     benefit SPELL when it is applied to large repellency sets.
