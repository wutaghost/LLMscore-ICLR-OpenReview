011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Matrix quantization is crucial for reducing the memory footprint of matrices
014            across various applications, including large-scale machine learning models and
015            data compression. We have observed that matrices in different application do-
016            mains exhibit heterogeneity in the distribution across columns. Leveraging this
017
               characteristic, we introduce HyperChr, a novel matrix quantization algorithm
               tailored for heterogeneous data distributions prevalent across different matrix
018
               columns. Unlike traditional quantization methods, HyperChr capitalizes on the
019
               heterogeneous distribution characteristics of each column to optimally partition
020            high-dimensional subspaces and perform compression within each subspace. This
021            technique enhances the compression effectiveness by grouping vectors with simi-
022            lar distribution ranges, enabling more precise quantization. Moreover, HyperChr
023            dynamically adjusts the number of centroids in each subspace based on the spe-
024            cific data distribution traits, optimizing both storage efficiency and data fidelity.
025            We evaluate HyperChr’s performance on diverse datasets, demonstrating its supe-
026            riority in reducing quantization errors compared to existing methods. Our results
027            show that HyperChr exhibits significant improvements at lower compression ra-
028            tios (θ = 2 − 8), reducing MAE by an average of 55.3% and MSE by 75.3%
029            compared to PQ. However, at higher compression ratios (θ = 10 − 16), the im-
030            provements are more moderate, with an average reduction of 14.9% in MAE and
031
               25.9% in MSE compared to PQ. In addition, our algorithm reduces the average
               dequantization time by 62.9%, which is crucial for large language model infer-
032
               ence.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Matrix quantization refers to the process of reducing the memory footprint of a matrix while mini-
039
      mizing certain performance metrics, such as mean squared error (MSE) or cross-entropy. The goal
      is to represent matrix elements in a more compact format without sacrificing too much accuracy.
040
      When the quantized matrix needs to be used, it can be dequantized to restore the matrix, enabling
041
      further computations with minimal loss in precision.
042
043   With the rise of big data and large models, the computation and storage of large matrices have
044   become increasingly critical. For instance, GPT-2 contains 1.5 billion parameters Solaiman et al.
045
      (2019), whereas GPT-3 has expanded to 175 billion parameters Brown et al. (2020). There-
      fore, matrix quantization plays a crucial role in Large Language Models (LLMs) weight quanti-
046
      zation Dettmers et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Shao et al. (2023); Xiao et al. (2023). Additionally,
047
      the KV cache accounts for over 30% of GPU memory during deployment, compared to the 65%
048   occupied by the parameters Kwon et al. (2023). Both the parameters and KV cache are stored in
049   the form of matrices, and many recent model quantization techniques have been applied to LLM k-v
050   cache quantization Liu et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Hooper et al. (2024); Kawakibi Zuhri et al.
051   (2024); Duanmu et al. (2024); Yue et al. (2024); Lee et al. (2024); Adnan et al. (2024). In Q4 2023,
052   Facebook reported 3.05 billion monthly active users, with user data typically represented as graphs
053   and matrices fac. Consequently, matrix quantization is widely used in graph compression Brisaboa
      et al. (2009); Claude & Ladra (2011). Matrix quantization also finds applications in other fields, such

055   Ning et al. (2016).

057   There has been a growing body of related work on matrix quantization, which can be broadly clas-
058
      sified into two categories: general-purpose matrix compression algorithms and task-specific matrix
      compression algorithms. General-purpose matrix compression techniques include product quanti-
059

060
      optimized product quantization (LOPQ) Kalantidis & Avrithis (2014). In contrast, task-specific ma-
061   trix compression algorithms are designed for particular use cases, such as large language models
062   (LLMs) or other machine learning applications. These include methods like LLM.int8 Ge et al.
063   (2013), Optimal Brain Damage LeCun et al. (1989), GPTQ Frantar et al. (2022), AWQ Lin et al.
064   (2023), SmoothQuant Xiao et al. (2023), OmniQuant Shao et al. (2023), and SqueezeLLM Kim et al.
065   (2023). The first category of methods primarily builds on the PQ algorithm, focusing on making
066   various optimizations for general use, while the second category integrates task-specific heuristics
067   into the Round-To-Nearest (RTN) algorithm Gray & Neuhoff (1998), which quantizes elements by
068   rounding each value to its nearest representable level.

084   However, many real-world datasets exhibit heterogeneous distributions across different columns.
085   This heterogeneity is particularly prevalent in applications such as Large Language Model (LLM)
086
      weights, LLM key-value (KV) caches, image retrieval, and other applications, where certain
      columns of data display vastly different statistical properties compared to others.
087
088   Figures 1a, 1b, and 1c illustrate the column distributions for LLM weights, GIST cache, and SIFT
089   descriptor, respectively. In each figure, the solid line represents the distribution of the entire column,
090   while the dashed line shows the distribution when randomly selecting half of the data from that
091   column. These visualizations highlight that while different columns exhibit unique distributions,
092
      the distributions within the same column are quite consistent, pointing towards the necessity for
      distribution-aware quantization strategies.
093
094   Traditional quantization methods, which typically assume a homogeneous distribution across all
095   elements, often yield suboptimal results when applied to these heterogeneous matrices, ignoring the
096   diverse nature of real-world data.
097   Existing matrix quantization algorithms, such as Product Quantization (PQ), RTN, and its variations,
098   have demonstrated promising performance. Nevertheless, they do not explicitly consider hetero-
099   geneity in data distributions. These methods typically apply uniform quantization without adapting
100   to the diverse nature of the data. This uniform approach can lead to suboptimal performance when
101   dealing with matrices that exhibit heterogeneous data distributions across their dimensions.
102   In this paper, we introduce HyperChr (HyperChromosome), a novel algorithm tailored to exploit
103   the heterogeneity in data distributions across matrix columns. Recognizing that data exhibit diverse
104   distributions along each column, HyperChr strategically partitions high-dimensional subspaces ac-
105   cording to the distribution properties of each column. This approach allows for the grouping of
106   vectors with similar distribution ranges into the same subspace, enhancing the efficacy of the quan-
107   tization process. Additionally, we propose a method to dynamically determine the compression ratio
      (number of centroids) for each subspace based on its characteristics.

      We validate the effectiveness of HyperChr across a range of datasets, demonstrating that it consis-
109   tently achieves lower quantization errors compared to conventional methods. The results show that
110   the HyperChr algorithm exhibits significant improvements at lower compression ratios1 (θ = 2 − 8),
111   reducing MAE by an average of 55.3% and MSE by 75.3% compared to PQ. However, for higher
112   compression ratios (θ = 10 − 16), the improvements are more moderate, with an average reduc-
113   tion of 14.9% in MAE and 25.9% in MSE compared to PQ. In addition, our algorithm reduces the
114   average dequantization time by 62.9%. Fast dequantization is crucial for large Language model
115   inference. Our code has been open-sourced 2 .
116   Our contributions in this work are threefold:
117
118       • We identify the heterogeneous distribution characteristics of matrices in different application
119         scenarios.
120       • Leveraging the heterogeneous distribution properties of matrices, we design the HyperChr al-
121         gorithm to improve the performance of matrix compression.
122       • We dynamically adjust the intervals according to the varying distribution characteristics of the
123         data.

      3     METHODS
194
195
      The core idea of HyperChr is to leverage the characteristic that data within the matrix column have
196   similar distributions, while the distributions differ across columns. By partitioning data within each
197   column based on their distribution, different column intervals form high-dimensional subspaces.
198   The vectors within each high-dimensional subspace exhibit similarity, leading to more effective
199   quantization results.
200
201   3.1   A LGORITHM L OGIC
202
203   The design and operations of the HyperChr algorithm are illustrated in Figure 2, which provides a
204   conceptual overview of the algorithm’s workflow and its key components. The algorithm is struc-
205   tured into four main parts: matrix partition, subspace generation, subspace ID generation, and matrix
206
      quantization. Each component plays a crucial role in optimizing the quantization process.
207   Matrix Partition: Firstly, during the matrix partition phase, we pre-partition the columns of the
208   original matrix to reduce the computational complexity of subsequent operations and to avoid the
209   exponential explosion associated with high-dimensional subspaces. Specifically, we divide the ma-
210   trix’s columns into several groups, such as grouping every four columns together as shown in Fig-
211   ure 2 This strategy helps reduce computational and storage overhead when dealing with large-scale
212
      data.
213   Subspace Generation: During the subspace generation phase, we generate intervals for columns in
214   the same position across different groups based on their combined data distribution. For example,
215   as illustrated in Figure 2, the first column of the first group and the first column of the second group
      are considered together. Considering each column’s data distribution separately would increase the


                                                                      4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      storage space of intervals and lead to suboptimal results; in the example shown, it would triple the
217   storage space. These intervals reflect the range and distribution characteristics of the data in each
218   column. By combining intervals from different columns, we generate high-dimensional subspaces.
219
220
      Subspace ID Generation: In the subspace ID generation phase, we map each vector in the origi-
      nal matrix using the intervals generated in the subspace generation step. Specifically, we determine
221
      the combination of intervals where each vector’s elements fall, thereby assigning it a unique sub-
222
      space identifier. This process maps data from the original high-dimensional space into predefined
223   subspaces, facilitating subsequent quantization operations.
224
225   Matrix Quantization: Finally, in the Matrix Quantization phase, we perform independent quanti-
226
      zation for the set of vectors within each subspace. Since vectors within a subspace exhibit similar
      features, the quantization algorithm can improve quantization effectiveness and accuracy. We com-
227
      press the vectors in each subspace into several centroids using a clustering algorithm, and then use
228
      the indices of the centroids to replace the data in the original matrix.

349   Platform and Implementation: We performed our algorithm evaluations on a high-performance
350   server configured with an Intel Core i9-10980XE processor, which boasts 18 cores and 36 threads,
351   operating at a base frequency of 3.00 GHz. The server is equipped with 128GB of 3200MHz DDR4
352   memory and a 24.8MB L3 cache, ensuring robust computational capabilities. All algorithms were
353   implemented in Python, specifically version 3.8.10. In each experimental scenario, we repeated the
354   evaluation 100 times.
355   Dataset: To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed algorithm, we conducted experiments using
356   one synthetic dataset and three real-world datasets: a synthetic normal distribution dataset, LLM
357   weight dataset, GIST dataset, and SIFT dataset. Below, we provide a detailed description of each
358   dataset. Each element in the matrix is represented with 32 bits.
359   (1) Synthetic normal distribution dataset: The dataset was constructed by sampling each entry of the
360   matrix from a truncated normal distribution, characterized by a mean of 0.5 and a standard deviation
361   of 0.16. Furthermore, approximately one element out of every ten thousand was substituted with an
362   outlier, randomly selected from the interval [-100, 100]. The matrix dimensions are set to 1,024 ×
363   128.
364
      (2) LLM weight dataset: This dataset includes weight matrices derived from the large language
365   model (LLM) LLaMA2 (Touvron et al. (2023)). The dimensions of the LLM weight matrices are
366   11,008 × 4096.
367
368
      (3) GIST dataset: The GIST dataset consists of feature vectors used for image recognition and

369
      et al. (2008)). Moreover, this dataset has been employed in Jegou et al. (2010) and Oliva & Torralba
370
      (2001). The dimensions of the GIST dataset are 100,000 × 960.

372   (4) SIFT dataset: The SIFT descriptors were obtained by extracting the learning set from Flickr
373   images, while the database and query descriptors were sourced from the INRIA Holidays image
374
      collection Jegou et al. (2008). The matrix size for the SIFT dataset is 100,000 × 128.

526     5            C ONCLUSION
527
528
529     Matrix quantization is essential for reducing memory usage while maintaining accuracy across vari-
530
        ous applications, especially with the increasing scale of models like GPT-3. Traditional quantization
        methods often overlook the heterogeneous distribution characteristics of real-world datasets, which
531
        can lead to suboptimal results.
532
533     In this paper, we introduced HyperChr, a matrix quantization algorithm tailored for heterogeneous
534     data distributions. By partitioning high-dimensional subspaces based on column-specific distribu-
535     tion properties, HyperChr enhances both compression effectiveness and computational efficiency.
536
        Our experimental results demonstrate that HyperChr significantly reduces quantization errors and
        dequantization time, particularly at lower compression ratios, outperforming traditional methods.
537
538     These improvements highlight the potential of HyperChr in optimizing large-scale matrix quanti-
539     zation for real-world applications, especially in large language models and other machine learning
        tasks.
