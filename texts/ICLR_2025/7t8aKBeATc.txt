011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The generalized smooth condition, (L0 , L1 )-smoothness, has triggered people’s
014            interest since it is more realistic in many optimization problems shown by both
015
               empirical and theoretical evidence. To solve the generalized smooth optimization,
               gradient clipping methods are often employed, and have theoretically been shown to
016
               be as effective as the traditional gradient-based methods(Chen et al., 2023; Xie et al.,
017
               2024). However, whether these methods can be safely extended to zeroth-order
018            case is still unstudied. To answer this important question, we propose a zeroth-order
019            normalized gradient method(ZONSPIDER) for both finite sum and general expec-
020            tation case, and we prove that we can find ϵ- stationary point of √
                                                                                 f (x) with optimal
021            decency on d and ϵ, specifically, the complexes are O(dϵ−2 n max{L0 , L1 })
022            in the finite sum case and O(dϵ−3 max{σ12 , σ02 } max{L0 , L1 }) in the general ex-
023            pectation case. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that sample
024            complexity bounds are established for a zeroth-order method under generalized
025            smoothness.

041   has been made in addressing this problem under the L-smooth assumption, with numerous studies
042   contributing to this area.Notable examples include stochastic gradient descent (SGD) (Ghadimi and
043   Lan, 2013), and variance reduction methods (Johnson and Zhang, 2013; Fang et al., 2018; Cutkosky

      Several works have explored the fastest achievable rates in stochastic

046

047   case. Despite these strong theoretical results, they all rely on the L-smooth assumption, which may
048   not hold in critical applications such as LSTM (Zhang et al., 2019). A key observation is that the
049   smoothness parameter L scales with the gradient norm, leading to the introduction of the generalized
050   (L0 , L1 )-smooth assumption:
051
                              ∥∇f (x) − ∇f (x′ )∥ ≤ (L0 + L1 ∥∇f (x)∥) ∥x − x′ ∥                           (3)
052
053   Since the traditional L-smooth assumption is a special case where L1 = 0, solving problems under
      the more general (L0 , L1 )-smooth condition is harder. To address this, (Zhang et al., 2019) introduced

054
      a gradient clipping method that finds an ϵ-stationary point in O(ϵ−4 ) iterations, demonstrating that
055   it can be arbitrarily faster than gradient descent (GD) when the problem has poor initialization.
056   In the traditional L-smooth case, variance reduction methods achieve an O(ϵ−3 ) rate, motivating
057   exploration of similar techniques under the (L0 , L1 )-smooth condition. Recent works (Chen et al.,
058   2023; Reisizadeh et al., 2023) achieved this O(ϵ−3 ) rate by incorporating SPIDER (Fang et al., 2018).
059   Thus, the (L0 , L1 )-smooth case can be as effective as the traditional L-smooth assumption with
060   first-order oracle.
061   On the other hand, these methods require access to the gradient of the objective function, however
062   in some important applications the explicit expressions of gradients of the objective function are
063   expensive or infeasible to obtain, and only function evaluations are accessible. Such as class of
064   applications include black-box adversarial attacks on deep neural networks (DNNs) (Papernot et al.,
065   2017; Chen et al., 2017) and reinforcement learning (Malik et al., 2018; Kumar et al., 2020). Zeroth-
066   order optimization is a fundamental research topic serving as a prototype module for above numerous
067   tasks. However, all of zeroth-order optimization only studied under traditional L-smooth assumption.
068   This motivates us to explore zeroth-order optimization methods under (L0 , L1 )-smooth case, as
069
      mentioned in the previous discussion, SGD can’t be directly applied to (L0 , L1 ) case, leading to the
      natural question:
070
071
072         Can zeroth-order methods solve generalized (L0 , L1 )-smooth nonconvex problems as effi-
073         ciently as solving traditional smooth nonconvex problems? In particular, what convergence
074
            rates can be achieved?
075
076   This paper answers this question by proposing a√zeroth-order normalized gradient method,
077   which can find a stationary point of f (x) with O(d nϵ−2 max{L1 , L0 }) in finite sum case and
078   O(dϵ−3 max{L1 , L0 } max{σ1 , σ0 }) in expectation case, both enjoy the optimal dependency on ϵ
079
      and d, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that sample complexity bounds are established
      for a zeroth-order method under generalized smoothness.

      Among the related works, the most relevant to ours are (Reisizadeh et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023;

087

091   2019a), while we both use a minibatch version of the rand gradient estimator, we extend the analysis
092   to (L0 , L1 )-smoothness and have additional analysis of expectation settings. Our contributions can
093   be summarized as follows:

098             order method (L0 , L1 ) can as effective as in L-smooth case. Especially, our method requires

520   5   CONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we address the question of whether zeroth-order methods can be safely applied to
523   problems that exhibit (L0 , L1 )-smoothness. We propose a variance-reduced zeroth-order method
524
      called ZONSPIDER, a variant of SPIDER (Fang et al., 2018), which utilizes normalized stepsizes
      and zeroth-order gradient estimators. We provide an analysis of both coordand rand estimators under
525
      the finite sum and general expectation cases, showing that the total number of function value queries
526
      required to obtain an ϵ-stationary point is upper bounded by O(dϵ−2 ) and O(dϵ−3 ), respectively. To
527   the best of our knowledge, this is the first application of zeroth-order methods to (L0 , L1 )-smooth
528   problems. A further direction for research is to explore whether zeroth-order methods can be safely
529   applied to additional problems under the (L0 , L1 )-smooth condition, such as P L-conditions, strongly
530   convex conditions, and general convex conditions.
