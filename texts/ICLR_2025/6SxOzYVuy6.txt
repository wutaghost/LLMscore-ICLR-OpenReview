011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series forecasting is crucial in various fields, including finance, energy con-
014            sumption, weather, transportation, and network traffic. It necessitates effective
015            and efficient sequence modeling to encapsulate intricate temporal relationships.
016            However, conventional methods often aggregate sequential information into repre-
017            sentations of each time point by considering other points in the sequence, thereby
018
               ignoring the intra-individual information and suffering from inefficiency. To ad-
               dress these challenges, we introduce a novel approach, DROSIA: Decoupled
019
               Representation On Sequential Information Aggregation, which only integrates
020
               temporal relationships once as an additional representation for each point, achiev-
021            ing sequential information aggregation in a decoupled fashion. Thus balancing
022            between individual and sequential information, along with a reduction in com-
023            putational complexity. We select several widely used time series forecasting
024            datasets, and previously top-performing models and baselines, for a comprehen-
025            sive comparison. The experimental results validate the effectiveness and efficiency
026            of DROSIA, which achieves state-of-the-art performance with only linear com-
027            plexity. When provided with a fair length of input data, the channel-independent
028            DROSIA even outperforms the current best channel-dependent model, highlight-
029
               ing its proficiency in sequence modeling and capturing long-distance dependen-
               cies. Our code will be made open-source in the subsequent version of this paper.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   A time series is a sequence of data points recorded in chronological order, which reflects the at-
036   tribute characteristics of an object at various stages of its dynamic development. Time series data
037   spans across numerous fields, including finance, energy consumption, weather, transportation, and
038   network traffic. This type of data typically presents high-dimensional features and long sequences,
039
      characterized by intricate nonlinear relationships between time points. These complexities make it
      challenging to predict future developments accurately based on historical data. Consequently, time
040
      series forecasting stands as one of the most significant and challenging domains within data analysis,
041
      demanding effective and efficient sequence modeling to capture complex temporal relationships.
042
043   In recent years, numerous studies on time series forecasting have shown that deep learning methods
044   significantly outperform traditional approaches, elevating deep learning forecasters to the forefront
045
      of research. For example, MLP-based models (Oreshkin et al., 2020; Tolstikhin et al., 2021; Zeng
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Han et al., 2024) have garnered significant interest
046
      for their simplicity, efficiency, and predictive accuracy. CNN-based (Bai et al., 2018; Wang et al.,
047
      2022; Gao et al., 2020; Sen et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023) and RNN-based (Lai et al.,
048   2018; Voelker et al., 2019; Salinas et al., 2020) models have enhanced forecasting effectiveness by
049   integrating local or global spatio-temporal information from time series data. Subsequently, meth-
050   ods based on attention mechanism have emerged as the dominant approach in sequence modeling,
051   empowering numerous deep learning forecasters (Qin et al., 2017) to further refine their temporal
052   relationship capturing capabilities. Particularly, Transformer-based models (Li et al., 2019; Chen
053   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2023; Nie et al.,
      2023; Liu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024), have showcased unparalleled prowess in sequence modeling.

054
      Existing sequence modeling methods typically aggregate sequential information into representations
055   of each time point by considering other points in the sequence, which overlooks the unique informa-
056   tion within individual points and may lacks efficiency. For instance, the self-attention mechanism,
057   attends to all time points to update the current one, leading to a quadratic computational complex-
058   ity that can become a bottleneck in the training and the inference processes (Dao et al., 2022).
059   Additionally, the distinct information within each point can be compromised during sequence mod-
060   eling. However, “the ‘structure’ (sedimented individual meanings) is powerful” (Fine, 1993). In-
061   spired from the concept of Transverse Interaction: Individuals recognize the physical environment
062   as a symbolic other and use this understanding to structure their interaction with a “generalized
063
      other” (Weigert, 1991). we propose a sociological perspective on the relationship between time se-
      ries and individual points, which emphasizes that individual information is of great significance and
064
      necessitates a full interaction with the collective to enhance sequence modeling. Current methods,
065
      however, may overly sacrifice individual information for the sake of sequential information.
066
067   To illustrate our concept and address the limitations of current sequence modeling methods, we have
068   developed a novel approach called DROSIA, which integrates rich temporal relationships as addi-
069
      tional representations for each time point, thereby enhancing the expressive power of the data and
      better balancing the trade-off between sequential information and individual point information. We
070
      have conducted comprehensive experiments on several prominent and frequently used multivariate
071
      long-term time series forecasting datasets. DROSIA has demonstrated exceptional sequence model-
072   ing capabilities, and the results suggest that our proposed model attains state-of-the-art performance
073   in downstream tasks while notably decreasing computational complexity. The contributions of this
074   paper can be summarized as follows:
075
076          • We propose a novel sequence modeling method – DROSIA, which aggregates sequential
077            information in a decoupled fashion, effectively balancing it with information of individuals.
078          • DROSIA exhibits exceptional proficiency in time series forecasting, achieving state-of-the-
079            art performance with linear complexity, especially in experiments involving long sequences
080            and large datasets, highlighting its efficacy in capturing long-distance dependencies.
081          • When compared to several previous state-of-the-art channel-dependent models, DROSIA
082            demonstrates superior performance across all datasets with a fair input length compared to
083            the channel amount. Note that DROSIA does not leverage any inter-channel information.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
087
      Sequential Information Aggregation Methods. Sequence information aggregation, or sequence
088
      modeling, is a pivotal technology across various fields, including natural language processing,
089
      speech recognition, and time series analysis. RNNs (Elman, 1990) process sequential information
090   through recursive computations. LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and GRU (Cho et al.,
091   2014) are two most commonly employed variants, which effectively manage the forgetting and re-
092   tention of information via gating mechanisms, thereby mitigating the challenges traditional RNNs
093   encounter when learning long-distance dependencies. RCNN (Girshick et al., 2014; Gu et al., 2021)
094   leverages the strengths of both RNNs and CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998), extracting local features
095   through convolutional operations before aggregating information via recursive computations.
096   Subsequently, the attention mechanism has become the dominant technology for sequence model-
097   ing. Traditional models have been bolstered by the integration of attention mechanisms (Qin et al.,
098   2017), and the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), which is built on self-attention, has seen remark-
099   able success across a wide range of tasks. However, the attention mechanism has drawbacks in
100   terms of computational efficiency. Its high computational cost can be a significant barrier for many
101   researchers and engineers, thereby hindering its widespread adoption and dissemination.
102   Time Series Forecasting Models. In recent years, deep networks have advanced significantly in
103   time series forecasting. RNN-based models (Lai et al., 2018; Voelker et al., 2019; Salinas et al.,
104   2020) are effective in capturing temporal relationships but suffer from computational inefficiency
105   and limited capability in modeling long-distance dependencies. CNN-based models (Bai et al.,
106   2018; Wang et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2020; Sen et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023),
107   which perform convolution to hierarchically extract temporal features, have achieved competitive
      forecasting performance. MLP-based models (Oreshkin et al., 2020; Tolstikhin et al., 2021; Zeng

      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Han et al.,
109   2024) have garnered considerable interest due to their efficient data processing and ability to capture
110   temporal relationships.
111
112
      Inspired by the capabilities of Transformer-based models (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Zhou
      et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024;
113
      Dai et al., 2024) in capturing long-distance dependencies and complex temporal relationships, they
114
      have been extensively applied across various time series tasks. Prior research has largely centered
115   on point-wise modeling. However, due to the computational complexity of Transformer, numerous
116   studies have sought to enhance efficiency. The PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) has demonstrated the
117   advantages of representing time series through patching, effectively reducing sequence length while
118   boosting forecasting performance. Nevertheless, Transformer-based methods still struggle with effi-
119   ciency in multivariate long-term prediction scenarios. iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024) approaches the
120   problem by representing each channel as a whole along the time axis and applying the Transformer
121   encoder to these representations, which significantly reduces complexity but at the cost of losing
122   temporal information, leading to suboptimal performance in cases with fewer channels and longer
123
      sequences. TimeXer (Wang et al., 2024b) leverages the benefits of both PatchTST and iTransformer,
      achieving promising results, yet the computational time remains a significant drawback.
124
125   Moreover, current research related to large language model (LLM) has attracted significant interest.
126   Numerous researchers leverage the pre-trained LLMs to time series analysis (Zhou et al., 2023; Sun
127   et al., 2024), including the forecasting (Chang et al., 2023; Gruver et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024;
128   Jin et al., 2024). Benefiting from the vast amount of pre-trained data and the well-structured em-
129
      bedding space, the LLM-based forecasters have demonstrated promising performance in time series
      forecasting tasks. LLM4TS (Chang et al., 2023) and “OneFitAll” (Zhou et al., 2023) finetune the
130
      LLMs to align the original word embedding with time series embeddings, While TEST (Sun et al.,
131
      2024), S2 IP-LLM (Pan et al., 2024), and TIME-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) tokenize the time series data
132   first, and align them to the semantic space of LLMs, then enhance the models’ effectiveness through
133   various prompt techniques. However, some researchers have also questioned the effectiveness of
134   LLM-based methods in time series forecasting (Tan et al., 2024), after conducting thorough experi-
135   ments for LLM and non-LLM forecasters, they claimed that ”despite the recent popularity of LLMs
136   in time series forecasting, they do not appear to meaningfully improve performance”.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
139
140
      Time series can be defined as X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xt }, x ∈ Rd , where t represents the current time
141   point, starting from 1, and d denotes the dimensionality of the features at each time point. The
142   objective of time series forecasting is to predict the sequence Y = {xt+1 , xt+2 , . . . , xt+h }, with
143   h being the prediction horizon. We propose a novel method called Decoupled Representation On
144   Information Aggregation, abbreviated as DROSIA, which comprises three components: patch em-
145   bedding, DROSIA encoding, and linear decoding. The overall architecture is depicted in Figure 1.
146
147   3.1   PATCH E MBEDDING
148
149   To enhance prediction accuracy and computational efficiency, we define a sliding window of length
150
      k as Ti = {xi+1 , xi+2 , . . . , xi+k } with a stride of s, to segment the time series into patches. We
      utilize a fully connected linear layer for patch embedding, which takes each patch as input and
151
      produces a single vector as the patch’s representation, referred to as Si for the i-th patch.
152
153                                     Si = Linear(Ti ), i = 1, 2, . . . , n                              (1)
154
      In Equation (1), n represents the total number of input patches. The linear layer treats the multivari-
155
      ate time series as multiple univariate series (in a channel-independent manner) (Han et al., 2023),
156   multiplying the k values within the sliding window by a matrix with dimensions k ×d, where d is the
157   dimensionality of patch embedding. This approach aligns with the methodology of PatchTST (Nie
158   et al., 2023). which has been shown the advantages in long-term time series forecasting tasks across
159   various related studies. After extracting patch-wise representations from the time series, PatchTST
160   utilizes the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) to encode these embeddings. In contrast, we employ
161   DROSIA as the encoder. The following subsection will delve into the implementation details of
      DROSIA and highlight its distinctions from the self-attention mechanism and other methods.

473   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
474
475   This paper introduces a novel approach - DROSIA, which incorporates rich temporal relationships
476   as additional representations within each time patch. This method achieves sequential information
477   aggregation in a decoupled fashion, effectively balancing sequential and individual information with
478   linear complexity for sequence modeling. Through comprehensive experimentation, we show that
479   DROSIA attains state-of-the-art performance, particularly in scenarios involving long sequences and
480   large scale data. Compared with previous top-performing channel-dependent models, the channel-
481   independent DROSIA exhibits superior performance across all datasets with a fair the input sequence
482
      length when compared with the amount of channnels. Notably, DROSIA does not rely on inter-
      channel information, highlighting its efficacy in sequence modeling and capturing long-distance
483
      dependencies. We contend that DROSIA is broadly applicable to a variety of scenarios.
484
485   In the ablation study, we have thoroughly demonstrated the efficacy of DROSIA through a multitude
      of meticulously designed experiments. However, we also observed that when the input length of time


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      series is inadequate and the dataset has a large variate size, the prediction accuracy of DROSIA may
487   fall short of channel-dependent methods. This underscores the significance of inter-channel infor-
488   mation. Consequently, our future research will concentrate on integrating inter-channel information
489   without excessively compromising the information within each channel, while also considering the
490   model’s overall efficiency to achieve a better balance. Through additional experiments (not detailed
491   in this paper), we have verified that inter-channel information significantly diverges from sequential
492   information, necessitating distinct integration strategies. Simply applying DROSIA to inter-channel
493   information aggregation may not be feasible. Overall, this paper presents a successful method for
494   enhanced intra-channel modeling and identifies a challenging research direction in time series fore-
495
      casting: how to efficiently model both intra- and inter-channel information simultaneously.
