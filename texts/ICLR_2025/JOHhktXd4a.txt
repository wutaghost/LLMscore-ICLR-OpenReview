011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL) have
014            made it a powerful tool for visual control tasks. Despite improved data efficiency,
015            it remains challenging to train MBRL agents with generalizable perception. Train-
016            ing in the presence of visual distractions is particularly difficult due to the high
017            variation they introduce to representation learning. Building on D REAMER, a
018
               popular MBRL method, we propose a simple yet effective auxiliary task to facil-
               itate representation learning in distracting environments. Under the assumption
019
               that task-relevant components of image observations are straightforward to iden-
020
               tify with prior knowledge in a given task, we use a segmentation mask on image
021            observations to only reconstruct task-relevant components. In doing so, we greatly
022            reduce the complexity of representation learning by removing the need to encode
023            task-irrelevant objects in the latent representation. Our method, Segmentation
024            Dreamer (SD), can be used either with ground-truth masks easily accessible in
025            simulation or by leveraging potentially imperfect segmentation foundation mod-
026            els. The latter is further improved by selectively applying the reconstruction loss
027            to avoid providing misleading learning signals due to mask prediction errors. In
028            modified DeepMind Control suite (DMC) and Meta-World tasks with added vi-
029
               sual distractions, SD achieves significantly better sample efficiency and greater
               final performance than prior work. We find that SD is especially helpful in sparse
030
               reward tasks otherwise unsolvable by prior work, enabling the training of visually
031
               robust agents without the need for extensive reward engineering.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recently, model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) (Sutton, 1991; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018;
038   Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; Hansen et al., 2022; 2023) has shown great promise in learning control
039
      policies, achieving high sample efficiency. Among recent advances, the D REAMER family (Hafner
      et al., 2020; 2021; 2023) is considered a seminal work, showing strong performance in diverse visual
040
      control environments. This is accomplished by a close cooperation between a world model and an
041
      actor–critic agent. The world model learns to emulate the environment’s forward dynamics and
042   reward function in a latent state space, and the agent is trained by interacting with this world model
043   in place of the original environment.
044
045
      Under this framework, accurate reward prediction is all we should sufficiently require for agent
      training. However, learning representations solely from reward signals is known to be challenging
046
      due to their lack of expressiveness and high variance (Hafner et al., 2020; Jaderberg et al., 2017). To
047
      address this, D REAMER employs image reconstruction as an auxiliary task in world model training
048   to facilitate representation learning. In environments with little distraction, image reconstruction
049   works effectively by delivering rich feature-learning signals derived from pixels. However, in the
050   presence of distractions, the image reconstruction task pushes the encoder to retain all image in-
051   formation, regardless of its task relevance. For instance, moving backgrounds in observations in
052   Fig. 1 are considered distractions. Including this information in the latent space complicates dynam-
053   ics modeling and degrades sample efficiency by wasting model capacity and drowning the relevant
      signal in noise (Fu et al., 2021).

      Distractions are prevalent in real-world visual control tasks. A robot operating in a cluttered envi-
072
      ronment such as a warehouse may perceive much task-irrelevant information that it needs to ignore.
073   When training with domain randomization for added policy robustness, task-irrelevant information
074   is actively added and must be denoised. Prior approaches (Zhang et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2021;
075   Deng et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2021; Bharadhwaj et al., 2022) address the noisy reconstruction prob-
076   lem by devising reconstruction-free auxiliary tasks, such as contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020).
077   However, many of them suffer from sample inefficiency, requiring many trajectories to isolate the
078   task-relevant information that needs to be encoded. Training with such methods becomes particu-
079   larly challenging in sparse reward environments, where the signal for task relevance is very weak.
080   Additionally, working with small objects, which is common in object manipulation tasks, poses
081
      difficulties for these methods because those objects contribute less to loss functions and are easily
      overlooked without special attention (Seo et al., 2022).
082
083   Inspired by these problems, we address the following question in this paper: How can we help world
084   models learn task-relevant representations more efficiently? Our proposed solution takes advantage
085   of the observation that identifying task-relevant components within images is often straightforward
086   with some domain knowledge. For instance, in a robotic manipulation task, the objects to manip-
087
      ulate and the robot arm are such task-relevant components, as shown in Fig. 1 (Left). Given this
      assumption, we introduce a simple yet effective alternative auxiliary task to reconstruct only the
088
      task-related components of image observations. We accomplish this by using segmentation masks
089
      of task-related objects which are easily accessible in simulations. Specifically, we replace Dreamer’s
090   auxiliary task to reconstruct raw RGB image observations with an alternative task to reconstruct im-
091   ages with a task-relevance mask applied to them. (Fig. 1c). By doing this, the world model can learn
092   features from a rich pixel-reconstruction loss signal without being hindered by the noise of visual
093   distractions.
094
      In contrast to previous work that incorporates segmentation masks in reinforcement learning (RL)
095
      as an input (James et al., 2019; So et al., 2022), we only use masks in an auxiliary task to improve
096   representation learning. This brings about several advantages. First, we only need segmentation
097   masks during training. The inputs for our method are still the original (potentially distracting)
098   images, so masks are not needed at test time, making our method more computationally efficient.
099   Moreover, the masks we use do not need to be perfect. Since they are used as a target for an auxiliary
100   task, these masks can contain errors as long as they guide feature learning to be informative for the
101   downstream task, which leaves room to replace a ground-truth (GT) mask with its approximation.
102   To this end, we present a way of training with our auxiliary task with segmentation estimates. This
103   can be useful in many practical cases where no GT mask is available during training. This is made
104   possible by recent advances in segmentation foundation models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
105   2023; Xie et al., 2021). Specifically, we fine-tune segmentation models with annotated training
106   data and use them to generate pseudo-labels for the auxiliary task. Fig. 1d shows an example of
107   an auxiliary target made from segmentation prediction. Although the performance with segmenta-
      tion estimates is impressive without further modification, we find that the training can sometimes

108
      be destabilized by incorrect learning signals induced by segmentation prediction errors. Thus, we
109   additionally provide a strategy to make training more robust to prediction errors and achieve higher
110   performance. Specifically, our strategy is to identify pixels where the foundation model mask pre-
111   diction disagrees with a second mask prediction given by our world model. We ignore the RGB
112   image reconstruction loss on these pixels to avoid training on potentially incorrect targets.
113
      As previously mentioned, our method assumes that task-relevant parts are easy to identify in im-
114
      age observations with prior knowledge. This is not a strong assumption in many object-centric and
115   robotic domains, where image observations can often be decomposed into relevant and irrelevant re-
116   gions. However, there are scenarios beyond our scope, where this assumption may not hold because
117   prior knowledge is unavailable or difficult to codify, such as Atari (Bellemare et al., 2013).
118
      We evaluate our method on various robotics benchmarks, including DeepMind Control Suite (Tassa
119
      et al., 2018) and Meta-World (Yu et al., 2019), perturbing both with visual distractions. We show
120
      that our method for reconstructing masked RGB targets using the ground-truth masks in the presence
121   of distractions can reach the same level of performance as training in original environment with no
122   distractions added. Our method for training with approximate masks also shows impressive perfor-
123   mance, often matching the performance of the ground-truth mask variant. In both benchmarks, our
124   approximate-mask method achieves higher sample efficiency and superior test returns compared to
125   previous approaches. Notably, this is accomplished with very few task-specific mask example data
126   points (1, 5, or 10 used for fine-tuning), with much of its strength coming from the power of seg-
127   mentation foundation models. Our method effectively addresses the challenge of training agents in
128   distracting environments by offloading the identification of task-relevant regions to out-of-the-box
129
      segmentation models, thereby achieving great sample efficiency and generalization ability. Further-
      more, our method proves particularly effective in sparse reward environments and those involving
130
      small objects, where prior approaches often struggle.

133   2   R ELATED W ORK
134
135   Model-Based RL for Distracting Visual Environments. Recent advances in MBRL have facil-
136   itated the learning of agents from image observations (Finn & Levine, 2017; Ha & Schmidhuber,
137   2018; Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; 2021; 2023; Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Hansen et al., 2022; 2023).
138   Nevertheless, learning perceptual representations in the presence of distractions remains challeng-
139
      ing in these models (Zhu et al., 2023). For effective representation learning, some works apply
      non-reconstructive representation learning methods (Nguyen et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2022), such as
140
      contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020) and prototypical representation learning (Caron et al., 2020).
141
      However, features learned with these methods do not necessarily involve task-related content since
142   they do not explicitly consider task-relevance in feature learning. Some other works design auxil-
143   iary objectives to explicitly use downstream task information (Zhang et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2021).
144   For example, DBC (Zhang et al., 2021) uses a bisimulation metric (Ferns et al., 2011) to encourage
145   two trajectories of similar behaviors to become closer in a latent space. Perhaps most relevant to
146   our method is TIA (Fu et al., 2021) which explicitly separates task-relevant and irrelevant branches
147   to distinguish reward-correlated visual features from distractions. Features from each branch are
148   combined later to reconstruct the original, distracting observation. Recently, a few approaches pro-
149   posed to leverage inductive biases such as predictability (Zhu et al., 2023) and controllability (Wang
150
      et al., 2022; Bharadhwaj et al., 2022) to learn useful features for visual control tasks. These methods
      have shown to be more effective than using the reward signal alone, but many of them suffer from
151
      sample inefficiency, requiring many samples to implicitly identify what is task-relevant from data.
152
      In contrast, our work proposes to leverage domain knowledge in the form of image masks to pro-
153   vide an explicit signal for identifying task-relevant information. Notably, training in sparse reward
154   environments with distraction has remained unsolved in the literature. Several methods for robust
155   representation learning have also been proposed for model-free RL (Laskin et al., 2020; Kostrikov
156   et al., 2021; Yarats et al., 2021; Hansen et al., 2021; Hansen & Wang, 2021; Nair et al., 2022; Zhang
157   et al., 2019). However, the results suggest that MBRL is more powerful and sample efficient for
158   visual control tasks, thus we focus on comparison with methods in the MBRL framework.
159
160   Segmentation for RL. Segmentation models (He et al., 2017; Redmon et al., 2016) have been
161   used in many downstream tasks, including RL, to assist in pre-processing inputs (Kirillov et al.,
      2023; Anantharaman et al., 2018; Yuan et al., 2018; James et al., 2019; So et al., 2022). Recently,

                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      using segmentation masks in new domains has been made easier by the introduction of one/few-
163   shot masks foundation models (Zhang et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2021) which can quickly adapt to
164   new use-cases. In the context of RL, the prevalent way to use segmentation models is to turn the
165   input modality from RGB images to segmentation masks (James et al., 2019; So et al., 2022). By
166   converting RGB images into semantic masks, agents can effectively handle complicated scenes and
167   thus also be trained with domain randomization. However, processing the input observation requires
168   additional computation at execution time, and an agent trained with predicted segmentation masks
169   can easily break when the segmentation model malfunctions. Our approach, on the other hand,
170   leverages segmentation masks for an auxiliary task, removing the need for the segmentation model
171
      after deployment while also achieving higher test-time performance. FOCUS (Ferraro et al., 2023) is
      another method that uses masked input as an auxiliary target. However, this is primarily devised for
172
      disentangled representation, not for handling distractions. Moreover, it only provides preliminary
173
      results with segmentation models and lacks results and analysis on the effects on downstream tasks.
174
175   Prior works (Wang et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024) integrate segmentation models with RL by pre-
176   processing input observations to isolate task-relevant components. While effective, these methods
177
      heavily rely on segmentation model quality at test time, making them vulnerable to failures in un-
      familiar scenarios that disrupt agent performance. Also, methods such as Zhong et al. (2024); So
178
      et al. (2022) require extensive fine-tuning on large synthetic datasets, resulting in substantial train-
179
      ing overhead, and introduce high computational costs during inference. By leveraging off-the-shelf
180   segmentation models with as few as 1 to 10 examples, our approach reduces training requirements
181   while maintaining robustness and runtime efficiency during deployment.

186

187

253   4.1   U SING S EGMENTATION M ASKS TO F ILTER I MAGE TARGETS
254
255
      We first introduce our main assumption, that the task-relevant components of image observations are
      easily identifiable with domain knowledge. In many real scenarios, it is often straightforward for a
256
      practitioner to know what the task-related parts of an image are, e.g. objects necessary for achieving
257
      a goal in object manipulation tasks. With this assumption, we propose a new reconstruction-based
258   auxiliary task that leverages domain knowledge of task-relevant regions. Instead of reconstruct-
259   ing the raw image observations (Fig. 1b) which may contain task-irrelevant distractions, we apply
260   a heuristic task-relevance segmentation mask over the image observation (Fig. 1c) to exclusively
261   reconstruct components of the image that are pertinent to control.
262
      Since our new masked reconstruction target should contain only image regions that are relevant for
263
      achieving the downstream task, our world model should learn latent representations where a larger
264   portion of the features are useful to the RL agent. By explicitly avoiding modeling task-irrelevant
265   observation components, the latent dynamics should also become simpler and more sample-efficient
266   to learn than the original (more complex, higher variance) dynamics on unfiltered observations. In
267   simulations, ground-truth masks of relevant observation components are often easily accessible, for
268   example, in MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012), through added calls to the simulator API. We term the
269   method trained with our proposed replacement auxiliary task as Segmentation Dreamer (SD) and
      call the version trained with ground-truth masks SDGT .

289   4.2       L EVERAGING A PPROXIMATE S EGMENTATION M ASKS
290
291
      A simulator capable of providing ground-truth masks for task-relevant regions is not always avail-
      able. For such cases where only RGB images are available from the environment, we propose to
292
      fine-tune a segmentation mask foundation model to our domain and integrate its predictions into the
293
      SD training pipeline. Below, we describe our method for training with approximate task-relevance
294   masks, termed SDapprox. .
295
296   As an offline process before training the world model, we fine-tune a segmentation model with
297
      a small number of example RGB images and their segmentation masks annotations that indicate
      task-relevant image regions. Thanks to recent advances in segmentation foundation models, we can
298
      obtain a new domain-specific mask model with a very small amount of training examples. For our
299
      experiments, we use the Personalized SAM (PerSAM) (Zhang et al., 2023) using one-shot adapta-
300   tion and SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021) fine-tuned with 5 and 10 examples. For the sake of controlled
301   and reproducible evaluation, we extract these RGB and mask training pairs from simulators, how-
302   ever, this number of required samples is small enough that it can be collected with expert human
303   annotation as well. Also, although we use these specific foundation models, our method should
304   also be compatible with any semantic masking method. Further details such as how we obtain the
305   fine-tuning data can be found in Appendix K. Once fine-tuning is complete, we incorporate the
306   segmentation model into the SD pipeline to create pseudo-labels for our proposed auxiliary task.
307
308   4.3       L EARNING IN THE P RESENCE OF M ASKING E RRORS
309
310   Although foundation segmentation models generalize well to new scenarios (e.g., different poses,
311   occlusions), prediction errors are inevitable (Fig. 1d). Since each frame is processed independently,
312   segmentation predictions can flicker along trajectories. False negatives in task relevance are particu-
313   larly detrimental when using naive L2 loss on image reconstruction. Missing relevant scene elements
314
      in reconstruction targets can lead the encoder to learn incomplete representations, dropping essen-
      tial task-related information. This variability disrupts the learning of accurate representations and
315
      dynamics in the world model.
316
317   Despite noisy targets, neural networks can self-correct if most labels are accurate (Han et al., 2018).
318   Additionally, D REAMER’s use of GRUs (Cho et al., 2014) provides temporal consistency even with
319   flickering targets. However, as illustrated in Fig. 2 (b)&(c), it’s undesirable to propagate gradients
320
      from regions where the original image has been incorrectly masked out. Allowing gradients from
      these regions provides misleading signals. If we could identify the incorrect regions in the recon-
321
      struction target, we could nullify the decoder’s L2 loss there—a technique we call selective L2 loss.
322
323   Since we cannot directly identify regions where the RGB target is incorrectly masked due to false
      negatives, we estimate them. Preliminary experiments show that a binary mask decoder from world


                                                                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      model states (as an added auxiliary task) can be less prone to transient false negatives, unlike RGB
325   prediction, which tends to memorize noisy labels. Therefore, we propose training a world model
326   with two reconstruction tasks (Fig. 2, right): one decoding masked RGB images and the other
327   predicting task-relevance binary masks. Both use the foundation model’s binary mask, maskFM , to
328   construct targets. The RGB branch decodes masked RGB images, while the binary branch predicts
329   maskFM . We denote the binary masks produced by the world model as maskSD , where pixels labeled
330   true indicate task relevance.
331   To avoid training on incorrectly masked-out regions, we estimate where maskFM may be falsely
332   negative by finding disagreements with maskSD . Specifically, we selectively nullify RGB decoder
333   L2 loss for regions marked false in maskFM but predicted true in maskSD . This prevents training on
334   potentially falsely masked-out pixels still considered task-relevant by a second predictor. Formally,
335   the mask for selective L2 loss is the set difference between true pixel locations in maskSD and
336   maskFM :
337                                   pixelMaskOut = pixelSD \ pixelFM                                 (6)
338
      where pixelMaskOut indicates pixels to nullify loss at, and pixelSD and pixelFM are pixels marked
339   true in maskSD and maskFM , respectively.
340
341   Fig. 2 (d–f) shows examples of maskSD , maskFM , and pixelMaskOut . See Appendix L for details
342
      on obtaining maskSD . Our experiments indicate that selective L2 loss effectively overcomes noisy
      segmentation labels and improves downstream agent performance.
343
344   Lastly, we observe better performance when we prevent gradients from the binary mask decoding
345   objective from propagating into the world model, so we apply a stop gradient to the inputs of the
346   mask decoder head (see Appendix G for ablations).

424
      lations from distractions introduce noise to value-function credit assignment that hinders repre-

      6     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we propose SD, a simple yet effective method for learning task-relevant features in
526
      MBRL frameworks like D REAMER by using segmentation masks informed by domain knowledge.
527
      Using ground-truth masks, SDGT achieves performance comparable with undistracted D REAMER
528   with high sample efficiency in distracting environments when provided with accurate prior knowl-
529   edge. Our main method, SDapprox. , uses mask estimates from off-the-shelf one-shot or few-shot
530   segmentation models and employs a selective L2 loss. It learns effective world models that produce
531   strong agents outperforming baselines.
532
      To the best of our knowledge, our approach appears to be the first model-based approach to success-
533
      fully train an agent in a sparse reward environment under visual distractions, enabling robust agent
534   training without extensive reward engineering. This work also advances the integration of computer
535   vision and RL by presenting a novel way to leverage recent advances in segmentation to address
536   challenges in visual control tasks. The proposed method achieves strong performance on diverse
537   tasks with distractions and effectively incorporates human input to indicate task relevance. This
538   enables practitioners to readily train an agent for their own purposes without extensive reward engi-
539   neering. However, SD has some limitations to consider in future work, which we further explore in
      Appendix O.

       E     E XPERIMENTS WITH D IFFERENT T YPES OF D ISTRACTIONS
1027
1028
       In this section, we investigate how our method performs when faced with types of distractions be-
1029
       yond background distractions. Specifically, we consider three additional types of distractions: fore-
1030   ground distractions, color changes in foreground objects, and camera angle perturbations. These
1031   experiments are conducted on the DeepMind Control Suite.
1032
1033
       E.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
1034
1035   To ensure robustness against distractions during testing, we introduce domain randomizations during
1036   training. Specifically, both the segmentation models and SD are trained under domain-randomized
1037   environments. And, we evaluate our method on a distribution of perturbations that matches the
1038   variability introduced at training time.
1039
1040   Foreground Distractions. To simulate distractions that occlude or block task-relevant parts of the
1041   scene, we introduce a moving foreground distractor. This is implemented as a blue rectangle ren-
1042   dered near the center of the scene for 4–6 frames every 18–22 frames. These intervals are uniformly
1043   sampled each time the distractor appears, meaning approximately 25% of the frames in an episode
1044
       include the distractor. The distractor moves along pixel-space trajectories defined by randomized
       ∆x and ∆y values within the range of (-3,3), which are sampled each time the distractor appears.
1045
       The goal of introducing this type of distractor is to assess whether our method remains robust in the
1046
       presence of occlusions that can interfere with task-relevant visual information.
1047
1048   Although this preliminary setup simplifies the distractor’s appearance and trajectory, it can easily
1049   be extended to incorporate more complex objects or movement patterns. Given the capabilities of
1050
       visual foundation models (VFMs), we hypothesize that our method will generalize well to a variety
       of foreground distractors with different properties.

1298
       Should the SDapprox. world model be shielded from gradients of the binary mask decoder head?
1299
1300   To estimate potential regions on RGB targets where task-relevant regions are incorrectly masked
1301   out, we train a binary mask prediction head on the world model to help detect false negatives in
1302
       masks provided by the foundation model. We see better performance when gradients from this bi-
       nary mask decoder objective are not propagated to the rest of the world model. Thus, the default
1303
       SDapprox. architecture is trained with the gradients of the binary mask branch stopped at its [ht ; zt ]
1304
       inputs, and the latent representations in the world model are trained only by the task-relevant RGB
1305   branch in addition to the standard D REAMER reward/continue prediction and KL-divergence be-
1306   tween the dynamics prior and observation encoder posterior. Tab. 2 shows that the performance
1307   drops significantly when training without stopping these gradients.
1308
       We also examine masks predicted by the binary mask decoder head in Fig. 19. Predictions are
1309
       coarser grained than their RGB counterparts, lacking details important for predicting intricate
1310
       forward dynamics. Overall, reconstructing RGB observations with task-relevance masks applied
1311   demonstrates itself as a superior inductive bias to learn useful features for downstream tasks com-
1312   pared to binary masks or raw unfiltered RGB observations.

       In this section, we describe how we fine-tune segmentation models and collect RGB and segmenta-
1407   tion mask examples to adapt them.
1408
1409   PerSAM. Personalized SAM (PerSAM) (Zhang et al., 2023) is a segmentation model designed
1410
       for personalized object segmentation building upon the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov
       et al., 2023). This model is particularly a good fit for our SD use case since it can obtain a person-
1411
       alized segmentation model without additional training by one-shot adapting to a single in-domain
1412
       image. In our experiments, we use the model with ViT-T as a backbone.
1413
1414   SegFormer. We use 5 or 10 pairs of examples to fine-tune SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021) MiT-b0.
1415   To collect a one-shot in-domain RGB image and mask example for DMC and MetaWorld exper-
1416   iments, we sample a state from the initial distribution p0 and render the RGB observation. In a
1417   few-shot scenario, we deploy a random agent in to collect more diverse observations from more
1418   diverse states.
1419
       To generate the associated masks for these states, we make additional queries to the simulation
1420   rendering API. We represent the pixel values for background and irrelevant objects as false and
1421   task-relevant objects as true. In multi-object cases, we may perform a separate adaptation operation
1422   for each task-relevant object, resulting in more than 2 mask classes. In such cases, before integrating
1423   masks with SDapprox. , we will combine the union of the mask classes for all pertinent objects as a
1424   single true task-relevant class, creating a binary segmentation mask compatible with our method.
1425
       In cases where example masks cannot be programmatically extracted, because such a small number
1426   of examples are required (1-10), it should also be very feasible for a human to use software to
1427   manually annotate the needed mask examples from collected RGB images.
1428
1429
1430   L    D ETAILS ON S ELECTIVE L2 L OSS
1431
1432   The binary mask prediction branch in SDapprox. is equipped with the sigmoid layer at its output. In
1433
       order to obtain binary maskSD , we binarize the SD binary mask prediction with a threshold of 0.9.

1435   M     D ETAILS ON BASELINES
1436
1437   It is known that RePo (Zhu et al., 2023) outperforms many earlier works (Fu et al., 2021; Hansen
1438   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Gelada et al., 2019) and that DreamerPro (Deng
1439   et al., 2022) surpasses TPC (Nguyen et al., 2021). However, theses two groups of works have been
1440   using slightly different environment setups and have not been compared with each other despite
1441   addressing the same high-level problem on the same DMC environments. In our experiments, we
1442   evaluate the representatives in each cluster on a common ground (See Appendix H) and compare
1443   them with our method.
1444   In our experiments, we use hyperparameters used in the original papers for all the baselines, ex-
1445   cept RePo (Zhu et al., 2023) in Meta-World. RePo does not have experiments on Meta-World in
1446   which case we use hyperparameters used for Maniskill2 (Gu et al., 2023) which is another robot
1447   manipulation benchmark.

1449   N    E XTENDED R ELATED WORK
1450
1451
       There are several model-based RL approaches which introduce new auxiliary tasks. Dynalang (Lin
1452   et al., 2024) integrates language modeling as a self-supervised learning objective in world-model
1453   training. It shows impressive performance on benchmarks where the dynamics can be effectively
1454   described in natural language. However, it is not trivial to apply this method in low-level control sce-
1455   narios such as locomotion control in DMC. Informed POMDP (Lambrechts et al., 2024) introduces
1456   an information decoder which uses priviledged simulator information to decode a sufficient statistic
1457   for optimal control. This shares an idea of using additional information available at training time
       with our method SDGT . Although this can be effective on training in simulation where well-shaped


                                                         27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       proprioceptive states exist, it cannot be applied to cases where such information is hard to obtain. In
1459   goal-conditioned RL, GAP (Nair et al., 2020) proposed to decode the difference between the future
1460   state and the goal state to help learn goal-relevant features in the state space.

1463
       O    L IMITATIONS
1464
1465
       Segmentation Dreamer achieves excellent performance across diverse tasks in the presence of dis-
       tractions and provides a human interface to indicate task relevance. This capability enables prac-
1466
       titioners to readily train an agent for their specific purposes without suffering from poor learning
1467
       performance due to visual distractions. However, there are several limitations to consider.
1468
1469   First, since SDapprox. harnesses a segmentation model, it can become confused when a scene contains
1470   distractor objects that resemble task-relevant objects. This challenge can be mitigated by combining
1471
       our method with approaches such as InfoPower (Bharadhwaj et al., 2022), which learns control-
       lable representations through empowerment (Mohamed & Jimenez Rezende, 2015). This integra-
1472
       tion would help distinguish controllable task-relevant objects from those with similar appearances
1473
       but move without agent interaction.
1474
1475   Second, our method does not explicitly address randomization in the visual appearance of task-
1476   relevant objects, such as variations in brightness, illumination, or color. Two observations of the
1477
       same internal state but with differently colored task-relevant objects may be guided toward differ-
       ent latent representations because our task-relevant ”pixel-value” reconstruction loss forces them to
1478
       be differentiated. Ideally, these observations should map to the same state abstraction since they
1479
       exhibit similar behaviors in terms of the downstream task. Given that training with pixel-value
1480   perturbations on task-relevant objects is easier compared to dealing with dominating background
1481   distractors (Stone et al., 2021), our method is expected to manage such perturbations effectively
1482   without modifications. However, augmenting our approach with additional auxiliary tasks based on
1483   behavior similarity (Zhang et al., 2021) would further enhance representation learning and directly
1484   address this issue.
1485
       Finally, our approximation model faces scalability challenges when task-relevant objects constitute
1486   an open set. For instance, in autonomous driving scenarios, obstacles are task-relevant but cannot
1487   be explicitly specified. While our method serves as an effective solution when task-relevant objects
1488   are easily identifiable, complementary approaches should be considered when this assumption does
1489   not hold true.
