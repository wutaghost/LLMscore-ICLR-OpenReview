009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Physical dynamics simulation plays a crucial role in various real-world appli-
012            cations. In this paper, we explore the potential of leveraging Transformers by
013            framing the task as autoregressive next-graph prediction based on spatiotemporal
014            graph inputs. To achieve this, we propose Geometric Spatiotemporal Transform-
015            ers (GSTs), which adopt the expressive encoder-decoder architecture of traditional
016            Transformers. At the core of GSTs are equivariant spatiotemporal blocks that
017            alternate between spatial and temporal modules while preserving E(3) symme-
018
               tries. Additionally, we introduce the Temporal Difference Graph (TDG), derived
               from the difference between the last two frames of historical input, to capture
019
               global dynamic patterns and mitigate cumulative errors in long-term prediction
020
               tasks. Unlike existing Graph Neural Network (GNN) methods, GSTs can process
021            full input sequences of arbitrary lengths to effectively capture long-term context,
022            and address cumulative errors over long-term rollouts thanks to the TDG mecha-
023            nism. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple challeng-
024            ing physical systems at various scales (molecular-, protein-, and macro-level),
025            demonstrating the robust dynamics simulation capabilities.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Accurately simulating the dynamics of physical systems forms the cornerstone of numerous appli-
031   cations. For example, in drug discovery, molecular dynamics simulations provide profound insights
032   into the binding interactions between drug molecules and their target proteins (Salo-Ahen et al.,
033
      2020). Plenty of methods (Battaglia et al., 2016; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020) have emerged to
      simulate physical dynamics as graph translation via Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), given that
034
      many physical systems can be effectively represented as graphs. Further advancements have been
035
      made by leveraging geometric GNNs (Satorras et al., 2021b; Fuchs et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022),
036   which ensure the dynamics to be independent to any rotation, reflection, or translation transfor-
037   mations, thereby aligning seamlessly with E(3) symmetries inherent in physics. Building upon
038   geometric GNNs, several studies (Xu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) adopt a spatiotemporal approach,
039   rather than the previous frame-to-frame setting, which leverages multiple frames to predict the next
040   one, thereby capturing long-term historical information and recovering non-Markovian interactions.
041
      In this work, we investigate the potential of Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) for simulating graph-
042   based physical dynamics. While the Transformer architecture has become the de facto standard in
043   Natural Language Processing (NLP) and various other domains, its application in physical dynamics
044   simulations—particularly in graph-based contexts—remains underexplored. Given that both natural
045   languages and physical trajectories are sequential data, it is promising to leverage the success of
046   Transformers for physical dynamics representation and generation. However, notable differences
047   exist between the two tasks. From a data structure perspective, we need to process spatiotemporal
048   graphs rather than text sequences, and the objective shifts from next-token prediction to next-graph
049   prediction. Importantly, the model must conform to certain physical rules, such as E(3) symmetries,
050
      to ensure generalizability across arbitrary coordinate systems. Additionally, addressing cumulative
      errors is crucial for long-term simulations, necessitating specific design considerations for the model.
051
052   To effectively bridge these gaps, we propose Geometric Spatiotemporal Transformers (GSTs). By
053   inheriting the encoder-decoder architecture from the original Transformer, GSTs can accept spa-
      tiotemporal inputs of arbitrary temporal length and predict long-term future graphs in an autoregres-

074   More importantly, to circumvent the issue of cumulative errors, we predict the difference from the

080   The encoder takes as input the initial trajectory and all predicted frames, while the decoder’s input
081   is further augmented with the TDG.
082   In contrast to existing methods (Satorras et al., 2021b; Wu et al., 2024), our GST exhibits several
083   crucial benefits. On one hand, as a derivative of the Transformer architecture, GST inherits its
084   strong expressivity and allows for more flexible settings. GST can process full input sequences
085   of variable lengths to capture long-term context or adapt to fixed-length inputs for efficiency. In
086   contrast, previous methods are limited to fixed-length input settings (Wu et al., 2024) or even one-
087   frame inputs (Satorras et al., 2021b). On the other hand, while current works primarily focus on
088
      predicting a single frame (Wu et al., 2024), we are concerned with estimating long-term trajectories
      to facilitate practical applications, which also confronts the cumulative error issue. To address this
089
      problem, we leverage the TDG to focus more on the temporal difference prediction and perform
090
      model training under a long-term autoregressive loss.
091
092   In summary, the contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
093
                  • We propose GST, a novel Transformer to simulate long-term physical dynamics autoregres-
094
                    sively. GST inherits the strong expressivity and flexible designs from original Transform-
095                 ers, while promisingly respecting the spatiotemporal geometries and E(3) symmetries.
096
                  • We define TDG to reduce the impact of cumulative errors in long-term rollouts. Initialized
097
                    as the difference between the last two frames, the TDG interacts with all other frames to
098                 capture global dynamics and is used alongside the last frame for next-frame prediction.
099
                  • We conduct extensive experiments on real-world datasets across three different scales:
100
                    molecules, proteins, and human motions. Results demonstrate that our model achieves su-
101
                    perior performance across various challenging settings and exhibits a larger gap in longer-
102                 horizon rollout tasks compared to existing State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) models.

105   2      R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Physical Dynamics Simulation. The simulation of physical dynamics has garnered significant at-
      tention due to its widespread applications in real-world scenarios. Early work (Wu et al., 2015;


                                                              2

108
      2017) combines physics engines with graphics engines, supplemented by deep learning techniques,
109   to achieve preliminary understanding and modeling of physical dynamics. Later the Interaction
110   Network (IN) (Battaglia et al., 2016) is proposed to employ GNN-like message passing to model
111   object interactions in physical systems. Inspired by IN, researchers increasingly recognized GNNs
112   as powerful tools for modeling physical dynamics, owing to their inherent ability to capture com-
113   plex relational structures. This realization spawned numerous extensions and improvements (Li
114   et al., 2020; Finzi et al., 2020). Some approaches also integrated ordinary differential equations
115   with GNNs to model complex dynamics (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2019). However, these meth-
116   ods often overlooked the crucial role of physical symmetries. Consequently, a series of equivariant
117
      GNN models encoding geometric information rapidly emerge (Thomas et al., 2018; Fuchs et al.,
      2020; Huang et al., 2022). Despite these advancements, existing methods frequently suffer from
118
      constraints such as fixed-length input requirements (Xu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) or one-frame
119
      input limitations (Satorras et al., 2021b). Moreover, many focus solely on single-frame predic-
120   tions (Han et al., 2022). In contrast, this paper presents a novel model capable of flexibly accommo-
121   dating variable-length or fixed-length inputs. Furthermore, we explore its performance on long-term
122   trajectory prediction tasks, addressing a critical gap in the current literature.
123
      Deep Spatiotemporal Models. Deep spatiotemporal models have gained prominence across di-
124
      verse real-world applications (Cini et al., 2024; Marisca et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). In traffic
125
      prediction, STGCN (Yu et al., 2017) and DCRNN (Li et al., 2017) leverage graph structures with
126   recurrent or convolutional layers. Attention-based architectures like Gaan (Zhang et al., 2018) and
127   AGL-STAN (Sun et al., 2022) capture temporal and spatial dependencies for traffic flow forecast-
128   ing and crime prediction. Transformer-based models such as MMST-ViT Lin et al. (2023), Multi-
129   SPANS(Zou et al., 2024), and MOIRAI (Woo et al., 2024) have been applied to various Spatiotem-
130   poral tasks including climate change-aware crop yield prediction, traffic forecasting, and general
131   time-series analysis. While effective in their domains, these models typically lack consideration
132   for 3D spatial symmetries inherent in physical systems, limiting their applicability to physical dy-
133   namics simulation. Addressing this, we adapt the standard Transformer architecture to 3D physical
134
      dynamics simulation tasks. By introducing equivariant designs, we ensure conformity with E(3)
      symmetries, bridging the gap between general spatiotemporal modeling and physical dynamics sim-
135
      ulation.

140   In this section, we first introduce the necessary preliminaries related to physical dynamics simula-
141
      tion. Then, we describe the framework of our model, which adopts an equivariant encoder-decoder
      architecture, along with a temporal difference graph to reduce the cumulative errors in long-term
142
      predictions. Fig. 2 illustrates the overall framework of our model.

      3.1    N OTATIONS AND D EFINITIONS
145
146   The trajectory of a physical system (e.g. a molecule) over a temporal length T and with a time lag
147   of ∆t = 11 , can be modeled as a spatiotemporal graph G1:T := {Gt = (Vt , E)}Tt=1 , where different
148   frame Gt shares the same node identities (e.g. atoms) and edge connections (e.g. bonds), and the
149   i-th node vt,i at time t is associated with an invariant feature ht,i ∈ Rc (e.g. atom types) and an
150   equivariant 3D coordinate vector x  ⃗ t,i ∈ R3 . Particularly for ht , we further add temporal position
151   embedding with the sine function. We do not leverage edge features in this work. We hereafter
152   denote by the matrices Ht and X   ⃗ t the collection of all node features and coordinates in Gt .
153
      Task Formulation. As illustrated in Fig. 1, given the observed trajectory G1:T , our goal is to learn a
154   function ϕ that autoregressively predicts the long-term future frames GT +1:T +R over a duration of
155   R ≫ 1. This process can be formally expressed as follows:

      Equivariance. An important inductive bias to consider is that the function ϕ should be E(3)-
194   equivariant, ensuring that the dynamics remain independent of the observation perspective. This
195   means that if the input trajectory undergoes any arbitrary translation, reflection, or rotation transfor-
196   mation, the output of ϕ should undergo a corresponding transformation.
197
      Comparisons with Previous Settings. One remarkable benefit of our function defined in Eq. (1)
198
      is its ability to accommodate inputs of variable temporal length, enhancing flexibility for various
199
      applications. It can take all the historical frames as input for modeling the complete context as
200   defined by default; in case of efficiency considerations, it can also be modified to accept only fixed-
201   length inputs, which degenerates to GT +r = ϕ(GT +r−L:T +r−1 ) using only previous L frames to
202   predict the next frame. On the contrary, previous methods such as EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021a),
203   GNS (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020), and ESTAG (Wu et al., 2024) can only admit the fixed-length
204   input setting. Furthermore, our task emphasizes long-term dynamics prediction, whereas existing
205   methods mostly focus on predicting only one future frame. The architecture of ϕ is specifically
206   designed and will be elaborated on in the following subsections.

      3.2   T HE P ROPOSED GST M ODEL
209
210   Inspired by the original Transformer framework (Vaswani, 2017), the architecture of our proposed
211   GST also consists of an encoder and a decoder, as displayed in Fig. 2. To predict GT +r , the encoder
212   takes as input the initial trajectory and all predicted frames, namely G1:T +r−1 . The decoder’s input is
213   further augmented with an artificial graph Gδ , forming the input (G1:T +r−1 , Gδ ). Both the encoder
214   and the decoder employ a certain number of equivariant spatiotemporal blocks for representation
215   learning. The output from the encoder is integrated into the decoder through an equivariant cross-
      attention layer. Further details are provided below.

500   4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
501
502   To validate the contribution of each module to our GST’s overall performance and to explore the in-
503   tricacies of the Encoder-Decoder framework in physical dynamics scenarios, we conducted ablation
504   and exploratory experiments. The results are presented in Table 4.
505
      Our observations and findings are as follows: 1. Disregarding equivariance led to a significant per-
506   formance degradation across all molecules (Row 1). This underscores the critical role of physical
507   symmetry in modeling 3D physical dynamics. 2. To assess the impact of the TDG, we remove
508   the TDG from the decoder (Row 2). The results indicate that the absence of TDG substantially
509   impairs model performance, suggesting a strong correlation between physical dynamics trajectories
510   and local frames of the input sequence, particularly the final two frames. 3. We experiment with
511   modifying the decoder input to solely include the TDG (Row 3). This modification resultes in a
512   notable performance decline, indicating the necessity of allowing TDG and original sequence to
513   interact, thereby extracting crucial temporal information. 4. To evaluate the benefits of the encoder-
514
      decoder structure, we implement a decoder-only structurte (Row 4). The results demonstrate that the
      encoder-decoder architecture outperforms the decoder-only variant for the majority of molecules. 5.
515
      We adopt the masking strategy from standard Transformer, replacing the current causal-attention in
516
      the encoder’s self-attention module with full-attention (Row 5). This substitution led to a slight per-
517   formance decrease for most molecules, suggesting that when performing self-attention on temporal
518   data, the model must adhere to objective physical laws, allowing the current frame to be updated
519   based solely on previous frames. 6. To ascertain whether the performance improvement is due to
520   the increased parameters introduced by the encoder-decoder architecture, we share parameters be-
521   tween the encoder and decoder modules, effectively halving the total parameters of the model (Row
522   6). We observe performance decreases for some molecules, but improvements for others. Notably,
523   when computing the average across all eight molecules, the shared-parameter version exhibited only
524   a marginal difference in performance compared to the original version. This further emphasizes the
525
      potential of the encoder-decoder architecture in enhancing the model’s long-term prediction capa-
      bilities.

530
531
      In this paper, we introduce Geometric Spatiotemporal Transformers (GST), a novel framework that
532
      integrates the original Transformer architecture with physical symmetries to simulate long-term
533
      physical dynamics. Leveraging the inherent characteristics of the Transformer, GST efficiently pro-
534   cesses historical inputs of arbitrary length. The overall GST architecture ensures E(3)-equivariance,
535   effectively encoding physical symmetries and enhancing the modeling capability for 3D objects. The
536   incorporation of a Temporal Difference Graph (TDG) significantly mitigates cumulative errors gen-
537   erated during long-term rollouts process. Extensive experiments across various scales (molecules,
538   proteins, and human motions) and diverse rollout steps demonstrate GST’s superior performance in
539   physical dynamics simulation. We envision GST serving as a robust baseline in this field, with broad
      applicability across various tasks, including drug discovery, robot control, and materials design.

      A     E QUIVARIANCE OF GST
649
650
      We know that the E(3) symmetry can be decomposed into the symmetry on the three-dimensional
651   translation group T(3) and the symmetry on the three-dimensional orthogonal group O(3). We will
652   prove these two separately below.
653
654
      A.1   T RANSLATION E QUIVARIANCE OF GST
655
656   With mean reduction (Puny et al., 2021), translational variability can always be easily achieved. We
657   just need to prove that subsequent operations are translation invariant. In fact, there are only Eqs. (2)
658   and (4) to (6) require to prove its translation invariant. And we find that, all sums of the coefficients
659   of these coordinate terms is 1 + (−1) = 0, which indicates its translation invariance.
660
661   A.2   O(3)- EQUIVARIANCE OF GST
662
663   O(3)-equivariance of whole model. Since the equivariance can be understood that group actions
664
      can be exchanged with mappings of any layer, so the equivariance of the entire model can be proved
      by proving that the individual modules of the model are equivariant. In general, we only need to
665
      prove the following three points:
666
667         1. O(3)-equivariance of spatial module.
668
            2. O(3)-equivariance of temporal module.
669
670         3. O(3)-invariance of objective function.
671
      Here we give the proof of O(3)-equivariance by the symbology from e3nn (Geiger & Smidt, 2022).
672
      O(3) consists of rotation and inverse, implying O(3) = SO(3) × Ci , where SO(3) is the rotation
673
      group and Ci = {e, i} denotes the inverse group. We thus specify the group representation of O(3)
674   as
675                                     ρ(l) (rm) := σ (l,p) (m)D (l) (r),                          (10)

677   where p ∈ {±1} called parity. σ (l) (m) = 1 for m = e (the identity) , σ (l) (m) = pl if m = i (the
