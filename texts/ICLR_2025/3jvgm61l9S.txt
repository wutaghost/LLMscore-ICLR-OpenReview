011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            With the development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), the eval-
014            uation of multimodal models in the context of mathematical problems has be-
015            come a valuable research field. Multimodal visual-textual mathematical reasoning
016            serves as a critical indicator for evaluating the comprehension and complex multi-
017
               step quantitative reasoning abilities of MLLMs. However, previous multimodal
               math benchmarks have not sufficiently integrated visual and textual information.
018
               To address this gap, we proposed MathScape, a new benchmark that emphasizes
019
               the understanding and application of combined visual and textual information.
020            MathScape is designed to evaluate photo-based math problem scenarios, assess-
021            ing the theoretical understanding and application ability of MLLMs through a
022            categorical hierarchical approach. We conduct a multi-dimensional evaluation
023            on 11 advanced MLLMs, revealing that our benchmark is challenging even for
024            the most sophisticated models. By analyzing the evaluation results, we iden-
025            tify the limitations of MLLMs, offering valuable insights for enhancing model
026            performance. The code is made available https://anonymous.4open.
027            science/r/MathScape-8742.

097   2     R ELATED W ORK
098
099   In the field of MLLMs, the benchmark for multimodal mathematical reasoning capability represents
100   a significant and novel research direction. Mathematical reasoning is a crucial indicator for evaluat-
101   ing the ability of LLMs to perform complex, multi-step reasoning and quantitative analysis within
102   visual contexts. Below, we highlight some relevant work and the latest developments in this area.
103
104   2.1   B ENCHMARK FOR M ATHEMATICAL E VALUATION
105
106   Recent research has seen significant advancements in mathematical reasoning benchmarks aimed at
107   evaluating mathematical abilities. In this summary, we review both pure text and multimodal math
      benchmarks.


                                                          2

108
      Pure Text Benchmarks GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021) is a dataset from OpenAI that includes 8.5K
109   high-quality elementary school math word problems, each requiring 2 to 8 steps to solve. These
110   problems primarily involve basic arithmetic operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication,
111   and division. MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021) offers a dataset of 12,500 problems sourced from high
112   school math competitions. SuperCLUE-Math Xu et al. (2024) is a Chinese benchmark for multi-
113   step reasoning in mathematics, containing over 2,000 problems that require multi-step reasoning
114   and offer natural language solutions. MathBench Liu et al. (2024b) includes 3,709 math problems
115   ranging from basic arithmetic to college-level questions, covering multiple difficulty levels.
116   All these benchmarks focus exclusively on text-based mathematical tasks. They are designed to
117   evaluate the mathematical capabilities of LLMs through specialized problem sets.
118
119   Multimodal Benchmarks With the rapid advancement of MLLMs, several high-quality bench-
120   marks have emerged to evaluate mathematical problem-solving in visual contexts. MathVista Lu
121   et al. (2023b) focuses on visual math QA tasks, assessing model performance across various math
122   domains, such as arithmetic and algebra, using visual scenarios. MATH-V (Wang et al., 2024a) is
123   another benchmark that targets multimodal mathematical understanding, with questions primarily
124   sourced from math competitions. MathVerse Zhang et al. (2024a) evaluates MLLMs’ comprehen-
125   sion of visual diagrams using CoT (Chain of Thought) strategies on 2,612 multimodal math prob-
126   lems. CMMU He et al. (2024) is a large-scale Chinese benchmark for multi-disciplinary, multimodal
127
      understanding, featuring questions from college exams and textbooks.
128   Compared to these existing multimodal mathematical benchmarks, which often have limitations in
129   question length, complexity, and openness to model answers, our MathScape benchmark is designed
130   to be longer and more open-ended.
131
132   2.2   MLLM S FOR M ATHEMATICS
133
134   Commonly Used VLLMs The integration of visual knowledge into LLMs has become a pivotal
135   area of research due to the rapid advancements in LLMs. VLLMs combine vision information from
136   vision encoders with LLMs, thus enabling these models to process and interpret visual inputs for
137
      various visual tasks Liu et al. (2023c); Zhang et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022b) with enhanced accuracy
      and efficiency. Pioneering frameworks like CLIP Radford et al. (2021) leverage contrastive learning
138
      on expansive image-caption datasets to align modalities, forming the groundwork for cross-modal
139
      comprehension. Various adapters Liu et al. (2023b;a); Li et al. (2023b; 2022a); Jian et al. (2023);
140   Lu et al. (2023a) are introduced to further integrate different modalities. For example, LLaVA Liu
141   et al. (2023b;a) employs a straightforward MLP to inject the vision information into LLMs. Whereas
142   more complex implementations like the Q-Former in BLIP Li et al. (2022a; 2023b) utilize cross-
143   attention to enhance modality integration.
144
      Recent studies Wang et al. (2024b); Chen et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b;a); Li et al. (2023a)
145
      aims to boost VLLM performance by focusing on the quality of both pre-training and fine-tuning
146   datasets. Models like LLaVA Liu et al. (2023b;a) and ShareGPT4V Chen et al. (2023) have shown
147   remarkable advancements in understanding and following complex instructions through instruction
148   tuning.
149
150   VLLMs Designed for Math Problems In real-world applications, vision inputs are commonly
151   used to present mathematical problems for models to solve. As a result, it is crucial for Vision-
152   Language Large Models (VLLMs) to demonstrate strong mathematical capabilities. Meidani et
153   al. Meidani et al. (2023) pioneered the use of symbolic data to train a Vision-Language Model
154   (VLM) with mathematical proficiency. Building on this work, UniMath Liang et al. (2023) com-
155   bined vision, table, and text encoders with LLMs, achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance at
156   the time. Additionally, Huang et al. Huang et al. (2024) succeeded in solving algebraic problems
157   that involved geometric diagrams.
158   Another noteworthy line of research involves using LLMs to tackle geometric problems. G-
159   LLaVA Gao et al. (2023) fine-tuned LLaVA Liu et al. (2023b) with geometric data, reaching SOTA
160   performance in geometry. Subsequently, MAViS Zhang et al. (2024b) and EAGLE Li et al. (2024)
161   achieved SOTA results by introducing math-specific encoders and amassing large amounts of math-
      ematical data.

      3.1      C ONSTRUCTION OF M ATH S CAPE
183   Data Preparation The data preparation module consists of three steps, as shown in Figure 3(a).
184   First, we collected a large number of mathematics questions from elementary, junior high, and
185   senior high school exams and homework as the evaluation sample. We gathered a total of 1,325
186   image mathematics questions. Next, the question documents were converted to PDF format using
187   Pandoc and subsequently transformed into images for further use.
188
189   Data Annotation As illustrated in Figure 3(b), the images are then transformed to closely align
190   with real-world scenarios by capturing photos of printed images and screen displays.
191
192   Data Check and Knowledge Classification After constructing the dataset, we perform a double-
193   check and knowledge-based classification to ensure its high quality. As illustrated in Figure 3(c),
194   we rigorously review the dataset to ensure that both the textual and graphical inputs are clear and
195   accurate. Once data quality is verified, we categorize the data according to knowledge points.
196
197   3.2      M ULTIDIMENSIONAL E VALUATION
198
      To comprehensively evaluate the performance of VLLMs, we designed multiple dimensions to clas-
199
      sify and assess their mathematical abilities across various categories. The classification types we
200
      used are as follows:
201
202
      Question Types: We first categorized the test questions into different types, such as multiple-
203   choice, fill-in-the-blank (Solution), and proof questions, to examine the model’s performance across
204   various question formats.
205
206   Knowledge Points: We also classified the questions based on mathematical knowledge areas, in-
207   cluding algebra, geometry, probability, and statistics, to assess the model’s proficiency in different
208   domains of mathematics.
209
210   Educational Stages: Additionally, the questions were divided according to the educational
211   stage—primary school, middle school, and high school—to evaluate the model’s adaptability and
212   accuracy at different levels of education.

248   3.4   DATASET S TATISTICS
249
250   In this section, we provide a summary of the statistics for our MathScape dataset. The dataset
251   primarily consists of Chinese image-text problems, along with question labels, attribute information,
252   problem-solving processes, and standard reference answers. Detailed statistics are presented in
253
      Figure 4.
254   As shown in Figure 4(a), our dataset thoughtfully incorporates the characteristics of multimodal
255   image-text questions. A significant portion of the questions are geometric, which often require the
256   integration of images for effective problem-solving. In contrast, topics like equations and inequali-
257   ties are less represented, aligning more closely with the specific demands of multimodal assessment.
258   Figure 4(b) illustrates that our dataset primarily includes solution questions and multiple-choice
259   questions, with fewer proof questions. This distribution indicates that our dataset is designed to
260   challenge models with diverse question types, while still reflecting the real-world emphasis on prac-
261   tical problem-solving.
262
      Overall, our dataset contains a total of 1,325 images, providing a robust resource for evaluating the
263   mathematical reasoning capabilities of MLLMs.

      5     C HALLENGES AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
433
434
      As highlighted in Section 4, none of the models achieved strong performance on the MathScape
435
      benchmark. In this section, we present several case studies to illustrate the challenges faced by cur-
436   rent MLLMs and propose potential future directions for enhancing their mathematical capabilities.
437
438
      5.1   C HALLENGES
439
440   In this subsection, we explore the main reasons why models provide incorrect answers to image-text
441   mathematical problems. These errors are mainly due to challenges in understanding and interpreting
442   the information. We can break down these challenges into the following specific reasons:
443
444   Unable to Retrieve Information from the Image: This is one of the most common errors, where
445   models may fail to extract all the relevant information from the image. For instance, when interpret-
446   ing complex geometric patterns, it’s easy to overlook certain data or conditions, leading to incorrect
447   answers. As shown in Case Study 1 in Figure 8, the model provided an incorrect proof due to its
448   incomplete understanding of the image.
449
450   Misunderstanding of Graphic Positioning: This issue involves the accurate understanding of the
451   spatial layout of graphics. For instance, in geometry problems, errors can occur if the model fails to
452   correctly recognize the lengths or angles of figures. Such mistakes often stem from a lack of deep
453   understanding of graphic properties or insufficient ability to shift perspectives.
                                                                                  √      In Figure 9, Case
454   Study 2, the model incorrectly interprets the distance from point A to 0 as 2.
455
456   Insufficient Reasoning Ability: This issue arises from the limited logical reasoning capabilities
457   of LLMs. Even when the image information is provided correctly, the LLM may still produce
458   incorrect responses. As shown in Case Study 3 in Figure 10, the LLM fails to solve the complex
459   problem correctly and makes errors in the process.
460   Overall, the challenges for multimodal large models primarily focus on the interpretation of visual
461   information and the inherent reasoning abilities of the models.
462
463   5.2   F UTURE D IRECTIONS FOR M ATH MLLM S
464
465   MathScape have introduced several challenges for MLLMs, as mentioned in section 5.1. In this
466   section, we summarize future directions for MLLMs.
467
468   Stronger LLMs As outlined in Section 5.1, it is clear that LLMs exhibit limitations in mathemati-
469   cal reasoning. Moreover, all visual information must be processed by the LLM, further constraining
470   its problem-solving capabilities. To enhance the mathematical reasoning proficiency of MLLMs, it
471   is essential to develop more advanced LLMs with stronger mathematical reasoning capabilities.
472
473   Better Pattern Recognition Improving pattern recognition is essential for enhancing the perfor-
474   mance of MLLMs, particularly in tasks involving complex visual information. Current models often
475   struggle with identifying and interpreting intricate patterns in images, such as geometric configura-
476
      tions, charts, and fine-grained visual details. Future research should focus on developing models
      that can more accurately recognize and differentiate patterns, especially when they are complex.
477
478   6     C ONCLUSION
479
480   Recently, MLLMs have emerged as powerful models for answering questions across multiple do-
481   mains. However, comprehensive benchmarks that reflect real-world scenarios are needed to evaluate
482   their mathematical performance. In this paper, we introduce MathScape, a new benchmark designed
483   to assess the math capabilities of MLLMs using entirely original, leak-free images. Additionally,
484   we propose a novel two-step evaluation method specifically for assessing long answers. MathScape
485   not only challenges existing MLLMs but also aims to inspire the development of more advanced
      math-focused MLLMs.
