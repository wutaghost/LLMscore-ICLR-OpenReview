009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks exhibit exceptional performance in processing complex data,
012            yet their internal structures remain largely unexplored. The emergence of
013            Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) represents a significant departure from
014            traditional Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs). In contrast to MLPs, KANs re-
015            place fixed activation functions at nodes (“neurons”) with learnable activation
016            functions on edges (“weights”), enhancing both accuracy and interpretability. As
017            data evolves, the demand for models that are both flexible and robust minimiz-
018
               ing the influence of input data variability continues to grow. Addressing this
               need, we propose a general framework for KANs utilizing a Variety Bernstein
019
               Polynomial Function Basis for Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (VBn-KAN). This
020
               framework leverages the Weierstrass approximation theorem to extend function
021            basis within KANs in theory, specifically selecting Bernstein polynomials (Bn )
022            for their robustness, assured by the uniform convergence proposition. Addition-
023            ally, to enhance flexibility, we implement techniques to vary the function basis
024            Bn when handling diverse datasets. Comprehensive experiments across three
025            fields: multivariate time series forecasting, computer vision, and function approx-
026            imation—demonstrate that our method outperforms conventional approaches and
027            other variants of KANs.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Since the 1990s, the advancement of neural network technology markedly improves the efficiency
033
      of CPU program execution, particularly in complex computational tasks and large-scale data pro-
      cessing (Oh and Jung, 2004; Nurvitadhi et al., 2016). This rapid evolution significantly enhances
034
      processing speeds, enabling the implementation of more complex models in practical applications
035
      (Nguyen and Widrow, 1990a;b; Sze et al., 2017). However, despite these substantial performance
036   improvements, the “black box” nature of neural networks continues to pose challenges, limiting the
037   interpretability and transparency of models (Agarwal et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Zhang and
038   Zhu, 2018).
039
      In response to these challenges, many researchers recently attempte to conduct in-depth analyses of
040
      neural network interpretability from various perspectives. However, providing a comprehensive ex-
041
      planation of neural networks remains daunting, largely due to their complex nonlinear structures and
042   vast parameter spaces. These factors make understanding the internal workings of neural networks
043   exceptionally difficult (Zhang et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2021; Youssef et al., 2023). In this context, the
044   Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLPs), based on the universal approximation theorem (Funahashi, 1989),
045   emerges as a classical neural network structure. It has become an important platform for research
046   due to its relatively simple design and clear hierarchical structure (Borghi et al., 2021; Sharma and
047   Kukreja, 2022; Alnuaim et al., 2022; Rana et al., 2018). The advantages of MLPs networks not
048   only enhance the transparency of the model but also lay the groundwork for further optimization
049   and application.
050   Although MLPs provide a certain degree of intuitive understanding of neural network structures,
051   their interpretability remains limited. This limited interpretability arises from the fixed activation
052   functions and linear weight structures of MLPs, which obscure the specific contributions of each
053   neuron to the final decision. In Liu et al. (2024), a new architectural approach named “KAN”
      based on the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation (K-A) theorem (Kolmogorov, 1957b) is proposed


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to enhance the interpretability of neural networks. Unlike MLPs, KANs entirely eliminate linear
055   weight matrices; each weight parameter is replaced by a learnable 1D function. Furthermore, they
056   generalize the original K-A representation to arbitrary widths and depths, thereby revitalizing and
057   contextualizing it within the modern deep learning framework.
058
      A variety of KANs exist (Hou et al., 2024), each adapts to different areas (Muyuzhierchengse, 2024;
059
      Khochawongwat, 2024; Qiu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Koenig et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b;
060
      De Carlo et al., 2024), enhancing the original KANs for specific downstream tasks. However, some
061   of these models maintain a relatively fixed form, and the principles underlying their mathematical
062   reasoning improvements are not systematically examined. As data evolves, issues such as noise
063   and integrity seem to influence the results of models. Relying solely on manual methods, such as
064   labeling and data cleaning, can consume significant resources. Therefore, reducing the algorithms’
065   dependence on data quality is particularly crucial.
066
      In this paper, we propose KANs with a variety function basis, the: Variety Bernstein Polynomial
067   Function Basis for Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (VBn-KAN). This framework allows the model
068   to adapt to various data types and provides more flexible forms of KANs. Specifically, the Weier-
069   strass approximation theorem (WEIERSTRASS, 1885) broadens the selection of function basis,
070   establishing that polynomials can approximate any continuous function. Based on this theorem, we
071   select the Bn polynomial for its uniform convergence, which ensures robustness in the basis during
072   processes like feature learning, particularly under conditions of uneven distributions or high noise
073   levels. To further enhance the adaptability and flexibility of the model across different scenes, a va-
074   riety of function bases can effectively meet these challenges. To accomplish this, we utilize versatile
075
      Bayesian optimization, leveraging low-dimensional, continuous functions for global optimization,
      thereby balancing accuracy with exploration.
076
077   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
078
079          • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first proposal of a general Kolmogorov-Arnold
080
               Network (KANs) with a variable function basis, presented at a mathematical level.
081          • We employ the Bernstein polynomial as the basis function for KANs, which ensures the
082            model’s robustness.
083          • To enhance the model’s flexibility, we utilize a dynamically learnable function basis.
084
085   The results of experiments in three fields: multivariate time series forecasting, image classification,
086   and function approximation, further demonstrate the flexibility and robustness of our method.

129   Our model, the Variety Bernstein Polynomial Function Basis for Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
130   (VBn-KAN), comprises two core components: the rationale for choosing Bernstein polynomials and

148   mated by a polynomial function with arbitrary accuracy. This can be viewed as an extension of the
149   theorem in Taylor (1717) to arbitrary continuous functions and as an expansion of the concept in
150   Fourier (1808) within the context of non-periodic basis functions.

154   ing multiple variables can be represented with a finite number of three-variable functions. Further
155   studies building on this research, as detailed by Arnol’d (1959), have established that functions of
156   just two variables are sufficient. More precisely:

285   3.2   D ISCUSSION : A DVANTAGES AND L IMITATIONS OF VARIETY F UNCTION BASIS Bn
286
      In existing KAN methods, the function basis and its parameters are not adaptive (Muyuzhierchengse,
287   2024; SynodicMonth, 2024b; Khochawongwat, 2024; Li, 2024a). Variable function basis, however,
288   play a crucial role in KANs by allowing the network to enhance the model’s representational power.
289   This enhancement is achieved by adaptively selecting basis functions that are best suited for specific
290   tasks.
291
292          • Data Features Perspective: The model can adapt to the non-stationary and multi-scale fea-
293            tures of different data types. For instance, in multivariate time-series data forecasting, it
294
               effectively captures both local and global features and better processes sudden events. In
               image classification, the model can adjust its form and parameters based on specific features
295
               such as edge density and color distribution, thereby better accommodating the characteris-
296
               tics of various image types.
297
298
             • Model Training Perspective: When dealing with non-stationary or high-noise data, choos-
               ing a higher degree enhances the model’s adaptability and accuracy; conversely, employing
299
               lower frequencies reduces computational complexity and helps prevent overfitting. Addi-
300
               tionally, the uniform convergence proposition minimizes the model’s data dependence by
301            maintaining a fixed interval ε with sufficient N , thus striking a balance between error and
302            computational costs.
303
304   However, employing a variety function basis might require more time and space than a fixed ba-
305   sis. The introduction of variable basis functions enables the KAN model to adjust adaptively based
306   on data features, thus improving prediction accuracy, enhancing generalization to new data, and
307
      effectively reducing the risk of overfitting. This enhanced adaptability not only boosts model per-
      formance but also facilitates more efficient resource allocation, making it particularly suitable for
308
      complex application scenarios that demand high performance and real-time responses. Therefore,
309
      although the initial resource consumption may be high, the investment proves cost-effective in the
310   long run, especially in dynamic and evolving data environments.

      3.3   R EALIZATION : ACHIEVING VARIETY IN F UNCTION BASIS
313
314   The selection of the Bn degree is a non-convex and combinatorial optimization problem, primarily
315   because the relationship between approximation error and Bn degree is non-monotonic; it can ex-
316   hibit non-smooth or discontinuous variations with changes in degree. Consequently, this scene may
317   present multiple local optimal solutions. For instance, under certain data distributions and objec-
318   tive function configurations, both lower and higher iterations might achieve comparable error levels,
319   while some intermediate iterations might result in higher errors. These local optima complicate
320
      the search for the global optimum. In this situation, there is a need to balance minimizing error
      (as discussed in Proposition 3.2) and adhering to computational resource constraints (as outlined in
321
      Theorem 3.3).
322
323   To determine the degree of the Bn basis, several adaptive models such as additive structures (Hastie,
      2017), Multi-armed Bandits (Burtini et al., 2015), and deep hedging (Chaudhuri et al., 2009) are


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      available. However, these models are typically employed for multivariate impact analysis or for
325   combining and selecting multiple models. In this context, we employ Bayesian Optimization (BO)
326   to address the aforementioned challenges. BO effectively navigates non-convex spaces to identify
327   the global optimum by constructing a probabilistic model, typically a Gaussian process, for the
328   objective function, such as approximation error. Furthermore, it automatically balances exploration
329   and exploitation (Garnett, 2023). Notably, traditional BO is a leading method in expensive black-box
330   optimization due to its data efficiency. A comprehensive and updated survey about BO is provided
331   in Wang et al. (2023).
332   To further optimize the variety of Bn polynomial bases, we employ the versatile Bayesian Optimiza-
333   tion tool SMAC3 (Lindauer et al., 2022) for its robustness and flexibility. SMAC3 is particularly
334   suited for low-dimensional and continuous functions, aligning with our objectives. Specifically, the
335   variety basis n is treated as a hyperparameter optimization problem. We use Mean Absolute Error
336   (MAE) as an example metric, consistent with other metrics discussed in Section 4.2. We define the
337   cost function L as

527   5       CONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we presente a general framework, the Variety Bernstein Polynomial Function Basis for
530   Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (VBn-KAN). This framework extends the range of basis functions
531   in theory and enhances flexibility and robustness through the variety of Bn function bases driven
532
      by the uniform convergence proposition. Furthermore, it reduces the influence of data on the model
      and provides a balanced approach in feature learning by minimizing error while managing comput-
533
      ing costs. Experiments in multivariate time series forecasting, image classification, and function
534
      approximation demonstrate that our VBn-KAN achieves significant results both theoretically and
535   practically.
