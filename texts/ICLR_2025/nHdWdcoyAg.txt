                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have great potential in medical tasks, like Vi-
012            sual Question Answering (VQA), where they could act as interactive assistants
013            for both patients and clinicians. Yet their robustness to distribution shifts on un-
014            seen data remains a critical concern for safe deployment. Evaluating such ro-
015            bustness requires a controlled experimental setup that allows for systematic in-
016            sights into the model’s behavior. However, we demonstrate that current setups
017            fail to offer sufficiently thorough evaluations, limiting their ability to accurately
018            assess model robustness. To address this gap, our work introduces a novel frame-
019            work, called SURE-VQA, centered around three key requirements to overcome
020
               the current pitfalls and systematically analyze the robustness of VLMs: 1) Since
               robustness on synthetic shifts does not necessarily translate to real-world shifts,
021
               robustness should be measured on real-world shifts that are inherent to the VQA
022
               data; 2) Traditional token-matching metrics often fail to capture underlying se-
023            mantics, necessitating the use of large language models (LLMs) for more accu-
024            rate semantic evaluation; 3) Model performance often lacks interpretability due
025            to missing sanity baselines, thus meaningful baselines should be reported that
026            allow assessing the multimodal impact on the VLM. To demonstrate the rele-
027            vance of this framework, we conduct a study on the robustness of various Fine-
028            Tuning methods across three medical datasets with four different types of dis-
029            tribution shifts. Our study reveals several important findings: 1) Sanity base-
030            lines that do not utilize image data can perform surprisingly well; 2) We con-
031
               firm LoRA as the best-performing PEFT method; 3) No PEFT method consis-
               tently outperforms others in terms of robustness to shifts. Code is provided at
032
               https://github.com/KOFRJO/sure-vqa.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent advancements in Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have seen increasing potential for appli-
038   cation in the medical domain, with one key area being Visual Question Answering (VQA). In this
039   task, VLMs could assist clinicians and can also function in medical chatbots for patient inquiries.
040   Several general medical pretrained VLMs, such as LLaVA-Med Li et al. (2023) and Med-Flamingo
041   Moor et al. (2023), have already been developed.
042   However, a crucial question remains: how robust are these models when faced with variations in
043   data distribution during real-world application? Robustness of VLMs in medical VQA tasks refers
044   to the ability of generating accurate answers despite variations in data, a concept also referred to as
045   Domain/OoD generalization Yoon et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2021b) . The datasets used for training or
046   fine-tuning may not fully capture the variations in real-world clinical data. As an example, Roberts
047   et al. (2021) highlights how the urgency of the COVID-19 pandemic led to many studies utilizing
048   datasets that insufficiently represent pediatric patients, introducing significant bias into the analyses.
049   These shifts, whether through unseen disease variations, variations in the image acquisition, or dif-
050
      ferent question subjects, may cause performance degradation. Understanding how robust VLMs are
      to these changes is key to ensuring their reliability in clinical environments.
051
052   Despite the importance of this research question, existing benchmarks fail to offer an adequate
053   framework to address it effectively. While several benchmarks exist for evaluating the robustness
      of VLMs under artificial image or text corruptions (Zhang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023)), there

      remains a notable gap in benchmarks that account for more realistic data shifts. We address this gap
077
      in the medical domain by utilizing existing medical VQA datasets and setting them up to test VLM
078
      robustness against realistic shifts inherent to the VQA data. This focus on realistic shifts is crucial,
079   as prior research has shown that robustness to synthetic shifts does not necessarily translate to ro-
080   bustness under real-world conditions (Taori et al. (2020)). Additionally, many current benchmarks
081   rely on traditional metrics, which use token matching between the ground truth and the model’s pre-
082   dictions. We highlight common flaws in these metrics and instead propose the use of large language
083   models (LLMs) as evaluators, validating this approach through a human rater study. Finally, current
084   benchmarks often overlook simple baselines, such as testing a model’s ability to answer questions
085   based solely on text. Including such sanity baselines can reveal language priors in the dataset, where
086   questions might be easily answered either by their content or by predicting the most common an-
087
      swer seen during training. To overcome these three apparent pitfalls in the current literature, we
      define three key requirements. Based on these requirements we present a flexible framework, called
088
      SURE-VQA, that enables meaningful evaluation of VLM robustness in the medical domain.
089
090   To showcase the relevance of SURE-VQA, we conduct a study comparing the robustness of various
091   fine-tuning (FT) approaches, similar to Chen et al. (2023), but with a focus on the medical domain,
092   using LLaVA-Med as medical VLM. We focus on FT methods, including full FT and parameter-
093
      efficient FT (PEFT) because fine-tuning large VLM models is crucial and common practice for
      specialized tasks like medical VQA, where precision is important (Li et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024);
094
      Singhal et al. (2023)). Our study provides valuable insights to the following questions: How do the
095
      FT methods perform in comparison to sanity baselines that, for instance, do not incorporate image
096   content? How does the performance of different FT methods vary across medical VQA datasets?
097   How does full FT compare to PEFT? How does the performance differ between FT methods regard-
098   ing both, i.i.d. performance and robustness? Which shift is most severe regarding model robustness?
099
      In summary, our contributions are:

                3. Perform a human rater study confirming the importance of LLM-based metrics.

      2   R EQUIREMENTS FOR A SYSTEMATIC EVALUATION OF THE ROBUSTNESS OF
109
110
          VLM S
111
112   We identify several key pitfalls in the current evaluation of VLM robustness, which our proposed
113
      framework in Figure 1 addresses. In the following section, we detail these pitfalls (P1-P3) and
      formulate requirements (R1-R3) to overcome them. Finally, we outline the exact setup we use in
114
      our work to fulfill these requirements.
115
116
117   P1: Robustness on synthetic shifts does not imply robustness on real-world shifts.
118
119   Many existing benchmarks that focus on the robustness of VLMs, such as those in Chen et al.
120   (2023), Shirnin et al. (2024), and Qiu et al. (2022), primarily introduce artificial perturbations to
121   the image or text content. A notable exception is Radford et al. (2021), where the robustness of
122   natural distribution shifts is explored in the context of their proposed CLIP model. They find that
123   CLIP is quite effective in being more robust on natural distribution shifts of ImageNet. However,
124
      their analysis is restricted to CLIP’s non-generative tasks and does not address the generative VQA
      task. In the medical domain, Nan et al. (2024) conduct a multimodal benchmark for evaluating
125
      robustness. Their study focuses on medical VQA tasks, but it remains limited to testing image
126
      corruptions as data shifts, leaving the crucial question of how realistic distribution shifts impact
127   model performance open. Another study by Jensen & Plank (2022) takes a step toward addressing
128   more realistic data shifts by examining the performance of VLMs on different versions of the VQA
129   dataset, when training them from scratch vs. fine-tuning a pretrained model. However, their focus
130   is primarily on linguistic variations, leaving shifts in image content under-explored. Similarly, even
131   though the benchmark proposed by Zhang et al. (2024) uses artificial image and text corruptions,
132   their content bias might be one step towards more realistic shifts.
133   However, while the robustness benchmarks in the VLM domain rarely address any realistic shifts,
134   in the unimodal domain there is evidence that models are not robust against natural distribution
135   shifts (Taori et al. (2020); Miller et al. (2020)). This issue has also been proven for fine-tuned,
136   domain-specific models (Yuan et al. (2023)). Furthermore, there is evidence that artificial shifts do
137   not necessarily translate to realistic shifts (Taori et al. (2020)).
138
      → R1: Evaluate VLMs under a diverse set of realistic shifts.
139
140   Implementation in SURE-VQA: We utilize three different datasets from the medical VQA domain,
141   including SLAKE (Liu et al. (2021a)), OVQA (Huang et al. (2022)), and MIMIC-CXR-VQA (Bae
142
      et al. (2023)). On these datasets, we define several realistic shifts, spanning a range of subtleties,
      with some having a more pronounced impact, such as modality shifts, while others, like gender
143
      shifts, are more subtle in their effects. Furthermore, certain shifts primarily affect the image con-
144
      tent, such as changes in the body location being imaged, whereas others influence the text input,
145   such as shifts in question types. In an ablation, we compared the model’s performance on image
146   corruptions (e.g., blur, noise, brightness) with the realistic shifts we defined in the SLAKE dataset.
147   The results demonstrate that artificial shifts fail to accurately capture the challenges presented by
148   realistic shifts, supporting our argument. Further details on this study are provided in Appendix D.
149   We also investigate the effect of multimodal shifts in comparison to unimodal ones in Appendix E.
150   In the context of foundation models, defining i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed) and
151   OoD (out-of-distribution) data is challenging due to the vast amount of training data. In our work,
152   we therefore define OoD specifically as data distributions that differ from the fine-tuning data. This
153
      approach allows us to precisely control how the data distribution is modified relative to the model’s
      fine-tuning environment.
154
155
156   P2: Traditional metrics do not capture the underlying semantics.
157
158   Many studies in the VLM field continue to rely on traditional metrics like BLEU and CIDEr (Sung
159   et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Qiu et al. (2022)), or accuracy-based metrics (Li et al. (2023);
160   Jensen & Plank (2022); Qiu et al. (2022)), which are dependent on word- or n-gram matches. We
161   refer to these as “traditional metrics” throughout the paper. Recent research, however, has begun to
      adopt a more sophisticated approach by employing LLMs to evaluate the output of VLMs (or other


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      LLMs) (Wang et al. (2024); Ostmeier et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023b); Chiang & Lee (2023); Fu
163   et al. (2024); Kocmi & Federmann (2023)).
164
165   The primary limitation of traditional metrics is their inability to capture the underlying semantics of
166
      a sentence. They fail to recognize synonyms or account for negation, often misjudging sentences
      that differ from the ground truth by a single token, such as ”not.” We illustrate examples of these
167
      failures in Appendix A.1, similar to Ostmeier et al. (2024). While we are not the first ones to propose
168
      using an LLM as an evaluator, the fact that previous studies have shown the subpar performance of
169   traditional metrics but they are still used in many papers underlines the need to formulate it as an
170   explicit requirement within an evaluation study for VLMs.
171
172
      → R2: Evaluate VLMs with appropriate metrics that capture the underlying semantics of the output.
173   Implementation in SURE-VQA: We employ the Mistral model (Jiang et al. (2023)) as an evalua-
174   tor, utilizing three distinct prompts tailored for different question types: open-ended, closed-ended
175   binary, and closed-ended multilabel. To optimize computational efficiency and reduce potential er-
176   rors from the LLM evaluator, we implement a hybrid metric. Specifically, when the answer exactly
177   matches the ground truth, we assign the highest score without invoking the LLM, thereby saving
178
      computational resources and minimizing the risk of evaluation failures. Additionally, we assess the
      feasibility of this evaluation by conducting a human rater study, where we empirically validate its
179
      performance in comparison to traditional metrics.
180
181
      P3: Model performance lacks interpretability due to missing sanity baselines.
182
183
184   Currently, the performance of VLMs is typically reported either in isolation or in comparison to
185   other VLMs. This is evident in papers that introduce new VLMs, such as Li et al. (2023); Moor et al.
186
      (2023), as well as in benchmark studies (Chen et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024); Qiu et al. (2022);
      Nan et al. (2024)). A notable exception is the work of Liu et al. (2023a), where the performance
187
      of a language-only GPT-4 is evaluated. However, the focus here is rather to improve the model by
188
      ensembling with the LLM instead of highlighting that it might be an issue of current VQA datasets
189   that so many questions can be answered based on the text only. Further, they do not provide a
190   no image sanity baseline of their own model, leaving the multimodal usage of their own model
191   unexplored. Another study by Parcalabescu & Frank (2023) contextualizes the multimodal use of
192   VLMs by employing Shapley values to assess the contribution of each modality to the output.
193
      The problem with many VQA datasets is that they tend to contain hidden patterns, allowing models
194
      to exploit shortcuts (Geirhos et al. (2020)) rather than using all available information, including the
195   image content (Kafle & Kanan (2017); Chen et al. (2024a); Kervadec et al. (2021); Goyal et al.
196   (2017); Dancette et al. (2021)). This means that high performance on these datasets does not guar-
197   antee that the model is actually utilizing the visual input to answer the question; instead, it might
198   be exploiting patterns in the questions themselves (Kafle & Kanan (2017); Kervadec et al. (2021)).
199   As demonstrated by Chen et al. (2024a), in many cases, the visual content in VQA datasets is un-
200   necessary, and models can achieve high performance simply by relying on the textual modality.
201   This indicates that the models are leveraging hidden biases in the question-answer pairs rather than
202   solving the task as intended.
203   → R3: Provide relevant sanity baselines to contextualize the benefits of VLM fine-tuning and multi-
204   modal information usage.
205
      Implementation in SURE-VQA: We propose that using relevant sanity baselines to reveal such dataset
206
      biases can be beneficial to explore how the models solve the given task and how the datasets are
207   structured. Thereby, we put the performance of the fine-tuned model into context by comparing it to
208   baselines in two aspects:
209
210         1. Not using the image information: Here, we a) choose the most frequent answer to the
211            question in the training set and answer the same question in the test set with this and b) train
212            the model without using any image information, which should lead to learning shortcuts
213            based on the language.
214         2. Not fine-tuning the model: Use the plain VLM without any fine-tuning and report the
215            performance on the test set. This serves at the same time as a baseline to see if or how
               much the shifts are inherently different between i.i.d. and OoD when not fine-tuning.

238   3.1       U TILIZED DATASETS
239
240   An overview of the utilized datasets is provided in Figure 2, with further details regarding the
241   datasets, preprocessing steps, and split sizes available in Appendix C.3. In total, we use three dif-
242
      ferent medical VQA datasets, each incorporating a variety of realistic shifts to meet the requirement
      outlined in R1. The taxonomy for shift categories is thereby partially taken from Castro et al. (2020),
243
      also used in related work such as Bungert et al. (2023); Roschewitz et al. (2023); Choi et al. (2023).
244
245
246   SLAKE We use the SLAKE dataset (Liu et al. (2021a)) with two different shifts: 1) Modality
247
      shift: Representing an acquisition shift (Castro et al. (2020)), we train the model exclusively on
      CT and MRI images (2D slices) and then test it on X-ray images. 2) Question type shift: During
248
      training, the model is exposed to questions about image content such as shape and color but excludes
249
      questions related to the size of organs. These size-related questions are introduced in the OoD test
250   set.
251
252
      OVQA The OVQA dataset (Huang et al. (2022)) is used with two shifts: 1) Body part shift:
253
      Representing a manifestation shift (Castro et al. (2020)), we train the model on images of the hand,
254   chest, and head, and test it on images of the leg. 2) Question type shift: In the training set, the
255   model is exposed to questions about various image contents like abnormalities and conditions, but
256   questions related to the organ system are reserved for the OoD test set.
257
258   MIMIC-CXR-VQA We use the MIMIC-CXR-VQA (Bae et al. (2023)) dataset with three dif-
259   ferent shifts, all representing population shifts (Castro et al. (2020)): 1) Gender shift: The model is
260   trained on male patients and tested on female patients. 2) Population shift: Training is conducted us-
261   ing data from white patients, with testing on patients from other ethnicities. 3) Age shift: The model
262   is trained on patients over the age of 60 and tested on patients under 40. A gap is intentionally
263   introduced between the i.i.d. and OoD groups to make the shift more explicit.
264

290   and answers makes traditional metrics more prone to failure. Tokens like ”fracture,” ”left”/”right”,

293   hand, MIMIC answers are highly structured, particularly for open-ended questions, where a fixed
294
      set of classes is listed in a comma-separated format. In these cases, traditional metrics perform well

297   relation with human ratings is in a similar range to that observed for the SLAKE dataset. Moreover,

299   does not put out the opposite than it should but rather a wrong tendency. Further, in the first example
300
      shown on the left, also some of the traditional metrics fail. In summary, although there are instances

305   4     E MPIRICAL S TUDY ON THE ROBUSTNESS OF F INE -T UNING M ETHODS
306
307   To show the relevance of SURE-VQA we performed an empirical study comparing the robustness
308   of various FT methods under realistic shift in medical VQA. This is especially important since
309   practitioners should be informed about the differences of the FT methods not only in terms of their
310   performance but also how robust they are when selecting a method.
311
312   4.1   S TUDY D ESIGN
313
314   We utilize (image, text) datasets from the medical domain, splitting them so that the training and
315
      testing distributions differ. As our base model, we employ LLaVA-Med 1.5, a state-of-the-art med-
      ical VLM (Li et al. (2023)). We fine-tune the model using four methods: full FT, prompt tuning
316
      (Lester et al. (2021)), LoRA (Hu et al. (2021)), and (IA)3 (Liu et al. (2022)). Hyperparameters for
317
      the PEFT methods are selected based on the full training set and corresponding validation set for
318   each dataset. Details regarding the hyperparameter search can be found in Appendix C.1. To mea-
319   sure robustness, we split the data into i.i.d. training and i.i.d. and OoD test sets, as outlined in
320   section 3.1, thereby fulfilling R1. We then evaluate the performance of the VLM using Mistral as
321   an evaluator, fulfilling R2. For robustness measurement, we calculate the relative robustness (RR)
322   (Chen et al. (2023)), defined as RR = 1 − ∆P/PI , where ∆P = (PI − PO ), and PI is the i.i.d
323   test performance and PO is the OoD test performance. For a better interpretation of the results, we
      compare them against relevant sanity baselines as described in R3.

352   Comparison to Sanity Baselines. Generally, the PEFT models outperform the no fine-tuned mod-
353   els, the most frequent baseline, and their respective no image baselines on the i.i.d. datasets. This is
354   in contrast to full FT, which mostly does not outperform the most frequent baseline. However, the
355
      gap between the no image baselines and the PEFT models varies across datasets. For example, it
      is largest on the SLAKE dataset, averaging 24%, but much smaller on the OVQA dataset at around
356
      11%, and similarly small on the MIMIC dataset at 7%. Interestingly, the MIMIC dataset yields rel-
357
      atively poor results with fine-tuned models, averaging just 61.2% mistral accuracy on closed-ended
358   questions and 3.2 Mistral score on open-ended ones on the i.i.d. set. Specifically, for prompt tuning,
359   the performance of the model fine-tuned with images is almost identical to its no image counter-
360   part, showing only about a 2% improvement. This indicates that, for the MIMIC dataset, the image
361   encodings contribute little to the question-answering task, suggesting that the vision encoder is not
362   able to extract meaningful information from the images. This could be because the MIMIC dataset
363   focuses on detailed chest X-ray images and the image encoder does not have this fine-grained ex-
364   pertise in such a specific task. While the no image baseline and the most frequent baseline both
365   mostly perform better than random classifier, this effect is particularly noticeable for the closed-
366
      ended questions on OVQA, where the no image baseline achieves on average 72%, and the most
      frequent baseline 73.6%. This suggests that the model can often rely on learned question-answer

      correlations without needing the images.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND TAKE - AWAYS
487
488
      We present a framework that allows testing the robustness of VLMs in medical VQA tasks. Thereby,
489
      we especially focus on three key requirements for a meaningful evaluation of robustness.
490
491
      Empirical Confirmation of R1-R3. While in section 2 we derived R1-R3 from flaws in the cur-
492   rrent literature, our study provides empirical evidence for their importance. R1: In an ablation
493   study (Appendix D), we show that corruption shifts do not necessarily translate to real-world shifts,
494   thereby justifying our claim to also work with more real-world shifts. R2: We present several criti-
495   cal failures of traditional token-matching metrics and prove the applicability of our LLM evaluation
496   setup by a human rater study. R3: We show that some sanity baselines that do not use the image
497   information already perform surprisingly well. This highlights two aspects: 1) As stated in R3,
498   reporting such sanity baselines is crucial for understanding the true multi-modal performance of
499   a VLM, beyond its language-only capabilities. 2) This observation further suggests that we need
500
      more elaborate data sets and tasks in medical VQA that minimize the potential for shortcut learning
      based solely on the language content. Achieving this may involve incorporating greater linguistic
501
      variability in questions, as seen in Bae et al. (2023), where questions were rephrased using GPT-4,
502
      and ensuring a broad range of semantic differences in the questions. Progress can be tracked as a
503   low performance of the no-image sanity baseline.
504
505
      Generalizability of the Framework. SURE-VQA, serves as a starting point for a comprehensive
506   evaluation of robustness and it can be flexibly extended to new datasets, methods, and domains.
507   Additionally, SURE-VQA can support method development aimed at enhancing the robustness of
508   VLMs. In our study, we define OoD as a data shift w.r.t the FT data. However, our framework also
509   allows to compare VLMs in a zero-shot setting without any FT, when simply re-defining OoD as a
510   data shift w.r.t the pre-training data. However, such a definition becomes increasingly challenging
511   to validate with foundation models, since the exact training data used is often not known. Notably,
512   R2, and R3 go beyond robustness analysis and should be integral to any well-designed VLM study.
513
514   Main Insights from the FT Robustness Study. Our exemplary study which compares the ro-
515   bustness of various FT methods reveals several key insights. While we confirm LoRA as the best-
516   performing FT method on the i.i.d. dataset, no single FT method consistently outperforms the others
517
      in terms of robustness. Further, in line with findings of Dutt et al. (2024), we find that PEFT methods
      are more efficient than full FT in the lower data regimes, especially present in the medical domain.
518
      As another finding, robustness trends appear to be more consistent within FT methods than across
519
      different dataset shifts, indicating that the type of shift has a greater impact on robustness than the
520   choice of FT method. This suggests that robustness alone is not a decisive factor when choosing a
521   FT method. However, the type of data shift anticipated in the test set is crucial, as different shifts
522   may uniquely challenge model performance. Additionally, the models generally exhibit robustness
523   against population shifts. However, further investigation is needed to determine whether this is due
524   to already low i.i.d. performance or only because of the nature of the shift.
525
526   Future Work. As mentioned above, SURE-VQA is a starting point and future work can include
527   the investigation of more datasets, shifts, methods, and models. A key research direction is the
528   development of additional VQA datasets, particularly in the medical domain. This would not only
529   address the need for greater diversity in question types but also improve the clinical relevance of
530   the questions posed. While current datasets cover various question types, questions about scanning
531
      modalities, for example, may be less valuable to clinicians. More relevant questions might include
      for example questions related to prognosis, such as the potential spread of a tumor. We believe that
532
      collaboration with clinicians could help define and incorporate a broader set of clinically meaning-
533
      ful questions into future datasets. Additionally, our framework provides valuable insights into the
534   factors affecting the robustness of VLMs. Future work could explore methods for enhancing robust-
535   ness, like in Yoon et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2024). Finally, the underperformance of LLaVA-Med,
536   one of the state-of-the-art models in medical VQA, on the MIMIC-CXR-VQA dataset indicates that
537   there is significant room for improvement in medical VLM development. Recent work by Chen
538   et al. (2024b) has focused on building foundation models specifically for chest X-ray data, using
539   datasets like MIMIC-CXR-VQA. However, future efforts could aim to develop more robust models
      capable of handling multiple modalities across a wider range of clinical scenarios.

       C.3.3    MIMIC-CXR-VQA
1400
1401   The MIMIC-CXR-VQA dataset Bae et al. (2023) is a chest X-ray dataset, which is built based on
1402   the MIMIC-CXR dataset Johnson et al. (2019), the MIMIC-IV dataset Johnson et al. (2023), and the
1403   Chest ImaGenome dataset Wu et al. (2021). For each question, the semantic type is specified. Three
       different semantic types are specified, which are ”choose”, ”query”, and ”verify”. For ”choose”, the


                                                             26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       task is to choose between two options provided in the answer, but also both or none of the options
1405   can be correct. For ”query”, the task is to list all the categories that match the questions, e.g. all
1406   anatomical findings. Lastly, ”verify” are yes/no questions. All the questions can be answered based
1407   on a fixed set of classes, where the dataset overall contains 110 answer labels. The answers are given
1408   as a list of the correct classes. We preprocess the questions differently, based on their semantic type:
1409   For the ”choose” questions, whenever the list of answers contains both options, we change the an-
1410   swer to ”both”, and whenever the list of answers is empty, we change the answer to ”none”. For
1411   the ”query” questions, we concatenate the list of answers to one string, with the answer labels being
1412   comma-separated. For the ”verify” questions, we do not apply any specific preprocessing.
1413
       The information for the patient’s gender, ethnicity, and age are taken from the MIMIC-IV dataset.
       Whenever the metadata information of a subject ID is not unique, we set it to ”none”. In the re-
1414
       spective shifts, we exclude questions where the corresponding metadata field is not known, which
1415
       includes all fields with ”none”, and for the ethnicity shift also the value ”unknown/other”. The exact
1416   sizes of the dataset splits are listed in Table 12.

                        For this sample study, we used the LLaVA-Med model fine-tuned on the SLAKE dataset with the
                 1554   (IA)3 method. The i.i.d. and OoD samples for realistic shifts were as previously described (R1). For
                 1555   artificial shifts, the i.i.d. train and test samples were identical to those used for realistic shifts, while
                 1556   OoD test samples were created by corrupting the i.i.d. test images with varying strengths of blur,
                 1557   brightness, and noise. Each corruption method was applied with a probability of 0.5, with at least
                 1558   one corruption always being applied.
                 1559
                        Table 21 shows the relative robustness results for both artificial and realistic shifts. The results show
                 1560   that both modality shift and question type shift exhibit lower relative robustness compared to all
                 1561   artificial shifts at low, medium, and high strengths. This suggests that artificial shifts, such as image
                 1562   corruption, fail to accurately represent the challenges posed by real-world, realistic shifts. The most
                 1563   prominent example here is the relative robustness of closed-ended questions under the question type
                 1564   shift (realistic shift), which is up to 96% compared to the realistic shift which only has 61%. The
                 1565   only exception where the realistic shift shows higher robustness is the question type shift on the
                        open-ended questions, which is already nearly 100% on the realistic shift.
