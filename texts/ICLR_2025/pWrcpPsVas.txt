009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, graph neural networks (GNNs) have shown tremendous promise
012            in solving problems in high energy physics, materials science, and fluid dynamics.
013            In this work, we introduce a new application for GNNs in the physical sciences:
014            instrumentation design. As a case study, we apply GNNs to simulate models of
015            the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) and show that
016            they are capable of accurately capturing the complex optical physics at play, while
017            achieving runtimes 815 times faster than state of the art simulation packages. We
018            discuss the unique challenges this problem provides for machine learning models.
019
               In addition, we provide a dataset of high-fidelity optical physics simulations for
               three interferometer topologies, which can be used as a benchmarking suite for
020
               future work in this direction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Gravitational waves (GWs) are stretches or contractions in the fabric of spacetime, predicted by
      Albert Einstein in 1916 as a consequence of the general theory of relativity. Gravitational waves
027
      enable the study of the most extreme events in our universe, including black hole and neutron star
028
      mergers. To that end, multiple ground-based gravitational wave observatories have been built. LIGO
029   is one observatory, which detects GWs using a dual recycled Michelson interferometer (DRMI).
030
031   In order to detect GWs, LIGO and future observatories like Cosmic Explorer (CE) require extremely
032
      high sensitivities, meaning that the interferometer itself must be robust to real-world errors in manu-
      facturing. Searching for design parameters that are optimally robust is a challenging computational
033
      problem, which requires running thousands of costly high-fidelity optical simulations and perform-
034
      ing optimization in a high-dimensional, non-convex loss landscape [Richardson et al. (2022)].
035
036   Neural networks have been used for data analysis and simulation of complex physical systems in
037   fields from computational fluid dynamics to particle physics to cosmology. However, to date, their
038
      use in instrumentation design is less explored. Instrumentation design tasks are usually highly ap-
      plication specific, and have large search spaces and complex design constraints. Furthermore, there
039
      are often intricate physical relationships between the design parameters, and simulating a particular
040
      set of parameters to evaluate its performance can be extremely computationally expensive.
041
042   These challenges make the application of deep learning to instrumentation design tasks particularly
043   appealing. Furthermore, in many cases, the surrogate model does not need to extremely accurately
044
      capture the simulation output. For example, in the case of LIGO and CE, it is not necessary for
      a network to be able to predict the exact field amplitudes with high accuracy; even being able to
045
      identify designs that are unlikely to perform well would enable any optimization routine to quickly
046
      prune large segments of the parameter space, thus enabling far more efficient design optimization.
047
048   We make the following contributions:
049
050
            1. We demonstrate, for the first time, that deep learning methods can accurately simulate
               electromagnetic (EM) field propagation in optical cavities at a fraction of the computational
051
               cost of traditional methods.
052
053         2. We provide a dataset of high-fidelity optical simulations over 3 different interferometer
               topologies, which can be used for training more advanced models.

058   Graph representation learning refers to the task of processing graph structured data with the aim of
059   learning “embeddings” or vector representations for nodes. The graph is encoded as a set of nodes
060   and edges, each of which may have a feature vector. These feature vectors are then aggregated

      3     R ELATED W ORK
163
164
      Classical methods for interferometer simulation rely on rely on representing the optical field in the
165
      interferometer, either using a modal decomposition (Bond et al. (2016)) or directly propagating the
166
      field.
167
168   In the modal decomposition approach, the EM field is represented in a particular basis. In the
169   simulations used to produce the dataset included with this paper, the field is represented in the HG
170
      basis of one of the cavities of the particular interferometer. The field propagation is then modelled
      via a series of “ABCD“ matrices, which model how the field amplitude and phase change either as
171
      the field passes through free space, or as it interacts with an optical component. Once these matrices
172
      are known, the field is propagated simply by multiplying a known field vector (usually at the laser),
173   sequentially by the matrices representing each interaction it undergoes. This is the approach taken by
174   the standard simulation package used to collect the dataset for this paper, FINESSE (Brown et al.).
175
176
      In this process, the main computational cost comes from the fact that a) in order to capture sharper
      spatial features, higher order modes must be included, and the dimension of each scattering matrix
177
      grows in O(n2 ), yielding a high dimensional system of equations to be solved, and b) computing the
178
      matrix elements themselves can be extremely computationally intensive, particularly when account-
179   ing for higher order effects like thermal lensing and scattering due to the finite mirror apertures.
180
181   Furthermore, in order to simulate a particular interferometer topology, multiple of these simulation
182
      subroutines must be run. This is because the interferometer must be “locked.” In effect, the mi-
      croscopic positions of the cavity mirrors must be adjusted in order to achieve resonance inside of
183
      the optical cavities, and determining this lock point requires iteratively adjusting mirror positions,
184
      re-running a simulation each time.
185
186   Deep learning based approaches have shown great success at accelerating computationally costly
187   physics simulation routines such as this. Previous works have applied neural networks towards the
188
      design of compound lens systems (Yang et al. (2024)). Other works have shown that neural networks
      can directly solve partial differential equations similar to those that govern EM-field propagation (Li
189
      et al. (2020), Alkin et al. (2024)), or emulate hydrodynamic simulations of the cosmic web (Zhang
190
      et al. (2023)). Some of these approaches give the neural network direct access to the underlying
191   physics, while in most cases, the approach is the data-driven one: large quantities of simulation
192   data are given to the network, and the underlying physics is then learned. We note two distinctions
193   between our problem setting and those covered by recent physics foundation models (Alkin et al.
194   (2024), Herde et al. (2024)). First, we are not concerned with the time evolution of the field, only
195   the steady state fields achieved inside the interferometer cavities. Secondly, the model does not need
196   to emulate the entire field in between optics in the interferometer; the primary points of interest are
197   in the interactions between the field and optics.
198   Graph neural networks in particular have been used simulating physical dynamics in settings where
199   there is a natural spatial ordering, for example in mesh based simulation (Pfaff et al. (2021)) and
200   in predicting the properties of molecules (Reiser et al. (2022)). GNNs can effectively capture local
201   interactions between nodes (i.e. points on a mesh, or bonded atoms in a molecule), and through
202   multiple rounds of message passing, can also incorporate long range dependencies. In the vein of
203   this approach, we employ a graph-based architecture, that captures the spatial relationships between
204   the different fields and optical components in the interferometer, and apply it to the problem of
205
      predicting steady state EM fields in interferometers.

210
211   Because the EM-field is modelled as interacting with a sequence of optics, this lends itself very
212   naturally to a graph representation. In this dataset, each mirror is split into four nodes, two for each
213   side of the mirror, and with the incoming and outgoing fields treated as separate nodes. The edges
214   in the graphs represent the spatial connection between fields. For example, the incoming field to a
215   mirror will be connected to both the reflected field and the transmitted field, while the outgoing field
      from that mirror would be connected to the node representing the incoming field at the next optic

241   4.2     M ODELS
242
243   We train a network to predict the incoming and outgoing field power at each point in the interfer-
244   ometer, and a network to predict the spatial intensity distribution at each point in the interferometer,
245   given the the interferometer topology, as a graph. Each node in the interferometer has three features:
246
      the radius of curvature of the wavefront, the reflectivity of the optic, and the angle at which it is
      oriented. Each edge in the graph has two features: its length and index of refraction. The model is
247
      trained on locked interferometer data but does not perform the locking procedure itself.
248
249
      4.2.1     L EARNING THE P OWERS
250
251   Our task is to predict the incoming and outgoing powers at each optic in the interferometer. Points
252   within optical cavities, particularly the arm cavities, have powers on the kilowatt (KW) scale, while
253   powers exiting the interferometer may be on the milliwatt scale. To account for this scale separation,
254   the power prediction model is trained to predict log P , instead of the raw power.
255   The model architecture consists of 20 GATv2 layers (Brody et al. (2022)), followed by 6 feed-
256   forward layers. LeakyReLU activation functions are used. Residual connections are placed between
257   each pair of consecutive message passing layers, and between each pair of consecutive linear layers.
258   For all the alternate model architectures tested, the number of layers of each type is kept the same.
259   For example, the GraphTransformer (Shi et al. (2021)) architecture, we use 20 graph transformer
260   layers, followed by the same 6 MLP layers.
261   The model is trained with a custom loss function, defined below:
262

267   of the input graph, and ||x||1 denotes the L1 norm of the vector. The first term is the standard mean
268   absolute error loss, and the second term is a regularization term that penalizes model outputs where
269   the sum of incoming powers to a node does not equal the power at that node (i.e. conservation of

      4.2.2    L EARNING THE I NTENSITY D ISTRIBUTION
298   Limiting our model to predicting field powers does not capture the entirety of physically relevant
299   phenomena for interferometer design. For example, we may be interested in the modal decomposi-
300   tion of the field, to ensure that power mainly stays in the TEM-00 mode. This kind of information is
301   captured in the field intensity distribution. In this section, we describe the model architecture used
302   to predict the intensity distribution.
303   The input interferometer graph is first passed through 15 GAT layers. The resulting node embedding
304   is then passed to the Deep Kolmogorov Arnold Networks (KAN) described below.
305
306
      Taking advantage of the radial symmetry of the field representation, as can be seen in Eq. 5, we pass
      the node embeddings to a DeepKAN, which learns to approximate the radial intensity distribution,
307
      which is then rotated to form the full 2D intensity profile. This enforces the physical requirement
308
      that in the cases we consider, the EM-field is azimuthally symmetric on the mirror surface. It also
309   has the advantage of reducing the number of degrees of freedom for the intensity map from O(n2 )
310   to O(n). This pipeline is shown in Fig. 2.
311
312
      The choice to use Kolmogorov Arnold Networks is informed by their demonstrated proficiency in
      learning other special functions from physics, such as the spherical harmonics (Liu et al. (2024))
313
      with fewer parameters than feed forward networks.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
487
488
      In this work, we introduce graph neural network models for simulating interferometer optical
489
      physics. We demonstrate that the models are capable of learning both basic quantities like the
490   power inside the interferometer, and more complex characteristics, like spatial intensity distribu-
491   tions, while being hundreds of times faster per run than standard simulation packages. We note that
492   our model shows some generalization capabilities, but the model does not achieve as low a loss on
493   unseen optical topologies as on topologies in the training set. We see this as a key limitation of the
494   model’s viability in accelerating the design of future interferometers. In future works, we would
495   like to explore ways of encoding the underlying physical quantities that would enable the model to
496   understand the field propagation, and thus be more agnostic to the specific interferometer topology.
497   For example, employing a mesh based approach similar to Pfaff et al. (2021) to learn the exact field
498
      amplitudes everywhere in the interferometer may generalize better.
499   In addition, in our work, we model a relatively small portion of the physics present in a full scale
500   interferometer. The model we present is promising in that it already demonstrates the ability to
501   accelerate low dimensional simulations, like those conducted in Richardson et al. (2022), but in
502   order to be used for full scale interferometer design, it needs to be extended to incorporate more of
503   the physics. In particular, in the future, we would like to extend our model to include higher order
504
      effects like point absorbers in the mirror surfaces, thermal lensing due to heating of the mirrors, and
      astigmatic beam shapes. These effects introduce new challenges in encoding feature representations
505
      for the 2D surface maps of each mirror.
506
507   In addition, a number of other works have applied generative models to similar problems in physics
508   simulation. For example, Paganini et al. (2018) et. al apply a GAN-based framework to generate
509   realistic particle showers to accelerate simulations of hadron calorimeters in the Large Hadron Col-
510
      lider. In future works, we could take this approach to model the distribution of a physical quantity
      of interest conditioned on realistic distribution of perturbations of a baseline topology.
511
512   In the larger context of instrument design, methods for accelerating simulations of interferometers
513   would also open the gates to training reinforcement learning agents to more efficiently explore the
514   parameter space of designs (Dworschak et al. (2022)).
