009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable perfor-
012            mance in numerous downstream tasks, their ubiquity has raised two significant
013            concerns. One is that LLMs can hallucinate by generating content that contradicts
014            relevant contextual information; the other is that LLMs can inadvertently leak pri-
015            vate information due to input regurgitation. Many prior works have extensively
016
               studied each concern independently, but none have investigated them simultane-
               ously. Furthermore, auditing the influence of provided context during open-ended
017
               generation with a privacy emphasis is understudied. To this end, we comprehen-
018
               sively characterize the influence and hallucination of contextual information dur-
019            ing summarization. We introduce a definition for context influence and Context-
020            Influence Decoding (CID), and then we show that amplifying the context (by fac-
021            toring out prior knowledge) and the context being out of distribution with respect
022            to prior knowledge increases the context’s influence on an LLM. Moreover, we
023            show that context influence gives a lower bound of the private information leak-
024            age of CID. We corroborate our analytical findings with experimental evaluations
025            that show improving the F1 ROGUE-L score on CNN-DM for LLaMA 3 by 10%
026            over regular decoding also leads to 1.5x more influence by the context. Moreover,
027
               we empirically evaluate how context influence and hallucination are affected by
               (1) model capacity, (2) context size, (3) the length of the current response, and (4)
028
               different token n-grams of the context.

063   Some works have investigated the role of context during generation and machine translation Fernan-

067   Our contributions are the following:
068
069         1. We propose a principled definition for context influence that follows from Point-wise
070            Cross-Mutual Information Fernandes et al. (2021) and Differential Privacy Dwork (2006).
071            And we introduce a slight reformulation of Context-Aware Decoding (CAD) Shi et al.
072            (2023), called Context Influence Decoding (CID), to better understand and control the in-
073            fluence of the context.
074         2. Using our context influence definition and CID, we analytically show that amplifying the
075            context by factoring out prior knowledge to reduce hallucination causes more influence of
076            the context by an LLM. Moreover, we show that we can use our context influence definition
077
               to lower bound the private information leakage of CID.
078         3. We corroborate our theoretic findings by measuring the context influence and hallucination
079            of various LLMs on summarization tasks. In particular, improving the ROGUE-L score by
080            10% on CNN-DM for LLaMA 3 increases the influence by 1.5x.
081         4. Furthermore, we experimentally analyze how context influence and hallucination are af-
082            fected by model capacity, context size, response length, and token n-grams of the context.

      3.1   C ONTEXT I NFLUENCE
120
121   Amplifying the posterior distribution pθ (yt |D, x, yt ) to mitigate hallucination seems relatively
122   straightforward. However, the context document D can contain private information. Hence, this
123   amplification can increase the regurgitation of the context and inadvertently leak privacy, which is
124   a concern not considered by the aforementioned decoding strategies. To measure how much an
125   LLM is influenced by the context, we present a context influence definition which is motivated by
126   P-XCMI (Eq. 1) and Differential Privacy (Def. 2.1):
127   Definition 3.1. Let D, D′ be contexts such that D′ is a substring of D, i.e. D′ ⊆ D, x be an
128   input query, and pθ be a pre-trained LLM. Then we say that the context influence of D′ on pθ when
129   generating the next token yt , is the following:
130           finfl (pθ , D, D′ , x, y<t , yt ) = | log (pθ (yt |D, x, y<t )) − log (pθ (yt |D \ D′ , x, y<t )) |   (3)
131                                                 |           {z          } |               {z              }
                                                     output probability of yt        output probability of yt
132                                                    given the context D        with D ′ removed from context
133
134   Section 3.3 discusses the connection between context influence fInfl and DP. fInfl measures the log
135   likelihood change of the generated next token yt when D′ is removed from the context. If pθ is
136   strongly influenced by D′ when answering a query x, then the probability of generating yt with
137   and without D′ will be substantially different and Eq. 3 will be large. Conversely, if the context
138   influence is small then that means pθ can sufficiently rely on the remaining context D \ D′ , the
139
      current generation y<t , and its prior knowledge θ. Hence, context influence measures the impact a
      subset of the context has on the generated next token. Alternatively, context influence definition can
140
      be interpreted as the absolute PMI between yt and D′ , i.e., measuring the dependency between the
141
      next token generated and the provided subset of the context.

      3.2   C HARACTERIZING T HE C ONTEXT I NFLUENCE -H ALLUCINATION T RADEOFF
144
145   Next, we slightly reformulate CAD by utilizing a tunable parameter λ that explicitly controls the
146   influence level of a context during decoding, which we will call Context Influence Decoding (CID).
147   First, we start with the prior pθ (yt |x, y<t ), which contains no information about the context D,
148   and increasingly adds more information from the PMI, which does contain information about the
149   context D, by increasing the weighing parameter λ. This induces a better privacy interpretation,
150   where λ = 0 achieves perfect privacy of the context, while λ > 0 leaks information about the
151   context from yt . The true distribution that CID samples from is a linear interpolation between the
152
      posterior and the prior logits:
153       yt ∼ pθ (yt |D, x, y<t ) = softmax[(λlogitθ (yt |D, x, y<t ) + (1 − λ)logitθ (yt |x, y<t )) /τ ]          (4)
154
      where τ is the temperature parameter: τ > 1 resulting in a more uniform distribution (i.e. higher
155
      entropy) and 0 < τ < 1 forcing a sharper output distribution. When λ = 0, then the next token
156   yt is sampled purely from the prior distribution pθ (yt |x, yt ), hence no context influence. When
157   0 < λ < 1, then yt is sampled from a weighted combination of the posterior logit logit(yt |D, x, y<t )
158   and the prior logit logit(yt |x, y<t ). When λ = 1, then yt is sampled purely from the posterior
159   distribution pθ (yt |D, x, y<t ). Now, for λ ≥ 1, CID resorts to CAD by amplifying the PMI. In other
160   words, when 0 ≤ λ < 1, CID focuses on reducing context influence by explicitly including the prior
161   knowledge when decoding, when λ = 1 CID is just regular decoding, and when λ > 1 CID focuses
      on mitigating hallucination by explicitly factoring out the prior knowledge.

169   In other words, Theorem 3.1 highlights a context influence-hallucination tradeoff, where the context
170   influence is bounded by the PMI, which is a fixed measure of how much the model relies on the
171   context given the query and current response, and λ, which controls how much one wants to mitigate
172   context-conflicting hallucination by factoring out prior knowledge. Amplifying the reliance on D
173   by selecting larger λ leads to more context influence. Inversely, limiting the influence by D with
174
      smaller λ increases the chance of hallucination due to more reliance on the prior knowledge θ for
      decoding. Furthermore, if the context D is out-of-distribution with respect to the prior knowledge
175
      θ, then the PMI will be larger since the posterior and the prior distribution can be widely different,
176
      requiring more context influence. However, some next-token samples do not depend on the source
177   document since certain generated tokens derive from general language structure/conventions (e.g.,
178   generating a period after the end of a sentence) learned from pre-training, or the next token derives
179   mostly from the previously generated tokens. Hence, these two scenarios can result in a small PMI.

181   3.3   C ONTEXT I NFLUENCE L OWER B OUNDS P RIVACY L EAKAGE OF CID
182
183   Since sampling from a probability distribution inherently induces privacy, it is not hard to show that
184   tokens generated by CID can achieve DP.
185   Theorem 3.2. Let yt ∼ p∗θ (D, x, y<t , yt , ϵ) be a token such that λ∗ = 2pmi(pθ (ytϵ;D,x,y<t )) . Then yt
186   is ϵ-DP with respect to D.
187
188   The proof can be found in Appendix B. The key idea is that using λ∗ for the linear interpolation of
189
      p∗θ with some pre-specified privacy leakage ϵ for generating the next token by CID satisfies
190                  finfl (p∗θ , x, y<t ) = maxD maxD′ maxyt finfl (p∗θ , D, D′ , x, y<t , yt ) ≤ ϵ.
191
192   Our context influence definition for CID, finfl (pθ , D, D′ , x, y<t , yt ), can actually be thought as the
193   privacy loss of CID, measuring how much privacy is leaked when releasing yt using CID. DP is a
194
      way to bound the worst case privacy loss of CID, finfl (p∗θ , x, y<t ), with ϵ. The worst case means
      no assumptions can be made about the context D, the subset D′ , and the generated next token yt .
195
      Hence, we have to bound the context influence of CID for all contexts, subsets, and possible gen-
196
      erated tokens, which can be infeasible to achieve due to additional compute and utility degradation.
197   However, since finfl (pθ , D, D′ , x, y<t , yt ) ≤ finfl (p∗θ , x, y<t ) our definition of context influence can
198   be thought of as a lower bound for a Differentially Private CID, which follows the more practi-
199   cal direction of auditing private algorithms Jagielski et al. (2020). In our setup, we choose the
200   value for λ and then measure the context influence. This does not achieve DP since it could be
201   ϵ = ∞, but it still gives a guarantee that the privacy leakage of D′ when releasing yt is at least
202   finfl (pθ , D, D′ , x, y<t , yt ).

420                 λ = 1.0. We observe a normal distribution behavior centered at n = 128 with the lowest influence

422                 tokens within the contexts. Sequences that are too large contain too much non-relevant information,

465   5     R ELATED W ORKS
466
467   Hallucination. Our work follows prior work on summarization factuality where the response from
468   an LLM conflicts with provided context Maynez et al. (2020); Pagnoni et al. (2021). Several works
469   have explored fine-tuning based methods to improve generation quality Zhu et al. (2020); Cao et al.
470   (2018). Our work focuses on hallucination mitigation during inference Lee et al. (2022). In particu-
471
      lar, we focus on techniques that utilize PMI to amplify focus on context rather than prior knowledge
      Van der Poel et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023). Our decoding formulation follows from contrastive de-
472
      coding methods Li et al. (2022); Chuang et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023) which contrasts an expert’s
473
      output distribution with an amateur’s output distribution. Our work differs from these works in that
474   it connects hallucination with context influence.
475
476   Memorization and Influence. It has been demonstrated that inadvertent memorization of training
477
      data can lead to privacy leakage Carlini et al. (2019); Song & Shmatikov (2019) in the form of ex-
      traction attacks Carlini et al. (2021); Thomas et al. (2020). Moreover, the inferential capabilities of
478
      LLMs can be exploited to infer undisclosed private information Staab et al. (2023). Hence, there has
479
      been a growing body of work analyzing an LLM’s memorization capabilities. Label memorization
480   Feldman & Zhang (2020) and its variants, exact memorization Tirumala et al. (2022) and counter-
481   factual memorization Zhang et al. (2023); Lesci et al. (2024), compare how the model performs
482   when trained with and without a particular example from the training set. In contrast, others present
483   more actionable definitions that describe a precise type of memorization Carlini et al. (2021; 2022);
484   Biderman et al. (2024). Works focusing on privacy of contextual information have investigated re-
485   gurgitation of prompt data by LLMs Priyanshu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023) and limited context
      influence via differential privacy Wu et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2024). Context


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      attribution Fernandes et al. (2021); Sarti et al. (2023); Cohen-Wang et al. (2024) is a body of work
487   that looks to attribute a prediction made by an LLM to which parts of the context and share a similar
488   goal to our work.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
492
493
      The goal of our work is primarily motivated by privacy, and, thus, the application of our results
      hope to inform practitioners of the privacy risks and design solutions with these results in mind.
494
      For example, we showed that information appearing earlier in the context influences an LLM more
495
      than later ones. Hence, practitioners who want to control the influence of certain sequences can place
496   undesirable/privacy-sensitive ones towards the end of the context/prompt. Additionally, practitioners
497   seeking to privatize LLM generations with respect to a provided context can use Figure 4a to adopt
498   an adaptive privacy level, where the privacy level is strict during the beginning of generating tokens,
499   due to higher influence by the context, then is relaxed as more tokens are generated, since the
500   model can rely on the previous privatized tokens. Lastly, we showed that two LLMs with identical
501   model capacity and architecture can have substantially different influences by the same context,
502   highlighting a greater concern that one must consider not only the prompt but also the pre-training
503   data when measuring the influence of private (contextual) data. It is paramount to identify which
504
      parts of the context greatly affect the generations of an LLM to mitigate hallucinations and improve
      performance, but due to the input regurgitation of LLMs, one must delicately balance context and
505
      prior knowledge when generating responses because of the serious utility and privacy implications.
