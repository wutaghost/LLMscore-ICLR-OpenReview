009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in deep learning have driven significant progress in loss-
012            less image compression. With the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs),
013            preliminary attempts have been made to leverage the extensive prior knowledge
014            embedded in these pretrained models to enhance lossless image compression, par-
015            ticularly by improving the entropy model. However, a significant challenge re-
016            mains in bridging the gap between the textual prior knowledge within LLMs and
017            lossless image compression. To tackle this challenge and unlock the potential of
018            LLMs, this paper introduces a novel paradigm for lossless image compression that
019
               incorporates LLMs with visual prompts. Specifically, we first generate a lossy re-
               construction of the input image as visual prompts, from which we extract local
020
               and global features to serve as visual embeddings for the LLM. The residual be-
021
               tween the original image and the lossy reconstruction is then fed into the LLM
022            along with these visual embeddings, enabling the LLM to function as an entropy
023            model to predict the probability distribution of the residual. Extensive experiments
024            on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate our method achieves state-of-the-art
025            compression performance, surpassing both traditional and learning-based lossless
026            image codecs. Furthermore, our approach can be easily extended to images from
027            other domains, such as medical and screen content images, achieving impressive
028            performance. These results highlight the potential of LLMs for lossless image
029            compression and may inspire further research in related directions.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Lossless image compression aims to reduce image size as much as possible without introducing any
035   distortion, making it essential for high-quality data storage and transmission. Furthermore, the tech-
036   niques used in lossless compression often play a key role in lossy compression methods. Over the
037   past few decades, numerous effective lossless image codecs have been developed. Among these, tra-
038   ditional codecs such as PNG (Boutell, 1997), WebP (Google, 2010), FLIF (Sneyers & Wuille, 2016),
039
      and JPEG-XL (Alakuijala et al., 2019) have achieved strong compression performance through
      hand-crafted coding algorithms. For example, JPEG-XL employs invertible transforms and a so-
040
      phisticated context model, including tree structure and pre-context predictor selection, to compress
041
      images effectively. In recent years, learning-based lossless image codecs (Mentzer et al., 2019;
042   2020; Zhang et al., 2021b;a; Bai et al., 2024) become increasingly popular. L3C (Mentzer et al.,
043   2019), for instance, utilizes a hierarchical probability prediction framework and introduces auxil-
044   iary latent representations to model the probability distribution of image data. These state-of-the-art
045   (SOTA) methods typically rely on empirical knowledge in image compression and employ meticu-
046   lously designed models to achieve better compression performance.
047
      Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved significant breakthroughs in Natural Lan-
048   guage Processing tasks, and their applications have extended to vision tasks, driving substantial
049   progress in areas such as image generation (Ge et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024) and image restora-
050   tion (Zheng et al., 2024). The primary objective of LLMs is to predict the probability distribution of
051   the next token in a sequence. Consequently, more advanced LLM results in more precise modeling
052   of data distribution. Similarly, entropy coding in lossless compression seeks to accurately model
053   data distribution to minimize the coding bitrate. This parallel suggests that LLMs could potentially
      serve as powerful tools for entropy coding.

054
      Recent work by Delétang et al. (2023) supports this perspective, demonstrating that LLMs not only
055   achieve impressive results in text compression but also demonstrate strong potential for lossless im-
056   age compression. This highlights the advantages of leveraging LLMs in the compression domain.
057   However, pretrained LLMs primarily encapsulate textual prior knowledge, whereas image compres-
058   sion relies more on visual information for optimal performance. Therefore, it is crucial to bridge
059   the gap between the textual nature of LLMs and visual data compression tasks. Unfortunately, the
060   existing approach (Delétang et al., 2023) directly treats the pixel values of input images as indexes
061   for LLMs, overlooking the inherent spatial relationships within the images. Consequently, the com-
062   pression efficiency of this method is suboptimal. For instance, the model proposed by Delétang
063
      et al. (2023) with 7B parameters performs only slightly better than PNG (Boutell, 1997). Thus, how
      to effectively unlock the prior knowledge of LLMs and activate their potential for lossless image
064
      compression remains a critical issue that deserves in-depth exploration.
065
066   In this work, we propose a novel framework for lossless image compression that leverages the LLM
067   with visual prompts. Specifically, the image is initially compressed using a lossy codec, and this
068   lossy reconstruction is then employed as visual prompts for the LLM. Subsequently, the LLM is used
069
      to predict the probability distribution of the residual between the lossy reconstruction and the original
      image. Finally, the probability distributions of the residual pixels are modeled using the Gaussian
070
      Mixture Model (GMM), where the parameters are predicted from the output features generated by
071
      the LLM. Furthermore, by finetuning the pretrained LLM with Low-Rank Adaption (LoRA) (Hu
072   et al., 2021), we further enhance our compression performance. Our approach has been evaluated
073   on several benchmark datasets, including Kodak, CLIC, and DIV2K. The results demonstrate that
074   our method achieves SOTA performance, comparable to other well-designed codecs. Our research
075   provides novel insights into lossless image compression and highlights the potential of LLMs for
076   this task.
077
      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
078
079          • By employing the lossy reconstruction as visual prompts for the LLM, we guide the LLM
080            for more efficient lossless data compression.
081
             • The extensive experimental results demonstrate the SOTA performance of our approach on
082            benchmark datasets. Moreover, our approach can be readily applied to images from other
083            domains, such as screen content images and medical images.

      2.1   L OSSY I MAGE C OMPRESSION
089
      Lossy image compression methods aim to minimize coding distortion at a given bitrate. Traditional
090   lossy image coding standards, such as JPEG (Wallace, 1991) and BPG (Bellard, 2018), employ
091   manually designed modules to improve the compression performance. For instance, the widely-
092   used JPEG codec leverages the discrete cosine transform (DCT) to reduce spatial redundancy and
093   employs Huffman coding to further reduce bitrates losslessly. Most lossy codecs adhere to the rate-
094   distortion principle, selecting optimal coding modes to achieve better compression performance.
095
      Recent advancements in learning-based lossy image compression (Liu et al., 2023; Jiang & Wang,
096   2023; Li et al., 2024) have surpassed the SOTA traditional codecs like VVC (Bross et al., 2021).
097   The hyperprior model by Ballé et al. (2018) has been studied as a powerful paradigm, apply-
098   ing lossy transforms, quantization, and efficient lossless encoding of latent representations. Some
099   works (Cheng et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2022) employ advanced architectures, such
100   as attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) and Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021), to improve
101   information retention during lossy transforms. Additionally, studies like Minnen et al. (2018) have
102   optimized the lossless latent coding, incorporating autoregressive components with the hyperprior
103   to capture causal context. Refinements of the context model have led to further improvements in
104
      compression (Minnen & Singh, 2020; He et al., 2021; 2022).
105   Many advancements in hyperprior-based methods focus on enhancing the lossless compression of
106   latent representations by achieving more accurate distribution estimation. Consequently, lossy and
107   lossless image compression are closely related, with lossless compression techniques often con-
      tributing to greater efficiency in lossy compression.

      2.2   L OSSLESS I MAGE C OMPRESSION
109
110   Traditional lossless image codecs, such as PNG (Boutell, 1997), WebP (Google, 2010), FLIF (Sney-
111   ers & Wuille, 2016), and JPEG-XL (Alakuijala et al., 2019), typically utilize hand-crafted tech-
112   niques to reduce intra-image redundancy. These methods typically follow a process of filtering,
113   transforming, quantizing, and applying entropy coding to generate the final bitstream. Recently,
114   learning-based lossless image compression has gained significant attention, typically consisting of
115   two stages: 1) constructing a statistical model to capture the probability distribution of image data.
116
      2) utilizing this statistical model to encode the image into a bitstream using entropy tools such as
      arithmetic coding (AC) or asymmetric numerical systems (ANS) (Duda, 2013). We employ AC as
117
      the lossless data compression technique, due to its widespread use in coding systems and its ability
118
      to generate nearly optimal-length codes based on a given probability distribution and input sequence.
119   It encodes an entire message as a single number within the interval [0, 1) (represented in binary),
120   using a probabilistic model to subdivide the interval into subintervals proportional according to each
121   symbol’s probability.
122
      To enhance statistical models for lossless image compression, deep generative models have been
123
      introduced and can be broadly categorized into three types: 1) Autoregressive models, such as Pix-
124
      elRNN (Van Den Oord et al., 2016) and PixelCNN (Van den Oord et al., 2016), which predict pixel
125   distributions based on conditional dependencies with previously obtained pixels via masked con-
126   volutions. 2) Flow models, such as iVPF (Zhang et al., 2021b) and iFlow (Zhang et al., 2021a),
127   which leverage invertible transforms to simplify latent distributions for efficient entropy coding. 3)
128   Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) models, like L3C (Mentzer et al., 2019), which employ VAE archi-
129   tectures to model image distributions. It is noteworthy that some studies have managed to achieve
130   lossless compression by first compressing the image using a lossy encoder, and then compressing
131   the residuals. For example, RC (Mentzer et al., 2020) integrates BPG for image compression and a
132   CNN for residual compression, whereas DLPR (Bai et al., 2024) combines VAE with autoregressive
133
      models to enhance performance.
134   However, these methods typically rely on complex network designs and are constrained by limited
135   training datasets, especially in the fields like medical images where data is scarce. This highlights
136   the need for a simple pipeline that leverages the extensive prior knowledge embedded in pretrained
137   models from other datasets to enhance compression efficiency.
138
139   2.3   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
140
141   Large language models (LLMs) have gained significant attention in natural language processing
142
      (NLP) and artificial general intelligence (AGI) for their impressive abilities in language generation,
      in-context learning, world knowledge, and reasoning (Wang et al., 2023). LLMs can quickly adapt
143
      to specific tasks using techniques like Adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019) and Low-Rank Adaptation
144
      (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021). Recent research has extended the potential of LLMs to computer vision
145   tasks, such as image classification and segmentation (Gou et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023). However,
146   these studies primarily focus on aligning textual and visual semantics while overlooking low-level
147   visual features. Addressing this gap, LM4LV (Zheng et al., 2024) employs LLMs for image restora-
148   tion, emphasizing their understanding of low-level visual features. Additionally, Delétang et al.
149   (2023) demonstrates that LLMs, when viewed as compressors, can outperform traditional codecs
150   like PNG in lossless image compression, highlighting their potential in this field.

294   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
295
296   Training Details. We train the entire framework in two stages. In the first stage, we freeze the LLM
297   and optimize all other modules. This stage is trained on the ImageNet2012 dataset (Russakovsky
298   et al., 2015) using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 . In
299   the second stage, we apply the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to finetune the LLM. For this, we utilize the
300   DIV2K training dataset (Ignatov et al., 2019) to finetune the entire framework.
301   In this paper, we use LLaMA3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) as the default LLM for all experiments. The
302   original images are lossy compressed using BPG (Bellard, 2018) with the compression parameter
303   of Q = 28. The lossy reconstructions and the original images are then randomly cropped into patch
304   pairs of size 16 × 16, which serve as inputs to the model. To model the distribution, we employ a
305   Gaussian Mixture Model with K = 5.
306   Our method is implemented using the PyTorch framework (Paszke et al., 2017) and requires 3 days to
307   train the entire model on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Additionally, the arithmetic coding is implemented
308   using the yaecl tool library (Xu et al., 2022).
309
      Datasets. To evaluate the performance of the model, we select four different datasets. 1) DIV2K (Ig-
310
      natov et al., 2019): This dataset contains 100 high-resolution color images. 2) CLIC.mobile (Toderici
311
      et al., 2020): The CLIC mobile validation dataset consists of 61 color images taken with mobile
312   phones, with most images in 2K resolution. 3) CLIC.pro (Toderici et al., 2020): The CLIC pro-
313   fessional validation dataset includes 41 color images captured by professional photographers, with
314   the majority of images in 2K resolution. 4) Kodak (Kodak, 1993): This dataset contains 24 uncom-
315   pressed 768×512 color images and is widely used as a benchmark for lossy image compression.
316
      Baseline Codecs. To validate the effectiveness of our method, we compare it against eight traditional

      In this section, we apply our proposed pipeline to images from various domains, including screen
450   content images (SCIs) and medical images. Traditional codecs often require specialized tools, such
451   as the intra block copy technique for SCIs, to improve compression performance, which introduces
452   additional design complexity (Xu et al., 2016). In contrast, learning-based codecs can adapt to these
453   diverse image types through training on sufficiently large datasets. Our proposed pipeline further
454   advances by leveraging the extensive prior information embedded in the LLM, resulting in enhanced
455   compression performance across these diverse image types.
456   Screen Content Image Compression. Screen Content Images (SCIs) typically contain text and
457   graphics, with computer-generated elements constituting over 90% of SCIs. Compared to natural
458   images, SCIs are characterized by sharp edges, a limited color palette, high contrast, and markedly
459   different regional complexity, often exhibiting little to no noise (Nguyen et al., 2021).
460
      In this experiment, we utilize HM-SCC (Xu et al., 2016) as the default lossy codec (QP=28). We
461
      evaluate performance on the SCID dataset (Ni et al., 2017), with the results presented in Table 6. The
462   results indicate that our method, finetuned on the natural image dataset (i.e., DIV2K), demonstrates
463   competitive generalization ability and can be effectively applied to the SCI domain, achieving a
464   5.1% improvement over DLPR. Furthermore, finetuning on the SCI dataset DSCIC (Wang et al.,
465   2024) significantly enhances the model’s performance within the SCI domain, reaching a SOTA
466   level with a bpsp of 1.11, representing a substantial improvement of 10.5% compared to JPEG-XL.
467
      Medical Image Compression. Traditional lossless image compression methods, such as PNG and
468   JPEG-XL, individually encode each slice of 3D medical images. In addition, video coding tech-
469   niques like HEVC (Sullivan et al., 2012) and VVC (Bross et al., 2021), along with traditional
470   medical image compression method JP3D (Bruylants et al., 2009), treat 3D medical images as
471   video sequences or volumetric data. The latest learned lossless compression methods, including
472   L3C (Mentzer et al., 2019), ICEC (Chen et al., 2022), and aiWave (Xue et al., 2022), are also used
473   as baselines.
474   Given that medical images are three-dimensional, we split the input medical images into 3-channel
475   slices for processing. In this experiment, we use JPEG-XL as our lossy codec, empirically setting the
476   corresponding quality to 68. Following prior work (Chen et al., 2022), our framework is finetuned
477   on the MRNet training dataset (Bien et al., 2018) and tested on the MRNet validation dataset. The
478   test results are presented in Table 7.
479
      Our model demonstrates superior compression performance for lossless medical image compres-
480   sion. For the Axial subset, the average bpsp of the proposed method is 4.46, compared to 4.72
481   for JPEG-XL. Moreover, when compared to the learning-based lossless codec L3C (Mentzer et al.,
482   2019), which is also finetuned on medical images in this experiment, our approach shows signif-
483   icantly better compression performance. On the Coronal subset, our method further saves 6.1%
484   bit consumption compared with aiWave (Xue et al., 2022). This improvement can be attributed to
485   our method’s utilization of the extensive prior information embedded in LLMs, enhancing overall
      performance.

521   5      L IMITATIONS AND POTENTIAL
522
523
      As illustrated in Figure 1, our approach involves pixel-by-pixel autoregressive coding, which can
      be time-consuming, although patch-level parallelization is possible. Additionally, by applying ac-
524
      celeration techniques such as distillation and pruning, the inference speed of LLMs is expected to
525
      improve, although this aspect falls outside the scope of our current work. Our main objective is to
526   demonstrate the potential of LLMs for lossless image compression and to propose a feasible archi-
527   tecture that leverages the powerful knowledge embedded in LLMs. There are also several methods
528   that could further enhance LLM-based codecs. For instance, replacing BPG (Bellard, 2018) with a
529   learning-based lossy image compression method could enable end-to-end joint optimization, lead-
530   ing to improved results. Additionally, incorporating more sophisticated and efficient context models
531   could help extract richer and more effective features for better compression performance.
532
533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   Our work demonstrates that LLMs hold significant potential for lossless image compression. By
536   designing embeddings tailored for image data and incorporating visual prompts, we achieve state-of-
537   the-art lossless compression performance. Additionally, this framework can be effectively adapted
538   to other image compression domains, such as screen content images and medical images. While our
539   exploration of this framework is still in its early stages, we believe that this LLM-based method has
      the potential to become a new paradigm for image compression in the near future.

      We further introduce JPEG as an additional lossy codec. Our findings reveal that, within an appro-
