                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               We address the challenge of explaining counterfactual outcomes in multi-agent
012
               Markov decision processes. In particular, we aim to explain the total counter-
013            factual effect of an agent’s action on the outcome of a realized scenario through
014            its influence on the environment dynamics and the agents’ behavior. To achieve
015            this, we introduce a novel causal explanation formula that decomposes the coun-
016            terfactual effect by attributing to each agent and state variable a score reflecting
017            their respective contributions to the effect. First, we show that the total counter-
018            factual effect of an agent’s action can be decomposed into two components: one
019            measuring the effect that propagates through all subsequent agents’ actions and
020            another related to the effect that propagates through the state transitions. Build-
021
               ing on recent advancements in causal contribution analysis, we further decompose
               these two effects as follows. For the former, we consider agent-specific effects –
022
               a causal concept that quantifies the counterfactual effect of an agent’s action that
023
               propagates through a subset of agents. Based on this notion, we use Shapley value
024            to attribute the effect to individual agents. For the latter, we consider the con-
025            cept of structure-preserving interventions and attribute the effect to state variables
026            based on their “intrinsic” contributions. Through extensive experimentation, we
027            demonstrate the interpretability of our decomposition approach in a Gridworld
028            environment with LLM-assisted agents and a sepsis management simulator.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Applying counterfactual reasoning to retrospectively analyze the impact of different actions in deci-
034   sion making scenarios is fundamental for accountability. For instance, counterfactual reasoning can
035   be employed to identify actual causes (Halpern, 2016; Triantafyllou et al., 2022), attribute responsi-
036   bility (Chockler & Halpern, 2004; Friedenberg & Halpern, 2019), generate explanations (Madumal
037   et al., 2020; Tsirtsis et al., 2021), evaluate fairness (Kusner et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2022) and
038
      measure harm (Richens et al., 2022; Beckers et al., 2022). To achieve such objectives, many stud-
      ies often rely on the notion of total counterfactual effects, which quantifies the extent to which an
039
      alternative action would have affected the outcome of a realized scenario.
040
041   In multi-agent sequential decision making, an agent’s action typically affects the outcome indirectly.
042   To illustrate this, consider the problem of AI-assisted decision making in healthcare (Lynn, 2019),
043   where a clinician and their AI assistant treat a patient over a period of time. Fig. 1a depicts a specific
044
      example, where treatment fails. We estimate that if the clinician had not followed the AI’s recom-
      mendation at step 10 and administered vasopressors (V) instead of mechanical ventilation (E), the
045
      treatment would have been successful with an 82% likelihood. Therefore, the considered alterna-
046
      tive action admits a high total counterfactual effect. This effect, however, propagates through all
047   subsequent actions of the clinician and the AI, as well as all the changes in the patient’s state. This
048   makes the interpretability of the effect more nuanced, as the change from action to outcome can be
049   transmitted by multiple distinct causal mechanisms. In this work, we ask:
050
      How to explain an action’s total counterfactual effect in multi-agent sequential decision making?
051

053   Bareinboim, 2018a;b) under the rubric of mediation analysis (Imai et al., 2010; 2011; Hicks &
      Tingley, 2011; VanderWeele, 2016), which aims to understand how effects propagate through causal

082
083   (Level 1) We first introduce a causal explanation formula, which decomposes the total counterfac-
084   tual effect of an agent’s action into the total agent-specific effect and the reverse state-specific effect.
085

      mulated via the recently introduced notion of agent-specific effects (Triantafyllou et al., 2024). The
086

087

088
089   (Level 2a) To further decompose the total agent-specific effect (tot-ASE), we propose an axiomatic
090   framework based on agent-specific effects for attributing the total effect to individual agents. The set
091
      of axioms includes efficiency, which requires that the agents’ contributions sum up to tot-ASE. We
      show how to operationalize Shapley value with agent-specific effects, in order to obtain a method
092
      for decomposing tot-ASE, which uniquely satisfies the set of proposed axioms.
093
094   (Level 2b) To further decompose the reverse state-specific effect (r-SSE), we utilize the notion of
095   intrinsic causal contributions (ICC) (Janzing et al., 2024). ICC enables us to quantify the infor-
096   mativeness of individual state variables regarding the counterfactual outcomes needed for the com-
097
      putation of r-SSE. We propose a method for decomposing r-SSE that is efficient under a relatively
      mild assumption that at least one state variable has non-zero ICC (i.e., is informative about the
098
      counterfactual outcomes).

100   We experimentally validate the interpretability of our approach using two multi-agent environments
101   with heterogeneous agents: a grid-world environment, where two RL actors are instructed by an
102   LLM planner to complete a sequence of tasks, and the sepsis management simulator from Fig. 1.

      Chiappa, 2019). As mentioned earlier, the main distinction between this line of work and ours

      is that we analyze how effects propagate through agents and state variables in an MMDP, instead
109   of causal paths in general SCMs. In a similar sense, our work also relates to the areas of causal
110   contributions (Janzing et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2022; Heskes et al., 2020) and flow-based attribution

113   effect. The latter considers the problem of assigning credit to the edges of a causal graph, instead of
114   the nodes, for explaining causal effects.

117
118   In this section, we present our formal framework, which is adopted from Triantafyllou et al. (2024)
119   and builds on Multi-Agent Markov Decision Processes (MMDPs) (Boutilier, 1996) and Structural
120   Causal Models (SCMs) (Pearl, 2009). A table summarizing the notation is provided in Appendix B.
121   Appendix N provides a graphical illustration of all counterfactual effects discussed in this section
122   and the next, using the 2-step Sepsis example from Triantafyllou et al. (2024).

125
126
      An MMDP is represented as a tuple ⟨S, {1, ..., n}, A, T, h, σ⟩, where: S is the state space; {1, ..., n}
      is the set of agents; A = ×ni=1 Ai is the joint action space, with Ai being the action space of agent i;
127
      T : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the transitions probability function; h is the finite time horizon; σ is
128
      the initial state distribution.1 Each agent i ∈ {1, ..., n} has a stationary decision-making policy πi ,
129   with the joint policy of all agents represented as π. The probability of agents jointly taking action
130   at = (a1,t , ..., an,t ) in state st at time t is thus given by π(at |st ) = π1 (a1,t |st ) · · · πn (an,t |st ),
131   while the probability of transitioning from state st to state st+1 is determined by T (st+1 |st , at ).
132   A sequence of such state-action pairs {(st , at )}t∈{0,...,h−1} and final state sh is called a trajectory.
133   With τ (X), we denote the value of variable X in trajectory τ .

178   Assumptions and counterfactual identifiability. Note that there might be multiple MMDP-SCMs
179   whose observational distribution is consistent with some MMDP-joint policy pair, but yield different
180   counterfactuals, e.g., different values for TCFE. This means that without further assumptions, coun-
181   terfactuals cannot be identified from observations alone. To enable counterfactual identifiability,
182   we thus make the following assumptions. First, we consider unobserved variables to be mutually
183   independent. Second, we assume that MMDP-SCMs satisfy the (weak) noise monotonicity condi-

185   MMDP distribution or the agents’ policies, i.e., every MMDP can be consistently represented by a
186
      noise-monotonic MMDP-SCM. What is restricted instead is the expressivity of the model’s counter-
      factual distribution. These assumptions suffice to render all counterfactuals discussed in this paper
187
      identifiable from observational data. Details about noise monotonicity can be found in Appendix E.

196

344   Next, we define a number of desirable properties for the attribution of the total agent-specific effect.

454
455   Environment. The two-agent variant of the sepsis treatment setting (Triantafyllou et al., 2024) we
456   consider here involves a clinician and an AI agent who take sequential actions in a turn-based manner
457   for treating an ICU patient. At each round, the AI recommends one of 8 possible treatments, which is
458   then reviewed and potentially overridden by the clinician. The likelihood of the clinician overriding
459
      the AI’s treatment at any given state is modeled by a parameter µ, which is varied in our experiment.
      Intuitively, µ serves as a proxy for the clinician’s level of trust in the AI’s recommendations: higher
460
      values of µ correspond to greater levels of trust. If the AI’s action is not overridden, then its selected
461
      treatment is applied. Otherwise, a new treatment selected by the clinician is applied. The outcome of
462   a trajectory is deemed successful if the patient is kept alive for 20 rounds or gets discharged earlier.
463
464   Evaluation of ASE-SV. We generate 600 trajectories with unsuccessful outcomes. We then measure
465
      the total counterfactual effect of all possible alternative actions on the final state of these trajectories
      and keep those that exhibit TCFE ≥ 0.8. Through that process, 8728 alternative actions are selected
466
      for the evaluation of ASE-SV. For all selected actions, we compute their total agent-specific effect,
467
      clinician-specific effect and AI-specific effect. As expected, the sum of the two individual effects
468   does not equal the total one, with discrepancies of up to 95%. In contrast, in our experiments,
469   ASE-SV always attributes the effect efficiently to the clinician and AI, as supported by Theorem 5.3.
470
471
      Plots 3a and 3b show the average percentage composition of the reverse state-specific effect and the
      agent scores attributed by ASE-SV w.r.t. the total counterfactual effect, for different trust levels. Plot
472
      3a (resp. Plot 3b) considers the average over all selected AI (resp. clinician) actions. Results reveal
473
      that our method demonstrates a trend similar to the one described in Triantafyllou et al. (2024). In
474   particular, the amount of tot-ASE attributed to the clinician (resp. AI) decreases (resp. increases) as
475   the level of trust rises, eventually reaching zero (resp. full) when the clinician completely trusts the
476   AI’s recommendations. This observation is intuitive, since the clinician is expected to contribute
477   less to the effect as it acts more infrequently in the environment, while at the same time the AI
478   is expected to contribute more as it assumes greater agency. Thus, we conclude that ASE-SV can
479   efficiently attribute tot-ASE without sacrificing the conceptual power of agent-specific effects.
480   Evaluation of r-SSE-ICC. We consider the same setup as before and categorize all selected actions
481   based on the difference between the round that they were taken and the final round of their respective
482   trajectory. For instance, if the action we consider was taken by the AI at the third round of a
483   trajectory with 8 rounds in total then the round difference for that action is 5. For our analysis, we
484   maintain actions with round difference between 4 and 10. For all selected actions, we compute their
485   reverse state-specific effect together with its variance. We keep those with absolute r-SSE ≥ 0.1 and
      variance ≥ 0.01, which yields a total of 437 alternative actions for the evaluation of r-SSE-ICC.

      7    D ISCUSSION
499
500   In this paper, we introduce a causal explanation framework tailored to multi-agent MDPs. Specifi-
501   cally, we decompose the total counterfactual effect of an agent’s action by attributing it to the agents’
502   behavior or environment dynamics. Our experimental results demonstrate that our decomposition
503   provides valuable insights into the distinct roles that agents and environment play in influencing the
504
      effect. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that looks into the problem of counter-
      factual effect decomposition in the context of multi-agent sequential decision making. While our
505
      findings are promising, there are several directions for future exploration, which we outline below.
506
507   Computational complexity. The computational complexity of our decomposition approach de-
508   pends on the total number of agents and the length of the MMDP’s time horizon. In our experiments,
509   we use a relatively small number of agents and a horizon of a few dozen time-steps. We believe that
510
      many interesting multi-agent settings belong to this regime, e.g., human-AI collaboration. Neverthe-
      less, there are settings in which computational complexity considerations can be important, and we
511
      see this as an interesting future research direction to explore. In Appendix I, we analyze the compu-
512
      tational complexity of the ASE-SV and r-SSE-ICC methods, and discuss some potential mitigation
513   strategies for when the number of agents or the time horizon are prohibitively large.
514
515   Causal assumptions. Making causal assumptions in order to enable counterfactual identifiability is
516
      quite common in the literature. There is a plethora of works at the intersection of decision making
      and counterfactual reasoning that assumes exogeneity alongside additional causal properties, such as
517
      weak (Triantafyllou et al., 2024) or strong (Tsirtsis & Rodriguez, 2024) noise monotonicity, counter-
518
      factual stability (Oberst & Sontag, 2019), or access to the ground-truth causal model (Richens et al.,
519   2022). However, these assumptions are often violated in practice. Thus, extending the applicability
520   of our proposed approach to domains where our theoretical assumptions (exogeneity and weak noise
521   monotonicity) do not hold would be of significant practical importance.
522
      Applications to accountable decision making. We deem the problem of decomposing counter-
523
      factual effects particularly relevant for multi-agent decision-making settings where accountability is
524
      paramount. Our approach can be applied in these settings, by integrating it into existing causal tools
525   for retrospectively analyzing decision-making failures. For instance, consider methods for blame at-
526   tribution in multi-agent systems (Halpern & Kleiman-Weiner, 2018; Friedenberg & Halpern, 2019).
527   Typically, these methods first identify the agents’ actions that were critical to the outcome, i.e., those
528   that, had they been different, would have likely prevented failure. Next, they assess the agents’ epis-
529   temic states, determining to what extent each agent could or should have predicted the consequences
530   of acting differently. Our approach can enhance these methods by offering a more granular notion
531   of blame. In the Sepsis scenario described in Section 1, for example, the clinician may be expected
532   to predict how their actions directly affect the patient’s state, but may not be expected to predict the
533
      AI’s responses, especially if they have never worked with the current version of the model before.
      According to the output of our decomposition approach (Plot 1b), the clinician would then receive
534
      73.5% of the total blame for their action, rather than bearing full responsibility. We see significant
535
      potential in combining our approach with existing works on blame attribution and related concepts
536   in accountable decision making, offering practical benefits across various multi-agent domains.

      E        A DDITIONAL INFORMATION ON NOISE MONOTONICITY
766
767
      In this section, we define the (weak) noise monotonicity property for categorical SCMs. It has
768   been shown that noise monotonicity enables counterfactual identifiability. For more details on noise
769   monotonicity and its connection to the identifiability problem, we refer the interested reader to
770   Triantafyllou et al. (2024).
771
      Definition E.1 (Noise Monotonicity). Given an SCM M with causal graph G, we say that vari-
772
      able V i ∈ V is noise-monotonic in M w.r.t. a total ordering ≤i on dom{V i }, if for any
773   pai ∈ dom{P ai (G)} and ui1 , ui2 ∼ P (U i ) s.t. ui1 < ui2 , it holds that f i (pai , ui1 ) ≤i f i (pai , ui2 ).
774
775   Essentially, noise monotonicity assumes that all observed variables in an SCM, or MMDP-SCM in
776   our paper, are monotonic w.r.t. their corresponding noise variable (for some specified total order-
777   ing). Note that noise monotonicity is not limiting for the MMDPs or agents’ policies. In simple
778   words, what noise monotonicity assumption restricts is the expressivity of counterfactual distribu-
779   tions. There can be many MMDP-SCMs whose observational distribution is consistent with the
780
      MMDP, but admit different counterfactual distributions. Theorem 4.3 in Triantafyllou et al. (2024)
      shows that by limiting the class of possible MMDP-SCMs to the ones that satisfy noise monotonic-
781
      ity, counterfactual identifiability is guaranteed.

      I     D ISCUSSION ON COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
894
895
      In this section, we analyze the computational complexity of the ASE-SV (Definition 5.2) and r-SSE-
896
      ICC (Definition 4.1) methods, and discuss some potential mitigation strategies for when the number
897   of agents or the length of the time horizon are prohibitively large. We conclude the section with a
898   discussion about the effect of agents’ capabilities on the computational complexity of our approach.
899
900   I.1   C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF ASE-SV
901
902   The number of agent-specific effect evaluations required by the exact ASE-SV calculation grows
903   exponentially with the number of agents n. One potential mitigation strategy for this problem is to
904   adapt to our setting sampling based approaches that efficiently approximate Shapley value without
905   violating efficiency, i.e., attributing the entire effect. Jia et al. (2019) propose such an algorithmic
906   approach, which requires O(n(log n)2 ) evaluations for any bounded utility. This means that their
907
      algorithm is applicable to ASE-SV in settings where the value of agent-specific effects is bounded,
      as is the case in both our experiments.
908
909
910
      I.2   C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF R -SSE-ICC
911
      Computing the contribution scores assigned by the r-SSE-ICC method to all state variables requires
912   O(h), where h denotes the time horizon, executions of Algorithm 1. When we deal with long-
913   horizon MMDPs, this linear dependence on the number of time-steps can slow down our method.
914   One intuitive strategy to reduce the number of computations in this case is by grouping together state
915   variables from consecutive time-steps. That way, the r-SSE-ICC method would attribute the effect
916   to sets of consecutive state variables instead of individual ones. If the time horizon (between action
917   and outcome) is partitioned in groups of the same fixed size k, except maybe for the last one, then
      the modified r-SSE-ICC method would require O( hk ) executions of Algorithm 1.


                                                            17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      In settings where it is reasonable to assume or empirically verify that r-SSE-ICC is sparse, in the
919   sense that only a few state variables have significant (intrinsic) contributions to the effect, as it is the
920   case in both our experiments, then we are able to further reduce the number of Algorithm 1 execu-
921   tions. More specifically, we can utilize the fact that the expected noise-conditional variance measure
922   satisfies monotonicity, i.e., E[Var(∆YI,ai,t |τ, U<Sk )|τ ]M ≥ E[Var(∆YI,ai,t |τ, U<Sk , USk )|τ ]M .
923   If we know, for example, that for most of the times there is at most one state variable with non-
924   negligible (intrinsic) contribution to the effect, we can simply use a binary search approach to pin-
925   point that state. This can reduce the complexity of r-SSE-ICC to O(log(h)) executions.
926
927   I.3   E FFECT OF AGENTS ’ CAPABILITIES ON COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
928
929   The complexity of decision-making agents affects the computational complexity of our decomposi-
930   tion approach, assuming that increased capabilities imply increased inference time. The reasoning
931
      is the following: our approach to estimating counterfactual effects involves sampling trajectories
      from the posterior distribution and then averaging the values of the response variable across these
932
      trajectories. Sampling a trajectory from the posterior distribution generally requires to prompt each
933
      agent once for every counterfactual state in which they need to act.
934
935   For reference, in the Gridworld environment, more than 90% of the time required to sample one
936   counterfactual trajectory is spent on the inference of the LLM agent, while the remaining ∼ 10% is
937
      shared between the two RL agents.4 Consequently, if we were to use an LLM agent with reduced
      cognitive capabilities, and hence less inference time, then the scalability of our approach in this
938
      experiment would significantly improve.
