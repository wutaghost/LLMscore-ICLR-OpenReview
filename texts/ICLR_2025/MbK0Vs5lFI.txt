011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Language models can solve complex reasoning tasks better by learning to generate
014            rationales for their predictions. Often these models know how to solve a task
015            but their auto-regressive decoding nature leads to incorrect results if they start
016            incorrectly. We observe that smaller models in particular when corrected, can
017            solve a task that they would have otherwise struggled with. We demonstrate this
018
               phenomenon by using a larger model to guide smaller models, which leads to
               significantly improved performance (up to +24 points on the GSM8K dataset by
019
               7B models). To assist smaller models in initiating the starting step, we propose
020
               QuestCoT, where a smaller model first asks itself how to start, before proceeding
021            with a chain of reasoning. On various multistep mathematical reasoning datasets
022            over multiple smaller models, we show that getting the right start can lead to sig-
023            nificant performance gains across all models (gains of up to +6 points on GSM8K,
024            +9 on SVAMP, +5 on ASDiv, and +7 on MultiArith).

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
030
      Over the years, large language models (LLMs) have improved their reasoning abilities by explaining
      their intermediate thoughts Wei et al. (2022). This trend has been extended to smaller models 1 ,
031
      either through pre-training Jiang et al. (2023); Magnusson et al. (2023), fine-tuning Yu et al. (2023);
032
      Shao et al. (2024), or knowledge distillation Shridhar et al. (2023b); Yuan et al. (2023); Magister
033   et al. (2023); Hsieh et al. (2023). While it is commonly assumed that smaller models acquire new
034   knowledge through fine-tuning or distillation, recent research by Gekhman et al. (2024) suggests that
035   the acquisition of new knowledge is quite slow. Instead, models often improve in the areas they are
036   already familiar with. This suggests that while models may have the knowledge to solve a given task,
037   they struggle to understand how to apply it effectively.
038
      Wang et al. (2023b) demonstrates that model accuracy improves significantly when multiple reasoning
039   chains are generated, indicating that the model understands how to answer the given problem.
040   However, models often struggle to select the correct initial chain, and if they start on an incorrect
041   reasoning path, it becomes difficult to fix it due to the autoregressive nature of decoding. Similarly, in
042   our work, we observed that if a smaller model initiates an incorrect reasoning chain, it will continue
043   down that incorrect path. Conversely, if the initial step is correctly determined, the model can
044   successfully complete tasks that it would otherwise find challenging.
045   In this work, we first investigate whether providing initial guidance can improve the reasoning
046   capabilities of smaller language models. We then investigate whether the quality of this initial
047   guidance varies depending on the expertise of different large language models (LLMs). In particular,
048   we investigate whether smaller models can use this guidance without fine-tuning or additional training,
049   and whether models of different sizes benefit equally. Finally, we investigate whether the benefits
050   of initial guidance extend beyond simple two-step problems to tasks requiring four to eight steps of
051   reasoning.

085

087   performs similarly to expert LLM guidance improving the quality of reasoning and outperforms

089   decomposition approaches (Shridhar et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023, Subques).

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      It is possible to elicit reasoning abilities from LLMs through in-context learning, either by providing
095
      the model with intermediate steps Wei et al. (2022); Kojima et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Wang
096   et al. (2023b), or by decomposing the problem into smaller sub-problems Shridhar et al. (2022); Zhou
097   et al. (2023) and solving them to reach the final answer. However, if the problem is misinterpreted, it
098   can lead to a cascade of errors in subsequent steps.
099
      To counter this, several techniques have been proposed to intervene and correct intermediate steps by
100
      providing feedback on their own generations, and eventually “self-correcting” their own generations
101
      Welleck et al. (2022); Madaan et al. (2023); Shridhar et al. (2023a). While the LLM’s ability to
102   revise its own generations may prove helpful in many cases, it sometimes leads to worse results in
103   refinement, requiring a “rollback” to the previous output Shridhar et al. (2023a). To address this,
104   Yao et al. (2023) introduces the Tree of Thoughts (ToT), which plans subsequent steps to solve a
105   reasoning task Huang et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023a;c). ToT conceptualizes the decision-making
106   process as a series of heuristically based decisions. Through deliberate search, ToT explores different
107   reasoning paths and self-reflects on its decision at each step. We, on the other hand, propose to get
      the first step right, thus reducing the cost of “finding” and “fixing” errors.

      Previous work has also focused on understanding when to intervene and correct the errors. Saha et al.
109   (2023) presented an approach based on Theory of Mind Kosinski (2023); Kadavath et al. (2022),
110   where a teacher model intervenes in a student model only for harder questions by creating an implicit
111   mental model of the student’s understanding. In contrast, an alternative that avoids the need to
112   backtrack and correct mistakes, thus saving time and effort, is to start right.

116   In this section, we explore the impact of initiating the correct reasoning chain on the performance
117   of smaller language models (LMs) in multi-step reasoning tasks. We address three key research
118   questions:
119
120         1. Are smaller models capable of solving a reasoning task?
121         2. What is the importance of taking the correct first step in reasoning?
122         3. Can smaller models learn to take the correct first step on their own?

197

490   5    D EEPER EXPLORATION OF WHY QuestCoT WORKS
491
492   Understanding why one technique outperforms another can be quite challenging. To address this, we
493
      examined instances where QuestCoT was successful and conducted a detailed analysis. We identified
      situations where CoT failed and broadly categorized these errors. Here are some error types where
494
      QuestCoT was beneficial:
495
496
      Unnecessary calculations A common mistake CoT makes is performing unnecessary calculations
497
      on the numbers in the statement. These numbers may be completely irrelevant to the problem, or they
498   may need to be used in a different way than the model uses them. QuestCoT helps to correct these
499   errors by initiating the reasoning process with an appropriate question. An example of an unnecessary
500   calculation is given in Figure 8 with the main error shown in red, where there was no need to calculate
501   the total cost of coffee for the entire week at the old price.
502
503   Real-world knowledge Often, the first step in CoT seems somewhat "rushed," focusing on quickly
504   manipulating numbers without considering real-world facts or knowledge. In these cases, the model
505   demonstrates its understanding of these facts and knowledge in the subsequent steps but cannot elicit
506   it immediately in the first step. This suggests that encouraging the model to think more deliberately
507
      in the first step (e.g., by allowing it to consider what needs to be done before it starts reasoning) may
      remedy this problem. These scenarios illustrate the effectiveness of QuestCoT. An example is shown
508
      in Figure 8, where the model fails to convert "half a dozen" to 6, and instead continues its calculations
509
      with 0.5 (as shown in blue). Although the model demonstrated its understanding of "dozen" later in
510   the problem, since it started incorrectly, it was unable to correct the chain later.
511
512   Context understanding With CoT, the model often confuses or misses the context in the problem
513   statement and makes incorrect initial assumptions that are difficult to recover from in later steps.
514   For example, in Figure 8 we can see that despite following a fairly elaborate reasoning template of
515   variable assumptions, the CoT reasoning misses the fact that the price of the Super Ticket already
516   includes the price of the popcorn. The incorrect assumption is highlighted in the response.
517
518   Other errors Other errors we have observed include that QuestCoT may be better at handling direct
519   numeric computations and understanding the simple arithmetic required by the problems. In contrast,
520   CoT may deviate or fail to capture the essential computational aspects of the query. In addition, CoT
521   sometimes takes more steps than necessary, resulting in an incorrect final solution.
522
523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   We find that smaller models sometimes struggle with taking the correct first step, but their performance
526   increases significantly once this step is corrected. We demonstrated this by using LLMs to guide
527   smaller models to take the correct first step, helping them to establish the correct reasoning chain. To
528   facilitate this for smaller models, we propose QuestCoT, which uses initial question-based guidance
529   to improve their reasoning themselves without any guidance. We demonstrate the effectiveness of
530   our approach on four multi-step mathematical reasoning datasets using different open-source small
531
      models.
