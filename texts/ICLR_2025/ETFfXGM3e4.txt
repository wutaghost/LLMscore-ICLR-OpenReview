011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The proliferation of AI-generated images necessitates effective watermarking to
014            protect intellectual property and identify fake content. While existing training-
015            based watermarking methods show promise, they often struggle with generalization
016            across diverse image styles and tend to produce noticeable artifacts. To this end,
017            we introduce a provably generalizable image watermarking method for Latent
018            Diffusion Models with Self-Augmented Training (SAT-LDM), which aligns the
019            training and testing phases by a free generation distribution to bolster the water-
020
               marking module’s generalization capabilities. We theoretically consolidate our
               method by proving that the free generation distribution contributes to its tight gener-
021
               alization bound without the need to collect new data. Extensive experimental results
022
               demonstrate that SAT-LDM achieves robust watermarking while significantly im-
023            proving the quality of watermarked images across diverse styles. Furthermore, we
024            conduct experimental analyses to demonstrate the strong generalization abilities
025            of SAT-LDM. We hope our method offers a practical and convenient solution for
026            securing high-fidelity AI-generated content.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent developments in diffusion models, notably commercial models like Stable Diffusion (SD)
033   (Rombach et al., 2022), Glide (Nichol et al., 2022), and Muse AI (Rombach et al., 2022), have
034   revolutionized image generation. These models exhibit exceptional capabilities in generating high-
035   quality and diverse images from textual descriptions, making them valuable tools across a range of
036   domains, such as fashion design (Baldrati et al., 2023) and education (Lee & Song, 2023). However,
037   the ease of generating such images also raises concerns about intellectual property rights and the
038   propagation of fake content, making it imperative to watermark generated content.
039   As a tailored approach, watermarking technology (Cox et al., 2007) can embed hidden messages
040   into images, facilitating copyright verification and source identification. Traditional post-hoc water-
041   marking techniques (Xia et al., 1998; Zhu et al., 2018) introduce watermarks after image creation,
042   adding extra workflow steps and potentially degrading image quality (Fernandez et al., 2023). In
043   response to these limitations, recent efforts (Fernandez et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
044   2024) have shifted towards diffusion-native watermarking, where watermarking is integrated directly
045
      within the image generation pipeline. Notable methods such as Stable Signature (Fernandez et al.,
      2023) and FSW (Xiong et al., 2023) fine-tune the VAE decoder within the latent diffusion model
046
      (LDM) as a watermarking module on public image datasets to embed watermarks. While these
047
      methods show promise, they still fall short when applied to real-world scenarios, often resulting in
048   noticeable artifacts that degrade image quality, as shown in Figure 1. This degradation may stem from
049   the biases in the public datasets, which fail to cover the diverse image styles present in actual use
050   and exceeds the practical ability of generative models, thus leading to a watermarking module with
051   limited generalization ability. A straightforward solution might involve larger datasets encompassing
052   more diverse styles, but this approach demands substantial resources and time, and may also raise
053   concerns about data privacy and copyright Khan & Hanna (2022). Driven by the significance of
      watermarking effectiveness—the invisibility and robustness of watermarks—a key question arises:

      2.1   P OST- HOC WATERMARKING
117
118   Post-hoc watermarking methods involve embedding watermarks into images after their creation and
119   can be classified into three categories: (1) Frequency domain method (Cox et al., 2007) embed water-
120   marks by manipulating the frequency components of image, balancing robustness and complexity.
121   (2) Per-image optimization (Kishore et al., 2021; Fernandez et al., 2022) customizes watermark
122   embedding for each image, allowing for more hidden information but increasing computational de-
123   mands. (3) Encoder-decoder networks (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik et al., 2020; Jia et al., 2021) enhance
124
      robustness against compression and real-world image transformations, with the option to incorporate
      targeted adversarial training to further improve watermark robustness against other attacks. However,
125
      when applied to images generated by diffusion models, these post-hoc methods introduce additional
126
      workflow steps independent of the generation pipeline. This not only increases time overhead but
127   also may degrade image quality (Fernandez et al., 2023).
128
129
      2.2   D IFFUSION - NATIVE WATERMARKING
130
131   Diffusion-native watermarking integrates the watermarking process directly into the diffusion model’s
132   workflow. This category can be further divided into training-free methods and training-based methods.
133
134   Training-free Methods. Tree-Ring (Wen et al., 2024) introduces the concept of embedding wa-
135   termarks in the initial noise during the diffusion process, achieving notable robustness but lacking
136   multi-key identification (Ci et al., 2024b). Subsequent methods enhance this by using improved
137   imprinting techniques (Ci et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024). Despite their advancements, these methods
138   can significantly alter the layout of the generated images, which may be undesirable in certain
139
      production scenarios (Ci et al., 2024a).
140
      Training-based Methods. DiffuseTrace (Lei et al., 2024) hides information in the initial noise by
141
      training a dedicated encoder/decoder. WaDiff (Min et al., 2024) and AquaLoRA (Feng et al., 2024)
142
      embed watermarks into the diffusion UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) backbone, leading to longer
143   training pipelines and modifications to the generated image layout. RoSteALS (Bui et al., 2023) and
144   work (Meng et al., 2024) imprint watermarks into the latent space of the VAE (Kingma & Welling,
145   2014), but face challenges with unstable training, requiring either multi-stage training processes
146   or precise hyperparameter adjustments. FSW (Xiong et al., 2023), StableSignature (Fernandez
147   et al., 2023), and WOUAF (Kim et al., 2024) also inject watermarks into the VAE feature space but
148   necessitate modifications to the VAE decoder, often degrading the quality of the generated images (Ci
149   et al., 2024a). Besides, these methods generally require collecting extensive external data for training
150   and remain prone to artifacts on watermarked images. In contrast, our method requires no external
151
      data and features a plugin-based design, offering convenience while preserving high image quality.

333   4.2       I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
334
      Architectures. We employ the FSW (Xiong et al., 2023) model structure, which is one of the latest
335
      training-based invisible watermarking methods. It jointly optimizes the parameters of the modified-
336
      decoder Dm and message-extractor Tm to embed l-bit messages into images, ensuring robustness
337   to transformations applied during training. Dm introduces a message processor to process the input
338   message m into a message matrix, which is then fused with the outputs of selected intermediate
339   layers (namely, the input convolutional layers, intermediate blocks, and the first four upsampling
340   modules) during the image generation process to create watermarked images. The message processor
341   mainly consists of 5 fully connected (FC) layers and 6 convolutional layers, and the message extractor
342   mainly comprises 7 convolutional layers and 3 FC layers. For further details, we refer the reader to
343   the original paper (Xiong et al., 2023). We implement two modifications to the model structure. (1)
344   We aim to ensure the entity which manages the model can easily replace the watermarked model with
345
      a non-watermarked version without disrupting the user experience. To achieve this, we preserve the
      parameters of the original VAE decoder and replicate the structure of selected intermediate layers
346
      to integrate them into the message processor for training. This method retains the original message
347
      fusion process while maintaining the integrity of the original VAE decoder’s parameters. (2) We
348   observe that Tm has limited robustness against perspective changes, even with adversarial training
349   added during training. To address this, we add a spatial transformer network (Jaderberg et al., 2015)
350   in front of Tm to improve robustness to slight perspective changes introduced when images are
351   printed and photographed.

528   6       C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530   In this work, we propose SAT-LDM, an enhanced image watermarking method for latent diffusion
531   model with self-augment training. Compared to existing methods, SAT-LDM offers effectiveness
532   and convenience by utilizing the free generation distribution for training. This approach does not
533
      alter the diffusion process, ensuring compatibility with most LDM-based generative models. We
      also provide a theoretical analysis of the generalization error to consolidate our proposed method.
534
      Extensive experiments validate its superior performance, particularly in the quality of watermarked
535
      images. In future work, we will explore the application of dataset distillation (Wang et al., 2018) to
536   further minimize the need for training samples, potentially eliminating that requirement altogether.
537   Our prompt design may still harbor biases, necessitating future ablation studies to evaluate their
538   impact comprehensively..

853   The pseudocode in this section outlines the core steps involved in the training process for the SAT-
854   LDM watermarking framework. The algorithm integrates the watermark embedding process within
855   the latent diffusion model (LDM) and applies a self-augmented training mechanism to enhance the
856   model’s generalization capabilities. Specifically, the watermarking module Gm and the message
857   extractor Tm are trained together. During each training epoch, the process involves sampling noise,
858   generating the latent variable through the denoising process, and embedding the watermark into
859   the image. Additionally, adversarial attacks are applied with increasing intensity to simulate real-
860
      world conditions and improve robustness. The training loss is minimized by balancing the image
      reconstruction loss and the message extraction accuracy, ensuring that the watermarked images
861
      maintain high quality while the embedded message remains recoverable under various conditions.

      E.1   D ETECTION AND I DENTIFICATION R ESULTS
892
893   In this section, we consider two common tasks in image watermarking: watermark detection and
894   user identification, to investigate the impact of different training distributions on watermarking
895   performance in practical scenarios.
896
897   Watermark Detection. The goal of the watermark detection task is to determine whether specific
898   watermark information exists in an image. Given an image I, the detection process involves decoding
899   and verifying the extracted watermark m′ to assess whether it matches the expected watermark. The
900   key challenge in this task lies in efficiently distinguishing between watermarked and non-watermarked
901   images while maintaining accuracy under various image attack scenarios.
902
903   User Identification. The user identification task not only requires detecting the presence of a
904   watermark in an image but also accurately identifying the embedded watermark message to trace it
905   back to the corresponding user. This task typically involves locating the specific message embedded
906   in an image from a pool of users. As the number of users increases, the complexity of identification
907   rises significantly, especially when the user pool becomes larger.
908   We refer to the evaluation protocols used in Tree-Ring(Wen et al., 2024), WAVE(An et al.), and
909   WaDiff(Min et al., 2024). Specifically, for the watermark detection task, we use 1,000 watermarked
910   images and 1,000 non-watermarked images to compute the area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver
911   operating characteristic (ROC) curve and the True Positive Rate at a False Positive Rate of 0.001%,
912   denoted as T@0.001%F. For the user identification task, we evaluate our method using user pools of
913   varying sizes, ranging from 10,000 to 1,000,000 users. For each user pool, we randomly select 1,000
914
      users and generate 5 images per user, resulting in a total of 5,000 images. Identification accuracy is
      then calculated based on these watermarked images.
915

1048
1049   In fact, lots of research has demonstrated that using classifier guidance significantly affects image
1050   quality and its alignment with the training prompt, depending on the guidance scale (Ho & Salimans,
1051   2022). (Moreover, generation under guidance is fundamentally different from unconditional genera-
1052   tion, the latter often resulting in blurry or content-less outputs.) Therefore, it is counter-intuitive to
1053   assume a direct alignment between free and conditional distributions. In the context of this study, it is
1054   particularly interesting to investigate how the guidance scale influences our distribution visualization
1055   results. Following the setup described in 5.3, we vary only the guidance scale (gs) value and visualize
1056
       the corresponding distributions.
1057   As shown in Figure 6, when gs ≤ 10, there is almost no noticeable difference between the “Free”
1058   and “Test” distributions. However, at gs = 14 and gs = 18, some differences become apparent.
1059   This phenomenon may result from excessively high guidance scales (gs = 14 and gs = 18), which
1060   amplify the guidance signal, causing a certain degree of distributional deviation. On the other hand,
1061   as seen in the guidance scale results of Table 2, when gs changes from 10 to 18, there are slight
1062
       indications that both the FID score and average watermark robustness may degrade. Watermarking
       methods typically aim to achieve Pareto optimality between watermark image quality and robustness.
1063
       In our case, the observed distributional deviation might shift this Pareto frontier (either one of the
1064
       objectives worsens, or both do simultaneously). Nevertheless, even under extreme conditions, such
1065   as gs = 18, the results remain exceptionally good.
1066
1067
1068   Why training with free distribution works? Our method might capture some deeper shared
1069   features between conditional and free/unconditional distributions. For instance, both are derived from
1070   denoising Gaussian noise. While the denoising processes are not identical, they might retain certain
1071   shared features. These features, while meaningless to humans, could be meaningful to the model. As
1072   a result, watermarking modules trained on unconditional distributions can generalize effectively to
1073   conditional distributions or their hybrids.

1107
1108   Detailed Explanation of Lipschitz Continuity Assumption. Lipschitz continuity ensures that the
1109   loss function does not change too abruptly with respect to its inputs, providing a bounded relationship
1110   between changes in predictions and changes in loss. Many widely used loss functions satisfy this
1111   property when their inputs are confined to bounded domains (e.g., image data or binary vectors in our
1112   case). For instance:
1113
1114   • Mean Squared Error (MSE) Loss: Defined as L(y, ŷ) = (ŷ − y)2 , the MSE loss is Lipschitz
1115
         continuous when predictions ŷ are restricted to a closed interval [a, b]. Within this range, the
         gradient ∇L = 2(ŷ − y) remains bounded, ensuring Lipschitz continuity.
1116
1117   • Cross-Entropy Loss: This loss is Lipschitz continuous when prediction probabilities ŷ are confined
1118     to the interval (0, 1), guaranteeing bounded gradients.
1119
       Additionally, activation functions commonly used in neural networks, such as ReLU and Sigmoid, are
1120   inherently Lipschitz continuous. Regularization techniques applied to model weights further help in
1121   preventing the Lipschitz constant K from becoming excessively large. Therefore, the assumption is
1122   not overly restrictive and can be satisfied by carefully selecting the loss functions, model architectures,
1123   and training strategies.
1124
1125   Estimation of the Lipschitz Constant K. Estimating the Lipschitz constant of neural networks
1126   is an active area of research. For instance, Fazlyab et al. proposed a convex optimization-based
1127   approach to efficiently estimate the upper bound of a neural network’s Lipschitz constant (Fazlyab
1128   et al., 2019). Additionally, Latorre et al. employed polynomial optimization techniques to compute
1129   tight upper bounds for K (Latorre et al., 2020). These methods provide both theoretical support and
1130
       practical tools for estimating K.
1131   Empirical methods complement these theoretical approaches by analyzing gradient norms and
1132   employing spectral norm analysis on validation datasets. Regularization techniques, such as weight
1133   decay and spectral normalization Miyato et al. (2018), not only aid in controlling K but also enhance
       the generalization capabilities of the model.
