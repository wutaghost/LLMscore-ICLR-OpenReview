009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reverse-Kullback-Leibler regularization has emerged to be a predominant tech-
012            nique used to enhance policy optimization in reinforcement learning (RL) and
013            reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which forces the learned
014            policy to stay close to a reference policy. While the effectiveness and necessity
015            of KL-regularization have been empirically demonstrated in various practical sce-
016            narios, current theoretical analysis of KL-regularized RLHF still obtains the same
017
               O(1/ϵ2 ) sample complexity as problems without KL-regularization. To under-
               stand the fundamental distinction between policy learning objectives with KL-
018
               regularization and ones without KL-regularization, we are the first to theoretically
019
               demonstrate the power of KL-regularization by providing a sharp analysis for KL-
020            regularized contextual bandits and RLHF, revealing an O(1/ϵ) sample complexity
021            when ϵ is sufficiently small.
022            We further explore the role of data coverage in contextual bandits and RLHF.
023            While the coverage assumption is commonly employed in offline RLHF to link
024            the samples from the reference policy to the optimal policy, often at the cost of
025            a multiplicative dependence on the coverage coefficient, its impact on the sam-
026            ple complexity of online RLHF remains unclear. Previous theoretical analyses of
027            online RLHF typically require explicit exploration and additional structural as-
028            sumptions on the reward function class. In contrast, we show that with sufficient
029            coverage from the reference policy, a simple two-stage mixed sampling strategy
030
               can achieve a sample complexity with only an additive dependence on the cover-
               age coefficient. Our results provide a comprehensive understanding of the roles
031
               of KL-regularization and data coverage in RLHF, shedding light on the design of
032
               more efficient RLHF algorithms.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recently, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a central tool for
038   aligning large language models (LLMs) and diffusion models with human values and preferences
039   (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Rafailov et al.,
040   2024), exhibiting impressive capabilities in applications, such as Chatgpt (Achiam et al., 2023),
041   Claude (Anthropic, 2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and LLaMA-3 (Meta, 2024).
042   RLHF methods treat the language model as a policy that takes a prompt x and produces a response
043   a conditioned on x, and they optimize the policy by aligning it with human feedback. There are
044   mainly two kinds of feedback: absolute rating and preference comparison. For absolute rating, the
045   collection typically involves human annotators to provide rating scores like 1 to 5 (Wang et al.,
046   2024a;b) for responses or hard 0-1 scores for math reasoning tasks since the reasoning tasks often
047   have gold standard answers (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2024b).
048   On the other hand, preference comparison is frequently applied in chat tasks when making com-
049   parisons is much easier for human labeler (Achiam et al., 2023). Although preference feedback is
050   believed to be more intuitive for human users and easier to collect, it also poses more challenges
051   for the RLHF algorithms to effectively leverage the feedback signals since the reward signals are
052   not directly observed. In practice, the learning process typically involves (a) constructing a reward
053   model based on the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) of Bradley-Terry (BT) (Bradley & Terry,
      1952b) model from the preference feedback; (b) applying RL algorithms like PPO (Schulman et al.,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2017b) to train the language model so that it maximizes the reward signals with KL regularization
055   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023). There is also a series of work on direct
056   preference learning algorithms, including Slic (Zhao et al., 2023b), DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024),
057   IPO (Azar et al., 2024), which directly optimize the preference signals to train the language model
058   without constructing the reward model. However, despite their efficiency, these types of algorithms
059   often suffer from overfitting, and DPO suffers from a drop of chosen probability (Song et al., 2024;
060   Liu et al., 2024; Amini et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024).
061   Since human value and preference are so complicated that they are unlikely to be encompassed by
062   the considered preference model classes, the learned model is easy to be hacked and become biased.
063   Practically, the policy may generate disproportionate bold words or emoji to please the learned re-
064   ward (Zhang et al., 2024). Hence, the KL-regularization between the learned policy and a reference
065   policy (the pre-trained model after supervised fine-tuning) plays a fundamental role in RLHF to
066   avoid overfitting. There is a line of RLHF work that realizes the significance of KL-regularization
067   and regards the problem as a reverse-KL regularized contextual bandit (Ziegler et al., 2019; Wu
068   et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024a; Ye et al., 2024b). How-
069
      ever, they adopt the techniques from bandit framework and neglect the characteristic of reverse-
      KL-regularization, thus obtaining almost the same sample complexity with problems without KL-
070
      regularization. Therefore, the question of whether there exists a fundamental distinction between
071
      policy learning objectives with and without KL-regularization is still largely under-explored.
072
073   Compared to the offline RLHF algorithms (Rafailov et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024)
074   that can only use planning to approximate the solution to the relative entropy minimization problem
075
      (Ziebart et al., 2008; Song et al., 2024), online RLHF has been demonstrated to outperform offline
      methods empirically and theoretically (Bai et al., 2023; Meta, 2024; Xiong et al., 2024a; Tajwar
076
      et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), because it has further interactions with human or the
077
      preference oracle. Most standard theoretical online RL techniques apply optimism to balance explo-
078   ration and exploitation (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021). However,
079   it is inefficient to implement exploration for practical RLHF algorithms. Meanwhile, an emerging
080   line of offline RLHF literature highlights the coverage of the reference policy π0 . The coverage of
081   π0 refers to the ability of the model to generate diverse responses for a wide range of prompts. A
082   model with good coverage can generalize well to unseen contexts and actions, which is essential for
083   the learned reward function to also generalize well. In practice, this is evidenced by the fact that the
084   simple best-of-n sampling based on π0 is competitive with the well-tuned PPO algorithm for general
085   open-ended conversation tasks (Dong et al., 2023), and the fact that the π0 can solve a majority of
086
      the math problems with multiple responses (Shao et al., 2024; Nakano et al., 2021). However, the
      theoretical understanding of the role of coverage in online RLHF is still largely understudied. Thus,
087
      it is natural to ask if explicit exploration is necessary for online RLHF with good coverage of π0 and
088
      how the coverage of π0 affects the sample complexity of online RLHF.
089
090   In this paper, we answer the above questions by
091   • providing a novel fine-grained analysis for KL-regularized contextual bandits and RLHF, which
092     adapts to the optimization landscape of the reverse-KL regularization and reveals a sharper sample
093     complexity than the existing results,
094   • proposing an efficient 2-stage mixed sampling strategy for online RLHF with good coverage of π0 ,
095     which achieves sample complexity with only an additive dependence on the coverage coefficient.
096
097   1.1   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
098
099   In this work, we make a first attempt to illustrate the statistical benefits of KL-regularization for
100   policy optimization in contextual bandits and reinforcement learning (RL) from preference feedback.
101   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
102   • In Section 3, we formulate RLHF with absolute-rating feedback as a contextual bandit problem
103     with KL-regularization. First, we provide a lower bound for the KL-regularized contextual bandit
104     problem, which indicates that the sample complexity of the problem is Ω(η log NR (ϵ)/ϵ) when
105     ϵ is sufficiently small, where NR (ϵ) is the covering number of the reward function class and η is
106
        the KL-regularization coefficient.
107   • Then we showcase a novel analysis to upper bound the suboptimality gap of the KL-regularized
        objective in contextual bandits, and propose a simple two-stage mixed sampling strategy for online

        RLHF which achieves a sample complexity of O(max(η 2 D2 , η/ϵ) log NR (ϵ/δ)) when the reward
109     scale is a constant, where D is the coverage coefficient of the reference policy π0 and δ is the

118   1.2   P REVIOUS U NDERSTANDING OF KL-R EGULARIZATION IN RL
119
120   While we mainly focus on the theoretical understanding of KL-regularization in RLHF, it is also
121   worth mentioning that our analysis for KL-regularized contextual bandits also contributes to the
122   theoretical understanding of the impact of KL-regularization in RL since contextual bandits can be
123
      viewed as a simplified version of Markov decision processes (MDPs).
124   In RL, KL-regularization has been widely used to stabilize the learning process and prevent the
125   policy from deviating too far from the reference policy. Here, we provide a brief overview of the
126   existing understanding of KL-regularization in decision-making problems. From the perspective of
127   policy optimization, KL-regularization captures entropy regularization as a special case 1 , which is
128   also an extensively used technique in RL literature (Sutton, 2018; Szepesvári, 2022). There is a
129
      large body of literature that has explored the benefits of entropy regularization or KL-regularization
      in RL (Schulman et al., 2015; Fox et al., 2016; Schulman et al., 2017a; Haarnoja et al., 2017; 2018;
130
      Ahmed et al., 2019). Most related to our work, Ahmed et al. (2019) provided a comprehensive
131
      understanding of the role of entropy regularization in RL, showing that entropy regularization can
132   improve the training efficiency and stability of the policy optimization process by changing the
133   optimization landscape through experiments on continuous control tasks (Brockman, 2016).
134
135
      Theoretically, Neu et al. (2017) provided a unified view of entropy regularization as approximate
      variants of Mirror Descent or Dual Averaging, and left the statistical justification for using entropy
136
      regularization in RL as an open question. Geist et al. (2019) provided a framework for analyzing the
137
      error propagation in regularized MDPs, which also focused on the proof of the convergence for the
138   policy optimization methods with regularization and lacked a sharp sample complexity analysis.

      1.3   OTHER R ELATED W ORK
141
142   Analyses for Policy Optimization with Regularization While it is previously unknown whether
143   regularization can improve the sample complexity of policy optimization without additional assump-
144   tions, there are some works that provided a sharp convergence rate in the presence of regularization
145   (Mei et al., 2020; Shani et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2020; 2021). However, these works either
146   assumed the access of exact or unbiased policy gradient or required uniform value function approx-
147   imation error, which are not the standard case in sample-based RL setting.
148   RLHF Algorithms There are mainly three types of RLHF algorithms: offline, online and hyrbid.
149   The most well-known offline algorithms are Slic (Zhao et al., 2023b), Direct Preference Optimiza-
150   tion (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), Identity-PO (IPO) (Azar et al., 2024) and (SPIN) (Chen et al.,
151   2024). They aim to approximate the closed-form solution of the optimization problem on a fixed
152   offline dataset. For the online algorithms, the most representative one is Proximal Policy Optimiza-
153   tion (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017b). PPO has been used in the Chat-GPT (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini
154   (Team et al., 2023), and Claude (Bai et al., 2022). However, the deep RL method PPO is known to be
155   sample inefficient and unstable, making its success hard to reproduce for the open-source commu-
156
      nity. In response to this, there have been many efforts to propose alternative algorithms to the PPO
      algorithm. The Reward ranked fine-tuning (RAFT) (also known as rejection sampling finetuning)
157
      (Dong et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Gui et al., 2024) is a stable frame-
158
      work requiring minimal hyper-parameter tuning, which iteratively learns from the best-of-n policy
159   (Nakano et al., 2021). This framework proves to be particularly effective in the reasoning task such
160

      as (Gou et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024). However, the RAFT-like algorithms only use the positive
163   signal by imitating the best-of-n sampling. To further improve the efficiency, there is an emerging
164   body of literature that proposes online direct preference optimization by extending DPO or IPO to
165   an online iterative framework (Xiong et al., 2024a; Guo et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Calandriello

173   choose the greedy policy that simply maximizes the reward. After this line of work, Xiong et al.
174   (2024a); Ye et al. (2024b); Song et al. (2024) highlight the KL-regularization theoretically and in-
175   corporate the KL term into the learning objective. However, they circumvent the special advantages
176   of KL-regularization and still follow the techniques in bandit analysis, thus obtaining loose bounds.
177   In our paper, we establish a new lower bound and a sharper upper bound for the KL-regularized
178   framework, thus validating the empirical advantage of KL-regularization. There are also some works
179
      extending KL-regularized RLHF from bandit problems to the Markov decision process (MDP) prob-
      lems (Zhong et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024b). We expect that our techniques can also be extended
180
      to the MDP setting, which we leave for future work.

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532   We have presented a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the role of reverse-KL regularization in
533   decision-making models including contextual bandits and reinforcement learning from preference
534   feedback, highlighting its significance in terms of sample complexity. Our results provide new
535   insights into the power of regularization extending beyond its traditional role of mitigating errors
536
      from the current critic (or reward) model.
537   Additionally, we examined the role of data coverage in both contextual bandits and RLHF. Our
538   analysis shows that with sufficient coverage from the reference policy, a mixed sampling strategy can
539   achieve a sample complexity that exhibits only an additive dependence on the coverage coefficient
      without the need for explicit exploration or additional structural assumptions.
