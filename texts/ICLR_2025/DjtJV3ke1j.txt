007                                               A BSTRACT
008
               A geometric graph associated with a set of points P = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xn } ⊂ Rd
009
               and a fixed kernel function K : Rd × Rd → R≥0 is a complete graph on P such that
010
               the weight of edge (xi , xj ) is K(xi , xj ). We present a fully-dynamic data structure
011            that maintains a spectral sparsifier of a geometric graph under updates that change
012            the locations of points in P one at a time. The update time of our data structure
013            is no(1) with high probability, and the initialization time is n1+o(1) . Under certain
014            assumption, our data structure can be made robust against adaptive adversaries,
015            which makes our sparsifier applicable in iterative optimization algorithms.
016            We further show that the Laplacian matrices corresponding to geometric graphs
017            admit a randomized sketch for maintaining matrix-vector multiplication and projec-
018            tion in no(1) time, under sparse updates to the query vectors, or under modification
019            of points in P .

024   Kernel methods are a fundamental tool in modern data analysis and machine learning, with extensive
025   applications in computer science, from clustering, ranking and classification, to ridge regression ,
026   principal-component analysis and semi-supervised learning von Luxburg (2007); Ng et al. (2002);
027   Zhu (2005a;b); Liu et al. (2019). Given a set of n points in Rd and a nonnegative function K :
028
      Rd × Rd → R≥0 , a kernel matrix has the form that the i, j-th entry in the matrix is K(xi , xj ).
         Kernel matrices and kernel linear-systems naturally arise in modern machine learning and op-
029
      timization tasks, from Kernel PCA and ridge regression Alaoui & Mahoney (2015); Avron et al.
030
      (2017a;b); Lee et al. (2020), to Gaussian-process regression (GPR) Rasmussen & Nickisch (2010)),
031   federated learning Konečnỳ et al. (2016), and the ‘state-space model’ (SSM) in deep learning Gu et al.
032   (2021b;a). In most of these applications, the underlying data points xi are dynamically changing
033   across iterations, either by nature or by design, and therefore computational efficiency of numerical
034   linear-algebraic operations in this setting requires dynamic algorithms to maintain the kernel matrix
035   under insertions and deletions of data points.
036      One motivating application of dynamic linear algebra on geometric graphs is dynamically main-
037   taining a spectral clustering Ng et al. (2002) of the kernel matrix of a weighted graph. In the static
038   setting, a common approach for spectral clustering is spectral sparsification Spielman & Srivastava
039
      (2011); Ng et al. (2002), i.e., to run a spectral clustering algorithm on top of a spectral-sparsifier
      for the Laplacian matrix of the weighted graph. This approach, however, fails to extend to the
040
      dynamic setting, where a small fraction of data points are continually changing, since rebuilding the
041
      spectral-sparsifier is prohibitively expensive – Changing a single point xi ∈ P changes an entire row
042   of K(xi , xj ).

065

066            Given a set of n points P ⊂ Rd and a function K, is there a dynamic algorithm
067            that can update the spectral sparsifier of the geometric graph G on P and K in
068            no(1) time, where in each iteration the location of a point xi ∈ P is changed? Is it
069            possible to maintain approximate matrix-vector queries w.r.t the Laplacian matrix
070

               no(1) time?
071
072      Prior to this work, no nontrivial dynamization algorithms were known for geometric graphs for the
073   above linear-algebra primitives. While fully-dynamic (1 ± ϵ)-spectral edge sparsifiers for general
074   graphs are known Abraham et al. (2016) (in amortized poly(log(n), 1/ϵ) update time), the setting
075
      of geometric graphs is fundamentally different, since as mentioned earlier in the introduction, each
      update of a point xi ∈ P results in an entire row update of K, i.e., O(n) edges, making Abraham
076
      et al. (2016) too slow.
077
078      The main technical contribution of this paper is a new dynamic well separated pair decomposition
079   (WSPD, Definition A.15, Fischer & Har-Peled (2005); Alman et al. (2020)). The core of this data
080   structure is a smooth resampling technique for efficiently maintaining a WSPD under point-location
081   updates, with mild weight-increase to the sparsifier, by reusing randomness (in an adversarially-robust
082   manner).

198   4.1 F ULLY DYNAMIC K ERNEL S PARSIFICATION DATA S TRUCTURE
199
      A geometric graph w.r.t. kernel function K : Rd × Rd → R and points x1 , . . . , xn ∈ Rd is a graph on
200   x1 , . . . , xn where the weight of the edge between xi and xj is K(xi , xj ). An update to a geometric
201   graph occurs when the location of one of these points changes.
202      In a geometric graph, when an update occurs, the weights of O(n) edges change. Therefore,
203   directly applying the existing algorithms for dynamic spectral sparsifiers (Abraham et al. (2016)) to
204   update the geometric graph spectral sparsifier will take Ω(n) time per update. However by using
205   the fact that the points are located in Rd and exploiting the properties of the kernel function, we can
206   achieve faster update.
207      Before presenting our dynamic data structure, we first have a high level idea of the static construc-
208
      tion of the geometric spectral sparsifier, which is presented in Alman et al. (2020).
209   4.1.1 B UILDING B LOCKS OF THE S PARSIFIER
210   In order to construct a spectral sparsifier more efficiently, one can partition the graph into several
211   subgraphs such that the edge weights on each subgraph are close. On each of these subgraphs, leverage
212   score sampling, which is introduced in Spielman & Srivastava (2011) and used for constructing
213   sparsifiers, can be approximated by uniform sampling.
214      For a geometric graph built from a d-dimensional point set P , under the assumption that each
215   edge weight is obtained from a (C, L)-Lipschitz kernel function (Definition A.1), each edge weight
      in the geometric graph is not distorted by a lot from the euclidean distance between the two points


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      (Lemma A.22). Therefore, we can compute this partition efficiently with by finding a well separated
217   pair decomposition (Callahan & Kosaraju (1995), WSPD, Definition A.15) of the given point set.
218      A s-WSPD of P is a collection of pairs (Ai , Bi ) of subsets of P , such that for all a ̸= b ∈ P , there
219   exists a unique i satisfying a ∈ Ai , b ∈ Bi , and the distance between Ai and Bi (as point sets) is at
220   least s times the diameters of Ai and Bi ((Ai , Bi ) is a s-well separated (WS) pair). In this case, the
221   distance between point sets Ai and Bi is a (1 ± 1/s)-multiplicative approximation of the distance
222   between any point in Ai and any point in Bi .
223      Each WS pair in the WSPD can be viewed as an unweighted biclique, where the two point sets
224   are the two sides of the bipartite graph. On an unweighted biclique, uniform random sampling and
225
      leverage score sampling are equivalent. Therefore, a uniformly random sample of the biclique forms
      a spectral sparsifier of the biclique, and the union of the sampled edges from all bicliques form a
226
      spectral sparsifier of the geometric graph.
227
         However, the time needed for constructing a WSPD depends exponentially on the ambient di-
228   mension of the point set and thus WSPD cannot be computed efficiently when the dimension is
229   high. To solve this problem, one can use the ultra low dimensional Johnson Lindenstrauss (JL)
230   projection to project the point set down to k = o(log n) dimension, such that with high probability,
231   the distance distortion (multiplicative difference between the distance between two points and the
232   distance between their low dimensional images) between any pair of points is at most nCjl ·(1/k) ,
233   where Cjl is a universal constant for the JL projection. This distortion becomes an overestimation of
234   the leverage score in the resulting biclique, and can be compensated by sampling nCjl ·(1/k) edges.
235      Then one can perform a 2-WSPD on the k-dimensional points. Since JL projection gives a bijection
236   between the d-dimensional points and their k-dimensional images, a 2-WSPD of the k-dimensional
237   point set gives us a (2 · nCjl /k )-WSPD of the d-dimensional point set P . The d-dimensional bicliques
238   resulted from this (2 · nCjl /k )-WSPD of P is what we use to sample edges and construct the sparsifier.
239      In summary, after receiving a set of points P , we use the ultra-low dimensional JL projection
240   to project these points to a k = o(log n) dimensional space, run a 2-WSPD on the k-dimensional
241
      points, and then map the k-dimensional WSPD result back to the d-dimensional point set to obtain a
242
      (2 · nCjl (1/k) )-WSPD of P . For each pair (A, B) in this d-dimensional WSPD, we randomly sample
      edges from Biclique(A, B). The union of all sampled edges is a spectral sparsifier of the geometric
243
      graph on P .

245   4.1.2 DYNAMIC UPDATE OF THE G EOMETRIC S PECTRAL S PARSIFIER .
246   For a geometric graph build on a point set P , we want the above spectral sparsifier to be able to
247   handle the following update2 :
248
                Point location change (xi ∈ P, z ∈ Rd ): move the point from location xi to
249             location z. This is equivalent to removing point x ∈ P and then adding z to P .
250
         However, in order to update the geometric spectral sparsifier efficiently, there are a few barriers
251
      that we need to overcome.
252
253   Updating WSPD When the point set P changes, we want to update the 2-WSPD such that the
254   number of WS pairs that are changed in the WSPD is small. Fischer & Har-Peled (2005) presented
255   an algorithm to update the list of WS pairs, but it cannot be used directly in this situation, because the
256   Fischer & Har-Peled (2005) algorithm is only able to return a list of WS pairs such that the singleton
257
      containing the inserted (or removed) point is one of the vertex subsets in these pairs. However, in
      order to use the up-to-date WSPD to update the sparsifier, we need to know not only the WS pairs
258
      (A, B) where A or B is a singleton consisting of the inserted (or removed) point, but also all other
259
      WS pairs (A, B) such that the inserted (or removed) point is in A or B. The Fischer & Har-Peled
260   (2005) algorithm is not able to do this.
261      Fortunately, one s-WSPD construction algorithm presented in Har-Peled (2011) has the property
262   that each point x appears only in sO(d) O(log α) WS pairs. This allows us to find all WS pairs
263   affected by a point location change in 2O(k) O(log α) time, since we are maintaining a 2-WSPD and
264   the dimension of the point set is k. We summarize this algorithm below. The detailed discussion of
265   the WSPD update can be found in Section D.4.
266      The Har-Peled (2011) WSPD algorithm constructs a compressed quadtree associated with the point
267   set P , and the WS pairs are pairs of nodes of the compressed quadtree. To summarize, a quadtree

469   5     C ONCLUSION
470
471   In this paper, we presented dynamic algorithms for maintaining geometric graphs efficiently. Our
472
      main contributions include the introduction of the DYNAMIC G EO S PAR data structure and techniques
      for handling adversarial queries and low-dimensional sketches. We demonstrated near-optimal
473
      initialization and update times, significantly improving existing methods. By combining spectral
474
      sparsification and Johnson-Lindenstrauss projections, we ensured efficient recomputation of graph
475   structures with sparse changes. We proved that our data structure can dynamically maintain a (1 ± ϵ)-
476   spectral sparsifier with high probability, leverage JL projections to maintain low-dimensional sketches
477   for efficient updates and queries, and design algorithms that are robust against adaptive adversarial
478   queries. These contributions have significant practical implications for real-time updates in geometric
479   graphs, with potential for broad impact across various domains in computer science.

      address these limitations and extend applicability.

1121      A s-WSPD of P is a collection of well separated (WS) pairs (Ai , Bi ) such that for all a ̸= b ∈ P ,
1122   there is a pair (A, B) satisfying a ∈ A, b ∈ B, and the distance between A and B is at least s times
1123   the diameters of A and B (A and B are s-well separated). Therefore, the distance between A and B
1124
       is a (1 + 1/s)-multiplicative approximation of the distance between any point in A and any point in
       B and each WS pair in the WSPD can be viewed as a unweighted biclique (complete bipartite graph).
1125
       On an unweighted biclique, uniform random sampling and leverage score sampling are equivalent.
1126
       Therefore, a uniformly random sample of the biclique forms a spectral sparsifier of the biclique, and
1127   union of the sampled edges from all bicliques form a spectral sparsifier of the geometric graph.
1128      However, the time needed for constructing a WSPD is exponentially dependent on the ambient
1129   dimension of the point set and thus WSPD cannot be computed efficiently when the dimension is
1130   high. To solve this problem, one can use the ultra low dimensional Johnson Lindenstrauss (JL)
1131   projection to project the point set down to k = o(log n) dimension such that with high probability
1132   the distance distortion (multiplicative difference between the distance between two points and the
1133   distance between their low dimensional images) between any pair of points is at most nCjl ·(1/k) ,
       where Cjl is a constant. This distortion becomes an overestimation of the leverage score in the
