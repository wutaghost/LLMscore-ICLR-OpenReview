009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Frontier Large Language Models (LLMs) can be socially discriminatory or sensi-
012            tive to spurious features of their inputs. Because only well-resourced corporations
013            can train frontier LLMs, we need robust test-time strategies to control such biases.
014            Existing solutions, which instruct the LLM to be fair or robust, rely on the model’s
015            implicit understanding of bias. Causality provides a rich formalism through which
016
               we can be explicit about our debiasing requirements. Yet, as we show, a naive appli-
               cation of the standard causal debiasing strategy, counterfactual data augmentation,
017
               fails under standard assumptions to debias predictions at an individual level at test
018
               time. To address this, we develop a stratified notion of debiasing called stratified
019            invariance, which can capture a range of debiasing requirements from population
020            level to individual level through an additional measurement that stratifies the pre-
021            dictions. We present a complete observational test for stratified invariance. Finally,
022            we introduce a data augmentation strategy that guarantees stratified invariance
023            at test time under suitable assumptions, together with a prompting strategy that
024            encourages stratified invariance in LLMs. We show that our prompting strategy,
025            unlike implicit instructions, consistently reduces the bias of frontier LLMs across
026            a suite of synthetic and real-world benchmarks without requiring additional data,
027
               finetuning or pre-training.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As large language models (LLMs) are used for increasingly high-stakes decision-making (Wu et al.,
032   2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Nay, 2023; Tamkin et al., 2021), it is important that their
033   predictions meet the expectations of users, as well as the aspirations of a fair and just society (Bender
034   et al., 2021; Ganguli et al., 2023). Unfortunately, LLMs will typically mimic the distribution of
035   real-world data, which may be biased relative to the intended use-case or may reflect injustice (Bender
036   et al., 2021). For instance, due to observational biases in its training data, an LLM might unfairly rely
037   on an individual’s gender when predicting their occupation, cf. Figure 1a.
038   Addressing this challenge is not easy. Although models can be debiased at training time (Feder
039   et al., 2023; Mouli et al., 2022), frontier LLMs’ high training costs limit their development, or even
040   finetuning, to a few well-resourced corporations. These issues are aggravated in closed-source models,
041   where the proprietary nature of data and training algorithms makes it difficult to enforce any set of
042   user requirements at training time. Thus, it is critical that we develop test-time solutions, i.e., methods
043   encouraging LLM predictions to meet users’ (or society’s) expectations that do not require pre- or
044
      retraining Bommasani et al. (2021); Tamkin et al. (2023).
045   To date, most test-time attempts to encourage certain expected behaviors in LLMs try to influence
046   the predictions through instructions in static prompts (Tamkin et al., 2023). For instance, Tamkin
047   et al. (2023) prompted the LLM with “Please ensure that your answer is unbiased and does not rely
048   on stereotypes.” (cf. Figure 1b). The challenge with this approach is that it implicitly relies on the
049   LLM’s unknown definitions of the biases associated with a task. This issue is more evident in the
050
      context of fairness, whose definition has been extensively debated across legal (Berk et al., 2021),
      policy (Chouldechova, 2016), and technological (Tamkin et al., 2023) domains.
051
052   We study the problem of test-time fairness and robustness through a causal invariance lens: a
053   framework that studies the stability of predictors under interventions on spurious or protected
      attributes. Unfortunately, the classical notion of invariance —counterfactual invariance— is mostly

      2    T EST-T IME FAIRNESS AND ROBUSTNESS VIA C AUSAL I NVARIANCES
102
      We take a causal perspective to define our notion of fairness and robustness. We are interested
103
      in debiasing predictions with respect to a random context Z ∈ Z, which represents a protected or
104
      spurious characteristic. Crucially, the distribution of other random variables, including our predictions,
105   can depend on this context and our goal is to be robust to changes in Z.
106
107   To model this, we define the potential outcome (PO) of a random variable to be its value when Z is
      intervened on. More precisely, let Ŷ be a prediction that depends on Z. We define the collection of


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      potential predictions Ŷ (z) for z ∈ Z and assume the observed prediction is given by Ŷ := Ŷ (Z),
109   with a slight abuse of notation. More generally, if B(z) is a potential outcome, we define B := B(Z).
110   Throughout, we assume that all random variables are discrete and that Z is finite.
111
112   To define the robustness of a predictor Ŷ , we require that its potential predictions Ŷ (z) satisfy
113   some notion of causal invariance to interventions on z. Counterfactual invariance is one common
                                                                                  a.s.
114   goal (Kusner et al., 2017; Veitch et al., 2021), which requires that Ŷ (z) = Ŷ (z ′ ), ∀z, z ′ ∈ Z. The
115   appeal of counterfactual invariance comes from its focus on individual fairness: we always make the
116
      same prediction if everything else, but the context, is fixed (Kusner et al., 2017; Fawkes & Evans,
      2023). Counterfactual invariance is a very strong requirement and its definition has caused a lot
117
      of misunderstandings in previous literature (Silva, 2024). Instead, other works trying to enforce
118
      conditional independences at training time (Veitch et al., 2021; Rosenblatt & Witter, 2023) induce
119                                                     d
      a population notion of causal invariance Ŷ (z) = Ŷ (z ′ ), ∀z, z ′ ∈ Z, which we call intervention
120
      invariance. This notion requires that, if we were to perform an experiment assigning contexts
121
      independently at random, predictions would have the same distribution across contexts.
122
123   Some train-time methods are able to achieve counterfactual invariance in the predictors they learn, but
124   enforcing counterfactual invariance in a given predictor at test time is considerably more challenging.
125
      Recent works (Feder et al., 2023; Mouli et al., 2022) have used LLMs to perform counterfactual
      data augmentation at training time, which randomly transforms an input X to a potential input
126
      X(z) by changing the context randomly. Under the assumption that one is able to transform X
127
      according to the true conditional, these methods will learn counterfactual-invariant predictors in the
128   limit of infinite data. The reason is that the learning algorithm will get to “see” potential inputs for
129   all exogenous noise realizations and become invariant to the context almost surely. Unfortunately, a
130   naive application of this at test time fails: even if we average out the context for a given test point,
131   we are still vulnerable to variations in exogenous noise and may fail to enforce invariance across
132   different realizations of the process. Therefore, in this section we investigate the questions: (i) What
133   type of invariance can counterfactual data augmentation provide at test time? (ii) Can we measure it?
134   (iii) What adaptations and assumptions in counterfactual data augmentation are needed at test time?
135
136   Stratified invariance. Our key idea is that additional measurements at test time, captured in a random
137   variable S ∈ S, can be used to construct stratified predictors, each of which ensures intervention
138   invariance locally across the context z. One can think of S as a view into the exogenous noise of the
139   system, and for the noise not captured in S we can ensure intervention invariance at best. We call this
140   flexible notion of invariance stratified invariance.
141   Definition 1 (Stratified invariance). Ŷ is S-stratified invariant to the context Z if
142
                  P (Ŷ (z) = y | S = s) = P (Ŷ (z ′ ) = y | S = s) ∀z, z ′ ∈ Z, s ∈ S, y ∈ Y.
143
144   Stratified invariance with particular choices of S appears in works studying counterfactual invariance.
145   As Fawkes & Evans (2023) note, the definition of counterfactual invariance in (Quinzan et al., 2022)
146   is better interpreted as Definition 1. We study Definition 1 in its own right, e.g., providing new
147   results like Lemma 1 and Theorem 1, which we hope will help shed light into these important criteria.
148
      To develop an intuition, consider two special cases. If S := c is a constant random variable, then
149   stratified invariance is just equality in distribution, i.e., intervention invariance. If S := {Ŷ (z)}z∈Z ,
150
      then stratified invariance is almost sure equality, i.e., counterfactual invariance.
151   Stratified invariance has a number of appealing properties: (i) it always implies interventional invari-
152   ance; (ii) if S contains all the randomness generating the prediction it is equivalent to counterfactual
153   invariance; and (iii) if S is an adjustment set (see below for a definition) for (Ŷ , Z) we can provide
154   a complete observational test for it. Therefore, stratified invariance is a middle ground between
155   intervention and counterfactual invariance. If we were to make multiple randomized experiments
156   stratifying S, the distribution of predictions would match across contexts with the same value of S.
157   As we add more variables (randomness) into S, we get finer-grained experimental stratification. If
158
      we add all the randomness, we consider only the same individual in each experiment and we are
      measuring counterfactual invariance.
159
160   To illustrate the concept, Figure 1 shows an example where X is a passage from a person’s biography,
161   Z represents the gender of the person, Y their occupation and S could be defined as the person’s
      occupation, i.e., S := Y .

163   An observational test for stratified-invariant predictions. One of the main difficulties with
164   counterfactual invariance is our inability to test it from observational data (Bareinboim et al., 2020).
165   As for interventional/stratified notions, we are not always able to run interventional experiments
166   to directly observe the potential outcomes Ŷ (z) for a given context z. Here, we derive a complete
167   observational test for stratified invariance using what is classically known as an adjustment set.
168
      Definition 2 (Adjustment set). Given Z ∈ Z and the potential outcomes {B(z)}z∈Z , we say that A
169   is an adjustment set for (B, Z) if A satisfies both (strong) ignorability and positivity assumptions:
170   {B(z)}z∈Z ⊥  ⊥ Z | A, and 0 < pZ|A (z | a) < 1, ∀(z, a) ∈ Z × supp(A) (Rubin, 1978).
171
172   See Appendix B for a discussion and illustration of possible adjustment sets.

      Designing invariant predictors at test time. The stratifying measurement S allows us to transform an
187   existing predictor h into one that is stratified-invariant at test time under some additional assumptions.
188   The key idea is to run a synthetic “randomized experiment” at test time, conditioned on S and the
189   given input X, by generating a new potential input X + = X(Z + ) via a randomized context Z + .
190   Once we have X + , we return the prediction h(X + ). By conditioning on S, we ensure almost sure
191   equality up to the exogenous noise not contained in S.
192

193   predictor h : X → Y. If we assume that we can both (i) exactly recover the context from X(z) given
194   S; and (ii) sample from the conditional distribution of X(z + ) given S and X(z); then the following
195   data augmentation procedure will produce an S-stratified invariant predictor.
196

219   augmentation with LLMs at test time. The core idea of OOC is to use the LLM itself to both (i)
220   simulate the transformations of Definition 3 and (ii) perform predictions over the transformed inputs.
221
222

      asking the model “what would X be if Z had been Z + ?” (Zhang et al., 2024; Feder et al., 2023).
223

264
      scaling laws than the LLM’s implicit fairness and robustness capabilities.

301   Our work is related to a wide variety of existing literature in safety, fairness, and causality. Next, we
302   will provide additional context about the key works related to OOC. Please, refer to Appendix C for a
303   review of prompting strategies.

305
      Other concepts of fairness and robustness. There exists an extensive literature on fairness in
306   machine learning (Barocas et al., 2023; Dwork et al., 2012). Most of the classical works focus on
307
      observational properties of fairness: demographic parity (Ŷ ⊥⊥ Z) (Jiang et al., 2022) and equalized
308
      odds (Ŷ ⊥ ⊥ Z | Y ) (Hardt et al., 2016). When S is an adjustment set, Lemma 1 implies that these
309
      concepts can be recovered from stratified invariance (Ŷ ⊥⊥ Z | S) when S is empty (demographic
310
      parity) or the label (equalized odds). Moreover, there are other continuous definitions of robustness
311
      that are not applicable in language tasks (Tramer & Boneh, 2019).
312
313
314   Counterfactual invariance. There has been a recent interest in studying counterfactual invariance
315
      of predictors, mostly due to fairness reasons (Kusner et al., 2017). The work of Veitch et al. (2021)
      is the first to formulate the almost sure equality requirement as we state, but we note that it is
316
      equivalent to counterfactual fairness as originally stated in Kusner et al. (2017); Fawkes & Evans
317
      (2023). As we note in Section 2, existing works that enforce conditional independence at training
318   time (Veitch et al., 2021; Rosenblatt & Witter, 2023) are not imposing counterfactual, but rather
319   stratified invariance. In fact, this was already pointed by Veitch et al. (2021), where the authors
320   acknowledge that counterfactual invariance implies conditional independence, but the other direction
321   is not valid in general. Finally, we note that Plecko & Bareinboim (2022) provides a rich literature on
322   graphical conditions for counterfactual invariance. Although this cannot be directly used to enforce
323   invariances at test time, it is a useful tool to measure it under (mostly restrictive) causal model
      assumptions.

325   Counterfactual data augmentation in text classification. The fairness and robustness solution
326   inspiring OOC is counterfactual data augmentation (Sauer & Geiger, 2021; Lu et al., 2020; Feder
327   et al., 2023). The main difference between OOC and previous works leveraging counterfactual
328   transformations is that OOC performs it at test time. Existing literature, such as Mouli et al. (2022),
329   is interested in applying counterfactual transformations as augmentations during the model training.
330   In this context, the recent work of Feder et al. (2023) is the most similar to ours. As we mention
331   in Section 2, the main difference with OOC is that, at test time, these procedures do not guarantee
332   counterfactual, or even stratified, invariance. OOC can be seen as an adaptation of these ideas to test
333
      time leveraging Definition 3 and Theorem 1.
334   Fairness and robustness in LLMs. Previous works in fairness and LLMs focus on one or two
335   of the following: (i) characterizing existing biases and discrimination in frontier LLMs (Bender
336   et al., 2021; Ganguli et al., 2023; Tamkin et al., 2023); and (ii) works designing safety instructions
337   to reduce such problems Schick et al. (2021); Tamkin et al. (2023); Ganguli et al. (2023); Si et al.
338   (2022). Our work is motivated by the findings in (i) and fundamentally differs from (ii) in its solution:
339
      instead of designing prompts that explore the model’s implicit notions of biases, we leverage the
      user’s causal knowledge of the task to design a prompting strategy explicitly enforcing a known, user-
340
      specified, causal invariance property. More recently, Li et al. (2024) proposed to mitigate selection
341
      bias by using in-context examples generated without the protected attributes. Unlike OOC, the work
342   requires the user’s ability to intervene in the data-generating process —an often limiting setting
343   with real-world data. Moreover, we highlight that there are works focusing on the characterization
344   of robustness/sensitivity of LLMs, but they mostly focus on sensitivity to prompts (Sclar et al.,
345   2023; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Lu et al., 2021), while offering task- and context-specific
346   solutions (Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Sharma et al., 2023).

349
350
      We conduct a broad set of experiments to study OOC’s zero-shot ability to boost stratified invariance
351
      in LLM predictions at test time. Concretely, we focus on answering four questions: (i) Can OOC
352   boost stratified invariance in real-world tasks? cf. Section 5.1; (ii) Does it retain the predictive
353   performance of LLMs? cf. Section 5.1; (iii) How does OOC interact with scale (model size)? cf.
354   Appendix E; (iv) How does stratification impact individual guarantees, i.e., counterfactual invariance,
355   in OOC? cf. Section 5.2.

532   6     C ONCLUSIONS
533
      We developed stratified invariance as a central notion of fairness and robustness in LLMs at test
534   time. We showed how, unlike counterfactual invariance, it possesses a complete observational test
535   and a valid counterfactual data augmentation procedure at test time. We then proposed to implement
536   stratified counterfactual data augmentation with Out-Of-Context (OOC) prompting. Our empirical
537   results demonstrated that OOC improves stratified invariance of LLM predictions in real-world tasks
538   across models of different families and sizes. Finally, we empirically showed that OOC reflects
539   a crucial theoretical notion of stratified invariance: as we condition on more exogenous variables
      generating X, we are more fair and robust at an individual (counterfactual invariance) level.

822   B    C HOOSING S AS AN ADJUSTMENT SET
823
824   How can we determine S such that it is an adjustment set? It is known that, unless we make
825   other assumptions or control the data-generating process, we cannot empirically test the validity
826   of adjustment sets (Bareinboim et al., 2020). In practice, they are taken as causal assumptions
827   from the user (Shpitser et al., 2012). In this context, it becomes useful to define S with graphical
828   representations of causal models Pearl (2009). A causal DAG represents the known, or assumed,
829   causal relationships between the variables of interest in our task. For instance, in Figure 5 (b,c) we
830   can see DAGs where our input X is generated from the context Z and an independent, usually hidden,
831
      part U . In the anti-causal DAG (a) X is also generated from the response variable Y , while in (b) and
      (c) Y is generated from X. Moreover, all three DAGs contain some kind of unknown, non-causal,
832
      association between Y and the context Z. This spurious dependence can come from an unobserved
833
      confounder (b), an observed collider (c), or either (a). We can identify S in the DAGs of Figure 5
834   using the back-door criterion (Pearl, 2009). In the anti-causal DAG (a), we see that S can be defined
835   as Y , since it blocks the only non-causal path from Z to X. Now, for the DAG (b), we note that Y
836   is a non-observed collider, and therefore it already blocks the existing non-causal path, i.e., S := ∅.
837   As for (c), the collider B is observed, therefore the non-causal path is unblocked and we need to
838   condition on Y , i.e., S := Y .

      Prompting strategies for LLMs. The impact of prompt design techniques significantly increased
      with the in-context learning capabilities presented in GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020). Since then, works
844
      have shown remarkable results when designing general techniques to improve the performance of
845
      LLMs. The most representative case is the one of zero-shot Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al.,
846   2022): induce an intermediate reasoning step with “Let’s think step by step” and get a drastic
847   improvement in the model’s performance. OOC prompting aims to be to fairness and robustness what
848   CoT is to performance, i.e., a simple and yet powerful technique that boosts fairness and robustness
849   in LLMs. Other relevant prompting algorithms that are not zero-shot but also focus on improving
850   the model’s performance are automatic prompt tuning methods, e.g., DLN (Sordoni et al., 2023),
851   APE (Sordoni et al., 2023), and other sophisticated in-context learning approaches (Lu et al., 2021;
852   Liu et al., 2021). Our method is different from theses classes of prompting algorithms in that i) we
853   are zero-shot and ii) we are not interested in boosting the model’s performance, but in boosting its
854
      fairness and robustness. Finally, we note that prompting techniques for tasks related to ours, such as
      diversity in generation (Lahoti et al., 2023) and moral reasoning (Ma et al., 2023) have been recently
855
      proposed. The work of Ma et al. (2023) is the closest to OOC since, in the same flavor of OOC, the
856
      authors also induce counterfactual generation as an intermediate step. However, the counterfactual
857   generation is done for a different purpose and in a different manner, i.e., the authors explicitly ask
858   for a counterfactual, instead of directly implementing the stratified counterfactual data augmentation
859   from Definition 3.
