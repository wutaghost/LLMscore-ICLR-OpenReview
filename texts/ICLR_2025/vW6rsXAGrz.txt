009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            High-cardinality categorical features are a common characteristic of mixed-type
012            tabular datasets. Existing generative model architectures struggle to learn the com-
013            plexities of such data at scale, primarily due to the difficulty of parameterizing the
014            categorical features. In this paper, we present a general variational autoencoder
015            model, CardiCat, that can accurately fit imbalanced high-cardinality and hetero-
016            geneous tabular data. Our method substitutes one-hot encoding with regularized
017            dual encoder-decoder embedding layers, which are jointly learned. This approach
018
               enables us to use embeddings that depend also on the other covariates, leading to
               a compact and homogenized parameterization of categorical features. Our model
019
               employs a considerably smaller trainable parameter space than competing meth-
020
               ods, enabling learning at a large scale. CardiCat generates high-quality synthetic
021            data that better represent high-cardinality and imbalanced features compared to
022            competing VAE models for multiple real and simulated datasets.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Recent years have seen dramatic improvement in the generative modeling of complicated stimulus
028   including images, audio and most recently natural languages. Generative models characterize the
029   joint distribution of the variables, and allow sampling from this distribution. They can therefore be
030   used for imputing missing values, regenerating data while hiding sensitive information, and detect-
031   ing anomalies. Variational Auto Encoders (VAEs, (Kingma & Welling, 2013)) are generative models
032   composed of two neural-networks: the encoder transforms a data example into a distribution on the
033
      p-dimensional latent space, and the decoder transforms a vector sampled from the latent space into
      a data example. The sampling increases the smoothness of the decoder, and the result is often more
034
      interpretable and better reflects the diversity of the original data.
035
036   Generative models and VAEs in particular are most successful when the data they are trained on
037   is homogeneous, meaning that the individual features are similarly distributed, and there are local
038   structures governing the interaction between features (e.g. pixels or words) (Ma et al., 2020; Suzuki
039
      & Matsuo, 2022). In contrast, generative models still struggle when modeling one of the most com-
      mon types of datasets, the tabular data (Nazabal et al., 2020). In tabular datasets, each example can
040
      be comprised of a set of data-fields from different types (mixed types), including numerical, inte-
041
      ger, and character strings. In addition, the features can exhibit complex dependencies, but the order
042   or topology is often arbitrary and provides little information regarding this structure (in contrast to
043   homogeneous signals such as images or language). Prominent examples include electronic medical
044   records (EMR), personal credit default, e-commerce and behavioral datasets from social networks.
045
      High cardinality categorical features is another pervasive characteristic of mixed-type tabular data.
046
      The cardinality of some categorical features, meaning the number of possible unique values, can be
047
      extremely high, with severe imbalances between the values (Xu & Veeramachaneni, 2018). Such
048   high-cardinality categorical features are often very informative - consider the information on a pa-
049   tient contained in the in features such as ”diagnosis”, ”occupation” or ”city / state”. For a generative
050   model to learn the behavior of a high-cardinality feature, the interplay between different values of
051   the feature need to be identified. Often, the decoders prefer not to guess rare values, and this leads
052   to a collapse in the marginal distributions towards the common categories. More technically, cur-
053   rent models usually require categorical features to be initially parameterized by one-hot encoding
      or string similarity encoding such as in Cerda & Varoquaux (2020), which relies on the information

054
      between pairs of categories. Yet, one-hot encoding high-cardinality features have many detrimental
055   effects on neural networks (Rodrı́guez et al., 2018; Avanzi et al., 2024). One-hot encoding forces a
056   flat label space (where each value is equally different), which might disregard the complex relation-
057   ships that exist amongst the values within a categorical variable. One-hot encoding also dramatically
058   increases the dimension of inner layers of the network, leading to poor statistical properties, memory
059   constraints and a strain on the optimization process (Choong & Lee, 2017).

086   We propose a VAE-based framework for the modeling and synthesis of tabular data; one that can
087
      efficiently represent high-cardinality categorical features in the context of mixed feature types. Our
      framework, CardiCat, adds embedding layers for the high-cardinality categorical features. The
088
      learned embedding layers are low-dimensional numeric vector representations of the values of the
089
      categorical features. CardiCat’s embedding layers are efficiently learned in tandem as part of the
090   complete encoder-decoder network optimization. Therefore, the topology of the embedding layer
091   can be influenced by the the joint-distribution of the categorical features and the other features in the
092   data. A key innovation is that the decoded output (and likelihood) of these features is evaluated in
093   the smooth and homogenized embedding space rather than in the observed categorical space. This
094   allows us to avoid all together the need to one-hot encode the non-binary categorical features at any
095   point in the process, thus reducing the number of trainable parameters significantly, and feeding the
096   network more homogenized data. As we show in our experiments, this encoding allows the VAE to
097   better recover the marginal and joint distributions of categorical features (see Figure 1).
098   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We propose a method to introduce regularized
099   categorical embedding into VAEs in a way that efficiently homogenizes categorical features and
100   prevents embedding layers collapse. We show that our method’s capacity to represent and accurately
101   reconstruct the marginal and multivariate trends of the data surpasses that of previous models. In
102   addition, it requires considerably fewer learned parameters; (2) We develop a public benchmarking
103   framework for tabular VAEs composed of simulated and real-life tabular datasets emphasizing high-
104
      cardinality categorical features; And (3) We make available an open-source implementation of the
      regularized embeddings architecture that can easily be adapted to different VAE frameworks.
105
106   The use of embedding layers to represent categorical features in neural networks is not new. How-
107   ever, to our knowledge, this is the first public and benchmarked end-to-end tabular generative model
      that uses dual-architecture regularized embedding layers for parameterizing categorical features.

109
110
      This section provides a brief overview of VAEs and their shortcomings as they relate to heteroge-
111
      neous data. It then discusses the main strategies for accommodating diverse data types.
112
113
114   2.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
115
      Variational autoencoders are deep latent-variable generative models that are based on autoencoders
116
      (AE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013). Latent-variable models assume that each data-point x ∈ X is
117
      generated from a true, latent distribution pθ (z) of some unknown random real-valued vector z of
118   dimension a. VAEs are different from autoencoders in their ability to learn the generating distri-
119   bution of the data. In contrast to AE, the VAE encoder outputs a set of parameters that defines the
120   latent distribution. In addition, VAEs impose regularization constraints on the latent distribution.
121   This forces the latent distribution to be as close as possible to a predefined prior distribution. Each
122   random value of the latent vector z that is fed through the decoder should produce a meaningful
123   output. Variational Bayesian inference is used to derive a tractable lower-bound on the likelihood of
124   the data, which can be maximized by gradient optimization:
125
                              log pθ (x) ≥ Ez [log pθ (x|z)] − Dkl (qϕ (z|x)|pθ (z))
126
127   The right hand side is called the evidence lower bound (ELBO) of the log likelihood of the data
128   log pθ (x). The ELBO is comprised of two terms, the reconstruction term Ez [log pθ (x|z)] and the
129   KL-divergence term Dkl (qϕ (z|x)|pθ (z)) that acts as a regularizer. The reconstruction term describes
130   how well the generated output resembles the input data.
131
132   2.2   A DAPTIONS OF VAE S FOR TABULAR DATA
133
134   In mixed-type tabular data, the features of each datapoint include both categorical and numerical
135   features. Modeling mixed tabular data is often difficult for VAEs due to the heterogeneous mixed-
136   type nature of the features. The model must be flexible enough to incorporate simultaneous learning
137   of discrete and numerical features with different distribution characteristics.
138   In adapting VAEs to heterogeneous tabular data, we can identify several approaches:
139
140   Accommodating prior: When learning VAEs for complex and high-dimensional data, the standard-
141
      normal VAE prior is often (1) too strong and over-regularizes the encoder, and (2) not expressive
      enough to represent well the underlying structure of the data. This can lead to the problem of
142
      posterior collapse: when the encoder fails to distill useful information from x into the variational
143
      parameters of the posterior. In addition, the standard normal distribution assumes data points are
144   centered around zero with a standard deviation of one, which is not a good fit for mixed-type tabular
145   data. The use of more flexible priors, such as Gaussian mixture model (GMM) priors with com-
146   ponents that are learned through back-propagation, can aid in the avoidance of over-regularization
147   (Tomczak & Welling, 2018; Guo et al., 2020; Apellániz et al., 2024).
148
      Type-specific likelihoods: This approach (implemented in RVAE and HI-VAE (Akrami et al., 2020;
149
      Nazabal et al., 2020)) deals with the heterogeneity by providing each feature type (e.g. real-valued,
150   categorical) with a different likelihood model. For example, on categorical features the decoder
151   network may output a vector of probabilities and the loss would be based on the cross-entropy
152   score. Note that in recent empirical studies HI-VAE has failed to recover the observed marginal
153   distributions as well as failed to surpass competing methods (Ma et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2021).
154
      Type homogenization: An alternative approach is to transform all input variables into Gaussian
155
      variables to produce a homogeneous VAE. In VAEM(Ma et al., 2020), the authors propose a two-
156   stage structure that avoids using type-specific likelihoods altogether. In the first stage individual
157   features are homogenized by learning an independent VAE for each feature. Then, the separately
158   learned factorized latent variables from the first stage models are used as inputs to a second stage
159   VAE. However, projecting categorical features into real-values without relying on other covariates
160   may result in subpar results (see Section 4). Focusing only on numerical data, Xu et al. (2019) tVAE
161   applies a mode-specific normalization using a variational Gaussian-mixture model (VGM) for each
      numerical feature.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      A conditional generator model is used to generate samples that are conditioned on some specific
163   values of the data or its associated labels. This is done by adding the condition to the encoder and
164   the decoder’s input so it can be better represented in the latent space and provide more control over
165   the generated output (Sohn et al., 2015). Conditional generators for VAEs are often used to add
166   categorical label information (as the conditioned label) to the homogeneous latent space (Mishra
167   et al., 2018; Lim et al., 2018). For tabular data with multiple discrete features, there are multiple
168   ways to set up the conditional generators.
169   All these extensions and adaptations strive to alleviate the VAE’s sensitivity to fail under complex,
170   multi-dimensional, non-normal data. However, they do not provide a sufficient solution for dealing
171   with high-cardinality heterogeneous tabular data. High-cardinality features exacerbate the hetero-
172   geneity of the input data. This is due to the need to one-hot encode features as long binary vectors.
173   Furthermore, one-hot inflates the space of the network’s trainable parameters, which places a heavy
174   burden on the computational resources needed to train the model. Finally, because high-cardinality
175   features are likely to be imbalanced, their minor values bear relatively small effect on the network’s
176   optimization regime, resulting in vanishing estimated marginal probability. Therefore, improving
177
      and adapting tabular VAEs to work with high-cardinality heterogeneous data can have a drastic ef-
      fect on their synthetic data generation abilities. In this work we focus on type homogenization and
178
      propose an embedding-based VAE framework that aims to homogenize both the categorical features
179
      and the likelihood models.

182   3     T HE C ARDI C AT MODEL
183
184   Our model deals with non-binary categorical features by embedding them into a low dimensional
185   space using information from the entire encoder-decoder neural network. CardiCat deviates away
186   from a traditional VAE by (a) substituting one-hot categorical encodings with embedding presen-
187
      tations throughout the network and its loss, and (b) by adding a loss regularization term to prevent
      embedding-collapse. These learned embeddings can be thought of as smoothed-out categorical pa-
188
      rameterizations that are learned from the network’s reconstruction loss. This allows us to efficiently
189
      parameterize discrete features in a self-learned mechanism that depends on the embedding space
190   and not on the original features. Our framework is implemented on a simple VAE architecture in
191   order to cleanly demonstrate the advantages of such framework.
192
193
      3.1   N OTATIONS FOR CATEGORICAL FEATURES AND EMBEDDINGS
194
195   Consider a mixed-type tabular dataset D = {x}i=1,..,n , where each of the n datapoints xi =
196   (xi,1 , . . . , xi,m ) is a vector of m features, some numerical and some categorical. We denote the
197   j’th feature vector xj , and xj when we consider it a random variable. Let H ⊆ {1, ..., m} = [m] be
198   the set of categorical features, marking any discrete feature with more than two values. For simplic-
199   ity, the domain of a categorical feature xj with cardinality cj is identified with [cj ]. We denote the
200   categorical distribution of the xj by Cat(π1 , ..., πcj ), meaning that P (xj = ℓ) = πℓ for ℓ ∈ [cj ].
201   We equip each categorical feature xj with an embedding, a learned mapping from a categorical value
202   to a real-valued vector embj : [cj ] → Rkj . The embedding dimension kj is often chosen such that
203   kj << cj . The embedding can be represented using matrix ej = (ej,1 , ..., ej,cj )′ . E represents the
204   set of all embeddings parameters E = {ej ; j ∈ H}.
205
206   3.2   C ARDI C AT ’ S DUAL ENCODER - DECODER EMBEDDINGS
207
208   In a traditional neural network with embedding layers, the embeddings are learned from back-
209   propagating the loss gradients throughout the network. For example, in supervised learning, where
210   each sample consists of a feature vector and target label pair (xi , yi ), the embedding layers are
211   learned using the network’s back-propagation gradients from L(ŷi , yi ). The resulting embedding
212
      geometry codes the relationship between different categorical values and the target.
213   Generative model architectures such as VAE require a different solution for integrating unsupervised
214   embeddings. Because the network’s decoder is tasked with generating the encoder’s inputs, the
215   embedding layers must be also represented as part of the decoder’s output. Therefore, CardiCat
      employs dual encoder-decoder embedding layers architecture where the embeddings appear both as


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      trainable layers in the encoder, and in the output of the decoder where they actively participate in
217   calculating the loss (instead of the original features). At each propagation step, the decoder tries
218   to reconstruct the embedding vectors, and any deviations from the expected embeddings will be
219   penalized by the ELBO. The reduced dimension space is therefore learned by back-propagating the
220   gradients of the ELBO through both the decoder’s and the encoder’s embedding weights.
221

251   3.4      M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
252
253   Figure 2 provides an overview of the CardiCat architecture. The input of the encoder is separated
254   into data types: binary features xl , l ∈ L, categorical features xh , h ∈ H, and numerical features
255   xm , m ∈ M . Binary features are one-hot encoded, while categorical features (cj ≥ 3) are encoded
256
      to a reduced embedding space of size kj (hyper-parameter). Using the parameterization trick, the
      encoder qφ (zi |xi ) outputs zi ∼ Na (µ, diag(σ)) ∈ Ra . The decoder outputs data types identical
257
      to the encoder’s input
                          Q so that loss can be calculated by assuming a factorized Gaussian likelihood
258
      model p(xi |zi ) = j pj (xi,j |zi )1 .
259
260   This suggested architecture was chosen for the following reasons. First, the VAE embeddings pro-
261   vide a natural homogenized parameterization of otherwise difficult to learn parameterizations such
262   as one-hot encoding. Second, the construction of each embedding space depends on the entirety
263   of data, promoting the further sharing of information throughout the VAE network. This is due to
264
      the fact that the presentations are learned and depend on the network-wide optimization and loss
      function. In addition, the geometries of the learned embedding spaces can reveal by analyzing the
265
      distance between different values of each category. Finally, avoiding one-hot encoding and instead
266
      using end-to-end embedding layers provides a meaningful reduction in the number of parameters
267   needed to train tabular VAEs. In the next chapter we show that these adaptations offer significant
268   advantages over other VAE architectures for learning high-cardinality large-scale tabular data.

415   5    D ISCUSSION
416
417
      This work attempts to bridge the gap that currently exists with learning high-cardinality tabular
418
      data using variational autoencoders. While this type of data is becoming more prevalent, current
419
      mixed-type tabular VAE models fail to adequately model and learn high-cardinality features. The
420   CardiCat architecture we propose homogenizes the high-cardinality categorical features through
421   their embedding parameterization. The regularized embeddings are learned as part of the VAE
422   training. From our benchmarks, we show that our model performs significantly better and is able
423   to produce high quality synthetic data compared to other VAE models of comparable size. Our
424   implementation is open-source, and can easily be extended by others.
425
      We note the following limitations of this work. First, the network architecture we use is basic, and
426   neither architecture nor training parameters have been optimized for individual datasets. Though
427   this experimental design choice is deliberate, there is a chance that gains observed here would not
428   carry over to much more sophisticated architectures. Nevertheless, the ideas and code can be easily
429   adapted to additional scenarios. For example, combining a refined model such as the mixture mod-
430   eling of tVAE should further improve the recovery of numerical features. Second, we evaluate the
431   joint distribution recovery by looking at marginal and pair interactions; we leave downstream effects
      on supervised learning and interpretation of the learned embeddings for later work.
