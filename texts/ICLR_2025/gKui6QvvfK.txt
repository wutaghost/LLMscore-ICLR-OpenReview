009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Compositional diffusion and energy-based models have driven progress in control-
012            lable image generation, however the challenge of composing discrete generative
013            models has remained open, holding the potential for improvements in efficiency,
014            interpretability and generation quality. To this end, we propose a framework for
015            controllable conditional generation of images. We formulate a process for compos-
016            ing discrete generation processes, enabling generation with an arbitrary number of
017            input conditions without the need for any specialised training objective. We adapt
018
               this result for parallel token prediction with masked generative transformers, en-
               abling accurate and efficient conditional sampling from the discrete latent space of
019
               VQ models. In particular, our method attains an average error rate of 19.3% across
020
               nine experiments spanning three datasets (between one and three input conditions
021            for each dataset), representing an average 63.4% reduction in error rate relative to
022            the previous state-of-the-art. Our method also outperforms the next-best approach
023            (ranked by error rate) in terms of FID in seven out of nine settings, with an average
024            FID of 24.23, and average improvement of −9.58. Furthermore, our method offers
025            a 2.3× to 12× speedup over comparable methods. We find that our method can
026            generalise to combinations of input conditions that lie outside the training data
027            (e.g. more objects per image for Positional CLEVR) in addition to offering an
028            interpretable dimension of controllability via concept weighting. Outside of the
029
               rigorous quantitative settings, we further demonstrate that our approach can be
               readily applied to an open pre-trained discrete text-to-image model, demonstrating
030
               fine-grained control of text-to-image generation. The accuracy and efficiency of
031
               our framework across diverse conditional image generation settings reinforces its
032            theoretical foundations, while opening up practical avenues for future work in
033            controllable and composable image generation.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Compositional generalisation in deep learning is the capacity of a trained model to respond correctly
039   to unfamiliar combinations of familiar concepts (1). The ability of deep learning models to perform
040   compositional generalisation is considered to be a pre-requisite for human-like artificial general
041   intelligence (2), since compositional generalisation is something humans do remarkably well with
042   relatively few training examples (3). Successful compositional generalisation remains an ongoing
043   challenge in conditional image generation (4), which involves sampling from very high-dimensional
044
      spaces and is the focus of this work.
045   In the area of diffusion-based and energy-based image generation approaches, earlier works (5; 6; 7)
046   have proposed methods for improving controllability of image generation via composition of energy-
047   based and diffusion models. This family of methods enables conjunction and negation of input
048   concepts by composing the probabilistic outputs of several feed-forward operations, each supplied
049   with a different input condition. While composed techniques exceed non-composed baselines in
050
      terms of accuracy and image quality (7), such approaches do not extend to image generation models
      with discrete sample spaces, which offer a number of trade-offs and outright improvements over their
051
      continuous counterparts (8; 9; 10; 11).
052
053   Discrete image generation methods include autoregressive sampling approaches (9), and more recently
      discrete absorbing diffusion (10; 12; 11; 13) (also referred to as“masked generative transformers”)

135   2     R ELATED W ORK
136
137   Product of experts: The concept of compositional generalisation of discrete outputs relates directly
138   to earlier approaches to classification, in particular the "product of experts" (PoE) framework (16).
139   PoE combines multiple probabilistic models operating on the same input space, leveraging each
140   of their individual “expertise” to improve overall generalisation performance. This approach has
141
      been shown to be particularly powerful in contexts involving high-dimensional inputs (16), in which
      each “expert” focuses on a subset of constraints, with the final outputs assigning high probability
142
      to outputs that satisfy constraints imposed by all experts. Our work seeks to adapt this idea to the
143
      compositional generation of images in discrete spaces, and is the first (to our knowledge) to apply
144   this idea to iterative generative processes over multiple successive time-steps.
145
146   Continuous compositional models: Earlier works proposed methods for conditioning energy-
147
      based (6; 5) and diffusion models (7) respectively based on the conjunction and negation of input
      attributes, drawing on formal analogues to PoE (16). (7) introduces an approach that enhances the
148
      capabilities of text-conditioned diffusion models in generating complex and photo-realistic images
149
      based on textual descriptions. Our method draws inspiration from these ideas idea (principally
150   in proposing formal derivations of probabilistic conjunction and negation operators), however our
151   mathematical formulation diverges significantly due to the fact that our method applies to discrete
152   iterative approaches (compare with EBM and diffusion, which operate in a continuous output space
153   (14; 15)). The novelty of our derivation, and its application to discrete generation go beyond the
154   literature by offering a novel formulation for composing iterative discrete generation models, while
155   empirically achieving state-of-the-art in image generation accuracy while attaining competitive FID
156   scores. All compositional approaches to date, our own included, somewhat resemble the mathematical
157   form of classifier-free guidance (CFG) (17), which is effective in obtaining extra dimension of
158
      controllability over the outputs of both continuous and discrete diffusion models (13).
159   Composition in sequential tasks: Earlier work in reinforcement learning (18) has sought to use
160   ideas relevant to our own for composing policies for the purposes of compositional generalisation in
161   multi-timestep environments. The main idea shared with our work is that of multiplying constituent
      “primitives” (as opposed to additive composition, as in mixture-of-experts (19)). According to


                                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (18), the benefit of this multiplicative approach over mixture-of-experts is that multiple policies
163   can be expressed in the same sequence. This shares some superficial similarities with our work on
164   compositional discrete image generation, where multiple attributes are required to be expressed in the
165   same output image, however it differs significantly in both the application and the formalism.
166
      Discrete representation learning and sampling: Discrete representation learning has emerged as
167
      the discrete counterpart to continuous VAE approaches (8; 20). Discrete representation learning is
168
      based on the concept of vector-quantization (VQ) (21), whereby features from a continuous vector
169   space are mapped to an element of a finite set of learned codebook vectors. This VQ family of models
170   includes VQ-VAE (8) and VQ-GAN (9). VQ-family approaches require a secondary prior model to be
171   trained to sample from the discrete latent space, which can be computationally expensive at both train-
172   and inference- time (8; 9; 10). A more recent sampling approach aims to address this with parallel
173   token prediction using a transformer encoder (10; 11; 13) (akin to masked language modelling (22)),
174   which introduces a controllable trade-off between sample speed and generation quality, as well as
175   the ability to control the diversity of outputs via temperature. The per-image generation time is still
176   linear in the size (W × H) of the image, albeit with a smaller constant than autoregressive models
177
      (10). The advantages of parallel token prediction over autoregressive approaches motivate our work
      in producing a method for composing discrete image sampling approaches.

180   3     M ETHOD
181
182   In this section we present our method for composing generative models for controllable sampling
183   from discrete representation spaces of images. We first derive a novel formulation that directly
184
      informs our method of composing conditional distributions over discrete spaces. Next we show how
      this extends generally to all discrete sequential generation tasks, in which categorical variables are
185
      sampled iteratively to produce a complete sample (e.g. via autoregressive or masked models). We
186
      show how this result can be specifically adapted for conditional parallel token prediction (10; 11) to
187   achieve high-quality and accurate controllable image synthesis. This is further enhanced by concept
188   weighting, which allows the relative importance of input conditions to be increased, decreased or
189   negated to the desired effect. We note that similar results can be obtained for other types of generative
190   model (provided they are iterative and discrete, see Appendix D.3 for example). This section lays the
191   groundwork for our later experiments with compositional sampling from the latent space of VQ-VAE
192   and VQ-GAN.
193

197

240   3.3   C OMPOSED PARALLEL TOKEN PREDICTION
241
      Parallel token prediction (10; 12; 11) is a non-autoregressive alternative to next-token prediction (9)
242
      for generative sampling from a discrete latent space. This allows a direct trade-off between sampling
243   speed and image generation quality (10) by controlling the rate at which tokens are sampled. Parallel
244   token prediction can be thought of as the gradual un-masking of a collection of discrete latent codes

246   information). This corresponds directly to the next-state prediction formulation in (3), but with the
247   time labels t reversed in order to reflect the “reverse process” which characterizes diffusion models

290   3.5     D ISCRETE ENCODING AND DECODING OF IMAGES
291
292   In order to compose categorical distributions for generating images, we must also define an invert-
293   ible mapping between RGB images and discrete latent representations. We utilise a convolutional
294
      down-sampling and up-sampling (autoencoder) to map between RGB image space and latent embed-
      ding space. Following the original VQ-VAE (8) formulation, we employ nearest-neighbour vector
295
      quantization, in which encoder outputs are mapped to their nearest neighbour in a learned codebook.
296
      Specifically, for each encoder output vector ze , the corresponding quantized vector is computed as
297   the nearest codebook entry ec , where
298
299                                               c = arg min ||ze − ej ||2                                             (7)
                                                              j
300
      and e0 , e1 ...eK−1 are entries in a learned vector codebook of length K.
301
302   Since the quantization step is non-differentiable, it is necessary to estimate the gradients during
303   backpropagation. For this purpose, straight-through gradient estimation (28) is used, whereby during
304   backpropagation the gradients are copied directly from the decoder input zc to the encoder output ze .
305   We use this vector quantization approach for all our experiments, which includes the embedding and
306
      commitment loss terms from the original VQ-VAE formulation (8).

      4.3    Q UANTITATIVE EVALUATION OF COMPOSITIONAL GENERATION
358
      For each dataset, following (7) we evaluate (compositionally) generated image samples according to
359
      both FID (Fréchet Inception Distance (32)) and binary classification accuracy (defined as whether a
360   specified attribute, or collection of attributes, is present or not in the corresponding generated output
361   image according to a pre-trained classifier). These two metrics are chosen in order to assess each
362   model’s ability to match the target distribution from the perspective of both perceptual image quality
363   and visual accuracy. We conduct all quantitative experiments for 1, 2 and 3 attributes per image for all
364   3 datasets, totalling 9 quantitative experiments. We use a temperature of 0.9 when generating samples
365   for our quantitative experiments. Details of how accuracy scores are obtained are in Appendix H.
366   In comparison to the 6 baseline results reported in (7), our method exceeds or matches the accuracy
367   of the previous state-of-the-art in all nine settings, while attaining highly competitive FID scores
368   across the three datasets. Particularly noteworthy are our accuracy results for Positional CLEVR, for
369   which our method scores 99.30%, 98.18% and 95.04% on 1, 2 and 3 input components respectively,
370   where the previous state-of-the-art scored 86.42%, 59.20% and 31.36% respectively (Table 1). We
371   see similarly dramatic improvements much harder Relational CLEVR dataset (Appendix Table 2)
372   and significant improvements on the FFHQ dataset (Appendix Table 3).
373   We speculate that the dramatic accuracy improvements offered by our method can be attributed to
374   the fact that the introduction of the discrete representation learning step (VQ-VAE or VQ-GAN)
375   facilitates the learning of an expressive and compositional “visual language” to represent images,
376   while the conditional parallel token model offers a strongly regularised and highly calibrated model
377   for the visual language. We conjecture that these effects compound to produce an efficient, accurate
      and robust compositional method.

      5      D ISCUSSION
477
478
      Through varied quantitative and qualitative experiments, we have demonstrated that our formulation
479
      for compositional generation with iterative sampling methods is readily applicable to a range of
480   tasks for both newly trained and out-of-the-box pre-trained models. We demonstrated state-of-the-art
481   performance in terms of the error rate of the generated results, in addition to obtaining competitive
482   sample quality as measured by FID scores. This is achieved with minimal extra cost in terms of
483   memory, since only the log-probability outputs need to be retained at inference time. The simplicity of
484   our method offers further advantages, including ease of implementation (facilitating integration with
485   existing discrete generation pipelines) in addition to improved interpretability, since the composition
      operator can be thought of as directly taking the “intersection” between two discrete distributions.

501   5.1        L IMITATIONS
502
      Similarly to compositional methods for continuous processes (7), our method requires (n + 1)
503
      times the number of feed-forward operations compared to standard iterative approaches of the same
504
      architecture, where n is the number of conditions imposed on the output. This is a direct consequence
505   of the mathematical formulation of the approach, however this is largely mitigated by the fact that
506   our method can produce accurate and high-qaulity outputs in only a small number of iterations (6; 7).
507
508
      Our method makes a strong assumption that input conditions are independent (P (c1 , c2 ) =
      P (c1 )P (c2 ) for all conditions c1 , c2 ). It is possible in practical scenarios that this underlying
509
      assumption of our approach is in some way violated, for example due to biases in the training data.
510
      The importance-weighting capability of our method can mitigate this in part by allowing the user
511   to compensate for potential biases, however we speculate that greater robustness would be better
512   achieved through an unbiased backbone model. Training unbiased models for image generation is
513   beyond the scope of this work and remains an open challenge, especially in the context of text-to-
514   image generation (37). Further to this, we have not yet explored principled methods for choosing
515   the condition weighting coefficients wi , which may be an interesting direction for future work (e.g.
516   producing a learned concept-weighting policy).
517
518   5.2        B ROADER IMPACTS
519
520   We have shown that our method can be applied directly to a publicly available pre-trained discrete
521
      text-to-image model without any fine-tuning to achieve fine-grained control over visual generation.
      While this presents an opportunity for positive impact by enabling creative works, we also wish
522
      to raise attention to the broader impact of our work from the perspectives of both societal bias
523
      and misuse. Image generation techniques in particular can be susceptible to perpetuating or even
524   amplifying societal biases (38), and our method will inherit whatever biases are present in the training
525   data or pre-trained model. In addition, readily accessible and controllable image generation presents
526   opportunities for misuse, for example for the purposes of misinformation (39) as well as defamation
527   and impersonation (40).

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      We have proposed a novel method for enabling precisely controllable conditional image generation
532
      by composing discrete iterative generative processes. The empirical success of our method across
533
      the axes of sampling speed, error rate and FID demonstrates a conceptual step beyond the previous
534   state-of-the-art for compositional generation. We further show that our approach can be applied to an
535   out-of-the-box pre-trained text-to-image model to allow for principled and controllable generation
536   without any fine-tuning. Though outside the scope of our present work with controllable image
537   generation, the prospect of applying our method for other compositional tasks (such as multi-prompt
538   text generation) remains an intriguing possibility for future work. Altogether, we believe our work
539   provides a strong foundation for future work in the direction of controllable image generation with
      composed parallel token prediction.

773   Here we clarify the relationship between equations (2) and (3) in the main text.
774
775   Equation (3) holds if and only if the joint distributions st , ci and st , ci are independent conditional on
776

778   P (st , V |st+1 ) = P (V |st+1 ) for any random variable or collection of variables V , since st is entirely

786   assumption and therefore (3) follows from (2) with the substitution of a categorical variable x with a

790
      In the main text we claim that our discrete compositional method can be applied to arbitrary generative
791
      methods provided they are discrete and iterative. Here we back up this claim by showing how our
792   method can be adapted to autoregressive (next-token) sampling.
793
794   In the specific case of discrete autoregressive image modelling, a single latent code (token) is
795
      generated at each time step, conditioned on some initial context in addition to previously generated
      tokens. In this situation, each successive state st+1 is simply the concatenation of the previous state
796
      st and the subsequent generated token xt+1 . Thus the random variable st+1 can be restated as:
797

800

      where ⊕ denotes the concatenation of two tokens or strings of tokens. Consequently, the conditional
      generation task in (3) can be reformulated in terms of sampling the next token given the previous
802
      state:
803
804                                                                          n
805
                                                                             Y P (xt+1 |st , ci )
                              P (xt+1 |st , c1 , ..., cn ) ∝ P (xt+1 |st )                                            (11)
806

                                                                                   P (xt+1 |st )
807
808   i.e. only a single token is considered at each generation step, making our formulation compatible with
809   autoregressive (next-token) prediction. In practice, we speculate that the autoregressive case is less
      compatible with our compositional method than parallel token prediction due to being less strongly

810
      regularised (and hence more prone to over-fitting: parallel token prediction is strongly regularised
811   by design (10)), in addition to being more sensitive to the accumulation of errors due to tokens
812   being generated “left-to-right, top to bottom” in the image. Furthermore, there is no guarantee that
813   autoregressive models provide a calibrated estimate of conditional/unconditional probabilities, which
814   may further limit hypothetical performance. For these reasons, we maintain our focus on parallel
815   token prediction which is found to to outperform the previous state-of-the-art on image generation

818   D.4    O MISSION OF THE DISJUNCTION (OR) OPERATOR
819
820
      While the implementation conjunction ("AND") and negation ("NOT") operators are highly effective
      for controllable generation, they do not correspond exactly to Boolean algebra: in our framework,
821
      negation is distributive, i.e. −(a + b) ≡ −a − b, but in Boolean algebra, it is not: NOT(a AND b) ̸=
822
      NOT a AND NOT b for a ̸= b. For this reason, our framework does not allow for the straightforward
823   implementation of the disjunction (OR) operator, which we do not explore in our qualitative or
824   quantitative results.

899

901   reconstructions for a variety of image datasets. We use a training batch size of 20 for CLEVR and
