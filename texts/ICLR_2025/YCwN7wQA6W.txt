011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Video Large Language Models (Video-LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable ca-
014            pabilities in coarse-grained video understanding, however, they struggle with fine-
015            grained temporal grounding. In this paper, we introduce Grounded-VideoLLM, a
016            novel Video-LLM adept at perceiving and reasoning over specific video moments
017            in a fine-grained manner. We identify that current Video-LLMs have limitations
018
               for fine-grained video understanding since they lack effective temporal modeling
               and timestamp representation. In light of this, we sharpen our model by incor-
019
               porating (1) an additional temporal stream to encode the relationships between
020
               frames and (2) discrete temporal tokens enriched with specific time knowledge to
021            represent timestamps. To optimize the training of Grounded-VideoLLM, we em-
022            ploy a multi-stage training scheme, beginning with simple video-captioning tasks
023            and progressively introducing video temporal grounding tasks of increasing com-
024            plexity. To further enhance Grounded-VideoLLM’s temporal reasoning capability,
025            we also curate a grounded VideoQA dataset by an automatic annotation pipeline.
026            Extensive experiments demonstrate that Grounded-VideoLLM not only excels in
027            fine-grained grounding tasks such as temporal sentence grounding, dense video
028            captioning, and grounded VideoQA, but also shows great potential as a versatile
029
               video assistant for general video understanding.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have made remarkable progress in image-level un-
035   derstanding (Liu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a). However, extending their capabilities
036   to the video domain poses distinct challenges. Unlike static images, the temporal nature of videos
037   challenges models to process not only visual content but also the sequence and timing of events.
038   While current Video-LLMs (Xu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023)
039
      are capable of capturing global visual semantics and generating coarse-grained captions for short
      clips, they struggle with fine-grained video understanding (Liu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024d),
040
      which requires decomposing the video along the temporal axis to accurately perceive and reason
041
      over specific moments, such as subtle actions, transitions, and events that unfold over time.
042
043   Previous research efforts (Ren et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a; Qian et al., 2024a; Huang et al.,
044   2024b; Guo et al., 2024) have explored temporal grounding to improve fine-grained video under-
045
      standing. However, two main challenges impede their potential for achieving effective fine-grained
      temporal grounding: (1) Models like Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024b), P-LLaVA (Xu et al.,
046
      2024a), and Video-LLAMA (Zhang et al., 2023b) typically sample multiple frames from a video
047
      and encode each frame independently using an image encoder, followed by a feature projector
048   (e.g., sliding Q-former (Ren et al., 2024), slot-based token compression (Guo et al., 2024), or
049   visual adapter (Huang et al., 2024a)). This approach focuses primarily on spatial details while
050   potentially neglecting the temporal relationships between frames (Maaz et al., 2024a). (2) Cur-
051   rent models also struggle with timestamp representation, which is crucial for pinpointing specific
052   moments in time for fine-grained understanding. Models such as TimeChat (Ren et al., 2024)
053   and VTimeLLM (Huang et al., 2024a) represent timestamps as plain texts, for example, ["from
      102.3 to 120.1 seconds"]. Despite being straightforward, this strategy needs to tokenize

075   To further improve video comprehension, we propose to sharpen the model with fine-grained tem-
076
      poral grounding, which allows the model to not only recognize what happens but also pinpoint when
      it happens in a fine-grained manner. We identify three essential capabilities of fine-grained temporal
077
      grounding, as illustrated in Figure 1, including Temporal Referring (Huang et al., 2024a), Localizing
078
      (Gao et al., 2017; Caba Heilbron et al., 2015), and Reasoning (Xiao et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024a).
079   Referring involves the model precisely describing events within user-specified time intervals. Lo-
080   calizing challenges the model to identify the timestamps of a given query and construct a coherent
081   storyline by locating a sequence of events within the video. Reasoning combines both Referring and
082   Localizing capabilities and further requires the ability to answer questions by understanding tem-
083   poral dynamics and applying wrold knowledge. Achieving these tasks necessitates both effective
084   temporal modeling and robust timestamp representation.
085   Targeting these goals, we introduce Grounded-VideoLLM, a novel Video-LLM that can perceive and
086   reason over specific video moments with fine-grained precision. From the perspective of model ar-
087   chitecture, Grounded-VideoLLM is built upon two key innovations: (1) Two-Stream Encoding: We
088   decompose each segment of the video into spatial and temporal components, and encode each with
089   an expert encoder respectively. The temporal stream extracts motion representations from dense
090   frames and complements the spatial stream which captures appearance representations. This dual-
091   stream approach forms comprehensive video representations enriched with both temporal and spatial
092   information. (2) Temporal Tokens: We extend the LLM’s vocabulary by introducing discrete to-
093
      kens specifically crafted for timestamp representation. These temporal tokens denote relative time
      positions within a video and share a unified embedding space with the LLM. This integration al-
094
      lows Grounded-VideoLLM to avoid the inefficiency of tokenizing numerical text, enabling seamless
095
      prediction of both timestamps and textual outputs in a single sequence of discrete tokens. From the
096   perspective of training and datasets, given that such a unified model requires extensive pre-training,
097   we start with an image-based MLLM (Microsoft, 2024) as the foundation and adopt a three-stage
098   training strategy to ensure efficiency. We meticulously select different tasks for each stage, and pro-
099   gressively refine the model in a “coarse-to-fine” manner, transitioning from image understanding to
100   video comprehension, and ultimately to fine-grained temporal grounding. Furthermore, we enhance
101   the model’s temporal reasoning capability by curating 17K grounded VideoQA (Xiao et al., 2024)
102   samples with the assistance of GPT-4. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Grounded-VideoLLM
103   shows promising results over existing Video-LLMs not only in traditional video temporal grounding
104
      tasks but also in general video understanding benchmarks.
105   In summary, we make the following contributions: (1) We present key insights into fine-grained
106   temporal grounding for Video-LLMs and introduce Grounded-VideoLLM, a Video-LLM adept at
107   perceiving and reasoning over specific video moments. This is achieved through a two-stream ar-
      chitecture for effective temporal modeling and the temporal tokens for efficient timestamp repre-

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116
      Video Large Language Models have caught a growing interest with the advancements in image-
117
      based MLLMs (Zhang et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023; Maaz et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2023). Despite
118   promising results, current Video-LLMs, such as P-LLaVA (Xu et al., 2024a) and Video-ChatGPT
119   (Maaz et al., 2024b), often struggle with temporal understanding (Liu et al., 2024b) and exhibit tem-
120   poral hallucination (Wang et al., 2024d) when answering questions about specific video moments.
121   These models encode each frame independently using a pre-trained image encoder, concatenating
122   the frame embeddings to create a video representation. This late fusion method can result in video
123   representations that lack inherent temporal information and heavily rely on the position embeddings
124   of LLM (Su et al., 2024) for temporal understanding, limiting the model’s capability to perform fine-
125   grained temporal grounding and understanding. In contrast to these studies, we employ a two-stream
126
      architecture that integrates a video expert to extract motion features to complement the appearance
      features during the early encoding process. Additionally, we employ a progressive training strategy
127
      that gradually adapts an image-based MLLM for fine-grained video understanding. Unlike con-
128
      current studies such as SlowFast-LLaVA (Xu et al., 2024b) and VideoGPT+ (Maaz et al., 2024a),
129   which also utilize a two-stream architecture, we specifically targets fine-grained temporal grounding
130   through a unique encoding/pooling/training strategy for dense frames and grounding design.
131
132
      Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) aims to associate specific video moments with their corre-
      sponding timestamps. Traditional VTG tasks include Temporal Sentence Grounding (Gao et al.,
133
      2017; Hendricks et al., 2018) and Dense Video Captioning (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015; Zhou et al.,
134
      2018). Other tasks, such as Grounded VideoQA (Xiao et al., 2024), emphasize reasoning over videos
135   with grounded information. Given the emerging reasoning capabilities of Video-LLMs, many stud-
136   ies have investigated how to adapt them for VTG tasks. For example, TimeChat (Ren et al., 2024),
137   VTimeLLM (Huang et al., 2024a), and LITA (Huang et al., 2024b) perform temporal grounding us-
138   ing a fully text-to-text approach through instruction-tuning datasets. Momentor (Qian et al., 2024a)
139   introduces a temporal perception module to address the quantization errors associated with time to-
140   kens, while VTG-LLM (Guo et al., 2024) incorporates a limited set of absolute-time tokens to han-
141   dle timestamp knowledge. Compared to these studies, we avoid textual representation of timestamps
142   and instead introduce discrete and relative temporal tokens for more efficient timestamp encoding.
143
      While Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023) also employs specialized tokens to indicate temporal positions, it
      relies heavily on large-scale pre-training from scratch using noisy transcribed speech and is limited
144
      to dense video captioning. Our two-stream architecture and progressive training strategy, however,
145
      enable MLLMs to efficiently comprehend videos and effectively handle diverse tasks of fine-grained
146   temporal referring, localizing, and reasoning.

      3     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
150
151
      Given that current MLLMs already exhibit strong image-understanding capabilities, our architecture
      aims to sharpen temporal awareness by capturing motion dynamics across frames, which serve as a
152
      vital supplement to spatial content. As shown in Figure 2, we develop Grounded-VideoLLM upon
153
      a well-established MLLM for spatial comprehension and integrate an expert video encoder for tem-
154   poral comprehension. Additionally, to avoid tokenizing numerical texts, we incorporate temporal
155   tokens into the LLM’s vocabulary for efficient and unified timestamp representation.

227   3.2    U NIFIED T EMPORAL T OKENS
228
229   Given a text span depicting a particular video clip and its associated timestamps, we employ a
230   relative time representation that converts continuous timestamps into a sequence of discrete temporal
231   tokens. For a video V with a duration of L seconds, we evenly divide V into M equal-length chunks,
232
      and then define M + 1 anchor points (ranging from <0> to <M>) across V, representing relative
      temporal positions. Each anchor point corresponds to a specific timestamp of V and is encoded as a
233
      temporal token. For instance, <0> denotes the very start of V while <M> indicates the end. These
234
      M + 1 tokens are added to the LLM’s vocabulary to enable unified modeling alongside text. A
235   specific continuous timestamp τ can be easily converted to a temporal token <t> and vice verse:
236

256   4     P ROGRESSIVE T RAINING
257
258   Different from previous methods (Zhang et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023) that train models from scratch
259   using mixed image and video datasets, we start with a pre-trained image-based MLLM (Microsoft,
260   2024) and progressively enhance its fine-grained temporal grounding capabilities. This strategy can
261   be applied to any off-the-shelf MLLM and is more efficient. Table 1 enumerates the datasets used at
262   different training stages, and Table 9 lists the prompts for different tasks.
263
      Stage-1: Video-Caption Alignment. Feature alignment is widely used to improve training effi-
264   ciency (Liu et al., 2023). In this stage, we leverage approximately 1.28 million video-text pairs
265   sampled from diverse sources (Wang et al., 2024a; Bain et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024b) to align the
266   video encoder’s features with the MLLM. This alignment allows the MLLM, which was pre-trained
267   solely on images, to gain a foundational understanding of videos. Since 2D down-sampling has been
268   applied to the visual features, only the two projectors f (·) and g(·) are set to be trainable, while the
269   video encoder, image encoder, and LLM remain frozen. As this stage does not involve any video
      temporal grounding tasks, the temporal tokens described in Sec.3.2 are not yet incorporated.

284   Stage-2: Temporal Token Alignment. While video-caption alignment effectively connects videos
285   and the MLLM at a coarse semantic level, a gap persists between this alignment and fine-grained
286   temporal grounding. To address this, we introduce the temporal tokens described in Sec.3.2 and
287   continue pre-training the model on a diverse range of grounding datasets (Huang et al., 2024a;
288   Qian et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024c), focusing on tasks such as Temporal Sentence Grounding,
289
      Dense Video Captioning, and Temporal Referring, which enables the model to refer to and localize
      temporal information effectively. Since new tokens are introduced, the trainable parameters in this
290
      stage include the two projectors, f (·) and g(·), the word embedding matrix, and the final classifier
291
      head of the LLM. This step enhances the model’s ability to comprehend multiple events and aligns
292   the temporal tokens with both the video timelines and the LLM’s semantic space.
293
294   Stage-3: Multi-Task Instruction Tuning. Following the initial two stages of pre-training, the
295
      model has developed a basic understanding of video content and the ability to refer to and locate
      specific timestamps. In this stage, we will further enhance the model’s fine-grained temporal ground-
296
      ing while improving its responsiveness to diverse user instructions. To achieve this, we gather two
297
      types of datasets: (1) We compile a wide range of public datasets for video temporal grounding tasks,
298   similar to Time-IT (Ren et al., 2024) and VTG-IT (Guo et al., 2024), which include tasks of dense
299   video captioning, temporal sentence grounding, and grounded VideoQA. (2) We incorporate a se-
300   lection of instructional video-to-text datasets from VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024), which feature tasks
301   such as conversations, classification, question answering, and captioning. Additionally, we include
302   ShareGPT-4Video (Chen et al., 2024a) to further enhance the model’s ability to generate detailed
303   video captions. By utilizing these diverse datasets, which encompass both temporal grounding and
304   video instruction tasks, Grounded-VideoLLM excels in temporal referring, localization, reasoning,
305   and general comprehension of video content. In this stage, the trainable parameters remain the same
306
      as in Stage 2, with the addition of LoRA parameters (Hu et al., 2022) for the LLM.

      Grounded VideoQA requires the model to not only answer questions but also identify relevant video
311
      moments that support predicted answers, thereby demonstrating the model’s temporal reasoning
312   abilities. The NExT-GQA dataset (Xiao et al., 2024) was manually developed by extending NExT-
313   QA (Xiao et al., 2021) with temporal labels for start and end timestamps. However, annotating
314   these temporal labels is labor-intensive and time-consuming, which has limited NExT-GQA only to
315   QA pairs for the validation and test sets. To create a scalable training dataset, we utilized OpenAI
316   GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to assist in constructing training sets for the grounded VideoQA task.
317   These sets were built on public datasets that already contain temporal labels, such as ActivityNet-
318   Caption (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015) and QVHighlights (Lei et al., 2021). We framed the task as a

321   generate corresponding question-answer pairs, as shown in Table 8. To create distractor options for
322   the multiple-choice questions, we retrieved the top 50 questions most similar to the generated ques-
323   tion, based on cosine similarity using an embedding model (Reimers, 2019). The answers to these
      50 retrieved questions served as candidates for distractors. From this pool, we randomly sampled

350
351   Implementation Details. We select the Phi3.5-Vision-Instruct-3.8B (Microsoft, 2024) as the base
352   MLLM for our Grounded-VideoLLM. For temporal stream encoding, we adopt InternVideo2-1B
353   (Wang et al., 2024b) as the video encoder. Each video is sampled as a sequence of T = 96 frames,
354   which are evenly divided into K = 12 segments. We set the pooling size σS = 2 for the spatial
355   stream while σT = 4 for the temporal stream respectively, which results in NS = 144 tokens per
356   frame for the spatial stream while NT = 16 tokens per frame for the temporal stream. Moreover,
357
      we introduce M = 300 temporal tokens into the LLM’s vocabulary for timestamp representation.
      More implementation details can be found in Appendix A.1.
358
359   Tasks and Benchmarks. To thoroughly evaluate Grounded-VideoLLM in fine-grained temporal
360   grounding, we assess it across three video temporal grounding tasks: Temporal Sentence Ground-
361   ing, Dense Video Captioning, and Grounded VideoQA, utilizing datasets such as Charades-STA
362   (Gao et al., 2017), ActivityNet-Captions (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015), and NExT-GQA (Xiao et al.,
363
      2024). We also show its reasoning capability by the task of Open-Ended VideoQA with datasets in-
      cluding MSVD-QA, MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2017), and ActicityNet-QA (Yu et al., 2019). Addi-
364
      tionally, to evaluate the model’s general video understanding capabilities, we benchmark Grounded-
365
      VideoLLM against existing models using modern Video-LLM benchmarks including VCG-Bench
366   (Maaz et al., 2024b) and MVBench (Li et al., 2024).
367
368   Evaluation Metrics. For temporal sentence grounding, we report the metric of Intersection over
369
      Union (IoU) (Gao et al., 2017) between the timestamps predicted by the model and the ground
      truth, including Recall at IoU thresholds of {0.3, 0.5, 0.7} and their mean IoU. For dense video
370
      captioning, we use metrics including SODA c (Fujita et al., 2020) which is specifically tailored
371
      for the video’s storyline, and METEOR score (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), which is the average of
372   traditional METEOR scores that are calculated based on matched pairs between generated events
373   and the ground truth across IoU thresholds of {0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9}. For Visually-grounded VideoQA,
374   we calculate both the Intersection of Prediction (IoP) (Xiao et al., 2024) and Intersection of Union
375   (IoU), and use Acc@GQA (Xiao et al., 2024) to measure the percentage of questions that are both
376   correctly answered and visually grounded with IoP ≥ 0.5. The responses of Open-Ended VideoQA
377   and VCG-Bench are evaluated by GPT-3.5 with the prompts introduced by Video-ChatGPT (Maaz
      et al., 2024b). More evaluation details are in Appendix A.3.

532
533
      We present Grounded-VideoLLM, a Video-LLM capable of fine-grained perception and reasoning
534   over specific video moments. This is achieved through a novel model architecture that incorporates
535   two-stream encoding for effective temporal modeling, along with the temporal tokens for efficient
536   timestamp representation. We employ a multi-stage training scheme, starting with an image-based
537   MLLM and progressively equipping it with fine-grained temporal grounding capabilities. Addi-
538   tionally, we curate a grounded-VideoQA dataset to further enhance the model’s temporal reasoning
539   ability. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Grounded-VideoLLM not only excels in video tem-
      poral grounding tasks but also performs strongly on general video understanding benchmarks.

      A.1   M ORE I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
759
760
      Phi3.5-Vison-Instruct (Microsoft, 2024) consists of a CLIP style ViT image encoder (Radford et al.,
761   2021), an MLP projector f (·), and the large language model Phi3.5-mini-3.8B (Abdin et al., 2024).
762   Each video is sampled as a sequence of T = 96 frames, which are evenly divided into K = 12
763   segments. For the spatial stream encoded by the ViT in Phi3.5-V, we adopt a higher resolution
764   336×336, but a lower resolution 224×224 for the temporal stream encoded by InternVideo-2.
765
      We set the pooling size σS to be 2 while σT to be 4 respectively. For the spatial stream, each frame
766
      takes up 24 × 24 = 576 tokens before while 12 × 12 = 144 tokens after pooling. For the temporal
767   stream, each frame takes up 16 × 16 = 256 tokens before while 4 × 4 = 16 tokens after pooling.
768                                                 T
      Therefore, we have an overall of K × (144 + K    × 16) = 3264 tokens in total.
769
770
      During training, we use the AdamW optimizer with a cosine learning rate decay and set the learning
      rate as 2e-5 and 1e-3 for projector f (·) and g(·) in stage-1. During stage-2 and stage-3, we set the
771
      learning rate for both projectors and word embeddings as 2e-5, while 2e-4 for LoRA parameters (r
772
      = 128 and α = 256). All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100/H800 GPUs.
