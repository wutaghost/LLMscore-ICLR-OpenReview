009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the inner workings of large-scale deep neural networks is chal-
012            lenging yet crucial in several high-stakes applications. Mechanistic interpretabil-
013            ity is an emergent field that tackles this challenge, often by identifying human-
014            understandable subgraphs in deep neural networks known as circuits. In vision-
015            pretrained models, these subgraphs are typically interpreted by visualizing their
016
               node features through a popular technique called feature visualization. Recent
               works have analyzed the stability of different feature visualization types under the
017
               adversarial model manipulation framework. This paper addresses limitations in
018
               existing works by proposing a novel attack called ProxPulse that simultaneously
019            manipulates two types of feature visualizations. Surprisingly, when analyzing these
020            attacks within the context of visual circuits, we find that visual circuits exhibit
021            some robustness to ProxPulse. Consequently, we introduce a new attack based
022            on ProxPulse that reveals the manipulability of visual circuits, highlighting their
023            lack of robustness. The effectiveness of these attacks is validated across a range
024            of pre-trained models, from smaller architectures like AlexNet to medium-scale
025            models like ResNet-50, and larger ones such as ResNet-152 and DenseNet-201 on
026            the ImageNet dataset.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) trained on vast amounts of data are becoming increasingly
031   important and deployed in the real world. In several high-stakes applications such as autonomous
032   driving, understanding the inner workings of these trained DNNs is crucial for assuring the safety
033   and reliance of these systems (Rudner & Toner, 2021; Wäschle et al., 2022). Inspired by neu-
034   roscience (Hubel & Wiesel, 1962; Olah et al., 2017), one classical approach relies on activation
035   maximization methods (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Olah et al., 2017), where the top images (real or
036   synthetic) that most activate a neuron are used to interpret the neuron’s behavior. A recently popular
037   direction for interpretability that often builds on activation maximization is mechanistic interpretabil-
038   ity. Mechanistic interpretability is an emergent field, which seeks to discover human-understandable
039
      algorithms stored in model weights (Wang et al., 2022). The discovery of these meaningful algo-
      rithms makes it possible to reverse-engineer the behavior of neural networks (Conmy et al., 2023)
040
      and can also permit to edit factual knowledge in large-scale models (Meng et al., 2022). Most of the
041
      research in mechanistic interpretability analyzes the functionality of DNNs by considering them as
042   computational graphs that can be decomposed into interpretable subgraphs known as circuits. In
043   pre-trained vision models, the emergence of circuits that implement meaningful algorithms such
044   as curve detectors and dog head detectors, etc. (Olah et al., 2020) has been demonstrated. These
045   circuits can be built by manually inspecting neurons, and hierarchically grouping them according
046   to feature visualization, which consists in finding, through activation maximization, either images
047   from the training set or synthetical optimization-based images (Olah et al., 2017). Circuits can also
048   be discovered using structured pruning (Hamblin et al., 2022).
049   Although activation maximization purports to provide the interpreter with a description of the behavior
050   of the neuron, recent work has cast some doubt on the reliability of these interpretations (Nanfack
051   et al., 2024; Geirhos et al., 2023; Bareeva et al., 2024). Notably, in these works, it has been shown
052   that models can be subtly perturbed (or “attacked”) to change completely the interpretation of either
053   synthetic or natural (i.e. from training set) images. This suggests that these interpretations might not
      be completely reliable. The existing works on model manipulations however have two limitations that

      2    R ELATED W ORK
082
083   Mechanistic Interpretability. Mechanistic interpretability is an emergent area in the interpretability
084   of large-scale DNNs, which tackles the problem of discovering meaningful algorithms stored in model
085   weights (Wang et al., 2022). Works in mechanistic interpretability either focus on individual neurons
086   or on sparse connections of neurons called circuits. Individual neurons are often interpreted through
087   techniques such as feature visualization (Zimmermann et al., 2021; Olah et al., 2017; Bau et al., 2020;
088
      Zimmermann et al., 2023), which is designed to interpret individual neurons by visualizing their top
      activating inputs. This can be applied to several modalities such as image (Olah et al., 2017) and
089
      text (Dai et al., 2022) using top-activating prompts. Works that build mechanistic interpretations
090
      using circuits have become popular due to the discovery of several meaningful subgraphs such as
091   those for curve detectors (Olah et al., 2020) in vision models and indirect object identification in
092   large language models (Conmy et al., 2023). While most of the studies manually build circuits, there
093   have been recent proposals to automate the discovery of circuits for language models (Conmy et al.,
094   2023) using edge attribution scores, and for vision models (Hamblin et al., 2022) using structured
095   pruning. This paper focuses on feature visualization and circuits for vision models and we adopt this
096   latter work to build visual circuits.
097   Manipulating Interpretability. Evaluating interpretability is difficult due to the absence of ground
098   truth. There is a recent trend in assessing the reliability of interpretability techniques through the lens
099
      of stability, which aims to evaluate how the interpretability results change under reasonable input
      and model manipulation (Heo et al., 2019; Yu, 2013). The motivation for examining the robustness
100
      of interpretability methods within the context of model manipulation stems from the “universality”
101
      assumption (Olah et al., 2020; Chughtai et al., 2023), which suggests that model interpretations are
102   similar for similarly performing networks of the same architecture. Some works study the lack of
103   robustness of feature attribution methods under input and adversarial model manipulations (Heo
104   et al., 2019; Adebayo et al., 2018; Dombrowski et al., 2019) and other works use these instabilities
105   to fool the model fairness (Aïvodji et al., 2021; Anders et al., 2020). This paper does not focus on
106   feature attribution methods. Instead, it examines the manipulability of feature visualization and visual
107   circuits for which two recent studies are very related. The first one Geirhos et al. (2023) shows that
      synthetic (formally defined in Section 3) feature visualization can be fooled under adversarial model

      manipulation. The key idea of their method is to add orthogonal weights to the original ones such
109   that activations of natural inputs (training data) remain the same, thus preserving model accuracy
110   while orthogonal weights allow fooling synthetic feature visualization. The second work Nanfack
111   et al. (2024) introduces an optimization framework that manipulates the result of natural feature
112   visualization (i.e., top activating inputs from the training set), and further observes the potential
113   decorrelation between natural and synthetic feature visualization. In this paper, we go beyond
114   these two works and propose a more complete manipulation, which we call ProxPulse. ProxPulse
115   simultaneously fools both natural and synthetic feature visualization. However, when analyzing
116   ProxPulse from the circuit perspective, we observe that ProxPulse also fails to fool circuits, leading
117
      us to propose a new manipulation for visual circuits, which has not been studied before.

445   Quantitative Assessment. The above analysis was a visual inspection of the manipulability of visual
446   circuits under the CircuitBreaker attack. Here we quantify its success using four criteria (CT).
447
      CT1: Functional behavior. First, to measure the preservation of the functional behavior of the
      circuit head, inspired by Hamblin et al. (2022), we measure the Pearson correlation (on a large subset
448
      of the training set) between (i) activations of training images on the circuit head and (ii) activations of
449
      the same training images still on the circuit head but with sparsity 1. Higher correlations will mean
450   high preservation of the functional behavior of the circuit head. Fig.7a reports these correlation scores
451   for several sparsity levels and different layers where circuit heads come from. It can be observed
452   from dotted lines (fooled circuits) that the functional behavior of fooled circuits is preserved in the
453   same way as the unfooled ones (bold lines), especially for moderate to high levels of sparsity (>.5).
454   CT2: Sanity check of accuracy. Second, as done in Section 4.1, we assess the performance
455   maintenance, ensuring that the fine-tuned model represents an adversarial model manipulation of the
456   initial model. We measure the performance of all finetuned models and report it in Fig. 30 (appendix).
457   The figure illustrates that our fine-tuning with CircuitBreaker maintains the same level of predictive
458
      performance of AlexNet accuracy on ImageNet, which is 56.52%.
      CT3: Correlation attribution scores. Third, as done in Sec. 5.2, we measure the rank correlation
459
      between kernel attributions scores from (i) the initial model and (ii) the final model, which is the
460
      fine-tuned model with CircuitBreaker. As a result, a lower rank correlation will indicate a small
461   change in the circuit, because these ranks are those that are used for circuit discovery. Fig. 7b shows
462   these rank correlations. It can be seen from this figure that the final ranks of kernel attribution scores
463   are weakly correlated to initial ones, except those of the circuit head’s layers, which is reasonable.
464   CT4: Similarity ratio between synthetic feature visualizations on the circuit. Finally, since with
465   fine-tuning, channels can switch their feature visualizations (thus decreasing the rank correlation but
466   not changing the interpretations of the circuit), we need a method to measure the change in synthetic
467   feature visualization. Inspired by the phenomenon called whack-a-mole in Nanfack et al. (2024), we
468   use a similarity ratio computed thanks to CLIP (Oikarinen & Weng, 2022) features. This similarity
469
      ratio is computed as follows. Given a final synthetic feature visualization from a layer, the numerator
      of the ratio is the maximum cosine similarity between this final synthetic image on the final model
470
      and any of the initial ones from the same layer on the initial model. The denominator is the cosine
471
      similarity between this final synthetic image on the final model and the synthetic image from the same
472   channel but on the initial model. Intuitively, the ratio quantifies the change in synthetic visualization
473   (initial vs final) relative to the initial synthetic visualization (using the final top channels). Fig. 7c
474   shows this similarity ratio per layer on different circuit heads. We observe that most values are lower
475   than one, suggesting that synthetic feature visualization has changed. It is also important to observe
476   that the similarity ratio (see the ending point of each curve) of the circuit head collapses to one, which
477   means that in general, there is negligible change in synthetic feature visualizations of the circuit head.

481
482   This paper proposes a manipulation technique called ProxPulse that extends the limitations of previ-
483   ous works, by showing that both types of feature visualizations can be simultaneously manipulated.
484   However, when analyzing ProxPulse within the framework of circuits –key components in mechanis-
485   tic interpretability–, we discover that circuits show some robustness against ProxPulse manipulations.
      We therefore introduce another attack that reveals the manipulability of circuits. We provide exper-


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      imental evidence of the effectiveness of these attacks using a variety of correlation and similarity
487   metrics. Our attack on circuits sheds light on the lack of uniqueness and stability of circuit-based
488   interpretations. We also observe a decrease in manipulability success when trying to attack several
489   circuits simultaneously without degradation in accuracy. Finally, further studies need to be done to
490   provide defense mechanisms and robust-circuit discovery methods.

672
673   Our work aims to study the lack of stability and robustness of popular interpretability techniques.
674   We consider the framework of adversarial model manipulation wherein model interpretations can be
675   intentionally manipulated in (un)targeted ways. Demonstrating this manipulability, unfortunately,
676   highlights the risk of individuals exploiting this knowledge to deploy models whose interpretations are
677   obfuscated. This can have a negative impact in high-stakes applications where interpretations may be
678   required to be reliable for model auditing. However, we believe that acknowledging and understanding
679
      these risks is a crucial first step in addressing vulnerabilities of interpretability techniques.
