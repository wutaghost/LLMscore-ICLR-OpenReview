009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The performance of neural network models deteriorates due to their unreliable
012            behavior on corrupted input samples and spurious data features. Owing to their
013            opaque nature, rectifying models to address this problem often necessitates ar-
014            duous data cleaning and model retraining, resulting in huge computational and
015            manual overhead. This motivates the development of efficient methods for rec-
016            tifying models. In this work, we propose leveraging rank-one model editing to
017            correct model’s unreliable behavior on corrupt or spurious inputs and align it with
018            that on clean samples. We introduce an attribution-based method for locating the
019
               primary layer responsible for the model’s misbehavior and integrate this layer lo-
               calization technique into a dynamic model editing approach, enabling dynamic
020
               adjustment of the model behavior during the editing process. Through extensive
021
               experiments, the proposed method is demonstrated to be effective in correcting
022            model’s misbehavior observed for neural Trojans and spurious correlations. Our
023            approach demonstrates remarkable performance by achieving its editing objective
024            with as few as a single cleansed sample, which makes it appealing for practice.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Neural network models exhibit unreliable behaviors in adapting to inherent or deliberately intro-
030   duced data distribution shifts (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Lapuschkin et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2019). Such
031   shifts; resulting from, e.g., spuriously correlated features or backdoor triggers, can misguide a model
032   and alter its behavior from the correct decision-making pathway (Ye et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2019).
033
      This compromises model reliability and robustness. Due to the inherent opacity of deep models,
      primary strategies for correcting such unreliable behavior involve data cleaning and model retrain-
034
      ing (Ross et al., 2017; Schramowski et al., 2020; Anders et al., 2022). However, these techniques ne-
035
      cessitate both labor-intensive manual data scrutiny and substantial computational overheads (Brown
036   et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Consequently, efficient techniques for cor-
037   recting unreliable model behaviors emerge as a critical requirement for enhancing their reliability
038   and sustaining the performance of developed models.
039
      This paper investigates efficient correction of unreliable model behavior through rank-one edit-
040
      ing (Bau et al., 2020). Originally proposed for editing generative rules encoded by generative
041
      models (Bau et al., 2020; Tewel et al., 2023), rank-one model editing has garnered attention for
042   its ability to revise model prediction rules. Expanding on this notion, recent works have adapted this
043   editing approach for domain adaptation in discriminative models (Santurkar et al., 2021; Raunak &
044   Menezes, 2022). However, we formally pinpoint two key challenges when applying rank-one editing
045   to domain adaptation, which inevitably lead to diminished model performance and necessitate labor-
046   intensive data preparation (details in § 4.1). In contrast, we establish that rank-one model editing is
047   well-suited for correcting unreliable model behavior as it intrinsically sidesteps these challenges. To
048   this end, we propose model editing for misbehavior correction with cleansed samples.
049   Current research on model editing often focuses on editing the deepest feature extraction layer, lever-
050   aging its high-level feature encoding capabilities (Santurkar et al., 2021; Raunak & Menezes, 2022).
051   However, our investigation reveals that editing different layers of a model leads to significantly dis-
052   tinct performances. Hence, to locate the layer primarily responsible for the unreliable behavior of
053   the model, we analyze the model’s prediction attributions across all its internal layers, comparing
      predictions for the corrupt samples to those for the cleansed samples. We find that the layer mainly

      2   R ELATED W ORK
093
      Unreliable Model Behaviors. Despite their impressive performance, neural network models have
094
      been found to exhibit numerous unreliable behaviors that lead to incorrect predictions on corrupted
095
      samples. For instance, the existence of spurious correlations, also known as Clever Hans behav-
096   ior (Pfungst, 1911), pose a substantial threat to the reliability of these models. A range of spuri-
097   ously correlated features have been identified including object backgrounds (Xiao et al., 2020), hair
098   color (Sagawa et al., 2019) and colored patches (Gutman et al., 2016). In addition to inherent bias,
099   training data can be intentionally poisoned by mislabeling samples and adding trigger patterns to
100   mislead model predictions (Gu et al., 2019). More attacks (Chen et al., 2019b; Li et al., 2021b;
101   Turner et al., 2019) are proposed to implant invisible triggers for concealed backdoors. Adversar-
102   ial attacks have demonstrated a significant capacity to alter model predictions (Goodfellow et al.,
103   2015; Madry et al., 2018). However, their practical applicability is often constrained by the neces-
104
      sity of full access to the target model. Consequently, this paper focuses specifically on investigating
      backdoor attacks and spurious correlations, recognizing their significant impact on undermining the
105
      security of deep learning models.
106
107   Model Explaining and Diagnosis. Various techniques have been proposed to explain and diagnose
      the vulnerable behaviors of deep models. Attribution methods, such as InputGrad (Simonyan et al.,

      2014), GradCAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and IG (Sundararajan et al., 2017), assign importance
109   to each input feature to provide explanations for model predictions, which are widely utilized for
110   visually inspecting model behavior (Lapuschkin et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021b). Other efforts (La-

117
      addressing the identified issues remains a significant challenge.

119   3     P RELIMINARY
120
      Model editing (Bau et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022) focuses on editing a specific model prediction
121
      rule while preserving the learned rules. When examining the l-th layer of a model f , an input sample
122
      x is mapped to a feature map fl (x). The mapped features fl (x) are recognized for their capability
123   to encapsulate semantic concepts in the representation space (Anderson, 1972; Kohonen, 2012).
124   This understanding is extended to characterize a layer as a linear associative memory. Specifically,
125   assuming a location of the input feature fl−1 (x) to be a “key” k ∈ Rn , the weights W ∈ Rm×n
126   within the l-th layer map this key k to a “value” v ∈ Rm of output features, achieved through
127   the operation v = W k. Considering a finite set of key-value pairs K = [k1 , k2 , . . . ] and V =
128   [v1 , v2 , . . . ], we can uniquely retrieve a value from a key if the keys are mutually orthogonal. Beyond
129   the exact equality, weightPW can be extended to arbitrary non-orthogonal keys by minimizing the
130   error as W = arg minW i ∥vi − W ki ∥2 . Given this characteristic, Bau et al. (2020) edited model
131
      weights to associate a key k ∗ with a new value v ∗ , effectively rewriting generative model rules.
132   Recent studies, inspired by the efficacy of the editing technique demonstrated in generative mod-
133   els (Bau et al., 2020; Tewel et al., 2023), apply this paradigm to discriminative models (Santurkar
134   et al., 2021; Raunak & Menezes, 2022). Santurkar et al. (2021) enhanced the domain adaptation
135   capability of classifiers by modifying their prediction rules. For instance, in the case where a “car”
136   classifier struggles to recognize cars featuring “wooden wheels”, the model’s rules are edited to es-
137
      tablish an association between the “wooden wheels” feature and the corresponding activations of
      “car”, enabling the recognition of cars equipped with wooden wheels. While incorporating a new
138
      key-value pair, it is critical to ensure the preservation of previously learned associations. Conse-
139
      quently, this editing process is formulated as a constrained least squares problem that creates a new
140   key-value associative memory, and preserves the established key-value associations as

150   In this section, we first pinpoint the intrinsic challenges in leveraging rank-one model editing in its
151
      known application of domain adaption. Following that, we propose using it to correct unreliable
      model behaviors such that these challenges are inherently sidestepped by the proposed technique.

      4.1   C HALLENGES OF M ODEL E DITING
155
      To understand the utility and limitations of rank-one model editing, let us revisit Eq. 1, which defines
156   the target function of rank-one model editing. To preserve the established key-value associations,
157   Eq. 1 updates the model weight W within the space mapped by the matrix C = KK ⊺ , derived from
158   the second-order characteristics of the learned keys K. The mapping by matrix C facilitates the
159   decorrelation of a key k ∗ from the existing keys ki ∈ K, thereby mitigating interference with the
160   established associative memories during optimization. However, critical challenges arise when the
161   new key k ∗ is not included in the statistical matrix C for applying rank-one model editing to domain
      adaption. Specifically, we establish the following lemma.

      Lemma 1. For K = [k1 , k2 , ..., kd ] ∈ Rn×d and C = KK ⊺ , when k ∗ ̸∈ K, the projection C −1 k ∗
163   leads to a residual component C −1 r outside the span of K, measurable by a residual vector r ∈ Rn .
164
165   The proof of Lemma 1 is provided in App. A.1. This lemma highlights that exclusion of the new,
166   unseen key k ∗ from the set K may adversely affect the preservation of the established key-value
167   associations as k ∗ does not fall within the span of K. This can degrade the overall performance of
168   the edited model. Giving it due importance, we mention this phenomenon as a challenge below.
169   Challenge 1. Diminished Performance: The exclusion of a key k ∗ from the statistic matrix C com-
170   promises the model’s ability to preserve established key-value associations. This omission poses a
171   risk to the performance of the model relying on the established associative memories K and V .
172
      To comprehend rank-one editing limits for domain adaption, we must also consider the disparity
173
      in the data distributions involved in the task. An implication of this disparity is that a new key k ∗
174   mapping from an unseen sample x∗ within a set X , puts an extra burden on data requirements.
175
      Lemma 2. Let x∗ → k ∗ s.t. x∗ ∈ X ∼ D′ and D′ ̸= D, where D is the original data distribution.

      Then ||k ∗ − f (x∗ ; WD )|| → 0 only when |X | ≫ 0, where |.| denotes cardinality of the set.

178   The proof of Lemma 2 is provided in App. A.1. This lemma emphasizes the necessity of sufficient
179   exposure to the samples from the new distribution D′ to accurately approximate k ∗ . Insufficient
180   number of samples can lead to inaccurate representations, which degrades model performance. We
181   note this fact as the following challenge to concisely present our findings.
182   Challenge 2. Labor-intensive Data Preparation: For an accurate mapping of a new key k ∗ derived
183   from an unseen sample x∗ in domain adaption, an extensive set of annotated samples is required for
184   effective rank-one model editing.
185
      In summary, the challenges of rank-one model editing in domain adaptation arise from its inability to
186
      preserve established associations when new keys fall outside the statistical representation of learned
187   keys, coupled with the need for extensive data to accurately represent keys from unseen distributions.

189   4.2   M ODEL E DITING FOR C ORRECTING U NRELIABLE B EHAVIOR
190
191
      We propose leveraging rank-one model editing to rectify a model’s unreliable behavior. To that
      end, we consider two suspect behaviors that result from feature spurious correlation (Pfungst, 1911;
192
      Arjovsky et al., 2019) and Neural Trojans (Gu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019b).
193
194   Feature Spurious Correlation: Given an input sample x ∈ Rp with label y ∈ Rc , and a classifier f :
195   Rp → Rc , feature spurious correlations occur when the classifier f exploits the spurious correlated
196   features inherent in corrupted samples x̃ to make predictions. While the model classifies x̃ to their
197   correct class y, its reliance on the spurious feature results in a flawed decision pathway, rendering it
198
      incapable of correctly classifying samples without the irrelevant spurious feature.
199   Neural Trojaning: In contrast to the spurious features inherent in training data, neural Trojaning is
200   executed by injecting a portion of clean samples with a backdoor trigger and modifying their true
201   label y to the incorrect target label ỹ. These poisoned samples x̃ are then integrated into the training
202   set to create a poisoned set. After being trained on this poisoned set, a Trojaned model f˜ is highly
203   likely to misclassify input samples containing the trigger to the target label ỹ.
204   The problems of spurious correlation and neural Trojaning are instances of a classifier’s unreliable
205   behavior which emerge from relying on non-robust features. To correct such behavior, we advocate
206   the application of rank-one model editing to rectify the established mapping rule between non-robust
207   features and their corresponding activations. When presented with a corrupted sample x̃ that leads
208   the model to exhibit an unreliable behavior, its cleansed counterpart x can guide the model toward
209   the correct prediction pathway. We designate the input feature derived from the corrupted input x̃
210   as the key k ∗ , and align activations of k ∗ to the corresponding value v ∗ mapped from the cleansed
211   sample x. We edit the model to make the feature k ∗ to yield correct activations v ∗ , thereby correcting
212
      the model’s unreliable behavior.
213   Sidestepping the Challenges. Our proposed process of model editing to correct unreliable behav-
214   iors involves the susceptible model that integrates both original samples x and their corrupted coun-
215   terpart x̃ into the training procedure. For a susceptible model, the training process integrates both
      clean samples and their corresponding corrupted counterparts. This integration ensures: C = KK ⊺ ,

      5     DYNAMIC M ODEL E DITING
237
      In this section, we first introduce an attribution-based method aimed at identifying the model layer
238
      responsible for its unreliable behavior. The identified layer serves as the foundation for effective
239
      model editing. We then integrate this localization technique to construct a dynamic model editing
240   framework, offering an enhanced capability to correct unreliable behavior of a model.
241
242
      5.1    L OCATING S USPECT L AYER WITH ATTRIBUTION
243
244   Rank-one model editing treats convolutional layers as linear associative memories, confining the
245   editing to a specific model layer. Current methods default to utilizing the final convolutional layer
246   for editing (Bau et al., 2020; Santurkar et al., 2021) owing to its capacity to encode high-level input
247   features. However, our investigation reveals a notable variability in the efficacy of model editing
248   when handling different layers. In Fig. 2, we empirically demonstrate that editing performed on
249   distinct model layers can yield significantly diverse results when dealing with unreliable model
250
      behavior - experiment details are provided in App. A.5. This motivates the need of a mechanism to
      locate the suspect layer that is primarily responsible for the observed behavior.
251
252   To identify the suspect layer, we leverage integral-based attribution (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Chen
253   et al., 2019a) to quantify the shift in attribution from the model’s predictions on corrupted samples
254   x̃ to cleansed samples x. Integral-based methods, such as Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan
255   et al., 2017), calculate the feature attribution by estimating the integral from a designated reference
256
      to the input sample. Semantically, the reference signifies absence of the true input feature. This
      resonates perfectly with the corrupted features in our context. Hence, we define the corrupted input
257
      x̃ as the reference to quantify the attributions from x̃ to x. We assess the attributions of the change
258
      in the final predictions on x̃ and x, i.e., f (x) − f (x̃), across all the internal layers in the model f .
259   We formulate the attribution M from the prediction on x̃ to x in the l-th layer of f as

335   Time Complexity Analysis. Let L represent the number of layers in the model, n the number of
336   key-value pairs, and T the number of iterations. The complexity is dominated by two components:
337   attribution computation, which involves a forward and backward pass through the model, scaling as
338   O(A); and rank-one editing, which requires calculation around a static matrix CC ⊺ ∈ Rn×n with a
339
      cost of O(n3 ). The overall time complexity is O(T · (A + n3 )). Since the algorithm uses a small
      number of cleansed samples, n remains small, minimizing the cost of calculating matrix. Moreover,
340
      T is typically less than L, ensuring the bound O(T · (A + n3 )) ≤ O(L · (A + n3 )). As a result, the
341
      proposed algorithm achieves efficient computational performance.

491   7   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
492
493   In this work, we propose an effective method for efficiently correcting a model’s unreliable behav-
494   iors. Despite its demonstrated efficacy across diverse scenarios, our approach depends on the iden-
495   tification of unreliabilities and necessitates the availability of both corrupted and cleansed samples.
496   In this section, we examine these limitations within the framework of existing robustness techniques
497   and explore how they relate to broader challenges in deep learning models.
498   Comparison with Backdoor Defense Methods. Current research on backdoor defenses focuses on
499   identifying and neutralizing Trojans embedded within deep models (Li et al., 2021a; Tian et al.,
500   2022). The prevailing strategies to mitigate backdoor attacks involve model retraining and prun-
501   ing (Liu et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2022). However, these methods are often constrained by the high
502   computational cost of recreating a clean model and the degradation in the model’s accuracy on clean
503   data. Furthermore, similar challenges are observed in the field of spurious correlations (Lapuschkin
504
      et al., 2019; Anders et al., 2022), where existing methods struggle to efficiently correct models’
      unreliable behaviors. In contrast, our approach utilizes rank-one model editing to mitigate backdoor
505
      attacks, addressing inherent challenges with both efficiency and effectiveness.
506
507   Identification of Unreliability. While detecting anomalous or Trojaned images is typically addressed
508   as a separate task (Qiao et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2024), our approach offers several
509   practical advantages by addressing the identification of unreliability in two critical aspects. First,
510
      it requires only a single pair of corrupted and cleansed samples to effectively correct the model’s
      behavior. This makes it particularly valuable in scenarios where access to large, cleansed datasets
511
      is limited, enabling robust model editing even under resource constraints. Second, our method
512
      facilitates image-level correction without the need for precise identification of backdoor triggers or
513   spurious features. By bypassing the need for exact identification of these elements, our approach
514   significantly reduces the complexity associated with pixel-level image cleansing. This adaptability
515   is crucial in practical applications where the availability of original, clean samples is restricted.
516   As a result, our approach allows for efficient model patching even with only coarse detection of
517   inconsistencies or anomalies, making it suitable for a broad range of real-world scenarios.
518
      In summary, our method introduces a robust and scalable paradigm for correcting unreliable behav-
519   iors in deep learning models, offering broad applicability across various domains while eliminating
520   the need for precise feature identification or extensive cleansed samples. The scope of this paper
521   is currently limited to image-based experiments. Future work can extend our method to other data
522   modalities. To address existing limitations, future focus on developing model diagnosis and data
523   cleansing framework integrates with the proposed editing technique. This integrated approach will
524   enhance the method’s applicability, enabling it to autonomously address a wider range of model
525   deficiencies. Additionally, while the ability to repeatedly edit a fixed layer has been explored in
526   previous work Gupta et al. (2024), the proposed dynamic layer localization method extends this
527
      concept to the entire model, which also represents a promising direction for further research.

531
      In this paper, we first establish that rank-one model editing is well-suited for model misbehavior cor-
532
      rection, circumventing the challenges inherent in existing application of domain adaption. We ad-
533
      vocate applying the model editing technique to correct model unreliabilities by aligning the model’s
534   decision pathways of corrupted inputs with those observed on cleansed inputs. We also introduced
535   an effective attribution-based layer localization method, facilitating the identification of the primary
536   suspect layer for the model’s observed misbehavior. We then developed a dynamic model editing
537   framework capable of dynamically adjusting the model for behavior correction. Extensive empirical
538   validation demonstrates remarkable performance of our framework across various scenarios. Par-
539   ticularly noteworthy is the fact that our editing technique requires only a single cleansed sample to
      achieve high performance levels, which portends its wide applicability in practical scenarios.

774
775   In our research, we utilize ZCA (Zero-phase Component Analysis) whitening to enhance the decor-
776   relation of the new key k ∗ from the established keys K, as previously described by Bau et al. (2020).
777   This process involves utilizing a decorrelation matrix Z = C −1/2 to further reduce the correlation
778   between the key k ∗ and the existing keys K by through the transformation Zk ∗ . Let P denote the
779   probability distribution of features at layer l − 1, and K represent a discrete distribution over t con-
780
      text examples provided by the user. We measure the information contained in K using cross-entropy
      H(K, P ), akin to the message length in a code optimized for the distribution P . In our model, P is
781
      assumed to follow a zero-centered Gaussian distribution with a covariance matrix C. By normaliz-
782
      ing with the ZCA whitening transform Z, P can be expressed as a spherical unit normal distribution
783                          ⊤ −1
      P (k) = (2π)−n/2 e−k C k/2 in the transformed variable k ′ = Zk. This transformation allows us
784
      to succinctly express cross-entropy using matrix traces.
785
786   Through the normalization of the basis using the ZCA whitening transform Z, we transform the
787   probability distribution P into a spherical unit normal distribution, characterized by the variable k ′ =
788
      Zk, which enables a compact matrix trace expression for cross-entropy. Leveraging the eigenvector
      decomposition C = U ΣU ⊺ , where U represents the matrix of eigenvectors and Σ is the diagonal
789
      matrix of eigenvalues, the expression for Z is given by
790
791                                        Z = C −1/2 = U Σ−1/2 U ⊺ .                                     (12)
792
793   This approach facilitates the decorrelation of the key k through ZCA whitening, effectively imple-
794   mented as k = Zk. In addition, we utilized the computed Z for locating the susceptible layer as
795   described in Section 5.1. Specifically, we map the attributions to focus on editable parameters as
796   M ∗ = ZM .

834
835   In this work, we adopt the blend attack Chen et al. (2019b) to train Trojaned models and spurious
836   correlation-based models. The blend attack was selected for evaluation due to its well-established
837
      effectiveness as a backdoor attack strategy. Unlike more recent attack methods Turner et al. (2019);
      Tian et al. (2022); Nguyen & Tran (2021) that prioritize stealth through minimal perturbations, the
838
      blend attack directly integrates triggers into the input, ensuring a substantial impact on the model’s
839
      predictions. This property makes the blend attack a particularly severe threat, as it strongly biases the
840   model’s output toward a predefined target class. By demonstrating robustness against such a potent
841   attack, our method provides compelling evidence of its efficacy. Furthermore, the blend attack’s
842   balance between potency and detectability suggests that our approach would generalize effectively
843   to newer or more sophisticated attacks that trade off between these factors.
