011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep neural networks have demonstrated remarkable performance across various
014            domains. However, they are vulnerable to adversarial examples, which can lead to
015            erroneous predictions. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) can leverage the
016
               generators and discriminators model to quickly produce high-quality adversarial
               examples. Since both modules train in a competitive and simultaneous manner,
017
               GAN-based algorithms like AdvGAN can generate adversarial examples with
018
               better transferability compared to traditional methods. However, the generation of
019            perturbations is usually limited to a single iteration, preventing these examples from
020            fully exploiting the potential of the methods. To tackle this issue, we introduce a
021            novel approach named Progressive Auto-Regression AdvGAN (PAR-AdvGAN). It
022            incorporates an auto-regressive iteration mechanism within a progressive gener-
023            ation network to craft adversarial examples with enhanced attack capability. We
024            thoroughly evaluate our PAR-AdvGAN method with a large-scale experiment,
025            demonstrating its superior performance over various state-of-the-art black-box
026            adversarial attacks, as well as the original AdvGAN. Moreover, PAR-AdvGAN
027
               significantly accelerates the adversarial example generation, i.e., achieving the
               speeds of up to 335.5 frames per second on Inception-v3 model, outperform-
028
               ing the gradient-based transferable attack algorithms. Our code is available at:
029
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PAR-01BF/

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Deep neural networks (DNNs) are widely used in different real-world applications, i.e., image
035
      classification (Li et al., 2020), emotional analysis (Qiang et al., 2020), and item recommendations (Pan
036   et al., 2020). DNNs demonstrate human-surpassed performance when properly trained. However,
037   DNNs can be vulnerable to adversarial examples crafted by attackers (Szegedy et al., 2013; Ma et al.,
038   2021; Deng et al., 2020), which is a concern in safety-critical scenarios. Thus, a practical approach
039   is to develop effective attack algorithms that can assess the robustness of DNNs against adversarial
040   attacks at an early stage, ultimately enhancing model safety.
041
      Currently, both white-box and black-box attack algorithms, such as gradient-based methods like
042   FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014b), NAA (Zhang et al., 2022), SSA (Long et al., 2022), and
043   optimization-based approaches such as PGD (Madry et al., 2017) and C&W (Carlini & Wagner, 2017),
044   require continuous computation of the model’s gradient information throughout the attack process.
045   However, they all require extensive running time. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfel-
046   low et al., 2014a) have demonstrated promising results for realistic sample generation by leveraging
047   both generator and discriminator for training (Karras et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022; Haidar &
048   Rezagholizadeh, 2019; Croce et al., 2020). While the generator constructs high-quality examples,
049   a discriminator learn to distinguish the original and generated examples. Furthermore, once the
050
      generator is trained, there will be no additional gradient computation for input examples.
051   As an early GAN-based model, AdvGAN incorporates a perturbation, denoted as G(x), into the
052   original image instance x for attack (Xiao et al., 2018). AdvGAN aims to obtain the manipulated
053   image x + G(x) from the original instance x through the discriminator. To achieve high attack
      success rates in both white-box and black-box attacks, AdvGAN introduces an adversarial loss on top

054
      of GANs loss, ensuring the adversarial image is generated in a direction more effective for adversarial
055   attacks. Additionally, it employs hinge loss to limit perturbation range, thereby preventing significant
056   deviations between the adversarial and original images. Subsequently, AdvGAN++ (Jandial et al.,
057   2019) further enhances the attack success rate by utilizing latent features instead of input image
058   instances x. It optimizes the latent features during adversarial examples generation.
059
      However, GAN-based methods suffer from several challenges. Both AdvGAN and AdvGAN++
060
      generate perturbations in a single iteration, which limits their control over these perturbations. We
061   observe the reason may be the generator continuously increases the perturbations during the iterative
062   process (as illustrated in the Appendix A.1). This may not be effective against adversarial defenses
063   and impacts the attack capability. It is critical since the goal is to maximize attack effectiveness with
064   minimal perturbation. Furthermore, the transferability performance of such attacks is concerning,
065   especially since internal model information is typically unavailable in real-world scenarios.
066
      Inspired by recent works that utilise auto-regressive properties to generate realistic images or
067   text (Chang et al., 2022; Yoon et al., 2019; Ni et al., 2020), we propose a novel GAN-based al-
068   gorithm, Progressive Auto-Regression AdvGAN (PAR-AdvGAN) to generate adversarial examples
069   with enhanced transferability. PAR-AdvGAN employs a progressive, auto-regressive iterative method
070   to effectively capture the specific structures of input examples. This process gradually generates more
071   diverse and realistic adversarial examples. Specifically, at time step t, we combine the input examples
072   xt−1                                                                                         t−1
        adv from time step t − 1 with the initial examples x0 to generate the perturbation G(xadv , x0 ) at
073   time step t. Consequently, the manipulated examples shifts from x + G(x) in the original AdvGAN
074   to xt−1         t−1
           adv + G(xadv , x0 ) in PAR-AdvGAN.
075   To achieve optimal performance with minimal perturbation, we propose Lp loss to limit the perturba-
076   tion range during iteration, thereby ensuring that the adversarial examples remain imperceptible to
077   human. Furthermore, to enhance the quality of adversarial examples and mitigate potential distortions
078   and significant noise during the generation process, we introduce Ld loss, imposing a stringent
079   constraint between the final adversarial examples xt and initial examples x0 . Finally, by indepen-
080   dently optimising the generator and discriminator during training, we fine-tune the parameters of
081   PAR-AdvGAN for more effective adversarial examples. Notably, owing to the high stealth and robust
082
      generalization capabilities, non-targeted adversarial attacks subject models to more rigorous evalua-
      tions and reveal more subtle vulnerabilities. Thus, we primarily focus on non-targeted adversarial
083
      attacks. We summarise the contributions as follows:
084
085          • We empirically study the limited transferability of adversarial examples generated by existing
086            GAN-based algorithms. To address this, we explore the use of progressive generator network
087            to enhance transferability.
088          • We propose an auto-regression iterative method and provide theoretical analysis on formu-
089            lating Lp and Ld loss to ensure minimal distortions in the adversarial samples.
090          • Extensive experiments demonstrate that our PAR-AdvGAN significantly outperforms other
091            methods, achieving highest attack success rates. Moreover, it outperforms traditional
092            gradient-based transferable attack algorithms in both transferability and attack speed. We
093            release the code of PAR-AdvGAN for future research development.

097   2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
098
099   While numerous adversarial algorithms are dedicated to generating high-quality and robust adversarial
100   samples, gradient-based attack algorithms constitute a main type. FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014b)
101   was the first to utilise the model’s gradients, which adds a small perturbation to the input data in the
102   direction of the gradient, thereby maximising the loss function through gradient ascent to achieve
103   optimal attack performance. MI-FGSM (Dong et al., 2018) incorporates a momentum factor in each
104
      iteration to mitigate the impact of local optima on the attack success rate. TI-FGSM (Dong et al.,
      2019) employs shifted images to calculate the input gradient, a process that involves convolving the
105
      original image’s input gradient with a kernel matrix.
106
107   Other adversarial attack algorithms, such as PGD (Madry et al., 2017), project samples onto suitable
      attack directions and limit the size of perturbations to generate robust adversarial examples. C&W


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      method minimises the attack’s objective function to optimise the generation process (Carlini &
109   Wagner, 2017). AdvGAN (Xiao et al., 2018) employs an adversarial training process between the
110   generator and discriminator. This process bolsters the generator’s ability to produce adversarial
111   samples, making them challenging for the discriminator to distinguish from genuine data. Besides
112   attack purpose, we also note some other iterative training methods for GANs, such as the Progressive
113   GAN (Karras et al., 2017) which divides the Generator into several layers, with each layer undergoing
114   individual training. In our approach, we consider an auto-regression methods, where each subsequent
115   generation is based on the results of previous step. Although auto-regression GAN has been improved
116   for continuous generation tasks, all we need is the attack result of the last state, so we need to redesign
117
      it for this situation.
118
119   2.2   A DVERSARIAL D EFENSES
120
      Adversarial defense represents an effective approach to mitigate the impact of attacks on DNNs.
121   Commonly used adversarial defense techniques include denoising and adversarial training. The
122   denoising technique employs preprocessing mechanisms to filter out adversarial examples, thereby
123   preventing the poisoning of training data and reducing the likelihood of subsequent attacks on the
124   model. Other notable works include HRGD (Liao et al., 2018), R&P (Xie et al., 2017) and so
125   on (Dziugaite et al., 2016; Cohen et al., 2019).
126
      Adversarial training enhances model robustness by incorporating adversarial examples into the
127   training process. Ensemble adversarial training (Hang et al., 2020) works by decoupling the target
128   model from adversarial examples generated by other black-box models, thereby defending against
129   transferable attacks. To enhance the robustness of our algorithm against adversarial defenses, we
130   validated the attack effectiveness of PAR-AdvGAN on the target model subjected to ensemble
131   adversarial training.

      3.2   P ERTURBATION ESCALATION IN A DV GAN
150
151   AdvGAN adopts a non-repetitive iterative approach to improve attack performance. However, as
152   iterations progress, the perturbation magnitude of the adversarial example increases rapidly. This
153   issue arises because it treats each iterated example as an independent instance, neglecting its relation
154   to the initial sample x0 . Additionally, AdvGAN fails to impose constraints on the distance between
155   the generated samples and the initial samples. This suggests that the perturbations generated in each
156   iteration will have significant magnitudes. To address this issue, we introduce three propositions:
157   Proposition 1 The generator should obtain information about the original sample x0 .
158
      Proposition 2 To train the generative model, the inputs to the generator should include a significant
159
      number of non-initial samples, particularly those encountered during the adversarial process.
160
161   Proposition 3 The generator should enforce constraints on the distance between adversarial samples
      and the initial sample x0 throughout the iterative progress.

      4.1.4       M ETRICS
335
336   Attack success rate (ASR) is a metric to evaluate the transferability of attacks. It quantifies the
337   average proportion of mislabeled samples among all generated samples after the attack. Thus, a
338   higher attack success rate signifies better transferability. Additionally, we use Frames Per Second
339   (FPS) to assess the attack speed. Another crucial measure, the perturbation rate, is utilised to ensure
340   that the adversarial images do not largely diverge from the original images in visual perception. A
341
      low value of this rate suggests that the adversarial examples maintain close visual fidelity to their
      originals. Detailed formulas are in the Appendix A.4.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we present a novel Progressive Auto-Regression AdvGAN (PAR-AdvGAN) algorithm
534   to boost adversarial attack capability through iterative perturbations. Specifically, to address the pertur-
535   bation escalation issue in AdvGAN, we first adopt a progressive generator network to incorporate the
536   initial sample x0 in the perturbation generation process. An auto-regression iterative method is then
537   proposed to include non-initial sample information in generator training. Furthermore, we constrain
538   the distance between initial samples and subsequent samples. Our extensive experimental results
539   exhibit the superior attack transferability of our method. Moreover, compared with the state-of-the-art
      gradient-based transferable attacks, our method achieves an accelerated attack efficiency.

735   As previously mentioned, in AdvGAN (Xiao et al., 2018), the adversarial sample xadv is synthesized
736   from the original input x and perturbation G(x). By employing a process of iterative and competitive

      C    L IMITATIONS
865
866
      We notice a slight difference in the attack success rate of PAR-AdvGAN on Inc-v3ens3 and In-
867   cResv2ens compared to the best baseline TI-FGSM in Tab. 3, although for overall mASR our method
868   achieves the best performance. This inconsistency suggests a possible dependency of our approach
869   on model selection. The main reason for the inconsistency is related to whether the method requires
870   further interaction with the model after training the Generator regarding the generation process. Our
871   method PAR-AdvGAN doesn’t require the interaction, while TI-FGSM needs to continuously interact
872   with the model for the generation process to obtain gradient information. It is always be feasible for
873   different models and we will consider reducing the dependence on model selection in the future to
874   generate more transferable adversarial examples.
