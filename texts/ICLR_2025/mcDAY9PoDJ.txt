009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Offline reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved notable progress in recent years.
012
               It enables learning optimized policy from fixed offline datasets and, therefore is
013            particularly suitable for decision-making tasks that lack reliable simulators or have
014            environment interaction restrictions. However, existing offline RL methods typi-
015            cally need a large amount of training data to achieve reasonable performance, and
016            offer limited generalizability in out-of-distribution (OOD) regions due to conserva-
017            tive data-related regularizations. This seriously hinders the usability of offline RL
018            in solving many real-world applications, where the available data are often limited.
019            In this study, we introduce a highly sample-efficient offline RL algorithm that learns
020            optimized policy by enabling state-stitching in a compact latent space regulated
021
               by the fundamental symmetry in dynamical systems. Specifically, we introduce a
               time-reversal symmetry (T-symmetry) enforced inverse dynamics model (TS-IDM)
022
               to derive well-regulated latent state representations that greatly ease the difficulty of
023
               OOD generalization. Within the learned latent space, we can learn a guide-policy
024            to output the latent next state that maximizes the reward, bypassing the conservative
025            action-level behavior constraints as used in typical offline RL algorithms. The
026            final optimized action can then be easily extracted by using the guide-policy’s
027            output as the goal state in the learned TS-IDM. We call our method Offline RL
028            via T-symmetry Enforced Latent State-Stitching (TELS). Our approach achieves
029            amazing sample efficiency and OOD generalizability, significantly outperforming
030            existing offline RL methods in a wide range of challenging small-sample tasks,
031            even using as few as 1% of the original data in D4RL tasks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Offline reinforcement learning (RL) has seen rapid progress in recent years. It bypasses the reliance
037   on environment interactions as in online RL methods, directly utilizing pre-collected offline data for
038   policy learning, thus being ideal for many real-world tasks that lack high-fidelity simulators or have
039   environment interaction restrictions (Levine et al., 2020; Fujimoto et al., 2018; Zhan et al., 2022).
040   However, offline RL is also known to be prone to value overestimation, caused by extrapolation error
041   when evaluating out-of-distribution (OOD) samples and amplified through the interactive dynamic
042   programming procedure in RL (Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto et al., 2019). In the past few years,
043   quite a few offline RL methods have been proposed, which commonly adopt the pessimism principle
044   using strategies such as adding explicit or implicit policy constraints to prevent the selection of
045
      OOD actions (Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Fujimoto and Gu, 2021),
      penalizing value function on unseen samples (Kumar et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2022b; Bai et al., 2021;
046
      Lyu et al., 2022), or adopting in-sample learning to implicit regularize policy optimization (Kostrikov
047
      et al., 2021b; Xu et al.; Mao et al., 2024). What’s in common with these methods is the use of some
048   kind of action-level constraints to avoid OOD exploitation. Although this could stabilize offline
049   value and policy learning, it inevitably leads to over-conservatism and crippled OOD generalization
050   performance (Li et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023). Most of the existing offline RL methods only
051   perform well when trained in sufficiently large amounts of offline data with reasonable state-action
052   space coverage (e.g., 1 million samples for simple D4RL tasks (Fu et al., 2020)). This forms a stark
053   contrast to the reality of most real-world scenarios, where the historical data are often limited and
      scaling up data collection can be rather costly (Zhan et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023). Hence although

054
      offline RL is initially proposed to solve a wide range of real-world tasks, we still haven’t seen too
055   many practical deployments to date.
056
057   Enhancing the sample efficiency and OOD generalization capability is essential to making offline RL
058
      widely applicable to real-world applications. This is particularly important for small dataset settings,
      as most of the state-action space will become OOD regions. Several recent attempts have been made to
059
      improve the generalization performance of offline RL, which mainly follows three directions. The first
060
      direction builds upon the empirical observation that deep value functions interpolate well but struggle
061   to extrapolate, thus allowing exploitation on interpolated OOD actions to promote generalization (Li
062   et al., 2022). However, this method has a smoothness assumption on the offline dataset geometry and
063   only applies to continuous action space. The second class of methods avoids the conservative action-
064   level constraint and instead performs reward maximization on the state-space (Xu et al., 2022a; Park
065   et al., 2024), which allows exploitation of OOD actions as long as the corresponding state transitions
066   are reachable (also referred to as "state-stitching" (Xu et al., 2022a)). Although these methods enable
067   promising state generalization capability, they still require the state-action space to have reasonable
068   dataset coverage to enable valid state-stitching. The last and also the most explored direction is to
069
      learn compact and robust latent representations that facilitate generalization (Laskin et al., 2020;
      Agarwal et al., 2021; Yang and Nachum, 2021; Uehara et al., 2021; Weissenbacher et al., 2022;
070
      Cheng et al., 2023). Most of the existing representation learning studies only focus on extracting
071
      statistical-level information from the data, using techniques such as contrastive learning (Laskin
072   et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2021; Yang and Nachum, 2021; Uehara et al., 2021). Although useful
073   for improving sample efficiency, these methods lack in-depth modeling of the underlying dynamics
074   patterns inside the sequential data, thus struggling to provide generalizable information beyond
075   data distribution. Some recent methods (Weissenbacher et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023) propose to
076   extract fundamental symmetries of dynamics to facilitate policy learning, such as the time-reversal
077   symmetry (T-symmetry) in Cheng et al. (2023) (i.e., the underlying physical laws should not change
078   under the time-reversal transformation: t → −t). If we can find and leverage such universally held
079   symmetries in the dataset, then it is possible to maximally promote OOD generalization without
080
      being restrained by data distribution-related information. Although promising, these methods are
      built upon existing action-level constraint offline RL backbone algorithms like CQL (Kumar et al.,
081
      2020) or TD3+BC (Fujimoto and Gu, 2021), which still suffer from the over-conservatism issue.
082
083   In this paper, we find that enabling state-stitching in a coherent, fundamental symmetry-enforced
084   latent space can actually lead to a surprisingly strong sample-efficient offline RL algorithm. We refer
085   to our method as Offline RL via T-symmetry Enforced Latent State-Stitching (TELS). Specifically, we
086
      introduce a T-symmetry enforced inverse dynamics model (TS-IDM) to not only learn well-behaved
      T-symmetry consistency representations that greatly alleviate the difficulty of OOD generalization,
087
      but can also facilitate effective action inference. Within the learned latent state space, we can optimize
088
      a T-symmetry regularized guide-policy to output the next state that maximizes the reward, bypassing
089   the conservative action-level behavioral constraints as used in typical offline RL algorithms. Lastly,
090   the optimized action can be easily extracted by plugging the output of the guide-policy as the goal
091   state in the learned TS-IDM. The resulting algorithm achieves amazing sample efficiency and OOD
092   generalization capability, significantly outperforming existing offline RL algorithms in a wide range
093   of challenging reduced-size D4RL benchmark datasets, even using as few as 1% of the original
094   samples. Our method greatly pushes the performance limit of offline RL under low data regimes,
095   offering a new opportunity to tackle many previously unsolvable real-world tasks.

      initial state distribution respectively, and γ ∈ (0, 1) is a discount factor. Our goal

142   Based on this intuition, TSRL constructed a T-symmetry enforced dynamics model (TDM) with an
143   encoder-decoder architecture, to learn a pair of T-symmetry consistent ODE latent forward and reverse
144   dynamics for representation learning. Although TSRL achieves impressive performance under small-
145
      sample settings, it still has limitations. First, TSRL only uses the learned encoder ϕ(s, a) = (zs , za )
      from TDM to derive the latent representations, without fully exploiting the rich dynamics-related
146
      information in the model for downstream policy learning. Second, it needs simultaneous access
147
      to both state and action to derive latent representations, making Q-function maximization the only
148   option for policy optimization, which inevitably requires adding conservative action-level constraints
149   (like the behavior cloning term in TD3+BC (Fujimoto and Gu, 2021)) to stabilize training. Moreover,
150   involving action as an input for representation learning is also prone to capturing the biased behaviors
151   in the data-generating policy (e.g., data generated from expert policy will have special action patterns),
152   which could impede learning fundamental, distribution-agnostic dynamics patterns in data.

154   3    O FFLINE RL VIA T- SYMMETRY E NFORCED L ATENT S TATE -S TITCHING

156   We now present our proposed method, TELS, which comprises a T-symmetry enforced inverse
157   dynamics model (TS-IDM) integrated with an effective offline policy optimization procedure operated
158   in the latent state space. Inspired by previous work TSRL (Cheng et al., 2023), we design the TS-IDM
159   to extract the fundamental, T-symmetry preserving representations from the limited data, which
160   not only facilitates OOD generalization for policy learning, but can also be seamlessly used as a
161   execute-policy for optimal action extraction. An overall illustration of our proposed framework is

401   4.2   I NVESTIGATION ON TS-IDM
402
403   In this section, we evaluate the functionality of the main components of TS-IDM to determine which
404   contributes most significantly to deriving an effective policy. We start with a basic autoencoder
405
      framework and incrementally add components until we develop TS-IDM. Throughout this process,
      we assess the performance at each stage to gain a clear understanding of how each component
406
      contributes to forming a well-behaved representation.
407
408   We perform the ablation studies on the “medium-expert” dataset using 10k data samples across three
409   tasks. Here, we also demonstrate the procedure for constructing the TS-IDM from a basic autoencoder
410   (AE) framework. We begin by encoding the states into a latent space using the autoencoder and
411
      generating actions through an inverse dynamics model, denoted as “AE +hinv .” We then learn
      the value functions and guide policy within the latent space to evaluate its performance. Next, we
412
      incorporate the forward and reverse dynamic models, hfwd and hrvs , into the representation learning to
413
      capture more information about the dynamical systems. In this step, we employ conventional methods
414   to train these dynamic models instead of using ℓs-ode for modeling the ODE systems. Subsequently,
415   we enforce the ODE properties on the forward and reverse dynamics using ℓs-ode to capture the
416   fundamental dynamical properties of the system. Finally, we involve ℓT -sym to couple the learning
417   processes of hfwd and hrvs , thereby enforcing the T-symmetry property on the latent representations.
418   We present the implementation details in Appendix A.

      5     R ELATED WORK
466
467
      To mitigate the issue of the distributional shift in offline RL, several existing methods adopt the
468   principle of pessimism by imposing explicit behavior regularization to explicitly penalize the action
469   divergence (Wu et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021; Fujimoto and Gu, 2021), or implicit
470   constraints the policy that prevents the agent from selecting OOD actions (Kumar et al., 2020; Xu
471   et al., 2022b; Bai et al., 2021; Lyu et al., 2022). Alternatively, employ an in-sample learning approach
472   to implicitly regularize policy optimization, which updates the critic functions without querying
473   the generated actions to stabilize the learning process of the value function (Brandfonbrener et al.,
474   2021; Kostrikov et al., 2021b; Xu et al.; Mao et al., 2024). There are also works incorporating the
475   pessimistic constraints on the policy based on the uncertainty measurement (Wu et al., 2021; An
476
      et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2021).
477   Most methods tend to perform well when provided with a sufficiently large dataset and under the
478   assumption of adequate state-action space coverage, which is often crucial for analyzing theoretical
479   performance guarantees (Kumar et al., 2019; Chen and Jiang, 2019). However, in real-world scenarios,
480   particularly when training data is limited, encountering a significant number of OOD regions is
481   inevitable. Applying strict data-dependent regularizations in such cases can lead to substantial
482
      performance degradation and poor generalization.
483   In order to improve the generalizability of the policy under the OOD regions. One approach leverages
484   the geometry with deep function approximators over the dataset, enables exploitation in generalizable
485   OOD areas. Another strategy moves away from conservative action-level constraints, focusing on
      reward maximization within the state space. This "state-stitching" approach permits the exploitation


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of OOD actions as long as their corresponding state transitions are feasible (Xu et al., 2022a; Park
487   et al., 2024). While these methods demonstrate their generalizability over OOD states, they still
488   require sufficient dataset coverage across the state-action space to support deriving a well-behaved
489   policy. Additionally, much research has focused on learning compact latent representations to improve
490   generalization (Laskin et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2021; Yang and Nachum, 2021; Uehara et al., 2021).
491   Whereas these approaches demonstrate their ability to improve the sample efficiency, these methods
492   lack in-depth modeling of the underlying dynamics patterns inside the sequential data, thus struggling
493   to provide generalizable representations for the OOD data. In order to retrieve the more fundamental
494   features of the dynamics, recent methods (Weissenbacher et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023) propose
495
      to extract fundamental symmetries of dynamics through an infinite-dimensional linear operator or
      revealing the time-reversal symmetry (T-symmetry) in TSRL Cheng et al. (2023). However, these
496
      methods are built on the downstream RL algorithm based on the action-level constraint and can still
497
      suffer from the over-conservatism problem that results in performance degeneration in small data
498   settings. By contrast, TELS incorporates a T-symmetry regularized guide-policy to output the next
499   valuable state in the learned latent space, which bypasses the conservative action-level behavioral
500   constraint approaches.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
504
505
      In this paper, we propose a highly sample-efficient offline RL algorithm that learns optimized
      policy within the compact informative latent space regulated by the T-symmetry in the dynamical
506
      systems. Specifically, we develop a T-symmetry enforced inverse dynamics model (TS-IDM) to
507
      construct a representative and generalizable latent space, effectively mitigating the challenges of OOD
508   generalization. By integrating a T-symmetry-regularized guide-policy within this latent space, we can
509   derive the valuable and reasonable next state for the learned latent inverse dynamics model to generate
510   the optimized action for control, which leads to an impressively strong performance. Moreover,
511   TS-IDM can simply function as a representation model to provide informative representations
512   and improve the performance of other methods under the small-sample setting. From extensive
513   experiments, we demonstrated that TELS essentially achieves the SOTA performance under the
514   small-sample D4RL dataset. One limitation of TELS is that in complex, high-dimensional scenarios
515   with limited datasets, extracting fundamental features becomes increasingly difficult. As part of
516
      future work, we aim to explore more expressive models to better capture these fundamental patterns.

785   B.2     A DDITIONAL A BLATION E XPERIMENTS
786
787   Impact of T-symmetry regularization on TS-IDM. To investigate the impact of T-symmetry
788   regularization strength controlled by the hyperparameter β in Eq. (9), we conduct additional ablation
789
      experiments by varying the value of β to assess how T-symmetry regularization influences the
      representation learning quality and downstream policy’s performance. Specifically, we train TS-
790
      IDM on reduced-size 10k D4RL MuJoCo datasets with β = {0.1, 1, 10}, representing different T-
791
      symmetry regularization strengths. The learning curves of TS-IDM’s overall learning loss LT S−IDM
792   in Eq. (9) are presented in Figure 3, and the final policy learning performances with different TS-IDM
793   models are presented in Table 8.
794
795
      From Figure 3, we can observe that choosing a proper β value impacts the learning quality of TS-IDM.
      A large β (e.g., β = 10) could impose overly strong regularization and hurt model expressiveness,
796
      which is reflected in the high learning loss at convergence. However, when the regularization strength
797
      is lowered, maintaining a proper scale of β is important to ensure both the quality and generalizability
798   of the learned representations. As we can see in Figure 3, in the Hopper and Walker2d tasks, choosing
799   β = 1 provides the lowest LT S−IDM loss; whereas in the Halfcheetah task, LT S−IDM is the lowest
800   when choosing β = 0.1. If we check the final policy’s performance under different TS-IDMs in
801   Table 8, we can see a clear correlation with what we have observed in Figure 3. TELS achieves the
802   highest score on Hopper and Walker2d tasks when β = 1, but the scores are higher for Halfcheetah
803   tasks when β = 0.1. This matches exactly with the learning performance of TS-IDM under different
804   β values. The strong correlation between TS-IDM’s learning performance and the final policy’s
805   performance of TELS shows that we can select the best β hyperparameter values by simply looking
806
      at TS-IDM’s training loss, and using the one that provides the lowest training loss. This avoids the
      need to perform potentially unsafe online policy evaluations or unstable offline policy evaluations,
807
      which is favorable in real-world deployments.
