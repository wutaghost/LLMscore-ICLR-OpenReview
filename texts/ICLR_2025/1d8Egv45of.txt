011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) offers enhanced transparency by reveal-
014            ing key features, relationships, and patterns within the input data that drive model
015            decisions. In healthcare and clinical applications, where physiological signals
016            serve as inputs to the models for decision making, such transparency is critical
017            for facilitating analysis of inference causality, ensuring reliability, identifying bi-
018
               ases, and uncovering new insights. In this work, we introduce a self-explaining
               multi-view deep learning architecture, that generates task-relevant and human-
019
               interpretable masks, attributing feature importance during model inference for
020
               stratifying key information from input signals. We implement the 2-view version
021            of the proposed architecture for three clinically-relevant regression and classifi-
022            cation tasks related to cardiovascular health, involving electrocardiogram (ECG)
023            or photoplethysmogram (PPG) signals. Experimental results demonstrate that
024            the complementary masks, self-generated by our proposed architecture, outper-
025            form well-established post-hoc methods (LIME and SHAP), both qualitatively and
026            quantitatively in explainability. Furthermore, the 2-view model offers task-level
027            performance comparable to or better than the state-of-the-art methods, display-
028            ing its broad applicability across various cardiovascular-related tasks. Overall,
029
               the proposed method offers new directions for interpretable machine learning and
               data-driven analysis of cardiovascular signals, envisioning self-explaining models
030
               for clinical applications.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Data-driven end-to-end deep learning methods find applications in problems that are difficult to char-
037   acterize using manually-defined features or traditional statistical analysis. Examples include image
038   classification (He et al., 2016), disease diagnosis (Ronneberger et al., 2015), speech recognition
039
      (Abdel-Hamid et al., 2014), and financial market prediction (Fischer & Krauss, 2018). However,
      the behavior of these models generally lacks transparency, making it difficult to understand how
040
      decisions are made by the model from its input.
041
042   To enhance the interpretability of deep learning methods, explainable artificial intelligence (XAI)
043   has recently received increased attention, contributing to reliable decision-making (Wang et al.,
044   2020; Hendricks et al., 2016; Ribeiro et al., 2016), analysis of model biases and failure modes (Ras
045
      et al., 2018; Karpathy et al., 2016; Geirhos et al., 2018; Vilone & Longo, 2021), and revealing
      key features, patterns and relationships within input data (Jumper et al., 2021). In healthcare and
046
      clinically-relevant applications, where deep learning models are used to make diagnostic decisions
047
      from physiological signals (Alberdi et al., 2016; Imani et al., 2016; Castaneda et al., 2018; Seshadri
048   et al., 2019; Betti et al., 2017; El-Hajj & Kyriacou, 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Mousavi et al., 2020;
049   Suresh & Duerstock, 2020), XAI has the potential to identify key patterns in physiological signals
050   that drive decision making, with implications for assessing clinical reliability (Mehta et al., 2023;
051   Chandrasekhar et al., 2020; González et al., 2023), personal health monitoring (Charlton et al.,
052   2022b), efficient distribution of medical resources (Kang & Exworthy, 2022), and continuous mon-
053   itoring of risk factors in critical care (Rao et al., 2023). However, at present, generalized solutions
      for interpretable learning from physiological signals are still limited.

074   In this paper, we introduce a generalized new self-explaining deep learning method that reveals
075   key patterns in cardiovascular signals for stratifying health-related information, with minimum help
076   from prior expert knowledge. Our work offers the following contributions:
077
078
      - We introduce a generalized approach for learning semantic information from consecutive intervals
        of an input signal, by attributing each sample in the signal to one of N semantic states. Samples
079
        attributed to the same semantic state are expected to form patterns that offer distinct information
080
        for making the final clinically-relevant decision.
081
082   - We propose a multi-view end-to-end deep learning architecture to implement our learning princi-
083
        ples. The proposed architecture includes a mask network that produces multiple mask-modulated
        versions of the signal, each representing a “semantic view” formed by samples in the signal cor-
084
        responding to the same semantic states. Each semantic view highlights distinct regions and in-
085
        formative patterns within the signal. Concatenated with an embedding network and a decision
086     network, during supervised training using the task labels, the created semantic views are updated
087     based on the saliency information with respect to the model’s output, making the highlighted re-
088     gions of the signal more relevant to the task. As such, the semantic views can improve model’s
089     interpretability through explaining the correspondence between regions in the input signal and the
090     clinically-relevant information inferred from the signal.
091   - We implement the 2-view version of the proposed multi-view architecture for 3 different classi-
092     fication and regression tasks involving stratifying clinically-relevant information from 2 differ-
093     ent cardiovascular signals. We validate the proposed models by quantitatively and qualitatively
094     comparing the correctness of their self-generated explanations, against those created from well-
095     established post-hoc methods, as well as comparing their task-level performance against state-
096     of-the-art methods. We also demonstrate the alignment between the interpretable representations
097     generated by our models, and the domain knowledge from human experts, for each task.

099   2    R ELATED W ORKS
100
101
      Figure 1 summarizes existing methods that facilitate interpretability in machine learning models.
102   Unlike white-box models with inherent explainability due to their simplicity, linearity or feature-
103   driven nature, learners in data-driven deep learning models, such as multi-layer perceptron (MLP),
104   convolutional neural network (CNN) or recurrent neural network (RNN), generally lack inter-
105   pretability when stacked with non-linear activations (Ali et al., 2023). To understand their working
106   principles, post-hoc model explanation methods were developed, to identify the input-output corre-
107   spondence learned by these models after training. For example, (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) proposed
      the occlusion sensitivity analysis for image classification models, to investigate how occluding each


                                                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      region in the input affects model’s output. Alternatively, (Simonyan, 2014) inspected the gradient
109   backpropagated from the class probability to each input pixel, to form a class-specific saliency map
110   that highlights regions in the input that changes the model’s output the most. Studies in (Zhou et al.,
111   2016) and (Selvaraju et al., 2017) used the 2-D feature maps generated by the last convolutional
112   layer in CNNs to produce class activation map (CAM) and gradient-weighted CAM (Grad-CAM)
113   that localize regions in the input that are most related to the output. The insights from these ap-
114   proaches were further generalized in later works, such as LIME, DeepLIFT and SHAP (Ribeiro
115   et al., 2016; Lundberg, 2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017), to explain any trained model by finding an
116   interpretable delegation model, with faithfulness to the original non-interpretable model.
117   Post-hoc model explanation methods explain previously-trained models based on local input-output
118   properties around each sample, which may limit their fidelity in representing the working princi-
119   ples of the original model (Rudin, 2019). Moreover, explanations provided by these methods are not
120   guaranteed to be human understandable, since the models are not regularized to encode interpretable
121   and task-specific concepts in the learned representations during training (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola,
122   2018; Park & Hwang, 2023). For example, (Troncoso-Garcı́a et al., 2022) applied the LIME method
123
      to a sleep apnea detection model that takes multi-modal inputs (blood pressure (BP), electrocar-
      diogram (ECG), electroencephalogram (EEG), and nasal respiratory signals), however, the LIME
124
      method only highlighted a few discrete samples in the input time series, offering limited insight into
125
      the key patterns in the signals that characterize sleep apnea.
126
127   To address these limitations, recent works focused on developing self-explaining models, with in-
128   trinsic interpretability either learned during training or built-in to the model architecture, for of-
129
      fering faithful, stable, and human-understandable explanations. (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018)
      proposed a locally-linear neural network, in which the model is regularized to have local linearity
130
      around each sample, for offering inherent explainability. Sharing similar insights, (Sel et al., 2023)
131
      proposed a physics-informed neural network (Raissi et al., 2019) for BP estimation, by optimiz-
132   ing an additional physics-based loss to embed physical constrains in the input-output correspon-
133   dence of the model. Besides, recent advancements in incorporating attention mechanisms in deep
134   learning models (Bahdanau, 2015; Dosovitskiy, 2021; Mousavi et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021) also
135   enhances model’s interpretability, through investigating the attention maps generated by the model
136   that highlights informative patterns or relationships in the model’s inputs. Furthermore, the flex-
137   ibility of learning interpretable representations during model training, enables better human-level
138   understanding of the explanations produced by the model. For example, (Hendricks et al., 2016)
139   considered joint training of classification and language models for image classification tasks, to
140
      generate human-understandable explanations to the produced classifications in natural language.
141   Due to the unique capabilities of self-explaining models, we seek to develop generalized solutions
142   that support healthcare decisions, by improving human understanding of health-related inference
143   from input signals.

148   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMATION
149
150   Let S = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xT } = {xt }Tt=1 denotes a continuous multivariate time series interval with
151   T samples, where xt = [x1,t , · · · , xD,t ] ∈ RD is a D-dimensional sample at time instant t. S is
152   labeled by y denoting the clinically-relevant information to be inferred from the signal. As such, the
153   stratification task can be defined in general as producing ỹ, an estimation of y, from S.
154
      To enable generalized and interpretable estimations, we build on prior work (Wang et al., 2011;
155
      Yue et al., 2022; Deldari et al., 2021; Gharghabi et al., 2019), which assumes that the input signals
156   reflect the behavior of their underlying system, whose dynamics are describable by a number of
157   latent semantic states closely related to y. To facilitate human-level interpretation and segment time
158   series into multiple distinct regions, each sample xt in the time series S is attributed to one and
159   only one of the N semantic states u1 , u2 , · · · , uN . Samples sharing the same nth semantic state
160   form a sub-series sn , referred to as a semantic view reflecting distinct characteristics of S relevant
161   to y. Therefore, through extracting information from all N semantic views, one can retrieve features
      from the time series S that comprehensively describe the characteristics of the underlying system, for

180   in 3 steps:
181
182   - Semantic segmentation: Attribute each sample xt in S to one of the N semantic states, yielding
183     N semantic views, s1 = {xt |xt ∈ u1 }, · · · , sN = {xt |xt ∈ uN }.
184   - Embedding extraction: Learn a low-dimensional embedding representation zn , from each of
185     the high-dimensional semantic views sn . As such, z1 , · · · , zN are expected to form complete
186     representations of all distinct semantic information that S carries.
187
      - Decision: Form a final output ỹ based on embeddings extracted from all semantic views.
188
189   Essentially, the semantic segmentation process is equivalent to performing a N -class sample-by-
190   sample classification on S. However, unlike its conventional form, where the segmentation is learned
191   under the supervision of manual annotations (Peimankar & Puthusserypady, 2021; Moskalenko
192   et al., 2020), here, no prior implication is specified to each of the N semantic states. During train-
193   ing, the model is left to spontaneously learn how samples in S should be attributed to each semantic
194   state, such that the patterns retained in each semantic view are most informative for optimizing the
195   estimation of y. As such, the semantic views produced by the model during inference, can explain
196   informative patterns in S that drives the estimation of y.

      3.2.2   L EARNING F OR M AKING D ECISIONS
254
255   An embedding network is employed to encode a low-dimensional embedding representation zn , for
256   each semantic view e sn created by the mask network. Inspired by the use of shared encoders for
257   learning representations from augmented views (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2015; Chen et al., 2020;
258   Yue et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022b; Deldari et al., 2021), we employ weight sharing in the embed-
259   ding network across all semantic views. This approach enables the learning of generalized filters and
260
      ensures informative gradients are propagated to all semantic states, such that the mask network can
      learn to segment the input signal properly. Moreover, weight sharing also ensures feature compara-
261
      bility across semantic views, enabling the decision network to distinguish and extract comparative
262
      features from different semantic states (Wang et al., 2024; Schlesinger et al., 2020).
263
264   Based on the embedding vectors z1 , · · · , zN extracted comprehensively from all semantic views,
265   the decision network in the model is trained to form the final output ỹ that estimates the task label
266
      y. For general tasks, a simple concatenation of all embeddings z = [z1 , · · · , zN ] forms the input of
      the decision network.
267
268   Overall, the proposed multi-view deep learning framework combines the mask, embedding, and
269   decision networks together, as one unified deep learning model trained under single supervision of
      the task label y, for self-explaining physiological signal stratification.

      For validation of usability and interpretability of the proposed architecture, we considered 3 dif-
273
      ferent tasks for stratifying clinically-relevant information (obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) detection

279   here we focus on N = 2 to highlight the most discernible patterns in the signal that deliver different
280   information for optimal human understandability, and leave explorations of other settings for future
281   studies. Detailed descriptions of each task as well as information of datasets used for validation can
282
      be found in Appendix A.1. The hyperparameter settings used for implementing the 2-view model

287   5.1   Q UANTITATIVE I NTERPRETABILITY A NALYSIS
288
289   For each task, we first quantitatively compare the correctness of self-generated explanations from our
290   proposed 2-view model against those created from two well-established post-hoc methods, LIME
291
      and SHAP.
292
      - LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) considers a linear surrogate model that maps the presence or absence
293
        of interpretable elements, encoded as binary vectors, to the local outputs of the explained model
294     around a particular input signal, such that the linear coefficients of the fitted model attribute the
295     importance of each element in the input signal toward model’s output.
296
      - SHAP (Lundberg, 2017) uses the Shapley value (Shapley, 1953) to evaluate the importance of
297
        each element in the input signal, which assesses changes in model’s output when trained on dif-
298
        ferent subsets of input elements, including or withholding the attributed element. SHAP ap-
299     proximates Shapley values using various methods (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Shrikumar et al., 2017;
300     Štrumbelj & Kononenko, 2014). Here, we used the Gradient SHAP method (Lundberg, 2017).
301
302   To evaluate the quality of semantic segmentation masks generated by the 2-view model as inter-
303   pretable representations, we treat the amplitudes of each of the two masks (denoted as mask 1 and
304   mask 2) as feature attribution weights that rank the importance of each length-L window in the in-
305   put signal toward model’s output. The interpretations self-generated by the 2-view model, are then
306   compared with feature attributions created on the same model, through LIME and SHAP.
307   For an objective quantification of interpretability, we used the well-established incremental dele-
308   tion method (Petsiuk, 2018; Nauta et al., 2023; Samek et al., 2016). This method evaluates how
309   incrementally perturbing important input features, identified by high mask amplitudes of the 2-view
310   model or large absolute values of attribution scores by LIME or SHAP, impacts the model’s output.
311   We investigated how perturbing input signal windows affects the 2-view model’s test performance,
312   to evaluate the correctness and sensitivity of window importance suggested by different explanation
313   methods. Additionally, a baseline is considered by perturbing each window in the input signal at a
314
      random sequence.
315   Figure 3 summarizes the incremental deletion curves evaluated on the three 2-view models trained
316   for each of the considered tasks. A lower area under deletion curve (AUDC) indicates better expla-
317   nations that highlight essential regions in the signal closely related to model’s decision.
318   Across all tasks, LIME and SHAP outperformed the baseline, with SHAP having improved perfor-
319   mance over LIME due to its better adherence to desirable properties of model explainers (Lundberg,
320   2017). Meanwhile, for the proposed 2-view model, one (HRV task) or both (OSA and ∆BP tasks)
321   self-generated masks offered optimal AUDC performance over the post-hoc and baseline methods.
322   This superior performance can be attributed to the multi-view network’s architecture, which uses the
323   created masks to modulate the inputs to the embedding and decision networks, ensuring a straight-
      forward correspondence between the interpretations and the model’s output. Interestingly, for the

511   6     L IMITATIONS
512
513   While we proposed a multi-view architecture for self-explainability, as a proof-of-concept, our ex-
514   periments were limited to a 2-view configuration and focused on cardiovascular signals. The pro-
515   posed model, however, has the potential to be extended to configurations with more views to uncover
516   hidden insights, and to be applied to a broader range of signal types or domains, which are left for
517   future studies. Furthermore, there are potentials to improve explainability. In the current multi-view
518   model, the embedding and decision networks lack integrated interpretability, thus making it hard to
519   quantify the correspondence between each semantic view and the model’s output directly. Using
520
      alternative architectures for the embedding network, or considering domain-agnostic model inter-
      pretation methods in combination with the semantic masks, may further improve the interpretability
521
      of the proposed model.

      7     C ONCLUSIONS
526
527   Self-explaining models provide unique opportunities for understanding the working principles of
528   deep learning models. To facilitate self-explaining learning from input signals, we introduced a gen-
529   eralized form for learning distinct semantic information from continuous intervals, and proposed
530   a generalized multi-view deep learning architecture that creates multiple complementary semantic
531
      views from the input signal for enhanced interpretability and feature extraction. Trained under the
      supervision of the task label only, the model optimizes its semantic views through the saliency of em-
532
      bedding and decision networks, achieving interpretability by highlighting input patterns that convey
533
      relevant physiological information. Tested on 3 real-world cardiovascular signal stratification tasks
534   with 2 different signals, the feature attributions self-created by 2-view implementation of the pro-
535   posed model outperforms post-hoc model explanation methods both quantitatively and qualitatively,
536   providing clearer explanations of patterns in the input signal that drive decisions, while achieving
537   task-level classification and regression performance comparable to or better than task-specific state-
538   of-the-art methods. Overall, we expect the proposed multi-view framework to enhance data-driven,
539   interpretable analysis of physiological signals, advancing self-explaining models for accurate, reli-
      able computer-aided diagnosis and health monitoring in clinical applications.

831   A.1   TASKS AND DATASETS I NFORMATION
832
      1) ECG-based OSA detection: OSA is a sleep breathing disorder with major negative effects
833
      on sleep quality, leading to fatigue, hypertension, cerebrovascular complications, and sudden deaths
834
      (Javaheri et al., 2017). Traditional OSA diagnosis requires overnight monitoring and manual scoring
835   of multiple signals at sleep labs, which is obtrusive, time consuming, and costly. Recent studies on
836   ECG-based OSA detection using machine learning envisions timely and automatic OSA diagnosis
837   outside hospital, through compact wearable devices (Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022a; Chang
838   et al., 2020). The Apnea-ECG database (Penzel et al., 2000) was considered in this study for OSA
839   detection, to form a binary classification task of attributing each 60-s ECG segment in the dataset
840   as “apnea” or “normal”. The proposed models are trained and tested on the predefined partition
841   of 17, 023 “released” and 17, 248 “withheld” segments in the dataset, for a fair comparison with
842   recently-proposed SOTA solutions validated using the same dataset and train-test partition (Yang
843
      et al., 2022a; Yeh et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2020).
844   2) PPG-based HRV estimation: Evaluation of ultra-short term HRV within 10-s intervals has found
845   emerging applications in assessing mental stress (Can et al., 2019; Landreani et al., 2017), cardio-
846   vascular risk factors (Kang et al., 2022), and cognitive functions (Mahinrad et al., 2016). However,
847   acquiring gold-standard HRV from the ECG signal can have limitations in certain scenarios, and the
848   PPG signal has been considered as an ideal and low-cost surrogate for HRV estimation (Georgiou
849
      et al., 2018; Mejı́a-Mejı́a et al., 2021). In this study, we formed training, validation and testing
      sets consisting of 524, 868, 61, 676, and 74, 736 10-s ECG and PPG segments from the “Training”
850
      and “Calibration-free testing” partitions of PulseDB (Wang et al., 2023). We extracted ground-truth
851
      HRV metrics (mean normal-to-normal interval (MNN), standard deviation of normal-to-normal in-
852   terval (SDNN), and root mean square of successive interval differences (RMSSD)) from each 10-s
853   segment of ECG, and evaluated the regression performance of estimating the same metrics from
854   only the PPG signal recorded simultaneously with the ECG.
855
      3) PPG-based BP elevation detection: Hypertension is a leading cause of death. PPG-based track-
856
      ing of changes in BP (∆BP) is essential for non-invasive and unobtrusive identification of hyperten-
857
      sive emergencies (Wang et al., 2024), which also forms the basis of cuff-less BP estimation (Stergiou
858   et al., 2023) for continuous tracking of cardiovascular risk factors. In this study, we used the same
859   training, validation, and testing partitions of PulseDB (Wang et al., 2023) used in (Wang et al.,
860   2024), with 202, 954, 23, 718 and 23, 684 training, validation and testing samples, to evaluate the
861   accuracy of detecting abrupt systolic BP (SBP) elevations from the PPG signal. In consistence with
862   (Wang et al., 2024), we considered a binary classification task of identifying the presence of acute
863   SBP elevation greater than 10mmHg, within 40-s intervals of PPG as well its first to third order
      derivatives.

904   Figure 7 depicts the layer-wise implementation of the multi-view model used for addressing all 3
905   tasks, following the architecture introduced in Figure 2. For the mask network, a CNN-LSTM archi-
906   tecture is considered for modeling the transitions between different semantic states over time. For
907   extracting low-dimensional embedding from each semantic view, a hierarchical CNN with ResNet
908   backbone (He et al., 2016) is considered for simplicity, with a final global average pooling (GAP)
909   layer in the network for reducing the temporal dimension of the embedding to 1, which has shown to
910   help enforces correspondence between each semantic view and the extracted embedding (Lin et al.,
911
      2014), and facilitate inclusion of all related regions in the signal in each semantic view (Zhou et al.,
      2016). Finally, a fully-connected decision network taking the concatenation of embedding vectors
912
      from all semantic views as input, is used to generate the final output of the model.
