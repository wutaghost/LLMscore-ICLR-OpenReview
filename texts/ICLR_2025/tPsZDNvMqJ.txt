009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph isomorphism (GI) is a fundamental problem in graph theory. Despite
012            recent advancements, determining whether two graphs are isomorphic remains
013            computationally challenging. This paper introduces the Polynomial Time Graph
014            Isomorphism (PTGI) algorithm, an optimization-based approach leveraging self-
015            supervision techniques to efficiently tackle the graph isomorphism problem. PTGI
016            aims to escape local optima caused by graph symmetries and provides high accu-
017            racy in identifying isomorphic graphs in polynomial time. Experimental results
018            demonstrate PTGI’s effectiveness across various graph types, making it a valu-
019
               able tool for practical applications.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Graph isomorphism (GI) is a fundamental problem in graph theory that deals with determining
025   whether two graphs are structurally identical, namely “isomorphic”, up to a relabeling of vertices.
026
      Formally, two graphs GA = (VA , EA ) and GB = (VB , EB ) are said to be isomorphic if and only if
      there exists a bijection f : VA → VB such that for any pair of vertices u, v ∈ VA , (u, v) ∈ EA if and
027
      only if (f (u), f (v)) ∈ EB . In other words, the two graphs have the same connectivity pattern, but
028
      the vertices may be labeled differently. Graph isomorphism has numerous applications in various
029   fields, including chemistry Balaban (1985); Merkys et al. (2023), biomedical sciences Sporns et al.
030   (2005); Singh et al. (2007), network analysis Cook & Holder (2006), computer vision Christmas
031   et al. (1995); Zaslavskiy et al. (2008), and pattern recognition Pelillo et al. (1999).
032
      Determining whether two graphs are isomorphic is computationally challenging. It belongs to the
033
      class of NP (nondeterministic polynomial time) problems and is one of the few remaining prob-
034
      lems in NP that is not known to be either P (polynomial time) or NP-complete Fortin (1996). The
035   main areas of research for graph isomorphism problem are design of fast algorithms and theoret-
036   ical investigations of its computational complexity, both for the general problem and for special
037   classes of graphs. While recent breakthroughs have shown that GI is solvable in quasipolynomial
038   (exp((log n)O(1) )) time Babai (2016), the demand for polynomial time algorithms remains, espe-
039   cially for real-world graphs that can be super large. Many existing polynomial time graph isomor-
040   phism algorithms only work with specific classes of graphs Hopcroft & Tarjan (1972); Babai et al.
041   (1980); Luks (1982); Grohe & Marx (2012); Babai et al. (2013); Lokshtanov et al. (2017), bringing
042   challenges for generalization to real-world graphs.
043   Due to the computational complexity of exact isomorphism checking, optimization-based graph
044   isomorphism algorithms have been proposed to efficiently tackle the graph isomorphism problem
045   Umeyama (1988); Zaslavskiy et al. (2008); Vogelstein et al. (2011); Aflalo et al. (2015). While these
046   algorithms offer improved efficiency compared to exact methods, they typically provide approximate
047   solutions rather than exact isomorphism checking. By formulating the problem as an optimization
048   task and iteratively refining the solution, optimization-based approaches strive to find a mapping
049   between the vertices of two graphs that maximizes a similarity metric or minimizes a dissimilarity
050
      metric. Despite not guaranteeing exact isomorphism, optimization-based algorithms are valuable
      tools for practical applications where efficiency is prioritized over correctness.
051
052   While optimization-based graph isomorphism algorithms have demonstrated efficient graph isomor-
053   phism checking, we found that in practice they often result in local optima when graphs possess
      symmetries, even for very simple graphs. In this case, an optimization-based GI algorithm fails

055   metries can lead to repeated eigenvalues that complicates graph isomorphism testing. This is a

062

064

066

081   2   R ELATED W ORKS
082
083   Graph Isomorphism Algorithms can vary significantly in terms of complexity, efficiency, and appli-
084   cability. Some algorithms focus on exact isomorphism checks and are suitable for small to medium-
085   sized graphs, while others employ heuristic or approximation techniques to handle larger graphs
086   more efficiently. Prominent exact graph isomorphism algorithms include:
087
            • Nauty McKay (2007): A widely-used algorithm developed by Brendan McKay for exact
088
              graph isomorphism testing and graph canonization. The worst-case time complexity of
089
              Nauty is exponential (O(2n )).
090
            • VF2 Cordella et al. (2004): A backtracking-based algorithm proposed by Cordella et al.
091
              which efficiently explores possible mappings between vertices using constraints. VF2 has
092
              a worse-case (O(n!)) factorial time complexity.
093
094
            • Ullmann’s algorithm Ullmann (1976): It uses backtracking combined with constraint prop-
              agation to explore the space of possible mappings between vertices of the two graphs.
095
              Ullmann’s algorithm has a worse-case (O(n!)) factorial time complexity.
096
097   Although these exact graph isomorphism algorithms have demonstrated efficient graph isomorphism
098   checking for many examples of large graphs, their theoretical worst-case complexity remains com-
099   putationally intractable.
100
      Due to the computational complexity of exact isomorphism checking, optimization-based graph iso-
101
      morphism algorithms leverage optimization techniques to efficiently tackle the graph isomorphism
102   problem. A commonly used optimization objective is to find a bijective vertex mapping, represented
103   by a permutation matrix, such that the adjacency disagreement of the two graphs being mapped is
104   minimized Aflalo et al. (2015). A variaty of existing approaches avoid the combinatory complexity
105   when searching for permutation matrices by relaxing the domain of permutation matrices to a track-
106   able convex or near-convex space when optimizing graph adjacency disagreement (denoted in (2)).
107   A popular approach is to relax the space of permutation matrices to the space of doubly stochastic
      matrices then solve the optimization problem in polynomial time using quadratic programming or


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      gradient descent Umeyama (1988); Zaslavskiy et al. (2008); Vogelstein et al. (2011); Aflalo et al.
109   (2015); Fiori & Sapiro (2015). An alternative relaxation technique is to replace permutation matrices
110   with orthogonal matrices Zavlanos & Pappas (2008); Klus & Sahai (2018). In practice, we found
111   that such type of optimization approaches often generate local optima for graphs possessing sym-
112   metries. Consequently, their accuracy on identifying isomorphic graph pairs is not guaranteed. For
113   example, Aflalo showed that such type of optimization approach only has high accuracy on “friendly
114   graphs”, namely graphs whose adjacency matrices have simple spectrum (i.e., all of its eigen values
115   are distinct) Aflalo et al. (2015) . Umeyama’s algorithm Umeyama (1988) requires graphs being
116   matched to be sufficiently close to each other in terms of eigenvectors. This limitation is significant
117
      from the perspective of generalization since real-world graphs are not neccessarily “friendly”.

121   3.1   T HE G RAPH I SOMORPHISM P ROBLEM
122
123   A graph is defined as G = (V, E) where V is a set of vertices and E is a set of edges. In the context
124   of this paper, we consider graphs as non-weighted and non-labeled. Two graphs GA = (VA , EA )
125
      and GB = (VB , EB ) are said to be isomorphic, denoted as GA ≃ GB , if and only if there exists
      a bijection f : VA → VB such that for any pair of vertices u, v ∈ VA , (u, v) ∈ EA if and only if
126
      (f (u), f (v)) ∈ EB . Such a bijection is called an isomorphism of graph GA and GB .
127
128   Graph isomorphism can also be equivalently defined using adjacency matrices. Given a graph G =
129   (V, E) with |V | = n, let A be the adjacency matrix of G, which is a n × n binary matrix defined as
130   follows:
131
                                                     1 if (vi , vj ) ∈ E,
                                        A = [aij ]                                                    (1)
132                                                  0 if (vi , vj ) ∈

133   Given two graphs GA = (VA , EA ) and GB = (VB , EB ) (|VA | = |VB | = n) with adjacency
134   matrices A and B, GA and GB are isomorphic if and only if there exists a permutation matrix P
135   such that A = PBP⊺ . A permutation matrix is a square binary matrix that has exactly one entry of
136   1 in each row and each column with all other entries 0. Each permutation matrix P corresponds to
137   a bijection f : VA → VB .
138   The computationional problem of determining whether two finite graphs are isomorphic is called
139   the Graph Isomorphism (GI) Problem. A graph isomorphism algorithm is a computational method
140   used to determine whether two given graphs are isomorphic.

142   3.2   A PARADIGM FOR O PTIMIZATION -BASED G RAPH I SOMORPHISM A LGORITHMS
143
144   Due to the computational complexity of exact isomorphism checking, optimization-based graph
145   isomorphism algorithms utilize optimization techniques to tackle the graph isomorphism problem
146   more efficiently but provide approximate (near-exact) rather than exact isomorphism checking. An
147   optimization-based graph isomorphism algorithm typically aims to find mapping between the ver-
148
      tices of two graphs that maximizes a similarity metric or minimizes a dissimilarity metric.
149   A commonly used dissimilarity metric in optimization is the adjacency (or connectivity) disagree-
150   ment. For two graphs GA and GB , with with adjacency matrices A and B, and a permutation matrix
151   P, the adjacency disagreement between GA and GB with respect to P is defined as ||A − PBP⊺ ||2 ,
152   where || · || denotes a norms such as the Euclidean norm (used in this paper). Let P(n) denote the
153   set of n × n permutation matrices P(n) = {P ∈ {0, 1}n×n : P1 = P⊺ 1 = 1}, where 1 is an
154
      n-dimensional column vector. Then, the graph isomorphism can be formulated as the following
      optimization problem:
155
156                                     P∗ = arg min ||A − PBP⊺ ||2 .                                    (2)
157                                             P∈P(n)
158
      The two graphs GA and GB are isomorphic if and only if ||A − P∗ BP∗⊺ ||2 = 0.
159
160   Equation (2) is a variant of the Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP), which is NP-hard and there-
161   fore has no known polynomial time solution yet. This complexity is due to the combinatorial com-
      plexity of the constraint P ∈ P(n). Relaxation techniques can reduce this complexity by replacing

      Next, we provide a theoretical time complexity analysis of the proposed PTGI algorithm. In each
325   iteration of gradient descent, the most complex computation is the multiply of three matrices, i.e.,
326   PBPT (line 6). The time complexity of this step is O(n3 ). There are at most n + 1 iterations until
327   a valid graph isomorphism is found or finally not found. Therefore, the worst-case and average-case
328   time complexity of PTGI is O(n4 ). In the best case when the graphs prossess no symmetries, a valid
329   graph isomorphism might be found in the first iteration. Therefore, the best-case time complexity of
330   PTGI is O(n3 ). PTGI has a space complexity of O(n2 ) to store n×n matrices in computer memory.
331   It is worth to note that PTGI is an approximate graph isomorphism algorithm because it does not
332   guarantee that an isomorphism between two isomorphic graphs can be found. It only guarantees
333   that two non-isomorphic graphs will not be identified as isomorphic because PTGI returns true if
334   and only if a valid isomorphism is found. Therefore, PTGI can only yield false negatives but no
335   false positives.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduced the Polynomial Time Graph Isomorphism (PTGI) algorithm, an
489   optimization-based approach leveraging self-supervision techniques to efficiently tackle the graph
490   isomorphism problem. PTGI aims to escape local optima caused by graph symmetries and provides
491   high accuracy in identifying isomorphic graphs in polynomial time. Experimental results on both
492   synthesized and real-world graph datasets demonstrated the effectiveness and efficiency of PTGI
493   compared to state-of-the-art peer methods. Moreover, the running time analysis revealed that PTGI
494   exhibits a polynomial increase in running time, running efficiently on small to medium-sized graphs
495   and scaling well to larger graphs with thousands of nodes. Overall, the results suggest that PTGI is
496   a promising tool for graph isomorphism identification tasks, offering high accuracy and efficiency
497
      across different graph types and sizes.

500
501   While the Polynomial Time Graph Isomorphism (PTGI) algorithm presented in this paper demon-
502   strates promising performance in terms of accuracy and efficiency, there are several avenues for
503   future research and improvement:
504
            • Scaling to Larger Graphs: Although PTGI shows efficient performance on graphs with
505
              up to ten thousand nodes, further optimization is needed to handle even larger graphs ef-
506
              ficiently. Exploring parallel processing techniques or distributed computing frameworks
507           could help improve scalability.
508
            • Extension to Weighted Graphs: The current version of PTGI is designed for unweighted
509
              graphs. Extending the algorithm to handle weighted graphs would broaden its applicability
510
              to a wider range of real-world scenarios.
511
512
            • Exploring Different Self-Supervision Techniques: While self-supervision has proven ef-
              fective in escaping local optima, exploring alternative self-supervision techniques or com-
513
              binations thereof could further enhance the algorithm’s performance.
514
515         • Integration with Deep Learning Approaches: Investigating the integration of deep learn-
516
              ing techniques, such as graph neural networks, into the PTGI framework could potentially
              improve its ability to capture complex graph structures and enhance its performance on
517
              challenging graph isomorphism tasks.
518
519         • Real-World Applications: Conducting extensive evaluations of PTGI on real-world appli-
520
              cations, such as molecular structure analysis, social network analysis, and bioinformatics,
              would provide valuable insights into its practical utility and effectiveness in real-world sce-
521
              narios.
