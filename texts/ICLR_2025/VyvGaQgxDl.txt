                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce Integer Scale, a novel post-training quantization scheme for large
012            language models that effectively resolves the inference bottleneck in current fine-
013            grained quantization (i.e. group-wise quantization) approaches while maintaining
014            similar accuracies. Integer Scale is a free lunch as it requires no extra calibration
015            or fine-tuning which will otherwise incur additional costs. It can be used plug-
016            and-play for most fine-grained quantization methods. Its integration results in at
017            most 1.85× end-to-end speed boost over the original counterpart with comparable
018            accuracy. Additionally, due to the orchestration of the proposed Integer Scale and
019            fine-grained quantization, we resolved the quantization difficulty for Mixtral-8x7B
020
               and LLaMA-3 models with negligible performance degradation, and it comes with
               an end-to-end speed boost of 2.13×, and 2.31× compared with their FP16 versions
021
               respectively.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The size of language models has continued to grow exponentially throughout recent years. To name
027   some iconic models, Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) initially bear 65M parameters, BERT (Devlin
028   et al., 2019) exceeds with 340M, GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) prevails with 175B, PaLM (Chowdhery
029   et al., 2022) trumps with 540B and most lately GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) is estimated to have reached
030   1.8T parameters. This seemingly unstoppable trend is largely promoted by the so-called scaling law
031   (Kaplan et al., 2020) where a model’s capability, via a proxy metric of auto-regressive maximum-
032   likelihood loss, exhibits a power-law relationship to its number of parameters, dataset sizes, and
033
      compute respectively. Not surprisingly, the intimidating number of parameters of Large Language
      Models (LLMs) place an almost insurmountable hurdle for inference, potentially preventing their
034
      pervasive applications.
035
036   However, optimizing the serving efficiency of LLMs is a non-trivial task. LLMs generally comprise a
037   compute-intense pre-filling stage and a memory-bound self-decoding stage. Exploiting integer matrix
038   multiplication speeds up the computation, but directly applying post-training quantization usually
039
      generates a large performance drop. Quantization-aware training methods like LLM-QAT (Liu et al.,
      2023) require costly computing resources to fine-tune all the weights. In contrast, post-training
040
      quantization is more affordable and commonly used in practice. For instance, SmoothQuant (Xiao
041
      et al., 2023) transforms activation outliers into weights for better quantization accuracy. Recently,
042   fine-granularity grouping (i.e. group-wise quantization as opposed to channel-wise quantization in
043   ‘coarse-grained’ quantization) (Park et al., 2022) is often used as a general paradigm to reduce the
044   quantization errors, as in ZeroQuant (Yao et al., 2022), GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), AWQ (Lin et al.,
045   2023) and FPTQ (Li et al., 2023b). FPTQ proposes a fine-grained W4A8 strategy to address the
046   memory-bound issue as a trade-off between W4A16 and W8A8. While its high quantization accuracy
047   benefits from fine-grained quantization, the actual inference is also stalled by inefficient operations
048   introduced by its intrinsic computational complexity due to fine granularity.
049   In this paper, we are driven to design a faster fine-grained quantization scheme called Integer Scale
050   that renders fewer quantization errors (Table 3) and simultaneously achieves boosted speed (see
051   Figure 1). Our contributions are multi-fold:
052
053         1. We unveil the intrinsic inference bottleneck of fine-grained LLM quantization approaches
               and find a hassle-free cure, called Integer Scale, with negligible accuracy loss. Our approach

098   Nevertheless, it becomes more challenging as we chase for the quantization of lower bit widths (e.g.

100   implementations that demand strenuous engineering effort.

358   scheme between W4A16 and non-fine-grained W4A8 for better accuracy. Interestingly, we discover
359   a “performance cliff” (gray-colored) where the acceleration ratio suddenly drops when it transits
360   from memory-bound to compute-bound scenarios. This is due to the sudden drop from the ideal 4×
361   speedup to 2× vs. FP16. It is however as expected. In small batch scenarios where the inference is
362
      mainly memory-bound, all W4 solutions could achieve nearly theoretical 4× acceleration (W4 vs.
      FP16). While in large batch scenarios where it is leaning towards compute-bound, the ratio turns into
363
      2× since INT8 tensor cores are theoretically 2× of FP16 (A8 vs. FP16). However, the fine-grained
364
      W4A8 kernel with float scale suffers from fine granularity and its speed is even inferior to W4A16,
365   for which reason we resolve it with Integer scale to achieve practical gain.

516
517   In this paper, we introduced a plug-and-play scheme called Integer Scale that can be applied to speed
518
      up the existing fine-grained quantization approaches. We showed through extensive experiments
      that the Integer Scale not only benefits from the performance boost due to fine granularity but also
519
      well resolves the intrinsic computational overhead. It can serve as a default free-lunch technique
520
      with fine-grained approaches of various bandwidths to render an overall competitive quantization
521   strategy. Moreover, the same strategy can be applied to Mixtral 8×7B based on a mixture-of-experts
522   and LLaMA-3, which were previously difficult to quantize at lower bit widths.

774   A.2   P ER - TENSOR , P ER - TOKEN , P ER - CHANNEL QUANTIZATION , G ROUP - WISE Q UANTIZATION
