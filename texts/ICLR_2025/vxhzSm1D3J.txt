009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Spectral clustering is a representative graph clustering technique with strong in-
012            terpretability and theoretical guarantees. Recently, degree-corrected spectral clus-
013            tering (DCSC) has emerged as the state-of-the-art for this technique. While prior
014            studies have provided several theoretical results for DCSC, their analysis relies
015            on some random graph models (e.g., stochastic block models). In this study, we
016            explore an alternative analysis of DCSC from a pure spectral view. It gives rig-
017            orous bounds for the mis-clustered volume and conductance w.r.t. the optimal
018            solution while involving quantities that indicate impacts of (i) high degree hetero-
019            geneity and (ii) weak clustering structures to DCSC. Inspired by recent advances
020
               in graph neural networks (GNNs) and the associated over-smoothing issue, we
               propose ASCENT (Adaptive Spectral ClustEring with Node-wise correcTion), a
021
               simple yet effective extension of DCSC. Different from most DCSC methods with
022
               a constant degree correction for all nodes, ASCENT follows a node-wise correc-
023            tion scheme. It can assign different corrections for nodes via the mean aggrega-
024            tion of GNNs. We further demonstrate that (i) ASCENT reduces to conventional
025            DCSC methods when encountering over-smoothing and (ii) some early stages be-
026            fore over-smoothing can potentially obtain better clustering quality.

032
      Graph clustering (a.k.a. disjoint community detection) is a classic inference task that partitions
      nodes of a graph into densely connected groups (a.k.a. clusters or communities). Since the extracted
033
      clusters have been validated to correspond to some substructures of real-world systems (e.g., func-
034
      tional groups in protein interactions (Berahmand et al., 2021)), many network applications (e.g.,
035   protein complex detection (Qin & Gao, 2010), cellular network decomposition (Dai & Bai, 2017),
036   and Internet traffic profiling (Qin et al., 2019)) are formulated as graph clustering.
037
038
      Spectral clustering is one of the representative techniques for this task. As summarized in Table 1, a
      typical spectral clustering algorithm includes the (I) eigen-decomposition (ED) on graph Laplacian,
039
      (II) arrangement of spectral embedding, (III) normalization of the arranged embedding, and (IV)
040
      KMeans clustering. In Table 1, A and D are the adjacency matrix and corresponding degree diag-
041   onal matrix of a graph; K is a pre-set number of clusters; λr denotes the r-th largest eigenvalue of
042   graph Laplacian L (e.g., L := D−1/2 AD−1/2 and L := A for NJW (Ng et al., 2001) and SCORE
043   (Jin, 2015)) with ur ∈ RN as the corresponding eigenvector. Different spectral clustering algo-
044   rithms usually differ in terms of the four steps. For instance, NJW, SCORE, and RSC (Qin & Rohe,
045   2013) only consider eigenvectors (u1 , · · · , uK ) w.r.t. the leading K eigenvalues. Whereas, step
046   (II) of SCORE+ (Jin et al., 2021) and ISC (Qing & Wang, 2020a) involves (u1 , · · · , uK , uK+1 ),
047   which are further reweighted by corresponding (K+1) eigenvalues (λ1 , · · · , λK , λK+1 ). Moreover,
048   NJW, RSC, and ISC adopt the row-wise l2 -normalization in step (III), while SCORE and SCORE+
049   use (reweighted) u1 to conduct column-wise normalization.
050   Recently, degree-corrected spectral clustering (DCSC), a.k.a. regularized spectral clustering in some
051   literature (Qin & Rohe, 2013; Zhang & Rohe, 2018), has emerged as a state-of-the-art class of
052   spectral clustering methods, due to their effectiveness in handling the high degree heterogeneity of
053   graphs. These approaches usually incorporate an additional degree correction term τ in their graph
      Laplacian for ED (e.g., RSC, SCORE+, and ISC with different settings of τ in Table 1).

070   Related Theoretical Analysis on DCSC. In the past few decades, a series of spectral clustering
071   methods have been proposed. Ding et al. (2024) provided a comprehensive overview of related
072   research. Table 2 summarizes some representative theoretical results regarding DCSC. We introduce
073   more related work about recent advances in deep graph clustering in Appendix A.
074   As in Table 2, Chaudhuri et al. (2012) proposed a DCSC method for graphs drawn from an extended
075   planted partition (EPP) model (Condon & Karp, 2001) and examined the performance guarantees.
076   Qin & Rohe (2013) analyzed the potential of RSC to handle the high degree heterogeneity of graphs
077   using the degree-corrected stochastic blockmodel (DCSBM) (Karrer & Newman, 2011) and pro-
078   vided guidance on the choice of τ . (Zhang & Rohe, 2018) theoretically studied the (i) failures of
079   spectral clustering and (ii) benefits of degree correction based on the relationship between graph
080   conductance and spectral clustering. Amini et al. (2013) introduced a fast pseudo-likelihood method
081
      for fitting DCSBM with theoretical guarantees, where a DCSC algorithm with perturbations was
      used for initialization. Qing & Wang (2020a) and Jin et al. (2021) proposed ISC and SCORE+,
082
      which were further validated to be effective in handling the (i) high degree heterogeneity and (ii)
083
      weak clustering structures (a.k.a. weak signals in (Qing & Wang, 2020b;a; Jin et al., 2021)) via the
084   theoretical analysis based on DCSBM.
085
086   In summary, most existing theoretical studies of DCSC rely on some assumptions of random graph
087
      models (e.g., EPP model and DCSBM). They usually fit the adjacency matrix or graph Laplacian
      using a certain random graph model (e.g., A := ΘZBZT Θ (Qin & Rohe, 2013) with {Θ, Z, B}
088
      as notations defined in DCSBM) and further give theoretical bounds related to such a model (e.g.,
089
      mis-clustered rate and Hamming error w.r.t. the ground-truth given by DCSBM).
090
091   Present Analysis on DCSC & Extension. Spectral clustering is a typical approximated algorithm
092   for the NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem of conductance minimization (Von Luxburg,
093
      2007). Based on this nature, some early studies (Peng et al., 2015; Mizutani, 2021) analyzed vanilla
      spectral clustering (e.g., NJW) using the spectral graph theory. Motivated by these studies, we
094
      consider an alternative analysis for DCSC from a pure spectral view, instead of using random graph
095
      models. Different from existing analysis on DCSC with bounds related to a random graph model,
096   we provide theoretical bounds for the mis-clustered volume and conductance w.r.t. the optimal
097   solution to conductance minimization. In contrast to early spectral-based studies on vanilla spectral
098   clustering (Peng et al., 2015; Mizutani, 2021), our analysis involves additional quantities about (i)
099   degree heterogeneity and (ii) weakness of clustering structures, which can help reveal impacts of (i)
100   high degree heterogeneity and (ii) weak clustering structures to DCSC.
101
      Inspired by recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) and the associated over-smoothing
102   issue (Rusch et al., 2023), we propose ASCENT (Adaptive Spectral ClustEring with Node-wise
103   correcTion), a simple yet effective extension of DCSC. Instead of using a constant correction term
104   τ for all nodes (e.g., RSC, SCORE+, and ISC in Table 1), ASCENT follows a node-wise correction
105   scheme, where nodes {vi } are allowed to be assigned with different corrections {τi }. Such a scheme
106   iteratively updates {τi } via the mean aggregation of GNNs, where nodes {vi } with more common
107   high-order neighbors (e.g., in the same cluster) are more likely to have close {τi }. Consistent with
      the over-smoothing issue of GNNs, {τi } will finally converge to a constant. In this case, ASCENT

      reduces to conventional DCSC methods. Our experiments demonstrate that some early stages of this
109   updating procedure (i.e., before over-smoothing) can potentially result in better clustering quality.

114   In general, an undirected and unweighted simple graph can be represented as a 2-tuple G := (V, E),
115
      where V := {v1 , · · · , vN } and E := {(vi , vj )|vi , vj ∈ V } are the sets of nodes and edges. One can
116
      use an adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N to describe the topology of G, where Aij = Aji = 1 if
      (vi , vj ) ∈ E and Aij = P Aji = 0 otherwise. Let D := diag(d1 , d2 , · · · , dN ) be the degree diagonal
117
      matrix of G, with di := j Aij as the degree of node vi .
118
119   Given a graph G and a pre-set number of clusters K, graph clustering (a.k.a. disjoint community
120   detection) aims to partition
                            S      V into K disjoint subsets (C1 , · · · , CK ), which are defined as clusters
121   or communities, with r Cr = V and Cr ∩ Ct = ∅ (∀r ̸= t) s.t. (i) within each cluster the edge
122   connections between nodes are dense but (ii) between clusters the connections are relatively loose.
123   Note that we follow the classic problem statement of spectral clustering, where graph topology is
124   the only available information source. Different from most deep graph clustering methods (Nazi
125   et al., 2019; Bo et al., 2020; Bianchi et al., 2020; Tsitsulin et al., 2023; Bhowmick et al., 2024), our
126   analysis does not consider graph attributes, due to the complicated correlations between topology
127   and attributes validated by prior studies (Newman & Clauset, 2016; Qin et al., 2018; Wang et al.,
128   2020; Qin & Lei, 2021). Concretely, the simple integration of attributes may bring inconsistent
129
      features or noise that lead to quality decline compared with the case only considering topology,
      although attributes may sometimes provide complementary information for better clustering quality.
130

281
      Mizutani, 2021), a graph is defined to be well-clustered, if ϕ̄K (G)/(1 − λK+1 ) is sufficiently
282   small, consistent with that ϕ̄K (G)/(1 − λK+2 ) is small (λK+2 ≥ λK+1 ). The well-clustered as-
283   sumption adopted by early work (Ng et al., 2001; Mizutani, 2021) indicates that the optimal solution
284   (Ŝ1 , · · · , ŜK ) describes an explicit clustering structure of G. Moreover, large dmin /[(dmax +τ )] in-
285   dicates that the degree heterogeneity should not be very high. Therefore, the condition in Theorem 8
286   implies an assumption that (i) G is well-clustered and (ii) the degree heterogeneity is not so high.
287   In particular, the adjustment of τ can also help resist the impacts of these two aspects. For instance,
288   a larger τ can result in smaller eigenvalues λ1 and λK+2 , which further lead to smaller ΨISC and
289   larger 1/[132(1 + λ1 )2 αµ̃K]. The condition is more likely to satisfy.

      6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we provided an alternative analysis of DCSC from a pure spectral view. Different from
530   most existing studies on DCSC that gave theoretical results associated with random graph models
531
      (e.g., DCSBM), our analysis gives bounds for the mis-clustered volume and conductance w.r.t. the
      optimal solution to conductance minimization objective without using random graph models. In
532
      contrast to early studies on vanilla spectral clustering, the presented analysis also includes quantities
533
      that indicate impacts of (i) degree heterogeneity and (ii) weakness of clustering structures to the clus-
534   tering quality of DCSC. Inspired by recent advances in GNNs and the associated over-smoothing
535   issue, we proposed ASCENT, a simple yet effective extension of DCSC. It follows a novel node-
536   wise correction scheme that assigns nodes {vi } with different correction terms {τi } via the mean
537   aggregation of GNNs. We further demonstrated that ASCENT reduces to conventional DCSC meth-
538   ods when encountering the over-smoothing issue. Experiments also validated that some early stages
539   before over-smoothing can potentially obtain better clustering quality for ASCENT. Due to space
      limit, we discuss limitations and possible future directions of this study in Appendix J.

           A     R ELATED W ORK OF D EEP G RAPH C LUSTERING M ETHODS
727
           In recent years, several deep graph clustering methods have been proposed based on different model
728
           architectures and training objectives as reviewed in (Yue et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022).
729
730        GraphEncoder (Tian et al., 2014) and DNR (Yang et al., 2016) are early studies that learn low-
731        dimensional community-preserving representations (a.k.a. embeddings) by reconstructing topology-
732
           related features (e.g., normalized adjacency matrices and modularity matrices) via a deep auto-
           encoder. A downstream clustering algorithm (e.g., KMeans) is then applied to the learned em-
733
           bedding to derive a feasible clustering result. SDCN (Bo et al., 2020) further combines deep auto-
734
           encoder with GNN and uses a dual self-supervised mechanism to unify these two deep architectures.
735        Moreover, GAP (Nazi et al., 2019), ClusterNet (Wilder et al., 2019), MinCutPool (Bianchi et al.,
736        2020), and DMoN (Tsitsulin et al., 2023) adopt a deep end-to-end structure, which contains a GNN
737        and an output module (e.g., a multi-layer perceptron for the derivation of clustering results), to fit
738        some classic graph clustering objectives (e.g., normalized cut minimization (Von Luxburg, 2007)
739        and modularity maximization (Newman, 2006)). DGCluster (Bhowmick et al., 2024) also uses the
740        modularity maximization objective to optimize GNN, which outputs community-preserving embed-
741        ding but derives final clustering results using BIRCH (Zhang et al., 1996).
742        Most of the aforementioned methods, especially those based on GNNs, were originally designed
743        for attributed graphs. We argue that most of them do not consider the complicated correlations
744        between graph topology and attributes as discussed in Section 2. Our empirical experiments (see
745        Section 5) also demonstrated that when attributes are unavailable, these deep graph clustering (with
746        standard strategies to extract auxiliary feature inputs from topology) cannot effectively handle the
747        (i) high degree heterogeneity and (ii) weak clustering structures of graphs. Different from spectral
748        clustering methods, most existing studies about deep graph clustering also lack interpretability and
749
           theoretical guarantees.

       G     C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
1116
       Given a large-scale graph, we usually have K, L ≪ N < |E|. Assume that the graph to be par-
1117
       titioned is sparse. For ASCENT, the time complexity of deriving node-wise corrections {τi } (i.e.,
1118
       lines 1-5 in Algorithm 1) is no more than O(|E|L) = O(|E|) by fully utilizing the sparsity of a
1119   graph and the sparse-dense matrix multiplication operation. ASCENT follows the same steps of (i)
1120   ED, (ii) spectral embedding arrangement, (iii) embedding normalization, and (iv) KMeans cluster-
1121   ing with ISC, which have complexities of (i) O((N + |E|)K) = O(|E|) (using the efficient Lanczos
1122   algorithm (Lehoucq et al., 1998) for ED), (ii) O(N K) = O(N ), (iii) O(N K) = O(N ), and (iv)
1123   O(N K 2 t) = O(N ) (with t ≪ N as the number of iterations in KMeans), respectively. In sum-
1124   mary, the overall time complexity of ASCENT is about O(|E|). It has the same complexity with
1125   most existing DCSC algorithms. Therefore, the additional step of deriving node-wise corrections
1126   {τi } will not increase the complexity of ASCENT.

1430   Learnable Node-wise Corrections {τi }. In ASCENT, we still manually set the node-wise correc-
1431   tions {τi } by adjusting hyper-parameters {θ, L}. We plan to extend it to a more advanced setting

1434   Better Efficiency and Scalability. As demonstrated in our efficiency analysis (cf. Appendix I),
1435   ED is the major bottleneck of ASCENT. We intend to further improve the efficiency and scalability
1436   of this bottleneck using the advanced Locally Optimal Block Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient
1437   (LOBPCG) solver (Knyazev, 2001; Zhuzhunashvili & Knyazev, 2017) and consider its parallel im-
1438   plementations (Yamada et al., 2022).
1439   Other Graph Clustering Objectives. In this study, we only considered the conductance mini-
1440   mization objective (or equivalently normalized cut minimization) as defined in Definition 1. Spec-
1441   tral clustering can be considered as an approximated algorithm for a relaxed version of this objec-
1442   tive. Some graph clustering algorithms may consider other objectives (e.g., ratio-cut minimization
1443   (Von Luxburg, 2007) and modularity maximization (Newman, 2006; Yu & Ding, 2010; Qin et al.,
1444   2024)) that have relations close to conductance minimization. We also plan to further extend our
1445
       analysis to these objectives.
1446   Improved Analysis with Looser Conditions. As discussed in Section 3, the condition in Theo-
1447   rem 8 implies an assumption that (i) G is well-clustered and (ii) the degree heterogeneity is not so
1448   high. It is possible for a given graph G that this condition may not hold. In our future work, we
1449   intend to further improve this condition by extending some new theoretical results on the combinato-
1450   rial optimization problem of graph-cut minimization (e.g., conductance minimization in this paper)
1451
       to DCSC.
