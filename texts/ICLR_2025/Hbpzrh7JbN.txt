009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative molecular design for drug discovery has very recently achieved a wave
012            of experimental validation, with language-based backbones being the most com-
013            mon architectures employed. The most important factor for downstream success is
014            whether an in silico oracle is well correlated with the desired end-point. To this end,
015            current methods use cheaper proxy oracles with higher throughput before evaluating
016            the most promising subset with high-fidelity oracles. The ability to directly opti-
017            mize high-fidelity oracles would greatly enhance generative design and be expected
018
               to improve hit rates. However, current models are not efficient enough to consider
               such a prospect, exemplifying the sample efficiency problem. In this work, we
019
               introduce Saturn, which demonstrates the first application of the Mamba architec-
020
               ture for generative molecular design. We elucidate how experience replay with data
021            augmentation improves sample efficiency and how Mamba synergistically exploits
022            this mechanism. Saturn outperforms 22 models on multi-parameter optimization
023            tasks relevant to drug discovery and may possess sufficient sample efficiency to
024            consider the prospect of directly optimizing high-fidelity oracles. The code is
025            available at https://figshare.com/s/21059896530e222b9cd5.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Within the last year, there has been a surge of works reporting experimental validation of generative
031   molecular design for drug discovery (Du et al., 2024). The fundamental task of generative molecular
032   design is learn a distribution of molecules with tailored property profiles. All generative models
033   achieve this in one of two ways: distribution learning, where a base model is subjected to transfer
034
      learning on a set of known positives, and goal-directed generation, which encompasses both condi-
      tional generation and using an optimization algorithm to shift the distribution. Experimental validation
035
      has been demonstrated for all methods, but with a notable over-representation from optimization algo-
036
      rithms as of the last 9 months, particularly reinforcement learning (RL) (Du et al., 2024). Algorithmic
037   molecular optimization always proceeds via the following workflow: generate molecules, assess
038   desirability using an in silico oracle, update the model, and repeat. When assessing the suitability of
039   molecules absent experimental validation, the crucial indicator to success is correlation of an in silico
040   oracle to the actual end-point. All protocols that directly optimize for an oracle without the use of a
041   surrogate predictor follow a funnel workflow where less resource-intensive oracles are initially used
042   to prioritize the most promising subset for evaluation with computationally expensive high-fidelity
043   oracles. A concrete and ubiquitous example is designing molecules with high binding affinity to a
044   protein target. By far the most common oracle used to estimate binding affinity is molecular docking,
045
      and many works (Guo et al., 2021; Thomas et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2021; Lee
      et al., 2023; 2024; Fu et al., 2022) have demonstrated the ability to generate molecules with improved
046
      docking scores. However, docking scores are often poorly correlated with binding affinity, especially
047
      when applied out-of-the-box (Guo et al., 2021; Crivelli-Decker et al., 2024). Correspondingly, the
048   most promising candidates from docking are subjected to higher-fidelity oracles, particularly molec-
049   ular dynamics (MD) simulations, which offer a much more accurate estimation of binding affinity
050   (Wang et al., 2019; Moore et al., 2022; 2023; Crivelli-Decker et al., 2024), but with industry-standard
051   methods typically being closed-source (Moore et al., 2023). Directly optimizing high-fidelity oracles
052   offers the prospect of learning the distribution and can greatly improve the quality of the generated
053   set (Eckmann et al., 2024). However, doing so is infeasible due to computational cost, exemplifying
      the sample efficiency problem. Either simulation protocols become much faster without sacrificing

054
      accuracy, or generative models become sufficiently efficient to optimize under an acceptable oracle
055   budget. We note that QSAR models are often used, which can have great predictive accuracy, but
056   may suffer from a narrow domain of applicability (within their training data) (Neves et al., 2018).
057
058   Recently, more works have explicitly considered sample efficiency by constraining the oracle budget
059
      on various drug discovery optimization tasks (Yang et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Guo & Schwaller,
      2024a;b; Lee et al., 2023; 2024; Shen et al., 2023). More recently, Guo et al. (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a)
060
      proposed Augmented Memory which is built on REINVENT (Olivecrona et al., 2017; Blaschke et al.,
061
      2020a). It combines experience replay with SMILES augmentation (Weininger, 1988; Bjerrum, 2017)
062   and empirically shows that this data augmentation can improve sample efficiency. In this work, we
063   push towards the prospect of direct optimization of high-fidelity oracles and release Saturn. First, we
064   elucidate the mechanism of Augmented Memory, which uses an LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
065   1997) recurrent neural network (RNN) as the language model backbone, and characterize exactly
066   how data augmentation and experience replay improve sample efficiency. Next, we systematically
067   assess more advanced generative architectures from just RNNs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) to
068   decoder transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019), and the recent Mamba (Gu & Dao,
069   2023) state space model (SSM). Our results show that the Mamba architecture, in conjunction with
070
      data augmentation and experience replay, displays synergistic behavior to improve sample efficiency
      by strategic overfitting. Our contribution is as follows:
071
072
            1. We show the first application of Mamba for molecular generative design and specifically for
073
               goal-directed generation with reinforcement learning.
074
075         2. We elucidate the mechanism into how Augmented Memory improves sample efficiency, as
076            the original work only showed its empirical benefits.
077         3. We comprehensively evaluate language model backbones (> 500 experiments, all across 10
078            seeds) including RNN, decoder transformer, and Mamba, which enables us to characterize
079            model-intrinsic and scaling properties that lead to improved sample efficiency.
080
            4. Through ablation studies, we demonstrate that local sampling in chemical space is a key
081
               component for sample efficiency. Our results provide discourse on the nature of optimization
082
               landscapes commonly encountered in drug discovery.
083
084         5. We propose Saturn, which leverages Mamba and outperforms 22 models on multi-parameter
085            optimization (MPO) drug discovery tasks under heavily-constrained oracle budgets.

090
      tailored molecular generation. Existing works have tackled this problem using a variety of architec-
091   tures, including SMILES (Weininger, 1988)-based RNNs (Olivecrona et al., 2017; Segler et al., 2018;
092   Popova et al., 2018; Neeser et al., 2023), transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019;
093   Bagal et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Mazuz et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; He et al.,
094   2024), variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018;
095   Jin et al., 2018; Zhavoronkov et al., 2019), adversarial approaches (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kadurin
096   et al., 2017; De Cao & Kipf, 2018; Ivanenkov et al., 2023), graph-based models (You et al., 2018;
097   Jin et al., 2020b; Mercado et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Maziarz et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023),
098   GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2023; 2021; Shen et al., 2023), genetic algorithms (GAs) (Mitchell, 1998;
099
      Jensen, 2019; Fu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024), and diffusion models (Lee et al., 2023; Igashov et al.,
      2024; Schneuing et al., 2023). However, many works do not explicitly consider an oracle budget (or
100
      use a very lenient budget) and focus mostly on showing that goal-directed generation is possible. The
101
      release of the PMO benchmark (Gao et al., 2022) highlighted that improvements in sample efficiency
102   are vital to even consider the prospect of directly optimizing high-fidelity oracles, e.g., MD may
103   take GPU hours per molecules (Moore et al., 2023). In the benchmark, the oracle budget is 10,000,
104   but as we push towards high-fidelity oracles, a more stringent budget would be necessary. More
105   recent works (Yang et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Guo & Schwaller, 2024a;b; Lee et al., 2023; 2024;
106   Shen et al., 2023) have enforced fixed oracle budgets when comparing performance against other
107   methods. All the objective functions considered in these works include docking, which is used in
      every single experimentally validated structure-based generative design case study (Du et al., 2024)

131   Language-based Molecular Generative Models. Text is one of the most widely used molecular
132   representations, with common ones being simplified molecular-input line-entry systems (SMILES)
133   (Weininger, 1988) and self-referencing embedded strings (SELFIES) (Krenn et al., 2020; 2022).
134   Recent work has shown that the former is generally more performant, despite not enforcing 100%
135   validity (Gao et al., 2022; Skinnider, 2024). Leveraging advances in natural language processing
136   (NLP), language-based molecular generative models are amongst the first and still widely used
137
      models, encompassing RNNsOlivecrona et al. (2017); Segler et al. (2018); Popova et al. (2018),
      transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019; Bagal et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Feng
138
      et al., 2023; Mazuz et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024), and recently SSM S4 (Özçelik
139
      et al., 2024). In early benchmarks (GuacaMol (Brown et al., 2019) and MOSES (Polykovskiy et al.,
140   2020)), language-based models have been shown to essentially solve the validity, uniqueness, and
141   novelty metrics. Subsequently, the non-injective syntax of SMILES confers advantageous properties
142   for generative design. Specifically, a single molecule can be expressed as at least N (number of
143   heavy atoms) SMILES, in a process known as SMILES augmentation, enumeration, or randomization
144   (Bjerrum, 2017). This mechanism can be exploited to pre-train models under low data regimes to
145   generalize in chemical space (Arús-Pous et al., 2019; Moret et al., 2020; Skinnider et al., 2021),
146   improve sample efficiency (Bjerrum et al., 2023; Guo & Schwaller, 2024a), and perform transfer
147   learning with a single positive example (Ballarotto et al., 2023). Despite the recent trend towards
148
      3D molecular generation (Igashov et al., 2024; Schneuing et al., 2023), language-based models
      have demonstrated the ability to generate molecules that satisfy 3D-dependent objectives, such as
149
      docking in a sample-efficient manner (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a;b). This suggests that language-based
150
      models are not entirely 3D-naive and can effectively explore relevant regions of the 3D chemical
151   space. Finally, language models are amongst the most sample-efficient models (Gao et al., 2022;
152   Polykovskiy et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2019) and most studies achieving experimental validation of a
153   generated molecule incorporate SMILES-based models (Du et al., 2024).

223   Differences to Previous Works. Saturn adapts Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a) but
224   differs in several important ways. Firstly, unlike the original work, we elucidate the mechanism into
225   why Augmented Memory can improve sample efficiency and explicitly show that it makes generating
226   the replay buffer molecules likely. The following Results section will show that high sample effi-
227   ciency can be achieved by local sampling, whereby the modeled distribution is strategically overfit
228   on these replay buffer molecules. Precisely, this means making the Agent particularly likely, but
229   not deterministic, to generate any SMILES sequence form of the replay buffer molecules. By nature
230   of multinomial decoding, stochastic generation means that unique sampled molecules might only
231   differ by a small number of tokens, which translates to the molecules differing by a small number
232   of atoms. Secondly, we show that Mamba synergistically enhances this mechanism by nature of
233   being a proficient distribution learner. Exactly because Mamba can overfit the distribution of replay
234   buffer molecules, it displays the greatest degree of local sampling. To accommodate repeat generated
235   molecules, we introduce an oracle cache under the assumption that oracle evaluations are near
236   deterministic (for docking oracles, we fix the seed). If the same SMILES is generated at a later epoch,
237   the reward is retrieved from the cache and does not impose an oracle call. Finally, by showing that
238   strategic overfitting can be beneficial, we further demonstrate that scaling up architectures (Appendix
239   F.6) can also improve sample efficiency. This offers discourse into benefits of architectural differences
240   in the small molecule goal-directed generation regime. We show that there are benefits despite the
241   modeled sequences being relatively short (< 80 tokens).

251   4.1   PART 1: E LUCIDATING THE O PTIMIZATION DYNAMICS OF S ATURN
252
253   We begin by identifying the optimal architecture and hyperparameters for Saturn. First, we experiment
254   with varying the batch size and augmentation rounds of the Augmented Memory algorithm (Guo &
255   Schwaller, 2024a), and explicitly demonstrate the trade-off between sample efficiency and diversity.
256   Unlike the original Augmented Memory work, which used an RNN backbone, we investigate more
257
      advanced architectures: decoder transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019) and Mamba
      (Gu & Dao, 2023). Our analysis elucidates how SMILES augmentation, combined with these
258
      architectures, synergistically improves sample efficiency in Saturn. The key mechanism is local
259
      sampling in chemical space, whereby relatively small atomic changes are made to high-reward replay
260
      buffer molecules.
261
262   Experimental Details. We define a toy experiment with the following MPO objective: molecular
263   weight (MW) < 350 Da, number of rings ≥ 2, and maximize topological polar surface area (tPSA)
264   (Guo & Schwaller, 2024b). Optimizing this objective requires generating molecules with rings
265   saturated with heteroatoms, which are dissimilar from the training data. Hence, it is also testing out-
266
      of-distribution optimization. All experiments in this section were run across 10 seeds (0-9 inclusive)
      with an oracle budget of 1,000, and the models were pre-trained with ChEMBL 33 (Gaulton et al.,
267
      2012) (Appendix C.1).

      Understanding the Limits of Augmented Memory. Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller,
      2024a) improves sample efficiency by repeated learning from high reward SMILES. With decreasing
275
      batch size, performance variance increases, as the approximation to the expected reward (Eq. 2)
276
      becomes more noisy. In return, fewer oracle calls are imposed, and the Agent learns from an
277   increasingly smaller set of unique SMILES. We hypothesize that as long as unique high reward
278   SMILES are still generated, sample efficiency can improve with decreasing batch size, at the expense
279   of diversity. We perform a grid search and vary the batch size (64, 32, 16, 8) and augmentation
280   rounds (0-20 inclusive) using the default RNN architecture (Appendix 5). We make the following
281   key observations: with increasing augmentation rounds and decreasing batch size, sample efficiency
282   improves, diversity decreases, and generating repeated SMILES becomes increasingly prevalent but
283   is tolerable with oracle caching. The optimal augmentation rounds and batch size are 5-10 and 16,
284   respectively, as pushing further introduces too much variance, such that apparent improvements are
285
      not statistically significant (at the 95% confidence level). In Appendix C.4, we explored the addition
      of Beam Enumeration (Guo & Schwaller, 2024b) but improvements were not consistently statistically
286
      significant. In Appendix C.5, we explored allocating a portion of the oracle budget to a GA, which
287
      decreases sample efficiency, but recovers diversity, in agreement with previous works (Liu et al.,
288   2021; Lee et al., 2024). Finally, see Appendix C.2 for systematic ablation studies on the effect of
289   every component of Saturn.

293

295   and inconsistently improves Yield and OB for RNN and transformer. Mamba more consistently
296   benefits from increasing augmentation rounds to generate more high reward molecules and also faster.
297   Across the Yield and OB metrics, Mamba consistently outperforms both the RNN and transformer
298   backbones. Given Mamba’s superior sample efficiency, we focus our analysis on comparing it to the
299   RNN baseline in the remainder of this section (transformer results are provided in Appendix C.3)
300   Mamba: Enhanced Maximum Likelihood. Table 15 shows that the Mamba architecture notably
301   generates repeated SMILES, which can be rationalized with the maximum likelihood objective.
302   Mamba (5.2M) and RNN (5.8M) have similar parameter counts but during pre-training, the former
303   converges to a lower loss during pre-training (Appendix C.1), indicating a better match to the data
304   distribution. Accordingly, and during RL, Eq. 4 aims to make generating high reward SMILES
305   more likely. Mamba generates repeated SMILES suggesting it overfits the data distribution. We
306   demonstrate this by cross-referencing Fig. 2a, which shows that with high augmentation rounds, the
307   average max conditional token probability (during generation) approaches 1, and near collapses to a
308
      Dirac delta function (less so for RNN). This makes it likely, but not deterministic, to generate the
      same SMILES repeatedly.

      Mamba: Hop-and-Locally-Explore. Mamba approaches Dirac delta function collapse (Fig. 2a)
355   when learning from repeated augmented SMILES and in the previous section, we have shown that
356   the Agent becomes increasingly likely to generate the buffer molecules. We hypothesized that
357   Mamba exhibits a "hop-and-locally-explore" behavior: because it is likely to generate some SMILES
358   representation of these molecules (strategic overfitting), small changes to any tokens in these set of
359   augmented sequences equates to small changes to the same molecular graph, essentially performing a
360   local exploration (similar molecules, on average, exhibit similar properties, provided the property
361   landscape is not too rough (Aldeghi et al., 2022)). We verify our hypothesis with the following
362   experiment: generate molecules (3,000 oracle budget) and separate the generated set into 10 chunks
363
      (each 300 SMILES). We trace the generation trajectory using UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) and plot
      the chunk centroids, comparing Mamba and the baseline (vanilla Augmented Memory) (Fig. 2d).
364
      Mamba traverses chemical space in an increased directional manner and the chunks are more locally
365
      confined. Further analysis into the intra- and inter-chunk Tanimoto similarity reveals that within
366   chunks, Mamba exhibits much greater similarity than the baseline, and similarity is always lower
367   between chunks (Fig. 2e). Taking these observations together, Mamba (batch size 16) with Augmented

369   "hop-and-locally-explore" behavior (see Appendix D for further quantitative and qualitative analyses).

485   and matches GEAM. We believe this is still a fair assessment as computing Tanimoto similarity is

      5     C ONCLUSION
517
518
519   In this work, we present Saturn, a framework for sample-efficient de novo molecular design us-
520
      ing memory manipulation. We demonstrate the first application of the Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023)
      architecture for generative molecular design with reinforcement learning and show how it syner-
521
      gistically leverages SMILES augmentation and experience replay for enhanced sample efficiency.
522
      Through systematic study, we elucidate the mechanism of Augmented Memory (original work only
523   showed its empirical benefits) and show it squeezes sequence generation likelihoods such that it
524   becomes increasingly likely to generate some SMILES representation of the replay buffer molecular
525   graphs. Next, we show how Mamba leverages this mechanism to improve sample efficiency through
526   "hop-and-locally-explore" behavior. With the optimal architecture and hyperparameters identified for
527   sample efficiency in a test experiment, we apply Saturn on two sets of MPO tasks relevant to drug
528   discovery, outperforming all baseline models and the recent GEAM (Lee et al., 2024) model which,
529   when released, outperformed all baselines by a large margin. Compared to GEAM, we further show
530   that Saturn achieves superior MPO, finding desirable molecules faster with fewer oracle calls, albeit
531
      with a trade-off in diversity. Our work opens up the prospect of directly optimizing expensive high-
      fidelity oracles (beyond docking), which are more correlated with relevant drug discovery end-points.
532
      Recent work has applied multi-fidelity learning (Eckmann et al., 2024) or active learning (Loeffler
533
      et al., 2024a; Dodds et al., 2024) to enable on-the-fly update of a surrogate model to predict such
534   oracle evaluations for generative design. These workflows can be applied directly with Saturn, but
535   importantly, we may be sufficiently efficient to directly optimize these oracles, mitigating surrogate
536   out-of-domain concerns. Moreover, it is straightforward to augment Saturn with known strategies
537   to improve sample efficiency, such as curriculum learning (Guo et al., 2022) as we have shown in
538   Part 3. Correspondingly, future work will stress-test Saturn on high-fidelity oracles and interrogate
539   the prospect of directly optimizing QM/MM and free energy (Wang et al., 2019; Moore et al., 2022;
      2023; Crivelli-Decker et al., 2024) protocols with modest computational resources.

      B    W HAT IS S ATURN ?
910
911
      Saturn is a language-based generative molecular design framework which features minimal imple-
912   mentations of Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a) and Beam Enumeration (Guo &
913   Schwaller, 2024b). These two methods were first implemented here: https://github.com/
914   schwallergroup/augmented_memory, which in turn was built on REINVENT version 3.2
915   (Olivecrona et al., 2017; Blaschke et al., 2020a): https://github.com/MolecularAI/
916   Reinvent. REINVENT is still under active development and version 4 (Loeffler et al., 2024b)
917   was recently released, supporting a wide range of generative tasks including small molecule design
      (Olivecrona et al., 2017; Blaschke et al., 2020a), library design (Fialková et al., 2021), linker design


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      (Guo et al., 2023), proposing small modifications (He et al., 2021), and sampling nearest neighbors
919   (Tibo et al., 2023).
920
921   Saturn (at the moment) focuses only on generative small molecule design and research development
922
      is on sample efficiency. It is a much smaller code-base than REINVENT 4 and with focus on
      minimal implementation. That being said, the key new additions to Saturn include: extending small
923
      molecule generative architecture from just RNN in REINVENT to Decoder transformer (Vaswani
924
      et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). Secondly, allowing oracle caching
925   to track repeated generations and allow pre-screening specified oracles (in an MPO objective, some
926   oracle components may be computationally inexpensive and it would be practical to first screen a
927   molecules through these oracles before any expensive components). Thirdly, implementation of
928   a genetic algorithm which couples GraphGA (Jensen, 2019) on the replay buffer such that new
929   molecules can be generated from the replay buffer parent sequences. In the ensuing subsections, we
930   describe in detail these key new additions.
931
932   B.1    G ENERATIVE A RCHITECTURE
933
934   Many initial language-based molecular generative models were RNN-based (Olivecrona et al., 2017;
935   Segler et al., 2018; Neil et al., 2018; Popova et al., 2018). Early benchmarks (GuacaMol (Brown
936   et al., 2019) and MOSES (Polykovskiy et al., 2020)) assessed whether generated molecules were
937   valid (RDKit parsable), unique, and novel (not in the training data). RNNs satisfy these metrics and
938
      can learn distributions well (Flam-Shepherd et al., 2022). More recently, with the prevalence of the
      transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019) architecture, many works (Bagal et al., 2021;
939
      Wang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Mazuz et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Thomas et al.,
940
      2022; Yang et al., 2020) have suggested a replacement of RNNs for generative design. However, many
941   performance assessments only focus on validity, uniqueness, novelty, and optimizing for permissive
942   oracles such as logP, QED (Bickerton et al., 2012) ("drug-likeness"), and the SA score (Ertl &
943   Schuffenhauer, 2009). Some works show that transformers can learn longer SMILES sequences
944   better than RNNs (Feng et al., 2023) (such as natural products). However, often, one actually wants
945   to limit sequence length to constrain design to small molecules. Furthermore, recent works have
946   coupled transformers with reinforcement learning (RL) (Feng et al., 2023; Mazuz et al., 2023; Hu
947   et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Thomas et al., 2022) but the performance is not necessarily better than
948   RNNs. Consequently, it is unclear whether the benefits of transformers are strictly advantageous for
949
      small molecule generation.
950   In this work, we extend Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a) to Decoder transformer
951   (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). Our results show that
952   transformers display similar performance to RNNs for small molecule generation, in agreement with
953   previous literature findings (Thomas et al., 2022). We further demonstrate the first application of
954   Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) for goal-directed generation, supplementing recent work investigating S4
955
      models for transfer learning (Özçelik et al., 2024).
956
957   B.2    M AMBA A RCHITECTURE
958
959   In Saturn, we empirically find that the Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) architecture is the most parameter-
960
      efficient in our RL framework when tuning for sample efficiency. Note that in Appendix F.6, we find
      that scaling up the decoder transformer (25.3M) Vaswani et al. (2017); Radford et al. (2019) to about
961
      5x the size of the Mamba (5.2M) results in similar performance. However, due to less GPU load for
962
      smaller models, we chose Mamba as the default architecture in Saturn. Mamba was recently proposed
963   as an alternative to transformers, with linear time training in contrast to the quadratic attention scaling.
964   In decoder transformers, self-attention is the key component:

1015
1016   In many reinforcement learning (RL) set-ups, the reward is assumed to be stationary, i.e., it does
1017   not change on repeat evaluation. This is an assumption that is not always true for physics-based
1018   oracles relevant in drug discovery. For example, docking depends on the initial conformer generated,
1019   and even more so for molecular dynamics simulations. However, it is reasonable to assume that the
1020   reward is near deterministic given a reasonably well behaved protein system (in which preliminary
1021   studies were made to verify the oracle stability). In effect, the reward for repeat molecules can be
1022
       retrieved from a cache, thus not imposing additional oracle evaluations. In this work, we show that
       under this assumption, Saturn can leverage the Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) architecture for enhanced
1023
       sample efficiency. In particular, Mamba displays low uniqueness, but we show this is not detrimental.
1024
1025   As any given molecule can have numerous SMILES representations (via augmentation (Bjerrum,
       2017)), it is important to store the canonical SMILES in the cache, and also to canonicalize sampled


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       batches when querying the cache. Canonicalization is simply a pre-defined traversal and can differ
1027   depending on the method used. As long as all canonicalization operations are performed with the
1028   same method, consistency can be guaranteed. In this work, we use RDKit.

       B.4   G ENETIC A LGORITHM
1031
1032   Genetic algorithms (GAs) by themselves can be sample-efficient molecular optimizers (Gao et al.,
1033   2022; Jensen, 2019; Tripp & Hernández-Lobato, 2023). Previous work has shown that GAs can
1034   improve diversity of the generated set (Liu et al., 2021). Recently, Lee et al. (Lee et al., 2024)
1035   proposed Goal-aware fragment Extraction, Assembly, and Modification (GEAM) which combines
1036   RL with a GraphGA (Jensen, 2019) and achieves impressive results on generating diverse hits. In
1037   Saturn, we implement GraphGA on the replay buffer itself, treating the highest rewarding molecules
1038
       generated in the entire run so far, as the parent population. Following GEAM (Lee et al., 2024),
       sampling the parents is done with probability proportion to their corresponding rewards. New
1039
       molecules from crossover and mutation operations are deposited into the Buffer if they are also
1040
       high rewarding, essentially refreshing the buffer, such that Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller,
1041   2024a) can learn from these new SMILES. The motivation was to leverage the GA to counteract
1042   decreases in diversity and potentially improve sample efficiency. In the results in the main text
1043   and in the following sections, we show that applying the GA does not lead to improved sample
1044   efficiency but does indeed recover diversity. We believe that this can be a useful modification to the
1045   optimization algorithm in cases where relatively expensive oracles are used and diversity is important
1046   due to prevalence of false positives. Concretely, higher-fidelity oracles should in principle model
1047   physical behavior more accurately, such that true positives are more common. This can be shown in
1048   previous works where using free energy simulations provide better correlations with binding affinity
1049
       (Eckmann et al., 2024; Crivelli-Decker et al., 2024). In such a case, sample efficiency becomes
       increasingly important, as the goal is to simply generate molecules satisfying this simulation and
1050
       lower diversity is not detrimental. However, when using lower-fidelity oracles, more false positives
1051
       means it is beneficial to have more diverse ideas for downstream triaging. Finally, we note that
1052   applying the GA and generating new molecules strictly means they were generated off-policy (in the
1053   RL context). Therefore, more meaningful updates to the Agent may be achieved with importance
1054   sampling (Schlegel et al., 2019), which we did not explore in the current work.

       C.2   U NDERSTANDING THE L IMITS OF AUGMENTED M EMORY
1364
1365   Augmented Memory (Guo & Schwaller, 2024a) improves sample efficiency by repeated learning on
1366   the high reward SMILES stored in the replay buffer (referred to as Buffer from here on). For com-
1367   pleteness, we first describe how repeated learning can be achieved via data augmentation. SMILES
1368   are string representations resulting from performing a depth-first search (DFS) on a molecular graph
1369   (as is done in RDKit). Depending on the starting node (atom in the molecule), a different SMILES
1370   representation results from DFS. In Augmented Memory, SMILES augmentation is performed by
1371   shuffling the atom order and yielding different SMILES representations of the same molecular graph.
1372   This is useful as data augmentation because all of these SMILES representations map to the same
1373   molecule, yet their sequence likelihoods are different. Since they map to the same molecule, the same
1374   reward can be assigned to all augmented SMILES.
1375
1376   In the original work, ablation experiments showed that updating the Agent with only the Buffer re-
1377
       sulted in minimal difference. This suggests that a viable way to exploiting the gains from Augmented
       Memory is to simply have new examples of high reward SMILES being added to the Buffer. In the
1378
       original work, the number of augmentation rounds was capped at two to mitigate mode collapse. In
1379
       this work, we assume near deterministic rewards and use caching to handle repeated generations. Un-
1380   der this assumption, our hypothesis in this subsection is: as long as unique high reward SMILES are
1381   generated, increasing augmentation rounds can further improve sample efficiency. Correspondingly,
1382   we perform a grid search using Augmented Memory’s default generator architecture (LSTM (Hochre-
1383   iter & Schmidhuber, 1997) RNN) and vary the batch size (64, 32, 16, 8) and augmentation rounds
1384   (0-20 inclusive except 1) where 0 augmentation rounds is equivalent to REINVENT (Olivecrona
1385   et al., 2017; Blaschke et al., 2020a). The results are shown in Tables 5, 6, 7, and 8.
1386   Increasing augmentation rounds:
1387
1388         1. Decreases diversity, as expected.
1389
1390
             2. Increases the number of repeated SMILES.
1391
       Decreasing batch size:
1392
1393
             1. Monotonically improves sample efficiency (though not always significant at the 95% confi-
1394
                dence level).
1395
1396         2. Benefits Augmented memory more than REINVENT (0 augmentation rounds).
1397         3. Increases the number of repeated SMILES.
1398
1399
             4. Increases variance, as expected (since the expected reward is being approximated with a
                smaller batch size so it is more noisy).
1400

1402

       can trade-off diversity for sample efficiency (inconsistently and with higher variance).

1547   Further note. We have observed that with low batch size and high augmentation rounds, Mamba
1548   can temporarily lose generative ability. Specifically, the validity of the generated batch can be 0.
1549
       Sampling a new batch can recover this validity but we have observed in extremely rare cases, that
       validity can be 0 for over 10 successive epochs. We observed this scenario twice in over 5,000
1550
       experiments, occurring with a batch size of 8 and augmentation rounds 19 and 20. We speculate the
1551
       reason is extreme mode collapse to a chemical space where syntax is sensitive. Consequently, once
1552   the Selective Memory Purge starts penalizing the reward and the Agent is brought back towards the
1553   prior, large gradient updates coupled with sensitive syntax may cause invalid SMILES. This process
1554   often recovers but in practice, with high-fidelity oracles, one would checkpoint models frequently
1555   (even every epoch), as each batch of oracle evaluation would be costly. Alternatively, as all high
1556   reward SMILES (so far) generated can be pre-emptively saved. It would be feasible to even start a
1557   new run with these SMILES seeded in the replay buffer, akin to inception in REINVENT (Olivecrona
1558   et al., 2017) (transfer learning would work too). This would kick-start the optimization and already
1559   guide the Agent to this chemical space, preventing optimization progress from completely "lost".
1560
       Moreover, we also do not recommend a batch size of 8 and augmentation rounds above 10 as the
       performance variance becomes high. This behavior is likely also highly dependent on the objective
1561
       function which affects the optimization landscape. Finally, in the rare cases this occurs, and when
1562
       validity recovers, the effect is minimal as sampling is cheap compared to oracle evaluations. We write
1563   this note for full transparency into all the behavior we have observed in our grid-search.

       C.4     A RE I NCREASED AUGMENTATION ROUNDS STILL S YNERGISTIC WITH B EAM
1729           E NUMERATION ?
1730
1731   Beam Enumeration (Guo & Schwaller, 2024b) extracts the most probable substructures for self-
1732   conditioned generation and has been shown to be synergistic with Augmented Memory (Guo &
1733   Schwaller, 2024a) such that the Yield and OB improve. In the original work, the oracle budget in
1734   the experiments was 5,000. In this work, we are interested in minimizing the oracle budget and all
1735   experiments thus far use a 1,000 oracle budget. Beam Enumeration has a Patience criterion which
1736
       controls when substructures are extracted: only when the average reward improves for Patience
       number of successive epochs. Since we are operating at a much lower oracle budget, it is especially
1737
       unclear whether Beam Enumeration can still benefit sample efficiency (we note that the explainability
1738
       aspect is still applicable). In the original work, a batch size of 64 was used and a Patience of 5. Under
1739   these parameters, the earliest that Beam Enumeration can execute is 320/1000 oracle calls, which is
1740   almost 1/3 the budget already. Moreover, Beam Enumeration decreases diversity and decreasing batch
1741   size and increasing augmentation rounds also decreases diversity. Too much decrease in diversity
1742   may be detrimental even with oracle caching. In this subsection, we systematically study the effect of
1743   Beam Enumeration when used in conjunction with decreasing batch size and augmentation rounds in
1744   a series of hypotheses.
1745   Based on observations from batch size and augmentation rounds grid-searches, the following
1746   design decisions were made in this subsection:
1747
1748         1. Augmentation rounds capped at 5 as diversity generally decreases more substantially past
1749            this point. Beam Enumeration itself will decrease diversity, so this is a preemptive measure
1750            against detrimental diversity-induced mode collapse.
1751         2. Investigate batch sizes of 64 and 32. Since Beam Enumeration executes on improved reward
1752            over successive epochs, lower batch sizes would likely increase performance variance too
1753            much.
1754         3. Focus only on RNN model as experiments will be the fastest (less repeated SMILES). If
1755            benefits are observed, move to Decoder and Mamba models. For clarity, repeated SMILES
1756            are not detrimental, as we have shown in the previous subsections but they add some wall
1757            time (this is insignificant when compared to expensive oracles).
1758         4. Beam Enumeration can pool improbable substructures. There is a Patience Limit denoting
1759            the number epochs permitted where the entire generated batch is filtered. This limit was

1762            (seldom) to manage wall times since we are performing grid searches.
1763         5. Use Minimum Structure Size = 15, unless otherwise stated. Enforcing larger substructure
1764            extraction was found to improve sample efficiency in the original work (Guo & Schwaller,
1765            2024b)

       C.5   H ALLUCINATED M EMORY: I S IT BENEFICIAL TO ALLOCATE A PORTION OF THE ORACLE
2107         BUDGET TO HALLUCINATION ?
2108
2109   In this section, we investigate coupling GraphGA (Jensen, 2019) to Saturn. GraphGA in itself a
2110   sample-efficient generative algorithm (Gao et al., 2022) and was recently used in the GEAM model
2111   proposed by Lee et al. (Lee et al., 2024) which achieves impressive MPO performance. Previously
2112   work (Liu et al., 2021) found that coupling a GA in RL can encourage diverse sampling. In the
2113   previous sections, we have identified Mamba with batch size 16 and 10 augmentation rounds as the
2114
       best hyperparameters so far. The improved sample efficiency comes at a trade-off in diversity. The
       objective in the experiments to follow is to investigate whether allocating a portion of the oracle
2115
       budget to GraphGA generation (which we call "hallucinating") is beneficial in recovering diversity
2116
       while maintaining sample efficiency.
2117
2118   Before presenting the grid-search results, we describe the GraphGA integration further. GraphGA
2119   is only activated when the replay buffer is full (100 SMILES). Once full, at every epoch thereafter,
2120
       the replay buffer itself is treated as the parent population to generate new SMILES. These new
       SMILES are then concatenated with the sampled batch (16 SMILES) and used to update the Agent.
2121
       Importantly, these hallucinated SMILES are also deposited into the replay buffer (if they possess
2122
       higher reward). Finally, 100 SMILES are hallucinated and either 5 or 10 are selected. The selection
2123   criteria are Random or Tanimoto Distance. Random selects at random while Tanimoto Distance
2124   selects via maximum fingerprint dissimilarity to the replay buffer. Our rationale is that dissimilar new
2125   SMILES will help encourage diversity since Augmented Memory heavily biases towards the replay
2126   buffer SMILES.
2127
       The grid-search investigated the following hyperparameter settings:
2128
2129         1. Fix Mamba with batch size 16
2130
             2. Augmentation Rounds = [5,20]
2131
2132
             3. GA with Random and Tanimoto Distance selection criterion
2133         4. Select 5 or 10 hallucinations at every epoch
2134
2135
       The reason we increased the augmentation rounds back to 20 in our grid-search is because if indeed
       the GA recovers diversity, then the "augmentation tolerability" of Saturn would probably be increased.
2136
       Higher augmentation rounds lead to more repeated SMILES precisely due to overfitting. If new
2137
       high reward SMILES refresh the replay buffer, Saturn may be more tolerable to higher augmentation
2138   rounds to potentially further improve sample efficiency. The results of the grid-search are presented
2139   in Tables 23 and 24.
2140
2141
       Observations. The results show that coupling a GA to the replay buffer does not improve sample
       efficiency. However, we make several interesting observations. Firstly, the number of repeated
2142
       SMILES notably drops and IntDiv1 (Polykovskiy et al., 2020) recovers. This is in agreement with
2143
       our hypothesis and previous work (Liu et al., 2021) that coupling a GA to RL can recover diversity.
2144   Secondly, hallucinating SMILES does indeed lead to some replacement of the replay buffer, and
2145   hence, these SMILES are necessarily are high reward. Thirdly, rarely are the hallucinated SMILES
2146   the best in the buffer. Finally, we note that hallucinated SMILES are generated off-policy and Agent
2147   updates may be more meaningful with importance sampling (Schlegel et al., 2019), which we did not
2148   explore this this work.
2149
2150   C.6   S ATURN : F INAL H YPERPARAMETERS
2151

2153
       and 10 augmentation rounds. The results in the immediate previous section shows that the GA can
       recover diversity, which can be a useful setting that can easily be activated on and off depending on
2154
       the oracle setting.

       F.4   S ATURN -TANIMOTO
2575
2576   In GEAM (Lee et al., 2024), the "Novel" in Novel Hit Ratio enforces molecules to possess <
2577   0.4 Tanimoto similarity to ZINC 250k (Sterling & Irwin, 2015). GEAM achieves this by use of
2578   their fragment assembly and genetic algorithm which directly uses GraphGA (Jensen, 2019). The
2579   crossover and mutation operations promote diversityLiu et al. (2021). Otherwise, generative models
2580   are pre-trained to model the training data distribution. This means that generated molecules would
2581   not necessarily be particularly dissimilar to the training data, especially if the training data actually
2582   possesses "good" molecules already. By virtue of pre-training on a selected dataset, we implicitly
2583
       assume that the pre-training dataset is "good" for our task, otherwise, we probably should not pre-train
       on this data. This is the rationale on why ChEMBL (Gaulton et al., 2012) and ZINC 250k (Sterling &
2584
       Irwin, 2015) are popular pre-training datasets: they contain bio-active molecules. To satisfy GEAM’s
2585
       "Novel" criterion, we take the base Saturn model and first teach it to generate molecules that are
2586   dissimilar to the ZINC 250k dataset which was used for pre-training. The objective function is then
2587   defined as minimizing the max Tanimoto similarity to any molecule in ZINC 250k. This experiment
2588   was run with an oracle budget of 1,500 and took about 10 minutes. The resulting Saturn-Tanimoto
2589   model generates molecules with low Tanimoto similarity to ZINC 250k. Starting from this model,
2590   we run GEAM’s case study and the results from this are reported in the main text and here in the
2591   Appendix. We finally note that this criterion is somewhat arbitrary and we do it so we can exactly
       match GEAM’s experiments.

       In the main text Part 1, we investigated why Mamba (5.2M) outperforms LSTM (Hochreiter &
2693
       Schmidhuber, 1997) RNN (5.8M) and Decoder transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al.,

2695   augmented forms of any replay buffer molecules. Increased capacity to match this distribution directly
2696   leads to the "hop-and-locally-explore" behavior which improves sample efficiency. We note that our
2697   observations are for optimization landscapes that are not too rough (Guo et al., 2022; Aldeghi et al.,
2698   2022). It is difficult to know a priori the roughness of optimization and also whether the benefits of
2699   "hop-and-locally-explore" behavior is beneficial in higher-fidelity oracle settings. We leave this for
       future work.

2701   transformer models. Increasing model size can lead to lower loss convergence, which in this case,
