                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               In the medical domain, numerous scenarios necessitate the long-form generation
012            ability of large language models (LLMs). Specifically, when addressing patients’
013            questions, it is essential that the model’s response conveys factual claims, high-
014            lighting the need for an automated method to evaluate those claims. Thus, we in-
015            troduce MedLFQA, a benchmark dataset reconstructed using long-form question-
016            answering datasets related to the biomedical domain. We use MedLFQA to facili-
017            tate a cost-effective automatic evaluations of factuality. We also propose O LAPH,
018            a simple and efficient framework that utilizes cost-effective and multifaceted au-
019            tomatic evaluation to construct a synthetic preference set and answers questions
020
               in our preferred manner. Our framework leads us to train LLMs step-by-step to
               reduce hallucinations and include crucial medical claims. We highlight that, even
021
               on evaluation metrics not used during training, LLMs trained with our O LAPH
022
               framework demonstrate significant performance improvement in factuality. Our
023            findings reveal that a 7B LLM trained with our O LAPH framework can provide
024            long answers comparable to the medical experts’ answers in terms of factuality.
025            We believe that our work could shed light on gauging the long-text generation
026            ability of LLMs in the medical domain. Our code and datasets are available.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the increasing versatility and exceptional performance of large language models (LLMs), their
032   utilization in the medical or clinical domain is expanding rapidly (Singhal et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
033
      2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2024; Labrak et al., 2024; Jeong
      et al., 2024). One of the greatest advantages of LLMs in these domains is their capability to assist
034
      or even replace physicians’ tasks (Egli, 2023; Tian et al., 2024). This includes scenarios such as
035
      question answering (multi-choice (Jin et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2019; Pal et al.,
036   2022; Xiong et al., 2024) or span-based (Krithara et al., 2023)), reporting a patient’s Electronic
037   Health Record (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022), and conversations based on patient
038   inquiries (Liévin et al., 2024). In the medical domain, numerous situations necessitate the long-
039   form text-generation ability of LLMs. Among these, answering questions posed by patients demands
040   conveying factual and crucial claims, highlighting the necessity for an automated method to evaluate
041   these responses.
042   To address this challenge, it is important to measure the ability of open-foundation LLMs to answer
043   in a long-form text. Thus, we aim to verify it through long-form question-answering (LFQA) tasks.
044   LFQA is a task that requires elaborate and in-depth answers to open-ended questions (Fan et al.,
045   2019; Stelmakh et al., 2022). Here, two main challenging points arise: One is that models should
046   not hallucinate or generate false information (Min et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Manes et al., 2024).
047   For example, when a patient asks, what could be causing the white tongue? the response should
048   convey crucial information about why white tongue occurs and its causes (e.g., white tongue is
049   usually caused by a buildup of bacteria and dead cells on the surface of the tongue) while ensuring
050
      that incorrect information (e.g., it is usually harmful and permanent) is not provided.
051   Another challenge lies in the difficulty of automatically evaluating long-text responses. Existing
052   tasks such as summarization or LFQA assess whether appropriate words are used and the seman-
053   tic meaning is well encapsulated (Min et al., 2023; Falke et al., 2019; Laban et al., 2022; Fabbri
      et al., 2022; Krishna et al., 2023). Furthermore, other methods consist of manually verifying the

068   2023a). In particular, in the medical field, it’s also important to ensure that the information provided

073   To this end, we aim to gather existing LFQA datasets and reconstruct them as a benchmark for

081   We then introduce a simple and efficient framework O LAPH (Optimizing Large language models’
082   Answers with Preferences of mitigating Hallucination), which leverages cost-effective and auto-

086   pre-trained LLM to a question-answering task (Ouyang et al., 2022) (Section 4.1). Then, we derive
087

      ence set by adopting cost-effective and multifaceted automatic evaluations (Section 4.2). Then, we
088
      construct a preference set in every steps using previous-step models with self-generated responses
089
      and iteratively train with alignment tuning until convergence (Section 4.3 and 4.4). Overall, our
090   framework generates synthetic preference sets using automatic evaluation metrics and iteratively
091   trains LLMs with preferred responses the model generates.
092
093
      Our findings reveal that learning through our O LAPH framework step-by-step can enhance long-
      text generation abilities by prioritizing factuality, semantic similarities, and word composition. We
094
      find that making a synthetic preference set with self-generated responses based on a wide range
095
      of evaluation criteria and iteratively training on the set increases the desired abilities in a long-text
096   generation. Our findings also highlight that, even on evaluation metrics not used during training,
097   LLMs equipped with our O LAPH framework demonstrate significant performance improvement in
098   factuality. Surprisingly, 7B models trained with our framework can generate long-form answers
099   comparable to medical experts’ answers which are proven to be high-quality answers.
100
      Overall, our contributions are as follows: (1) We introduce MedLFQA, a benchmark dataset with
101
      restructured formats of current biomedical LFQA benchmark datasets that enables automatic evalu-
102   ation of the long-text generation ability of open foundation LLMs. (2) In this process, we constitute
103   two statements that can automatically evaluate factuality cost-effectively through medical claims
104   originated by long answers, aiding in a comprehensive understanding of long-text generation abil-
105   ities. (3) We introduce the simple and efficient O LAPH framework, which leverages automatic
106   evaluation to generate synthetic preference sets and employs iterative learning through preference
107   optimization. (4) In our findings, we demonstrate that 7B models can generate long answers com-
      parable to the medical experts’ answers in terms of factuality.

112   Long-form question answering (LFQA) is a task requiring elaborate and in-depth answers to open-
113   ended questions (Fan et al., 2019; Stelmakh et al., 2022; Krishna et al., 2021). In the biomedical
114   and clinical domains, LFQA is essential for effectively integrating AI into real-world applications.
115   Despite its importance, there has been relatively little effort to construct patient-centered LFQA
116   datasets due to its domain specificity. In other words, numerous scenarios necessitate the long-text
117
      generation ability of LLMs in these domains but provided with restricted amounts of usable data
      due to removing the identifying details for privacy. To expand the facilitation of clinical situations,
118
      we adopt LFQA benchmark datasets to explore how well open foundation LLMs respond to the
119
      content that consumers or patients typically inquire about, utilizing benchmarks that gather such
120   inquiries (Singhal et al., 2023; Manes et al., 2024; Abacha et al., 2017; 2019).

      2.2   E VALUATING L ONG -T EXT G ENERATION

124   The main challenge in conducting comprehensive research on the LFQA benchmark is the diffi-
125   culty in automatic evaluation (Xu et al., 2023). Prior works provide various metrics for evaluating
126   language models’ responses such as comparing the quality of machine-generated text to reference
127   text (Lin, 2004; Ganesan, 2018) and capturing non-trivial semantic similarities (Papineni et al., 2002;
128   Sellam et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019). With the increasing demand for using responses generated
129   by LLMs, concurrent research also focuses on whether these responses accurately contain factual
130
      content and avoid generating false knowledge (i.e., hallucination) (Wei et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2022;
      Lin et al., 2022; Pal et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
131
      2024; Dahl et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a).

189   3.1      R ECONSTRUCTION OF B IOMEDICAL L ONG -F ORM Q UESTION -A NSWERING DATASETS
190
191
      The essential part of answering the patient’s question is conveying factual claims without false
      knowledge. To this end, the authors (Manes et al., 2024) provide 1,212 patient questions originating
192
      from real-world conversations held on AI-driven clinical platform (i.e., K Health) containing long-
193
      form answers and two optional statements: Must Have Statements indicating that a model must
194   include this statement to be medically accurate (e.g., providing all contraindications for a drug) and
195   Nice to Have Statements indicating that the statements are supplemental (e.g., providing additional
196   conditions where this drug may be helpful). These two statements provide an effective way to con-
197   duct an automatic evaluation of identifying factuality. Although the pairs of questions and answers
198   are curated by medical experts, the dataset containing long-form answers is only limited to 202 pairs.
199
      In this work, we introduce MedLFQA, which is constructed by expanding and reformulating current
200   LFQA benchmark datasets to evaluate models’ responses automatically. To this end, we gather four
201   biomedical LFQA datasets: LiveQA (Abacha et al., 2017), MedicationQA (Abacha et al., 2019),
202   HealthSearchQA (Singhal et al., 2023), and K-QA (Manes et al., 2024). We describe the statistics
203   of the benchmark datasets in Table 1. Each MedLFQA instance is comprised of four components:
204   question (Q), long-form answer (A), Must Have statements (MH), and Nice to Have statements
205   (NH). Specifically, LiveQA and MedicationQA datasets contain patients’ questions and their medi-
206   cal experts’ answers. HealthSearchQA only includes patients’ questions without their answers and
207   crucial claims. In the K-QA dataset, the remaining examples (83%) that only consist of consumer
208
      questions are referred to as the K-QA Silver dataset.
209   In detail, if a medical expert’s answer exists, we create the two statements by decomposing the an-
210   swer. For datasets containing only patients’ questions, we generate answers and statements using
211   proprietary large language models such as GPT-4.1 For example, Figure 1 shows that the long-form
212   answer generated by GPT-4 contains essential information, such as the pros and cons effects of
213   Lexapro, compared to the golden answer that is curated with medical experts. We qualify the gener-
214
      ated answers and statements through medical experts and provide the details in further section 3.2.

      We introduce O LAPH (Optimizing Large language models’ Answers with Preferences of mitigating
258
      Hallucination), a simple and efficient framework designed to optimize responses of language models
259   (LMs) by aligning them with preference collections. We first train with supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
260   to familiarize the model with the question-answering task using relatively small data samples (Sec-
261   tion 4.1). Then, we obtain k sampled predictions sampled with temperature sampling (Guo et al.,
262   2017). We evaluate these predictions using diverse evaluation metrics to distinguish preferred and
263   dispreferred answer (Section 4.2). Then, we make a preference set in every steps using previous-step
264   model with self-generated responses and train with direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
265   et al., 2024) (Section 4.3). Finally, we iteratively tune our LLMs until convergence (Section 4.4).

      4.1   S UPERVISED F INE - TUNING

272   SFT leverages relatively smaller data of labeled samples to tailor a pre-trained LLM to specific
273   tasks (Ouyang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023). Rather than training on human annotations or pseudo-
274   optimal responses generated by larger language models, we set a self-generated response as a labeled
275   answer of next step training to remove the dependency on resources in annotation datasets (Chen
276   et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). In other words, we generate multiple self-generated responses using
277   sampling-based inferences of temperature sampling, and from these responses we select the one that
278
      scores the highest according to the automatic evaluation categories as the gold-standard label for the
      next step of training. We train the LLM with SFT as below,
279
280                                     πSF T = maxE(x,a∗ )∼D∗ log π(a∗ |x)                                      (3)

306   4.3   D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
307
308   We use the concept of direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) to optimize a
309   student model πθ to maximize the likelihood of generating less hallucinated text (Tian et al., 2023;
310
      Zhang et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024; Dahl et al., 2024). We agree with the notion that language
      models already embody a certain level of knowledge about potential responses (Saunders et al.,
311
      2022; Kadavath et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b). Hence, we believe that among the responses generated
312
      through sampling, there may be predictions that closely resemble the desired ways of answering the
313   question. Therefore, we aim to enhance the quality of long-text responses through DPO learning,
314   adjusting the student model πθ finely to generate the preferred response rw over the dispreferred
315   response rl . We train the student model πθ as below,
316
317                              L(θ) = E(x,rw ,rl )∼DC log σ(rθ (x, rw )) − rθ (x, rl ))
318                       rθ (x, r) = β(log πθ (r|x) − log πSF T (r|x))
319   where the student model πθ is first initialized with SFT model πSF T and trained through preferred
320
      response rw over dispreferred response rl . DC refers to the collected preference and dispreference
321
      sets, β controls to prevent the πθ deviating from πSF T , and σ refers to the sigmoid function.

352   4.4       I TERATIVE L EARNING WITH S ELF - GENERATED P REFERENCE S ET
353
354   Our O LAPH framework iteratively trains LLMs through DPO multiple times, presenting situations
355   where each step contains distinguishing between preferred and dispreferred responses based on the
356   cost-effective automatic evaluations to make preference set. Through this process, we have two
357   benefits: (1) In each step, constructing a synthetic preference set with self-generated responses
358
      using temperature sampling can eliminate dependency on human-annotated datasets, which require
      labor-intensive work. (2) Applying cost-effective and multifaceted evaluation metrics enhances the
359
      overall quality of long-form answers, showing improvement in unseen evaluation metrics as well.
360
      These benefits lead us to design O LAPH framework to train iteratively until convergence.
361
362   In summary, our O LAPH framework utilizes cost-effective and multifaceted automatic evaluation to
363   construct a synthetic preference set and answers questions in our preferred manner, which leads us
364
      to train LLMs step-by-step to reduce hallucinations and include crucial medical claims.

368   Training Setup. We employ SFT to familiarize the model with the question-answering task and
369   proceed to self-generate labels with a relatively small data sample. Subsequently, in the first DPO
370   training, we encourage the model to prefer responses with high evaluation scores from its self-
371   generated sampling predictions, while discouraging responses that are nonsensical or repetitive.
372   Then, the iterative DPO training steps are conducted to subtly differentiate between properly gener-
373   ated responses using a lower learning rate compared to the first DPO training. The model focuses
374   on learning from well-generated responses, as well as prioritizing factuality, semantic similarities,
375   and word composition.

517   7   C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
518
519
      We introduce O LAPH, an efficient framework designed to reduce hallucinations and include crucial
      claims by utilizing cost-effective and multifaceted automatic evaluation to select the best response
520
      from sampled predictions and structuring answers in a preferred format. We also present MedLFQA
521
      which has been reconstructed into a unified format containing long-form answers and crucial state-
522   ments, facilitating cost-effective automatic evaluation. Our findings show that current 7B LLMs
523   are not reliable enough to generate long-form answers to medical questions. However, by utilizing
524   our O LAPH framework, which includes step-by-step processes like SFT, preference set construction
525   based on multifaceted automatic evaluation, and iterative alignment tuning, 7B models can produce
526   answers with sufficient factual accuracy, semantic similarity, and coherent word composition.
527
      One limitation could be that we compare and analyze models with a size of 7B parameters, which
528   is suitable for environments with limited resources. It is necessary to consider models with smaller
529   or larger parameter sizes to determine the effectiveness of our method in confirming results and
530   analysis. However, if the model is smaller than 7B, there is a lower probability of generating correct
531   predictions, and sampling predictions may not yield proper responses. Also, MedLFQA consists of
532   biomedical knowledge predefined within a fixed timestamp, which could raise outdated issues in the
533   future. Finally, there is a possibility of error propagation in the evaluation due to the use of trained
534   NLI models. However, our approach is aimed at establishing evaluation metrics that can replace
535   tremendous API costs in a cost-effective manner.
536   With the advancement of LLM’s generation abilities, our study demonstrates that 7B LLMs are ca-
537   pable of producing long-text medical answers at a desirable level, when trained with the appropriate
538   data and methods. For future work, if 7B or even larger LLMs are enabled to comprehend a patient’s
539   history and engage in multi-turn conversations, they could be sufficiently utilized to assist physicians
      as a conversation agent specialized at responding in a personalized situation.

823   A.2     S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS OF H YPERPARAMETERS
824
825   Sensitivity Analysis of α3 . We aimed to conduct a comprehensive sensitivity analysis on the hy-
826   perparameter settings to determine the factuality. In Table 5 and 6, we provide the detail experiments.
827   In our experiments, we fixed α1 and α2 at a value of 1, while varying α3 in increments of 0.2 from
828   0 to 1. These experiments were carried out using the BioMistral-7B and Self-BioRAG 7B models,
829   with training data of LiveQA, MedicationQA, HealthSearchQA, and KQA-Silver, and evaluation
830   data of KQA-Golden dataset.
831   A notable observation is that while performance on evaluation metrics such as word composition
832   and semantic similarity consistently improves, setting α3 to 0 results in minimal changes in factu-
833   ality. Furthermore, increasing the value of α3 (moving to higher values) correlates with improved
834   factuality scores. We also found that iterative DPO training reduces the standard deviation in over-
835   all scores, indicating that as training progresses, the model’s confidence increases, leading to more
836   reliable answers.
837
838   Sensitivity Analysis of Pre-determined Threshold. The criteria for dividing the actual prefer-
839   ence set and dispreference set are determined according to Equation 4. The threshold defines what
840   response should align to preferred or dispreferred response. If the model’s response exceeds the
841   threshold, that response is included in the preferred set, while responses below the threshold are in-
842
      cluded in the dispreferred set, creating pairs that are used in next-step DPO training. In other words,
      the threshold helps construct the pair dataset by distinguishing between preferred and dispreferred
843
      responses based on whether their automatic evaluation scores are above or below the threshold.
844
845   To comprehensively understand why the threshold is needed, we first introduce our range of each
846   evaluation category used in the Equation 4. We want to note that the scaling normalization was ap-
847   plied to ensure all evaluation metrics have an equal scale range. For example, in the case of ROUGE
848
      scores, each score value is set between 0 and 1. However, for comprehensiveness or hallucination,
      which measures factuality, the score range is from -100 to 100. To eliminate variations due to these
849
      scaling differences, we performed scale normalization so that ROUGE, BLEURT, and BERTScore
850
      all operate on the same scale. Below are the lower and upper bounds for each evaluation metric,
851   followed by the score ranges for each evaluation category:
852
853           • Words Composition: [0, 3] with α1 = 1.0; scaling up to [0, 300] to match the number
854             range to the other hyperparameters.
855
856           • Semantic Similarity: [Unknown, 2] with α2 = 1.0; scaling up to [Unknown, 200] to match
857
                the number range to the other hyperparameters.
858           • Factuality: [-100, 100] with α3 = 1.0.
859
860   The pre-determined threshold of Equation 4 is set as 200. This value was intuitively determined by
861   manually evaluating the model’s responses according to the authors’ preferences. Generally, when
862   evaluating multiple answers, responses that scored high on average across all items exceeded 200, so
863   this value was set as our threshold. However, recognizing that this method requires careful review,
      we conduct the experiments by setting a broad range of thresholds (0, 50, 100, 150, 200) in Table 7.

      A.3     E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
892   We use 8 Nvidia A100 with 80GB memory to train our O LAPH framework. Our code is written
893   in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and HuggingFace (Wolf et al., 2019). We use Deepspeed stage
894   3 (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) to implement multi-GPU settings and FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022)
895   for efficient training. We use a 5e-7 learning rate with a 0.1 warmup ratio in the initial step and
896   use a 1e-7 learning rate after Step-2 DPO training. We use a 0.01 β value to train through DPO
897   learning. For sampling predictions using temperature sampling (Guo et al., 2017), we generate k=6
898   predictions: one for deterministic prediction (τ = 0) and five for sampling predictions (τ = 1.0).
899   To preserve the quality of long-form responses, we set the pre-determined threshold as 200. For
900
      inference, we use vllm (Kwon et al., 2023) to speed up our inference time.

       Details of Biomedical Knowledge and Retriever. In the fields of medical and clinical domains,
1000   researchers and doctors often supplement their knowledge with additional information to address
1001   challenging issues effectively. Similarly, for a language model to solve problems, it needs to retrieve
1002   relevant documents if necessary. To accomplish this, we utilize the MedCPT (Jin et al., 2023)
1003   retriever off-the-shelf, which is contrastively trained on an unprecedented scale of 255M query-
1004   article pairs from PubMed search logs. We compile data from four sources to retrieve relevant
1005   documents: PubMed Abstract, PMC Full-text, Clinical Guidelines, and English Medical Textbooks.
1006   To ensure computational efficiency, we encode these data offline. The documents are segmented
1007   into chunks of 128 words with 32-word overlaps to form evidence, following previous works(Wang
1008
       et al., 2019; Karpukhin et al., 2020). Initially, we retrieve the top-20 evidence from each source
       data, resulting in a total of 80 evidence pieces. Subsequently, we employ the reranking module to
1009
       obtain the final top-20 evidence relevant to the query. Table 10 presents the overall statistics of the
