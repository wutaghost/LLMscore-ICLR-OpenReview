009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Watermarking, the practice of embedding imperceptible information into media
012            such as images, videos, audio, and text, is essential for intellectual property pro-
013            tection, content provenance and attribution. The growing complexity of digital
014            ecosystems necessitates watermarks for different uses to be embedded in the same
015            media. However, in order to be able to detect and decode all watermarks, they
016            need to coexist well with one another. We perform the first study of coexistence
017
               of deep image watermarking methods and, contrary to intuition, we find that vari-
               ous open-source watermarks can coexist with only minor impacts on image quality
018
               and decoding robustness. The coexistence of watermarks also opens the avenue
019
               for ensembling watermarking methods. We show how ensembling can increase
020            the overall message capacity and enable new trade-offs between capacity, accu-
021            racy, robustness and image quality, without needing to retrain the base models.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Watermarking, the encoding of information into media such as images (Zhu et al., 2018), video
027   (Doerr and Dugelay, 2003; Luo et al., 2023), audio (Hua et al., 2016) or text (Liu et al., 2024) in
028   imperceptible ways, has long been a cornerstone tool for intellectual property protection. While
029
      watermarking is not a new technology, it is experiencing a resurgence in interest as a result of the
      recent growth of AI-generated content and the increased societal expectations and scrutiny (Longpre
030
      et al., 2024). Watermarking has been proposed as a tool for restoring stripped content provenance
031
      metadata (Collomosse and Parsons, 2024), and for content attribution, where it can be used to trace
032   what training data influenced a newly generated sample (Asnani et al., 2024). In spite of concerns
033   about stripping and spoofing watermarks (Zhao et al., 2023), standards such as C2PA (C2PA, 2024)
034   implement solutions with visual similarity search, manifest databases, and robust fingerprinting.
035
      Watermarking adoption is increasing, especially if legally required as recently proposed (Ricketts,
036
      2023; Wicks, 2024), making it necessary to consider complex ecosystems of watermark providers.
037   However, in a world with many watermarking algorithms, a user —or more likely, their web
038   browser— would not know which detector to use. As such, a sign-posting super watermark can
039   be added alongside the original watermark with the goal of indicating how the original watermark
040   has been encoded and what detector should be used to decode it. Furthermore, different actors
041   need to encode different watermarks in the same media, e.g. the author might want to add content
042   provenance watermark, the distributor could apply an intellectual property tracking watermark, and
043   a generative model developer might need a content attribution watermark. Hence, we will likely see
044   more and more cases of multiple watermarks added to the same media.
045   Embedding multiple watermarks in one image requires their coexistence without mutual interfer-
046   ence, enabling independent and accurate decoding of each watermark’s message. Yet, the coexis-
047   tence of multiple deep learning-based watermarks has not been studied, possibly with the assumption
048   that they overwrite each other. That is why we conducted a comprehensive analysis demonstrating
049   they can indeed effectively coexist within the same image. While coexistence incurs minor reduc-
050
      tions in image quality and decoding robustness, it persists even when controlling for these factors.
051   The coexistence of watermarks opens up an avenue for building watermarking ensembles. If two
052   watermarks, with capacity m1 and m2 bits, generated with different methods can both be present
053   in a media, then one can effectively encode m1 + m2 bits in the image. By combining such water-
      mark ensembling with watermark strength clipping and error-correcting codes, we can modify the

054
      capacity-accuracy-robustness-quality trade-offs of existing methods with no need to retrain them.
055   We show how one can improve existing watermarking techniques by ensembling them with others.
056
057   In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
058          i. We evaluate whether image watermarking techniques can coexist when applied to the same
059             image and demonstrate that coexistence happens to a much greater degree than expected.
060         ii. We demonstrate that some level of coexistence persists even when one controls for the small
061             quality and robustness degradation resulting of the application of the second watermark.
062        iii. We show how coexistence enables the ensembling of watermarking models and can be used
063             to modify the performance of existing methods without needing any retraining.
064
      Our experiments focus on image watermarks as this is the domain with most mature watermarking
065
      but the same principles likely apply also to video, audio, and to some extent, text watermarking.

068   2   P RELIMINARIES
069
070   Image watermarking. Image watermarking is the act of encoding a string of bits (a secret) by
071   perturbing the pixel values of an image (cover image) in a way that is minimally disruptive and is
072   robust to edits. We will consider the setting when the detector does not have access to the cover
073
      image (blind watermarking). Watermarking requires a trade-off between four competing objectives:
074           i. Capacity: the length of the secret message string (in bits);
075          ii. Image quality: the amount of distortion added to the cover image to embed the secret,
076              often measured in peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), larger values indicating better quality;
077        iii. Accuracy: the fraction of correctly decoded secrets, usually over a test dataset of images;
078
            iv. Robustness: the fraction of secrets we can decode correctly when certain transformations
079              or edits have been added to the image after watermarking. There is no commonly agreed on
080              set of transformations, so we consider the augmentations used for training several popular
081              methods, namely RivaGAN, SSL, TrustMark (low, medium, high), see the details in App. D.
082
      Increasing capacity often lowers the image quality, accuracy, and robustness. Similarly, improving
083
      accuracy and robustness typically reduces quality and/or capacity (if using error-correction), while
084
      enhancing image quality tends to lower accuracy and robustness. These trade-offs make watermark-
085   ing a multi-objective problem with no single “best” method, thus only Pareto-optimal solutions can
086   be achieved. In the literature, bit accuracy is often measured instead of full secret accuracy. We be-
087   lieve that full secret accuracy is a better indicator of performance, as recovering the entire message
088   is usually necessary. Therefore, we report the fraction of cases with all bits being correct decoded.
089
090
      Watermarking methods. Classical watermarking methods are based on perturbing the represen-
      tation of an image in some transform domain: discrete cosine transforms (Dct Tang and Aoki, 1997),
091
      discrete wavelet transforms (Dwt Wang et al., 1998, or a combination of the two (DwtDct Al-Haj,
092
      2007 and DwtDctSvd Navas et al., 2008). Deep watermarking methods leverage neural networks
093   to do the encoding and decoding of images, leading to more robust watermarks with less distortion
094   with methods such as HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018), RivaGAN (Zhang et al., 2019), RoSteALS (Bui et al.,
095   2023b). SSL uses a pretrained image encoder and allows selecting a carrier vector, target image
096   quality and message length at inference time (Fernandez et al., 2022). TrustMark (Bui et al., 2023a)
097   comes in versions with higher accuracy (TrustMark B) and higher quality (TrustMark Q) and has a
098   controllable watermark strength, set to 0.95 by default. Generative watermarking methods integrate
099   image generation and watermarking to produce watermarked images directly (Wen et al., 2023; Fer-
100   nandez et al., 2023). However, since these methods do not produce a non-watermarked image, we
101
      cannot measure quality degradation and, therefore, we will not consider them in this work.

122   with some proposals to mandate it by law (Ricketts, 2023; Wicks, 2024). With many actors intro-
123   ducing watermarks using diverse techniques, it is challenging for users —and their web browsers—
124   to determine which decoder to use for a given image. For example, C2PA, the most widely adopted
125   content provenance standard, is agnostic to watermarking technology and supports any watermark
126   (C2PA, 2024). Naı̈vely trying all decoders is impractical, as it requires storing all of them locally or
127   making numerous API calls, which is costly and inefficient. Furthermore, different providers might
128
      use the same watermarking method but encode the messages in different ways, leading to multiple
      watermarks being decoded with only one being the intended one.
129
130   We need an efficient way to identify the decoder for a given watermark. One solution is adding a
131   second watermark carrying the provider identifier, similarly to a disk partition table. We call this
132   identification watermark a super watermark (see Fig. 1A). With a unified standard for the super
133
      watermark, a web browser could support many decoders without evaluating every image against all
      of them.1 For a super watermark to be effective, it must coexist with the main watermark without
134
      hindering its decodability. Another scenario requiring watermark coexistence is the increasingly
135
      complex media production and distribution chains, with more and more actors adopting watermark-
136   ing. For instance, a photo might come with a watermark and editing it in Photoshop might add an-
137   other to recover its provenance metadata, if stripped. Publishers may add watermarks for copyright
138   detection and developers of generative models might watermark the image to track its contribution
139   to new content and remunerate the copyright holder (Fig. 1B). These varied uses necessitate that
140   watermarks can be applied and decoded independently, i.e., watermarks that can coexist.
141   These two settings, super watermarks for selecting the correct decoder and multi-actor content
142   provenance chains, depend on different watermarks coexisting in the same image. Yet, to our knowl-
143   edge, watermark coexistence has not been studied before. While coexistence has been proposed for
144   classical frequency-based methods (Sheppard et al., 2001; Wong et al., 2003; Zear et al., 2018),
145   these techniques do not apply to modern deep-learning-based approaches.
146   Watermarks can coexist in the same image. Considering that watermark coexistence is critical
147   for multi-actor provenance chains and super watermarking, we study to what extent existing open-
148   source techniques can coexist. In the simplest setting, we can apply two watermarks sequentially
149   and measure the accuracy (fraction of correctly decoded secrets) for both methods. We expect the
150   second watermark to be detectable, as the presence of the first should not affect the addition of the
151   second. However, we anticipate that the second watermark would overwrite the first, resulting in
152   0% accuracy for the first watermark. Similarly, we expect that applying the same watermarking
153   method twice with different secrets would yield low accuracy for the first secret. In Table 1, we
154
      present results for 8 watermarking methods, applying each pair in both possible orders. We report
      the accuracy of the first method, followed by the second. The accuracy when a method is applied
155
      alone is shown in grey. When we apply SSL after itself, we use two different carrier vectors.

198   Channel coding interpretation of why watermark coexistence is surprising. Watermarking
199   transmits information (the message) over a medium (the image). Traditionally, communication
200
      channels are studied in frequency domain (Goldsmith, 2005). Analogous to multiple watermarking,
      frequency-division multiple access (FDMA) splits the frequency spectrum among users to prevent
201
      interference. However, FDMA requires explicit coordination —with authorities dividing the radio
202
      frequency spectrum— limiting users to a fraction of the channel’s capacity. Watermark coexistence
203   suggests capacity division might be occurring here as well, even without explicit coordination, akin
204   to radio communicators randomly selecting different frequency bands and voluntarily restricting
205   their bandwidth. As deep watermarking is highly non-linear, we cannot directly apply this fre-
206   quency intuition. Nevertheless, coexistence might indicate that existing methods fail to fully utilize
207   the image capacity to carry information imperceptibly. If a method fully utilized the “channel”, it
208   would overwrite any other watermark, which Table 1 indicates to be not happening. In App. A we
209   do offer a possible geometric explanation as to why watermark coexsistence might naturally occur.
210   Watermark coexistence does not immediately imply that existing methods are suboptimal.
211   One might assume that current watermarking techniques are close to Pareto-optimal. However, wa-
212   termark coexistence suggests that deep watermarking methods may not fully utilize the available
213   capacity, i.e., they fail to use the whole channel. If messages of lengths m1 and m2 can be encoded
214   and decoded using different methods, then the true watermarking capacity is at least m1 +m2 , im-
215   plying that a method reaching this capacity should exist. While tempting, this view is too simplistic.

341   4       E NSEMBLING AS A WATERMARK MODEL MODIFICATION TOOL
342
343   Applications of invisible watermarks have varying requirements. An image generator developer
344
      might be less concerned about artifacts, since users lack a non-watermarked image for comparison,
      but may wish to encode extra information like the prompt used. In contrast, content provenance
345
      frameworks need high image quality to ensure adoption among creators. Conversely, print media
346
      watermarking might need higher robustness to remain detectable after printing and photographing,
347   with lower capacity and quality requirements. Unfortunately, watermarking methods are not easily
348   customizable. Once trained for a specific trade-off between capacity, quality, accuracy and robust-
349   ness, adjusting this balance typically requires retraining.2 Consequently, each application might
350   require a slightly different model. Ideally, one would want to modify a watermarking method post-
351   training to adapt it to a new application. We have only seen two tools used for this purpose:
352
353             i. Strength clip: One can perform a linear interpolation between the original image and the
354
                   watermarked image. By bringing the final image closer to the original image, one can
                   improve the image quality, while reducing the detection accuracy and robustness.
355
356            ii. Error-correcting codes (ECCs): Error-correcting codes are used for reducing errors in
357
                   data transmission over unreliable or noisy communication channels by reducing the number
                   of information carrying bits from n to k and introducing n − k redundancy bits in their
358
                   place (Hamming, 1950). Applying ECCs to the watermarked message reduces the effective
359
                   capacity of the watermarking method but improves the accuracy and robustness.
360
361   While these tools can improve the image quality, accuracy and robustness of existing methods, they
362   a. cannot increase the information capacity, and b. leave one of the objectives worse off and hence
363   cannot be used to obtain a strictly better watermarking method without retraining.
364
      Watermark ensembling boosts capacity. Our observation of watermark coexistence in Sec. 3
365
      offers a potential avenue for a new post-training modification tool: watermark ensembling. If the
366   watermarks generated by two different models coexist, then the application of the two methods can
367   be effectively considered to be a new watermarking method having the sum of the capacities of the
368   individual methods. Hence, ensembling directly addresses the first limitation of strength clip and
369   ECCs: it can be used to increase the capacity without retraining (see the third column in Fig. 4). For
370   example, ensembling TrustMark B and SSL (42 dB, 100 bits) gives us 200bit capacity at the cost of
371   lower accuracy and robustness (the capacity doubling can be seen in the first column of Fig. 5A).
372
      Combining strength clip, ECC and ensembling could, in principle, result in strictly better
373
      models. As seen in Sec. 3, coexistence may come at a small quality, accuracy and robustness cost,
374   thus ensembling alone cannot result in strictly better models. Still, the application of all three tools
375   together could, in principle, outperform either of the two models we start with. Ensembling two
376   watermarking methods coexisting well together increases the capacity to the sum of the individual

      5    D ISCUSSION AND OPEN QUESTIONS
515   While we showed that different watermarks can coexist in the same image, we still lack understand-
516   ing and control of coexistence. Classic frequency analysis offers a good mental model but cannot
517   be directly applied to non-linear deep models. We need a new theory and tools to characterize and
518   evaluate the non-linear channels for encoding information in images, given resolution, quality and
519   robustness constraints. Such a theory would enable coexistence by design: by limiting the channels
520   individual methods use, designing techniques with explicit channel selection allowing multiple co-
521   existing watermarks with the same method, or, conversely, building methods that fail to coexist with
522   any other watermarks thus fully utilising the information-carrying capacity of the image.
523   Despite ensembling being used frequently for increasing the accuracy of classification and regression
524   tasks (Dong et al., 2020), for boosting robustness (Pang et al., 2019; Petrov et al., 2023), or for
525   achieving computational efficiency for large models (Fedus et al., 2022), we have not seen it applied
526   to watermarking, possibly because it was not obvious that watermarks can coexist. As we showed
527
      that watermarks do coexist, we opened an avenue for their ensembling. We observed that ensembling
      offers nuanced benefits: it boosts the capacity of existing models and open new trade-off points, but
528
      sometimes one might be better-off by simply applying ECCs and strength clipping to a single well-
529
      performing model as linear codes correct few errors relative to the extra capacity they use. Perhaps,
530   modern ECCs —for instance soft-decision ones which factor in the confidence with which each bit
531   is decoded (Fossorier and Lin, 2002)— could better utilise the extra capacity. Beyond parallel and
532   series ensembling, there could be more advanced adaptive techniques or small learnable mixers that
533   one could use for more performant ensembles. Finally, a further boost to the accuracy and robustness
534   of the ensembles could be possible by fine-tuning their decoders on the jointly watermarked images.
535   Our analysis was limited in several ways. We used PSNR as a proxy for image quality despite it not
536   tracking human quality perception well; in practice one might want to also consider other quality
537   metrics. Ensembling typically has a computational cost: both the encoding and the decoding steps
538   require inference through both watermarking models. This could be addressed by distilling the two
539   models into a single one. Finally, we focused on images, but watermarking (and hence coexistence
      and ensembling of watermarks) could be applied to other domains such as video, audio and text.

      A     G EOMETRIC INTUITION AS TO WHY WATERMARKS CAN COEXIST
703
704   We would like to offer a geometric intuition as to why watermarks could coexist in the same im-
705   age without overwriting one another. One explanation is that watermarks are designed to be robust
706   to various augmentations, which induces sparsity into what perturbations can be considered valid
707   watermarks. However, there are many possible sparse patterns that can satisfy the robustness re-
708   quirements and different watermarking methods might be —either by random selection, or due to
709
      particular design decisions— picking different such patterns. As a result, the perturbations used by
      different methods could be non-overlapping and might be sufficiently different so that they are still
710
      identifiable even after composing.
