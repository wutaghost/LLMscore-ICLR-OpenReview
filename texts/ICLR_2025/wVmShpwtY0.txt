009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to engineer optimized protein variants has transformative potential for
012            biotechnology and medicine. Prior sequence-based optimization methods struggle
013            with the high-dimensional complexities due to the epistasis effect and the disre-
014            gard for structural constraints. To address this, we propose HADES, a Bayesian
015            optimization method utilizing Hamiltonian dynamics to efficiently sample from a
016            structure-aware approximated posterior. Leveraging momentum and uncertainty
017            in the simulated physical movements, HADES enables rapid transition of propos-
018
               als toward promising areas. A position discretization procedure is introduced to
               propose discrete protein sequences from such continuous state system. The poste-
019
               rior surrogate is powered by a two-stage encoder-decoder framework to determine
020
               the structure and function relationships between mutant neighbors, consequently
021            learning a smoothed landscape to sample from. Extensive experiments demon-
022            strate that our method outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in in-silico evalua-
023            tions across most metrics. Remarkably, our approach offers a unique advantage
024            by leveraging the mutual constraints between protein structure and sequence, fa-
025            cilitating the design of protein sequences with similar structures and optimized
026            properties.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Designing proteins with improved fitness is a fundamental but challenging task in protein engineer-
032   ing. The search space of protein variants is vastly expanded as the length of the protein sequence
033
      grows, which is typically 20L for a sequence composed of L amino acids for 20 types of amino
      acids. Optimizing protein fitness is difficult due to the complex epistasis effect, which creates a
034
      rugged, multi-peaked landscape (Poelwijk et al., 2011). Traditional directed evolution approaches
035
      rely on random variation combined with screening and selection, without incorporating models to
036   understand the sequence-function relationship (Arnold, 1998). This process is costly and time-
037   consuming, limiting the exploration to only a small fraction of possible mutations, even with the
038   advent of reasonably high-throughput techniques.
039
      The challenge of identifying novel protein designs for maximal fitness has driven scientists to adopt
040
      machine learning approaches, starting with (Fox et al., 2007) and followed by many others, increas-
041
      ingly utilizing in physics- and machine learning-based exploration beyond traditional experimental
042   methods (Sinai et al., 2020; Frey et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2024). Recent advancements in protein
043   language models and structural-informed models also offer new opportunities for modeling protein
044   mutational effects and generalizing fitness knowledge across different datasets(Zheng et al., 2023;
045   Notin et al., 2023; Hie et al., 2024).
046
      The primary research challenge in designing an iterative protein engineering strategy is navigating
047
      the vast combinatorial space to uncover the sequence-to-function landscape and find the optimal
048   sequences (Romero & Arnold, 2009). This daunting task can be tackled by employing a black-
049   box optimization approach, where the inputs are optimized to get better outputs with limited and
050   often expensive black-box oracle calls. However, protein landscapes are notoriously non-smooth,
051   with fitness levels that can drastically change due to a single mutation, and most mutants have poor
052   fitness. As a result, existing machine learning methods often falter, struggling with noisy fitness
053   landscapes that lead to false positives (Kirjner et al., 2023) and failing to venture beyond local
      optima due to inefficient exploration (Brookes et al., 2019).

      Compared to sequence-based searching strategies, leveraging structural information offers a more
055   natural approach by providing useful constraints for navigating the noisy fitness landscape, given the
056   decisive role of 3D structure in determining protein function (Branden & Tooze, 2012). Therefore,
057   when optimizing a protein for a desired function, designing it in structure latent space seems more di-
058   rect, allowing the use of gradient-based sampling methods in conjunction with carefully engineered
059   potentials (Trippe et al., 2022; Watson et al., 2023). However, a drawback of this approach is that
060   the optimized structure must be converted back to an amino acid sequence for synthesis(Dauparas
061   et al., 2022), while there is no guarantee that the optimized structure can be realized by an actual
062   sequence. Additionally, current structural models are computationally intensive and constrained by
063
      the scarcity of high-quality structural data, posing challenges to optimization and search.

081   To address these issues, we propose a new learning-based protein engineering framework that
082   combines Bayesian Optimization and HAmiltonian dynamics for protein Directed Evolution in a
083   Structure-informed manner (HADES, Figure 1). Our motivation is based on the mutual constraints
084   between protein sequence and structure. The distribution of conformational perturbations among a
085   series of mutants can serve as prior information to model a smoothed landscape of protein function,
086   potentially enhancing the efficiency of gradient-based algorithms for exploration and sampling. We
087   achieve this by designing a surrogate fitness predictor powered by a two-stage encoder-decoder to
088
      learn the structure perturbations distilled from ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023) as the prior. To address
      the inefficiency in exploring high-dimensional sequence space, we introduce an acquisition function
089
      based on Hamiltonian Monte Carlo that proposes distant samples for the Metropolis-Hastings algo-
090
      rithm in MCMC sampling steps by introducing momentum variable in a continuous state space. We
091   further combine the sampling process under Bayesian optimization with estimation of model uncer-
092   tainty to balance between exploration and exploitation. Our approach offers a significant advantage
093   by leveraging the mutual constraints between protein structure and sequence, facilitating the design
094   of protein sequences with similar structures and enhanced properties.

      Hamiltonian mechanics and MCMC Markov Chain Monte Carlo defines a transition kernel to ex-
      plore target distribution, and Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is a common method to automatically
113

123   3    BACKGROUND
124
125   In this study, we address the challenge of machine-learning-guided protein directed evolution within
126   a black-box optimization framework. The process initiates with a single wild type protein sequence,
127   denoted as xwt , of length L. A protein sequence is represented as a concatenation of one-hot vectors,
128   xwt = (x1 , x2 , ..., xL ), each vector corresponding to an amino acid. The ground-truth fitness of an
129   unknown protein is assessed through wet-lab evaluations, functioning as the black-box oracle F(x).
130   The primary objective of this research is to engineer protein variants exhibiting enhanced fitness
131   scores, particularly under the constraint of limited oracle evaluations. The directed evolution process
132   is conducted iteratively: in each iteration n, where n ∈ {1, 2, ..., N }, a batch of protein variants Dn ,
133   with |Dn | = K, is analyzed by the black-box oracle to determine the true fitness scores Gn =
134   {(x, F(x)), x ∈ Dn }. The optimization sequence concludes after N rounds of oracle calls.
135
      Typically, a surrogate fitness predictor f (x) is trained to approximate the black-box oracle, utiliz-
136   ing samples collected through an acquisition function. This function strategically proposes new,
137   promising protein variants for subsequent rounds. Operating under a fixed budget of K oracle
138   queries per round, the acquisition process must effectively balance exploration of the expansive,
139   high-dimensional sequence space with the exploitation of regions potentially close to optima. The
140   surrogate fitness predictor, serving as an approximate posterior, provides fitness predictions that
141   guide the acquisition function in selecting promising variants for further evaluation.
142   Hamiltonian dynamics and MCMC Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) with the Metropolis-
143   Hastings algorithm is a widely-used technique for generating samples from an approximate posterior

      4.2   T WO - STAGE SURROGATE MODEL LEARNING
263
      The surrogate model aims to approximate the ground-truth fitness landscape within a constrained
264   budget of labeled samples. Given that the model inputs are discrete one-hot vectors, it is essential
265   to model the continuous relationships among mutants derived from discrete amino acid variations to
266   facilitate a gradient-based optimization strategy. In this context, protein structure data is utilized as
267   a prior to model these relationships. The fundamental rationale is to leverage the mutual constraints
268   between protein structure and sequence, facilitating the design of protein sequences with similar
269   structures, thereby avoiding being trapped in local optima induced by epistasis effect. Our surrogate
      model employs an encoder-decoder architecture, and its training proceeds in two distinct stages. The


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      encoder is utilized in both stages, while two separate decoders are dedicated to learn about protein
271   structure and fitness sequentially.
272
273   Sequence encoder Inspired by (Hamamsy et al., 2023) that learns structure homology through se-
274
      quence information, we input a candidate sequence x and apply three pairformer-like shallow atten-
      tion layers (Abramson et al., 2024) to capture intra- and inter-interactions of amino acid embeddings
275
      and produce a latent vector representation, h(x), for each sequence.
276
277   Structure decoder This first decoder is designed to learn the Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD)
278   score between a sequence candidate and the wild type across mutation sites. We employ ESMFold
279   (Lin et al., 2023) to predict the structures and compute the conformation perturbations through
280
      RMSD scores. During the training phase, the encoder captures the latent representations that reflect
      the structural distance from input protein to the wild type. Our assumption is that understanding this
281
      distribution of structural distances provides prior knowledge of how mutations reflect the structural
282
      changes, which benefits the gradient calculation and fitness learning.
283
284   Fitness decoder In the second stage, the fitness decoder focuses on learning fitness scores, operat-
285   ing with the frozen encoder parameters obtained from the first stage. This separation ensures that
286
      the fitness assessment is based on stable, well-defined structural representations derived in the first
      training stage. Both decoders stack three 1-D convolution layers followed by max pooling and a two
287
      multi-layer perceptrons. We did not use complex architectures because these scenarios involve low-
288
      resource training, where intricate networks and representations can easily lead to model overfitting.

701   Baseline. We conducted a comprehensive comparison of our method against four advanced baseline

702
      Bayesian Optimization: A classical approach for sequential design problems, it uses an ensemble
703   of models to estimate uncertainty and construct the acquisition function for exploration (Boender,
704   1991).
705
706
      CMA-ES: A well-known evolutionary search algorithm, CMA-ES adapts the search strategy of sub-
      sequent generations by estimating the covariance matrix to update mutation distributions adaptively
707
      (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001).
708
709   AdaLead: An advanced model-guided evolution technique, AdaLead performs a hill-climbing
710   search on the learned landscape model at each batch query round and then queries sequences with
711   high predicted fitness. This straightforward implementation of model-directed evolution has shown
712
      competitive performance against more elaborate algorithms (Sinai et al., 2020).
713   PEX: This method balances fitness scores and mutation distances by exploring regions near the
714   wild type through a proximal frontier. It uses a regularized objective function to search locally for
715   sequences with a low mutation count from the wild-type sequence (Ren et al., 2022).
716   EvoPlay: EvoPlay combines reinforcement learning techniques such as Monte Carlo Tree Search
717   (MCTS) and a policy-value neural network. It focuses on mutating single-site residues in protein se-
718   quences, optimizing their properties through an iterative, strategic process akin to gameplay. (Wang
719   et al., 2023)
720
      For BO, CMA-ES and AdaLead, we run implementations provided by FLEXS (Sinai et al., 2020).
721
      The surrogate models of these methods share the same architecture with our fitness/structure de-

723   a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with default hyperparameters. We run our method on a single
