009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in Text-to-3D generation have yielded remarkable progress,
012            particularly through methods that rely on Score Distillation Sampling (SDS).
013            While SDS exhibits the capability to create impressive 3D assets, it is hindered
014            by its inherent maximum-likelihood-seeking essence, resulting in limited diver-
015            sity in generation outcomes. In this paper, we discover that the Denoise Diffusion
016            Implicit Models (DDIM) generation process (i.e. PF-ODE) can be succinctly ex-
017            pressed using an analogue of SDS loss. One step further, one can see SDS as
018
               a generalized DDIM generation process. Following this insight, we show that
               the noise sampling strategy in the noise addition stage significantly restricts the
019
               diversity of generation results. To address this limitation, we present an inno-
020
               vative noise sampling approach and introduce a novel text-to-3D method called
021            Flow Score Distillation (FSD). Our validation experiments across various text-to-
022            image Diffusion Models demonstrate that FSD substantially enhances generation
023            diversity and quality. Project page: https://flowscoredistillation.
024            github.io/

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In the realm of 3D content creation, a crucial step within the modern game and media industry
030   involves crafting intricate 3D assets. Recently, 3D generation has facilitated the creation of 3D assets
031   with ease. 3D generative models could be trained directly on certain representations (e.g. point
032   clouds (Achlioptas et al., 2018; Luo & Hu, 2021), voxel (Xie et al., 2018; Smith & Meger, 2017) and
033
      mesh (Zhang et al., 2021)). However, despite the recent efforts of Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023), 3D
      data remains relatively scarce, especially when compared to the abundant 2D image data available
034
      on the internet. This scarcity constrains the generative capabilities of models trained solely on 3D
035
      datasets. Notably, the most prevailing text-to-3D approach is based on Score Distillation Sampling
036   (SDS), proposed by Dreamfusion (Poole et al., 2022) and SJC (Wang et al., 2023a). SDS effectively
037   tackles the scarcity of 3D data by leveraging pretrained 2D text-to-image Diffusion Models, without
038   directly training models on 3D datasets.
039
      SDS is designed to optimize any representations (e.g. Neural Radiance Field (Mildenhall et al.,
040
      2021; Müller et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021), 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023),
041
      Mesh (Laine et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2021) or even 2D images) that could render 2D images through
042   probability density distillation (Oord et al., 2018) using the learned score functions from the Dif-
043   fusion Models. One of the main limitations of current SDS-based methods is that their distillation
044   objectives will maximize the likelihood of the image rendered from the 3D representations, which
045   leads to limited diversity. Despite several subsequent efforts (Wang et al., 2024; Zhu & Zhuang,
046   2023; Liang et al., 2023; Katzir et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
047   2023b; Armandpour et al., 2023) to enhance SDS, the maximum-likelihood-seeking essence of the
048   method remains unchanged. Notably, ProlificDreamer (Wang et al., 2024) introduces Variational
049   Score Distillation (VSD) and uses a fine-tuned Diffusion Model to model distribution on particles,
050
      which could alleviate the maximum-likelihood-seeking issues. However, training costs could grow
      linearly with the particle number of VSD. ESD (Wang et al., 2023b) points out that single-particle
051
      VSD is equivalent to SDS, which remains rooted in the essence of maximum likelihood seeking.
052
053   In this paper, we present a fresh perspective on SDS by viewing it as a generalized DDIM (Song
      et al., 2020a) generation process for 3D representations. Specifically, we discovered that the DDIM

150           • We provide an in-depth analysis of SDS, an effective method in text-to-3D generation.

      2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
165
166   Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) are a family of
167   powerful generative models that are trained to gradually transform Gaussian noise to samples from a
168   target distribution p0 . Their generation ability is further enhanced given datasets comprising billions
169   of image-text pairs (Changpinyo et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2018).
170   Assume the target distribution is p0 and condition y. Diffusion Models define a forward process
171   {xt }t∈[0,T ] starting from x0 ∼ p0 (·|y), such that for any t ∈ [0, T ] the distribution of xt conditioned
172   on x0 satisfies:

      2.2   D IFFUSION PF-ODE AND DDIM
188   To generate samples from Diffusion Models, there exist different methods among the Diffusion
189   Models family. Denoise Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020a) designed a de-
190   terministic method for fast sampling from Diffusion Models. Later works (Salimans & Ho, 2022;

204   2.3   S CORE D ISTILLATION S AMPLING
205
206   Recently, DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) and SJC (Wang et al., 2023a) proposed Score Distilla-
207
      tion Sampling (SDS) to generate 3D models by optimizing a differentiable 3D representation using
      priors from text-to-image Diffusion Models. Follow-up works tried to improve upon SDS through
208
      various aspects, e.g. coarse-to-fine training strategy (Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
209
      2023), disentangled 2D-3D priors (Chen et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) and refined
210   formulas (Zhu & Zhuang, 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
211   2023b; Yu et al., 2023; Armandpour et al., 2023). Moreover, due to the lack of comprehensive 3D-
212   aware knowledge, multi-face Janus problem often arises when using SDS (Poole et al., 2022). To
213   mitigate this challenge, one can consider replacing the text-to-image Diffusion Models with Diffu-
214   sion Models designed for object novel view synthesis (Liu et al., 2023b; Long et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
215   2023c; Weng et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) or multi-view Diffusion Models (Shi et al., 2023). Such
      an adaptation can alleviate the multi-face Janus problem encountered in 3D generation using SDS.

      3.3   A NALYSIS OF THE N OISE S AMPLING S TRATEGY
340
      Our empirical investigation reveals that the generation results produced by SDS exhibit an undesir-
341   able tendency toward over-smoothness and lack of diversity. Remarkably, even with the timestep
342   annealing technique, this issue persists. This tendency originates from the maximum-likelihood-
343   seeking nature implied by its definition (Eq. 6) where it models the current distribution using a
344   Dirichlet function centered at gθ (c). As a result, the generation results of SDS will be centered
345   around a few modes (Poole et al., 2022), i.e. a few maximum likelihood points on the smoothed dis-
346   tribution pt . In contrast, not only does FSD yield diverse outcomes, but it can also generate highly
347   detailed samples. This can be attributed to that FSD is more aligned with a DDIM process, which
348   can generate samples from exactly the target distribution p0 . So we conclude that the noise sampling
349
      strategy can affect the generation diversity greatly.

384   function that generates correlated noise between views, which aligns with the prior that the nearby
385   views are correlated. The design of FSD ensures the optimization directions are consistent, partic-
386   ularly when similar cs are sampled. As the ϵ(c)s are similar, the generated ground-truth images
387
      should be consistent as well. Notably, recent works (Ge et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Chang et al.,
      2024) on video generation also find using designed video noise prior can improve the capabilities of
388
      Video Diffusion Models.
389
390
      4.1     D ESIGNING ϵ̃.
391
392   4.1.1     FAILURE OF A VANILLA D ESIGN OF ϵ̃
393
394   A vanilla design of ϵ(c) can be ϵ(c) = ϵ, which is a constant function. However, according to
395   our experiments on text-to-3D generation, such a design can lead to poor geometry of the generated
396   samples. Typically, holes on the surfaces are observed (see Appx. Sec. B.3). We attribute this effect
397
      to the uneven convergence speed of FSD in 3D space caused by the constant noise function. Our
      experiments on 2D show that the local textures of noise added during FSD optimization are highly
398
      correlated with the local textures of the final image (Demonstrated in Fig. 4). And in text-to-3D
399
      generation with FSD, the generated ground-truth images have more consistent textures at the center
400   point than other points in 3D space, due to the sampling strategy of camera view c. As a result, the
401   convergence speed at the center point is much higher than at other points, leading to holes on the
402   surfaces. Flaws are also observed in Video Diffusion Models that adopt fixed noise prior, due to
403   similar reasons, which is known as the textures sticking problem (Chang et al., 2024).
404

515   6     C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this work, we systematically study the problem of text-to-3D generation. We first review the
518   theorems of SDS and reveal a simple but profound underlying connection between DDIM and SDS.
519   Following this insight, we propose FSD to tackle the diversity degradation challenge. By using a
520   consistent noise sampling schedule that aligns noise coarsely in 3D space, FSD breaks free from the
521   maximum-likelihood-seeking nature of SDS and could generate diverse results with high quality.
522
      Additional, our methods incorporate a multiview diffusion model that has rich shape prior in coarse
      stage and a image diffusion model that has rich texture prior in refine stage, our methods can generate
523
      diverse and high quality 3D objects.
524
525   Limitations and future works. Although FSD could improve the diversity and quality of 3D gen-
526   eration, we found that it is still difficult to generate 3D models as diverse as the images generated
527   through DDIM generation process. We believe this mainly originates from our direct generalization
528
      from 2D DDIM to 3D. The deterministicity requirement on noise function only make sure the up-
      date is aligned with DDIM process when only a single camera view is considered. It can be hard
529
      to guarantee that the DDIM trajectory is followed exactly, especially when the updates from other
530
      camera views are considered. Second, even through our proposed noise function provided more
531   aligned guidance across camera views, hindered by the special property of Gaussian noise, we only
532   find a design of worldmap noise map function that only aligns noise on a sphere independent of
533   object surface. This misalignment could potently hinder the performance of FSD and may not work
534   for objects with complex geometry. We do not specify the noise function ϵ(c) in the general form
535   of FSD (Eq. 15), and better designs of ϵ(c) may exist. Third, like other score distillation methods,
536   our method can still suffer from multi-face problems. Seeking help from multi-view image diffusion
537   models that are trained on limited amount of 3D data, our 2 stage pipeline can generate shapes with
538   high success rate. But our 2 stage pipeline may not work for complex prompts due to the limited
539
      ability of the multi-view diffusion model. Lastly, like other score distillation methods, the generation
      of our methods may take several hours.

1177   standard way of using FID to evaluate GANs (Heusel et al., 2017) and 3D-FID in Sec. 5.2.2.

1181
       Difference with Consistent3D Recent work Consistent3D (Wu et al., 2024) also applied fixed noise
1182   when conducting SDS-like generation. In Consistent3D, they follow the idea of Consistent Train-
1183   ing (Song et al., 2023) and use the rendered image perturbed with fixed noise to approximate the
1184   starting point of the deterministic flow. In our method, for the same camera view, we also add fixed
1185   noise to the rendered image, but the noised image is used to simulate a variable in the middle of a
1186   PF-ODE trajectory, which is different from Consistent3D. Our FSD loss is also different from the
1187   CDS loss in Consistent3D, even when our view-dependent noise function gives the same noise for
       all camera views, implying an essential difference between our method and Consistent3D.
