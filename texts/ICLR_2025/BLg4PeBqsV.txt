011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The Gumbel-Softmax probability distribution allows learning discrete tokens in
014            generative learning, whereas the Gumbel-Argmax probability distribution is useful
015            in learning discrete structures in discriminative learning. Despite the efforts in-
016
               vested in optimizing these models, their properties are underexplored. In this work,
               we investigate their representation properties and determine for which families of
017
               parameters these probability distributions are complete, that is, can represent any
018
               probability distribution, and minimal, i.e., can represent a probability distribution
019            uniquely. We rely on convexity and differentiability to determine these conditions
020            and extend this framework to general probability models, denoted Perturb-Softmax
021            and Perturb-Argmax. We conclude the analysis by identifying two sets of pa-
022            rameters that satisfy these assumptions and thus admit a complete and minimal
023            representation. A faster convergence rate of Gaussian-Softmax in comparison
024            to Gumbel-Softmax further motivates our study, as the experimental evaluation
025            validates.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Learning over discrete probabilistic models is an active research field with numerous applications.
031   Examples include learning probabilistic latent representations of semantic categories (Rolfe, 2017)
032   and beliefs (Mnih & Gregor, 2014; Salakhutdinov & Hinton, 2009). The Gumbel-Argmax and
033
      Gumbel-Softmax probability distributions are widely applied in machine learning to model and
      analyze such discrete probability distributions.
034
035   The Gumbel-Argmax is an equivalent representation of the softmax operation and plays a key role in
036   the “Follow The Perturb-Leader” (FTPL) family of algorithms in online learning (Hannan, 1957; Kalai
037   & Vempala, 2002; 2005; Rakhlin et al., 2012). Its extension to Gaussian-Argmax allows better bounds
038   on their gradients and consequently provides better regret bounds in linear and high-dimensional
039
      settings (Abernethy et al., 2014; 2016; Cohen & Hazan, 2015). The argmax operation allows for
      efficient sampling, making the Perturb-Argmax probability models pivotal in discriminative learning
040
      algorithms of high-dimensional discrete structures (Berthet et al., 2020; Pogančić et al., 2020; Song
041
      et al., 2016; Cohen Indelman & Hazan, 2021; Niculae et al., 2018). The Gumbel-Softmax (or the
042   Concrete distribution) probability distribution, which replaces the argmax operation with a softmax
043   operation is easier to optimize and therefore plays a key role in generative learning models (Jang et al.,
044   2017; Maddison et al., 2017; Kusner & Hernández-Lobato, 2016; Ramesh et al., 2021a). The discrete
045   nature of these probability models provides a natural representation of concepts, e.g., in zero-shot
046   text-to-image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021b). While the Gumbel-Argmax and Gumbel-Softmax
047   probability distributions are widely applied in machine learning, their representation properties are
048   still underexplored.
049   In this work, we investigate the representation properties of the Gumbel-Argmax and Gumbel-
050   Softmax probability distributions. We aim to determine for which families of parameters these
051   distributions are complete and minimal. A distribution is complete if it can represent any probability
052   distribution, and minimal if it can uniquely represent a probability distribution. A complete and
053   minimal distribution is also defined as identifiable. We identify the conditions that determine their
      representation properties by investigating the Gumbel-Argmax and the Gumbel-Softmax probability

101   2      R ELATED WORK
102
103   The exponential family realized by the softmax operation over its parameters is extensively used in
104   machine learning. However, sampling high-dimensional models is challenging due to its normalizing
105   factor (Geman & Geman, 1984; Goldberg & Jerrum, 2007). The Gumbel-Argmax probability
106   distribution measures the stability of the argmax operation over Gumbel random variables. It is
107   an equivalent representation of softmax operation, thereby enabling efficient sampling from the
      exponential family (Gumbel, 1954; Luce, 1959). In the context of machine learning, the Gumbel-


                                                                  2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Argmax probability models underlie the FTPL online learning algorithm (Hannan, 1957; Kalai &
109   Vempala, 2002; 2005). The Gaussian-Argmax probability models extend the FTPL family of online
110   algorithms and improve their regret bounds (Rakhlin et al., 2012; Abernethy et al., 2014; 2016; Cohen
111   & Hazan, 2015). Our work complements these studies by exploring the properties of Gumbel-Argmax
112   and Gaussian-Argmax. We prove the conditions under which Perturb-Argmax probability models are
113   complete, making them suitable for use in machine learning, and when they are minimal, they can
114   uniquely identify a probability distribution from Perturb-Argmax probability models.
115   The Gumbel-Softmax probability models were introduced as an alternative to the exponential family
116   and its Gumbel-Argmax equivalent in generative learning (Maddison et al., 2017; Jang et al., 2017).
117   This alternative allows for efficient sampling, making it highly effective for learning with stochastic
118   gradient methods. The discrete nature of the Gumbel-Softmax sampling has been utilized to tokenize
119   the visual vocabulary in the celebrated zero-shot text-to-image generation, DALL-E (Ramesh et al.,
120   2021b). The Gaussian-Softmax probability models were introduced in variational auto-encoders
121   as their closed-form KL-divergence makes it easier to realize as regularization (Potapczynski et al.,
122   2020). Our work extends these works and sets the properties of Gumbel-Softmax and Gaussian-
123
      Softmax. We also show that Gaussian-Softmax enjoys faster convergence as the Gaussian distribution
      decays faster than the Gumbel distribution when approaching infinity.
124
125   Berthet et al. (2020) introduced a framework for optimizing discrete problems based on Perturb-
126   Argmax probability models. This framework applies to discriminative learning using the Fenchel-
127   Young losses and relies on convexity to propagate gradients over discrete choices. Similar to our
128   approach, this work adopts a general view and utilizes convexity to explore the gradient properties of
129
      Perturb-Argmax models. Our work differs in that we use convexity and differentiability to investigate
      their representation properties, specifically when these models are complete and minimal. Other
130
      methods include blackbox differentiation based on gradients of a surrogate linearized loss (Pogančić
131
      et al., 2020), the direct loss minimization technique (Hazan et al., 2010; Keshet et al., 2011; Song
132   et al., 2016; Cohen Indelman & Hazan, 2021) based on gradients of the expected discrete loss, and
133   entropy regularization techniques (Niculae et al., 2018; Martins & Astudillo, 2016). Unlike these
134   methods, we focus on studying the properties of randomized discrete probability models rather than
135   on optimization frameworks.

      7      C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
524
525
      Our main contribution is a theoretical study of the representation properties of Gumbel-Softmax
      and Gumbel-Argmax probability models. Our results provide a theoretical justification that for
526
      fitting probabilities one need not limit oneself to Gumbel random perturbations, as the representation
527
      properties with any smooth probability density perturbation distribution are the same. In particular,
528   the Gumbel-Softmax distribution has no representation benefits over the Normal-Softmax distribu-
529   tion. Moreover, we show that the representation of the Perturb-Argmax probability distribution is
530   unidentifiable whenever the perturbations follow a discrete distribution, hence one may fail in fitting
531   probabilities from parameters since the Perturb-Argmax probabilities are multi-valued mappings
532   from parameters. Though our analysis shows that Gaussian-Softmax and Gaussian-Argmax share the
533   same representation properties, the Gaussian distribution enjoys faster convergence in probabilistic
534   estimation. These advantages are demonstrated in our experiments.
535   As our framework extends the Gumbel-Softmax and Gumbel-Argmax probability models, it suffers
536   the same limitations as these models. Specifically, these models cannot be efficiently applied to
537   probability spaces for which the softmax function is too expensive to be computed. Further, our
538   investigation does not easily apply to high-dimensional probability distributions (e.g., the Gumbel-
539   Sinkhorn distribution that manifests in learning latent matching representations (Mena et al., 2018)),
      and the investigation of such distributions’ properties is left for future work.

707
708   We consider convex functions f : Θ → R over its convex domain Θ = dom(f ) and follow the
709
      notation in Rockafellar (1970). A function over a domain Θ is convex if for every θ, τ ∈ Θ and
      0 ≤ λ ≤ 1 it holds that f (λθ + (1 − λ)τ ) ≤ λf (θ) + (1 − λ)f (τ ). A function is strictly convex
710
      if for every θ ̸= τ ∈ Θ and 0 < λ < 1, it holds that f (λθ + (1 − λ)τ ) < λf (θ) + (1 − λ)f (τ ).
711
      Whenever f (θ) is twice differentiable, a function is strictly convex if its Hessian is positive definite.
712
713   Convexity is a one-dimensional property. A function f : Θ → R is convex if and only if its one-
714   dimensional reduction g(λ) ≜ f (θ + λv) is convex in every admissible direction v, i.e., whenever
715   θ, θ + λv ∈ Θ. A twice differentiable function f (θ) is strictly convex if the second derivative of
716
      g(λ) is positive in every admissible direction v. In this case, we denote g ′ (λ) by ∇v f (θ) and call it a
      directional derivative:
