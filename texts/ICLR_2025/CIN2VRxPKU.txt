009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Machine unlearning has emerged as an important component in developing safe
012            and trustworthy models. Prior work on unlearning in LLMs has mostly con-
013            sidered unlearning tasks where a large corpus of copyrighted material or some
014            specific training data are required to be removed. In this work, we consider
015            the task of unlearning a fact from LLMs, which can be challenging as related
016            facts can be deduced from each other, and investigate how well current unlearn-
017            ing methods for LLMs succeed at this task. Specifically, we formally propose
018            a new setting of unlearning, deep unlearning, which considers fact unlearning
019            under logical deductions between facts. We design a metric recall, to quantify
020
               the extent of deep unlearning. To enable us to systematically evaluate the ex-
               tent of deep unlearning undistracted by other factors, we construct a synthetic
021
               dataset EDU-RELAT, which consists of a synthetic knowledge base of family re-
022
               lationships and biographies, together with a realistic logical rule set that connects
023            them. We use this dataset to test four unlearning methods in four LLMs at dif-
024            ferent sizes. Our findings reveal that in the task of deep unlearning only a single
025            fact, they either fail to properly unlearn with high recall, or end up unlearning
026            many other irrelevant facts. Our results suggest that more targeted algorithms
027            may have to developed for fact unlearning in LLMs. Our dataset and code are
028            publicly available at : https://anonymous.4open.science/r/deep_
029            unlearning_anonymous-2C73.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large language models (LLMs) of today are trained on massive amounts of uncurated data obtained
035
      from the internet. Machine unlearning in LLMs aims to remove specific pieces of data, concepts,
036   or facts from these models in a more efficient way than retraining from scratch. These diverse
037   definitions of unlearning (data, concept or fact unlearning) are tailored to different use cases. For
038   instance, compliance with regulations such as the GDPR (Parliament & of the European Union,
039   2016) mandates the removal of a user’s data. Similarly, unlearning (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023)
040   can be used to address concerns that models retaining copyrighted material, for example the Harry
041   Potter books, or offensive content.
042   In this paper, we consider the problem of unlearning facts from an LLM, which is important in
043   scenarios with privacy requirements. Research has shown that LLMs can memorize personal and
044   sensitive information (Carlini et al., 2021; Nasr et al., 2023), including relationships, work histories,
045   and personal addresses. Such information can be readily accessed by LLM users, posing significant
046   privacy risks and raising ethical concerns over uncontrolled exposure of private data. This motivates
047   the need to unlearn facts.
048   Prior work have looked at fact unlearning problem, but the focus has been on removing the target
049   fact itself. However, this can be superficial – LLMs not only know single facts in isolation, but
050   many connected facts – and very often, the fact that has been unlearnt can be deduced from facts
051   that are already known by the model. Thus, successful unlearning in this setting should also remove
052   other facts that imply the fact to be unlearnt. As a concrete example, consider Figure 1. Here, the
053   target fact “Camila Flores’s child is Wyatt Ross” can be deduced from fact A “Wyatt Ross’s father
      is Xavier Ross” and fact B “Camila Flores’s husband is Xavier Ross”. If the LLM only unlearns

      We consider a new setting for unlearning, which we call deep unlearning, and investigate to what
      extent current unlearning methods succeed at this setting. Deep unlearning is formulated by stating
069
      a set of facts and logical rules that connect the facts. The fact is deeply unlearnt if the target fact
070
      cannot be deduced from the retained facts in the LLM through the given logical rules. We further
071   propose two metrics, recall and accuracy, for evaluating unlearning methods at deep unlearning.
072   Recall measures to how well an unlearning method unlearns the relevant facts so that the target fact
073   cannot be deduced; while accuracy measures what extent other irrelevant facts are retained by the
074   unlearning process.
075
      In order to have better control in evaluating deep unlearning, we construct a synthetic dataset EDU-
076
      RELAT as a benchmark. The dataset consists of two parts: a synthetic knowledge base and a
077   realistic logical rule set. The knowledge base contains biographical information about a group of
078   people (e.g., “The birthyear of Sloane Lee is 1908”), as well as family relationships (e.g., “Wyatt
079   Ross’s father is Xavier Ross”). The logical rules are realistic rules describing family relationships
080   (e.g. (X, husband, Z)∧ (Y, father, Z) → (X, child, Y)). The unlearning method is tested on a subset
081   of facts on family relationships together with the rules.
082
      We then use our dataset to evaluate four common unlearning methods (Gradient Ascent, Nega-
083   tive Preference Optimization, Task Vector, and Who’s Harry Potter) on four popular LLMs (Phi-1.5,
084   GPT2-XL, Llama2-7b, Llama3-8b). We find that while these methods are good at unlearning the tar-
085   get fact itself without losing accuracy, they either fail to deeply unlearn with high recall or lose more
086   than 20% irrelevant facts while deeply unlearning just single target fact. Additionally, it is found
087   that the unlearning methods have better performance on larger LLMs; this validates the hypothesis
088   that more inherent understanding of facts in larger LLMs helps with deep unlearning naturally.
089   This illustrates that the machine unlearning methods of today are largely insufficient for properly
090   unlearning facts from LLMs; either they do not unlearn what is needed to deduce a target fact, or
091   they over-unlearn and unlearn many extraneous facts. We hypothesize that this might be because
092   the existing unlearning methods do not sufficiently account for the nature of facts and the reasoning
093   capabilities of LLMs. We posit that future methods that unlearn facts suitably from LLMs should
094   be aware of these enhanced capabilities.

099   Given a set of objects O and relations T , a fact k is represened by the triplet (o1 , r, o2 ) of the relation
100   r and two objects o1 , o2 . For example, “Camila Flores’s child is Wyatt Ross” can be represented in
101   (Camila Flores, child, Wyatt Ross). The knowledge base K ⊆ O × T × O is a set of facts.
102   Facts can be deduced from each other through reasoning; an example has been shown in Figure 1.
103   A popular way to formulate the reasoning among facts is through a logical rule R, which is widely
104   used to discover new knowledge (Galárraga et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2022; Cheng
105   et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023). The rule R has the form of B1 ∧ · · · ∧ Bn → A, where B1 · · · , Bn
106   and A are atoms and each atom is a tuple (X, r, Y ) of logical variables X, Y and a relation r. One
107   example of rule is (X, husband, Z)∧ (Y, father, Z) → (X, child, Y). By substituting the objects in O
      to the logical variables in B1 , · · · , Bn , A, facts on the left can together deduce the fact on the right.

131   With a set of rules R, a knowledge base K is deductively closed (Cheney et al., 2009; Cohen,
132   2016; Huang et al., 2021) with respect to R, if there is no new fact can be deduced from K and R.
133   For example, suppose (Camila Flores, husband, Xavier Ross), (Xavier Ross, child, Wyatt Ross) ∈ K
134
      and (X, husband, Z)∧ (Y, father, Z) → (X, child, Y) is in the rule set R. If K is deductively closed
      with respect to the rule set including the rule set R, (Camila Flores, child, Wyatt Ross) must be in
135
      the knowledge base K too. The deductive closure is defined as follows.
136
137   Definition 1 (Deductive closure). The deductive closure of K with respective to the rule set R,
138
      notated as Ω(K, R), is the smallest set such that 1. K ⊆ Ω(K, R); 2. Ω(K, R) is deductively closed
      with respect to a set of rules R.

141   3     DEEP UNLEARNING
142
143   Prior work in fact unlearning from LLMs focuses on simply unlearning the target fact in isolation,
144   and not other facts that logically imply it. This might cause the LLM to forget only this one specific
145   fact, but retain others that can be combined to deduce the fact in question. In this section, we
146   introduce the new setting of unlearning, deep unlearning, which considers the logical deductions
147
      between facts. To assess the effectiveness of unlearning methods in this setting, we propose two
      evaluation metrics: recall and accuracy.
148
149
150   3.1     FACT DEEP UNLEARNING
151
      Let K represent the knowledge base of the LLM prior to unlearning and let UkA ⊆ K denote the set
152   of facts that has been removed by any unlearning method A aimed at unlearning the target fact k. If
153   method A deeply unlearns the fact k, it is expected that the fact k should not be deduced from the
154   retained facts K\UkA by the rule set R, i.e. k should not be in the deductive closure Ω(K\UkA , R).
155
      Definition 2 (deep unlearning ). The unlearning method A deeply unlearns the fact k with respect
156
      to the rule set R if the fact k does not belong in the deductive closure Ω(K\UkA , R).
157

161   fact cannot be deduced by any rules. We further notice that in the example of deep unlearning (Fig-

286   One plausible way of constructing such a dataset is to use real-world knowledge bases such as the
287   triplets in Wikipedia dump1 . However, we find that evaluating unlearning on real-world facts can be
288   noisy due to two factors:
289
      1. Partial Observation of the underlying LLM-Knowledge-Base: Reconstructing a real-world
290      knowledge base from an existing public one only gives us a partial observation of the underlying
291      knowledge base in the LLM, because public real-world knowledge bases are already incomplete
292      and the process of checking if a fact is in an LLM is difficult at the engineering level as mentioned
293      in Zhong et al. (2023). A partial observation of the underlying knowledge base in the LLM can
294      falsely indicate the success of deep unlearning: it is possible that even post unlearning, some facts
295      that deduce the unlearnt target are still retained, while the evaluation result indicates the success
296      of this unlearning just due to the absences of the retained facts in the observed knowledge base.
297   2. Different underlying knowledge bases across LLMs: The underlying knowledge bases for differ-
298
         ent LLMs are different. Hence the same target fact can have different minimal unlearning sets,
         leading to different behavior of a given unlearning method across different LLMs. This makes it
299
         harder to make consistent conclusions for an unlearning method across LLMs (for example, any
300
         unlearning method is best for all LLMs).
301
302   Therefore, to have better control on the evaluation, we construct a synthetic dataset named EDU-
303   RELAT for systematically evaluating deep unlearning through relationships in the family. We locate
304
      our synthetic dataset in a family network, which is a common scenario to study rule mining and
      knowledge discovery in the literature (Galárraga et al., 2013; Cheng et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023).
305
      This synthetic dataset includes a synthetic knowledge base consisting of 400 family relationships
306
      and 300 biographical facts among 100 fictitious people, as well as a set of realistic logical rules,
307   which are deductions among family relationships. Family relationships include child, father, mother,
308   husband, wife, brother, sister, aunt, uncle, nephew, niece. Biographies include birthyear, birthplace,
309   and job. Table 1 shows some examples of facts in family relationships and biographies, together with
310   the question-answer pairs for checking whether this fact is in the LLM or not. Moreover, the rule set
311   R has 48 rules, which are used to deduce the facts in family relationships. Table 2 shows all rules
312   that can imply the fact that has child as the relationship.
313   We make several efforts to better mimic a knowledge base of real-world including:
314
315   • Family network generation. We recursively expand the network. Given a node (person), with a
316
        certain probability, we generate the parents, spouse, and children of this person. We control the
        whole family network in 4 generations. The number of children from any couple is sampled from
317
        a truncated geometric distribution between 1 and 4.
318
      • Name generation. We collect two lists of first names for males and females separately and assign
319     the first name to each person according to gender. As for the last name, each person’s last name
320     is the same as the father’s if the father exists in the network. There is only one special case where
321     the female’s last name has a small probability of switching to her husband’s.
322   • Biography generation. We have three biographical attributes, birth year, birthplace, and job:

344   In this section we investigate to what extent current unlearning methods succeed at deep unlearning.

      We release our dataset and code as a benchmark at : https://anonymous.4open.science/

      5.1    S ET- UP
348
349   Unlearning methods. For each target LLM, we evaluate four common unlearning methods in the
350   literature: gradient ascent, negative preference optimization, task vector and who’s harry potter.
351
352
      • Gradient ascent (GA; (Jang et al., 2022)) maximizes the loss on the target data, which is a re-

      6   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Benchmarks and evaluations in LLM unlearning. TOFU (Maini et al., 2024) is a benchmark
489   containing fictitious authors and their related biographic question-answering texts such as “Awards”
490   and “Book”. They evaluate the unlearning utility by comparing the answer from LLM given the
491   to-be-unlearned question and the ground truth answer. WMDP (Li et al., 2024) provides knowledge
492   in biosecurity, cybersecurity, and chemical security, which matches the realistic desire for studying
493   unlearning. A more recent benchmark MUSE (Shi et al., 2024) in the domain of news articles and
494   books enriches the evaluation by introducing the metric from both memorization and privacy leakage
495   aspects. Yao et al. (2024) introduces a benchmark of evaluating the unlearning in pre-trained data
496   and the metric of unlearning utility is to compute the perplexity of the data from the memorization
497
      aspect. Patil et al. (2024) and Łucki et al. (2024) evaluate the unlearning from an adversarial attack
      aspect of knowledge extraction. This branch of work focuses on proposing more realistic domains
498
      and more robust ways to evaluate the unlearning, and the challenge at their benchmark is to unlearn
499
      a large batch of facts or texts while keeping the model utility. However, none of them consider the
500   interrelation between the target facts and other facts also in the LLM. Deep unlearning in our paper
501   is introduced to capture the connections among multiple facts in the unlearning, and we found that
502   deep unlearning is challenging enough for just unlearning a single fact.
503
      Unlearning methods in LLM. In addition to the methods evaluated in Section 5, one popular exten-
504
      sion is assuming the existence of a “retain” set independent of the target facts. When doing gradient
505
      ascent or other gradient-based variants, Yao et al. (2023) and Chen & Yang (2023) minimize the
506   loss on the “retain” set simultaneously to avoid quickly losing other irrelevant facts and hence help
507   with the model utility. Another popular category is the model-editing based (Meng et al., 2022;
508   2023; Wang et al., 2024), which hypothesizes that the knowledge is saved in certain MLPs in the
509   transformer and proposes an explicit-form solution for the weight update to unlearn the target facts.
510
      Other settings of machine unlearning. There are other settings of machine unlearning because of
511
      different purposes and model structures. Ginart et al. (2019) and Guo et al. (2020) define the goal of
512   unlearning as the model trained without a single person’s data to meet the data regulation for data
513   owner’s rights. Tiwary et al. (2023); Kong & Chaudhuri (2024) study the concept or feature removal
514   in the generative models, e.g. remove “white breast” in all generated bird photos; this is to avoid
515   generating violent and racial content.
516
517
      7   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
518
519
      Conclusion. In this paper, we propose a new setting for machine unlearning, referred to as deep
520
      unlearning, aimed at identifying reliable fact unlearning. As a starting point, we construct a synthetic
521   dataset EDU-RELAT of family relationships and biographies as a benchmark for research in this
522   emerging setting. From empirical evaluation using our metrics, we find that current unlearning
523   methods are not capable of deeply unlearning even a single fact while keeping the model utility. We
524   hypothesize that this shortcoming arises from these methods not fully considering the nature of facts
525   and the deductions between each other.
526
      Discussion on deep unlearning real-world facts. Current unlearning methods would likely have
527   numerically higher accuracy on real data than on our benchmark, because the LLM knowledge base
528   is not complete and the minimal deep unlearning set can be smaller. However, deep unlearning in
529   real data is actually more challenging – because the unlearner would need to reason about missing
530   facts, and decide if they might be in the knowledge base of the LLM. This is also indicated in
531   the algorithm for finding the deep unlearning set (Algorithm 3), where the crucial step 4 involves
532   deductive closure of the knowledge base Ω(K, R) rather than the knowledge base K itself.
533   Future work. This work opens several promising directions for future research. Firstly, more
534   effective methods can be developed with the deep unlearning setting, with an awareness of the con-
535   nections between facts. One can assume the complete set of rules and the entire knowledge base
536   may or may not be fully known, referred to as the white-box or the black-box scenarios. Addi-
537   tionally, there is potential to construct a more nuanced framework that captures more intricate facts
538   and sophisticated deductive processes beyond the current scope of relations and logical rules. Such
539   advancements will enhance the modeling of deep unlearning and contribute to the development of
      methods for deeply unlearning a broader range of facts.
