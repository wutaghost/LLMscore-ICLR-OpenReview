009                                                A BSTRACT
010            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved significant performance progress in
011            various natural language processing applications. However, LLMs still struggle
012            to meet the strict requirements for accuracy and reliability in the medical field
013            and face many challenges in clinical applications. Existing clinical diagnostic
014            evaluation benchmarks for evaluating medical agents powered by LLMs have
015            severe limitations. Firstly, most existing medical evaluation benchmarks face
016            the risk of data leakage or contamination. Secondly, existing benchmarks often
017
               neglect the characteristics of multiple departments and specializations in modern
               medical practice. Thirdly, existing evaluation methods are limited to multiple-
018
               choice questions, which do not align with the real-world diagnostic scenarios.
019
               Lastly, existing evaluation methods lack comprehensive evaluations of end-to-
020            end real clinical scenarios. These limitations in benchmarks in turn obstruct
021            advancements of LLMs and agents for medicine. To address these limitations,
022            we introduce ClinicalLab, a comprehensive clinical diagnosis agent alignment
023            suite. ClinicalLab includes ClinicalBench, an end-to-end multi-departmental
024            clinical diagnostic evaluation benchmark for evaluating medical agents and LLMs.
025            ClinicalBench is based on real cases that cover 24 departments and 150 diseases.
026            We ensure that ClinicalBench does not have data leakage. ClinicalLab also includes
027            four novel metrics (ClinicalMetrics) for evaluating the effectiveness of LLMs
028
               in clinical diagnostic tasks. We evaluate 17 general and medical-domain LLMs
               and find that their performance varies significantly across different departments.
029
               Based on these findings, in ClinicalLab, we propose ClinicalAgent, an end-to-
030
               end clinical agent that aligns with real-world clinical diagnostic practices. We
031            systematically investigate the performance and applicable scenarios of variants
032            of ClinicalAgent on ClinicalBench. Our findings demonstrate the importance of
033            aligning with modern medical practices in designing medical agents.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities across a wide variety
038   of natural language processing tasks (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Cobbe et al., 2021; Song et al., 2024).
039
      Recent studies attempt to apply LLMs to the medical field (Singhal et al., 2023), aiming to improve
      the efficiency of healthcare systems through early disease diagnosis and timely health management,
040
      thereby reducing the workload of medical professionals. However, the high sensitivity of the medical
041
      field and the strict requirements for accuracy and reliability in clinical decision-making pose great
042   challenges to LLMs. Recent studies find that LLMs are prone to producing hallucinations in medical
043   diagnoses (Singhal et al., 2022; Bubeck et al., 2023), and such erroneous diagnoses could harm the
044   physical and psychological health of patients and also potentially lead to serious medical negligence.
045   How to effectively, reliably, and comprehensively evaluate the true capabilities of LLMs and the
046   accuracy of their diagnostic results, as well as reveal their limitations to avoid potential misdiagnosis,
047   remains an unresolved problem.
048   Recent studies find that existing benchmarks cannot effectively evaluate the medical capabilities
049   of LLMs (Zhou et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). (1) Firstly, existing benchmarks are often based
050   on data collected from online consultation platforms or medical textbooks (Wang et al., 2023),
051   which could easily be included in the training data of LLMs, that is, leading to data leakage or
052   contamination and thus biasing the performance evaluation of LLMs. (2) Secondly, the departmental
053   setup in modern medicine is designed to address the complex medical needs of different structures
      and functions of human organs. The specific skills and treatment methods vary significantly across

084   Additionally, we propose four novel metrics (ClinicalMetrics) to precisely measure the effectiveness
085

086

087
      that different LLMs typically excel in different departmental areas, and no single LLM can
088   perform excellently well in all departmental domains. This behavior conforms to the needs of
089   modern medical specialization.
090
091
      Inspired by the findings, we propose ClinicalAgent, a novel clinical diagnostic agent that dynamically
      allocates the K most relevant departments and assigns N clinicians from each department for a
092
      collaborative consultation based on the patient’s chief complaint. Leveraging flexible department
093
      scheduling and clinician allocation strategies, ClinicalAgent delivers a unified diagnostic result.
094   Figure 1 depicts the workflow of ClinicalAgent. Experiments show that ClinicalAgent outperforms
095   top-performing LLMs in clinical diagnostic performance, demonstrating the importance of aligning
096   with modern medical practice for advancing agentic medical systems. We release ClinicalLab, a
097   comprehensive clinical diagnosis agent alignment suite, including ClinicalBench, ClinicalMetrics,
098   and ClinicalAgent, to promote development of clinical diagnostic agents. Our contributions can be
099   summarized as follows:

102

103
104   • ClinicalMetrics: We introduce four novel metrics to evaluate the practicality and effectiveness of

118   2.1      E XISTING M EDICAL B ENCHMARKS
119
120   The Chinese Medical Benchmark (Wang et al., 2023) consists of CMB-Exam and CMB-Clin,
121   with data collected from various public examination databases and multiple-choice questions from
122   textbooks. MedBench (Cai et al., 2024a) also includes multiple-choice questions from the Chinese
123
      mainland medical licensing examination and 1,025 QA pairs based on electronic medical records.
      MMedBench (Qiu et al., 2024) is a multilingual medical evaluation benchmark covering six languages,
124
      with data sourced from medical textbooks and open-source medical websites in different languages.
125
      Different from our method of collecting real cases from different departments, MMedBench and
126   CMExam (Liu et al., 2023) utilize GPT-4 to categorize each question according to a pre-defined list
127   of departments. However, according to the experimental results shown in Table 3, GPT-4 exhibits
128   significant errors in departmental classification. MedQA (Jin et al., 2020b) consists of multiple-choice
129   questions collected from professional medical board examinations. Its USMLE-style English subset
130   offers four possible answer options for each question and is widely used to evaluate the performance of
131   LLMs in the medical domain. PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) is a biomedical QA dataset collected from
132   the PubMed website, where questions need to be answered with Yes/No/Maybe. MMLU (Hendrycks
133   et al., 2021) is a benchmark covering 57 tasks across multiple domains, recent work (Singhal et al.,
134
      2022) extracts 6 medicine-related tasks from MMLU to evaluate the medical capabilities of LLMs.
      Furthermore, HealthSearchQA (Singhal et al., 2022) is a dataset based on medical conditions and
135
      related symptoms frequently searched by users on search engines, aiming to evaluate a model’s ability
136
      to handle real-world user’s concerns about their health.
137
138   Table 1 compares statistics of existing benchmarks and datasets for evaluating LLMs in the medical
139   field and our ClinicalBench, including the number of samples, task types, language coverage, and
140
      data sources. In summary, the main shortcomings of existing evaluation benchmarks include: (1)
      lack of end-to-end and evenly distributed departmental coverage to prevent evaluation bias; (2)
141
      data sources often come from easily accessible online consultation platforms, medical textbooks,
142
      and professional examinations, which poses high risks of data leakage; (3) existing benchmarks
143   primarily evaluate medical knowledge through multiple-choice questions, which not only differ
144   significantly from real-world diagnostic scenarios but also lack robustness.
145
146   2.2      E XISTING AGENTS FOR M EDICAL A PPLICATIONS
147
148   Recent works attempt to solve medical and healthcare issues through paradigms of dividing tasks and
149   collaboration among multiple agents. MedAgent (Tang et al., 2023) is the first multi-agent framework
150   in the medical field that improves the accuracy of solving medical multiple-choice questions by
151   allowing the same LLM to play different roles in multi-round collaborative dialogues. However, the
152   design of MedAgent still relies on the multiple-choice question format, which differs significantly
153
      from the diagnostic process in the real world. On the other hand, Williams et al. (2023) effectively
      simulate real-world human behavior patterns through agents played by ChatGPT to address the
154
      challenge of incorporating human behavioral factors into epidemic models. MDAgents (Kim et al.,
155
      2024) is a multi-agent framework that employs an adaptive decision-making mechanism, addressing
156   medical multiple-choice questions through multiple stages of checking problem complexity, dynam-
157   ically recruiting experts, reasoning, and decision-making. Meanwhile, Agent Hospital (Li et al.,
158   2024) improves diagnostic accuracy by simulating nearly all medical processes within a hospital
159   and trains doctor agents through doctor-patient interaction simulations. However, due to the lack
160   of a comprehensive dataset covering the entire medical process, currently its effectiveness is only
161   validated on the MedQA multiple-choice dataset. In summary, existing medical agents suffer from
      severe limitations and constraints in designs and evaluations due to the lack of benchmarks and

      3.4.1    D EPARTMENT G UIDE (M ULTI -C HOICE QA WITH 24 OPTIONS )
271
272   When patients first enter the hospital, guiding them to the correct department for further consultation,
273   examination and treatment is crucial for providing targeted medical services. The Department
274   Guide (DG) task requires the model to choose the most reasonable department from a given set of
275   24 departments based on a CC, i.e., DP = DG(CC), where DP denotes the selected department.
276   This task evaluates the decision-making accuracy, and instruction-following capability of LLMs in
277
      real-world medical scenarios.
278
      3.4.2    C LINICAL D IAGNOSIS (G ENERATIVE QA)
279
280   The Clinical Diagnosis task evaluates the LLM’s clinical diagnostic capabilities in real cases and its
281   analysis and interpretation abilities. Clinical Diagnosis includes 6 sub-tasks, each designed according
282   to the standard diagnostic process recommended by the Chinese official record (NHC, 2022) and
283   using the chain-of-thought pattern. The 6 sub-tasks are defined as follows.
284
      Preliminary Diagnosis (PD): The model generates a list of possible diseases P based on CC,
285   MH, and PE, i.e., P = PD(CC, MH, PE). This task primarily evaluates the model’s information
286   synthesis and reasoning abilities.
287
288
      Diagnostic Basis (DB): The model needs to provide supportive medical evidence B for each possible
      disease in P, i.e., B = DB(E, P). This task mainly evaluates the model’s evidence extraction ability
289
      and helps mitigate hallucinations.
290
291   Differential Diagnosis (DD): After considering B, the model should perform a differential analysis D
292   to exclude other diseases that have different causes but similar manifestations, i.e., D = DD(E, P, B).
293   This task primarily evaluates the model’s abilities in analytical comparison and decision-making.
294   Final Diagnosis (FD): Integrating all information, the model needs to determine the final diagnosis
295   F, i.e., F = FD(E, P, B, D). This task mainly evaluates the model’s comprehensive judgment ability
296   and the accuracy of clinical diagnosis.
297
      Principle of Treatment (PT): The model determines the treatment principles and guidelines G for
298   F, i.e., G = PT(F). This task primarily evaluates the model’s memorization of medical standard
299   operating procedures and knowledge points.
300
301
      Treatment Plan (TP): The model formulates specific treatment steps and plans T for the given case
      E, including medication, surgical intervention, and physical therapy, i.e., T = TP(E, P, B, D, F, G).
302
      This task mainly evaluates the model’s abilities in knowledge application and strategic planning.
303
304
      3.4.3    I MAGING D IAGNOSIS (G ENERATIVE QA)
305
306   The Imaging Diagnosis (ID) task requires LLMs to understand detailed textual reports of medical
307   images, identify key features of lesions, such as tumors, inflammation, or other pathological changes,
308   and provide imaging diagnosis results IDR, i.e., IDR = ID(IR). This task evaluates the LLM’s
309   ability to analyze and interpret medical imaging reports for diagnostic support.

442    5.1      C LINICAL AGENT: M ETHODOLOGY
443
444    Our findings in Section 4.3, shows obvious performance deficiencies when requiring a single model
445
       to play different departmental roles in highly specialized medical scenarios. We believe that setting
       specific agents for different departments, i.e., the agent for each department is powered by the
446
       LLM that performs best in the specific domain, aligns better with the real-world practices of human
447
       medical experts. Therefore, we propose ClinicalAgent, an End-to-End Clinical Agent Aligned
448    with Real-World Multi-Departmental Clinical Diagnostic Practices, with its diagnostic process
449    shown in Figure 1. ClinicalAgent operates in 6 stages, as follows. Details of the stages and the
450    workflow algorithm can be found in Appendix A.3.
451
       Departmental Guide: The patient PA presents a chief complaint CC to the patient navigator agent
452
       PN . Based on CC and the prior knowledge PK of LLM rankings obtained in Table 2, agent PN
453
       arranges N clinicians from the K most relevant departments in the consultation room to prepare a
454    preliminary consultation for PA. The purpose of this stage is to quickly and accurately identify the
455    patient’s initial needs and arrange for the appropriate specialty medical team to diagnose.
456
457
       Preliminary Consultation: The PA consults with multiple clinicians from different departments in
       the consultation room, such as pediatrics clinician powered by GPT-4, orthopedic clinician powered
458
       by InternLM2, and hematology clinician powered by Gemini-Pro. Each clinician makes a preliminary
459
       diagnosis P based on the CC, MH, and PE, and decide on further laboratory tests and imageological
460    examinations.
461
462    Laboratory Examination: According to the preliminary consultation advice, the PA proceeds to
463
       the biochemistry laboratory for a series of necessary examinations, such as blood tests and urine
       analysis. Subsequently, based on the laboratory report LR generated by the medical instruments, the
464
       biochemist provides the laboratory diagnostic results LDR.
465
466    Imageological Examination: According to the preliminary consultation advice, the PA undergoes
467    necessary imageological examinations in the radiology room, such as X-rays, CT scans, or MRI.
468    Subsequently, the radiologist provides an imaging diagnosis result IDR based on the radiological
469
       reports IR of these medical images.
470    Final Consultation: The PA returns to the consultation room and provides LDR and IDR to the
471    medical team. Based on the newly acquired reference information, the medical team conducts a
472    step-by-step analysis and ultimately provides results including B, D, F, G, and T .
473    Medical Treatment: The PA receives corresponding treatment based on G and T , including
474    treatments conducted in the treatment room and medication dispensed in the pharmacy. Finally, the
475    patient leaves the hospital after completing the treatment.

      A.2       E VALUATION M ETRICS
846
847   Accuracy indicates the proportion of samples correctly classified by the department to the total
848   number of samples. BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) measures the degree of overlap between the
849   predicted output and the reference output, ranging from 1-gram to 4-gram. Rouge (Lin & Hovy,
850   2003) primarily evaluates based on the longest common subsequence found between the generated
851   texts and the human-written reference ones. BertScore (Zhang et al., 2020) leverages a pre-trained
852   BERT model and its deep contextual representations to gauge the semantic similarity between the
853
      generated and reference texts. In the experiment, we use the “bert-base-chinese”6 model along with
      the “jieba”7 Chinese segmentation tool to ensure accurate word tokenization.
854
855   To ensure the accuracy of BLEU, ROUGE, and BertScore when evaluating medical synonyms, we
856   implement several key steps. First, we compile a list of synonyms for 150 diseases from medical
857   textbooks and online encyclopedias, encompassing a range of expression variations. To address
858   discrepancies in terminology arising from differences in model training data, we also request that
859
      17 models independently generate synonym lists for each disease. We then merge the synonyms
      collected from textbooks and encyclopedias with those generated by the models. This combined
860
      list is rigorously screened by three medical experts among the authors to produce a standardized
861
      synonym list.

1023   A.6   L IMITATIONS
1024
1025   To date, no medical agents have been evaluated on real clinical data, including the recent MedA-
       gents (Tang et al., 2023) and Agent Hospital (Li et al., 2024). Existing agents are primarily designed


                                                        19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       for handling multiple-choice questions and are not suitable for simulating complex real-world scenar-
1027   ios. Specifically, after carefully reviewing the original paper and code of MedAgents, we find that
1028   evaluating it would require significant modifications to its experimental design and prompts. These
1029   changes would force MedAgents’ core process, which is designed for multiple-choice questions, to
1030   shift toward a generative question-answering approach, likely impacting its performance negatively.
1031   In addition to the previously mentioned differences in design goals, data incompatibility, and vari-
1032   ations in evaluation metrics and scope, we are particularly concerned that such comparisons may
1033   lead to an unfair evaluation of MedAgents. Therefore, ClinicalAgent cannot be directly compared
1034   with other agents. However, as a foundational approach, ClinicalAgent can integrate well with other
1035
       agents.
1036   We have not conducted practical engineering tests on widely recognized methods such as few-shot
1037   learning and RAG, although these methods have proven effective in other medical agents. Future
1038   research can consider combining these mature techniques with our ClinicalAgent approach to explore
1039   their potential applications in clinical settings. We view this as a direction for future work.
1040   The data in ClinicalBench comes from mainland China and only follows the officially recommended
1041   diagnostic methods and procedures in mainland China (NHC, 2022). Therefore, there may be a lack
1042   of representativeness for other regions and countries.
