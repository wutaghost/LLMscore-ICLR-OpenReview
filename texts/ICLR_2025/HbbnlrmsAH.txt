009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series foundation models have demonstrated impressive performance as zero-
012            shot forecasters, i.e., they can tackle a wide variety of downstream forecasting tasks
013            without explicit task-specific training. However, achieving effectively unified train-
014            ing on time series remains an open challenge. Existing approaches introduce some
015            level of model specialization to account for the highly heterogeneous nature of time
016
               series data. For instance, M OIRAI pursues unified training by employing multiple
               input/output projection layers, each tailored to handle time series at a specific fre-
017
               quency. Similarly, TimesFM maintains a frequency embedding dictionary for this
018
               purpose. We identify two major drawbacks to this human-imposed frequency-level
019            model specialization: (1) Frequency is not a reliable indicator of the underlying
020            patterns in time series. For example, time series with different frequencies can
021            display similar patterns, while those with the same frequency may exhibit varied
022            patterns. (2) Non-stationarity is an inherent property of real-world time series,
023            leading to varied distributions even within a short context window of a single time
024            series. Frequency-level specialization is too coarse-grained to capture this level of
025            diversity. To address these limitations, this paper introduces M OIRAI -M O E, using
026            a single input/output projection layer while delegating the modeling of diverse time
027
               series patterns to the sparse mixture of experts (MoE) within Transformers. With
               these designs, M OIRAI -M O E reduces reliance on human-defined heuristics and
028
               enables automatic token-level specialization. Extensive experiments on 39 datasets
029
               demonstrate the superiority of M OIRAI -M O E over existing foundation models
030            in both in-distribution and zero-shot scenarios. Furthermore, this study conducts
031            comprehensive model analyses to explore the inner workings of time series MoE
032            foundation models and provides valuable insights for future research.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Foundation models have transformed several fields, such as natural language processing (Dubey et al.,
038   2024) and computer vision (Kirillov et al., 2023), demonstrating impressive zero-shot performance.
039
      Inspired by these successes, time series forecasting is experiencing a similar shift (Liang et al.,
      2024). The traditional approach of developing separate models for each dataset is being replaced
040
      by the concept of universal forecasting (Woo et al., 2024), where a pretrained model can be applied
041
      across diverse downstream tasks in a zero-shot manner, regardless of variations in domain, frequency,
042   dimensionality, context, or prediction length. This new paradigm significantly reduces the complexity
043   of building numerous specialized models, paving the way for forecasting-as-a-service.
044
045
      To excel in zero-shot forecasting, time series foundation models are pretrained on massive data from
      a variety of sources. However, unlike language and vision modalities which benefit from standardized
046
      input formats, time series data is inherently heterogeneous, posing significant challenges for unified
047
      time series training. Existing solutions such as TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024) and UniTime (Liu et al.,
048   2024a) leverage language prompts to provide data identification information, thereby discerning the
049   source of data and achieving model specialization at the dataset level. M OIRAI (Woo et al., 2024)
050   goes a step further and proposes a more granular categorization based on a time series meta feature –
051   frequency. Specifically, they design multiple input/output projection layers with each layer specialized
052   to handle data corresponding to a specific frequency, thereby enabling frequency-level specialization.
053   Similarly, TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) is also at this level of specialization, distinguishing the data by
      maintaining a frequency embedding mapping.

072
073   Given the heterogeneity of time series, we acknowledge the value of model specialization; however,
074   we argue that human-imposed frequency-level specialization lacks generalizability and introduces
075   several limitations. (1) Frequency is not always a reliable indicator and might not effectively capture
076   the true structure of time series data. As shown in Figure 1, time series with different frequencies
077   can exhibit similar patterns, while those with the same frequency may display diverse and unrelated
078   patterns. This human-imposed mismatch between frequency and pattern undermines the efficacy
079   of model specialization, resulting in inferior performance. (2) Furthermore, real-world time series
080   are inherently non-stationary (Liu et al., 2022), displaying varied distributions even within a short
081
      context window of a single time series. Clearly, frequency-level specialization is too coarse-grained
      to capture this level of diversity, underscoring the need for more fine-grained modeling approaches.
082
083   To address the aforementioned issues, this paper introduces M OIRAI -M O E, an innovative solution
084   for effective time series unified training, inspired by recent developments of Sparse Mixture of Experts
085   (MoE) Transformers (Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024). The core idea of
086   M OIRAI -M O E is to utilize a single input/output projection layer while delegateing the modeling
087
      of diverse time series patterns to the sparse specialized experts in Transformer layers. With these
      designs, specialization of M OIRAI -M O E is achieved in a data-driven manner and operates at the
088
      token level. Moreover, this study investigates existing expert gating functions that generally use a
089
      randomly initialized linear layer for expert assignments (Shazeer et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2024) and
090   introduces a new function that leverages cluster centroids derived from a pretrained model to guide
091   expert allocations.
092
093
      We extensively evaluate M OIRAI -M O E using a total of 39 datasets in in-distribution and zero-
      shot forecasting scenarios. The results confirm the superiority of M OIRAI -M O E over state-of-
094
      the-art foundation models including TimesFM (Das et al., 2024), Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024),
095
      and M OIRAI (Woo et al., 2024). Additionally, we conduct comprehensive model analyses, as the
096   first attempt, to explore the inner workings of time series MoE foundation models. It reveals that
097   M OIRAI -M O E acquires the capability to achieve frequency-invariant representations and essentially
098   performs progressive denoising throughout the model. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
099
100   • We propose M OIRAI -M O E, the first mixture-of-experts time series foundation model, achieving
101     token-level model specialization in a data-driven manner. We introduce a new expert gating function
102     for accurate expert assignments and improved performance.
103
      • Extensive experiments on 39 datasets reveal that M OIRAI -M O E delivers up to 17% performance
104
        improvements over M OIRAI at the same level of model size, and outperforms other time series
105     foundation models with up to 65× fewer activated parameters.
106
107   • We conduct thorough model analyses to deepen understanding of the inner workings of time series
        MoE foundation models and summarize valuable insights for future research.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Foundation Models for Time Series Forecasting Time series foundation models serve as versatile
111   zero-shot forecasting tools. A key challenge in training these models is accommodating the high
112   diversity of time series data, underscoring the possible need for designing specialization modules.
113   Current approaches like TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024) and UniTime (Liu et al., 2024a) utilize language-
114   based prompts to identify data sources, facilitating model specialization at the dataset level. M OIRAI
115   (Woo et al., 2024) advances this by focusing on a time series meta feature – frequency. This method
116   designs separate input/output projection layers for specific frequencies, allowing for frequency-
117   specific specialization. Similarly, TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) operates at this level of specialization
118   by incorporating a frequency embedding dictionary to differentiate data. Some methods, like Chronos
119
      (Ansari et al., 2024), Lag-LLaMA (Rasul et al., 2023), Moment (Goswami et al., 2024), and Timer
      (Liu et al., 2024c), do not incorporate any specialization modules. Instead, they utilize the same
120
      architecture for all time series data, which can potentially increase the learning complexity and
121
      demand a large number of parameters to memorize the diverse input patterns. In this work, we
122   propose to achieve automatic token-level specialization by using sparse mixture of experts, where
123   diverse time series tokens are processed by specialized experts, while similar tokens share parameter
124   space, thereby reducing learning complexity.
125
126   Sparse Mixture of Experts Mixture of experts (MoE) has emerged as an effective method for
127   significantly scaling up model capacity while minimizing computation overhead in Large Language
128   Models (LLMs) (Fedus et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). A common approach for
129   integrating MoE into Transformers involves replacing Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs) with MoE
130   layers. An MoE layer consists of multiple expert networks and a gating function, where each expert
131   shares the same structure as a standard FFN. The gating function is responsible for producing a
132   gating vector that indicates the expert assignment. The assignment is usually sparse to maintain
133
      computational efficiency in the MoE layer, meaning that each token is generally processed by only
      one (Fedus et al., 2022) or two (Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024) experts. In time series
134
      forecasting, several studies employ the concept of mixture of experts (Zeevi et al., 1996; Yuksel et al.,
135
      2012; Ni et al., 2024). In their contexts, the term experts typically refers to linear-centric models,
136   such as autoregressive linear models and DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023). However, these methods are
137   trained on specific datasets, limiting their ability to generalize and function as foundation models.
138

140   3     M ETHODOLOGY
141
142   In this section, we present M OIRAI -M O E, a mixture-of-experts time series foundation model built
143   upon M OIRAI (Woo et al., 2024). Figure 2 presents a comparison. While M OIRAI -M O E inherits
144
      many of the strengths of M OIRAI, its major enhancement lies in: rather than using multi heuristic-
      defined input/output projection layers to model time series with different frequencies, M OIRAI -M O E
145
      utilizes a single input/output projection layer while delegating the task of capturing diverse time series
146
      patterns to the sparse mixture of experts in the Transformer. In addition, M OIRAI -M O E proposes a
147   novel gating function that leverages knowledge from a pretrained model, and adopts a decoder-only
148   training objective to improve training efficiency by enabling parallel learning of various context
149   lengths in a single model update. We describe each model component in the following parts.
150
151   3.1   T IME S ERIES T OKEN C ONSTRUCTION
152
153   Patching techniques, first introduced in PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), have become a prevalent method
154   in many state-of-the-art time series models (Das et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Woo et al., 2024). By
155   aggregating adjacent time series data into patches, this technique effectively captures local semantic
156   information and significantly reduces computational overhead when processing long inputs. Given
157   a time series with length S, we segment it into non-overlapping patches of size P , resulting in a
158
      sequence of patches x ∈ RN ×P , where N = ⌈ PS ⌉.
159   We then normalize the patches to mitigate distribution shift issues (Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023).
160   In a decoder-only (autoregressive) model, where each patch predicts its succeeding patch, applying a
161   causal normalizer to each patch is the most effective way to achieve accurate normalization. However,
      this approach generates N subsequences with different lengths, diminishing the parallel training

228   Token Clusters as Gating Function. In this work, we propose a new gating mechanism that
229   leverages cluster centroids derived from the token representations of a pretrained model to guide
230
      expert allocations. The intuition behind this approach is that clusters of pretrained token embeddings

231
      compared to a randomly initialized linear projection layer. Specifically, we first pretrain a M OIRAI
232

235   clustering on them to continuously learn clusters at each layer. The number of clusters is set to match
236   the total number of experts. During MoE training, for each layer, each token computes the Euclidean
237   distance to learned cluster centroids C ∈ RM ×D , and these distances serve as token-to-expert affinity

469   Obs 3: Shallow layers have more routing preferences than deep layers. According to Figure
470   6, as the layer index increases, expert selection gradually converges, with only 3 out of 32 experts
471   being chosen by the final layer. This behavior contrasts with patterns observed in LLMs (Zhu et al.,
472   2024), where earlier layers typically concentrate on a limited number of experts to capture common
473   linguistic features, while deeper layers target more task-specific characteristics. This divergence may
474
      stem from the dynamic and noisier nature of time series tokens, which are generated from small
      time windows, unlike language tokens derived from a fixed vocabulary. Our findings suggest that
475

476
      conclusions from GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023), which found that as the layer depth increases, token
477   vectors are projected into the low-dimensional top eigenvector space of input patterns. Additionally,
478   we recognize that some experts in M OIRAI -M O E are rarely selected. Pruning these underutilized
479   experts for model compression is left for future work.

807   The following is a brief introduction to the models used in the evaluation process.
808
          1
809           https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/dharanikra/electrical-power-demand-in-turkey
          2
              https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/leonardo00/istanbul-traffic-index

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      • TiDE (Das et al., 2023) encodes the historical data of a time series along with covariates using
811     dense multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs). It then decodes the time series while incorporating future
812     covariates, also utilizing dense MLPs for this process.
813
      • PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) employs Transformer encoders combined with patching and channel
814
        independence techniques to enhance the performance of time series forecasting.
815
816
      • iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024b) treats independent time series as tokens to effectively capture
        multivariate correlations through self-attention.
817
818   • MoLE-DLinear (Ni et al., 2024) trains multiple linear-centric models (i.e., experts) and a router
819
        model that weighs and mixes their outputs. In this study, we use the DLinear model as the experts.
820   • LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023) is a method for time series forecasting that leverages Large
821     Language Models by encoding numerical data as text and generating possible future values through
822     text completions.
823   • TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) is a decoder-only time series foundation model that pretrained on a
824     large corpus of time series data, including both real-world and synthetic datasets.
825   • TTM (Ekambaram et al., 2024) is a foundation model based on the light-weight TSMixer architec-
826     ture, incorporating innovations like adaptive patching, diverse resolution sampling, and resolution

      B.3                 E FFECTS OF M ASKING R ATIO
931
932   In this study, we introduce the masking ratio r as a hyperparameter that determines the portion of the
933   entire sequence used solely for robust normalizer calculation, helping to mitigate distribution shift
934   issues. We conduct experiments to assess the effects of different masking ratios, with the evaluation
935   results on the Monash benchmark shown in Figure 9 (right). A masking ratio of 0.3 delivers the best
936   performance. A ratio of 0.1 uses too little data to compute a robust normalizer, potentially failing to
937   accurately represent the overall sequence statistics. Conversely, a ratio of 0.5 masks half of the data,
938   which may hinder the parallel learning efficiency in decoder-only training. Therefore, it is crucial
939
      to select an appropriate data range that is small enough to avoid excessive masking, yet sufficiently
      representative for robust normalizer computation.

      In this part, we present an in-depth comparison of the different gating functions explored in this study.
944
945   First, we provide additional details on the implementation of the proposed token clustering method.
946   The core idea of this approach is to leverage cluster centroids derived from the token representations
947   of a pretrained model to guide expert allocations. Specifically, we perform inference on our training
948   corpus, LOTSA, using data amount corresponding to 100 epochs. During this process, we extract the
949
      self-attention output representations from a pretrained M OIRAI model and apply mini-batch k-means
      clustering to continuously update the clusters. The number of clusters is set to match the total number
950
      of experts. During the training of the MoE model, each token computes the Euclidean distance
951
      to each cluster centroid, and these distances are used as token-to-expert affinity scores for expert
952   assignments. Empirical evaluations have demonstrated the effectiveness of this approach compared
953   to randomly learned gating from scratch, indicating that the clustering method better aligns with the
954   inherent distribution of time series representations.
955
      Using the three gating functions explored in this study, i.e., linear projection, linear projection with
956
      load balancing, and token clustering, we present their expert allocation distributions aggregated across
957
      all datasets in the Monash benchmark, as illustrated in Figure 10. In terms of selection diversity, we
958   observe the following relationships: Token Clusters (least diverse) < Pure Linear Projection (neutral)
959   < Linear Projection with Load Balancing (most diverse). According to their performance results
960   shown in Figure 4, we can establish the following ranking: Token Clusters > Linear Projection with
961   Load Balancing > Pure Linear Projection. Based on all these observations, we offer the following
962   explanation:
963
964   • In the token clusters approach, the expert selections are less diverse because the routing is grounded
965     in pretrained knowledge. The clustering step creates centroids that represent well-structured
966     patterns in the data, and then tokens are routed to specific experts that are particularly suited to
967     handle the type of data represented by their corresponding cluster. While this targeted routing
968
        reduces diversity, it enhances performance due to the selection of experts based on more meaningful
        criteria.
969
970   • The addition of load balancing loss increases the diversity of expert selection by spreading the
971     workload and encouraging the use of all experts more evenly. This diversity prevents over-reliance
        on specific experts, potentially improving generalization and performance compared to pure linear
