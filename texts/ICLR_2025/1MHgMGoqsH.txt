011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce a Model Predictive Control (MPC) framework for training deep
014            neural networks, systematically unifying the Back-Propagation (BP) and Forward-
015            Forward (FF) algorithms. At the same time, it gives rise to a range of intermediate
016
               training algorithms with varying look-forward horizons, leading to a performance-
               efficiency trade-off. We perform a precise analysis of this trade-off on a deep linear
017
               network, where the qualitative conclusions carry over to general networks. Based
018
               on our analysis, we propose a principled method to choose the optimization horizon
019            based on given objectives and model specifications. Numerical results on various
020            models and tasks demonstrate the versatility of our method.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Neural Networks (NN) are rapidly developing in recent years and have found widespread application
026   across various fields. While Back-Propagation (BP) (Rumelhart et al.) stands as the predominant
027   training method, its high memory requirements limit its application to deep model, large batch sizes,
028   and memory-constrained devices, such as those encountered in large language models (Touvron et al.,
029   2023; OpenAI et al., 2023; Gu & Dao, 2023). To address these limitations, recent research has sought
030   to mitigate the drawbacks associated with BP or explore alternative training methods altogether.
031   Notably, Hinton (2022) proposed a Forward-Forward (FF) algorithm that uses layer-wise local loss
032   to avoid backward propagation through layers. Xiong et al. (2020) also proposed a local loss learning
033
      strategy. Local loss methods are theoretically more memory-efficient and biologically realizable than
      BP. However, the performance of FF algorithms is often inferior to that of BP, particularly in deep
034
      models. Moreover, the mechanism of FF is unclear, and lacks understanding of why and when FF
035
      can work.
036
037   Is there an optimization method that can balance the memory usage and accuracy? Inspired by the
038   parallels between Back-Propagation and the Pontryagin Maximum Principle (Li et al., 2017), we
039
      discover the similarity between local loss and the greedy algorithm. Utilizing the concept of the Model
      Predictive Control (MPC), we introduce the MPC framework for deep learning. In this framework,
040
      FF and BP are firstly unified, representing two extremes under this framework. Some other previous
041
      works (Xiong et al., 2020; Nøkland & Eidnes, 2019; Belilovsky et al., 2019) (Remark 3.1) are also
042   incorporated in this framework. This framework offers not only a spectrum of optimization algorithms
043   with varying horizons to balance performance and memory demand but also a dynamical viewpoint to
044   understand the FF algorithm. Additionally, our theoretical analysis of the deep linear neural network
045   shows that the gradient estimation converges polynomially as the horizon approaches full back-
046   propagation (Theorem 3.4), leading to diminishing returns for sufficiently large horizons. However,
047   the memory demand grows constantly with respect to the horizon, indicating an intermediate region
048   offering a favorable trade-off between memory and accuracy. Based on this analysis, we propose
049   horizon selection algorithms for different objectives to balance the accuracy-efficiency trade-off.
050   The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
051
052          • We propose a novel MPC framework for deep neural network training, unifying BP and FF
053            algorithms and providing a range of optimization algorithms with different accuracy-memory
               balances.

      2    L ITERATURE R EVIEW
069
070
      Despite its great success in deep learning, it has been argued that Back-Propagation is memory
      inefficient and biologically implausible (Hinton, 2022). Recently, numerous studies have been
071
      focused on addressing the shortcomings of BP, such as direct feedback alignment algorithm (Nøkland,
072
      2016), synthetic gradient (Jaderberg et al., 2017), and LoCo algorithm (Xiong et al., 2020). Notably,
073   Hinton (2022) proposed a forward-forward algorithm that uses layer-wise local loss to avoid backward
074   propagation through layers. Although these methods mitigate the memory inefficiency of BP, they
075   often suffer from inferior performance or introduce additional structures to the models and the
076   theoretical understanding of when and why these methods work is insufficient.
077
      The connection and comparison between residual neural networks and control systems were observed
078
      by E. Subsequently, Li et al. (2017) used relationships between Back-Propagation and the Pontryagin
079   maximum principle in optimal control theory to develop alternative training algorithms. Following
080   this, several works have introduced control methods aimed at enhancing optimization algorithms
081   in neural network training (Li et al., 2017; Weng et al.; Nguyen et al., 2024). These prior works
082   have primarily relied on powerful PID controllers or optimal control theory, which, in practice,
083   are rooted in the concept of backward propagation and thus inherit the drawbacks associated with
084   BP. Conversely, MPC (Grüne & Pannek), another well-known controller, has yet to be extensively
085   explored in the realm of deep learning (Weinan et al.).
086   The analysis of deep linear networks has been extensively explored in the literature like (Cohen
087   et al., 2022; Arora et al., 2018b). Furthermore, research has investigated the convergence rates of
088   coarse gradients in various contexts, such as quantifying neural networks (Long et al.), zeroth-order
089   optimization methods (Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), and truncated gradients in recurrent
090   neural networks (Aicher et al., 2019). Our work builds upon these analyses combine insights from
091   both domains to analyze the performance of the proposed MPC framework on deep linear networks.

      3.1    BACK -P ROPAGATION AND F ORWARD -F ORWARD A LGORITHM
116
117   The Back-Propagation algorithm (Rumelhart et al.) computes the gradient of the loss function for the
118   weights of the neural network:
119
                                      gBP (u(t)) = ∇u(t) J(u) = ∇u(t) L(x(T )).                                       (3)
120
121   Recently, Hinton (2022) proposed the Forward-Forward (FF) algorithm as an alternative to BP. The
122   FF algorithm computes gradients using the local loss function of the current block:
123
                                              gFF (u(t)) = ∇u(t) L(x(t + 1)).                                         (4)
124
125   In the original paper (Hinton, 2022), the author used the Euclidean norm of the layer output as a loss,
126   but here we use the more general form of the loss function.
127   While the FF algorithm is more memory-efficient than BP, its performance may be worse due to the
128   lack of global information. In contrast, BP is typically more accurate but demands more memory.
129   Moreover, the theoretical understanding of when and why FF might perform well remains limited.
130   Leveraging the concept of MPC in deep learning models, we propose a novel MPC framework that
131   unifies the BP and FF algorithms. This framework offers a spectrum of optimization algorithms with
132   varying horizons, allowing for a balance between performance and memory demand.

355   5       E XPERIMENT R ESULTS
356
357   To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed MPC framework for deep learning models, we conduct
358   some distinct experiments: 1) linear residual Neural Network (Res Linear NN); 2) residual MLP
359   (Res MLP); 3) 62-layer ResNet model (Sec 4.2 (He et al., 2016)) (ResNet-62); 4) fine-tuning ResNet-
360   50 (He et al., 2016) and ViT-b16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). Detailed model structure, dataset, and
361   training settings can be found in Appendix D, and all the experiments are conducted in NVIDIA
362   GeForce RTX 3090 using TensorFlow (Abadi et al., 2016).
363   In summary, our findings reveal that:
364
365             1. The theoretical result of the linear NN model is qualitatively consistent with numerical
366                experiments on nonlinear models with respect to polynomial convergence of gradient and
367                the linear growth of memory usage (Section 5.1).
368             2. The performance of larger horizons will be better and will converge to the results of BP
369                algorithm (Full horizon) quickly (Section 5.2).
370             3. The optimal horizon depends on the task, model structure, and objective. Both BP and
371                FF algorithms can be optimal horizons, but in most cases, an intermediate horizon will

498   6   D ISCUSSION
499
500   This study presents a systematic framework that integrates the Forward-Forward algorithm with
501   Back-Propagation, offering a family of algorithms to train deep neural networks. Theoretical analysis
502   on deep linear networks demonstrates a polynomial convergence of gradients concerning the horizon
503   and the diminished return for large horizons. Based on the analysis result and various objectives,
504   we propose an objective-based horizon selection algorithm to balance performance and memory
505
      efficiency. Additionally, numerical experiments across various tasks verify the qualitative alignment
      of theory and underscore the significant impact of horizon selection. However, many phenomena in
506
      the MPC framework are not fully analyzed or understood. Here are some limitations of this study.
507
508   Firstly, the analysis is primarily based on deep linear networks, but analyzing nonlinear deep networks
509   remains challenging, and existing results heavily rely on the BP algorithm and are thus not applicable
510   to the proposed framework. Nevertheless, numerical experiments show that our theoretical result
511
      qualitatively complies with the behavior observed in nonlinear models and the analysis provides
      enough insight for the horizon selection algorithm.
512
513   Secondly, while the numerical result demonstrates the significant potential of the MPC framework,
514   our proposed horizon selection algorithm involves simplifications and empirical fittings. However,
515   loss estimation is not our primary focus, and the experiments show that the proposed algorithm
516   achieves a good balance between performance and memory efficiency compared to baselines. Future
517
      studies could refine this algorithm with more precise loss estimation techniques.
518   Thirdly, our research focuses solely on the horizon’s influence, neglecting other MPC framework
519   hyperparameters like block selection and loss splitting methods which are also important. We leave
520   these for the future studies.

1110   We also verify the memory usage on the first three tasks. In TensorFlow 2.0 or above versions, there
1111   are two modes: eager mode and static mode. The eager mode will release memory efficiently and
1112   give the theoretical linear dependency between memory usage and horizon, while the static mode
1113   will create the whole computation graph, causing every back-propagation to be counted, resulting
1114   in O(h(T − h + 1)) memory usage (refer to Figure 1). In the paper, we hypothesis that the model
1115   is running in the eager mode, i.e. the memory is efficiently used thus linear/affine with respect to
1116   horizon h.
