                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Machine unlearning aims to provide privacy guarantees to users when they request
012
               deletion, such that an attacker who can compromise the system post-unlearning
013            cannot recover private information about the deleted individuals. Previously pro-
014            posed definitions of unlearning require the unlearning algorithm to exactly or ap-
015            proximately recover the hypothesis obtained by retraining-from-scratch on the re-
016            maining samples. While this definition has been the gold standard in machine
017            unlearning, unfortunately, because it is designed for the worst-case attacker (that
018            can recover the updated hypothesis and the remaining dataset), developing rig-
019            orous, and memory or compute-efficient unlearning algorithms that satisfy this
020            definition has been challenging. In this work, we propose a new definition of un-
021
               learning, called system aware unlearning, that takes into account the information
               that an attacker could recover by compromising the system (post-unlearning). We
022
               prove that system-aware unlearning generalizes commonly referred to definitions
023
               of unlearning by restricting what the attacker knows, and furthermore, may be eas-
024            ier to satisfy in scenarios where the system-information available to the attacker is
025            limited, e.g. because the learning algorithm did not use the entire training dataset
026            to begin with. Towards that end, we develop an exact system-aware-unlearning
027            algorithm that is both memory and computation-time efficient for function classes
028            that can be learned via sample compression. We then present an improvement
029            over this for the special case of learning linear classifiers by using selective sam-
030            pling for data compression, thus giving the first memory and time-efficient exact
031            unlearning algorithm for linear classification. We analyze the tradeoffs between
032
               deletion capacity, accuracy, memory, and computation time for these algorithms.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In the era of large-scale machine learning (ML) models, which are often trained on extensive datasets
038   containing sensitive or personal information, concerns surrounding privacy and data protection have
039
      become increasingly prominent (Yao et al., 2024). These models, due to their high capacity to mem-
      orize patterns in the training data, may inadvertently retain and expose information about individual
040
      data points (Carlini et al., 2021). This presents significant challenges in the context of privacy regula-
041
      tions such as the European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (2016) (GDPR), California
042   Consumer Privacy Act (2018) (CCPA), and Canada’s proposed Consumer Privacy Protection Act,
043   all of which emphasize the “right to be forgotten.” As a result, there is a growing need for meth-
044   ods that enable the selective removal of specific training data from models that have already been
045   trained, a process commonly referred to as machine unlearning (Cao & Yang, 2015).
046
      Machine unlearning addresses the need to remove data from a model’s knowledge base without
047
      the need to retrain the model from scratch each time there is a deletion request, since this can be
048   computationally expensive and often impractical for large-scale systems. The overarching objective
049   here is to ensure that, post-unlearning, a model “acts” as if the removed data were never part of the
050   training process (Sekhari et al., 2021a; Ghazi et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2019). Traditionally, this has
051   been defined through notions of exact (or approximate) unlearning, wherein the model’s hypothe-
052   sis after unlearning should be identical (or probabilistically equivalent) to the model obtained by
053   retraining from scratch on the entire data after removing just the deleted points. While such defini-
      tions offer rigorous guarantees even in the most pessimistic scenarios, they often impose stringent

054
      requirements, limiting the practical applicability of machine unlearning. This is evidenced by a dire
055   lack of exact/approximate unlearning algorithms beyond the simple cases of convex loss functions.
056
057   At the core of the unlearning problem lies a fundamental question: What does it truly mean to “re-
058
      move” a data point from a trained model? And more importantly, when we provide privacy guar-
      anteed for deleted points against an outside observer/attacker, what information can this attacker
059
      reasonably possess? The current definitions of exact/approximate unlearning take a worst-case per-
060
      spective here and focus on the output hypothesis being indistinguishable from a retrained model
061   (Sekhari et al., 2021a; Ghazi et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2019; Cherapanamjeri et al., 2024). However,
062   this approach overlooks a key aspect of the unlearning problem—the observer and its knowledge of
063   the system. In the real world, the feasibility and complexity of unlearning should depend on what
064   the observer can access—be it the model parameters, data retained by the ML system in its memory,
065   data ever encountered by the ML system etc. For instance, consider a learning algorithm that relies
066   on only a fraction of its training dataset to generate its hypothesis and hence the ML system only
067   stores this data. In such cases, unlearning a data point should intuitively be more straightforward.
068   Even if the entire data in the memory of the system is compromised at some point, only the privacy
069
      of the stored points are at jeopardy as long as the learnt model does not reveal much about points
      that were not used by the model. Even if an observer/attacker has access to larger public data sets
070
      that might include parts of the data the system was trained on, in such a system we could expect
071
      privacy for data that the system does not use directly for building the model to be preserved. Con-
072   versely, if the algorithm utilizes the entire dataset and retains all information in memory, unlearning
073   becomes far more challenging, potentially requiring retraining from scratch. This suggests that, in
074   practice, the difficulty of unlearning is not solely determined by the learning algorithm but also by
075   the observer’s ability to detect traces of the removed data stored in the system or otherwise observed.
076
077   Contributions. We propose a new, system-aware formulation of machine unlearning, which in-
078   corporates the observer’s perspective into the unlearning process. By explicitly considering what the
079   observer knows about the system, we argue that exact unlearning, as traditionally defined, is often
080   unnecessarily strict and computationally inefficient. Our framework leverages the fact that many
081   ML systems do not depend on the entirety of their training data equally, allowing for more efficient
082
      and targeted unlearning approaches that better balance computational cost and privacy guarantees.
083   We then present a general-purpose, exact system-aware unlearning algorithm using data sharding
084   for function classes that can learned using sample compression, establishing theoretical bounds on
085   its computation time, memory requirements, deletion capacity, and excess risk guarantees. Previ-
086   ous works using data sharding for unlearning, such as Bourtoule et al. (2021), lack such theoretical
087   guarantees. We also provide an improved system-aware unlearning algorithm for the special case of
088
      linear classification thus providing the first efficient exact unlearning algorithm for linear classifica-
      tion requiring sublinear in the number of samples. This is particularly noteworthy because under the
089
      traditional definition of unlearning, Cherapanamjeri et al. (2024) proved that exact unlearning for
090
      linear classification requires Ω(n) memory, essentially requiring the storage of the entire dataset.
091
092   Through this new lens on machine unlearning, we aim to bridge the gap between having rigorous
093   theoretical guarantees and providing practical unlearning algorithms, thus hoping to develop scalable
094
      solutions for privacy-preserving machine learning (Tran et al., 2024; Cummings et al., 2023).

104   Our goal in machine unlearning is to provide a privacy guarantee to data samples that request to be
105   deleted, while ensuring that the updated hypothesis post-unlearning still has small excess risk. We
106   first present the standard definition of machine unlearning, as stated in Sekhari et al. (2021b); Guo
107   et al. (2019), often referred to as certified machine learning, which generalizes the commonly used
      data deletion guarantee from Ginart et al. (2019).

118   Sekhari et al. (2021a) also defined a notion of deletion capacity, which controls the number of
119   samples that can be deleted while satisfying the above definition, and simultaneously ensuring good
120   excess risk performance.
121   While the above definition, or its variations, have been the go-to definitions in machine unlearning
122   research, we argue with a very simple example that it may, unfortunately, be an overkill even in
123   some toy scenarios where we want to unlearn. Consider an algorithm that learns by first randomly
124   sampling a small subset C ⊆ S of size m and then uses C to train a model. Now, consider an
125   unlearning algorithm that, when given some deletion requests U , simply retrains from scratch on
126   C ∖ U . Note that this is a valid unlearning algorithm from the perspective of an attacker who can
127
      only observe the model after unlearning because the model after unlearning contains no information
      about the deleted individuals U . On the other hand, this unlearning algorithm is not equivalent to
128
      rerunning the algorithm from scratch on S ∖ U which would involve sampling a different subset C ′
129
      of m samples from S ∖ U and then training a model on C ′ . Since C ′ contains m samples whereas
130   C ∖ U contains m − ∣U ∣ samples, the hypotheses learned using the respective datasets will likely
131   not be statistically indistinguishable from each other. Thus, under Definition 1, this is not a valid
132   unlearning algorithm, even though the above-mentioned attacker can gain no information about the
133   deleted individuals.
134
      The crucial thing to note is that Definition 1 considers a worst-case scenario that every point en-
135
      countered by the unlearning algorithm except for the deletion requests, regardless of whether it is
136   used or stored, are known to the attacker. However, a model trained on C ∖ U reveals no information
137   about U to an outside observer of the model after unlearning. In particular, samples that were never
138   used for learning or stored in memory can never be leaked to the attacker. Unfortunately, previous
139   definitions are unable to benefit from this aspect which is apparent from the lack of any non-trivial
140   memory / compute efficient unlearning algorithms (Ghazi et al., 2023). However, before we provide
141   a new definition of unlearning, we need to formalize the information that a learner can access about
142   the system post-unlearning.
143   Definition 2 (State-of-System). For an unlearning algorithm A, define the function IA ∶ Z ∗ × Z ∗ ↦
144   Z ∗ to denote the state of the system that is visible to an external observer post-unlearning. In
145   particular, for any S ⊂ Z ∗ , and deletion requests U ⊆ S, the quantity IA (S, U ) ⊆ S is the subset
146   of data points from dataset S that is stored by or used in the output of the unlearning algorithm A
147   after A has finished processing deletions requests U after initially learning on S. This represents
148
      the information that an external observer/attacker gains about the original sample by observing the
      system after unlearning (e.g. the model, any stored samples, auxiliary data statistics, etc.).
149
150
      Whenever clear from the context, we will drop the subscript A from IA to simplify the notation.
151   For some examples of the state-of-system, for an unlearning algorithm that stores multiple models
152   trained on different subsets of data, the state of the system denotes the union of the training data
153   splits, and for an unlearning algorithm that upon a deletion request deletes every sample and returns
154   a null predictor, the state of the system is the empty set. The adversary can access more information
155   in the former scenario than the latter; thus, it should be more challenging to unlearn in the former.
156   Definition 3 (System-Aware-(ε, δ)-Unlearning). Let A ∶ Z ∗ × Z ∗ ↦ ∆(F) be a (possibly random-
157   ized) learning-unlearning algorithm, such that for a dataset S and deletion requests U , A(S, U )
158   returns a hypothesis in F after first learning on sample S and then processing a set of deletion
159   requests U . We say that the algorithm A is system-aware-(ε, δ)-unlearning if for all S and U ⊆ S,
160   there exists a S ′ such that IA (S ′ , ∅) = IA (S, U ) and S ′ ∩ U = ∅, such that for all F ⊆ F
161
                                Pr(A(S, U ) ∈ F ) ≤ eε ⋅ Pr(A(S ′ , ∅) ∈ F ) + δ

167   System aware unlearning requires that the model output after initially learning on S and then un-
168   learning U be indistinguishable from a model that learns on some plausible S ′ from the perspective
169   of the attacker and processes no deletion requests. Notice that S ′ contains no information about U .
170   Thus, we have properly unlearned if we can match the model and system state of the algorithm on
171   S ′ . By taking S ′ = S ∖ U , we recover the traditional notion of unlearning from Definition 1. Infor-
172
      mally speaking, Definition 3 requires us to output a hypothesis that is statistically indistinguishable
      from retraining-from-scratch on a dataset that has no information about U . If an unlearning algo-
173
      rithm satisfies Definition 3 with ε, δ = 0, then we say that the algorithm is an exact system aware
174
      unlearning algorithm.
175
176   Why is only considering the system-state sufficient to provide privacy guarantees? Consider
177   when the unlearning algorithm A satisfies A(S, U ) = f (IA (S, U )) for some fixed (possibly random-
178
      ized) function f . This implies that A(S, U ) = A(S ′ , ∅) since IA (S ′ , ∅) = IA (S, U ), which means
      that Definition 3 is satisfied with ε, δ = 0. Satisfying Definition 3 with ε, δ = 0 implies that the Kull-
179
      back–Leibler (KL) divergence between Pr(A(S, U ) ∣ IA (S, U ), U ) and Pr(A(S ′ , ∅) ∣ IA (S ′ , ∅)) is
180
      0. Through the relationship between KL-divergence and mutual information along with A(S, U ) =
181   A(S ′ , ∅) and IA (S ′ , ∅) = IA (S, U ), satisfying Definition 3 with ε, δ = 0 implies that the conditional
182   mutual information of I(U ; A(U, S) ∣ IA (S ′ , ∅)) = 0. This means that given the state of the system
183   after unlearning IA (S, U ) = IA (S ′ , ∅), there is no mutual information between the deleted individ-
184   uals U and the output of the unlearning algorithm A(S, U ). Thus, we simply need to ensure that
185   the state of the system does not contain any information about the deleted individuals.

202   The output of A LG CS (S) only relies only on samples in C(S). We can think of the core set C(S ∖U )
203   as the state of the system IA (S, U ) and use the properties of core set algorithms to design exact
204   unlearning algorithms. Many sample compression-based learning algorithms for classification tasks,
205   such as SVM or selective sampling, are core set based learning algorithms (Hanneke & Kontorovich,
206   2021; Floyd & Warmuth, 1995). Additionally, the unlearning algorithms based on core set based
207   learning algorithms are extremely fast because the deletion of a point outside the core set can be

294   Using sharding is a good generic starting point for unlearning, but can we improve upon some of the
295   tradeoffs of sharding using a different technique? In this section, we show that for linear classifica-
296   tion, we can use selective sampling to design an exact unlearning algorithm that demonstrates better
297   tradeoffs between deletion capacity, memory requirements, and excess error compared to sharding,
298   thus resulting in the first space and time efficient exact unlearning algorithm for linear classification.
299   Selective sampling (Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2009; Dekel et al., 2012; Zhu & Nowak, 2022; Sekhari et al.,
300   2023; Hanneke et al., 2014) is the problem of finding a classifier with low error while only using
301   the label of very few points and has become particularly important as datasets become larger and
302   labeling them becomes more expensive. Selective sampling algorithms only query the label of points
303   whose label they are uncertain of and only update the model on points that they query. Furthermore,
304   unqueried points are never stored in memory and never used in learning. Selective sampling is a
305   core set based learning algorithm where the core set is exactly the set of queried points.
306   Linear classification is a fundamental learning problem in both theory and practice. While it is a
307   useful theoretical primitive in algorithm design, this simple problem also has relevance for practice,
308   for example, in large foundation models and generative models, the last layers of these models are
309   often fine-tuned using linear probing, which trains a linear classifier on representations learned by
310   a deep neural network (Belinkov, 2022; Kornblith et al., 2019). As unlearning gains increasing
311   attention for these large-scale ML models, we hope that the following improvements for unlearning
312
      linear classification will find practical applications.

316   that the Bayes optimal predictor for yt is sign(u⊺ xt ). Our goal in linear-classification is to find a
317   hypothesis that performs well under 0 − 1 loss, i.e. set ℓ(f (x), y) = 1{f (x) ≠ y}. With this goal in

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   We proposed a new definition for unlearning, called system aware unlearning, that takes into ac-
531
      count the information about the sample S that could be leaked to an attacker who compromises the
      system, and we developed exact system aware unlearning algorithms based on sample compression
532
      learning algorithms. In particular, we used selective sampling to design a memory and time effi-
533
      cient unlearning algorithm for linear classification. It would be interesting to explore whether or
534   not this analysis can be extended to general function classes and prove that function classes with
535   finite eluder dimension (Russo & Van Roy, 2013) lead to memory and computation efficient exact
536   system aware unlearning algorithms. Beyond exact unlearning algorithms, it would be interesting
537   to explore how allowing for approximate system aware unlearning (ε, δ ≠ 0) can lead to even faster
538   and more memory-efficient unlearning algorithms. Furthermore, we believe that accounting for the
539   information that an attacker could have access to is an interesting direction to explore in generic
      privacy, beyond unlearning.

721   Our theoretical results provide guarantees for the worst case deletions. We experimentally verify our
722   theory, and we demonstrate that the deletion capacity and error rates after unlearning for Algorithm
723   2 are much better in practice. We randomly generate 50,000 points in dimension d = 100 with a
724   hard margin condition of γ = 0.1. We learn a classifier on these 50,000 points and process 50,000
725
      deletions using Algorithm 2. After each deletion, we compare the test error of the classifier after
      unlearning from Algorithm 2 to test error of a fully supervised linear classification algorithm which
726
      learns on all of the undeleted points in the sample, including points which are unqueried and thus
727
      never used by Algorithm 2. The test error of the fully supervised algorithm represents the best
728   possible error Algorithm 2 could hope to achieve after deletion. Note that Algorithm 2 can maintain
729   comparable test error with the fully supervised predictor while only using ∼ 4% of the samples.

       A.2                 OTHER R ELATED W ORK
750
751   Chourasia & Shah (2023) proposes a data deletion definition under adaptive requesters which does
752   not require indistinguishability from retraining from scratch. They require that the model after
753   deletion be indistinguishable from a randomized mapping π on S with zi replaced. This assumes
754   that the attacker does not have knowledge of the unlearning algorithm itself. If the data deletion
755   requesters are non-adaptive, then π can be replaced by the unlearning algorithm A, but in general,
      system aware unlearning does not generalize this definition. The data deletion definition under


                                                                                                          14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      adaptive requesters makes the stronger assumption that the unlearning algorithm uses the entire
757   sample to unlearn, but the weaker assumption that the attacker does not know the learning algorithm.
758
759   Beyond unlearning definitions, there has been much work in the development of unlearning algo-
760
      rithms. The current literature generally falls into two categories: exact unlearning algorithms which
      exactly reproduce the model from retraining from scratch on S ∖ {zi } (Ghazi et al., 2023; Cher-
761
      apanamjeri et al., 2024; Bourtoule et al., 2021; Cao & Yang, 2015; Chowdhury et al., 2024) or
762
      approximate unlearning algorithms which use ideas from differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2014)
763   to probabilistically recover a model that is “essentially indistinguishable” from the model produced
764   from retraining from scratch on S ∖ {zi } (Izzo et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021a; Chien et al., 2024).
765   The exact unlearning algorithms are typically memory intensive and require the storage of the entire
766   dataset and multiple models, while the approximate unlearning algorithms are much more mem-
767   ory efficient. Furthermore, existing lower bounds prove that there exist model classes with finite
768   VC and Littlestone dimension where traditional exact unlearning requires the storage of the entire
769   dataset (Cherapanamjeri et al., 2024). For large datasets, this makes exact unlearning under the tra-
770   ditional definition impractical. We prove that we can design practical exact system aware unlearning
771
      algorithms for linear classification which require sublinear memory in the number of samples.
