011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Time trends can be classified into intrinsic (real) and measurement (false) trends.
014            There has long been a critical need for techniques to discern them, especially
015            in investment decision-making. In causal discovery, these measurement trends,
016
               essentially measurement errors, can significantly impact the performance of algo-
               rithms, making it crucial to identify and eliminate them before analysis as well.
017
               Recognizing this need, we present a novel algorithm, termed Trend Differentiator
018
               (TrendDiff). It is capable of detecting all trend-influenced variables and differenti-
019            ating between those affected by measurement trends and those displaying intrinsic
020            trends, relying on changing causal module detection and trend-influenced vari-
021            ables’ structural properties, respectively. Extensive experiments on synthetic and
022            real-world data demonstrate the efficacy of this approach.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Emerging in the early 1990s, causal discovery algorithms have undergone substantial growth in
028   the past decades (Spirtes and Zhang, 2016). These algorithms strive to infer causality from purely
029   observational data, serving as valuable instruments in situations where randomized controlled trials
030   are rendered impractical due to ethical concerns, financial constraints, and other obstacles. Standing
031
      at the intersection of explosive data volumes and advancements in computational capabilities, a
      surge in theoretical and applied causal research has ensued. However, the rapid accumulation of data
032
      presents not only exciting possibilities but significant challenges in causal discovery.
033
034   A prevalent challenge is the presence of time trends, frequently encountered in time series. As
035   articulated by Phillips (2005), “No one understands trends, but everyone sees them in data”. Prior
036   research has extensively investigated the impact of trends on the efficacy of conventional statistical
037
      algorithms (White and Granger, 2011; Wu et al., 2007). Yet their influence on causal discovery
      remains largely unexplored. Based on the origin, trends can be classified into two distinct categories:
038
      intrinsic (real) and measurement (false) trends. In this context, we define the terms "trend", "intrinsic
039
      trend", and "measurement trend" as follows:
040
041   Definition 1. A trend is a function concerning time within a given data span. Specificly, time trend
042
      T = f (t), for tstart ≤ t ≤ tend .
043   Definition 2. An intrinsic trend is inherent to the fundamental mechanisms governing the variables
044   (e.g., global warming, the temperature is really increasing).
045   Definition 3. A measurement trend is essentially an observation error unique to the recorded values
046   (e.g., an observed increase in diagnosed thyroid nodule patients due to enhanced medical techniques,
047   despite a stable real incidence rate over time, see Figure 1).
048
049   The two types of trends originate from distinct sources, exert disparate impacts, and necessitate
050
      differential treatment.
051   However, there is this impression – time trends, be it an intrinsic trend, or a measurement trend,
052   should be removed before analyses – which is not accurate. Undoubtedly, measurement trends, being
053   a form of measurement error, necessitate removal. Take constraint-based causal discovery methods,
      which rely on conditional independence tests, for example. Figure 2 (a) shows the true causal graph,

073   in earlier research regarding measurement error in causal discovery, this influence is not limited to
074   constraint-based causal algorithms but also extends to other methodologies, including those based on
075   functional causal models (Zhang et al., 2017). Conversely, intrinsic trends are integral components
076   of the variables and mechanisms, facilitating the identification of underlying causal relationships.
077   Removal of intrinsic trends would decrease the signal-to-noise ratio, leading to lower detection power,
078
      and thus should be avoided. Consequently, discerning between intrinsic and measurement trends is
      crucial before conducting causal discovery analyses.

090   This study introduces the Trend Differentiator (TrendDiff) algorithm, designed to pinpoint variables
091   influenced by trends and differentiate between those affected by measurement trends and those
092   displaying intrinsic trends. It is not only critical in data pre-processing for causal discovery but carries
093
      substantial practical importance in decision-making. Discerning true market trends from transient
      fluctuations is essential for avoiding misallocation of resources in non-viable market opportunities.
094
      The ability to accurately identify trend types is key to reducing such investment risks.
095
096   The principal contributions of our work are shown below:
097
      • Problem Formulation. We parameterize variables with intrinsic and measurement trends using
098     graphical models. While there has already been research regarding measurement errors in causal
099     discovery, no attention has been paid to differentiating time trends. However, as we motivated
100     above, distinguishing intrinsic from measurement trends is of great theoretical and practical value.
101     To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to formally propose this problem.
102   • TrendDiff Algorithm. Employing the method of detecting changing causal modules, we can
103     efficiently identify all variables affected by trends. Subsequently, by harnessing the unique causal
104     structures under intrinsic and measurement trends, we are able to distinguish between them.
105     Integrating these technologies, we present the TrendDiff algorithm, a novel solution specifically
106     designed for the discernment of time trends.
107   • Experimental Validation. We use extensive experimental evaluations, including analyses of a
        real-world dataset, to demonstrate the robustness and utility of our algorithm.

      2.1   PARAMETERIZING TIME TRENDS
111
112   To put intrinsic and measurement trends clearer, we resort to structural equation models (SEMs),
113   where each variable Vi is formulated as a function of its direct causes and an error term εi . Here
114   εi encapsulates all other unmeasured causes of Vi , with the εi values for different variables being
115   mutually independent. Figure 3 (a) depicts a simple causal model, where a direct causal chain is
116   established from variable X1 , leading to X2 , and subsequently to X3 . Each variable is associated
117
      with a structural equation, and the model can be parameterized by assigning exact functions to
      f (Vi ), as well as a joint normal distribution to ε1 , ε2 , ε3 ∼ N (µ, Σ2 ). In this case, Σ2 is diagonal,
118
      reflecting the independence among the error terms ε1 , ε2 , and ε3 . Regardless of the functions
119
      and free parameter values assigned, the model in Figure 3 (a) exhibits conditional independence:
120   X1 ⊥  ⊥X3 |X2 . In Figure 3 (b), we present the same model as in Figure 3 (a) but with an added
121   intrinsic trend T2 affecting X2 . The intrinsic trend T2 impacts the generation of X2 and is an inherent
122   part of its underlying mechanisms. In this case, the observed and real values of X2 are identical. The
123   added intrinsic time trend can go into the causal network through X2 without altering the original
124   causal structure. Consequently, a trend in X3 can be observed, which arises due to the influence
125   of T2 . In Figure 3 (c), we depict the same model but with true values X2 being “measured" as
126   X2 , accompanied by a measurement trend T2 . In this case, the real and observed values of X2
127   differ. The measurement trend T2 is present only in the observed X2 . Due to the collider at X2 , T2
128
      cannot influence the real values X2 and is unable to propagate through the original causal network.
      To summarize, intrinsic and measurement trends are fundamentally the same in form (a function
129
      concerning time within a given data span). However, intrinsic trends affect the true value of variables,
130
      whereas measurement trends do not.

144   2.2   R ELATED WORK
145
      Measurement error in causal discovery. Fundamentally, measurement trends represent a problem
146
      of measurement error, which adversely affects causal discovery performance. There has already been
147   some research on measurement error in causal discovery. In linear Gaussian contexts, Scheines and
148   Ramsey (2016) parameterized measurement error using SEMs and explored the effect of Gaussian
149   measurement error on fast greedy equivalence search (FGES). Then identifiability conditions in linear
150   Gaussian situations are discussed in Zhang et al. (2017) through factor analysis, with a key identified
151   challenge being the unknown variances of measurement errors E. If known, the covariance matrix
152   of X̃ would be easily accessible and readily used. To address this, Blom et al. (2018) offers an
153   estimate for the upper bound of E, while Saeed et al. (2020) proposes a consistent partial correlations
154   estimator. In linear non-Gaussian scenarios, Zhang et al. (2018) demonstrates the identifiability of
155   the ordered group decomposition of G̃, which contains crucial causal information. However, this
156   method depends on over-complete independent component analysis (OICA (Hyvärinen and Oja,
157   2000)), hindered by issues of local optima and high computational complexity (Hoyer et al., 2008;
158
      Shimizu et al., 2009), making the practical application of Zhang et al. (2018)’s theoretically sound
      results challenging. Given this, Dai et al. (2022) defined the Transformed Independent Noise (TIN)
159
      condition and exploited it to identify the ordered group decomposition by independence tests.
160
161   Particularly regarding the differentiation of intrinsic and measurement trends, this study stands as the
      first to offer a solution. Distinct from the above-mentioned studies on causal discovery in the presence

      3    A SSUMPTIONS
169
      Instead of relying on the conventional assumption of causal sufficiency, this research adopts a modified
170
      concept, termed “pseudo causal sufficiency" (Huang et al., 2020). Traditional causal sufficiency
171   assumes that all common causes (confounders) influencing observed variables are captured in the
172   dataset. However, the occurrence of time trends presents a challenge to this assumption. Time trends
173   typically emerge from intricate, compounded factors, and time trends across various variables might
174   be interlinked owing to certain hidden confounders. These confounders could represent high-level
175   background factors, like economic policies in the stock market. Therefore, rather than assuming the
176   absence of unobserved confounders, our approach operates under the assumption of pseudo causal
177   sufficiency. This assumption signifies that the only unobserved confounders are those inherent in
178   time trends.
179   Assumption 1 (Pseudo causal sufficiency). Any potential confounders can be encapsulated by a
180   mathematically smooth time function. It follows that at each time instance, the values of these
181   confounders are fixed.

      In this section, we introduce the proposed algorithm, TrendDiff, designed to identify all variables
227   influenced by trends (phase 1) and distinguish between those affected by measurement trends and

231   4.1   P HASE 1: D ETECTION OF TREND - INFLUENCED VARIABLES AND CAUSAL STRUCTURE
232         RECOVERY
233
234   In this section, we leverage the detection of changing causal modules to detect variables exhibiting
235   time trends and deduce the causal network for V ∪ {C}. The core concept hinges on using the
                                                                       L              n
236   (observed) variable C as a surrogate for the unobserved {gl (C)}l=1 ∪ {θm (C)}m=1 . In essence, we
237   utilize C to encapsulate the C-specific information. Under the assumptions in Section 3, it is feasible
238
      to deploy conditional independence tests on the combined set of V ∪ {C} to detect variables with
      time trends and recover the structure. This is achieved by Algorithm 1 and supported by Theorem 1.
239
240   In Algorithm 1, we first construct a complete undirected graph, denoted UC , which incorporates both
241   C and V. In Step 2 of the algorithm, the decision regarding whether a variable Vi exhibits a time trend
242   is contingent upon the conditional independence between Vi and C, given a subset of other variables.
243   If a time trend is present in Vi , then the module of Vi evolves in conjunction with C.  Consequently,
244   the probability distribution of Vi given its non-C parents, namely P Vi | PAi \ {C} , will not remain
245
      constant across different values of C. As a result, Vi and C are conditionally dependent regardless of
246   any subset of variables. Based on this, we assume that if Vi ⊥⊥ C | PAi \ {C}, there should be no
247
      time trend in Vi . Conversely, if this assumption does not hold, then we claim to detect variables with
      time trends. After this, all variables linked to C, referred to as “C-specific variables", are considered
248
      to be with time trends. Step 3 aims to discover the skeleton of the causal structure over V. It leverages
249
      the results from Step 2: if neither Vi nor Vj is adjacent to C, then C does not need to be involved in
250   the conditioning set. In practice, one may apply any constraint-based search procedures on V ∪ C,
251   e.g., SGS (the Spirtes, Glymour and Scheines algorithm) and PC (the Peter-Clark algorithm) (Spirtes
252   et al., 1993). Its (asymptotic) correctness is justified by the following theorem 1. Finally, step 4 is
253   employed to orient the obtained skeleton based on both standard orientation rules and distribution
254   shift. For a comprehensive explanation of the step 4 orientation procedure and the complete proof of
255   Theorem 1, please refer to (Huang et al., 2020).
256
257
      Algorithm 1 Detection of Time-trend Variables and Recovery of Causal Structure
258
259    1: Build a complete undirected graph UG on the variable set V ∪ C.
       2: (Detection of time-trend variables) For each i, test for the marginal and conditional independence
260
          between Vi and C. If they are independent given a subset of {Vk | k ̸= i}, remove the edge
261
          between Vi and C in UG .
262
       3: (Recovery of causal skeleton) For every i ̸= j, test for the marginal and conditional independence
263       between Vi and Vj . If they are independent given a subset of {Vk | k ̸= i, k ̸= j} ∪ {C}, remove
264       the edge between Vi and Vj in UG .
265    4: (Orientation) For the obtained skeleton, orient it by standard orientation rules and distribution
266       shift. After the orientation process, we can get the causal network for V ∪ C, called Gphase1 .

272
273   After Phase 1, we procured the set of variables exhibiting time trends (those associated with C) as
274   well as the causal network Gphase1 for V ∪ C. In phase 2, we demonstrate that by examining the
275   different structures within causal networks, it is feasible to differentiate variables with intrinsic trends
276   from those influenced by measurement trends.

278   4.2.1    D ISTINGUISH BETWEEN INTRINSIC AND MEASUREMENT TRENDS BY Gphase1
279
      As depicted earlier, intrinsic-trend variables do not change the causal network, whereas those variables
280
      characterized by measurement trends can induce structural alterations in causal discovery from the
281
      observed variables. Next, we delve into how a measurement-trend variable influences the causal
282   structure of Gphase1 and leverage this understanding to partly distinguish between the two trend types.
283
284   Figure 4 illustrates how a measurement-trend variable alters the output causal structure of Phase 1. In
285
      Figure 4 (a), we depict a chain with a measurement trend in X2 . During Phase 1, the time index C is
      integrated into our analysis to pinpoint all trend variables. Due to the presence of a measurement trend
286
      in X2 , a connection from C to X2 is established. Furthermore, based on the conditional independence
287
      observed in the actual structure Figure 4(a), we have T ⊥⊥ X3 and, crucially, T ̸⊥⊥ X3 |X2 . By
288   extension, because C is a proxy for T , the relationships C ⊥⊥ X3 and C ̸⊥⊥ X3 |X2 should hold.
289   Therefore, X2 is a collider and the direction is from X3 to X2 . The dependency dynamics between
290   X1 and C follow suit. As a result, the Phase 1 structural outcome for observed variables should be
291   the one shown in Figure 4 (b). It’s worth noting that since the measurement trend T is independent
292   across all variables within the real causal network, no arrow can stem from the measurement-trend
293   variable to other variables in Gphase1 (cause the observed measurement-trend variable X2 would
294   always be identified as a collider). In essence, any linkage from a “C-specific variable" to other
295   entities indicates an intrinsic trend.

479
480   The need to discern intrinsic trends from measurement trends has been a longstanding challenge.
481   TrendDiff, our innovative algorithm, is tailored to address this difficulty as evidenced by its successful
482   application in both simulated and real-world scenarios. However, we recognize a few limitations of
483   this algorithm. Firstly, although we assume trend-influenced variables are non-leaf nodes, differenti-
484   ating trend types in leaf nodes also holds value. Secondly, in reality, intrinsic and measurement trends
485   may coexist in variables, a scenario that TrendDiff currently cannot handle. We leave improving
      TrendDiff’s ability to differentiate trends in leaf nodes and mixed types for future work.

598   A.1.1    U NDERSTANDING PSEUDO CAUSAL SUFFICIENCY THROUGH A SIMPLIFIED EXAMPLE
599
600   In environmental epidemiology, time series analyses are frequently employed to evaluate the im-
601
      mediate effects of air pollution on respiratory health. Here, the exposure of interest is the daily
      concentration of air pollutants, while the outcome is the daily number of hospital admissions for
602
      respiratory diseases. The primary objective is to determine whether day-to-day fluctuations in air pol-
603
      lutant levels influence the day-to-day variation in respiratory-related hospital admissions. Typically,
604   factors like temperature and relative humidity are adjusted for as known confounders in these studies.
605
606   Nonetheless, in addition to these controlled variables, there exist other unobserved confounders that
607
      can significantly impact the analysis. These include variables such as socioeconomic status, changes
      in policy, and seasonal variations. Given that the duration of this kind of time series studies often
608
      spans several years or more, it’s evident that these unobserved confounders could have a substantial
609
      effect on the findings. Therefore, it’s crucial to acknowledge and attempt to account for these unseen
610   factors to ensure the robustness and accuracy of the results.
611
612   In this scenario, the notion of pseudo causal sufficiency emerges. Traditionally, it’s assumed in
613
      such studies that any unobserved confounders are associated with time and can be encapsulated as a
      smooth function of time, typically represented through splines. By incorporating this spline function
614
      into the analysis, the model effectively accounts for unobserved confounders. This assumption has
615
      been widely adopted across environmental health studies. However, it’s important to note that this
616   example is provided for the sake of understanding pseudo causal sufficiency more easily. In reality,
617   these traditional studies do not explicitly introduce this concept.
618
619   A.1.2    N ON - TIME - DELAYED RELATIONSHIPS
620
621   In real-world scenarios, both non-time-delayed and time-delayed causal relationships are crucial. In
622   theory, all causal relationships involve some form of delay (as we often say, cause precedes effect).
623
      However, in real life, due to our limited knowledge of the true nature of various relationships and the
      limited precision of the data available to us, we often observe relationships that appear to have no
624
      delay. For example, the true mechanism by which air pollution affects lung function may involve
625
      a delay of one hour—meaning exposure to severe air pollution now might lead to weakened lung
626   function an hour later. However, since we only have access to daily data on air pollution and lung
627   function, this inherently delayed relationship may appear in the data as if there is no delay. Thus, in
628   real life, both non-time-delayed and time-delayed relationships are very common.
629
      In our study, the assumption of no time-delayed relationships is partly made for the sake of clarity
630
      and readability of the paper. As this is the initial introduction of intrinsic and measurement trends
631
      using graphical models, and the first formal presentation of the differentiation challenge, clarity is
632   paramount. Some of the figures (Figure 5 for example) in the manuscript are already quite complex
633   and difficult to understand without considering delays. If time delays were to be taken into account,
634   the number of nodes would increase manifold, severely affecting the presentation of the problem and
635   the algorithm.
636
637   A.1.3    T REND - INFLUENCED VARIABLES ARE NON - LEAF NODES
638
639
      The impact of measurement trends on causal discovery outcomes is much smaller for leaf nodes
      compared to non-leaf nodes. This is why we believe that our method remains highly valuable even
640
      if it cannot differentiate the trend types in leaf nodes. As described in paragraph 3 and Figure 2,
641
      measurement trends introduce two issues for constrained-based causal discovery algorithms: 1. the
642   dependence between measurement-trend variables and their neighbors weakens with increasing
643   trends; 2. the conditional independence given the measurement-trend variable vanishes, yielding
644   increasing dependence. For non-leaf nodes, such as X2 and X3 in Figure 2, both these two issues are
645   present. In contrast, leaf nodes like X1 and X4 in the same figure are only subject to the first issue.
646   This is because a leaf node, having no children, does not act as a mediator in any relationship.

735   Our study extends to evaluating the performance of the PC (Peter-Clark) algorithm both before and
736   after the removal of measurement trends identified by TrendDiff. This comparative analysis highlights
737   the effectiveness of our methodology in enhancing causal discovery through data preprocessing. In
738   our approach to handle variables affected by measurement trends, we utilized the Savitzky-Golay
739   filter. This process involves subtracting the trend, as determined by the Savitzky-Golay filter, from
740   the original data to produce detrended data. The Savitzky-Golay filter is a well-known polynomial
741
      smoothing technique that fits a polynomial of a specified degree to consecutive data points within a
      moving window, using linear least squares regression. Once the polynomial is fitted, the filter can
742

743

744   dealing with noisy data. The two primary parameters governing the Savitzky-Golay filter are the
745   window size and the polynomial order. The window size determines the number of data points
746   used for each polynomial fit, thereby influencing the degree of smoothing. On the other hand, the
747   polynomial order specifies the complexity of the model used in the fitting process.

755   w, are elucidated in Figure 9. The data reveals that as T increases, so does performance efficiency.

      Below we detail the PCMCI+ algorithm:
955   PCMCI+ belongs to the so-called constraint-based causal discovery methods family, which is based
956   on conditional independence test(Runge, 2020). Here “PC” refers to the developers Peter and Clark,
957   “MCI” means that the momentary conditional independence (MCI) test idea is added to the traditional
958   PC algorithm, and “+” reminds users that it extends the earlier version of PCMCI to include the
959   discovery of contemporaneous links(Runge et al., 2019). Like other causal graphic models, PCMCI+
960
      works under the general assumptions of the causal Markov condition (each variable in the system
      is independent of its non-descendants, given its parent variables) and faithfulness (probabilistic
961
      information in data emerges not by chance but from causal structures) (Runge, 2018). On top of the
962
      general assumptions, two specific assumptions are also requested: causal stationarity (i.e., the causal
963   links hold for all the studied time points) and causal sufficiency (i.e. measured variables include all
964   of the common causes).
965
