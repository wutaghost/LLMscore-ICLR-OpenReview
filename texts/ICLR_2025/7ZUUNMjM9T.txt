011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Flow matching, one of the attractive deep generative models, has recently been
014            used in wide modality. Despite the remarkable success, the flow matching objec-
015            tive of the vector field is insufficient for maximum likelihood estimation. Previous
016
               works show that adding the vector field’s high-order gradient objectives further
               improves likelihood. However, their method only minimizes the upper bound
017
               of the high-order objectives, hence it is not guaranteed that the objectives them-
018
               selves are indeed minimized, resulting in likelihood maximization becoming less
019            effective. In this paper, we propose a method to directly minimize the high-order
020            objective. Since our method guarantees that the objective is indeed minimized,
021            our method is expected to improve likelihood compared to previous works. We
022            verify that our proposed method achieves better likelihood in practice through ex-
023            periments on 2D synthetic datasets and high-dimensional image datasets.
024

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b) have shown a high ability to sample from the
029   data distribution in broad modalities, such as image generation (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b;
030   Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022), audio generation (Kong
031   et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020), and video generation (Ho et al., 2022; Singer
032   et al., 2022). Moreover, to improve the quality of data generation, many previous works have pro-
033
      posed various formulations (Rombach et al., 2022; Karras et al., 2022; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021;
      Salimans & Ho, 2022). However, an issue requiring a large number of sampling steps to reduce the
034
      discretization error has remained.
035
036   Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) is one of the variants of diffusion models,
037   which has achieved the state-of-the-art generated data quality. The main modifications from general
038   diffusion models are parameterization and noise scheduling; the model predicts the vector field,
039
      which is the sample’s time derivative (velocity), and noises are added to training samples to make its
      path straight. Although we need the marginal vector field to generate new unseen samples, we can
040
      use the vector field conditioned by a data sample in the training objective calculation, which enables
041
      us to train flow matching simulation-free as diffusion models. Since the discretization error becomes
042   smaller thanks to the straight-like path, flow matching has been used in broad modalities (Esser et al.,
043   2024; Shi et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023; Vyas et al., 2023).
044
045
      However, the general flow matching objective is insufficient for maximum likelihood estimation
      (MLE), or equivalent, minimizing KL divergence between the data distribution and the generated
046
      distribution (Zheng et al., 2023). Lu et al. (2022a) prove that the KL divergence is bounded by the
047
      integration of the Fisher divergence in ordinary differential equation (ODE) form diffusion models.
048   Moreover, they prove that the Fisher divergence is bounded by an increasing function of errors of
049   high-order gradients of the score function and the model. In flow matching, Zheng et al. (2023)
050   verify the identical theory. However, since the actual values of high-order gradients of the score
051   function or the vector field are intractable, their proposed method only minimizes the upper bound
052   of their objectives, not the objectives themselves. Hence, their methods do not strictly guarantee
053   that the actual objectives necessarily decrease and that the upper bound of the KL divergence is also
      minimized, resulting in likelihood maximization becoming less effective.

065   In this paper, we propose a method to minimize the second-order objective directly in flow match-
066   ing. The second-order objective derived in the previous works (Lu et al., 2022a; Zheng et al.,
067   2023) includes the gradient of the marginal score function or the vector field. On the contrary, our
068   method uses the gradient of the conditional one in the second-order objective. Modification from
069   the marginal to the conditional one enables us to calculate the second-order objective simulation-
070   free. Additionally, although the second-order objective is defined by its matrix norm, our proposed
071
      method uses its trace instead for efficiency. In contrast to the previous works, our proposed method
      guarantees that the second-order objective is indeed minimized as long as the training loss decreases
072
      since we can calculate the second-order objective itself and minimize it directly. By minimizing the
073
      second-order objective itself in all timesteps, we can expect that the value of the function bounding
074   the Fisher divergence is minimized and that the Fisher divergence is also minimized, furthermore,
075   the upper bound of the KL divergence is minimized. To verify the effectiveness of our method in
076   practical settings, we conduct experiments on 2D datasets and high-dimensional image datasets. We
077   show that our method actually improves the likelihood from the original flow matching in 2D syn-
078   thetic datasets. On image datasets, we show that our method achieves better likelihood than previous
079   works of 3.07 bits/dim of negative log-likelihood on MNIST and 2.62 bits/dim on CIFAR-10, and
080   competitive likelihood of 4.12 bits/dim on ImageNet32×32. Furthermore, we conduct an ablation
081   study to verify that our method minimizing the second-order objective directly indeed maximizes
082
      likelihood compared to the methods minimizing its upper bound. Table 1 summarizes the differences
      between our proposed method and previous works.

174   2.3    C ONNECTION TO DIFFUSION MODELS
175
176   Now, we can interpret flow matching as a variant of diffusion models with two modifications. The
177   first modification is the model parameterization. In diffusion models, the model predicts the score
178   function ∇ log qt (xt ). On the other hand, in flow matching, the model predicts the vector field
179
      ut (xt ), and ut (xt ) is formulated by Equations (7) and (12) as
180                                 1                                            1
               ut (xt ) = f (t)xt − g(t)2 ∇ log qt (xt ), vθ (xt , t) = f (t)xt − g(t)2 sθ (xt , t).  (17)
181                                 2                                            2
182   The second modification is the scheduling of the ratio of noises in Equation (2). While αt and σt
183   are defined as non-linear functions in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022), they
184   are defined as linear functions of t by Equation (10) in flow matching. Therefore, we can perceive
185   flow matching as the ODE form diffusion models.

      3.3   D IRECT HIGH - ORDER FLOW MATCHING
244
245   The general flow matching objective (14) is not derived for the purpose of MLE. As flow matching is
246   a variant of the ODE form diffusion models as described in Sec. 2.3, we can apply the previous work
247   described in Sec. 3.2 to flow matching. By considering the description in Sec. 3.1 in flow matching
248   formulation, although the general flow matching objective (14) can minimize the KL divergence,
249   we can further minimize it by minimizing JFisher . As described in 3.2, we need to minimize high-
250   order objectives to minimize JFisher . Zheng et al. (2023) already propose to minimize high-order
251   objectives in flow matching with several techniques.
252   However, Lu et al. (2022a) and Zheng et al. (2023) proposed methods minimize the upper bounds
253   of the high-order objectives, not objectives themselves. Hence, their methods do not necessarily
254   minimize the upper bound of the KL divergence, as a result, their methods reduce the effect of
255   likelihood maximization. Then, we propose a method to minimize the high-order flow matching
256   objectives directly.
257   First, we show that the Fisher divergence is bounded by a function of high-order flow matching
258   objectives, similarly to Sec. 3.2.

303   4    R ELATED WORK
304
305   Flow matching. One of the advantages of flow matching is the straight-like path, which mitigates
306   the discretization error in the inference phase. Thanks to that feature, flow matching achieved state-
307   of-the-art quality in image generation (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). Sequentially, flow
308   matching has been used in broad modalities, such as image generation (Esser et al., 2024; Dao et al.,
309
      2023; Yan et al., 2024), audio generation (Mehta et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Prajwal et al., 2024),
      and discrete data generation (Gat et al., 2024; Nisonoff et al., 2024). Regarding the training theory
310
      of flow matching, there are some works regarding theoretical error bounds in terms of Wasserstein
311
      distance (Benton et al., 2023; Fukumizu et al., 2024). To minimize the KL divergence in flow
312   matching, although Zheng et al. (2023) proposed some techniques, including minimizing second-
313   order objective, they only minimize an upper bound of the objective instead of the objective itself,
314   as described in Sec. 3.3.
315
      MLE for diffusion models. Song et al. (2021b) showed that minimizing the denoising score match-
316
      ing loss (6) corresponded to maximizing the ELBO of likelihood. Unlike the theory, it was known
317
      that DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), which minimized the ELBO weighted by σt2 , achieved better results
318   than denoising score matching experimentally. Kingma & Gao (2024) showed that the DDPM ob-
319   jective can be perceived as the ELBO with data augmentation of noise adding, and unified previous
320   works proposing other objectives (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Karras et al., 2022; Salimans & Ho,
321   2022). However, Lu et al. (2022a) verified that ODE form diffusion models have different ELBO,
322   and that we can further minimize the KL divergence by minimizing high-order objectives. However,
323   as described in Sec. 3.3, their method also minimizes the upper bounds of the objectives, not the
      objectives themselves.

443   5.2.1   A BLATION STUDY
444
445   In this section, we investigate how directly minimizing the objectives is effective compared to min-
446   imizing the upper bound. As described in Sec. 3, we need to minimize the high-order objectives
447   to minimize the upper bound of the KL divergence. Furthermore, our proposed method minimizes
448
      the second-order objective directly, while the previous works (Lu et al., 2022a; Zheng et al., 2023)
      minimize its upper bound. We verify that directly minimizing the objective improves likelihood than
449
      minimizing its upper bound through an experiment on image datasets, MNIST and CIFAR-10.
450
451   We compare four methods: original flow matching, MLE by minimizing the upper bound of the
452   second-order matrix norm objective, MLE by minimizing the upper bound of the second-order trace
453   objective, and MLE by minimizing the second-order trace objective directly (ours). To calculate
454
      the matrix norm, we used an equality of ∥A∥2F = tr(AT A) and Hutchinson’s method, following
      Lu et al. (2022a). We investigated various values of weight parameter λdiv for each method and
455
      recorded the best NLL.
456
457   Table 5 shows the comparison of NLL by four methods. The methods minimizing the upper bound
458   have worse likelihoods on either dataset than the original flow matching. On the contrary, our pro-
459   posed method achieves the best NLL thanks to minimizing the second-order trace objective directly.
460
      Therefore, we can justify that directly minimizing the second-order objective maximizes likelihood
      more than minimizing the upper bound. We additionally emphasize that i-DODE(SP), which has
461
      better NLL than our proposed method on CIFAR-10 as shown in Table 4, includes minimizing the
462
      upper bound of the second-order trace objective. From this result, our method may further improve
463   NLL by combining techniques proposed in previous works with our proposed method. Verification
464   of this hypothesis is the subject of future work.

467   6   D ISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSIONS
468
469   Our proposed method has several weak points. First, our proposed method makes training time
470   longer since additional backpropagation process is required in our proposed method. For instance,
471
      while the training time of original flow matching is about 16 A100 hours, our proposed method is
      about 20 A100 hours, which is 1.25 times longer on CIFAR-10 and 2 times longer on ImageNet32.
472
      Additionally, usage memory size also increases. However, since the training time increases linearly
473
      with the dimension of the model’s hidden layers, it is realistically possible to scale our method for
474   larger models. Second, the improvement of NLL by our proposed method is not large. Since the
475   improvement in likelihood does not mean an improvement in image quality, it is unclear whether
476   our proposed method is useful in practical applications. Lastly, our proposed method has general
477   weak points of flow matching, e.g., requiring iterative calculations in the inference phase.
478
      We proposed a method to directly minimize the second-order flow matching objective in addition to
479
      the original flow matching objective. Our proposed method guarantees that it minimizes the upper
480   bound of KL divergence between the data distribution and the generated distribution by directly min-
481   imizing the second-order flow matching objective, while previous works do not since they minimize
482   only the upper bound of the second-order objective. We verified that our proposed method achieves
483   competitive likelihood with previous works through experiments with 2D synthetic datasets and
484   image datasets. Moreover, we showed that directly minimizing the second-order objective indeed
485   improves the likelihood more than minimizing its upper bound through the ablation study. Further-
      more, we showed that our proposed method can be potentially improved by combining techniques

      proposed in previous works. We expect our work will further enrich the learning theory of flow
487   matching.

860   B.1    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS OF THE EXPERIMENTS ON 2D DATASETS
861
862   We follow Tong et al. (2024) for the implementation. We use four layers MLP with 64 hidden units
863   and SELU activation. We use the AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017) optimizer with the learning rate of
      0.001, batch-size of 256, training iterations of 2000, σmin = 0.01, and λdiv = 0.001.


                                                                  16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      B.2   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS OF THE EXPERIMENTS ON IMAGE DATASETS
865
866   We use UNet architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) as the neural network vθ . Due to the upsam-
867   pling and downsampling modules in UNet, we padded MNIST images by 2 zero pixels vertically
868   and horizontally such that the size of images is to the size of 32×32.
869
      On MNIST and CIFAR-10, we followed the setting of Tong et al. (2024). As UNet setting, we use
870   2 blocks with 128 channels and a dropout of 0.1. We also use AdamW optimizer with the learning
871   rate of 2 × 10−4 , batch-size of 128, σmin = 0.0, and λdiv = 0.001. We set the training iterations to
872   100k and 400k on MNIST and CIFAR-10, respectively. Additionally, we calculate the exponential
873   moving average (EMA) with a decay of 0.9999 for the parameter of UNet, and we use the EMA
874   parameter for the inference phase.
875
      On ImageNet32×32, we followed the original setting of Lipman et al. (2023). As UNet setting, we
876   use 3 blocks with 128 channels and no dropout. We also use AdamW optimizer with the learning
877   rate of 1 × 10−4 , batch-size of 1024, and 250k iterations. Other setting is the same as MNIST and
878   CIFAR-10.
