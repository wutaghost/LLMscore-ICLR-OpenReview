009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding human driving behaviors is crucial for developing a reliable vehi-
012            cle and transportation system. Yet, data for learning these behaviors is scarce and
013            must be carefully labeled with events, causes, and consequences. Such data may
014            be more difficult to obtain in rare driving domains, such as in high-performance
015            multi-car racing. While large language models (LLMs) show promise in inter-
016            preting driving behaviors, the integration of multi-modal inputs (e.g., language,
017            trajectory, and more) and generation of multi-modal output in low-data regimes re-
018            mains under-explored. In this paper, we introduce Bi-Gen: a Bi-directional Driv-
019
               ing Data Generator, Bi-Gen is a bi-directional multi-modal model that connects a
               trained encoder-decoder architecture with a pre-trained LLM, enabling both auto-
020
               annotation and generation of human driving behaviors. Our experiments show
021
               that Bi-Gen, despite its smaller size, matches the performance of much larger
022            models like GPT-4o in annotating driving data. Additionally, Bi-Gen generates
023            diverse, human-like driving behaviors, offering a valuable tool for synthetic data
024            generation in resource-constrained settings. Taken together, our experiments are a
025            significant step towards applying LLMs to complex, multi-agent driving data.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large language models (LLMs) and large multi-modal models (LMMs) have emerged as capable
031   and general-purpose tools for understanding driving data in the wild (Kuo et al., 2022; Felemban
032   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2024; Sima et al., 2023). However, the key
033   ingredient to the success of such models is the vast amounts of data required to pre-train or fine-tune
034   such models to contain relevant world knowledge for target tasks. This data dependency becomes a
035   significant limiting factor when extending LLMs to driving data that is not well-represented in pub-
036   licly available datasets. For example, most empirical human driving data is naturalistic, which in-
037   herently biases it against capturing rare events (e.g., safety critical scenarios, drifting, etc.). Though
038   synthesizing data using driving simulators or applying variance reduction techniques (e.g. impor-
039
      tance sampling, etc.) could augment or extend existing datasets under specific scenarios (Feng et al.,
      2023; Ding et al., 2023), these approaches are inadequate for generating a diverse range of driving
040
      behaviors that capture the implicit heterogeneity of human driving. Furthermore, while some ma-
041
      chine learning models (Wang et al., 2019; Krajewski et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2020; Phan-Minh
042   et al., 2020; Nayakanti et al., 2023) may function as synthetic data generators, such methods often
043   require significant amounts of labeled data to learn to generate realistic in-distribution examples.
044
045
      To this end, we aim to to develop an efficient, low-cost multi-modal model that can quickly learn
      to interpret and annotate unlabeled driving trajectories in the low-data domain of high-performance
046
      multi-car racing (Weiss & Behl, 2020; Wurman et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Werner et al., 2023).
047
      Learning such a model involves two core tasks: (1) trajectory generation and (2) trajectory descrip-
048   tion. Trajectory generation involves the creation of new driving trajectories, either conditioned on a
049   language prompt, a partially-complete trajectory, or some other form of driving context. Trajectory
050   description is the annotation of an unlabeled driving trajectory, giving the model the ability to act
051   as an annotator or discriminator for unlabeled data. One promising method is to extend LLMs for
052   the two tasks, as such models have been shown to successfully serve as both synthetic data gener-
053   ators for training large-scale models (Dubey et al., 2024; Adler et al., 2024) and as annotators for
      unstructured data (Tan et al., 2024).

077   The community has seen a growing interest in leveraging LLMs (e.g., GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023),
078   ChatGPT (Biswas, 2023), etc.) for trajectory generation, particularly for automated vehicles (AVs)
079   (Chen et al., 2023b; Lan et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024c; Tan
080   et al., 2023; Munir et al., 2024). These works demonstrate a strong capability of LLMs to directly
081
      translate high-level textual driving commands to lower-level trajectory data in the form of generated
      way-points, or by aligning LLM outputs to a vehicle’s action space (e.g., steering, acceleration).
082
      Pre-training a decoder to align the LLMs’ hidden-state to driving state vectors (Chen et al., 2023b;
083
      Mao et al., 2023) is effective for generating immediate actions or reasoning within specific time
084   steps but lacks flexibility for scaling to varying time horizons. This task is complicated further by
085   the need to understand temporal and spatial interactions across multiple modalities and multiple
086   agents in a scene. Modeling such interactions requires dedicated fine-tuning of a pre-trained LLM,
087   and in particular requires sufficiently rich multi-agent data.
088
      Efficient trajectory generation demands a comprehensive representation of the complex driving envi-
089
      ronment, which necessitates the integration of multi-modal inputs. Challenges arise in establishing
090   multi-modal connections within LLMs, in terms of the representation gap across modalities, cross-
091   modality generalization, modality collapse, etc. (Yin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Peng et al.,
092   2023; Driess et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024). Particularly in the human-driving domain, generation
093   is more complicated due to the scarcity of large-scale labeled trajectory-language paired datasets
094   that incorporate multiple modalities (Tan et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024b; Shao et al., 2024). These
095   complexities are further amplified in the uncharted domain of high-performance multi-agent rac-
096   ing, which features complex multi-agent dynamics, no pre-trained data encoders, and no readily
097   accessible world-knowledge baked into existing LLMs.
098   Finally, these methods only enable one-directional generation, focusing exclusively on language-
099   conditioned trajectory generation. Equally important, however, is the reverse process: trajectory-
100   conditioned language generation. The ability to generate multi-modal outputs (i.e., language and tra-
101   jectory) from multi-modal inputs is essential for achieving bi-directionality in data synthesis. While
102   prior works have individually tackled one element of this process, a bi-directional model capable of
103   mutual conditional generation between language and trajectory is key to gaining a comprehensive
104
      understanding of human driving dynamics. Without this, the model is limited in generalizing across
      different driving contexts and behaviors, as it cannot fully explain or generate the diverse factors
105
      that shape and influence various driving behaviors.
106
107   To address these gaps, we introduce a Bi-directional Driving Data Generator, Bi-Gen, an end-to-
      end learning framework developed through LMM tuning. This bi-directional pipeline is capable

128
129   LMMs in driving: Recent advancements in multi-modal integration in LLMs, such as Instruct-
130
      BLIP (Li et al., 2023) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024), have demonstrated significant success in tasks
      involving both visual and textual data, showcasing their potential for interpreting and generating
131
      meaningful content across modalities. Aligning text with a single additional modality often involves
132
      training a projection layer (Li et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2023) to map features from the new modal-
133   ity into the language space. Most prominent efforts to integrate multiple modalities within unified
134   LLMs primarily target visual-language tasks, such as visual question answering, object detection
135   and image-text similarity (Alayrac et al., 2022; Girdhar et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
136   2023; Ye et al., 2024).
137
      Prior work as begun to extend the application of LMMs to driving tasks, particularly for AVs. These
138
      efforts build on the success of LMMs in visual-language tasks to improve the understanding of driv-
139   ing scenarios captured by onboard cameras and then generate the control signals (Xu et al., 2024;
140   Sima et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). These control signals are treated as the same modality as the text
141   domain, without requiring decoder transformation (i.e., controls are specified in language). How-
142   ever, this approach may face challenges when applied to long-horizon trajectory prediction due to
143   context window or memory constraints. LMMs in prior AV work also focus on single-turn interac-
144   tions, neglecting longer form conversations or rollouts. Moreover, the use of LMMs for learning
145   human driving behaviors remains less studied, and the scarcity of large-scale paired trajectory-
146   language datasets poses a significant challenge to advancing LMMs in this domain. The work
147
      presented in this paper, to the best of our knowledge, is the first attempt to integrate multi-modal
      inputs into LLMs to generate a diverse range of multi-modal outputs for the multi-turn inference in
148
      the human driving domain. Our approach enables multi-turn question-answering tasks that seam-
149
      lessly alternate between trajectory description and generation, enabling annotation or generation of
150   trajectory-language paired data in low-data regimes.
151
152   Trajectory-Language Interactions: Recent works focus on leveraging LLMs in trajectory gener-
153
      ation, enabling AVs to make informed, contextually aware decisions in real-time, and guiding low-
      level motion control to enhance both safety and operational efficiency (Seff et al., 2023; Nguyen
154
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b). However, such approaches may introduce mod-
155
      eling biases that might fail to capture rare events (e.g., generating unsafe trajectories). Prior works
156   (Nguyen et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023; Hu & Sadigh, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024c) addressed this by
157   incorporating a reinforcement learning agent to assess behavioral alignment with different trajecto-
158   ries by the finite state rewards. These works heavily rely on retrieval-augmented generation to pro-
159   vide sufficient context about trajectories. While they perform well in static or deterministic settings,
160   but they have struggled to generalize to interactive environments characterized by high stochasticity
161   and unpredictability. Our work focuses on understanding the diversity and stochastic nature of hu-
      man driving behaviors, and introduces a multi-modal pipeline based on LMM tuning. This pipeline

187   We extend LLaVa (Liu et al., 2024) to a multi-modal architecture as shown in Fig. 2. For both
188   the trajectory and language generation tasks, the model consumes an ego-agent driving trajectory
189   X1:T
        e , a task-specific prompt P, and any available driving environment information. We differentiate
190   between global, static information (e.g., map, road conditions) and local, dynamic information (e.g.,
191   movement of surrounding objects, vehicles, or pedestrians) in the driving environment. For a typical
192   annotation task given to the model (i.e., to describe the ego vehicle’s behavior), the multi-modal
193   input consists of a system prompt, Psys , static map information M, a dynamic opponent vehicle
194   X1;T
        o   and an ego-centric trajectory X1:T
                                            e .
195   To process the driving data, our model employs a trajectory encoder, g(·), that is responsible for
196   embedding both opponent and ego driving data into the LLM’s latent space. Specifically, for an
197   input sequence of trajectory states, X1:T , we embed X into the embedding space of the language
198   model, creating a sequence of T trajectory tokens, Hτ . This process is repeated for each vehicle in
199   the scene, translating from trajectory features into trajectory tokens that the LLM can interpret.
200
      Similarly, a map encoder, o(·), embeds relevant map data for each sequence. As with trajectory data,
201   we pass a sequence of points of map data in, M1:K , and the resulting map embedding is a sequence
202   of K map tokens, Hϕ . Prior to embedding, the map data and all driving trajectories (ego and any
203   opponents) are normalized into the same coordinate frame.
204
      We built our model on top of TinyLlama (Zhang et al., 2024b), a lightweight 1B model, for easy
205
      deployment and fast inference. The static map and trajectory encoders, o and g, are also lightweight
206
      networks, constructed as 2-layer multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) with residual connections. Finally,
207   our model includes a trajectory decoder, u(·), which is an MLP composed of two linear layers with
208   ReLU activation. This trajectory decoder is designed to project from the LLM’s hidden dimensions
209   down to the original trajectory dimension.

248   as shown in Fig. 2b. Similar to the trajectory description task, the input is a sequence consisting

252   prompt Pgen as the input and train a decoder, u(·) that learns to map from an LLM hidden state
253   back into trajectory features. The training objective is an autoregressive mean-squared error (MSE)
254   loss between the predicted trajectory output, X̂2:T

      3.3   T RAINING P ROCEDURE
257
258   The two tasks of trajectory description and trajectory generation can be trained independently to
259   achieve one-directional data generation (i.e., learning to annotate trajectories with language or learn-
260   ing to generate new trajectories from language prompts). However, training the two tasks together
261   enables the model to both see a greater diversity of data as well as learn to leverage the shared struc-
262   ture of the two tasks to learn a more robust model of the relationship between language and driving
263   data. We therefore train the two tasks jointly with a single model (Fig. 2).
264   As described in Sec. 3.1 & 3.2, the input token sequence includes static map information and oppo-
265   nent and ego trajectory data. The static map information includes inner and outer edges of the track,
266   centered around the ego agent’s location. Trajectory data for both agents includes three-dimensional
267   position and velocity and a quaternion for orientation. As mentioned above, trajectory and map data
268   are all normalized to the same coordinate frame before being passed to the model, and are further
269   normalized to have a zero-mean and unit variance for numerical stability. While future work may
      consider more sophisticated feature normalization or unification strategies to further improve per-

354   4.1   A NNOTATOR E XPERIMENT
355
      We first evaluate Bi-Gen as an automated labeler or annotator for entirely unseen and unlabeled
356
      trajectory data. In this setting, we formulate the task as a multi-turn conversation, reflecting a pos-
357
      sible deployment of our lightweight model to a real-time annotation platform. For each new sample
358   consisting of a system prompt, map, opponent trajectory, and ego-trajectory, we ask questions one-
359   at-a-time, allowing the model to generate a short response to each question before moving on to the
360   next question. Each sample is followed by nine total question-answer pairs.
361
      Note that this task is more challenging than a conventional annotation task because of the sequential,
362
      multi-turn structure of the evaluation. If the model generates an unrelated response early, or begins to
363
      wander out-of-distribution, the entire conversation can collapse. Therefore, the model must remain
364   accurate for all nine turns to maximize its score.
365
366   We compare Bi-Gen to GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) as a baseline for a closed-source, expensive,
367
      large baseline model. GPT-4o is given sub-sampled trajectory data in the form of a JSON, and is
      asked to answer all questions simultaneously. For both our model and GPT-4o, generated answers
368
      are binned into target topics using GPT-4o as a judge. We provide further details on this process in
369
      Appendix C. After this binning, the F1 score for our model is 43.9%, comparable with the GPT-
370   4o’s score of 43.8%. Despite using a fraction of parameter count of a state-of-the-art LLM, Bi-Gen
371   is able to achieve comparable auto-annotation accuracy, and is even able to do so in a multi-turn
372   conversational format.

422   generate a different completion to a given input trajectory. Note that this task is never encountered
423   during training, and this combination of tasks in a single interaction is also never encountered during
424   training. While the model is trained on both tasks in the same batch, there are no training examples
425   of two tasks in one conversation.

427   new, unseen input data (i.e., unseen opponent and ego trajectories). We present the model with a

444   5   C ONCLUSION
445
446   In this work, we presented Bi-Gen, a bi-directional large multi-modal model designed to bridge
447   the gap between trajectory description and generation. By leveraging the strength of LLMs, we
448
      demonstrated that Bi-Gen can serve as an automated annotator or synthetic data generator, providing
      rich augmentations to existing data that prove particularly valuable in low data regimes, such as
449
      multi-car racing.
450
451   The bi-directionality of Bi-Gen marks a significant advancement over prior works, which focus on a
452   single aspect of the trajectory modeling problem. By leveraging the joint structure and complemen-
453   tary data for trajectory description and generation, Bi-Gen is able to learn a richer understanding of
454
      trajectory data, leading to an enhanced ability to bolster existing datasets.
455   Looking forward, Bi-Gen is a step towards further advancements in multi-modal modeling in em-
456   bodied domains. As a framework for integrating multiple modalities for real-time data synthesis
457   and understanding, Bi-Gen could be applied to driving, robotics, or other digital interaction do-
458   mains. Future work may seek to further extend Bi-Gen with additional modalities, such as video or
459   other real-time sensors, expanding the capabilities of Bi-Gen to more complex data synthesis and
460
      modeling problems.

      While the data collection and dataset are not contributions of this work, we briefly discuss them
651
      here for completeness and clarity in the submission. The dataset was captured during a user study,
652   which was designed to gather human driving behavior data in the racing domain we use in the paper.
653   The purpose of the study was to gather qualitative and statistical data on individuals’ behavior and
654   objectives in a racing context, and to use that to inform what criteria are important for building
655   models of human objectives. We recruited 48 participants to drive a simulator with the hairpin and
656   straightaway segments of the two-mile racing track, the same domains for the computational results
657   in this paper. The scenarios were chosen so as to present overtake opportunities in portions of the
658   track of varying levels of difficulty, while keeping the overall task short enough to ensure there
659   is a rich interaction between the ego and opponent. Participants completed a series of warm-up
660
      trials in each domain, with three trials devoted to the straightaway segment and eight trials in the
      hairpin segment, each featuring different opponents of varying difficulty (fixed trajectories) to race
661
      against. Again, these were the same trajectories used in our domains. At the conclusion of each trial,
662
      participants answered the question: “Did you attempt to pass the other vehicle?” on an iPad. We
663   also gathered, from trajectory data, whether or not the participant actually completed an overtake
664   without collisions or spin-outs. 877 trajectories are collected. We then further manually label the
