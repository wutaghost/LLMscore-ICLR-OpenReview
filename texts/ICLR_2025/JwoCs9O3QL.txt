009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language models (VLMs) are essential for contextual understanding of
012            both visual and textual information. However, their vulnerability to adversarially
013            manipulated inputs presents significant risks, leading to compromised outputs and
014            raising concerns about the reliability in VLM-integrated applications. Detecting
015            these malicious prompts is thus crucial for maintaining trust in VLM generations.
016            A major challenge in developing a safeguarding prompt classifier is the lack of a
017            large amount of labeled benign and malicious data. To address the issue, we intro-
018
               duce VLMG UARD, a novel learning framework that leverages the unlabeled user
               prompts in the wild for malicious prompt detection. These unlabeled prompts,
019
               which naturally arise when VLMs are deployed in the open world, consist of both
020
               benign and malicious information. To harness the unlabeled data, we present an
021            automated maliciousness estimation score for distinguishing between benign and
022            malicious samples within this unlabeled mixture, thereby enabling the training of
023            a binary prompt classifier on top. Notably, our framework does not require extra
024            human annotations, offering strong flexibility and practicality for real-world ap-
025            plications. Extensive experiment shows VLMG UARD achieves superior detection
026            results, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods. Disclaimer: This
027            paper may contain offensive examples; reader discretion is advised.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Safeguarding vision language models (VLMs) against persistent threats of adversarial prompts has
033
      become a crucial yet challenging problem in safely deploying these multimodal foundation mod-
      els in the wild, where the user prompts in the deployment time can naturally arise from a mixture
034
      distribution of both benign and malicious sources (Zou et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; Yin et al.,
035
      2024). Compared with text-only language models, Modern VLMs process both text and images,
036   making them particularly vulnerable to malicious prompts, which can target not only the textual
037   input but also the visual component and thus allow attackers to manipulate both channels simulta-
038   neously (Zhang et al., 2024). These malicious prompts can elicit harmful outputs (Shayegani et al.,
039   2024) or trigger unintended actions of VLM-integrated tools, such as personal assistants (Yi et al.,
040   2023), and thus place critical decision-making at risk. This risk underscores the need for VLMs to
041   not only generate coherent responses but also detect potentially malicious prompts before producing
042   outputs (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Xie et al., 2024).
043   Malicious prompt detection, which involves determining whether a user-provided input is harmful, is
044   essential for the safe deployment of VLMs. However, a primary challenge in learning a safeguarding
045   prompt classifier is the limited availability of labeled datasets that include both benign and malicious
046   samples. Constructing reliable datasets often requires extensive human annotation, which is time-
047   consuming and difficult to scale given the evolving nature of generative models and the diversity
048   of user inputs. Ensuring the quality of such labeled data further demands rigorous quality control,
049   making manual annotation an unsustainable solution as models and user interactions become more
050
      complex. These significant challenges highlight the necessity of exploring methods that leverage
      unlabeled data for effective malicious prompt detection.
051
052   Motivated by these challenges, we introduce VLMG UARD, a novel learning framework designed to
053   leverage unlabeled user data in the wild to enable the language model to distinguish between benign
      and malicious prompts. Unlabeled data naturally arises from interactions on chat-based platforms,

128   In this paper, we propose a learning framework that facilitates malicious prompt detection by lever-
129   aging unlabeled user prompts collected in real-world settings. These prompts naturally arise from
130   user interactions within chat-based applications. For instance, consider a vision-language model
131
      such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) deployed in the wild, which processes a vast array of visual and
      textual user queries. This data can be collected with user consent, yet often contains a mixture of
132
      benign and potentially malicious content. Formally, the unlabeled user prompts can be modeled
133
      using the Huber contamination model (Huber, 1992) as follows:

      Overview. Despite the availability of unlabeled user prompt datasets, leveraging such data presents
      significant challenges due to the absence of explicit labels indicating whether samples are benign or
146
      malicious within the mixture data D. To overcome this challenge, our framework VLMG UARD is
147
      designed to create an automated function that estimates the maliciousness of samples in the unla-
148   beled data. This functionality enables the subsequent training of a binary classifier (see Figure 1).
149   We detail these two steps in Section 3.1 and Section 3.2, respectively. Our study represents an initial
150   effort to address this intricate problem and provides a foundation for future research on leveraging
151   unlabeled data for malicious prompt detection.

      4.1    S ETUP
232
      Datasets and models. We evaluate our approach under two threat models–adversarial meta-
233
      instruction and jailbreak prompts. For the meta-instruction, we leverage the dataset from Zhang
234   et al. (2024), which comprises 25 benign and 300 malicious images in ImageNet, each associated
235   with 60 questions. Malicious images are generated by injecting adversarial noise into benign data
236   using projected gradient descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2017) over 40 training question-answer pairs.
237   These pairs are categorized under one of five meta-objectives: L ANGUAGE, P OLITICS, F ORMAL -
238   ITY , S PAM , and S ENTIMENT . For instance, the malicious images are optimized to prompt VLMs to
239   produce biased responses, such as answers in different languages, with political or formality bias,
240   sentiment alterations, or appended spam texts.
241   To simulate the unlabeled prompt data Punlabeled , we mix the benign image-text pairs (Pbenign ) with
242   malicious pairs (Pmalicious ) under various π ∈ {0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1}. Twenty benign images
243   and their corresponding synthesized malicious versions are in the unlabeled data with 40 questions
244   as the textual prompts. We then test with 20 held-out questions and the remaining 5 images.
245
      For the jailbreak prompts, we create both benign and malicious data by combining 250 safe textual
246   prompts and 200 unsafe prompts from the XSTest dataset (Röttger et al., 2023) with 5 benign and
247   adversarial images from Qi et al. (2023). The unlabeled dataset is constructed based on benign
248   image-text pairs (by pairing 200 safe textual prompts with 3 benign images) and malicious pairs
249   (by pairing 100 unsafe textual prompts with 3 adversarial images), while the remaining prompts
250   are reserved for evaluation. We apply the same mixing strategy as used for the meta-instruction,
251   with varying ratios π. Additional details on the dataset and inference procedures are provided in
252   Appendix A.
253   We evaluate our method using two families of models: LLaVA-1.6-7B & 13B (Liu et al., 2024)
254   and Phi-3-Vision (Abdin et al., 2024), which are popularly adopted public multimodal foundation
255   models with accessible internal representations. Following the convention, we use the pre-trained
256   weights and conduct zero-shot inference in all cases.
257
      Baselines and evaluation metric. We compare our approach with a comprehensive collec-
258   tion of baselines, which include: (1) Uncertainty-based malicious prompt detection approaches–
259   Perplexity (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023), GradSafe (Xie et al., 2024) and Gradient Cuff (Hu et al.,
260   2024); (2) LLM-based methods–Self detection (Gou et al., 2024) and GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023);
261   (3) Mutation-based approach JailGuard (Zhang et al., 2023); and (4) Denoising-based methods–
262   MirrorCheck (Fares et al., 2024) and CIDER (Xu et al., 2024). To ensure a fair comparison, we
263   assess all baselines on identical test data, employing the default experimental configurations as out-
264   lined in their respective papers. Consistent with a previous study (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Xie
265   et al., 2024), we evaluate the effectiveness of all methods by the area under the receiver operator
266
      characteristic curve (AUROC), which measures the performance of a binary classifier under varying
      thresholds. We discuss the implementation details for baselines in Appendix A.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
449
450
      Malicious prompt attack for LMs has attracted a surge of interest nowadays, where the main threats
451   are prompt injection and jailbreak prompt. The former one is a class of attacks against applications
452   built on top of LMs that concatenate untrusted user input with a trusted prompt constructed by the
453   application’s developer (Greshake et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;c; Shi et al., 2024;
454   Rossi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). For VLM, Bagdasaryan et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2024)
455   proposed to inject adversarial noise to the visual model inputs to generate arbitrary fixed strings
456   or texts that have adversary-chosen bias. By contrast, jailbreak prompt aims to trick the models
457   into generating outputs that violate their safety guardrails, e.g., toxic text (Chao et al., 2023; Zou
458   et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024; Russinovich et al., 2024; Gu et al.,
459
      2024). The current multimodal jailbreak attack mainly worked by optimizing the input images to
      elicit harmful generations (Shayegani et al., 2024; Carlini et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2024; Schlarmann
460
      et al., 2024) or leveraging typography images (Gong et al., 2023). We evaluate our algorithm on
461
      representative approaches in both categories (Zhang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023)
462
463   Malicious prompt detection is crucial for ensuring LMs’ safety and reliability. Existing research
464   is mostly developed based on text-based LLM and specifically for jailbreak prompts. One line
465
      of work performs detection by devising uncertainty scoring functions, such as perplexity (Alon &
      Kamfonas, 2023) and gradient scores (Xie et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024). Another line of research
466
      utilized LM as a judge by querying the model itself (Gou et al., 2024) or another model, such as GPT
467
      for detection. In the multimodal domain, Xu et al. (2024); Fares et al. (2024) took an embedding-
468   based approach, where it relies on the embedding difference between the original image and its
469   denoised version for jailbreak detection. Pi et al. (2024) employed labeled data for harm detection,
470   which differs from our scope on harnessing unlabeled prompts. Note that our studied problem is
471   different from mitigation-based defense (Robey et al., 2023; Piet et al., 2023; Hines et al., 2024;
472   Wang et al., 2024b; Zeng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), which aims at preventing
473   LM to generate compromised outputs given malicious prompts. Zou et al. (2023a) explored probing
474   meaningful representation direction to detect hallucinations while VLMG UARD aims for malicious
475   prompt detection and presents a different algorithm design.
476
477   6    C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this paper, we propose a novel learning algorithm VLMG UARD for malicious prompt detec-
480   tion in VLMs, which exploits the unlabeled user prompts arising in the wild. VLMG UARD first
481   estimates the maliciousness for samples in the unlabeled mixture data based on an embedding de-
482   composition, and then trains a binary safeguarding prompt classifier on top. The empirical result
483   shows that VLMG UARD establishes superior performance on different malicious data and families
484   of VLMs. Our in-depth quantitative and qualitative ablations provide further insights on the efficacy
485   of VLMG UARD. We hope our work will inspire future research on malicious prompt detection with
      unlabeled prompt datasets.

734   Design Rationale. We briefly provide our reasoning on the estimation score.
735   design rationale of utilizing embedding decomposition for maliciousness estimation. Firstly, sub-
736   space primary vectors derived through SVD often encapsulate the dominant patterns and variations
737   within the internal representations of a model 2 . These vectors can highlight the primary modes of
738   variance in the unlabeled data, which are not purely random but instead capture significant struc-
739
      tural features of the model’s processing. In our case, it could be the maliciousness information. Even
      though these vectors could, in theory, capture various features, they are particularly informative for
740
      detecting malicious samples because malicious and benign patterns are among these primary modes
741
      of variation in the unlabeled data. This phenomenon can be verified by the empirically observed
742   separability in Figure 6 and literature (Zou et al., 2023a).
