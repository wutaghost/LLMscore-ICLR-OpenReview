009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            City-scale road volume prediction is a fundamental task in traffic management.
012            However, the observation data are often incomplete and biased, posting a chal-
013            lenge for accurate prediction. Existing methods address this issue through in-
014            terpolation techniques or manual priors, but they typically provide only a deter-
015            ministic restoration, overlooking the influence of other potential scenarios. To
016            overcome these limitations, we propose a novel neural network-based probabilis-
017            tic model, the Trajectory Probability Network (TraPNet), which predicts traffic
018            volume through the aggregation of the joint distribution of potential trajectories.
019
               TraPNet makes full use of current observations, historical data, and road network
               information to offer a comprehensive inference of road volumes. Unlike autore-
020
               gressive methods, TraPNet makes predictions in a single step, substantially reduc-
021
               ing computational time while maintaining high predictive accuracy. Experiments
022            on real-world road networks demonstrate that TraPNet outperforms state-of-the-
023            art methods, and can keep the advantage with only 20% observation ratio. The
024            code will be made publicly available.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Traffic volume prediction is a crucial task in urban traffic management, offering valuable insights
030   into traffic congestion, road safety, and infrastructure planning. This task involves estimating the
031   number of vehicles passing through each road at specific times, with predictions derived from current
032   observations, historical data, and road network information.
033
      Researchers have developed various methods to predict traffic volume, including traditional time
034
      series models Vlahogianni et al. (2014), deep learning models Lv et al. (2014), and graph neural
035
      networksYu et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017). Many of these methods rely on historical in-route data
036   collected via sensors deployed across road networks or GPS services Fang et al. (2020); Chen et al.
037   (2024). However, gathering complete, city-wide traffic data remains a challenge: GPS data cannot
038   capture all vehicles, and sensors are typically deployed only at key intersections, resulting in data
039   that is both incomplete and unevenly distributed.
040
      To address this challenge, some studies have focused on checkpoint-based data, providing a more
041
      accessible alternative Chen et al. (2023). Traditional methods often rely on prior probabilities to
042   estimate missing traffic volumes Yu et al. (2023); Bao et al. (2023), while deep learning-based
043   approaches reconstruct missing trajectories using current observations Zhang et al. (2019); Guo
044   et al. (2024). However, the majority of these methods provide only deterministic reconstructions of
045   the missing data, overlooking the inherent uncertainty in other potential scenarios. Furthermore, in
046   some underdeveloped areas, observational data can be extremely sparse, rendering these methods
047   less applicable.
048   To provide a more comprehensive prediction, we propose a novel probabilistic road volume predic-
049   tion model. In our approach, we treat the volume on each road as the sum of random variables, with
050   probabilities determined by individual vehicles on the road network. For each vehicle, we estimate
051   the probability of its presence on any given road at each time step, rather than determining a single
052   fixed trajectory. By accumulating these probabilities for all vehicles, we aggregate the distributions
053   of all potential trajectories, leading to a more thorough prediction of road volumes. The posterior
      probability given incomplete observations is inferred using a neural network.

      In addition to the integration mechanism, we enhance the model’s robustness by fully utilizing dif-
055   ferent views of the data. Our model, Trajectory Probability Network (TraPNet), integrates current
056   observations, historical trajectories, and road network information to provide a comprehensive in-
057   ference of road volumes. All heterogeneous data are embedded into a unified latent space, and
058   trajectory probabilities are estimated through a multi-view attention mechanism. Experiments on
059   real-world road networks demonstrate that TraPNet outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both
060   accuracy and efficiency. Notably, even when the observation ratio is as low as 20%, our model
061   maintains its advantage. The primary contributions of this paper are as follows:
062
063          • We propose TraPNet, a neural network that leverages diverse sources of information, inte-
064
               grating the joint distribution of all potential trajectories to predict road volume.
065          • TraPNet performs complete volume prediction in a single step, significantly reducing com-
066            putational time while maintaining high predictive accuracy.
067          • TraPNet demonstrates exceptional tolerance to missing data. With only 20% observations,
068            TraPNet outperforms other models that require 50% observation ratio. This makes TraPNet
069            applicable to a wider range of urban traffic scenarios.

074
      Traffic volume prediction is a crucial task in urban traffic management, with various approaches
      developed over the years. These approaches can be broadly categorized into three types: traditional
075
      methods Vlahogianni et al. (2014), deep learning methods Ma et al. (2015); Yu et al. (2017), and
076
      checkpoint-based methods Chen et al. (2023). Traditional methods typically rely on historical in-
077   route data collected by sensors or GPS services, utilizing models such as ARIMA. Deep learning
078   methods, which use models like LSTM and GNN, also require complete volume data for accurate
079   prediction. In contrast, checkpoint-based methods focus on incomplete data collected from key
080   intersections, making them more applicable to real-world scenarios.
081
      Despite their effectiveness, these methods face several challenges. Both traditional and deep learning
082
      approaches struggle with acquiring complete citywide data, limiting their practicality in real-world
083   scenarios. Although checkpoint-based methods are more accessible, they also fail to handle ex-
084   tremely incomplete data scenarios. Furthermore, many existing methods overlook historical data or
085   road network information, which can lead to inaccurate predictions.

      2.1   T RAFFIC VOLUME P REDICTION
088
089   Traffic volume prediction has traditionally relied on historical in-route data, captured by sensors
090   deployed across road networks or GPS service, using models like LSTMs and GNNs Zhang et al.
091   (2017); Li et al. (2017); Yu et al. (2017); Diao et al. (2019). However, the challenge of acquiring
092   complete, citywide data makes these methods impractical in real-world settings. To address this
093   challenge, some studies focus on checkpoint-based data from key intersections, offering a more
094   accessible alternative Liu et al. (2020); Kalander et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2018). While these methods
095
      predict traffic for regions or entire cities, they often lack the granularity to forecast at a city-wide
      per-road level. In addition, time series models, though effective at capturing temporal dependencies,
096
      suffer from cumulative errors and long inference times, limiting their scalability for large urban
097
      networks Ma et al. (2015); Zhao et al. (2019); Wong et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2023).

      2.2   T RAJECTORY I NTERPOLATION
100
101
      In many real-world scenarios, incomplete trajectories are the primary source of data, necessitating
102   the use of interpolation or reconstruction methods. One common approach relies on prior probabil-
103   ities, assuming that missing trajectories follow specific patterns, such as the shortest route Patterson
104   et al. (2020); Hunter et al. (2013); Iio et al. (2023). However, this approach can be inaccurate, as
105   real vehicles often deviate from these assumptions. Another approach utilizes deep learning-based
106   reconstruction, which, while effective, often relies solely on current observations without incorpo-
107   rating historical data or road network information. Additionally, both probabilistic and deep learn-
      ing methods typically assume that vehicles follow a single fixed path, overlooking the possibility of

114   3.1   BASIC N OTATIONS
115
      Suppose the road network is represented as a graph G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes and
116
      E is the set of edges. V = {1, 2, ..., V }, which are the number of intersections (node). E =
117   {e1 , e2 , ..., eE }, ei = (oi , di , li ), which represents the origin, destination and weight of the ith road
118   (edge). The weight can be length, average speed, traffic level or any other information of the road.
119
120
      Considering that the number of nodes is much smaller than the number of edges, we use nodes to
      represent trajectories. A trajectory can be represented as a sequence of nodes, [v1 , v2 , ..., vT ], where
121
      vi is a rearrangement of {0, 1, ..., V }, T is the maximum time step, 0 means that the car disappears.
122
      To indicate the time spent on each road, we repeat vi in the trajectory, e.g. [1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 3, 4, 0] means
123   that a car spend 1 time step on edge (1,2), 4 steps on (2,3), 1 step on (3,4), and finally disappear. We
124   denote all the trajectories as X ∈ {0, 1, ..., V }B,T , where B is the number of observed trajectories.
125
126   3.2   ROAD -L EVEL VOLUME P REDICTION
127
128   In real-world scenarios, traffic is recorded by a limited number of unevenly distributed sensors,
129   such as cameras at intersections, resulting in incomplete observations. For example, assume that
130   only node 1 and node 3 are observable, in the former case where the complete trajectory is
131   [1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 3, 4, 0], the corresponding observation should be [1, 0, 0, 0, 0, 3, 0, 0]. Given all the ob-
132   served trajectories during T time steps, Road-Level Volume Prediction is to estimate the volume of
133
      each road at each time, where the volume is defined as the number of vehicles passing through a
      given road. The volume is denoted as Vol ∈ RE,T , where Vol[i, t] is the volume of road i at time t.
134
135   Trajectory Probability is the distribution of X. We assume that each trajectory is independently dis-
136   tributed, and denote the trajectory probability as Y ∈ [0, 1]B,T,V , where Y [b, t, v] is the probability
137   that the bth vehicle at time t is on v. Once the trajectory probability distribution is obtained, the vol-
138   ume can be either calculated by the expectation or the MAP estimation. In this paper, we focus on
139
      predicting volume by the aggregation of qθ (Y |X, Xhis , A), where θ is the parameter of our model,
      Xhis is the historical trajectory information and A is the road network information.

143
144   In this section, we introduce our model, Trajectory Probability Network (TraPNet). First, we in-
145   troduce the overall architecture of the model in section 4.1. The process of embedding different
146   types of information is described in section 4.2. The core component of our model, the Multi-view
147   Attention Block, is detailed in section 4.3. Finally, we discuss the model’s optimization and the
148
      aggregation to inference road volume in section 4.4.

150   4.1   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
151
      The overall architecture of our model is shown in fig. 1. The model consists of three components:
152
      embedding layers, multi-view attention blocks, and linear projections. The embedding layers project
153   the observed trajectories, road network, and historical trajectories into the same hidden space. These
154   different sources of embeddings are then aggregated within the multi-view attention block, which
155   captures complex relationships across different views. The output from the multi-view attention
156   block is passed through a linear projection layer with softmax activation to compute the trajectory
157   probabilities. Finally, the trajectory probabilities are used to estimate the road volume.

      6     D ISCUSSION
506
507
508
      6.1        BALANCE BETWEEN P ERFORMANCE AND E FFICIENCY
509
      TraPNet is designed to achieve a balance between performance and computational efficiency. The
510
      multi-view attention mechanism effectively integrates observational data, historical information, and
511   road network information, enhancing performance but also introducing a substantial computational
512   burden. The discretization mechanism and multi-query attention mechanism mitigate this burden by
513   slightly compromising performance. Similarly, the pooling mechanism strikes a balance between
514   efficiency and GPU memory usage. On smaller road networks, these mechanisms may be optional,
515   depending on specific requirements, but for larger road networks, they are essential for practical
516   deployment.
517
518   6.2        T HE C HOICE OF O NE -H OT L ABELS
519
520   Our primary goal is to estimate trajectory probabilities given incomplete observations. However,
521   during the training process, we rely exclusively on complete trajectories as the ground truth. An
522   alternative approach would be to use prior distributions as the ground truth, which can be derived
523   from road weights. However, as demonstrated in section 5, manually assigned prior distributions can
524   introduce bias. To ensure accuracy and reliability, we use only complete trajectories as the ground
525   truth. In the future, we aim to explore the potential of adapting manual prior distributions to more
526   closely reflect real-world distributions.

      7     C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we proposed a novel probabilistic approach to address the challenge of city-scale road
      volume prediction with incomplete observations. We introduced Trajectory Probability Network
532
      (TraPNet), a model capable of estimating trajectory probabilities based on incomplete observations,
533
      historical trajectories, and road network information. The road volume can be comprehensively
534   estimated by aggregating the trajectory probabilities. TraPNet’s one-step road volume prediction,
535   combined with various computation-efficient mechanisms, ensures both high performance and com-
536   putational efficiency. We conducted extensive experiments on real-world road networks, demon-
537   strating that TraPNet outperforms state-of-the-art methods. With only 20% observations, TraPNet
538   outperforms other models that require 50% observation ratio. Furthermore, our ablation study high-
539   lights the impact of different computation-efficient mechanisms. TraPNet is highly adaptable to
      real-world scenarios, as its inputs can be flexibly defined.
