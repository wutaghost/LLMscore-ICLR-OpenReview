011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are often trained on individual datasets, requir-
014            ing specialized models and significant hyperparameter tuning due to the unique
015            structures and features of each dataset. This approach limits the scalability and gen-
016            eralizability of GNNs, as models must be tailored for each specific graph type. To
017            address these challenges, we introduce G RAPH FM, a scalable multi-graph pretrain-
018
               ing approach designed for learning across diverse graph datasets. G RAPH FM uses
               a Perceiver-based encoder with learned latent tokens to compress domain-specific
019
               features into a shared latent space, enabling generalization across graph domains.
020
               We propose new techniques for scaling up graph training on datasets of different
021            sizes, allowing us to train G RAPH FM on 152 distinct graph datasets, spanning 7.4
022            million nodes and 189 million edges. This allows us to study the effect of scale
023            on pretraining across domains such as molecules, citation networks, and product
024            graphs, and show that training on diverse datasets improves performance over
025            single-source pretraining. Our results demonstrate that pretraining on diverse real
026            and synthetic graphs enhances adaptability and stability, leading to competitive
027            performance with state-of-the-art models across various node classification tasks.
028            This approach reduces the burden of dataset-specific training and provides a single
029
               generalist model capable of performing across multiple diverse graph structures
               and tasks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Graphs are a fundamental data structure used across diverse fields such as biology, social networks,
036   and recommendation systems (Hamilton et al., 2017). However, most graph neural network (GNN)
037   architectures are designed in a highly specialized way, optimized for specific types of graphs. For
038   example, architectures that work well on homophilic graphs, such as citation networks, often fail to
039
      generalize to heterophilic graphs, like certain social or biological networks, due to the differences
      in their topologies (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019; Yan et al., 2022). This specialization leads to a
040
      fragmentation in model development, where the optimal architecture for one type of graph must be
041
      significantly altered or redesigned for another. As the use of GNNs grows across diverse applications,
042   this piecemeal approach limits scalability and generalization, highlighting the need for a generalist
043   model that can handle a wide variety of graph structures without manual tuning.
044
045
      A core challenge in building a generalist graph model lies in integrating diverse graphs, each with
      unique topologies, node features, and sizes, while enabling knowledge transfer across them. Without a
046
      shared “vocabulary” for graph structures, models struggle to generalize effectively, as the differences
047
      between graph types hinder the transfer of learned patterns (Galkin et al., 2023). At the same time,
048   recent advances in large-scale language models have shown that scaling up both model size and
049   data diversity is essential for unlocking emergent capabilities and improving generalization across
050   tasks (Wei et al., 2022). This makes scaling an equally critical factor in graph models. Pretraining
051   on diverse graphs requires algorithms that can efficiently handle large, heterogeneous inputs, while
052   ensuring the model can still capture robust, transferable patterns. Therefore, building a generalist
053   graph model necessitates solutions that not only integrate diverse graph structures but also scale
      effectively, allowing the model to learn from vast, varied datasets without sacrificing performance.

054
      In this work, we introduce G RAPH FM , a multi-graph pretraining framework aimed at addressing
055   this gap. Instead of building specialized models for each graph type, G RAPH FM uses a Perceiver-
056   based transformer encoder (Jaegle et al., 2021b) to create a shared latent space that abstracts away
057   graph-specific details while preserving core structural properties. This enables the model to process a
058   wide range of graph types within a unified framework, moving beyond the specialist architectures
059   that dominate current GNN design. Our approach seeks to answer a key question: can pretraining on
060   diverse, multi-graph datasets lead to effective generalization and transfer across unseen graphs?
061   When tested on a variety of homophilic and heterophilic datasets, we demonstrate that our model
062   achieves performance comparable to all of the best baseline models, each of which is individually
063   tuned for its respective dataset. Overall, we achieve the best rank when compared with these models,
064   demonstrating that our approach has strong generalist performance. By combining datasets from
065   biology, social networks, and recommendation systems, we show that our model can generalize
066   across graphs with varying topologies and features, providing the flexibility that specialized models
067   often lack. Moreover, our framework efficiently handles large mixtures of diverse graph datasets,
068   leveraging distributed training techniques to manage graphs of different sizes and complexities.
069   Our results show that increasing both the scale of the model and the diversity of the training data leads
070   to significant improvements in downstream performance on new, unseen graphs and node-level tasks.
071   This demonstrates that it is indeed possible to train a generalist model on diverse graphs, which can
072   effectively learn from and adapt to a wide range of graph types. In total, we pretrain on 152 distinct
073   graph datasets, comprising over 7.4 million nodes and 189 million edges across a wide variety of
074   graph types—an unprecedented number of different graph datasets in the literature. This extensive
075
      pretraining allows our model to capture and transfer knowledge across a broad spectrum of graph
      structures, showcasing the feasibility and advantages of building a unified model that generalizes
076
      well to unseen tasks.
077
078   The main contributions of this work are as follows:
079
080
              • Scalable Pre-training Approach: We introduce a scalable framework for pretraining on
                diverse graphs using a Perceiver-based encoder with latent tokens, which efficiently handles
081
                graphs with varying sizes and topologies. Our approach includes advanced multi-graph
082
                sampling techniques that optimize GPU utilization, enabling large-scale pretraining across a
083             wide range of graph datasets.
084
              • Demonstration of Benefits from Across-Graph Pretraining: We show that pretraining
085
                on diverse graphs significantly improves the model’s ability to generalize and transfer
086
                knowledge to unseen graphs. This demonstrates that a generalist model can leverage
087             common structural features across different datasets to outperform specialized models.
088
              • Scaling Analysis and Impact of Multi-Graph Pretraining: We provide the first scaling
089
                analysis for multi-graph pretraining on different domains, showing that larger models
090
                pretrained on more diverse graph datasets result in better generalization. Our results highlight
091             that increasing both the scale of the model and the diversity of the training data improves
092             performance on downstream tasks.

      2.2.1             M ULTI - GRAPH PACKING
204
205   Typically when creating batches for training graph transformers, padding is used to extend the smaller
206   graphs to have the same size as the largest graph in the batch (Rampášek et al., 2022; Ying et al.,
207   2021). This approach is likely inherited from the transformer architectures found in other domains
208   where the context window (or sequence length) is usually fixed. But for graphs, the problem with
209   padding is particularly pronounced when there is a significant size disparity among different graphs
210   in the same batch. Alternative solutions exist, and in particular, the graph community have been
211   pioneers in batching variable-sized graphs. Message-passing frameworks combine multiple graphs
212
      into a single large graph over which message passing is conducted (Fey & Lenssen, 2019b; Krell
      et al., 2022). However, these out-of-the-box implementations are not suited for transformers which
213
      use fully-connected attention.
214
215   We implement a custom data collator, which merges all graphs in the batch into a single large
      sequence of tokens, and adapts the attention mask to restrict each graph to itself. In particular, we


                                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      leverage Flash Attention (Dao, 2023) which makes computing attention over very large sequences
217   extremely efficient. By doing so, we avoid any superfluous padding, and this in turn improves the
218   computational efficiency during training.
219
220   2.2.2       BALANCED GPU UTILIZATION WITH THE D ISTRIBUTED SSS AMPLER
221   During multi-GPU distributed training, a global batch is formed by randomly sampling graphs from
222   different datasets, which is then equally split among the GPUs. Naively splitting the batch can lead
223   to unbalanced GPU utilization. On one hand, we can have a large batch of relatively small graphs,
224   and another where we can only have a batch with one or two very large graphs. This means that we
225   would be forced to lower the batch size, to avoid going out of memory when multiple large graphs
226   are batched together. Our Distributed Snake Strategy Sampler (DistributedSSSampler) employs a
227   bidirectional filling strategy, where graphs, sorted by their size, are distributed in a snake-like pattern,
228   initially assigned to GPUs from right to left, then left to right and so on. This method effectively pairs
229
      large graphs with small ones in subsequent passes, preventing the concentration of multiple large
      graphs on the same GPU, thus achieving efficient load balancing and uniform GPU utilization. A
230
      detailed algorithm and more details are provided in Appendix C.1.
231
232   We show the effectiveness of this approach in Figure 3,
233   where we demonstrate significantly lower variance in GPU
234   load compared to the default PyTorch batch sampler and
235
      near 100% utilization. The effectiveness is more pro-
      nounced the more GPUs are used1 . This subsequently
236
      allows us to use substantially larger batch sizes, resulting
237
      in further improvement in stability and a significant 2 to
238   4x speed-up in training time.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
290
291   Training: To train all of our models, we employed the LAMB optimizer (You et al., 2019) with
292   a learning rate of 10−4 . The learning rate is scheduled based on a linear warmup of 2 epochs,
293   followed by cosine decay until the end of training. We use bfloat16 mixed-precision and flash
294   attention (Dao, 2023) for higher compute efficiency while training. We trained our largest model
295   (75M parameters) for 6.4 days on 8 NVIDIA A40 GPUs. We point the reader to further details on the
296   architecture and model training in Appendix A.1.
297   Baselines: We compared G RAPH FM against six baseline models that were consistently reported in
298   both heterophilic and homophilic benchmarks. This included two GNN-based models: GCN(Kipf
299   & Welling, 2016) and GAT(Velickovic et al., 2017), two transformer-based models: SAN (Kreuzer
300   et al., 2021) and NAGphormer (Chen et al., 2022b), and two heterophily-based models: MLP and
301   H2GCN (Zhu et al., 2020). For all of the baseline models, we include the best reported accuracy,
302   and when there are no reported results for a dataset, we extensively tuned each model as in standard
303   practice (see Appendix B.4). We also provide additional baselines in Appendix D.4 reported for
304
      subsets of the datasets tested.
305   Evaluation: To evaluate the generalization of our pretrained model on new datasets that it hasn’t
306   encountered during pretraining, we employed two fine-tuning strategies: (i) Low-resource MLP
307   fine-tuning (MFT), where we freeze the encoder and node decoder weights and only update the
308   feature MLP weights, and (ii) combined MLP and node decoder fine-tuning (NFT), where we also
309   adapt the node decoder weights. MFT is aimed at evaluating near out-of-the-box performance by
310
      leveraging the model’s pretrained knowledge, with minimal additional training, whereas NFT allows
      for more flexibility by adjusting weights of the pretrained node decoder to better align with the OOD
311
      data. For all the fine-tuning experiments, we used a learning rate of 10−3 and a weight decay of 10−5 ,
312
      optimized using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), and use a gradual unfreezing
313   strategy to update the node decoder weights in our NFT experiments. Further details are provided in
314   the Appendix A.3.

426   Q3: H OW DOES OUR MODEL GENERALIZE OUT- OF - THE - BOX ?
427
428   A major challenge in applying graph-based models is the extensive tuning often required to achieve
429   competitive performance. Most models are highly sensitive to hyperparameters like learning rate,
430   depth, and weight decay. Tuning these hyperparameters, especially across datasets with different
431   graph topologies and sizes, requires significant time and computational resources, and even then,
      finding a good configuration can be difficult (Guo et al., 2022; Tsitsulin et al., 2022).


                                                                                                            8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      In contrast, G RAPH FM offers strong out-of-the-box performance without requiring any significant
433   tuning. To demonstrate this, we evaluated G RAPH FM using the same fixed learning rate and weight
434   decay across multiple datasets (learning rate = 10−3 , weight decay = 10−5 ) and observed stable
435   and high performance across all datasets (Figure 5). Fine-tuning G RAPH FM with our simple MFT
436   strategy resulted in low variance and rapid convergence, without the need for extensive hyperparameter
437   exploration. This makes G RAPH FM highly efficient and cost-effective compared to models that
438   require substantial tuning.
439   To highlight this contrast, we compared the performance of G RAPH FM with 100 randomly configured
440   versions of GCN and the best performing transformer-based NAGformer (Chen et al., 2022b). Both
441   baseline models exhibit a wide range of performance depending on the hyperparameter choices,
442   with some configurations leading to significant instability or poor results. For instance, in the Texas
443   dataset, GCN required exhaustive exploration of hyperparameter settings to find a stable and effective
444   configuration. Similarly, NAGformer’s performance fluctuated greatly depending on the dataset and
445   the selected parameters, further emphasizing the cost of tuning.
446   Additionally, G RAPH FM demonstrates quick convergence,
447   reaching near-optimal performance within 10-20 training steps,
448   in stark contrast to GCN, which required considerably more iter-
449   ations to converge. This efficiency is a direct result of leveraging
450   a pretrained model, which allows G RAPH FM to start from a
451   robust initialization and quickly adapt to the target task. The
452   reduced need for hyperparameter tuning and faster convergence
453
      further solidify the advantages of pretraining in minimizing com-
      putational overhead and time-to-solution. Ultimately, our results
454
      position G RAPH FM as a cost-effective and reliable choice for
455
      a wide range of node classification tasks.

478   5    R ELATED W ORK
479
480   Graph foundation modeling approaches. Foundation models have achieved significant success
481   for language, vision and timeseries data (Radford et al., 2018; Dehghani et al., 2023; Das et al.,
482   2023). These models are pre-trained on large datasets and can be adapted to a wide range of
483   downstream tasks, effectively utilizing both prior knowledge from the pre-training stage and data
484   from the downstream tasks to enhance performance (Brown et al., 2020). The concept of foundation
485   models has recently extended into graph learning, leading to the development of Graph Foundation
      Models (GFMs) (Ibarz et al., 2022; Beaini et al., 2023; Galkin et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024). These


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      models aim to generalize across diverse graph-structured data by leveraging large-scale pretraining,
487   similar to foundation models in vision and language domains. Recent efforts have primarily focused
488   on domain-specific GFMs, such as Mole-BERT for molecular graphs, which utilizes pretraining to
489   improve property prediction for molecules and materials (Xia et al., 2023). Additionally, large-scale
490   models like MatterSim (Yang et al., 2024), designed to predict molecular behaviors across different
491   elements and conditions.
492
      Beyond domain-specific applications, GFMs are increasingly being developed for more general
493   tasks. Similarly, recent advancements have explored scaling laws in graph models, showing that
494   larger models can lead to improved transfer learning and generalization (Liu et al., 2024). Similar to
495   theirs, our work shows that scale improves performance. However, unique from all of these works is
496   our result for cross-domain pretraining to enhance generalization across diverse graph topologies.
497   Triplet-GMPNN (Ibarz et al., 2022) which is a foundational GNN for algorithmic reasoning tasks that
498   is trained to perform various tasks from the CLRS benchmark (Veličković et al., 2022), or ULTRA
499   (Galkin et al., 2023), a foundation model for knowledge graphs trained on graphs with arbitrary entity
500   and relation vocabularies. Recent work has also shown how to use language modeling to help unify
501
      many graphs (Liu et al., 2023b).
502
      Scaling up graph transformers. Graph transformers bypass standard local learning rules in GNNs
503
      by allowing all nodes on the graph to interact through self-attention (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020).
504
      However, due to the high computational cost and benefits of the inductive bias in message passing, a
505   number of methods have been proposed to move beyond full self-attention or combine transformers
506   with GNNs. One class of methods combine transformer blocks with GNNs, including GraphTrans
507   (Wu et al., 2021), GraphGPS (Rampášek et al., 2022), and SAT (Chen et al., 2022a). Another
508   strategy is to reduce the computational complexity by using the transformer module on a coarsened
509   or compressed graph. For instance, ANS-GT (Zhang et al., 2022) introduced a node-sampling-based
510   graph transformers, incorporating hierarchical attention and graph coarsening, and Gapformer (Liu
511   et al., 2023a) uses k-hops local pooling and global pooling to coarsen the large graph into a smaller
512   set of nodes. Exphormer (Shirzad et al., 2023) coarsens the graph by doing computation through
513
      expander graphs (Deac et al., 2022). This idea of compression has also been studied through the lens
      of “skeletonization” (Cao et al., 2024) where they learn to identify uninformative background nodes
514
      (Cao et al., 2024) and use this information to compress them to achieve competitive performance
515
      with as little as 1% of the nodes in the graph. Many of these approaches leverage virtual nodes to
516   facilitate message passing across large graphs, however, the compression techniques used in these
517   works are often based on heuristics like pooling layers or expander graphs, in contrast to our work
518   where the compression is fully learned.

521   6    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we introduced G RAPH FM, a novel approach for multi-graph pretraining that effectively
524   handles diverse graph datasets across various domains. A key finding of our work is the positive
525   effect of scaling both model size and data diversity. We show that cross-domain pretraining leads to
526   better out-of-distribution performance, proving that the inclusion of diverse graph types significantly
527   enhances the model’s ability to adapt to new, unseen data. This reveals the potential for graph
528   foundation models to benefit from combining datasets across domains, facilitating more efficient and
529   robust training processes.
530   While our results are promising, there are several areas for future exploration. Our current work
531   primarily focuses on node-level classification tasks; extending G RAPH FM to support tasks like
532   graph-level classification, link prediction, and self-supervised learning could broaden its applicability.
533   Moreover, expanding the diversity of pretraining datasets, such as including point clouds, mesh
534   graphs, or knowledge graphs, may further enhance the model’s generalization capabilities and impact
535   across domains.
536   Looking ahead, we believe that generalist graph models like G RAPH FM have the potential to
537   transform a variety of fields, particularly in scenarios where data is scarce or incomplete. Our work
538   represents an important step toward more universal and adaptable graph models, and we anticipate
539   further research into cross-domain pretraining as a promising direction for the future of graph
      learning.

791   A.2     R ESCALING THE LEARNING RATES FOR DIFFERENT GRAPH SIZES
792
793   When training on variable sized graphs, the MLP and linear decoder for each dataset receive updates
794   based on the number of nodes from their respective datasets present in the batch and thus smaller
795   graphs get updated less frequently when compared to large graphs. To mitigate this imbalance, we
796   implemented dataset-specific learning rates for the feature MLP and linear decoders. Since they
797   receive updates less frequently, when they do, we use a larger learning rate to update them. Without
798
      this adjustment, the weights of the common Perceiver encoder and node decoder would advance more
      quickly than those of the dataset-specific components, potentially leading to suboptimal learning for
799
      smaller datasets.

      A.3     F INE -T UNING S TRATEGIES
803
      In our evaluation of GraphFM’s generalization capability, we employed two fine-tuning strategies
804   aimed at adapting the model to out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets.
805
806   A.3.1    L OW- RESOURCE MLP F INE - TUNING (MFT)
807
808   This approach is designed to assess how well the pretrained model performs out-of-the-box on
809   different OOD graphs without extensive training. In MFT, we freeze the pretrained model and only
      fine-tune a lightweight multi-layer perceptron (MLP) on top of the learned representations. This


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      strategy allows us to quickly adapt the model to new tasks while retaining the majority of the original
811   learned parameters. MFT is particularly useful in low-resource settings, where computational power
812   or time is limited, as it requires minimal additional training while still providing insight into how
813   well the pretrained model generalizes. For all MFT experiments, we used a learning rate of 10−3 and
814   a weight decay of 10−5 , optimized using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017).
815
816   A.3.2    MLP AND N ODE D ECODER F INE - TUNING (NFT)
817
818   In contrast to MFT, the NFT strategy involves fine-tuning part of the and is recommended when
819   sufficient computational resources are available and the goal is to extract the maximum performance
820
      from the model. In NFT, we gradually unfreeze the node decoder, enabling the model to more
      effectively adapt to the new dataset. Specifically, we set a predefined epoch U at which unfreezing
821
      begins, starting from the bottom layers of the node decoder. After every S epochs, additional layers
822
      are unfrozen in a bottom-up manner, facilitating gradual transition to full finetuning of the model.
823   Concurrently, the learning rate is decayed by a factor of 1.5 each time a new layer is unfrozen,
824   ensuring controlled parameter updates. For all datasets, we tune the hyperparameters U and S, with
825   U set to 10, 20, or 30 epochs and S set to 5 or 10 epochs. This gradual unfreezing mitigates training
826   instability, as smaller perturbations are made to higher-level feature representations. As a result, NFT
827   allows for better adaptation, particularly for out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets, and is well suited for
828   case when exploiting the capacity of pretrained models is critical.

       C.3   RAM O PTIMIZATION IN M ULTI -GPU E NVIRONMENTS
1081
1082
       In multi-GPU training environments, efficient use of system memory is crucial, especially when
1083   handling large graph datasets. Traditional approaches lead to substantial memory redundancy, as
1084   each GPU process typically loads a complete dataset into system RAM. This results in each process
1085   duplicating the dataset in system memory, leading to inefficient memory usage and potential system
1086   overload.
1087
       To address this, we utilize a shared memory management approach using Python’s
1088
       multiprocessing.Manager() to coordinate dataset access across multiple GPU processes.
1089   This method ensures that each dataset is loaded into RAM only once, regardless of the number of
1090   GPUs, thereby avoiding duplication and conserving memory resources.
