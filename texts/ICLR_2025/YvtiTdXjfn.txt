009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training a diverse ensemble of models has several practical applications such as providing candidates
012
               for model selection with better out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, and enabling the detection of
013
               OOD samples via Bayesian principles. An existing approach to diverse ensemble training encourages
014            the models to disagree on provided OOD samples. However, the approach is computationally expen-
015            sive and it requires well-separated ID and OOD examples, such that it has only been demonstrated in
016            small-scale settings.
017
               Method. This work presents a Hardness-based Diversification Regularizer (HDR) applicable to
018
               large-scale settings (e.g. ImageNet) that does not require OOD samples. Instead, HDR identifies hard
019            training samples on the fly and encourages the ensemble members to disagree on these. To improve
020            scaling, we show how to avoid the expensive computations in existing methods of exhaustive pairwise
021            disagreements across models.
022
               Results. We evaluate the benefits of diversification with experiments on ImageNet. First, for OOD
023
               generalization, we observe large benefits from the diversification in multiple settings including output-
024            space (classical) ensembles and weight-space ensembles (model soups). Second, for OOD detection,
025            we turn the diversity of ensemble hypotheses into a novel uncertainty score estimator that surpasses a
026            large number of OOD detection baselines.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Training an ensemble of diverse models is useful in multiple applications. Diverse ensembles are used to enhance
033   out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, where strong spurious features learned from the in-domain (ID) training data
034   hinder generalization (Lee et al., 2023; Pagliardini et al., 2023; Teney et al., 2022a;b). By learning multiple hypotheses,
035   the ensemble is given a chance to learn more predictive features that may otherwise be overshadowed by prominent
036   non-robust and spurious features (Chen et al., 2024; Yashima et al., 2022). In Bayesian machine learning, diversification
037   of the posterior samples has been studied as a means to improve the precision and efficiency of sample uncertainty
038   estimates (D’Angelo & Fortuin, 2021; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020).
039   A common strategy to train a diverse ensemble is to introduce a diversification objective while training the models in the
040   ensemble in parallel (D’Angelo & Fortuin, 2021; Lee et al., 2023; Pagliardini et al., 2023; Ross et al., 2020; Scimeca
041   et al., 2023). The main loss (e.g. cross-entropy for classification) encourages the models to fit the labeled training, while
042   the diversification loss encourages the models to disagree with one another on unlabelled OOD samples (Lee et al.,
043   2023; Pagliardini et al., 2023) (Figure 1). The models are thus driven to discover different hypotheses that all explain
044   the in-domain (ID) data but behave different out of distribution.
045   The above approaches to diversification rely on the availability of two distinct sets of data: labeled in-domain (ID)
046   examples for the main training objective and unlabeled OOD examples for diversification.
047
      The existing methods are moreover computationally expensive, and have thus only been tested on small-scale artificial
048
      settings where the data can be clearly delineated into ID and OOD sets (Lee et al., 2023; Pagliardini et al., 2023).
049   To relax the latter requirement for separate ID and OOD sets, some attempts were made to generate OOD data for
050   diversification synthetically (Scimeca et al., 2023). It is however still unclear how to apply these methods to realistic
051   large-scale applications (e.g. ImageNet scale) where distinct OOD samples are not readily available.

134   2.2   DYNAMIC S ELECTION OF H ARD E XAMPLES
135
136   With no OOD data, it is difficult to apply disagreement methods since the main training objective encourages all models
137   to fit the training examples, hence to agree on all available data. In practice, extra OOD for disagreement, which should
138   clearly differ from the ID data, may not be readily available. Considering e.g. ImageNet as the training data, it is not
139   even clear how to define and obtain data that qualifies as OOD or where the feature-label correlations clearly differ.
140   We sidestep these limitations by arguing that the reason OOD data are needed for diversification is not because they are
141   out of distribution (i.e. out of the training dataset) but because they contain some hard data points that are needed to
142   make the models diversify in plausible ways. Previous approaches sourced these data points from a separate dataset.
143   We argue that such a dataset is not needed because eventually what we need are "hard" data points where models can
144   plausibly differ in their responses.
145   For this reason, we propose to replace the OOD disagreement data with a set of hard training examples identified
146   dynamically during training. The models are then encouraged to disagree on these examples. The desiderata for these
147   hard samples are twofold: (a) we wish to discriminate samples where the ensemble members make mistakes and (b) we
148   only trust the ensemble prediction for the hard sample identification when the ensemble is sufficiently trained.

197   2.3   S TOCHASTIC S UM AND S HALLOW D ISAGREEMENT
198
199   Many diversification algorithms are based on exhaustive pairwise comparisons between all the models in the ensemble
200   (see the second term of Equation 6). This scales quadratically with the size M of the ensemble and makes ensemble
201
      training inefficient for higher values of M .
202
203   Stochastic sum. To overcome this quadratic scaling law we propose to use a stochastic sum. For every mini-batch
204
      B, we use a random subset of models |I| ∈ {1, · · · , M } on which to compute the diversification term in Equation 6.
      In our experiments, we randomly sample one pair of models per batch (|I| = 2). Such stochastic sum size allows
205
      to reduce training of an ensemble of 50 models from theoretical 663 GPU hours per epoch to 30 minutes per epoch
206
      (see Appendix A.7). In addition to the computational benefits, stochastic sums contribute to the ensemble diversity by
207   exposing each member to different subsets of training data.


                                                                   4
208
      Shallow disagreement. To further speed up the training, we consider updating only a subset of the layers of the
209   model with the HDR objective, keeping others frozen. More specifically, each ensemble member in the experiments
210   of Section 3 is based on a frozen Deit3b model (Touvron et al., 2022) of which we diversify only the last two layers.
211   Diversifying only the last layer results in worse performance presumably due to the convexity of the optimization
212   problem (see Appendix A.1).

435   4    R ELATED W ORK
436
437   Ensembling is a well-known technique that aggregates the outputs of multiple models to make more accurate predic-
438   tions (Breiman, 1996; 2001; Hansen & Salamon, 1990; Ho, 1995; Krogh & Vedelsby, 1994). It was shown that diversity
439   in the outputs of the ensemble members leads to better gains in performance (Krogh & Vedelsby, 1994; Brown et al.,
440
      2005; Abe et al., 2022) because they make independent errors (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Hansen & Salamon, 1990).
441   In addition, it has been shown empirically (Dong et al., 2022) and theoretically (Yong et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024) that
442   diverse ensembles can also improve OOD generalization.
443
      Diversity through regularizers. Various auxiliary training objectives have been proposed to encourage diversity across
444   models’ weights (D’Angelo & Fortuin, 2021; de Mathelin et al., 2023a;b; Wang & Ji, 2023), features (Chen et al.,
445   2024; Yashima et al., 2022; Yong et al., 2024), input gradients (Ross et al., 2020; Teney et al., 2022a;b; Trinh et al.,
446   2024), or outputs (D’Angelo & Fortuin, 2021; Lee et al., 2023; Liu & Yao, 1999; Pagliardini et al., 2023; Scimeca et al.,
447   2023). D’Angelo & Fortuin (2021) showed that a regularizer that repulses the ensemble members’ weights or outputs
448   leads to ensembles with a better approximation of Bayesian model averaging. This idea was extended by repulsing
449   features (Yashima et al., 2022) and input gradients (Trinh et al., 2024). Since ensemble are most useful when the errors
450   of its members are uncorrelated (Krogh & Vedelsby, 1994), the closest of the above objective is to diversify their
451   outputs. This cannot be guaranteed with other objectives such as weight diversity for example, since two models could
452
      implement the exact same function with different weights due to the many symmetries in the parameter space of neural
      networks. For this reason, this paper focuses on methods for output-space diversification (Lee et al., 2023; Pagliardini
453
      et al., 2023). These were also highlighted as state-of-the-art in a recent survey on diversification (Benoit et al., 2024).
454
455   Diversity without modifying the training objective. The most straightforward way to obtain diverse models is
456   to independently train them with different seeds (Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) and Bayesian
457   extensions (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020)), hyperparameters (Wenzel et al., 2020), augmentations (Li et al., 2023), or
458
      architectures (Zaidi et al., 2021). A computationally cheaper approach is to use models saved at different points during
      the training (Huang et al., 2017) or models derived from the base model by applying dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016)
459
      or masking (Durasov et al., 2021). The “mixture of experts” paradigm (Zhou et al., 2018) can also be viewed as an
460
      ensemble where diversification happens by assigning different training samples to different ensemble members. Our
461   experiments use Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) and ensembles of models trained with different
462   hyperparameters (Wenzel et al., 2020) as baselines since they are strong approaches to OOD detection (Ovadia et al.,
463   2019) and OOD generalization especially when combined with “model soups” (Wortsman et al., 2022).
464
      Hard samples mining methods for OOD generalization. Our approach to identifying hard samples in the training set
465
      is similar to hard sample mining methods used for worst-group robustness (Liu et al., 2021; Qiu et al., 2023; LaBonte
466
      et al., 2023). These methods aim to improve model performance on test samples from minority groups underrepresented
467   in the training set (e.g. photos of animals in unusual contexts, such as a cow on a beach).


                                                                    9
468
      While older worst-group robustness methods often required additional training labels for minority groups. The above
469
      mentioned approaches address this by upweighting the cross-entropy loss for the samples misclassified by a model
470   preliminary trained with Empirical Risk Minimisation (ERM) on the full training dataset (Liu et al., 2021). Extensions
471   to this work include retraining only the last layer of the model (Qiu et al., 2023) and using disagreements between
472   multiple models in addition to misclassification to identify which samples to up-weight (LaBonte et al., 2023).
473
      Our approach differs. We do not require training a separate model with ERM first. We use hard samples for
474
      diversification, not for classification objectives.

477   5    C ONCLUSIONS
478
479   Ensemble diversification has many implications for treating one of the ultimate goals of machine learning, handling
480   out-of-distribution (OOD) samples. Training a large number of diverse hypotheses on a dataset is a way to generate
481   candidates that may have the desired OOD behaviour (i.e. better OOD generalization). And the diversity of hypotheses
482
      can help distinguish ID from OOD samples by measuring disagreements across ensemble members. Despite these
      benefits, diverse-ensemble training has previously remained a lab-bound concept for two reasons. Previous approaches
483
      were computationally expensive (scaling quadratically with ensemble size) and required a separate OOD dataset to
484
      nurture the diverse hypotheses.
485
486   We have addressed these challenges through the novel Hardness-based Diversification Regularizer (HDR). HDR
487   identifies hard samples suitable for disagreement from the training data, bypassing the need to prepare a separate OOD
488
      data. HDR also employs a stochastic pair selection to reduce the quadratic complexity of previous approaches to a
      constant one. We have demonstrated good performance of HDR on OOD generalization and detection tasks, both at the
489
      ImageNet scale, a largely underexplored regime in the ensemble diversification community. In particular, for OOD
490
      detection, our novel diversity measure of Predictive Diversity Score (PDS) amplifies the benefits of diverse ensembles
491   for OOD detection.
492
493   Limitations. This work has focused on solving the applicability of disagreement-based diversification on realistic
494
      datasets. The contributions are thus mostly in the implementation, and the results focus on empirical benefits. Work is
      needed to examine theoretical justifications for the method and characterize the exact conditions under which it should
495
      provide benefits. Similarly, the proposed PDS is a conceptually sound measure of epistemic uncertainty, but work is
496
      also needed to characterize the exact conditions where it is practically superior to alternatives.

749   To perform an ablation study on the way samples for disagreement are selected in Table 6 we compared an ensemble
750   trained with Equation 6 (called "joint" in the table) against a 2-stage approach. Instead of disagreeing on all samples

752   all samples in ImageNet training split and then selected samples with a confidence lower than 0.2 which resulted in
753   18002 samples (to approximately match the sizes of ImageNet-A and ImageNet-R). Then we trained an ensemble by
754   minimizing A2D disagreement regularizer on these samples while minimizing cross-entropy on all other samples. Both
755
      ensembles had size 5 and stochastic sum size |I| = 2. While such an approach might sound simpler, HDR is more
      straightforward and efficient, since there is no need to train an initial model to determine samples for disagreement.
756
      Both ensembles have a similar generalization performance, with the biggest difference for ImageNet-R where ensemble
757
      accuracy dropped from 48.5% for 2-stage approach to 48.1% for the joint. In contrast, OOD detection performance
758   is significantly better across the board for the joint approach, for example, the detection AUROC scores are 0.845 vs
759   0.896 for ImageNet-C with corruption strength 5 and 0.911 vs 0.941 for OpenImages. We think that such a drastic
760   difference in OOD detection performance can be caused by the fact that the set of samples selected for disagreement
761   may be suboptimal which makes the confidence threshold (set as 0.2 for this experiment) an important hyperparameter

1086   As we have seen in § A.8 the classification and diversification objectives may clash. Let’s consider the simplified form
1087   of Equation 6 for sample n and model pair (m, l): LHDR = Lmain + αn · Ldiv . When αn > 0, both terms are applied
1088   to the same sample n, leading to potential clash in objectives. By default we control the relative importance of the terms
1089   through αn : for harder samples, we make αn greater, such that the relative weight of the diversification term is greater
1090   and vice versa.
1091   Another option is to control the weights in the "convex sum" way: LHDR = (1 − αn )Lmain + αn Ldiv .

1106   To observe which data points are identified as hard samples by the models and how this is connected to quantitative
1107   performance we conducted a fluctuation analysis for an ensemble at the beginning and at the end of training. For that
1108   reason, we recorded αn (Equation 6) values throughout the training, sorted them according to their magnitude, and
1109   grouped into 100 bins, so that the biggest weights corresponded to bin 0, while the smallest to bin 99. For each bin we
1110   computed the fluctuation ratio, i.e. the ratio of fluctuating samples per bin. We considered a sample as fluctuating if it
1111
       changed relative position with respect to the median weight value among all sample-wise weights for the current epoch.
       For example, if during the current epoch, a sample’s weight is higher than the median value and during the next epoch it
1112
       is lower than the median value, then we call such sample as a fluctuating sample.
1113
1114   The fluctuation analysis revealed that approximately half of the samples fluctuate during the early stages of training
1115   (fluctuation ratio is around 0.5 for all bins during the first epoch in Figure 11) when ensemble is undertrained and its
1116   performance is lower than during the later epochs. As training progresses, the models gradually converge on a more
1117
       stable in comparison to the early stages of training set of hard samples (when comparing the first epoch to the last epoch
       fluctuation ratio drops from 0.5 to 0.3 for the samples from the first 15 bins, i.e. the hardest samples as can be seen in
1118
       Figure 11). In addition to that we notice that average weights magnitude per bin stabilises after a few epochs (when
1119
       comparing the first and the last epochs difference between weights magnitude is between 5 and 0.5 depending on the
1120   bin as shown in Figure 12; when comparing the last and the second last epoch difference is almost zero for all bins as
1121   shown in Figure 13) with samples from bins between 0 and 14 (the hardest samples) having noticeably higher weights
1122   than the samples from the other bins (during the last or the second last epochs weights are between 1 and 5 for them,
1123   while for other bins they are below 1 in Figure 13). The latter is not the case during the first epoch when average weight
1124   magnitude is uniformly low across all the bins (during the first epoch average weight magnitude is around 0.15 for all
1125   the bins in Figure 12).
