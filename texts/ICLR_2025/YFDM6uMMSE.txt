009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Expansive Matching of Experts (EMOE) is a novel method that utilizes support-
012            expanding, extrapolatory pseudo-labeling to improve prediction and uncertainty
013            based rejection on out-of-distribution (OOD) points. We propose an expansive data
014            augmentation technique that generates OOD instances in a latent space, and an
015            empirical trial based approach to filter out augmented expansive points for pseudo-
016            labeling. EMOE utilizes a diverse set of multiple base experts as pseudo-labelers
017            on the augmented data to improve OOD performance through a shared MLP with
018            multiple heads (one per expert). We demonstrate that EMOE achieves superior
019
               performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on both image and tabular data.

107   Domain Generalization Domain generalization (DG) aims to learn a model that is able to gener-
      alize to multiple domains. A typical approach is to learn a domain invariant representation across


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      multiple source domains. Domain invariant representation learning can be done by minimizing
109   variations in feature distributions (Li et al., 2018; Ding et al., 2022) and imposing a regularizer to
110   balance between predictive power and invariance (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Koyama and Yamaguchi,
111   2020). Another line of research incorporates data augmentation to improve generalizability. Basic
112   transformations like rotation and translation, varying in magnitude, are commonly used on images
113   to diversify the training data (Cubuk et al., 2019; Berthelot et al., 2020). More sophisticated aug-
114   mentation techniques have recently surfaced: (Zhang et al., 2018) introduced mixup, which linearly
115   combines two training samples; (Yun et al., 2019) proposed CutMix, blending two images by re-
116   placing a cutout patch with a patch from another image; (Zhong et al., 2022) adversarially augment
117
      images to prevent over fitting to source domains. We focus on augmentations that are general and
      applicable across modalities.
118
119   Self-Training Self-training aims to utilize an earlier model as a pseudo-labeler to populate the
120   training data with more labelled instances by labelling unlabelled data at each iteration. Then, the
121   new labelled set is combined with the previous training set to train a new model. The concept of
122   pseudo-labeling was initially proposed by (Lee, 2013), suggesting a straightforward approach of
123
      retaining instances where the teacher model has high prediction probabilities. Following (Lee, 2013;
      Zou et al., 2018) proposed to use a proportion of the most confident unlabelled data points instead
124
      of a fixed threshold. Researchers then combined curriculum learning with pseudo labeling, where
125
      thresholds for acquiring unlabeled data for each class is dynamically adjusted at different time steps,
126   allowing the most informative unlabeled data to be incorporated (Cascante-Bonilla et al., 2020;
127   Zhang et al., 2021). Another line of research improves robustness of pseudo-labeling by encouraging
128   diversity in the pseudo-labeler. In their work, Ghosh et al. (2021) employed an ensemble of models
129   as teacher to provide pseudo-labels for the student model. On the other hand, (Xie et al., 2019)
130   injected noise into the pseudo-labeler model by incorporating Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) and
131   data augmentation techniques to provide more robust pseudo-labels. In this work we show how to
132   utilize multiple pseudo-labelers on extrapolatory augmented data to improve OOD performance.
133   Selective Classification Reject option methods (also known as selective classification) aim to
134   identify instances where the model should not predict. Many selective classification approaches
135   rely on a post-training processing strategy. Following this strategy, once the model has finished
136   training, a rejection metric is computed. Then, predictions are rejected or accepted based on a
137   predefined threshold. A simple choice for a rejection metric is to utilize the conditional output
138   probability from ML models (Stefano et al., 2000; Fumera et al., 2000). Building upon these works,
139   (Devries and Taylor, 2018) proposed to train a confidence branch alongside of the prediction branch
140
      by incentivizing a neural network to produce a confidence measure during training; Geifman and
      El-Yaniv (2017) proposed a method for constructing a probability-calibrated selective classifier with
141
      guaranteed control over the true risk. Recently, methods adopting end-to-end training approaches
142
      have been proposed (Thulasidasan et al., 2019) (Ziyin et al., 2019) (Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2019). In
143   these works, an extra class is added when predictions are made. If the extra class has the highest class
144   probability for a sample, the sample is rejected. Most reject-option approaches are geared towards
145   in-distribution rejection and utilize novelty-rejection when encountering any OOD points (Torralba
146   and Efros, 2011; Liu et al., 2021; Freiesleben and Grote, 2023); instead, we propose to learn better
147   conditional output probabilities on OOD data for more effective, capability-aware rejection.
148
      Ensemble Modeling Ensemble techniques aim to utilize a diverse set of models jointly for better
149   performance. Early methodologies for ensembles aggregate (bag) predictions from all models
150   (Dietterich, 2007)(Kussul et al., 2010) or a subset of the models in the ensemble (Jordan and Jacobs,
151   1993), (Eigen et al., 2013). In the OOD setting, prior works addressed this problem by enforcing
152   prediction diversity on OOD data (Pagliardini et al., 2023), ensembling moving average models
153   (Arpit et al., 2022a), and training an ensemble of domain specific classifiers (Yao et al., 2023). EMOE
154   adopts a multi-headed architecture that produces an ensemble to improve predictions on OOD data.

200   Recent works have noted that confidence based filtration of pseudo-labels improves self-training
201   techniques (Lee, 2013; Sohn et al., 2020). Here we present a novel, complementary approach to
202   filter out pseudo-labels on expansive augmented data (as above). We wish to filter out expansive
203   augmentations where predicted labels may not be accurate or helpful. Of course, predicting the
204   quality of extrapolatory performance is in itself a difficult task, since we do no have access to any
205   OOD data in our setting. Our approach is based on empirical non-iid held-out trials to judge the
206   difficulty of labeling extrapolatory instances in some direction (in latent space), and keep track of
207
      directions that are easiest to extrapolate to for later augmentation. That is, for a given direction on
      the hyper-sphere v ∈ Ss−1 , we withhold the training instances that have the highest projections
208

286   4      E XPERIMENTS
287
288   We conduct experiments on a varied set of real-world datasets to test the OOD generalizabil-
289   ity of EMOE. We considered the single source domain generalization setting (e.g., (Qiao et al.,
290   2020)), where our model is trained solely on ID data without any (labeled or unlabelled) OOD data
291   during training/validation (e.g., precluding typical semi-supervised approaches), and without any
292   accompanying environmental/domain/source information from ID training instances. Moreover, we
293   note that we avoided utilizing any modality-specific information in EMOE (e.g., we do not utilize
294   any domain specific augmentations) for generality. We utilized XGB Classifiers (Chen and Guestrin,
295
      2016) fitted to random subsets of data instances and features as the base collection of experts. For
      a fair/realistic evaluation, we avoided any hyper-parameter tuning on EMOE and utilized a fixed
296
      architecture of a 2 layer 512 ELU (Clevert et al., 2015) hidden-unit MLP with 1024 linear-output
297
      heads (please see other hyperparameters in Supp. Mat.A.1). For our latent space, we utilize PCA with
298   128 components. While OOD generalization is an active field of research (Freiesleben and Grote,
299   2023; Liu et al., 2021), methodology for general (non-modality specific) single source domain gener-
300   alization is more limited. Given that EMOE integrates numerous techniques, we provide context for
301   our results and compared EMOE with existing strong domain generalization methods that approach
302   the problem from various perspectives and strategies (and are applicable in the single-source setting).
303
      In our experiments, we include two baselines that utilize data augmentation: AdvStyle (Zhong et al.,
304   2022) and Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018), as well as two baselines employing ensemble methods: D-BAT
305   (Pagliardini et al., 2023) and EoA (Arpit et al., 2022a). Mixup linearly combines two ID samples,
306   AdvStyle adversarially augments ID data, D-BAT enforces prediction diversity on OOD data, and EoA
307   ensembles moving average models. For prediction thresholding (rejection), we directly utilize the
308   conditional probability P (Y = 1 | X = x) generated by the models. Many real-world applications
309   (e.g. in drug discovery and virtual screen, see Fig. 2) shall utilize only high-confidence predictions.
310   Thus, alongside AUPRC and AUROC, we paid close attention to high-confidence filtration and
311   reported percent of AUPRC at conservative recall thresholds (e.g., ‘AURPC@R≤.2’). This metric
312
      is computed as the area under the PR-curve up to the specified recall threshold and dividing by the
      maximum possible area for that threshold (i.e., the threshold). We report both base expert (‘EMOE
313
      Base’) and EMOE (eq. 7) ensemble performance. Our implementation shall be open-sourced upon
314
      publication.

503   5   C ONCLUSION
504
505   In summary, this work presents Expansive Matching on Experts (EMOE), a novel method that
506
      utilizes support-expanding, extrapolatory pseudo-labeling to improve prediction and uncertainty
      based rejection on OOD points. Our techniques are general and not specific to any data-modality,
507
      nor do they require additional unlabeled data or domain information. Moreover, they are largely
508
      complementary to other existing approaches. Thus, we envision the potential for future work that
509   incorporates our methodology jointly with other OOD generalization techniques. Moreover, we are
510   encouraged by results in chemical-property prediction and shall further explore future work in these
511   directions.

760
761   A.1   EMOE TRAINING DETAILS
762
763   In all of our experiments we used the Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2014) optimizer and mini-batches of
764   size 256. One Nvidia A100 GPU with 40GB GPU memory was used to run our experiments, and
765   duration for model training is approximately 0.5 hours. During the extrapolatory directional mining
766   trials, we held out 0.1 of the data for each of the 1000 trials. Subsequently, from the top-performing
767   trials, determined by accuracy evaluation, we selected the top held-out points from 0.15 of the
768   best-performing trials for data expansion. λ = 0.5 was used for the Lmatch for the expanded points.
769   As noted in Sec. 3.1 we trained the EMOE models directly in the latent space to avoid the need for the
770   decoder (and also allowed baselines to do this if it aided their performance). In the experiments on
771
      hERF, A549 cells, CYP 2D6, Ames, core ec50, refined ec50, EMOE was trained for 20000 iterations.
      Arithmetic mean between EMOE and EMOE base was reported. On the M/C-D and M/F-D datasets,
772
      we took the raw pixel values as input and trained EMOE networks for 4000 iterations due to lower
773
      observed losses. Harmonic mean between EMOE and EMOE base was reported on these datasets.
774   We performed 8 trails on each of the datasets for EMOE.
775
776
777   A.2   BASELINE S ETUP
778
779   We re-implemented all baselines we are comparing against EMOE following the implementation
780   details in their paper and/or using Github implementations (if available). Since the fingerprints
781   representation of chemicals are quite sparse, we preformed dimension reduction using PCA with
782
      128 components on all chemical datasets. For D-BAT(Pagliardini et al., 2023) with existing im-
      plementations designed for tabular data, we utilized their original model architectures. For the
783
      other three baseline methods without implementation specifically for tabular data, we adopted a
784
      structure comprising two 512 ELU(Clevert et al., 2015) layers to closely mimic the EMOE network
785   architecture. The Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2014) optimizer was used for training baseline models.
786
787   D-BAT In our experiments, the D-Bat(Pagliardini et al., 2023) models used MLP architecture with one
788
      128 LeakyRelu(Maas, 2013) layer following the architecture in their Github. Their paper (Pagliardini
      et al., 2023) discussed two settings, and we focused on the scenario where perturbation data differs
789
      from the distribution of test data, adhering to the single-source domain generalization setting. We
790
      trained an ensemble of five models sequentially for the D-bat baseline models and the predictions
791   from the 5 models were averaged to obtain the final prediction.
792
793   EoA We trained an ensemble of 5 simple moving average model following the method described in
794
      (Arpit et al., 2022a). We start calculating the moving average at iteration 50 and trained the models
      for 200 iterations. The predictions from the 5 models were averaged to obtain the final prediction for
795
      EoA.
796
797   For AdvStyle (Zhong et al., 2022) and Mixup(Zhang et al., 2018), the methodologies were straight-
798   forward. We experimented with training using various numbers of iterations and reported the
799   most promising results. Note that we used alpha=0.7 when combining the 2 samples for Mixup.
800
      We executed all baseline experiments five times on each dataset to ensure a precise estimation of
      performance.

849   In this ablation study, we focused on examining the impact of the vanilla vs. Mixture of Experts
850   (MoE) architecture on OOD performance. Both the MoE MLP and Vanilla MLP were configured
851   with two layers of 512 ELU units. The MoE MLP has 1024 output heads, whereas the vanilla MLP
852   had only one. For noisy augmentation, we applied small perturbations drawn from a standard normal
853   distribution to the training set while retaining the original labels. We run 3 trials on each of the
854   CheMBL dataset and the results are shown in Table 10 . Although we observed that the MoE model
855
      achieved a AUPRC compared to the vanilla MLP, we found that simple noisy augmentation did not
      lead to any significant difference in performance.
