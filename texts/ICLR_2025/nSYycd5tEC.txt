011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Replay-based methods have shown superior performance to address catastrophic
014            forgetting in continual learning (CL), where a subset of past data is stored and
015
               generally replayed together with new data in current task learning. While seem-
               ingly natural, it is questionable, though rarely questioned, if such a concurrent
016
               replay strategy is always the right way for replay in CL. Inspired by the fact in
017
               human learning that revisiting very different courses sequentially before final ex-
018            ams is more effective for students, an interesting open question to ask is whether a
019            sequential replay can benefit CL more compared to a standard concurrent replay.
020            However, answering this question is highly nontrivial considering a major lack of
021            theoretical understanding in replay-based CL methods. To this end, we investigate
022            CL in overparameterized linear models and provide a comprehensive theoretical
023            analysis to compare two replay schemes: 1) Concurrent Replay, where the model
024            is trained on replay data and new data concurrently; 2) Sequential Replay, where
025            the model is trained first on new data and then sequentially on replay data for
026
               each old task. By characterizing the explicit form of forgetting and generalization
               error, we show in theory that sequential replay tends to outperform concurrent re-
027
               play when tasks are less similar, which is corroborated by our simulations in linear
028
               models. More importantly, our results inspire a novel design of a hybrid replay
029            method, where only replay data of similar tasks are used concurrently with the
030            current data and dissimilar tasks are sequentially revisited using their replay data.
031            As depicted in our experiments on real datasets using deep neural networks, such
032            a hybrid replay method improves the performance of standard concurrent replay
033            by leveraging sequential replay for dissimilar tasks. By providing the first com-
034            prehensive theoretical analysis on replay, our work has great potentials to open up
035            more principled designs for replay-based CL.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Continual learning (CL) (Parisi et al., 2019) seeks to build an agent that can learn a sequence of
040
      tasks continuously without access to old task data, resembling humanвҖҷs capability of lifelong learn-
041
      ing. One of the major challenges therein is the so-called catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al.,
042   2017), i.e., the agent can easily forget the knowledge of old tasks when learning new tasks. A large
043   amount of studies have been proposed to address this issue, among which replay-based approaches
044   (Rolnick et al., 2019) have demonstrated the state-of-the-art performance. The main idea behind is
045   to store a subset of old task data in the memory and replay them when learning new tasks, where a
046   widely adopted strategy for training is concurrent replay (Evron et al., 2024), i.e., train the model
047   concurrently on new task data and the replay data.
048   While the concurrent replay strategy seems very natural and has shown successful performance to
049   address catastrophic forgetting, it is indeed questionable whether this strategy is always the right
050   way for replay in CL as we consider the following aspects. 1) From the perspective of human
051   learning. In daily life, a common strategy to prevent forgetting is to review old knowledge. For
052   example, suppose a student needs to learn a series of topics over a semester before taking an exam,
053   and each topic corresponds to one task in CL. Intuitively, if these topics are highly related to each
      other, incorporating the knowledge of old topics into learning a new topic can be an effective strategy


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to strengthen the new learning and simultaneously reduce the forgetting of old knowledge, which is
055   analogous to concurrent replay. However, if the topics are very different from each other, a common
056   practice that a student often takes is to learn new topics first and then go over old topics to mitigate
057   the forgetting. Here, such a sequential replay may lead to better outcome in the exam. 2) From the
058   perspective of multi-task learning. Learning multiple tasks all at once may lead to poor learning
059   performance due to the potential interference among gradients of different tasks Yu et al. (2020),
060   whereas standard CL without regularization and replay may even achieve less forgetting for more
061   dissimilar tasks Lin et al. (2023). Thus motivated, an interesting and open question to ask is:
062   Question: Whether sequential replay will serve as an appealing replay strategy to complement the
063   standard concurrent replay, and when will it be advantageous over concurrent replay for CL?
064
      To answer this question from a theoretical perspective, we study replay-based CL through the lens of
065
      overparameterized linear models to gain useful insights, by following a recent series of theoretical
066
      studies in CL (Lin et al., 2023; Evron et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). However, none
067   of those previous studies analyzed the replay-based methods. The only theoretical work that studied
068   the replay-based methods is the recent concurrent work (Banayeeanzade et al., 2024). But this work
069   considered only the standard concurrent replay method, not from the new perspective of sequential
070   replay.
071
      In this work, to capture the idea and advantage of sequential replay, we propose a novel replay
072
      strategy, in which the agent sequentially revisits each old task and trains the model with the corre-
073   sponding replay data after the current task is well learned.
074
075   Main Contributions. We summarize our main contributions as follows.
076
          вҖў First of all, we provide the first explicit closed-form expressions for the expected value of
077         forgetting and generalization error for both concurrent replay strategy and sequential replay
078         strategy under an overparameterized linear regression setting. Note that the blending of samples
079         from old tasks in concurrent replay introduces significant intricacies related to task correlation
080         in theoretical analysis. To address this challenge, we partition training data into blocks based on
081         different tasks, which enables us to further calculate the task interference using the properties of
082         block matrix. In particular, our theoretical results demonstrate how the performance of replay-
083         based CL is affected by various factors, including task similarity and memory size.
084       вҖў Secondly, we propose a novel replay strategy, i.e., sequential replay, to sequentially revisit old
085         tasks after the current task is fully learned. By characterizing the explicit closed-form expres-
086         sions for the expected forgetting and generalization error for sequential replay and comparing
087         with the concurrent replay, we give an affirmative answer to the open question above. More
088         importantly, we rigorously characterize the conditions when sequential replay can benefit CL
089         more than concurrent replay, in terms of both forgetting and generalization error, which is also
090         consistent with our motivations above: Sequential replay outperforms concurrent replay if tasks
091
            in CL are dissimilar, and the performance improvement is larger when the tasks are more dis-
            similar. Numerical simulations on linear models further corroborate our theoretical results.
092
093       вҖў Last but not least, our theoretical insights can indeed go beyond the linear models and guide
094         the practical algorithm design for replay-based CL with deep neural networks (DNNs). More
095         specifically, we merge the idea of sequential replay into standard replay-based CL with concur-
096         rent replay, leading to a hybrid replay approach where 1) old tasks dissimilar to the current task
097
            will be revisited by using sequential replay (guided by our theory that suggests more benefit
            if dissimilar tasks are revisited sequentially) and 2) the replay data for the remaining old tasks
098
            (that are sufficiently similar to the current task) will still be used concurrently with current task
099
            data. Our experiments on real datasets with DNNs verify that our hybrid approach can perform
100         better than concurrent replay and the advantage is more apparent when tasks are less similar.

103   2     R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Empirical studies in CL. CL has drawn significant attention in recent years, with numerous em-
106   pirical approaches developed to mitigate the issue of catastrophic forgetting. Architecture-based ap-
107   proaches combat catastrophic forgetting by dynamically adjusting network parameters (Rusu et al.,
      2016) or introducing architectural adaptations such as an ensemble of experts (RypesМҒcМҒ et al., 2024).


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Regularization-based methods constrain model parameter updates to preserve the knowledge of pre-
109   vious tasks (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Magistri et al., 2024). Memory-based methods address forget-
110   ting by storing some information of old tasks in the memory and leveraging the information during
111   current task learning, which can be further divided into orthogonal projection based methods and
112   replay-based methods. The former stores gradient information of old tasks and uses this to mod-
113   ify the optimization space for the current task (Saha et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022), while the latter
114   stores and reuses a tiny subset of representative data, known as exemplars. Critical design consider-
115   ations in empirical replay-based methods mainly include varying exemplar sampling and utilization
116   schemes. Exemplar sampling methods involve reservoir sampling (Chrysakis & Moens, 2020) and
117
      an information-theoretic evaluation of exemplar candidates (Sun et al., 2022). Some other work
      such as Shin et al. (2017) retains past knowledge by replaying вҖқpseudo-dataвҖқ constructed from input
118
      data instead of storing raw input. Replay methods mostly assume a concurrent training scheme that
119
      trains the model using a mix of input data and sampled exemplars (Dokania et al., 2019; Rebuffi
120   et al., 2017; Garg et al., 2024). Other exemplar utilization methods include Lopez-Paz & Ranzato
121   (2017) and Chaudhry et al. (2018), which use exemplar to impose constraints in the gradient space.
122
123
      Theoretical studies in CL. Compared to the vast amount of empirical studies in CL, the theoretical
      understanding of CL is very limited but has started to attract much attention very recently. Bennani
124
      et al. (2020); Doan et al. (2021) investigated CL performance for the orthogonal gradient descent
125
      approach in NTK models theoretically. Yin et al. (2020) focused on regularization-based methods
126   and proposed a framework, which requires second-order information to approximate loss function.
127   Cao et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022) characterized the benefits of continual representation learning from
128   a theoretical perspective. Evron et al. (2023) connected regularization-based methods with Projec-
129   tion Onto Convex Sets. Recently, a series of theoretical studies proposed to leverage the tools of
130   overparameterized linear models to facilitate better understanding of CL. Evron et al. (2022) studied
131   the performance of forgetting under such a setup. After that, Lin et al. (2023) characterized the
132   performance of CL in a more comprehensive way, where they discuss the impact of task similarities
133   and the task order. Goldfarb & Hand (2023) illustrated the joint effect of task similarity and over-
134
      parameterization. Zhao et al. (2024) provided a statistical analysis of regularization-based methods.
      More recently, Li et al. (2024) further theoretically investigated the impact of mixture-of-experts on
135
      the performance of CL in linear models.
136
137   Different from all the previous studies, we seek to fill up the theoretical understanding for replay-
138   based CL. Note that one concurrent study Banayeeanzade et al. (2024) also investigates replay-based
139   CL in overparameterized linear models with concurrent replay. However, one key difference here is
140
      that we propose a novel replay strategy, i.e., the sequential replay, and theoretically show its benefit
      over concurrent replay for dissimilar tasks. Our theoretical results further motivate a new algorithm
141
      design for CL in practice, which demonstrates promising performance on DNNs.

      7   C ONCLUSION
525
526
527   In this work, we took a closer look at the replay strategy in replay-based CL and questioned the effec-
528   tiveness of the widely used training technique, i.e., concurrent replay, as inspired by human learning.
529   In particular, we proposed a novel replay strategy, namely sequential replay, which replays old tasks
530   in the memory sequentially after current task learning. By leveraging overparameterized linear mod-
531
      els with equal memory allocation, we provided the first explicit expressions of the expected value of
      both forgetting and generalization errors under two replay methods, concurrent replay and sequential
532
      replay. Comparisons between their theoretical performance led to the insight that sequential replay
533
      outperforms concurrent replay in terms of forgetting and generalization error when the tasks are less
534   similar, which is consistent with our motivations from human learning and multitask learning. Our
535   simulation results on linear models further corroborated the correctness of our theoretical results.
536   More importantly, based on our theory, we proposed a novel hybrid replay framework for practical
537   CL and experiments on CIFAR100 with DNNs verified the superior performance of this framework
538   over concurrent replay. To the best of our knowledge, our work provides the first comprehensive the-
539   oretical study on replay for replay-based CL, which will hopefully motivate more principled designs
      for better replay-based CL.

2399
2400   Dataset. We evaluate our Hybrid Replay on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al. (2009)), a real-world
2401   dataset for image classification. ItвҖҷs composed of a total of 100 different classes, each containing 500
2402   non-overlapping training images and 100 testing images. In line with prior works Guo et al. (2022)
2403
       and Sun et al. (2022), we randomly split the original dataset into 10 tasks under a task-incremental
       setup, each containing 10 non-overlapping classes.
2404
2405   Implementation Details. For training on CIFAR-100, we employ a non-pretrained ResNet-18 as
2406   our DNN backbone. Following Van de Ven et al. (2022), we adopt a multi-headed output layer such
2407   that each task is assigned its own output layer, consistent with the typical Task Incremental CL setup.
2408   During supervised training, we explicitly provide the task identifier (ranging from 0 to 9) alongside
2409
       the image-label pairs as additional input to the model. For simplicity, we use a reservoir sampling
       strategy to construct the replay buffer. Our replay buffer size is 50 per class. Other than the image
2410
       corruption, we didnвҖҷt apply any data augmentation prior to training.
2411
2412   For all experiments on Concurrent Replay, we use the SGD (Stochastic Gradient Descent) optimizer
2413   for 30 epochs per task, with a minibatch size of 128, momentum of 0.9, weight decay of 1eвҲ’4 , and
2414   an initial learning rate of 0.05 that is reduced by a factor of 0.1 after 20 epochs.
2415   For all experiments on Sequential Replay, we use the SGD optimizer for 30 epochs per task, with a
2416   minibatch size of 64, momentum of 0.9, weight decay of 1eвҲ’3 , and an initial learning rate of 0.001
2417   that is reduced by a factor of 0.1 after each 12 epochs. We slightly adjust these training parameters
2418   for hybrid training due to the relatively smaller number of trained images which increases the risk
2419   of overfitting.
2420   Task Corruption. For experiments described in Section 6.2, we control the similarity level of the
2421   dataset by applying data corruption to different number of tasks. We provide a list of sample images
2422   under different image corruption schemes in fig. 3. For the scenario вҖқOriginal DatasetвҖқ, we donвҖҷt
2423   apply any image corruption. For the scenario вҖқ1 CorruptionвҖқ, we apply the Glass corruption on T1 .
2424   For the scenario вҖқ2 CorruptionвҖқ, we apply Glass corruption onT1 , and rotational color swaping on
2425   T2 . For the scenario вҖқ3 CorruptionвҖқ, we apply Glass corruption onT1 , rotational color swaping on
2426   T3 , and elastic pixelation on T5 .
