009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            As a biologically inspired computing paradigm, Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs)
012            process information through discrete spike sequences, mimicking the brain’s tem-
013            poral dynamics and energy efficiency. The combination of backpropagation
014            through time (BPTT) and direct input encoding (i.e., feeding decimal data di-
015            rectly into the network) has emerged as the mainstream training approach for
016
               SNNs. However, this combination introduces varying temporal dependency re-
               quirements across the network’s spatial dimension. These differences are often
017
               neglected in existing studies, which typically apply uniform temporal dependency
018
               configurations throughout the network. Consequently, this could result in missing
019            key gradients or introducing redundant ones in the temporal dimension, ultimately
020            affecting the network’s performance. To address this gap, we propose a novel
021            Spatio-Temporal Dependency-Aware Neuron Optimization (ST-DANO) method
022            for SNNs, which consists of two key components: neuron design and neuron
023            search. Specifically, to overcome the limitations of traditional Leaky Integrate-
024            and-Fire (LIF) neurons in adapting to varying temporal dependencies, we de-
025            signed two variants, Long-LIF and Short-LIF, which improve the neuron’s ability
026            to capture long-term and short-term dependencies, respectively, by dynamic mod-
027
               ulation of membrane potential thresholds and time constants. After validating our
               neuron designs through ablation studies, we developed a layer-wise neuron search
028
               strategy that automatically selects the optimal neuron type for each layer to en-
029
               sure optimal temporal dependency configurations across the network. Extensive
030            experiments on static and neuromorphic datasets demonstrate that ST-DANO can
031            effectively adapt to temporal dependency differences across the spatial dimension
032            in SNNs under various time-step configurations. The resulting architectures sur-
033            pass state-of-the-art performance, achieving a remarkable 83.90% accuracy on the
034            DVS-CIFAR-10 dataset—a more than 5% improvement over the baseline.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) diverge from traditional paradigms by processing information
      through discrete spikes, enabling them to capture the temporal dynamics while significantly reduc-
040
      ing energy consumption (Ding et al., 2022). While conventional Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs)
041
      merely process static data in a single flow, SNNs handle information through binary spike sequences
042   and integrate temporal features across multiple time steps. This distinctive processing method en-
043   dows SNNs with the ability to handle complex temporal patterns; however, it complicates the use of
044   backpropagation, rendering conventional training methods inapplicable (Neftci et al., 2019).
045
      To address this challenge, Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) with surrogate gradients was in-
046
      troduced (Wu et al., 2018). BPTT is a spatio-temporal propagation method that unfolds the network
047
      across both spatial and temporal dimensions, with gradients propagating from the output layer to the
048   input layer but also recursively accumulating along the time axis (Meng et al., 2023). Building on
049   BPTT, Rueckauer et al., 2017 proposes direct input encoding to feed decimal data directly into the
050   network. Compared to traditional Poisson (Heeger et al., 2000) and temporal encodings (Mostafa,
051   2017), direct input encoding significantly reduces the number of time steps required for information
052   representation, lowering both training and inference costs. This efficiency gain has made the combi-
053   nation of BPTT and direct input encoding one of the mainstream training methods in recent research
      (Deng et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a).


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      However, this combination introduces varying requirements for temporal dependencies across the
055   network spatial dimension—an aspect overlooked by existing research. This phenomenon stems
056   from two key factors. First, BPTT operates by updating network weights only after all time steps
057   have been processed, keeping the weights constant throughout the entire forward pass (Dampfhoffer
058   et al., 2023). Second, in direct input encoding, the inputs to the network remain constant at each
059   time step. These two constant conditions cause shallow1 neurons to receive nearly identical input
060   across time steps, resulting in diminished temporal dynamics. As the network deepens, the influence
061   of these constant conditions weakens due to the increasing introduction of continuous-to-discrete
062   transformations and nonlinear operations. As a result, deeper neurons are tasked with processing
063
      more abstract temporal features, thereby exhibiting much stronger temporal dynamics. This spatial
      variation in temporal dynamics indicates that neurons at different layers of the network have varying
064
      requirements for temporal dependencies. However, existing research based on BPTT and direct
065
      input encoding often employs the same type of neuron throughout the entire network, assuming that
066   temporal dependency needs of neurons at all layers are identical, overlooking these critical varying
067   requirements. This approach may lead to the loss of key gradients or the introduction of redundant
068   gradients across the temporal dimension, ultimately constraining network performance.
069
      To address this limitation, we propose a Spatio-Temporal Dependency-Aware Neuron Optimization
070
      (ST-DANO) method for SNNs, which optimally adapts temporal dependencies along the spatial di-
071
      mension. ST-DANO consists of two key modules, neuron design and neuron search. Specifically,
072   to address the limitations of traditional Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neurons in adapting variable
073   temporal dependencies, we designed two novel neurons: Long-LIF and Short-LIF, which leverage
074   dynamic modulation of membrane potential thresholds and time constants to adaptively capture and
075   flexibly model long-term and short-term dependencies, respectively. Through ablation studies, we
076   validate the effectiveness of designed neuron variants and investigate how their configurations at
077   different network layers affect overall performance. Next, we propose a layer-wise neuron search
078   strategy that selects optimal neuron types at different network depths, ensuring that temporal de-
079   pendencies are effectively adapted along the spatial dimension. Extensive experiments on static
080
      and neuromorphic datasets demonstrate that the architectures with optimized neuron configurations
      outperform state-of-the-art models. Remarkably, ST-DANO achieves a top accuracy of 60.24% on
081
      Tiny-ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), while delivering a significant improvement of more than 5%
082
      over the baseline on DVS-CIFAR-10 (Amir et al., 2017).

085   2       R ELATED W ORK
086
087   Training method of SNNs. There are two mainstream training methods for SNNs: ANN-to-SNN
088   (A2S) conversion and direct training. A2S involves training the network using traditional backprop-
089   agation on an ANN, then converting it to an SNN by applying techniques such as activation function
090   mapping or scaling factors (Fang et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2021a; Bu et al., 2023). However, since the
091   training process mirrors that of an ANN, A2S struggles to effectively capture the temporal dynam-
092
      ics inherent in time-series data, limiting its compatibility with neuromorphic datasets (Deng et al.,
      2020). Direct training methods can be divided into two subcategories: Spike-timing-dependent plas-
093
      ticity (STDP) and backpropagation-based training. As an unsupervised learning method, STDP does
094
      not rely on large amounts of labeled data for learning, but its performance is significantly lower than
095   backpropagation-based supervised learning methods (Dong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2018). Among
096   backpropagation methods, BPTT with surrogate gradients (SG) dominates, as numerous studies have
097   demonstrated its ability to effectively train high-performance SNNs on both static and neuromorphic
098   datasets (Deng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b). Based on BPTT, various techniques have been pro-
099   posed to further enhance network performance, such as efficient spatial learning (Meng et al., 2023),
100   normalization methods (Jiang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023b), and extra learnable parameters (Sun
101   et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2021b).
102   Temporal dependency of SNNs. Most existing studies on the temporal dependencies of SNNs
103   treat the network as a whole, emphasizing the overall enhancement of long-term dependencies on
104   external inputs. For instance, Lotfi Rezaabad & Vishwanath, 2020 integrates LSTM with SNNs and
105   proposed an error backpropagation method to improve the ability of SNNs to learn long-term de-
106   pendencies. Stan & Rhodes, 2024 introduces a state space model to SNNs, allowing them to learn

      In this section, we present our ST-DANO. The implementation of ST-DANO has two parts: neuron
165
      design and neuron search. Specifically, we design two LIF neuron variants, Long-LIF (LLIF) and
166   Short-LIF (SLIF), to adaptively enhance neurons’ long-term and short-term dependencies, respec-
167   tively. We then propose a layer-wise neuron search strategy based on LIF, LLIF, and SLIF neurons,
168   which selects the most suitable neuron type for each layer in the SNN architecture.

      4.1         N EURON D ESIGN
171
172   To enhance long-term and short-term dependencies in LIF neurons, we approached the design from
173   the perspective of gradient contributions. Following the BPTT process, gradients are accumulated
174   after a total of T time steps. For neurons designed to enhance long-term dependencies, the propor-
175   tion of gradient contributions from early time steps2 must increase relative to the overall gradient.
176   Conversely, for neurons emphasizing short-term dependencies, the gradient contributions from re-
177   cent time steps should have a stronger impact. According to the gradient Eq. (4), three key factors
178
      influence the gradient: the membrane potential at each time step, the membrane potential threshold
      Vth , and the membrane time constant τ . Since the membrane potential is part of the data flow and not
179
      suitable for direct modification, we focus on adjusting the adaptive Vth and τ to influence gradient
180
      contributions, thereby reinforcing neurons’ short-term and long-term dependencies.
181
182   Theoretically, a higher Vth requires the neuron to accumulate more input before firing, meaning the
183   neuron does not immediately respond to each time step but rather reacts to information accumulated
184
      over a longer period. This enables the neuron to integrate context across multiple time steps, thereby
      improving its capacity to capture long-term dependencies. Conversely, a lower Vth increases the neu-
185
      ron’s sensitivity to short-term dependencies. As for the membrane time constant τ , a larger value
186
      causes the membrane potential to decay more slowly over time, allowing the neuron to retain infor-
187   mation from several past time steps, which also aids in capturing long-term dependencies. On the
188   other hand, a smaller τ enhances the neuron’s focus on short-term dependencies by limiting the re-
189   tention of past information. From the perspective of firing rate, we enhance long-term dependencies
190   by adaptively increasing the membrane potential threshold and strengthen short-term dependencies
191   by reducing the membrane time constant. Both strategies work to suppress spike firing, which not
192   only improves network performance but also reduces energy consumption.

      Although it may seem intuitive to optimize the network by placing SLIF neurons in shallow layers
263
      and LLIF neurons in deep layers based on the dynamics of dependency and neuron design dis-
264   cussed earlier, the reality is far more complex. Due to the intricate connection structures in modern
265   network architectures, such as skip connections (He et al., 2016), the temporal dependency varies
266   non-linearly along the spatial dimension. Moreover, the spiking neuron layers’ requirements for
267   temporal dependency differ depending on the task type—for example, static datasets versus neuro-
268   morphic datasets have inherently distinct needs, which is intuitive but challenging to address manu-
269   ally. In this section, we demonstrate through ablation experiments that manual placement of neurons
      is suboptimal, which motivates the development of our neuron search strategy.

      would release the potential of design neurons, optimizing both model performance and stability.

435   5.1   C OMPARISON WITH S TATE - OF - THE - ART A PPROACHES
436
437   To demonstrate the effectiveness of ST-DANO in enhancing the performance of SNNs, we conduct
438   experiments on static datasets CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al.,
439
      2009), and Tiny-ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), as well as neuromorphic datasets DVS-CIFAR-10
      (Li et al., 2017) and DVS-Gesture (Amir et al., 2017). The results are then analyzed and compared
440
      against the state-of-the-art methods. Detailed descriptions and process methods of the datasets are
441
      provided in Appendix E. The training process of ST-DANO across all datasets is divided into two
442   phases: searching and retraining. In the first phase, the optimal neuron configuration is searched
443   based on ST-DANO, while in the later one, the network with optimal neuron configuration will
444   be retrained. The complete and detailed implementation of both phases is provided in Appendix
445   F. Additionally, to thoroughly evaluate the performance of ST-DANO, we search for the optimal
446   neuron configurations corresponding to different time-step settings across participated datasets.
447
      Static Datasets. As shown in Table 2, ST-DANO achieves the best performance on the CIFAR-10
448   and Tiny-ImageNet datasets among the three static datasets, with accuracies of 95.92% and 60.24%,
449   respectively. Although the Joint A-SNN (Guo et al., 2023a) outperforms in CIFAR-100 with an
450   accuracy of 77.39%, our method still surpasses other surrogate gradient-based learning methods in
451   this dataset. An interesting observation is that on CIFAR-10, our method performs slightly better
452   with a time step setting of 4 compared to that of 6. This suggests that when ST-DANO searches for
453   the neuron configuration on the CIFAR-10 dataset with a time step setting of 6, it finds a suboptimal
454   rather than the desired optimal configuration. Despite this, our method still significantly outperforms
455   other approaches, underscoring its tremendous advantages.
456   Neuromorphic Datasets. On the DVS-CIFAR-10 dataset, our method achieves the best accuracy
457   of 79.30% without data augmentation, and further improves to 83.90% with augmentation applied.
458   On DVS-Gesture, ST-DANO achieves an accuracy of 98.61%, only slightly below that of SLTT
459   (Meng et al., 2023) (98.62%). Overall, our method demonstrates equally strong performance on
460   challenging neuromorphic datasets as it does on static datasets.

      5.2   O PTIMAL N EURON C ONFIGURATIONS
464
465
      Table 3 provides the optimal neuron configurations obtained by ST-DANO and the search cost, with
      the corresponding architecture parameters visualized in Appendix H. Notably, on the CIFAR-10 and
466
      CIFAR-100 datasets, the first and fifth layers of the shallow network tend to use SLIF, while the
467
      deeper layers, especially the last four, prefer LLIF. This observation aligns with our original analy-
468   sis in designing ST-DANO: shallow layers prefer short-term dependencies, while deep layers prefer
469   long-term dependencies. Meanwhile, it also confirms that the network’s temporal dependency re-
470   quirements are not linear. For example, in the optimal neuron configurations of CIFAR-10 and
471   CIFAR-100, shallow layers 2, 3, and 4 do not favor LLIF but instead use standard LIF. For neuro-
472   morphic datasets, they predominantly favor long-term dependency neurons. This is because, unlike
473   static datasets, the input data in neuromorphic datasets is inherently dynamic and discrete time-series
474   data, requiring the overall network to integrate information over longer time ranges. Interestingly,
475   the optimal neuron configuration for DVS-CIFAR-10 with a time step of 10 is coincidentally identi-
476
      cal to the LLIF-4 in VGG-11 we manually set in Section 4.2.
477   From the heatmaps of the architecture parameters provided in Table 3, it can be observed that on
478   static datasets, as the network deepens, the architecture parameters of SLIF neurons show a down-
479   ward trend, while those of LLIF neurons increase. However, on neuromorphic datasets such as
480   DVG-Gesture, LLIF neurons dominate across all layers of the network. These findings are consis-
481   tent with our previous analysis of temporal dependencies.
482   Furthermore, in Appendix I, we provide a comparison of the spike firing rates during the infer-
483   ence phase between the optimal neuron configuration found by our method and the conventional
484   LIF network across all cases. The results demonstrate that our approach not only improves net-
485   work performance but also effectively suppresses spike activity, thereby reducing inference energy
      consumption, making it more favorable for real-world deployment.

      6           C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   The BPTT method has streamlined the training of SNNs, while direct input encoding dramatically
529   cuts down the number of time steps needed. Together, these two approaches have drastically en-
530   hanced training efficiency and effectiveness, establishing themselves as the dominant framework in
531
      SNN research. However, the variation in neurons’ temporal dependency requirements across the
      spatial dimension, introduced by this combination, poses a significant barrier to unlocking the full
532
      performance potential of SNNs. To address this issue, we propose a Spatio-Temporal Dependency-
533
      Aware Neuron Optimization (ST-DANO) method to find the optimal dependency configuration for
534   the network architecture. Specifically, we first design two variants of LIF neurons: LLIF and SLIF,
535   which are capable of enhancing neurons’ ability to capture long-term and short-term dependencies,
536   respectively. Building on this, we design a layer-wise neuron search strategy based on DARTS,
537   which utilizes a differentiable search process to find the optimal temporal dependency configuration
538   for each spiking neuron layer, enabling adaptive temporal dependency along the spatial dimension
539   in SNNs. Finally, extensive experiments on static and neuromorphic datasets demonstrate the supe-
      riority of our method.

1153   E    DATASETS
1154
1155   CIFAR-10. The CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 60,000 color images, each
1156   of size 32×32 pixels, divided into 10 different classes, such as airplanes, cars, birds, cats, and
1157   dogs. Each class has 6,000 images, with 50,000 images used for training and 10,000 for testing.
1158   Normalization, random horizontal flipping, random cropping with 4 padding, and CutOut (DeVries
1159   & Taylor, 2017) are applied for data augmentation.
1160   CIFAR-100. The CIFAR-100 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 60,000 color images, each
1161   of size 32×32 pixels, categorized into 100 different classes. Each class contains 600 images, with
1162   500 used for training and 100 for testing. The same processing methods as for dataset CIFAR-10
1163   are applied to dataset CIFAR-100.
1164
       Tiny-ImageNet. The Tiny-ImageNet dataset is a scaled-down version of the ImageNet dataset
1165
       (Deng et al., 2009). It contains 200 different classes, with 500 training images and 50 testing images
1166   per class, resulting in a total of 100,000 training images and 10,000 testing images. Each image is
1167   resized to 64×64 pixels. Normalization, random horizontal flipping, and random cropping with 4
1168   padding are applied for data augmentation for the Tiny-ImageNet dataset.
1169
       DVS-CIFAR-10. The DVS-CIFAR-10 dataset (Li et al., 2017) is a neuromorphic version of the
1170
       traditional CIFAR-10 dataset. DVS-CIFAR-10 captures the visual information using a Dynamic
1171
       Vision Sensor (DVS), which records changes in the scene as a series of asynchronous events rather
1172   than as a sequence of frames. The dataset consists of recordings of 10 object classes, corresponding
1173   to the original CIFAR-10 categories, with each object presented in front of a DVS camera under
1174   various conditions. The dataset contains 10,000 128×128 images, of which 9,000 are used as the
1175   training set and the remaining 1,000 as the test set.
1176
       DVS-Gesture. The DVS-Gesture dataset (Amir et al., 2017) is a neuromorphic dataset, consisting
1177
       of 11 different hand gesture classes, such as hand clapping, arm rolling, and air guitar, performed by
1178   29 subjects under various lighting conditions. Each gesture is represented by a sequence of events
1179   rather than frames. The dataset contains 1,176 training samples and 288 testing samples.
