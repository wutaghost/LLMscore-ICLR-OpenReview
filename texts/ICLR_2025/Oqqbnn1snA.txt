009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning is a distributed approach to training a global model over multiple
012            clients without sharing their local data. In graph data, the data heterogeneity can
013            correspond to subgraph structures and node features varying extremely different,
014            and the model isolation to federated graph task corresponds to exclusive schema
015            on handing data for specific task in clients, e.g., anomaly user setting in Twitter is
016            rather different from LinkedIn. Although most federated graph learning approaches
017            are employed to address the data heterogeneity challenge, we find that the model
018
               isolation to federated graph task challenge has been overlooked. This model
               isolation to federated graph task will prevent existing models into the federated
019
               graph learning framework. In this paper, we propose F E D G R A P H : a new paradigm
020
               for federated graph learning. The key idea is to utilize the graph structure without
021            private node features as structure knowledge bridging all task specific knowledge in
022            clients. Our extensive experiments show that F E D G R A P H significantly outperforms
023            the other state-of-the-art federated learning algorithms on anomaly detection tasks.
024            Two deep learning models and one existing anomaly subgraph detection model are
025            transferred to F E D G R A P H framework.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph applications are becoming increasingly prevalent in social activities, such as account recom-
031   mendation, hot event detection, and short video ranking on Twitter, LinkedIn, and TikTok. Most
032   applications developed by these companies deliver personalized content to users based on users’
033
      personalized interests inferred from private data. In one social network service (e.g., Twitter), when
      users exhibit similar behaviors, such as focusing on "US election" tweets, they tend to fall into a simi-
034
      lar interest distribution. The extra data from Weibo also fit the personal interest distribution in detail.
035
      Moreover, the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) regulates the collection, storage, and
036   processing of personal data, and has an effect on most major companies worldwide Gruschka et al.
037   (2018).
038
039
      However, an important challenge raised from graph data heterogeneity is tackled by recent Federated
      Graph Learning (FGL) methods Baek et al. (2023) from the personalized parameter perspective. In
040
      graph data, the number of users features exhibit extremely non-independent and identically (non-IID)
041
      properties, and the graph structure also exhibits more heterogeneity. In the federated learning (FL)
042   setting, most Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) consider the structure embedding, and the structure
043   heterogeneity is tackled by the personalized weighted parameters of the deep models Baek et al.
044   (2023); Yang et al. (2021). However, as the size of the graph increases, GNN-based models incur
045   significant communication costs. Additionally, traditional non-GNN models cannot be directly used
046   for federated learning.
047
      Moreover, a significant challenge, model isolation to federated graph task, that has been overlooked
048   by the existing FL methods since graph learning models can be easily adapted to different graph
049   structures. The isolation of graph data and computation-specific algorithms has resulted in a gap in
050   performing federated tasks across multiple graph data, referred to as model isolation to federated
051   graph task. In Figure 1, data usually exist in the form of isolated islands Yang et al. (2019) in
052   most industries, and the task of detecting trending events on platforms such as Twitter and Weibo
053   is straightforward, but the specific knowledge of hot events (significant structure of the community
      and users’ features) is often achieved within black boxes to protect algorithm execution processes.

071   In traditional federated graph learning, multiple tasks cannot be performed collaboratively if graph
072   data and algorithm are both private. Graph data can be categorized into structures and features. We
073   protect graph features and algorithm execution processes, but the graph structure can be shared. So
074   federated graph learning tasks are aligned on the graph structures.
075
      Motivated by these challenges, we propose a new paradigm for federated graph learning that treats the
076
      graph structure as a bridge to private node features (in this paradigm, ’knowledge’ refers to subgraphs
077   mined by algorithms). For instance, a data owner (e.g., Twitter) obtains a graph that incorporates
078   its node features, allowing the use of exclusive graph learning methods within the organization. We
079   take the global graph structure as structural knowledge, which can communicate with other data
080   owners. We protect node feature privacy across all data fields. In this new paradigm, we consider
081   node features as privacy, and the graph structure in the server can be accessed by clients. For example,
082   a client may recommend a set of users as influencers on Twitter; however, those users may remain
083   unrecognized by other clients due to the absence of their private features. This paper takes anomaly
084   detection tasks as an example. Unless otherwise specified, anomaly detection will be considered
085
      as the downstream task in the following text. For anomaly detection on multiple graphs, limited
      exploration has been done for federated anomaly detection without data leakage (Zhao et al., 2019;
086
      Chen et al., 2019; Ying et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2020). A well-known cost function Fα (S) can be
087
      specifically modeled in the ith industry data to anomaly detection task. We employ an alignment
088   function Qσ (S, U ) for measuring the distance between subgraphs of S and U . F is the abnormal
089   score of S (e.g., the work Tree-Shape-Priors Subgraph Detection (TSPSD) (Wu et al., 2018) as F ).
090   Q is the alignment score of S and U (e.g., the work Cross-Network Embedding for Multi-Network
091   Alignment (CrossMNA) (Chu et al., 2019) as Q). The parameters α and σ are significant level and
092   alignment threshold. We refer to this new paradigm as Federated Graph learning (FedGraph).

      2    R ELATED W ORK .
109
110
      Federated learning. Federated learning, being an efficient technique for privacy protection and
111
      suitable for cross-data, cross-domain, and cross-enterprise applications, is usually categorized into
112   horizontally federated learning, vertically federated learning, and federated transfer learningYang
113   et al. (2019). Kesici et al. (2024) detect real-time false data injection attacks in distribution networks
114   by utilizing split learning-based vertical FL. Qiu et al. (2024) detect the heart sound abnormality
115   in multi-center institutional heart sound databases by aligning the feature spaces of horizontal FL
116   participating institutions. Quan et al. (2024) integrate federated transfer learning and differential
117   privacy to classify diffuse waste data while ensuring data privacy.
118   Federated Graph Learning. In this article, the Federated Graph Learning (FGL) we study refers to
119   standard FL with structured data (i.e., server and clients work on graph data). Existing FGL methods
120   focus on reconstructing cross-client information, embedding overlapping nodes during collaborative
121   training, and dealing with data heterogeneity from multiple clientsFu et al. (2022). For instance,
122   Xie et al. (2021) and He et al. (2021) focus on addressing the challenge of the heterogeneity among
123   non-IID graphs, they assume that different clients have completely disjoint graphs. Li et al. (2020)
124   and Xie et al. (2021) tackle statistical heterogeneity by considering local partial data or server-side
125   proxy data as model parameters. When the graphs owned by clients are parts of an original global
126
      graph, Wu et al. (2021) and Zhang et al. (2021) address the missing links and overlapping nodes
      between subgraphs by augmenting nodes with information from other subgraphs. McMahan et al.
127
      (2017) train the decentralized data on mobile devices and learn the shared model by aggregating
128
      and averaging locally computed updates. Baek et al. (2023) focus on the joint improvement of local
129   models working on interrelated subgraphs, offering a novel solution to the challenges in subgraph-
130   level FL. However, these approaches can compromise privacy and increase communication overhead.
131   Hence, no published research studies task-specified structured knowledge federated learning. Our
132   method tackles the above challenge differently by exploring subgraph communities.

198
199   To address the challenge of model isolation to federated graph task, we proposed to perform the
200   proximal local structure knowledge update, and to mask unrelated structure knowledge.

202   4.1     L OCAL STRUCTURE KNOWLEDGE UPDATE WITH PROXIMAL TERM
203
204   We aim to capture the local structure knowledge sequence Si1 , Si2 , · · · for each client i ∈ [N ], and the
205   private knowledge Si is transformed to public knowledge Ui over the global graph G0 in the server.
206   The local private knowledge Si is achieved by accessing private feature data Xi for the i-th client,
207   and the public knowledge Ui is achieved by minimizing a distance function −λQiσ (Si , U ) where the
208
      value of Q is proportional to the number of anchor links between Si and U . The public knowledge is
      acquired from only the graph structure without accessing the local private node feature data.

      A.1     F EDERATED L EARNING L IMITATIONS
652
      Traditional federated learning cannot be applied in scenarios where both data and algorithms are
653   private. Specifically, let Di represent the private data of client i, and fi represent the private algorithm
654   of client i. Since fi is private, the model parameters θi learned by each client’s algorithm are
655   inconsistent across different clients. Traditional federated learning McMahan et al. (2017) relies on
656   the assumption that model parameters θi can be shared and aggregated across clients to create a global
      model Θ = k=1 PK|Dk|D       |
                   PK
657                                    θi , where K is the number of clients. However, due to the inconsistency
                              i=1   i|
658   in θi arising from private fi , this aggregation is not feasible, rendering traditional federated learning
659   ineffective. The new federated graph learning paradigm proposed in this paper addresses this issue
660   by focusing on the structure of graph data. It utilizes graph structures to connect knowledge from
661   different clients, thereby overcoming the isolation caused by private data Di and private algorithms
662   fi . This approach bridges the knowledge gap without relying on the sharing of parameters θi .

692   A.2.1    A NOMALY I NJECTION
693
694   Since the base graphs lack ground-truth anomalies, we employ the following methods to inject
695   structural and attribute anomalies for each dataset.
696   1) Injecting structural anomalies: A method involving perturbation of network topology is utilized
697   to inject the structural anomalies. Specifically, we generate q small anomalous clusters (q ∈ R).
698   Initially, q nodes are randomly selected from the node-set V , and random walk approach is utilized to
699   sample q connected subgraphs. Subsequently, these q connected subgraphs are transformed into q
700   fully connected subgraphs. Suppose each fully connected subgraph includes p nodes; these selected
701   p nodes are labeled as “Structural Anomaly Nodes.” In total, p × q structural anomalies are injected.
      The quantity of injected anomalies is adjusted based on the dataset size.


                                                          13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      2) Injecting attribute anomalies: We perturb the attributes of nodes. To ensure an equal number
703   of attribute and structural anomalies, we conduct q random walks, each time selecting p connected
704   nodes as targets. Subsequently, for each selected node vi , we randomly sample k other nodes
705   Vc = (vc1 , ..., vck ) to form a candidate set. For each vc ∈ Vc , we calculate the Euclidean distance
706   between its attribute vector xc and the attribute vector xi of vi . Following that, we select the node
707   vcj ∈ Vc with the maximum Euclidean distance and replace the attribute xi with xcj . In our paper, p
708   is set to 60, and q is set to 3 for the ACM, Cora, and Citeseer datasets. One of the selected subgraphs
709   is injected with anomalous structures.
