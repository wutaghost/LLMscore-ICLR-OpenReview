011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional language model alignment methods, such as Direct Preference Opti-
014            mization (DPO), are limited by their dependence on static, pre-collected paired
015            preference data, which hampers their adaptability and practical applicability. To
016            overcome this limitation, we introduce Self-Augmented Preference Optimization
017            (SAPO), an effective and scalable training paradigm that does not require existing
018
               paired data. Building on the self-play concept, which autonomously generates neg-
               ative responses, we further incorporate an off-policy learning pipeline to enhance
019
               data exploration and exploitation. Specifically, we employ an Exponential Moving
020
               Average (EMA) model in conjunction with a replay buffer to enable dynamic
021            updates of response segments, effectively integrating real-time feedback with in-
022            sights from historical data. Our comprehensive evaluations of the LLaMA3-8B
023            and Mistral-7B models across benchmarks—including the Open LLM Leader-
024            board, IFEval, AlpacaEval 2.0, and MT-Bench—demonstrate that SAPO matches
025            or surpasses established offline contrastive baselines, such as DPO and Odds Ratio
026            Preference Optimization, and outperforms offline self-play methods like SPIN.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the rapidly evolving field of artificial intelligence, aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with
032   human preferences has emerged as a critical area of research (Agrawal et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023;
033   Kadavath et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2021; Sheng et al., 2019; Christiano et al., 2017). Classical methods,
034   such as Reinforcement Learning (RL) from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang
035   et al., 2022), have progressed by training models to optimize responses via a reward model that
036   reflects human preferences. However, the necessity of a separate reward model introduces additional
037   complexity and computational demands. To streamline this, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
038   (Rafailov et al., 2023) directly utilizes preference data to optimize language models, eliminating
039
      the need for an auxiliary reward model. Odds Ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO) (Hong et al.,
      2024) further streamlines the alignment process by removing the reference model entirely. ORPO
040
      employs an odds ratio to directly evaluate preferences between different responses during Supervised
041
      Fine-Tuning (SFT), thus simplifying the alignment process. However, despite enhancements with
042   DPO, ORPO, and many other offline contrastive preference learning algorithms (Hong et al., 2024;
043   Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023), their reliance on static, pre-collected preference datasets
044   poses challenges, especially in sensitive domains where privacy concerns and the scarcity of expert
045   input limit adaptability and application scope.
046
      The Self-Play Fine-Tuning (SPIN) method (Chen et al., 2024) tackles the challenge of data collection
047
      by using a self-play approach, where the model autonomously generates its own responses to serve
048   as rejected inputs. This strategy enables SPIN to function with minimal data requirements—only
049   requiring prompts and selected responses—thereby alleviating the difficulty of gathering paired
050   preference datasets. However, SPIN’s methodology comes with significant limitations. Its primary
051   drawback is the reliance on offline, pre-generated responses, which hampers the model’s ability
052   to dynamically adjust training data in real-time. Additionally, this dependency necessitates a rigid
053   training procedure, where complete data generation must precede the start of training, introducing
      significant delays.

076   Recent work has underscored the significance of online training in enhancing the alignment per-
077   formance of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Guo et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024). However,
078   these methods largely rely on reward models or powerful teacher models like GPT-4 to provide
079   guidance signals. We introduce Self-Augmented Preference Optimization (SAPO), as depicted in
080   Figure 1, a general self-play preference learning algorithm without depending on any external reward
081
      models or teacher models. We derive the motivation for SAPO from the principles of off-policy RL
      training. (Lillicrap et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022). SAPO consists of three
082
      main components: the current policy, an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) model of the current
083
      policy, and a first-in-first-out replay buffer.
084
085   The learning of SAPO involves two stages at each iteration: sampling and training. During the
086   sampling stage, the EMA model is used to generate responses, creating self-augmented rejected
087
      samples. Both the original responses and these generated samples, along with the prompts, are stored
      in the replay buffer. In the training stage, we randomly select a batch of tuples from the replay
088
      buffer and employ typical preference learning methods, such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) and
089
      ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), to train the current policy. After training, we update the EMA model at
090   a fixed rate. These two stages work in tandem to incrementally improve the policy. By using the
091   EMA model and replay buffer, we reduce the impact of volatility from any single training iteration
092   and ensure more consistent learning signals. The progression of training data within the replay
093   buffer adopts principles akin to curriculum learning (Xu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024b; Pattnaik
094   et al., 2024), starting with simpler training pairs and gradually incorporating more complex training
095   samples, allowing the model to build competency progressively.
096   SAPO employs a teacher-forcing segment-level supervision strategy, truncating a chosen response at a
097   random point to create a supervision segment. As illustrated in Figure 1, with the prompt “Explain the
098   role of ATP in cellular processes,” a chosen response segment B might be “When ATP is hydrolyzed,
099   it releases energy, which is then harnessed to perform cellular work.” Conversely, the EMA model
100   might generate a less accurate rejected segment B ′ , such as “providing energy only when other
101   sources are depleted,” positioning ATP erroneously as a secondary reserve. By focusing on generating
102   tailored segments instead of entire rejected responses from scratch, SAPO is more likely to produce
103   meaningful outputs. This method facilitates more tailored adjustments to the training data.
104   Experimental evaluations across four benchmarks—Open LLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023),
105   which tests question answering ability; IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), measuring instruction-following
106   ability; MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024) and AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024), assessing conversa-
107   tional ability-demonstrate that SAPO can either match or exceed the performance of existing offline
      contrastive learning methods like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) and ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), despite

      2.1   D EFINITION OF L EARNING PARADIGMS IN LLM A LIGNMENT
115   For ease of understaning RL concepts in LLMs, we provide the following definitions:
116
117          • Offline Learning: Offline learning involves the LLM being trained on a pre-collected
118            dataset without further interaction with additional reward models or acquiring new human
119            annotated data during training.
120          • On-Policy Learning: This learning approach ensures that the training data is generated and
121            utilized under the current policy of the LLM. It implies that the model is refined using data
122            derived from the decisions it would make under its current strategy.
123          • Off-Policy Learning: Off-policy learning enables the use of data generated from a different
124            policy than the one currently being trained. This approach allows the model to leverage data
125            from past policies or alternative strategies, providing a broader range of experiences for the
126            model to learn from, which may not necessarily align with its current operational policy.
127

152   2.3   S ELF -P LAY F INE -T UNING
153
      The Self-Play Fine-Tuning (SPIN) method (Chen et al., 2024) introduces a self-play mechanism
154
      that reduces the reliance on pre-collected paired preference data. Unlike traditional approaches,
155
      SPIN necessitates only annotated chosen data, as it autonomously generates its own rejected data
156   from its previous iterations. SPIN then utilizes existing RLHF methods, e.g., DPO, to iteratively
157   refine model responses by discerning these self-generated responses from those obtained from
158   human-annotated data, a process inspired by principles of game theory (Samuel, 1959). While
159   SPIN effectively eliminates the reliance on offline contrastive learning’s need for paired data by
160   autonomously generating rejected responses, it utilizes pre-generated training data from the model’s
161   prior iterations. This data remains unchanged throughout subsequent training cycles, which may limit
      the model’s ability to adapt to new information or evolving training conditions.

      3    S ELF -AUGMENTED P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
163
164
      Recent advancements in offline contrastive preference learning algorithms (Rafailov et al., 2023;
165   Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) have shown promising results in aligning LLMs with
166   human preferences. However, these methods typically rely on pre-collected paired preference datasets.
167   Our goal is to create a robust and efficient self-augmented algorithm that eliminates the requirement
168   for paired data. This new algorithm autonomously generates high-quality rejected responses which,
169   combined with chosen responses from SFT datasets, form the necessary pairs for preference learning.
170   Recent initiatives like SPIN (Chen et al., 2024) utilize an iterative training method involving self-play,
171   where sampling and training occur in separate phases. Initially, rejected responses are sampled across
172   the entire dataset, followed by a distinct training phase. This iterative paradigm tends to be slow
173
      because it alternates between sampling for the entire dataset and training phases. As the training
      progresses, the effectiveness of the generated preference learning pairs may diminish. This is because
174
      the model continues to evolve, whereas the sampled dataset remains static for each iteration and
175
      cannot adapt to the latest model updates.
176
177   Instead, we start from a standard off-policy RL framework. To efficiently sample high-quality
178   preference pairs, we introduce segment-level supervision. This approach involves replacing a
179
      segment of rejected responses with outputs generated by LLM, thereby naturally creating challenging
      negative sample pairs through targeted local modifications. Furthermore, we integrate an EMA model
180
      and a replay buffer—techniques well-established in RL—to facilitate standard off-policy training.
181
      This strategy ensures timely feedback and updates to the training data, operating independently of
182   external feedback mechanisms. The implementation of SAPO is summarized in Algorithm 1, and a
183   comparison with on-policy training is provided in Section 5.3.
184
185   Segment-Level Supervision in SAPO. Unlike SPIN (Chen et al., 2024), which generates rejected
186   responses from scratch, SAPO utilizes a teacher-forcing segment-level supervision method to refine
187   the learning process. Consider a SFT dataset D consisting of tuples (x, y + ), where x is a prompt and
188   y + is the selected response. During each training iteration, the model randomly selects a truncation
189   point within each response y + , defining segment B of length Nseg starting from this point. Segment
190   A comprises the tokens preceding B, and segment C includes the tokens following B. The original
191   response y + can thus be expressed as:
192                                              y+ = A ⊕ B ⊕ C                                             (4)
193
                                                 ′
194   The model attempts to regenerate B as B based on the prompt and segment A. For continuity and to
195
      maintain the contextual integrity of the response, segment C is concatenated, resulting in:
196                                             y − = A ⊕ B ′ ⊕ C,                                          (5)
197
198   where ⊕ denotes concatenation. This segmentation strategy not only improves supervision granularity
199
      by focusing on specific response segments – by regenerating only the middle segment B ′ , the model
      can concentrate its learning efforts on specific parts of the response that may be problematic or less
200
      accurate. In addition, sampling segments is more time efficient than sampling the complete sentences.
201
202
      Off-Policy Learning Setting. Inspired by off-policy RL training methods (Lillicrap et al., 2015;
203
      Haarnoja et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022), the proposed SAPO framework incorporates several
204   fundamental components: the current policy, an EMA model of this policy, and a first-in-first-out
205   replay buffer. In this context, the replay buffer B plays a crucial role, mirroring curriculum learning
206   principles (Xu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024b; Pattnaik et al., 2024). The recent Curry-DPO study
207   (Pattnaik et al., 2024) also highlights the effectiveness of sequencing preference pairs from simpler
208   to more complex throughout training, which gradually increases task complexity and enhances
209   learning efficiency. This approach prevents the model from being overwhelmed by challenging tasks
210   at early stages, thus improving learning outcomes and enhancing model robustness. However, the
211   Curry-DPO approach requires an additional reward model to manually order training pairs, which can
212
      introduce biases and require extra resources. Our method automates this process, naturally achieving
      a curriculum learning effect and reducing dependency on supplementary models.
213

215   (y − ) are distinctly different from the chosen responses (y + ), and these responses are generated
      by the early iterations of the model. As the model evolves and its capacity for generating better

      4.1    R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK (RLHF)
264
265   RLHF Methods can be categorized into two primary approaches: reward-based methods and reward-
266   free methods. Reward-based methods like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al.,
267   2017) utilize a trained reward model (Ziegler et al., 1909; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022)
268   to provide feedback signals for online RL algorithms. The training of multiple models (policy,
269   reward, and advantage model) increases computational demands and can lead to instability during the
      training process (Gao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). In contrast, reward-free methods simplify


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the training process by eliminating the need for a separate reward model. DPO (Rafailov et al.,
271   2023) integrates the reward modeling stage directly into the preference learning stage. This method,
272   based on a closed-form solution derived from the Bradley-Terry model Bradley & Terry (1952), is
273   noted for its efficiency and stability. (Zhao et al., 2023) introduce Sequence Likelihood Calibration
274   with Human Feedback (SLiC-HF), which employs a contrastive ranking calibration loss combined
275   with a regularization loss to refine the scoring of responses. The Kahneman-Tversky Optimization
276   (KTO) algorithm (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) leverages human utility principles to optimize language
277   models using unpaired data, moving away from the dependency on pairwise preference datasets.
278   Relative Preference Optimization (RPO) (Yin et al., 2024) utilizes a contrastive weighting mechanism
279
      that evaluates preferences across not only identical but also semantically similar prompts, allowing
      for both paired and unpaired data scenarios. Recently, the ORPO algorithm (Hong et al., 2024)
280
      simplifies preference alignment by integrating supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization
281
      into a single training stage without requiring a reference model. However, a major issue with
282   offline contrastive preference learning approaches is their dependence on static, pre-collected paired
283   preference datasets, typically involving a single optimization procedure. This reliance can lead to a
284   distribution shift between the offline training data and the fine-tuned model, potentially impacting the
285   model’s effectiveness and adaptability.
286
287   4.2   I TERATIVE F INE -T UNING LLM S
288
289   Iterative fine-tuning enhances language models by using outputs from the model itself or external
290   models as inputs for subsequent training iterations, aiming to improve performance from each training
291
      cycle. A family of iterative methods (Li et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Mukobi
      et al., 2023) involves continuously refining language models by supervised fine-tuning models on
292
      carefully selected, preferred responses. This iterative approach is further applied within the DPO
293
      framework, as demonstrated by a number of recent works (Xu et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Yuan
294   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Utilizing iterative DPO-type training, updated models generate new
295   preference pairs at each iteration (Xu et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). These pairs
296   are then scored using feedback from additional reward models or human evaluations. Yuan et al.
297   (2024) introduce Self-Rewarding Language Models, where the model annotates its own responses.
298   Integrated into the iterative DPO framework, this allows the model to autonomously generate and
299   assess preference pairs, streamlining fine-tuning by reducing external feedback reliance. However,
300   this self-judging approach heavily relies on the model’s own evaluative capabilities, making it more
301   suitable for larger parameter language models. The SPIN algorithm (Chen et al., 2024) uses a self-play
302
      framework for iterative DPO-style fine-tuning, labeling human-generated responses as winners and
      model-generated ones as losers. However, SPIN generates datasets for the next cycle offline, which
303
      limits the incorporation of fresh outputs from updated models. Additionally, its reliance on offline
304
      learning can lead to a shift problem as the fine-tuned model increasingly diverges from the one used
305   to generate the preference dataset. To address these challenges, we have integrated real-time data
306   sampling within the self-play framework, facilitating immediate updates to the training data. Some
307   recent works (Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) have also highlighted the importance of online
308   iteration in preference learning. Unlike these approaches, which often rely on additional reward
309   models or more advanced teacher models like GPT-4, our method seeks to develop a general and
310   effective self-augmented algorithm that functions independently of external supervision.
311   Some research focuses on domain-specific applications to self-improve language models. For instance,
312   Lee et al. (2024) target low-data regime tasks, optimizing language models with limited initial datasets.
313   Other works concentrate on enhancing reasoning capabilities, as seen in Pang et al. (2024). In contrast,
314   our research focus on enhancing language models for general instruction-following tasks.

329   Datasets and Base Models. We utilize the Distilabel-Capybara dataset1 , a multi-turn dialogue
330   preference dataset comprising 7.6k entries, designed to enhance the conversational abilities of open-

      preference learning algorithms like DPO and ORPO, both chosen and rejected responses are required

336
      the Distilabel-Capybara dataset are necessary.

      For the DPO-based LLaMA model, we use Meta-LLaMA-3-8B as the base model, following the

      the llama-3-8b-sft model. For the ORPO algorithm, our base models include Mistral-7B-v0.1 and

      Meta-LLaMA-3-8B. These models are then utilized in various preference learning algorithms to

      Huggingface under wandb/mistral-7b-zephyr-sft. For the LLaMA-3-8B model, we replicated the
353   Mistral’s training setup, with a learning rate set to 2.0e-05. The models were fine-tuned over three
354   epochs on the Deita 10k dataset, known for its high quality and suitability for SFT. We employed a
355   cosine learning rate scheduler and set the maximum sequence length at 2048 tokens.
356
      For DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), the KL regularization coefficient β was set at 0.1 with a learning
357
      rate of 5.0e-7, using the rmsprop optimizer. The training spanned three epochs.
358
359   For ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), we adhered to the default settings used on the Distilabel-Capybara
360   dataset. The odds ratio weight coefficient λ was set at 0.05, with a learning rate of 5.0e-6, using the
361   AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). The ORPO training was conducted for three epochs
362
      on the Mistral model and four epochs on the LLaMA model.
363   SPIN (Chen et al., 2024) was configured similarly to DPO and ORPO, running across four iterations,
364   each consisting of three epochs. The learning rate was 5.0e-7 for the first two iterations and reduced
365   to 1e-7 for the remaining two. The β coefficient was increased to 5.0 for the final iteration.

      6    C ONLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce the Self-Augmented Preference Optimization (SAPO) framework, a dy-
532   namic off-policy learning paradigm that updates training data in real-time. Leveraging an Exponential
533   Moving Average (EMA) model and a replay buffer, SAPO ensures stable and consistent performance,
534   drastically reducing dependence on large pre-collected datasets. Through extensive evaluations across
535   diverse Large Language Model (LLM) architectures such as LLaMA-3-8B and Mistral-7B, and
536   using contrastive preference learning algorithms like DPO and ORPO, our method demonstrates
537   superior performance on benchmarks including the Open LLM Leaderboard, IFEval, MT-Bench, and
538   AlpacaEval 2.0. Furthermore, our method’s independence from annotated paired data and freedom
539   from iterative training as seen in SPIN, positions it for broader applicability in diverse large-scale
      post-training tasks, pointing towards promising future directions.

      The Self-Augmented Preference Optimization (SAPO) framework improves the alignment of Large
543   Language Models (LLMs) with human values by dynamically updating training data, which reduces
544   dependence on large datasets and helps mitigate potential biases. The use of public datasets enhances
545   transparency and compliance with ethical standards, fostering responsible AI practices. However,

      A    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
811
812
      While the Self-Augmented Preference Optimization (SAPO) framework improves LLM alignment
813   with human preferences via dynamic data updates, its reliance on a static, human-generated data distri-
814   bution limits performance to human levels. For future work, since SAPO does not require paired data,
815   applying the algorithm to large-scale, high-quality SFT datasets for fine-tuning presents a promising
816   direction. Additionally, while current efforts primarily focus on general instruction-following tasks,
817   exploring the application of SAPO to specific domains such as mathematics, reasoning, and code
818   generation offers potential for further advancements.
