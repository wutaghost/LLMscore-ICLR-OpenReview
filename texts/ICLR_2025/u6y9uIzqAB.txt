                                                A BSTRACT
039
040
              We present Lay-Your-Scene (shorthand LayouSyn), a novel diffusion-
041
              Transformer based architecture for open-vocabulary natural scene layout gen-
042           eration. Prior works have used close-sourced scene-unaware Large Language
043           models for open-vocabulary layout generation, limiting their widespread use and
044           scene-specific modeling capability. This work presents the first end-to-end text-to-
045           natural-scene-layout generation pipeline that utilizes lightweight open-source lan-
046           guage models to predict objects in the scene and a new conditional layout diffusion
047           Transformer trained in a scene-aware manner. Extensive experiments demonstrate
048           that LayouSyn outperforms existing methods on open-vocabulary and closed-
049           vocabulary layout generation and achieves state-of-the-art performance on chal-
050
              lenging spatial and numerical reasoning tasks. Additionally, we present two ap-
              plications of LayouSyn: First, we demonstrate an interesting finding that we can
051
              seamlessly combine initialization from the Large Language model to reduce the
052
              diffusion sampling steps. Second, we present a new pipeline for adding objects to
053           the image, demonstrating the potential of LayouSyn in image editing applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Generating visual layouts, i.e. determining the positions, sizes, and categories of elements, plays
057   an indispensable role in downstream vision tasks such as document analysis (Arroyo et al., 2021)
058   and graphical design (Lee et al., 2020). Recent works tackle layout generation through a contin-
059   uous (Wang et al., 2024b) or discrete (Gupta et al., 2021; Inoue et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a)
060   diffusion process, where Transformers are often used to model the relationships between elements.
061   Although these methods achieve competitive results across various benchmarks, they primarily focus
062   on unconditional layout generation, such as document layouts. Additionally, these models either
063   assume a fixed set of object categories or are incapable of dealing with complex text conditions,
064   which limits their applicability in open-vocabulary settings for natural scenes.
065   With the advancement of text-to-image generative models (Ramesh et al., 2021; Nichol et al., 2021;
066   Rombach et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023c; Xue et al., 2024), there has been a growing interest in
067   controllable generation (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b), where users can explicitly control the
068   spatial locations (Xie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a), and counts (Binyamin et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
069   2023) of objects in the generated images. While these frameworks can achieve satisfactory control
070   over image generation, users still need to manually supply fine-grained conditioning inputs, such
071
      as plausible scene layouts. A text-to-layout generation framework is therefore needed to reduce the
      manual effort involved. Some works (Feng et al., 2023; Gani et al., 2024) try to automate this process
072
      by generating layouts with close-sourced large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (Ouyang
073
      et al., 2022) with in-context prompting. While LLMs can generate reasonable scene layouts, they
074   often produce unrealistic relative object sizes or unnatural bounding box placements (Gani et al.,
075   2024), especially with longer scene descriptions. Additionally, relying on LLMs introduces opacity
076   in the generation process, along with latency and increased costs.
077
      To overcome these limitations, we introduce LayouSyn (Lay-Your-Scene), an open-vocabulary text-
078
      to-natural-scene-layout generation framework that combines the strengths of both language models’
079
      open-vocabulary capabilities and the strong inductive bias of vision-based models. Our approach
080   divides the scene layout generation task into two stages. In the first stage, a lightweight language
081   model is used to extract a set of labels from the given prompt. In the second stage, we design a new
082   conditional diffusion-transformer network to predict the scene layout, working directly within the
083   bounding box state space.
084
      Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
085
086       • Novel framework and new module: We propose LayouSyn, the first end-to-end scene-aware
087         text-to-natural-scene-layout generation framework. It adopts small-size language models to
088         predict objects in the scene, and it creates a new conditional diffusion Transformer trained
089         in a scene-aware manner for layout generation. A schematic illustration for the training and
090         inference pipeline can be found in Figure 2.
091       • Versatile applications: We demonstrate the versatility of LayouSyn with two applications.
092         LLM-initialization: we use coarse layouts generated by LLMs such as ChatGPT to initialize
093         LayouSyn, achieving better results with equal or fewer sampling steps. Object-addition: we
094         leverage LayouSyn to perform layout completion, which guides image inpainting to add the
095         new object.
096       • State-of-the-art results: Extensive experiments show that LayouSyn can generate scene lay-
097         outs that are both semantically and geometrically plausible. LayouSyn outperforms existing
098         methods on multiple closed-vocabulary and open-vocabulary scene generation benchmarks.

100   2   R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Closed-vocabulary layout generation Previous works on closed-vocabulary layout generation
103   focus on a fixed set of object categories and have proposed various architectures to address this task.
104   LayoutGAN (Li et al., 2019), a GAN-based framework, generates both labels and bounding boxes
105   from noise simultaneously. However, it cannot perform generation conditioned on specific label sets,
106   and its evaluations are limited to documents with a small number of elements. LayoutVAE (Jyothi
107   et al., 2019) improves upon this by generating layouts conditioned on label sets autoregressively us-
      ing LSTM-based VAEs, allowing it to handle a larger number of objects, such as those found in the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2015). VTN (Arroyo et al., 2021) further enhances this approach by em-
109   ploying Transformers as the building block for VAEs, better capturing inter-element relationships
110   within a layout. Another line of research formulates layout generation as a sequence generation
111   problem, effectively addressed using Transformers. BLT (Kong et al., 2022) employs a bidirectional
112   Transformer for iterative decoding, while LayoutTransformers (Gupta et al., 2021) uses the standard
113   next-token prediction approach. LayoutFormer++ (Jiang et al., 2023) introduces decoding space
114   restrictions to align layouts more effectively with user-defined constraints. More recently, diffusion
115   models have been explored for layout generation. Dolfin (Wang et al., 2024b) applies continuous
116   diffusion in the bounding box coordinate space, while LayoutDM (Inoue et al., 2023) and LayoutD-
117
      iffusion (Zhang et al., 2023a) address the task using discrete diffusion on both coordinate and type
      tokens. Beyond unconditional generation, these models also demonstrate utility in conditional gen-
118
      eration tasks, such as layout refinement and type-conditioned generation. Despite these progresses
119
      the majority of these works are benchmarked on document layouts, and the closed-vocabulary nature
120   of the models limits their generalizability to layouts for natural scenes.
121
122
      Open-vocabulary layout generation Open-vocabulary layout generation is an important task that
123
      is often coupled with controllable text-to-image generation. For example, GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023),
124   ReCo (Yang et al., 2023), and Boxdiff (Xie et al., 2023) can generate images based on a given scene
125   layout and corresponding region prompts. This requires open-vocabulary layouts, where object cat-
126   egories are not limited to a predefined set, but can include any valid nouns from natural language.
127   Recent approaches predominantly address this challenge by leveraging the reasoning capabilities of
128   large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022). For instance, LayoutGPT (Feng
129   et al., 2023) introduces a style sheet-like structural language, combined with in-context exemplars
130   to generate layouts with GPT models. Additionally, it proposes Numerical and Spatial Reasoning
131   (NSR-1K) to assess the spatial and counting accuracy in generated layouts, a benchmark we also
132
      use to evaluate our LayouSyn. LLM Blueprint (Gani et al., 2024) goes further by generating object
      descriptions alongside layouts to better guide image generation. While these approaches achieve
133
      promising results, their reliance on LLMs reduces transparency and can introduce additional com-
134
      putational costs. In contrast, our LayouSyn relies on a smaller, more efficient language model that
135   can be hosted locally, yet demonstrates strong open-vocabulary capabilities and surpasses competing
136   methods across various benchmarks.
137
138   Diffusion Transformers Diffusion Transformers were first introduced in (Peebles & Xie, 2023)
139   to address class-conditional image generation. The self-attention layers in Transformers allow for
140   more effective modeling of relationships between tokens. Beyond text-to-image generation (Esser
141   et al., 2024), this architecture has been adapted for layout generation (Inoue et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
142   2024b), 3D shape generation Mo et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024), and video generation Brooks et al.
143   (2024). Our approach builds upon Diffusion Transformers for layout generation but operates directly
144   on the continuous bounding box coordinate space, without the need for any VAE encoding.

147
148
      This section describes our approach to generating a natural scene layout conditioned on the text
149
      prompt and label set in an open-vocabulary manner. Formally, we define a layout L = {(oi , bi )}N   i=1 ,
150
      where oi is the natural language description or label of the ith object and bi ∈ R4 represents a
151   bounding box in the (top-left, bottom-right) format. Our objective is to generate the layout L condi-
152   tioned on the text prompt p and object label set O. We provide a brief overview of diffusion models
153   in Sec. 3.1, describe our architecture in Sec. 3.2, discuss the need for scaling inputs in Sec. 3.3, and
154   present an automated approach to generating label set O in Sec. 3.4.

192   3.2       A RCHITECTURE
193
194   We adopt Diffusion-Transformer (DiT) architecture for denoising and operate directly in bounding
195   box coordinate space to generate layouts conditioned on the text prompt p, object label set O, and
196   aspect ratio ar. We scale the bounding box coordinates by width and height to range [0, 1] and
197   further normalize the coordinates to the range [−1, 1]. We encode the object label oi , bounding box
198
      coordinates bi , and position i of an object oi into a single fixed-size d-dimensional token ti ∈ Rd .
      Formally, the token is computed as:
199
200                                    ti = MLP(bi ) + Embedder(oi ) + PositionalEncoding(i)                                                                                (3)
201
202   where Embedder is a sentence embedding model that maps the object label oi to a fixed-size em-
203
      bedding, MLP is a multi-layer perceptron that maps the bounding box coordinates to d-dimensional
      embedding, and PositionalEncoding is 1D sinusoidal positional encoding. We condition the
204
      denoiser on the timestep t and the aspect ratio ar = Lw /Lh , where Lw and Lh are the width and
205
      height of the layout, respectively and incorporate the global conditioning information with adaptive
206   layer normalization (Perez et al., 2017). Finally, we modify the DiT blocks and add a cross-attention
207   layer (Chen et al., 2023b) to incorporate information from the text prompt p. We visualize the com-
208   plete architecture in Fig. 2.

      3.4   L ABEL S ET G ENERATION
240
241   The label set O is a function of prompt p and contains the object labels present in the scene described
242   by the prompt. A large language model (LLM) trained on a large corpus of text data is suitable to
243   predict the object labels and their counts from the prompt. We prompt LLM to extract noun phrases
244   from the prompt, assign a count to each noun phrase, and filter out the noun phrases that cannot
245   be visualized in the scene. This allows us to generate the label set O from the prompt p in an
246   open-vocabulary manner, and our method can work in settings where object labels are not present
247   or provided by the users. The details for prompting LLM are in Appendix A.2 and we visualize a
248   few results in Tab. 1.

285   Setup Following previous works (Jyothi et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2023), we evaluate LayouSyn on
286   COCO17 (Lin et al., 2015) Instance dataset. We modify LayouSyn to remove cross-attention layers
287   and use 6 DiT blocks, each with 6 heads for multi-head attention, and a hidden dimension of size
288   144. We use 250 diffusion steps at a scale of 2.0, Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) optimizer with a
289   learning rate of 10−4 , batch size 32, and train for 1M steps on 1 NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPUs. We
290   sample with 250 DDPM steps. We call this architecture LayouSyn-CV.
291
292   Baselines We compare our work with LayoutVAE and LayoutTransformer trained on the COCO17
293   Instance dataset using the open-source implementation provided by LayoutTransformer. Note that
294   LayouSyn and LayoutVAE are conditioned on the label set, whereas LayoutTransformer predicts the
295   next object labels during generation. To ensure a fair comparison, we modify the sampling algorithm
296
      of LayoutTransformer and force the token predictions to match the label set.
297
298   Metrics We evaluate the quality of generated layouts on two criteria: (1) Layout Quality: Fol-
299   lowing Document Layout Generation literature (Wang et al., 2024b; Jyothi et al., 2019; Li et al.,
300
      2019), we draw the layout as an image and map each object to a specific color and compare the
      generated images using Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2018). (2) Image Quality:
301
      We use Layout2Im (Zhao et al., 2019) to generate images from layouts and compute the Fréchet
302
      Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2018) and Inception Score (IS) (Salimans et al., 2016) with
303   the COCO17 Instance validation dataset.
304

306
      LayouSyn-CV achieves state-of-the-art performance on the FID metric and comparable performance

308

      5.2   O BJECT ADDITION P IPELINE
510
      Image inpainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022) with the diffusion model is widely used for adding objects
511   to images. However, these models need users to specify the spatial location of the objects to be
512   added, requiring a human-in-the-loop to guide the inpainting process. In this paper, we answer
513   the following question: Given an image I, a list of objects to add to the image A, and a prompt
514   p describing the final image, can we add objects to the image without human intervention in an
515   automated manner? To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that addresses the problem
516   of adding objects to images without any human intervention in an end-to-end pipeline with layout
517   completion. We discuss components of our pipeline below and visualize examples in Fig. 7.
518
519
            1. Label set: We use an LLM to generate an object set O from the prompt p. O contains a
               list of objects that need to be considered during the object addition process.
520
521         2. Object Detection: We use a pre-trained object detection model to detect objects from the
522            label set O in the image I. We obtain a set of bounding boxes B for the detected objects
523
               and create a layout L with obtained bounding boxes B and label set O.
524         3. Layout Completion: We inpaint (Lugmayr et al., 2022) the bounding box locations of the
525            objects to add with LayouSyn, and obtain an inpainting mask M based on the predicted
526            bounding boxes for objects in A.
527         4. Object Inpainting: We use inpainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022) with GLIGEN (Li et al.,
528            2023) to inpaint objects in the set A into the image I using the inpainting mask M .

      We present Lay-Your-Scene (abbreviated as LayouSyn), a novel diffusion Transformer architecture
      for open-vocabulary natural scene layout generation. We demonstrate that LayouSyn can be com-
534
      bined with small-sized LLMs for an end-to-end text-to-layout generation pipeline. Extensive ex-
535
      periments demonstrate that LayouSyn outperforms existing methods on multiple layout generation
536   benchmarks, including the challenging spatial and numerical reasoning tasks. Further, we demon-
537   strate an interesting finding that we can seamlessly combine initialization from LLMs to reduce
538   the diffusion sampling steps and refine the LLM predictions. Finally, we present a new pipeline for
539   adding objects to the image, demonstrating the potential of LayouSyn in image editing applications.

884   We use a pre-trained LLM to generate a set of object labels O from a given prompt. Our overall
885
      is to ask LLM to follow a series of steps to extract the noun phrases from the prompt which can
      be visualized in the scene and output the object and the count in a JSON format. Our prompt is as
886
      follows:
887
888         You are a creative scene designer who predicts a scene from a natural
889
                 language prompt. A scene is a JSON object containing a list of
                noun phrases with their counts {"phrase1": count1, "phrase2":
890             count2, ...}. The noun phrases contain **ONLY** common nouns. You
891              strictly follow the below process for predicting plausible
892             scenes:
893
894
            Step 1: Extract noun phrases from the prompt. For example, "happy
                people", "car engine", "brown dog", "parking lot", etc.
895         Step 2: Limit noun phrases to common nouns and convert the noun
896             phrase to its singular form. For example, "happy people" to "
897             person", "tall women" to "woman", "group of old people" to "
898             person", "children" to "child", "brown dog" to "dog", "parking
899
                lot" remains "parking lot", etc.
            Step 3: Predict the count of each noun phrase and ensure consistency
900             with the count of other objects in the scene. If a particular
901             object does not have any explicit count mentioned in the prompt,
902             use your creativity to assign a count to make the overall scene
903             plausible but not too cluttered. For example, if the prompt is "a
904
                 group of young kids playing with their dogs," the count of "kid"
                 can be 3, and the count of "dog" should be the same as the count
905              of "kid".
906         Step 4: Output the final scene as a JSON object, only including
907             physical objects and phrases without referring to actions or
908             activities.
909
            Complete example:
910
911         Prompt: Three white sheep and few women walking down a town road.

913         Step 1: noun phrases: white sheep, women, town road

            Step 3: Since the count of women is not mentioned, we will assign a
915             count of 2 to make the scene plausible. The count of "sheep" is 3
916              and the count of "town road" is 1.
917         Step 4: {"sheep": 3, "woman": 2, "town road": 1}
