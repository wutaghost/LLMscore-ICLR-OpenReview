011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-task model merging offers an efficient solution for integrating knowledge
014            from multiple fine-tuned models, mitigating the significant computational and
015
               storage demands associated with multi-task training. As a key technique in this
               field, Task Arithmetic (TA) defines task vectors by subtracting the pre-trained
016
               model (θpre ) from the fine-tuned task models in parameter space, then adjusting the
017
               weight between these task vectors and θpre to balance task-generalized and task-
018            specific knowledge. Despite the promising performance of TA, conflicts can arise
019            among the task vectors, particularly when different tasks require distinct model
020            adaptations. In this paper, we formally define this issue as knowledge conflicts,
021            characterized by the performance degradation of one task after merging with a
022            model fine-tuned for another task. Through in-depth analysis, we show that these
023            conflicts stem primarily from the components of task vectors that align with the
024            gradient of task-specific losses at θpre . To address this, we propose Task Arith-
025            metic in Trust Region (TATR), which defines the trust region as dimensions in
026
               the model parameter space that cause only small changes (corresponding to the
               task vector components with gradient orthogonal direction) in the task-specific
027
               losses. Restricting parameter merging within this trust region, TATR can effec-
028
               tively alleviate knowledge conflicts. Moreover, TATR serves as both an indepen-
029            dent approach and a plug-and-play module compatible with a wide range of TA-
030            based methods. Extensive empirical evaluations on eight distinct datasets robustly
031            demonstrate that TATR improves the multi-task performance of several TA-based
032            model merging methods by an observable margin.

067   When merging models, only the components of task vectors in the trust region are added to the
068   pre-trained weights; other dimensions are discarded. TATR can be used independently or jointly
069
      with other techniques like Ties-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023), AdaMerging (Yang et al., 2024b), and

070
071   TATR utilizes the first-order Taylor series to compute the changes in the task-specific losses. It
072   contrasts with a simplistic approach that selects components of task vectors that align with the
073   negative gradient direction. While the simplistic approach is intuitive, empirical evidence reveals
074   that it usually does not alleviate knowledge conflicts. We contend that, as the task vectors have
075
      large magnitudes, the first-order Taylor series fails to approximate the function well, leading to
      performance degradation. In contrast, TATR identifies directions that are orthogonal to the gradient,
076
      along which minimal cross-task interference happens. Due to overparameterization and parameter
077
      redundancy in the models (Dalvi et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022b), such directions are usually not
078   difficult to find.
079

      2.1   T RADITIONAL M ULTI -TASK L EARNING
095
      Multi-task learning (MTL) aims to improve performance by sharing knowledge across related tasks
096   (Zhang & Yang, 2022). A significant challenge for MTL is negative transfer (Liu et al., 2017; Zhang
097   et al., 2023b), where joint training on conflicting tasks yields performance lower than training on the
098   tasks individually. Various solutions to negative transfer have been proposed, such as modularization
099   (Tang et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2018), sparsification (Ding et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
100   2019), and soft parameter sharing (Gao et al., 2020; Hazimeh et al., 2021). Other strategies focus
101   on optimizing task interactions, such as adjusting task-specific loss weights (Sener & Koltun, 2018;
102   Liu et al., 2019; 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022a), resolving gradient direction conflicts (Yu
103   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a; Javaloy & Valera, 2022; Navon et al., 2022), or
104
      preventing the dominance of certain tasks (Chen et al., 2018; He et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023).
105   Traditional MTL are not well-suited for merging foundation models. First, retraining these models
106   using vast amounts of data incurs significant computational costs. Large-scale foundation models
107   are already resource-intensive, and training them with multi-task objectives further amplifies these
      demands, requiring immense computation and time. Additionally, retraining from scratch wastes


                                                         2
108
      valuable knowledge optimized in each individual expert model. These considerations have driven
109   the development of model merging as an alternative to multi-task learning.
110
111
112   2.2   M ULTI -TASK L EARNING THROUGH M ODEL M ERGING
113
114   Model merging techniques, which aim to integrate knowledge across models, have attracted in-
115   creasing attention in recent years. As a precursor, Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA) (Izmailov
116   et al., 2018) averages model weights near the end of training. This concept was further advanced
117   by approaches like SWAD (Cha et al., 2021) and Ensemble of Averages (EoA) (Arpit et al., 2022).
118   Empirical evidence from Ilharco et al. (2023a) demonstrates that parameter averaging effectively
119   integrates knowledge from models trained on diverse tasks. DLCPA (Sun et al., 2023) proposes
120   to apply cumulative parameter averaging (CPA) to continually assimilate knowledge across dis-
121
      tinct tasks. Fisher-Merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022) leverages the Fisher information matrix Fisher
      (1925) to measure the importance of model parameters and merge models using weighted averaging.
122
      Additionally, RegMean (Jin et al., 2023) formulates an optimal merging model by minimizing the
123
      distance to each model in the parameter space.
124
125   Recently, Task Arithmetic (TA) (Ilharco et al., 2023b) innovatively proposes the concept of “task
126   vector”, defined as the vector from a pre-trained model to its fine-tuned counterpart in the parameter
127
      space. By weighting these task vectors and adding them back to the pre-trained model, TA strikes a
      harmonious balance between generalized knowledge from the pre-train model and the task-specific
128
      knowledge in the task vectors. Following this insight, Ties-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) refines the
129
      fusion process by discarding parameters deemed insignificant or of low magnitude. PEFT (Zhang
130   et al., 2023a) and MoLE (Wu et al., 2024) further extend TA by integrating it with LoRA (Hu et al.,
131   2022) modules. Furthermore, Ortiz-Jimenez et al. (2023) suggests fine-tuning models in the tangent
132   space, which can effectively mitigate conflict between task vectors.
133
      Furthermore, several approaches combine test-time adaptation techniques with TA, yielding superior
134
      MTL performance. These test-time adaptation-based methods typically allocate merging weights
135
      and fine-tune them during testing using unsupervised test data. For instance, AdaMerging (Yang
136   et al., 2024b) trains a set of merging coefficients for layers, while other methods fit lightweight
137   adapter modules, such as representation surgery (Yang et al., 2024a) and MoE router (Tang et al.,
138   2024).

146   Formally, let θpre ∈ RN denote the set of N parameters of a pre-trained model, which is initially
147

      Subsequently, θpre undergoes fine-tuning for K distinct downstream tasks, yielding a set of fine-

151   models {θk }K k=1 into a single model θMTL . This merged model θMTL aims to achieve effective
152   generalization across all K tasks without resorting to trivial solutions such as retraining from scratch
153   or requiring full access to the training datasets of all tasks.

181   Knowledge conflict frequently arises when merging MTL models, as the expert models encapsulate
182

200   Knowledge conflict can be regarded as a special case of negative transfer, although these concepts
201
      emphasize different aspects. In traditional MTL, negative transfer typically refers to the dynamic
      interference between tasks during joint training, where conflicting gradients impede the model from
202

203
      here highlights a static nature among the fine-tuned model parameters, where further training to
204   resolve task interference is prohibited. Each fine-tuned model has already encoded task-specific
205   knowledge, which may be inherently incompatible with that of other tasks. As a result, knowl-
206   edge conflict in model merging presents a unique challenge, necessitating methods that can align or
207   reconcile parameters without resorting to retraining.

      The above observations are reasonable since neural network parameters, particularly those in pre-
279   trained foundation models, often exhibit high redundancy (Dalvi et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022b).
280   Additionally, task-specific knowledge is often low-rank Hu et al. (2022), i.e., only a few parameter
281   directions are critical for learning the task. In order to identify a small set of critical parameters that
282   should not be altered during model merging and alleviate knowledge conflicts, we propose defining
283   the following trust region:
284   Definition 2 (Trust Region for Knowledge Conflict). Given a pre-trained model θpre , the trust region
285   specific in the dimension space is defined as follows:

292   to merge.

      cause knowledge conflicts. Conversely, when ∇θ Lj (θpre ) and ∆i are orthogonal, their projections

        Figure 4 illustrates the average sensitivity of each dataset to task vectors across different layers.
517     Three key characteristics can be observed. Firstly, the shallow layers exhibit greater sensitivity than
518     other layers. Shallow layers typically encode task-generalized knowledge, and the increased sensi-
519     tivity highlights the importance of preserving this information in the TATR method. Secondly, the
520     sensitivity exhibits periodic variations across layers, with bias layers generally exhibiting higher sen-
521     sitivity than weight layers. This trend is reasonable, as bias layers have a more pronounced impact
522     on network outputs, making them more susceptible to knowledge conflicts. Lastly, datasets com-
523     prising digit data (e.g., SVHN and MNIST) show relatively lower sensitivity to knowledge conflicts,
524     which can be attributed to their significant domain differences from other real-world datasets.
525
526     7                    C ONCLUSION
527
528     In this paper, we delve deep into the critical challenge of knowledge conflict in multi-task model
529     merging with a focus on task arithmetic. We began by formalizing the concept of knowledge conflict
530     as the degradation in model performance caused by the interference between task vectors. Our
531     analysis and empirical findings suggest that components of task vectors orthogonal to the gradient
532     direction exhibit minimal knowledge conflict. This insight motivates us to define a trust region based
533
        on orthogonality and propose Task Arithmetic in the Trust Region (TATR). Extensive experiments
        across eight diverse datasets demonstrate that TATR effectively mitigates the knowledge conflict,
534
        enhancing the overall multi-task performance of task arithmetic-based methods.

789   A.3   BASELINES .
790
791   In our experiments, we compare our methods with several baseline approaches, which are grouped
792   into four categories: basic baseline methods, test-time training-based methods, training-free meth-
793
      ods, and our proposed methods. The details of these methods are as follows:
794   i) Basic baseline methods:
795
796         • Pre-trained directly employs a pre-trained model to predict across multiple tasks. Since it
797           does not incorporate any downstream task-specific information during model training, its
798
              performance on downstream tasks is typically suboptimal.
799         • Individual. In this approach, an independent fine-tuned model is used for each task. While
800           it avoids interference between tasks, it cannot perform multiple tasks simultaneously. It
801           serves as a reference upper bound for model merging approaches.
802         • Traditional MTL aggregates the original training data from all tasks to train a single multi-
803           task model.
804
805   ii) Test-time training-based methods:
806
807
            • AdaMerging (Yang et al., 2024b) leverages an unlabeled test set to adaptively learn the
              merging coefficients at either a layer-wise or task-wise level in Task Arithmetic.
808
809         • AdaMerging++ (Yang et al., 2024b) an enhanced version of AdaMerging, integrates the
              principles of Ties-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023).


                                                      15
810
             • Surgery (Yang et al., 2024a) introduces a feature transformation module, trained to align
811            features during the merging process. In this work, we adopt the basic version of Surgery
812            combined with task arithmetic for evaluation
813
814   iii) Training-free methods:
815
816          • Weight Averaging directly averages model parameters from multiple tasks into a single
817
               model, enabling multi-task learning without additional training.
818          • Fisher Merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022) leverages the Fisher information matrix to assess
819            parameter importance, merging model parameters based on this importance.
820          • RegMean (Jin et al., 2023) refines weight matrices by adjusting and linearly combining
821            rows, utilizing statistical information derived from the training data.
822          • Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023b) introduces the concept of a “task vector,” defined
823            as the difference between fine-tuned model parameters and pre-trained model parameters.
824            Multiple task vectors are then combined and added to the pre-trained model to facilitate
825            multi-task learning.
826
             • Ties-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) eliminates unimportant parameters from the task vector
827            and resolves sign conflicts among parameters, reducing interference during the final task
828            vector merging process.
829
830   iv) Our methods:
831
832          • TATR. This is the core method introduced in our work, which applies task arithmetic within
833
               the trust region.
834          • TATR zero-shot A zero-shot variant of TATR that utilizes task vectors to estimate the
835            gradient as described in Eq.(7). Other zero-shot variations follow a similar approach.
836          • Ties-Merging & TATR integrates TATR into the Ties-Merging framework by applying
837            TATR’s mask on the task vectors after processing them with Ties-Merging.
838          • AdaMerging & TATR plugged TATR into AdaMerging, where TATR is applied prior to
839            training the AdaMerging coefficients.
840
             • Surgery & TATR. Similarly, TATR is integrated into Surgery by applying it before training
841
               the additional modules introduced by Surgery.

846   B.1   M ETHODOLOGY FOR V ISUALIZING THE L OSS L ANDSCAPE
847
848
      In this section, we outline the methodology for visualizing the loss landscape, which involves three
      key steps:
849
850   Task vector decomposition. To effectively visualize the loss landscape, we first decompose a task-
851   specific vector into three essential components: a positive component (aligned with the gradient
852   direction), a negative component (opposed to the gradient), and an orthogonal component (orthog-
853   onal to the gradient). These components are derived by analyzing the relationship between the task
854
      vector and the gradient, as detailed in Section 5. This decomposition allows us to explore how dif-
      ferent aspects of the task vector interact with the gradient, each contributing uniquely to the overall
855
      optimization behavior.
856
857   Constructing the 2D plane. We arrange these three components in a 2D coordinate system, with
858   the positive component anchored at (0,1), the negative component at (0,0), and the orthogonal com-
859   ponent at (1,0). Additionally, we project the parameters of the pre-trained model onto this plane
860
      using linear combinations of the three components. Although this projection is an approximation, as
      the pre-trained model’s parameters may not lie perfectly within the plane defined by these vectors,
861
      it provides sufficient insight into the interaction between task vectors and knowledge conflict.
862
863   Contour plot generation. Finally, we sample points across the plane by selecting coordinates
      within the range of [-0.2, 1.2] for both axes at intervals of 0.1. For each sampled point, we adjust


                                                        16
864
      the model’s parameters accordingly and compute the corresponding loss value. These loss values
865   are then used to generate a contour plot, providing a visual representation of the loss landscape.
866
867   B.2    L OSS L ANDSCAPE FOR E ACH I NDIVIDUAL TASK
868
869   In this section, we present the loss landscape for each individual task, along with the components
870   of task vectors from the other seven tasks within the landscape. From Figure 5, we observe the
871   same patterns as described in the manuscript. Specifically, the positive component tends to ascend
872   along the gradient direction, while the negative component, despite aligning with the gradient de-
873   scent direction, often overshoots local optima, leading to performance degradation. The orthogonal
874
      component, in general, shows little sensitivity to performance changes. These findings further sup-
      port the generality of our conclusions and provide additional evidence for the effectiveness of the
875
      TATR method.
876
877
      B.3    L OSS L ANDSCAPE FOR A LL TASKS
878
879   Furthermore, we visualize the overall loss landscape across all tasks, including the components of
880   the task vectors. We present the loss landscape under different mask ratios. As shown in Fig-
881   ure 6, we observe similar patterns: both the positive and negative components negatively impact the
882   model’s overall multi-task performance, leading to knowledge conflicts. In contrast, the orthogonal
883   component contributes to improving the model’s multi-task capability.
