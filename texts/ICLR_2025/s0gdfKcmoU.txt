009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper examines multiplayer symmetric constant-sum games with more than
012            two players in a competitive setting, including examples like Mahjong, Poker,
013            and various board and video games. In contrast to two-player zero-sum games,
014            equilibria in multiplayer games are neither unique nor non-exploitable, failing to
015            provide meaningful guarantees when competing against opponents who play dif-
016            ferent equilibria or non-equilibrium strategies. This gives rise to a series of long-
017            lasting fundamental questions in multiplayer games regarding suitable objectives,
018
               solution concepts, and principled algorithms. This paper takes an initial step to-
               wards addressing these challenges by focusing on the natural objective of equal
019
               share—securing an expected payoff of C/n in an n-player symmetric game with
020
               a total payoff of C. We rigorously identify the theoretical conditions under which
021            achieving an equal share is tractable and design a series of efficient algorithms, in-
022            spired by no-regret learning, that provably attain approximate equal share across
023            various settings. Furthermore, we provide complementary lower bounds that jus-
024            tify the sharpness of our theoretical results. Our experimental results highlight
025            worst-case scenarios where meta-algorithms from prior state-of-the-art systems
026            for multiplayer games fail to secure an equal share, while our algorithm succeeds,
027            demonstrating the effectiveness of our approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
      In recent years, AI systems have achieved remarkable success in multi-agent decision-making prob-
032
      lems, particularly in a wide range of strategic games. These include, but are not limited to, Go (Silver
033
      et al., 2016), Mahjong (Li et al., 2020), Poker (Moravčı́k et al., 2017; Brown & Sandholm, 2018;
034   2019), Starcraft 2 (Vinyals et al., 2019), DOTA 2 (Berner et al., 2019), League of Legends (Ye et al.,
035   2020), and Diplomacy (Gray et al., 2020; Bakhtin et al., 2022; , FAIR). Many of these games are
036   two-player zero-sum games1 , where Nash equilibria always exist and can be computed in polynomial
037   time. Nash equilibria in two-player zero-sum games are also non-exploitable—an agent employing
038   a Nash equilibrium strategy will not lose even when facing an adversarial opponent who seeks to
039   exploit the agent’s weaknesses. Although such equilibrium strategies do not necessarily capitalize
040   on opponents’ weaknesses or guarantee large-margin victories, human players often adopt subop-
041   timal strategies that deviate significantly from equilibria in complex games with large state spaces.
042
      Consequently, AI agents who adopt equilibrium strategies often outperform humans in practice for
      two-player zero-sum games.
043
044   In contrast, multiplayer games—defined here as those with more than two players—exhibit fun-
045   damentally different game structures compared to two-player zero-sum games. This distinction
046   introduces several unique challenges. Firstly, Nash equilibria are believed to be no longer com-
047   putable in polynomial time (Daskalakis et al., 2009; Chen & Deng, 2005). Moreover, there may
048
      exist multiple Nash equilibria with distinct values. Such non-uniqueness in equilibria raises a criti-
      cal concern about the adoption of equilibrium strategies in multiplayer settings: if a learning agent
049
      adopts an equilibrium that is different from other players, collectively, they are not playing any sin-
050
      gle equilibrium, which undermines the equilibrium property that dissuades the agent from changing
051   its strategy as long as others maintain theirs. Finally, in multiplayer games, equilibrium strategies

      or without regularization. While the success of these self-play algorithms is remarkable, their per-
068

069

074
075   In this paper, we consider multiplayer symmetric constant-sum games, which are prevalent in games
076   involving more than two players. Examples include previously discussed multiplayer games like
077   Mahjong, Poker, and Diplomacy, as well as a variety of board games such as Avalon (Light et al.,
078   2023), Mafia, and Catan.2 Symmetry brings fairness among players, providing a natural baseline
079   where a learning agent should at least secure an equal share — achieving an expected payoff of
080   C/n in an n-player symmetric game with a total payoff of C. This paper takes an initial step toward
081
      addressing the two fundamental questions highlighted above by focusing on securing an equal share
      in multiplayer symmetric normal-form games3 . The main contributions are summarized as follows:
082
083   1. Regarding the question of solution concepts, we first demonstrate the insufficiency of classical
084   equilibrium concepts and general self-play frameworks (learning from scratch) in achieving an equal
085   share in symmetric games. We then proceed to identify the structural conditions where equal share
086   is achievable. In contrast to two-player zero-sum games, we prove that in order to achieve an equal
087
      share in multiplayer games: (1) all opponents need to deploy the same strategy; (2) all opponents
      must have limited adaptivity, and the learning agent has to model the opponents (See Section 4). We
088
      show that without either condition, an equal share can not be attained in the worst case. We prove
089
      that our identified conditions apply to practical multiplayer gaming platforms with a large player
090   base. They are also tightly connected to the design of many prior modern multiplayer AI agents.
091
092   2. Regarding the question of principled algorithms, this paper illustrates how we can leverage ex-
093
      isting tools from no-regret learning and no-dynamic-regret learning communities to achieve equal
      share with provable guarantees. Concretely, this paper considers several opponent settings: fixed,
094
      slowly adapting, and opponents that adapt at intermediate rates. For all cases, we design algorithms
095
      that approximately achieve equal share, with an error tolerance of 1/poly(T ), where T is the total
096   number of games played. Additionally, we provide matching lower bounds, demonstrating that these
097   guarantees cannot be significantly improved in the worst-case scenario.
098
099
      3. We further complement our theory by experiments on two basic multiplayer symmetric games.
      Our experimental results illustrate that (1) the self-play meta-algorithms from prior state-of-the-art
100
      systems for multiplayer games can fail to secure an equal share even under favorable settings, while
101
      our principled algorithm always succeeds; (2) prior meta-algorithm has no clear advantage over our
102   algorithm on exploitability in the worst case. This indicates that prior self-play algorithms are not
103   general-purpose and highlights the effectiveness of our theoretical framework.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
109
110   AI gaming agents in practice Building superhuman AI has long been a goal in various games. A
111   large body of works in this line focus on two-player or two-team zero-sum games like Chess (Camp-
112   bell et al., 2002), Go (Silver et al., 2016), Heads-Up Texas Hold’em (Moravčı́k et al., 2017; Brown
113   & Sandholm, 2018), Starcraft 2 (Vinyals et al., 2019), DOTA 2 (Berner et al., 2019) and League of
114   Legends (Ye et al., 2020). Most of them are based on finding equilibria via self-play, fictitious play,
115   league training, etc. There is comparatively much less amount of work on games with more than two
116   players, whose game structures are fundamentally different from two-player zero-sum games. Sev-
117
      eral remarkable multiplayer successes include Poker (Brown & Sandholm, 2019), Mahjong (Li et al.,
      2020), Doudizhu (Zha et al., 2021) and Diplomacy (Bakhtin et al., 2022; , FAIR). Despite lacking a
118
      clearly formulated learning objective, these works typically design meta-algorithms, which include
119
      initially training the model using behavior cloning from opponents, then enhancing it through self-
120   play, and finally applying adaptations based on the game’s specific structure or human expertise. It
121   remains elusive whether such a recipe is generally effective for a wide range of multiplayer games.
122
123
      Existing results for symmetric games Von Neumann & Morgenstern (1947) gave the first defi-
124   nition of symmetric games and used the three-player majority-vote example to showcase the stark
125   difference between symmetric three-player zero-sum games and symmetric two-player zero-sum
126   games. In his seminal paper that introduced Nash equilibrium, Nash proved that a symmetric finite
127   multi-player game must have a symmetric Nash equilibrium (Nash, 1951). However, this existence
128   result holds little significance from an individual standpoint, as there is no reason a priori to assume
129   that other players are indeed playing according to this symmetric equilibrium. Papadimitriou &
130   Roughgarden (2005) studied the computational complexity of finding the Nash equilibrium in sym-
131   metric multi-player games when the number of actions available is much smaller than the number
132
      of players and gave a polynomial-time algorithm for the problem. In this case, symmetry greatly
      reduced the computational complexity (as computing Nash in general is PPAD-hard). Daskalakis
133
      (2009) proposed anonymous games, a generalization of symmetric games.
134
135
136
      No-regret learning in games There is a rich literature on applying no-regret learning algorithms
      to learning equilibria in games. It is well-known that if all agents have no regret, the resulting
137
      empirical average would be an approximate Coarse Correlated Equilibrium (CCE) (Young, 2004),
138
      while if all agents have no swap-regret, the resulting empirical average would be an ϵ-Correlated
139   Equilibrium (CE) (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006). Later work continuing
140   this line of research includes those with faster convergence rates (Syrgkanis et al., 2015; Chen &
141   Peng, 2020; Daskalakis et al., 2021), last-iterate convergence guarantees (Daskalakis & Panageas,
142   2018; Wei et al., 2020), and extension to extensive-form games (Celli et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022b;a;
143   Song et al., 2022) and Markov games (Song et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021).

155
156   Normal-form game An n-player normal-form game consists of a finite set of n players, where
157   each player has an action space Ai and a corresponding payoff function Ui : A1 × · · · × An →
158   [−1, 1] with Ui (a1 , . . . , an ) denotes the payoff received by the i-th player if n players are taking
159   Pn actions (a1 , . . . , an ). We define a game as constant-sum if there exists constant C such that
      joint
160      i=1 Ui (a1 , . . . , an ) = C for all joint actions. We further denote a game as zero-sum if it is a
161   constant-sum game with a total payoff of C = 0. Normal-form games can represent a wide range of
      games as their special cases, including sequential games such as extensive-form or Markov games.

      Strategy A (mixed) strategy of a player is a probability distribution over the player’s actions. For
163   i ∈ [n], we use ai ∈ A and xi ∈ ∆(A) to denote an action and a mixed strategy of the i-th player
164   respectively. We use a−i ∈ A⊗n−1 and x−i ∈ ∆(A⊗n−1 ) to denote the actions and the mixed
165   strategies of the other players. We denote Ui (xi , x−i ) := Eai ∼xi ,a−i ∼x−i [Ui (ai , a−i )] .
166
167   Learning protocol We assume that the learner knows the game rule, and thus her own payoff
168   function U1 . At every round t, all players take action simultaneously, and the learner only observes
169   the opponents’ noisy actions (at2 , at3 , . . . , atn ) that are sampled from their strategies.
170
171   Best response Given a mixed strategy x−i of the other n − 1 players, the best response set
172   BRi (x−i ) of the i-th player is defined as BRi (x−i ) := arg maxai ∈Ai Ui (ai , x−i ).
173
174   Equilibrium Nash Equilibrium (NE) is the most commonly-used solution concept for games: a
175   mixed strategy x ∈ ∆(A1 × · · · × An ) of all players is said to be NE if x is a product distribution
      4
176     , and no player could gain by deviating from her own strategy while holding all other players’
177
      strategies fixed. That is, for all i ∈ [n] and a′i ∈ Ai , Ea∼x [Ui (ai , a−i )] ≥ Ea∼x [Ui (a′i , a−i )].
178   There are also two equilibrium notions relaxing the notion of NE by no longer requiring x to be
179   a product distribution. It allows general joint distribution x which describes correlated strategies
180   among players. In particular, (1) x is a Correlated Equilibrium (CE) if for all i ∈ [n] and a′i ∈ Ai ,
181   Ea∼x [Ui (ai , a−i ) | ai ] ≥ Ea∼x [Ui (a′i , a−i ) | ai ], and (2) x is Coarse Correlated Equilibrium
182   (CCE) if for all i ∈ [n] and a′i ∈ Ai : Ea∼x [Ui (ai , a−i )] ≥ Ea∼x [Ui (a′i , a−i )]. The major difference
183
      between those two notions is in the cases when the agent deviates from her current strategy, whether
      she is still allowed to observe the randomness in drawing actions from the correlated strategy. The
184
      relationship among various equilibrium concepts is encapsulated by NE ⊂ CE ⊂ CCE.
185
186
      Two-player zero-sum games It is well-known that in two-player zero-sum games, all Nash equi-
187
      libria share the unique payoff value 0. Furthermore, a Nash equilibrium is non-exploitable against
188   any strategy that is not necessarily an equilibrium. In math, if (µ⋆ , ν ⋆ ) is the Nash equilibrium, we
189   have minν U1 (µ⋆ , ν) = maxµ minν U1 (µ, ν) = 0.
190
191   Multiplayer or general-sum games When the number of players is greater than two, or the games
192   are no longer constant-sum games, Nash equilibria become PPAD-hard to compute (Daskalakis
193   et al., 2009) while CEs and CCEs can be still computed in polynomial time. All of these three
194   concepts admit multiple equilibria with distinct payoffs. Furthermore, they no longer own strong
195   guarantees, such as non-exploitness, when competing against non-equilibrium players.

197   2.2      S YMMETRIC GAMES AND EQUAL SHARE
198
199   Symmetric games For an n-player normal-form game with an action space {Ai }ni=1 and a payoff
200
      {Ui }ni=1 , we say the game is symmetric if (1) Ai = A, for all i ∈ [n]; (2) for any permutation
      σ : [n] → [n], we have Ui (a1 , · · · , an ) = Uσ−1 (i) (aσ(1) , · · · , aσ(n) ).
201
202   In short, the payoffs of a symmetric game for employing a specific action are determined solely by
203   the actions used by others, agnostic of the identities of the players using them. Thus, the payoff
204   function of the first player denoted as U1 , is sufficient to encapsulate the entire game.
205   Symmetric games are popular in practice as they bring fairness among players. Technically, all
206   asymmetric can be converted to symmetric games by randomizing the roles of the players at the
207   beginning of the game. Nevertheless, casting the games in the symmetric form gives a natural and
208   minimal baseline — the learning agent should attain an equal share in the long run.
209
210   Equal share We say an agent attains an equal share, if the agent’s average payoff of the games is
211   at least C/n for a n-player symmetric constant-sum game with a total payoff of C.
212   It is not hard to see that shifting the total payoff of a game by any absolute constant will not alter the
213   strategic aspects of the game. Therefore, without loss of generality, this paper sets a total payoff of
214   C = 0 and focuses on achieving an equal share in multiplayer symmetric zero-sum games.

223   3   I NSUFFICIENCY OF E QUILIBRIA AND S ELF - PLAY FOR E QUAL S HARE
224
225   In this section, we demonstrate that existing equilibria notions and the self-play from scratch al-
226   gorithm are not sufficient to secure an equal share in multiplayer symmetric zero-sum games even
227   under very basic settings. To illustrate this, we consider the following 3-player majority vote game:
228   Example 1 (Three-player majority vote game). Every player chooses either 0 or 1. If all players
229   take the same action, then they receive a payoff of 0. Otherwise, being in the majority yields a
230   positive payoff of 1/2, while being in the minority results in a negative payoff of −1.
231
232   Insufficiency of equilibria In this setup, both pure strategies (0, 0, 0) and (1, 1, 1) constitute NE.
233   However, the existence of multiple NEs creates a predicament for the learning agent. It must choose
234   which equilibrium to follow, yet there is always the risk that the two opponents are both playing the
235
      other NE, leading to a negative payoff for the learner. In other words, adhering to a single NE does
      not reliably ensure an equal share when multiple equilibria exist. Since NE ⊂ CE ⊂ CCE, we
236
      know the same limitation also holds for CE and CCE.
237
238
      Insufficiency of self-play from scratch Self-play is a training method in which the learning agent
239   improves its performance by repeatedly playing against copies of itself without human supervision.
240   See pseudo-code in Algorithm 1. The learner maintains its own strategy {xt }Tt=1 . At the tth iteration,
241   the learner first pretends that all opponents are employing its current strategy xt , and samples actions
242   from them. Then the learner updates its own strategy to xt+1 using the gradient information from
243   the gameplay. The updates can be made using any optimizer such as gradient descent or Hedge.
244
      Here, we argue that self-play from scratch (the algorithm adopted in Brown & Sandholm (2019))
245   again fails to secure an equal share in the same three-player majority vote game: Consider two
246   opponents play the same fixed strategy that is one of the NEs. In this case, the learner has no choice
247   but to play the exact same NE as the opponents to secure an equal share. That is, if the learner’s
248   algorithm is agnostic to the strategies of the opponents, it is doomed to fail. We note that while
249   recent systems (Li et al., 2020; Jacob et al., 2022) combine self-play with behavior cloning which is
250   no longer agnostic to opponents’ strategies, our experiment shows that their meta-algorithms remain
251   insufficient to secure an equal share in the worst-case scenario (See Section 6).

253   4   S UFFICIENT C ONDITIONS FOR S ECURING E QUAL S HARE
254
255   In this section, we identify the structural conditions of the games where equal share is achievable.
256   We will show that the following two conditions are needed to achieve equal share:
257
258   Condition 1. All opponents need to deploy the same strategy, i.e., x2 = . . . = xn ;
259   Condition 2. All opponents must have limited adaptivity and the player has to model the opponents,
260                i.e., {xj }nj=2 can not adversarially change across different rounds of the game.
261

262
      attained in multiplayer symmetric games in the worst case. We remark that both conditions restrict
263
      the strategies of opponents rather than the type of games.

433
434   In this section, we focus on the scenario where one learning agent competes against n − 1 opponents
435   who play the identical meta-strategy. For simplicity, we restrict ourselves to the setting of fixed
436   opponents. We aim to answer: (Q1) Can existing algorithmic frameworks in previous superhuman
437   AI systems consistently secure an equal share under this favorable setting? If not, what are the
438   failure cases? (Q2) Are these trained agents exploitable by adversarial opponents? We design the
439   following two games to compare our algorithm with prior self-play-based algorithms.
440
441   Majority Vote (MV). We first consider the standard 3-player majority vote game (Example 1). It
442   is not hard to see that [1, 0], [0, 1], and [1/2, 1/2] are all NEs, where [p, 1 − p] denotes the mixture
443
      strategy that takes the first action with probability p and the second action with probability 1 − p.
      We fix the opponents’ meta-strategy ymeta = [0.49, 0.51] for all rounds.
444
445
      Switch Dominance Game (SDG). In each round, players simultaneously choose an action from
446
      set {A, B, C}. Let n be the total number of players and nA be the number of agents choosing action
447
      A, We define the game rule as:
448
449                                        B ≻ A ≻ C if nA > 0.2n,
450                                        C ≻ B ≻ A otherwise ,
451   where the rule i ≻ j ≻ k intuitively means that action i dominates both j and k, and action j
452   dominates k. SDG is designed so that C is a dominated action when there is a reasonable number
453   of players taking action A, but a dominating action otherwise. Concretely, for i ≻ j ≻ k, we assign
454   the following payoff (ri , rj , rk ) to players taking actions (i, j, k) respectively, where:
455                 ri = I[nj + nk > 0], rj = I[nk > 0] − I[nj + nk > 0] · ni /(nj + nk ),
456                 rk = −I[nj + nk > 0] · ni /(nj + nk ) − I[nk > 0] · nj /nk .
457
      This payoff design guarantees that SDG is a symmetric zero-sum game. Throughout our ex-
458   periments, we choose n = 30 and pick the fixed meta-strategy of the opponents ymeta =
459   [0.399, 0.6, 0.001] (in the order of action A, B, C) for all rounds. Note that while this game has
460   an NE strategy [0, 0, 1], its utility is negative against our chosen meta-strategy ymeta .
