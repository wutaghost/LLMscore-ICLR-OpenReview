009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                Large Language Models (LLMs) have become foundational in the realm of natu-
012                ral language processing, demonstrating performance improvements as model sizes
013                increase. The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) approach offers a promising way to scale
014                LLMs more efficiently by using fewer computational FLOPs through sparse ac-
015                tivation. However, it suffers from significant memory overheads, necessitating
016                model compression techniques. Post-training quantization, a popular method for
017
                   model compression, proves less effective when directly applied to MoE models
                   due to MoE’s overlooked inherent sparsity. This paper explores several MoE
018
                   structure-aware quantization heuristics, ranging from coarse to fine granularity,
019
                   from MoE block to individual linear weight. Our investigations reveal critical
020                principles: different MoE structures (i.e., blocks, experts, linear layers) require
021                varying numbers of weight bits for effective and efficient quantization. Conclu-
022                sions are supported by extensive benchmarking across two representative MoE
023                models and six tasks. We further introduce novel enhancements to more accurately
024                identify the most critical weights in MoE quantization that necessitate higher bit
025                allocations, including the linear weight outlier scorer and MoE block scorer. Ad-
026                ditionally, subsequent experiments validate our findings in the context of both
027                weight and activation quantization. Our code for reproducing all our experiments
028
                   is provided as supplemental material.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in various natural language pro-
033
      cessing tasks, such as language understanding, reasoning, and generation, demonstrating superior
034   performance and adaptability Brown et al. (2020); Jiang et al. (2023); Kaplan et al. (2020); OpenAI
035   et al. (2024); Touvron et al. (2023). However, the rapid growth in model size, with state-of-the-art
036   LLMs containing billions of parameters, poses significant challenges to computational resources
037   and memory consumption Aminabadi et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2024); Shoeybi et al. (2020). The
038   Mixture of Experts (MoE) Shazeer et al. (2017) architecture has emerged as a promising solution
039   to address these challenges. MoE allows for the scaling up of LLMs while maintaining roughly
040   constant FLOPs. By incorporating multiple expert networks and employing a sparse gating mech-
041   anism, MoE achieves efficient computation, enabling the development of larger models within the
042
      constraints of limited computational resources Dai et al. (2024); Fedus et al. (2022); Jiang et al.
      (2024).
043
044   Despite its advantages, MoE suffers from extensive memory costs, which hinder its practical de-
045   ployment and widespread adoption. For example, the Mixtral-8x7B Jiang et al. (2024) MoE
046   model takes around 180 GB memory while only 28 GB parameters are activated for each input
047   token1 . Model compression techniques tailored to MoE architectures are essential to address this is-
048
      sue. Existing MoE compression methods can be categorized into two main approaches: merging and
      pruning. Expert merging, such as MC-MoELi et al. (2024), aims to reduce the memory footprint
049
      by combining similar experts based on routing policy and compressing the resulting model using
050
      low-rank decomposition. On the other hand, expert pruning, such as task-specific pruning Chen
051   et al. (2022), focuses on identifying and removing the least important experts or connections based
052   on their contribution to a specific task. However, these approaches ① necessitate model retraining,
053

      which is both extremely costly and time-consuming, particularly for state-of-the-art MoE LLMs
055   of billion-size scale, and ② operate under task-specific settings, which limits their practicality for
056   real-world applications.
057
058
      Post-training quantization has emerged as a promising compression method widely applied to dense
      LLM models. Recent works, such as GPTQ Frantar et al. (2023a), which adapts quantization in-
059
      tervals based on the Hessian information, SmoothQuant Lin et al. (2024), which jointly quantizes
060
      the model weight and activation by offline migrating the activation outliers, have demonstrated the
061   effectiveness of post-training quantization for LLMs toward 4 bits compression.
062
063   However, directly applying existing quantization methods to MoE models in a more extreme quanti-
064
      zation setting, e.g. under 3 bits, leads to suboptimal results, potentially due to the overlooked sparsity
      nature of the MoE architecture. The sparse activation patterns and the dynamic routing mechanism
065
      in MoE pose unique challenges and opportunities for quantization, requiring novel approaches to uti-
066
      lize it effectively. The sparse expert activations in MoE models exhibit different statistical properties
067   methodologies compared to dense activations, making conventional quantization methods difficult.
068   Moreover, the dynamic routing mechanism, which selects a subset of experts for each input token,
069   introduces additional complexity in terms of quantizing the routing weights and maintaining the
070   sparsity pattern during inference. This yields the primary question to be explored:
071
      (Q) Can we leverage the sparsity nature of MoE architecture to establish more efficient and effective
072
      coarse-grained mixed-precision MoE quantization methods?
073
074   To answer (Q), we explore a wide range of MoE structure-aware quantization heuristics, ranging
075   from coarse to fine granularity. We conduct a detailed comparative analysis of each of them, reveal-
076
      ing critical principles: different MoE structures (i.e., blocks, experts, linear layers) require varying
      numbers of weight bits for effective and efficient quantization. Extended from the gained insights,
077
      we propose methods to further improve the efficiency and effectiveness of mixed-precision quanti-
078
      zation, including linear weight quantization scorer and MoE block quantization scorer.
079
080   In summary, our key contributions are listed below:
081

               the Mixture-of-Experts architecture. This benchmark encompasses investigations into four
083
               critical MoE-related heuristics, evaluations across two MoE LLMs, six benchmark tasks,
084
               and a combination of both weight and activation quantization.
085

            2. Our benchmark study uncovers a range of previously unexplored quantization principles
               and insights for MoE. These insights include empirical rules supporting optimal bit allo-

               cation strategies, highlighting the trade-offs such us those between attention and FFNN

095   2    R ELATED W ORKS
096
097   Mixture-of-Experts. The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) approach Shazeer et al. (2017) enhances
098   neural network scalability by using router networks to activate model segments according to input to-
099
      kens selectively. As the dominant architecture in NLP, numerous efforts have adapted feed-forward
      neural networks (FFNNs) within Transformers to incorporate MoE layers, constructing MoE lan-
100
      guage modelsDai et al. (2024); Fedus et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2024). Additionally, several variants
101
      of the standard MoE architecture exist. For example, DeepSeek-MoE Dai et al. (2024) employs
102   numerous finely segmented experts and designates a select few as shared experts to capture com-
103   mon knowledge. MoE’s application in LLMs is widely acknowledged for its superior generative
104   abilities and remarkable computing efficiency Artetxe et al. (2022); Dai et al. (2024); Fedus et al.
105   (2022); Jiang et al. (2024); Krajewski et al. (2024); Rajbhandari et al. (2022). The recent work Mix-
106   tral Jiang et al. (2024) illustrates that MoE can match the performance of equivalent full-parameter
107   LLMs while utilizing far fewer active parameters. However, MoE suffers from significant memory
      overhead issues, posing challenges to its efficient deployment Li et al. (2024).

108
      MoE Compression. MoE models benefit from reduced FLOPs but are constrained by their sig-
109   nificant memory overhead. Current works to reduce the memory overhead of MoE models mainly
110   focus on reducing the number of experts. An earlier approach Chen et al. (2022) involves pruning
111   non-essential experts for a specific downstream task during fine-tuning, utilizing statistics based on
112   cumulative usage frequency. Another method, MC-SMoE Li et al. (2024), introduces a pipeline
113   that identifies and groups similar experts, subsequently merging them and further decomposing the
114   merged expert into low-rank components within each group. However, these approaches are de-
115   veloped under task-specific fine-tuning settings and do not explore the development of the MoE
116   compression towards a general post-training model.
117
118   Post-Training Quantization. Post-training quantization reduces computational and storage de-
119   mands by converting pre-trained models from high-precision to lower-precision formats without
120   extensive retraining Frantar et al. (2023b;a). It has been widely applied to LLMs, optimizing
121
      them for deployment on resource-constrained devices. Techniques like layer-wise quantization and
      mixed-precision schemes are designed for minimal performance degradation while reducing model
122
      size and computational requirements efficiently Liu et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2023); Sharify et al.
123
      (2024). Recent methods such as SmoothQuant Xiao et al. (2024), GPTQ Frantar et al. (2023a),
124   AWQ Lin et al. (2024), and address specific challenges for LLMs. SmoothQuant Xiao et al. (2024)
125   ensures smooth precision transitions across layers, reducing quantization errors and maintaining
126   performance. GPTQ Frantar et al. (2023a) employs layer-wise and mixed-precision quantization
127   to balance efficiency and accuracy. AWQ Lin et al. (2024) adapts to weight sensitivity, preserving
128   critical weights’ precision while aggressively quantizing less sensitive ones. These advancements
129   in PTQ enable significant reductions in computational and storage requirements while preserving
130   LLM performance.

136   The primary objective of this work is to benchmark several MoE-related heuristics combined with
137   established LLM quantization techniques. Given that the substantial memory overhead of MoE
138   models predominantly originates from their weights, we adopt GPTQ Frantar et al. (2023a), a pop-
139   ular weight quantization method. GPTQ executes layer-by-layer weight quantization by addressing
140   a specific reconstruction problem for each layer. Specifically, let W represent the weights of a lin-
141   ear layer and X denote the input to that layer derived from a small subset of calibration data, the
142   reconstruction problem is defined as follows:

351   Insight. From the quantization perspective, the larger the range of a weight magnitude group, the
352
      more difficult it will be for quantization. We found that, in MoE, each FFNN linear weight matrix
      consists predominantly of values within a narrow range, interspersed with a few significant outliers.
353

      challenging to quantize effectively, thereby necessitating a higher allocation of quantization bits.

357   the largest to the average absolute magnitude within each column. Specifically, for a weight matrix

418
      Inspired by Q3 in Section 4.2, which demonstrates that allocating more bits to different MoE blocks
419
      yields variable performance improvements, we propose a novel method to identify and quantize
420
      those critical blocks with additional bits. Specifically, this section outlines our approach to calculat-
421   ing importance scores for bit allocation using a data-driven method with a lightweight predictor.
422
      Insight. We find an increasing cosine similarity between the tensors generated before and after
423   the FFN blocks for some of the MoE blocks, indicating less important computation results produced
424   by these blocks. This observation also aligns with observations on dense models in previous lit-
425   erature Jaiswal et al. (2024). Therefore, the basic idea is that less accurate output of these blocks
426   producing tokens with high cosine similarity will not affect the overall model performance much,
427   thus lower weight bits might not hurt performance much.
428   Methodology. To capture the generalized hidden states’ dynamic information of each MoE block,
429   we train a small two-layer FFNN with a tangent activation function. This network predicts the
430   cosine similarity between the input and output hidden states. We utilize a dataset of 400 random
431   sequences, each containing 1024 tokens from the WikiText dataset Merity et al. (2016), for training.
      The detailed training procedure is in Algorithm 2. During quantization, we employ this predictor

      6    C ONCLUSION
478
      This work investigates various heuristic-based MoE quantization methods in the post-training set-
479
      ting. While vanilla quantization techniques (e.g., GPTQ) prove less effective and efficient when ap-
480   plied directly to MoE models, determining which MoE model components should be allocated more
481   quantization bits remains an open question. We present the first benchmark study on MoE quantiza-
482   tion, revealing critical heuristic-based principles, such as the importance disparities among different
483   MoE blocks. Drawing on these insights, we introduce innovative techniques, including a block im-
484   portance predictor and a linear layer outlier range scorer, to more precisely identify components
485   that benefit from increased bit quantization. These methods substantially improve the quantization
      process’s effectiveness and efficiency for MoE models.

      A.1   E VALUATION DATASETS
651
652   In this section, we introduce details of the datasets in our evaluation. For a more comprehensive
653   study, we have selected six popular benchmark tasks: WinoGrande, COPA, OpenBookQA (OBQA),
654   HellaSwag, and MMLU.
655   WinoGrande ai2 (2019) is a large-scale dataset designed for commonsense reasoning, consisting
656   of pronoun resolution problems. Each instance in the dataset presents a sentence with an ambiguous
657   pronoun that needs to be resolved based on context. This task tests the model’s ability to understand
658   and reason about everyday situations.
659
      The Choice of Plausible Alternatives (COPA) dataset Gordon et al. (2012) focuses on causal
660
      reasoning. Each question in COPA consists of a premise and two choices, where the model must
661   select the more plausible alternative. This task evaluates the model’s understanding of cause-and-
662   effect relationships in natural language.
663
664
      OpenBookQA Mihaylov et al. (2018) is a multiple-choice question-answering dataset that requires
      the model to use both scientific facts and commonsense knowledge. The dataset challenges the
665
      model’s ability to combine factual knowledge with reasoning to answer questions correctly.
666
667   HellaSwag Zellers et al. (2019) is a benchmark for commonsense NLI (Natural Language Inference)
668   that tests the model’s ability to predict the most plausible continuation of a given sentence. The
669   dataset contains scenarios from various domains, such as cooking and sports, requiring the model to
670
      understand context and plausibility.
671   The Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark Hendrycks et al. (2021)
672   evaluates models across a wide range of subjects, from elementary mathematics to law. For this
673   study, we report performance on MMLU with a 5-shot setting, where the model is given five exam-
674   ples per task before evaluation, allowing us to gauge the model’s few-shot learning capabilities.
675   We perform a zero-shot evaluation on WinoGrande, COPA, OpenBookQA, and HellaSwag, where
676   the model is not provided with any task-specific training examples. For MMLU, a 5-shot evaluation
677   protocol is adopted, providing five examples per task. This setup helps us assess the generalization
678   ability of the models across different types of reasoning and knowledge-based tasks.
679
680   A.2   R ANDOM S EED
681
682   For all the random selection experiments, we use random seeds {42, 43, 44} to conduct three inde-
683   pendent trials and then report the standard deviation and mean.
