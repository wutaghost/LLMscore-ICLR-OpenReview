                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            As deep learning continues to evolve, the need for data efficiency becomes increas-
012            ingly important. Considering labeling large datasets is both time-consuming and
013            expensive, active learning (AL) provides a promising solution to this challenge by
014            iteratively selecting the most informative subsets of examples to train deep neural
015            networks, thereby reducing the labeling cost. However, the effectiveness of differ-
016            ent AL algorithms can vary significantly across data scenarios, and determining
017            which AL algorithm best fits a given task remains a challenging problem. This work
018
               presents the first differentiable AL strategy search method, named AutoAL, which
               is designed on top of existing AL sampling strategies. AutoAL consists of two
019
               neural nets, named SearchNet and FitNet, which are optimized concurrently under
020
               a differentiable bi-level optimization framework. For any given task, SearchNet and
021            FitNet are iteratively co-optimized using the labeled data, learning how well a set
022            of candidate AL algorithms perform on that task. With the optimal AL strategies
023            identified, SearchNet selects a small subset from the unlabeled pool for querying
024            their annotations, enabling efficient training of the task model. Experimental results
025            demonstrate that AutoAL consistently achieves superior accuracy compared to
026            all candidate AL algorithms and other selective AL approaches, showcasing its
027            potential for adapting and integrating multiple existing AL methods across diverse
028            tasks and domains.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   With the development of deep learning (DL) techniques, large volumes of high-quality labeled data
033   have become increasingly crucial as the model training processes are usually data hungry Ren et al.
034   (2021); Zhan et al. (2022). Active learning (AL) addresses this challenge by iteratively selecting
035   the most informative unlabeled samples for annotation, thereby boosting model efficiency Xie et al.
036   (2021); Wang et al. (2023). Deep active learning strategies are typically divided into two categories:
037   uncertainty-based and representativeness/diversity-based approaches Sener & Savarese (2017); Zhu
038   & Bento (2017); Ash et al. (2019). Uncertainty-based methods focus on querying samples the model
039
      is most uncertain about, while diversity-based strategies aim to select a diverse subset of samples
      that effectively represent the entire dataset Citovsky et al. (2021). Both strategies have limitations,
040
      especially in batch selection and varying dataset complexities, leading to the inconsistency of the
041
      strategy efficiency Ren et al. (2021). Hybrid strategies, which balance uncertainty and diversity Zhan
042   et al. (2021a;b), offer partial solutions. However, these strategies rely on strategy choices and
043   subjective experiment settings, which can be ineffective in real-life applications.
044
045
      Previous works Baram et al. (2004); Hsu & Lin (2015); Pang et al. (2018) have explored selecting
      among different AL strategies to achieve optimal selections without human effort. SelectAL Hacohen
046
      & Weinshall (2024) introduces a method for dynamically choosing AL strategies for different deep
047
      learning tasks and budgets by estimating the relative budget size of the problem. It involves evaluating
048   the impact of removing small subsets of data points from the unlabeled pool to predict how different
049   AL strategies would perform. However, this strategy depends on approximating the reduction in
050   generalization error on small subsets, which would not fully capture the complexities of real-world
051   tasks. Zhang et al. (2024) proposes to select the optimal batch of AL strategy from hundreds of
052   candidates, aiming to maximize future cumulative rewards based on noisy past reward observations
053   of each candidate algorithm. However, both the computational cost and the lack of differentiability
      make the optimization of these works inefficient.

054
      To address these challenges, we propose AutoAL, an automated active learning framework that
055   leverages differentiable query strategy search. AutoAL offers two key advantages: 1) it enables
056   automatic and efficient updates during optimization, allowing the model to adapt seamlessly; 2) it is
057   inherently data-driven, leveraging the underlying data distribution to guide the AL strategy selection.
058   However, integrating multiple AL strategies into the candidate pool makes achieving differentiability
059   difficult. To overcome this issue, AutoAL leverages a bi-level optimization framework that smoothly
060   integrates and optimizes different AL strategies. We design two neural networks: SearchNet and
061   FitNet. FitNet is trained on the labeled dataset to adaptively yield the informativeness of each
062   unlabeled sample. Guided by FitNet’s task loss, SearchNet is efficiently optimized to accurately
063
      select the best AL strategy. Instead of searching over a discrete set of candidate AL strategies, we
      relax the search space to a continuous domain, allowing SearchNet to be optimized via gradient
064
      descent based on FitNet’s output. The gradient-based optimization, which is more data-efficient than
065
      traditional black-box searches, enables AutoAL to achieve state-of-the-art performance in strategy
066   selection with significantly reduced computational costs. Our key contributions are as follows:
067
068   • We introduce a novel AutoAL framework for differentiable AL query strategy search. To the best of
069     our knowledge, AutoAL is the first automatic query strategy search algorithm that can be trained in
070     a differientiable way, achieving highly efficient updates of query strategy in a data-driven manner.
071   • AutoAL is built upon existing AL strategies (i.e., uncertainty-based and diversity-based). By
072     incorporating most AL strategies into its search space, AutoAL leverages the advantages of these
073     strategies by finding the most effective ones for specific AL settings, objectives, or data distributions.
074   • We evaluate AutoAL across various AL tasks. Given the importance of AL in domain-specific tasks,
075     particularly where data quality and imbalance are concerns, we conducte extensive experiments on
076     both nature image datasets and medical datasets. The results demonstrate that AutoAL consistently
077     outperforms all candidate strategies and other state-of-the-art AL methods across these datasets.

080   2    R ELATED W ORK
081
082   Active Learning. Active Learning has been studied for decades to improve the model performance
083   using small sets of labeled data, significantly reducing the annotation cost Cohn et al. (1996); Settles
084   (2009); Zhan et al. (2021b). Most AL research focuses on pool-based AL, which identifies and
085
      selects the most informative samples from a large unlabeled pool iteratively. Pool-based AL sampling
      strategies are generally categorized into two primary branches: diversity-based and uncertainty-based.
086
      The diversity-based methods select the batch of samples that can best represent the entire dataset
087
      distribution. CoreSet Sener & Savarese (2017) selects a batch of representative points from a core set,
088   which is a subsample of the dataset that effectively serves as a proxy for the entire set. In contrast,
089   uncertainty-based methods focus on selecting the samples with high uncertainty, which is due to
090   the data generation or the modeling/learning process. Bayesian Active Learning by Disagreements
091   (BALD) Gal et al. (2017) chooses data points that are expected to maximize the mutual information
092   between predictions and model posterior. Meta-Query Net Park et al. (2022) trains an MLP that
093   receives one open-set score and one AL score as input and outputs a balanced meta-score for sample
094   selection in the open-set dilemma. Yoo & Kweon (2019) incorporates a loss prediction strategy by
095   appending a compact parametric module designed to forecast the loss of unlabeled inputs relative to
096
      the target model. This is achieved by minimizing the discrepancy between the predicted loss and the
      actual target loss. It requires subjective judgment and strategic selection of active learning methods
097
      to be effective on specific datasets in real-world settings.
098
099
      Adaptive Sample Selection in AL. Neither diversity-based nor uncertainty-based methods are
100
      perfect in all data scenarios. Diversity-based methods tend to perform better when the dataset
101
      contains rich category content and large batch size, while uncertainty-aware methods typically
102   perform better in opposite settings Zhan et al. (2021b); Citovsky et al. (2021). Several past works
103   have explored adaptive selection in active learning to solve this problem. A common approach is to
104   choose the best AL strategy from a set of candidate methods and use it to query unlabeled data, such
105   as Hacohen & Weinshall (2024) and Zhang et al. (2024) introduced in Section 1. Active Learning By
106   Learning (ALBL) Hsu & Lin (2015) adaptively selects from a set of existing algorithms based on
107   their estimated contribution to learning performance. It frames each candidate as a bandit machine,
      converting the problem into a multi-armed bandit scenario. However, none of these methods leverage

108
      the differentiable framework for automatic AL selection. Compared to the methods mentioned above,
109   AutoAL first relaxes the search space to be continuous, thus can optimize automatically via gradient
110   descent. This framework enables AutoAL to seamlessly integrate multiple existing AL strategies
111   and rapidly adapt to select the optimal strategy based on the labeled pool. The simple gradient-based
112   optimization is much more data-efficient than traditional black-box searches. Therefore, AutoAL can
113   significantly reduce computational costs compared to these methods.
114
115
      Bilevel Optimization. Bilevel optimization is a hierarchical mathematical framework where the
116   feasible region of one optimization task is constrained by the solution set of another optimization
117   task Liu et al. (2021). It has gained significant attention due to its nested problem structure, which
118   allows the two tasks to optimize jointly. Bilevel optimization adapts to many DL applications Chen
119   et al. (2022), such as hyperparameter optimization Chen et al. (2019); MacKay et al. (2019), meta-
120   knowledge extraction Finn et al. (2017), neural architecture search Liu et al. (2018); Hu et al. (2020),
121   and active learning Sener & Savarese (2017). For instance, Sener & Savarese (2017) formulates AL
122   as a bi-level coreset selection problem and designs a 2-approximation method to select a subset of
123   data that represents the entire dataset. In this work, we novelly formulate the Al query strategy search
124
      as a bi-level optimization problem, i.e., iteratively updating FitNet and using its task loss to guide the
      optimization of SearchNet. We further reformulate it as a differentiable learning paradigm to enable
125
      a more efficient selection of examples across various objectives and data distributions.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING

132
      Pool-based AL focuses on selecting the most informative data iteratively from a large pool of
133   unlabeled independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data samples until a fixed budget is exhausted
134   or the expected model performance has been reached. Specifically, in AL processes, assuming that
135   we have an initial seed labeled set L = {(xj , yj )}M                                                N
                                                          j=1 and a large unlabeled data pool U = {xi , }i=1 ,
136   where M ≪ N , yi ∈ {0, 1} is the class label of xi for binary classification and yi ∈ {1, ..., p}
137   for multi-class classification. In each iteration, we select a batch of the most informative data
138   Q∗ = arg maxbx∈U α(x, Ω) with batch size b from U based on the basic learned model Ω and the
139   acquisition function α(x, Ω), and query their labels from oracle/annotator. With these samples, the
140   expected loss function f of the basic learned model can be minimized. L and U are then updated.
141   The performance of existing AL methods relies on the choice of query strategies, which should be
142   carefully adapted to different tasks or applications. To achieve a robust and adaptive AL performance,
143   this work novelly creates an automated AL strategy selection framework. AutoAL integrates two
144   neural nets, FitNet ΩF and SearchNet ΩS upon each basic learned model Ω to select the best AL
145   strategy before selecting Q. To facilitate automatic selection, AutoAL incorporates them into a
146   bi-level optimization framework and relaxes the search space to enable differentiable updates. We
147   discuss more details of AutoAL framework in the following sections.
148
149   3.2   AUTOMATED ACTIVE L EARNING
150
151   We propose Automated Active Learning (AutoAL), which aims to adaptively deliver the optimal
152   query strategy for each sample in a given unlabeled data pool. Specifically, AutoAL consists of
153   two neural networks: FitNet ΩF and SearchNet ΩS . ΩS selects the optimal AL strategy from a
154   set A, which contains K candidate AL sampling strategies {Aκ }κ∈[K] (e.g., BALD, Maximum
155   Entropy, etc). ΩF models the data distribution within the unlabeled dataset and guides the training of
156   SearchNet ΩS . Since annotations for the unlabeled data cannot be accessed directly by the model Ω,
157   AutoAL requires no data from the unlabeled pool U , using only the labeled dataset L for training.
158   In AutoAL, each AL iteration consists of C cycles. In each cycle c, the labeled data L is randomly
159   split into two equal-sized subsets: training and validation sets. FitNet ΩF computes the cross-entropy
160   loss L Zhang & Sabuncu (2018) for data. Meanwhile, SearchNet ΩS generates scores for the samples,
161   which are used to rank them. This raises a key question: If only the training set is available, without
      access to the unlabeled pool, can ΩS still effectively select the optimal samples?

442   4.4            AL S ELECTION S TRATEGY
443
444   Here, we examine which AL strategy is prioritized by AutoAL in different active learning rounds.
445
      For each AL strategy candidate, we calculate the AL score of each image according to Eq. 6. Notice
      that we don’t sum all the scores according to the dimension of each active learning, but compute the
446
      average value of all images over a round, and normalize the results for visualization. As illustrated in
447
      Fig. 5, in both experiments, KMeans dominate the sample selection during the initial rounds, such
448   as the first round, but be de-prioritized in subsequent rounds. This indicates that diversity-based
449   measures, which select samples that represent diverse regions of the data distribution, are more
450   critical in the early stages of active learning. In the early rounds, when only a small percentage of the
451   data has been labeled, the model’s understanding of the entire data distribution is limited. As a result,
452   selecting samples that cover a broad spectrum of the dataset becomes crucial to provide the model
453   with a more comprehensive view of the data.
454   On Cifar-100 dataset, both Least Confidence and MeanSTD, two uncertainty-based measures, demon-
455   strate improved performance in the final rounds. AutoAL assigns them higher priority to enhance
456   overall performance. As the AL process progresses and more diverse data become available, the
457   model attempts to develop a better understanding of the underlying structure of the target data.
458   Uncertainty-based measures are more effective now because the major challenge switches to refining
459   the decision boundaries. The transition from diversity to uncertainty-based strategies is consistent
460   with the model’s evolving needs. In early rounds, the model requires diversity to build a broad
461   understanding of the data, but once that foundation is established, its focus shifts toward uncertainty,
462
      which helps fine-tune model decision-making ability. This adaptive approach ensures that the model
      targets the most diverse subsets at each stage of the learning process, from broad exploration in the
463
      early rounds to fine-grained exploitation in the later rounds. By automatically adjusting the priorities
464
      of these strategies, AutoAL improves the overall accuracy and shows stronger robustness.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this paper, we have introduced AutoAL, the first automated search framework for deep active
530   learning. AutoAL employs two neural networks, SearchNet and FitNet, integrated into a bilevel
531
      optimization framework to enable joint optimizations. To efficiently solve the bi-level optimization
      problem, we have proposed a probabilistic query strategy that relaxes the search space from discrete to
532
      continuous, enabling a differentiable and data-driven learning paradigm for AutoAL. This framework
533
      not only accelerates the search process but also ensures that AutoAL can generalize across a wide
534   range of tasks and datasets. Furthermore, AutoAL is flexible, allowing for easy integration of most of
535   the existing active learning strategies into the candidate pool, making it adaptable to evolving active
536   learning techniques. Our extensive empirical evaluation, conducted across both natural and medical
537   image datasets, has demonstrated the robustness and generalizability of AutoAL. The results show that
538   AutoAL consistently outperforms baselines, highlighting its capability to optimize sample selection
539   and enhance model performance. In future, we plan to apply AutoAL to boarder machine learning
      tasks such as structured prediction and consider more sophisticated AL method combinations.
