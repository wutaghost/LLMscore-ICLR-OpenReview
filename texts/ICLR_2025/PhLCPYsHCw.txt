009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Hierarchical Federated Learning (HFL) advances the classic Federated Learning
012            (FL) by introducing the multi-layer architecture between clients and the central
013            server, in which edge servers aggregate models from respective clients and further
014            send to the central server. Instead of directly uploading each update from clients
015            for aggregation, the HFL not only reduces the communication and computational
016            overhead but also greatly enhances the scalability of supporting a massive num-
017            ber of clients. When HFL operates for applications having a large-scale clients,
018            edge servers train their models in a cyclic pattern (a ring architecture) as opposed
019
               to the star-type of architecture where each edge develops their own models in-
               dependently.We refer it as Cyclic HFL(CHFL). Driven by its promising feature
020
               of handling data heterogeneity and resiliency, CHFL has a great potential to be
021
               deployed in practice. Unfortunately, the thorough convergence analysis on CHFL
022            remains lacking, especially considering the widely-existing data heterogeneity
023            issue among clients. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to provide a
024            theoretical convergence analysis for CHFL in strongly convex, general convex,
025            and non-convex objectives. Our results demonstrate √       the convergence rate are
026            Õ(1/M N RKT ) for strongly  √   convex  objective, O(1/     M N RKT ) for general
027            convex objective, and O(1/ M N RKT ) for non-convex objective, under standard
028            assumptions. Here, M is the number of edge servers, N is the number of clients
029            in edge, K is local steps in client, and R is the edge training round. Through
030            extensive experiments on real-world datasets, besides validating our theoretical
031            findings, we further show CHFL achieves a comparable or superior performance
032            when accounting for both inter- and intra-edge data heterogeneity.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Federated Learning (FL) McMahan et al. (2017) has emerged as a promising distributed learning
038   framework in which a large number of distributed devices collaborate to train a joint model without
039
      sharing their data. Although FL has attracted a significant interest on theoretical research, the standard
      FL architecture does not always perform well in practical scenarios. When the number of clients
040
      (devices) participate in the FL, the communication burden in between the central server and each
041
      client for model update and aggregation may significantly impact the FL performance. As a solution,
042   the edge-based FL has been proposed Wu et al. (2020) to replace a single central server with multiple
043   edge servers. In particular, each edge server acts as a parameter server responsible for a smaller set
044   of clients, who can meet specific communication or data requirements. However, as stated in Wang
045   et al. (2021a), this edge-based FL still suffers the performance drop due to the limited number of
046   participating clients managed by each edge.
047
      To alleviate the communication burden on a central server, Hierarchical Federated Learning (HFL)
048   Liu et al. (2020); Deng et al. (2021) introduces a three-layer architecture, i.e., a central server, multiple
049   edge servers, and clients. Each edge server, along with its associated clients, forms an edge. The HFL
050   has two levels of model updates including edge model update and global model update, in which
051   many edge model updates follow the star architecture Lee et al. (2020) to aggregate local model
052   updates from clients. On the other hand, most HFL works Liu et al. (2022; 2023; 2020); Khan et al.
053   (2023) also assume the global model update follows the star architecture, i.e., the centralized HFL,
      and provides convergence analysis based on various assumptions. Rather than the star architecture,

054
      for many FL applications across a large geographic regions Zhu et al. (2021); Paulik et al. (2021);
055   Yang et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2020), the ring architecture is a better fit in terms of the participation
056   pattern, where each edge server updates their models to another edge server rather than the central
057   server, namely, the Cyclic HFL (CHFL). Compared to the star-like architecture with centralized HFL,
058   CHFL improves scalability by accommodating more clients without being affected by central server
059   dropout issues when the communication burden increases. Unfortunately, while a few recent works
060   Li & Lyu (2024); Cho et al. (2023) have discussed the convergence rate in the cyclic pattern, they are
061   limited to FL, and a comprehensive analysis under standard assumptions in HFL remains lacking. In
062   this work, our contributions are as follows,
063
064          • We derive convergence guarantees for CHFL on heterogeneous data under standard as-
065            sumptions for strongly convex, general convex, and non-convex objectives, all of which
066            are compared with the state-of-the-art as in Tab. 1. We note that our convergence rates
067            have the highly desirable speedup effect in terms of both edge server number M and edge
068
               round R. As a result of generality, several well-studied FL variants such as Li & Lyu (2024);
               Karimireddy et al. (2020) become special cases of our framework, further echoing the
069
               correctness of our conclusion. Compared with other centralized HFL variants, we achieve
070
               the best convergence rate without considering the transmission latency between the central
071            server and the edge server.
072
073          • We provide insights into achieving optimal convergence improvements by clustering clients
074            with different objectives. Unlike current clustering policies, such as solely grouping clients
075            with similar data Liu et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2022) or ensuring edges share similar data
076            Mhaisen et al. (2021); Deng et al. (2021), our approach determines the best policy based on
077            the settings of the number of edges, clients, local steps, and edge training rounds for various
078
               objectives. As in the general convex case, having all edges sharing similar data will lead to
               an optimal convergence improvement when the number of edges is relatively small. On the
079
               other hand, clustering clients with similar data will help achieve an optimal convergence
080
               improvement if the number of edges is large.
081
082          • We validate our findings with comprehensive simulation-based study on real-world datasets.
083            The experimental results show that CHFL can achieve comparable or superior performance
084            in terms of accuracy and convergence speed measured by local model updates. Meanwhile,
085            we show that the edge training epoch accelerates the convergence speed, and the inter-edge
086            heterogeneity has more effect on convergence speed than the intra-edge heterogeneity in
087            specific conditions.

093   2.1   H IERARCHICAL F EDERATED L EARNING
094
095   In addressing the challenges of high communication overhead and latency in vanilla FL, HFL Liu
096   et al. (2020); Bonawitz et al. (2019); Zhou & Cong (2019) was proposed to add a layer of edge
097   servers, which simplifies the communication process to occur only between edge servers and central
098   server Wang et al. (2021b). Liu et. al in Liu et al. (2020) prove HFL could achieve convergence
099
      amidst inter-edge data heterogeneity, yet the impact of intra-edge data heterogeneity on convergence
      remains unknown. Similarly, Abad et.al Abad et al. (2020) show the reduced communication latency
100
      by deploying HFL in a real mobile edge computing system. Xu et al. in Xu et al. (2021) introduce
101
      an adaptive HFL approach, focusing on optimal resource allocation and control of edge intervals to
102   enhance training accuracy. OUEA Mhaisen et al. (2021) and SHARE Deng et al. (2021) consider the
103   clients cluster problem in HFL. OUEA Mhaisen et al. (2021) cluster clients with similar data into one
104   edge to improve performance in HFL but they only consider the convex objective. SHARE Deng et al.
105   (2021) ensures each edge shares similar data to reduce data heterogeneity among edges to improve
106   performance. Both discuss cluster policies in the context of centralized HFL, but these policies
107   cannot be directly applied to CHFL to enhance performance. Our convergence analysis reveals that
      the effectiveness of cluster policies depends on system settings and their specific objectives.

135   2.2     C YCLIC /S EQUENTIAL F EDERATED L EARNING
136
137   In vanilla Federated Learning, clients exhibit system heterogeneity. Hence, it is advisable to select
138   qualified devices suited for FL, ensuring they have a stable network for efficient model updates,
139   sufficient charging to manage energy use, and idle status to avoid disruptions. Compared with the
140   vanilla FL setting with random device selection Hard et al. (2018); Huba et al. (2022); Paulik et al.
141
      (2021), those qualified devices usually participate in FL at specific time and follow a cyclic pattern
      Zhu et al. (2021); Paulik et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2020). Cho et al. in Cho
142
      et al. (2023) explores various gradient update methods in FL under a cyclic pattern. However, their
143
      convergence analysis is based on the assumption that the local client’s objective conforms to the
144   Polyak-Łojasiewicz condition Karimi et al. (2016), a limitation considering that objectives in FL
145   are often general non-convex Das et al. (2022). Li et al. in Li & Lyu (2024) offer the convergence
146   analysis for both Parallel Federated Learning (PFL) and Sequential Federated Learning (SFL) with
147   convex and non-convex objectives. They have the result that SFL has a better guarantee than PFL in
148   specific conditions. However, they both discuss the convergence analysis on the two-layer FL, for
149   which the cyclic pattern on HFL is still missing. To address this gap, we extend the application of the
150   cyclic pattern to HFL and provide a convergence analysis for both convex and non-convex objectives.
151   Furthermore, we delve into the impact of data heterogeneity on convergence speed across various
152
      client participant patterns in HFL. We attain optimal convergence rate compared to other studies Liu
      et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2021).

194   3.3   D ESCRIPTION OF C YCLIC HFL
195
196   We assume all edge servers participate in a natural cyclic pattern without the guidance from the
197   central server, as shown in Fig.1. The training process of CHFL is as follows. In each global update
198   t = 0, ..., T − 1, the edge servers randomly forms a participated queue, Q, with |Q| = M . Hence,
199   the cyclic process is as follows, when t = 0, the initialized model x0 is randomized or pre-trained by
200   a public dataset. This model can be used as an initialized model by the first edge server in Q. Then,
201
      the traditional FL is run in the first edge, in which N clients are selected to train their own model with
      K steps and upload model updates to the edge server. The above process repeats for R edge rounds.
202
      After that, the aggregated edge model will be sent to the next edge server in Q as an initialized model
203
      for training, until all selected edges finish the training process. We refer to the above process as one
204   global round. In the next global round, the first edge server will receive an updated model from the
205   last edge server of the previous global round. A more detailed process is shown in Alg. 1.

332   T , but it cannot always benefit convergence speed in other scenarios. For example, as in the strongly
333   convex case, larger edge training round R has a negative effect on the convergence speed when the
334   intra-edge data heterogeneity dominates convergence. Moreover, when the dominant term depends
335
      on inter-edge data heterogeneity for both strongly convex and general convex case, increasing R can
      not affect the convergence speed. For the non-convex case, edge training round R affects both terms
336
      of SGD variance σ and data heterogeneity, increasing R improves convergence speed.

478   6                     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we derive convergence guarantees for CHFL on heterogeneous data for strongly convex,
481   general convex, and non-convex objectives, considering both full and partial participation. Compared
482   to other FL and HFL variants, we verify that our convergence rate is optimal to date. Based on our
483   theoretical results, we find that clustering clients solely by similar or opposing data distributions
484   does not achieve the best improvement in convergence speed. Instead, optimal clustering depends on
485   the system settings under various objectives. We hope the insights in this paper will facilitate the
      deployment of CHFL in real-world applications.
