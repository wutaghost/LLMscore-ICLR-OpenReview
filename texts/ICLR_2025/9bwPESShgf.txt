009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have emerged as the backbone neural network architecture in today’s
012            AI applications. Due to their high complexity, sparsifying transformers, at both
013            pre-training and fine-tuning stages, is very attractive for lower the training and
014            inference costs. In this paper, we propose TranSpa, an efficient structured sparse
015            training approach for language and vision transformers. Unlike prior works focus-
016            ing on individual building blocks, TranSpa fully considers the correlation between
017            the weight matrices and their component rows/columns, and performs the coupled
018
               estimation and coupled sparsification. To achieve that, TranSpa introduces the use
               of new granularity when calibrating the importance of structural components in
019
               the transformer and removing the insignificant parts. Evaluations across different
020
               models, in both pre-training and fine-tuning scenarios, demonstrate the effective-
021            ness of the proposed approach. TranSpa can bring 1.6× size reduction with 0.6
022            lower perplexity when training GPT-2 model from scratch. It also enables 1.6×
023            training speedup over the existing sparse pre-training method. For training sparse
024            LLaMA-1B from scratch, our approach reduces GPU memory usage by 50%,
025            decreases training time by 21%, and achieves a 1.6× speedup in inference through-
026            put while maintaining model performance. Experiments of applying TranSpa for
027            fine-tuning tasks also show significant performance improvement with respect to
028            model accuracy and pruning cost reduction.

      Thanks to their excellent performance for sequence modeling and scalability, transformers Vaswani
      et al. (2017) have served as the backbone neural network architecture across various important
034
      domains, such as natural language processing (NLP) Vaswani et al. (2017); Devlin et al. (2019);
035
      Wang et al. (2019a); OpenAI et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023), computer vision Dosovitskiy et al.
036   (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Carion et al. (2020) and speech processing Dong et al. (2018); Gulati et al.
037   (2020); Hsu et al. (2021). However, despite their unprecedented popularity, modern transformers
038   suffer from high model complexity, causing expensive costs in both training and inference phases.
039
      To alleviate these challenging issues, sparse training, a strategy that originated for optimizing
040
      convolutional neural networks (CNNs), has emerged as an attractive solution for efficient transformers.
041
      In general, given an input model θ, sparse training aims to output a sparse model θs with high task
042   performance. As illustrated in Fig. 1, when θ is randomly initialized, sparse training serves as an
043   efficient pre-training method that can reduce the computational and memory costs of the expensive
044   training-from-scratch process. When θ is a pre-trained high-performance dense model, sparse training
045   is essentially the well-known pruning technique, which imposes the sparsity on θ in the fine-tuning
046   process and trims down the inference cost.
047

126   Sparse Training for Transformer Pre-training. Applying sparse training at the pre-training stage
127   is very attractive for reducing the high complexity of the costly train-from-scratch process. However,
128   unlike the well-studied sparse CNN pre-training, to date sparse transformer pre-training is still
129   under-explored. Chen et al. (2021a) dynamically extracts and trains sparse sub-networks, either
130
      in unstructured or structured ways, thereby alleviating the training memory bottleneck for Vision
      Transformers. Inspired by the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis originated in CNN, Chen et al. (2021b)
131
      performs early detection of the structured winning lottery tickets for transformers, improving the pre-
132
      training efficiency. In Dao et al. (2022), a class of structured matrices, which can closely approximate
133   the dense weight matrices, are proposed for hardware-efficient sparse pre-training, accelerating the
134   overall training process. Notice that the methods developed in Chen et al. (2021b;a); Dao et al. (2022)
135   can also be applied at the fine-tuning stage for transformer pruning.
136
      Sparse Training for LLM Pruning. Due to the high costs of pre-trained LLM models, using sparse
137
      training to trim down LLM size is a promising solution for efficient deployment. In general, pruning
138
      LLM can be performed in either unstructured or structured way. Unstructured pruning Frantar &
139   Alistarh (2023); Sun et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2023); Malla et al.
140   (2024) focus on removing unimportant individual weights, achieving high compression performance
141   but limited computational efficiency due to the unstructured sparsity pattern. Structured pruning
142   Ma et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2023); Ashkboos et al. (2024); An et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023b;a);
143   Zhao et al. (2024a); Yang et al. (2024) aims to sparsify some building components of transformer
144   architecture, e.g., head, row, layer, etc., offering wall-clock time inference speedup. To guide the
145   guide the selection of elements to be removed, typical pruning metrics include magnitude-based
146   Sun et al. (2023); An et al. (2024) and loss-based Ma et al. (2023); van der Ouderaa et al. (2023).
147
      Magnitude-based methods use the absolute values of weights, whereas loss-based approaches assess
      the impact of pruning on model loss, often using the gradient information obtained from Taylor
148
      expansion.
149
150   Sparse Training for CNN Pre-training/Pruning. Sparse training for CNN, at pre-training and
151   fine-tuning stages, has been well-studied in the literature. For efficient sparse CNN pre-training,
152   Mocanu et al. (2018) explores to prune and grow the same amount of weights periodically and
153
      iteratively. Mostafa & Wang (2019) proposes to automatically adjust the sparsity levels, achieving
      good scalability and high computational efficiency. Evci et al. (2020) uses the gradient-based criteria
154
      to grow the weights with Erdos-Renyi Kernel initialization, and a memory-economic scheme is
155
      proposed in Yuan et al. (2021) for training on the edge devices. Recently, Chen et al. (2023c)
156   proposes to automatically sparsify the CNN model from scratch, obtaining a compact model without
157   iterative fine-tuning. On the other hand, CNN pruning can be roughly categorized to unstructured
158   pruning (for weights) Han et al. (2015a;b) and structured pruning (for channels/filters) He et al.
159   (2019); Wang et al. (2019b); Yvinec et al. (2021); Hou et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2020); Dubey et al.
160   (2018); Sui et al. (2021); Tan & Motani (2020); Luo et al. (2017). Considering the importance of
161   structured sparsity for inference speedup, most of the state-of-the-art CNN pruning works focus on
      structured pruning.

      As extensively studied in the literature Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Sun et al. (2023); van der Ouderaa
189   et al. (2023), estimating the unimportant components of neural networks plays a crucial role for
190   model sparsification. In general, importance estimation can be performed at different granularity
191   levels, such as weight, neuron, layer, and head. More specifically, when aiming for obtaining the
192   structured sparse transformers, identifying the insignificant heads and layers is the most common
193   practice Chen et al. (2021a;b); Men et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2023).
194

195   formers using a new granularity, namely coupled weight matrix. This idea is motivated by the
196   observation that the transformer has its unique computing pattern and model topology, and hence
197   coupling the weight matrices can provide rich information for importance estimation. Next, we
198   describe the details of our proposal.

240   3.2   C OUPLED S PARSIFICATION : R EMOVING THE C OUPLED ROW /C OLUMN PAIR
241
242   Upon obtaining the importance information of all the coupled weight matrices, the next step is
243   to sparsify W1 and W2 belonging to those less significant Wcouple . To that end, we perform
244   row/column-wise sparsification, a strategy with finer granularity than removing the entire Wi ,

246   removing the rows/columns in the weight matrices of transformers has been reported in Chen et al.

511   5   C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this paper, we propose TranSpa, an efficient structured sparse training approach for transformers.
514
      By estimating the coupled weight matrix-wise importance and removing the coupled row/column
      pair during training, TranSpa brings a significant reduction in training costs and model complexity
515
      with preserving high task performance. Experiments across various transformer models demonstrate
516
      the superior performance of TranSpa in both pre-training and fine-tuning scenarios.

760   Experimental Setting. We evaluate the pruning performance of TranSpa on the pre-trained BERT De-
761   vlin et al. (2018) models. For experiments on BERT, the model is pruned and evaluated on
762   GLUE Wang et al. (2018) and SQuAD Rajpurkar et al. (2016) benchmarks. When performing
763   fine-tuning on the GLUE benchmark, the learning rate and weight decay are set as 5 × 10−5 and
764   0, respectively, with 3 training epochs and batch size of 32. The maximum sequence length is
765
      128. For fine-tuning on the SQuAD benchmark, the learning rate is set to 3 × 10−5 at the 3-epoch
      fine-tuning stage, and the batch size, maximum sequence length, and document stride are 12, 320,
766
      128, respectively. Both training and testing are conducted on a single Nvidia GeForce RTX 3090.
767
      For single-sentence tasks, we include the CoLA Warstadt et al. (2019) task, evaluated based on
768   Matthew’s correlation, and the SST-2 task, assessed using classification accuracy. For sentence
769   similarity tasks, we consider three distinct evaluations: MRPC Dolan et al. (2005) evaluated using
770   F-1 score, STS-B Cer et al. (2017) assessed through Pearson-Spearman correlation, and QQP Chen
771   et al. (2018) measured in F-1 score. The evaluation also covers three natural language inference tasks:
772   MNLI Williams et al. (2017), measured in classification accuracy with the average of the matched
773   and mismatched subsets, and QNLI Rajpurkar et al. (2016), assessed in terms of accuracy. For the
774   reading comprehension task, SQuAD consists of questions generated by crowd workers based on
775   a collection of Wikipedia articles. Each question requires extracting an answer, in the form of a
776
      segment of text or span, from the corresponding reading passage.
