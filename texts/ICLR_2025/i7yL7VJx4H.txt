                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               LoRA has emerged as one of the de facto methods for fine-tuning foundation mod-
012            els with low computational cost and memory footprint. The idea is to only train
013            a low-rank perturbation to the weights of a pre-trained model, given supervised
014            data for a downstream task. Despite its empirical sucess, from a mathematical
015            perspective it remains poorly understood what learning mechanisms ensure that
016            gradient descent converges to useful low-rank perturbations.
017            In this work we initiate the study of low-rank fine-tuning in a student-teacher
018            setting. We are given the weights of a two-layer base model f , as well as i.i.d.
019            samples (x, f ∗ (x)) where x is Gaussian and f ∗ is the teacher model given by
020            perturbing the weights of f by a rank-1 matrix. This generalizes the setting of
021            generalized linear model (GLM) regression where the weights of f are zero.
022            When the rank-1 perturbation is comparable in norm to the weight matrix of f ,
023            the training dynamics are nonlinear. Nevertheless, in this regime we prove under
024
               mild assumptions that a student model which is initialized at the base model and
025
               trained with online gradient descent will converge to the teacher in dk O(1) iter-
               ations, where k is the number of neurons in f . Importantly, unlike in the GLM
026
               setting, the complexity does not depend on fine-grained properties of the activa-
027
               tion’s Hermite expansion. We also prove that in our setting, learning the teacher
028            model “from scratch” can require significantly more iterations.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Modern deep learning at scale involves two phases: pre-training a foundation model with self-
034   supervised learning, and fine-tuning the model towards various downstream tasks. Given the signif-
035   icant computational cost of the former, effective fine-tuning has been essential to the deployment of
036   these models under hardware constraints and the development of powerful open-source models.
037   In this space, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has emerged as one of the most successful and widely
038   adopted methods (Hu et al., 2021). The idea is to freeze the weights of the pre-trained model and
039   only train low-rank perturbations to the weight matrices. Remarkably, this works well even with
040   rank 1 perturbations, reducing number of trainable parameters by up to four orders of magnitude.
041
      Despite the surprising effectiveness of LoRA in practice, it is poorly understood from a theoretical
042   perspective why this method works so well. While it is known that for sufficiently deep and wide
043   pre-trained networks, any sufficiently simple target model can be approximated by a low-rank pertur-
044   bation of the larger model (Zeng & Lee, 2024), it is largely unknown what mechanisms ensure that
045   gradient-based training converges to these perturbations. Recent works have made initial progress
046   towards understanding this question from the perspective of kernel approximations of neural net-
047   works in the lazy training regime (Jang et al., 2024; Malladi et al., 2023). These works consider a
048   setting where the perturbation is small enough relative to the weights of the pre-trained model that
049   the fine-tuned model is well-approximated by its linearization around the pre-trained model.
050   While the kernel picture provides useful first-order intuition for the dynamics of fine-tuning, it only
051   partially explains its success. For one, the kernel approximation is mainly relevant in the few-shot
052   setting where the network is only fine-tuned on a small number of examples (e.g. a few dozen), but
053   the gap between what is possible with few- vs. many-shot fine-tuning is significant. Even within the
      few-shot setting, (Malladi et al., 2023) found that fine-tuning for certain language tasks is not well-


                                                        1
054
      explained by kernel behavior, and neither is prompt-based fine-tuning if the prompt is insufficiently
055   aligned with the pre-training task. The gap is even more stark for fine-tuning without prompts.
056
057   In this work we ask:
058
        Why does gradient descent for low-rank fine-tuning converge to a good solution even when the
059
                                    kernel approximation breaks down?
060
061   To answer this question, we initiate the study of fine-tuning in a natural student-teacher setting where
062   the training dynamics are inherently non-linear.

124   1.2.1    A SSUMPTIONS
125
      Our guarantees will apply to a very wide family of activations σ including all standard ones, e.g.
126
      ReLU, sigmoid, polynomial, etc. As the conditions are rather technical, we defer them to Assump-
127   tion 5 in the supplement and henceforth refer to such activations as nice.
128
129   More importantly, we make the following assumptions on the base model and teacher model. Denote
130
      the rows of W , i.e. the pre-trained features, by w1 , . . . , wk ∈ Rd . Then we have:
131   Assumption 1 (Normalization). ∥wi ∥2 = 1 for all i = 1, . . . , k.
132   Assumption 2 (Orthogonality of perturbation). The vector u for the teacher model (see Eq. (3)) is
133   orthogonal to the span of w1 , . . . , wk .
134                                                                          √
      Assumption 3 (Random quantized c). c is sampled uniformly from {±1/ k}k .
135
136   Assumption 1 is without loss of generality when σ is positive homogeneous like in the case of ReLU
137   activation. For general activations, note that one can also handle the case of ∥wi ∥2 = R for all i for
138   arbitrary constant R > 0 by redefining σ. This assumption is not essential to our analysis and we
139   assume the scales of the pre-trained features are the same to keep the analysis transparent.
140
      Assumption 2 is crucial to our analysis. To motivate this, in Appendix D.1, we give a simple example
141
      where it fails to hold and the low-rank fine-tuning problem ends up having multiple global optima,
142   suggesting that the dynamics in the absence of Assumption 2 may be significantly more challenging
143   to characterize. We leave this regime as an interesting area for future study.
144                                                                                                     √
145   The third assumption consists of two parts: 1) the entries of c are constrained to lie within {±1/ k},
146
      and 2) they are random. The former is for technical reasons. First note that the connection to GLMs
      still holds under this assumption. Our main reason to make this is that our proof uses Hermite
147
      analysis, and while it is in principle possible to handle neurons with different norms, assuming the
148
      ci ’s are quantized renders our analysis more transparent without sacrificing descriptive power. As
149   our simulations suggest, the phenomena we elucidate persist without this assumption (see Figure 1).
150
151   As for the randomness of c, while we conjecture that fine-tuning should be tractable even in the
152
      worst case over c (see Remark 3) albeit with more complicated dynamics, in this work we only show
      guarantees that hold with high probability over c. We primarily use the randomness to ensure that
153
      certain quantities that are generically non-vanishing indeed do not vanish, in the spirit of smoothed
154
      analysis (Spielman & Teng, 2004). One could equivalently formulate our guarantees as holding
155   under a certain set of deterministic nondegeneracy conditions on the rank-1 perturbation.

      In this work, we consider two regimes: (1) when {wi } are orthogonal, and (2) when {wi } have very
189
      mild angular separation but are otherwise arbitrary.

      Orthonormal features. For this case, we will consider the regime where √ the scale ξ of the rank-
      1 perturbation defining the teacher model is large, namely ξ = Θ( k). Because the norm of
193
      the perturbation is comparable to the Frobenius norm of the weight matrix of the base model, the
194

239   1.3   R ELATED WORK
240
241   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning. Following the popularization of LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), there
242   have been a large number of proposed refinements thereof (Fu et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2024;
243   Lialin et al., 2023); a thorough review of the empirical literature is beyond the scope of this work.
244   Within the mathematical literature on fine-tuning, the works directly related to ours are the afore-
245   mentioned results of Malladi et al. (2023); Jang et al. (2024). Malladi et al. (2023) primarily
246   presented empirical evidence of kernel behavior for prompt-based fine-tuning methods, including
247   LoRA, in the few-shot regime. Their main theoretical result regarding LoRA roughly states that if
248   standard (full-rank) fine-tuning exhibits kernel behavior, then low-rank fine-tuning exhibits kernel
249   behavior, provided the rank of the perturbation is at least Ω(1/ε2 ). Jang et al. (2024) build upon this
250
      as follows. In the kernel regime where the student model is well-approximated by its linearization
      around the base model throughout training, they consider the resulting linearized empirical loss for
251
      an arbitrary dataset. This is still non-convex if one tries jointly training
                                                                               √ the factors of the low-rank
252
      perturbation, but they nevertheless show that this loss has a rank-O( N ) global minimizer, where
253
      N is the number of training examples. They then show that all local minimizers of this loss are
254
      global minimizers, using tools from prior work on low-rank matrix factorization.
255
256   These works are incomparable to ours in several regards. Firstly, they operate in the few-shot regime
257   so that the number of training examples N is relatively small, and the perturbation is small enough
258
      that one can work with a linear approximation. In contrast, we consider “full” low-rank fine-tuning,
      for which N must scale at least with the ambient dimension, and we are trying to learn much larger
259
      perturbations; as we show in Figure 2, this puts us well outside the regime where the kernel ap-
260
      proximation does well. In addition, the aforementioned works do not handle the regime where the
261   rank is extremely small, even though LoRA still works quite well in this case. That said, there is no
262   free lunch: our work derives insights in the challenging rank-one, non-linear setting at the cost of
263   working with a specific set of assumptions on the data-generating process.
264
265   GLMs and single/multi-index model regression. Generalized linear models have received sig-
266   nificant attention in learning theory as a stylized model for feature learning, see Dudeja & Hsu
267   (2018) for an overview of older works on this. Most relevant to our work is Arous et al. (2021)
268   which studied the gradient dynamics of learning GLMs models σ(⟨w, ·⟩) over Gaussian examples
269   with online SGD. Their main finding was that online SGD achieves high correlation with the ground
                                ∗
      truth direction in Θ̃(d1∨l −1 ) iterations/samples, where l∗ is the information exponent, defined to

      be the lowest degree at which σ has a nonzero Hermite coefficient. We draw upon tools from Arous
271   et al. (2021) to analyze online SGD in our setting, one important distinction being that the popula-
272   tion gradient dynamics in our setting are very different and furthermore our finite-sample analysis
273   makes quantitative various bounds that were only proved asymptotically in Arous et al. (2021).

      By a result of Szörényi (2009), the information exponent also dictates the worst-case complexity

      of learning generalized linear models: for noisy gradient descent (and more generally, correlational

280   work is that quantities like information exponent do not dictate the complexity of fine-tuning.

      6    O UTLOOK
478
479
      In this work we took the first steps towards understanding the gradient dynamics low-rank fine-
480   tuning beyond NTK. We identified a rich student-teacher framework, specialized to two-layer net-
481   works, and proved in various settings that online SGD efficiently finds the ground truth low-rank
482   perturbation. This student-teacher framework is also appealing because it offers a natural way of in-
483   terpolating between fine-tuning in the lazy training regime and generalized linear model regression
484   in the feature learning regime. The parameter regime we consider occupies an intriguing middle
485   ground between these extremes where the dynamics are nonlinear yet tractable and not overly sen-
      sitive to fine-grained properties like the Hermite coefficients of the activation function.
