011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We consider solving the low-rank matrix sensing problem in the over-
014            parameterized setting, where the specified rank is larger than the true rank. Pre-
015            cisely, our main objective is to recover a matrix X ∗ ∈ Rn1 ×n2 with rank r⋆
016
               using an over-parameterized form LR⊤ , where L ∈ Rn1 ×r , R ∈ Rn2 ×r and
               min{n1 , n2 } ≥ r > r⋆ with the true rank r⋆ being unknown. The commonly used
017
               methods tackling such a problem such as Factorized Gradient Descent (FGD) can
018
               only demonstrate sub-linear convergence behavior, and their performance could
019            significantly deteriorate when the matrix condition number is relatively large. To
020            address this issue, we propose the alternating preconditioned gradient descent
021            (APGD) method that an inexpensive right preconditioner with a constant damping
022            parameter is applied to the original gradient. We prove that even starting from a
023            random initialization, APGD can recover the target matrix at a linear convergence
024            rate in the over-parameterized situation, independent of the condition number. No-
025            tably, unlike previous FGD-based methods, APGD alternates between updating
026            the two factor matrices, which eliminates the reliance on a small step size, thereby
027
               enabling faster convergence. Through a series of experiments, we demonstrate
               that APGD achieves the fastest convergence speed compared to other methods,
028
               and further possesses strong robustness with respect to step size, condition num-
029
               ber and other parameters.

033
034   Low-rank matrix sensing is a fundamental problem encountered in various fields, including image
035   processing (Candès et al., 2011; Li et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019), phase retrieval (Vaswani et al.,
036   2017; Nayer & Vaswani, 2021), quantum tomography (Rambach et al., 2021), etc. The primary ob-
037   jective is to recover a rank r⋆ matrix X⋆ ∈ Rn1 ×n2 (r⋆ ≪ min{n1 , n2 }) from linear measurements
038   {(yi , Ai )}m
                  i=1 of the form
039                                        yi = ⟨Ai , X⋆ ⟩, i = 1, ..., m.                                (1)
040   This model can be concisely expressed as y                       =    A(X⋆ ), where A(X⋆ )     =
041   [⟨A1 , X⋆ ⟩ , ⟨A2 , X⋆ ⟩ , · · · , ⟨Am , X⋆ ⟩] is the so-called measurement operator. A prevalent
042   method for recovering a low-rank matrix X ∈ Rn1 ×n2 involves solving the following problem:
043
                                        arg min rank(X) s.t. y = A(X).                                     (2)
044
045   However, such an optimization problem is NP-hard due to the nonconvex rank constraint. To address
046   this challenge, researchers have proposed relaxing the rank constraint to a convex nuclear norm
047   constraint (Recht et al., 2010; Candes & Plan, 2011; Candes & Recht, 2012; Candès & Tao, 2010).
048   Although this kind of convex relaxation approach provides a tractable solution, it fails to fully exploit
049
      the low-rankness of X⋆ , and as a result, the computational cost significantly increases as the matrix
      size grows. To mitigate the computational overhead, a common approach is to decompose the matrix
050
      X into a factorized form LR⊤ , where L ∈ Rn1 ×r , R ∈ Rn2 ×r and r is the estimated rank, also
051
      known as the Burer-Monteiro method (Burer & Monteiro, 2003; 2005), and then solve

      However, the following challenges remain in practice:
059
060          • ill-conditioning It is well known that gradient methods are susceptible to the condition
061            number κ of a matrix, defined as the ratio of the largest to the smallest singular value.
062            The number of gradient descent iterations increases at least linearly with the condition
063            number (Zheng & Lafferty, 2015). Unfortunately, most practical datasets exhibit very large
064            condition numbers, e.g., (Cloninger et al., 2014) notes that certain applications of matrix
065            sensing have condition numbers as high as κ = 1015 .
066          • Over-parameterization A major challenge is the lack of prior information about the true
067            rank r⋆ . Therefore, in the Burer-Monteiro method, we typically set r⋆ ≤ r ≤ min{n1 , n2 },
068            a scenario referred to as over-parameterization. However, recent studies (Zhuo et al., 2021;
069            Zhang et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2024) have shown that in the case of over-parameterization,
070
               the convergence behavior of factorized gradient methods can be significantly affected.
071          • Initialization For factorized gradient descent method, obtaining an initial point is the first
072            step. Many previous studies (Zhuo et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021) rely
073            on spectral initialization to obtain a good initial point that is close to the true solution X⋆ .
074
               However, recent researches (Stöger & Soltanolkotabi, 2021; Lee & Stöger, 2023; Xiong
               et al., 2024) have shown that small random initialization can achieve similar results to
075
               spectral initialization. Nevertheless, small initialization typically requires a larger number
076
               of iterations and is less practical in some big data applications.
077
078   Given these challenges, the main goal of this work is to address the following question: Can
079   one develop an efficient and robust method for solving ill-conditioned matrix sensing in the
080   over-parameterized setting at a linear convergence rate with a proper random initialization ?
081

086   descent (APGD) method to solve the over-parameterized matrix sensing problem. Preconditioning
087

088
      also been applied to matrix sensing problems (Tong et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023).
089
      However, existing preconditioned gradient descent methods are mostly tailored for symmetric posi-
090   tive definite matrices, which is not a very practical assumption.
091

      speeding up convergence. As a result, we developed the alternating preconditioned gradient descent

119   We summarize our contributions as follows:
120
             • Firstly, we propose a preconditioned alternating gradient descent algorithm, which can con-
121
               verge from a random initial point to the true solution at a linear rate. Even in cases with a
122
               large condition number and severe rank overestimation, APGD achieves the fastest conver-
123            gence. Compared to previous methods, APGD maintains convergence even with large step
124            sizes, offering a faster convergence rate. Moreover, unlike other globally convergent algo-
125            rithms, APGD does not rely on extremely small initialization, thus reducing the number of
126            iterations.
127
             • Secondly, we develop a two-phase analytical framework that divides the convergence of
128            APGD into an initial phase and a local convergence phase. Using this framework, we ana-
129            lyze the convergence rate of APGD under over-parameterization and random initialization,
130            demonstrating that APGD exhibits linear convergence. We believe this framework can also
131            be extended to other tasks such as matrix completion, one-bit matrix sensing, and phase
132            retrieval.
133          • Thirdly, we conduct a series of experiments, showing that APGD achieves the fastest con-
134            vergence speed compared to other methods. Additionally, we perform sensitivity tests on
135            APGD’s parameters, such as step size, and damping parameter, initialization scale, demon-
136            strating that APGD exhibits strong robustness to parameter variations.

158
159   In recent years, a major research direction in the field of matrix sensing has been the development of
160   fast and efficient non-convex algorithms, with the factorized gradient descent algorithm, particularly
161   the Burer-Monteiro (BM) factorization (Tu et al., 2016; Zhuo et al., 2021; Chen & Wainwright,
      2015; Sun & Luo, 2016), being a representative example. Despite the significant progress made

162
      in the study of the FGD algorithm, it still performs poorly in cases of ill-conditioning and over-
163   parameterization, which has led to extensive research efforts addressing these issues. Additionally,
164   the initialization of FGD has become another prominent research focus. We present a comparison
165   of several works most relevant to our approach in Table 1.
166
      ill-conditioning
167
      Gradient-based methods are highly sensitive to the condition number of matrices, and the iter-
168
      ation complexity of the FGD algorithm grows linearly with the matrix condition number, i.e.
169   O(κ log 1/ϵ), as the condition number increases, the convergence rate of FGD significantly slows
170   downZheng & Lafferty (2015); Zhang et al. (2023). In recent years, there has been a series of studies
171   focused on addressing this problem using preconditioning methods (Mishra et al., 2012; Wei et al.,
172   2016; Mishra & Sepulchre, 2016; Tanner & Wei, 2016; Tong et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; 2023;
173   2022; Bian et al., 2023). Most of these works rely on a good initial point and only conduct local
174   convergence analysis.
175   Over-parameterization In earlier years, a series of works (Tu et al., 2016; Tong et al., 2021; Chen &
176   Wainwright, 2015; Li et al., 2018) demonstrated that under the exact rank assumption, the factorized
177   gradient descent method could converge to the ground truth at a linear rate. However, since it is
178   difficult to obtain the rank of the matrix to be recovered in practice, recent research has focused
179   on matrix recovery in the overestimated rank setting (Zhuo et al., 2021; Li et al., 2018; Stöger &
180   Soltanolkotabi, 2021; Soltanolkotabi et al., 2023). However, over-parameterization exacerbates the
181   ill-conditioning of the problem, leading to slower convergence rates. Studies by Zhang et al. (2021;
182   2023); Xu et al. (2023); Cheng & Zhao (2024) have investigated the issue of slow convergence in
183
      over-parameterized settings.
184   Initialization
185   Early methods demonstrated that, starting from an initial point obtained through spectral initializa-
186   tion (Chen & Wainwright, 2015; Sun & Luo, 2016), which is close to the ground truth, the factoriza-
187   tion gradient descent algorithm can converge to the optimal solution. In the past two to three years,
188   some studies (Bhojanapalli et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2019; Ge et al., 2016; 2017; Zhu et al., 2021)
189
      have shown that, under certain conditions, all local minima of the low-rank matrix sensing problem
      are also global minima. Consequently, global convergence with random initialization has become a
190
      research focus Jin et al. (2023); Ding et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2023); Soltanolkotabi et al. (2023);
191
      Chen et al.. (Stöger & Soltanolkotabi, 2021) revealed that in the noiseless case, gradient descent
192   with small random initialization performs similarly to spectral initialization.

      In this section, we conduct simulation experiments to empirically validate our theoretical results.
435   Our experiments demonstrate that with random initialization, APGD exhibits condition number-
436   independent linear convergence rates in matrix factorization and matrix sensing problems, even in
437   over-parameterized settings. We compared our method with the vanilla gradient descent, alternating
438   gradient descent, ScaledGD(λ) and PrecGD, and showed that our approach achieves the fastest
439   convergence rate. Furthermore, compared to ScaledGD(λ), our method is more robust to the choice

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We propose the APGD algorithm, to solve the low-rank asymmetric matrix sensing problem in the
533
      over-parameterized setting, where the true rank is unknown and overestimated. We theoretically
534   and empirically demonstrate that APGD exhibits a faster convergence rate compared to previous
535   FGD algorithms and preconditioning methods, and also possesses of great robustness to parame-
536   ter sensitivity. Specifically, We develop a new two-stage analytical framework to investigate the
537   global convergence behavior of APGD, proving that it can converge to the global optimum from the
538   universal random initialization at a linear rate. We believe the developed framework can be natu-
539   rally extended to analyze other related problems, such as 1-bit matrix sensing and low-rank matrix
      completion, which will be part of our future work.

      B    C OMPARISON WITH OTHER WORKS
811
812   Comparison with Xu et al. (2023) The work most closely related to ours is Xu et al. (2023), which
813   proposed a ScaledGD(λ) algorithm that can converge to the global minimum from a sufficiently
814   small initialization at a linear rate. Our approach differs from theirs in several ways. First, they
815   focus on the recovery of symmetric positive semidefinite matrices, which is not practical, whereas
816   we focus on the recovery of arbitrary matrices. Second, while they employ a preconditioned gradient
817   method, we use an alternating preconditioned gradient method, which is more robust to step sizes
818   and converges faster. Third, their method relies on an extremely small initialization, adding an extra
819   term log κ · log(κn) to the iteration complexity, whereas our method does not require such small
820
      initial values, thereby reducing the number of iterations needed.
821   Comparision with Zhang et al. (2021; 2023) In the second stage, our analytical approach is similar
822   to that of Zhang et al. (2021; 2023), but have significant differences. One major difference is that
823   Zhang et al. (2021; 2023) used a preconditioned gradient descent method, and their analysis relies
824   on a good choice of damping parameter α. Specifically, the convergence of preconditioned gradient
825   descent is (1 − µl ). Thus, in their analysis, µ should be lower bounded, while l needs to be upper
826
      bounded and these two bounds are highly related to damping parameter α, leads to the upper bound
      and lower bound of α in each iteration, i.e. Clb ∥Lt Rt⊤ − X⋆ ∥F ≤ αt ≤ Cub ∥Lt Rt⊤ − X⋆ ∥F .
827
828   However, in our analysis only the lower bound of µ is related to α, and the upper bound of l is only
829   related to RIP constant δ2r . And this is what the alternating gradient method gives us. Therefore,
830   we can take α ≤ O(∥LT RT⊤ − X⋆ ∥2F ) for all t ≤ T iterations.
