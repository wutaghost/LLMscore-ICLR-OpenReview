011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Classical neural ordinary differential equations (ODEs) are powerful tools for ap-
014            proximating the log-density functions in high-dimensional spaces along trajecto-
015            ries, where neural networks parameterize the velocity fields. This paper proposes
016
               a system of neural differential equations representing first- and second-order score
               functions along trajectories based on deep neural networks. We reformulate the
017
               mean field control (MFC) problem with individual noises into an unconstrained
018
               optimization problem framed by the proposed neural ODE system. Addition-
019            ally, we introduce a novel regularization term to enforce characteristics of viscous
020            Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman (HJB) equations to be satisfied based on the evolution
021            of the second-order score function. Examples include regularized Wasserstein
022            proximal operators (RWPOs), probability flow matching of Fokker–Planck (FP)
023            equations, and linear quadratic (LQ) MFC problems, which demonstrate the ef-
024            fectiveness and accuracy of the proposed method.
025

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Score functions have been widely used in modern machine learning algorithms, particularly genera-
030   tive models through time-reversible diffusion (Song et al., 2021). The score function can be viewed
031   as a deterministic representation of diffusion in stochastic trajectories (Carrillo et al., 2019). In
032
      this representation, one reformulates the Brownian motion by the gradient of the logarithm of the
      density function, after which deterministic trajectories involving score functions can approximate
033
      the probability density function. These properties have inspired algorithms for simulating stochas-
034
      tic trajectories or sampling problems that converge to target distributions (Wang et al., 2022; Lu
035   et al., 2024). Typical applications include modeling the time evolution of probability densities for
036   stochastic dynamics and solving control problems constrained by such dynamics.
037
038
      While score functions provide powerful tools for modeling stochastic trajectories, their computa-
      tions are often inefficient, especially in high-dimensional spaces. Classical methods, such as kernel
039
      density estimation (KDE) (Chen, 2017), tend to perform poorly in such settings due to the curse of
040
      dimensionality (Terrell & Scott, 1992).
041
042   Recently, neural ODEs have emerged as efficient ways of estimating densities. In particular, one uses
043   neural networks to parameterize the velocity fields and then approximates the logarithm of density
044
      function along trajectories. The time discretizations of neural ODEs can be viewed as normalization
      flows in generative models.
045
046   Several natural questions arise. Can we approximate the trajectory of score functions by constructing
047   a set of neural ODEs (in continuous time) or normalization flows (in discrete time)? Furthermore,
048   can we efficiently solve stochastic control problems with these neural ODEs or normalization flows?
049   In this paper, we propose a formulation for the first- and second-order score functions using two ad-
050   ditional neural ODEs involving high-order derivatives of the velocity fields. We also develop a class
051   of high-order normalizing flows in the discrete-time update of these neural ODEs. As an applica-
052   tion, we use this high-order normalizing flow to solve the MFC problem with individual noises. The
053   MFC problem generalizes stochastic control problems by incorporating a running cost that depends
      on the state distribution, thus capturing the interaction between individual agents and the population


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      density. In our reformulation, the MFC problem is recast into an optimal control problem involving
055   state trajectories, densities, and score functions, which can be efficiently approximated using the
056   proposed neural ODE system. Additionally, a regularization term from the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker
057   (KKT) system of the MFC problem is proposed to enhance the optimization. In this regulariza-
058   tion, we approximate the viscous HJB equations using the first- and second-order score dynamics.
059   Numerical examples in various MFC problems demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed opti-
060   mization method with high-order neural differential equations.
061   Related work. The normalizing flow has emerged as a powerful technique for solving inference
062   problems, allowing for the construction of complex probability distributions in a tractable manner.
063   This approach was popularized by works such as Rezende & Mohamed (2015); Papamakarios et al.
064   (2021). The advent of Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018) has attracted considerable attention in the
065   community, opening up a new paradigm for continuous-time deep learning models. Following this
066   breakthrough, numerous extensions have been proposed to enhance the expressiveness and flexibility
067   of these models, such as the augmented Neural ODEs by Dupont et al. (2019).
068   Regarding score-based models, Song et al. (2021) studies the score-based reversible time diffusion
069   models in generative modeling. In this case, the score function often comes from the OU process.
070   One needs to solve the score-matching problem to learn the time-dependent score function. In
071   addition, the first-order score dynamic from neural ODEs has been introduced by Boffi & Vanden-
072   Eijnden (2023) to work on the simulation of the Fokker-Planck (FP) equation. Rather than using
073   score dynamics directly to compute the probability flow ODEs associated with FP equations, they
074   employed score-matching methods to achieve efficient computations.
075   The study of MFC problems has become crucial in the last decade Cardaliaguet et al. (2019); Ben-
076   soussan et al. (2013); Fornasier & Solombrino (2014). MFC studies strategic decision-making in
077   large populations where individual players interact through specific mean-field quantities. This
078   control formulation is also useful in generative models Zhang & Katsoulakis (2023). For exam-
079   ple, neural network-based methods have been employed to solve MFC problems. Hu & Lauriere
080   (2023) provided a comprehensive review of neural network approaches for MFC. For first-order
081
      MFC problems, Ruthotto et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2023) developed Neural ODE-based methods.
      In parallel, Ruthotto et al. (2020) introduced numerical algorithms based on the characteristic lines
082
      of the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation. For second-order MFC problems, neural networks
083
      have also been employed. Lin et al. (2021) utilized generative adversarial networks (GANs) to
084   approximate minimization systems for MFC problems, where one neural network models the pop-
085   ulation density, and the other parameterizes the value function. Reisinger et al. (2024) proposed a
086   PDE-based iterative algorithm to tackle MFC problems. Along this line, MFC problems are general-
087   izations of dynamical optimal transport problems, known as Benamou-Brenier’s formulas Benamou
088   & Brenier (2000). A famous example is the Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto (JKO) scheme (Jordan et al.,
089   1998), a variational time discretization in approximating the gradient drift FP equation. The one-step
090   iteration of the JKO scheme can be viewed as an MFC problem. In machine learning computations,
091   the neural JKO scheme (Xu et al., 2023; Vidal et al., 2023) was introduced to compute the FP equa-
092
      tion, and Lee et al. (2024) extended this to approximate general nonlinear gradient flows through a
      generalized deep neural JKO scheme. This scheme approximates a deterministic MFC problem in
093
      each time interval. Other works that use machine learning methods to solve MFC problems include
094
      Dayanikli et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024); Dayanikli et al. (2023). Different from previous results,
095   our work designs first- and second-order score dynamics to compute second-order MFC problems.
096
097   The organization of this paper is as follows. In section 2, we formulate the neural dynamical system
098
      involving first- and second-order score functions, with a specific example based on the Gaussian
      distribution in terms of linear mapping equations. Section 3 discusses the discrete time evolution of
099
      the neural ODE system, which can be viewed as a system of normalization flows for approximating
100
      first- and second-order score functions. In section 4, we design an algorithm using a score-based
101   ODE system to solve the second-order MFC problem. Several numerical examples are presented in
102   section 5 to demonstrate the accuracy and effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

      6    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we propose a neural ODE system to compute evolutions of first- and second-order
528   score functions along trajectories. The forward Euler discretization of neural ODE system satisfies
529   a system of normalization flows along a deep neural network. We then apply the proposed neural
530   ODE system to solve second-order MFC problems. The effectiveness and accuracy of our method
531   are validated through numerical examples of RWPO problems, score-based flow matching problems
532   for FP equations, LQ problems, and the double well potential problems, providing reassurance of its
533   reliability and effectiveness.
534   In future work, we shall conduct a thorough numerical analysis of the optimal control problem
535   within the neural ODE system. Specifically, for second-order MFC problems, error analysis for the
536   score estimations is necessary. Although we observe some numerical advantages in using the score
537   function to approximate the solution of viscous HJB equation, the underlying error in the current nu-
538   merical scheme remains unclear. Additionally, exploring neural ODE systems for inverse problems
539   presents an exciting avenue for future research. This direction involves learning and approximating
      stochastic trajectories from data using the neural ODE system.

1785
1786   The score dynamic (3c). Boffi & Vanden-Eijnden (2023) proposed a similar equation (in equation
1787   (13)) before. But we have concrete numerical example. Also, we believe that we are the first to
1788
       propose the ODE dynamic for the second-order score function (3d). This equation significantly
       enhances the accuracy of the regularized MFC solver, as demonstrated in Table 3 and 4.
1789
1790   Parametrization of neural network. In this paper, we parametrize f as a neural network with a
1791   single (hidden) layer. As a consequence, the whole map from z0 to ztend is slightly different from the
1792   typical structure of a deep neural network, as described by Chen et al. (2018). The layer structure for
1793   f is affine-activation-affine, so the composition of multiple layers becomes affine-activation-affine-
1794
       affine-activation-affine. In contrast, a typical structure for a deep neural network is affine-activation-
       affine-activation-affine. The composition of two affine functions is still an affine function, so it is
1795
       not a big difference.
1796
1797   Related work on theoretical MFC. From a theoretical perspective, convergence properties of MFC
1798   problems have been extensively studied by Carmona & Laurière (2021; 2022). In addition, Lacker
1799   (2017) investigated the limit behavior as the number of agents approaches infinity. The maximum
1800
       principle, a crucial concept in optimal control, has also been extended to the mean field setting by
       Meyer-Brandis et al. (2012), offering an understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of MFC
1801
       problems. The mean field game (MFG) problem (Cardaliaguet, 2010) is closely related to MFC.
1802
       Cardaliaguet et al. (2019) generalize the HJB equation to the master equation, whose properties
1803   have been further investigated by Gangbo & Mészáros (2022); Gangbo et al. (2022). Bayraktar et al.
1804   (2024) study the convergence of particle system on Wasserstein space.
1805
1806
       HJB regularization for MFC solver. For the HJB regularization technique in B.1, we used absolute
       value instead of a squared residual in (48) because it performs slightly better. In fact, both forms of
1807
       regularization could significantly reduce errors.
1808
1809   As for the reason why it reduces errors, we recall that we minimize the discretized loss function in
1810   the original Algorithm 1. As a consequence, there is a trend of overfitting to the discretized loss.
1811   Adding a regularizer could resolve this overfitting issue. Such issues for discretizations are also
1812
       discussed in Zhou & Lu (2024).

1849   Other methods to compute the score function. For the KDE method, an approximation for the
1850   density function is obtained through taking the kernel convolution with samples directly. Then, one
1851   compute its spatial derivative and obtain the score function via ∇x log ρ(t, x) = ∇x ρ(t, x)/ρ(t, x).
1852   However, the choice of kernel and bandwidth could be tricky, and the numerical error could be large,

1854

1858   Regularity for the MFC problem. Formula (3) requires third order derivatives for the velocity field
1859   f . As a consequence, we need regularity assumption for the MFC problem to guarantee that the ODE
1860
       system is meaningful. In this work, we assume that L and F are C 1,2,2 Hölder continuous, and G
       is C 2,2 Hölder continuous. According to Schauder estimates (Ladyzhenskaia et al., 1968, Chapter
1861
       4), these regularity assumptions guarantee that the velocity field has up to third-order derivatives,
1862
       ensuring that the ODE system (3) is meaningful.

1866
       the next stage. When the dynamic becomes complicated, the length of the time interval could also
       be adapted to suit the problem (Zhou et al., 2021).
1867
1868   Also, the neural network from the previous stage is probably not an accurate velocity for the new
1869   stage, but it does contain more information than a random initialization. Therefore, this nice initial-
1870   ization could serve as a warm-up (Zhou et al., 2023) for the training in the new stage, which could
1871   accelerate the convergence of this algorithm.

1874   scheme, and the Runge-Kutta scheme. The numerical results for different schemes are similar. We
1875   believe that the time discretization error is not the bottleneck of our algorithm at this moment. It is
1876   probably more important to train the neural network properly.
