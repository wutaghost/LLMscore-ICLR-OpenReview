                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Automatically adapting novels into screenplays is important for the TV, film, or
012                opera industries to promote products with low costs. The strong performances
013                of large language models (LLMs) in long-text generation call us to propose a
014                LLM based framework Reader-Rewriter (R2 ) for this task. However, there are
015                two fundamental challenges here. First, the LLM hallucinations may cause incon-
016                sistent plot extraction and screenplay generation. Second, the causality-embedded
017                plot lines should be effectively extracted for coherent rewriting. Therefore, two
018                corresponding tactics are proposed: 1) A hallucination-aware refinement method
019                (HAR) to iteratively discover and eliminate the affections of hallucinations; and 2)
020
                   a causal plot-graph construction method (CPC) based on a greedy cycle-breaking
                   algorithm to efficiently construct plot lines with event causalities. Recruiting
021
                   those efficient techniques, R2 utilizes two modules to mimic the human screen-
022
                   play rewriting process: The Reader module adopts a sliding window and CPC to
023                build the causal plot graphs, while the Rewriter module generates first the scene
024                outlines based on the graphs and then the screenplays. HAR is integrated into
025                both modules for accurate inferences of LLMs. Experimental results demonstrate
026                the superiority of R2 , which substantially outperforms three existing approaches
027                (51.3%, 22.6%, and 57.1% absolute increases) in pairwise comparison at the over-
028                all win rate for GPT-4o.1

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Screenplays are the bases of TV, film, or opera-like variants, which are often adapted directly from
034   novels. For example, 52% of the top 20 UK-produced films between 2007-2016 were based on
035
      adaptations of novels (Association & Economics, 2018) and the monthly average of TV or movie
      adaptations in USA for the first nine months of 2024 is more than 10 (Vulture, 2024). Generally,
036
      adapting novels into screenplays requires long-term efforts from professional writers. Automatically
037
      performing this task could significantly reduce production costs and promote the dissemination of
038   these works (Zhu et al., 2023). However, current work (Zhu et al., 2022; Mirowski et al., 2023; Han
039   et al., 2024; Morris et al., 2023) can only generate screenplays from predefined outlines. Therefore,
040   such an automatic novel-to-screenplay generation (N2SG) is highly expected.
041
      Considering the remarkable performances of large language models (LLMs) in text generation and
042
      comprehension tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022), we are interested in the large lan-
043
      guage model (LLM) based approach to perform N2SG. However, there are two fundamental chal-
044   lenges ahead before building such a system.
045
046   1) How to eliminate the affections of hallucinations in N2SG? Current LLMs like GPT-4 strug-
047
      gle with processing entire novels and often generate various inconsistent contents when processing
      lengthy input owing to the LLM hallucinations (Liu et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023).
048
      Existing refinement methods can only roughly reduce such inconsistency and cannot cope with long
049
      input data (Madaan et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023). 2) How to extract effective plot lines capturing
050   the complex causal relationships among events? Eliminating inconsistency alone cannot ensure that
051   generated stories have coherence and accurate plot lines as the original novels. Existing plot graph
052   based methods (Weyhrauch, 1997; Li et al., 2013) depict plot lines in the linearly ordered events.

073
      For the first challenge, we could just part-by-part refine the context associated with the inconsistency
      caused by hallucinations. Therefore, a hallucination-aware refinement method (HAR) is proposed
074
      in this work to iteratively eliminate the affections of LLM hallucinations for better information
075
      extraction and generation from long-form texts.
076
077   For the second one, the plot lines including causalities should be extracted for coherent rewriting.
078   Plot graphs are convenient to represent sequential events and can be extended as causal plot graphsto
079
      embed the causalities. Therefore, a causal plot-graph construction (CPC) is proposed in this article
      to robustly extract the causal relationships of events with the causal plot graphs.
080
081   Now the question is how to build an N2SG system with HAR and CPC. Looking at human screen-
082   writers (Figure 1), we see that they can successfully do it by a reading and rewriting composed
083   procedure (McKee, 1999): First, they read the novels to extract the key plot events and character
084   profiles (i.e., character biographies and their relationships) for constructing plot lines of the novels;
085
      Then, they rewrite the novels into screenplays according to those plot lines which are adapted into
      the story lines and scene goals as outline guiding the script writing. Both reading and rewriting steps
086
      may apply multiple times with multiple refinements until satisfaction is achieved.
087
088   Inspired by the iterative-refinement based human rewriting process, we propose the Reader-Rewriter
089   (R2 ) framework (Figure 2). The Reader adopts a sliding window based strategy to scan the whole
090   novel by crossing the chapter bounds, so that events and character profiles can be effectively captured
091
      for the following CPC process to build the causal plot graph, in which HAR is deployed to extract
      accurate events and character profiles. The Rewriter adopts a two-step strategy to first obtain the
092
      storylines and goals of all scenes as global guidance and then generate the screenplay scene by
093
      scene under the precise refinement from HAR, ensuring coherence and consistency across scenes.
094
095   Experiments on R2 are conducted on a test dataset consisting of several novel-screenplay pairs and
096   the evaluation is based on the proposed seven aspects. The GPT-4o-based evaluation shows that R2
097
      significantly outperforms the existing approaches in all aspects and gains overall absolute improve-
      ments of 51.3%, 22.6%, and 57.1% over three compared approaches. Human evaluators similarly
098
      confirm the strong performances of R2 , demonstrating its superiority in N2SG tasks.
099
100   In summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
101   1) Hallucination-aware refinement method (HAR) for refining the LLM outputs, which can eliminate
102   the inconsistencies caused by the LLM hallucinations and improve the applicability of LLMs.
103
104
      2) A causal plot-graph construction method (CPC), which takes a greedy cycle-breaking algorithm
      to extract the causality embedded plot graphs without cycles and low-strength relations of events.
105
106   3) A LLM based framework R2 for N2SG, which adopts HAR and CPC, and mimics the human
107   screenplay rewriting process with the Reader and Rewriter modules for the automatically causal-
      plot-graph based screenplay generation.

141
142
      There are two challenges for the LLM based N2SG. First, the LLM outputs can be quite different
143
      from the expected ones owing to the hallucinations. Consequently, LLMs may extract and gen-
144   erate non-existent events and screenplays. Second, understanding the plot lines of novels is very
145   important to generate coherent and consistent screenplays. Plot graphs are often used to describe
146   the plot lines, which should capture the complex causalities among events. For the first challenge,
147   the hallucination-aware refinement meth (HAR) is introduced, so that the affections of LLM hal-
148   lucinations can be significantly mitigated (Sec. 2.1). For the second challenge, a causal plot-graph
149   construction method is proposed to efficiently build the causalities embedded plot graphs (Sec. 2.2).
150
151
152   2.1   H ALLUCINATION -AWARE R EFINEMENT
153
154   HAR prompts the LLM to identify the intrinsic inconsistencies caused by the hallucinations, locate
155   where the hallucinations occur in the LLM outputs, and provide suggestions for refinement.
156   Denote the LLM as M. In the round t, HAR (Figure 2 (b)) first identifies the hallucination locations
157   loct where the intrinsic inconsistencies occur in the input xt and generates suggestions sugt describ-
158   ing how M refines them. Then the hallucination-aware context ct is extracted from the input and
159   corresponding support texts based on the hallucination locations, and input to M to refine the hallu-
160   cination part in xt as rt . Next, rt is merged into xt as xt+1 for the t + 1-th round of self-refinement.
161   This self-refinement process continues until the initial input data is fully processed and consistent,
      culminating in the refined output. Algorithm 1 presents the full process of HAR.

206   2.2        C AUSAL P LOT- GRAPH C ONSTRUCTION
207
208   The Causal Plot Graph The causal plot graph (Figure 3) embeds causalities of events by graphs.
209   Here causal means the graph is built according to the critical connections between key events, not
210   just their sequences in novels. Especially, this type of plot graph is designed as a directed acyclic
211   graph (DAG) for the causal relationships (edges) between the plot events (nodes).
212   Formally, the causal plot graph is a tuple G = ⟨E, D, W ⟩, where E denotes the events composed
213   of place and time, background, and description; D describes the causal relationships of events;
214   and W indicates the strengths of causal relationships, classified into three levels: High for direct
215   and significant influences; medium for partial or indirect influences; and low for minimal or weak
      influences.

      The Greedy Cycle-breaking Algorithm The initial extracted plot graph by LLMs often contains
217   cycles and low-strength relationships owing to the LLM hallucinations. Therefore, a variant of
218   Prim’s algorithm (Prim, 1957) is proposed to remove these cycles and unimportant relationships.
219   Called as the greedy cycle-breaking algorithm, it breaks cycles based on the relationship strength
220   and the degree of event node.
221
      Specifically, the causal relations are first ordered by their weights W as D from high level to low
222
      one. Two relations of the same level will be set to a lower sum of the degrees of its connected event
223   endpoints, so that more important edges between more important nodes are prioritized. Denote
224   each directional relation d(a, b) with a and b being the start and end points of d respectively. The
225   forward reachable set of endpoint x is Sx . If the end b of d ∈ D is already reachable to its start
226   a via previously selected causal relation edge, d is skipped to avoid forming a cycle. Otherwise,
227   it is added to the edge set F , and Sx of all endpoints of the edges in F is updated to reflect the
228   new connections, ensuring the set F remains acyclic. As a result, the set F forms the edge set
229   of the directed acyclic graph (DAG) that preserves the most significant causal relationships while
230   preventing cycles. Algorithm 2 gives the details of this algorithm.

246   3     T HE P ROPOSED R EADER -R EWRITER F RAMEWORK
247
248   R2 (Figure 2 (a)) consists of two main components according to the human rewriting process, the
249   Reader and the Rewriter. The former extracts the plot events and character profiles, and constructs
250
      the causal plot graphs, while the latter adapts the novels into screenplays with the graphs and profiles.
251
252   3.1   LLM- BASED R EADER
253
      The LLM-based Reader takes two sub-modules: The character event extraction and the plot graph
254
      extraction.
255
256   Character Event Extraction The Reader first identifies the plot events from the novel and extracts
257   them in a chapter-by-chapter way because of the limited input context window of LLMs. Here the
258   LLMs extract event elements such as description, place, and time (Figure 3). This is implemented
259   by prompting LLMs to generate structured outputs (Bi et al., 2024).
260
      To better cope with long texts of novels, a sliding window based technique is first introduced during
261
      event extraction. Sliding through the full novel with a chapter-sized window, this strategy ensures
262   the extracted events consistent across chapters. It is also applied to extract character profiles in
263   each chapter (Figure 2). Then HAR (Sec. 2.1) is taken to reduce the inconsistencies in plot events
264   and character profiles caused by LLM hallucinations. Here, the LLM is recursively prompted to
265   identify the inconsistencies and refine them according to the relevant chapter context, so that the
266   inconsistencies between the events and profiles are significantly reduced.
267
268   Plot Graph Extraction The extracted events are utilized to further construct the causal plot graphs
269   by the proposed CPC method. Specifically, firstly the LLM is recursively prompted to identify the
      new casual relationship according to the relevant chapter context. After the graph is connected and


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      no new relationships are added to it, CPC is performed to eliminate the cycles and low-weight edges
271   in the graph, so that the obtained causal graphs can more effectively and accurately reflect the plot
272   lines in the novels.
273
274   3.2      LLM-BASED R EWRITER
275
276   The Rewriter is organized into two subsequent steps: The first step is to create the screenplay outlines
277   of all scenes with the second for iteratively generating the screenplay of each scene. Those two
278   steps are packed as two corresponding sub-modules: The outline generation and the screenplay
279   generation. The final screenplay is iteratively refined by HAR.
280
281
      Outline Generation A screenplay adaptation outline can be constructed with the plot graph and
      character profiles (Figure 2), which consists of the story core elements, the screenplay structure,
282
      and a writing plan including the storyline and goal for each scene. Three different methods are
283
      used to traverse the plot graphs, depth-first traversal (DFT), breadth-first traversal (BFT), and the
284   original chapter order (Chapter), corresponding to three different screenplay adaptation modes, i.e.,
285   adapting the screenplay based on the main storyline (depth-first), the chronological sequence of
286   events (breadth-first), or the original narrative order of the novel.
287
      The misalignment of events and characters often happens during the outline generation, especially
288
      when generating the scene writing plans. Therefore, R2 performs HAR (Sec. 2.1) to get the initial
289
      screenplay adaptation outlines. This process focuses on the alignment of key events and major
290   characters and returns the final adaptation outlines.
291
292   Screenplay Generation Now each scene can be written based on its writing plan (Figure 2) which
293   includes the storyline, goal, place and time, and character experiences. The LLM is prompted to
294   generate each scene with the scene-related context which consists of the relevant chapter and the
295   previously generated scene. Then HAR verifies whether the generated scene meets the storyline
296   goals outlined in the writing plan. This approach ensures the consistency between the generated
297   screenplay scenes and maintains alignment with the related plot lines of the novels.

301   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
302
303   Dataset and Evaluation A novel-to-screenplay dataset was created by manually cleaning pairs
304   of novels and screenplays collected from public sources to evaluate the performance of N2SG. The
305   novels are categorized into popular and unpopular groups based on their ratings and number of
306
      reviews. To ensure fairness, both types are included in the testing sets. Such dataset will be open for
      future research of both trainable and train-free applications.
307
308   To ensure a balanced assessment, the proposed R2 method adopts five novels—two from the popular
309   category and three from the unpopular category as testing set, and no training samples are involved.
310   To further minimize subjective bias caused by reading long texts at one time, we select a total of 15
311   excerpts in novels for every human evaluator, with each excerpt limited to around 1000 tokens.
312   In the evaluation, 15 human evaluators are employed to focus on seven aspects, including Inter-
313   esting, Coherent, Human-like, Diction and Grammar, Transition, Script Format Compliance, and
314   Consistency. The pairwise comparisons through questionnaires are designed. Their responses were
315   then aggregated to compute the win rate (Equation 1) for each aspect. However, since human evalu-
316   ators often exhibit large variances in their judgments, GPT-4o2 is also utilized as the main evaluator
317   to give the judgment according to the same questionnaires. This can enhance objectivity and reduce
318
      potential bias in the results. Appendix A presents further details of the dataset and evaluation.
319

446   5     R ELATED W ORK
447
448   Long-form Generation Recently, many studies (Yang & Klein, 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Lei et al.,
449   2024) have emerged on long-form text generation with LLM, which aim at solving challenges in-
450   clude long-range dependency issues, content coherence, premise relevance, and factual consistency
451
      in long-form text generation, etc. Re3 (Yang et al., 2022) introduces a four-stage process (plan, draft,
      rewrite, and edit) for long story generation, using recursive reprompting and revision; DOC (Yang
452
      et al., 2023) focuses on generating stories with a detailed outline linked to characters and uses a
453
      controller to ensure coherence and control. Compared to those multi-stage generation frameworks
454   driven by the story outline, our approach uniquely leverages a condensed causal plot graph and
455   character profiles for automatic and consistent screenplay generation from novels.
456
457
      Other work focuses on constructing human-AI collaboration frameworks for screenplay genera-
      tion (Zhu et al., 2022; Mirowski et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023). Dramatron
458
      (Mirowski et al., 2023) presents a hierarchical story generation framework that uses prompt chaining
459
      to guide LLMs for key screenplay elements, building a human collaboration system for long-form
460   screenplay generation. IBSEN (Han et al., 2024) allows users to interact with the directors and char-
461   acter agents to control the screenplay generation process. These studies emphasize collaborative,
462   multi-agent approaches with human-LLM interactions. In contrast, our approach solves N2SG by
463   automatically generating long-form screenplays from novels, minimizing the user involvement.
464
465   LLM-Based Self-Refine Approach Iterative self-refinement is a fundamental feature of human
466   problem-solving (Simon, 1962). LLMs can also improve the quality of their generation through
467   self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023; Saunders et al., 2022; Scheurer et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2023;
468   Peng et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023). LLM-Augmenter (Peng et al., 2023) uses a plug-and-play
469   module to enhance LLM outputs by incorporating external knowledge and automatic feedbacks.
470   Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) demonstrates that LLMs can improve their outputs across various
471   generation tasks by multi-turn prompting. In this paper, R2 utilizes the LLMs-based self-refinement
472   approach to tackle challenges in causal plot graph extraction and long-form text generation.
473

      6     C ONCLUSION
475
476
477
      This paper introduces a LLM based framework R2 for the novel-to-screenplay generation task
      (N2SG). Two techniques are first proposed, a hallucination-aware refinement (HAR) for better ex-
478
      ploring LLMs by eliminating the affections of hallucinations and a causal plot-graph construction
479
      (CPC) for better capturing the causal event relationships. Adopting those techniques while mimick-
480   ing the human rewriting process leads to the Reader and Rewriter composed system for plot graph
481   extraction and scene-by-scene screenplay generation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that R2
482   significantly outperforms the competitors. The success of R2 establishes a benchmark for N2SG
483   tasks and demonstrates the potential of LLMs in adapting long-form novels into coherent screen-
484   plays. Future work could explore integrating control modules or multi-agent frameworks into R2
485   to impose more stringent constraints and expand it to broader long-form story generation tasks to
      further develop the capability of our framework.

612   Evaluation Methods Similar to the prior work such as Re3 (Yang et al., 2022) and DOC (Yang
613   et al., 2023), pairwise experiments are conducted by designing questionnaires and presenting them
614   to human raters. Each questionnaire consists of an original novel excerpt or a logline (depending
615   on the competitors), two screenplay excerpts (denoted as A and B, with random ordering), and a
616   set of questions evaluating seven aspects (Table 5). Each aspect includes one to two questions, with
617   control questions taken to ensure accuracy in the responses. Each survey question has only four
618   options: A, B, or both are good, or neither are good.
