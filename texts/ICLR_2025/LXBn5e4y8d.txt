011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Generating consecutive images of lip movements that align with a given speech
014            in audio-driven lip synthesis is a challenging task. While previous studies have
015
               made strides in synchronization and visual quality, lip intelligibility and video
               fluency remain persistent challenges. This work proposes FluentLip, a two-stage
016
               approach for audio-driven lip synthesis, incorporating three featured strategies. To
017
               improve lip synchronization and intelligibility, we integrate a phoneme extractor
018            and encoder to generate a fusion of audio and phoneme information for multi-
019            modal learning. Additionally, we employ optical flow consistency loss to ensure
020            natural transitions between image frames. Furthermore, we incorporate a diffu-
021            sion chain during the training of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to im-
022            prove both stability and efficiency. We evaluate our proposed FluentLip through
023            extensive experiments, comparing it with five state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches
024            across five metrics, including a proposed metric called Phoneme Error Rate (PER)
025            that evaluates lip pose intelligibility and video fluency. The experimental results
026
               demonstrate that our FluentLip approach is highly competitive, achieving signif-
               icant improvements in smoothness and naturalness. In particular, it outperforms
027
               these SOTA approaches by approximately 16.3% in Fréchet Inception Distance
028
               (FID) and 35.2% in PER.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Audio-driven lip synthesis, also known as Talking Face Generation (TFG), generates a coherent
034   sequence of mouth movements that are consistent with the given audio input. It has become a
035   prominent topic of research (Jamaludin et al., 2019) due to its wide range of real-world applications,
036   such as film dubbing (Kim et al., 2018), video bandwidth reduction (Suwajanakorn et al., 2017)
037   and face animation (Song et al., 2019). Despite its potential, achieving perfect lip synchronization
038
      remains a significant challenge. Hence, it has attracted considerable attention from researchers.
039   Numerous methods have been proposed to improve synchronization and visual quality in audio-
040   driven lip synthesis. To enhance synchronization, Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020) extends Sync-
041   Net (Chung & Zisserman, 2017) to the RGB space, using a lip sync discriminator to calculate sync
042   loss and penalize asynchronous lip pose generated. SyncTalkface (Park et al., 2022) calculates sync
043   loss by measuring lip pose feature distances between synthesized and ground truth videos. Talk-
044
      Lip (Wang et al., 2023a) leverages a pre-trained lip-reading expert (Shi et al., 2022) to guide lip
      pose synthesis. Moreover, many approaches, like Wav2Lip, incorporate Generative Adversarial
045
      Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) to enhance visual quality.
046
047   While synchronization and visual quality have been well-studied, less attention has been given to
048   improving lip pose intelligibility and video fluency. Notably, TalkLip employs a lip-reading ex-
049   pert to enhance lip pose intelligibility (Wang et al., 2023a). In our work, we address these gaps
050
      by proposing a phoneme-based two-stage approach with optical flow consistency (denoted as Flu-
      entLip), specifically designed to improve both lip pose intelligibility and video fluency.
051
052   Specifically, we utilize a phoneme extractor to automatically recognize and align phonemes from
053   the audio and a phoneme encoder to generate the corresponding phoneme embeddings. These em-
      beddings are then fused with audio embeddings to serve as the reference input for the lip sync

      discriminator and generator within GANs. To further enhance fluency, we introduce an optical flow
055   consistency loss that penalizes unnatural transitions between frames during training. Additionally,
056   we employ a diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) with a diffusion chain to accelerate convergence and
057   stabilize the training process (Wang et al., 2023b), ultimately improving visual quality.
058
      The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows.
059
060          • We leverage a phoneme extractor and encoder to create a fusion of phoneme and audio
061            embeddings, improving lip pose intelligibility. Additionally, we develop an optical flow
062            consistency loss to guide training, ensuring smooth transitions between frames and en-
063            hancing the naturalness of synthesized videos. Our approach specifically addresses the
064            underexplored challenges of lip pose intelligibility and video fluency in audio-driven lip
065            synthesis.
066          • We integrate a diffusion chain into the training process of GANs, leading to faster conver-
067            gence and enhancing the stability of the training process. The quality of the synthesized
068            videos is improved, providing realistic and visually appealing outputs that align closely
069            with the corresponding audio input.
070          • We evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed FluentLip with five state-of-the-art (SOTA)
071            approaches, demonstrating a notable performance of approximately 16.3% in Fréchet In-
072            ception Distance (FID) and 35.2% in Phoneme Error Rate (PER). Additionally, we intro-
073            duce a novel metric that leverages insights from the lip-reading expert and the Grapheme-
074            to-Phoneme (G2P) model to assess the perceptual performance of various approaches.

      2.1   S PEECH -D RIVEN TALKING FACE G ENERATION
079
080   Talking face generation was first proposed in the 1990s (Yehia et al., 1998), with early approaches
081   primarily using Hidden Markov Models (HMM) (Bregler et al., 1997). In recent years, deep learn-
082   ing has emerged as the dominant TFG method, which can be generally classified into intermediate
083   representation-based approaches and reconstruction-based approaches (Park et al., 2022).
084   The intermediate representation-based approaches focus on learning facial representations, such as
085   3D meshes, which are used for facial synthesis. For example, SadTalker (Zhang et al., 2023) gen-
086   erates 3D-aware face renders for synthesizing talking faces, while Everybody’s Talkin’ (Song et al.,
087   2022) reconstructs 3D meshes from extracted facial parameters to generate video sequences. How-
088   ever, these approaches are limited in their generalizability to arbitrary characters, and 3D modeling
089   often struggles to represent mouth details (Wang et al., 2023a).
090   In contrast, reconstruction-based approaches primarily rely on end-to-end encoder-decoder architec-
091   tures, which avoid the limitations of intermediate representations and offer improved mouth details
092   synthesis. It began with ObamaNet (Kumar et al., 2017), which focused on a specific character.
093   This was followed by Speech2Vid(Jamaludin et al., 2019) and LipGAN (KR et al., 2019), which
094   improved generalizability allowing for video generation of arbitrary characters. A breakthrough
095   came with Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020), which introduced SyncNet (Chung & Zisserman, 2017)
096   as a lip sync expert, achieving SOTA synchronization performance. More recent efforts have aimed
097   at improving visual quality based on the Wav2Lip model. Gupta et al. (2023) pre-trained a VQGAN
098
      model (Esser et al., 2021) to train Wav2Lip in quantized space, improving visual quality to a max-
      imum of 4K resolution. Diff2Lip (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024) uses a diffusion model (Ho et al.,
099
      2020) to replace the Seq2Seq framework in Wav2Lip, further improving visual quality.
100
101   At the same time, some works have taken an alternative approach by focusing on issues that impact
102   human viewing, particularly by integrating generated videos into real-life scenarios. Among the
103   most novel concerns is lip pose intelligibility. Wang et al. (2023a) is the first to highlight this issue
104
      in the context of TFG, introducing AV-HuBERT (Shi et al., 2022) as a lip-reading expert to improve
      lip pose intelligibility and opening a new direction for TFG research.
105
106   Despite the increasing number of works in TFG, surprisingly little attention has been given to video
107   fluency. Although these subtle details may be difficult to perceive with the naked eye, as visual
      quality continues to improve, fluency will become a critical issue. To fill this gap, our work intro-

      duces phonemes, commonly used in the Text-to-Speech (TTS) domain, along with a novel metric to
109   promote assessing lip pose intelligibility. Furthermore, we incorporate optical flow consistency loss
110   to improve the fluency of generated videos.

      2.2   P HONEME -BASED M ULTIMODAL L EARNING
113
114   Most previous TFG studies employ audio or text as the input driver with their unimodal learning
115   model. ATVGnet (Chen et al., 2019) and Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020) employ audio as driven
116   input, while ParaLip (Liu et al., 2022) and Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2023) are text-driven.
117   Since audio varies with different speakers and text may contain homophones, it’s difficult to rep-
118   resent speech content with just one of them. Phoneme (Zhang et al., 2022) is a more microscopic
119   concept widely used in the TTS domain, focusing on syllables rather than words. Although some
120   previous works employ phonemes in TFG, such as Text2video (Zhang et al., 2022) and text-based
121   editing video (Fried et al., 2019), few of them have extended unimodal to multimodal learning.
122
      Thus, we introduce multimodal learning in our work by combining audio and phoneme as driven in-
      puts. Phonemes capture precise speech content, while audio conveys robust and ample information,
123
      helping to judge speech content accurately and ultimately enhancing lip pose intelligibility.

126   2.3   C ONSECUTIVE I MAGE G ENERATION
127
128
      Video generation can be viewed as a process of generating consecutive images in frames, and the
      three primary frameworks are prevalent for solving it: Seq2Seq, GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014),
129
      and diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020). Among these, Seq2Seq serves as the foundational model,
130
      while diffusion models have demonstrated outperforming GANs in image generation (Dhariwal &
131   Nichol, 2021). Most of the previous TFG works use Seq2Seq to generate frame images, often
132   coupled with GANs to enhance the visual quality of these images, such as Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al.,
133   2020). Some approaches use the diffusion model as an image generator, which also achieves good
134   results, such as Diff2Lip (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024). Nevertheless, GANs training often comes
135   up with the mode collapse (Wang et al., 2023b), a challenge that has been overlooked in previous
136   TFG works that leverage GANs. To mitigate this, Wang et al. (2023b) propose Diffusion-GAN,
137   which integrates a diffusion model into the GANs training to generate Gaussian instance noises in
138   high-dimensional data space, effectively improving the stability and overall performance of GANs
139
      training. Inspired by it, our work also employs a diffusion model to stabilize GANs training and
      improve its performance.
140
141   Unlike naive image generation, consecutive image generation should consider the fluency between
142   frames. In real-life videos, objects are moving regularly with specific trends, so the pixel points
143   move more smoothly. Naive image generation methods often neglect this, leading to irregular and
144   trendless pixel point movement between frames. Optical flow (Horn & Schunck, 1981), a tech-
145
      nique frequently used to measure pixel displacement between two consecutive frames, is estimated
      by FlowNet (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015; Ilg et al., 2017) or more popular Recurrent All-pairs Field
146
      Transforms (RAFT) (Teed & Deng, 2020) and is often applied in dynamic image detection. For
147
      example, self-driving automobiles use optical flow to predict the motions and traces of surrounding
148   objects (Hu et al., 2020). Therefore, we assert that optical flow is an effective measure of video
149   fluency and design a novel loss function based on optical flow to penalize irregular pixel moves gen-
150   erated, enhancing video fluency. Note that our work first employs optical flow in the TFG domain.

      bedding and optical flow consistency strategies to address lip pose intelligibility and video fluency,
      and to improve lip synchronization.

201

202
      side a Transformer-based architecture, which improves the model’s ability to capture the sequential
203

335   4.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETTINGS
336
337   Dataset We train our model using the LRS2 dataset (Afouras et al., 2018) and evaluate it on unseen
338   test sets of both the GRID (Cooke et al., 2006) and LRS2 datasets. The GRID is a large multi-
339   talker audiovisual sentence corpus whose video files have a resolution of 720×576 and a frame rate
340   of 25 fps. The audio from each video file is extracted with a maximum amplitude value of 1 and
341   downsampled to 16 kHz. Sentences of GRID consist of a relatively fixed length of independent
342   short words. The LRS2 is an open-world audio-visual speech recognition dataset whose video and
343
      extracted audio files have the same parameters of 25fps and 16kHz with GRID, respectively. Unlike
      GRID, sentences of LRS2 have more meaningful content of varying lengths, and the scenes are more
344
      diverse and irregular, making LRS2 more reflective of real-life scenarios.
345
346   Metrics We evaluate lip synchronization and the quality of synthesized images using widely used
347   metrics such as FID (Heusel et al., 2017), SSIM (Wang, 2004), LSE-D and LSE-C (Prajwal et al.,
348   2020). LSE-D and LSE-C are calculated via a pre-trained sync net to measure the synchronization
349
      of lip movements, while FID and SSIM quantitatively assess image quality. In addition, we pro-
      posed a novel metric called Phoneme Error Rate (PER), which evaluates lip pose intelligibility and
350
      video fluency. PER is computed by comparing phonemes predicted from synthesized video with
351
      real phonemes extracted from audio, using a pre-trained lip-reading model AV-HuBERT (Shi et al.,
352   2022). Unlike the Word Error Rate (WER) metric proposed by AV-HuBERT and adopted by Talk-
353   Lip (Wang et al., 2023a) in TFG, PER focuses directly on phonemes, avoiding the shortcomings of
354   word-based evaluation, as the same phoneme sequence can represent multiple distinct words.
355
      Baselines We compare our model against several SOTA lip synthesis models, including
356
      ATVGnet (Chen et al., 2019), Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020), SadTalker (Zhang et al., 2023), Talk-
357
      Lip (Wang et al., 2023a), and Diff2Lip (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024). ATVGnet is the first model to
358   use an Attention-based Transformer Network (AT Network) and a Video Generator Network (VG
359   Network) for generating talking face videos. Wav2Lip introduced the innovative use of a lip sync
360   net in its reconstruction-based method. SadTalker generates videos by leveraging intermediate 3D
361   Morphable Models (3DMM) and a 3D-aware face renderer. TalkLip builds upon Wav2Lip by in-
362   tegrating lip-reading loss and contrastive loss with guidance from a lip-reading expert. Diff2Lip is
363   the latest SOTA model, adopting a diffusion model instead of the traditional Seq2Seq framework,
364   achieving superior performance in lip synthesis.
365   Implementation Details We have trained our models in environment configuration as follows: OS
366   of Ubuntu20.04, CPU of AMD EPYC 9754 (18v CPU), GPU of RTX4090D (24GB) and RAM of
367   60GB. Our model has been trained in stage 1 for 90k steps with a batch size of 40, and in stage 2 for
368   35k steps with the same batch size. To ensure fairness and rigor, we carry out the experiments for
369   both our model and other approaches under the same setting and on a consistent range of the dataset.

528   5      C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we have studied the challenges inherent in the talking face generation by propos-
531   ing the FluentLip approach, which synthesizes facial videos with improved fluency and lip pose
532   intelligibility. Unlike previous approaches that primarily focus on synchronization and visual qual-
533   ity, our FluentLip emphasizes lip intelligibility and video fluency by incorporating several novel
534   components. We introduce optical flow consistency loss and utilize phonemes as input to enable
535   multimodal learning, while also employing a diffusion model to stabilize the training of GANs.
536   Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed FluentLip approach, show-
537   casing highly competitive performances in lip synchronization and visual quality compared to five
538   SOTA approaches from the literature. Notably, FluentLip outperforms these approaches in terms
539   of fluency. In addition to these computational results, we conduct an in-depth analysis of the key
      components to shed light on their roles in the performance of the proposed approach.
