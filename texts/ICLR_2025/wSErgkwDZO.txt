009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            As the capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) continue to
012            improve, the need for higher-order capability evaluation of MLLMs is increasing.
013            However, there is a lack of work evaluating MLLM for higher-order perception
014            and understanding of Chinese visual content. To fill the gap, we introduce the
015            Chinese Image Implication understanding Benchmark, CII-Bench, which aims
016
               to assess the higher-order perception and understanding capabilities of MLLMs
               for Chinese images. CII-Bench stands out in several ways compared to existing
017
               benchmarks. Firstly, to ensure the authenticity of the Chinese context, images in
018
               CII-Bench are sourced from the Chinese Internet and manually reviewed, with
019            corresponding answers also manually crafted. Additionally, CII-Bench incorpo-
020            rates images that represent Chinese traditional culture, such as famous Chinese
021            traditional paintings, which can deeply reflect the model’s understanding of Chi-
022            nese traditional culture. Through extensive experiments on CII-Bench across mul-
023            tiple MLLMs, we have made significant findings. Initially, a substantial gap is
024            observed between the performance of MLLMs and humans on CII-Bench. The
025            highest accuracy of MLLMs attains 64.4%, where as human accuracy averages
026            78.2%, peaking at an impressive 81.0%. Subsequently, MLLMs perform worse
027
               on Chinese traditional culture images, suggesting limitations in their ability to un-
               derstand high-level semantics and lack a deep knowledge base of Chinese tradi-
028
               tional culture. Finally, it is observed that most models exhibit enhanced accuracy
029
               when image emotion hints are incorporated into the prompts. We believe that
030            CII-Bench will enable MLLMs to gain a better understanding of Chinese seman-
031            tics and Chinese-specific images, advancing the journey towards expert artificial
032            general intelligence (AGI).

053   With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
      (Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023c; Ye et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024) have demonstrated exceptional

056   2023b;a; Xu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Jin
057   et al., 2024). These models are not only capable of processing and generating text but also excel at in-
058   tegrating and interpreting information across multiple modalities, such as images, sound, and video.
059   However, despite the significant progress made in tasks like image recognition and generation, a
060   crucial research question remains: Can these models truly understand and interpret images that have
061   deep implications? (Liu et al., 2024b) construct an English image implication understanding dataset,
062   II-Bench, and the experiments on MLLMs and human subjects reveal a substantial gap in the mod-

065
      impressive accuracy of 80.9% on II-Bench, approaching the average human accuracy of 90.3%. This
066   progress underscores the need for more challenging benchmarks that incorporate richer scenes and
067   deeper implications to continue pushing the boundary of image implication understanding task.

069
      In contrast to English images, Chinese images often embody richer scenes (Xu, 2023) and deeper
      implications as Figure 1 shows. For instance, Chinese traditional landscape paintings not only depict
070
      natural scenery but also convey profound philosophical concepts, such as the harmony between
071
      humans and nature, through artistic techniques like the interplay of void and solid, the use of negative
072   space, and the brushwork. As the famous Chinese poet Su Shi noted, “Poetry and painting share
073   the same essence, embodying both craftsmanship and purity”. The depth of Chinese images lies
074   not only in their aesthetic appeal but also in the underlying spirit and philosophy they express.
075   Similarly, New Year paintings, as a significant carrier of Chinese traditional culture, typically use
076   symbolism and implication to convey wishes for good fortune, prosperity, and peace. Unlike the
077   directness often found in English imagery, Chinese images emphasize the creation of atmosphere
078   and subtle expression, requiring viewers to possess certain cultural knowledge to accurately grasp
079   their meanings. This cultural disparity leads to significant differences in the modes of expression and
080
      meaning conveyed between Chinese and English images, highlighting the need to consider cultural
      context when evaluating the capability of MLLMs to understand the deep implications of images.
081

083   higher-order perception, reasoning, and understanding abilities of models within a Chinese context.
084   This benchmark allows us to gain a clearer understanding of these models’ interpretive capacities,
085   offering new insights into their application in cross-cultural environments, and thus advancing the
086
      research and development of MLLMs.

120   2.1    M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
121
122
      With the rapid development of large language models (LLMs) (Aakanksha et al., 2022; Won et al.,
      2022; Chiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023a;b; Team, 2024; Cai et al., 2024),
123
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have made significant improvements. Many works
124
      incorporate additional module inputs on LLMs, effectively bridging the gap between visual and lan-
125   guage. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023c) encodes images using ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and employs
126   a Q-Former to map visual features into the language space. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b;a; 2024a;
127   Li et al., 2024a) utilizes an MLP as the connector between the visual encoder and the LLM back-
128   bone. Similarly, mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye et al., 2023) employs a modality-adaptive module to facilitate
129   the collaboration between visual and language modalities by mapping them into a unified represen-
130   tation space. Subsequent works (Wang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c; Young et al.,
131   2024; Laurençon et al., 2024; GLM et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024; Wang et al.,
132   2024)further enhance MLLMs by designing novel modules for more sufficient modality alignment.
133
134   2.2    I MAGE I MPLICATION U NDERSTANDING
135
136   Image implication understanding represents a more complex and challenging task than conventional
137   image understanding. This advanced cognitive process necessitates multi-hop reasoning ability and
138   sophisticated theory of mind (ToM), capabilities that are intrinsic to human cognition (Desai et al.,
139   2022; Hessel et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024; Strachan et al., 2024; Street et al.,
140   2024; Horvitz et al., 2024). II-Bench (Liu et al., 2024b) is the first benchmark specifically designed
141
      to evaluate MLLMs in both image understanding and reasoning through English image implication.

      3.1    OVERVIEW OF CII-B ENCH
147
      We present the Chinese Image Implication Understanding Benchmark (CII-Bench), a novel bench-
148
      mark designed to assess the perceptual, reasoning, and comprehension abilities of MLLMs in the
149
      context of Chinese imagery. This benchmark includes a diverse range of visual content such as
150   traditional Chinese traditional artworks, comics, posters, and Chinese Internet memes, all rich in
151   visual information and cultural significance. The main goal of CII-Bench is to evaluate if current
152   MLLMs can leverage their understanding and knowledge of Chinese culture to accurately interpret
153   the deeper implications and abstract information within these images.
154
      CII-Bench comprises 698 images across various categories, with detailed classification and domain
155
      statistics provided in Appendix A. These images are manually collected and annotated by 30 under-
156   graduate students from different disciplines and institutions, sourced from several well-known image
157   websites. Each image is paired with 1 to 3 multiple-choice questions, each offering six options with
158   only one correct answer. One fixed question asks, “What is the implication in this image?” Ad-
159   ditional questions for the same image probe different levels of understanding, such as overarching
160   interpretation and nuanced details. The benchmark includes 800 multiple-choice questions, with 765
161   for the test set and 35 for developing and validating few-shot tasks. Figure 3 provides representative
      examples from CII-Bench.

190   3.2.1        DATA C OLLECTION
191
192   We collect 17,695 raw images from various renowned illustration websites, ensuring a sufficiently
193
      extensive raw dataset. Our collectors are well instructed to adhere to copyright and license regula-
      tions, avoiding data from sites prohibiting copy and redistribution. For detailed information on the
194
      specific websites from which we collect images, please refer to Appendix D.
195
196
      3.2.2        DATA F ILTRATION
197
198   After collecting the raw images, we meticulously design a three-stage data filtering process: In the
199   first stage, we focus on image deduplication. We utilize image similarity algorithms for pixel-level
200   comparison to eliminate duplicates and preserve dataset uniqueness; In the second stage, we regulate
201   text prevalence in images. Optical Character Recognition (OCR) technology identifies textual areas
202   and disqualifies images exceeding set text-area ratios, maintaining a visual-centric dataset; In the
203   third stage, images undergo rigorous visual inspection, discarding those without metaphorical depth
204   based on strict criteria. This process refines the dataset, rejecting over 95% of initial images and
205
      securing under 1,000 high-quality ones.
206
207   3.2.3        DATA A NNOTATION
208
      The annotation process for the benchmark was meticulously designed through several steps to ensure
209   rigor and precision as following. The detailed annotation protocol can be found in Appendix D.
210
211   Preparation and Consistency Check: Before formal annotation, annotators first acquaint them-
212
      selves with standard templates and guidelines. A pre-annotation round on a shared image batch
      ensures uniform standard understanding, with discrepancies resolved through discussion.
213
214   Multiple Rounds of Annotation and Cross-Validation: To reduce bias, each image receives an-
215   notations from two different annotators. Cross-validation follows, with a third-party review for
      significant discrepancies, guaranteeing accuracy.


                                                                             4

216
      Refinement of Annotation Content: Annotators annotate each image’s difficulty, type, emotional
217   label, domain, and rhetorical devices based on specific criteria, ensuring consistency and compara-
218   bility. They also craft 1 to 3 refined questions per image, each with one correct answer among five
219   distractor options, including the default question, “What is the implication in this image?”
220
      Context Analysis: During the annotation process, annotators assess the image’s cultural and back-
221
      ground significance, especially for implications and rhetorical devices, consulting relevant materials
222
      for accuracy.
223
224   Post-Annotation Review: Upon completion, annotations undergo a thorough quality review for
225   any oversight, errors, or inconsistencies. Based on the evaluation results, feedback is provided to the
226
      annotators, with re-annotations as necessary to maintain data quality.

228   3.3   DATASET S TATISTICS
229
230   CII-Bench comprises 698 images, each accompanied by 1 to 3 multiple-choice questions, totaling
231
      800 questions. We randomly select 35 of these questions to construct a few-shot development set
      and validation set. On average, each question is approximately 11 characters long, while each option
232
      has an average length of 28 characters. Additionally, each image is supplemented with a manually
233
      written description by the annotators, which provides a detailed explanation of the image’s content,
234   nuances, and the human interpretation of its deep implication.
235
236   CII-Bench covers images across six distinct domains: Life, Art, Society, Politics, Environment,
237
      and Chinese Traditional Culture. The types of images are diverse, including Illustration, Meme,
      Poster, Single-panel Comic, Multi-panel Comic, and Painting. Based on human understanding,
238
      these images are categorized into three levels of difficulty: Easy, Medium, and Hard. Moreover,
239
      the images are classified according to the emotional information they convey: Positive, Neutral, or
240   Negative. Each image is also manually annotated with the rhetorical devices employed, including
241   Metaphor, Exaggeration, Symbolism, Visual Dislocation, Antithesis, Analogy, Personification, and
242   Contrast. Detailed statistical information is provided in Appendix A.

245   4     E XPERIMENT
246
247   We conduct systematic experiments on both open-source and closed-source MLLMs using CII-
248   Bench. For each model, we employ eight different configurations: None (zero-shot), 1-shot, 2-shot,
249   3-shot, CoT, Domain, Emotion, and Rhetoric. “None” represents the use of a standard prompt
250
      without any additional information. “Emotion” indicates the inclusion of information related to the
      emotional polarity of the image (e.g., positive, negative) in the prompt, “Domain” involves adding
251
      information about the image’s domain (e.g., life, art), and “Rhetoric” refers to including details
252
      about the rhetorical devices used in the image (e.g., metaphor, contrast) in the prompt. Additionally,
253   to verify the necessity of images in problem-solving, we select a portion of LLMs to complete tasks
254   without image input. For consistency across all MLLMs and LLMs, we use identical prompts and
255   experiment setup, with specific details available in Appendix E.

376   Analysis of Chain-of-Thought (CoT). In Table 2, we evaluate the impact of Chain-of-Thought

379   CoT, but this drops to 38.9% with CoT; similarly, LLaVA-1.6-72B scores decrease from 48.0% to

382
      Upon analyzing the models’ responses, we find that those models showing a decrease in accuracy
      with CoT often suffer from overinterpretation, where questions that were initially answered correctly

390   on model accuracy, we conduct an ablation study by providing relevant label information (such
391   as emotion, domain, and rhetoric) in the prompts. The results in Table 2 show that emotion labels
392   significantly improve model accuracy, followed by domain and rhetoric labels, both of which exhibit
393   similar effectiveness.
394   This result aligns with human intuition. The answer options typically include negative, positive, and
395   neutral choices. When the model receives emotional information, it can eliminate some irrelevant
396   options, naturally leading to higher accuracy. In contrast, domain and rhetoric information gener-
397   ally do not effectively help the model eliminate options, resulting in more limited improvements.
398   Additionally, from a model training perspective, models tend to have a more mature understanding
399   of emotions, while specific nouns in rhetoric and domain labels are often custom-defined. During
400
      pre-training, the model may not have encountered a large number of descriptions for such specific

423   4.3.1    E VALUATION M ETRIC
424
425   Chinese traditional painting, a cornerstone of Chinese traditional culture, encompasses a rich
426   tapestry of styles and techniques developed over millennia. These paintings are typically cate-
427   gorized based on their subject matter (e.g., landscape paintings, flower-and-bird paintings, figure
428
      paintings, and New Year paintings) or their stylistic and skill (e.g., court paintings, meticulous brush
      paintings, freehand brush paintings, and color-and-ink paintings). Each category embodies unique
429
      characteristics that reflect China’s artistic evolution and philosophical underpinnings.
430
431   To comprehensively assess MLLMs’ understanding of Chinese traditional paintings, we develop a
      multifaceted evaluation metric. This metric is designed to probe both the surface-level information

432
      readily apparent in the artwork and the deeper culture and history that informs its creation and in-
433   terpretation. Our evaluation metric encompasses five key perspectives: Surface-level Information,
434   Aesthetic Characteristics, Brush and Ink Skills, Culture and History, and Deep Implications. For
435   each perspective, we give its detailed description in Appendix C.

496   5.1   I NTERPRETABILITY A NALYSIS OF C HINESE I MAGE I MPLICATIONS
497
498   The essence of Chinese image implications is deeply rooted in deep cultural heritage and complex
499   contextual associations, which enables them to convey profound messages through nuanced expres-
500   sions. For example, in traditional Chinese art forms such as landscape and New Year paintings, the
501   imagery transcends mere depiction of nature or daily occurrences. Instead, it embodies emotions,
502
      philosophical insights, and societal norms through metaphorical and highly symbolic expressions.
      These symbols, like the pine tree, plum blossom, and crane, are not superficial meaning but are
503
      steeped in centuries of cultural tradition, representing resilience, purity, and longevity.
504
505   However, deciphering these complex messages can be challenging, particularly for those unfamiliar
506   with the cultural and historical narratives tied to these symbols. This contrasts with English image
507   implications, which often convey messages through more straightforward and explicit symbolism.
508
      As a result, the interpretability of Chinese image implications depends to some extent on recon-
      structing and resonating with the cultural context, which is what makes them unique: their meaning
509
      is not only visual but also culturally resonant, bridging across time and space.
510
511   Moreover, the interpretability of Chinese image implications has new changed in the modern era.
512   Globalization and the surge of internet culture have intertwined foreign elements with traditional
513   Chinese culture, birthing new symbols and implications. This intersection introduces additional
514
      layers of meaning, complicating the interpretation of traditional symbols.
515
516   5.2   W HY C HOOSE C HINESE T RADITIONAL PAINTINGS TO E VALUATE C HINESE
517         T RADITIONAL C ULTURE ?
518
      The imagery associated with Chinese traditional culture often embodies complex implications, en-
519
      compassing customs, historical anecdotes, and legendary tales, making direct evaluation particularly
520
      challenging. Chinese traditional painting, intrinsically intertwined with Chinese traditional culture,
521   offers a viable proxy for this assessment. The unique value of Chinese traditional painting lies in its
522   embodiment of Chinese cultural connotations, aesthetic implications, and distinctive artistic expres-
523   sion. The core philosophical concepts of Confucianism, Taoism, and Buddhism, along with their
524   humanistic essence, have consistently permeated the entire trajectory of Chinese painting history.
525   Consequently, we have chosen to evaluate MLLMs’ comprehension of Chinese traditional culture
526   through an in-depth analysis of their understanding of Chinese traditional paintings.

531
      The development of CII-Bench marks a significant step forward in evaluating the capabilities of
      multimodal large models (MLLMs) and brings us closer to achieving expert artificial general intel-
532
      ligence (AGI). This benchmark promotes a deeper exploration of the higher-order theory of mind in
533
      MLLMs. Experimental results indicate that current MLLMs still exhibit a significant gap compared
534   to humans in understanding the implications of images within a Chinese context. We found that
535   most MLLMs lack a deep knowledge base of Chinese traditional culture, leading to a superficial

537   emotion hints into prompts often improves model performance, suggesting that models still struggle
538   with emotional understanding, which in turn leads to misinterpretation of implications. We believe
539   that CII-Bench will inspire the academic community to further develop the next generation of mul-
      timodal foundational models that move toward expert AGI.

      D.2   G ENERAL G UIDELINES
883
884   General Principles:
885
886    • Annotations should be accurate and consistent.
887    • All questions, options and explanations should be written in Chinese.
888
       • Any images without metaphorical implications should be discarded.
889
890   Specific Instructions:
891
892    • Each image needs to be categorized as one of the following image types: single-panel comic,
893      multi-panel comic, poster, meme, illustration or painting.
894    • Each image needs to be categorized as one of the following difficulty levels from a human un-
895      derstanding perspective: easy, middle, or hard.
896    • Each image needs to be categorized as one of the following domains: life, art, society, politics,
897      environment or Chinese traditional culture.
898
       • Each image needs to be categorized as one of the following emotions: positive, neutral or nega-
899      tive.
900
       • Each image needs to be categorized as one or more of the following rhetoric: metaphor, exag-
901
         gerate, symbolism, contrast, visual dislocation, antithesis, analogy, personification or others.
902
903    • Each image needs a human explanation and implication description.
904    • Each image needs 1-3 questions about the fine-grained metaphorical implications of the image,
905      each with one correct answer and five distractor options.
906
907   D.3   DATA Q UALITY A SSURANCE
908
909   To further ensure the quality and reliability of the data, the annotated datasets were double-checked
910
      and cross-validated. Each question was manually validated by at least five annotators. Any in-
      consistencies or misinterpretations found were thoroughly examined and resolved by consensus of
911
      the annotation team, thus improving the reliability of the dataset while ensuring consistency of the
912
      annotations. In total, we conducted five rounds of data quality checks to ensure data quality and
913   ultimately obtain CII-Bench.

       H    OTHER E RRORS
1297
1298
       Lack of Cultural Background Knowledge (16%):
1299
1300   CII-Bench requires a model’s deep understanding of Chinese traditional culture. Lacking knowledge
1301   of traditional symbols, historical figures, and classical allusions, GPT-4o struggles with interpreting
1302
       deeper implications within images. Despite reasonable Chinese language handling, the model’s
       cultural deficiency affects its reasoning and performance.
1303
1304   Superficial Reasoning (12%):
1305
       Understanding extended meanings within images is crucial. However, GPT-4o often only focus on
1306   surface-level elements, neglecting the deep implications and deeper cultural connotations behind
1307   them. This superficial reasoning hinders the model from fully grasping profound messages that the
1308   artist or designer intends to convey.
1309
       Misunderstanding of Visual Information (11%):
1310
1311   Accurate identification of visual information is vital. We find that GPT-4o sometimes misidentifies
1312   visual elements within images, particularly when dealing with abstract images. The abstract nature
1313   of such images often stems from the inclusion of exaggerated imaginative elements, sometimes even
1314   defying physical laws. Therefore, correctly identifying these abstract elements requires the model
1315
       to have a deep understanding of the essence of objects, a capability that current models clearly do
       not yet possess.
