                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015           Memory behavior modeling is a critical topic in cognitive psychology and ed-
016           ucation. Traditional psychological approaches describe the dynamic properties
017           of memory through memory equations derived from experimental data, but these
018
              models often lack accuracy and are frequently debated in terms of their form. In
              recent years, data-driven modeling methods have improved predictive accuracy
019
              but often suffer from poor interpretability, limiting their ability to provide deeper
020
              cognitive insights. While knowledge-informed neural network models have
021           achieved significant success in fields such as physics, their application in behav-
022           ior modeling remains limited. This paper proposes a Self-evolving Psychology-
023           informed Neural Network (SPsyINN), which leverages classical memory equa-
024           tions as knowledge modules to constrain neural network training. To address
025           challenges such as the difficulty in quantifying descriptors and the limited in-
026           terpretability of classical memory equations, a genetic symbolic regression al-
027           gorithm is introduced to conduct evolutionary searches for more optimal expres-
028           sions based on classical memory equations, enabling the mutual progress of the
029
              knowledge module and the neural network module. Specifically, the proposed ap-
              proach combines genetic symbolic regression and neural networks in a parallel
030
              training framework, with a dynamic joint optimization loss function ensuring ef-
031
              fective knowledge alignment between the two modules. Then, for addressing the
032           training efficiency differences arising from the distinct optimization methods and
033           computational hardware requirements of genetic algorithms and neural networks,
034           an asynchronous interaction mechanism mediated by proxy data is developed to
035           facilitate effective communication between modules and improve optimization ef-
036           ficiency. Finally, a denoising module is integrated into the neural network to en-
037           hance robustness against data noise and improve generalization performance. Ex-
038           perimental results on four large-scale real-world memory behavior demonstrate
039           that SPsyINN outperforms state-of-the-art methods in predictive accuracy. Abla-
040
              tion studies further show that the proposed approach effectively achieves mutual
              progress between different modules, improving model predictive accuracy while
041
              uncovering more interpretable memory equations, highlighting the potential ap-
042
              plication value of SPsyINN in psychological research. Our code is released at:
043           https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SPsyINN-3F18

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Memory is a crucial component of human cognition and a major focus of research in psychology and
050   neuroscience. Memory behavior modeling aims to establish a relationship model between historical
051   memory behavior and memory performance (e.g., the recall probability for specific materials) to
052   elucidate key patterns of human memory behavior, predict performance, and simulate the forgetting
053   process. These models help researchers better understand the mechanisms of memory and develop
      effective memory strategies, offering significant academic and practical value (Clark, 2018).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The earliest memory behavior model dates back to 1885 when Ebbinghaus proposed the forgetting
055   curve (Ebbinghaus et al., 1913), suggesting that the relationship between memory performance and
056   time interval follows an exponential function. Subsequently, models such as the generalized power
057   law (Wickelgren, 1974), the adaptive control of thought-rational model (Anderson et al., 2004), and
058   the multi-scale contextual model (Pashler et al., 2009) were introduced. These classical models
059   describe the relationships between memory performance and key memory behavior features (e.g.,
060   interval time, repetition frequency, and material difficulty) using mathematical formulas. Derived
061   by experts based on experimental data, these theories lack consensus due to the complexity of mem-
062   ory behavior. Current models often face limitations such as insufficient interpretability, inadequate
063
      predictive accuracy, and difficulty quantifying descriptors (Brown & Brown, 2018).
064   Recently, data-driven approaches have emerged for memory behavior modeling. Techniques like
065   machine learning and deep learning have been extensively applied to large-scale memory behavior
066   datasets, resulting in various parametric models (Settles & Meeder, 2016; Ma et al., 2023; Tu et al.,
067   2020; Liu et al., 2023). These models exhibit significant advantages in predictive accuracy compared
068   to classical theories. However, their complexity makes them difficult to interpret, offering limited
069
      theoretical insights. Moreover, data-driven models demand high-quality and large-scale datasets
      (Rudin, 2022; Wang et al., 2024b), posing additional challenges (Li et al., 2022).
070
071   Knowledge-informed neural network models incorporate domain knowledge into neural network
072   construction, enhancing stability and interpretability. These models have achieved remarkable suc-
073   cess in natural science tasks (Wang et al., 2024a). For instance, physics-informed neural networks
074   (Raissi et al., 2019) use known equations and boundary conditions as constraints, reducing data de-
075
      pendency and improving both stability and interpretability. However, their application in memory
      behavior modeling remains limited, primarily due to the insufficient explanatory power of mem-
076
      ory knowledge and difficulties in quantifying descriptors. Existing memory equations are neither
077
      as precise nor as universally accepted as physical equations for describing or predicting real-world
078   phenomena. Furthermore, abstract descriptors used in classical memory equations, such as memory
079   strength (Wickelgren, 1974) and word difficulty (Lindsey et al., 2014), lack precise formulations,
080   making them challenging to convert into computable variables and complicating knowledge repre-
081   sentation.
082
      Based on this analysis, we aim to develop a knowledge-informed neural network model for memory
083
      behavior modeling by constraining neural network training using existing memory theory equa-
084   tions to achieve knowledge injection and alignment. To address the limited explanatory power and
085   quantification challenges of classical memory equations, genetic symbolic regression algorithm is
086   introduced. It is initialized with classical memory equations as the population and evolves through
087   mutations to search for improved descriptors and memory equations. Compared to other symbolic
088   regression methods, genetic symbolic regression allows the use of initial equations to fully lever-
089   age existing theories and can control equation complexity by limiting symbolic tree depth, ensuring
090   model interpretability. Ultimately, we aim to enable mutual learning and co-optimization between
091   memory equation models and neural networks, enhancing both performance and interpretability.
092   Based on this framework, we developed a Self-evolving Psychology-Informed Neural Network (SP-
093   syINN), comprising a genetic symbolic regression (GSR) module and a neural network module, with
094   knowledge alignment achieved through interaction and constraint mechanisms. Specifically, we pro-
095   pose a Dynamic Asynchronous Optimization (DAO) method to address dynamic differences during
096   training, including model capability differences and optimization efficiency differences. Model ca-
097   pability differences arise as the GSR module, initialized with classical theories, significantly out-
098
      performs the randomly initialized neural network in fitting ability at the start, requiring the neural
      network to learn more from the GSR module while minimizing its influence on the memory equa-
099
      tions. As training progresses, this gap dynamically shifts, so we adjust of different training objectives
100
      using a dynamic knowledge alignment method to ensure stable optimization. In addition, optimiza-
101   tion efficiency differences stem from genetic symbolic regression relying on CPU-based genetic
102   algorithms, while neural networks leverage gradient-based GPU optimization, which is significantly
103   faster. To address this, we introduce a proxy dataset to facilitate asynchronous knowledge trans-
104   fer, ensuring flexible interactions, and design multiple asynchronous interaction strategies to enable
105   decoupled module training while achieving efficient knowledge alignment, allowing synchronized
106   co-optimization across both modules.
107   The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:

      2   BACKGROUND
128
129   Traditional Memory Theory Equations: Memory modeling aims to explain and predict human
130
      memory (often referred to as forgetting) behavior using mathematical models. Early psychological
      studies predominantly relied on controlled experimental paradigms, analyzing data from such ex-
131
      periments to establish relationships between memory behavior features and memory performance,
132
      typically defined as the recall probability of specific memory materials.
133
134   The earliest research on human memory can be traced back to 1885 when Ebbinghaus proposed
135   an approximate forgetting curve equation. He suggested that the interval since the initial memory
136
      event is a key factor affecting memory retention, which declines over time at a decreasing rate. This
      relationship was approximated using an exponential function. Subsequently, researchers explored
137
      other reasonable models for memory behavior. In 1974, Wickelgren (Wickelgren, 1974)proposed
138
      the generalized power law model (R = λ(1 + βt)−ψ ), where the recall probability (R) is mod-
139   eled as a power-law function of initial memory strength (λ), time scale factor (β), forgetting rate
140   (ψ), and time interval (t) since the last memory event. In 1995, Wozniak (Woźniak et al., 1995)
                                                                                   t
141   introduced the dual-component model of long-term memory (R = e− S ), modeling recall prob-
142   ability (R) as an exponential function of memory strength (S) and time interval (t). In 2004,
                                                                             PN

152   of the variables in these memory equations are provided in Appendix A.1.

      Despite over a century of exploration, researchers have yet to identify a universally accepted mem-
154   ory equation. While theoretical memory equations are concise and interpretable, they have limited
155   explanatory power for memory behavior and insufficient predictive accuracy for memory perfor-
156   mance. Furthermore, many theoretical models include abstract psychological descriptors that are
                                                                                             t
157   difficult to quantify. For instance, in Wozniak’s dual-component model (R = e− S ), the descrip-
158   tor memory strength (S) reflects the depth of impression left by a memory behavior on the learner.
159   However, current research struggles to fully identify the factors influencing memory strength or to
160   provide precise calculation methods, even though it clearly impacts memory performance. In prac-
161   tice, memory strength is often treated as a constant, which is evidently unrealistic. These issues pose
      significant challenges to building knowledge models based on psychological theories.

      Data-driven Parametric Model: The widespread adoption of word memory software has opened
163   new opportunities for memory research. Researchers have utilized data-driven paradigms and
164   machine learning methods to develop parameterized memory behavior models, treating words as
165   knowledge components in knowledge tracing (KT) (Bai et al., 2024). This approach integrates
166   memory modeling with KT tasks, driving improvements in both model performance and theoretical
167   insights. Advances in deep learning have further accelerated KT research. Piech et al. introduced the
168   Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT) model (Piech et al., 2015), the first to apply Recurrent Neural Net-
169   works (RNNs) (Lipton, 2015) to KT. DKT captures the temporal dynamics of student interactions
170   with questions to predict responses to new ones, significantly outperforming traditional KT models
171
      and highlighting the potential of deep learning in modeling learning behaviors. Subsequent research
      adopted temporal models like Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) (Ma et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024)
172
      and Transformer (Liu et al., 2023), refining model structures (Sun et al., 2024) and incorporating
173
      factors such as difficulty levels (Han et al., 2013), review conditions (Shu et al., 2024), and material
174   relevance (Chen et al., 2023) to enhance performance. While deep learning-based models excel in
175   data fit and prediction accuracy, their “black-box” nature remains a challenge, limiting interpretabil-
176   ity and educational applications. Moreover, building these models requires large-scale, high-quality
177   behavioral data, which is still difficult to obtain.
178
      Physics Informed Neural Networks: In recent years, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
179
      have emerged as one of the most successful knowledge-driven neural network models, achieving
180   significant breakthroughs in fields like dynamics modeling (Hoffer et al., 2022), fluid mechanics
181   (Wang et al., 2024a), and solving differential equations (Moseley et al., 2023). Unlike traditional
182   purely data-driven neural networks, PINNs integrate domain-specific physical knowledge with deep
183   learning, offering a novel approach to modeling. The core idea of PINNs is to embed physical laws
184   (such as conservation laws and boundary conditions) directly into the neural network’s loss func-
185   tion, ensuring that predictions and simulations always adhere to physical constraints. This approach
186   not only enhances the physical interpretability of the model but also improves its generalization
187   ability across various scenarios (Cuomo et al., 2022), demonstrating substantial potential for sci-
188
      entific computation and engineering applications. For example, the Navier-Stokes equations were
      applied to analyze the energy extraction efficiency of hydrokinetic turbines, while also improving
189
      the high-dimensional design of the turbine blades and ducts (Park et al., 2023).
190
191   However, in the domain of memory behavior modeling, the exploration of knowledge-informed
192   neural network models remains scarce. Existing memory theory equations find it challenging to
193   describe or predict real-world phenomena with the precision of physical equations. Their mathe-
194
      matical forms are often contentious, making it difficult to offer precise guidance to neural network
      models. Furthermore, psychological domain knowledge is challenging to express in computational
195
      models. Many abstract descriptors introduced in classical memory theory equations are difficult to
196
      translate into computable variables, posing significant challenges for knowledge representation.

      3.2      SP SY INN
245
246
      We propose a Self-evolving Psychology-Informed Neural Network (SPsyINN), combining neural
247   networks and genetic algorithms to design two independent modules: the Denoising Neural Network
248   (DNN) and Genetic Symbolic Regression (GSR). The corresponding memory models are denoted
249   as fDN N and fGSR . Here, fDN N is a parameterized neural network trained via gradient-based opti-
250   mization, while fGSR is a mathematical function optimized using genetic algorithms, with classical
251   memory theory equations as the initial population.
252
      To align the outputs of fDN N and fGSR , we adopt techniques from knowledge distillation and
253   PINN models, enabling knowledge integration while fitting training data. This chapter introduces
254   the method in three parts: Denoising Neural Network, Genetic Symbolic Regression, and Dynamic
255   Asynchronous Optimization. The first two sections detail the construction of fDN N and fGSR ,
256   while the last explains their knowledge alignment and collaborative optimization. The overall frame-
257   work is illustrated in Figure 2.
258

415   4.1   C OMPARISON E XPERIMENTS
416
417   To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed SPsyINN, we compared its prediction accuracy
418   with 13 baseline methods on four datasets. The results are shown in Table 1. From Table 1, the
419   following observations can be made: 1.Compared to other baseline models, the proposed SPsyINN
420   method significantly outperforms all benchmarks, demonstrating the validity of our approach and
421   providing a novel framework and perspective for memory behavior modeling. 2.Symbolic regres-
422   sion performs suboptimally, with methods such as SBP-GP (Pawlak et al., 2014) and TPSR (Shojaee
423
      et al., 2023) producing highly complex formulas that lose interpretability and often underperform
      compared to classical theoretical equations. We believe this is likely due to significant noise in the
424
      original data, which adversely affects the performance of symbolic regression models. 3.Neural
425
      network models exhibit clear advantages over symbolic regression and memory theory equations
426   because they can flexibly incorporate multiple complex attributes, automate relationship learning,
427   and process these factors in parallel, enabling them to capture intricate interactions more accu-
428   rately and provide more precise predictions. 4.Experimental results with different waiting strategies
429   (SPsyINN-C, SPsyINN-I, SPsyINN-W) show that per-round waiting (SPsyINN-W) achieves the
430   best performance. Strategies with higher synchronization rates yield better performance but at the
431   cost of lower training efficiency, requiring users to balance performance and efficiency when choos-
      ing an appropriate strategy.

517
      We propose a novel psychologically interpretable dynamic asynchronous training model, SPsyINN,
518
      which effectively models memory behavior through knowledge injection and dynamic asynchronous
519   optimization. Extensive experiments demonstrate that constraining neural networks with knowledge
520   in memory scenarios is effective. Our framework enables efficient collaborative optimization of
521   neural networks and symbolic regression, significantly improving the predictive performance of
522   neural networks and the fitting accuracy of equations, thereby alleviating the issue of insufficient
523   explanatory power of theoretical equations in memory scenarios. Methodologically, the dynamic
524   alignment strategy enhances synergy, while in the asynchronous strategy, we observed a positive
525   correlation between synchronization and model performance, though at the cost of training speed.
526   In practical applications, SPsyINN reveals memory equations consistent with classical theories and
527
      identifies the dual influence of time intervals and learners’ historical behaviors, offering valuable
      insights for memory modeling.
528
529   Future research will explore broader applications of SPsyINN, such as analyzing cognitive abilities
530   like attention distribution and problem-solving, as well as applications in fields like cognitive science
531   and finance. We aim to further enhance the model’s generalizability, enabling it to integrate with
532   other symbolic regression methods and offering a novel approach to scientific discovery.

      Continuous Optimization Strategy (SPsyINN-C):This strategy seeks to maximize the optimiza-
921   tion efficiency of each module by eliminating wait times. The modules operate independently, com-
922   pleting updates and reading the proxy dataset without waiting for each other, enabling fully asyn-
923   chronous interaction. This strategy maximizes training efficiency but minimizes the frequency of
924   effective interaction between the modules.
925
      Waiting Optimization Strategy (SPsyINN-W): This strategy aims to maximize the frequency of
926
      effective interaction between the models. The faster training module waits for the slower module to
927   complete its training after finishing one epoch and then synchronously updates the proxy dataset be-
928   fore proceeding with the next round of training. This strategy maximizes the frequency of interaction
929   but results in lower training efficiency with the longest wait times.
930
      Interval Optimization Strategy (SPsyINN-I):In this strategy, the neural network interacts every
931
      two iterations, and the symbolic regression model interacts on every iteration.

1272   From the model performance perspective:
1273
1274         • The proposed SPsyINN method outperforms traditional memory state equations in terms of
1275           performance and significantly outperforms the pure symbolic regression algorithm in terms
1276
               of effectiveness.
1277         • The symbolic regression algorithm based on genetic programming (such as PySR (Cran-
1278           mer, 2023) and different strategies of SPsyINN) can find relatively simple equations that
1279           align with memory theory. This is in contrast to symbolic regression algorithms based
1280
               on reinforcement learning (DSR (Petersen et al., 2020)) or pre-training (TPSR (Shojaee

               interpretability.

1284
               tasks (such as SRBench), but may not be suitable for domain-specific pattern mining tasks.
               This method typically generates equations with higher complexity during the search pro-
1285
               cess, making interpretation more difficult, similar to the TPSR method.

       We performed Total-order indices sensitivity analysis on the equations generated by different strate-
1351   gies to assess the overall impact of input variables (or parameters) on the model output, including
1352   both the direct effects of variables and their interactions with other variables. Since the equations
1353   generated by the model are often in exponential form, the choice of base significantly affects the rate
1354   of change of the exponential function, making the base a key factor in determining sensitivity.

       From the sensitivity analysis results, under the waiting strategy (SPsyINN-W), the sensitivity of the

1357   cant impact on memory prediction. However, there are differences in focus between the datasets:
1358
1359          • Duolingo: More focused on learners’ time interval information, which is reflected in the
1360            higher sensitivity indices for time-related variables in the analysis results.
1361          • MaiMemo: Shows more sensitivity to learners’ historical performance, indicating that the
