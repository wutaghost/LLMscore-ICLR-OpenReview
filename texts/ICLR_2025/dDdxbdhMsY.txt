011                                            Abstract
012
013           Access to spatio-temporal trajectory data is essential for improving infras-
014           tructure, preventing the spread of disease and for building autonomous
015           vehicles. However, it remains underutilized due to limited availability, as
016           it cannot be shared publicly due privacy concerns or other sensitive at-
017
              tributes. Generative time-series models have shown promise in generating
              non-sensitive data, but show poor performance for large-scale and complex
018
              environments. In this paper we propose a spatio-temporal generative model
019
              for trajectories, TDDPM, which outperforms and scales substantially better
020           than state-of-the-art. The focus is primarily on trajectories of peoples’ move-
021           ment in cities. We propose a conditional distribution approach which unlock
022           out-of-distribution generalization, such as to city-areas not trained on, from
023           a spatial aggregate prior. We also show that data can be generated in a
024           privacy-preserving manner using k-anonymity. Further, we propose a new
025           comprehensive benchmark across several standard datasets, and evaluation
026           measures, considering key distribution properties.

029   1   Introduction
030
031   Time-series data of human mobility enables applications such as pandemic forecasting and
032   management (Ilin et al., 2021), smart city development (Wang et al., 2022), urban governance
033
      (Xiong et al., 2024), human rights violation detection (Tai et al., 2022) and monitoring of
      global migration induced by war and climate change (Niva et al., 2023; Alessandrini et al.,
034
      2020). Two major challenges stand in the way for using time-series data to these ends.
035
      The first is a shortage of publicly available data (Ansari et al., 2024). Data can only be
036   collected and shared in limited capacity due to privacy concerns, business concerns and
037   national security, creating a silo effect. Secondly, predictions about unobserved parts in space,
038   about the future or even possible futures - given that different actions are taken - is often a
039   necessary complement to the readily collectable data. One such case is generating high-fidelity
040   realistic spatio-temporal trajectory data, such as individual pedestrians navigating a city or
041   a building. Open problems within the road traffic domain (Lana et al., 2018), and using
042   human mobility data at large, is (1) high quality large-scale trajectories, (2) adaptation
043   to sudden environmental changes and (3) facilitation of what-if analysis for environmental
044
      changes.
045   A solution to both challenges is to use time-series generative models to accurately capture
046   the data distribution. These generative models can then be adapted to generate samples
047   that are private (Yoon et al., 2019b; Wang et al., 2023), resulting in synthetic non-private
048   datasets that can be made publicly available. Further adaptation allows for tasks such as
049   imputation or forecasting (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2023). However, current approaches have
050
      crucial limitations limiting their real-world applicability: they can only generating very
      short sequence length and they struggle to model complex distributions with sparse support,
051
      including even the smaller of spatio-temporal datasets used in this work.
052
053   In this work, we introduce a new method for generating high-fidelity trajectory data for complex
      large-scale environments, Temporal Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (TDDPM),

      2   Problem Definition
109
110
111
      The task of learning an unconditional generative model is defined as learning a mapping f
      from samples drawn from a known distribution Dknown , e.g. standard normal distribution,
112
      to samples from an unknown target distribution Dunknown . The mapping is learned without
113
      direct access to the unknown distribution, and is instead limited to a set of samples Xtrain =
114   {x1 , . . . , xN }, where xi ∼ Dunknown . Once the mapping has been learned, synthetic data can
115   be generating by first sampling the known distribution, then passing the individual samples
116   through the mapping function Xsynthetic = {f (yi )}M  i=0 , where yi ∼ Dknown . The goal of this
117   mapping is for the synthetic samples to be similar to samples from the known distribution.
118

120   3   Related Work
121
122   Previous work on unconditional generation of time-series data has focused on variations of
123   the generative adversarial networks (GAN) architecture (Esteban et al., 2017; Yoon et al.,
124   2019a; Jeon et al., 2022) and, more recently diffusion models (Zhu et al., 2023; Yuan & Qiao,
125   2024). See Appendix A.1 for a short description of the GAN-based architectures. There has
126   also been an interest in using time-series generation for imputation and forecasting (Alcaraz
127   & Strodthoff, 2023; Tashiro et al., 2021; Shen & Kwok, 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Feng et al.,
128   2024). Transformer-based time-series foundation models has been proposed as a general
129
      purpose forecasting tool (Ansari et al., 2024), but has not been evaluated on the unconditional
      generation task.
130
131   DiffTraj (Zhu et al., 2023) adapts the denoising diffusion architecture (Ho et al., 2020) to
132   generate GPS-trajectories. The U-Net architecture used in (Ho et al., 2020) for denoising,
133   is also adapted to work with trajectories. As part of the generation process, the model
134   is conditioned on individual trajectory-specific statistics such as velocity, distance, and
135
      departure time.
136   Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao, 2024) also adapts the denoising diffusion architecture (Ho et al.,
137   2020) to generate time-series data by implementing the denoising step with a multilayer
138   neural network each consisting of a transformer block, a fully connected neural network as
139   well as time-series specific layers with the aim of improved performance and interpretability.
140   The architecture also has support for conditional generation to enable using the models for
141
      imputation and forecasting.
142   As Diffusion-TS move away from U-Net-approach in DiffTraj to a partial Transformer
143   architecture, we extend this further to a full Transformer-based architecture. By doing so,
144   we can simultaneously avoid the additional time-series specific induction bias in Diffusion-TS
145   while gaining increased performance and capabilities for synthetic data generation. While
146   both DiffTraj and Diffusion-TS have support for using conditional information in their models,
147
      our approach differs both in terms of flexibility and in the purpose. The conditional modeling
      in Diffusion-TS is not for increased sample quality, but instead for enabling additional
148
      capabilities for additional tasks, namely forecasting and imputation, which is different than
149
      synthetic data generation, and cannot be directly adapted for use of local information.
150
151   In DiffTraj, conditional information is used to improve sample quality. Our approach also
152   use conditional information to improve performance, the difference is the type of information
153
      used for the conditioning. We use statistics related to the local environment, which we refer
      to as local information, which in general cannot be directly tied to any individual sample. In
154
      DiffTraj the statistics are directly related to individual samples, which in practice create a
155
      one-to-one mapping between sample and conditional. The use of local information allows
156   our method to generalize to entirely new environments and scenarios, while conditioning
157   on sample-level statistics only allows for generating samples within the same spatial region
158   trained on. Conditioning on information that is sample-specific also increase the risk of
159   memorization, as the model might simply learn to store and retrieve individual training
160   data points (trajectories) when conditioning on the individual statistic of any single training
161   data point. In our work we use distinctly different conditional information for training and
      sampling for evaluation, making our method less susceptible to memorization issues.

195   4   Temporal Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (TDDPM)
196
197
      We propose the decomposition of unconditional generation into two separate steps: (1)
198
      calculating an information rich, interpretable and concise aggregation of the available data
199
      and (2) use a conditional generative model to de-aggregate into synthetic samples. More
200   precisely, instead of learning to generate samples from p(x) directly, we learn to generate
201   samples from the joint distribution p(x, l), where l is aggregates of local information. This is
202   achieved via the chain rule: p(x, l) = p(x|l) p(l), where p(x|l) is a conditional deep generative
203   model and p(l) can be modeled using a explicit and interpretable distribution. This allows for
204   more accurate and representative modelling on challenging distributions, as well as enabling
205   out-of-distribution generalization when l include sufficient information.
206   Sampling p(x) is done by first sampling p(l) and then using these samples to sample
207   the conditional distribution p(x|l). In practice, we learn a conditional mapping function
208   g : Dknown × L → Dunknown , which maps samples from a known distribution Dknown and
209   local information l ∈ L to synthetic samples, which should be similar to samples from
210   the unknown distribution Dunknown . For a collection of local information L = {li }M         i=0 , a
                                                                      M
      synthetic dataset is generated by Xsynthetic = g(yi , li ) i=0 , where yi ∼ Dknown . For certain

211
212   problems, if the choice of l is sufficiently informative, we can learn to generate data for a new
213   distribution p∗ (x) without having to retrain p(x|l). This is achieved by estimating a new
214   distribution p∗ (x, l), s.t. p∗ (x, l) ≈ p(x|l) p∗ (l). This is not only a computationally efficient
215   way to estimate p∗ (x), but can also be useful in cases where we only have access to l, e.g. to
      model scenarios or account for data distribution shifts.

248   4.3   Architecture
249
250   To approximate p(x|l) we propose an architecture based on the denoising diffusion architec-
251   ture (Ho et al., 2020), using a transformer encoder (Vaswani et al., 2017) for denoising. At
252   each denoising step, the transformer takes the entire noisy sequence, the current denoising
253
      step and the local information as input and predicts the noise added at the current step. An
      overview of the approach is shown in Figure 3.
254
255   We set l to be a local occupancy map of the training data trajectories for a given region
256   r = (x, y, θ) where θ is rotation in [0, 2π). The occupancy map contain information about
257   relative frequency, i.e. where which areas in the regions are more frequently visited and
258   which areas are never visited. They are calculated using all available training data in the
259
      given region and we set the dimensions to be 64 × 64. The occupancy maps are tokenized
      similar to the vision transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), by splitting the occupancy map
260
      into 8 × 8 sub-maps and then linearly projecting them. See Appendix A.2 for full details.
261
262
      4.4   Training
263
264   We extract a training data set by uniformly sampling regions (including their rotation)
265   and, for each region, saving a tuple consisting of an occupancy map of the region and a
266   scaled sub-trajectory from the region. The sub-trajectories are transformed subtracting the
267   regions origin point and by rotating it accordingly. If the sub-trajectory is longer than the
268   target sequence length, a random sub-sequence is chosen. All regions have the same size,
269   which corresponds to approximately 1% of the total area used for training. See Figure 1 for
      examples of occupancy maps and trajectories.

297   4.5   Sampling
298
299   The target region is split up into several subregions. For each subregion, an occupancy map
300
      is calculated and the number of total users in that subregion is counted. The occupancy map
      is calculated by dividing the region into a set of cells, counting the number of observations
301
      in each cell and lastly normalizing it. The model is then sampled once for each subregion,
302
      passing the occupancy map and drawing a number of trajectories linearly proportional to
303   the total number of observations in the region.
304
305   When sampling the model, we follow the original sampling schema for denoising diffusion
306
      probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020). Note the full occupancy map is available to the model
      throughout the sampling process to help guide it and that no noise is added to it. The
307
      occupancy map can also be manually modified to model hypothetical scenarios, such as a
308
      road being removed or even moved.

311   5     Evaluation
312
313   In this section, we start with a comparative study on the unconditional generation task. We
314   compare with state-of-the-art methods from all three major paradigms: GAN, VAE and
315   Diffusion. These include TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019a), TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021),
316   COSCI-GAN (Seyfi et al., 2022), DiffTraj (Zhu et al., 2023) and DiffusionTS (Yuan & Qiao,
317   2024). We compare across two large-scale GPS-based human trajectory datasets and across
318   several evaluation measures, which together capture all wanted properties.
319   We then show how our proposed method can generalize to new unseen environments. First
320   by training on a limited part of the map and, using only local information, generating for the
321   remaining map. Secondly by synthetic local information, showcasing the capability of our
322   approach to answer what-if questions about hypothetical environments. Current approaches
323   in the literature are incapable of either. Lastly, we perform an ablation study on the use of
      local information.

      Evaluation Measures
325   There are several perspectives on synthetic data quality in the literature (Alaa et al., 2022;
326   Wu et al., 2021; Esteban et al., 2017) covering a range of partially-overlapping aspects. In
327   this work, we propose to harmonize these and consequently span high fidelity, covering the
328   support, moving beyond copying the training data, downstream task informativeness and
329   distributional proportionality, as captured by the following five qualities:
330
331         (I) Fidelity: (Alaa et al., 2022) The individual synthetic samples should have similar
332
                characteristics to, or be indistinguishable from, samples from the original distribution.
333         (II) Diversity: (Alaa et al., 2022) It should be possible for synthetic data to be drawn
334              from any part of the unknown distribution’s support.
335     (III) Proportionality: (Wu et al., 2021) The probability of a sample occurring in the
336           synthetic distribution should be proportional to the probability of a sample occurring
337           in the unknown distribution.
338     (IV) Usefulness: (Esteban et al., 2017) The synthetic data should capture aspects of the
339          unknown distribution that is useful for downstream tasks.
340
            (V) Generalization: (Alaa et al., 2022) Synthetic samples should not be copies of the
341             training data.
342
343   As there is not a single measure that can encapsulate all qualities, we propose the use of the
344   following evaluation measures to paint a fuller picture of the synthetic data quality:
345
346           • TSTR: Train on synthetic, test on real. We follow the evaluation done by
347
                TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019a) and use the synthetic data to train a GRU-based
                RNN on task of one step prediction using the synthetic data. The resulting model is
348
                then evaluated on the training data and the mean absolute value is reported. This
349
                evaluates the usefulness, fidelity and diversity of the synthetic data. Lower is better.
350
351
              • KL Divergence: Evaluates support coverage as well as proportionality.
                KL(real || synthetic) measures how well the synthetic distribution represents the real
352
                distribution, with 0 being identical. KL(synthetic || real) measures how well the
353
                synthetic distribution fits inside the real distribution, i.e. it is sufficient that the
354             synthetic distribution matches a single mode of the real distribution in order to get
355             a low divergence value. In the latter case, the synthetic distribution consequently do
356             not need to have the same support, nor have any probability density outside of this
357             mode. Symmetric KL weight these two together and Jensen–Shannon divergence is
358             a more stable version of symmetric KL with regions without probability in either of
359             the distributions.
360
361
      We also make use of the sample-level measures Density Error, Trip Error, Length Error
      and Pattern Score, introduced in Zhu et al. (2023) (see A.3.3 for a high-level description).
362
363   We use two publicly real-world GPS-trajectory datasets to run the evaluation: Geolife (Zheng
364   et al., 2011) and Porto (Moreira-Matias et al., 2013). Geolife consists of data from 182 users
365   collected over three years, resulting in 17,621 trajectories mostly centered around Beijing,
366   China. Porto was originally released as part of the Taxi Service Trajectory challenge and
367
      contains data on 442 taxis in Porto, Portogal. The data was collected over more than a year.
      For both datasets, we use only time, longitude and latitude. The data is also filtered for
368
      the time to be approximately even between samples, one observation every 10 seconds for
369
      Geolife and one every 15 second for Porto.

      protection of our model.

      A.1   Extended Related Work
651
652   TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019a) consists of a generative adversarial network (GAN) operating
653   inside the latent space of an autoencoder. To further improve performance, they add an
654   additional network with the task of predicting one time step ahead. The encoder, decoder,
655   supervisor, generator and discriminator are all implemented using autoregressive models and
656   in practice they use gated recurrent units (GRUs).
657   The TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021) architecture is a variant of the popular variational autoen-
658   coder architecture.The autoencoder is trained with an additional loss component to have
659   the latent space conform to a known statistical distribution, in this instance a multivariate
660   normal distribution. The autoencoder is trained to both minimize the reconstruction loss,
661   as well as minimizing the divergence between the embedded data and the prior set for the
662   latent space.
663   COSCI-GAN (Seyfi et al., 2022) proposes to use a separate generative adversarial network
664   for each channel of the data. The individual GANs all share single source of noise as input to
665   the generator and, additionally they a central discriminator that is given the stacked output
666   from the all the generators as input.

      A.3.2   Privacy Preserving trajectory generation
778
779   Here we elaborate on the privacy protection of our model and how it extends beyond the use
780   of k-privacy.
781
      We have two groups of users: 1) A set of users who have given consent to having their data
782
      used for training models (we chose bottom-left for ease of exposition) and 2) a set of users
783
      whose privacy we want to protect (remaining 3/4 of the data, again for ease of exposition).
784   More specifically, we would like to protect against a membership inference attack where the
785   trajectory of a single user could be extracted.
786
787
      We use data from the first group to train the model, by extracting local information and
      training a conditional model, which maps from local information to individual trajectories.
788
      The trajectories of these users are not protected, as the model might memorize their
789
      trajectories.
790
791   For the second group we calculate local information, which is discretized spatially and
792   time is marginalized out. The result is a heatmap, where each cell contains the number of
793
      observations in the corresponding geographical area. The private information left in the
      local information is already minimal. In the worst case scenario, if a single person were to
794
      travel in a less commonly traveled part of the map, a set of locations that the person visited
795
      could be extracted. The exact timing and in which order the areas were visited would still
796   be unknown (protected by how the local information is calculated). For more visited areas,
797   this risk would be minimal, as it would be difficult to isolate a single person’s set of visited
798   locations.
799
      By applying k-privacy to the local information, we set cells with fewer than k observations
800
      to 0. This protects users (or groups of users) with fewer than k observations in each cell.
801
      Once applied, the risk of membership inference attacks is eliminated; an adversary cannot
802   extract a sequence of locations a user visited (now even in less frequently visited areas).

817             for all trajectories. The Jensen-Shannon divergence is calculated once for each pair
818             and then the average is reported.
819
              • Length Error: The distance between consecutive observations are calculated, once
820             for the training data and once for the synthetic data. Histograms are calculated of
821             each, with the number of bins set to 16. Finally, the Jensen-Shannon divergence
822             is calculated from the histogram of training data to the histogram of the synthetic
823             data.
824           • Pattern Score: Using the heatmaps from Density Error, the top N areas (highest
825             count) from the training and synthetic data are collected. The F-score is then
826             calculated and reported.

855   A.3.4     The effect of training data selection on generalization
856
      In this experiment, we investigate the impact of the choice of geographical data on general-
857
      ization. We train TDDPM on two different sub-regions, the bottom left quadrant of Geolife
858   100% and the top right quadrant. We then compare to all other models, including TDDPM,
859   trained on data from all of Geolife 100%. The results are presented in Table 7. We see that
860   TDDPM trained on top-right quadrant performs best on the TSTR measure, giving our
861   approach the best score out of all models we have compared. We also see that training on
862   the bottom-left yields an improvement over using training data from all of Geolife 100%,
863   at the cost of slightly higher KL(S||R). This suggests that data from some geographical
      areas might contribute more towards certain scores or aspects of performance. Further, if
