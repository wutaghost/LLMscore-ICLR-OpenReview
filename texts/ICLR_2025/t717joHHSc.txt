009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly applied in various real-world
012            scenarios due to their excellent generalization capabilities and robust generative
013            abilities. However, they exhibit position bias, also known as "lost in the middle", a
014            phenomenon that is especially pronounced in long-context scenarios, which indi-
015            cates the placement of the key information in different positions of a prompt can
016            significantly affect accuracy. This paper first explores the micro-level manifesta-
017            tions of position bias, concluding that attention weights are a micro-level expression
018
               of position bias. It further identifies that, in addition to position embeddings, causal
               attention mask also contributes to position bias by creating position-specific hidden
019
               states. Based on these insights, we propose a method to mitigate position bias
020
               by scaling this positional hidden states. Experiments on the NaturalQuestions
021            Multi-document QA, KV retrieval, LongBench and timeline reorder tasks, using
022            various models including RoPE models, context window-extended models, and
023            Alibi models, demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of our approach.
024            Our method can improve performance by up to 15.2% by modifying just one
025            dimension of hidden states.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Long-context large language models (LLMs) (Gradient, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;
031   Young et al., 2024; Abdin et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI, 2024) have recently garnered significant
032   attention within the community, enabling LLMs to handle longer and more complex tasks such as
033
      long-context question-answering (Caciularu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) and repository-level code
      understanding (Bairi et al., 2023). However, recent researches (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Li
034
      et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023), indicates that these long-context LLMs struggle to
035
      effectively and consistently utilize all the information provided in the context, exhibiting a position
036   bias known as "lost in the middle", which means LLMs tend to ignore information in the middle of
037   the prompt, even though they can utilize the information at the beginning and end of the prompts
038   well. This issue occurs in nearly all LLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Junqing et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024),
039   whether they are decoder-only models or encoder-decoder models, powerful models or small LLMs.
040   For example, for the GPT-3.5-Turbo model in the NaturalQuestion multi-document QA task, the
041   performance difference between ground-truth information placed in the middle of the prompt versus
042   at the ends is 22 points with 2.3k tokens prompt (Liu et al., 2024b). This significantly impacts the
043   practical application of LLMs in real-world scenarios. Studies (Kamradt, 2023; Zhao et al., 2024)
044
      show that this position bias becomes more severe as the context length increases, hindering the
      practical application of long-context LLMs.
045
046   Previous works have analyzed this issue from the perspectives of data distribution (Junqing et al.,
047   2023; Yu, 2023; An et al., 2024) and position embeddings (Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b). For
048   example, FILM (An et al., 2024) addresses position bias by constructing data with key information
049   distributed in various positions for supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Ms-PoE (Zhang et al., 2024)
050
      mitigates position bias by interpolating RoPE (Su et al., 2024) using head-wise scaling factors.
      However, these methods require additional overhead for training or online estimation of scaling
051
      coefficients and are currently applicable to only a few models, limiting their generalizability.
052
053   To fundamentally understand and alleviate position bias in LLMs, we first explored the micro-level
      manifestation of position bias in LLMs and observed patterns in the attention weights consistent with


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      position bias. Next, we investigated the underlying causes of attention weight-induced position bias.
055   By respectively modifying position embedding and causal mask, we found that, in addition to position
056   embedding, the causal mask also significantly affects position bias. Further analysis revealed that
057   the causal mask introduces "positional hidden states", which are positively correlated with absolute
058   positions, thereby conveying positional information to LLMs. These positional hidden states appear
059   regardless of what position encoding method is used, including RoPE (Su et al., 2024), Alibi (Press
060   et al., 2022), and even NoPE (Haviv et al., 2022).
061   Based on the above findings, we propose a position bias mitigation method named "scale positional
062   hidden states". Specifically, we first design a prior-based searching algorithm that quickly identifies
063   which dimensions of hidden states within the model are positional hidden states, using monotonicity,
064   smoothness, and loss on validation sets as indicators. Next, we design an attention modification
065   algorithm that only let the scaled hidden states influence the attention of the last token of the prompt,
066   efficiently implemented using FlashAttention (Dao, 2023).
067   Extensive experiments on various models, including LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), Vicuna (Chiang
068   et al., 2023), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023a), Gemma (Team et al., 2024), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023a),
069   and MPT (Team, 2023), and across different tasks, including Multi-document QA, KV retrieval,
070   LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b) benchmark, and the timeline reorder task (Li et al., 2023), demonstrate
071   that our method effectively mitigates position bias by modifying only one dimension of the hidden
072   states of the model, achieving improvements of up to 15.2%. Our method is compatible with various
073   position embeddings, including RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and Alibi (Press et al., 2022), and shows good
074   generalization.
075   Our main contributions are as follows:
076
077         1. We find that position bias can be reflected in attention patterns.
078         2. We discover that the causal mask also introduces position bias and generates positional
079            hidden states correlated to absolute positions in the hidden layers.
080         3. We propose a method for identifying and scaling the positional hidden states to mitigate
081            position bias.

173   2.3                                                C ASUAL M ASK S TORES P OSITION I NFORMATION IN S PECIFIC H IDDEN STATES
174                                                      C HANNELS
175
      Definition 2.1 (Positional Hidden States). Let hk (p) denote the k-th dimension of the hidden states
176
      across each token’s position p. We define positional hidden states ht as hidden states whose values
177   vary consistently and monotonically with the position sequence. Therefore, their derivative (after
178   curve fitting) should always be positive or negative:
179
180                                                      • h′t (p) > 0, ∀p or h′t (p) < 0, ∀p
181
182   Previous works (Haviv et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2023) have found that the positional
183   information generated by causal mask is implicitly stored in hidden states. However, in fact, we find
184   it can be observed explicitly, from “positional hidden states”.
185   To further analyze how positional information is transmitted in transformers, we define a special
186   type of hidden state that directly reflects absolute positional information with high correlations to
187   position IDs, called positional hidden states, as defined in Definition 2.1. We employ monotonicity
188   rather than correlation as the primary property of positional hidden states, as correlation does not
189   account for the sequential nature of positions. As shown in Figure 3, our experiments reveal that
190   causal LLMs consistently possess such hidden states across most layers (details in Appendix F), even
191   though these models do not have explicit absolute position embeddings, which means the causal
192   mask is a very possible factor that provides absolute positional information. To demonstrate that
193
      these position hidden states are formed under the influence of the causal mask rather than the position
      embeddings, we conduct perturbation experiments on the causal mask and position embedding, as
194
      shown in Appendix C.
195
196   Based on the findings from Section 2.2, we conclude that the causal mask encodes positional
197   information in certain hidden states, which subsequently influences attention weights and introduces
198   position bias.

      4.1   S ETUP
299
300   Evaluation Tasks and Models We apply our method to a wide range of state-of-the-art open-source
301   LLMs, including: 1) RoPE (Chen et al., 2023a) models: LLaMA-2 (7B, 13B) (Touvron et al., 2023),
302   Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023a), Gemma-7B (Team et al., 2024), Qwen1.5-7B (Bai et al., 2023a);
303   2) Context window extended models: Vicuna (7B, 13B) (Chiang et al., 2023); 3) Alibi (Press et al.,
304   2022) models: MPT-30B (Team, 2023). All the models we use are instruction-tuned versions.
305   And we evaluate the performance across three aspects: 1) Position-bias-related tests on NaturalQues-
306   tion multi-document QA (Liu et al., 2024b) and KV retrieval (Liu et al., 2024b) with ground-truth at
307   different positions in the prompt. The NaturalQuestion task includes 20 documents with a prompt
308   length of about 2.3k tokens, while the KV retrieval task includes 140 KV pairs with an average length
309   of about 10k tokens. 2) General long-context benchmark on LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b), including
310   multi-document QA, single-document QA, summarization, few-shot learning, synthetic tasks, and
311   code completion, totaling 16 tasks with an average length of 37k tokens. 3) Position-sensitive tasks on
312
      timeline reordering in LooGLE (Li et al., 2023), with an average length of 10k tokens. For prompts
      that exceed the context windows of LLMs, we follow LongBench’s approach by truncating from the
313
      middle and retaining the head and tail of the prompt to fit within the context windows. We use the
314
      provided metrics and scripts from the following benchmarks for evaluation.
315
316
      Implementation Details In this paper, we implement our approach using PyTorch, HuggingFace
317
      Transformers, and FlashAttention (Dao, 2023) in an A100 GPU. To ensure stable and reproducible
318   results, we use greedy decoding in all experiments. For the search part, we set the top-k size of
319   positional hidden states to 10 and ε to L/4, where L is the number of layers. The validation set
320   is a synthetic KV retrieval dataset consisting of 100 examples, which do not overlap with the test
321   set. The search process takes approximately 10 minutes. For the scaling part, we only modify the
322   intermediate layers of the model to minimize the negative impact on performance. The details of

370

504   5   R ELATED W ORKS
505
506   Long-Context LLMs Recent research has focused on expanding the context window size of LLMs
507   through four main approaches: 1) Staged pre-training (Nijkamp et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024), which
508   gradually increases the context window size during training; 2) Modifying or interpolating position
509   embeddings (Press et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Peng et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024); 3) Using
510
      external memory modules for context storage (Bertsch et al., 2023; Tworkowski et al., 2023); 4)
      Distributed computation across devices (Liu et al., 2023). While these methods address context
511
      expansion, their impact on positional bias in downstream tasks has not been thoroughly explored.
512
513
      Addressing Position Bias Despite explicit positional encoding methods like RoPE (Su et al., 2024)
514   and Alibi (Press et al., 2022), LLMs often exhibit position bias, such as the "lost in the middle"
515   phenomenon (Liu et al., 2024b; Kamradt, 2023). Recent efforts to mitigate this bias fall into several
516   categories: 1) RoPE-based methods: These approaches modify the RoPE computation process to
517   alleviate long-distance information decay, including Attention Bucket (Chen et al., 2023b), which
518   uses an ensemble of multiple RoPE bases to mitigate position bias, and Ms-PoE (Zhang et al.,
519   2024), which dynamically interpolates with a small coefficient for different heads. 2) SFT-based
520   methods (Junqing et al., 2023; Yu, 2023; An et al., 2024): These methods construct data with more
521   diverse key information distributions or employ system2think SFT tasks to mitigate position bias.
522
      They require further training of the model. 3) Attention mask-based methods (He et al., 2024):
      These methods modify attention mechanisms, including Attention Transition (Gao et al., 2023),
523
      which redirects attention to significant parts of the context and Stable Mask (Yin et al., 2024), which
524
      introduces pseudo attention into the causal mask, ensuring stable attention distribution when facing
525   lengthy texts. 4) Prompt-based methods (Jiang et al., 2023b; Peysakhovich & Lerer, 2023): These
526   methods introduce an external module to reorder or compress information in the prompt, thereby
527   mitigating position bias.
528
529
530
      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper proposes a method for scaling positional hidden states to mitigate position bias issue
532
      in LLMs. Specifically, the study first confirms that attention weights manifest position bias within
533
      transformers. Additionally, experiments demonstrate that, besides position embeddings, the causal
534   mask also contributes to position bias, which is transmitted to other modules through the hidden
535   states containing absolute positional information, termed as positional hidden states. Based on this,
536   we introduce a prior-based positional hidden search algorithm and mitigate the model’s position bias
537   by scaling the positional hidden states searched. Testing eight open-source models with different
538   position embeddings on tasks such as NaturalQuestions Multi-document QA, KV Retrieval, and
539   LongBench, the results show that our method effectively reduces position bias and improves model
      performance.

      A.1   DATASETS D ETAILS
759
760   We choose NaturalQuestion Multi-document QA and Key-Value Retrieval datasets used in "lost in the
761   middle" paper (Liu et al., 2024b) to evaluate the degree to which our method alleviates position bias.
762   NaturalQuestion Multi-document QA require the model to answer the question based on one key
763   information document which is inserted in a long context consisting of many irrelevant documents.
764   And Key-Value Retrieval needs the model to retrieve the value corresponding to the given key from a
765
      list consisting of hundreds of Key-Value pairs. These two datasets are both classic in-context tasks
      which aim to evaluate the differences of model performance when key information is located at
766
      different positions in the context. The evaluation metric is accuracy, based on whether the model’s
767
      response contains a string of the correct answer. In addition, we evaluate our method’s improvements
768   across multi task types, using LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b), a benchmark for bilingual, multitask,
769   and comprehensive assessment of long context understanding capabilities of LLMs. It contains
770   six major categories, covering single-document QA, multi-document QA, summarization, few-shot
771   learning, synthetic tasks and code completion. The evaluation metrics are: F1 for single-document
772   QA and multi-document QA, Rouge-L for summarization, accuracy (exact match) for few-shot
773   learning and synthetic tasks, and edit similarity for code completion. During inference, since the
774   original context may sometimes be too long, the input sequences will be truncated in the middle part
775   to avoid exceeding the context window of the model.

      Curve Fitting When we perform curve fitting on h(p), we use least-squares cubic polynomial fit.
779
      And when judging its monotonicity, we skip the first 100 positions because the first a few values are
780
      often outliers. Since h(p) is originally a discrete function, in practice, we employ the second-order
781   difference to approximate the second-order derivative when computing smoothness.
782
783   Ms-PoE on Mistral When applying Ms-PoE (Zhang et al., 2024) to mistral-7b (Jiang et al., 2023a)
784   with its default parameters (minimal scale factor is 1.2 and maximal is 1.8), we found the model fail
785   to generate normal responses, so we set the maximal scale factor to 1.2, under which Ms-PoE (Zhang
786   et al., 2024) is equal to PI (Chen et al., 2023a) with scale factor 1.2.
787
788   Ablation of the Searching Algorithm We conducted ablation experiments to demonstrate the
789   necessity of using the three indicators (monotonicity, smoothness, validation loss) in our searching
790   algorithm. Ours w/o monotonicity means we just select top-10 smoothest dimensions and then use
791   the validation loss to determine. Ours w/o smoothness means we select top-10 dimensions with the
792
      highest number of monotonic layers and then use validation loss. Ours w/o validation loss means we
      first select top-10 dimensions with the highest number of monotonic layers and then just choose the
793
      smoothest one among them.

849
      In the method 1 in section 2.2, we crop the causal mask to let the "key tokens" unable to attend the
      previous tokens. As shown in Figure 7, the white part represents the cropped part, which means
850
      attention weights are 0, and the orange part represents the attention between tokens within key tokens.
851
      In addition, we have retained the attention of key tokens to the first token to maintain the stability of
852   attention distribution. What is more, we only modify the causal mask in layers 1~8, but as the results,
853   the attention to the key information is still significantly improved in layers 15~31, which indicates the
854   positional information generated by causal mask in former layers can be transmitted to latter layers
855   using posisional hidden states as the medium, thus modifying the causal mask solely in the former
856   layers can induce a profound shift in the model’s comprehension of positional information.
857
      In the method 2 and 3 in section 2.2, we modify the position embeddings through altering the position
858   ids. The specific operation is shown in the Figure 8, in which we directly replace the position ids
859   corresponding to the key tokens with the position ids of the starting tokens (or the ending tokens) ,
860   and actually only the attention weights of the last token to previous tokens are modified. We apply
861   this modification in all the layers. Compared to modifying the causal mask, if only modify position
862   embedding in former layers, the attention in the latter layers remains almost unchanged, which
863   indicates the positional information generated by position embedding may be temporary and can
      hardly be transmitted across layers.
