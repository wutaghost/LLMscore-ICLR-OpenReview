011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In the realm of Topological Data Analysis, persistent homology has traditionally
014            served as a primary tool for extracting topological features. However, approaches
015            relying on persistent homology often encounter practical challenges due to their
016            high computational costs. To address this issue, we propose a computationally
017            efficient novel topological layer tailored for general deep learning architectures,
018
               leveraging the Euler Characteristic Curve (ECC). Unlike methods based on persis-
               tent homology, ECC offers computational advantages by circumventing the need
019
               for persistent homology calculation, while still allowing access to crucial infor-
020
               mation about the underlying topological structure. The proposed layer can readily
021            adapt to diverse data modalities by allowing appropriate filtration according to the
022            user‚Äôs preference, enabling its application across various learning problems with-
023            out data preprocessing. We present a novel technique for stable backpropagation
024            that effectively mitigates the vanishing gradient problems commonly encountered
025            in existing methods, allowing for seamless integration of our layer into deep learn-
026            ing models. We go on to present stability analysis, showing that the proposed layer
027            is robust against noise and outliers. We apply our method to topological autoen-
028            coders, showing that the standard loss function can effectively regularize topo-
029
               logical structures of the latent space. Through classification experiments across
               various datasets, we illustrate the benefits of our approach in mitigating informa-
030
               tion loss under conditions of data scarcity or data contamination.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, machine learning communities have witnessed increasing efforts to incorporate
037   Topological Data Analysis (TDA) into deep learning workflows, an emerging paradigm known as
038   topological deep learning (Carlsson & Gabrielsson, 2020; Papamarkou et al., 2024). Topological
039
      deep learning integrates tools from TDA to exploit essential topological features within the data
      that elude conventional methods, or to enhance understanding and control of computational models.
040
      Persistent homology (PH), a primary tool in TDA, captures multi-scale topological features of the
041
      underlying data structure by tracking the birth and death of homology features, thereby producing
042   topological summaries such as persistence diagrams or barcodes (Chazal & Michel, 2021). Given
043   that PH is a multiset by nature, a number of strategies have been proposed to transform these PH-
044   based topological summaries into alternative representations that are more suitable for subsequent
045   machine learning tasks (e.g., Bubenik et al., 2015; Adams et al., 2017; Umeda, 2017) (see, for ex-
046   ample, Hensel et al. (2021) for a review).
047
      Recent efforts have shed light on the possibility of incorporating PH-based topological summaries
048   as input features for neural networks, enhancing their ability to learn from the intrinsic geometric
049   structure of the data. Hofer et al. (2017); CarrieŐÄre et al. (2020) introduced topological layers aimed at
050   learning vector embeddings of persistence diagrams using a particular parametrization, yet lacking
051   differentiability required for enabling gradient backpropagation. Hofer et al. (2019); Gabrielsson
052   et al. (2020); Carriere et al. (2021); Leygonie et al. (2022) explored the differentiability aspects of
053   the PH-based functions or losses, highlighting the potential of incorporating topological insights
      into deep learning frameworks. Kim et al. (2020) were the first to propose a generic differentiable


                                                          1

054
      topological layer allowing backpropagation, offering flexibility in its integration within arbitrary
055   network architectures.
056
057   Despite its popularity, computations involving PH can demand significant computational resources,
058
      rendering them impractical for large-scale deep learning applications. Its time complexity gener-
      ally scales poorly with the size and dimensionality of the data; when the number of simplices is
059
      given by N , the time complexity of the standard PH computation algorithm is O(N 3 ) (Otter et al.,
060
      2017). As a result, there has recently been a growing need for alternative features capable of captur-
061   ing topological information in a computationally efficient manner. The Euler Characteristic Curve
062   (ECC) is one such feature that can be computed without the need for PH calculation. Due to their
063   ability to drastically boost computational efficiency, ECC-based descriptors have recently received
064   increased attention (e.g., Beltramo et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; DŇāotko & Gurnari, 2023; Hacquard
065   & Lebovici, 2023; Malott & Wilsey, 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Richardson & Werman, 2014; Laky
066   & Zavala, 2024). However, these descriptors have been primarily utilized in a static manner within
067   the framework of feature engineering. A more recent contribution in this field is the Differentiable
068   Euler Characteristic Transformation (DECT) (RoŐąell & Rieck, 2024), though detailed analytical ex-
069
      plorations were not conducted. As indicated by its name, DECT utilizes ECT; a collection of ECCs
      computed from various directions (Turner et al., 2014).
070
071   In this paper, we aim to develop a novel computationally efficient ECC-based topological layer that
072   facilitates integration with general deep learning models via stable backpropagation. The preceding
073   work most closely related to ours is the recent developement of Kim et al. (2020) and RoŐąell & Rieck
074   (2024). Kim et al. (2020) adopted persistence landscapes to construct a differentiable topological
075
      layer. Notwithstanding its merits, persistence landscapes inherit the high computational complexity
      of PH, and their gradients can often be highly sparse, lacking substantial information (see Figure
076
      5 in Appendix). The proposal for a topological layer utilizing DECT (RoŐąell & Rieck, 2024) relies
077
      specifically on the height filtration, which works best with graphs and meshes, but not necessarily
078   with other data structures; it may be applicable to point clouds, for example, yet with a compromise
079   in connectivity information. Moreover, RoŐąell & Rieck (2024) achieve differentiability of ECT by
080   employing a sigmoid approximation, which may result in inconsistent gradients or even vanishing
081   gradient problems when evaluated at discretized points; this will be further discussed in Section 4.
082
      This work proposes a novel fast and robust ECC-based topological layer, ECLayr, designed to ad-
083
      dress all the aforementioned drawbacks comprehensively. Our proposed method obviates the need
084   for PH calculations, thereby significantly enhancing computational efficiency while preserving the
085   capability to extract key topological information from underlying data structures. ECLayr is ca-
086   pable of utilizing generic filtrations, exhibiting versatility across various data modalities without
087   necessitating data preprocessing or resorting to a particular filtration. Importantly, we introduce a
088   novel approach for stable backpropagation with respect to the layer input, addressing the inconsis-
089   tent/vanishing gradient issues associated with the sigmoid approximation in DECT. We also provide
090   a stability analysis, showing that the proposed layer is robust against noise and outliers. Our exper-
091   imental analysis show that ECLayr delivers performance comparable to state-of-the-art PH-based
092
      topological layers, while being significantly faster by several orders of magnitude. Using our pro-
      posed backpropagation algorithm, ECLayr exhibits improved performance and greater efficiency
093
      than DECT. We further demonstrate its versatility through the application on topological autoen-
094
      coders.

403   6.2    T OPOLOGICAL AUTOENCODER
404
405   The idea of imposing topological constraints on the latent space was first explored by Hofer et al.
406   (2019); Moor et al. (2020). Whereas existing works rely on a topology-based loss term to regularize
407   the latent space, our formulation allows for the utilization of standard loss functions, such as Mean
408   Squared Error (MSE) or Mean Absolute Error (MAE) to achieve a similar goal. Inspired by the
409   stability results regarding L1 distance in Section 5, we employ the MAE loss between ECC of input
410   and ECC of latent representation as our topological constraint. The respective ECCs are computed
411
      using Vietoris-Rips filtration, with maximum dimension set to 1. For the experiment, we use the
      synthetic Spheres dataset from Moor et al. (2020). The dataset consists of ten 100-spheres with
412
      radius r = 5 enclosed by one larger 100-sphere with radius = 25, all embedded in 101-dimension.
413
      The ten smaller spheres are shifted in random directions according to Gaussian noise.
414
415   Result. We discover that our approach effectively preserves the underlying shape of the encompass-
416   ing sphere (yellow points in Figure 3), in contrast to the vanilla autoencoder, which loses this shape.
417
      Moreover, it constrains the smaller spheres to remain on the boundary of the encompassing sphere,
      whereas in the vanilla autoencoder, numerous smaller circles lie far beyond the boundaries of the
418
      encompassing circle. However, with this simplistic architecture, its capacity to comprehensively
419
      articulate the nested relationship inherent in the data was somewhat restricted. While our method
420   demonstrates capability of regularizing the latent space, we do not claim superiority over alternative
421   approaches. Rather, we present it as a motivating example of how topological characterization in

      7   D ISCUSSION
523
      ECLayr is a novel topological layer that offers computationally efficiency and stable backprop-
524
      agation, allowing for seamless integration into a wide range of deep learning architectures while
525
      enhancing both robustness and convergence behavior. Our proposed layer can be used generically
526   for an extensive variety of data structures as long as the filtration is differentiable with respect to
527   the input data. Nonetheless, there are some important caveats and limitations which should be
528   addressed. First, while ECCs offer computational efficiency, PH-based summaries provide more
529   detailed, multi-scale topological information. Understanding this tradeoff is essential. Therefore,
530   our proposed ECLayr is particularly well-suited for applications where computational efficiency
531   is prioritized over detailed topological insights. Moreover, as discussed in greater detail in Section
532   5, ECCs are topologically weaker invariants compared to PHs. Consequently, the ECC-based layer
533   generally exhibits less robustness than the PH-based layers. Next, as with other topological layers,
534
      further research is necessary to achieve successful systematic hyperparameter exploration. Finally,
      extending our analysis to other filtrations, such as the clique complex of a multigraph, and applying
535
      ECLayr to time-series embeddings (Kim et al., 2018; Umeda, 2017) would be a valuable direction
536
      for future research, which could further demonstrate the versatility of our proposed methods.

      Let X ‚ąą RH√óW be a 2D image. There are two methods of constructing a filtered cubical complex:

801
802   T-construction In T-construction, each pixel in the image is mapped to a top-dimensional cell in
803   the cubical complex, which is a square in case of 2D images. The filtration value of each square
804   is assigned as the intensity of its corresponding pixel, and these filtration values are recursively
805   extended to lower dimensional cubes. The filtration value of each edge is assigned as the minimum
806
      of the filtration values of its neighboring squares. Similarly, the filtration value of each vertex is
      assigned as the minimum of the filtration values its neighboring edges.

1267   H    C HOICE OF TDA HYPERPARAMETERS
1268
1269   In this section, we discuss the choice of several TDA hyperparameters in ECLayr: filtration, Tmin ,
1270   Tmax , v, and ő≤.
1271
       Choice of filtration. Although numerous filtration options exist, certain filtrations are commonly
1272   favored for specific data modalities and training contexts. For example, Vietoris-Rips and Alpha fil-
1273   trations are extensively utilized for point clouds, while a sub/superlevel filtration on a filtered cubical
1274   complex is a natural choice for data with grid structure. DTM filtration provides robustness against
1275   outliers, and thereby preferable in scenarios of data contamination. Despite not being discussed
1276   here, other choices of filtrations are also available. Nevertheless, the fundamental idea remains the
1277   same; choose a filtration that can mostly effectively extract topological features from the given data.
1278   Choice of [Tmin , Tmax ]. A naive and convenient approach is to assign Tmin and Tmax as the
1279   minimum and maximum of possible filtration values, respectively. For instance, one can set Tmin =
1280   0 and Tmax = 1 for image data with min-max normalized pixel values. An alternative method is
1281   to select [Tmin , Tmax ] as a tighter interval within the range of possible filtration values, focusing on
1282   regions of the filtration that contain meaningful topological and geometrical information. Such an
1283   interval can be identified via hyperparameter search, or chosen intuitively by examining the ECC
1284   computed for some data. For example, the ECCs in Figure 6-(b) reveal that [0.06, 0.7] is a suitable
1285   interval for capturing the underlying loop structure depicted in Figure 6-(a).
1286   Choice of v. Spacing between grid points is important as our vectorized ECC does not account
1287   for cycles that are born and dead between ti and ti+1 , where ti , ti+1 ‚ąą tseq. Provided that the
1288   discretization is not overly sparse, the uncaptured cycles are often small (noise) generators with life
1289   span shorter than ti+1 ‚ąí ti . This implies that with appropriate discretization, noise can be partially
1290   filtered by design. Therefore, using a highly dense discretization is not necessarily beneficial, as
1291   it captures even the small (noise) generators (see Figure 6-(b)). Conversely, using an excessively
1292
       sparse discretization may jeopardize the capturing of essential global features. The optimal choice
       of v is not always evident; we recommend hyperparameter search using cross validation to determine
1293
       the adequate v that balances the two circumstances.
1294
1295   Choice of ő≤. The hyperparameter ő≤ regulates the gradient‚Äôs magnitude, with smaller values of ő≤
       yielding larger gradients. However, the optimal choice of ő≤ is somewhat ambiguous. Unfortunately,


                                                             24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       we do not have a clear rationale for choosing ő≤; it is contingent upon numerous factors, including
1297   model architecture and the specific task at hand. Therefore, we recommend conducting hyperpa-
1298   rameter search via cross validation to select an appropriate ő≤.

1315   I.2   T OPOLOGICAL AUTOENCODER
1316
1317   The encoder and decoder network each consists of three fully connected layers, with input dimension
1318   size 101, hidden dimension size 32, and latent dimension size 2. BatchNorm and ReLu nonlinearity
1319   is used after each layer, except for the latent dimension. We place one ECLayr at the beginning of
1320   the encoder and one ECLayr at the latent dimension in order to compute the MAE loss between
1321
       ECC of input point cloud and ECC of latent representation. This MAE loss acts as a topology
       regularizing term, with lambda=0.001 controlling its magnitude. Vietoris-Rips filtration is employed
1322
       to compute ECC and max dimension is restricted to 1. v is set to 1000 over interval [0, 2], where
