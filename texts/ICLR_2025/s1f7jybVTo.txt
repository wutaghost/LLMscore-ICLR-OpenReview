009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces a visual sandbox designed to explore the training dynamics
012            of a small-scale transformer model, with the embedding dimension constrained to
013            d = 2. This restriction allows for a comprehensive two-dimensional visualization
014            of each layer’s dynamics. Through this approach, we gain insights into training
015            dynamics, circuit transferability, and the causes of loss spikes, including those
016            induced by the high curvature of normalization layers. We propose strategies to
017            mitigate these spikes, demonstrating how good visualization facilitates the design
018
               of innovative ideas of practical interest. Additionally, we believe our sandbox
               could assist theoreticians in assessing essential training dynamics mechanisms and
019
               integrating them into future theories.
020

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      The scaling hypothesis in deep learning states that a model’s performance will continue to improve
025
      as the size of the model and its training set increase (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020).
026
      This idea has led to a focus on developing very large models, with significant resources dedicated to
027   their training. While this approach has yielded great empirical successes, it requires a substantial
028   computational budget. This makes it challenging to conduct meaningful ablation studies to analyze
029   the importance of different components or factors in a system. As a result, there is still a limited
030   understanding of the training dynamics of these large models and much of the knowledge in this area
031   is based on unpublished tricks and techniques rather than rigorous scientific investigation.
032
      We believe that improving the understanding of this matter is of great importance not only to enhance
033   performance but also to limit the failure cases that occur when scaling the size of the models
034   (Chowdhery et al., 2024; Dehghani et al., 2023; Molybog et al., 2023). This may notably contribute to
035   the reduction of memory and budget costs, along with carbon emissions associated with training large
036   models (Faiz et al., 2024). In this paper, we deliberately choose to focus on a small model to gain a
037   deeper understanding of its training dynamics. In particular, the crux of our approach is to consider a
038   model small enough to visualize it fully. Specifically, we focus on a transformer architecture with an
039   embedding dimension d = 2, which allows us to plot the dynamics of each layer in a two-dimensional
040   plane. This approach enables us to analyze the model’s internal workings and visualize the underlying
041
      mechanisms that drive its behavior, which may be obscured in larger models. We hope that insights
      gained from small-scale studies could provide a foundation for understanding the complex dynamics
042
      at play in larger models, ultimately shedding light on their behavior and performance.
043
044   Our main contribution is to provide a visual sandbox which can be of great interest for several
045   reasons. Theoreticians can use it to build intuition on key mechanisms underlying training dynamics,
046   potentially leading to new theorems. Practitioners can use it to test new ideas, such as optimizer
047
      modifications. In particular, modifications to the architecture, to the training setting, or to the data
      can be seamlessly integrated into our pipeline. By providing this sandbox, we aim to facilitate the
048
      understanding of the complex dynamics at play in larger models.
049
050
      Summary of our contributions. Our contributions are as follows:
051
052          • We develop a visual sandbox to visualize the training dynamics of transformers. Our code is
053            made available to the reviewers and will be open-sourced. It is designed to create videos
               that help comprehend the model’s internal dynamics thoroughly.

054
             • Using our visual sandbox, we provide insights into the training dynamics of our model. We
055            classify the types of circuits learned for our problem and illustrate a two-phase learning
056            process, consisting of representation learning followed by classifier fitting.
057          • We detail the transferability of circuits to showcase the usefulness of curriculum learning
058            and data curation.
059          • We investigate loss spikes, suggesting potential strategies for mitigation, which could lead
060            to more stable training processes.
061   Overall, we hope that our study will guide theorists towards new theorems that capture important
062   aspects beyond the training dynamics of transformers and help practitioners build intuition on
063   potentially promising changes to current training pipelines.

065
      Related Work. Our work bridges explainability and training dynamics studies.
066
067   Opening the Black Box. As neural networks increasingly permeate civil society, concerns about
068   their opacity intensify. This has fueled the ongoing quest to "open the black box." To tackle this
069   issue, researchers have developed various methods, such as extracting meaningful features from
070   neural network activations (Fel et al., 2023), and assessing the impact of perturbations on model
071
      inputs (Fel et al., 2021; Koh & Liang, 2017), among others. Recently, the field of mechanistic
      interpretability has advocated for exposing the internal mechanisms of transformers to provide novel
072
      insights into their capabilities (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022). While some methodologies
073
      may apply to large language models (Templeton et al., 2024), precise ablation studies often focus on
074   controlled environments and simplified architectures (Cabannes et al., 2024a; Charton, 2022; Geva
075   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023; Rauker et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
076   2023). Our work aligns with this field, as we aim to make the internal behavior of transformers more
077   explicit through carefully selected visualizations in controlled settings. In particular, by utilizing
078   low-dimensional embeddings, we effectively circumvent the curse of dimensionality as defined
079   by Olah (2023). Although low-dimensional embeddings make learning the task more challenging,
080   they enable comprehensive visualization of every model component. However, unlike mechanistic
081   interpretability, our goal is to provide insights into the training dynamics of these models, in the spirit
082
      of Wortsman et al. (2024).
083   Training Dynamics of Neural Networks. With the advent of deep learning, new optimization chal-
084   lenges have emerged. Training loss functions are no longer necessarily convex or smooth, the
085   optimization landscape has expanded enormously, and computations are distributed to manage this
086   scale. As the scale of models increases, the absence of theoretical results and closed-form solutions
087   for optimizing neural networks underscores the need to better understand their behavior in practice
088
      during training, which is increasingly becoming a computational bottleneck. One approach to address
      this issue is to employ mathematical abstractions, as seen with neural tangent kernels (NTK) Chizat
089
      et al. (2020); Jacot et al. (2018) and mean-field analysis (Chen et al., 2024; Mei et al., 2018), among
090
      others (Abbe et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2024; Cabannes et al., 2024b). Unfortunately, obtaining formal,
091   rigorous results often requires simplifications that deviate from practical implementations. This type
092   of analysis can overlook crucial details that significantly alter the training dynamics.

100   2.1   S PARSE M ODULAR A DDITION
101
102   In terms of data, we concentrate on a straightforward mathematical task. We draw inspiration from
103   the sparse parity problem (Abbe et al., 2023; Barak et al., 2023) and the modular addition problem
104
      (Nanda et al., 2023; Power et al., 2022) to formulate the sparse modular addition problem. This
      problem is characterized by the following parameters:
105
106          • Input length L ∈ N,
107          • Vocabulary size p ∈ N,
             • Sparsity index k ∈ [L], and a set of indices I ⊂ [L] with cardinality |I| = k.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Our default configuration is set to L = 12, k = 5, and p ∈ {2, 3}. Without loss of generality, we
109   assume I = [k] := {1, 2, . . . , k}. Inputs are sequences of L tokens xt in Fp = Z/pZ ≃ [p], and the
110   corresponding targets are the sum of the first k terms modulo p. Formally, we aim to learn a mapping:
111
112
                              f∗ :            FLp                 →    PFp                      (objective)
                                     x = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xL ) 7→ y = t∈[k] xt .
113
114   This mapping defines deterministic conditional distributions linking input and output data through
115   the formula p(Y = y|X = x) = 1{f ∗ (x)=y} .
116
      The sparse modular addition task offers the benefits of simplicity while allowing for the observation
117   of a variety of training behaviors.

122
123   Sequence Embeddings. The sparse modular addition problem is inherently discrete. To handle

127

175   2.3   V ISUALIZATION T OOLS
176
      The primary motivation of this paper is to embed all computations in dimension d = 2, enabling us to
177
      visualize everything occurring within our model in a relatively straightforward manner. Our codebase
178
      is designed to generate videos of the training dynamics, tracking several key aspects. We notably
179   track the following.
180
181   Position Embeddings. We visualize P (t) for t ∈ [L] as a point cloud. In this visualization,
182
      "spurious" embeddings, P (t) for t ∈/ [k], are represented by squares, and "non-spurious" ones, P (t)
      for t ∈ [k], by circles. Ideally, the transformer would collapse all spurious (resp. non-spurious)
183
      position embeddings into a single point, learning invariance of y to sentence suffixes (resp. to
184
      non-spurious token position permutation).
185
186   (Normalized) Embeddings. We visualize the normalized version Z(x, t) of E(x) + P (t) for
187   (x, t) ∈ Fp × [L]. We maintain the same circle and square distinction and use the same color for both
188
      E(x) + P (t) and E(x′ ) + P (t), for x′ ∈ Fp \ {x}. On the normalized plot, we also plot the query q
189
      as an arrow in R2 , helping us understand how the attention learned where to focus.
190   Attention Map. A concatenation of attention vectors for different sentences is represented as a
191   vectorized image. This visualization enables us to follow the change in activation patterns, even
192   though it is a pure function of the normed embedding visualization.
193   Value transform. We visualize V Z(x, t) as a point cloud. This allows us to understand how sequence
194   embeddings are built and how the value matrix may overcome faulty attention patterns.
195
196
      Sequence embeddings/transforms. We visualize the sequence embeddings ξ (or their transforms
      ζ) for a set of predefined sentences. These sentences are built by iterating over prefixes (xt )t∈[k]
197
      and suffixes (xt )t∈[k]
                          / . Sentences that share the same prefix have the same color. Sentences whose
198
      prefixes are equivalent up to a token position permutation have similar colors. Squares, circles, and
199
      triangles are used to distinguish between the classes of the sentences. The sequence embeddings
200   visualization is a direct function of the value transformation and the normalized embeddings.
201
202   Transform level lines. We visualize the mapping from sentence embedding ξ to their associated
203
      learned probabilities pζ (y = 1). We also plot the sentence embedding on the same plot to better
      understand the level line changes.
204
205   MLP receptors (and assemblers). We visualize the wi ∈ R2 (and ui ∈ R2 ) defining the MLP
206   transform as a point cloud in R2 . A consistent color scheme is used to link receptors with the
207   corresponding assemblers.
208   Loss and accuracy. We visualize the current train/test loss and accuracy. These are classical
209   quantities to track. It is interesting to put them in relation to the other visualizations to better
210   understand the loss spikes, loss plateaus, and phase transitions.

254   3.1    I DEALIZED C IRCUITS
255
256   In the sparse modular addition problem, the output y is invariant to two sets of transformations of the
257   inputs x = (x1 , . . . , xL ).
258              • “Permutation invariance:” y does not depend on permutation of non-spurious tokens, i.e., if
259                π ∈ Sk is a permutation, then y = f ∗ ((xi )) = f ∗ ((xπ(1) , . . . , xπ(k) , xk+1 , . . . , xL )).
260              • “Suffix invariance:” y does not depend on the suffix (xk+1 , . . . , xL ).
261
      These two sets of invariants can be easily enforced by the embedding layer. Any architecture where
262
      the position embeddings satisfy pt = p1 for t ∈ [k], will be permutation invariant, meaning that its
263
      output will be invariant to permutations of the non-spurious tokens. Similarly, suffix invariance can
264   be enforced by ensuring that the query vector primarily aligns with Z(x, t) for t ∈ [k], allowing
265   the sequence   embedding ξ to be invariant to the sequence suffix. Such a construction would yield
266    k+p−1

          k     clusters of sequence embeddings,1 which the feedforward layer could scatter into as many
267   decision regions to map each sequence embedding ξ to the correct output class y ∈ [p].
268
      1
269    This number corresponds to the number of ways to split k into p buckets, which is also the number of stars and
      bars configurations with k stars and p − 1 bars.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      Figure 1 illustrates this ideal model with our visualization. From left to right, we first plot the two
271   token embeddings E(x) for x ∈ [p] with p = 2. We then plot the twelve position embeddings
272   P (t). The spurious (resp. non-spurious) positions are represented with squares (resp. circles), they
273   all collapse to a single point. The normalized embeddings Z(x, t) are plotted in the third frame,
274   annotated with (x, t), with the query vector q represented as a red arrow. This arrow points in the
275   direction of the embedding Z(x, t) for t ∈ [k], allowing the attention mechanism to focus exclusively
276   on non-spurious tokens. Finally, we plot some sequence embeddings, annotated with (xt ), and
277   the output of the feedforward transform. The feedforward layer is able to map each cluster to the
278   appropriate output class. Its output is a probability vector over the classes, which we map to a color

      according to the RGB color wheel.

281   3.2   C ONCRETE R EALIZATIONS
282
      In practice, the model weights learned through gradient descent deviate from the idealized model.
283
      We observe several variations of the idealized model, which we categorize as follows:
284
285   Faulty Attention, Corrected by Value. In many instances, we find that the attention scores are not
286   fully concentrated on the first five tokens. However, this faulty attention pattern is compensated by
287   the value matrix, which effectively collapses the embeddings of the spurious tokens that are attended
288   to. This adjustment allows the sequence embeddings to remain invariant to the suffix of the sequence.
289   An example of such a configuration is depicted in Figure 2. We also observe examples where the
290   attention focuses solely on non-spurious tokens that are not, for example, zeros, which still allows the
291   sequence embedding to encode for the prefix sums.
292
293   Partially Learned Invariants. Frequently, we observe that the sequence embeddings have not fully
294   learned all the suffix and prefix invariants, resulting in more than six clusters of sequence embeddings.
295
      Specifically, Figure 3 illustrates a scenario where the sequence embeddings are not invariant to the
      value x6 , as evidenced by the positions of the blue squares (0, 6) and (1, 6) on the plot. They also lack
296
      invariance to permutations of the token in the first or fifth positions with another of the non-spurious

      tokens. This results in a sequence embedding that presents more clusters than the idealized model,
298   leading to a greater number of connected decision regions in the feedforward layer.

300   Fuzzy Constructions. Occasionally, we encounter fuzzy constructions where the sequence embed-
301   dings are clustered according to unconventional patterns that nonetheless generalize to unseen data.
302   Such a construction is presented in Figure 4.

      To conclude, we observe numerous variations from the idealized construction. The networks discover
304
      a variety of weight configurations to effectively solve our task. While these configurations do not
305
      exhibit particularly striking geometric structures, they often capture the invariants of our problem.

456   4.3   L OSS S PIKES
457
458   One interesting aspect of our sandbox is that it generates loss spikes that we can study quite precisely.
459   Our visual inspection showcases two aspects beyond loss spikes. The high curvature of multi-layer
460   perceptrons with heavy weights, or with numerous small correlated weights, as well as the high
461   curvature of the RMS normalization layer near the origin.

471     Figure 12: Loss spikes are linked to the high curvature of internal network functions. A small update to an
472     element x can result in a substantial change to its normalized version x/ ∥x∥, significantly altering the
        network’s subsequent behavior.
473
474   Figure 12 illustrates that a small modification to an element can result in a disproportionately large
475   change in its normalized version. In theory, gradient points towards directions that would reduce the
476   training loss. However, considering a large step size in these directions could be counterproductive.
477
      This is especially true for functions with high curvature, such as the normalization layer f (x) =
      x/ ∥x∥ near the origin. At any point xt , the gradient descent update rule suggests that one can update
478
      xt+1 as xt − ηt ut without changing f (xt ), where ut = xt /∥xt ∥ and ηt is the learning rate. This
479
      holds true only if the learning is small enough, ηt < ∥xt ∥. When xt is close to zero, ensuring
480   ηt < ∥xt ∥ becomes challenging, particularly if the step size ηt was predetermined by some scheduler.
481   This behavior is related to the "edge-of-stability" phenomenon highlighted by Cohen et al. (2022),
482   further theoretical insights being provided by Cabannes et al. (2024b). Interestingly, this analysis
483   suggests removing some loss spikes by smoothing out the normalization layer. For example, consider
484   using f (x) = σ(∥x∥)x/ ∥x∥ where σ is a smooth function with σ(0) = 0 and σ([1, ∞)) = {1}.
485   This demonstrates the usefulness of our visual sandbox in gaining insights and building intuition,
      which can then be validated on a larger scale in subsequent works.

524   C ONCLUSION
525
      This paper aims to advance our understanding of neural network training dynamics through a detailed
526   study of a small-scale transformer model, facilitated by a novel visual sandbox. This tool allows
527   us to observe and analyze the model’s internal mechanisms vividly, providing both theoretical and
528   practical insights that could lead to more efficient training strategies.
529
      Our findings highlight a hierarchical learning process where low-level features are developed first,
530
      followed by a more predictable model refinement. We also emphasize the impact of initial con-
531
      figurations on the final model outcomes and the seemingly unpredictable nature of some feature
532   learning phase transitions during training. Additionally, we explored the transferability of learned
533   behaviors, which relates to the importance of data curation and curriculum learning in enhancing
534   model performance. We also addressed loss spikes caused by high curvature in the model’s internal
535   functions and proposed potential solutions to mitigate these issues. Future work will aim to apply
536   these insights to larger and more complex models, assess the scalability of observed phenomena, and
537   enhance our visualization tools for higher-dimensional models.

815   When the model’s training stagnates, the loss plateaus for several epochs before decreasing again.

818   yet reached a local minimum. We illustrate this phenomenon in the full-batch and mini-batch setup,
819   respectively in Figures 15 and 16. The connection between gradient norms and learning phases is
820   salient in Figure 15. We can see that loss drops occur in tandem with high gradient norms for each
821   layer. As studied in Section 4.1, these drops correspond to successive learning phases. This is even
822
      more salient in the last subfigure of Figure 15 where the pics precisely match the drops. This shows
      the connection between the exit of a saddle point and high gradient norms. Similarly, in Figure 16,
823
      we can see that the first drop in the loss (around epoch 250 in the first plot) appears when the first
824
      increase in gradient norm occurs and the second point of inflection (around epoch 700) also match
825   an infection point on the gradient norms. It should be noted that this is less salient compared to the
826   full-batch setup.
827
828
      However, this setup enables us to study another training behavior mentioned in Section 4.3. Indeed,
      we observe in Figure 16 that the loss spikes appear in tandem with high gradient norms, indicating
829

830
      to where it was with large updates. It has been shown in the literature (Foret et al., 2021; Ilbert et al.,
831   2024; Zhang et al., 2024) that studying the gradient and the hessian of the loss could provide valuable

      A.2              I MPACT OF ACTIVATION S PARSITY ON M ODELS P ERFORMANCE
865
866
      Sparsity is a phenomenon of interest in many fields such as signal processing, neuroscience, and
867   machine learning (Barth & Poulet, 2012; Chen et al., 1998; Mairal et al., 2009). Recent studies
868   focused on the sparsity in deep neural network activations. In particular, Li et al. (2023) showed
869   that trained transformers have sparse activations and concluded that it was caused by the training
870   dynamics rather than by a compact representation of the training data as commonly thought in
871   computer vision and NLP. Mirzadeh et al. (2024) observed a similar phenomenon and showed how to
872   leverage sparsity to reduce the inference cost of large language models. Inspired by this line of work,
873   we study the activation sparsity of our model from a performance viewpoint. It should be noted that
874   those works study deep transformers and identified that the sparsity increases with the depth while
875
      we only consider a one-layer transformer.
876   Following the framework from Li et al. (2023), we recall that the activation sparsity corresponds to the
877   percentage of non-zero entries of the feed-forward activation map. Without loss of generality, the feed-
878   forward block is an MLP with weights W1 , W2 and a non-linear activation σ that outputs for any input
879   x a vector z = W1 σ(W2 x). Formally, the activation sparsity is the percentage of non-zero entries in
880   σ(W2 x) and takes values in [0, 1]. In the classical setting with ReLU activation (Fukushima, 1969),
881
      this is equivalent to computing the percentage of non-negative neurons before the activation. However,
      some activations do not have non-negative outputs. This is the case of the SiLU (Elfwing et al.,
882
      2018), used in Llama models (Touvron et al., 2023a), and of the GeLU Hendrycks & Gimpel (2023)
883
      used in Falcon (Almazrouei et al., 2023), PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2024), and in our transformer
884   implementation. Instead of replacing such activations by a ReLU (Li et al., 2023; Mirzadeh et al.,
885   2024), we compute a smoothed sparsity1 as the percentage of entries with an absolute value lower
886   than ε > 0. A sparsity of 1 means that all entries are ε-close to 0 (i.e., sparse activations) and a
887   sparsity of 0 means that all entries are at least ε-away from 0 (i.e., dense activations).
