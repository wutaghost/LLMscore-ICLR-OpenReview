009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) require frequent updates to correct errors and
012            keep pace with continuously evolving knowledge in a timely and effective man-
013            ner. Recent research in model editing has highlighted the challenges in balancing
014            generalization and locality, especially in the context of lifelong model editing.
015            We discover that inserting knowledge directly into the model often causes con-
016
               flicts and potentially disrupts other unrelated pre-trained knowledge. To address
               this problem, we introduce UniAdapt, a universal adapter for knowledge calibra-
017
               tion. Inspired by the Mixture of Experts architecture and Retrieval-Augmented
018
               Generation, UniAdapt is designed with a vector-assisted router that is responsi-
019            ble for routing inputs to appropriate experts. The router maintains a vector store,
020            including multiple shards, to construct routing vectors based on semantic similar-
021            ity search results. UniAdapt is fully model-agnostic and designed for seamless
022            plug-and-play integration. Experimental results show that UniAdapt outperforms
023            existing lifelong model editors and achieves exceptional results in most metrics.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown their outstanding abilities in understanding and gen-
      erating texts, resulting in widespread deployment across various applications with significant social
029
      impacts Vaswani (2017); Radford et al. (2018). Although LLM is trained with up-to-date and highly
030
      accurate data, it still can make mistakes Huang et al. (2023), generating hallucinated responses. Fur-
031   thermore, its world knowledge may quickly become out-dated. Due to computational cost, retraining
032   or fine-tuning the model frequently is impractical. This demands a model editor that corrects the
033   errors and keeps pace with continuously evolving knowledge in a timely and effective manner.
034
      In recent years, model editing has emerged as a highly effective method for updating knowledge
035
      within LLMs. It aims to insert or update the responses for certain target queries, referred to as ed-
036
      its, while ensuring that responses on unrelated queries remain intact. For instance, ROME Meng
037   et al. (2022a) locates and edits knowledge within LLMs. It treats a multi-layer perceptron (MLP)
038   as a key-value store, where the key encodes a subject and the value encodes knowledge about that
039   subject. ROME uses rank-one modification to insert key-value pairs into the MLP module directly.
040   In contrast to ROME, MEND Mitchell et al. (2021) trains a meta-network to edit the target LLM.
041   The meta-network learns to generate parameter updates that adapt the LLM to perform well on a
042   set of tasks, without modifying the original model weights. SERAC Mitchell et al. (2022) takes
043   a different approach. It utilizes an external cache to store edits. Rather than modifying the LLM
044   itself, SERAC retrieves relevant information from the cache to augment the model’s responses when
045
      necessary. This allows SERAC to flexibly incorporate new knowledge without directly altering the
      LLM’s parameters. While these approaches can apply multiple edits sequentially, they often en-
046
      counter challenges such as over-fitting and a tendency to forget previous edits quickly. In a harder
047
      setting, known as lifelong model editing, the editor is expected to insert thousands of edits effec-
048   tively. Multiple approaches have been proposed. GRACE Hartvigsen et al. (2024) constructs a
049   codebook that caches the edits, enabling longer sequences of edits than prior works. In contrast,
050   WISE Wang et al. (2024) employs a dual parametric memory scheme that consists of a main mem-
051   ory for pre-trained knowledge and a side memory for edited knowledge. It further introduces an
052   activation routing mechanism that determines which memory to access when given a query, thus
053   optimizing the knowledge retrieval process. MEMoE Wang & Li (2024b) and LEMoE Wang &
      Li (2024a) introduce an adapter based on the Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture. Their rout-

054
      ing algorithm performs classification based on anchor embeddings, which overlooks the relation
055   in a sentence and thus considers a dataset-specific approach. Despite the extensive effort, existing
056   methods still suffer from either limited success in achieving generalizability (i.e., successfully in-
057   troducing the new knowledge) or locality (i.e., successfully maintaining the model performance on
058   unrelated knowledge).
059
      To address the above-mentioned problem, we introduce UniAdapt, a universal adapter leveraging
060
      the MoE Shazeer et al. (2017); Fedus et al. (2022) architecture and Retrieval-Augmented Genera-
061   tion (RAG) Lewis et al. (2020); Sachan et al. (2021); Asai et al. (2023) for knowledge calibration.
062   UniAdapt edits a model by adding an adapter to the selected MLP layer, never changing the model’s
063   weights. The adapter comprises a vector-assisted router and multiple parallel experts. The core
064   idea is that the router is responsible for routing relevant queries to the corresponding experts. Ad-
065   ditionally, if no suitable expert is found, the output of the selected layer remains unaltered to save
066   resources. To achieve this, the vector-assisted router maintains multiple shards of a vector store,
067   storing the sentence embeddings of newly introduced knowledge. When a query is received, the
068   router constructs a routing vector where each element represents the highest semantic similarity
069
      score regarding each shard. This routing vector determines which experts are activated to handle
      the current query. The output of our adapter is combined with the original output to achieve precise
070
      calibration. Overall, UniAdapt is a fully model-agnostic, plug-and-play, and cost-effective lifelong
071
      model editor.
072
073   Our contributions are summarized as follows.
074
             • We analyze and identify the weakness of the existing lifelong model editors relying on
075
               memory, highlighting opportunities for potential enhancements.
076
077          • We develop UniAdapt, a lifelong model editor that is designed to route queries to the most
078
               relevant experts based on semantic similarity. Our architecture is model-agnostic.
079          • Our experiments show that UniAdapt outperforms existing lifelong model editors by a
080            substantial margin. UniAdapt possesses the ability to memorize and generalize effectively,
081
               making it a superior choice for lifelong learning tasks.

134   2.2     L IFELONG M ODEL E DITING U SING M EMORY
135
136   Multiple recent methods, shown in Table 1, incorporate memories and routing mechanisms to pro-
137   cess inputs efficiently. The router is crucial in detecting and forwarding inputs to designated mem-
138   ories. If an input falls inside the scope of the existing edits, the router forwards it to the designated
139   memory, which contains the new knowledge, thereby increasing reliability and generality. Con-
140   versely, inputs that fall outside of the edits are routed to the original model, maintaining locality.
141
      Due to the importance of the router Zhou et al. (2022); Dikkala et al. (2023), we prioritize optimiz-
      ing routing mechanisms over memory enhancements. In the following, we discuss existing efforts
142
      on improving both routing inputs and routing algorithms and justify the design choices that we make
143
      for developing our method.
144
145   Routing Input. Recent research opts for activation scores, sentence embeddings, or anchor em-
146   beddings to construct the routing vectors. In our method, we rely on sentence embeddings over
147
      activation scores and anchor embeddings for the following reasons. First, the works Geva et al.
      (2020); Dai et al. (2021) discover that activation scores at a specific block capture various patterns
148
      (i.e., shallow, semantic, or shallow + semantic). They also suggest that lower blocks capture shal-
149
      low patterns, while upper blocks capture semantic patterns. However, there is no definitive evidence
150   that the activation scores at any specific layer can effectively capture the complete semantics of the
151   input. Anchor embedding enhances the classification algorithm within the router. However, this ap-
152   proach is dataset-specific. When applied to factual knowledge, anchor embedding overlooks the full
153   sentence context, focusing only on the subject and objects. This may lead to misclassification if the
154   relation between the entities changes. In contrast, sentence embeddings are widely recognized for
155   their ability to compute the semantic similarity of the inputs Reimers (2019); Gao et al. (2021); Cer
156   et al. (2018); Feng et al. (2020). Second, sentence embeddings are model-agnostic, which means
157   that they remain the same across different target models (i.e., the models that we aim to edit). On
158
      the other hand, activation scores and anchor embeddings are model-specific, varying across different
      target models. This potentially compromises the generalizability of methods that rely on them.
159
160   Routing Algorithm. In recent studies, research on the routing algorithms primarily focuses on
161   searching for thresholds for separating relevant and irrelevant input. In the binary classification set-
      tings, SERAC defines a single threshold β = 0.5 for any pair of inputs. In multi-class classification

197

200   The outputs of all experts are aggregated as a weighted sum to produce the final output. This choice

      3.2   V ECTOR -A SSISTED ROUTER
232   The core concept of UniAdapt is that the router has its own vector store to streamline the routing
233   process. Our goal is to direct inputs that share similar knowledge with the edits to the appropriate ex-
234   perts, while inputs unrelated to any edits will bypass expert activation, leaving the output unchanged.
235   To achieve this, we start with training a router to distinguish between related and unrelated inputs
236   using our modified loss function. Once trained, the router’s parameters are frozen. We fine-tune
237   the adapter to incorporate edits using the default loss function of the model. In the following, we
238   introduce the details of the router.
239   Router Construction. Similar to the existing approaches De Cao et al. (2021); Mitchell et al. (2021;
240   2022), our vector-assisted router is trained with a dataset. To decide whether an input x is in P(xe )
241   of some edit xe , we introduce a threshold ϵ. If the similarity score ∆(x, xe ) ≥ ϵ, x is considered
242   an in-scope input of xe . Otherwise, x is irrelevant to xe . Thus, we want the similarity scores of
243   in-scope edits to be larger than out-scope edits by a large margin.

501
502
      Lifelong model editing is an active research area with many attempts Wang et al. (2024); Meng
503
      et al. (2022b); Yu et al. (2024) demonstrating encouraging results. Recently, MoE gained significant
504
      research attention for enhancing the performance of large language models. They have shown their
505   potential in various applications. In the following, we highlight some of the most relevant works.
506
507   Model editing. UniAdapt is related to model editing which aims to update knowledge of pre-
508
      trained LLMs. Instead of retraining the model which is infeasible, the task of model editing is to
      fine-tune the model by either directly modifying the model parameters or dynamically loading new
509
      knowledge from external storage. MEND Mitchell et al. (2021) trains a meta-network that modifies
510
      the parameters of the target model. ROME Meng et al. (2022a) insert key-value pairs into a layer
511   of a feed-forward layer by considering the layer as linear associative memory. While MEND and
512   ROME are effective, they suffer from low locality. To address this, SERAC Mitchell et al. (2022)
513   employs a router mechanism that directs inputs to the appropriate model (i.e., either the new model
514   or the original model). IKE Zheng et al. (2023) teaches the targeted model to revise the output
515   with high-quality demonstrations. Both SERAC and IKE achieve comparable results to MEND and
516   ROME.
517   Lifelong model editing. UniAdapt is closely related to lifelong model editing, where thousands of
518   edits are inserted continually. MEMIT Meng et al. (2022b) extends ROME to insert thousands of
519   key-value pairs. GRACE Hartvigsen et al. (2024) assigns knowledge into multiple clusters, allowing
520   the system to query and apply appropriate patches when needed. MELO Yu et al. (2024) extends
521   GRACE by using dynamic Lora to store patches. WISE Wang et al. (2024) relies on activation
522   scores to route inputs to either the main memory or side memory. Overall, these tools employ a
523   routing mechanism, except for MEMIT. Both MEMoE Wang & Li (2024b) and LEMoE Wang & Li
524   (2024a) rely on anchor embeddings to distribute tokens to the corresponding experts.
525   Spare Mixture of Experts (SMoE). UniAdapt is closely related to SMoE, where a gate network
526   or router is responsible for dispatching tokens to a subset of experts. The work Fedus et al. (2022)
527   introduces an approach named switch transformer to scale neural networks up to a trillion parame-
528   ters. It selectively activates relevant experts for each input. Shazeer et al. (2017) features a trainable
529   gating network to optimize expert selection.
530

      6    C ONCLUSION
533
534
535   In this work, we present UniAdapt, a universal adapter for knowledge calibration. UniAdapt is fully
536   model-agnostic and designed for seamless plug-and-play integration. It has MoE-style architecture
537   and is attached to the MLP layer to calibrate the original output. The router with multiple shards
538   can precisely forward queries to the experts that store knowledge and make no modifications when
539   the queries are irrelevant. The experimental results show that UniAdapt achieves the significantly
      improved performance on various models and datasets.
