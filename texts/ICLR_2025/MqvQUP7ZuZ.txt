                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Recent advancements in deep generative models, particularly diffusion models,
               have shown remarkable capabilities in generating high-fidelity 3D objects. In this
011
               work, we explore the application of diffusion models for 3D object classification
012
               by integrating the LION model with diffusion-based classifiers. Due to the avail-
013            ability of pretrained model weights, our study focuses on two categories from the
014            ShapeNet dataset: chairs and cars. We propose DC3DO, a method that lever-
015            ages the generative strengths of diffusion models for domain generalization in
016            3D classification tasks. Our approach demonstrates improved performance over a
017            multi-view baseline, highlighting the potential of diffusion models in handling 3D
018            data. We also examine the model’s ability to generalize to data from different dis-
019            tributions, evaluating its performance on the IFCNet and ModelNet datasets. This
020            study underscores the potential of using diffusion models for 3D object classifica-
021
               tion and sets the stage for future research involving more categories as resources
               become available.

048   The classification of 3D shapes is increasingly important in fields such as computer vision, robotics,

050   ability inherent in 3D data. To address these challenges, we adopt a diffusion approach (Ho et al.,
051   2020). Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), a recent class of likelihood-based generative
052   models, have shown significant promise in various tasks (Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Poole
053   et al., 2022) by transforming random noise into coherent data samples through an iterative nois-

      Preechakul et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2024) have demonstrated remarkable results in both generative
055   tasks (Luo & Hu, 2021) and classification tasks (Meng et al., 2023; Lou et al., 2024). These mod-
056   els belong to a class of generative approaches that model the data distribution of datasets, similar
057   to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014), Generative Adversarial Networks

      Therefore, a question arises: Can we use diffusion models for 3D classification tasks? More criti-
061   cally, given their remarkable ability to generate original objects beyond the initial dataset distribu-
062   tion (Zeng et al., 2022a; Nam et al., 2022; Tono et al., 2024; Luo & Hu, 2021; Zheng et al., 2023),
063   how do these models perform on OOD data? While these models have excelled on standard bench-
064   marks, they often struggle with novel OOD data—a limitation attributed to biased training datasets
065   that fail to encompass the full spectrum of real-world possibilities (Jahanian et al., 2020). Despite
066   these challenges, deep generative models can synthesize highly realistic and diverse images, objects,
067   and text, and have demonstrated improved performance in zero-shot (Jain et al., 2021; 2022; Sanghi
068   et al., 2023) and few-shot classification tasks (Shen et al., 2024).
069   In this work, we propose DC3DO, a novel approach that leverages diffusion models for the clas-
070   sification of 3D objects. By integrating Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho
071   et al., 2020), known for their immense representational capacity, into the 3D domain, we address the
072   inherent complexity of 3D data. Traditional methods often fall short in effectively handling 3D data,
073   highlighting the need for innovative approaches that can manage its unique characteristics. Three-
074   dimensional data can be represented in various formats, including point clouds (Yu et al., 2021; Luo
075
      & Hu, 2021; Zhou et al., 2021), voxels (Choy et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2017), signed distance func-
      tions (Tono et al., 2024; Nam et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2022b), and multi-view projections (Su et al.,
076
      2015). In our approach, we adopt the commonly used representation of point clouds, following
077
      works (Zhou et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2022a), and integrate them with latent representations (Nam
078   et al., 2022) within the framework of diffusion models. Due to the availability of pretrained model
079   weights from LION, our study focuses on two categories from the ShapeNet dataset (Chang et al.,
080   2015): chairs and cars. This integration allows us to utilize the strengths of these representations
081   while leveraging the generative capabilities of diffusion models.
082
      Our method, DC3DO, focuses on exploiting the generative power of diffusion models for zero-shot
083
      classification (Li et al., 2023b). To evaluate its effectiveness, we compare our approach against a
084   direct extension of its 2D counterpart applied to images (Li et al., 2023a). For a fair comparison,
085   we adapt MVCNN (Su et al., 2015), which traditionally employs a view pooling method, into a
086   U-Net-based architecture compatible with diffusion classifiers. In today’s dynamic data landscape,
087   the ability to classify data into previously unseen categories—such as architectural structures (Tono
088   et al., 2020; 2021; Stanislava et al., 2021)—is of paramount importance. Diffusion models, with
089   their inherent generative strengths, are particularly well-suited for this challenge. By moving be-
090   yond traditional 2D prior models (Liu et al., 2023c;b;d) and incorporating the LION model (Zeng
091   et al., 2022a), renowned for generating high-fidelity 3D shapes, we enhance the effectiveness of the
092
      diffusion classifier in performing discriminative tasks, particularly in 3D object classification.
093   Therefore, our contributions are as follows:
094
095
             • Novel method for 3D shape classification: We present DC3DO, a diffusion model-based
096
               method for classifying 3D shapes, addressing the limitations of traditional classification
097            methods when applied to 3D data.
098
099          • Comparative analysis: We compare our method against multi-view 3D representations
100            using a 2D diffusion classifier Li et al. (2023a). We adapt MVCNN (Su et al., 2015), which
101            utilizes a view pooling method, into a U-Net based architecture compatible with diffusion
102            classifiers. This allows for a fair comparison between multi-view representations and our
103            proposed method.
104
105          • Evaluation of domain generalization: Through empirical analysis, we demonstrate that
106            our method maintains strong performance on OOD datasets, focusing on domain gener-
107            alization within the chairs category. This showcases the adaptability and generalization

      Multimodal large language models (LLMs) strengths are leveraged in many current works (Qi et al.,
112
      2024; Ji et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023). LLMs can handle diverse tasks through
113
      conversational interaction, specifically in the context of 3D objects. Typically, this is achieved by
114   training a 3D shape encoder and aligning it with other modalities (e.g., text, images, and audio).
115   The entire pipeline is then fine-tuned during an instruction-tuning phase, resulting in a model that
116   is better aligned with user requests for specific 3D tasks. This fine-tuning stage is conducted using
117   synthetic datasets or captioning datasets. These approaches highlight the vast potential of integrating
118   3D shapes into foundation models, although they still necessitate the fine-tuning of large models.
119   Other methods, such as 3DAxiesPrompts (Liu et al., 2023a), enhance images and prompts with
120   additional artifacts to be able to exploit the 2D vision abilities of LLM for 3D objects.
121   PEVA-Net (Lin et al., 2024) employs a pre-trained CLIP model in a multiview pipeline to classify 3D
122   objects in zero-shot or few-shot environments. It leverages CLIP’s zero-shot classification abilities
123   for each view of the 3D object, subsequently aggregating these results to make the final prediction.
124   Although this approach effectively exploits the zero-shot capabilities of vision-language models
125   (VLMs), transforming 3D shapes into multiview images is an oversimplification that can lead to
126   suboptimal results.
127   TAMM (Zhang et al., 2024) demonstrates that when aligning 3D object representations with other
128   modalities, the image modality contributes less significantly than the text modality. To address this,
129   their method learns to separate visual features from semantic features within the 3D object repre-
130   sentation, enabling a more effective alignment with the other modalities and enhancing performance
131   in downstream tasks. These findings suggest that the alignment between modalities for integrating
132   3D representations into existing methods can sometimes be inadequate (Xue et al., 2024). Regard-
133   ing 3D representation learning, Zhang et al. (2022) takes a different approach and incorporates 2D
134
      guidance. Their work, dubbed I2P-MAE (Zhang et al., 2022), learns advanced 3D representations,
      achieving state-of-the-art performance on 3D tasks and significantly lowering the need for large-
135
      scale 3D datasets. On the contrary concurrent work, DiffCLIP (Shen et al., 2024) demonstrates that
136
      the integration of CLIP and diffusion models for 3D classification facilitates zero-shot classification,
137   achieving state-of-the-art results. This methodology utilizes a pre-training pipeline that incorporates
138   a Point Transformer for few-shot 3D point cloud classification, wherein the CLIP model extracts
139   style-based features of the class, synergistically combined with image features. While DiffCLIP
140   (Shen et al., 2024) used Point Transformer, we used LION(Zeng et al., 2022a), a latent point-voxel
141   representation that leverages a hierarchical two stages diffusion process with state-of-the-art gen-
142   erative performances(Liu et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2021). Following the line of latent and implicit
143   representations, Yu et al. (2023) used a Classifier Score Distillation (CSD) method, which utilizes
144   an implicit classification model for generation.

148   3   M ETHODOLOGY
149
150
151   We present and compare two distinct approaches for 3D object classification: Multi-View Diffusion
152   Classifier (MVDC, Section 3.1) and DC3DO (Section 3.2). MVDC adapts the Diffusion Classifier
153   (Li et al., 2023a) to handle multiple 3D views by implementing a majority vote mechanism across
154   different perspectives. This serves as an alternative to the widely-used MVCNN, integrating diffu-
155   sion models into the multi-view classification framework. DC3DO combines the generative model
156   LION (Zeng et al., 2022a) with diffusion-based classification to enable domain generalization of
157   complex 3D shapes, focusing on categories such as cars and chairs due to the availability of pre-
158
      trained models. By incorporating LION’s high-fidelity 3D generation capabilities, DC3DO aims to
      enhance classification performance on shapes not seen during training.
159
160   Due to resource constraints and the availability of pretrained models, we focus our comparative
161   analysis between MVDC and DC3DO, providing insights into the effectiveness of diffusion-based
      methods in 3D classification.

220   3.2   D IFFUSION C LASSIFIER FOR 3D O BJECTS (DC3DO)
221
222   DC3DO represents the primary contribution of our research, combining the LION model (Zeng
223   et al., 2022a) with the diffusion classifier (Li et al., 2023a) for domain generalization of complex
224   3D shapes such as cars and chairs. By integrating LION’s capability to generate diverse 3D shapes
225   with the diffusion classifier, DC3DO aims to achieve accurate categorization of 3D objects within
226   the classes it was trained on.
227   DC3DO integrates LION’s hierarchical latent space with the diffusion classifier framework. LION
228   employs a latent representation to capture both global and local features of 3D structures. The global
229   latent space z0 ∈ RDz encodes the overall spatial structure of the 3D shape, while the local point-
230   structured latent space h0 ∈ R(3+Dh )×N captures detailed, fine-grained features for each point in
231   the point cloud.
232   Hierarchical Latent Space. LION’s hierarchical latent space encodes 3D point clouds x ∈ R3×N ,
233   where N represents the number of points, into two distinct latent representations:
234
235          • Global Latent Space: z0 ∈ RDz captures the overall structure and large-scale features of
236            the 3D object.
237          • Local Point-Structured Latent Space: h0 ∈ R(3+Dh )×N represents detailed features for
238            each point, including xyz-coordinates and additional latent dimensions.
239
240   Encoding. The 3D point cloud data x is encoded into the global latent space using LION’s PVCNN
241   encoder. The global latents effectively capture the object’s shape and high-level features necessary
242
      for classification. Additionally, the global latent space is smaller than the local point-structured
      latent space, simplifying the diffusion process, which involves multiple inference steps per sample.
243
      By focusing on the global latent space for the diffusion process, we reduce computational demands
244
      and capture essential structural information for classification.
245
246   Diffusion Process. Once the 3D point cloud data x is encoded into the hierarchical latent space, the
247   latent representations undergo a diffusion process. This involves iteratively adding Gaussian noise
248
      to the latents over T = 1000 diffusion steps:
249                                     zt = αt z0 + σt ϵ,        ϵ ∼ N (0, I)                            (3)
250                                     ht = αt h0 + σt ϵ,        ϵ ∼ N (0, I)                            (4)
251
      where αt and σt are coefficients that control the scaling of the original signal and the noise added at
252
      each timestep t, respectively.
253
254   Denoising and Classification. A neural network conditioned on class labels c performs the denois-
255   ing of the perturbed latent data, reversing the diffusion process to retrieve the latent representations
256   ẑ0 and ĥ0 that approximate the original data distribution. The network aims to minimize the recon-
257   struction error by approximating the posterior distributions qϕ (z0 | x, c) and qϕ (h0 | x, z0 , c). For
258   classification, we compute the class-conditional likelihoods based on the denoising process of the
259
      global latent z0 , evaluating which class label ci best explains the observed data.
260   The classification decision is based on evaluating the class-conditional likelihoods pθ (x0 | ci ) for
261   each class ci :

271   on class labels rather than textual descriptions. By using class labels such as ”chair” and ”car”,

274   enhance classification performance within the evaluated classes. Incorporating textual descriptions
275   into 3D diffusion models remains an area for future exploration.

480   5     L IMITATIONS
481
482   One of the primary limitations of our approach is the computational cost. The 3D diffusion process
483   currently requires approximately 20 minutes per object on a T4 GPU, making it a time-intensive task.
484   Similarly, the multi-view approach, while effective, is also relatively slow due to the independent
485   processing of each view. Additionally, our experiments were limited to two categories—chairs and
      cars—due to the availability of pretrained models.

486
      Regarding the MVDC, a significant limitation is that the views are processed individually and then
487   aggregated through a majority vote, rather than being combined into a global latent vector as in the
488   approach used by MVCNN (Su et al., 2015). This method of independent view processing may
489   not fully capture the holistic structure of 3D shapes, which could be better represented through a
490   more integrated multi-view approach. redMoreover, the majority vote mechanism is simplistic and
491   may not effectively leverage the combined information from multiple views, potentially limiting
492   classification accuracy.
493   Due to time and computational constraints, we limited our experiments to 200 shapes per category.
494   Future work could expand to more categories and objects, and enhance the diffusion and aggregation
495   methods to improve scalability and performance. Access to more powerful GPUs and resources
496   would enable more comprehensive experiments.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
499
500
      The classification accuracy on ID data indicates that the model effectively captures the distinguish-
501
      ing features of various 3D objects within the evaluated categories. However, the performance on
502   OOD data reveals areas for improvement, highlighting the challenges of generalizing to unseen
503   distributions.
504
505
      The hierarchical latent space of LION played a crucial role in accurately representing both global
      and local features of 3D shapes, contributing to the model’s overall performance compared the
506
      multi-view (see Section 5 for more details). The diffusion process further enhanced the model’s
507
      ability to denoise and classify complex 3D structures, providing a reliable mechanism for domain
508   generalization.
509
510   Our results highlight the potential of integrating generative models like LION with diffusion clas-
511
      sifiers for advanced 3D shape analysis and classification tasks, particularly in scenarios involving
      diverse and unseen data. However, the limitations identified, such as computational costs and chal-
512
      lenges in capturing holistic structures, suggest that further optimization is necessary to fully realize
513
      this potential.
514
515   In fact, in this work, we delved into 3D diffusion models and present our method that enables domain
516   generalization of 3D shapes in a robust manner.
517   For future work, we aim to explore the integration of 3D diffusion capabilities with state-of-the-art
518   multimodal methods such as ULIP-2 (Xue et al., 2024), combined with PointBERT (Yu et al., 2021)
519   architectures similar to the concurrent work (Shen et al., 2024). We believe this will enhance the per-
520   formance of these architectures and make them more capable of comprehensive 3D understanding.
521   Additionally, future research will focus on improving the efficiency of the diffusion process, expand-
522   ing the range of evaluated categories, and implementing more sophisticated aggregation mechanisms
523
      to better capture the holistic structure of 3D objects.
524
525   7   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we proposed DC3DO in which we seamlessly integrate LION Zeng et al. (2022a)
528   with a diffusion classifier Li et al. (2023a) to achieve accurate classification of 3D cars and chairs.
529   The model’s success is driven by the hierarchical latent space and diffusion process, when com-
530   bined enable precise representation and classification of complex 3D shapes from the ShapeNet
531   dataset Chang et al. (2015). Our method demonstrates a 12.5% improvement on average compared
532
      to multi-view methods, highlighting the potential of generative models in 3D object classification.
      In future research, we would like to adapt generative models to discriminative tasks for enhanced
533
      classification and regression performance as well as incorporate group structure into the diffusion
534
      model for improving data efficiency.
