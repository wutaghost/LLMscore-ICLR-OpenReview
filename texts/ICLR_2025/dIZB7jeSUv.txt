009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advancements have integrated camera pose as a user-friendly and physics-
012
               informed condition in video diffusion models, enabling precise camera control.
013
               In this paper, we identify one of the key challenges as effectively modeling noisy
014            cross-frame interactions to enhance geometry consistency and camera controlla-
015            bility. We innovatively associate the quality of a condition with its ability to reduce
016            uncertainty and interpret noisy cross-frame features as a form of noisy condition.
017            Recognizing that noisy conditions provide deterministic information while also in-
018            troducing randomness and potential misguidance due to added noise, we propose
019            applying epipolar attention to only aggregate features along corresponding epipo-
020            lar lines, thereby accessing an optimal amount of noisy conditions. Additionally,
021            we address scenarios where epipolar lines disappear, commonly caused by rapid
022
               camera movements, dynamic objects, or occlusions, ensuring robust performance
               in diverse environments. Furthermore, we develop a more robust and reproducible
023
               evaluation pipeline to address the inaccuracies and instabilities of existing camera
024
               control metrics. Our method achieves a 25.64% improvement in camera control-
025            lability on the RealEstate10K dataset without compromising dynamics or gen-
026            eration quality and demonstrates strong generalization to out-of-domain images.
027            Training and inference require only 24GB and 12GB of memory, respectively, for
028            16-frame sequences at 256×256 resolution. We will release all checkpoints, along
029            with training and evaluation code. Dynamic videos are available for viewing on
030            our supplementary anonymous web page.

067   1   I NTRODUCTION
068
069   The remarkable 3D consistency demonstrated in videos generated by Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) has
070   highlighted the powerful capabilities of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022),
071   showcasing their potential as a world simulator. Many researchers have attempted to enable the
072   model to understand real-world knowledge (Chen et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023).
073   Condition or guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) is widely recognized as
074   a crucial factor in enhancing generation quality. This is attributed to the fundamental principles
075   that diffusion models denoise along the gradient of the log probability density function (score func-
076   tion) (Song et al., 2020), moving towards a high density region. However, this characteristic has
077   varying effects at different noise levels (Tang et al., 2023a). As shown in Fig. 1(a), the high density
078   region under high noise level becomes the overlap of numerous noisy samples, resulting in visual
079   blurriness. By providing the model with conditions such as cdog and ccat , it can rapidly eliminate
080   incorrect generations. This illustrates that adding more conditions can guide the model towards
081
      desired outcomes while reducing uncertainty.
082   Consequently, incorporating physics-related or more detailed conditions into the diffusion model is
083   an effective way of improving its world understanding. Considering that video generation requires
084   providing condition for each frame, it is essential to identify a condition that is physics-related but
085   also user-friendly. Recently, some camera-conditioned text-to-video diffusion models such as Mo-
086   tionCtrl (He et al., 2024a) and CameraCtrl (Wang et al., 2024d) have proposed using camera poses
087
      of each frame as a new type of condition. However, these methods simply inject camera condi-
      tions through a side input (like T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2024)) and neglect the inherent physical
088
      knowledge of camera pose, resulting in imprecise camera control, inconsistencies, and also poor
089
      interpretability.
090
091   In this paper, we identify one of the key challenges of camera-controlled image-to-video diffusion
092   models as how to effectively model noisy cross-frame interactions to enhance geometry consistency
093
      and camera controllability. As illustrated in Fig. 2, separated spatial and temporal attention serves
      as an indirect form of 3D attention. The cross-frame interaction in temporal attention is confined to
094
      features at the same location in the image, rendering it ineffective for tracking significant movements
095
      resulting from large camera shifts. 3D full attention is widely applied in advanced video diffusion
096   models such as OpenSora (Zheng et al., 2024) and CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024b), due to its
097   extensive receptive field. From the novel perspective of the noisy conditions mentioned in Fig. 1,
098   the broad receptive field of 3D full attention allows it to access more noisy conditions. However, we
099   argue that accessing more noisy conditions does not necessarily reduce uncertainty and thus not
100   necessarily lead to better performance due to the randomness inherent in the noise. As previously
101   highlighted in Fig. 1, the quality of a condition is determined by its ability to reduce the model’s
102   uncertainty, rather than its quantity.
103   To address these issues, we have found that applying epipolar constraints is one of the most
104   suitable way to prevent the model from being misled by noise. By restricting attention to fea-
105   tures along the epipolar lines, the model can interact with more relevant and less noisy information,
106   improving cross-frame interactions in diffusion models. Specifically, we propose to apply Plücker
107   coordinates (Plücker, 1828) as absolute 3D ray embedding for implicit learning of 3D space and
      propose a epipolar attention mechanism that introduces an explicit constraint. By doing so, our

129

149   researchers have sought to adapt existing text-to-image (T2I) models for text-to-video (T2V) gen-
150   eration. Some efforts involve integrating temporal blocks into original T2I models, training these
151   additions to facilitate the conversion to T2V models. Examples include AnimateDiff (Guo et al.,
152   2023), Align your Latents (Blattmann et al., 2023b), PYoCo (Ge et al., 2023), and Emu video (Gird-

      har et al., 2023). Additionally, methods such as LVDM (He et al., 2022), VideoCrafter (Chen et al.,

154
      tory Wang et al. (2024a) have adopted a similar structure, using T2I models as initialization weights
155
      and fine-tuning both spatial and temporal blocks to achieve better visual effects. Building on this
156   foundation, Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) and CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024b) have significantly en-
157   hanced video generation capabilities by introducing Transformer-based diffusion backbones (Pee-
158   bles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024) and leveraging 3D-VAE technology, thereby
159   opening up the possibility of world simulators. Furthermore, works such as Dynamicrafter (Xing
160   et al., 2023), SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023a), Seine (Chen et al., 2023b), I2vgen-XL (Zhang et al.,
161   2023b), and PIA (Zhang et al., 2024) have extensively explored image-to-video generation, achiev-
      ing substantial progress.

      ious evaluation protocols, resulting in inconsistent metrics due to the lack of a common benchmark.
355   The structure-from-motion (SfM) method such as COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016), strug-
356   gles to produce stable and accurate predictions when applied to generated videos. This challenge
357   arises because SfM relies on SIFT operators for keypoint identification, which can lead to erroneous
358   matches when assessing generated content. Such inaccuracies may result in unsolvable equations or
359   significantly flawed estimates of camera extrinsics. Contributing factors include the low resolution
360   of these videos (256x256), the presence of dynamic scenes, the absence of true 3D consistency, and
361   issues related to lighting variations and object distortion.
362   To address these limitations, we adapt the global structure-from-motion method GLOMAP (Pan
363   et al., 2024) to validate camera pose consistency. Our evaluation pipeline comprises three steps:
364   feature extraction, exhaustive matching, and global mapping. To enhance robustness, we share GT
365   priors for camera intrinsics (fx , fy , cx , cy ) and allow the structure-from-motion process to focus
366   primarily on optimizing camera extrinsics. Detailed CLI parameters can be found in Appendix B.
367   Before calculating metrics, we canonicalize the estimated camera-to-world matrices by converting
368   each frame relative to the first frame and normalizing the scene scale using the L2 distance from
369   the first camera to the furthest cameras. To account for randomness introduced by GLOMAP, we
370   conduct five individual trials for each of the 1,000 sampled videos, averaging only those trials that
371   are successful per sample. The final metrics, including RotError, TransError, and CamMC, are
372   averaged on a sample-wise basis.
373   RotError (He et al., 2024a). We evaluate per-frame camera-to-world rotation accuracy by the

410
411   Dataset. We train our model on RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) dataset, which contains approx-
412   imately 70K video clips at the resolution of around 720P with camera poses annotated by SLAM-
413   based methods. We resize video clips from dataset to 256 while keeping the original aspect ratio
414   and perform center cropping to fit in our training scheme. We sample 16 frames from single video
415
      clip when training with a random frame stride ranging from 1 to 10. We set fixed frame stride of 8
      for inference. We take random condition frame for generation as data augmentation.
416
417   Implementation Details. We choose DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023) as our base image-to-video
418   (I2V) model and implement proposed method on the top of it. For fair comparision, we also make
419   reproduction work of MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024d) and CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024a), since their
420   public accessible versions are either T2V or SVD-based. We project Plücker embedding into base
421
      model by a pose encoder similar to the architecture in CameraCtrl. We freeze all parameters from
      base model and train proposed method at the resolution of 256×256. We set 2 register tokens for
422
      the epipolar module to attend when no relevant pixels are on the epipolar line. We apply the Adam
423
      optimizer with a constant learning rate of 1 × 10−4 . We follow DynamiCrafter to choose Lightning
424   as our training framework with mixed-precision fp16 and DeepSpeed ZeRO-1. We train proposed
425   method and variants on 8 NVIDIA 3090 GPUs with effective batch size of 64 for 50K steps.

448   5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
449
450   Adding more conditions to generative models typically reduces uncertainty and improves genera-
451   tion quality (e.g. providing detailed text conditions through recaption). In this paper, we argue that
452   it is also crucial to consider noisy conditions like latent features zt , which contain valuable infor-
453   mation along with random noise. For instance, in SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) for image-to-image
454   translation, random noise is added to the input z0 to produce a noisy zt . The clean component z0
455   preserves overall similarity, while the introduced noise leads to uncertainty, enabling diverse and
456
      varied generations.
457   In this paper, we argue that providing the model with more noisy conditions, especially at high
458   noise levels, does not necessarily reduce more uncertainty, as the noise also introduces ran-
459   domness and misleadingness. This is the key insight we aim to convey.
460   To validate this point, we designed experiment with the following setups:
461
462         1. Plücker Embedding (Baseline): This setup, akin to CameraCtrl, has minimal noisy condi-
463            tions on cross frames due to the inefficiency of the indirect cross-frame interaction (spatial
464            and temporal attention).
465
            2. Plücker Embedding + Epipolar Attention only on reference frame: Similar to CamCo,
466
               this setup treats the reference frame as the source view, enabling the target frame to refer
467            to it. It accesses a small amount of noisy conditions on the reference frame. However,
468            some pixels of the current frame may have no epipolar line interacted with reference frame,
469            causing it to degenerate to a CameraCtrl-like model without epipolar attention.
470
            3. Plücker Embedding + Epipolar Attention (Our CamI2V): This setup can impose epipo-
471
               lar constraints with all frames, including adjacent frames that have interactions in most
472            cases to ensure an sufficient amount of noisy conditions.
473
474         4. Plücker Embedding + 3D Full Attention: This configuration allows the model to directly
475
               interact with features of all other frames, accessing the most noisy conditions.
476
477
      The amount of accessible noisy conditions of the above four setups increase progressively. One
      might expect that 3D full attention, which accesses the most noisy conditions, would achieve the
478
      best performance. However, as shown in Tab. 2, 3D full attention performs only slightly better than
479
      CameraCtrl and is inferior to CamCo-like setup who only applies epipolar attention on reference
480   frame. Notably, our method achieves best result by interacting with more noisy conditions along
481   the epipolar lines. It can be clearly seen in the comparison part in supplementary that CamCo-like
482   setup reference much on the first frame and cannot generate new objects. The 3D full attention
483   generates objects within large movement due to its access to all frames pixels while it is affected by
484   incorrect position of pixels. These findings confirm our insight that an optimal amount of noisy
485   conditions leads to better uncertainty reduction, rather than merely increasing the quantity of
      noisy conditions.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we address the integration of camera poses into diffusion models to enhance their
532   understanding of the physical world in text-guided image-to-video generation. We propose a novel
533   framework utilizing Plücker coordinates as 3D ray embeddings and introduce an epipolar attention
534
      mechanism that aggregates features along epipolar lines, ensuring robust tracking even under high
      noise conditions. Additionally, we incorporate register tokens to manage scenarios where frames
535
      lack intersections due to rapid camera movements or occlusions. Our methods significantly improve
536
      controllability and stability, achieving state-of-the-art performance on RealEstate10K and out-of-
537   domain datasets. However, challenges remain in high-resolution generation, handling complex cam-
538   era trajectories, and maintaining generation quality in long videos. Future work will focus on these
539   aspects, alongside releasing checkpoints and training/evaluation codes to support further research.
