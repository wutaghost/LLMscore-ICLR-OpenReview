011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The online k-server problem on graphs is a fundamental computational problem
014            that can model a wide range of practical problems, such as dispatching ambu-
015
               lances to serve accidents or dispatching taxis to serve ride requests. While most
               prior work on the k-server problem focused on online algorithms, reinforcement
016
               learning promises policies that require low computational effort during execution,
017
               which is critical in time-sensitive applications, such as ambulance dispatch. How-
018            ever, there exists no scalable reinforcement-learning approach for the k-server
019            problem. To address this gap, we introduce a scalable computational approach
020            for learning generalist policies for the k-server problem. Besides scalability, the
021            advantage of generalist policies is transferability: a generalist policy can be ap-
022            plied to an entire class of graphs without the need for retraining, which is crucial
023            for practical applications, e.g., in ambulance dispatch problems where road con-
024            ditions or demand distributions may change over time. We achieve scalability and
025            transferability by introducing a novel architecture that decomposes the action-
026
               value into a global and a local term, estimated from a shared graph-convolution
               backbone. We evaluate our approach on a variety of graph classes, comparing to
027
               well-established baselines, demonstrating the performance and transferability of
028
               our generalist policies.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The k-server problem, originally proposed by Manasse et al. (1988; 1990), is one of the most funda-
034   mental problems from the perspective of mixed-integer programming, online algorithms, and com-
035   petitive analysis (Bertsimas et al., 2019b). Koutsoupias (2009) claims that it is “perhaps the most
036   influential online problem” in computer science. The problem setting is surprisingly simple—given
037   a metric space or a weighted graph and k mobile servers, the problem deals with moving the servers
038   to the locations of requests that appear at arbitrary points in the metric space (or the graph) over
039
      time. At each time step, a request appears, and the decision-maker must immediately dispatch a
      server to serve the request. Usually, it is assumed that a new request arrives only after the previous
040
      request is served (Bertsimas et al., 2019b). The decision-maker seeks to minimize the distance trav-
041
      eled by the servers over time. There are two canonical versions of the problem—the online version,
042   where the decision-maker must dispatch a server based on the current request and the past requests,
043   and the offline version, where the decision-maker knows the set of requests (to be responded to)
044   a priori. Despite the apparent simplicity of the problem setting, the k-server problem serves as a
045   rich framework for in-depth analysis of many online problems (e.g., paging and caching (Bertsimas
046   et al., 2019b), network analysis (Alon et al., 1995), dynamic resource allocation (Mukhopadhyay
047   et al., 2016), and manufacturing (Privault & Finke, 2000)).
048   If the sequence of requests is known in advance, i.e., in the offline setting, the problem can be re-
049   duced to the standard network flow optimization problem and solved efficiently (Chrobak et al.,
050   1991; Bertsimas et al., 2019b). The offline solution can then be used to gauge the efficacy of on-
051   line approaches; indeed, a large part of prior work on solving the k-server problem focuses on
052   establishing competitive ratios for the solution algorithms, which is defined as the worst-case ra-
053   tio between the performance of an online algorithm and the optimal offline algorithm. One of the
      most well-known online approaches is the Work-Function Algorithm (WFA), which combines two

      perspectives—a strategic view by considering past requests and a tactical greedy view by consider-
055   ing the current request. Many approaches have relied and built upon the central idea of WFA, e.g.,
056   the holistic adaptive robust optimization algorithm, also known as HARO (Bertsimas et al., 2019b)
057   combines adaptive robust optimization techniques with WFA. It is worth pointing out that several
058   simple algorithms that are not competitive (e.g., greedy algorithm that always dispatches the nearest
059   server to a request) often work reasonably well in practice (Bertsimas et al., 2019b).
060
      We focus on the stochastic k-server problem (Dehghani et al., 2017) where the arrival of requests
061   at the nodes of a graph is governed by a known probability distribution.1 Such a setting is largely
062   motivated by practical problems of dynamic resource allocation under uncertainty, e.g., dispatching
063   emergency responders to service distress calls and optimizing micro-transit fleets (Dehghani et al.,
064   2017; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2016). The availability of the arrival distribution is fairly common
065   in real-world applications. Consider the problem of micro-transit, where the decision-maker must
066   optimize the allocation of taxis to passengers; in such scenarios, the arrival distribution can be
067   empirically approximated by using historical data of passenger requests. Naturally, in real-world
068   use cases, decisions must be made in an online manner, i.e., the exact sequence of requests is not
069
      known in advance. As a result, we focus on the online version of the problem.
070   We view the k-server problem as a sequential decision-making problem under uncertainty, where
071   the decision-maker must optimize dispatch decisions over time to minimize the expected distance
072   traveled by the servers; the expectation is taken with respect to a known probability distribution
073   that governs the arrival of requests. We model the k-server problem as a Markov decision process
074   (MDP). Our goal is to leverage the known arrival distribution and the graph topology to compute
075
      an optimal policy (i.e., a mapping from an arbitrary state of the problem to a dispatch action) in an
      offline manner. Once the policy is learned, it can be used to make decisions online as requests arrive
076
      in a stochastic manner.
077
078   Crucially, instead of learning a policy on a given graph, we seek to learn a generalist policy for any
079   graph topology (drawn from the same distribution as the training topologies) and any request arrival
080   distribution (drawn from the same distribution as the training distributions). Learning a generalist
081
      policy is critical for practical applications; e.g., consider the problem of dispatching emergency
      responders on a road network. In such a situation, traffic conditions can alter the edges of the
082
      graphs; indeed, some roads might close down, altering the structure of the graph. While re-training
083
      a policy from scratch is computationally expensive, a generalist policy can be invoked in constant
084   time during decision-making without additional training.
085
086   We point out that the k-server problem has been modeled as an MDP before; e.g., Lins et al. (2019a)
087
      transform the k-server model to a visual task problem and model it as an MDP, and Even-Dar
      et al. (2009) discuss the k-server problem in the context of online MDPs (although they relax the
088
      Markovian assumption). However, prior work has significant limitations, both in terms of scalability
089
      and generalizability. For example, the training approach used by Lins et al. (2019a) does not scale to
090   graphs of non-trivial size, and the trained policies cannot be transferred to other problem instances,
091   thereby requiring re-training if the graph topology changes. To the best of our knowledge, we
092   make two seminal contributions: 1) we present the first effort to directly model (i.e., without any
093   transformations) the k-server problem as an MDP by simply encoding the available information at
094   any stage of the problem as the state of the MDP; and 2) we present the first generalist learning
095   approach for the k-server problem, which can be applied to arbitrary topologies and request arrival
096   distributions (drawn from the same distribution as the training instances).
097   Naturally, this modeling paradigm presents several challenges, a major bottleneck being an enor-
098   mously large state space that requires extremely specific actions to avoid long-term degradation of
099   performance. Additionally, as we show empirically, online approaches do not scale to large problem
100   instances. We address this challenge by presenting the first scalable reinforcement learning-based
101   solution to the k-server problem. Second, ensuring scalability for a generalist policy is a major
102   bottleneck—during execution, the size of the graph could be significantly larger than training. We
103   ensure scalability to arbitrarily large problem instances by extracting local structural infor-
104
      mation embedded in the graph topology, i.e., we hypothesize that local information is often a
      determinant of global actions in this problem, thereby making our approach scalable by construc-

129   2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
130
131   We begin by formally introducing the stochastic k-server problem on graphs (a summary of notation
132
      is presented as a table in the appendix). We consider a connected graph G = ⟨N , E⟩, where N is
      the set of nodes and E is the set of edges. We let n = |N |. We use d(u, v) to denote the shortest
133
      path distance (i.e., number of edges) between two graph nodes u and v, where u, v ∈ N . The
134
      problem considers a total of T discrete time steps, and we use t to denote an arbitrary time step.
135   Requests arrive on the nodes of the graph according to a known probability distribution p, where pv
136   denotes the probability of a request arriving at node v ∈ N . We denote the sequence of requests
137   by {σ 1 , σ 2 , . . . , σ T }, where σ t ∈ N is the location (i.e., a node of the graph) of the request at time
138   t. We use xt = {xt1 , . . . , xti , . . . , xtk } to denote the locations of the k servers in time step t, where
139   xti ∈ N is the location of the i-th server. For a list of symbols, please see Table 2 in the appendix.
140
141   2.2   M ODEL
142
143   We model the k-server problem as a Markov decision process (MDP). An MDP can be described
144   by the tuple ⟨S, A, ρ, r⟩ where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, ρ is the state transition
145   function with ρa (s, s′ ) being the probability with which the process transitions from state s to
146
      state s′ when action a is taken, and r represents the reward function with r(s, a) being the reward
      for taking action a in state s. In our setting, the MDP is defined as follows.
147
148   States The state st ∈ S of the process at time step t is the tuple ⟨xt , σ t ⟩, where xt are the locations
149   of the k servers (before dispatching one of them to serve the request), and σ t is the location of
150   the request. For the initial state s0 , the tuple ⟨x0 , σ 0 ⟩ captures the initial locations of the k servers
151   and of the first request, both chosen uniformly at random. Server locations are chosen ensuring that
152
      no two servers occupy the same location.
153   Actions At each time step t, the agent can choose an action at ∈ A that involves moving one of
154   the servers to serve the current request σ t . The action space A is represented as at ∈ {1, 2, . . . , k}
155   where at = j specifies the action of moving the j-th server at time step t. In other words, the
156   action at denotes the index of the server that is moved to serve the request at time t. If the request
157   arrives at the same location as one of the servers, the action is simply dispatching that server to fulfill
158
      the request.
159   Reward Function We model the reward function r(st , at ) as the negative of the distance traveled by
160   the chosen server to serve the request, i.e., r(st , at ) = −d(xtat , σ t ), where d(xta , σ t ) is the distance
161   between the location xtat of the chosen server at at time t and the location σ t of the request. The
      distance between two nodes is measured as the number of hops between the nodes.

205   DQN with Experience Replay and Target Network We employ the classical Deep Q-Network
206   (DQN) approach as our learning algorithm (Mnih et al., 2013), incorporating two key techniques:
207
      experience replay and target network (Van Hasselt et al., 2016). Experience replay involves storing
      past experiences in a replay buffer and sampling mini-batches during the training process. This helps
208
      in breaking the temporal correlation in the sequence of observations and stabilizes the training. The
209
      target network is a separate network used to estimate target Q-values during training, which improves
210   the stability of the learning process.
211
212   Our training process follows the standard DQN approach with target network and experience replay.
213
      Let R be the replay buffer containing experiences st , at , rt , st+1 sampled during interactions with
      the environment. The Q-network is trained iteratively to minimize the temporal difference error:

      Graph Convolutional Network To address the challenges posed by the large state and action
219   spaces in our graph-based problem and to ensure transferability, we employ Graph Convolutional
220   Networks (GCNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017). GCNs allow us to exploit the relational information
221   present in the graph structure, enabling more efficient and effective learning. We use graph convolu-
222   tional layers to aggregate information from neighboring nodes, capturing the dependencies between
223   nodes in the state representation.

225   3.2   D ECOMPOSITION OF ACTION -VALUE
226
227   A key challenge in the k-server problem is that action-values depend on the entire state, suggesting
228   a need for aggregation over all nodes. However, such aggregation is difficult in large graphs. To
229
      address this challenge, we decompose the estimation of action-value Q (s, a) into two terms: global
      value Qglobal (s), which captures the overall value of the entire state, providing an estimate of the
230
      global context for the action-value estimation, and local value Qlocal (slocal
                                                                                 xa , a), which focuses on
231
      the specific server chosen by the action a and its neighborhood slocal
                                                                          xa , capturing local context that
232
      determines the advantage of choosing a particular action in a given state.
233
234   This decomposition allows for more efficient learning by separately processing global and local
235   information. By combining classical DQN with GCN and a decomposition of action-values, we
236   strive to develop a scalable and transferable solution for the stochastic k-server problem on graphs.
237   Note that for ease of presentation, we omit the superscript t from the description of the neural-
238   network architecture for our action-value estimator Q (st , at ) as all calculations are performed for
239   time step t.

      approaches, it faces significant challenges in terms of scalability and transferability, which motivates

339   4.2      P ROBLEM I NSTANCES
340
341   Graphs We evaluate our approach against benchmark algorithms on multiple classes of graphs.
342   Random Grids: We employ the GRE method introduced by Peng et al. (2012) and Peng et al. (2014)
343   to generate two-dimensional grids that resemble real-world road networks. First, we create an m×m
344   grid of nodes and edges (n = m · m). For the sake of simplicity, we generate grids that are square
345   shaped (n = 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100 nodes). Then, horizontal and vertical edges are randomly
346   removed with different probabilities, and diagonal edges are randomly added. By using probabilities
347   from Peng et al. (2014), we generate graphs that resemble real-world road networks in terms of their
348
      topology; we refer to the description from Peng et al. (2014) for details.
349   Random Trees: We generate random trees of a given size through an iterative process of adding
350   nodes one-by-one. For each node u (excluding the first), a parent node v is chosen uniformly at
351   random from the set of previously added nodes: v ∼ Uniform{1, 2, . . . , u − 1}. An edge is then

502      5                   R ELATED W ORK
503
504     The k-server problem is one of the most widely studied online optimization problems in the last
505     three decades, and providing a comprehensive summary of prior work is beyond the scope of this
506     paper. We refer readers to prior work by Bertsimas et al. (2019b) and Koutsoupias (2009), who
507     provide excellent overviews of related work in this domain. We present a relatively short account
508     of prior work here (largely inspired by the detailed account given by Koutsoupias (2009)). The k-
509     server problem was first introduced by Manasse et al. (1988; 1990). As Koutsoupias (2009) points
510     out, the problem setting was proposed at a time when several important developments happened in
511
        the context of online algorithms. In particular, Sleator & Tarjan (1985) introduced the notion of
        competitive analysis (also known as “amortized efficiency”), a principled paradigm for evaluating
512
        online algorithms. This framework marked a rapid development in the field of online algorithms,
513
        including the introduction of the k-server problem (Manasse et al., 1988). The problem has remained
514     a popular choice among computer scientists over the last three decades as it presents a rather simple
515     setting that serves as an abstraction for many practical problems (e.g., emergency resource allocation
516     and caching (Bertsimas et al., 2019b) while presenting several algorithmic challenges. Much of
517     prior work has focused on designing online algorithms, evaluating their competitive ratios, and
518     generalizing the competitive ratios of online algorithms (Chrobak et al., 1991; Chrobak & Larmore,
519     1991; Grove, 1991). We present a more detailed description of offline and online versions of the k-
520     server problem and prior work (albeit limited) on deep reinforcement learning to solve the k-server
521     problem in Appendix A.
522
523      6                   C ONCLUSION
524
525     Despite the fundamental nature and significance of the k-server problem, there is extremely limited
526     prior work on solving this problem using reinforcement learning, even though learning has obvious
527     advantages (e.g., evaluation of WFA on a few instances took significantly longer than training our
528     DRL policies). We hypothesize that this gap is due to the difficulty of handling the large and combi-
529     natorial state and action spaces of the k-server environment. We addressed this gap by introducing
530     a novel approach that decomposes action-values into a global value and local advantage, estimated
531
        from a shared graph convolutional network. Not only did this approach scale to larger graphs than
        any prior learning-based approach, but we also demonstrated that the resulting generalist policies
532
        can be transferred to other instances with no further training. In fact, generalist policies outper-
533
        formed graph specific ones—an interesting phenomenon, which we explain with generalist policies
534     being forced to generalize more effectively during training.

      k-server Problem There are two major types of the k-server problem—the offline version, in
672   which the set of requests to be served is known beforehand, and the online version, in which the
673   requests arrive as decisions are made. The offline problem can be reduced to the standard network
674   flow optimization problem and solved efficiently (Chrobak et al., 1991; Bertsimas et al., 2019b).
675   The reduction centers around adding a source and a sink to to the existing graph, along with an
676   additional node for each of the requests, manipulating the weights of the edges to direct the flow
677   between the source and the sink, and solving a minimal-cost maximal-flow problem problem on the
678   modified graph (Chrobak et al., 1991). The offline solution can then be used for competitive anal-
679   ysis of the online problem, where the requests are not known in advance. Many online approaches
680
      have been proposed for solving the k-server problem, and despite the complexity of the problem,
      several simple algorithms work reasonably well. For example, a simple greedy algorithm is a de-
681
      terministic approach that always dispatches the server closest to a request for service. Although
682
      non-competitive, such an approach has been noted to work well in several settings (Bertsimas et al.,
683   2019b). As opposed to deterministic approaches, there are several randomized algorithms that work
684   well; e.g., HARMONIC is a competitive algorithm that selects servers with probability inversely
685   proportional to the distance from the request (Raghavan & Snir, 1989). In an orthogonal categoriza-
686   tion of the problem type, Dehghani et al. (2017) introduced the stochastic k-server problem, where
687   the requests are drawn from a (potentially time-varying) probability distribution; this setting mim-
688   ics many real-world problems such as emergency response and micro-transit optimization, where a
689   distribution over the arrival of requests can be approximated using historical data.
690
691   Deep Reinforcement Learning The sequential nature of the k-server problem naturally leads to
692   it being modeled as a stochastic control process. Surprisingly, this modeling paradigm has been
693
      under-explored in prior work. The earliest effort was made by Junior et al. (2005), who used rein-
      forcement learning (Q-learning) to compute the optimal decisions for small problem instances. Lins
694
      et al. (2019a) transformed the k-server problem to a visual task problem and used reinforcement
695
      learning, but neither approach can scale to large problem instances. Our work presents the first
696   decision-theoretic approach to model the k-server problem that can scale to large problem instances.
697   Moreover, we exploit the information embedded in the local structure of graphs to learn generalist
698   policies that perform well even when the graph topology or the arrival distribution of the requests
699   changes (as long as the change is captured during training of the policy). We point out that such
700   generalist policies are particularly desirable in practice. Consider emergency response as an exam-
701   ple, where the underlying graph denotes the road network of a city. Roads might get closed or be
      affected by traffic congestion, thereby changing the underlying network Pettet et al. (2021). While

735   Estimated Arrival Probabilities Approach: This approach involves comparing other methods with
736   the WFA while incorporating estimated arrival probabilities. Initially, precise information about the

      ities for the first request from all nodes are assumed to be zero. As requests are processed, these
      probabilities are iteratively updated. This iterative process continues as more data is collected.

740   The estimated arrival probability at time t for a request originating from node j can be denoted as
741   p̂tj . The iterative update process for the estimated arrival probabilities can be expressed as follows:
