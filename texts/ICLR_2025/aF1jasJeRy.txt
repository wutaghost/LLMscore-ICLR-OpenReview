008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Efficiently exploring complex loss landscapes is key to the performance of deep
011            neural networks. While momentum-based optimizers are widely used in state-
012            of-the-art setups, classical momentum can still struggle with large, misaligned
013            gradients, leading to oscillations. To address this, we propose Torque-Aware
014
               Momentum (TAM), which introduces a damping factor based on the angle between
               the new gradients and previous momentum, stabilizing the update direction during
015
               training. Empirical results show that TAM, which can be combined with both
016
               SGD and Adam, enhances exploration, handles distribution shifts more effectively,
017            and improves generalization performance across various tasks, including image
018            classification and large language model fine-tuning, when compared to classical
019            momentum-based optimizers.

074   2   R ELATED WORK
075
076   Momentum-based methods have been widely studied for their ability to improve convergence speed
077   and exploration of the loss landscape. For instance, Xing et al. (2018) showed that as mini-batch
078   gradients aligns with the top eigenvectors of the Hessian, SGD’s exploration slows due to oscillatory
079   behaviour, particularly at larger batch sizes. Similarly, Fu et al. (2023) showed that SGDM accelerates
080   convergence by deferring this oscillation, referred to as abrupt sharpening, where gradients and the
081   Hessian suddenly align, making SGDM more effective for larger learning rates.
082   Several momentum variants aim to improve generalization by utilizing the curvature of the loss
083   surface (Gilmer et al., 2021; Foret et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2021; Tran & Cutkosky, 2022; Kaddour
084   et al., 2022).
085
      Popular optimizers like Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) combine adaptive learning rate with momentum
086
      for faster convergence, while Ziyin et al. (2020) proposed leveraging parameter updates, rather than
087   gradients, to compute momentum. However, while these methods improve convergence speed, they
088   do not specifically address the challenge of torqued gradients on noisy loss surfaces.
089
090
      Lucas et al. (2018) introduced AggMo, an optimizer combining multiple momentum vectors with
      different decay rates, but requires storing multiple copies of model states (Cutkosky & Mehta, 2020a;
091
      Xie et al., 2021), unlike our method TAM, which maintains the same memory footprint as SGDM.
092
      Closest to our work, S.K. Roy & Chaudhuri (2021) tackle gradient misalignment by considering
093   angles between consecutive gradients. However, we argue that focusing on the angle between
094   momentum and gradients is more criticial for stability, as demonstrated by our comparisons with
095   their method, AngularGrad (seeSection 4).

      5   C ONCLUSION
442
443
      We propose Torque-Aware Momentum (TAM), an enhancement of classical momentum that mitigates
444   the detrimental effects of torqued gradients, enabling more stable and consistent exploration of
445   the loss landscape. By incorporating a damping factor that adjusts momentum based on gradient
446   alignments, TAM helps models escape sharp minima and improve generalization across diverse tasks.
447
      Our evaluation of TAM spans multiple experimental setups, including image classification, large
448
      language model fine-tuning, and online learning with distribution shifts. Across these tasks, TAM
449
      consistently performs on par with, and often surpasses, traditional SGD and SGDM. In particular,
450   TAM shows significant advantages in tasks involving distribution shifts, where it stabilizes learning
451   and adapts more effectively than SGDM, especially when tasks share little overlap. Additionnaly,
452   TAM proves valuable as a warm-up strategy, leading to faster convergence and lower loss barriers
453   compared to SGDM.
454
      While our results demonstrate TAM’s effectiveness in tasks with distribution shifts and gradient
455
      misalignment, further work is needed to test its capabilites in more challenging non-stationary envi-
456   ronments, such as continual learning. Our preliminary continual learning experiments in Appendix
457   A.2.3 highlight TAM’s potential to address catastrophic forgetting by retaining gradient direction
458   from previous tasks. However, a thorough investigation is required to fully understand and optimize
459   TAM’s performance in this domain. Another exciting avenue is to explore TAM’s potential in other
460   training paradigms, such as self-supervised learning and reinforcement learning, where effective
461   exploration and stability is critical for model success.

774
775   In subsection 4.3, we evaluate TAM on an online learning setup where we showed that TAM helps in
776   maintaining the plasticity of MLP across a large number of tasks. In this section, we evaluate TAM
777   in a more challenging setting - continual learning - where the goal is to maintain both the stability
778   and plasticity of the model. In particular, we train a ResNet50 model on CLEAR benchmark (Lin
779   et al., 2021) which consists of 10 sequential image recognition tasks (or experiences) with the goal of
780   maximizing average accuracy on all tasks without forgetting. We follow the implementation provided
781   by Zhang et al. (2023) to compare SGDM and TAM optimizers. We evaluate these two optimizers on
782   top of two continual learning setups: Naive and Learning without forgetting (LwF) (Li & Hoiem,
783
      2017) which is a well-known continual learning method. We conduct a grid search across learning
      rate (from set {0.005, 0.1, 0.2}) and select the best setup based on performance on a held-out dataset.
784
      The learning rate of 0.005 performed best for both SGDM and TAM.
