009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Dynamic view synthesis (DVS) from monocular videos has remarkably advanced
012           in recent years, achieving high-fidelity rendering with reduced computational
013           costs. Despite these advancements, the optimization of dynamic neural fields
014           still relies on traditional structure from motion (SfM), requiring that all objects
015           remain stationary during scene capture. To address this limitation, we present
016           SC-4DGS, a pose-free optimization pipeline for dynamic Gaussian Splatting
017           (GS) from monocular videos, which eliminates the need for SfM through self-
018
              calibration. Specifically, we jointly optimize dynamic Gaussian representations
              and camera poses by utilizing DUSt3R, enabling accurate calibration and render-
019
              ing. Furthermore, we introduce a comprehensive benchmark, Kubric-MRig, that
020
              includes extensive camera and object motions along with simultaneous multi-view
021           captures. Unlike previous benchmarks for DVS, where ground truths for camera
022           information are absent due to the difficulty of capturing multiple viewpoints si-
023           multaneously, it facilitates evaluating both calibration and rendering quality in
024           dynamic scenes. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method out-
025           performs previous pose-free dynamic neural fields and achieves competitive per-
026           formance compared to existing pose-free 3D neural fields.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   We live in a dynamic world where objects with intricate geometries and textures undergo complex
032   motions and deformations. In daily life, such scenes with motions and deformations are often cap-
033
      tured by monocular videos, which do not directly provide the underlying geometries of the scenes.
      In recent years, computer graphics researchers have explored effective representations and methods
034
      to reconstruct 3D scene structures and motions from native visual data. Especially, recent advances
035
      in dynamic view synthesis (DVS) (Pumarola et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b) have
036   demonstrated unprecedented fidelity in capturing motions and synthesizing novel views from multi-
037   view input images. The pioneering work, D-NeRF (Pumarola et al., 2020), extends NeRF to learn
038   deformable volumetric field from a set of monocular views without ground truth geometry. To over-
039   come the limited representation power of NeRF, more recent DVS methods tend to use 3D Gaussian
040   Splatting (3DGS) as an alternative representation of scene geometry.
041
      Despite recent advances, existing DVS methods heavily rely on Structure from Motion (SfM), which
042   is susceptible to deformation and motion of objects; for real-world scenes where ground truth cam-
043   era information is unavailable, the conventional DVS pipeline typically assumes camera information
044   extracted by COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016) as ground truth. However, the bundle adjust-
045   ment process of COLMAP with pair-wise image correspondences often fails to converge. To avoid
046   dependence on SfM, recent approaches (Wang et al., 2021; Jeong et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021) at-
047   tempt to jointly optimize camera poses and scene representations, showing successful calibration
048   and rendering quality even when trained without ground truth or COLMAP-extracted camera in-
049   formation. However, they require that all objects remain stationary while capturing videos, which
050
      greatly limits their usage in practical scenarios.
051   To tackle these limitations, we introduce SC-4DGS, an optimization pipeline for pose-free dynamic
052   neural fields. Recent work of RoDynRF (Liu et al., 2023) also jointly estimates camera parameters
053   and neural fields from monocular video in a similar spirit with ours, but optimizing RoDynRF in
      scenes with extensive camera and object movements is challenging, as the randomly initialized

054
      camera parameters tend to fall into local minima, leading to degraded rendering. To overcome this
055   limitation, SC-4DGS leverages geometric priors from DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024c), a geometric
056   foundation model for multi-view stereo. To fully take advantage of using DUSt3R, we propose
057   an efficient algorithm for initializing camera poses and the 3D point cloud of 3DGS. Specifically,
058   we introduce batchwise optimization and an extended motion representation tailored for DUSt3R
059   initialization. Additionally, we incorporate physical regularization terms to enable geometrically
060   accurate rendering, which was previously infeasible in RoDynRF due to its fully implicit design.
061   Furthermore, existing benchmarks encounter difficulties in assessing both calibration and render-
062   ing quality because they either lack ground truth (GT) camera poses or simultaneous multi-view
063   captures. Therefore, we introduce a much more challenging benchmark, Kubric-MRig, which in-
064   cludes photorealistic scenes with a variety of simultaneously captured viewpoints with extensive
065   camera and object movements. Our experiments show that SC-4DGS outperforms prior pose-free
066   4D neural fields on Kubric and results competitive performance compared to pose-free 3D neural
067   fields.
068   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
069
070         1. We introduce a pose-free optimization pipeline for dynamic Gaussian Splatting from
071            monocular videos, eliminating the need for Structure from Motion (SfM) through self-
072            calibration.
073         2. SC-4DGS effectively utilizes geometric priors from DUSt3R by introducing batchwise op-
074            timization and an extended motion representation designed for DUSt3R. Additionally, SC-
075            4DGS incorporates regularization terms to ensure geometrically accurate rendering.
076         3. We introduce a challenging dataset, Kubric-MRig, to evaluate both camera calibration and
077            novel view synthesis performance on dynamic scenes, which was challenging in previous
078            benchmarks.
079         4. Our optimization pipeline achieves superiority over the pose-free 4D neural fields and com-
080            petitive performance over previous pose-free 3D neural fields.

      2.1   N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS ON S TATIC S CENES
085
086   Novel View Synthesis (NVS) is a task of generating novel viewpoints from a set of observations.
087   Pioneer work in NVS leverages point clouds (Kopanas et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
088   2022), meshes (Riegler & Koltun, 2020; 2021), and planes (Hoiem et al., 2005) for geometrically
089   convincing view synthesis. Recently, NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) has achieved ground-breaking
090   rendering quality by representing volumetric scene functions via MLPs. To accelerate the training
091
      and inference of NeRF, subsequent research has focused on baking trained NeRFs (Hedman et al.,
      2021) or directly optimizing explicit representations (Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022;
092
      Müller et al., 2022).
093
094   More recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) introduces a novel rendering
095   algorithm that rasterizes anisotropic 3D Gaussians into image planes. Its efficient tile-based alpha-
096   blending CUDA implementation offers real-time rendering with no quality degradation, achiev-
097
      ing state-of-the-art results on NVS benchmarks. Subsequent work based on 3DGS has proposed
      methods to improve fidelity (Kheradmand et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024), enable training with sparse
098
      views (Xiong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), and facilitate editing (Chen et al., 2024; Dou et al.,
099
      2024). Despite their advancements, these approaches assume all objects remain stationary when
100   scene captures and that camera information is fully available, restricting their practical applicability.
101
102
      2.2   N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS ON DYNAMIC S CENES
103
104   Following the success of NVS in stationary scenes, researchers moved on to extend neural fields for
105   capturing both the underlying motions and geometries of scenes from a set of observations. The pi-
106   oneer work (Pumarola et al., 2020; Park et al., 2021a;b) learns additional time-varying deformation
107   fields to . Several studies (Li et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023) instead
      learn multi-dimensional feature fields to encode scene dynamics without explicit motion modeling.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      With the advent of 3DGS, (Luiten et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) propose to learn the trajectories
109   of individual Gaussians over time. Subsequent research has introduced more efficient representa-
110   tions, such as factorized motion bases (Kratimenos et al., 2023) and sparse control points (Huang
111   et al., 2024). Another line of work by (Yang et al., 2024b) extends spherical harmonics into a 4D
112   spherindrical harmonics function, integrating both time-dependent and view-dependent components.
113
      As highlighted by Dycheck (Gao et al., 2022b), many existing approaches focus on unrealistic sce-
114
      narios, such as camera teleportation or ambient-motion scenes, whereas multi-view capture is typi-
115   cally done using casually captured videos that involve substantial motion. Reconstructing 4D scenes
116   from these videos is a highly ill-posed problem, often failing without additional cues due to the am-
117   biguity between camera and object movements. To resolve the motion ambiguity, recent efforts (Liu
118   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a;b; Lee et al., 2023) leverage pretrained depth estimation models
119   (Ranftl et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024a) or long-term trajectory tracking models (Karaev et al., 2023).
120   In this study, we tailor DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024c), a geometric foundation model for initial point
121   clouds and camera poses. For 4DGS optimization, we leverage depth estimation (Yang et al., 2024a)
122   and optical flow (Teed & Deng, 2020) pipelines to ensure geometrically accurate rendering.

125   2.3   P OSE - FREE N EURAL F IELDS
126
127   Traditional novel view synthesis (NVS) pipelines strongly rely on structure from motion
128   (SfM) (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016) to obtain camera information from a set of observations. Be-
129   cause SfM pipelines are time-consuming and error-prone, researchers are attempting to obtain accu-
130
      rate camera poses without relying on them. There has been growing interests in optimizing neural
      fields without pre-calibrated camera poses. The pioneer work iNeRF (Yen-Chen et al., 2021) solves
131
      an inverse problem that estimates camera poses from pre-trained NeRF by minimizing photometric
132
      loss between query views and rendered views. NeRFmm (Wang et al., 2021) and SC-NeRF (Jeong
133   et al., 2021) use photometric loss and geometric regularization to eliminate the required prepro-
134   cessing step of camera estimation by jointly optimizing camera and NeRF parameters. BARF (Lin
135   et al., 2021) and GARF (Chng et al., 2022) address the gradient inconsistency issue caused by high-
136   frequency parts of positional embeddings to handle complex camera motions. Nope-NeRF (Bian
137   et al., 2023) leverages geometric priors and continuity of camera motions, achieving both high-
138   fidelity rendering and accurate camera trajectory estimation. After the emergence of 3DGS, CF-
139   3DGS (Fu et al., 2024) proposes progressively growing 3DGS for pose estimation. InstantSplat
140   (Fan et al., 2024) shares similar inspiration with our work, leveraging DUSt3R for pose initializa-
141
      tion. However, it is designed for static scenes and is restricted to a limited number of viewpoints due
      to the high memory demands of camera alignment.
142
143   RoDynRF, our competitive method, introduces a pose-free optimization pipeline for dynamic scenes
144   by decoupling static backgrounds from dynamic objects. However, it is limited to specific scenarios
145   such as forward-facing scenes or videos with ambient motion. Moreover, its fully implicit repre-
146   sentation makes enforcing physical constraints difficult. To address these limitations, our approach
147
      employs the geometric foundation model DUSt3R to handle a variety of video capture scenarios.
      Furthermore, by leveraging the explicit nature of 3DGS, our optimization incorporates geometric
148
      regularization to enhance rendering quality.

      3.2   L EVERAGING DUS T 3R FOR G EOMETRIC P RIORS
242
243   In the absence of inherent camera pose priors in monocular videos, previous work has relied on
244   COLMAP (Schönberger et al., 2016) to generate them, though COLMAP is time-consuming and
245   often fails to converge in dynamic scenes. Recently, DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024c) has shown re-
246   markable performance in real-world settings by training on large-scale 2D-to-3D data. It produces
247   dense per-pixel point maps from two-view inputs with high accuracy, even in dynamic environments.
248
      In addition, it supports global alignment through a graph-based optimization for multi-view scenar-
      ios. We utilize DUSt3R to initialize the pose and point cloud for 3D Gaussian Splatting. However,
249
      DUSt3R’s multi-view optimization requires high memory capacity and computational cost, making
250
      it unsuitable for temporally densely captured data. Its fully connected graph-based optimization
251   has O(N 2 ) memory and time complexity because of its pairwise inference, which makes aligning a
252   large number of frames significantly more time-consuming. To overcome this, we introduce an effi-
253   cient batch-wise optimization pipeline for DUSt3R that effectively acquires camera poses in dense
254   view situations.
255

288

289   optimization but also from the inability to utilize information from all images during local batch
290   optimization, leading to minor discrepancies between camera poses from different batches. Our

365         L = Lrecon + λdepth,g Ldepth,g + λdepth,l Ldepth,l + λmotion Lmotion + λstatic Lstatic + λcam Lcam .   (20)

529
530
531
      In this paper, we introduced SC-4DGS, a camera-free optimization pipeline for dynamic Gaussian
      Splatting (GS) from monocular videos. Our method addresses the limitations of existing dynamic
532
      view synthesis (DVS) models, which still heavily rely on structure from motion (SfM) and assume
533
      static scenes during capture. By fully exploiting geometric priors from geometric foundational
534   models, SC-4DGS achieves geometrically accurate and high-quality rendering in dynamic scenes
535   without requiring ground truth camera information. Additionally, we proposed Kubric-MRig, a
536   challenging benchmark designed to evaluate both calibration and novel view synthesis performance
537   under extensive object and camera motions. SC-4DGS demonstrates superior performance over pre-
538   vious pose-free dynamic neural fields and achieves competitive results when compared to existing
539   pose-free 3D neural fields, marking a significant step forward in the optimization of dynamic neural
      fields.

758   A.1     K UBRIC -MR IG DATASET
759
760   We provide further details on the process of generating the Kubric-MRig dataset. Our data genera-
761   tion script is based on the Movi script, which is part of the official implementation of Kubric (Greff
762   et al., 2022). We randomly select 10 to 20 static objects and 1 to 3 dynamic objects from the
763   Google Scanned Objects dataset. We then choose a background from the publicly available HDRI
764   environments in Kubric. The static objects are randomly placed on the ground, while the dynamic
765
      objects are positioned to float in the air. Next, we run a physics simulation to achieve realistic object
      movements, resembling real-world scenarios.
766
767   For the training set, we deploy 100 cameras that follow circular trajectories around the objects, with
768   equal spacing between each frame to ensure consistent scene coverage. For the evaluation set, we use
769   the same camera positions as in the training setup, but with two distinct evaluation scenarios: ”pose-
770   freeze time-varying” and ”time-freeze pose-varying”. In the ”pose-freeze time-varying” setup, we
771
      fix the camera viewpoint to the first training camera position, then capture the scene across the 100
      timesteps used during training. In the ”time-freeze pose-varying” setup, we fix the timestep to 0 and
772
      capture the scene from the same viewpoints used in the training data.
773
774   All cameras are positioned equidistant from the world center, with distances randomly sampled
775   between 15 and 20 units. To ensure the viewpoint coverage of the scenes for evaluation, we fix
776   the elevation angle, which is randomly sampled from 30◦ to 60◦ during data capture. We provide
777
      visualization of the generated dataset in Figure 4.

      A.2.1    O PTIMIZATION
805
806   4DGS Optimization. We use the official implementation for 3DGS with adding gradient com-
807   putation over camera poses. When running DUSt3R batchwise optimization process to get initial
808   camera poses, we use the window size M of 10 and set the number of iteration to 250 on each batch.
809   After optimization, we uniformly sample points from globally aligned point cloud of all viewpoints
      with a factor of 0.01 to initialize point cloud for GS. To extract monocular depths and optical flows,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      we use DepthAnything(Yang et al., 2024a) and RAFT(Teed & Deng, 2020). When optimizing SC-
811   4DGS, we set the loss weights as follows: λSSIM = 0.2, λl1 = 0.8, λm = 0.2, λms = 0.05,
812   λdepth,g = 0.15, λdepth,l = 0.05, λstatic = 1.0, and λcam = 0.1. Additionally, rigidity loss is applied
813   every 5 iterations with λrigid = 0.2. Similar to DynMF, for the first 5000 steps, we do not optimize
814   motion networks for stable training. Then, we linearly warm-up learning rates for the 10% of the
815   total steps and anneal with cosine functions for the rest of iterations. We set the peak learning rate
816   of camera rotation to 0.0001 and translation to 0.0002. For the rest of configurations, we follow the
817   official implementation of GS.
818   3DGS Optimization. In contrast to the Kubric-MRig dataset, for the Tanks and Temples dataset,
819   we have disabled the learning of Gaussian motion. As a result, we obtained camera poses from the
820   DUSt3R batch-wise optimization process using the SQ strategy. Therefore, we optimized the Gaus-
821   sians and camera poses without applying the pose regularization process and set the loss weights
822   as follows: λSSIM = 0.2, λl1 = 0.8, λdepth,g = 0.001, and λdepth,l = 0.01. We also applied cosine
823   annealing throughout the iterations and set the learning rates for camera rotation and translation to
824   0.000005 and 0.00005, respectively. During training, we optimized the Gaussians and camera poses
825
      without resetting opacity, without learning motion, and without applying the pose regularization
      process.

      A.2.2    O BTAINING M OTION M ASKS WITH E PIPOLAR E RRORS
828
829   RoDynRF (Liu et al., 2023) utilizes RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020) to first predict forward and back-
830   ward optical flows from video frames. Then, it estimates the fundamental matrix between adjacent
831   frames using the 8-point algorithm. Afterward, it computes the error between the points projected
832   using the fundamental matrix and those derived from the predicted flows. Regions with high error
833   are assumed to correspond to dynamic parts of the frames. However, in our Kubric-MRig dataset,
834   this method often fails due to the large motions observed between adjacent frames. To mitigate this
835   issue, we directly compute the fundamental matrix using the calibrated poses during training. We
836
      then assume that regions with an epipolar error below the median correspond to the static parts of
      the scene.
837
838
      A.2.3    DUS T 3R O PTIMIZATION S TRATEGIES
839
840   In ablation studies, we have proposed three additional strategies to optimize camera poses of dense
841   views. Here, we elaborate details of each strategy. In the naive strategy, we sequentially accumulate
842   pair-wise predictions without any extra optimization. For the SQ strategy, we sample 10 consecu-
843   tive frames from the entire sequence to create each local batch, with the final batch containing the
844   remaining frames. After running DUSt3R pose optimization on each local batch, we align the last
845   frame of the N -th local batch with the first frame of the (N +1)-th batch. Lastly, for the SB strategy,
846
      we first sample the first K frames (where K is the quotient of the total number of frames divided by
      10) to form a global batch, and then sample every 10th frame starting from each frame in the global
847
      batch to form local batches. After running DUSt3R on all batches, we align each local batch with
848
      the corresponding frames in the global batch. Remark that our strategy samples every 10th frame
849   starting from the first frame of the entire sequence to form the global batch. For each local batch,
850   we sample frames sequentially, starting from the K-th frame to the (K + 1)−th frame in the global
851   batch. We then run DUSt3R pose optimization on each batch and align each local batch with the
852   corresponding frames in the global batch.

854   A.3     FAILURE C ASES AND F UTURE W ORK
855
856   Failure Cases. While our model outperforms existing baselines on the Kubric-MRig dataset, it
857   has several limitations where failure cases can occur. One major limitation is that our model can-
858   not handle temporally sparse videos with large camera motions. Such videos provide insufficient
859   observations, leading to potential optimization failures. Additionally, our pose regularization may
860
      oversmooth sparse camera viewpoints with significant pose variations, resulting in suboptimal per-
      formance in these settings.
861
862   Another limitation is that our pipeline is vulnerable to failures in Dust3R optimization. In scenarios
863   like dynamic scenes with textureless backgrounds, Dust3R optimization can fail similarly to other
      SfM pipelines(Schonberger & Frahm, 2016), causing the subsequent training process to fail entirely.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      In some cases, the camera poses optimized by Dust3R lead the training to fall into local minima.
865   Slight misalignments between camera poses across frames result in insufficient gradients, hindering
866   further optimization. These issues indicate that the robustness of our method is contingent on the
867   success of Dust3R optimization, highlighting the need for mechanisms to handle or mitigate such
868   failures to enhance overall reliability.
869
870   Future Work. Additionally, to handle in-the-wild videos, we aim to integrate static and dynamic
871   scene training procedures. Our current approach distinguishes between static and dynamic scenes
872   by disabling motion learning when training on static scenes. However, separating dynamic and
873   static settings is unrealistic for handling diverse in-the-wild videos. Real-world scenes often contain
874   both static and dynamic elements, and our model’s inability to seamlessly integrate both limits its
875
      applicability. This suggests the need for a unified framework that can adaptively handle both static
      and dynamic components within a scene.
