009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized machine learning method that enables
013            participants to collaboratively train a model without sharing their private data.
014
               Despite its privacy and scalability benefits, FL is susceptible to backdoor attacks,
               where adversaries poison the local training data of a subset of clients using back-
015
               door triggers, aiming to make the aggregated model produce malicious results
016
               when the same backdoor conditions are met by an inference-time input. Exist-
017            ing backdoor attacks in FL suffer from common deficiencies: fixed trigger pat-
018            terns and reliance on the assistance of model poisoning. State-of-the-art defenses
019            based on analyzing clients’ model updates exhibit a good defense performance
020            on these attacks because of the significant divergence between malicious and be-
021            nign client model updates. To effectively conceal malicious model updates among
022            benign ones, we propose DPOT, a backdoor attack strategy in FL that dynam-
023            ically constructs backdoor objectives by optimizing a backdoor trigger, making
024            backdoor data have minimal effect on model updates. We provide theoretical jus-
025
               tifications for DPOT’s attacking principle and display experimental results show-
               ing that DPOT, via only a data-poisoning attack, effectively undermines state-of-
026
               the-art defenses and outperforms existing backdoor attack techniques on various
027
               datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032   Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized machine-learning approach that has gained widespread
033   attention recently. Unlike traditional centralized model training, FL enables model updates to be
034   computed locally on distributed data, offering enhanced data privacy, reduced communication over-
035   head, and scalability for a large number of clients. In each round of FL, a central server distributes
036   a global model to participating clients, each of whom independently trains the model on its local
037   data, and its model updates are aggregated by the server for updating the global model. Despite its
038   advantages, FL has been proven susceptible to backdoor attacks (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020). Back-
039
      door attacks in FL involve adversaries manipulating the local models of a subset of clients to learn
      backdoor information from poisoned data with triggers, causing the global model, after aggregating
040
      these compromised local models, to produce adversary-desired results when the same trigger con-
041
      ditions are met. In this work, we term clients manipulated by adversaries during local training as
042   malicious clients, and those unaffected as benign clients.
043
044   Existing backdoor attacks in FL present two common deficiencies. First, the patterns of backdoor
045
      triggers are pre-defined by the attacker and remain unchanged throughout the entire attack pro-
      cess (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020). Consequently, the optimization objective brought by backdoored
046
      data (backdoor objective) is static and incoherent with the optimization objective of main-task data
047
      (benign objective), resulting in distinct differences in model updates after training. These mali-
048   cious clients’ model updates are therefore easily canceled out by robust aggregations. Second, many
049   approaches rely on model-poisoning techniques to enhance the effectiveness of backdoor attacks.
050   Implementing model-poisoning attacks requires attackers to change the training procedures of a cer-
051   tain number of genuine clients to make their local training algorithms different from other clients.
052   However, achieving this condition is challenging, as advanced defense mechanisms (Riege et al.,
053   2024) have introduced Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) to ensure the secure execution of
      client-side training, making it harder to maliciously modify the training procedure.

054
      Existing defenses against backdoor attacks in FL rely on a hypothesis that backdoor attacks will
055   always cause the updating direction of a model to deviate from its original benign objective, be-
056   cause the backdoor objectives defined by backdoored data cannot be achieved within the original
057   direction (Fung et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2021). However, the capabilities of backdoor attacks are not
058   limited to this hypothesis. To counter this hypothesis, adversaries can align the updating directions
059   of a model with respect to backdoor and benign objectives by strategically adjusting the backdoor
060   objective. Applying this idea to FL, if the injection of backdoored data has minimal effect on a
061   client’s model updates, then detecting this client as malicious becomes challenging for defenses
062   based on analyzing clients’ model updates.
063   In this work, we propose Data Poisoning with Optimized Trigger (DPOT), a backdoor attack on
064   FL that dynamically constructs the backdoor objective to continuously minimize the divergence be-
065   tween clients’ model updates in the backdoored states and the non-attacked states. We construct the
066   backdoor objective by optimizing the backdoor trigger such that the current round’s global model
067   exhibits minimal loss on the backdoored data. When the global model becomes more optimized to
068   the backdoored data, further training on this data will lead to smaller updates to the global model’s
069
      current state. Therefore, when a malicious client’s local dataset is partially poisoned by the opti-
      mized trigger while the rest remains benign, the model updates produced on them can be dominated
070
      by benign model updates within a limited number of local training epochs. We provide theoretical
071
      justification that trigger optimization can cause small differences in a client’s model updates be-
072   tween the non-attacked and backdoored states (in Appendix C). Our experiments demonstrate that
073   these small differences enable malicious model updates to bypass defenses and integrate into global
074   models, resulting in backdoored global models.
075
      Without any assistance of model-poisoning techniques, DPOT can be conducted simply by executing
076
      a normal training process on the poisoned local data. To ensure the stealthiness of the trigger, we
077
      constrain its L0 -norm by developing two algorithms to separately optimize the placement and values
078   of trigger pixels. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose algorithms for generating
079   an optimized trigger with free shape and placement while specifying its exact size.
080
081
      We evaluated DPOT on four image data sets (FashionMNIST, FEMNIST, CIFAR10, and Tiny Ima-
      geNet) and four model architectures including ResNet and VGGNet. We assessed the attack effec-
082
      tiveness of DPOT under a variety of defense conditions, testing it against eleven defense strategies
083
      that are based on analyzing clients’ model updates along with one defense strategy that uses client-
084   side adversarial training to recover the global model (Zhang et al., 2023). We compared DPOT attack
085   with three state-of-the-art data-poisoning backdoor attacks that employ fixed-pattern triggers, dis-
086   tributed fixed-pattern triggers (Xie et al., 2020), and partially optimized triggers (Zhang et al., 2024),
087   respectively. Using a small number of malicious clients (5% of the total), DPOT outperformed ex-
088   isting data-poisoning backdoor attacks in effectively undermining defenses without affecting the
089   main-task performance of the FL system.

      2.1   BACKDOOR ATTACKS IN FL
093
094   FL is very vulnerable to backdoor attacks. As training data are privately held by clients, the secu-
095   rity of data is hard to track and protect. We discussed existing backdoor attacks in FL for image
096   classification tasks based on their important properties (more details can be found in Appendix B.2).
097   With vs. Without model poisoning. Backdoor attacks in FL primarily rely on data poisoning, where
098   attackers embed triggers in local training data and alter labels to train malicious models. Model
099   poisoning (Fang et al., 2020) is often introduced to strengthen these attacks, by directly manipulating
100   clients’ model updates or training algorithms. However, it is challenged by the security provided by
101   Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs), which authenticate and protect client-side training. In
102   contrast, data poisoning is easier for attackers to conduct and harder to prevent, as clients would
103   gather data from open, vulnerable sources. DPOT attack only relys on data poisoning.
104   Static objective vs. Dynamic objective. A static objective in backdoor attack represents a pre-
105   defined and unchanging objective that is independent to the training system’s status, such as asso-
106   ciating certain input features or patterns with incorrect predictions. Having static objectives make
107   malicious model updates easier to detect due to their inconsistency with main-task optimization. In


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      contrast, a backdoor attack that adjusts its objective based on the training system’s status is referred
109   to as having a dynamic objective. For example, Gong et al. (2022) and Fang & Chen (2023) opti-
110   mized the trigger pattern based on a hypothesis that maximizing the activation of certain neurons in
111   the backdoored local model can enhance the attack’s persistence on the global model; however, these
112   lack theoretical justification and proof-of-concept implementations. Zhang et al. (2024) optimized
113   triggers specifically for a corner case where the global model is directly trained to unlearn the trig-
114   ger, but the effectiveness of its triggers in more general FL training scenarios remains unaddressed.
115   DPOT dynamically adjusts objectives to minimize the impact of backdoored data on model updates,
116   and is provably effective in general FL training scenarios.
117   L2 -norm vs. L0 -norm bounded trigger. Designing effective backdoor triggers requires ensuring
118   their stealthiness. L2 -norm bounds restrict the magnitude of changes added by the trigger, resulting
119   in subtle perturbations spreading within a single input (Lyu et al., 2023). L0 -norm bounds restrict
120   the number of components (e.g., pixels in an image) that can be replaced by the trigger. L2 -norm
121   bounded triggers require the attacker to access and alter a figure’s values before it is physically
122   printed for use, and they are easily disrupted and filtered during data preprocessing. An L0 -norm
123
      bounded trigger, given its stable shape, consistent values, and compact size, are easier to apply.
      However, current works with optimized L0 -norm bounded triggers (Zhang et al., 2024; Fang &
124
      Chen, 2023) are still limited by fixing triggers’ shape and placement, reducing their potential for
125
      more effective FL attacks. DPOT optimizes a trigger with an L0 -norm constraint, while allowing
126   flexibility in its shape, placement, and value.

128   2.2   D EFENSES AGAINST BACKDOOR ATTACKS IN FL
129
      In this work, we focus on defenses that adhere to the privacy-preserving principles of FL originally
130
      introduced by McMahan et al. (2017): clients’ private data are kept local, and their model updates
131   are not shared with any entities other than the server. For a discussion on additional defenses with
132   varying privacy-preserving properties, please refer to the Appendix B.3.
133
134
      In existing defenses, the server and clients are the two subjects commonly considered for imple-
      menting defense strategies. For benign clients as the defense subject, the global model of each
135
      round is the input they receive from the FL system. Zhang et al. (2023) proposed using trigger in-
136
      version on the global model and adversarial training on local models to mitigate the impact of the
137   backdoor trigger. However, its effectiveness against continually evolving optimized triggers remains
138   unaddressed. For server as the defense subject, clients’ model updates are the input that the server
139   receives from the FL system. Numerous studies proposed to defend against backdoor attacks by
140   analyzing clients’ model updates, which can be further classified into the two categories below.
141
      Excluding model updates with outlier values (in certain features). Some existing works presume
142
      that a malicious client’s model updates will exhibit significant differences from those of benign
143   clients in values or certain features extracted from values. Nguyen et al. (2022) and Fung et al.
144   (2020) exclude a client’s model updates that have outlier cosine similarity in values to other clients’
145   model updates. Sharma et al. (2023) and Ozdayi et al. (2021) reduce or penalize the contribution of
146   model updates that show a certain degree of sign dissimilarity, either on a client-wise or element-
147   wise basis. Kumari et al. (2023) and Fereidooni et al. (2024) assess the probabilistic distribution
148   and frequency transformation of clients’ model updates, and eliminate outliers in these features.
149   Mozaffari et al. (2023) create a sparse space of model updates for clients to vote, and the server
150   rejects outlier votes and aggregates the rest.
151   Byzantine-robust aggregation. Some existing works propose aggregating only the most trustwor-
152   thy model updates to tolerate the presence of malicious clients. Yin et al. (2018) aggregate reliable
153   model updates element-wise by taking median or trimmed mean, while Blanchard et al. (2017), Cao
154   et al. (2022), and Pillutla et al. (2022) select and aggregate reliable model updates client-wise.
155   Analyzing clients’ model updates can effectively defend against backdoor attacks that cause dis-
156   tinctions between malicious clients’ and benign clients’ model updates. However, when a backdoor
157   attack can conceal malicious clients’ model updates among benign ones, defenses based on this strat-
158   egy will struggle (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020). In this work, we show that this goal can be achieved
159   by dynamically changing the backdoor objectives defined on poison data, so that malicious clients’
160   model updates are effectively manipulated.

182   Attacker’s capability and background knowledge: As shown in Figure 1, we assume that each
183   FL client—even a malicious one—is equipped with trustworthy training software that conducts cor-
184   rect model training on the client’s local training data and transmits the model updates to the FL
185   server. Aligning with the security settings in the state-of-the-art defense work (Riege et al., 2024),
186   we assume that both the client training pipeline and the FL server, as well as the communication be-
187   tween them, faithfully serve FL’s main task training and cannot be undetectably manipulated. These
188
      properties would be achievable by executing FL training within Trusted Execution Environments
      (TEEs) (Schneider et al., 2022; Riege et al., 2024), for example, by applying cryptographic protec-
189
      tions to the updates (e.g., a digital signatures) to enable the FL server to authenticate the updates as
190
      coming from the TEEs.
191
192   The capability of malicious clients in our attack is limited to the manipulation of their local training
193   data that are input to their training pipelines. In addition, in line with existing works (Lyu et al.,
194
      2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Fang & Chen, 2023; Gong et al., 2022), we do not assume the secrecy of
      the global model provided by the FL server, as it would typically need to be accessible outside TEEs
195
      for use in local inference tasks. As such, in each FL round, clients are granted white-box access
196
      to the global model. Originating from initially benign clients that have been compromised, these
197   malicious clients possess some local training data for the FL main task as background knowledge.
198
199   Attacker’s goals: The malicious clients aim to accomplish the following goals.
200
201   • Effectiveness. For classification tasks, Attack Success Rate (ASR) is the accuracy of a model in
202     classifying data embedded with a backdoor trigger into a target label. The DPOT attack aims to
203     make the post-aggregation global model misclassify data embedded with a trigger, optimized for
204
        the clients’ most recently received global model, into a target label. Our effectiveness goal is for
        the global model to achieve an ASR of over 50% in the final round and even maintain an average
205
        ASR of over 50% across all rounds.
206
      • Stealthiness. The stealthiness goal of a backdoor attack is to maintain the Main-task Accuracy
207     (MA) of the global model at a normal level, ensuring the functionality of the global model on its
208     main-task data.

235   Formulating the optimization problem. We generate a new backdoor trigger for each round’s
236   global model, with the optimization process operating independently across FL rounds. In this part,
237   we introduce the trigger optimization algorithms within a single round of FL.
238

239
      an image as the parameter space. Our approach aims to find a subset of parameters that have the
240   most significant impact in producing the malicious output result (i.e., target label), and subsequently
241   optimize the values of the parameters in this subset for the malicious objective (i.e., a high ASR).
242   In the end, the pixels in this subset with their optimized values will serve as a backdoor trigger.
243   This trigger will increase the likelihood that an image containing it will yield the malicious output
244   when employing the same model Wg for inference. The optimization objective to resolve the above
245   problem can be written as formula (1).

290
      The last step of our attack is to poison malicious clients’ local training data using the optimized
291
      trigger τ = (Et , Vt ) and its target label yt by a certain data poison rate. The data poison rate can
292
      be specified on a scale from 0 to 1, while smaller data poison rate induces stealthier model updates,
293   making them more difficult for defenses to detect and filter. In the following, we set the data poison

      Comparisons: As DPOT is exclusively a data-poisoning attack, we compared it with existing

305   three existing attacks as described below.
306
307   • Fixed Trigger (FT). Following recent research on backdoor attacks on FL (Baruch et al., 2019;
308
        Xie et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2021; Bagdasaryan et al., 2020), we used a global pixel-pattern trigger
        with fixed features (values, shape, and placement) for all experiments in this attack category.
309
      • Distributed Fixed Trigger (DFT). DBA (Xie et al., 2020) proposed to slice a global pixel-pattern
310
        trigger into several parts and distributes them to malicious FL clients for data poisoning. The
311     Attack Success Rate measures the effectiveness of this global trigger on the global model.
312   • A3FL Trigger A3FL (Zhang et al., 2024) proposed to adversarially optimize a trigger’s value
313     using a local model that continuously unlearns the optimized trigger information. The shape and
314     placement of the A3FL trigger are fixed during optimization. Comparison results between DPOT
315     and A3FL attacks are shown in Appendix E.1.
316
      The visualization of various trigger types on different datasets are demonstrated in Appendix F.
317

319   fense strategies against backdoor attacks. We selected defenses that have open-sourced their proof-
320   of-concept code to ensure accurate implementation of their proposed ideas. Here we present the
321   evaluation results against ten defense strategies that rely solely on server-side execution; these de-
322   fenses are described in Appendix D.1. In addition, evaluation results of defenses requiring client-
323   side execution, Flip (Zhang et al., 2023) and FRL (Mozaffari et al., 2023), are given in Appendix E.2
      and Appendix E.3 due to space limitations.

      Evaluation metrics: We considered three metrics to evaluate the effectiveness and stealthiness of
325   backdoor attacks when confronted with different defense strategies. To evaluate the effectiveness of
326   the DPOT attack, we tested the post-aggregation global model using poisoned data with a trigger
327   optimized for the clients’ most recently received global model.
328
      • Final Attack Success Rate (Final ASR). This metric quantifies the proportion of backdoored
329     test images that were misclassified as the target label by the global model at the end of training.
330     In order to reduce the testing error caused by noise on data or model so as to maintain the fairness
331     of comparison, we tested ASR on the global models of the last five rounds and took their mean
332     value as the Final ASR.
333   • Average Attack Success Rate (Avg ASR). Since the attack cycle of DPOT spans just a single
334     round, we introduced Avg ASR to assess the average attack effectiveness across all rounds during
335     the FL process. The implication of a high Avg ASR of an attack is that this attack had consistently
336     significant effectiveness during the whole FL process, ensuring a high Final ASR no matter when
337
        the FL process ended.
      • Main-task Accuracy (MA). We evaluate this metric by testing the accuracy of a global model on
338
        its clean main-task test dataset. A backdoor attack is seen to be stealthy if its victim model does
339
        not show a noticeable reduction in MA compared to the benign model.
340
341   FL training configurations: Please see details of FL training settings in Appendix D.2.
342
      Attack configurations: Table 5 shows the default settings of DPOT attack for experiments.
343
344   • Trigger size. The number of pixels that a backdoor trigger can alter is specified by the trigger size
345     attribute. Selection of trigger sizes for various datasets are discussed in Appendix D.3.
346   • Round. We determine the number of training rounds for each dataset by measuring the conver-
347
        gence time on an FL pipeline using FedAvg as the aggregation rule. Convergence is considered
        achieved when the test accuracy on the main task stabilizes within a range of 0.5 percentage points
348
        over a period of five consecutive rounds of training.
349
      • Number of clients. The number of clients varies across different datasets due to a balance between
350     our available computational resources and the size of the datasets/models. All clients participate
351     in the aggregation for each round of FL training.
352   • MCR. Malicious Client Ratio (MCR) is a parameter defining the proportion of compromised
353     clients compared to the total number of clients in each-round aggregation. We consider 5% as the
354     default MCR (for FL systems having 50 clients, 2 of them are malicious clients).
355   • Local data poison rate. It indicates the proportion of data manipulated by a backdoor attack
356     relative to the total data available on each malicious client.

400   5.2.2       A NALYSIS OF THE DPOT WORKING PRINCIPLES
401   In this section, we analyzed the attack effectiveness of each component of the DPOT attack’s work-
402   ing principles and report evidence that it effectively conceals malicious clients’ model updates,
403   thereby getting them integrated into the global models through aggregation.
404
405
      In the i-th round, DPOT generates a trigger τ (i) by optimizing its shape, placement and values to
406   make the global model of this round Wg (i) achieve a maximum ASR. However, what we were truly
407
      interested in is its ASR on the global model after the i-th round aggregation, which is the next-round
408   global model denoted as Wg (i+1) . The attack effectiveness of the trigger τ (i) on the global model
409   Wg (i+1) stems from two factors:
410   1. Trigger Optimization: Trigger optimization using Wg (i) results in an improvement of the trig-
411
         ger’s ASR on Wg (i+1) due to the small difference between Wg (i+1) and Wg (i) .
412
413
      2. Aggregation of Backdoored Model Updates: Model updates that were trained on data partially
414
         poisoned by τ (i) exhibit small differences from those were trained on data without poisoning.
415
         Therefore, they bypassed defenses and made Wg (i+1) incorporate backdoored model parameters.
416

417   analyzed the experiment results.

517   5.2.4       I MPACT OF T RIGGER S IZE
518
      Trigger Size, determining how many pixels in an image we can alter, is an important parameter for
519   DPOT attack. Larger trigger size generally results in a better optimization performance. However, it
520   is essential to strike a balance because an excessively large trigger size will make a trigger obscure
521   important details of images, making the trigger easier to perceive by humans. In this section, we
522   assessed the impact of different trigger sizes on the performance of different attacks. We explored
523   trigger sizes across four different settings (9, 25, 49, and 100) while maintaining other settings in
524   accordance with those outlined in Table 5.
525   Tables 3 shows that DPOT maintained a significant advantage in ASR over FT and DFT across
526   various trigger sizes, ranging from small to large. According to the results, we found that FT and
527   DFT did not benefit from larger trigger sizes in achieving higher ASR when encountering with ro-
528   bust aggregations that have advanced defense effectiveness, such as FLAIR and FLAME. A possible
529   explanation on that is when malicious model updates were trained on data poisoned with larger FT
530   or DFT triggers, they exhibited greater divergence from benign model updates, making them more
531   susceptible to detection and filtering by defense mechanisms. In contrast, DPOT demonstrated a
532   continuous improvement in ASR as the trigger size increased. Table 10 presents the Main-task
533
      Accuracy results for each experiment considered in this section. Results in it indicate all backdoor
      attacks achieved their stealthiness goals during attacking.

536
537   In this work, we proposed DPOT, a novel backdoor attack method relying solely on data poisoning
538   in federated learning (FL). DPOT dynamically adjusts the backdoor objective to conceal malicious
539   clients’ model updates among benign ones, enabling global models to aggregate them even when
      protected by state-of-the-art defenses.

707   B.1     F EDERATED L EARNING (FL)
708
      The Federated Learning (McMahan et al., 2017) (FL) training process involves four main steps:
709
      1) Model Distribution: A central server distributes the most recent global model to the participating
710   clients. 2) Local Training: Each client independently trains the global model on its local training
711   dataset and obtains a local model. 3) Model Updates: Each client calculates the parameter-wise
712   difference between its local model and the global model, referred to as model updates, and then
713   sends them to the central server. 4) Aggregation: The central server aggregates clients’ model
714   updates to create a new global model. This entire process, consisting of step 1 to 4, constitutes a
715   global round. The FL system repeats these steps for a certain number of rounds to obtain a final
716   version of the global model.

732
733   FL is easily suffered from backdoor attacks. As training data are privately held by clients, the
734   security of data is hard to track or protect. Adversaries can inject backdoors into the global model
735   simply by compromising a few vulnerable client devices and poisoning their data with backdoor
736   triggers. To date, many variations of backdoor attacks targeting FL have emerged, and we summarize
737   those specific to image classification tasks in Figure 4.
738   With model poisoning v.s. Without model poisoning
739
740   The foundation of backdoor attacks in FL is through data poisoning - attackers embed backdoor
741
      triggers into the local training data of certain clients and change the ground-truth labels of the in-
      fected data to malicious labels. As a result, clients’ local models trained on the poisoned data will
742
      be backdoored, and consequently, the global model that aggregates these backdoored models will
743
      also be backdoored.
744
745   A standalone data poisoning is found challenging to succeed when employing some types of triggers.
746   Therefore, many works introduce model poisoning to assist backdoor attacks in FL. Model poison-
747
      ing aims to either directly manipulate clients’ model updates or indirectly achieve this by changing
      their local training algorithms. Three main approaches in model poisoning were widely adopted in
748
      existing attacks: 1) Scaling based (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020; Gong
749
      et al., 2022). Attackers amplify malicious model updates generated from backdoored models before
750   clients send them to the server. These malicious updates can overpower the aggregation results,
751   causing the global model to quickly incorporate backdoors. However, this approach is vulnerable to
752   defenses that exclude outlier model updates from the aggregation. 2) Constraint based (Bagdasaryan
753   et al., 2020; Lyu et al., 2023). Attackers change clients’ local training algorithms by adding extra
754   constraints to their loss functions, giving backdoored models specific characteristics, such as being
755   less distinguishable from benign models. 3) Projection based (Zhang et al., 2022; Baruch et al.,
      2019; Wang et al., 2020; Fang & Chen, 2023). Attackers constrain backdoor implementation to


                                                          14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      bounded model parameters: by clipping parameter values or using Projected Gradient Descent,
757   backdoor models are L2 -norm bounded to a chosen model state; by selectively updating a subset of
758   parameters, they are L0 -norm bounded to a chosen state.
759
760
      Model poisoning requires attackers to modify certain clients’ local training procedures. However,
      with the introduction of Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) by state-of-the-art defense mech-
761
      anisms (Riege et al., 2024), client-side execution for training can be authenticated and secure, thus
762
      increasing the difficulty of conducting model poisoning. In contrast, data poisoning is easier to
763   conduct and harder to prevent since clients may collect their local data from open resources where
764   attackers can also get access to and make modifications.
765
766
      Static objective v.s. Dynamic objective
767   If a backdoor attack has a specified and unchanging objective that is independent to the training sys-
768   tem’s status, we refer to this as a static objective. For instance, Semantic trigger as backdoor (Bag-
769   dasaryan et al., 2020) aims to associate certain features from input that is unrelated to the main
770   training tasks with an attacker-chosen output, causing the model to make incorrect predictions on
771   those inputs; Edge-case backdoor (Wang et al., 2020) selects data that share certain commonalities
772
      but are from the tail end of the input data distribution as the backdoored input, causing the model to
      mispredict them; Artificial trigger as backdoor (Sun et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Baruch et al.,
773
      2019; Xie et al., 2020) embeds a few pixels forming a specific artificial pattern into the input, leading
774
      the model to mispredict any input containing this pixel pattern. In FL, since the static objectives of
775   backdoor attacks are inconsistent with the optimization objectives defined by the main-task data, ma-
776   licious models will exhibit distinct differences in their model updates compared to benign models,
777   making them easy to detect.
778
      In contrast to a static objective, a backdoor attack that adjusts its objective based on the training
779
      system’s status is referred to as having a dynamic objective. By adjusting its objective, a backdoor
780
      attack is expected to achieve greater effectiveness. Several approaches have been proposed in recent
781   attack studies to attempt to accomplish this. For example, Model-dependent attack (Gong et al.,
782   2022) and F3BA (Fang & Chen, 2023) optimized the trigger pattern based on a hypothesis that
783   maximizing the activation of certain neurons in the backdoored local model can enhance the attack’s
784   persistence on the global model, which is however lack of theoretical evidence and proof-of-concept
785   codes; A3FL (Zhang et al., 2024) optimized triggers specifically for a corner case in FL training,
786   where the global model is directly trained to unlearn the trigger, but the effectiveness of A3FL
787   triggers in more general FL training scenarios remains unaddressed.
788   L2 -norm bounded optimized trigger v.s. L0 -norm bounded optimized trigger
789
790
      A critical consideration in designing backdoor triggers is ensuring their stealthiness when applied
      to input data, resulting in a substantial disparity between human perception and the backdoored
791
      model’s interpretation. Existing dynamic objective attacks achieve this by constraining the opti-
792
      mized triggers’ L2 -norm or L0 -norm bounds.
793
794   An L2 -norm bound on a trigger or perturbation means that the total magnitude of the changes intro-
795   duced by the backdoor is limited. This makes the perturbation subtle, ensuring it doesn’t drastically
796
      alter the input data. For example, CerP (Lyu et al., 2023) generates optimized perturbations of the
      same size as a data point for each round and adds them to clients’ local data to induce their local
797
      models learn to misclassify the perturbed data to a specified target label.
798
799   An L0 -norm bound restricts the number of components (e.g., pixels in an image) that can be altered
800   by the trigger. This constraint ensures that the trigger is sparse, meaning it only affects a small
801   portion of an input data. For example, optimized triggers in Model-dependent attack (Gong et al.,
802
      2022), F3BA (Fang & Chen, 2023), and A3FL (Zhang et al., 2024) all consist of a small number of
      pixels arranged in a square shape and are placed in a fixed corner location on the data to poison.
803
804   An L2 -norm bounded trigger is less practical for real-world data poisoning because it spreads
805   changes across many pixels, requiring the attacker to access and alter a figure’s values before it
806   is physically printed for use. Additionally, these small perturbations are easily disrupted by data
807   preprocessing techniques that filter out unnecessary noise. In contrast, an L0 -norm bounded trigger
808
      is easier to apply to data (e.g., a sticker on an image) due to its stable shape, consistent values, and
      compact size. However, existing works in optimizing L0 -norm bounded triggers are limited by fix-
809



                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      ing their shapes and placements and only updating triggers’ values, which fails to fully leverage the
811   potential of optimized triggers for attacking FL.
812
813   Clean-label attacks
814   Clean-label attacks (Shafahi et al., 2018) involve manipulating input data with subtle perturbations
815   while keeping labels unchanged. Although this assumption aligns with scenarios like Vertical Fed-
816   erated Learning (Liu et al., 2024) (VFL), where participants possess vertically partitioned data with
817   labels owned by only one participant, our study does not consider VFL as our attack scenario. Fur-
818   thermore, we focus on examining the effects of different backdoor triggers on hiding malicious
819   model updates rather than their imperceptible characteristics. Therefore, discussions of clean-label
820
      attacks are beyond the scope of our work.
821   B.3   D EFENSES WITH DIFFERENT PRIVACY- PRESERVING PROPERTIES
822
823   Recent defense works have introduced several unconventional FL pipelines aimed at enhancing the
824   security of FL against various types of attacks. These novel architectures provide different levels of
825
      privacy protection and often require additional techniques (e.g., Secured Multi-party Computation)
      to ensure privacy for FL clients. In light of these privacy considerations, we have chosen to focus our
826
      analysis on the conventional FL structure that was originally proposed in the concept of Federated
827
      Learning (McMahan et al., 2017). Although defenses built on newly proposed FL structures fall
828   outside the scope of our main comparison, we offer a discussion of these related works in this
829   section.
830
831   Clients’ private data were shared to the server: Some approaches allow the server to have access
832   to a small portion of main-task data shared by clients. To mitigate backdoor attacks, server-side de-
833   fense strategies use this data to either independently train a model and use its updates as a reference
834
      for each round of aggregation (e.g., FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021)), or to validate clients’ model updates
      and eliminate those with abnormal outputs (e.g., SSDT (Mo et al., 2024), SHERPA (Sandeepa et al.,
835
      2024)). However, both of these methods still rely on analyzing clients’ model updates, making them
836
      vulnerable to backdoor attacks with dynamic objectives that conceal malicious updates. FedREde-
837   fense (Xie et al., 2024) detects and filters out artificial model updates by reconstructing distilled data
838   shared by clients, but this approach is not effective against backdoor attacks where malicious clients
839   genuinely train their models on poisoned local data rather than fabricating artificial updates.
840
841   Clients’ model updates were shared to each other: Some approaches propose allowing clients
842   to share their model updates with one another, rather than just with the server. CrowdGuard (Riege
843   et al., 2024) and FLShield (Kabir et al., 2024) suggest that a subset of clients validate other clients’
844
      model updates using their own data, assuming that malicious clients’ updates would produce ab-
      normal outputs on benign data. However, this hypothesis fails when malicious clients’ updates are
845
      indistinguishable from non-backdoored updates, a state that can be achieved through backdoor at-
846
      tack with optimized triggers. Fang et al. (2024) proposed a decentralized FL framework without a
847   central server, where clients exchange model updates and apply Byzantine-robust aggregation using
848   their own updates as a reference. Like other defenses that rely on analyzing clients’ model updates,
849   this approach is also vulnerable to backdoor attacks with optimized triggers.

1047   D.1    D ESCRIPTIONS OF D EFENSES
1048   We implement our attack on FL systems integrated with 9 different defense strategies and provide a
1049   brief introduction for each of them:
1050
1051
       FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), a basic aggregation rule in FL, computes global model updates
       by averaging all clients’ model updates. Despite its effectiveness on the main task, it is not robust
1052
       enough to defend against backdoor attacks in the FL system.
1053
1054   Median (Yin et al., 2018), a simple but robust alternative to FedAvg, constructs the global model
1055   updates by taking the median of the values of model updates across all clients
1056   Trimmed Mean (Yin et al., 2018), in our implementation, excludes the 40% largest and 40% small-
1057   est values of each parameter among all clients’ model updates and takes the mean of the remaining
1058   20% as the global model updates.
1059
       Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017) identifys an honest client whose model updates have the
1060
       smallest Euclidean distance to all other clients’ model updates and takes this honest client’s model
1061   updates as the global model updates. Despite its robustness to prevent the FL system from being
1062   compromised by a minor number of adversaries, Multi-Krum is not able to ensure the convergence
1063   performance of the FL system on its main task when the data distribution of clients is highly non-IID.
1064
       RobustLR (Ozdayi et al., 2021) adjusts the aggregation server’s learning rate, per dimension and
1065
       per round, based on the sign information of clients’ updates.
1066
1067   RFA (Pillutla et al., 2022) computes a geometric median of clients’ model updates and assigns
1068   weight factors to clients depending on their distance from the geometric median. Subsequently, it
1069   computes the weighted average of all clients’ model updates to generate the global model updates.
1070   FLAIR (Sharma et al., 2023) assigns different weight factors to clients according to the similarity
1071   of the coefficient signs between client model updates and global model updates of the previous
1072   round, and then takes the weighted average of all clients’ model updates to form the global model
1073   updates. FLAIR requires the knowledge of exact number of malicious clients existing in the FL
1074   system.
1075   FLCert (Cao et al., 2022) randomly clusters clients, calculates the median of model updates within
1076   each cluster, incorporates them into the previous round’s global model, and derives the majority
1077   inference outcome from these cluster-updated global models as the final inference result for the
1078   entire FL system. In our implementation, we cluster clients into 5 groups, use FLCert inference
1079   outcome for testing the Attack Success Rate, and employ Median as the aggregation rule for updating
       the global model in each round.


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       FLAME (Nguyen et al., 2022) first clusters clients’ model updates according to their cosine simi-
1081   larity to each other, and then aggregates the clipped model updates within the largest cluster as the
1082   global model updates.
1083
1084
       FoolsGold (Fung et al., 2020) reduces aggregation weights of a set of clients whose model updates
       constantly exhibit high cosine similarity to each other.

1086   D.2      FL TRAINING CONFIGURATIONS
1087
1088   The FEMNIST dataset (Caldas et al., 2019) provides each client’s local training data with a naturally
1089
       non-IID guarantee. For Fashion MNIST, CIFAR10, and Tiny ImageNet datasets, we distributed
       training data to FL clients using the same method introduced by FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021), where
1090
       we set the non-IID bias to be 0.5.
1091
1092   For all datasets training experiments, we used SGD optimization with CrossEntropy loss. In the
1093   experiments on Tiny ImageNet, we set the mini-batch size to 64, while for the other datasets, we set
1094   it to 256. Each FL client trained a global model for nepoch = 5 local epochs with its local data in
1095
       one global round.
1096   For training Fashion MNIST and FEMNIST datasets, we used a static local learning rate (lr ) of
1097   0.01. For training the larger and more complicated datasets such as CIFAR10 and Tiny ImageNet,
1098   we applied the learning rate schedule technique following the instructions in the related machine
1099   learning works (He et al., 2016; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) to boost DNN models’ performance.
1100
       D.3      S ELECTION OF TRIGGER SIZES
1101
1102   We defined trigger size for different datasets according to the following three criteria. First, a trigger
1103   of the defined trigger size should not be able to cover important details of any images and lead
1104   humans to misidentify the images from their original labels. To show that we follow this criteria,
1105   we demonstrated poisoned images from different datasets that are embedded with DPOT triggers, as
1106
       shown in Figure 2 and 11. Second, on basis of the first criterion, we adjust trigger size to match the
       image size and the feature size of different datasets. Specifically, if a dataset contains images with
1107
       high resolution (large image size), then a large trigger size is needed to effectively match it (Tiny
1108
       ImageNet vs. CIFAR10). If images in a dataset contain large visual elements or patterns, then a large
1109   trigger size is needed to effectively match it (Fashion MNIST vs. FEMNIST). Third, we found that
1110   when using models with deep model architectures or having large number of parameters, a small
1111   trigger size is sufficient for conducting DPOT attack (CIFAR10 vs. Fashion MNIST).

1145   E.2   E VALUATION OF DPOT ATTACK AGAINST F LIP (Z HANG ET AL ., 2023)
1146   Flip (Zhang et al., 2023) is a client-side defense strategy where benign clients perform trigger inver-
1147   sion and adversarial training using their local data to recover the global model from backdoors. In
1148   this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of the DPOT attack against the Flip defense. We imple-
1149   mented the DPOT attack by modifying the data preparation approach in Flip’s open-source project,
1150   replacing it with the method used in this work, and injecting our data-poisoning algorithms into a
1151   subset of clients. Additionally, as DPOT is a pure data-poisoning attack, we removed any additional
1152   steps in their project specified to malicious clients but not existed in benign clients’ training, to en-
1153   sure consistency between malicious clients and benign clients in FL training. We selected Fashion
1154
       MNIST as the main-task dataset for our evaluation and directly adopted Flip’s default experiment
       settings provided in their project - the total number of clients was 100 and 4% of them were mali-
1155
       cious clients; the aggregation rule was set to FedAvg; the global model’s parameters were initialized
1156
       by a pre-trained state. The size of DPOT trigger was set to 64, consistent with our default attacking
1157   settings.
1158
1159   We compared the performance of the DPOT attack under two attack patterns provided by Flip’s
1160
       project: 1) Single shot: Each of the 4 malicious clients conducts a one-time attack at the beginning
       of training. 2) Continuous: All 4 malicious clients continuously execute the attack algorithms in
1161
       every round during training.
1162
1163   Figure 5 shows the performance of the DPOT attack on an FL system using Flip as its defense,
1164   measured by the Attack Success Rate (ASR). In the single-shot attack pattern, DPOT maintains a
1165   stable ASR of around 15% across all training rounds, exceeding the random guess accuracy of 10%
1166
       for the 10-class dataset. In the continuous attack pattern, DPOT achieves a significant ASR, peaking
       at 80.03% during training and stabilizing around 40%, which is higher than the single-shot pattern.
1167
       These results indicate that Flip is vulnerable to optimized triggers with varying appearances across
1168
       different rounds, because recovering from backdoors is an after-effect strategy which is unable to
1169   stop new and distinct backdoors from injecting into the model.
1170
1171   Figure 6 illustrates the global model’s performance on the main task data when using Flip as a
1172
       defense while under DPOT attack. We observed that employing Flip reduces the global model’s
       main-task performance compared to not using it. In our baseline experiment on Fashion MNIST,
1173
       with the same data distribution and aggregation rule (FedAvg), the model achieved an 86.7% MA.
1174
       However, Flip’s global model achieved only 82.8% MA at its best by the end, even with pre-trained
1175   model initialization. Additionally, under continuous attack by the DPOT trigger, the global model’s
1176   MA further declined compared to the less frequent attack pattern. This raises concerns about Flip’s
1177   ability to maintain stable and normal performance on the main task while effectively defending
1178   against attacks.

1420   F.4            T RIGGER EVOLUTION DURING TRAINING
1421
       In Figure 15 and Figure 16, we demonstrated how DPOT trigger changes during the FL training.
1422
1423   In Figure 15, we showed one screenshot of the trigger on a blank background in the same size of
1424   the cifar10’s figure for every ten global rounds. These trigger screenshots were collected during a
1425
       DPOT attacking experiment that trains ResNet18 as the global model on the CIFAR-10 dataset, with
       Trimmed Mean used as the aggregation rule. Figure 13 displays the Main-task Accuracy and Attack
1426
       Success Rate of the global model over 150 global rounds in this experiment.
1427
1428   Similarly, in Figure 16 we showed one screenshot of the trigger on a blank background in the same
1429   size of the Tiny ImageNet’s figure for every ten global rounds. These trigger screenshots were col-
1430   lected during a DPOT attacking experiment that trains VGG11 as the global model on the Tiny Im-
1431
       ageNet dataset, with Trimmed Mean used as the aggregation rule. Figure 14 displays the Main-task
       Accuracy and Attack Success Rate of the global model over 100 global rounds in this experiment.
1432
1433   According to Figure 15 and Figure 16, the DPOT trigger does not change drastically over rounds;
1434   instead, it develops gradually and coherently. Since the DPOT trigger is optimized based on the
1435   global model’s parameters, and the global model is in turn influenced by malicious model updates
1436   backdoored by the DPOT trigger, the DPOT trigger and the global model form a Markov chain.
1437
       During training, as the global model evolves coherently and gradually, the states of the DPOT trigger
       evolve as well in the same pattern.
