009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit graph neural networks (IGNNs), which exhibit strong expressive power
012            with a single layer, have recently demonstrated remarkable performance in cap-
013            turing long-range dependencies (LRD) in underlying graphs while effectively
014            mitigating the over-smoothing problem. However, IGNNs rely on computationally
015            expensive fixed-point iterations, which lead to significant speed and scalability
016
               limitations, hindering their application to large-scale graphs. To achieve fast fixed-
               point solving for IGNNs, we propose a novel graph neural solver, IGNN-Solver,
017
               which leverages the generalized Anderson Acceleration method, parameterized by
018
               a small GNN, and learns iterative updates as a graph-dependent temporal process.
019            Extensive experiments demonstrate that the IGNN-Solver significantly accelerates
020            inference, achieving a 1.5× to 8× speedup without sacrificing accuracy. More-
021            over, this advantage becomes increasingly pronounced as the graph scale grows,
022            facilitating its large-scale deployment in real-world applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Implicit graph neural networks (IGNNs) [20; 33; 7] have emerged as a significant advancement in
      graph learning frameworks. Unlike traditional graph neural networks (GNNs) that stack multiple
028
      explicit layers, IGNNs utilize a single implicit layer formulated as a fixed-point equation. The solution
029
      to this fixed-point equation, known as the equilibrium, is equivalent to the output obtained by iterating
030   an explicit layer infinitely. This allows an implicit layer to access infinite hops of neighbors, providing
031   IGNNs with global receptive fields within just one layer [12]. As a result, IGNNs effectively address
032   the long-standing issue of over-smoothing in conventional explicit GNNs and capture of long-range
033   dependencies in graph-structured data [30; 1; 56].
034
      However, existing IGNNs suffer from slow speeds and have difficulty scaling to large-scale graphs [34;
035
      18; 52; 39]. This is primarily because IGNNs derive features by solving for fixed points, demanding
036   substantial computational resources. For example, even on the Citeseer dataset [26] classification
037   task with a small-scale graph, IGNNs require more than 20 forward iterative computations to nearly
038   converge to a fixed point [20]. The computational overhead of solving fixed points is amplified by
039   the task scale, resulting in notably slow inference speeds compared to explicit GNNs. This substantial
040   drawback poses challenges for IGNNs in generalizing to large-scale graphs in practical scenarios.
041
      In response to this challenge, we propose a novel graph neural solver for IGNNs, termed IGNN-Solver.
042   It takes the IGNN layer as input, which includes the graph information matrix and layer parameters,
043   and outputs the solution to the corresponding fixed-point equation. Unlike conventional solvers
044   relying on root-finding algorithms like Broyden’s method [2], which compute output features through
045   iterative forward passes in a potentially large GNN, IGNN-Solver offers a distinct advantage by
046   predicting the next iteration step via a tiny graph network. The proposed IGNN-Solver remarkably
047   accelerates the model’s inference speed without compromising accuracy and with only a slight
048   increase in training overhead. This advantage becomes increasingly pronounced as the scale of the
049   graph grows, making it particularly beneficial for deploying IGNNs in large-scale graph tasks.
050   Our IGNN-Solver comprises two components. First, we introduce a learnable initializer that estimates
051   an optimal initial point for the optimization process. Second, we propose a generalized version of
052   Anderson Acceleration (AA) [2], employing a tiny graph network to model the iterative updates as
053   a sequence of graph-dependent steps. Compared to the solvers proposed for conventional Implicit
      Neural Networks (INNs) [4; 5; 6], we introduce novel improvements: learning solver parameters

054
      through a GNN-based method. This approach circumvents the potential loss of graph information,
055   thereby improving the model’s performance. Moreover, IGNN-Solver has significantly fewer parame-
056   ters compared to IGNN, and its training is independent of the IGNN’s inference. Consequently, the
057   training of IGNN-Solver proceeds rapidly without sacrificing generalization.
058
      In our experiments, we apply IGNN-Solver to 9 real-world datasets from diverse domains and
059
      scales, including 4 large-scale datasets: Amazon-all [51], Reddit [21], ogbn-arxiv [22] and ogbn-
060
      products [22]. Our results demonstrate that the IGNN-Solver achieves higher accuracy with reduced

062   constituting only 1% of the IGNN training time.

079   2    R ELATED W ORK
080
081   The typical GNN [26] and its variants [42; 21] have been widely used for graph data modeling in
082   various tasks. Different GNNs have been proposed to utilize attention mechanism [42], neighbors
083   sampling [21], pseudo-coordinates [38] and graph fusion [45]. However, due to issues such as over-
084
      smoothing, depth, and bottlenecks, these models typically involve finite aggregation layers [30; 1; 56].
      To address it, recent works [20; 33; 7] have developed Implicit Graph Neural Networks, encouraging
085
      these models to capture long-range dependencies on graphs. Here, we highlight the contributions
086
      of our proposed IGNN-Solver through a detailed comparison with Implicit Graph Neural Networks
087   (IGNNs) and Deep Equilibrium Models (DEQs), both of which are closely related to our approach.
088
089
      Implicit Graph Neural Networks. Instead of stacking a series of operators hierarchically, implicit
090   GNNs define their outputs as solutions to nonlinear dynamical systems, which is initially introduced
091   by [20] to tackle challenges associated with learning long-range dependencies in graphs. [34]
092   proposes a new implicit graph model enabling mini-batch training without sacrificing the ability to
093   capture long-range dependencies. Subsequently, [43] introduces a novel approach based on implicit
094   layer to model multi-scale structures on graphs. Additionally, [7] theoretically investigates the
095   well-posedness of the IGNN model from a monotone operator viewpoint.
096   Although the aforementioned IGNN works well by alleviating the problem of over-smoothing of
097   features by allowing meaningful fixed points to propagate implicitly, the inherent slow inference
098   speed of implicit networks poses a major obstacle to its scalability. The main reason is that the solver
099   of the fixed-point network is inefficient (e.g., the Picard solver used by IGNN [20] and Anderson
100   acceleration solver used by MIGNN [7].), makes the overhead of the fixed-point solver magnified
101   by the task scales. In comparison, our proposed IGNN-Solver accelerates the iteration process of
102   IGNNs, which addresses the most limiting drawback compared to traditional feedforward models.
103   Another class of IGNNs based on Neural ODEs [13] has emerged to address issues like depth and
104   bottlenecks. For example, [3] models continuous residual layers using GCNs. [40] proposes methods
105   for modeling static and dynamic graphs with GCNs, along with a hybrid approach where latent states
106   evolve continuously between RNN steps in dynamic graphs. [49] tackles the problem of continuous
107   message passing. [10] introduces a graph neural diffusion network based on the discretization of
      diffusion PDEs on graphs. [41] enhances graph neural diffusion with a source term and connects the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      model to random walk formulation on graphs. However, they essentially view deep learning on graphs
109   as a continuous diffusion process, which differs from IGNNs based on fixed-point networks, thus
110   requiring manually tuning the termination time and step size of the diffusion equation. In contrast,
111   our IGNN-Solver uses implicit formulations instead of explicit diffusion discretization, which admits
112   an equilibrium corresponding to infinite diffusion steps and expands the receptive field.
113
114   Fixed-point Solvers for Deep Equilibrium Models. Traditional deep learning models use multi-
115   layered networks, while DEQs [4] find a fixed-point of a single layer, representing the network’s
116   equilibrium state. Viewed as infinitely deep networks, DEQs use root-finding for forward propagation
117   and implicit differentiation for backward propagation. Therefore, DEQs capture complex patterns
118   with constant memory and computational costs [31]. Monotone operator theory has been used to
119
      guarantee the convergence of DEQs [46] and to improve the stability of implicit networks [6].
120   However, it is well-known that DEQs, as typical implicit models, suffer from slow training and infer-
121   ence speeds [32; 34], which is highly disadvantageous for large-scale scenarios like GraphGPT [58].
122   To alleviate this issue, recent efforts have explored certain improved solver methods for DEQs,
123   further optimizing root-finding problems and making these models easier to solve: [23] discusses the
124   amortization of the cost of the iterative solver that would otherwise make implicit models slow. [18]
125
      proposes a novel gradient estimate for implicit models, named phantom gradient, which significantly
      accelerates the backward passes in training implicit models. [6] discusses the superiority of learnable
126
      solvers over generic solvers in implicit models using a tiny neural network and significantly enhances
127
      the efficiency of such models through custom neural solvers.
128
129   In contrast, IGNN has a similar network representation to DEQ, both defining the output as the
130   solution of the equation to obtain network outputs. But the difference lies in the fact that IGNN’s
131
      equilibrium equations encode graph structure while DEQ does not, which will undoubtedly deepen
      its weakness of slow inference speed and make IGNN’s solution slower. Insight on it, our proposed
132
      IGNN-Solver leverages graph information to guide solver acceleration, achieving fast and meaningful
133
      implicit graph network propagation, especially in the case of graph data being large-scale.

201   4   I MPLICIT GRAPH NEURAL NETWORKS S OLVER
202
203   Although traditional fixed-point solvers for IGNNs (as discussed in Section 1 and 2) demonstrate
204   functionality, they are characterized by relatively slow performance, necessitate manual parameter
205   tuning, and are not well-suited for graph-based applications. To address these limitations, we have
206   designed a lightweight, learnable, and content-aware fixed-point solver for IGNN, which integrates
207   a tiny graph neural network, combining the speed advantages of the solver with the information
208   benefits of graph learning. It encodes the relationship between past residuals and the parameters α[k]
209   and β, allowing for more efficient adjustments of the parameters α and β to improve the learning
210   capabilities of the IGNN-Solver, as illustrated in Figure 1.
211   Due to the characteristics of the implicit model—where its representation capability is independent
212   of the forward computation (i.e., the solver has no knowledge of the original task, such as predicting
213   the node’s class, and vice versa)—, we can train this neural solver in a lightweight and unsupervised
214   manner. After the IGNN-Solver is learned, we can utilize it to solve the fixed-point equation of the
215   IGNN layer, thereby further updating the model. The whole training strategy with IGNN-Solver can
      be found in Appendix B.1 and B.2.

238
      equation Z = fθ (Z, Â, X) = σ(ÂZW + bΩ (X)) has an exact solution at its fixed point. This

242   The overall structure of the IGNN-Solver is shown in Algorithm 1. Specifically, it learns the
243
      parameters α that control the weights among past m steps approximate solution, and β that control

244

245
      function Ltotal by back-propagating through this K-step temporal process, which is discussed in

248   that corresponds to input x. This implies that IGNN-Solver can also be fine-tuned during inference
249   after deployment.

      6    C ONCLUSION
493
494
      This paper introduces IGNN-Solver, a novel graph neural solver tailored for achieving fast fixed-point
      solving in IGNNs. By leveraging the generalized Anderson Acceleration method and parameterizing
495
      it with a tiny GNN, IGNN-Solver learns iterative updates as a graph-dependent temporal process.
496
      Our extensive experiments demonstrate the significant acceleration in inference achieved by IGNN-
497   Solvers, with a speedup ranging from 1.5× to 8×, all while maintaining accuracy. Notably, this
498   acceleration is particularly pronounced as the scale of the graph increases. These findings underscore
499   the potential of IGNN-Solver for large-scale end-to-end deployment in real-world applications.
500
501   Limitations. In contrast to previous works on IGNNs [20; 33; 7], the theoretical guarantee of
502   fixed-point existence and stability in IGNN-Solver remains uncertain, notwithstanding favorable
503   empirical findings in Figure D.2. This is because we make no specific constraint on the formulation
504   of IGNNs, a departure from the approach adopted in previous IGNN-related studies.

719   Given an implicit network layer fθ and an input X, DEQs aim to find the fixed point Z ∗ in the
720   system that represents the equilibrium state of the network output by solving a root-finding problem:
721
                                     gθ (Z ⋆ , X) := fθ (Z ⋆ , X) − Z ⋆ = 0.                             (12)
722
723
724
      In practical solving of DEQs, it is often necessary to utilize fixed-point solvers during the training
      process, such as the classical Broyden method [9] or Anderson Acceleration (AA) [2], which can
725
      directly seek the equilibrium point Z ⋆ through quasi-Newton methods. This approach demonstrates
726
      super-linear convergence properties.
727
728   We briefly introduce AA-solver here, as our approach relies on it. Algorithm 2 is a pseudocode
729   example illustrating it, where G[k] = [gθ (z[k−mk ] ) . . . gθ (z[k] )] is the past residuals. It can be
730   observed that the basic idea of Anderson Acceleration (AA) is to accelerate the generation of the
731
      next approximate solution by utilizing the information stored from the history m iteration steps,
732
      constructing a normalized linear combination with weights α[k] . It computes the weights greedily
      to minimize the linear combination of past AA update steps. This approach is typically particularly
733
      effective for iteratively slow-converging processes, such as solving implicit functions.
734
735   Subsequently, during the backward process, the implicit function theorem [28] can be employed to
736   implicitly differentiate through the equilibrium point and generate gradients with respect to the model
737   parameters θ by solving linear equations based on the Jacobian matrix. Since the computation process
738
      relies only on the final output and does not require storing any intermediate activation values [4], the
      memory consumption during the training process remains constant, which is also a key reason why
739
      DEQs are attractive.

      B.2     A DVANCED IGNN T RAINING S TRATEGY WITH IGNN-S OLVER
769
770   Given the fact that model parameter θ gradually updates only with the model training iterations (i.e.,
771   we assume ∥θt+1 − θt ∥ is only related to θt at training step t), we propose training the lightweight
772   IGNN-Solver sξ , hϕ and the large model fθ in an alternating way. Specifically, we adopt the following
773   procedure:
774
775          (i) Warm up and train the IGNN model and its solver (IGNN-Solver) for some steps.
776         (ii) Fix the IGNN-Solver sξ , hϕ and solving the fixed points of fθ in IGNN via Algorithm 3 for
777              T1 steps.
778
            (iii) Fix the current model parameters θ and start fine-tuning the IGNN-Solver sξ , hϕ via Algo-
779
                  rithm 1 over some T2 steps.
780
781         (iv) Repeat steps (ii) and (iii) until reaching the maximum training steps for the IGNN model.
782
783
      Here T1 is approximately 4% to 10% of T2 , adjusted as needed. The sum of all T2 values is referred
      to as epochmax . It is reassuring that the additional cost of fine-tuning the IGNN-Solver (step (iii))
784
      is sufficiently offset by the substantial benefits of accelerated solving (step (ii)). Moreover, we are
785
      surprised to find that the proposed IGNN-Solver does not exhibit a decline in expressive capability
786   for excessively small or large T1 values. This indicates that, thanks to its robust stability, accidentally
787   setting T1 high does not significantly affect the normal training of the IGNN. Conversely, if T1 is set
788   slightly lower, the model still demonstrates good generalization capabilities. Thus, the IGNN-Solver
789   shows low sensitivity to T1 .
