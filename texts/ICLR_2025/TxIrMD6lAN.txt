009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Incremental learning aims to continuously acquire new knowledge while preserving
012            previously learned information. Existing literature primarily focuses on improving
013            model stability, often at the cost of plasticity, to prevent the forgetting of earlier
014            tasks. In this paper, we argue that inter-task differences are the primary driver of
015            catastrophic forgetting. To address this challenge, we propose a novel network
016            architecture compromising two distinct components: one dedicated to learning
017            invariant features shared across tasks and another for capturing task-specific details.
018
               Specifically, we repurpose adapters, originally introduced for parameter-efficient
               fine-tuning, as feature modifiers to capture task-specific details, while the backbone
019
               network focuses on learning invariant features. Unlike prior approaches that keep
020
               the backbone frozen and only fine-tune adapters, we co-train both the backbone
021            network and adapters, employing an additional regularization term that encourages
022            the backbone to learn shared features. Our approach integrates seamlessly with
023            established methods, such as Learning without Forgetting (LwF). Extensive experi-
024            ments on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet datasets demonstrate that our adapter-based
025            methods consistently outperform non-adapter counterparts across diverse learning
026            scenarios, including various task orders and data scales. Our approach improves
027            both plasticity and stability, effectively addressing the stability-plasticity dilemma.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Real-world data often arrive in a sequential manner, in batches, or through periodic updates. These
033
      data dynamics are further complicated by constraints such as limited storage capacity and privacy
      considerations. In such contexts, employing concurrent or multitask learning, where models are
034
      trained on a single, static, large dataset, can be costly or impractical. Incremental learning (IL), also
035
      known as lifelong learning, is specifically designed to handle these dynamic environments. This
036   paradigm allows a model to continuously learn and update its knowledge from new data without
037   retraining from scratch. Unlike traditional methods that require access to the entire dataset for training,
038   incremental learning enables the model to adapt to new tasks over time using only the data provided
039   for each new task while retaining knowledge from previous tasks.
040
      Feature extraction and fine-tuning are two common approaches for learning new tasks without
041
      accessing previous training data. In feature extraction, the weights of a pre-trained model are kept
042   fixed, and the outputs of the top layer are used as features for the new task (Donahue et al., 2014;
043   Belouadah & Popescu, 2018). While this approach helps maintain existing knowledge, it may struggle
044   to effectively learn new tasks. Fine-tuning improves upon feature extraction by updating the model
045   weights for the new task. A low learning rate is typically used to ensure the model retains the
046   structure and knowledge from previous tasks (Girshick et al., 2014). While this approach enables
047   better adaptation to new tasks, it carries the risk of catastrophic forgetting, where performance on
048   previously learned tasks deteriorates rapidly (Goodfellow et al., 2013; McCloskey & Cohen, 1989).
049   Forgetting occurs when the task-specific weights of the previous tasks are altered to accommodate
050
      new tasks. These methods may not fully address the challenges of incremental learning, which
      requires balancing the preservation of existing knowledge with the acquisition of new skills.
051
052   An ideal incremental learning algorithm should at least possess the following key properties: efficient
053   memory usage and stability-plasticity balance. First, the algorithm should optimize memory usage,
      preserving no or only essential data points from previous tasks. This may reflect realistic scenarios

054
      where data might be transient or unavailable due to privacy or memory constraints. Second, it
055   should maintain a balance between stability (retaining past knowledge) and plasticity (learning
056   new information), avoiding catastrophic forgetting (losing old knowledge when learning new data).
057   Balancing stability and plasticity is a fundamental challenge in incremental learning, often referred to
058   as the stability-plasticity dilemma (Mermillod et al., 2013).
059
      Existing research in incremental learning primarily focuses on mitigating catastrophic forgetting by
060
      improving stability at the expense of plasticity. One popular approach is to directly regularize weight
061   changes (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Aljundi et al., 2018) or to limit divergence in
062   output predictions, between the old and new models (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Dhar et al., 2019). This
063   strategy can be extended by incorporating data retained from previous tasks, which further prevents
064   forgetting by replaying some of the prior knowledge (Chaudhry et al., 2018; Rebuffi et al., 2017;
065   Zhang et al., 2020b). However, enhancing stability often hurts plasticity. Few works simultaneously
066   improve stability and plasticity.
067
068   Contribution In this paper, we argue that the inter-task differences contribute to catastrophic
069   forgetting and propose to model these differences. Specifically, we propose a network design
070   consisting of two blocks: a backbone network for learning invariant features across all tasks and
071   many small networks for modeling task-specific knowledge. Specifically, we use adapters, originally
072   introduced for parameter-efficient fine-tuning of large language models (Houlsby et al., 2019), as these
073   small networks to capture the task-specific information. Our approach differs from the conventional
074
      use of adapters, which are typically added to a frozen network to attain comparable performance
      to full fine-tuning. Instead, we re-purpose adapters as feature modifiers and train them together.
075
      This strategy enables the adapters to encapsulate task-specific information in the layers closer to the
076
      output, while squeezing task-invariant knowledge into layers nearer the input. Our approach can be
077   integrated with many existing methods such as EWC and LwF. Our empirical results demonstrate that
078   various adapter-assisted methods consistently outperform non-adapter counterparts along the learning
079   process, ranging from regularization-based to rehearsal-based approaches, across both CIFAR-100
080   and ImageNet datasets. The advantage remains robust among dataset choices, task scales, and task
081   orderings. Our approach improves both plasticity and stability, eliminating the stability-plasticity
082   dilemma.

      2    R ELATED WORK
086
087   This section reviews works that are closely related to ours.
088
089   Multi-task Learning Multi-task learning involves training on all tasks simultaneously, leveraging
090   shared network parameters to exploit inter-task commonalities, in contrast to incremental learning
091   (Caruana, 1997; Ruder, 2017). However, multi-task learning can be costly or impractical due to
092   the substantial computational burden of training on large datasets, alongside challenges such as
093
      limited storage capacity and privacy concerns (Kendall et al., 2018). These limitations highlight the
      importance of incremental learning as a critical area of research.
094
095
096   Incremental Learning Incremental learning involves learning tasks sequentially. However, this
097   approach is susceptible to catastrophic forgetting, where learning new tasks can overwrite previously
098   acquired knowledge (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). To address this challenge, three primary strategies
099
      are commonly employed: regularization-based, rehearsal-based, and parameter-isolation methods.
      Regularization-based methods mitigate forgetting by regularizing differences in weights or output
100
      predictions between the old and new models (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Aljundi
101
      et al., 2018; Li & Hoiem, 2017; Dhar et al., 2019; Joseph et al., 2022). Rehearsal-based methods
102   preserve prior knowledge by retaining instances from previous tasks and training on a combined
103   dataset that includes these instances alongside data from the new task. This retained data may consist
104   of exemplar images (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2018), publicly available external datasets
105   (Lee et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020b), or synthetic data generated by generative models (Shin et al.,
106   2017; Kemker & Kanan, 2017; He et al., 2018). Parameter-isolation methods improve stability by
107   freezing parameters that are critical to previously learned tasks (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Serra
      et al., 2018).

      While each of the aforementioned strategies has its strengths, they also present notable limitations.
125
      Regularization-based and parameter-isolation approaches improve model stability but often com-
126
      promise plasticity, and rehearsal-based methods encounter practical challenges, including storage
127   constraints, privacy concerns, and scalability issues. Recent research has proposed adapter-based
128   methods to address catastrophic forgetting(Rajasegaran et al., 2020; Bhat et al., 2023; Pham et al.,
129   2021; Wang et al., 2022; Liang & Li, 2024; Zhang et al., 2020a). Some of these works incorporate
130   adapter-like subnets. However, they are limited in several ways: 1) freezing the backbone (Liang
131   & Li, 2024; Zhang et al., 2020a) can negatively impact performance as the shared information
132   can not be learned effectively; 2) relying on custom, complex loss functions (Liang & Li, 2024;
133   Bhat et al., 2023) reduces compatibility with new algorithms and limits broader applicability; and
134   3) none incorporates regularization- or prediction-based approaches. By focusing exclusively on
135
      network architecture, these methods fail to leverage valuable insights from robust yet foundational
      baselines such as Learning without Forgetting (Li & Hoiem, 2017). To address these shortcomings,
136
      we propose leveraging adapters to develop a lightweight and compatible solution that combines
137
      strong performance with insights from both adapter design and algorithmic principles.
138
139
      Fine-tuning with adapters Another line of research explores the potential of adapters for trans-
140   ferring knowledge to downstream tasks, particularly within large language models. Adapters are
141   compact modules inserted between the layers of a pre-trained large model (Houlsby et al., 2019).
142   These modules are typically fine-tuned while the original network remains frozen. Fine-tuning with
143   adapters achieves performance comparable to full fine-tuning across diverse tasks (Li & Liang, 2021).
144   Moreover, adapters facilitate rapid adaptation to new tasks without catastrophic forgetting (Pfeiffer
145   et al., 2020a), addressing challenges in multi-domain (Chronopoulou et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2022)
146   and multilingual settings (Pfeiffer et al., 2020b).

      3.2   I NTRODUCING ADAPTERS
193
      In this section, we propose a network architecture comprising two distinct components: a backbone
194   network for learning invariant features shared across all tasks and multiple lightweight adapters for
195   capturing task-specific information. These adapters enhance the plasticity of the architecture, enabling
196   it to adapt to new tasks, while the backbone network maintains stability by focusing on shared and
197   invariant features. Unlike existing approaches that train adapters while keeping the backbone frozen,
198   we use adapters as task-specific feature modifiers and co-train them alongside the backbone network.
199   As the model trains on additional tasks and samples, the backbone network refines its ability to learn
200   invariant features, further enhancing stability. This architectural design improves both stability and
201   plasticity, effectively eliminating the stability-plasticity dilemma.
202   The adapters are positioned between the backbone feature extractor, denoted as φ, and the label
203   predictor layer, serving as task-specific feature modifiers β t for each task t. As illustrated in Figure
204   2, the adapters adopt a conventional bottleneck structure. Starting with an initial dimension d and a
205   bottleneck width b, we design the down-projection layer to reduce dimensionality from d to b using a
206   fully-connected neural network with a weight matrix Wd×b and a non-linear activation function g,
207   expressed as
208                                             Downd→b (x) = g (xWd×b ) .
209   Similarly, the up-projection layer is defined as
210
                                                  Upb→d (x) = g (xWb×d ) .
211
212   Our adapters consist of both a down-projection and an up-projection step and are connected to
213
      the output via a skip-connection (He et al., 2021). This bottleneck design allows the adapter to
      utilize both backbone features from φ(x) and the modified features processed through the down- and
214
      up-projection layers:
215

      Our adapter module incorporates three key features specifically designed for incremental learning: (i)
217   a compact design with a small number of parameters; (ii) compatibility with and enhancements over
218   existing methods; and (iii) the simultaneous updating of feature extractor layers and adapters. The
219   compact design addresses memory constraints, while the latter two features jointly improve stability
220   and plasticity.
221
      For (i), our method leverages adapters to control the growth rate of the overall model size when ac-
222
      commodating additional tasks. When applied to large backbone networks, the parameters introduced
223   by the adapters are negligible, making our approach well-suited for models with memory constraints.
224   This contrasts with strategies that rely on dynamically expanding networks (Yoon et al., 2017; Yan
225   et al., 2021).
226
      For (ii) and (iii), we address the stability-plasticity dilemma through a novel two-block design and
227
      their co-training. Specifically, adapters are trained to enhance plasticity, while the continuous training
228
      of backbone networks focuses on improving invariant feature learning, thereby further enhancing
229   stability. Such a distinction is particularly challenging in traditional, non-adapter architectures, as
230   their holistic design inherently exacerbates the stability-plasticity dilemma.
231
232
      Lastly, to enforce that the backbone network learns invariant features and thus the adapters learn
      task-specific information, we develop method-specific regularization techniques. For the prediction-
233
      regularized methods such as LwF, we impose a knowledge distillation loss on the backbone, encour-
234
      aging the backbone is similar before and after learning each new task. For the weight-regularized
235   methods such as EWC, we free adapters from regularization. The approaches and designs are
236   discussed in the following section in detail.
237

353   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS
354
355   On regularization-based methods In this section, we study the effect of adapters combined with
356   various weight-regularized methods in incremental learning, including Elastic Weight Consolidatio
357   EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), Memory Aware Synapses (MAS) (Aljundi et al., 2018), Path Integral
358   (PathInt) (Zenke et al., 2017), as well as prediction-regularized methods such as Learning without
359
      Forgetting (LwF) (Li & Hoiem, 2017) and Learning without Memorizing (LwM) (Dhar et al., 2019).
360   Figure 3 compares different methods with and without adapters in task-IL on CIFAR-100. From the
361   first task onward, weight-regularized methods with adapters exhibit an approximate 3% increase in
362   average accuracy compared to those without adapters. This improvement is consistently maintained
363   throughout the learning process across all methods. For prediction-regularized methods, the accuracy
364   advantage further escalates to as much as 5%. The observed increase in accuracy can be primarily
365
      attributed to the model’s improved plasticity to learn new task-specific knowledge through the use of
      adapters, while the backbone was continuously trained as well.
366
367
      On task scale This paragraph examines the effect of the task scale. The benefits of utilizing adapters
368
      diminish as the number of classes increases within each task. As illustrated in Figure 4, when learning
369
      either 5 or 10 classes simultaneously, our adapter-based approach continues to effectively capture
370   inter-task differences and significantly outperforms methods without adapters. However, while the
371   advantages are still present, they become less pronounced as the number of classes per task increases.
372   This reduction in the performance margin is understandable, as learning more classes per task not
373   only provides more data for the model to learn but also requires more memory and storage, with fewer
374   regime shifts. In the extreme case, when the number of classes per task reaches 100, incremental
375   learning reduces to a multi-task learning problem.

      backbone.

509   5   C ONCLUSION
510
511   In this paper, we propose a network design consisting of two blocks: a backbone network for learning
512   invariant features across all tasks and multiple adapters for modeling task-specific knowledge. The
513   backbone and adapters are co-trained continuously in an incremental learning framework. Our
514   extensive experiments conducted on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet, across various orderings and task
515   scales, show that introducing task-specific adapters consistently improves the performance of all
516   considered methods, and exhibits extensive compatibility with all of them. Consequently, we offer
517   an effective solution to resolve the stability-plasticity dilemma for incremental learning, and we
518
      envision that future IL algorithms can be benefited from our work, a simple but effective integration
      of adatpers.

731
      A.2    L EARNING RATE SEARCH AND HYPERPARAMETER TUNING
732
733   We apply the Continual hyperparameter Framework (De Lange & Tuytelaars, 2021), a common
734   framework to select learning rates (LRs) and tune hyperparameters for methods of incremental
735   learning. The framework incorporates two phase: the Maximal Plasticity Search phase to search
736   LR with fine-tuning on the new task, and the Stability Decay phase to search for the optimal
737   hyperparameters.
738   For the Maximal Plasticity Search phase, we fine-tune the model on the new task and select the
739   optimal LR to achieve high plasticity. Specifically, we train the model from scratch for the first task
740   and apply LR search on {5e-1, 1e-1, 5e-2}. Starting from the second task, the LR search space is
741   limited to 1e-1, 5e-2, 1e-2, 5e-3, 1e-3}. LR decay is applied, with a decay factor of 3 and patience of
742   10 epochs. The stopping criteria is either the LR below 1e-4 or 100 epochs have passed.
743
      For the Stability Decay phase, we fix the LR and select the hyperparameter starting from a high
744   value, with a gradual decay to achieve the optimal stability-plasticity trade-off. We start from a high
745   value of hyperparameter because this is close to freezing the network such that old knowledge is
746   preserved, and through gradual decay the model becomes less intransigence and slowly converges
747   towards higher forgetting, which would ultimately corresponds to fine-tuning of the previous phase.
748   Specifically, we reduce the hyperparameter value in half if the method accuracy is below the 95% of
749   the fine-tuning accuracy in the previous LR search phase.
750   Next, we present the starting values for hyperparameters across methods. Most of the methods
751   discussed in our paper are regularization-based approach, which include a hyperparameter λ for the
752   regularizer that controls the trade-off between stability and plasticity. The starting values for λ are
753   summarized in Table 5. For other method-specific parameters, we generally follow the corresponding
754   original work. For instance, we fix the temperature parameter as 2 for LwF. For PathInt, the dampling
755   parameter is set to 0.1. For LwM, the attention distillation parameter is set to 1 based on an empirical
      evaluation in (Masana et al., 2022).
