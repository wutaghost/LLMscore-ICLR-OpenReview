                                                                           A BSTRACT
026
027
                                The rapid progress in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has signifi-
028
                                cantly advanced their ability to process and understand complex visual and textual
029                             information. However, the integration of multiple images and extensive textual
030                             contexts remains a challenge due to the inherent limitation of the models’ capacity
031                             to handle long input sequences efficiently. In this paper, we introduce S EEKER, a
032                             multimodal large language model designed to tackle this issue. S EEKER aims to op-
033                             timize the compact encoding of long text by compressing the text sequence into the
034                             visual pixel space via images, enabling the model to handle long text within a fixed
035                             token-length budget efficiently. Our empirical experiments on six long-context
036                             multimodal tasks demonstrate that S EEKER can leverage fewer image tokens to
037
                                convey the same amount of textual information compared with the OCR-based
                                approach, and is more efficient in understanding long-form multimodal input and
038
                                generating long-form textual output, outperforming all existing proprietary and
039
                                open-source MLLMs by large margins.
040

042    1                I NTRODUCTION
043
044   The success of Large Language Models (LLMs) OpenAI (2022); Touvron et al. (2023b); Bai et al.
045   (2023a); DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024) has significantly impacted various fields, notably Multimodal
046   Large Language Models (MLLMs) OpenAI (2023b); Liu et al. (2023c); Bai et al. (2023b); Lu et al.
047   (2024). And there is a burgeoning interest in enhancing LLMs to handle longer context Xiong
048   et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024); Jin et al. (2024), for example, the recent GPT-4O OpenAI (2024)
049   can support up to 128k tokens, paving the way to unlock many real-world applications from long-
050
      document understanding, summarization to document translation, among others.
051   In many applications involving long-form documents that integrate images and text, there is a
052   significant demand for the strong long-context understanding ability of MLLMs. As shown in
053   Figure 2, the long context in the multimodal domain falls into two main categories: 1) long-form
      inputs consisting of multiple text-rich images, and 2) long-form text outputs. In the first category,

      multiple images increase the context length with image tokens and additional text tokens if the images
063
      are text-rich. This requires the model to efficiently integrate textual data with multiple images and
064   reason across them. In the second category, the model must produce coherent and attentive long
065   responses to the input context, avoiding irrelevant or hallucinated content and minimizing reliance on
066   the model knowledge without considering the specific multimodal context.
067
      The existing MLLMs Liu et al. (2023c;a); Lu et al. (2024) leverage pretrained LLMs Chiang
068
      et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023a) and inherit their advanced language understanding capabilities.
069
      Although these MLLMs demonstrate strong performance across various vision-language bench-
070   marks Liu et al. (2024b); Yu et al. (2023), their effectiveness in long-form multimodal contexts is less
071   explored. This issue becomes significant in tasks with very long input or output, which may exceed
072   the context length limit (e.g., 2048 tokens for LLaMA) and increase computational overhead.
073
      While only a few MLLMs OpenAI (2023b); McKinzie et al. (2024) are capable of handling multiple
074
      images in the multimodal context, efficiency emerges as another critical challenge. “A picture is
075
      worth a thousand words”, for human, it is more natural to fully utilize our bandwidth to process an
076   image than words. However, this might not be the case for models. In this paper, we aim to represent
077   information in a more compact form, enabling conveying more information within the same context
078   length. Specifically, we investigate the “visual token representation” as an alternative to text tokens,
079   and introduce S EEKER, an efficient method for managing long contexts within a constrained length
080   budget. This approach allows us to process more context within a fixed token length.
081
      As shown in Figure 3, an OCR-based approach might yield 10k tokens from an eight-page document
082   for the LLM with a context limit of 8k tokens. While, S EEKER processes each of the eight pages as
083   separate images, converting them into 576 tokens each. This generates a total of 4, 608 tokens for the
084   whole document, which are then fed into the S EEKER model for reasoning and generation.
085
      To the best of our knowledge, S EEKER is the first to address this in the long-context MLLMs by
086
      employing a compact tokenization strategy that leverages visual tokens for textual information,
087
      thus reducing the number of tokens required and enabling the processing of longer texts without
088   additional computation overhead. S EEKER’s design allows for sophisticated reasoning across multiple
089   images. By interleaving image tokens with textual data, S EEKER can preserve context coherence
090   and continuity across extended sequences, enabling more effective interpretation and integration
091   of visual data in scenarios where traditional text-based models may struggle. To sum up, our main
092   contributions are as follows:
093
           • We present S EEKER, a novel approach to leverage the visual tokens to represent both image and
094
             text information in long documents. Our approach is more efficient than OCR text tokens, when
095
             given the same token length constraint.
096
           • Our S EEKER supports long-context multimodal reasoning, effectively handling long-form multi-
097
             image input and generating long-form text output.
098
           • Our instruction-tuned S EEKER model demonstrates promising results compared to the existing
099
             MLLMs on six long-context multimodal tasks.

144   3     S EEKER : L ONG - CONTEXT V ISION AND L ANGUAGE U NDERSTANDING
145
146   We propose S EEKER, a multimodal large language model designed to handle long-context images
147   and texts, as depicted in Figure 3. In Section 3.1, we discuss the innovative use of image tokens
148   to represent lengthy textual data compactly. Then we introduce long-context multimodal task and
149   instruction data in Section 3.2. Finally, in Section 3.3, we illustrate the architecture of our S EEKER to
150   support both long-context and short-context multimodal understanding.

183   3.2      L ONG -C ONTEXT M ULTIMODAL TASK
184
      We mainly consider two categories of long-context multimodal capabilities, as outlined in Table 1: 1)
185
      Long-form multimodal input: This involves multiple text-rich images interleaved with text as the
186   input context. 2) Long-form text output: This requires generating long text.
187
188
189   Instruction Data for Long-Form Multi-Image Input First, we combine an arbitrary number of
190   single-image visual instruction data Liu et al. (2023c) sourced from CC3M into the multi-image
191   format for the intra-image reasoning task. This helps initiate model’s capability of understanding
192
      sequences of images (e.g., <img1 > This image depicts a... <img2 > This image shows a...). We
      then curate inter-image reasoning instruction data from NLVR2 Suhr et al. (2019) (e.g., <img1 >
193
      <img2 > Considering the images on both sides, is ‘At least one of the televisions is turned off.’ valid?
194
      Answer yes or no.), Mimic CGD (e.g., <img1 > <img2 > What’s the difference between the two sinks
195   in the images?), and annotate multi-image conversation data on COCO images Lin et al. (2015) using
196   GPT-4V (e.g., <img1 > <img2 > <img3 > How many birds are in all the provided images?). To
197   enable understanding of long-form text-rich image sequences, we collect compiled PDFs from arXiv
198   documents. Each page from these documents is processed as images, ranging from 4 to 24 pages. We
199   use GPT-4V to generate descriptive or conversational instruction data for these scientific documents.
200   To further improve the model’s understanding of each provided image, we create a multi-image text
201   grounding task, requiring the model to ground the question to the referred image (e.g., <img1 >
202   <img2 > ... <img8 > Which image contains the answer to the question / Which image contains the
203
      sentence...).
204
205   Instruction Data for Long-Form Text Output To enhance long-form text generation capabilities
206
      related to the given image, we propose a task that involves reading the text in the image verbatim (e.g.,
      <img1 > Quote the text in the image verbatim.). This challenging task requires the vision backbone to
207
      encode character-level image details and the language backbone to attend to the image token while
208
      producing very long text without hallucinating on previously generated content.

      3.3      L ONG -C ONTEXT M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
211
212   To enable long-context multimodal reasoning, our model architecture should: 1) encode multiple
213   images interleaved with text, 2) align images and text at a fine-grained level, and 3) decode long texts
214   that attend to extended multimodal contexts. The following paragraphs illustrate the design of our
215   proposed S EEKER for this purpose.

234   Additionally, to preserve the model’s capability with single-image data without necessitating re-

239
240   Dense Image-Text Alignment We inherit the general image-text alignment from the pre-training
241   image-text pairs. To enhance the visual representation of dense text in images, and improve the
242   alignment between image and text representation of rendered text, we curate a visual-embedded
243   task that renders text into visual space. Specifically, we render text paragraphs from Wikipedia into
244   1024 × 1024 images using Arial font, with sizes ranging from 18 to 30, providing various word
245
      densities per image. We observe that it is essential to start by learning image-text alignment at a
      sparse level (large font size, low word density) and gradually incorporate dense text-rendered image
246
      data. Task types we consider include question answering on multiple images rendered with text from
247
      Wikipedia, and reading the text verbatim from rendered images.

266   4.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
267
268   The language model backbone of S EEKER is the DeepSeek LLM DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024), which
269   has a design similar to LLaMA. It is supervised-finetuned on 2T tokens with additional DPO and
      surpasses LLaMA-2 and GPT-3.5 on numerous open-eval tasks. To enable to process high-resolution


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      images and ensure adept performance in real-world scenarios, we instruction-tune the stage-3 model
271   from the DeepSeek-VL series of model DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024). The vision encoder of S EEKER -
272   T INY is SigLIP, and the vision encoder of S EEKER is a hybrid of SigLIP-L Zhai et al. (2023) and
273   SAM-B Kirillov et al. (2023). This enables processing 1024 × 1024 images into a fixed token length
274   of 576. This fixed token length for high-resolution image processing provides an optimal balance of
275   fine-grained and compact visual representation. The adaptor used is a hybrid MLP, the same as in
276   DeepSeek-VL Lu et al. (2024).
277
278   4.2   T RAINING
279
280
      We use the AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2019) optimizer to train our models for 1 epoch with a
      batch size of 32. The learning rate is linearly warmed up during the first 5% of steps to 1e − 4 and
281
      then reduced to zero using a cosine learning rate scheduler. The context sequence length is set to
282
      4096 during instruction-tuning on single-image data. Both the vision-and-language pre-training data
283   (e.g., MMC4 Zhu et al. (2023)) and single-image instruction-tuning data (e.g., ShareGPT4V Chen
284   et al. (2023)) are adopted from DeepSeek-VL Lu et al. (2024). For continual training on our proposed
285   long-context multimodal instruction data (Section 3.2), we set the maximum length to 8192 to
286   accommodate a long sequence of images and long-form text output. We set the rank to 8 for low-rank
287   adaptation (LoRA Hu et al. (2021)). Our S EEKER and S EEKER -T INY are trained on a single
288   8-A100-40G node for 30 hours and 12 hours, respectively.

290   4.3   E VALUATION
291
292   We consider four long-form multi-image input tasks: 1) Index: the multiple-choice image indexing
293
      task, given a sequence of images and a question, the model selects the option with the index of the
      image that contains the answer, 2) SentRetrie: the sentence retrieval task, given a sequence of
294
      images of rendered text sampled from Wikipedia, the model is required to retrieve the first sentence
295
      from the first image, 3) ArxivQA: the question answering on arxiv documents, the model is required
296   to answer the question according to visual image of arxiv documents. 4) PassKey: the passkey
297   retrieval task slightly modified for multimodal model, given the sentence with a masked word, the
298   model need to answer what is the masked word by reading the visually-situated text content from
299   arxiv document. We consider two long-form text output tasks: 1) ArxivVerb: extract text from
300   the image of arxiv documents verbatim, 2) WikiVerb: extract text from the image of rendered text
301   from Wikipedia verbatim. Details of each long-context multimodal task are introduced in Table 1,
302   with more details presented in Appendix B.1.
303   Each long-context multimodal task contains 80 diversified samples. We use the accuracy metric for
304   the multiple-choice task (Index) and the Rouge-L score for all other text generation tasks. For
305   standard multimodal tasks, which require fewer than four image inputs and text answers that are less
306   than 400 tokens. We use the accuracy metric for multiple-choice NLVR2 Suhr et al. (2019) test-public
307   split and the BLINK Fu et al. (2024) validation split. We validate models on the official evaluation
308   metrics and test splits for general single-image multimodal benchmarks, MMB EN, MMB CN (MMC)
309   and Circular Eval for MMB (CCBench) Liu et al. (2024b), SEED Li et al. (2023a), AI2D Kembhavi
310
      et al. (2016), LLaVAB Liu et al. (2023c), ChartQA Masry et al. (2022), TextVQA Singh et al. (2019)).
      We follow the inference configurations in VLMEvalKit Contributors (2023).

      We aim to evaluate the general multimodal understanding and reasoning capabilities of our model in
398   comparison with state-of-the-art models in the field. In Table 4, our model, S EEKER , demonstrates

401   requiring significant additional resources or tuning.
402
      Moreover, even though we did not explicitly include general single-image instruction data during
403
      the continual instruction tuning phase for long-context tasks, our model still retains competitive
404   performance. In fact, S EEKER performs on par with other MLLMs in this domain and even
405   outperforms all other models on certain tasks. This ability to maintain performance, despite the
406   absence of further instruction tuning data, can be attributed to our approach of employing a distinct
407   image identifier for multi-image processing, while continuing to use the single-image template
408   during inference. This strategy allows the model to handle multi-image tasks efficiently without
409   compromising its performance on single-image tasks.

       C.1    L IMITATIONS
1191
1192   While our model, SEEKER, has made significant strides in processing extended-context multimodal
1193   inputs, it encounters several critical limitations that require deeper investigation. The process of com-
1194   pressing textual information into visual tokens, although efficient, may inadvertently overlook precise
1195   textual understanding. Future endeavors should focus on developing hybrid encoding strategies that
1196   balance token compression with the preservation of essential information. Additionally, SEEKER
1197
       could inadvertently learn and perpetuate biases present in its training data. It is imperative that further
       research is conducted to identify, understand, and address these biases, ensuring the model’s equity
1198
       and inclusiveness.
1199
1200
1201
       C.2    S OCIETAL I MPACT
1202   By integrating visual tokens with textual data, SEEKER addresses the limitations of traditional
1203   models and supports the handling of longer input sequences. This innovation could transform various
1204   sectors, improving information accessibility and retrieval systems across academic research, legal
1205   document analysis, and extensive data processing tasks. Particularly beneficial in educational and
1206   professional environments, SEEKER enables rapid and accurate extraction of vast informational
1207   content, fostering better decision-making and knowledge dissemination. However, this advancement
1208   might exacerbate information disparities if not equitably accessible. Steps should be taken to make
1209   sure it is both affordable and available to everyone.
