009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper proposes a variant of multiple-play stochastic bandits tailored to resource
012
               allocation problems arising from LLM applications, edge intelligence applications,
013            etc. The proposed model is composed of M arms and K plays. Each arm has
014            a stochastic number of capacities, and each unit of capacity is associated with a
015            reward function. Each play is associated with a priority weight. When multiple
016            plays compete for the arm capacity, the arm capacity is allocated in a larger priority
017            weight first manner.
                                  √ Instance independent and instance dependent regret lower
018            bounds of Ω(α1 σ KM T ) and Ω(α1 σ 2 M∆K ln T ) are proved, where α1 is the
019            largest priority weight and σ characterizes the reward tail. When model parame-
020            ters are given, we design an algorithm named MSB-PRS-OffOpt to locate the
021            optimal play allocation policy with a computational complexity of O(M 3 K 3 ).
022
               Utilizing MSB-PRS-OffOpt as a subroutine, an approximate upper confidence
               bound (UCB) based algorithm is designed, which has instance independent and
023
               instance dependent√regret upper bounds matching the corresponding lower bound
024
               up to factors of K ln KT and α1 K respectively. To this end, we address non-
025
               trivial technical challenges arising from optimizing and learning under a special
026
               nonlinear combinatorial utility function induced by the prioritized resource sharing
027            mechanism.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The Multi-play multi-armed bandit (MP-MAB) is a classical sequential learning framework Anan-
034   tharam et al. (1987a). The canonical MP-MAB model consists of one decision maker who pulls
035   multiple arms per decision round. Each pulled arm generates a reward, which is drawn from an
036   unknown probability distribution. The objective is to maximize the cumulative reward facing the
037   exploration vs. exploitation dilemma. MP-MAB frameworks are applied to various applications such
038   as online advertising Lagrée et al. (2016); Komiyama et al. (2017); Yuan et al. (2023), power system
039
      Lesage-Landry & Taylor (2017), mobile edge computing Chen & Xie (2022); Wang et al. (2022a);
      Xu et al. (2023), etc. Recently, various variants of MP-MAB were studied, which tap potentials of
040
      MP-MAB framework for resource allocation problems and advance the bandit learning literature
041
      Chen & Xie (2022); Moulos (2020); Xu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022a); Yuan et al. (2023).
042
043   This paper extends MP-MAB to capture the prioritized resourcing sharing mechanism, contributing
044   a fine-grained resource allocation model. We aim to reveal fundamental insights on the interplay
045
      of this mechanism and learning. Prioritized resourcing sharing mechanisms are implemented in a
      large class of resource allocation problems arising from mobile edge computing Chen et al. (2021);
046
      Gao et al. (2022); Ouyang et al. (2019; 2023), ride sharing Chen & Xie (2022), etc., and have the
047
      potential to enable differentiated services in LLM applications. For example, in LLM applications,
048   reasoning tasks and LLM instances can be modeled as plays and arms respectively. Multiple LLM
049   reasoning tasks (plays) share an instance of LLM (an arm) according to their priority quantified by
050   price, membership hierarchy, etc. In mobile edge computing systems, the infrastructure of edge
051   intelligence, tasks and edge servers can be modeled as plays and arms respectively. When multiple
052   tasks (or plays) are offloaded to the same edge server (or arm), the available computing resource is
053   shared among them according to the differentiated pricing mechanism (an instance of prioritized
      resourcing sharing mechanism).

054
      Formally, we proposed MSB-PRS (Multiple-play Stochastic Bandits with Prioritized Resource
055   Sharing). The MSB-PRS is composed of K ∈ N+ plays and M ∈ N+ arms. Each play has a
056   priority weight and movement costs. Each arm has a stochastic number of units of capacities. Plays
057   share the capacity in a high priority weight first manner. A play receives a reward scaled by its
058   weight only when it occupies one unit of capacity. The objective is to maximize the cumulative
059   utility (rewards minus costs) in T ∈ N+ rounds. Some recent works tailored MP-MAB to the
060   same or similar applications Chen & Xie (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022a;b); Yuan
061   et al. (2023). The key difference to this line of research is on the stochastic capacity with bandit
062   feedback and prioritized capacity sharing. This difference poses new challenges. One challenge
063
      lies in locating the optimal play allocation policy. The movement cost and the prioritized capacity
      sharing impose a nonlinear combinatorial structure on the utility function, which hinders locating the
064
      optimal allocation. In contrast to previous works Xu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022a;b), top arms
065
      do not warrant optimal allocation. This nonlinear combinatorial structure also makes it difficult to
066   distinguish optimal allocation from sub-optimal allocation from feedback. As a result, it is nontrivial
067   to balance the exploring vs. exploitation tradeoff. We address these challenges.
068

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
070
071   Model and fundamental learning limits. We formulate MSB-PRS, which captures the prioritized re-
072   sourcing sharing nature of resource allocation
                                                 √ problems. We prove instance independent and instance
073   dependent regret lower bounds of Ω(α1 σ KM T ) and Ω(α1 σ 2 M    ∆ ln T ) respectively. Technically,
074   we tackle the aforementioned nonlinear combinatorial structure challenge by identifying one special
075   instances of the MSB-PRS that are composed of carefully designed multiple independent groups of
076   classical multi-armed bandits and batched MP-MAB.
077
      Efficient learning algorithms. (1) Computational efficiency. Given model parameters, to tackle
078   the computational challenge of locating the optimal play allocation policy, we characterize the afore-
079   mentioned nonlinear combinatorial structure by constructing a priority ranking aware bipartite graph.
080   A connection between the utility of arm allocation policies and the saturated, monotone and priority
081   compatible matchings is established. This connection enables us to design MSB-PRS-OffOpt,
082   which locates the optimal play allocation policy with a complexity O(M 3 K 3 ) from a search space
083   with size K M . (2) Sample efficiency. Utilizing MSB-PRS-OffOpt as a subroutine, we design
084   an approximate UCB based algorithm, which reduces the per-round computational complexity of
085   the exact UCB based algorithm from K M to O(K 3 M 3 ). We prove sublinear instance independent

094   Anantharam et al. Anantharam et al. (1987a) proposed the canonical MP-MAB model, where they
095   established an asymptotic lower bound on the regret and designed an algorithm achieving the lower
096   bound asymptotically. Komiyama et al. Komiyama et al. (2015) showed that Thompson sampling
097   achieves the regret lower bound in the finite time sense. Anantharam et al. Anantharam et al. (1987b)
098   extended the canonical MP-MAB model from IID rewards to Markovian rewards. This Markovian
099
      MP-MAB model was further extended to the rested bandit setting Moulos (2020). MP-MAB with
      a reward function depending on the order of plays was studied in Lagrée et al. (2016); Komiyama
100
      et al. (2017). This reward function was motivated by clicking the model of web applications. They
101
      established lower bounds on the regret and designed a UCB based algorithm to balance the exploration
102   vs. exploitation tradeoff. MP-MAB with switching cost is studied in Agrawal et al. (1990); Jun
103   (2004). They proved the lower bound on the regret and designed algorithms that achieve the lower
104   bound asymptotically. MP-MAB with budget constraint is considered in Luedtke et al. (2019); Xia
105   et al. (2016); Zhou & Tomlin (2018) and a stochastic number of plays in each round is considered in
106   Lesage-Landry & Taylor (2017), which is motivated by power system. Recently, Yuan et al. Yuan
107   et al. (2023) extended the canonical MP-MAB classical to the sleeping bandit setting, for the purpose
      of being tailored to the recommender systems.

      Our work is closely related to Chen & Xie (2022); Wang et al. (2022a); Xu et al. (2023). Chen et al.
109   Chen & Xie (2022) tailored the canonical MP-MAB model for the user-centric selection problems.
110   Their model considered homogeneous plays and expert feedback on capacity. They designed a
111   Quasi-UCB algorithm for this problem with sublinear regret upper bounds. Our work generalizes
112   their model to capture heterogeneous plays, prioritized resourcing sharing, and bandit feedback on
113   the capacity. This extension not only be more friendly to real-world applications, but also incurs
114   new challenges for locating the optimal allocation and design learning algorithms. We design a UCB
115   based algorithm and prove both regret upper bounds and lower bounds. Wang et al. Wang et al.
116   (2022a) proposed a model that also allowed multiple plays to share capacity on an arm. Their model
117
      considers a deterministic capacity provision. The capacity is unobservable and coupled with the
      reward. They proved regret lower bound on regret and designed an action elimination based algorithm
118
      whose regret matches the regret lower bound to a certain level. Xu et al. Xu et al. (2023) extended
119
      this model to the setting with strategic agents and competing for the capacity. They analyzed the Nash
120   equilibrium in the offline setting and proposed a Selfish MP-MAB with an Averaging Allocation
121   approach based on the equilibrium.
122
123
      Various works share some connections to the MP-MAB research line. Combinatorial bandits Cesa-
      Bianchi & Lugosi (2012); Chen et al. (2013); Combes et al. (2015b) generalize the reward function
124
      of the canonical MP-MAB from linear to non-linear. Various variants of combinatorial bandits were
125
      studied: (1) combinatorial bandits with semi-bandit feedback Chen et al. (2013; 2016); Gai et al.
126   (2012); Combes et al. (2015b), i.e., the reward of each pulled arm is revealed; (2) combinatorial bandits
127   with bandit feedback: Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi (2012); Combes et al. (2015b), i.e., only one reward
128   associated with the pulled arm set is revealed; (3) combinatorial bandits with different combinatorial
129   structures, i.e., matroid Kveton et al. (2014), m-set Anantharam et al. (1987a), permutation Gai
130   et al. (2012), etc. Cascading bandit Combes et al. (2015a); Kveton et al. (2015b); Wen et al. (2017)
131   extends the reward function of the canonical MP-MAB from linear to a factorization form over the
132   set of selected arms. Decentralized MP-MAB (a.k.a. multi-player MAB) Agarwal et al. (2022);
133   Anandkumar et al. (2011); Rosenski et al. (2016); Bistritz & Leshem (2018); Wang et al. (2020))
134
      considers the setting that players either cannot communicate with others or their communication is
      restrictive.

                        √       of O(M 3 K 3 ). Instance independent and instance dependent regret lower

      A.4   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 4.1
811
812   This proof relies on the construction of a special instance of our model. The special instance is
813   constructed as follows. The move cost of each arm is fixed to zero, formally
814
                                                 ck,m = 0, ∀k, m.
815
816   All arms have the same weights, i.e.,
817
                                              α1 = α1 = · · · = αK .
818
819   The reward of each follows a Normal distribution:
820
                                                 Rm ∼ N (µm , σ).
821
822   In each round, each arm generates K units of resource, and the number of resource is deterministic,
823   i.e.,
824                                     dmax = K, Pm,d = 1, ∀m, d.
825   Suppose that the reward mean satisfies:
826
                                              µ1 > µ2 > . . . > µM .
827
828   Then the optimal action profile is assigning all plays to arm m = 1, formally
829
                                                    a∗k = 1, ∀k.
830
831
      Now we prove the regret lower bound based on the above constructed special instance of our problem.
832   In each round the decision maker needs to assign K plays to arms, and multiple arms can be assigned
833                                                (t)                                (t)
      to the same arm. For each assigned play ak , if it hits the optimal arm, i.e., ak = a∗k , it does not
834                                                  (t)
835
      incur regret. If it hit a suboptimal arm, say ak = m, where m ̸= 1, it incurs an expected regret of
836   µ1 − µm . Thus in each round, the action profile a(t) incurs an expected regret of
837
                                                X
                                                     α1 (µ1 − µa(t) ).
838                                                                k
                                               k∈[K]
839
840   Note that this is not MP-MAB, as multiple plays is allowed be assigned to the same arm and generates
841   multiple independent rewards with the same distribution associated with this arm. We construct a
842   relaxed variant of this problem to make it easier for learning. Four model, though the decision maker
843
      received K rewards, but these reward can be used to update the decision only when this round ends.
      Namely, the decision maker can only update the decision when a round ends. To make the learning
844
      easier, we allow the decision maker to update the decision within each round, when new rewards is
845
      received. Specifically, we treat each round as K sub-rounds. Each sub-round receives one reward
846   and the decision maker can update the decision in each sub-round. This relaxation improves the data
847   utilization and making the learning easier. To unify, we treat each sub-round as a real round. This
848   leads to that the decision maker needs to assign one play in each round, but in total the decision maker
849   needs to play KT rounds. In each round of play, the decision maker observes all historical rewards
850   before this round. Then this becomes a tradition M -armed bandit problem with KT rounds of play.
851   Applying Theorem 15.2 of Lattimore & Szepesvári (2020), this problem has a regret lower bound of
852                                                1     √
