009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Bio-inspired neural networks have gained traction due to their adversarial ro-
012            bustness, energy efficiency and for being biologically plausible. While these bio-
013            inspired networks have shown significant progress, they still fall short in terms of
014            accuracy and are hard to scale to complex tasks. In this paper, we propose to use
015            neural architecture search to further improve state-of-the-art bio-inspired neural
016            networks. We achieve this thanks to BioNAS, a framework for neural architec-
017
               ture search designed for bio-inspired neural networks. It explores different bio-
               inspired neural network architectures and explores also the use of different learn-
018
               ing rules for the layers of models being explored. The motivation for this choice
019
               comes from recent work in the field suggesting that different learning mecha-
020            nisms might be used in different regions of the human brain. Using BioNAS, we
021            get state-of-the-art bio-inspired neural network performance achieving an accu-
022            racy of 94.86 on CIFAR10, 76.48 on CIFAR-100 and 43.42 on ImageNet16-
023            120, surpassing state-of-the-art bio-inspired neural networks. We show that a part
024            of this improvement comes from the use of different learning rules instead of
025            using a single learning rule for all the layers. To the best of our knowledge,
026            BioNAS is the first neural architecture search framework that allows the explo-
027            ration of using different bio-inspired learning rules for a neural network. We
028
               release BioNAS to the community and make the code available via this link
               (https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LR-NAS-DFE1).

030
031   Bio-inspired neural networks have gained significant traction recently. They are more biologically
032   plausible, more robust to adversarial attacks Sanfiz & Akrout (2021a); Moraitis et al. (2022a), and
033
      have higher energy efficiency Malcolm & Casco-Rodriguez (2023). Despite their significant success,
      these methods are still in development and fall short in terms of accuracy and in scaling effectively
034
      for complex tasks. In this paper, we aim to use neural architecture search to improve state-of-the-art
035
      bio-inspired neural networks.
036
037   Neural Architecture Search (NAS) is a method that automatically searches for neural network archi-
038   tectures and effectively finds high-quality architectures. This is usually done by exploring different
039
      types of architectures and picking one that best satisfies an objective (e.g., minimizing the loss).
      Early work based on Evolutionary Algorithms (EAs) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) was able to
040
      find interesting architectures, however such methods take significant amount of time. AmoebaNet-
041
      B Real et al. (2019), for example, takes around 3150 GPU days. Several efficient NAS methods have
042   been developed since then, offering a trade-off between search time and accuracy. Differentiable Ar-
043   chitecture Search (DARTS) Liu et al. (2018) is a notable example. It has shown success in designing
044   differentiable neural architecture search. EG-NAS Cai et al. (2024), a follow-up work, improved on
045   top of DARTS using genetic algorithms. It has shown an ability to find an accuracy competitive with
046   DARTS in 0.1 GPU days compared to 1 GPU day for DARTS on CIFAR10.
047
      In this paper, we build on top of DARTS and EG-NAS to propose BioNAS, a framework for neural
048   architecture search that explores different bio-inspired neural network architectures and learning
049   rules. BioNAS is the first NAS framework that explores the use of different bio-inspired learning
050   rules for different layers of the model. The motivation for this choice comes from recent work in the
051   field suggesting that the brain uses different learning mechanisms across different layers and regions
052   in the brain, from Hebbian Learning to more complex optimization techniques Marblestone et al.
053   (2016). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to address incorporating learning rules
      as part of the neural architecture search. We show that interesting architectures can be discovered

      within this framework surpassing state-of-the-art bio-inspired neural networks, and achieving an
055   accuracy of 94.86 on CIFAR10, 76.48 on CIFAR-100 and 43.42 on ImageNet16-120. We show
056   that a part of this improvement comes from the use of different learning rules instead of using a
057   single algorithm for all the layers.

      The contributions of this paper are as follows
059
060         1. We propose BioNAS, a framework for neural architecture search designed for bio-inspired
061            neural networks.
062

      1   R ELATED W ORK

072
      Backpropagation (BP) Rumelhart et al. (1986) is the most commonly used algorithm to train neural
073
      networks it relies on a forward pass where a prediction is made, and a backward pass that updates
074   the weights according to the derivative of the loss. The backward pass relies on a weight-transposed
075   matrix and requires symmetry. This is known as the weight transport problem. Many alternatives
076   have been proposed to relax this requirement especially since evidence from the brain has shown
077   that it doesn’t learn this way. Lillicrap et al. (2016) has proposed Feedback Alignment (FA) in
078   its simplest form that instead of using a transposed weight gradient matrix in the backward pass,
079   it uses a fixed random feedback matrix that’s shared across the network. Another variant called
080   Direct Feedback Alignment has been proposed by Nøkland (2016) and uses a random feedback
081   matrix shared across the network, however, the way the feedback synapses are wired is different: it
082
      is skipped directly to each layer separately from the output layer. Coherent learning happens even
      with these random feedback matrices, credit assignment occurs as a result of a partial alignment of
083
      the feedforward and backward weights. Liao et al. (2016) has shown that by using different sign
084
      concordance matrices, the performance can be improved and can approximate backpropagation.
085   Sanfiz & Akrout (2021a) has benchmarked these different techniques and their intrinsic adversarial
086   robustness.
087   On another note, Dellaferrera & Kreiman (2023) proposes ”Present the Error to Perturb the Input
088   To modulate Activity” (PEPITA), which is an algorithm that removes the backward pass and uses
089   a second forward pass instead, to modulate the input signal based on the error. Hinton (2022)
090   proposed the Forward-Forward algorithm that relies on a goodness metric for each layer and two
091   forward passes. The goodness metric can theoretically be set to any metric that is high for positive
092   data and low for negative data, such as the sum of the squared activities in a layer, this algorithm
093
      has been tested on simple tasks on shallow networks and has yet to be scaled for complex image
      classification tasks.
094
      Brain-inspired plasticity rules such as Hebbian Learning have long been used in computational
095
      neuroscience as a plasticity rule implemented by certain synapses in the brain. Yet this mechanism
096   of strengthening the synaptic weights when neurons fire together or inhibit them (Anti-Hebbian
097   Learning) remained very simple and ineffective in learning complex tasks, Amato et al. (2019);
098   Lagani et al. (2022b) have trained convolutional neural networks on complex tasks such as CIFAR10
099   and ImageNet, however Journé et al. (2023) have shown that Hebbian Learning can scale up and
100   perform well on these complex tasks in an unsupervised way using a Winner-Take-All mechanism.
101   Their method relies heavily on the choice of hyperparameters and relies on an adaptive learning rate.
102
103   Adversarial attacks are malicious attacks that try to fool models to erode their predictions. They
104   use information about the architecture, the data that it has been trained on, or simply in a black
105   box manner. Famous adversarial attacks include One or Few Pixel attacks Su et al. (2019), PGD
106   Madry (2017), FGSM Goodfellow et al. (2014). Wu et al. (2024) have benchmarked the adversarial
107   robustness of architectures resulting from NAS. Few works have investigated the search for robust
      architectures such as Zhu et al. (2023), where they include the adversarial score in the objective func-

193   2.2   DARTS
194
      Differentiable Architecture Search (DARTS) Liu et al. (2018); Chu et al. (2020) is a neural ar-
195
      chitecture search (NAS) method that performs continuous relaxation of the search space to enable
196   differentiable optimization. This technique reduces the computational cost by enabling weight shar-
197   ing across candidate architectures during the search phase. In DARTS, a supernetwork composed
198   of stacked normal and reduction cells is trained, with reduction cells positioned at certain layers to
199   downsample feature maps and capture hierarchical representations.
200
      Each cell in DARTS is represented as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), where the nodes correspond
201
      to feature maps, and the edges correspond to operations such as convolutions or pooling. The search
202   process optimizes architecture parameters α alongside the network weights w via gradient-based
203   optimization. A softmax over the candidate operations defines the mixed operation for each edge in
204   the DAG:
205

209   After the search phase, the architecture is derived by selecting the operations with the highest
210   weights for each edge, thus producing a finalized network architecture based on learned architecture
211   parameters. We provide more details in Appendix A.4.1.

214
215   EG-NAS Cai et al. (2024) builds upon DARTS by integrating evolutionary strategies into the
      search process, specifically leveraging Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-

      ES) Hansen (2016); Loshchilov & Hutter (2016). EG-NAS alternates between gradient descent
217   to update network weights and evolutionary updates to explore the architecture parameters α. By
218   sampling architectures from a Gaussian distribution and evolving them using CMA-ES, EG-NAS
219   enhances the exploration of the search space and reduces the risk of converging to local optima.

      2.4   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
225
226   We evaluate the robustness of bio-inspired neural networks against the following adversarial attacks.
227
228          • Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) Goodfellow et al. (2014): A white-box attack that
229            perturbs the input in the direction of the gradient of the loss with respect to the input,
230            bounded by an L∞ norm constraint.
231          • Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) Madry (2017): An iterative extension of FGSM,
232            which applies multiple small perturbations to maximize the loss, followed by a projection
233            back onto the L∞ constraint set.
234
             • Targeted Projected Gradient Descent (TPGD) Zhang et al. (2019): A variant of PGD
235            that uses KL-divergence loss to balance robustness and accuracy.
236
237
             • One-Pixel Attack Su et al. (2019): An attack that manipulates only a few pixels in the
               input image to fool the neural network while retaining the visual integrity of the input.
238
239          • Auto Projected Gradient Descent (APGD) Croce & Hein (2020): A parameter-free iter-
240            ative attack that automatically adjusts the step size and adapts to the model’s gradients for
241
               more efficient and robust perturbation.

243   3     M ETHODS
244
245   Our method builds upon DARTS and EG-NAS but introduces learning rules in the search space.
246   Specifically, we propose BioNAS-DARTS and BioNAS-EG, which extend the baseline methods
247   by incorporating a more comprehensive set of candidate operations based on bio-inspired learning
248   rules.
249   We implement different variants of feedback alignment, each based on a backward bio-inspired
250   connection. For simplicity, we do not use DFA as it requires a change in the computational graph of
251   the neural network, which would affect other methods relying on a backward pass using PyTorch.
252   We also do not use methods that rely on two forward passes such as Hinton (2022); Dellaferrera &
253   Kreiman (2023). Instead, we rely on other feedback alignment techniques that can be separated into
254   blocks and are not skip-connected.

280   3.2     B IO NAS-DARTS
281
      BioNAS-DARTS builds upon DARTS by introducing the expanded search space detailed above.
282
      During the search phase, both architecture parameters α and network weights w are optimized si-
283   multaneously. The search is conducted over a supernetwork composed of normal and reduction cells,
284   where each edge between nodes represents a candidate operation from our enhanced operation set.
285   The continuous relaxation method of DARTS allows us to optimize over this complex search space
286   efficiently. Figure 3 illustrates the search strategy for BioNAS-EG.
287
      In our framework, each operation on the edge can be selected from multiple bio-inspired modes,
288
      making the search process more diverse and capable of handling different types of data and learning
289   tasks.
290
291
      3.3     B IO NAS-EG
292
293   BioNAS-EG utilizes the faster, more scalable approach of EG-NAS, while incorporating our bio-
294   inspired operations into the search space. We retain the combination of gradient descent and CMA-
295   ES to explore architecture parameters efficiently. The evolutionary strategy explores multiple di-
296   rections, avoiding local optima and enhancing the search for robust and diverse architectures.
297
298   The compound fitness function integrates
299   cross-entropy loss and cosine similarity to bal-
300   ance performance and diversity during the
301   search, ensuring that the selected architectures
302   perform well across various metrics.
303
      BioNAS-EG provides a scalable solution for
304   neural architecture search that is suited for large
305   and complex datasets since it takes less search
306   time.

522   5      C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we show that a good choice of the learning rule used for each layer in a network
525   and the operation can result in a bio-inspired neural network with a good accuracy competing with
526   backpropagation-trained neural networks, achieving state-of-the-art bio-inspired neural network ac-
527   curacy. The mixed-rule approach provides advantages over using a single learning rule throughout
528   the network by reducing gradient variance, facilitating better exploration of the loss landscape, and
529   enhancing adversarial robustness. This theoretical framework aligns with the empirical results ob-
530   served in our experiments, where architectures searched using NAS with mixed learning rules out-
531   perform those using a uniform rule across all layers. Further investigation should be done on the
532   learning dynamics in this searched architecture and the representations learned.

826
827   Consider a neural network consisting of multiple layers, each trained with different feedback learn-
828   ing rules. The general weight update in a layer i can be written as:
829
830
831                                    δWi = −(Bi+1 δzi+1 )ϕ′ (zi )yi−1 ,
832
833   where Bi+1 is the feedback matrix and δzi+1 is the gradient of the loss with respect to the input of
834
      layer i + 1.
835   When a network uses a single learning rule throughout, this feedback matrix Bi+1 is updated uni-
836   formly across all layers. However, when different learning rules are applied in different layers, each
837   rule adapts the feedback matrix differently, allowing for a broader range of gradient dynamics across
838   the network. This diversity in updates helps mitigate issues like gradient vanishing or explosion, pro-
839   moting better gradient flow.
840
841   A.3.2    E XPLORATION AND O PTIMIZATION E FFICIENCY
842
843   Each learning rule contributes differently to the exploration of the loss landscape. The variation
844   across layers helps the network strike a balance between exploration and exploitation during opti-
845   mization. For example, some rules might encourage the network to explore different parts of the
846   loss surface by generating different gradient directions, while other rules help converge efficiently
847   by stabilizing the updates.
848   The overall gradient update at time t for a mixed-rule network is:
849
850                                                       X
851                                          ∆θtMixed =       wk ∆θtk ,
852                                                       k
853
854   where ∆θtk is the gradient update under rule k and wk is the weight assigned to that rule. By com-
855   bining these updates, the mixed-rule network benefits from diverse gradient dynamics that prevent
856   overfitting and improve convergence.
857
858   A.3.3    A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
859
860   Using mixed learning rules enhances adversarial robustness by disrupting the consistency in gradient
861   signals across layers. In adversarial attacks, the goal is to align perturbations with the network’s
862   gradient, but with different learning rules in different layers, the adversary faces a moving target.
863   This makes it harder to craft perturbations that generalize across the network, improving resistance
      to adversarial examples.
