                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               The field of artificial intelligence (AI) in quantitative investment has seen signif-
012            icant advancements, yet it lacks a standardized benchmark aligned with industry
013            practices. This gap hinders research progress and limits the practical application
014            of academic innovations. We present QuantBench, an industrial-grade bench-
015            mark platform designed to address this critical need. QuantBench offers three
016            key strengths: (1) standardization that aligns with quantitative investment indus-
017            try practices, (2) flexibility to integrate various AI algorithms, and (3) full-pipeline
018            coverage of the entire quantitative investment process. Our empirical studies us-
019            ing QuantBench reveal some critical research directions, including the need for
020
               continual learning to address distribution shifts, improved methods for modeling
               relational financial data, and more robust approaches to mitigate overfitting in low
021
               signal-to-noise environments. By providing a common ground for evaluation and
022
               fostering collaboration between researchers and practitioners, QuantBench aims
023            to accelerate progress in AI for quantitative investment, similar to the impact of
024            benchmark platforms in computer vision and natural language processing.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Although artificial intelligence (AI) for quantitative investment has been extensively studied by re-
030   search communities and many new trading algorithms have been developed in recent years, there is
031   a lack of a standardized benchmark that aligns with the testing standards used in the quantitative in-
032   vestment industry and by numerous trading firms. The absence of a standard benchmark dataset and
033
      test pipeline, coupled with the diverse standards used in existing papers to evaluate algorithm perfor-
      mance, may hinder research progress and limit the practical application of these advancements in the
034
      industry. Meanwhile, the establishment of standardized benchmarks, as demonstrated by some rep-
035
      resentative examples in computer vision (Deng et al., 2009) and natural language processing (Wang
036   et al., 2019a), has proven instrumental in advancing research in their respective fields.
037
038   To address this need, we propose QuantBench, an industrial-grade benchmark platform that of-
039
      fers universality and comprehensive pipeline coverage. QuantBench offers (1) Standardization:
      QuantBench adheres to research standards that are consistent with industrial practices in quanti-
040
      tative investment. (2) Flexibility: QuantBench is designed to support the scalable integration of
041
      various AI algorithms into the system. (3) Full-pipeline Coverage: QuantBench encompasses the
042   entire pipeline of general quantitative investment strategies, offering broad support for standardized
043   datasets, model implementations, and evaluations.
044
045

      Figure 1, integrating diverse tasks and learning paradigms into a single platform. By incorporat-
046
      ing a broad array of models, QuantBench bridges the gaps created by the segmented nature of the
047
      field, enhances reproducibility through open-source implementations, and facilitates the integration
048   of advancements across disciplines. In addition, QuantBench also emphasizes the importance of a
049   unified dataset, constructing datasets with both breadth and depth while maintaining consistency in
050   data format across varied data types. Moreover, QuantBench offers a detailed market simulation
051   framework that can vary in realism depending on the specific trading scenario. This robust evalu-
052   ation framework accommodates quant-specific metrics that are both task-specific (e.g., signal and
053   portfolio performance) and task-agnostic (e.g., alpha correlation and decay). By aligning evalua-
      tion metrics with tasks, QuantBench ensures that the chosen metrics capture the intricacies of these

      2.1     D ESIGN OF Q UANT B ENCH
138
      QuantBench adopts a structured, layered approach, integrating all research phases into a compre-
139   hensive framework, as depicted in Figure 1. At the foundation, the bottom layer consists of a diverse
140   array of models and datasets that underpin the entire quant research pipeline. This layer ensures
141   that a broad spectrum of financial data types and modeling techniques are accessible for rigorous
142   analysis. The middle layer of QuantBench processes outputs from the models, incorporates feed-
143   back mechanisms, and applies evaluation metrics. This layer effectively translates complex data
144   and model interactions into quantifiable results that facilitate direct comparisons and iterative im-
145   provements in model performance. At the top, the framework outlines the learning objectives and
146   the criteria for comprehensive evaluation. This layer aims to align the research outcomes with spe-
147
      cific investment goals, such as maximizing utility or optimizing asset allocation, ensuring that the
      training and evaluation stages are directly relevant to real-world financial strategies.
148
149   Within this structured design, the training and evaluation processes follow distinct yet interconnected
150   pathways. For training, the flow is: model + data → output → learning objective → feedback →
151   model. For evaluation, the sequence is: model + data → output → evaluation → metric. These
152   pathways ensure that both training and evaluation are systematic and aligned with the overarch-
153
      ing objectives of the research. Table 1 offers a detailed comparison of each task according to this
      hierarchical structure.

      Enhancing data resolution provides a detailed view of market dynamics. On a broader scale, quar-
245
      terly or monthly data can uncover long-term trends and cyclical patterns, which are invaluable for
246   strategic asset allocation and portfolio planning. On a finer scale, tick-level data captures the in-
247   tricacies of intraday price movements and the effects of market microstructure, which are crucial
248   for developing high-frequency trading strategies. The selection of modeling techniques is also in-
249   fluenced by data frequency, with high-frequency data often necessitating more computationally ef-
250   ficient and scalable methods. In QuantBench, data frequencies range from quarterly (fundamental
251   data derived from financial statements) to tick-level (detailing trades and quotes), enabling a wide
252   array of quant tasks. Lower frequency data suits applications like factor investing and risk modeling,
253   while higher frequency data is essential for analyses such as order book scrutiny and trade execution
254
      optimization. The capability to integrate data at varying frequencies also facilitates the development
      of innovative multi-scale strategies. A comprehensive description of these data levels is available in
255
      the supplementary materials.

      Temporal Models Temporal models leverage the historical data of the individual assets for pre-

291   4.2   T RAINING O BJECTIVE
292
      The selection of a training objective is informed by task-specific requirements and the intended out-
293
      come. For example, classification tasks predict binary outcomes, such as the direction of stock price
294
      movements, which is useful for market timing strategies. Regression tasks forecast continuous out-
295   comes such as stock returns, which are usually used for downstream portfolio optimization in stock
296   cross-sectional strategies. Ranking focuses on the relative order of assets rather than their precise
297   values, enhancing the profitability of characteristic-sorted portfolio. In contrast, utility maximiza-
298   tion directly seeks to enhance financial metrics that account for both risk and return, which is usually
299   used for end-to-end modelling that directly generates positions.
300
      Notably, some objectives, especially those involving complex simulations or non-standard feedback
301   mechanisms, present unique challenges for optimization. For instance, objectives involving direct
302   financial gain, such as price advantage, are not inherently differentiable due to the inclusion of
303   trading simulation steps. These require alternative approaches such as reinforcement learning, where
304   a model is refined based on a reward system derived from its trading performance.
305
      Remark: While we strive to faithfully reimplement models and reproduce results, discrepancies may
306
      arise between our versions and the original works. To foster transparency and community engage-
307   ment, we will open-source QuantBench’s implementation. We encourage contributions, including
308   corrections from original authors and new methods, to enrich and refine QuantBench.

      5     E VALUATION
312
      Task-specific Metrics Different tasks have different goals and thus require different evaluation
313
      metrics.
314
315   • Signal Model outputs used as alpha signals or stock scores can guide investment decisions. Com-
316     mon metrics for evaluating signal quality include Information Coefficient (IC), which measures
317     correlation between the signal and future returns, and ICIR, which adjusts IC for signal volatility.
318   • Portfolio Investment strategy performance can be measured using metrics like annualized return
319     for profitability and risk-adjusted returns like the Sharpe ratio. Other key metrics include max
320     drawdown (maximum loss during a period) and turnover (frequency of portfolio rebalancing).
321   • Execution These metrics assess the efficiency of trade implementation. Slippage measures the
322     difference between expected and actual execution prices, while market impact evaluates the ad-
323     verse effect of trades on market prices. Other costs such as commissions and transaction fees are
        also considered.

      Task-agnostic Metrics Due to the inherent nature of quantitative finance, we consider the follow-
325   ing additional metrics:
326
327   • Robustness This measures the model’s performance under varying market conditions and stress
328     tests. Key metrics include return stability, drawdown consistency, and sensitivity to input pertur-
329     bations, helping assess how well the model adapts to different environments.
330   • Correlation In multi-model or multi-strategy setups, correlation metrics like pairwise correlation
331     coefficients help assess the diversification benefits. Lower correlations indicate more diversified
332     signals or portfolios, which can improve overall risk-adjusted returns.
333

334

335     a model’s predictive power fades and when updates might be needed.

465   research into more efficient online learning and continual learning techniques that can reduce the
466   computational burden while still allowing for timely model updates.

484   fragments across the historical period). We also tested two training strategies: normal, where the
485   validation data is not used in training, and retrain, where the model is retrained using the entire

520   7     R ELATED W ORKS
521
522
      Quantitative modeling traditionally starts with multi-factor models. While being explainable, these
523   conventional models relying on linear regression and predefined factors often miss capturing com-
524   plex non-linear interactions among factors. In contrast, recent advancements have integrated AI into
525   quantitative finance (Kelly & Xiu, 2023; Cheng et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021; Sonkiya et al., 2022),
526   utilizing techniques from gradient-boosted trees (Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Ke et al., 2017) to deep
527   learning, which excel in identifying intricate patterns that enhance predictive accuracy. AI models
528   (Zhang et al., 2017; Qin et al., 2017; Du et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021a; Sawhney et al., 2021b;
529   Deng et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021b; Xu
530   et al., 2021) are trained to forecast market trends and guide portfolio optimization through mecha-
531
      nisms like reinforcement learning (Jiang et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021c; 2019b)
      and imitation learning (Niu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020), reflecting a holistic approach to modern
532
      quant modeling. Moreover, the specific properties of financial data calls for relevant studies in causal
533
      inference (Zhu et al., 2021), transfer learning (Li et al., 2022), ensemble learning (Sun et al., 2023b),
534   continual learning (Zhao et al., 2023), etc. Existing works have also explored evaluating these AI-
535   driven methods. Qlib (Yang et al., 2020) constructs a benchmark for stock prediction, but mostly
536   focus on temporal models and has an earlier cutoff (early 2022). FinRL-Meta (Liu et al., 2022) and
537   TradeMaster (Sun et al., 2023a) are two comprehensive platforms for quantitative investment with
538   a special focus on reinforcement learning. Compared with these works, QuantBench is designed to
539   provide a holistic view of the full quant research pipeline, rather than focusing on specific techniques
      such as reinforcement learning.
