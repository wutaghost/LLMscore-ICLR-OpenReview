009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Data valuation is crucial for assessing the impact and quality of individual data
012             points, enabling the ranking of data by importance for efficient data collection,
013             storage, and training. Many data valuation methods are sensitive to outliers and
014             require a large level of noise to effectively distinguish low-quality data from high-
015             quality data, making them particularly useful for data removal tasks. In particular,
016             optimal transport-based methods exhibit notable performance in outlier detection
017             but show only moderate effectiveness in high-quality data selection, due to their
018
                sensitivity to outliers and insensitivity to small variations. To mitigate the issue
                of insensitivity to high-quality data and facilitate effective data selection, in this
019
                paper, we propose a straightforward two-stage approach, JST, that initially does
020
                data valuation as usual, but then performs a second-round data selection where the
021             identified low-quality data points are designated as the validation set to perform
022             data valuation again. In this way, high-quality data become outliers with respect to
023             the new validation set and can be naturally identified1 . We empirically evaluate an
024             instantiation of our framework based on optimal transport method for data selection
025             and data pruning on several standard datasets and our framework demonstrates
026             superior performance compared to pure data valuation, especially under small noise
027             conditions. Additionally, we show the general applicability of our framework to
028             influence function based and reinforcement learning based data valuation methods.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Access to large, high-quality datasets is essential in machine learning. However, in real-world data
034   collection and curation pipelines, individual data points often inherit different levels of quality and
035   vary in their importance on the impact of training (Liang & Zou, 2022; Sorscher et al., 2022; Liu
036   et al., 2021). Therefore, it is critical to understand and assess such properties of data and to effectively
037
      prioritize highly valuable data sources for subset selection. This can assist practitioners in improving
      model performance efficiently (Karr et al., 2006; Jiang et al., 2023) and make strategic, cost-effective
038
      decisions in data marketplaces and exchanges (Olaleye & Adusei, 2024).
039
040   There have been considerable recent efforts to develop various data valuation methods aimed at
041   evaluating individual data points and assigning a value to each one (Jiang et al., 2023; Sim et al., 2022).
042   This can help to quantify differences between points and rank them based on their assigned value,
043
      establishing a certain order of quality or importance in the training process. For instance, methods
      based on optimal transport (Just et al., 2023) and influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017) employ
044
      sensitivity analysis to quantify the impact of individual data points on dataset distance and training
045
      outcome, respectively. Additionally, importance weights can be obtained through reinforcement
046   learning (Yoon et al., 2020) to evaluate individual data points.
047
048   In essence, these data valuation methods should naturally provide a metric for high-quality data
049
      subset selection. However, unfortunately, most data valuation methods have predominantly excelled
      in scenarios with large noise, including intensely corrupted samples (Just et al., 2023), randomly
050
      flipping labels (Just et al., 2023; Koh & Liang, 2017; Yoon et al., 2020), or domain adaption due to

074   in outlier detection but exhibits only moderate effectiveness in selecting high-quality data due to its

      To this end, we propose JST (Just Select Twice), a data subset selection framework that augments
081   existing data valuation methods by leveraging outliers detected by these methods, as illustrated in
082   Figure 2. Our key insight is that incorporating a second-round subset selection, using detected outliers
083   as the new validation set, allows high-quality data to be identified as outliers with respect to the new
084   validation set in this new context. This approach enhances the sensitivity of data valuation methods
085   to high-quality data, establishing a more meaningful order of "importance" or "quality" for subset
086   selection. Even with the inclusion of numerous high-quality data points within the new validation
087   set alongside low-quality data in the second-round subset selection due to the non state-of-the-art
088   performance of data valuation methods, we observe that the signals from such a validation set continue
089
      to suffice in achieving superior performance in subset selection compared to pure data valuation
      methods.
090
091    Before diving into the details, we summarize our contributions as follows: (1) JST, a novel and
092    straightforward two-stage framework augmenting existing data valuation methods, rendering them
093    more applicable for data subset selection tasks. (2) We empirically evaluate an instantiation of our
094    framework based on optimal transport method for data selection and data pruning on six standard
095
       datasets, showing its superior outperformance to pure data valuation, particularly in small noise
       settings. We also demonstrate its general applicability to influence function and reinforcement
096
       learning based data valuation methods.

099    2                          BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
100
101   We provide a small amount of background and describe related work.
102   Data Selection. Active learning and coreset construction are two widely used methods for data subset
103   selection. These methods aim to identify the most representative training data points. Active learning
104   involves iteratively choosing points to label from a large unlabeled dataset based on the model’s
105   uncertainty or other heuristics, such as the entropy of predicted class probabilities (Sener & Savarese,
106   2018; Coleman et al., 2020). Recently proposed data selection methods in continual learning are
107   related to these approaches, determining examples to be stored or labeled for ongoing model training
      (Castro et al., 2018; Aljundi et al., 2019).

124
      In contrast, coreset construction begins with the entire dataset and tries to select a small subset that
125
      encapsulates the essence of the full dataset. The goal is to have the model trained on the subset
126
      perform approximately as well as the one trained on the entire dataset (Feldman, 2020; Huang
127   et al., 2021). Many works have tackled this setting to accelerate clustering and learning, proposing
128   coresets in k-means and k-medians clustering (Har-Peled & Kushal, 2005), SVMs (Tsang et al.,
129   2005), Bayesian logistic regression (Huggins et al., 2016), and Bayesian inference (Campbell &
130   Broderick, 2019). Recently, newly proposed coreset selection methods have demonstrated efficiency
131   in neural network training (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021b;a; Tukan et al.,
132   2023). In addition, related coreset techniques have been applied to active learning (Sener & Savarese,
133   2018) and continual learning (Tiwari et al., 2022).
134   Data Valuation. Data valuation assesses the contribution of each individual data point to the overall
135   performance of a model, aiming to distribute the validation performance across the training data
136   points (Jiang et al., 2023). Formally, given a training dataset Dtr = {zi }N i=1 , a validation dataset
137   Dv , and a model performance metric PERF evaluated on the validation set Dv , data valuation
138   methods assign a scalar score to each training sample zi in Dtr to split PERF evaluated on Dv . For
139   instance, if we define a utility function U over all subsets S ⊆ Dtr of the training data as U (S) :=
140   PERF(A(S)) evaluated on the validation set Dv with a learning algorithm A, a straightforward
141
      method to evaluate the contribution of a training sample zi is to calculate the leave-one-out (LOO)
      value, U (Dtr ) − U (Dtr \ {zi }). This represents the change in model performance when the point is
142
      excluded from the training set (Just et al., 2023).
143
144   In practice, feasible alternatives to LOO can be used to estimate the impact of the weight change of a
145   data point on the model performance. Two such approaches are optimal transport-based methods
146   (LAVA) (Just et al., 2023) and influence function-based methods (Koh & Liang, 2017). The latter
147
      technique employs the Wasserstein distance between training and validation sets as the performance
      metric, while the influence function-based approach uses the validation loss. Both methods measure
148
      the gradient as the scalar value score with respect to the weight change of a data point. In addition,
149
      reinforcement learning based-methods (DVRL) (Yoon et al., 2020) learn a weight function to minimize
150   the weighted empirical risk using policy gradients, and so obtain optimal importance weights as the
151   scalar value scores.
152
153
      Indeed, data valuation is intimately related to data selection methods, but it formalizes the data
      selection problem by aiming to select a subset of the training set that matches a desired target
154
      distribution, as represented by the validation set (Just et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2020), because data
155
      valuation methods incorporate validation performance into individual training samples. This approach
156   is essentially different from active learning and coreset construction, which instead seek to compress
157   the full training dataset. Therefore, data valuation can be applied to data selection in the scenario of
158   robust learning with a mismatch between training set and target set (Yoon et al., 2020). However,
159   as demonstrated in Figure 1, data valuation methods are typically more sensitive to outliers rather
160   than high-quality data. This property makes data valuation an ineffective approach when seeking
161   to distinguish high-quality data at a finer-grained scale, especially when there is a small mismatch
      between the training and target sets. To address this issue, motivated by the property, we propose

      a straightforward two-stage framework, called JST, to make data valuation more suitable for data

        Practical Implementation. In practice, similar to other data valuation and selection methods
217     (Sorscher et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Just et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023), we begin by using a
218     neural network trained on the validation set Dv to extract features into a low-dimensional space.
219     This allows us to leverage feature relationships effectively and compute data value scores efficiently.
220     Given that the manually cleaned validation set Dv is often small, a ResNet-18 model (He et al., 2016)
221     is an appropriate choice for feature extraction. However, in the second round, prior knowledge from
222     the clean validation set Dv can erroneously align the new validation set Dsel containing low-quality
                                                     ′
223     data with the remaining training samples Dtr   , negatively impacting performance. Given our limited
224     access to noisy data, we find that leveraging a pretrained ResNet-18 model on ImageNet1K (Deng
225
        et al., 2009) suffices.
226     To maintain a perfect match between our algorithm and pure data valuation methods, we use the same
227     number |Dv | of backward training data points Dsel in the second round as the validation set, which
228     is always mixed with a non-obvious proportion of high-quality data. However, we find that behavior
229     does not affect the performance of our algorithm. This is because even a small portion of signals
230     from low-quality data is sufficient to pop out high-quality data with low value scores. Therefore,
231
        in practice, we can reduce the size of the validation set |Dsel | to involve more training samples for
        selection in the second round without degrading performance.

      5     E XPERIMENTAL E VALUATION
271
272
      In this section, we conduct experiments to verify the effectiveness of our proposed JST framework in
273   augmenting data valuation methods for selecting data that align with the distribution of validation set
274   from training set. We focus on two main tasks: high-quality data selection (Section 5.1) and raw
275   dataset pruning (Section 5.2), using the JST framework instantiated with the optimal transport based
276   data valuation method. Additionally, we demonstrate the general applicability of our JST framework
277   to other data valuation methods, including influence function based and reinforcement learning based
278   approaches (Section 5.4). To cover the complexity of tasks across different scales, we utilize several
279   datasets, including MNIST, Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky
280   et al., 2009), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), and Food-101 (Bossard et al., 2014).
281
282   5.1   H IGH - QUALITY DATA S ELECTION
283
      In real-world scenarios, training data often contain corrupted images (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019;
284
      Li et al., 2020). We evaluate the effectiveness of JST framework in selecting high-quality data in the
285
      presence of corrupted images.
286
287   Setup. We employ four standard datasets: MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.
288   For all four datasets, we inject white noise into 25% of the training set and utilize the clean test set
289   as the validation set. To demonstrate the superior performance of JST framework compared with
290   the pure data valuation method, we add two levels of small noise to the training set as depicted
291
      in Figure 3 for noise-level comparative analysis. To achieve the goal of identifying "high-quality"
      training samples, we rank data points based on their value scores in descending order, selecting a
292
      subset with the highest values. For every selection budget, we compute the selection precision, i.e, the
293
      percentage of the points that are uncorrupted within the selected points. We compare our framework
294   with pure data valuation method and random selection baseline across these four datasets.
295
296   Results. As depicted in Figure 3, our experimental results show a notable enhancement in selection
297   precision across all four datasets, where our JST framework outperforms the pure data valuation
298   method on nearly all selection budgets. Globally, our framework significantly improves the mean
299
      rank of uncorrupted data compared to pure data valuation methods. With a very small noise level of
      standard deviation 0.6 and 3.0 (Figure 3 A), our framework push forward the mean rank by 3,487 for
300
      MNIST, 1,798 for Fashion-MNIST, 700 for CIFAR-10, and 861 for CIFAR-100, from initial values
301
      of 24,814, 24,269, 22,212, and 22,106, respectively. This underscores the feasibility of employing
302   data selection using our framework under challenging circumstances with small noise. When facing
303   relatively larger noise with a standard deviation of 1.2 and 6.0 (Figure 3 B), our framework still
304   achieves improvements of 785 for MNIST, 607 for Fashion-MNIST, 843 for CIFAR-10, and 1,167
305   for CIFAR-100, from initial values of 23,064, 23,072, 21,869, and 21,937, respectively. Remarkably,
306   as illustrated in Figure 4, with a noise level of standard deviation 0.6, the data valuation method
307   after being augmented by our framework achieves near-perfect separation between uncorrupted and
308   corrupted data in the simple datasets MNIST and Fashion-MNIST. Furthermore, Figure 4 clearly
309   confirms the feasibility of the principle of our framework, wherein high-quality data are assigned
310
      low value scores and popped out as outliers in the second-round data selection, vice versa to the
      first-round data valuation.

      5.2   R AW DATASET P RUNING
314

385   5.3   C ROSS -D OMAIN R ETRIEVAL
386
387   In real-world scenarios, datasets often consist of samples from multiple domains with imbalanced
388   proportions. Cross-domain retrieval aims to identify and select relevant samples from such a mixed-
389   domain dataset to align with the distribution of a given target domain.
390   Setup. We conduct experiments on one time-series tabular dataset: HHAR (Stisen et al., 2015).
391   HHAR dataset is designed to predict human activities within specific time segments, classifying them
392   into six categories: biking, sitting, standing, walking, walking upstairs, and walking downstairs.
393
      Following the data preprocessing method outlined in previous studies (Ragab et al., 2023; He et al.,
394
      2023), HHAR dataset is segmented into non-overlapping segments of 128 time steps for classification
395
      and then split into training and testing sets as applied in prior research. We further divide HHAR
396   sequential data segments into four domains based on the devices and motion sensors used: phone-
397   accelerometer, watch-accelerometer, phone-gyroscope, and watch-gyroscope. To comprehensively
398   evaluate the effectiveness of our JST framework for cross-domain retrieval, we conduct experiments
399   using each of these domains as the validation set, extracted from the testing set. The training set is
400   treated as a mixed-domain dataset.
401
      Based on previous studies (Kwon & Zou, 2021; Wang & Jia, 2023; Kwon & Zou, 2023), we apply the
402   k-means clustering algorithm to the data values. This approach allows us to partition the training set
403   into two groups: a potential target domain data group and an other data group. Given our expectation
404   that potential target domain data are likely to have high values, we identify the cluster with the higher
405   average data value as the potential target domain data group for retrieval. Following common practice
406   in the literature, we compare the clustering results against the annotated ground truth and calculate
407   the F1-score to evaluate our JST framework. We compare our JST framework with the pure data
408   valuation method. Note that for a fair comparison, we calculate the F1-score using only the data
409   propagated into the second round of our JST framework for the pure data valuation method.

      6   W HERE D OES THE JST F RAMEWORK U NDERPERFORM ?
487
488
      Finally, we discuss scenarios where our JST framework underperforms, showing a similar selection
489   precision to the pure data valuation method. Specifically, we observe that marginal contribution based
490   data valuation methods yield unsatisfactory results, as these methods fail to demonstrate a higher
491   detection rate for outliers compared to in-distribution data. We test our framework on three marginal
492   contribution based methods: LOO (Jiang et al., 2023), AME (Lin et al., 2022), and Data Banzhaf
493   (Wang & Jia, 2023). The experimental setup is the same as in Section 5.4 on CIFAR-10 with white
494   noise standard deviation of 10.0, except the reduced size of training set (5,000) and validation set
495   (500) for LOO to save runtime. As shown in Figure 8 A, these three methods augmented by our
496   framework do not improve selection precision. This is because these methods lack the property of
497
      higher sensitivity to outliers (Figure 8 B). Based on this underperformance analysis, we propose
      a simple test framework to validate the expected behavior of our approach: before applying it to
498
      real-world applications, we can assess whether the data valuation method exhibits higher sensitivity
499
      to outliers on a specific type of data.

502   7   C ONCLUSION
503
504   In this paper, we introduce JST, a straightforward two-stage framework designed to enhance the
505   sensitivity of existing data valuation methods to high-quality data for subset selection. The JST
506
      framework identifies low-quality data points as a validation set, thereby allowing high-quality
      data to stand out as outliers during the data valuation process. Experiments on multiple datasets
507
      demonstrate the effectiveness of JST framework in selecting high-quality data and pruning raw
508
      datasets, particularly in scenarios with small noise. We successfully apply JST to different data
509   valuation methods, highlighting the general applicability of our framework, thus making it a valuable
510   tool for enhancing data selection in machine learning.
511
512
      However, our work focuses solely on data valuation and selection based on loss and accuracy, without
      considering other important aspects such as fairness across subpopulations, which is crucial in data-
513
      centric methods. Additionally, adapting our framework to a wider range of data valuation methods
514
      and the currently heated LLM model settings remains a practical interest. We leave these two aspects
515   for future work.

708

      method. Although we have defined notations in the preliminaries section, a thorough set of notations
709
      is presented here for clarity. The input space is denoted by X and the output space by Y. We denote
710

713   Typically, n > m and psrc (z) is not required to be the same as ptrg (z ′ ), i.e., psrc (z) ̸= ptrg (z ′ ).
714   The goal of data valuation is to distribute the validation performance across training data points and
715   compute a data score value s(zi ) for each training data point zi .

717   LAVA (O PTIMAL T RANSPORT BASED )

721   set Dv changes when a particular data point in Dtr is assigned increased weight. The sensitivity is
722
      determined by calculating the gradient of the optimal transport cost with respect to the probability
      mass.
