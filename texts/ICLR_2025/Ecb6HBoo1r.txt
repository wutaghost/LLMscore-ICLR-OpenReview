009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Gene regulatory network (GRN) inference is crucial for cell fate decision, as it
012            outlines the regulations between genes, which direct cell differentiation. Although
013            there have been some work to infer cell lineage GRN, they fail to capture the con-
014            tinuous nature of the differentiation process as they group cells by cell type or
015            cluster and infer GRN in a discrete manner. In this paper, we hypothesize GRN
016
               can forecast future gene expression based on history information and transform
               the inference process into a multivariate time series forecasting problem, linking
017
               cells at different time to learn temporal dynamics and inferring GRN in a contin-
018
               uous process. We introduce MTGRN, a transformer-based model that only takes
019            single cell data as input to infer the cell lineage GRN by forecasting gene expres-
020            sion. MTGRN consists of temporal blocks and spatial blocks, effectively captures
021            the connections between cells along their developmental trajectories and leverages
022            prior knowledge to elucidate regulatory interactions among genes. It significantly
023            outperforms six other methods across five datasets, demonstrating superior per-
024            formance even compared to multimodal approaches. Based on the inferred GRN,
025            MTGRN pinpoints three crucial genes associated with the development of mouse
026            embryonic stem cells and depicts the activity changes of these genes during cel-
027
               lular differentiation. Beyond this, MTGRN is capable of conducting perturbation
               experiments on key genes and accurately modeling the change of cell identity
028
               following the knockout of the Gata1 in mouse hematopoietic stem cells.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Organism develops from a single fertilized egg, which undergoes differentiation to produce various
034   specialized cell types necessary for maintaining essential life activities. Although all cells in a
035   multicellular organism share the same genome, the complex regulatory relationships between genes
036   lead to selective gene expression in different cell types, resulting in the translation of distinct proteins
037   that drive cells toward specific functional fates (Keller, 2005). As shown in Figure 1 (a), gene
038   expression is primarily regulated by transcription factors (TFs), which are proteins that bind to distal
039
      cis-regulatory elements (CREs) on DNA and work in concert with cofactors and other proteins to
      recruit and stabilize the RNA polymerase complex. This, in turn, modulates the transcriptional rate
040
      of target genes (TGs), either positively or negatively. In addition to TFs, other elements such as
041
      splicing factors, microRNAs, and metabolites can also regulate gene expression. However, in this
042   paper, we focus exclusively on the regulatory interactions between TFs and TGs.
043
044   Gene regulatory network (GRN) serves as computational models to explain the regulation of gene
045
      expression, which are mathematically defined as graphs (Dai et al., 2024). It’s noteworthy that TFs
      are gene products, the regulatory relationship between a TF and its TG can be understood as the
046
      relationship between the gene which encodes the TF (TFG) and the TG. Figure 1 (b) demonstrates
047
      the topology of GRN, the nodes of the GRN are composed of genes, with some genes are TFGs,
048   while the remaining genes are TGs. The edges of the GRN represent regulatory interactions be-
049   tween genes, with directionality pointing from TFGs to TGs. Inferring cell lineage GRN has been
050   a longstanding goal in biology. Since GRN can be represented as graph, uncovering its topological
051   features and dynamic changes is crucial for understanding how cellular identities are established
052   and maintained, which holds significant potential for engineering cell fates and disease prevention.
053   Originally, GRN was typically constructed by inferring gene co-expression from bulk transcriptomic
      data (Margolin et al., 2006; Langfelder & Horvath, 2008; Huynh-Thu et al., 2010). However, such

076   statistically-driven approaches require a large number of samples, which severely limit their practi-
077   cal applicability. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) (Tang et al., 2009) has addressed some
078   of these limitations by enabling high-throughput sequencing of tens of thousands of cells, allowing
079   researchers to infer GRN at relatively low cost. However, scRNA-seq data do not directly cap-
080   ture many underlying regulatory mechanisms, such as the TF protein abundance and DNA binding
081   events. As a result, some researchers incorporated additional regulatory information, such as single-
082   cell ATAC sequencing (scATAC-seq) (Buenrostro et al., 2015) data into their models, leading to the
083
      development of multimodal GRN inference methods (Kartha et al., 2022; Kamimoto et al., 2023;
      Wang et al., 2023a). Despite the promising results of these approaches, several challenges remain:
084
      1) Due to technical limitations and cost constraints, it is difficult to obtain samples with multiple
085
      modalities in practical settings, making large-scale application of these methods challenging. 2)
086   These methods often split cells into groups based on cell type or cluster and infer GRN separately
087   for each group. It disregards the continuous nature of cellular development and fails to capture the
088   dynamic changes during cell differentiation.
089
      To address these challenges, we introduce MTGRN, a transformer-based model comprising both
090
      temporal and spatial blocks. MTGRN only utilizes scRNA-seq data as input and frames GRN in-
091
      ference as a multivariate time-series (MTS) forecasting task by assigning pseudotime to each cell
092   along the developmental trajectory. Incorporating prior knowledge from databases and experimental
093   results, MTGRN predicts gene expression at the next N time points based on the gene expression of
094   cells from the previous M time points, learning an attention matrix in the spatial block. This matrix
095   captures the attention scores between genes, which we interpret as the inferred GRN along the cell
096   lineage. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
097
098
              • Our method is the first work to frame GRN inference task in a MTS forecasting problem.
                Unlike previous methods that group cells and infer GRN in a discrete manner, we link
099
                cells along the developmental trajectory using pseudotime and infer GRN in a continuous
100
                process through MTS prediction, enabling a deeper understanding of the dynamic changes
101             in cellular development.
102
              • Our method achieves superior performance across five datasets compared to other ap-
103
                proaches.
104
105           • Our method is capable of identifying key TFGs that have been validated by biological
106
                experiments.
107           • Our method enables in silico perturbation experiments by propagating the effects through
                the inferred GRN, simulating the cellular response to perturbations.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Over the past few decades, numerous methods for GRN inference have emerged. We categorize
111   these methods into unimodal and multimodal approaches, which we will introduce in the follow-
112   ing paragraphs. Additionally, we will present the trajectory inference (TI) methods employed in
113   MTGRN for pseudotime calculation.
114
115   Unimodal GRN inference. These methods only take scRNA-seq data as input, aiming to infer
116   regulatory relationships between genes based solely on gene expression information. One of the
117   most commonly used approaches is Weighted Gene Co-expression Network Analysis (WGCNA)
118   (Langfelder & Horvath, 2008), which identifies modules of co-expressed genes by calculating pair-
119   wise correlations across the transcriptome. However, since WGCNA built undirected networks
120
      based purely on co-expression, it lacks the ability to infer causal regulatory relationships, leading
      to many false positive associations. To overcome these issues, methods like GENIE3 (Huynh-Thu
121
      et al., 2010) and its more efficient variant GRNBoost2 (Moerman et al., 2019) incorporated prior
122
      knowledge of regulatory activity to predict target gene expression based only on TF expression,
123   thereby reducing the number of potential interactions. In addition, NetREX (Wang et al., 2018) re-
124   configured the prior network to find the optimal topology that explains the observed expression data,
125   enhancing the inference of regulatory networks and CEFCON (Wang et al., 2023b) utilized graph
126   neural network to refine the prior network and obtained the ultimate GRN. DTGN (Guo & Xiao,
127   2024) proposed a framework for identifying phenotype-specific transcription factors and pathways
128   by constructing dynamic transcriptional regulatory networks using time-series gene expression data
129   and a graph autoencoder model. However, relying exclusively on transcriptomic data will generate
130   false positives, as other regulatory mechanisms, such as chromatin accessibility, are often neglected.
131
      These shortcomings limit the accuracy of network inference.
132
133
      Multimodal GRN inference. Recent advances in scATAC-seq, which provides insights into chro-
      matin accessibility, identifying regulatory elements and transcription factor binding sites, have al-
134
      lowed for the refinement of GRN reconstruction in a multimodal manner. CellOracle (Kamimoto
135
      et al., 2023) integrated scATAC-seq data to simulate changes in cell identity following TF perturba-
136   tions. By combining computational perturbation with GRN modeling, CellOracle enabled a system-
137   atic interpretation of context-dependent TF functions in regulating cell identity. Dictys (Wang et al.,
138   2023a) constructed a initial TF binding network using scATAC-seq data and refined it by modeling
139   transcriptional dynamics with an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process to capture biological variabil-
140   ity. While multimodal approaches have delivered promising results in GRN inference tasks, most of
141   them clustered cells and then inferred a GRN for each cluster. This discretized strategy disrupts the
142   continuous nature of cellular development.
143
144   Trajectory inference. TI methods assign to every cell a so-called pseudotime, a numeric value in
145   arbitrary units which measures how far a particular cell is within a dynamic process of interest. By
146
      ordering the cells according to this pseudotime, it becomes possible to define the different transition
      stages through which a cell progresses during its dynamic process. Currently, there are numerous TI
147
      methods, such as PAGA (Wolf et al., 2019), Slingshot (Street et al., 2018), and Waterfall (Shin et al.,
148
      2015). These methods take either a single snapshot of a mixture of cells at different stages or a set
149   of samples collected at multiple time points as input. According to these single cell data, they aim to
150   order the cells based on an underlying dynamic process that accounts for the heterogeneity observed
151   in the sample. The goal of TI methods is to automatically reconstruct the cellular dynamic process by
152   arranging individual cells sampled and profiled from the process along a trajectory. This trajectory
153   is then used to identify the various stages in the dynamic process and reveal their interrelationships.

      To address the limitation of previous methods, which require cells to be split into groups by cell
207   type or cluster, we employ TI method to transform the original single-cell data into time-series data

213   thus transforming E into a time series data E (t) ∈ R T × G , where T is the number of time steps.
214   It’s worth noting that T is equal to C, as each cell corresponds to a pseudotime on differentiation
215   trajectory. In this way, our MTS data E (t) = [e 1 , . . . , e T ] T possesses the temporal relationships

301   Once the model is trained, we fix the parameters of MTGRN and use the test data to compute the
302

400   5.2      MTGRN I DENTIFIES KEY TFG S
401
402   Identifying and understanding the key TFGs that drive cell differentiation is crucial for uncovering
403   the fundamental biological mechanisms of life. These TFGs not only play a central role in under-
404   standing cell fate decisions in basic research but also have significant potential applications in stem
405
      cell therapy, disease research. For example, recognizing specific TFGs can deepen our understand-
      ing of the molecular basis of diseases, particularly those caused by aberrant gene regulation, such
406
      as cancer or developmental disorders (Anderson et al., 2023; Walsh et al., 2017). Additionally, by
407
      manipulating these critical TFGs, scientists can precisely control cell differentiation in experimental
408   settings, paving the way for the development of innovative cell therapies and organ regeneration
409   techniques.
410
411
      We chose mouse embryonic stem cells, the mESC dataset shown in Table 1 to evaluate our model’s
      ability in identifying key TFGs. Mouse embryonic stem cells exhibit high pluripotency, allowing
412
      them to differentiate into nearly all cell types within the embryo, including neurons, cardiomyocytes,
413
      and hepatocytes. This remarkable differentiation potential makes mESC an ideal model for study-
414   ing developmental biology and the underlying mechanisms of cellular differentiation. We trained
415   MTGRN on the mESC dataset and used the AUCELL algorithm proposed by SCENIC (Aibar et al.,
416   2017) to score the activity of each TFG’s regulon in each cell and identify the key TFGs based on
417   the activity score.
418
      Specifically, AUCELL takes a gene set as input and outputs the gene set activity score for each cell.
419
      As shown in Figure 5 (a), these gene sets correspond to regulons, which are composed of TFGs and
420   their predicted target genes. AUCELL calculates the enrichment of each regulon by determining the
421   area under the recovery curve (AUC) based on the ranking of all genes in a given cell, where genes
422   are ranked according to their expression levels. In brief, as illustrated in Figure 5 (b), the x-axis
423   represents the ranking of all genes by expression level (genes with identical expression values, such
424   as 0, are randomly ordered), and the y-axis represents the number of genes recovered from the input
425   TFG regulon. AUCELL uses the AUC to assess whether a critical subset of the input regulon is
426   enriched at the top of the ranking for each cell (by default, selecting the top 5% of genes). This way,
427   the AUC score reflects the proportion of genes from the TFG regulon that are highly expressed in
428
      each cell, with higher scores indicating greater TFG activity in that cell. The output of this step is a
      matrix containing AUC scores for each TFG regulon across all cells.
429
430   After the calculation of the activity score for all TFGs involved in the GRN inferred by MTGRN, we
431   divided the cells into two groups based on developmental pseudotime, representing early and late
      developmental stages. For each TFG, we conducted differential analysis between the early-stage

462   5.3        MTGRN P ERFORMS I N S ILICO G ENE P ERTURBATION
463
464   MTGRN not only can identify key TFGs involved in cell development but also enable in silico per-
465
      turbation experiments. Unlike traditional laboratory perturbations, in silico experiments allow for
      the rapid and cost-effective exploration of potential changes within large gene regulatory networks,
466
      eliminating the need for extensive experimental resources or time-consuming wet-lab procedures.
467
      By simulating gene knockouts or overexpression scenarios, we can predict how gene regulatory
468   networks will respond to various perturbations, thus accelerating our understanding of critical regu-
469   latory factors.
470
471
      We selected the mouse hematopoietic stem cell (mHSC) dataset from Table 1 for analysis. Figure 6
      (a) illustrates a force-directed (FA) visualization of the three differentiation lineages (i.e., mHSC-E,
472
      mHSC-GM, mHSC-L). The cell type annotations were obtained from Nestorowa et al. (2016): HSC
473
      refers to hematopoietic stem cells, MPP to multipotent progenitors, LMPP to lymphoid multipotent
474   progenitors, CMP to common myeloid progenitors, MEP to megakaryocyte-erythrocyte progenitors,
475   and GMP to granulocyte-monocyte progenitors. Figure 6 (b) provides a detailed representation of
476   the three differentiation lineages of mHSC. It starts from HSC and eventually leading to the forma-
477   tion of lymphoid cells, erythrocytes, granulocytes and monocytes. Gata1 is known to orchestrate
478   significant changes in the expression of genes throughout the erythrocytes differentiation process,
479   driving critical steps required for the proliferation and differentiation of erythrocytes progenitors. As
480   shown in Figure 6 (c), Gata1 expression is initiated at the CMP stage during early erythrocytes com-
481   mitment and gradually decreases as erythrocytes mature (Moriguchi & Yamamoto, 2014). Given
482
      that Gata1 is a well-established TFG, we aim to assess whether MTGRN can accurately simulate
      the effects of knocking out this gene.
483

      6      C ONCLUSION
526
527   We introduced MTGRN, the first approach that formulated the GRN inference process as a MTS
528   problem. Our method inferred GRN in a continuous process compared to approaches that group cells
529   by type or cluster. MTGRN uses only scRNA-seq data as input, combined with prior knowledge to
530   learn cell lineage GRNs. MTGRN outperformed six other inference methods (one for multimodal
531
      inference method) across five datasets. Furthermore, in the mESC dataset, MTGRN successfully
      identified three key TFGs previously reported and the activity scores of theses TFG all exhibited
532
      clear trends of change (initially high then low or initially low then high), with Nanog, Sox2, and
533
      Pou5f1 (Oct3/4) all showing a trend from high to low. This is consistent with previous reports that
534   the three TFGs are crucial for maintaining the pluripotency of stem cells, in other words, they are
535   highly expressed in the initial embryonic stem cells and have lower expression in differentiated cells.
536   In addition, in the mHSC-E dataset, after knocking out Gata1 and propagating the influence through
537   our inferred GRN, we validated that the knock out of Gata1 significantly inhibited the differentiation
538   of CMP and MEP cells, which is consistent with the finding that Gata1 promotes the differentiation
539   of these two cell types into erythrocytes. All of results demonstrate the accuracy of our inferred
      GRN and we think MTGRN offers a novel approach to GRN inference.
