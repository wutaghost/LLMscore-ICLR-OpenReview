                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
                Meta reinforcement learning (meta-RL) methods such as RL2 have emerged as
012             promising approaches for learning data-efficient RL algorithms tailored to a given
013             task distribution. However, they show poor asymptotic performance and struggle
014             with out-of-distribution tasks because they rely on sequence models, such as re-
015             current neural networks or transformers, to process experiences rather than sum-
016             marize them using general-purpose RL components such as value functions. In
017             contrast, traditional RL algorithms are data-inefficient as they do not use domain
018             knowledge, but do converge to an optimal policy in the limit. We propose RL3 ,
019             a principled hybrid approach that incorporates action-values, learned per task via
020
                traditional RL, in the inputs to meta-RL. We show that RL3 earns greater cumu-
                lative reward in the long term compared to RL2 while drastically reducing meta-
021
                training time and generalizes better to out-of-distribution tasks. Experiments are
022
                conducted on both custom and benchmark discrete domains from the meta-RL lit-
023             erature that exhibit a range of short-term, long-term, and complex dependencies.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Reinforcement learning (RL) has been shown to produce effective policies in a variety of appli-
029   cations including both virtual (Mnih et al., 2015) and embodied (Schulman et al., 2017; Haarnoja
030   et al., 2018) systems. However, traditional RL algorithms have three major drawbacks: they can be
031   slow to converge, require a large amount of data, and often have difficulty generalizing to out-of-
032   distribution (OOD) tasks not practiced during training. These shortcomings are especially glaring
033   in settings where the goal is to learn policies for a collection or distribution of problems that share
034   some similarities, and for which traditional RL must start from scratch for each problem. For exam-
035
      ple, many robotic manipulation tasks require interacting with an array of objects with similar but not
      identical shapes, sizes, weights, materials, and appearances, such as mugs and cups. It is likely that
036
      effective manipulation strategies for these tasks will be similar, but they may also differ in ways that
037
      make it challenging to learn a single policy that is highly successful on all instances. Recently, meta
038   reinforcement learning (meta-RL) has been proposed as an approach to mitigate these shortcomings
039   by deriving RL algorithms (or meta-RL policies) that adapt efficiently to a distribution of tasks that
040   share some common structure (Duan et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016).
041
      While meta-RL systems represent a significant improvement over traditional RL in such settings,
042
      they still require large amounts of data during meta-training time, can have poor asymptotic perfor-
043
      mance during adaptation, and although they “learn to learn,” they often generalize poorly to tasks

056   models cannot handle arbitrary amounts of data effectively and lack integrated general-purpose RL
057   components that could induce a broader generalization bias.
058
      Hence, we propose RL3 , an approach that embeds the strengths of traditional RL within meta-RL.
059
      Table 1 highlights our primary aims and the foremost insight informing our approach. The key
060
      idea in RL3 is an additional ‘object-level’ RL procedure executed within the meta-RL architecture
061   that computes task-specific optimal Q-value estimates as supplementary inputs to the meta-learner,
062   in conjunction with sequences of states, actions and rewards. In principle, our approach allows
063   the meta-learner to learn how to optimally fuse raw experience data with summarizations provided
064   by the Q-estimates. Ultimately, RL3 leverages Q-estimates’ generality, ability to compress large
065   amounts of experiences into useful summaries, direct actionability, and asymptotic optimality to
066   enhance long-term performance and OOD generalization and drastically reduce meta-training time.
067   While Q-value estimates can be injected into any other meta-RL algorithm, for clarity of exposition,
068   we implement RL3 by injecting Q-value estimates into one of the most popular and easily understood
069   meta-RL algorithm, RL2 (Duan et al., 2016) (hence, the name RL3 ). However, it should be noted
070   that our baseline implementation of RL2 includes significant enhancements like using transformers
071   instead of LSTMs to improve long-context reasoning, in addition to incorporating numerous recom-
072   mendations from Ni et al. (2022) that have been shown to make recurrent model-free RL algorithms
073   like RL2 competitive with state-of-the-art meta-RL baselines like VeriBAD (Zintgraf et al., 2020).
074   The primary contribution of this paper is a proof-of-concept that injecting Q-estimates obtained via
075   traditional object-level RL alongside the typical experience histories within a meta-RL agent leads
076   to higher long-term returns and better OOD generalization, while maintaining short-term efficiency.
077   We further demonstrate that our approach can also work with an abstract, or coarse, representation
078   of the object-level MDP. We experiment with discrete domains that both reflect the challenges faced
079   by meta-RL and simultaneously allow transparent analysis of the results. Finally, we examine the
080   key insights that inform our approach and show theoretically that object-level Q-values are directly
081
      related to the optimal meta-value function.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Although meta-RL is a fairly new topic of research, the general concept of meta-learning is decades
086   old (Vilalta & Drissi, 2002), which, coupled with a significant number of design decisions for
087   meta-RL systems, has created a large number of different proposals for how systems ought to best
088   exploit the resources available within their deployment contexts (Beck et al., 2023). At a high
089   level, most meta-RL algorithms can be categorized as either parameterized policy gradient (PPG)
090   models (Finn et al., 2017; Li et al., 2017; Sung et al., 2017; Al-Shedivat et al., 2018; Gupta et al.,
091   2018; Yoon et al., 2018; Stadie et al., 2018; Vuorio et al., 2019; Zintgraf et al., 2019; Raghu et al.,
092   2019; Kaushik et al., 2020; Ghadirzadeh et al., 2021; Mandi et al., 2022) or black box models (Duan
093
      et al., 2016; Heess et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2018; Mishra et al., 2018; Humplik
      et al., 2019; Fakoor et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2020; Zintgraf et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Emukpere
094
      et al., 2021; Beck et al., 2022). PPG approaches assume that the underlying learning process is best
095
      represented as a policy gradient, where the set of parameters that define the underlying algorithm
096   ultimately form a differentiable set of meta-parameters that the meta-RL system may learn to
097   adjust. The additional structure provided by this assumption, combined with the generality of policy
098   gradient methods, means that typically PPG methods retain greater generalization capabilities on
099   out-of-distribution tasks. However, due to their inherent data requirements, PPG methods are often
100   slower to adapt and initially train.
101
      In this paper we focus on black box models, which represent the meta-learning function as a neural
102   network, often a recurrent neural network (RNN) (Duan et al., 2016; Heess et al., 2015; Wang et al.,
103   2016; Humplik et al., 2019; Fakoor et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2020; Zintgraf et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
104   2021) or a transformer (Mishra et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021; Melo, 2022). There are also several
105   hybrid approaches that combine PPG and black box methods, either during meta-training (Ren et al.,
106   2023) or fine-tuning (Lan et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2021). Using black box models simplifies the
107   process of augmenting meta states with Q-estimates and allows us to retain relatively better data effi-
      ciency while relying on the Q-value injections for better long-term performance and generalization.

108
      Meta-RL systems may also leverage extra information available during training, such as task
109   identification (Humplik et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021). Such ‘privileged information’ can of course
110   lead to more performant systems, but is not universally available. As our hypothesis does not rely
111   on the availability of such information, we expect our approach to be orthogonal to, and compatible
112   with, such methods. Black box meta-RL systems that do not use privileged information still vary in
113   several ways, including the choice between on-policy and off-policy learning and, in systems that
114   use neural networks, the choice between transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) and RNNs (Elman,
115   1990; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Cho et al., 2014).
116   The most relevant methods to our work are end-to-end methods, which use a single function
117   approximator to subsume both learner and meta-learner, such as RL2 (Duan et al., 2016), L2L
118   (Wang et al., 2016), SNAIL (Mishra et al., 2018), and E-RL2 (Stadie et al., 2018), and methods
119   that exploit the formal description of the meta-RL problem as a POMDP or a Bayes-adaptive MDP
120   (BAMDP) (Duff, 2002). These methods attempt to learn policies conditioned on the BAMDP belief
121   state while also approximating this belief state by, for example, variational inference (VariBAD)
122   (Zintgraf et al., 2020; Dorfman et al., 2020), or random network distillation on belief states
123
      (HyperX) (Zintgraf et al., 2021). Or, they simply encode enough experience history to approximate
      POMDP beliefs (RL2 ) (Duan et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016).
124
125   Our proposed method is an end-to-end system that exploits the BAMDP structure of the meta-RL
126   problem by spending a small amount of extra computation to provide inputs to the end-to-end learner
127   that more closely resemble important constituents of BAMDP value functions. Thus, the primary
128   difference between this work and previous work is the injection of Q-value estimates into the meta-
129
      RL agent state at each meta-step, in addition to the state-action-reward histories. In this work, our
      approach, RL3 , is implemented by simply injecting Q-value estimates into RL2 alongside experience
130
      history, although any other meta-RL algorithm can be used.

138   3.1   PARTIALLY O BSERVABLE MDP S
139
140   We use the standard notation defining a Markov decision process (MDP) as a tuple M =
141
      ⟨S, A, T, R⟩, where S is a set of states; A is a set of actions; T is the transition and R is the reward
      function. A partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) extends MDPs to settings with
142
      partially observable states. A POMDP is described as a tuple ⟨S, A, T, R, Ω, O⟩, where S, A, T, R
143
      are as in an MDP. Ω is the set of possible observations, and O : S × A × Ω → [0, 1] is an observa-
144   tion function representing the probability of receiving observation ω after performing action a and
145   transitioning to state s′ . POMDPs can alternatively be represented as continuous-state belief-MDPs
146   where a belief state b ∈ ∆|S| is a probability distribution over all states. In this representation, a
147   policy π is a mapping from belief states to actions, π : ∆|S| → A.
148
149
150
      3.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
151
      Reinforcement learning (RL) agents learn an optimal policy given an MDP with unknown dynamics
152
      using only transition and reward feedback. This is often done by incrementally estimating the opti-
153   mal action-value function Q∗ (s, a) (Watkins & Dayan, 1992), which satisfies the Bellman optimality
154   equation Q∗ (s, a) = Es′ [R(s, a) + γ maxa′ ∈A Q∗ (s′ , a′ )]. In large or continuous state settings, it is
155   popular to use deep neural networks to represent the action-value functions (Mnih et al., 2015). We
156   denote the vector representing the Q-estimates of all actions at state s as Q(s), and after t feedback
157   steps, as Qt (s). Q-learning is known to converge asymptotically (Sutton & Barto, 2018), provided
158   each state-action pair is explored sufficiently. As a rough general statement, ||Qt (s) − Q∗ (s)||∞
159   is proportional to ≈ √1t , with strong results on the convergence error available (Szepesvári, 1997;
160   Kearns & Singh, 1998; Even-Dar et al., 2003). The theoretical objective in RL is to optimize the
161   value of the final policy i.e., the cumulative reward per episode, disregarding the data cost incurred
      and the cumulative reward missed (or regret) during learning due to suboptimal exploration.

203   4     RL3
204
205   To address the limitations of black box meta-RL methods, we propose RL3 , a principled approach
206   that leverages (1) the inherent generality of action-value estimates, (2) their ability to compress ex-
207
      perience histories into useful summaries, (3) their direct actionability & asymptotic optimality, (4)
      their ability to inform task-identification, and (5) their relation to the optimal meta-value function,
208
      in order to enhance out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization and performance over extended adap-
209
      tation periods. The central, novel mechanism in RL3 is an additional ‘object-level’ RL procedure
210   executed within the meta-RL architecture, shown in Fig. 1, that computes task-specific optimal Q-
211   value estimates Qti (st ) and state-action counts as supplementary inputs to the meta-RL policy in
212   conjunction with the sequence of states, actions and rewards (s0 , a0 , r0 , ..., st ). The Q-estimates are
213   computed off-policy, and may involve model estimation and planning, for greater data efficiency.
214   The estimates and the counts are reset at the beginning of each meta-episode as a new task Mi is
215   sampled. In all subsequent text, Q-value estimates used as input entail the inclusion of state-action
      counts as well. We now present a series of key insights informing our approach.

234   First, estimating action-values is a key component in many universal RL algorithms, and
235   asymptotically, they fully inform optimal behavior irrespective of domain. Strategies for optimal
236   exploration-exploitation trade-off are domain-dependent and rely on historical data, yet many explo-
237
      ration approaches use estimated Q-values and some notion of counts alone, such as epsilon-greedy,
      Boltzmann exploration, upper confidence bounds (UCB/UCT) (Auer, 2002; Kocsis & Szepesvári,
238
      2006), count-based exploration (Tang et al., 2017), curiosity based exploration (Burda et al., 2019)
239
      and maximum-entropy RL (Haarnoja et al., 2018). This creates a strong empirical case that using
240   Q-value estimates and state-action counts for efficient exploration has inherent generality.
241
242   Second, Q-estimates summarize experience histories of arbitrary length and order in one constant-
243
      size vector. This mapping is many-to-one, and any permutation of transitions (⟨s, a, r, s′ ⟩ tuples) or
      episodes in a history of experiences yield the same Q-estimates. Although this compression is lossy,
244
      it still “remembers” important aspects of the experienced episodes, such as high-return actions and
245
      goal positions (see Fig. 2) since Q-estimates persist across episodes. This simplifies the mapping the
246   meta agent needs to learn as Q-estimates represent a smaller and more salient set of inputs compared
247   to all possible histories with the same implication.
248
249
      Third, Q-estimates are actionable. Estimated off-policy, they explicitly represent the optimal ex-
      ploitation policy for the current task given the data insofar as the RL module is data-efficient, reliev-
250
      ing the meta-RL agent from performing such calculations inside the transformer/RNN. Over time,
251
      Q-estimates become more reliable and directly indicate the optimal policy whereas processing raw
252   data becomes more challenging. Fortunately, by incorporating Q-estimates the meta-RL agent can
253   eventually ignore the history in the long run (or towards the end of the interaction period) and simply
254   exploit the Q-estimates by selecting actions greedily.
255
      Fourth, Q-estimates are excellent task discriminators and serve as another line of evidence vis-
256
      à-vis maintaining belief over tasks. In a simple domain like Bernoulli multi-armed bandits (Duan
257
      et al., 2016), Q-estimates and action-counts combined are sufficient for Bayes-optimal behavior even
258   without providing raw experience data – a result surprisingly unstated in the literature to the best
259   of our knowledge (see Appendix A.1). However, Q-estimates and action-counts may not always be
260   sufficient for Bayes-optimal beliefs1 . In more complex domains, it is hard to prove the sufficiency
261   of Q-estimates regarding task discrimination. However, via empirical analysis in Appendix D, we
262   argue that i) it is highly improbable for two tasks to have similar Q∗ functions and ii) Q-estimates
263   tend to become accurate task predictors in just a few steps. This implies that the meta-agent may use
264   this finite summary for task inference rather than relying completely on arbitrarily long histories,
265   potentially contributing to enhanced performance over long adaptation periods.
266   It can be theoretically argued that since the meta agent is a BAMDP policy, it is meta-trained to
267   select greedy actions w.r.t. the BAMDP meta-value function and thus should not require construct-

336   Without conditioning on Q-estimates, error in V̄ ∗ (b̄) would instead increase as t → ∞, as the meta-
337   critic would be conditioned on a larger history, which could destabilize the meta-value learning for
338   all preceding belief states during meta-training.
339
340   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION
341
342   Implementing RL3 involves simply replacing each MDP in the task distribution with a corresponding
343   value-augmented MDP (VAMDP) and solving the resulting VAMDP distribution using RL2 . Each
344
      VAMDP has the same action space and reward function as the corresponding MDP. The value aug-
      mented state ŝt ∈ S × Rk × Ik includes the object level state st , k real values and k integer values
345
      for the Q-estimates (Qt (st , a)) and action counts (N t (st , a)) for each of the k actions.In practice,
346
      we provide action advantages along with the max Q-value (value function) instead of Q-estimates.
347   When the object-level state space S is discrete, st needs to be represented as an |S|-dimensional
348   one-hot vector. Note that the value augmented state space is continuous. In the VAMDP transition
349   function, the object-level state s has the same transition dynamics as the original MDP, while the
350   dynamics of Q-estimates are a function of T , R, and the specific object-level RL algorithm used for
351   estimating Q-values. An episode of the VAMDP spans the entire interaction period with the corre-
352   sponding MDP, which may include multiple episodes of the MDP, as Q-estimates continue to evolve
353   beyond episode boundaries. In code, a VAMDP RL environment is implemented as a wrapper over a
354   given MDP environment. The pseudocode, additional implementation details and hyperparameters
355
      for RL2 and RL3 are mentioned in Appendix B.
356
357   5     E XPERIMENTS
358
359   We compare RL3 to our enhanced implementation of RL2 . In our implementation, we replace
360   LSTMs with transformers in both the meta-actor and meta-critic for the purpose of mapping ex-
361   periences to actions and meta-values, respectively. This is done to improve RL2 ’s ability to handle
362   long-term dependencies instead of suffering from vanishing gradients. Moreover, RL2 -transformer
363
      trains significantly faster than RL2 -LSTM. Second, we include in the state space the total number
      of interaction steps and the total number of steps within each episode during a meta-episode (see
364
      Fig. 1). Third, we use PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) for training the meta actor-critic, instead of
365
      TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015). These modifications and other minor-implementation details incor-
366   porate the recommendations made by Ni et al. (2022), who show that model-free recurrent RL is
367   competitive with other state-of-the-art meta RL approaches such as VeriBAD (Zintgraf et al., 2020),
368   if implemented properly. RL3 simply applies the modified version of RL2 to the distribution of
369   value-augmented MDPs explained in section 4.2. Within each VAMDP, our choice of object-level
370   RL is a model-based algorithm to maximize data efficiency – we estimate a tabular model of the
371   environment and run finite-horizon value-iteration using the model. Once again, we emphasize that
372   the core of our approach, which is augmenting MDP states with action-value estimates, is not inher-
373   ently tied to RL2 and is orthogonal to most other meta-RL research. VAMDPs can be plugged into
374
      any base meta-RL algorithm with a reasonable expectation of improving it.

376   parameterized distribution, which the meta-actor interacts with for a fixed adaptation period, or
377   interaction budget, H. This interaction might consist of multiple object-level episodes of variable
      length, each of which are no longer than a maximum task horizon. For a given experiment, each

416   and less stochastic tasks, which we attribute to the increased accuracy of the Q-value estimates, iii)

509   7    L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
510
511   Though it compares favorably to strong meta-RL approaches like RL2 -transformer where applicable,
512   RL3 does have some limitations. First, it assumes the object-level decision-making model is an
513   MDP, which although a common assumption in the literature, may be challenged in practice. While
514   in principle we could extend RL3 to POMDPs using methods like point-based value iteration, this
515   has yet to be tested empirically. Second, RL3 relies on fast, potentially approximate methods for
516
      object-level RL, and using value iteration complicates application to problems with continuous state
      spaces. However, we speculate that a crude linear function approximation would suffice. Finally,
517
      inference time is slightly slower at deploy time due to running object-level RL. However, the overall
518
      training time is actually faster because of better meta-training efficiency. In fact, RL3 could enable
519   working with adaptation periods that are otherwise prohibitively long for many meta-RL approaches.
520
521   To conclude, in this paper, we introduced RL3 , a principled hybrid approach that combines the
522
      strengths of traditional RL and meta-RL and provides a more robust and efficient meta-RL algorithm.
      We advanced intuitive and theoretical arguments regarding its suitability for meta-RL and presented
523
      empirical evidence to validate those ideas. Specifically, we demonstrated that RL3 holds potential
524
      to enhance long-term performance, generalization on out-of-distribution tasks and reducing meta-
525   training time. In future work, we plan to explore extending RL3 to handle continuous state spaces.

802   meta-level value function V̄ ∗ (b̄), which will be an expectation over object-level values, will simplify

808   the above proof demonstrates that RL3 can infer this equivalency implicitly in the limit without

827   B     A RCHITECTURE
828
829   B.1   RL2
830
831   Our modified implementation of RL2 uses transformer decoders (Vaswani et al., 2017) instead of
832   RNNs to map trajectories to action probabilities and meta-values, in the actor and the critic, respec-
833   tively, and uses PPO instead of TRPO for outer RL. The decoder architecture is similar to (Vaswani
834
      et al., 2017), with 2 layers of masked multi-headed attention. However, we use learned position
      embeddings instead of sinusoidal, followed by layer normalization. Our overall setup is similar
835
      to (Esslinger et al., 2022).
836
837   For each meta-episode of interactions with an MDP Mi , the actor and the critic transformers look
838   at the entire history of experiences up to time t and output the corresponding action probabilities
839   π1 ...πt and meta-values V̄1 ...V̄t , respectively. An experience input to the transformer at time t
840
      consists of the previous action at−1 , the latest reward rt−1 , the current state st , episode time step
      tτ , and the meta-episode time step t, all of which are normalized to be in the range [0, 1]. In order to
841
      reduce inference complexity, say at time step t, we append t new attention scores (corresponding to
842
      experience input t w.r.t. the previous t − 1 experience inputs) to a previously cached (t − 1) × (t − 1)
843   attention matrix, instead of recomputing the entire t × t attention matrix. This caching mechanism
844   is implemented for each attention head and reduces the inference complexity at time t from O(t2 )
845   to O(t).
846
847
848
      B.2   RL3
849
      The input of the transformer in RL3 includes a vector of Q estimates (in practice, they are supplied as
850
      the vector of advantage estimates (Q − maxa Q) along with the value function (maxa Q) separately)
851
      and a vector of action counts at each step t for the corresponding state. As mentioned in Section
852   4.2, this is implemented in our code simply by converting MDPs in the problem set to VAMDPs
853   using a wrapper and running our implementation of RL2 thereafter. The pseudocode is shown in the
854   algorithm 1. The Markov version of RL3 uses a dense neural network, with two hidden layers of 64
855   nodes each, with the ReLU activation function.
856
      For object-level RL, we use model estimation followed by value iteration (with discount factor
857
      γ = 1) to obtain Q-estimates. The transition probabilities and the mean rewards are estimated using
858   maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), with Laplace smoothing (coefficient = 0.1) for transition
859   probabilities estimation. For unseen actions, rewards are assumed to be zero, and transitions equally
860   likely to other states. States are added to the model incrementally when they are visited, so that
861   value iteration does not compute values for unvisited states. Moreover, value iteration is carried out
862   only for iterations equal to the task horizon (which is 1, 10, 250, 350 for Bandits, MDPs, 11x11
863   GridWorld, 13x13 GridWorld domains, respectively), unless the maximum Bellman error drops
      below 0.01.

884   B.3     RL3 - COARSE
885
886
      During model estimation in RL3 -coarse, concrete states in the underlying MDP are incrementally
      clustered into abstract states as they are visited. When a new concrete state is encountered, its
887
      abstract state ID is set to that of a previously visited state within a ‘clustering radius’, unless that
888
      previous state is already part of a full cluster (determined by a maximum ‘cluster size’ parameter).
889   If multiple visited states satisfy the criteria, the ID of the closet one is chosen. If none of the visited
890   states that satisfy the criteria, then the new state is assigned a new abstract state ID, increasing the
891   number of abstract states in the model. It is worth noting that this method of deriving abstractions
892   does not take advantage of any structure in the underlying domain. However, this simplicity makes
893   it general purpose, efficient, and impartial, while still leading to excellent performance. For our
894   GridWorld domain, we chose a cluster size of 2 and a clustering radius such that only non-diagonal
895   adjacent states are clustered (Manhattan radius of 1).
896   The mechanism for learning the transition function and the reward function in the abstract MDP is
897   the same as before. For estimating Q-values for a given concrete state, value iteration is carried out
898   on the abstract MDP and the Q-estimates of the corresponding abstract state are returned.

       Given an MDP with 3 states and 2 actions, we want to find the probability that ||Q∗1 − Q∗2 ||∞ < δ,
997    where Q∗1 and Q∗2 are 6-entry (3 states × 2 actions) Q∗ -tables. The transition and reward functions
998    are drawn from distributions parameterized by α and β, respectively. Transition probabilities are
999    drawn from a Dirichlet distribution, Dir(α), and rewards are sampled from a normal distribution,
1000   N (1, β). In total, we ran 3 combinations of α and β, each with 50,000 MDPs, a task horizon of
1001   10, and δ = 0.1. To get the final probability, we test all ((50, 000 − 1)2 )/2 non-duplicate pairs and
1002   count the number of max norms less than δ.
1003   Results: For α = 1.0, β = 1.0, we found the probability of a given pair of MDPs having duplicate
1004   Q∗ -table to be ϵ = 2.6 × 10−9 . For α = 0.1, β = 1.0, which is a more deterministic setting, we
1005   found ϵ = 4.6 × 10−9 . Further, with α = 0.1, β = 0.5, where rewards are more closely distributed,
1006   we found ϵ = 1.1×10−7 . Overall, we can see that even for a set of very small MDPs, the probability
1007   of numerically mistaking one Q∗ -table for another is vanishingly small.
