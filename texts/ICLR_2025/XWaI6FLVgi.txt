009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Environment augmentation methods have gained some success in overcoming
012            the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization challenge in Graph Neural Networks
013            (GNNs). Yet, there exists a challenging trade-off in the augmentation: On one hand,
014            it requires the generated graphs as diverse as possible to extrapolate to unseen
015            environments. On the other hand, it requires the generated graphs to preserve
016
               the invariant substructures causally related to the targets. Existing approaches
               have proposed various environment augmentation strategies to enrich spurious
017
               patterns for OOD generalization. However, we argue that these methods remain
018
               limited in diversity and precision of the generated environments for two reasons: i)
019            the deterministic nature of the graph composition strategy used for environment
020            augmentation may limit the diversity of the generated environments, and ii) the
021            presence of spurious correlations may lead to the exclusion of invariant subgraphs
022            and reduce the precision of the generated environments. To address this trade-off,
023            we propose a novel paradigm that accurately identifies spurious subgraphs, and
024            an environment augmentation strategy called spurious subgraph diversification,
025            which extrapolates to maximally diversified spurious subgraphs by randomizing
026            the spurious subgraph generation, while preserving the invariant substructures. Our
027
               method is theoretically sound and demonstrates strong empirical performance on
               both synthetic and real-world datasets, outperforming the second-best method by
028
               up to 24.19% across 17 baseline methods, underscoring its superiority in graph
029
               OOD generalization.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Xu et al., 2019; Veličković et al., 2017) have demonstrated exceptional
035   performance in learning from graph-structured data across diverse fields (Qiu et al., 2018; Wu et al.,
036   2022b; Yu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022c). However, it generally assumes that the training and test
037   graphs are independently drawn from the identical distribution, which often fails in many real-world
038   graph applications (Hu et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2022; Koh et al., 2021). Such
039
      distribution shifts can drastically undermine the generalization capabilities of GNNs, hindering their
      applicability in practical situations.
040
041   To address the OOD generalization challenge, recent studies have explored data augmentation
042   methods across various domains (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Yao et al., 2022; Park et al., 2022;
043   Kong et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022). In the context of graph data, graph data augmentation (GDA)
044   methods such as DropEdge (Rong et al., 2019), FLAG (Kong et al., 2022), and M-Mixup (Wang
045
      et al., 2021) perturb graph features or structures to enlarge the training distribution to facilitate
      generalization on unseen environments. However, GDA methods are prone to perturbing stable
046
      features or patterns that are critical for the predictive task, potentially limitting their effectiveness for
047
      OOD generalization. Inspired by causality (Peters et al., 2016) and invariant learning (Arjovsky et al.,
048   2020; Krueger et al., 2021), recent studies have proposed environment augmentation methods (Wu
049   et al., 2022c; Liu et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) that generate environment-sensitive
050   substructures while preserving invariant patterns. The goal is to capture stable features by learning
051   equipredictive encoders across different environments. A key condition for the success of these
052   methods lies in the diversity and precision of the generated environments, represented by spurious
053   subgraph patterns that capture rich environmental variations (Sui et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Notably,
      DIR (Wu et al., 2022c) and GREA (Liu et al., 2022) perform input-level and latent-level augmentation


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      respectively to create diverse environments. However, these methods rely on interpolation paradigms,
055   which limit the diversity of the generated environments. In addition, some other studies explore
056   environment extrapolation to generate rich spurious patterns for unseen environments, utilizing
057   adversarial augmentation (Sui et al., 2023), and linear extrapolation in graph space (Li et al., 2024).
058
      Despite these advancements, we argue that the diversity and precision of the generated environments
059
      in existing methods remain limited for two reasons. First, most of these methods adopt closed-
060
      form combination strategies (See Appendix G for details) to composite invariant and spurious
061   subgraphs (Wu et al., 2022c; Liu et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). This paradigm
062   inherently constrain the diversity of the generated spurious patterns due to their deterministic nature.
063   Second, the presence of spurious correlations can lead to the incorrect exclusion of (a portion of)
064   invariant subgraphs during environment augmentation, thereby reducing the precision of the generated
065   environments. This raises our research question:
066
      How can we generate high-quality spurious patterns in terms of diversity and precision to enhance
067   OOD generalization for graph data?
068
069   To address this challenge, we propose a novel learning framework, which differs from previous
070
      environment augmentation methods. Specifically, our approach builds on the theoretical results in
      Prop. 2 that, under mild assumptions (Assumption 1 and 2), edges in the invariant subgraphs tend to
071
      exhibit higher predicted probabilities in the learnable data transformation compared to spurious edges.
072
      Therefore a) to identify edges from spurious subgraphs accurately, we utilize the bottom K% of edges
073   with the lowest predicted probabilities as estimated spurious edges, and the subsequent diversification
074   process operates exclusively on these edges, ensuring the invariant subgraphs unaffected. b) To
075   address the diversity issue of generated environments, we propose spurious subgraph diversification,
076   an effective environment extrapolation strategy that randomizes the generation of spurious subgraphs
077   to encourage diversity of the generated environments. We additionally propose a graph size constraint
078   to prune spurious edges and reduce the candidate space for spurious subgraphs to be generated,
079   achieving more effective environment extrapolation. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
080
             • Novel framework. We propose iSSD, a novel learning framework that: i) identifies
081
               spurious subgraphs accurately, ensuring that invariant patterns remain unaffected during
082
               augmentation; and ii) randomizes the generation of spurious subgraphs within a reduced
083            search space with Spurious Subgraph Diversification for effective environment extrapolation
084            and OOD generalization.
085
             • Theoretical guarantee. We provide theoretical analysis showing that a) The proposed graph
086
               size constraint provably enhances OOD generalization by tightening the OOD generaliza-
087            tion bound (Theorem 3.1); b) Spurious subgraph diversification provably enhances OOD
088            generalization by identifying the true invariant subgraphs (Theorem 4.2).
089
             • Strong empirical performance. We conduct experiments on both synthetic datasets and
090            real-world datasets, compare against 17 baselines, our method outperform the second-best
091            method by up to 24.19%, highlighting the superiority of our proposed method.

      5    D ISCUSSION
285
286
      Although adopting a learnable data transformation function, or a subgraph selector t(·) is not a
      novel concept in the literature, our study provides a new perspective on how to utilize t(·) for
287
      identifying the invariant subgraph Gc for OOD generalization on graphs. Specifically, in previous
288
      works such as Li et al. (2022b); Wu et al. (2022c), the learned subgraph is utilized for environment
289   inference and environment generation respectively to learn an equipredictive classifier for OOD
290   generalization; In Miao et al. (2022), t(·) is employed to identify Gc based on the information
291   bottleneck principle (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). Chen et al. (2022) leverages t(·) to identify
292   Gc through supervised contrastive learning, while Sui et al. (2023) uses the learned subgraph for
293   distribution perturbation to enhance model robustness; In Gui et al. (2023), t(·) is utilized to isolate
294   Gc under the regularization conditions Gc ⊥       ⊥ E and Gs ⊥   ⊥ Y , where the environments E are
295   assumed observable. Lu et al. (2024) utlizes subgraph selector to extract graph rationals, followed
296   by generating virtual samples by perturbing these substructures using Extreme Value Theory (Haan
297
      & Ferreira, 2006). In Jia et al. (2024), the subgraph selector is utilized to separate invariant and
      spurious subgraphs, followed by invariant mixup and environmental mixup to augment the training
298
      distribution. In contrast, our approach is fundamentally different from all previous works in how to
299
      utilize t(·) to identify invariant subgraphs. By leveraging spurious subgraph diversification with graph
300   size constraint, our method is able to marginalizing over diversified (enriched) spurious patterns,
301   effectively extrapolate to unseen environments, and identifies Gc using t∗ (·) for OOD generalization.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
305   Invariant learning. Recently, there has been growing attention on the graph-level representations
306   under distribution shifts from the perspective of invariant learning. Some works focus on environment
307   inference (Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022b) or augmentation strategies (Wu et al., 2022c; Liu et al.,
308   2022; Zhuang et al., 2023; Sui et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Another line of works employs alternative
309   strategies to identify Gc without tackling the hidden environment labels (Chen et al., 2022; 2023a;
310   Miao et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021; 2020), Most of these works assume a causal data generating process
311
      that assumes the existence of an invariant subgraph Gc causally related to the target label Y , which
      remains invariant across different distribution shifts. In this work, we also adopt this assumption, and
312
      our approach aims to generate diversified environments without affecting the invariant substructures.
313
314   Graph data augmentation. Recent studies (Rong et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022)
315   have introduced various graph data augmentation methods to enhance the performance of semi-
316   supervised node classification tasks. Despite these advancements, these methods mainly focus on
317
      improving performance within the training distribution and do not directly target OOD data. Inspired
      by causality (Peters et al., 2016) and invariant learning principles (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Kreuzer
318
      et al., 2021), recent works have shifted focus towards environment augmentation, operating both at
319
      the input level (Wu et al., 2022c) and latent level (Liu et al., 2022), to generate interpolated (Wu et al.,
320   2022c; Liu et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2023) or extrapolated environments (Sui et al., 2023; Li et al.,
321   2024), which typically rely on masking matrices produced by subgraph selectors. Meanwhile, our
322   study introduces spurious subgraph diversification, which enriches the generated spurious patterns,
323   allowing our method to extrapolate to unseen environments more effectively, and capture the stable
      relationship P(Y | Gc ) more accurately.

490   8   C ONCLUSION
491
492
      In this work, we focus on the trade-off between generating diverse environments and preserving
      invariant substructures. To overcome the limitations in existing studies, we proposed spurious
493
      subgraph diversification, which randomizes the generation of spurious subgraphs to encourage
494
      diversity of the generated environments, along with a graph size constraint to reduce the search space
495   of spurious subgraphs for more effective environment extrapolation. Our theoretical analysis and
496   extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the superiority of our
497   approach for graph OOD generalization.

871   C    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
872
873   OOD Generalization. OOD generalization is a critical challenge in machine learning, where models
874   trained on a specific data distribution often fail to generalize well to unseen distributions. Several
875   approaches have been proposed to address this issue, including domain generalization, distributional
876   robustness optimization (DRO), and invariance learning. Domain generalization aims to learn features
877   that are invariant across different domains or environments. Previous studies, such as Ganin et al.
878   (2016); Sun & Saenko (2016); Li et al. (2018); Dou et al. (2019), regularize the learned features to be
879   domain-invariant. DRO methods focus on training models to perform robust against the worst-case
880   scenarios among diverse data groups. Namkoong & Duchi (2016); Hu et al. (2018); Sagawa et al.
881
      (2019) regularize models to be robust to mild distributional perturbations of the training distributions,
      expecting the models to perform well in unseen test environments. Building upon this, Liu et al.
882
      (2022) Zhang et al. (2022b) and Yao et al. (2022) propose advanced strategies to improve robustness
883
      by assuming that models trained with ERM have a strong reliance on spurious features. Invariance
884   learning leverages the theory of causality Peters et al. (2016); Pearl (2009) and introduces causal
885   invariance to the learned representations. The Independent Causal Mechanism (ICM) assumption in
886   causality states that the conditional distribution of each variable given its causes does not inform or
887   influence other conditional distributions. Despite changes to the intervened variables, the conditional
888   distribution of intervened variables and the target variable remains invariant. Arjovsky et al. (2020)
889   proposes the framework of Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) that allows the adoption of causal
890   invariance in deep neural networks, inspiring various invariant learning works such as Parascandolo
891   et al. (2020); Mahajan et al. (2021); Wald et al. (2021); Ahuja et al. (2020; 2021). These works
892
      aim to discard spurious signals while keeping causally invariant signals. However, most of these
      methods require explicit environment partitions within the dataset, which is often impractical in
893
      real-world scenarios. To address this limitation, EIIL (Creager et al., 2021) and Heterogeneous Risk
894
      Minimization (HRM) (Liu et al., 2021) propose methods for invariance learning without explicit
895   environment partitions.
896
897   OOD Generalization on Graphs. Recently, there has been a growing interest in learning graph-
898
      level representations that are robust under distribution shifts, particularly from the perspective of
      invariant learning. MoleOOD (Yang et al., 2022) and GIL (Li et al., 2022b) propose to infer
899
      environmental labels to assist in identifying invariant substructures within graphs. DIR (Wu et al.,
900
      2022c), GREA (Liu et al., 2022) and iMoLD (Zhuang et al., 2023) employ environment augmentation
901   techniques to facilitate the learning of invariant graph-level representations. These methods typically
902   rely on the explicit manipulation of unobserved environmental variables to achieve generalization
903   across unseen distributions. AIA (Sui et al., 2023) employs an adversarial augmenter to explore OOD
904   data by generating new environments while maintaining stable feature consistency. To circumvent the
905   need for environmental inference or augmentation, CIGA (Chen et al., 2022) and GALA (Chen et al.,
906   2023a) utilizes supervised contrastive learning to identify invariant subgraphs based on the assumption
907   that samples sharing the same label exhibit similar invariant subgraphs. LECI (Gui et al., 2023)
908   and G-Splice (Li et al., 2023) assume the availability of environment labels, and study environment
909
      exploitation strategies for graph OOD generalization. LECI (Gui et al., 2023) proposes to learn
      a causal subgraph selector by jointly optimizing label and environment causal independence, and
910
      G-Splice (Li et al., 2023) studies graph and feature space extrapolation for environment augmentation,
911
      which maintains causal validity. On the other hand, some works do not utilize the invariance principle
912   for graph OOD generalization. DisC (Fan et al., 2022) initially learns a biased graph representation
913   and subsequently focuses on unbiased graphs to discover invariant subgraphs. GSAT (Miao et al.,
914   2022) utilizes information bottleneck principle (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015) to learn a minimal
915   sufficient subgraph for GNN explainability, which is shown to be generalizable under distribution
916   shifts. OOD-GNN (Li et al., 2022a) proposes to learn disentangled graph representation by computing
917   global weights of all data. iSSD also holds significant potential for node-level and link-level OOD
      generalization. For example, iSSD could be extended by diversifying the K-hop subgraphs for each


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      node to mitigate spurious correlations. Given the rich node features typically available in node-level
919   and link-level tasks, further diversification of node features could serve as an enhanced augmentation
920   strategy. Similarly, constraining the size of ego-networks and adjusting the information density of
921   node features could improve generalization performance in these tasks, which presents promising
922   future directions.
923
      Diverse feature learning. Recent studies have shown that ERM tends to encourage models to learn
924
      the simplest predictive features (Hermann & Lampinen, 2020; Kalimeris et al., 2019; Neyshabur
925   et al., 2014; Pezeshki et al., 2021). This simplicity bias causes the models to rely on simple (spurious)
926   but non-causal features, ignoring more complex patterns that might be equally predictive. To address
927   this challenge, RSC (Huang et al., 2020) employs a self-challenging mechanism to force the model to
928   learn diverse patterns by discarding dominant features, while DivCLS (Teney et al., 2022) constructs
929   diverse features by training a collection of classifiers with diversity regularization. Additionally,
930   Zhang et al. (2022a); Chen et al. (2023b) adopt DRO to iteratively explore new features. These works
931   primarily focus on Euclidean data and take a model-centric approach, whereas our proposed method
932   is a data-centric approach specifically designed for graph data, which generates diverse negative
933
      feedbacks (spurious subgraphs), from which causal features can be identified.

1177   GOOD-HIV is a molecular dataset derived from the MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018) benchmark,
1178   where the primary task is to predict the ability of molecules to inhibit HIV replication. The molecular
1179   structures are represented as graphs, with nodes as atoms and edges as chemical bonds. Following
1180   Gui et al. (2022), We adopt the covariate shift split, which refers to changes in the input distribution
1181   between training and testing datasets while maintaining the same conditional distribution of labels
1182   given inputs. This setup ensures that the model must generalize to unseen molecular structures that
1183   differ in these domain features from those seen during training. We focus on the Bemis-Murcko
1184
       scaffold (Bemis & Murcko, 1996) and the number of nodes in the molecular graph as two domain
       features to evaluate our method.
1185
1186   GOOD-Motif is a synthetic dataset designed to test structure shifts. Each graph in this dataset is
1187   created by combining a base graph and a motif, with the motif solely determining the label. The base
       graph type and the size are selected as domain features to introduce covariate shifts. By generating

1189   generalize to new graph structures not seen during training. We employ the covariate shift split, where

1191   where underlying graph structures may change.

1283
       the existence of a single invariant substructure causally related to each target label, many real-world
1284   graph applications (Hu et al., 2020; Gui et al., 2022) may contain multiple such invariant subgraphs.
1285   However, Assumption 1 can be reformulated to accommodate multiple Gc without compromising
1286   the validity of our assumptions and theoretical results. Specifically, suppose there are K invariant
1287   subgraphs, denoted as Gc,i for i ∈ [K]. For any specific Gc,i , the spurious subgraph G′s can be
1288   redefined as G′s = Gs ∪ {Gc,j | j ̸= i}. Given this redefinition, and under the presence of Gs ,
1289   our assumption I(Gc,i ; Y ) > I(G′s ; Y ) holds for any i ∈ [K]. Consequently, the assumptions
1290   and theoretical results presented in this work remain valid, even when multiple Gc exist within the
1291   datasets. To further support our claim, we curated a dataset based on SPMotif (Wu et al., 2022c),
1292
       where in the train/valid/test datasets, two invariant substructures are attached to the spurious subgraph.
       Our method performs effectively under this scenario, as shown in Table 4.
