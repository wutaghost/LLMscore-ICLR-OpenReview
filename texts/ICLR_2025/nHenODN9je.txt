009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011           Preference tuning of large language models (LLMs) relies on high-quality human
012           preference data, which is often expensive and time-consuming to gather. While
013           existing methods can use trained reward models or proprietary model as judges
014           for preference annotation, they have notable drawbacks: training reward models
015           remain dependent on initial human data, and using proprietary model imposes
016           license restrictions that inhibits commercial usage. In this paper, we introduce
017           Guided Density Ratio, a training-free and highly effective method that leverages off-
018
              the-shelf LLMs for preference data annotation. Our approach uses the log-density
              ratio between a better-aligned LLM and a less aligned LLM as a reward signal. We
019
              explores 221 different LLMs pairs and empirically demonstrate that increasing the
020
              performance gap between paired LLMs correlates with better reward generalization.
021           Furthermore, we show that tailoring the density ratio reward function with specific
022           criteria and preference exemplars enhances performance across domains and within
023           target areas.
024           In our experiment using density ratio from a pair of Mistral-7B models, Guided
025           Density Ratio achieves a RewardBench score of 82.6, outperforming the best
026           trained reward functions from same model class and demonstrating competitive
027           performance against SoTA models in Safety (91.0) and Reasoning (88.0) domains.
028           We use Guided Density Ratio to annotate an on-policy preference dataset with
029           which we preference tune Llama-3-8B-Instruct with SimPO. Using reward signals
030           from two relatively weak models, our approach pushes Llama-3-8B to achieve
031
              a 37.4% (+15.1%) win rate on ArenaHard and a 40.7% (+17.8%) win rate on
              Length-Controlled AlpacaEval 2.0, along with a score of 8.0 on MT-Bench.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Preference tuning has advanced the capabilities of large language models (LLMs), but this progress
038   relies on high-quality human preference data which is both costly and time-consuming to gather.
039
      Cutting-edge models (e.g., ChatGPT, GPT-4, Claude-3) are aligned with curated, quality-controlled
      human preference data, typically provided by specialized companies. While effective, this approach
040
      limits broader adoption due to prohibitive costs and limited transparency in data collection (Wang
041
      et al., 2024c). AI-feedback solutions are emerging as an alternative—either through a trained reward
042   model (Dong et al., 2024) or proprietary LLM-as-a-judge (Cui et al., 2023). However, training reward
043   models still rely on costly initial human preference data, and proprietary LLM-as-a-judge approaches
044   introduce licensing restrictions that generally prevent commercial use.
045
      This paper introduces Guided Density Ratio that leverages the density ratio between off-the-shelf
046
      LLMs to efficiently facilitate preference data annotation. Our method uses the log-density ratio
047
      between a better-aligned model and less-aligned model to annotate preference data. We show that
048   a higher alignment gap between model pairs yields improved preference signal. This observation,
049   referred to as the “Strong-over-Weak Hypothesis”, is supported by our experiments across 221 model
050   combinations (Figure 1). Notably, log-density ratios between a post-DPO model and a pre-DPO
051   model, known as the DPO implicit reward (Rafailov et al., 2023), have not gained widespread
052   adoption due to limitations in generalizability and high reward variance in reward performance
053   across different model choices (Lambert et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). We show that the implicit
      DPO reward is an empirically suboptimal special case of the strong-over-weak density ratio reward,

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
118
119
      Density ratios for alignment Density ratio as rewards is popularized as implict DPO reward
      (Rafailov et al., 2023). Chen et al. (2024) uses implicit DPO reward to bootstrap an LLM through
120
      iterative DPO training. Zhong et al. (2024) trains a DPO model and uses the density ratio to derive
121
      a token-level characterization for response quality, and uses it as a reward signal in PPO training.
122   Yang et al. (2024b) uses the density ratio between DPO vs SFT model as quality filter. Though one
123   study Lin et al. (2024) finds that implicit DPO reward struggles to generalize on OOD examples
124   compared with just training a classifier using (BradleyTerry; Bradley & Terry, 1952) objective. This
125   paper shows that implicit DPO reward is only a special case in the class of strong-over-weak density
126   ratio reward. Our findings provide guidelines to identify suitable pairs to construct DR. In particular,
127   the denominator model must be sufficiently weak and unaligned to contrast with numerator model to
128   provide generalizable preference signal.
129
130   Discriminative & generative preference Trained classifiers and generative rewards are the existing
131
      approaches for preference data annotation. They top leaderboards such as RewardBench (Lambert
      et al., 2024) and are widely adopted by both industry and the community to preference align well-
132
      known models (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Adler et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a;
133
      Cui et al., 2023). In fact, due to the scarcity and noise of human preference data, GPT-4 based
134   generative rewards have long been harvested to align models, such as reinforcement learning from AI
135   feedback (RLAIF; Bai et al., 2022). High quality and popular preference datasets are actually often
136   times annotated by LLM-as-a-judge, both in the forms of scalar score and textual assessment and
137   critiques (Cui et al., 2023). With high quality human data or LLM generated data available, one can
138   fine-tune a LLM to be a better generative judge (Wang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
139   2024a; Kim et al., 2024), or to tune a linear layer on top of the LLM to be a sequence classifier (Adler
140   et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Liu & Zeng, 2024). However, such approaches either require quality
141   data for training or a powerful closed-source LLM which may be prohibitive for license concerns.
142
143   Weak-to-strong generalization Many works have explored the idea of using a weak and a strong
144
      model to obtain better performance than the strong model. Contrastive decoding, for instance,
      enhances LLM generation quality by searching for sequences that maximizes the likelihood different
145
      between an expert model and an amateur model. O’Brien & Lewis (2023) shows CD consistently
146
      improves reasoning tasks. Li et al. (2022) shows improved generation quality in wikipedia, news
147   and story domains. Chuang et al. (2023) shows improvement in LLM facutuality by contrasting
148   the differences between logits in later layers and earlier layers. In addition to contrastive decoding,
149   E X P O (Zheng et al., 2024) is a model extrapolation method that leverages the delta between an
150   aligned model and pre-aligned model as a global gradient update to the aligned model, yielding
151   surprising improvement over various evaluation benchmarks. Burns et al. (2023) found that using
152   supervision from a weak model to train a strong model can yield better-than-supervisor performance
153   from the stronger model.

157   We study two research questions critical to reward function design based on density ratio. First, we
158   discuss how to find successful pairs of LLMs to construct effective density ratio reward (section 3.1).
159   Our results reveal a strong correlation between the alignment gap of model pairs (measured by the
160   ArenaHard score) and the effectiveness of the reward function (evaluated through the RewardBench

190
      According to DPO implicit reward theory (see Section 5.1 in Rafailov et al. (2023)), optimizing
191
      DPO objective also learns an implicit reward function r(x, y) = β log ππrefθ (y|x)

192
      ground-truth reward. This function is named DPO implicit reward, and follow-up works (Lambert
193
      et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) have directly leveraged it to annotate preference data.
194
195   To assess this theory, we conduct an experiment applying online iterative DPO (Xiong et al., 2023;
196   Xu et al., 2023; Swamy et al., 2024) to both the Mistral and Llama-3 model families. The key
197   ideas of online iterative DPO training are: (1) the reference model is updated at each iteration (i.e.,

199

200
      from 3 DPO optimization iterations (checkpoints from Chen et al. (2024)). The DPO model (πθt ) at
201   iteration t uses the policy model at the previous iteration (πθt−1 ) as its reference model. We write the

      We ensure that the DPO objective is effectively optimized at each iteration, as we see consistent

      Reward Function Design We use the following reward function to annotate preference data.
217
218                                       πstrong (y | x)
                          r(x, y) = log                   = log πstrong (y | x) − log πweak (y | x).                   (2)
219                                       πweak (y | x)
220
221   Here πstrong and πweak are two off-the-shelf LLMs from the same model family with πstrong outper-
222   forming πweak across all dimensions of human preference, such as safety, correctness, and relevance.
223   In Section 4.1, we conduct extensive experiments on density ratio reward involving 221 distinct
224   model pairs. Our findings reveal a strong correlation between the alignment gap of πstrong and πweak
225   and the effectiveness of the reward function, as quantified by the RewardBench score. As shown in
226   Figure 1, achieving an effective reward function in Eq. (2) necessitates a substantial human-alignment
227   level difference between πstrong and πweak . We term this insight the “Strong-over-Weak Hypothesis"
228   , which can serve as a guiding principle for selecting models in density ratio reward computation.
229
      Furthermore, our experiments span a range of models fine-tuned using different preference tuning
      strategies (e.g., DPO, SimPO, KTO, ORPO) to show that density ratio reward is not exclusive for
230
      DPO models, with further details provided in Figure 5. We summarize our key observations below.
231
232      • We recommend using a weak model for the denominator in (2) that has not been fine-tuned
233        on human preference data, such as an SFT or base model. For the numerator, a stronger
234        model that aligns more closely with human preferences (e.g., AlpacaEval2.0 or ArenaHard
235        benchmarks) should be used. This approach maximizes the performance gap, often leading to
236        better performance of the reward function.
237      • We recommend using both strong and weak models from the same model family. If the weak
238        model is an SFT model, we suggest using a strong model that has been preference-tuned
239        from this SFT model. This approach ensures that when leveraging existing benchmarks (e.g.,
240        AlpacaEval 2.0 or ArenaHard) to evaluate the performance gap in human preference alignment,
241        potential confounding factors, such as differing inductive biases between unrelated models, are
242        minimized.

      3.2     G UIDED D ENSITY R ATIO
263
264   Human preferences are multi-dimensional (e.g., safety, trustworthiness, reliability, faithfulness) (Bai
265   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024c; Naseem et al., 2024), and an effective reward function should
266   adapt its criteria according to the specific domain requirements. For example, a chatbot explaining
267   corporate vacation policies should emphasize faithfulness to company policy and the accuracy of its
268   responses, rather than focusing on aspects like conversational style or user engagement. However,
269   vanilla log-density ratio reward function provides a single, aggregated reward signal, merging various,
      potentially conflicting preference aspects. Therefore, it is crucial to define preferences clearly and


                                                               5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      provide concrete examples to tailor the contrastive reward signal to the specific domain or aspect of
271   human preference.
272
273   We introduce Guided Density Ratio, which specifies the domains for each prompt and incorporates
274
      instructions and in-context-learning (ICL) examples to define criteria for positive and negative
      preferences (see figure 4). Each domain has customized instructions and ICL examples, and we ensure
275
      diversity by preparing multiple ICL demonstrations, sampling one randomly for each instruction.
276
      Formally, for each original user prompt x, we inject ICL examples and domain-specific instructions
277   T(x) to guide the annotation toward relevant preference dimensions. This is equivalent to adapting
278   the reward function into the following form, incorporating T(x) before applying the log-density ratio
279   for annotation.
280
                   rGuided Density Ratio (x, y) = log πstrong (y | T(x), x) − log πweak (y | T(x), x).   (3)
281
282   To automate annotation, we introduce a domain router that identifies the most relevant domain
283   for each user query, allowing us to apply the appropriate preference criteria for each example in
284   annotation set. For instance, a sensitive query is routed to a Safety expert, while a math or coding
285   query goes to a Math/Code expert. In this paper, we employ the Mixtral 8x7B Instruct v0.1 model
286   (Jiang et al., 2024) with zero-shot prompting to classify prompts into predefined categories (e.g.,
287   safety, reasoning, chat) based on a system prompt and task description.
288   These in-context examples and instructions serve as both demonstrative and descriptive tools to
289   help define and refine the model’s preference criterion. Example templates we used can be found at
290   figure 3 and figure 4. For domains like safety, instructions should include guidelines on how to avoid
291   risky outcomes, while in domains like math, demonstrating the preference criterion through examples
292   may be more effective. These instructions provide high-level guidance by defining overarching
293   principles or definitions that shape the reward function’s preferences during data annotation.

      Setup We collect off-the-shelf LLMs from Mistral and Llama model family with varying levels of
318   human alignment. We use ArenaHard (Li et al., 2024) benchmark (see A.3) to approximate each
319   model’s degree of alignment to human preference. We then test distinct pairs of models’ density ratio
320   reward by evaluating on RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). Each test sample in RewardBench
321   consists of human verified pairwise responses, one chosen and one rejected. The reward function
322   then assigns annotations by comparing the density ratio scores of these two responses. The final
323   RewardBench score reflects the accuracy of a reward function’s predictions against human-annotated
      ground truth.

356   Results Our findings, visualized in Figure 1, reveal a strong correlation between the accuracy of
357   the reward function in Equation (2) and the strong-over-weak alignment gap. As the alignment gap
358   widens, the reward function achieves stronger results. When the alignment gap is near zero, the
359   signal becomes noisy, with the RewardBench accuracy approximating 50%, indicative of a random
360   guess. Further details are presented in Figure 5, where each row represents a numerator model and
361
      each column a denominator model. Each cell displays the paired density ratio reward function’s
      RewardBench score. The heatmap illustrates that the choice of denominator model substantially
362
      influences reward generalization; more effective and stable reward functions can be obtained by
363
      selecting weaker denominator models (e.g., Base or SFT) to ensure a sufficient alignment gap.
364
365   The finding also shows considerable flexibility in constructing density ratio reward. For instance, as
366   shown in Figure 1 (left), SFT-RDPO as the numerator performs well with various checkpoints—such
367
      as Base, SFT, KTO, RRHF, SLiC-HF, and IPO—as denominators, producing high reward accuracy
      due to these models being less aligned than RDPO. Conversely, using a stronger model as the
368
      denominator with SFT-RDPO as the numerator leads to a noticeable drop in reward accuracy. Finally,
369
      when Base or SFT models serve as the denominator, nearly any preference-tuned numerator model
370   yields an effective reward function, underscoring that the key to effective reward performance lies
371   in maintaining a meaningful alignment gap rather than requiring DPO or other preference-specific
372   tuning for the numerator model.

      4.3   A LIGNMENT WITH DENSITY RATIO ANNOTATED DATA
465
466   Setup To assess the usefulness of GDR reward function, we design an on-policy preference
467   alignment experiment similar to setup in Meng et al. (2024); Dong et al. (2024). It is intended to
468   give head-to-head comparisons between proposed log-density ratio reward functions (2) with SoTA
469   reward functions in how well they can preference align a policy. We use Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct
470   as the starting model and SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) as the preference optimization algorithm. Our
471   evaluation methods include AlpacaEval2.0, ArenaHard, and MT-Bench, with details in A.3.
472
473   Preference Data Annotation We use input prompts D = {x(i) }N           i=1 from the UltraFeedback
474   dataset (Cui et al., 2023). On-policy alignment dataset is created by Best-of-N sampling, and
475
      constructing chosen/rejected pairs using different reward functions. For each prompt x ∈ D, we
      sample 32 model completions {yi }32i=1 from the starting policy. To construct positive-negative paired
476
      preference data, we select the preferred response yi∗ as the one that maximizes the reward function:
477
      i∗ = arg maxi r(x, yi ). A dispreferred response is then randomly sampled from the remaining
478   set. For all experiments, the completions {yi }32
                                                     i=1 are pre-computed and fixed, with only the choice
479   of reward function r varying, as indicated in the Reward Function column in Table 2. To address
480   possible length imbalances between preferred and dispreferred responses, we apply a length threshold
481   before randomly selecting the rejected sample. This procedure ensures variety in rejected samples,
482   reduces the risk of reward hacking, and maintains a length-balanced preference dataset.
483
484   Reward Functions We focus on two model pairs to define the log-density ratio reward function:
485   (i) SFT model vs. Base model, and (ii) DPO model vs. SFT model. The first model pair (SFT vs.
      Base) is chosen because neither model has undergone preference tuning, allowing us to test whether


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      a preference reward can be derived based purely on the overall capability improvement after SFT
487   training. The second model pair (DPO vs. SFT) is selected for its highest reward performance, as
488   shown in Table 1. For the GDR reward function, we experiment with safety domain instructions,
489   math/coding domain instructions, and adaptively assigned instructions tailored to the domain of each
490   input prompt.
491
492   Training Details We use SimPO as our preference optimization method, which optimizes the
493   average log-likelihood margin between positive and negative responses directly without requiring a
494   reference model. Its loss function is:

495                               β                               β
                    − log σ               log π(yaccept | x) −            log π(yreject | x) − γ ,   (4)
496                            ∥yaccept ∥                      ∥yreject ∥
497
498   where σ is the sigmoid function, β is the scaling term for reward difference, and γ is the reward
499
      margin term. We choose SimPO for its strong alignment results, matching or even outperforming
      those of DPO, with the added advantage of better efficiency by eliminating the memory and compute
500
      demands of a reference model. We perform hyper-parameter sweep to find the best checkpoint, with
501
      details in Appendix A.1.
502
503   Results As shown in Table 2, Llama-3-instruct preference fine-tuned using data annotated by the
504   DPO-vs-SFT density ratio achieve strong performance, with 39.9 on AlpaceEval 2 and 34.6 on
505   ArenaHard. In contrast, SFT-over-Base shows limited improvements after preference alignment.
506   This demonstrates again that the effectiveness of reward function in (2) requires a well-defined gap
507   in human-value alignment between the numerator and denominator models. For the base and SFT
508   models, narrow gap in their human-aligned level results in noisy reward signal that fails to preference
509   fine-tune effectively.
510   We also evaluate preference data annotated with the customized reward functions (Guided Density
511   Ratio). Table 2 shows that reward functions customized for specific domain can not be applied
512   universally to all examples, doing so would result in suboptimal performance, as in ”safety” and
513   ”code/math” Guided Density Ratio results. We find that by using adaptive instructions—currently
514   categorized into Chat, Code/Math, and Safety— that finds best specialized reward for each example,
515   we achieve the highest overall alignment performance, with 40.7 on AlpacaEval 2 and 37.4 on
516   ArenaHard, competitive against SoTA reward from ArmoRM. Notably, for the (SFT, base) model
517
      pair, adaptive customization of reward significantly enhances alignment performance across all three
      benchmarks, making a weak density ratio reward signal much more effective.

520   5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we introduce Guided Density Ratio, an accessible approach that leverages off-the-
523   shelf LLMs for preference annotation. Guided Density Ratio overcomes the limitations of existing
524   methods by eliminating the need for extensive human annotation or proprietary models to obtain a
525
      high-performance reward function. This advancement makes preference fine-tuning more attainable
      for individual researchers and small companies. Our approach uses the alignment gap between
526
      a better-aligned and an under-aligned model as a reward signal for data annotation, and enables
527
      tailored reward functions for specific domains through customized instructions. We also demonstrate
528   a complete process for applying Guided Density Ratio in preference fine-tuning: first, generating
529   an on-policy preference dataset, and then aligning the Llama-3-8B-Instruct model to competitive
530   performance levels across extensive benchmarks.
531
      In addition to presenting an effective annotation method, we conduct extensive experiments to validate
532
      our Strong-over-Weak hypothesis to guide the design of density ratio reward. Our results reveal a
533
      strong correlation between alignment gaps and reward function performance. It mitigates concerns
534   over density ratio reward’s performance variance by showing consistently generalizable reward
535   from picking weak denominator model. Finally, we show that domain-specific customization gives
536   significant boost to density ratio’s reward performance and alignment results. This work establishes
537   the strong-over-weak approach as a promising, training-free strategy for generating high-quality
538   reward signals. For future research, automated instruction generation for density ratio reward or
539   improved domain categorization are all promising avenues that could further boost the utility and
      appeal of density ratio reward functions.

      A.3   E VALUATION
811
812   RewardBench We use RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) to evaluate DR’s out-of-distribution
813   reward performance. It is a comprehensive benchmark designed test the performance of reward
814   models across a range of scenarios, including challenging, clean, and out-of-distribution (OOD)
815   tasks. The dataset consists of 2,850 prompt-chosen-rejected trios, where reward models are tasked
816   with accurately identifying the preferred response. RewardBench is structured around four key
817   dimensions—Chat, ChatHard, Safety, and Reasoning—each targeting different capabilities of the
818
      models. The overall RewardBench score is calculated by averaging the classification accuracy across
      these dimensions, providing a balanced assessment of model performance.
819
820
      ArenaHard We use ArenaHard (Li et al., 2024) score as proxy for a model’s human preferred
821
      level, it is shown to have the highest correlation and separability against gold human judgments in
822   ChatArena. While it doesn’t not score individual dimensions of preference, it provides an aggregate
823   signal for overall human preference. The delta is calculated as the difference between strong model
824   and weak model’s arena hard score.
825
826   AlpacaEval2.0 Both AlapcaEval2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024) and ArenaHard are win-rate based metrics
827   against answers generated by a reference model; and we use the recommended default choices of
828   reference models and judge models for both benchmarks. AlpacaEval2.0 addresses LLM-as-a-judge’s
829   bias for longer responses by providing a length adjusted win-rate that better correlates with human
830   ranking.
831
832   MT-Bench MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023) is a multi-turn benchmark that measures model perfor-
833   mance on 8 dimensions compared to a reference ground-truth.

951   Using a simple prompt, “You are a helpful AI assistant.”, we observe an overall improvement of 2.9
952   points on the RewardBench score compared to the baseline without a prompt. This result is surprising,
953   as it demonstrates that even minimal prompt engineering can significantly enhance performance.
954
      Notably, most of the gains occur in the Reasoning domain, which includes coding and math tasks.
955   To better understand the role of safety principles in the prompt design shown in Figure 3, we
956   conducted an iterative ablation study. Starting with a single safety guideline, we incrementally added
957   more principles (detailed in Figure 6) to the system prompt to assess their impact on performance:
958
959
       • safe1 includes only the first safety guideline.
960    • safe2 incorporates the first two guidelines.
961    • safe3 builds on this with three guidelines.
962    • safe4, our final design, includes all four safety guidelines.
963
       • safe5, adds additional guideline, but leads to performance regression.
964
965
      Interestingly, while adding the first few guidelines (safe1 to safe3) yielded consistent improvements
966   in Safety scores, including the fourth guideline (safe4) showed diminishing returns and even slight
967   regressions in some domains like Reasoning. We also experimented with adding a fifth guideline
968   (safe5), which led to performance degradation, suggesting that overloading the prompt with rules
969   may reduce effectiveness. Ultimately, we selected safe4 as our final configuration, as it provides
970   comprehensive coverage of safety scenarios while balancing performance across domains. However,
971   we acknowledge that leaner prompts like safe2 or safe3 deliver comparable results in safety-focused
      metrics.
