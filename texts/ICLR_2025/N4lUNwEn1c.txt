                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               In recent years, machine learning (ML) has significantly impacted the field of
               chemistry, facilitating advancements in diverse applications such as the prediction
016
               of molecular properties and the generation of molecular structures. Traditional
017
               string representations, such as the Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System
018            (SMILES), although widely adopted, exhibit limitations in conveying essential
019            physical and chemical properties of compounds. Conversely, vector representa-
020            tions, particularly chemical fingerprints, have demonstrated notable efficacy in
021            various ML tasks. Additionally, graph-based models, which leverage the inher-
022            ent structural properties of chemical compounds, have shown promise in improv-
023            ing predictive accuracy. This study investigates the potential of language models
024            based on fingerprints within a bimodal architecture that combines both graph-
025            based and language model components. We propose a method that integrates the
026
               aforementioned approaches, significantly enhancing predictive performance com-
               pared to conventional methodologies while simultaneously capturing more accu-
027
               rate chemical information.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The integration of machine learning (ML) has emerged as a transformative force in the natural sci-
      ences, particularly in the discipline of chemistry (Chithrananda et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2016; Wang
034
      et al., 2022). This integration encompasses various tasks, ranging from the regression of molecu-
035
      lar properties, exemplified by quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) models (Wu et al.,
036   2021; Rácz et al., 2021), to complex challenges, such as predicting nuclear magnetic resonance
037   (NMR) spectra from molecular structures (Yao et al., 2023). As an ever-evolving discipline, the
038   latest advancements in machine learning are gradually being adapted for applications in chemistry,
039   albeit with some delay. Molecular representations are fundamental to the application of machine
040   learning in chemistry, and three primary types are typically employed: graph-based (Reiser et al.,
041   2022), string-based (Heller et al., 2015; Weininger, 1988; Krenn et al., 2020), and vector represen-
042   tations (Rogers & Hahn, 2010; Durant et al., 2002).
043   Graph-based representations conceptualize molecules as molecular graphs, effectively capturing
044   their structural properties. This format naturally aligns with graph neural networks, which have
045   been successfully applied to numerous chemical problems, demonstrating their efficacy in molecu-
046   lar analysis.
047
      String representations, particularly the Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMILES)
048   (Weininger, 1988), are widely regarded as a standard method for the linear representation of molec-
049   ular structures. SMILES is typically used for storing compounds in databases and, despite its limita-
050   tions, effectively represents the structure of a molecule. However, it presents notable shortcomings.
051   Initially designed for efficient storage and representation of molecular data, SMILES lacks com-
052   prehensive information regarding the physical and chemical properties of compounds. As machine
053   learning progresses, the inadequacies of SMILES in facilitating in-depth semantic analysis have
      become increasingly evident.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The other type of representation comprises vector representations, notably fingerprints, which were
055   developed for substructure identification and similarity searching. Numerous studies, such as those
056   presented by (Sabando et al., 2022) and (Wang et al., 2020), have highlighted the effectiveness of
057   fingerprints as molecular representations in machine learning tasks, demonstrating promising results
058   even with conventional algorithms like Support Vector Machines (SVM). However, the potential of
059   fingerprints in the context of modern language models remains largely underexploited.
060
      Recent methods have sought to combine graph models with SMILES-based natural language pro-
061   cessing (NLP) transformers (Chithrananda et al., 2020; Ahmad et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019;
062   Shamshad et al., 2023; Cong et al., 2024), thereby integrating the strengths of both methodologies.
063   Building on this concept, our study aims to develop a transformer-based architecture, which serves
064   as our primary goal. We believe this approach is particularly advantageous due to the transformer’s
065   inherent versatility and flexibility, allowing it to address a variety of physicochemical tasks that
066   differ significantly in nature. For example, our architecture could potentially handle tasks such as
067   predicting molecular property values, classifying compounds based on their biological activity, gen-
068   erating novel molecular structures, exploring molecular interactions and complex challenges, such
069
      as predicting nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra from molecular structures or solving the
      co-crystallisation problem.
070
071   Given the diverse challenges presented by these tasks, we seek to create a unified framework that
072   requires only minor modifications for each specific application. By leveraging the strengths of
073   transformer models, we anticipate that our architecture will enable efficient and robust performance
074   across multiple domains within the field of cheminformatics. Ultimately, this comprehensive ap-
075
      proach aims to enhance predictive capabilities and foster innovation in drug discovery and molecular
      design.
076
077   The questions we want to tackle with our research are the following:
078
079         • To what extent can a transformer model based on fingerprints, either independently or
080           in conjunction with graph models, enhance performance compared to conventional ap-
081           proaches?
082
083         • Furthermore, can transformers trained on fingerprint representations improve the quality
084
              of multitasking embeddings, thereby providing more robust and nuanced representations
              that capture the complexities across diverse tasks?

      1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
088
089
      Despite the advancements made in machine learning applications within chemistry, existing meth-
090   ods, particularly those relying on traditional string representations such as SMILES, exhibit notable
091   shortcomings. These limitations include an inability to comprehensively capture the essential physi-
092   cal and chemical properties of compounds, which hampers their effectiveness in facilitating in-depth
093   semantic analysis. Furthermore, while vector representations like chemical fingerprints have proven
094   effective for certain tasks, their potential within modern language models remains largely unex-
095   ploited.
096   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have been effectively utilized to address a variety of challenges
097   within the field of chemistry (David et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2020). However, many GNNs are
098   specialized for specific tasks and are not inherently designed to generate vector representations of
099   chemical compounds. Several methodologies have been proposed to enhance GNN-based embed-
100   dings. For instance, (Hu et al., 2016) introduced two primary concepts: the recovery of masked
101   properties of a molecule, such as the type of a specific atom, and the application of contrastive
102   learning to minimize discrepancies between two subgraphs within a molecule. Additionally, Mol-
103   CLR (Wang et al., 2022) presents a framework based on augmenting molecular graphs by removing
104
      atoms, edges, and subgraphs, followed by training a model to reconstruct these components.
105   In our approach, we it implement more physically accurate atom masking and graph augmentation
106   techniques, enhancing the model’s understanding of molecular properties and ensuring a more robust
107   representation of the chemical structure. Furthermore, in the case of the Graphormer, we leverage a
      more advanced architecture that captures intricate relational patterns within the molecular graphs.


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      In this paper, we propose a novel methodology1 that integrates graph-based representations with
109   language models based on fingerprints, effectively addressing these limitations. Our approach en-
110   compasses four distinct architectures: the first is a single model based on the RoBERTa framework
111   utilizing Extended Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP), which serves as a baseline for our investiga-
112   tions. The second and third architectures are bimodal models that pair the RoBERTa model as the
113   language component with graph convolutional networks (GCNs) and Graph Isomorphism Networks
114   (GINs), respectively, utilizing contrastive learning for improved feature extraction. While these
115   smaller models train faster, they may exhibit less potential in complex tasks compared to the fourth
116   architecture, which employs a bimodal structure combining the RoBERTa model with Graphormer,
117
      an advanced graph transformer that captures more intricate relational patterns.
118   In each of the graph models – GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GIN (Xu et al., 2018), and Graphormer
119   (Ying et al., 2021) – we implement a mechanism that synthesizes existing concepts by masking atom
120   and edge features. Each of these models is trained not only to predict these masked features but also
121   to align the embeddings of two augmented versions of the same molecule. This approach reflects a
122   modification of contrastive learning, which remains underutilized in the chemistry domain.
123   By leveraging the structural attributes of molecules alongside the semantic richness of fingerprints,
124   our innovative bimodal architecture significantly enhances predictive performance and facilitates
125   application across a variety of physicochemical tasks.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
129
130   2.1   E XTENDED -C ONNECTIVITY F INGERPRINTS
131
      Extended-connectivity fingerprints, or shortly ECFP (Rogers & Hahn, 2010), are so-called circular
132
      fingerprints that assign a two-dimensional hash array to each molecule. Each element of such an
133   array is a hash corresponding to one atom. It encrypts a fixed set of physical and chemical properties
134   of this atom, such as charge, as well as information about its neighbours.
135
136
      As applied to our task, there are three significant particularities of ECFP. Firstly, the fingerprint
      of a single molecule consists of an array of hashes (i.e. in NLP terms, we can think of the array
137
      as a sentence and each individual hash as a word). Secondly, each hash is constructed based on a
138
      set of physical properties. Thus, each array element is based on physical and chemical data. And
139   lastly, there is a so-called diameter, which shows neighbouring atoms in one iteration. That being
140   said, we cover only those atoms, that are within the diameter’s reach. This sensible of surrounding
141   environment representation can be very useful for such tasks as molecular NMR spectroscopy, where
142   chemical environment plays crucial role in spectrum definition.
143
      Over the last few years, a number of methods ((Wang et al., 2020; Sturm et al., 2018)) have pointed
144
      out the effectiveness of using ECFP as features for training quite simple models to solve various
145   chemical problems.
146
147   Several approaches have employed Extended Connectivity Fingerprints as a representation for train-
148
      ing data in natural language processing (NLP) algorithms; however, these methods predominantly
      rely on relatively conventional machine learning techniques. One notable example is Mol2Vec
149
      (Jaeger et al., 2018), which implements the Word2Vec algorithm utilizing ECFP data representa-
150
      tion.

      2.2   SMILES- BASED NLP MODELS
154
      Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) were initially introduced to facilitate the generation of vector repre-
155
      sentations for natural language processing tasks. Since their inception, they have found widespread
156   application across a variety of domains, including speech recognition, medicine, and neuroscience
157   ((Shamshad et al., 2023; Cong et al., 2024)). There have been several efforts to adapt transformers
158   for chemical applications, exemplified by models such as SmilesBERT (Wang et al., 2019), Chem-
159   BERTa (Chithrananda et al., 2020), and ChemBERTa-2 (Ahmad et al., 2022).

      Many of these models have been trained on substantial datasets, including ZINC (Irwin et al., 2012)
163   and PubChem (Kim et al., 2023), demonstrating commendable performance in classification and
164   regression tasks across various established chemical benchmarks. By leveraging a more physics-
165   based input format, namely ECFP, and employing one of the most sophisticated language models,
166   we achieved a significant milestone: a large language model (LLM) trained from scratch on two
167   subsets of the PubChem dataset, comprising 2.5 million and 10 million entries, respectively. This
168   model exhibits performance comparable to those trained on the largest datasets within the field.

170   2.3   G RAPH MODELS
171
172   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have been effectively utilized to address a variety of challenges
173
      within the field of chemistry (David et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2020). Many GNNs are highly spe-
      cialized for specific tasks and are not inherently designed for generating vector representations of
174
      chemical compounds.
175
176   Several methodologies have been proposed to enhance GNN-based embeddings. For instance, (Hu
177   et al., 2016) introduced two primary concepts: the recovery of masked properties of a molecule, such
178   as the type of a specific atom, and the application of contrastive learning to minimize discrepancies
179
      between two subgraphs within a molecule. Additionally, MolCLR (Wang et al., 2022) presents a
      framework based on the augmentation of molecular graphs through the removal of atoms, edges,
180
      and subgraphs, followed by the training of a model to reconstruct these components.
181
182   In the graph component of our model, we advocate for an approach that synthesizes these concepts
183   and leverages state-of-the-art models. Specifically, we implement a mechanism to mask atom fea-
184   tures and edge features in the case of Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021). The model is trained not only
185
      to predict these masked features but also to align the embeddings of two augmented versions of the
      same molecule. This approach represents a modification of contrastive learning, a technique that
186
      remains underutilized in the chemistry domain.
187
188   Moreover, (Zhu et al., 2023) introduced a bimodal architecture incorporating a BERT-based large
189   language model (LLM) trained on SMILES alongside a GNN as the graphical representation model.
190   In contrast, we propose a distinct language model that is trained on fingerprints, thus providing a
191
      more physically informed perspective and an advanced graph model. Additionally, our approach
      includes notable differences in the final projection and the processing of embeddings derived from
192
      both the language and graph models.

      3.3   G RAPH MODEL
271
272   Creation and augmentation of graph. A graph is constructed from SMILES representations uti-
273   lizing the RDKit package, wherein each atom is represented as a vertex. Two parameters – atom
274   number and chirality – are designated as attributes of the vertices. In this framework, each bond is
275   represented as an edge, with the bond multiplicity (single, double, triple, or aromatic) serving as the
276   attribute for the edges.
277   Subsequently, 20% of the atomic attributes are masked, replacing them with a designated mask
278   token. In the case of graphomers, an equivalent approach is applied where 20% of the edge attributes
279   are also masked, transforming these attributes into the mask token.

375   3.6   S OME ADDITIONAL FEATURES
376
377   In this study, we utilize Extended Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFPs) as the data representation for
      the language model. Unlike SMILES, ECFPs not only encapsulate information pertaining to the


                                                                 7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      structural design of molecules, but also provide insights into the physical and chemical properties
379   of individual atoms and, importantly, their substructures. As previously discussed, this represen-
380   tation offers a more physically grounded, language-like framework for describing molecules. The
381   inclusion of a defined radius in ECFPs facilitates the adjustment of substructure sizes, which is
382   particularly significant for various chemical tasks, such as predicting NMR spectra or analyzing re-
383   action centers. Consequently, the application of a language model is justified when relevant data
384   reside within small substructures or individual atoms.
385   Conversely, a graph model comprehensively captures the entire molecular structure, which is ad-
386   vantageous for analyzing extensive connections characterized by numerous substructures. Such
387   scenarios are frequently encountered in biochemistry, particularly when addressing pharmacolog-
388   ical compounds or polymer-related challenges. The incorporation of a bimodal architecture that
389   combines both graph and language models enhances the vector representation of molecules in these
390   complex tasks.
391   In summary, our proposed architecture enables the generation of efficient vector representations for
392   molecules that exhibit significant variability in structure and physicochemical properties.

445   4.2   QSAR TASKS
446
447
      For the zero-shot evaluation of our proposed architecture, we selected a set of widely recog-
448
      nized cheminformatics benchmarks based on the quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR)
449   framework. While specially designed descriptors often outperform transformer-based models in
450   these contexts, the simplicity of these benchmarks allows for an assessment of the quality and ver-
451   satility of our architecture without the confounding influence of the large-scale superstructures typ-
452   ically encountered in more complex problem-solving scenarios.
453
      We evaluated four distinct models: RoBERTa (denoted as ECFP-BERT), which was trained on
454
      Extended Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP); ECFP-BERT in conjunction with Graph Isomorphism
455   Network (GIN); ECFP-BERT combined with Graph Convolutional Network (GCN); and ECFP-
456   BERT integrated with Graphormer. The first three models utilized a dataset comprising 10 million
457   entries sourced from the PubChem database, while the fourth model was trained on a smaller dataset
458   of 1 million entries from the same source.
459
      Several of the utilized datasets – namely, BBBP (Sakiyama et al., 2021), Tox21 (Richard et al.,
460
      2020), ClinTox (Wu et al., 2018), BACE (Wu et al., 2018), MUV (Rohrer & Baumann, 2009), and
461   HIV (Pan et al., 2007) – are specifically oriented towards classification tasks. The results for these
462   datasets, along with comparisons to other proposed architectures, are summarized in Table 1. The
463   receiver operating characteristic area under the curve (ROC-AUC) was employed as the evaluation
464   metric.
465
      Conversely, the remaining datasets – QM7 (Blum & Reymond, 2009), (Rupp et al., 2012), QM8
466
      (Ramakrishnan et al., 2015), QM9 (Ruddigkeit et al., 2012a), (Ruddigkeit et al., 2012b), FreeSolv
467   (Mobley & Guthrie, 2014), ESOL (Delaney, 2004), and Lipo – are focused on regression tasks. The
468   mean absolute error (MAE) was utilized as the metric for the QM7, QM8, and QM9 datasets, while
469   the mean squared error (MSE) served as the metric for FreeSolv, ESOL, and Lipo. The findings and
470   comparative analysis with other architectures are presented in Table 2.
471
      Most classification datasets are intrinsically linked to biochemical tasks, often featuring relatively
472
      large molecules. It has been observed that language models trained on SMILES representations,
473   such as ChemBERTa, yield only modest performance metrics. This limitation arises from their
474   inability to effectively account for atoms that are situated at significant distances from one another.
475   In contrast, our models exhibit substantial improvements in metric performance when incorporating
476   a graph component, which enhances the capture of molecular structural information.
477
      In the context of regression problems, where smaller molecules are more prevalent, language models
478
      demonstrate comparatively strong performance.
479
480   Thus, it becomes evident that both components of the architecture – namely the graph and language
481   models – are generally advantageous for achieving optimal model performance.
482   Graphormer, being a more complex model, tends to exhibit superior performance on large datasets.
483   However, it often struggles with smaller datasets due to insufficient data for effective pre-training.
484   Consequently, we recommend utilizing the BERT+GIN and BERT+GCN models for tasks charac-
485   terized by limited data availability. Conversely, the BERT+Graphormer architecture is more suitable
      for intricate tasks that require the establishment of complex internal connections among nodes.

522   Our proposed set of architectures, consisting of ECFP RoBERTa (ECFP-BERT) and bimodal config-
523   urations that integrate ECFP-BERT as the language branch alongside Graph Convolutional Network
524
      (GCN), Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN), or Graphormer as the graph branch, has demonstrated
      some of the most promising performance metrics compared to existing models in the domain for var-

      ious quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) problems across a range of well-established
526
      benchmarks.
527
528   While specialized descriptors typically outperform transformer-based models for these challenges,
529   these benchmarks serve as a simplified context, thereby allowing us to assess the quality and versa-

      tility of our architecture without the confounding influence of large-scale superstructures commonly
      encountered in more complex scenarios.
531
532   To further validate our model, we intend to explore additional challenges, including co-crystal pre-

534   other physicochemical tasks. For such demanding tasks, transformer-based architectures often yield
535   significantly superior results compared to straightforward augmentations of task-specific descrip-

      tors.

      A.1   ECFP CONSTRUCTION ALGORITHM
705
706   ECFP (Rogers & Hahn, 2010) is a so-called circular fingerprint that assigns a two-dimensional hash
707   array to each molecule using the following algorithm:
708
709         1. The initial step is assigning an integer identifier to each atom.
710         2. The iterative update stage, in which the identifier of each atom is updated with the identi-
711            fiers of its neighbours.
712         3. Duplicate removal - a stage in which several occurrences of the same feature are reduced
713            to a single representation in the feature list.
714
715   One iteration for a single atom is as follows:
716
717         1. An array of integers containing the iteration number and the ID of the given atom is initial-
718
               ized.
719         2. The attached atoms are sorted in deterministic order using the bond order (single, double,
720            triple, and aromatic) and the current ID of each attached atom. or each attachment, the
721            attachment ID and bond order are added to the array.
722         3. The array is hashed into a single 32-bit integer. This is the new atom identifier.

842   Additionally, the atomization energies for the QM7 molecules are computed using methods aligned

961   MUV. The Maximum Unbiased Validation (MUV) dataset serves as a benchmark for evaluating
962   virtual screening techniques in drug discovery. Selected from the PubChem BioAssay database,
963
      the MUV dataset comprises 17 challenging tasks associated with approximately 90,000 chemical
      compounds, strategically designed to facilitate robust validation of virtual screening methodologies.
964
      A key feature of this dataset is its foundation in refined nearest neighbor analysis, a technique de-
965
      rived from spatial statistics that offers a mathematical framework for the nonparametric analysis of
966   mapped point patterns. This methodology enables the systematic design of benchmark datasets by
967   purging compounds that exhibit activity against pharmaceutically relevant targets while eliminating
968   unselective hits. Through topological optimization and experimental design strategies, the refined
969   nearest neighbor analysis constructs data sets of active compounds and decoys, ensuring they are
970   unbiased concerning analogue bias and artificial enrichment. Consequently, the MUV dataset pro-
971   vides an essential resource for Maximum Unbiased Validation, empowering researchers to assess
      and improve the predictive performance of virtual screening methods in a more rigorous manner.
