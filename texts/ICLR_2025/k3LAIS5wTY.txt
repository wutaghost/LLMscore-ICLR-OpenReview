008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010
               In response to critiques of existing evaluation methods for temporal link prediction
011
               (TLP) models, we propose a novel approach to verify if these models truly capture
012            temporal patterns in the data. Our method involves a sanity check formulated
013            as a counterfactual question: “What if a TLP model is tested on a temporally
014            distorted version of the data instead of the real data?” Ideally, a TLP model that
015            effectively learns temporal patterns should perform worse on temporally distorted
016            data compared to real data. We provide an in-depth analysis of this hypothesis
017            and introduce two data distortion techniques to assess well-known TLP models.
018            Our contributions are threefold: (1) We introduce two simple techniques to distort
019            temporal patterns within a graph, generating temporally distorted test splits of
020
               well-known datasets for sanity checks. These distortion methods are applicable to
               any temporal graph dataset. (2) We perform counterfactual analysis on six TLP
021
               models JODIE, TGAT, TGN, CAWN, GraphMixer, and DyGFormer to evaluate
022
               their capability in capturing temporal patterns across different datasets. (3) We
023            introduce two metrics – average time difference (ATD) and average count difference
024            (ACD) – to provide a comprehensive measure of a model’s predictive performance.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In static graphs, link prediction refers to the task of predicting whether an edge exists between two
030   nodes after having observed other edges in the graph. Temporal link prediction (TLP) is a dynamic
031   extension of link prediction wherein the task is to predict whether a link (edge) exists between any
032   two nodes in the future based on the historical observations (Qin and Yeung, 2023). The predictive
033
      capability of TLP models make them useful in applications pertaining to dynamic graphs, such as
      product recommendations (Qin et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2021), social network content or account
034
      recommendation (Fan et al., 2019; Daud et al., 2020), fraud detection in financial networks (Kim
035
      et al., 2024), and resource allocation, to name a few.
036
037   In the TLP literature (Kumar et al., 2019; Trivedi et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; Rossi et al., 2020;
038   Wang et al., 2020; Cong et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023), the TLP task is treated as a binary classification
039
      problem where the query
040
               q1 : “Does an edge exist between the nodes u and v at time t?”
041
042   is processed by a model and then compared with the ground truth following which metrics such as
043   area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AU-ROC), and average precision (AP) are
044   reported. The ground truth consists of positive samples, and a fixed number of random negative
045   samples. There are a couple of issues in the binary classification approach. Firstly, the timestamps in
046   the query are restricted to the timestamps present in the ground truth, which makes the evaluation
047   biased and does not test the model’s performance in the continuous time range. Secondly, checking
048   for the existence of an edge at a specific timestamp is an ill-posed question, and instead the existence
049   of an edge should be queried within a finite time-interval. Lastly, the negative edge sampling strategy,
050
      and the number of negative samples per positive sample impact the performance metrics as seen in
      EXH (Poursafaei and Rabbany, 2023).
051
052   Alternatively, in a rank-based approach, the query is formulated as:
053
               q2 : “Which nodes are likely to have an edge with node u at time t?”


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this case, the model returns an ordered list of nodes arranged from most likely to least likely.
055   Then, the rank of the ground truth edge is returned if a match is found, and if not, a high number is
056   reported. For all the edges in the test data, metrics such as Mean Average Rank (MAR) or Mean
057   Reciprocal Rank (MRR) can be reported to assess the performance of the model (Huang et al., 2024).
058   While the rank-based metrics are more intuitive than AU-ROC and AP, the issues regarding binary
059   classification mentioned above still remain unaddressed. To give a true picture of the predictive power
060   of the TLP models, a penalty term should be introduced to account for the nodes that are incorrectly
061   estimated to form an edge with node u at time t.
062   In a recent work, Poursafaei et al. (2022) highlighted that the state-of-the-art (SoTA) performance
063   of some TLP models on the standard benchmark datasets is near-perfect. This is counterintuitive
064   because TLP is a challenging task, even more challenging than link prediction of static graphs, due
065   to the additional degree of freedom in the data induced by the temporal dimension. The flaw in the
066   evaluation method is attributed to the limited negative sampling strategy, and the authors propose a
067   new negative edge sampling strategy which results in a different ranking of the baselines.
068   Inspired by the critique of the evaluation method, we propose a method to conduct sanity check of
069   the TLP models to determine if they truly capture the temporal patterns in the data. The sanity check
070   is formulated as the counterfactual question (Pearl, 2009):
071
072            “What if a TLP model which is trained on a temporal graph is tested on temporally
073            distorted version of the data instead of the real data?”
074
      Ideally, a TLP model which is capable of learning the temporal patterns should perform worse on
075
      temporally distorted data compared to the real data. We conduct an in-depth analysis of this argument
076   and introduce various data distortion techniques to assess well-known TLP models.
077
078   Contributions The contributions of our work can be summarised as follows:
079
080          • We introduce simple techniques to distort the temporal patterns within a graph. These
081            techniques are then used to generate temporally distorted version of the test split of some
082            famous datasets which can be used for sanity check. Moreover, the distortion methods can
083            be applied to any temporal graph dataset [Link to code repository].
084          • We perform counterfactual analysis on TLP models such as JODIE (Kumar et al., 2019),
085            TGAT (Xu et al., 2020), TGN (Rossi et al., 2020), CAWN (Wang et al., 2020), GraphMixer
086            (Cong et al., 2023), and DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023) to check whether they are capable of
087            capturing the temporal patters within various datasets.
088          • We propose two metrics: average time difference (ATD), and average count difference
089            (ACD) to measure the performance of TLP models. These metrics can provide a holistic
090            picture of a model’s predictive performance.
091          • Lastly, we propose an alternative evaluation strategy for TLP through which the existing
092            pitfalls of binary classification and ranking methods can be avoided.

      2.1   D EFINITIONS
097
098   In TLP literature, continuous-time temporal graphs with instantaneous edges are often considered,
099   where edges represent interaction events between two nodes at a specific point in time. Alternatively,
100   temporal graphs can be defined with edges that appear at a certain time and either persist for a duration
101   (Celikkanat et al., 2024) or accumulate indefinitely. In this work, we focus on the instantaneous
102   edge temporal graph, also known as interaction graphs (Qin et al., 2024) or unevenly sampled edge
103   sequence (Qin and Yeung, 2023).
104   Definition 2.1. A temporal graph with m ∈ N instantaneous edges formed between nodes in U and
105   V is defined as G = (U, V, E), where E ≜ {(ui , vi , ti ) : i ∈ [m], ui ∈ U, vi ∈ V, ti ∈ R} denotes
106   the set of edges. The tuple (u, v, t) is referred to as an edge event.
107
      While the definition caters to bipartite structure, with U = V, it can also represent general graphs.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Definition 2.2. The occurrences of a particular edge (u, v) in E is denoted as E(u,v) and defined as
109
      E(u,v) ≜ {(u, v, t) : (u, v, t) ∈ E}.
110
111   Definition 2.3. The slice of edges in E with timestamps in the range (t1 , t2 ) is denoted as E(t1 , t2 ),
112   and defined as E(t1 , t2 ) ≜ {(u, v, t) : (u, v, t) ∈ E, t ∈ (t1 , t2 )}.
113   Definition 2.4. The timestamps in E consisting of m ∈ N edges can be extracted through a function
114   T : (U × V × R)m → Rm as T (E) ≜ {t : (u, v, t) ∈ E}.

146   3     C OUNTERFACTUAL A NALYSIS
147
148   A temporal graph is characterized by (1) the order in which the edges appear, (2) the frequency
149   with which edges appear over time, and (3) the time gap between any two edge events. In this
150   work, we refer to these characteristics as temporal patterns. Furthermore, if temporal patterns
151   observed in the past enable predictions of future temporal patterns that outperform naïve estimates on
152   a specific performance metric, then the temporal data is considered learnable. This does not require
153
      the temporal pattern to remain consistent over time; rather, it suggests that future changes can be
      estimated from past observations.
154
155
      Experiment Setup A model f is trained on a temporal graph Etrain and tested on Etest through the
156
      binary classification approach resulting in a performance metric such as AP. The train and test data
157   are chronologically split from the same temporal graph which is assumed to be generated through a
158   common causal mechanism, i.e., Etrain = E(0, τ0 ), and Etest = E(τ0 , T ). In light of the experimental
159   setup, we ask the following question:
160
161            Would the model f which is trained on Etrain perform well if tested on a distorted
               version of Etest instead of Etest ?

      To formalise the question in the counterfactual framework (Pearl, 2019), we consider the following
163   statements:
164
165       x′ : The model f is tested on Etest
166        y ′ : The performance metric is α
167       x : The model f is tested on a temporally distorted version of Etest
168
           y : The performance metric is less than α
169   Additionally, yx is read as y when x. The counterfactual question is framed as P (yx | x′ , y ′ ), i.e.,
170
171            The probability that the performance metric would be less than α had the test data
172            been a temporally distorted version of Etest , given the performance metric was
173            observed to be at least α when the model was tested on Etest .

      5    D ISCUSSION
433
434
      Some of the TLP models used in this work such as GraphMixer, and DyGFormer are considered
435   the SoTA on most datasets, with near-perfect performance. However, as we showed earlier, a higher
436   metric alone is not indicative of good performance without sanity checks. The counterfactual question
437   helps make the evaluation more explainable, as models that perform worse on temporally distorted
438   data with high ATD and ACD can claim superiority over models that do not. An ideal TLP model
439   should be able to capture the difference in the count of edge events, their order, and the temporal
440   shifts in the edge events.
441   To reiterate, if the performance of the model on the temporally distorted test data is similar or better
442   than the performance on the original test data, then it implies one the following:
443
444          • the model has not made use of the temporal information in the training set,
445          • there is no useful temporal information in the dataset,
446
             • the temporal distortion is weak.
447
448
      In the absence of a guarantee that the dataset has useful temporal information that can aid prediction,
449   we can compare different models by comparing the performance gaps.
450
451   Future Work Moving away from the binary classification approach to assess the performance of
452   temporal link prediction, future research should explore a generative approach where after observing
453   a temporal graph from time t ∈ (0, τ0 ), the model can generate a temporal graph in t ∈ (τ0 , T ). This
454   generated temporal graph can then be compared with the ground truth to measure similarity and
455   assess the performance of the model. The proposed metrics ATD and ACD can be used to measure
456   the difference in the timestamps, as well as the occurrence frequency of the edges. Furthermore, the
457   same model architecture used by the TLP models discussed in this paper, can be further improved
458   by devising new training objectives that incorporate counterfactual analysis through the distortions
459
      S HUFFLE and I NTENSE.
460
461
      Conclusion In this work, rather than introducing novel datasets, we present techniques for generat-
      ing temporally distorted versions of any temporal graph dataset. This makes the contribution relevant
462
      even for datasets which will be introduced in the future. To the best of our knowledge, we are the
463
      first to apply counterfactual analysis to TLP and hope that it can help standardize the assessment of
464   TLP models.

573   We make use of the following models4 to test the counterfactual framework:
574
575           • JODIE (Kumar et al., 2019) uses a recurrent neural network (RNN) to generate node
576
                embeddings for each interaction event. The future embedding of a node is estimated through
                a novel projection operator which is turn in used to predict future edge events.
577
578           • TGAT (Xu et al., 2020) relies on self-attention mechanism to generate node embeddings to
579             capture the temporal evolution of the graph structure.
580           • TGN (Rossi et al., 2020) combine memory modules with graph-based operators to create an
581             encoder-decoder pair capable of creating temporal node embeddings.
582           • CAWN (Wang et al., 2020) propose a novel strategy based on the law of triadic closure,
583             where temporal walks retrieve the dynamic graph motifs without explicitly counting and
584             selecting the motifs. The node IDs are replaced with the hitting counts to facilitate inductive
585             inference.
586           • GraphMixer (Cong et al., 2023) use a simple architecture where the encoder and decoder
587             are designed using multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs).
588           • DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023) use a transformer to learn from nodes’ first-hop interactions
589             and report SoTA results on most of the datasets.
