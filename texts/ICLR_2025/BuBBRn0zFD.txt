009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Pairwise interactions between perturbations to a system can provide evidence for
013
               the causal dependencies of the underlying underlying mechanisms of a system.
               When observations are low dimensional, hand crafted measurements, detecting
014
               interactions amounts to simple statistical tests, but it is not obvious how to detect
015            interactions between perturbations affecting latent variables. We derive two in-
016            teraction tests that are based on pairwise interventions, and show how these tests
017            can be integrated into an active learning pipeline to efficiently discover pairwise
018            interactions between perturbations. We illustrate the value of these tests in the
019            context of biology, where pairwise perturbation experiments are frequently used to
020            reveal interactions that are not observable from any single perturbation. Our tests
021            can be run on unstructured data, such as the pixels in an image, which enables a
022
               more general notion of interaction than typical cell viability experiments, and can
               be run on cheaper experimental assays. We validate on several synthetic and real
023
               biological experiments that our tests are able to identify interacting pairs effectively.
024
               We evaluate our approach on a real biological experiment where we knocked out 50
025            pairs of genes and measured the effect with microscopy images. We show that we
026            are able to recover significantly more known biological interactions than random
027            search and standard active learning baselines.

      Across the sciences, measurement of pairwise interaction between perturbations often reveals the
      existence of underlying mechanisms that single perturbations cannot. For example, quantum entan-
033
      glement experiments reject classical laws by showing that entangled particles’ spins are perfectly
034
      anti-correlated once we make a measurement, when classical laws predict independence. In eco-
035   nomics, people may be more willing to pay for goods when presented in a bundle than they are to
036   pay for each good in isolation, which reveals complements in the underlying consumer preferences.
037   And in biology—the application area on which we focus—the concept of synthetic lethality (Nijman,
038   2011) occurs when the simultaneous perturbation of two genes results in cell death while individual
039   perturbations do not, thereby revealing complementary roles in the underlying cellular mechanisms.
040   To demonstrate a pairwise interaction a scientist has to carefully design three steps of the experiment:
041
      1. Measurement: the expert to selects properties of the system to measure to reveal an interaction.
042
         E.g., measuring cell viability may reveal synthetic lethality, while the cell’s colour will not.
043
      2. Hypothesis testing: an interaction is a deviation from what we expect under a null hypothesis
044      that assumes independent effects. The expert needs to predict the outcome under independence
045      and compare this prediction to the actual outcome.
046   3. Selection: there are typically many variables that one could perturb, but only a small fraction will
047      exhibit surprising interactions. The expert selects pairs of perturbations from all possible pairs.
048
      Measuring interactions is further complicated by the fact that measurement and hypothesis testing
049   are interdependent. We can see this in synthetic lethality example, where if we choose to measure
050   the fraction of cells that survived the respective perturbations, then in order to test independence we
051   would need to test whether P (survivea ∩ surviveb ) = P (survivea )P (surviveb ). If we had instead
052   measured the fraction of cells that died, we would have needed to test whether P (diea ∪ dieb ) =
053   P (diea ) + P (dieb ) − P (diea )P (dieb ). The state of the cells in the respective petri dishes is the same
      in both cases, but correctly testing (in)dependence depends on how the expert chose to measure

074   Modern high throughput screening platforms (Dove, 2007; Baillargeon et al., 2019; Blay et al., 2020;
075   Bock et al., 2022; Fabio et al., 2023; Morris et al., 2023; Messner et al., 2023) enables running

      example, cell painting assays (Bra, 2016; Fay et al., 2023; Chandrasekaran et al., 2023) provide
      microscopy images of cells, capturing the same information a human expert would measure, but
078
      without pre-selecting a specific property like cell viability. This lets us measure all the potential
079   properties of interest at once—albeit as unstructured signal data, such as raw pixel images or sensor
080   measurements, rather than preprocessed properties—but it is not clear how to use these measurements
081   for interaction testing and selection in order to efficiently discover pairwise interactions. This suggests
082   the primary question that this paper seeks to address: if we dispense with prespecified structured
083   measurements, can we automate the discovery of pairwise interactions by designing testing and
084   selection procedures that efficiently detect interactions from unstructured data?
085   To test for interactions, we show that pairwise perturbations are separable if they provide no additional
086   information beyond what is already known from the single perturbations. This separability property
087   can be tested by comparing density ratios of the single and double perturbations to the control
088   distribution. We also develop a test for the case where pairs of perturbations affect disjoint subsets of
089   the outcome space. For example, if two perturbations affect different organelles in the cell, each of
090   which affect disjoint pixels in an image. This strong form of non-interaction allows us to compose
091
      summary statistics from single perturbations to predict the corresponding statistics from double
      perturbations. In particular, this lets us compose embeddings of single perturbations to predict the
092
      embeddings of double perturbations, similar to word-vector analogies Mikolov et al. (2013).
093
094   With this notion of an interaction, we address the second task of selection via efficient experimental
095
      design. We can search the space of pairwise experiments by selecting pairs of perturbations that are
      likely to result in large test statistics. In doing so, we reduce the problem of finding interacting pairs
096
      of perturbations into an active matrix completion problem. By defining this notion of interaction,
097
      we avoid the need to characterize uncertainty over the pixel-level outcomes (or some embedding
098   thereof). Instead we directly model the posterior over an unknown reward matrix defined by the test
099   statistics and at every round select actions striking a balance between exploration and exploitation via
100   information directed sampling (IDS; Russo & Van Roy, 2016; Xu et al., 2022).
101
      We evaluated our approach on both synthetic and real biological experiments. On the synthetic tasks,
102
      we found that our tests were able to detect both forms of dependence, validating our theory. On a
103   benchmark consisting of all pairs of gene knockouts for 50 genes in HUVEC cells, we found that our
104   approach using IDS discovers pairs of genes with higher interaction scores significantly faster than
105   baselines, resulting in 15% more known biological interactions being discovered and potentially far
106   more novel interactions. The interactions we detected were also complementary to those which would
107   have been discovered using existing cosine similarity-based approaches, and as a result, the two
      approaches can be combined to get a more detailed estimate of the relationships between genes from

119   2    R ELATED WORK
120   Causal representation learning Our approach to the problem of detecting interactions builds
121   on the modelling assumptions developed in nonlinear independent component analysis (Hyvärinen
122   & Pajunen, 1999; Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2016; Hyvarinen et al., 2019) and causal representation
123   learning (Schölkopf et al., 2021), where we assume we observe some nonlinear mixing function, g(·),
124   of the underlying latent variables. The causal representation learning literature typically focuses on
125   disentangling latent variables, whereas we look for testible implications without disentanglement. Our
126
      data generating process assumptions are most similar to the interventional setting (Ahuja et al., 2023;
      Squires et al., 2023; Buchholz et al., 2023; Varıcı et al., 2024). If we could successfully disentangle
127
      latent variables, then our task would be straightforward, but disentanglement is challenging in
128
      practice because it is not possible to validate whether an algorithm has succeeded in disentangling
129   latent variables without access to ground truth. Like us, Jiang & Aragam (2023) also attempt
130   to learn latent dependence properties (they characterize the whole latent DAG, not just marginal
131   dependence) without disentanglement, but they assume a fixed bipartite graph of dependence between
132   latents and observations, which does not apply to the pixel-level observations that we study. Zhang
133   et al. (2023) develop causal representation learning techniques to disentangle latent variables and
134   characterize conditions for extrapolation. Like us, they focus on biological applications, but they rely
135   on stronger polynomial assumptions to achieve disentanglement. Their conditions for extrapolation
      are complementary to our separability tests: they argue extrapolation is possible when interventions
136
      affect non-overlapping latents, while we aim to discover when interventions affect overlapping latents.
137
      Finally, our separability test is closely related to, and inspired by Wang et al. (2023), but whereas they
138   assume separability of concepts to manipulate generative models, we aim to test for an analogous
139   notion of independence in experimental data.
140
      Representation learning for gene knockouts Our experiments build on a number of recent works
141
      showing the effectiveness of embedding cells presented into a representation space Sypetkowski et al.
142
      (2023); Kraus et al. (2023); Xun et al. (2023). These works show that you can infer that genes code
143   for proteins that form part of complexes by finding embeddings that are highly cosine similar. This
144   works because knocking out proteins on the same pathway will induce the same morphological effect,
145   but this approach is limited to effects that are revealed by single perturbations. There have also been
146   a number of papers that have attempted to learn disentangled representations of cells (e.g. Lotfollahi
147   et al., 2023; Bereket & Karaletsos, 2023; Lopez et al., 2023), mostly from gene expression data.
148   Design of Gene Knockout Experiments There is growing literature on developing algorithms
149   for the design of gene knockout experiments. Typically the problem is studied in the context of
150   discovering single gene-knockouts (Mehrjou et al., 2021) which result in a particular phenotypic
151   effect of interest. A variety of methods including bandits (Pacchiano et al., 2023) and traditional
152   experimental design (Lyle et al., 2023) approaches have been studied in this context. Further,
153   approaches which aim to learn predictors for the effects of unseen gene knockouts typically operate
154
      on RNA-seq data (Huang et al., 2023). RNA-Seq data is far more structured than the image data we
      consider: the former is a high-dimensional vector of count data where each element corresponds to a
155
      particular gene’s expression, while the latter is just an unstructured collection of pixel intensities.
156
157   Experimental design. The task of efficiently designing experiments to gain the most information
158   about a system can be formalized as sequential Bayesian optimal experimental design (BOED;
159
      Ryan et al., 2016; Foster, 2021; Rainforth et al., 2023), where the goal to design experiments
      x ∈ X with outcomes y ∈ Y governed by a generative process y ∼ p(y | γ, x) with parameters
160
      γ. The experiments (x1 , . . . , xT ) are performed sequentially, with the objective of maximizing the
161   information gain (Lindley, 1956; Sebastiani & Wynn, 2000). However, applying this framework in

286   4    S ELECTING PERTURBATION PAIRS TO EFFICIENTLY DISCOVER
287        INTERACTIONS
288
      With frameworks for testing separability and disjointedness in place, the next question is how to
289   select which experiments to run. The space of possible perturbation pairs is too large to test all
290   combinations, as experiments are costly. For instance, pairwise knockouts of 20,000 genes would
291   require about 200 million experiments (not including replicates). In this section, we explore how
292   experimental design and bandit algorithms can efficiently select perturbation pairs likely to interact.
293   Selection of perturbation pairs as active matrix completion. The testing frameworks discussed in
294   Section 3 prescribe test statistics which can be used to detect pairwise interactions. The test statistics,
295   however, require samples from p(x|δi,j ), which entails running pairwise perturbation experiments.
296   We are thus interested in developing an approach for selecting pairs of perturbations which are likely
297   to have high values for the test statistics and are thus likely to reveal pairwise interactions.
298   The test statistics for pairs of perturbations can be viewed as an (unknown) symmetric matrix
299   R ∈ Rn×n , where each entry Ri,j contains the value of the test statistic that we will observe if we
300   run perturbation δij . R is unknown a priori but we are allowed to select entries to observe. This
301   can be viewed as an active matrix completion problem (Chakraborty et al., 2013). Active matrix
302   completion is a variant of the standard matrix completion problem (Laurent, 2009) where values of
303   entries of the matrix can be sequentially queried. Framing the problem of selecting perturbation pairs
304   as an active matrix completion allows us to leverage existing efficient algorithms.
305   Adaptive sampling for discovery. In particular, we use the framework of adaptive sampling
306   for discovery (ASD; Xu et al., 2022) which provides a bandit-based approach for active matrix
307   completion. ASD involves using information directed sampling (IDS; Russo & Van Roy, 2016) in
308   a “discovery” setting, where an action is only selected once. This is an instantiation of the general
309
      sleeping experts setting (Kanade et al., 2009), where the set of available actions shrinks every round.

327   reward matrix from our posterior, Ṙ ∼ (R|Ht ), and then comparing the reward from action, a, to the
328   reward an agent would have received from selecting the optimal action, a∗ = arg maxa∈∆t Ṙ(a);

      thus far. Next step involves selecting a batch of perturbations based on the information ratio. The
337

343   Xu et al. (2022) to approximate gt with the conditional variance vt (a) = Vart (E[Ra |a∗1 , a]). vt (a) is

356   are no efficient algorithms for combinatorial bandits in the discovery setting, we resort to a simple
357   greedy scheme to select batches with ASD. Specifically, instead of picking a single action which
358   minimizes the information ratio, we pick b actions with the lowest information ratio.

      6                                   D ISCUSSION
516   This paper presented a method for efficiently detecting interactions. We present interactivity scores
517   for both separability and disjointedness which both allow us to measure interactions between pertur-
518   bations, and run experiments only on pairs of perturbations for which we will fail to compositionally
519   generalize. From an active learning perspective, disjointedness is powerful: when we can learn
520
      a good posterior over this test statistic, we effectively have a “confidence score” for whether two
      perturbations are likely to compose additively. This allows us to dramatically reduce the number of
521
      experiments we run by experimenting only where embeddings are unpredictable. Separability is a
522   more intuitive notion of (in-)dependence in that we directly test for whether perturbations interact in
523   latent space; we aim to explore whether this can be used to recover intervention targets.
524
525
      Limitations While many known interactions scored highly according to our test statistics, the
      overall correlation between known interactions and this metric was relatively low. It is not clear
526
      whether this is the result of a lack of specificity, or whether this is because we are in fact discovering
527   real relationships that are not known to biology. To test this would require additional experimentation
528   with orthogonal assays. Additionally, the results for the separability tests depended on the quality of
529   the KL estimator. We used the SMILE estimator which is somewhat sensitive to the choice of the
530   clipping parameter, τ . In our experiments, we simply used a default of τ = 5, but we need a robust
531   method for choosing this hyperparameter to ensure this test is agnostic to modalities. Finally, the
532   separability score test lacks a rigorous rejection criterion, and a characterization of type I and type II
533   error. While this is less critical when the goal is to use the test statistic for active learning (introduced
534
      later in Section 4), a formal hypothesis testing framework is important from a statistical perspective.
      This issue has been addressed in a follow-up work.

892   A    R ELATED WORK ON INTERACTION DETECTION
893
894
895   Biological interactions. Ahlmann-Eltze et al. (2024) is concurrent work that explores the feasibility
896   of predicting measurements from double gene knockouts based on single gene knockouts. They show
897   empirically that for certain gene pairs, the simple additive relationship E[x|δi,j ] = E[x|δi ]+E[x|δj ]−
898   E[x|δ0 ] holds for many pairs i, j, where x denotes the measurements of gene expression. This model
899   is a special case of Eq. (5) when the embedding function h(·) is the identity function. Consequently,
900   definition 4 can be view as a sufficient condition for when the additive model proposed in Ahlmann-
901
      Eltze et al. (2024) provides accurate predictions for double knockouts and our disjointedness test
      can be seen as a method for testing whether this holds. Our approach, however, is more general, as
902
      it establishes sufficient conditions for additivity under any embedding function h(·). Rather than
903   predict double perturbation outcomes directly—which, as noted by Ahlmann-Eltze et al. (2024),
904   has seen limited success—we focus on identifying cases where the additive assumption fails so
905   that we can measure these outcomes experimentally. The heat maps in Fig. 5 (left and middle)

       Modeling assumptions. In terms of the specific concern about model assumptions in the active
1373   matrix completion problem for our gene-gene interaction example, we adopt the following Bayesian
1374   model to recover R:
1375                              R = U V T + ϵ, U, V ∈ Rd×r , ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 ),
1376
       where r is a hyperparameter smaller than d, encoding the low-rank structure of R. We impose a
1377   Gaussian prior distribution on the entries of U and V , and the inference result is characterized by
1378   the posterior distribution p(U, V |R). The active matrix completion problem aims to approximate
1379   p(U, V |R) without requiring all entries of R.
1380
       As noted earlier, the low-rank assumption of R is justified by the morphological similarity between
1381   genes, particularly those within the same biological pathways. Our empirical results in subsection 5.2
1382   show that gene-gene interaction scores tend to cluster by relevant pathways.
1383
       Regarding the choice of the prior, we emphasize that the validity of this Bayesian inference does
1384
       not depend on U and V being generated from a Gaussian distribution. The Gaussian prior is merely
1385
       a modeling assumption for U and V , while the posterior distribution is non-Gaussian. We remark
1386   that our ADS pipeline does not require any changes if other priors for U and V are adopted. Poorly
1387   specified priors might lead to suboptimal posterior characterizations of U and V , reducing the
1388   effectiveness of the active matrix completion problem. However, our empirical results suggest that
1389   the active matrix completion method performs effectively – validating the choice of the Gaussian
1390   priors.
1391   Details. To obtain samples from the posterior distribution over the low-rank reward matrix (with
1392   rank m), we use stochastic variational inference (Wingate & Weber, 2013; Ranganath et al., 2014),
1393   and specifically the implementation from numpyro (Bradbury et al., 2018; Bingham et al., 2019;
1394   Phan et al., 2019). We train the variational posterior for 5000 epochs with a learning rate of 0.01
1395   using the Adam optimizer. We then generate k samples from the posterior. For each algorithm, we
1396   run a sweep over all a set of hyperparameters. We then pick the best hyperparameters and run the
1397
       experiment over 10 different seeds to get the final results. For IDS, we tune m ∈ {3, 5, 7, 10, 12}, λ ∈
       {2, 3, 4, 5} and k ∈ {500, 750, 1000, 1500}. For TS and US, we tune m ∈ {3, 5, 7, 10, 12} and k ∈
1398
       {500, 750, 1000, 1500}. For UCB we tune m ∈ {3, 5, 7, 10, 12}, β ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5}
1399
       and k ∈ {500, 750, 1000, 1500}, where β controls the exploration.
