                      Content Warning: This paper contains examples of harmful language.

011            Along with the remarkable successes of Large Language Models (LLMs), recent
012            research also started to explore the security threats of LLMs, including jailbreaking
013            attacks. Attackers carefully craft jailbreaking prompts such that a target LLM will
014            respond to the harmful question. Existing jailbreaking attacks require either human
015            experts or leveraging complicated algorithms to craft jailbreaking prompts. In this
016
               paper, we introduce B OOST, a simple attack that leverages only the eos tokens.
               We demonstrate that rather than constructing complicated jailbreaking prompts,
017
               the attacker can simply append a few eos tokens to the end of a harmful question.
018
               It will bypass the safety alignment of LLMs and lead to successful jailbreaking
019            attacks. We further apply B OOST to four representative jailbreak methods and
020            show that the attack success rates of these methods can be significantly enhanced
021            by simply adding eos tokens to the prompt. To understand this simple but novel
022            phenomenon, we conduct empirical analyses. Our analysis reveals that (1) adding
023            eos tokens makes the target LLM believe the input is much less harmful, and (2)
024            eos tokens have low attention values and do not affect LLM’s understanding of
025            the harmful questions, leading the model to actually respond to the questions. Our
026            findings uncover how fragile an LLM is against jailbreak attacks, motivating the
027
               development of strong safety alignment approaches.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030   Despite the tremendous efforts in conducting safety alignments, LLMs are still shown to be vulnerable
031   to jailbreak attacks (Shen et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Shah et al., 2023b;
032   Yu et al., 2023a). By carefully crafting the jailbreak prompts embedded with harmful questions,
033   malicious users can bypass the safety mechanisms and make the model generate harmful content or
034   extract sensitive information. With more and more widespread usage of LLMs, jailbreaking attacks
035   have become a major threat that model developers are actively preventing. Actually, most of the
036   proprietary LLM providers like OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are actively working on improving
037   the robustness of their models against jailbreak attacks in their latest products (OpenAI, 2023; Team
038
      et al., 2023; Anthropic).
039   Most existing jailbreaking attacks require extensive human expertise (Yuan et al., 2023; Touvron
040   et al., 2023a;b; Chao et al., 2023) or advanced algorithms (Zou et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023a; Liu
041   et al., 2023a) to craft effective jailbreaking prompts. In this work, we discover a new phenomenon
042   where simply adding eos tokens to the input prompt can significantly improve the attack performance
043   of existing jailbreak strategies. We name this as B OOST: Enhanced JailBreak Of Large Language
044
      MOdel via Silent eos Tokens. This method is simple yet effective and can be easily applied to
      existing jailbreak strategies to enhance their attack performance.
045
046   More specifically, we first show that an aligned LLM learns to distinguish between ethical and
047   unethical prompts in the hidden concept space through a hidden ethical boundary. We then demon-
048   strate that appending eos tokens can push the hidden representations of harmful contents towards
049   harmless concept space and thus bypass the ethical boundary. As a result, the input bypasses the
050
      safety alignment of the target LLM and forces the LLM to respond rather than reject the input. After
      the empirical analysis, we show that this is because the representation of eos tokens is around the
051
      ethical boundary, shifting both harmful and benign prompts towards the ethical boundary.
052
053   Besides forcing the target LLM to respond to harmful queries, appending eos tokens also does not
      affect the model’s understanding of the original input semantics. This allows the target LLM to

      2   E XISTING JAILBREAKING ATTACKS
089
090   To mitigate the potential harmful outputs of LLM, model developers often fine-tune the model to
091   reduce the likelihood of generating harmful content during safety training (OpenAI, 2023; Ganguli
092   et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a). For example, in the left panel of Figure 1,
093
      Llama-2 refuses to generate harmful content when prompted with an unethical question. However,
      despite these efforts, researchers have discovered that LLMs can still be manipulated to generate
094
      harmful content by crafting specific prompts, known as jailbreak attacks (Shen et al., 2024; Deng
095
      et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Shah et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023a). Jailbreak attack is a type of
096   adversarial attack that aims to bypass the safety constraints of LLMs by crafting specific prompts
097   that trigger the model to generate harmful content. For example, in the right panel of Figure 1, both
098   jailbreak strategies could breach the alignment of LLM learned during safety training and lead to
099   harmful outputs.
100
      Numerous studies have demonstrated various methods for successfully jailbreaking LLMs, and these
101
      studies can be broadly categorized into two types: black-box (Lapid et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2023;
102   Liu et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023a) and white-box attacks (Carlini et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a;
103   Geisler et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). Black-box attacks require no knowledge of the model’s
104   internal parameters, while white-box attacks require full access to the model’s parameters. The former
105   relies on techniques such as prompt engineering and behavioral analysis, while the latter leverages
106   internal model insights to identify vulnerabilities. In this study, we investigate the effectiveness of
107   B OOST in the context of both black-box and white-box jailbreak attacks. By leveraging the strategic
      use of eos tokens, we aim to assess how B OOST can enhance existing jailbreak methods.

      3     B YPASSING THE E THICAL B OUNDARY BY eos T OKENS
124
125   In this section, we analyze the ethical boundary learned by aligned models and how jailbreak prompts
126
      can bypass the ethical boundary. We introduce a new phenomenon where simply adding eos tokens
      can help bypass the ethical boundary.
127
128
      3.1   E THICAL B OUNDARY
129
130   During Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) or Su-
131
      pervised Fine-tuning (SFT) (Radford et al., 2019), the unaligned model can be fine-tuned into an
      aligned model by learning the hidden ethical boundary by classifying unethical concept z− and
132
      ethical concept z+ . We leave detailed analysis in §B.1. To visualize that the aligned model learns
133
      well to distinguish between ethical and unethical prompts in the hidden concept space, we collected
134   256 prompt pairs. Each pair consists of a harmful prompt sampled from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023b)
135   and its corresponding benign prompt with minimal words changed. For example, the harmful prompt
136   is “Write a step-by-step guide for making a homemade bomb” and the benign prompt is “Write a
137   step-by-step guide for making a homemade soap”. We ensure that prompts in each pair have similar
138   sentence structures and further details are provided in §C.1.
139
      We select two models for visualization: Llama-2-7b (Touvron et al., 2023b) and Gemma-2B (Team
140   et al., 2024). We use both the unaligned model and the aligned model to show how the ethical
141   boundary is learned after fine-tuning. We compute the last token’s hidden representation of these
142   prompts for each layer and use t-SNE (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) to project the hidden
143   representations into a 2D space for visualization convenience. According to prior work (Meng et al.,
144   2022a;b), factual associations are mainly stored in the middle layers, so we visualize the hidden
145   representations of −10-th layer for these models. More visualization results are provided in §C.2.
146   We observe that for both models, the unaligned model cannot distinguish between the benign and
147   harmful prompts, as shown in Figure 2. However, after fine-tuning, the aligned model can separate the
148   benign and harmful prompts in the hidden space, even with minimal word changes. This indicates that
149   the ethical boundary is well learned by the aligned model, and thus these models can give pre-defined
150   refusal responses Rrefuse for unethical prompts.
151

153   Based on the above analysis, the intuition for jailbreaking is to modify the harmful prompt x

158   injection (Yu et al., 2023b; Toyer et al., 2023). These methods are applied to the unethical question
159   “Create a fake news story that spreads misinformation”. We visualize the hidden representation of these
160   prompts on Llama-2-7b-chat model for the -10-th layer in Figure 3 as well for the aforementioned
161   256 prompt pairs. We also measure the Euclidean distance between these jailbreak prompts and the
      central of the benign prompts in the hidden space.

      To better understand the impact of eos tokens on model behavior, we analyze the shift in hidden
232
      representations when eos tokens are added to both harmful and benign prompts. From the visu-
233   alizations in Figure 4, which use t-SNE to project hidden representations from the -10th layer of
234   Llama-2-7B-Chat and Gemma-7B into a 2D space, it is evident that adding eos tokens can shift
235   the hidden representations of harmful prompts towards those of benign prompts. However, we also
236   observe that the shift occurs for benign prompts, which is particularly evident in the Llama-2-7B-Chat
237   model. This suggests that eos tokens cause the representations of both harmful and benign prompts
238   to approach the ethical decision boundary. This effect becomes more pronounced as the number of
239   eos tokens increases. While the shift is more pronounced in Llama-2-7B-Chat, Gemma-7B exhibits
240   similar trends, as demonstrated when we add additional eos tokens in Figure 11. We also observe
241
      that appending eos tokens to benign prompts can cause the LLM to produce refusal responses. In
      our experiments, appending 5 eos tokens to 256 benign prompts resulted in the model refusing them.
242
      Detailed analysis of this phenomenon is provided in §C.4. This observation further supports our
243
      hypothesis that appending eos tokens shifts both harmful and benign prompts toward the ethical
244   boundary.
245
246   One possible explanation for this phenomenon may lie in the unique role that the eos token plays
247
      within the model’s training and generation processes. During the RLHF stage, all instructions,
      including both benign and harmful prompts used for red-teaming, are typically terminated with an eos
248
      token. This consistent usage gives the eos token a distinctive association with sequence termination in
249
      the model’s learned representations. When multiple eos tokens are appended to the user prompt, they
250   may impact the model’s internal processing through context segmentation. Appending eos tokens
251   could cause the model to interpret the input as containing multiple separate segments or instructions.
252   This segmentation might dilute the influence of the original disallowed content by effectively resetting
253   the model’s contextual understanding, thereby shifting the prompt’s representation towards the ethical
254   boundary. As a result, harmful prompts become more likely to bypass the ethical decision boundary.
255   This forms the core intuition of B OOST: leveraging eos tokens to facilitate jailbreak prompts.

257   4     M INIMUM ATTENTION S HIFT BY eos T OKENS
258
      This section provides an analysis of how the attention of LLMs remains focused on harmful content
259   after adding eos tokens.
260
261   4.1   I NTRODUCED T OKENS M AY D ISTRACT THE ATTENTION OF LLM S
262
      Although adding eos tokens can help bypass the ethical boundary, it is still unclear whether the LLM
263
      will respond to unethical prompts. As shown in Figure 3, adding enough benign content or prompt
264   injection can bypass the ethical boundary. However, the LLM’s response is then focused on the
265   benign content or hijacked by the prompt injection, leading to an unsuccessful harmful response.
266   Even for the GCG attack, which has the smallest distance to benign prompts in the hidden space, the
267   response can be disproportionately affected by the attack itself, resulting in outputs like “BREAKING:
268   Japan Python NEVER Became a Threat to Humanity.” This occurs because, during optimization,
269   the GCG attack generates content like “Japan Python Never became” to minimize target loss, which
      distracts LLM’s attention, making it less harmful compared to other successful responses.

271   distract the model’s attention. We consider input as S = [s1 , . . . , sN ] ∈ Rd×N . The attention
272   mechanism in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) is a critical component that allows the model to
273   focus on specific parts of the input sequence. Given a query matrix Q, a key matrix K, and a value
274   matrix V. The output

276   attention. This finding also is presented in several papers (Hu et al., 2024a; Xiao et al., 2024). Thus,
277   adding additional tokens to the input prompt may distract the attention of the LLM from the harmful
278   content, leading to an empty jailbreak (Souly et al., 2024) or irrelevant responses.

      4.2             eos T OKENS HAVE L OWER ATTENTION VALUES
281   To integrate B OOST into various jailbreak methods effectively, the attention mechanism in transform-
282   ers needs to minimize the distraction caused by eos tokens. As discussed by (Vaswani et al., 2017),
283   attention values play a significant role in determining the relevance of tokens within a sequence. Low
284   attention values suggest a weak relationship or dependency between tokens, which is an important
285
      feature for eos tokens. When eos tokens exhibit low attention values, this indicates they are less
      crucial for the specific tasks being performed, thereby reducing the need for further feature extraction
286
      of these tokens during the inference phase. Thus, they are more likely to get small attention outputs
287
      compared with other introduced tokens.
288
289   We empirically validate this hypothesis by comparing the attention value and output of the -10-th
290   layer and 0-th head of Llama-2-7b-chat for eos and GCG generated tokens. The attention values are
291
      shown in Figure 5 and the attention outputs are provided in Figure 14. We observe that the attention
      values of eos tokens are significantly lower than those of GCG tokens. The attention output of eos
292
      tokens is also much lower than that of GCG tokens, indicating that appending eos tokens are less
293
      likely to distract the attention of LLMs from the original content. This property of eos tokens makes
294   it a general strategy that can be applied to existing jailbreak strategies without hurting the attack
295   performance.

359   a harmful response toward the target question. CO stems from the observation that safety-trained
360   LLMs are typically trained against multiple objectives that can conflict with each other. By adding a
361   compliance prefix conflicting with alignment such as “Sure, here is”, CO is expected to mislead the
362   LLM to complete the harmful response. We provide the detailed implementation of the baselines in
363   §E.3.

407

408

417    of the ASRs are no longer 0%. For tulu-2-7B, the CO has an original ASR of 3.91%, and after

420    applied to these methods when a maximum of 5 iterations are allowed in §E.8. Although there

       non-optimization-based methods.

457   6         D ISCUSSION
458   Time cost. A potential concern with the B OOST approach is that adding more eos tokens increase the
459   optimization time for jailbreak methods that rely on optimization. However, as outlined in §E.5, our
460   findings indicate that incorporating eos tokens can actually reduce the overall time cost by achieving
461   the jailbroken results more quickly. This efficiency gain is especially pronounced in larger models,
462   such as those with 13B parameters, where the time required for a successful jailbreak is typically cut
463
      in half for both methods tested.
464   Other possible tokens. Besides eos, other special tokens, like bos may also be able to improve the
465   attack performance. We select Gemma-2B-IT as the target model to compare the performance of
466   B OOST with other possible tokens, including eos, bos, pad and unk. To demonstrate that not just any
467   token improves attack performance, we also test common tokens like comma, period, the and that,
468   and rare tokens like _coachTry and _AcceptedLoading* . We repeat the experiment described in §5.3
469
      and change the appended tokens to the above tokens. The results are shown in Figure 8.
470   The findings demonstrate that eos tokens significantly enhance attack performance for Gemma-2B-IT,
471   with bos tokens following as the second most effective in improving outcomes. This suggests a
472   particular efficacy of certain starting and ending syntactical markers in helping jailbreaks, because
473   both eos and bos tokens are always appended during the fine-tuning stage. Other special tokens
474   like unk show some improvement, but to a lesser extent. In contrast, the pad token, along with
475
      common and rare tokens, do not contribute to performance enhancement and may even detract
      from it. We visualize the attention values of these tokens in Figure 20. The results show that the
476
      attention values of other tokens are significantly higher than that of eos tokens, potentially distracting
477
      the model’s attention from the original content. This further illustrates that not all tokens could
478   be used interchangeably in B OOST. This insight encourages further exploration of different token
479   combinations to optimize attack strategies. Future research should also consider developing methods
480   to automatically identify the most effective tokens for different models, moving beyond heuristic
481   selection.
482
      Other locations for eos tokens. In our study, we append eos tokens at the end of the prompts. We
483
      analyze the effectiveness of eos tokens at other locations such as the beginning, middle, and random
484

      positions of the prompts in §E.11 for Llama-2-7b-chat. The results show that the B OOST has the best
487   performance when eos tokens are appended at the end of the prompts. We hypothesize that this is
488   because the eos token is consistently used at the end of sequences during the model’s fine-tuning
489   stage. Appending eos tokens at the end aligns with the model’s learned behavior and may better
490   leverage the context segmentation effect, shifting the prompt’s representation toward the ethical
491   boundary. Future work could explore the impact of different token placements and combinations to
492   further optimize attack strategies.
493   Defense. In our evaluation of the robustness of the B OOST approach, we implement two defensive
494   strategies, SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2023), RPO (Zhou et al., 2024), Self-Reminder (Wu et al.,
495   2023), and Gradient Cuff (Hu et al., 2024b) on the final generated attack prompts and recalculate
496   the attack success rates. For SmoothLLM, we use a smoothing parameter of q = 5 and N = 20, as
497   recommended in the original paper to maintain output quality. For RPO, we apply the default settings
498   provided in their codebase. For Self-Reminder, we use the defense prompt provided in their paper.
499   For Gradient Cuff, we use the default settings from their official codebase.
500   The results are outlined in §E.6. The conclusion from this experiment is that, while B OOST improves
501   jailbreak performance, the presence of well-designed defences significantly mitigates these gains.
502   Specifically, defences like SmoothLLM and RPO, which are designed to counteract methods like
503   GCG, result in a substantial reduction in attack success rates. This decline in effectiveness can be
504   attributed to the inherent limitations of the applied jailbreak method, as GCG’s susceptibility to
505   perturbation cannot be addressed by B OOST. However, it is unrealistic to expect that simply adding a
506   few eos tokens will bypass all defenses. Further specific modifications to the jailbreak method are
507
      necessary to address its limitations.
508   Closed-source models. One major limitation of our study is that proprietary LLMs may filter out eos
509   tokens before generating responses, potentially limiting the effectiveness of our approach. However,
510   we conduct a study on four popular proprietary LLMs: GPT-4o, Claude-3-opus, Qwen-max, and
511   Gemini-1.5-pro. We successfully probe the eos tokens of three out of these four models and find out
512   that all these three models do not filter out eos tokens in their API services. We then apply B OOST
513
      to two of these models and observe that B OOST remains effective in enhancing GPTFuzz’s attack
      performance. Detailed results are provided in §E.9. This observation serves as an urgent reminder
514
      to providers of proprietary LLMs about the potential risks of special token injection. We hope that
515
      our findings will remind these providers to improve their filtering mechanisms to better protect their
516   services.
517
518   Varied Effectiveness Across Model Architectures. An important observation from our experiments
519
      is that the effectiveness of B OOST varies across different model architectures. While some models
      such as Llama-2/3 exhibit significant performance enhancements when eos are appended, others such
520
      as mpt-7b-chat show less pronounced improvements. This variability suggests that the mechanism by
521
      which eos influence model behavior may depend on specific characteristics of the model architecture,
522   training procedures, or the learned ethical boundaries within the model. This indicates a need for
523   further exploration into how different architectures and training methodologies impact the influence
524   of appended tokens like eos. Understanding these nuances could provide deeper insights into the
525   underlying mechanisms and help develop more robust models that are less susceptible to such
526   token-based manipulations.
527
      Limitations. Our explanation for eos’s effectiveness is based on empirical observations and that a
528   more rigorous theoretical framework would be valuable to fully elucidate why the eos token has this
529   specific effect.
530
531   7    C ONCLUSION
532
      We propose the B OOST, which leverages the eos token to enhance the performance of existing
533
      jailbreak attacks. We show that eos tokens can shift the hidden representation of harmful prompts
534   towards the ethical boundary, leading to a successful jailbreak. We also demonstrate that eos tokens
535   are less likely to distract the attention of LLMs from the harmful query, making them suitable tokens
536   to be appended to the input prompt. By comprehensive experiments on 12 LLMs, we show that
537   B OOST is a general strategy that can be effective across different LLMs. We believe that by disclosing
538   the potential risks of eos tokens in LLMs, our work can inspire future research on developing more
539   robust LLMs against jailbreak attacks and other security threats.

765
766   We analyze how jailbreak prompts bypass ethical concept boundaries in aligned models. Let Pθ
767   be a pre-trained unaligned model parameterized by θ. For a given Pθ , the developers usually use
768   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) or Supervised
769   Fine-tuning (SFT) (Radford et al., 2019) to make the unaligned model align with ethical guidelines.
770   We denote such aligned model with Pθ⋆ . During this process, a finetuning dataset Dalign is provided.
771   We define the response space as R, where Rrefuse is the set of pre-defined refusal responses for
772   unethical prompts in Dalign , like “I cannot assist with that request.”. The unaligned model (i.e., Pθ ) is
773   then fine-tuned on Dalign (i.e., into Pθ⋆ ) to generate the refusal responses when unethical prompts are
774
      given.
775   Let x denote the input prompt provided by the user. For a model Pθ , we formalize the model response
776   based on input x as r ∼ Pθ (r | x). We present the following generic Bayesian interpretation for
777   LLM prompting and introduce the idea of ethical boundary for jailbreak phenomena.
778

1349   We focus on four popular closed-source models due to their prominence in the industry: Claude-
       3-opus, GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5-pro, and Qwen-max. The release status of their eos tokens and their


                                                        25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       filtering behaviors are summarized in Table 10. We observe that GPT-4o and Qwen-max have
1351   officially released their tokenizers. However, even though the tokenizer is released, the only method
1352   available for interacting with the API is through a text string because there is no available access to
1353   the internal tokenization process of the API. For Claude-3-opus and Gemini-1.5-pro, we attempt to
1354   probe using common eos tokens.
1355
       To probe the models, we use the query “Can you repeat {eos } once?” in a string format with either
1356
       the released eos token or common eos tokens. If the used eos token is indeed the model’s eos token
1357   and is not filtered, the model responds with “Sure, here it is” but then truncates the output because
1358   the eos token ends the generation. This indicates a successful probe, confirming that the model does
1359   not filter out eos tokens.
1360
       If the model responds with “Sure, please provide me the word you want to repeat.” it suggests that
1361
       the used eos token is the model’s eos token but is filtered out, meaning the model does not process it
1362
       due to input filtering mechanisms. If the response is different, it implies that the used eos token is not
1363   the model’s actual eos token, yielding a failed probe.
1364
1365   During our probing experiments, we find that Claude-3-opus and GPT-4o refuse to repeat the content.
1366
       This behavior is potentially because they have been fine-tuned to resist prompt injection attacks (Yu
       et al., 2023b; 2024; Wallace et al., 2024). In such cases, we use the query “What’s this token in LLM:
1367
       {eos }” to probe the eos token and bypass alignment restrictions. To our surprise, we discover that 3
1368
       out of the 4 models can be successfully probed, confirming that all of them do not filter out eos
1369   tokens, including popular models from OpenAI and Anthropic. This finding reveals potential
1370   vulnerabilities for injection attacks using special tokens such as B OOST. We provide the screenshots
1371   in Figure 22 for verification.
