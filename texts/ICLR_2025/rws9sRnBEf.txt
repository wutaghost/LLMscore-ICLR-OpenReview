011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present an explainable and effective Neural Architecture Search (NAS) frame-
014            work for Reinforcement Learning (RL). We model a feed-forward neural network
015            as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) that consists of scalar-level operations and
016
               their interconnections. We train the model for RL tasks using a differentiable
               search method, followed by pruning the search outcomes. This process results
017
               in a compact neural architecture that achieves high performance and enhances
018
               explainability by emphasizing crucial information for solving the RL problem.
019            We apply our NAS framework to the Actor-Critic PPO algorithm, targeting both
020            actor and critic networks. We evaluate its performance across various RL tasks.
021            Extensive experiments demonstrate that our architectures achieve comparable per-
022            formance with significantly fewer parameters while also enhancing explainability
023            by highlighting key features.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine learning, a subset of artificial intelligence, has undergone a huge evolution driven by the
029   advent of deep learning. A large number of deep learning methods hinge on effective neural network
030   architectures, which are typically crafted by human experts through a time-consuming and often
031   intuition-based process. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) has made a significant paradigm shift by
032   automating the design of neural network architectures. Since the seminal work of Zoph & Le (2017),
033
      extensive research in NAS has systematically explored vast architecture spaces, discovering novel
      and high-performing network designs across various domains like Natural Language Processing
034
      (NLP), Computer Vision (CV) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) Klyuchnikov et al. (2020); Kang
035
      et al. (2023); Parker-Holder et al. (2022a). Among these, NAS for RL has been relatively less
036   explored due to its unique challenges, such as fragile RL algorithms, high computational costs, and
037   sensitivity to hyperparameters. There is still much potential to be explored in the context of NAS
038   for RL, pushing the boundaries of what machine learning can achieve White et al. (2023).
039
      Despite its considerable potential, NAS encounters various challenges and limitations. One of the
040
      major challenges arises from the need for expertise in designing the search space for NAS, which
041
      results in a trade-off between the investment in the search space design and the search complexity.
042   In addition, an overly customized search space aimed at optimizing architecture may hinder the
043   exploration of potentially better architectures and introduce bias into the search process Liu et al.
044   (2018). It has been reported that in such overly constrained search spaces, even random search
045   performs equally well Li & Talwalkar (2020).
046
      Another important challenge is the insufficient explainability of the searched architectures. It is hard
047
      to understand how they make their decisions and which information is crucial for the decisions. This
048   issue mirrors a broader challenge in deep learning, where the intricate network structure often results
049   in a black-box model with limited insight into their decision-making process. In deep learning, there
050   have been several studies to improve the model explainability Montavon et al. (2017); Lundberg &
051   Lee (2017), which, however, often necessitate several assumptions or are constrained to specific
052   models. Also in NAS, there have been continuing efforts to enhance the explainability of NAS-
053   generated models Kadra et al. (2023), yet there is still much room for improvement. Moreover,
      in practical RL problems, the agent has to solve sequential decision-making problems given the


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      input of the system state. This state information often includes a number of measurements from
055   various sensors that may be arbitrarily correlated or uncorrelated. In this case, model explainability
056   is crucial for identifying key features needed to solve the problem, which also contributes to reducing
057   the model size.
058
      In this work, we introduce a novel NAS approach that enhances search flexibility and offers high
059
      explanability through high-precision DAG modeling. We decompose a typical feed-forward neural
060
      network into scalar-level operations using single-output vertices. By relaxing discrete requirements
061   on the operations and interconnections, we employ a fully-differentiable architecture search. The
062   resulting architectures are then discretized and pruned to achieve a lightweight and high-performing
063   design. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach across various RL tasks.
064
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows.
065
066          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to introduce scalar-level DAGs for mod-
067            eling neural networks in architecture search. Our method reduces the effort required for
068            designing the search space, enhances search flexibility, and offers high explanability.
069
             • We have developed an effective framework of our DAG-NAS for searching and select-
070            ing neural architectures. (i) We extend constraint relaxation to both vertices and edges
071            in DAGs, making a fully-differentiable search process applicable. (ii) We introduce a
072            correlation-based pruning to achieve a compact architecture with high explainability.
073
             • We evaluate our framework across a broad spectrum of RL tasks and demonstrate that
074
               the architectures discovered by our framework have significantly fewer parameters while
075            achieving comparable performance.

078   2   R ELATED W ORKS
079
080   Neural Architecture Search (NAS) is a prominent branch in automating the machine learning
081
      pipeline, and revolves around three core components: search space, search strategy, and performance
      estimation strategy Elsken et al. (2019). The search space encompasses the set of architectures that
082
      can be explored, the search strategy defines how to explore the search space, and the performance es-
083
      timation strategy determines how to evaluate interim and candidate results. For example, the seminal
084   work of Zoph & Le (2017) searched a CNN architecture with high classification accuracy, adopting
085   a cell-based search space, RL-based search strategy which is rewarded by validation accuracy.
086
087
      An important and common challenge of NAS is the search space design. It should be sufficiently
      large to enclose high-performing architectures, and at the same time, carefully constrained for ef-
088
      ficient search with feasible computational complexity. This has led to the development of several
089
      structured search spaces with chain, cell, or hierarchy structure White et al. (2023), whose effective-
090   ness heavily depends on tasks. Notable works for the improved search space design include dynamic
091   search space Xia et al. (2022); Ci et al. (2021), unlimited search space Geada & McGough (2022), or
092   space evolution Zhou et al. (2021). However, all these approaches still require a substantial amount
093   of manual design for cell structure or feature size.
094
      Another significant challenge in NAS is the difficulty of explaining the searched architecture. Ex-
095
      planability is particularly crucial for NAS-for-RL, since it can be used to identify important features
096   and lighten the model in practice. There have been several efforts to improve the explainability,
097   such as using Bayesian optimization to identify effective motifs Ru et al. (2021) and employing an
098   evolutionary algorithm to examine input-output relationships Carmichael et al. (2023). Also, there
099   were attempts to model cells using DAG, aiming to enhance the interpretability of architectures Lee
100   et al. (2021); White et al. (2020). All these efforts, however, suffer from insufficient explainabil-
101   ity, performance degradation, or additional complexity in the cell design, leaving much room for
102   improvement.
103   Besides the difficulties of designing the search space and explaining the outcomes, NAS-for-RL has
104   unique challenges related to RL, including task design, learning algorithm selection, hyperparameter
105   configuration, and neural architecture design Parker-Holder et al. (2022b). In this work, we focus
106   on the neural architecture design, which has been less explored with only a few studies Franke
107   et al. (2021); Mysore et al. (2021). A noteworthy related work is Weight Agnostic Neural Network
      (WANN) Gaier & Ha (2019), which examined the potential of architectures in RL tasks without


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      training their weights. WANN uses an evolutionary search strategy to develop the architectures,
109   growing initial small architectures to large ones. Despite its flexibility in searching architectures,
110   WANN suffers from high computational complexity and often results in complex architectures with
111   a substantial number of parameters.
112
      To address the aforementioned challenges, we develop a novel NAS-for-RL solution that signifi-
113
      cantly reduces human involvement in the architecture design for RL tasks, and yields explainable,
114
      compact neural architectures with comparable performance.

117   3       P RELIMINARIES
118
119   RL is deeply rooted in the mathematical framework of Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), which
120   provide a structured way of modeling an environment. An MDP is formally defined as a tuple
121
      (S, A, T, R, γ), where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, T : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the
      transition probability function, R : S × A → R is the reward function, and γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount
122
      factor. Given state St = s at time t, an agent takes action At = a and the state transits to state
123
      St+1 = s′ at time t + 1 with probability T (s, a, s′ ) = Pr(St+1 = s′ |St = s, At = a), and the agent
124   obtains reward Rt+1 .
125
126   The agent learns to make decisions through trial-and-error interactions with the environment. The
127
      goal of the RL agentPis to find an optimal policy π : S → A that maximizes the expected reward sum
                             ∞
      E[Gt ] where Gt = k=t γ k−t Rk+1 . With the advance of deep learning, neural networks have been
128
      employed as an approximate decision-making function, replacing the agent. This technique, known
129
      as Deep RL (DRL), can be divided into two categories, value-based and policy-based methods. In
130   the value-based method, neural models estimate the value of states and actions, and in the policy-
131   based method, often referred to as the policy gradient method, they directly yield an action for a
132   given state.
133
      PPO is one of the most popular policy gradient methods in DRL. It utilizes two neural networks: one
134
      for the policy (actor) and the other for value estimation (critic). Their weights are adjusted based on
135
      the gradients of the following loss function:

      5       E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      We apply our DAG-NAS framework to the well-known RL environments in the Gymnasium with
327
      actor-critic PPO agents. The learning of the PPO agent and the architecture search of DAG-NAS
328   occur simultaneously. We consider a three-layer DAG supernetwork architecture for similar repre-
329   sentation power as typical MLPs. The results show that the search outcomes of DAG-NAS achieve
330   comparable performance in most RL tasks, witnessing its effectiveness. Further, the outcome ar-
331   chitectures clarify which input elements are of importance to solve the RL problems, demonstrating
332   better explainability.
333
      Our implementation of actor-critic style PPO follows CleanRL Huang et al. (2022). We slightly
334   modify it by separating the actor and critic networks, both of which are a simple 2-layer4 architecture
335   with nn.Linear in PyTorch library. They have a×b+b and a×1+1 learnable weights, respectively,
336   where a and b are the input and output dimensions, respectively. This will serve as the baseline
337   architecture throughout our experiments. Accordingly, we design the DAG supernetwork such that
338   the number of parameters of DDAG does not exceed that of the baseline. We train the weights of
339   the baseline for 10 million steps. Also, we train DAG supernetworks for the same number of steps.
340   Note that the training of DAG supernetworks involves both architecture and weight training under
341   the single-level optimization equation 5. On completion of the training, we fix the weights of the
342
      architecture outcome and test it across 100 episodes, yielding 100 final rewards. The final score will
      be their average. During the training, we do not set a seed to control the randomness. Instead, we
343
      repeat the training 5 times (trials) and select the one with the highest final score. Throughout the
344
      entire procedure involving multiple searches and evaluations, we consistently observed a stable final
345   architecture and evaluation results, demonstrating its robust behavior.
346
347   Our experiments were conducted across a total of 17 RL environments, which include Classic Con-
348
      trol (Acrobot, CartPole, MountainCar, MountainCarContinuous, Pendulum), Box2D (LunarLan-
      der, LunarLanderContinuous, BipedalWalker, BipedalWalkerHardcore), and MUJOCO (Pusher,
349
      Reacher, Hopper, Ant, HalfCheetah, InvertedDoublePendulum, InvertedPendulum, Walker2d).
350
351   In the following, we compare the performance of baseline, DAG supernetwork, and DDAG (i.e., the
352   outcome of DAG-NAS). We then discuss the explainability of the search outcomes and the impact
353   of architecture on sample efficiency. Details regarding environmental versions, hyperparameters, re-
354
      ward values, and diagrams of the searched architectures are available in the supplementary material.

357
358   We evaluate our method by comparing the performance of the baseline architecture, DAG supernet
359   obtained after the differentiable search, and DDAG obtained after discretization. For each pair of
360
      PPO actor-critic networks, we evaluate their performance over 100 episodes and collect the corre-
      sponding rewards.
361
362   Fig. 3 presents the evaluation results in terms of the achieved rewards and the number of parameters
363   of the searched architectures. Specifically, across 17 RL environments (x-axis), it illustrates the
364   interquartile range (IQR) of the rewards using min-max normalized boxplots (top figure), and the
365   number of the parameters using log-scaled bars (bottom figure).
366   In classic control and Box2D environments, all three achieve comparable performance, with the
367   DAG supernet substantially outperforming the others in certain environments, such as CartPole,
368   BipedalWalker, and BipedalWalkerHardcore. In MUJOCO environments with continuous robotic

372
      putational complexity due to its significantly larger number of parameters. Third, despite the large

373
      DDAG surpasses the DAG supernet in the Pendulum environment.
374
375   In summary, our DAG-NAS discovers DDAGs that achieve performance comparable to their base-
376   line counterparts in most environments, while being up to ten times smaller in size.

475   5.3   A RCHITECTURE I MPACT ON S AMPLE E FFICIENCY
476
477   In the previous sections, we set the weights w of DDAG to be the same as those of the DAG supernet,
478   allowing us to bypass the typical weight-retraining process of NAS and directly evaluate the neural
479   network model (α∗ , w∗ ). In this section, we further focus on architectural superiority. To this end,
480   we initialize the weights of DDAG at random and then retrain them, which is denoted by DDAG-
481   RW. By doing this, we aim to demonstrate that DDAG excels not only in achieving high rewards but
482
      also in rapidly acquiring knowledge, highlighting its significant contribution to sample efficiency.
483   We compare the training performance of the baseline, DDAG, and DDAG-RW over 1 million steps.
484   We conduct 5 trials for each experiment and report the mean and standard error of rewards with a
485   rolling window of 20. Fig. 5 shows their learning curves in 17 environments. Typically, DDAG starts
      strong and maintains its performance throughout training, but sometimes its performance declines

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      as training continues. In contrast, DDAG-RW initially exhibits lower performance yet eventually
487   matches or even surpasses DDAG. Compared to the baseline, DDAG-RW attains higher rewards in
488   fewer steps, likely due to effective feature selection and fewer parameters. However, there are a
489   few cases, such as Hopper, where baseline outperforms both DDAG and DDAG-RW. We observe
490   that their corresponding DAG supernet also underperforms the baseline, indicating an unsuccessful
491   architecture search.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495   We develop a fully differentiable NAS framework for RL through scalar-DAG modeling of neu-
496   ral networks. We simplify cell designs and structure their connections, and successfully relax the
497   discrete constraints, creating a DAG Supernetwork. We then develop a correlation-based pruning
498   method that produces a Discrete DAG (DDAG) architecture with significantly fewer parameters.
499   Additionally, we eliminate the conventional weight-retraining step in NAS, making the architecture
500   search process more practical.
501   Testing across various RL environments, we demonstrate the effectiveness and flexibility of DAG-
502   NAS. The derived DDAGs achieve high rewards despite their lightweight nature and are self-
503   explainable through the connections on important features. We also highlight the architectural su-
504   periority of DDAGs in terms of sample efficiency. Notably, retrained DDAGs exhibit accelerated
505   learning compared to the baselines.
