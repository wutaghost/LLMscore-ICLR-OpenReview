                                                                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
                                          Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely deployed in real-world applications,
010
                                          yet little is known about their training dynamics at the token level. Evaluation
011
                                          typically relies on aggregated training loss, measured at the batch level, which over-
012                                       looks subtle per-token biases arising from (i) varying token-level dynamics and (ii)
013                                       structural biases introduced by hyperparameters. While weight decay is commonly
014                                       used to stabilize training, we reveal that it silently introduces performance biases
015                                       detectable only at the token level. In fact, we empirically show across different
016                                       dataset sizes, model architectures and sizes ranging from 270M to 3B parameters
017                                       that as weight decay increases, low-frequency tokens are disproportionately depre-
018                                       ciated. This is particularly concerning, as these neglected low-frequency tokens
019                                       represent the vast majority of the token distribution in most languages, calling for
020
                                          novel regularization techniques that ensure fairness across all available tokens.

064   1     I NTRODUCTION
065
066   A major challenge in machine learning is designing algorithms that generalize well from training
067   data. One of the classic methods for promoting generalization (Krogh & Hertz, 1991; Shalev-
068   Shwartz & Ben-David, 2014) is the use of regularization techniques, such as L2 regularization, to
069   limit model complexity. Empirical evidence from classification problems with balanced classes
070   shows that increasing weight decay, while effectively fitting the data and minimizing training loss,
071
      generally improves performance on unseen data. However, a recent study by Balestriero et al. (2024)
      demonstrates that when training classifiers like ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) on computer vision
072
      tasks such as ImageNet classification (Russakovsky et al., 2015), higher weight decay leads to
073
      undesired behavior, causing the model to neglect certain classes. This class-dependent effect of
074   regularization is further amplified by imbalanced class distributions, as increasing weight decay does
075   not result in a uniform performance decline across all classes. Instead, the model underperforms on
076   low-probability classes while performing significantly better on more prevalent ones.
077
      This class-dependent behavior is not unique to vision tasks. In Natural Language Processing (NLP),
078
      a shift from traditional classification settings has led to a lack of attention toward token frequency
079
      and the per-token effects of regularization. In modern large language models (LLMs) trained on text
080   data (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018; OpenAI et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2023; Ortiz, 2023), the
081   task of predicting the next token from a large vocabulary also results in significant token frequency
082   imbalances. For instance, as shown in Figures 3, in the IMDB dataset (Maas et al., 2011), 95% of the
083   total tokens in the data are captured by the top 0.01% of tokens. This indicates that the vast majority
084   of tokens appear infrequently, while a small set of tokens dominates, creating a substantial imbalance.
085   Additionally, the proportion of low-frequency tokens tends to increase as the vocabulary expands.
086   This raises a critical question:
087       Can regularization techniques, such as weight decay, typically used to promote generalization,
088       ensure fairness across tokens when applied to LLMs trained on imbalanced token distributions?
089
090   Contributions. In order to study that question, we investigate the influence of weight decay on the
091   token-level prediction performance of large language models, uncovering critical insights that are
092   often overlooked when relying solely on aggregated performance metrics. Our study provides several
093   key contributions:
094
      • The next-token classification task suffers from severe class imbalance. Figures 3 demonstrate
095
        that the class distribution follows a heavy-tailed pattern, with the vast majority of classes being
096
        low-frequency and only a small portion being high-frequency.
097
      • We trained the Apple OpenELM models with 270M and 3B parameters, as well as Qwen2 models
098
        with 0.5B and 1.5B parameters, on both the IMDB dataset and its extended version IMDB-xl,
099
        using varying levels of weight decay that yielded acceptable training losses. As observed in
100     Figure 1, the models’ performance on low-frequency tokens significantly degrades as weight decay
101     increases.
102
      • We observe that higher-frequency tokens are consistently learned faster than low-frequency tokens
103     across multiple random seeds, with the gap in learning speed widening as weight decay increases,
104     suggesting that regularization may disproportionately disadvantage rare tokens.
105
106   These findings expose a critical dilemma. Practitioners often use aggressive weight decay to train
107   LLMs—intended to stabilize training—but unintentionally and silently degrade the model’s perfor-
      mance on low-frequency tokens, which make up the majority of the data. While conventional wisdom

      advocates for increased weight decay to promote generalization, in language modeling, this strategy
109   results in the unintended consequence of “neglecting” low-frequency tokens. This introduces harmful
110   biases, favoring more common tokens, often reflecting the language patterns of majority groups. Our
111   results emphasize the need for regularization techniques that explicitly address token imbalances.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
115
      Weight Decay and Generalization. L2 regularization was initially introduced to stabilize solutions
116
      to ill-posed problems (Tikhonov, 1943) and later adopted to enhance the generalization of neural
117
      networks (Krogh & Hertz, 1991). While many studies have linked low-norm solutions to improved
118   generalization (Neyshabur et al., 2015; Golowich et al., 2020; Bartlett & Mendelson, 2001; Bartlett
119   et al., 2017; Arora et al., 2018; Galanti et al., 2023c; Wei & Ma, 2019; Li et al., 2018), the pre-
120   cise relationship between L2 regularization and generalization remains a topic of debate. Several
121   works (Zhang et al., 2017; Jiang* et al., 2020) argue that norm-based measures alone are insufficient
122   to fully explain generalization in deep learning. For example, Zhang et al. (2017) found that although
123   weight decay can improve test accuracy, the overall effect is typically modest—around 1 − 2% on
124   ImageNet. Nonetheless, other studies have demonstrated that weight decay helps alleviate (Nakki-
125   ran et al., 2021; Pezeshki et al., 2022) the double descent phenomenon (Belkin et al., 2019; Nakkiran
126
      et al., 2020) and is critical for achieving Grokking in mathematical reasoning tasks (Power et al.,
      2022; Varma et al., 2023).
127
128   Weight Decay, Optimization, and Inductive Biases. Despite its modest impact on generalization,
129   weight decay is widely employed in many state-of-the-art language models, including GPT-3 (Brown
130   et al., 2020), Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2024), and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey
131   et al., 2024). These models are typically trained using a “one-pass” stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
132
      regime, where the optimizer directly minimizes the population error.
133   As shown in (Andriushchenko et al., 2023), the training and validation losses remain closely aligned
134   across different levels of weight decay. While weight decay’s effect on generalization is limited, it
135   plays a crucial role in improving optimization. For example, both the Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann
136   et al., 2024) (see Figure A7) and (Andriushchenko et al., 2023) (see Figure 4) demonstrate that weight
137   decay in AdamW leads to lower training loss compared to Adam, particularly toward the end of
138
      training. Other studies (van Laarhoven, 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Li & Arora, 2019; Li et al., 2020;
      Lewkowycz & Gur-Ari, 2020) have shown that weight decay enhances training stability by controlling
139
      the “effective learning rate” in scale-invariant neural networks. Additionally, other works (Galanti
140
      et al., 2023b; Rangamani & Banburski-Fahey, 2022; Pan & Cao, 2024; Beneventano et al., 2024)
141   reveal that weight decay contributes to various inductive biases, such as rank minimization and neural
142   collapse, which are beneficial for network compression (Denton et al., 2014; Alvarez & Salzmann,
143   2017; Tukan et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2017) and downstream performance (Galanti et al., 2022; 2023a).
144
      Training with Imbalanced Classes and Minority Collapse. Training with imbalanced classes
145
      presents a significant challenge in machine learning. Empirical studies consistently show that the
146
      weight vectors associated with the more frequent classes tend to have larger norms, which pushes
147   the decision boundary toward the minority classes. As a result, the feature space allocated to less
148   frequent classes shrinks, leading to a notable drop in performance (Kim & Kim, 2020; Kang et al.,
149   2019; Cao et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2020; Balestriero et al., 2022).
150   For instance, (Balestriero et al., 2022) showed that when training neural networks for classification
151   of visual data, higher levels of weight decay introduce a stronger bias for the model to prioritize
152   higher-probability classes over lower-probability ones.
153   To gain deeper insights into this issue, several works have investigated this phenomenon from a
154   theoretical perspective. Fang et al. (2021) proposed the unconstrained features model (UFM) as a
155   simplified framework for exploring the geometric properties of the global minima in cross-entropy
156   loss with regularization, particularly in overparameterized neural networks. In the case of a balanced
157   dataset, they demonstrated that neural collapse (NC) occurs at any global minimizer of the loss
158   function combined with regularization. However, in the case of class imbalance, neural networks
159   exhibit distinct geometric patterns, and some of the NC properties no longer hold (Dang et al., 2023;
160   Thrampoulidis et al., 2022; Hong & Ling, 2023; Dang et al., 2024). While last-layer features for
161
      samples in the same class still collapse to their respective class means (NC1), the class means and
      classifier weights no longer form a Simplex Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF), violating NC2 (Fang


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2021). In more extreme cases, when the imbalance becomes severe, the classifier weights
163   for minority classes can collapse onto each other, rendering them indistinguishable from other
164   classes (Fang et al., 2021). This phenomenon, referred to as “Minority Collapse,” explains the sharp
165   decline in accuracy for minority classes in imbalanced settings.
166
      Building on these finding, we explore whether similar behavior arises in next-token prediction tasks,
167
      where the problem can be viewed as a (very noisy) classification task, with the next token acting as
168
      the “class” for a given sequence.

186   Model Architecture. For our experiments, we train multiple models, including the Apple OpenELM
187   models with 270M and 3B parameters (Mehta et al., 2024), as well as the Qwen Qwen2 models
188   with 0.5B and 1.5B parameters (Yang et al., 2024). The small models (< 1B parameters) were
189
      configured with a context length of 128 whereas the large ones with a context length of 64. These
      architectures’s moderate size allows us to conduct multiple training runs with different λ values,
190
      enabling a comprehensive exploration of weight decay’s impact on token-level dynamics across
191
      various regularization choices.
192
193   Dataset and Tokenizer. We trained our models on the IMDB dataset (Maas et al., 2011), a widely
194   used benchmark due to its balanced sentiment-labeled data. The IMDB training split contains 25000
195
      samples. For the scope of this study, we discarded the labels, focusing solely on the raw text data
      to analyze the impact of hyperparameters on token-level generation performance. Additionally, we
196
      created an extended version of the dataset, termed IMDB-xl, by incorporating all the unsupervised
197
      samples from IMDB, which increased the training set to a total of 75000 samples. We used Byte Pair
198   Encoding (BPE) (Gage, 1994; Sennrich et al., 2016) as our tokenization method, training a tokenizer
199   on the IMDB dataset’s training set with a target vocabulary size of 32005 tokens. BPE ensures that
200   both frequent and infrequent tokens are well represented, providing a suitable basis for analyzing
201   token-level learning. This choice allows us to examine how different tokens, particularly rare ones,
202   are influenced by various weight decay values throughout the training process.
203
      Hyperparameters and Training. We conducted experiments across a range of weight decay values
204   λ ∈ (0.0, 2.0). To account for the stochastic nature of training, each configuration was run with
205   the same 5 different random seeds, with results averaged and presented with confidence intervals.
206   The models were trained using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) to decouple the
207   weight decay from gradient updates, with a learning rate of 5e−5 and a cosine decay schedule. A
208   warm-up period of 10 training steps was used for stabilization during early training. We trained each
209   LLM for a total of 10000 steps, evaluating token-level metrics every 100 steps to understand how
210   different tokens are learned and represented under varying weight decay values. For small models
211   (less than 1B parameters), we used a batch size of 64 per device on a single NVIDIA A100 32GB
212
      GPU, whereas for large models, we used a batch size of 16 with a gradient accumulation step of 4 on
      a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU. Additionally, mixed precision training (fp16) was employed to
213
      optimize GPU memory usage, reduce computational load, and accelerate convergence.
214

250   4.1   T EXTUAL DATA IS H IGHLY I MBALANCED
251
252   As a preliminary step in our study, we investigated how imbalanced textual data actually is. For this
253
      purpose we conducted several statistical evaluations of the IMDB dataset in order to verify that indeed
      the vast majority of tokens are of low-frequency and a small minority of the tokens appear very often
254
      in the data as predicted by the Zipf law.
255
256   Token Frequency Percentiles. Figure 3(a) highlights the extreme token frequency imbalance in
257   the IMDB dataset, tokenized using a BPE tokenizer with a vocabulary size of 32005. The vast
258   majority of the token frequency mass is concentrated in a very small portion of high-frequency
259
      tokens. Specifically, 95% of the total tokens in the data is captured by the top 0.01% of tokens,
      which demonstrates the steep distribution of token frequencies, where very few tokens dominate. To
260
      illustrate the calculation: suppose we have a dataset with 100 samples, each consisting of 10 tokens.
261
      If a set of 1% of the tokens in the vocabulary appear 800 times across those 1000 tokens, we say that
262   80% of the total tokens in the data is captured by the top 1% of tokens. Figure 3(b) further emphasizes
263   this imbalance, showing how the proportion of low-frequency tokens (those below the 95th percentile
264   in the data) grows as the vocabulary size increases. This suggests that token imbalance is inherent to
265   language and further amplified as the vocabulary size expands with larger tokenizers.

463   6   C ONCLUSION
464
465   We investigated the impact of weight decay on token-level learning dynamics in large language models.
466   Our findings reveal critical insights into how weight decay affects the learning process of individual
467   tokens—effects that are hidden when relying solely on aggregated metrics. We demonstrated that
468   increasing weight decay disproportionately harms the performance of low-frequency tokens, even
469   when the overall average loss remains largely unchanged. Additionally, we observed that higher-
470
      frequency tokens are generally learned faster than their low-frequency counterparts. This interplay
      between token frequency, performance, and regularization highlights the nuanced effects of training
471
      techniques on different parts of the model’s vocabulary.
472
473   These results expose a significant oversight in current LLM training practices. Weight decay is
474   commonly employed to reduce overfitting and enhance optimization. While this seems beneficial at
475   first—due to improved convergence and stability in overall loss metrics—our analysis uncovers a
476
      hidden pitfall: weight decay can severely compromise the model’s ability to handle low-frequency
      tokens. Crucially, this degradation goes unnoticed when only aggregated loss metrics are considered.
477
      This discrepancy between aggregated performance and token-specific learning underscores the
478
      need for fine-grained, token-level evaluations. Without such assessments, models risk sacrificing
479   performance on rare or specialized vocabulary, potentially limiting their effectiveness in domains that
480   require precise handling of low-frequency terms.
481
482
      As illustrated in Figure 3, the imbalance becomes more pronounced as vocabulary sizes increase.
      This is particularly concerning as vocabulary sizes are continuously expanded to improve model
483
      performance and broaden capabilities (Takase et al., 2024; Tao et al., 2024; Toraman et al., 2023).
484
      For example, while LLaMA-v1 (Touvron et al., 2023a) and LLaMA-v2 Touvron et al. (2023b) used
485   a vocabulary of 32000 tokens, LLaMA-v3 (Dubey et al., 2024) expanded this to 128256 tokens,

486
      Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024) further extended it to 151936 tokens, and Gemma-2 (Team et al., 2024)
487   increased the size to 256128 tokens.
488
489   Broader Impact. Our work enhances understanding of how weight decay affects token-level
490
      performance in LLMs while remaining undetected by aggregated metrics. This is crucial for ensuring
      fairness and reliability in real-world applications. While our societal impact is indirect, it underscores
491
      the need for more granular evaluation metrics to detect and mitigate potential biases on low-frequency
492
      tokens. These findings contribute to the development of more equitable and robust AI systems.
