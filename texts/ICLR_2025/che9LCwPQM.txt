009                                                  ABSTRACT
010
011            Curating a desirable dataset for training has been the core of building highly capable
012            large language models (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024).
013            Gradient influence scores (Pruthi et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2024) have been shown to
014            be correlated with model performance and are commonly used as the criterion for
015            data selection. However, existing methods are built upon either individual sample
016            rankings or inefficient matching process, leading to suboptimal performance or scaling
017            up issues. In this paper, we propose Gradient Trajectory Pursuit (GTP), an algorithm
018
               that performs pursuit of gradient trajectories via jointly selecting data points under an
               L0-norm regularized objective. The proposed algorithm highlights: (1) joint selection
019
               instead of independent top-k selection, which automatically de-duplicates samples; (2)
020
               higher efficiency with compressive sampling processes, which can be further sped up
021            using a distributed framework. In the experiments, we demonstrate the algorithm in both
022            in-domain and target-domain selection benchmarks and show that it outperforms top-k
023            selection and competitive algorithms consistently, for example, our algorithm chooses
024            as low as 0.5% data to achieve full performance on the targeted instruction tuning tasks.

027   1    INTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models encompasses an enormous amount of knowledge acquired through pretraining
030   corpus (Brown et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). A crucial component that makes
031   language models practically useful is the post-training phase, which empower the model with a variety
032   of extra abilities and skills, such as aligning language models to follow human instructions (Ouyang et al.,
033
      2022; Taori et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2023), teaching models the tool use (Schick et al.,
      2023) or exploring environments (Carta et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023).
034
035   Serving as the core of model training, choosing and utilizing desirable datasets are critical for both
036   quickly adapting models given a fixed budget (Xie et al., 2023) and maximizing the performance for
037   a targeted task (Wang et al., 2023a; Xia et al., 2024). An automatic data selection algorithm that chooses
038   a subset based on certain information is gaining its needs given the massive amount of corpora available
039
      online. Gradient information, which reflects the optimization process and has direct correlation with
      the final model performance, is one of the most common criterion for data selection (Mirzasoleiman
040
      et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021; 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024). However,
041
      how to effectively utilize gradient for actual selection process is still a challenging problem. Top-k
042   selection is fast and the most straightforward way, where data quality is ranked via the similarity scores
043   of individual gradient vectors and the main gradient trajectory (Xia et al., 2024). Nonetheless, due to its
044   independence assumption, its performance is often suboptimal compared to joint selection (Evans et al.,
045   2024). Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (Cai & Wang, 2011; Killamsetty et al., 2021) can perform joint
046   selection through interatively removing subspace projections, but is highly time-inefficient and requires
047   solving a non-negative least square for as many iterations as the target subset sample size.
048   In this paper, we introduce Gradient Trajectory Pursuit (GTP), an algorithm that selects data samples
049   through matching the trajectory on a gradient subspace. As discussed in previous works, a model’s
050   optimization process can be seen as the Langevin dynamic of an energy-based model (Welling & Teh,
051   2011), and matching the optimization process can lead to model weights with similar performance (Zhao
052   et al., 2021; Cazenavette et al., 2022). This sets the base rationale for our algorithm. Further drawing
053   inspiration from studies demonstrating that the optimization process happens in a subspace (Gur-Ari et al.,
      2018; Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Singhal et al., 2023), we project the gradients onto a small subspace and

082
083          • We propose an algorithm that jointly selects influential language data given a subset budget, via

099
      Coreset data selection. Building an effective training dataset has evolved to the cornerstone of foundation
      model construction. Due to the heavy cost of human annotation and filtering, a flux of works are developed
100
      to perform automated data selections. Features of data carry information that discriminates samples and
101
      are often used for selecting representative points (Sener & Savarese, 2017; Kaushal et al., 2019; Xia et al.,
102   2020; Wang et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023). N-gram features (Xie et al., 2023) are used for
103   re-weighting and selecting samples for pre-training, demonstrating strong correlations with target domains.
104   Deep learning features (Zhang et al., 2018; Hanawa et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2024) are common choices
105   for effective baselines measuring data similarities. Although frequently adopted, feature-based methods are
106   not guaranteed to have correlation with model optimization. Gradient information is another natural choice
107   for selecting influential data (Yu et al., 2020; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Mindermann et al., 2022; Han
      et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). Different from features, gradients often reflect more information about the

109   selection (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021) and suffer from the curse of dimensionality,
110   given the current model sizes (Brown et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023). Instead, our work allows for both
111   in-domain selection and target domain transfer, and focuses low-dimensional representations. Besides
112   the choices of representations, selection algorithms also play a crucial role. Various sampling methods

118   Data influence. Computing the data influence for model is another line of work for determining the

120   can approximate the influence of dropping training samples. Trajectory influence (Pruthi et al., 2020; Han
121   et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024) is different definition introduced to measure how each data sample affects
122   the whole model training dynamics. Top-k selection and variants are adopted for selecting data with higher
123
      trajectory influences (Han et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024).
124   Model training dynamics and SDEs. It has been shown that machine learning model trainings are also
125   a diffusion processes (Welling & Teh, 2011; Hoffman et al., 2017; Wenzel et al., 2020) defined through
126   stochastic differential equations. Commonly known in generative models, diffusion processes converge
127   to desired distributions when following the target score vector (gradient) field (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song
128   et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020). Several works (De Bortoli, 2022; Zhu et al., 2024; Benton et al., 2024) also
129
      explore the mixing rate and convergence bounds of diffusion models. Similarly, in optimization processes,
      when following the gradient vector field, trained models can converge to the same parameter distribution
130
      (Zhao et al., 2020; Cazenavette et al., 2022).

135   In this section, we introduce our algorithm Gradient Trajectory Pursuit (GTP), a framework designed
136   to select influential language data through joint data selection. We start from introducing the problem
137   formulation in section 3.1, discussing the base rationale behind trajectoy matching, then explain the main
138
      algorithm and the distribtued variants in section 3.2. Finally, we discuss the complexity and computation
      time of our algorithm in section 3.3.

      3.1   PROBLEM STATEMENT
142
      Problem definition. Given a large collection of training samples Dtr ={(xi,yi)}N     i=1 , the goal is to select
143
      a small subset DS ={(xsi ,ysi )}Mi=1  that maximizes    the generalization performance of models trained on it
144   when evaluated on a test set Dte. This test set may either be drawn from the same distribution as the training
145   data or from a different, task-specific distribution, leading to in-domain or targeted-domain data selection.
146
147
      Main idea. We approach the subset selection problem from considering model training dynamics. With
      a training set, the gradient descent process converges to the posterior distribution p(θ|D)∝p(D|θ) over
148
      the model parameter θ. Assuming the existence, if a selected subset DS has a gradient vector field w.r.t.
149
      p(θ|DS ) that closely matches the gradient vector field of the full dataset, a model trained using the subset
150   can converge to the same distribution over θ. Considering the intensive computation cost for computing
151   and matching over the whole gradient vector field1, we only focus on a part of parameter states obtained
152   from teacher training and perform matching in a subspace. This draws inspiration from findings that the
153   optimization process actually happens in a subspace (Gur-Ari et al., 2018; Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Singhal
154   et al., 2023) where a random guess can already perform decently well. We use parameter states from early
155   trajectories, assuming that the early training provides the most diversity and larger gradient subspace since
156   optimization is an information collapse process. To minimize the matching objective, we collectively solve
157   |DS | samples, and perform joint selection among all data points. This differs from previous works, where
158
      Killamsetty et al. (2021) focuses on online settings (ours is offline) and uses OMP to select one sample
      per iteration (leading to scaling issue in offline cases), while compared to Xia et al. (2024) which uses

243   The above is the equation 2 with simplified notation. To solve the objective function, we discuss two
244   algorithms: Iterative Compressive Sampling Pursuit and Distributed Compressive Sampling Pursuit. The
245   two algorithms are summarized in algorithm 2 and 3.
246   Iterative Compressive sampling pursuit. Equation 3 cannot be directly minimized through differentia-
247   tion. Instead, a greedy selection algorithm that solves this problem is developed in Needell & Tropp (2009)
248   through iterative greedy selection based on a residual vector. In the selection process, 2M top data points
249   are jointly selected all at once, a non-negative least square step is used to adjust the weights (i.e. implicit de-
250   duplication), then the re-ranked top M samples are used to update the residual vector. Compared to orthog-
251   onal matching pursuit (OMP) used in Killamsetty et al. (2021), the algorithm is much faster and scalable.
252   Distributed Compressive sampling pursuit. Given the potential larger number of time steps in
253   consideration, and the amount of data samples to be selected, compressive sampling matching pursuit
254   can still be not mostly optimal in terms of computation time. We further design an algorithm that can
255   distribute the pursuit process across multiple machines. We use a star model, where one center machine
256   communicates with multiple machines and adopt column partition to build separate As that are distributed
257   and parallelized. Details are shown in algorithm 3.

259   3.3   COMPUTATION TIME ANALYSIS
260   As pointed out in Xia et al. (2024), the computation time cost for all steps should be considered. Our
261   algorithm consists of warmup model training, gradient subtraction and storing, subspace computation,
262   and data selection, which follows the design in Xia et al. (2024). The main difference come from our
263   subspace computation and selection algorithm, where we adopt PCA instead of random projection and
264   use GTP instead of top-k for data selection.

288   4.1     LLM AGENT: ALFWORLD
289
290   Benchmark setup. ALFWorld is a suite of text environments built upon interactive Textworld (Côté et al.,
291
      2019) that benchmarks agents on solving sequential tasks. Typical tasks include giving agents the current
      partial observed environment information and require the agents to achieve a goal (e.g., finding objects).
292
293   We focus on offline imitation learning settings. For train and test datasets, we use offline trajectories
294   from the official ALFWorld repository (Shridhar et al., 2021) which consists of 3,553 tasks, each with an
295   average of 19 steps. The training set contains 48k state-action pairs, and test set totals at 1,861 state-action
296   pairs. In practice, we find that using 10k training data already achieves similar generalization performance
297
      compared to full training set, i.e., dataset reduction beyond 10k is not very meaningful since the selection
      can be easily done through random subset picking, and thus choose to use a subset with 10k data points as
298
      our main subject of study. In model evaluation, for convenience, we directly compare the model’s per-step
299
      action prediction accuracy. Per-step accuracy is known to be reflective on model’s ability and has a strong
300   correlation with final success rates.
301
302   Model setup. For evaluating whether a dataset is effective for training, we use supervised fine-tuning on
303
      OPT-125M (Zhang et al., 2022) with LoRA (Hu et al.) as the basic training setup. Given a selected subset
      dataset for training, all models are optimized using Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate
304
      5e-4 and batch size 80 for 20 epochs to ensure its convergence. We follow the most recent prior method
305
      (Xia et al., 2024) to generate a warm-up training trajectory for gradient extraction and data selection. The
306   warm-up trajectory is trained using learning rate 1e-4 and batch size 10 for 5 epochs. A model checkpoint
307   is saved every 500 interations, resulting in model states on across timesteps.
308
309
      Baselines. The most relevant prior method is LESS Xia et al. (2024), where we build upon most of its
      pipeline, including warm-up training and gradient computation. LESS uses top-k selection with scores
310
      maxing across pre-defined subtasks, which does not exist in ALFWorld. We instead directly compare
311
      individual gradient vectors with gradient trajectories. G-DIG (Pan et al., 2024) is a baseline that uses
312   clustering directly on gradients and samples within each cluster. Following (Xia et al., 2024), we also
313   compare with RDS (Representation-based Data Selection) Zhang et al. (2018); Hanawa et al. (2020),
314   which uses the mean of last layer hidden states for selection.
315
      Main results. We summarize the main results in table 1. As demonstrated in previous works (Xie et al.,
316
      2023; Xia et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024), random sampling is a strong baseline to beat. Gradient-based
317
      algorithms tend to pick highly redundant examples when matching gradients. LLM Agent benchmarks
318   contain traces with incremental steps in the training data, and need algorithms to be sensitive to sample
319   duplication. Why does top-k fail? We visualize the selected top three examples based on the scores
320   computed from two algorithm in figure 2, showing that the initial top k selections are highly duplicated,
321   while after our pursuit algorithm the selected samples exhibit more diversity. Representation-based
322   selection (RDS) shows higher performance since it uses the features from pretrained model. Across
323   different data ratios, our algorithm, both standard and distributed versions, consistently outperform the
      baselines. For the distributed version, we use separate the 10 timesteps into 5 machines.

      4.2       TARGETED INSTRUCTION TUNING
379
380   Task setup. We follow the setup in Xia et al. (2024) and evaluate our algorithm on the targeted instruction
381   tuning task. Compared to standard instruction tuning where mixed datasets are used to finetune large
382   language model to follow human instructions, targeted instruction tuning individually selects data samples
383   for different target benchmarks for finetuning language models. The selection algorithm hence plays an
384   important role for improving the final trained model performance. For example, multiple works find that
385   when various data sources are mixed together, it can negatively impact the model performance for different
386
      target tasks Wang et al. (2023a); Xia et al. (2024), while Xia et al. (2024) achieves equivalent or better
      results with only 5% of the full dataset.
387
388   Datasets and models. We follow the settings from Xia et al. (2024) and use a diverse and mixed collection
389   of public instruction tuning datasets as the main training set to select from. The datasets include: Flan
390   V2 (Longpre et al., 2023), CoT (Wei et al., 2022), Dolly (Conover et al., 2023), and Open Assistant (Köpf
391   et al., 2024). The four datasets are commonly used in various models and benchmarks. For the language
392
      model being tested upon, we use Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023)2.
393   Implementation details. To build the initial parameter states for extracting gradients, we warmup train
394   Mistral-7B (also with LoRA) for 4 epochs using 5% of the full dataset. Data gradient extractions are
395   performed in parallel across machines. We use the selected data from each benchmark as the target dataset
396   and compute subspace and target gradient b. These are exactly following the standard setups in Xia et al.
397   (2024). The GTP iterations are performed 5 times to select the final subset.

416   5       CONCLUSIONS
417
418   In this paper, we propose an algorithm (GTP) that can both perform joint data selection and has the
419   scalability towards larger target sample sizes. The algorithm automatically de-duplicates samples and
420   can potentially serve as a standard selection technique other than top-k. Both in-domain selection and
421   target-domain selection are applicable for GTP. For potential limitations, gradient-based methods still rely
422   on a few steps of model training, which can be more expensive than pure representation-based methods or
423
      influence function methods. It will be worth attempting to connect matching pursuit with representation-
      based or influence function methods and develop an algorithm that does not require additional model
424
      training for data selection.
