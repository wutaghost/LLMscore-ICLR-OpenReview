009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The performance of embodied agents has been shown to improve by increasing
012            model parameters, dataset size, and compute. This has been demonstrated in do-
013            mains from robotics to video games, when generative learning objectives on of-
014            fline datasets (pre-training) are used to model an agent’s behavior (imitation learn-
015            ing) or their environment (world modeling). This paper characterizes the role of
016            scale in these tasks more precisely. Going beyond the simple intuition that ‘bigger
017            is better’, we show that the same types of power laws found in language modeling
018            (e.g. between loss and optimal model size), also arise in world modeling and imi-
019            tation learning. However, the coefficients of these laws are heavily influenced by
020
               the tokenizer, task & architecture – this has important implications on the optimal
               sizing of models and data.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Much progress in AI in the early 2020’s has been driven by increasing model size, dataset size, and
027   training compute. Whilst conceptually simple, the importance of this practice has led to an emerging
028   subfield studying the science of scaling. This field answers questions such as how to estimate the
029
      benefit of increased compute investment, or how to optimally trade-off model and dataset size.
030   The role of scale in pre-training is until now best understood in the context of large language models
031   (LLMs). Following the observation that the empirical relationship between loss and key scaling
032   quantities can be accurately described by power laws (Kaplan et al., 2020), ensuing work studied the
033   precise trade-off between model and dataset size (Hoffmann et al., 2022), as well as considerations
034   about inference compute (Sardana and Frankle, 2023), repeated training data (Muennighoff et al.,
035
      2024), parameter counting (Pearce and Song, 2024), and more (Section 2).
036   In comparison, less is understood about scaling in embodied AI. Recent high-impact works suggest
037   increasing model and dataset size can lead to ever more capable agents for two pre-training objec-
038   tives; behavior cloning (BC) (Reed et al., 2022; Baker et al., 2022; Brohan et al., 2023) and world
039   modeling (Hafner et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Bruce et al., 2024). Such works
040   typically demonstrate the benefit of scale through ablations over only a few model sizes, shown in
041
      terms of downstream agent performance, confirming the intuition that ‘bigger is better’ (Sartor and
      Thompson (2024) provide an aggregated analysis). However, this leaves a large gap to the pre-
042
      cise understanding of scale in LLMs, where for a given increase in compute, models can be sized
043
      optimally, and their expected performance accurately predicted.
044
045   This paper helps close this gap. Similar to the initial study of scale in LLMs, we focus on the effect of
046   scaling on a generative pre-training loss (rather than on downstream agent performance, or reward-
047
      or representation-centric objectives), in the infinite data regime, on a fixed offline dataset. Under this
      setting, we train families of transformers on next-token prediction tasks using architectures popular
048
      in both world modeling and BC tasks. This leads to several contributions, summarized in Figure 1.

052
053    • The optimal trade-off between model and dataset size in world modeling is influenced by the
         tokenizer’s compression rate (number of tokens per observation) (Section 4.1, Figure 1a & b).

      2    R ELATED WORK
109
110
111
      Scaling laws origin. The term scaling laws is used throughout the engineering and physical sciences
112
      to denote power law relationships between two quantities, e.g. duration of a volcanic eruption and
113   the probability of it continuing (Cannavò and Nunnari, 2016). The name derives from the scale-
114   invariant1 property of power laws. While early work suggested that power laws could be good
115   empirical descriptors of important variables in deep learning (Hestness et al., 2017; Rosenfeld et al.,
116   2019), it was Kaplan et al. (2020) who provided a comprehensive study of power laws in transformer
117   LLMs, and popularized the usage of scaling laws in this context.
118
      Scaling laws in LLMs. As the real-world value of LLMs was understood, scaling in LLMs became
119   a high-priority research topic. Hoffmann et al. (2022) conducted a precise analysis into the trade-off
120   of model and dataset size, finding they should be increased in equal proportions. This conflicted
121   with Kaplan et al.’s suggestion that model size should be prioritized – an incorrect conclusion that
122   Pearce and Song (2024) showed largely arose from counting only non-embedding parameters.
123
      Many other aspects of LLM scaling analyses are beginning to be refined. Su et al. (2024) revis-
124
      ited the methodology used to find scaling coefficients. Hägele et al. (2024) found that multiple
125   independent cosine schedules could be reproduced more efficiently through a constant learning rate
126   with multiple short decays, or stochastic weight averaging. Pearce and Song (2024) & Porian et al.
127   (2024) found that well-tuned constant learning rates were sufficient to recover certain coefficients.
128   Bi et al. (2024) study the effect of various hyperparameters on scaling. Muennighoff et al. (2024)
129   looked at repeated epochs, finding up to four epochs produce negligible departures from the infinite
130   data regime. Sardana and Frankle (2023) factored in inference compute to the definition of what is
131   compute-optimal. Isik et al. (2024) study the link between pre-training loss and downstream per-
132   formance. A further line of research aims to explain why power laws are such a good descriptor of
133
      empirical deep learning (Hutter, 2021; Maloney et al., 2022; Bahri et al., 2024).
134   Scaling in embodied AI. Compared to LLMs, our understanding of scale in embodied settings is
135   less advanced. Early successes in competitive games showed that running reinforcement learning
136   (RL) at scale could surpass human performance, e.g. (Silver et al., 2017; Berner et al., 2019). In
137   self-play RL, power laws were observed between certain quantities by Neumann and Gros (2022).
138   Meanwhile, Hilton et al. (2023) noted that, in general, reward signals do not follow power laws, and
139
      defined a transformation of reward (intrinsic performance) that created self-consistent scaling laws.
140   Inspired by the effectiveness of scaling in LLMs, embodied AI research has recently begun to ex-
141   plore the effectiveness of generative pre-training objectives on offline datasets, when executed at
142   scale. This includes behavior cloning objectives in video games (Baker et al., 2022; Raad et al.,
143   2024), robotics (Brohan et al., 2022; 2023; Padalkar et al., 2023; Bousmalis et al., 2023), or multiple
144   domains (Reed et al., 2022), as well as world modeling objectives (Hu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023;
145
      Bruce et al., 2024). In these studies, the benefit of scale is generally shown through increasing model
      size on a specific downstream task of interest (e.g. measured by completion rate) – an aggregated
146
      survey is provided by Sartor and Thompson (2024).
147
148   Whilst such studies provide strong evidence that scale can be effective in embodied domains, the
149   complexity introduced by downstream task evaluation makes quantifying the effects, and asking
150   more nuanced questions very challenging (e.g. how to trade-off model and dataset size). To ad-
151
      dress this, we argue that a better starting point for studying scale in these generative pre-training
      approaches, is to study the effect of scale on pre-training loss. Whilst downstream task performance
152
      may be of ultimate interest, LLM research evidences the utility of focusing on this clean intermedi-
153
      ate signal, which is more straightforward to analyze. Few prior works have taken such an approach.
154   In Appendix A we contrast our BC efforts with Tuyls et al. (2023) who provide a valuable initial
155   study on the relationship between compute and pre-training loss for BC. We also discuss scaling
156   analyses of image and video modeling, which are related to world modeling.

176   3       M ETHODOLOGY
177
178   This section provides details for our main experiments. We describe the pre-training tasks, architec-
179   tures, and datasets considered. We also detail the methodology used in the scaling law analyses.
180
181   3.1       TASKS
182
183   We consider trajectories constructed as sequences of alternating observations ot and actions at for
184   timestep t ∈ N. In this work, observations are always images, ot ∈ R3×w×h and any continuous
185   actions are discretized during preprocessing leaving, at ∈ {0, 1}da .
186   Given this data format, we consider two tasks. World modeling (WM) (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018)
187   predicts future observations from previous observations and actions. This allows an agent to ex-
188   plicitly understand how its environment works, which can be used for planning, or dyna-style RL
189   (Sutton, 2018). Behavior cloning (BC) predicts the future actions that the dataset’s demonstrators
190   take (Bakker et al., 1996). This creates a policy that can be directly used to act in the environ-
191   ment, either as-is or following further fine-tuning. Concretely, these two tasks require modeling the
192   following quantities,
193                                   World modeling: P (ot+1 |ot . . . ot−k , at . . . at−k ),                                         (1)
194
                                     Behavior cloning: P (at |ot . . . ot−k , at−1 . . . at−k−1 ).                                      (2)
195
196   This work focuses on generative pre-training aiming to model this full conditional probability dis-
197   tribution. We leave a study of scaling laws for alternative objectives, e.g., explicitly targeting repre-
198   sentation learning (Nair et al., 2022) or reward-centric models (Hafner et al., 2020), to future work.
199
200   3.2       A RCHITECTURES
201
      All experiments revolve around GPT-2 style causal transformers (Radford et al., 2019) as the core
202
      of the model. However we consider two different methods for inputting image observations, sum-
203
      marized in Figure 2. Section 3.4 details how we measure the model size of each.
204
205   Tokenized architecture. Our first architecture tokenizes each image observation into multiple
206   discrete tokens. This is done with a frozen VQGAN encoder Encθ (ot ) → zt , where zt ∈
207
      {1, 2, ..., Vo }dz , for vocabulary size Vo and latent dimension dz . Discretized actions are mapped
      to a non-overlapping vocabulary. Following tokenization, training sequences take the form,
208
209                     [zt1 , zt2 , ..., ztdz , a1t , a2t , ..., adt a , zt+1
                                                                           1      2
                                                                               , zt+1       dz
                                                                                      , ...zt+1 , a1t+1 , a2t+1 , ..., adt+1
                                                                                                                          a
                                                                                                                             , ...],    (3)
210
211   where each item of the sequence is an integer within our vocabulary. A transformer is then trained to
212
      maximize the likelihood of either the latent image tokens (world modeling), or action tokens (BC).
213   This tokenized architecture is widely used both in world modeling (Micheli et al., 2022) and BC
214   tasks (Bousmalis et al., 2023). Gato (Reed et al., 2022) used a similar design but with continuous
215   patches rather than discrete tokens. Our implementation tests both a ‘small’ (28M parameters, dz =
      256) and ‘large’ (150M parameters, dz = 540) VQGAN – further details in Appendix B.

      3.3   DATASETS
226
227   This paper focuses on the effect of scaling on the pre-training loss over an offline dataset. To study
228   this cleanly, datasets must meet two criteria.
229
230         1. Dataset size. Repeated training on the same data alters the effect of scaling. Therefore,
231            datasets should be large enough that all model sizes use a low number of training epochs.
232         2. Dataset diversity. Both the behavior and environment must contain enough richness and
233            variety that pre-training loss does not saturate across the model sizes tested.
234
235   Many existing benchmark datasets fail to fulfill these criteria – if not due to limited size, then because
236
      behavior is generated from a pre-trained fixed policy, or the environment is too simple.
237   Our work instead focuses on a dataset of human behavior collected in a video game named Bleeding
238   Edge. This is a fast-paced 4 vs 4 multiplayer game, with a range of characters, abilities and maps.
239   Game play is highly complex due to the cooperative and competitive dynamics. Success requires
240   selecting high-level strategies (e.g. choosing which map regions to fight for), as well as fine-grained
241   reactive control during combat. Figure 9 shows example sequences from our dataset.
242   Supported by the game’s developer Ninja Theory, we compiled a dataset of 8.6 years of anonymized
243   game play, containing both image observations and controller actions. We refer to this as the 7 map
244   dataset. We also use a subset of this for some experiments, of around 1.1 years from a single map,
245   which we name the Sky Garden dataset. Appendix B.3 provides further technical details.

247   3.4   S CALING ANALYSIS METHODOLOGY
248
249   We are interested in studying the relationship between several quantities defined below.
250
             • Model size N , the total number of trainable parameters (ignoring VQGAN parameters
251            for WM-Token & BC-Token, but including the fixed-size CNN for BC-CNN). Embedding
252            parameters are included in the count following Pearce and Song (2024).
253
             • Dataset size D, the total number of inputs the transformer sees during training. For WM-
254
               Token and BC-Token this is dz + da per observation & action pair, and for BC-CNN this
255            is one per observation & action pair.
256
             • Compute C, the number of floating point operations (FLOPs) used during training. The
257
               common approximation of C = 6N D (Kaplan et al., 2020) is used.
258
259          • Loss L, the standard classification cross-entropy loss (all targets are discretized). We as-
260
               sume training loss is an accurate proxy for test loss (Appendix B.3.1 analyzes further).
261
      More specifically, we are interested in ‘compute-optimal’ versions of each quantity. For loss, this is
262   defined as the minimal loss possible for a given FLOPs budget,
263
264                                      Loptimal (C) =      min        L(N, D),                              (4)
                                                          s.t. C=6N D
265
      where L(N, D) is the empirical loss achieved with an N parameter model trained on D tokens. We
266
      further define optimal model and dataset sizes as the configuration that produce this minimal loss
267
      given a FLOPs budget,

284

289   as those falling on the efficient frontier (see Figure 1). Coefficients can then be estimated straight-
290   forwardly through a line of best fit on a plot of FLOPs vs parameters or data for these efficient
291
      models.
292   Frontier fit is our preferred method when available – it avoids making any assumptions about the
293   training curves, directly fitting the best models observed. However, it requires training models past

      a constant learning rate per model, so each requires only one training run. We aim to train models

436   5       F URTHER ANALYSIS
437
438   Section 4 made several observations about the effect of scale in the pre-training of embodied agents.
439
      This section aims to understand these results further, and provide intuition for why they occur.
      Specifically we target three questions.
440
441             • Q1: Why does BC-Token produce training curves that do not plateau, while WM-Token
442               does, given an identical architecture and dataset? (Section 5.1)
443
                • Q2: Why does moving from BC-Token to BC-CNN resolve this issue? (Section 5.2)
444
445             • Q3: Why does increasing the amount of tokens per image observation (256 to 540) lead to
446
                  an increase in the optimal model size coefficient (0.49 to 0.62)? (Section 5.3)
447
448   5.1      Q1: BC-T OKEN VS . WM-T OKEN
449
      The lack of saturation of BC-Token models compared to WM-Token models can be attributed to two
450
      factors. The first is a sparser loss. A single observation-action pair is discretized into dz + da total
451   tokens. With the large VQGAN tokenizer, world modeling receives supervision for dz /(dz + da ) =
452   540/556 ≈ 97% tokens, while BC is supervised for da /(dz + da ) = 16/556 ≈ 3% of tokens.
453
454
      The second factor is the granularity of the targets. The large tokenizer creates a world modeling
      vocabulary size of 4096. Each vocabulary item roughly corresponds to a specific color and texture
455
      for an image patch. Many vocabulary items may only be used to model specific map regions or
456
      special abilities. Hence, the world modeling loss is very granular. On the other hand, a player can
457   take the same action in multiple different situations – continue straight could be used to escape an
458   enemy, chase an enemy, or navigate to a checkpoint. Hence, the supervision for BC is more vague
459   and abstracted. We can think of this as a super-classed label.
460
      To demonstrate the effect of these two factors on optimal model size coefficients, we run a set of
461
      tiny-scale experiments in language modeling. Transformers are trained on next-character prediction,
462
      on a dataset of Shakespeare text3 using a single character for each token. Model sizes are varied from
463   4k parameters to 17M parameters. Context length is fixed at 16 characters/tokens.
464
465   Figure 8 (left) shows training curves over all 16 tokens, followed by a sparse loss where supervision
466
      is only provided from the final token (middle), and then additionally under a super-classed setting
      (right). This super-classes the final target – rather than using all 128 ASCII characters, they are
467
      randomly shuffled into one of two macro classes.
468
469   These modifications are intended to mirror the effect of moving from WM-Token to BC-Token. We
470   compute optimal model size coefficients using the parametric fit as most models are not trained long
471   enough for the frontier fit method. Indeed, we see that the coefficient drops from 0.63 to 0.15 with
472
      both the sparse and super-classed loss. This matches the magnitude of decrease seen in Table 1 from
      0.66 to 0.32, indicating that the proposed mechanisms explain our findings.
473
474
475
      5.2      Q2: BC-T OKEN VS . BC-CNN
476   Despite the same non-granular loss signal, why does switching architecture from BC-Token to BC-
477   CNN makes the loss of similar model sizes plateau under a much smaller compute budget?
478
479
      Consider each architecture using a transformer with 1M parameters. Observe from Figure 2 that
      BC-Token receives dz + da = 556 inputs for every action ât it predicts, while BC-CNN receives
480
      just one input for every action predicted. Hence, BC-Token uses around 556 times more compute in
481
      its action prediction (556 × 2 × 1M ≈ 1 × 109 FLOPs) than BC-CNN (1 × 2 × 1M ≈ 2 × 106
482   FLOPs). This means that even with the same number of parameters, BC-Token can learn a far
483   more expressive function than BC-CNN. Hence, BC-Token requires far more tokens to match this
484   expressivity, and training curves for a given model size plateau much later.

      5.3          Q3: WM-T OKEN -256 VS . WM-T OKEN -540
503
      Finally, we seek to understand why the optimal model size coefficient increases when moving from
504   the 256 to the 540 token VQGAN. As the number of tokens per image observation are increased, the
505   compression rate of the tokenized representation decreases. We would expect that each individual
506   token becomes easier to predict in this less compressed representation. This would mean a less
507   expressive function is needed (smaller model size), but also a smaller number of examples would
508   need to be seen (smaller dataset size). It is less clear what ratios these ingredients decrease in, and
509   hence what effect a lower compression rate has on the optimal model size coefficient.
510   For further insight into how compression affects the optimal model size coefficient, we ran a small
511   scale experiment in language modeling using two text representations; 1) ASCII character-level
512   tokenization. (low compression) 2) GPT-2 tokenizer (high compression). We used the BookCorpus
513   dataset (Zhu et al., 2015), and trained models past their compute-optimal point, so the Frontier fit
514   method could be used for coefficient estimation.
515
      Appendix C shows results. Under the character-level tokenizer (low compression), we find
516   Noptimal ∝ C 0.66 . For the GPT-2 tokenizer (high compression), we find Noptimal ∝ C 0.44 . The
517   key takeaway is that the more compressed representation has led to a lower optimal model size
518   coefficient, which reflects what we see in the world modeling experiments.

      6         D ISCUSSION & CONCLUSION
522
523   This paper establishes a deeper understanding of scaling laws for world modeling and behavior
524
      cloning, two tasks that underpin embodied AI applications in domains such as video games and
      robotics. Focusing on generative pre-training of such models, we show that it is possible to recover
525
      scaling laws similar to those established in the LLM literature. Establishing such a link is key to
526
      making efficient use of available resources, and to training compute-optimal models.
527
528   Considering the task of world modeling, we find that models can be smoothly scaled following
529   best practices and insights from the LLM literature. Surprisingly, the scaling coefficients for our
530
      WM-Token-256 architecture very closely match those established for LLMs. Comparing to our
      WM-Token-540 model and additional analysis, we further establish that scaling is affected by the
531
      tokenizer’s compression rate.
532
533   Turning to pre-training BC policies for agents, the choice of architecture is extremely important in
534   determining optimal scaling behavior. When using architectures with tokenized image observations,
535   dataset size should be increased much more rapidly than model size. Meanwhile, for BC-CNN
536
      architectures, model size should be increased faster than dataset size.
537   Limitations. While we show that scaling laws can be precisely described in the infinite data regime
538   and for appropriate architectures, future work is needed to establish scaling laws for alternative mod-
539   els and under varying dataset quality. In addition, we focus on loss as an intermediate quantity that
      can be effectively optimized in pre-training. Many additional considerations are required for effec-

                                                                                          10

540
      tive AI models, such as downstream task performance and model inference times. How valuable
541   scaling laws can be in providing insights relevant to those choices remains an open question.

      A     E XTENDED RELATED WORK
715
716
      Here we contrast more granular details of several related works.
717   Tuyls et al. (2023) provide (amongst other things) a valuable initial study on the relationship between
718   compute and pre-training loss for BC. They focus on Atari and Nethack games, where the dataset is
719   generated by a fixed pre-trained agent. They test one architecture – a single-layer LSTM network,
720   scaling width-wise to increase parameter count. They find the model size coefficient Noptimal ∝ C a
721   varies between 0.58 to 0.79 for different games.
722   By contrast, our work focuses on transformer-based models, and we consider two variants of archi-
723   tecture. We also train on human behavior, rather than pre-trained agents (which could be viewed as
724   model distillation). Additionally, we go beyond BC and also study scale in world modeling.
725
      Scaling laws in other domains. Scaling laws have also been observed in auto-regressive model-
726   ing of other modalities. Most relevant to our world modeling task are those of video and images.
727   Henighan et al. (2020) found the optimal trade off between model and dataset size to match their re-
728   ported LLM coefficient (Noptimal ∝ C 0.7 ) and was not affected by tokenizer. Our experiments offer
729   different findings in the domain of world modeling on human data – we use updated methodologies
730   to measure this trade-off (Hoffmann et al., 2022), finding it is affected by the tokenizer. Tian et al.
731   (2024) show scaling laws emerge in image modeling with non raster-ordered next-token prediction.

850   B.3   DATASET DETAILS
851
852   Image observations were stored in MP4 format at 60fps, alongside binary files containing the asso-
853   ciated controller actions. A time code extracted from the game was stored for each frame, to ensure
854   actions and frames remained in sync at training time.
855
      The 7 Maps dataset comprised 60,986 matches, yielding 530,713 individual player trajectories (each
856
      around 9 minutes), totaling 27.89 TiB on disk. This amounted to around 8.6 years of gameplay. After
857   downsampling to 10Hz (the frequency models are trained on), this equated to 1.63B frames. This
858   was then divided into training / validation / test sets by dividing the matches with an 80:10:10 split.
859
860
      Our filtered Sky Garden dataset used the same 80:10:10 split and 10Hz downsampling, but focused
      on just one map, yielding 71,940 individual player trajectories, or 355.5M frames (around 1.12 years
861
      of game play).

863   For discretizing the controller actions, while the buttons are natively discrete, we discretize the x
      and y values of the left and right joysticks into eleven buckets.

      B.3.1               O N THE INFINITE DATA REGIME
865
866   We wish to study scaling in the infinite data regime, where training loss is not significantly effected
867   by models repeatedly training on the same datapoints which can lead to overfitting effects. This
868   section calculates the number of training tokens allowed for each model family trained in this work.
869   Viewing Figure 1 alongside these numbers confirms that models remain in the infinite data regime
870
      for all our experiments.
871   WM-Token-540, BC-Token-540. We trained on the 7 maps dataset, with 1.63B observation-action
872   pairs. Models used the tokenized architecture with the large VQGAN, so each observation-action
873   pair creates 540 + 16 = 556 transformer inputs, for a total of 1.63B×556 = 906B training tokens.
874   Muennighoff et al. (2024) observe that tokens may be reused up to four times with negligible de-
875   parture from the infinite data regime. This produces 3.6T tokens. For a 200M parameter model the
876
      compute allowed by the infinite data regime is C = 6N D = 6 × 200M × 3.6T = 4.3 × 1021 FLOPs.
877   WM-Token-256. This is trained on the Sky Garden dataset, with 355M observation-action pairs.
878   Each pair is split into 256 + 16 = 272 tokens, for 97B training tokens, or 97B×4 = 386B effective
879   tokens. For a 200M parameter model the compute allowed by the ‘infinite data regime’ is C =
880   6N D = 6 × 200M × 386B = 4.6 × 1020 FLOPs.
881   BC-CNN. Trained on 7 maps dataset, but now with one token per observation-action pair, this
882   creates a possible 1.63B × 4 = 6.52B effective tokens. A 50M parameter model uses C = 6N D =
883   6 × 50M × 6.52B = 2.0 × 1018 FLOPs.
