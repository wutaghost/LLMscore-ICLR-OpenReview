                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in learning decision-making policies can largely be attributed to
012
               training expressive policy models, largely via imitation learning. While imita-
               tion discards non-expert data, offline and/or online fine-tuning via reinforcement
013
               learning (RL) can still learn from suboptimal data. However, instantiating RL
014
               training of a new policy class often presents a different challenge: most deep RL
015            machinery is co-developed with assumptions on the policy class, resulting in poor
016            performance when the policy class changes. For e.g., SAC utilizes a low-variance
017            reparameterization policy gradient for Gaussian policies, but this is unstable for
018            diffusion policies and intractable for autoregressive (e.g., transformer) categorical
019            policies. To address this issue, we develop an offline RL and online fine-tuning ap-
020            proach called parameterization-agnostic RL (PA-RL) that can effectively train
021            multiple policy classes, with varying architectures. The basic idea is that a uni-
022            versal supervised learning loss can replace the policy improvement step in RL, as
023
               long as it is applied on “optimized” actions. To obtain these optimized actions, we
               first sample multiple actions from a base policy, and run global optimization (i.e.,
024
               re-ranking multiple action samples using the Q-function) and local optimization
025
               (i.e., running gradient steps on an action sample) to maximize the critic on these
026            candidates. PA-RL enables fine-tuning diffusion and autoregressive policies via
027            RL, while improving performance and sample-efficiency compared to existing on-
028            line RL fine-tuning methods. PA-RL allows us to successfully fine-tune diffusion
029            policies and OpenVLA, a 7B parameter generalist robot policy on real robots.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033   Recent successes in training decision-making policies in a number of domains such as robotics
034   and language agents largely stem from the use of expressive models combined with large-scale
035   imitation-style training (Zitkovich et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), an approach that
036   has been tried and tested in other sub-fields of machine learning, such as vision and NLP (Ouyang
037   et al., 2022). However, we have also realized that training a policy once and freezing it is not good
038   enough for many real-world deployment scenarios, where some adaptation is needed: for example,
039
      a robot must adapt its behavior as the surrounding environment or task changes. The hallmark of an
      adaptation process is in its use of autonomous, non-expert data.
040
041   In these cases, imitation alone is not enough to guarantee the most efficient learning and RL provides
042   an appealing alternative. In principle, off-the-shelf RL algorithms could be used to fine-tune any pol-
043   icy. For instance, by running actor-critic RL (Sutton & Barto, 2018), a policy can be trained towards
044   maximizing the Q-function. However, most existing deep RL algorithms entangle the choice of
045
      training objectives and algorithm design decisions with the choice of the policy class. For exam-
      ple, soft actor-critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018a), the base learner for many offline and online
046
      fine-tuning algorithms (Kumar et al., 2020; Nakamoto et al., 2024), has been extensively tuned for
047
      Gaussian (and tanh-Gaussian) policies: swapping the policy for a diffusion policy causes instabil-
048   ity (Wang et al.). These instabilities can be severe to the extent that much weaker policy extraction
049   techniques, e.g., critic-based re-ranking (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) can outperform the complete
050   policy gradient Wang et al., even though theoretically and with other policy classes this is not the
051   case (Fujimoto et al., 2018a; Ghasemipour et al., 2021). Likewise, in order to extend conservative
052   Q-learning (CQL) (Kumar et al., 2020) to autoregressive policies, Chebotar et al. (2023) had to to
053   make many modifications to the loss in the CQL algorithm. Overall, this means that adapting the
      best policy training methodologies or parameterization from one policy class to another can be chal-

054
      lenging, and depending upon the policy itself, practitioners are forced to choose a weaker algorithm
055   or spend cycles modifying other components of their approach.
056
057   In this paper, we tackle this challenge by developing a single offline RL and online fine-tuning
058
      approach, which we call parameterization-agnostic RL (PA-RL), that works well regardless of the
      choice of policy class or backbone. We can train any type of policy class and architecture, as long
059
      as the policy updates use a supervised learning loss. Now, to perform policy improvement, we pro-
060
      pose that the RL algorithm directly optimizes the action (instead of policy parameters). Doing so
061   decouples policy improvement from training the parameteric policy, which can now be done via
062   supervised learning, by maximizing the likelihood of “optimized” actions found by policy improve-
063   ment. To obtain these optimized actions, we first sample from the base policy several times to get
064   multiple action candidates, and then take gradient steps with respect to the value function to improve
065   those actions in the direction of maximizing values. Then these optimized action samples replace
066   the use of samples from the policy in any value-based RL algorithm, and are used to train the policy
067   themselves. Note that while prior work does use supervised losses for policy training, our main
068   contribution is to show that single approach of this sort can effectively train multiple policy classes.
069   We evaluate PA-RL empirically on a number of domains including simulated robotic manipula-
070   tion tasks and real robots, with Gaussian, diffusion and autoregressive categorical policies based
071   on transformer backbones, on offline RL and offline-to-online RL fine-tuning problems. Our re-
072   sults show that PA-RL attains state-of-the-art performance, outperforming the next best fine-tuning
073   approach by 13% in aggregate over various domains. PA-RL produces the largest gains on long-
074   horizon tasks that present multimodal offline data distributions (e.g., CALVIN (Mees et al., 2022) in
075
      our experiments), where a more expressive policy class beyond standard tanh-Gaussian is necessary
      for performance. Most notably, PA-RL improves diffusion policies on two manipulation tasks by
076
      20-35% within only 1-2 hours of online RL fine-tuning on a real WidowX robot. We also show that
077
      PA-RL is the first RL method to improve 7 billion parameter OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024) by 75%
078   within 40 minutes of real-world interaction. We also perform a number of ablation experiments.
079
080   Our main contribution is PA-RL, a single approach for offline RL and online fine-tuning policies
081
      with different parameterizations and classes via a supervised learning update on optimized actions.
      The use of a supervised learning loss renders simplicity and universality to our approach. By com-
082
      bining global optimization and local optimization, PA-RL is able to effectively train diffusion and
083
      transformer policies with offline RL and offline-to-online RL algorithms (Nakamoto et al., 2024;
084   Kostrikov et al.; Ball et al., 2023). To the best of our knowledge, our results are the first to fine-tune
085   diffusion policies (Chi et al., 2023) (both in simulation and in the real-world), and autoregressive
086   categorical transformer policies (in simulation), all via a single actor-critic RL approach.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
089   Contrary to prior belief, recent work (Park et al., 2024) shows that policy learning can be a big
090   bottleneck in RL, especially in offline RL (Levine et al., 2020). One implication is that enhancing
091   the policy extraction step with the most expressive architectures and the best loss functions would
092   be important, but prior works often tailor the RL approach to a specific policy class (e.g., most
093
      work has focused on Gaussian policies). In principle, designing effective algorithms for only one
      policy class can “overfit” resulting in methods that are actually worse for other policy classes. For
094
      instance, while algorithms that use Gaussian policies reparameterize the policy gradient (Lillicrap
095
      et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018a; Fujimoto et al., 2018b), doing so for diffusion policies (Wang
096   et al.) or flows (Mazoure et al., 2020) can be quite unstable and requires per-task tuning. Hence,
097   to make a stable algorithm, Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023) resort to Q-function re-ranking on top
098   of a frozen behavior policy, resulting in a somewhat less powerful policy improvement operator
099   (e.g., compared to EMaQ (Ghasemipour et al., 2021), which uses a similar reranking-based policy
100   improvement operator to TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), which optimizes the policy through
101   the use of full policy gradient and generally performs better). Most offline RL algorithms that use
102   autoregressive categorical transformer policies run conditional (Kumar et al., 2019) or unconditional
103   supervised regression (Janner et al., 2021; Yamagata et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), but Park et al.
104
      (2024) show that such approaches are unable to extract the best possible policy. In fact, to fine-tune
      transformer policies directly via offline RL, Chebotar et al. (2023) had to modify value function
105
      training.
106
107   Motivated by these findings, in this paper, we build a single actor-critic RL algorithm that is effective
      for fine-tuning arbitrary policy classes, with a focus on diffusion and transformer policies. Related

121   of Chebotar et al. (2023), we make no modifications to value function learning.
122
123
      Methodologically, our method PA-RL appears similar to prior approaches that pose “RL as super-
      vised learning”, and use weighted or filtered negative log likelihood (NLL) losses for training (Peng
124
      et al., 2019; Peters et al., 2010; Peters & Schaal, 2007; Oh et al., 2018; Abdolmaleki et al., 2018).
125
      However, note a crucial difference: while these works largely use the dataset or replay buffer action
126   for training via an NLL loss, PA-RL samples new actions from the policy, optimizes them against the
127   critic, and then trains the policy via NLL on this action. This allows PA-RL to make aggressive up-
128   dates, thus avoiding the “slowness” associated with supervised regression (Tajwar et al.; Kostrikov
129   et al.; Park et al., 2024), while inheriting its simplicity.
130
      Action optimization from PA-RL also resembles prior work that uses CEM optimization to obtain
131
      actions from a Q-function in the online RL setting (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Simmons-Edler et al.,
132   2019; Pourchot & Sigaud, 2019), and supervised learning to improve a policy based on the obtained
133   actions (Neumann et al.; Shao et al., 2022). Unlike PA-RL, these methods do not make use of offline
134   pre-training to train the proposal distribution, which we show is important in offline RL and online
135   fine-tuning settings since the critic can give erroneous values outside the support of the dataset seen
136   so far (see Figures 12 and 13; initilization from the offline policy is important).

      Can we devise a simple yet universal approach to policy optimization in offline RL and online
217   fine-tuning? In order for an approach to be universal across parameterizations, one natural point is
218   to modify policy training to use a negative log likelihood (NLL) loss from supervised learning, since
219   most deep learning machinery is built around optimizing this loss (or a functional approximation).
220   To be able to do so, our method (Fig. 1) builds on the insight that policy improvement can be
221   performed via a supervised learning loss (Neumann et al.; Shao et al., 2022), as long as the loss
222   is applied on optimized actions. Thus, we can decompose the policy improvement step into two
223   stages: (1) directly optimizing action samples produced by the policy, and (2) training the policy to
224   imitate these “optimized” actions. This decomposition avoids having to compute ∇ϕ Q(s, πϕ (s)), or
225
      estimate high-variance policy gradient estimates. We would expect this approach to inherit appealing
      scaling, reliability, and tuning properties of supervised learning losses. In this section, we will detail
226
      each of the two stages of the decomposition, and then describe the resulting algorithm.
227
228   4.1 ACTION O PTIMIZATION
229   Given a state s, a policy πϕ (·|s) checkpoint that appears in the process of learning, and a fixed
230   Q-function Qθ (s, a), the objective of this stage is to obtain an action sample that optimizes the Q-
231   function as much as possible, while staying close to the support of seen actions at state s. We use
232   πϕ (·|s) as an initializer for the action optimization procedure. In the offline setting, doing so allows
233   us to find the best action close to the support at the current state (and wihtin the support of actions
234   at the current state for a pessimistic algorithm). During fine-tuning, this enables us to still leverage
235   priors learned by the offline policy while adapting it to maximize returns on the task.
236   To produce an optimized action, we utilize a combination of different types of action optimization
237   procedures. First, we consider global optimization or sampling that samples multiple actions from
238   the pre-trained policy and discards all but top few actions with highest Q-values under the critic
239   (for computational efficiency). Let Aπϕ ,k (s) := {a0 , a1 , · · · , ak−1 } ∼ πϕ (·|s) denote k sampled

241   actions put in order of their ranking obtained from the Q-function, i.e., Qθ (s, a[i]) ≥ Qθ (s, a[j]),
242   for i ≤ j. Then, global optimization retains the following subset:

      from the current policy, instead of a previous policy or a behavioral policy (Peng et al., 2019), (b)
287
      local optimization and global optimization employed by PA-RL enable aggressive updates on action
288
      samples to draw them to novel regions that are otherwise not possible with non-parametric methods
289   that operate on the space of actions directly. While these differences might appear small, we show
290   in our experiments that they have a substantial impact on downstream efficiency of RL training.
291
292   4.3 P UTTING IT A LL T OGETHER : F INAL PA-RL A LGORITHM
293   PA-RL can be used to replace the policy improvement step in multiple RL algorithms. In our ex-
294   periments, we primarily focus on online fine-tuning and adaptation of offline RL. Hence, we in-
295   stantiate PA-RL using two popular RL fine-tuning methods: Cal-QL (Nakamoto et al., 2024) and
296   IQL (Kostrikov et al.). PA-RL only modifies the policy improvement step of each of these meth-
297   ods, while keeping the critic training as it is. Since IQL training does not utilize policy backups,
298
      using PA-RL in conjunction with IQL is straightforward: simply replace the advantage-weighted
      regression (AWR) update with the above supervised learning update (e.g., Equation 4.5 for diffu-
299
      sion policies). On the other hand, for Cal-QL and other actor-critic algorithms, where the policy
300
      πϕ (·|s) is used to generate action samples for performing the TD-backup, we utilize the optimized

346   The goal of our experiments is to understand the efficacy of PA-RL in fine-tuning policies of various
347   parameterizations and classes via RL. To this end, we evaluate PA-RL and several prior approaches,
348   on a number of benchmark domains that require learning policies from static offline data (offline
349   RL (Levine et al., 2020)) and then fine-tuning them with limited online interaction in the MDP
350   (offline-to-online fine-tuning (Nair et al., 2020)). Then, we will also present results validating the

352   with PA-RL in Appendix C. Finally, we perform ablation experiments to understand the utility of

      Discussion and Conlcusion. In this paper, we developed PA-RL, a method to fine-tune policies
534   of various classes and parameterizations via actor-critic RL. We showed state-of-the-art online fine-
535   tuning results across a number of simulation tasks and on two real-robot tasks. Despite promising
536   results, PA-RL still has some limitations that future work should aim to address. Most importantly,
537   PA-RL requires sampling multiple actions from the policy, which is expensive for large foundation
538   policies. That said, future work can attempt to reduce this computational cost by caching actions
539   from past rounds and training on them using ideas from off-policy policy gradient. Understanding
      interplay between global and local optimization better is also a viable direction.

778   D4RL AntMaze: We test methods across two maze sizes (medium and large) and two dataset
779
      types (play and diverse). The diverse and large datasets differ in the starting locations and goal
      locations of trajectories. The diverse dataset consists of trajectories with random initial and goal
780
      locations, whereas play contains a set of specific hand-picked locations. The offline datasets for this
781
      benchmark have high coverage over states and actions.

783   D4RL FrankaKitchen: The FrankaKitchen benchmark contains three tele-operated datasets:
784   kitchen − complete, which contains trajectories that fully solve all sub-tasks, but is 37 times smaller
785
      than the other datasets; kitchen − partial, where there are both trajectories that fully solve all sub-
      tasks, and undirected data that performs unrelated behaviors; and kitchen − mixed, where no tra-
786
      jectory solves all tasks, requiring exploration from the agent.

789   complete four tasks (OpenDrawer, TurnonLightbulb, MoveSliderLeft, and TurnonLED), with
790   the distinction that we only use image observations (i.e., the agent doesn’t have access to propri-
791
      oception nor object states). To ensure Markovian rewards, we make the reward function is equal
      to the number of completed sub-tasks at each time-step (i.e., the agent only gets reward +4 if all
792
      sub-tasks are completed). The evaluation score for a trajectory is the maximum number of sub-tasks
793
      completed simultaneously at any single point in the trajectory.

      Action optimization hyperparameters: For all experiments shown on the paper except for abla-
804   tions, the number of actions sampled from the base policy is 32, which are filtered down to the
805   top ten, and then propagated through the Q-function for ten gradient steps with gradient step size
806   of 3e-4. While we find that these values are robust to all the tested settings, these choices might
807   require changes according to the characteristics of the available dataset and action space. For exam-
808   ple, larger action spaces (such as bimanual manipulation) might require larger gradient step sizes or
809   close-to-optimal datasets might perform well with significantly fewer action samples and gradient
      steps.

1262   E     T RAINING TIME DISCUSSION
1263
1264   PA-RL optimizes actions using the procedure described in Section 4 any time an action from the
1265   policy is needed. We discuss how this affects the App of our method at different stages.
1266
1267
1268
1269
1270
1271
1272
1273
1274
1275
1276
1277
1278
1279
1280   Figure 18: Performance on CALVIN task as a function of wall clock time for PA-RL, IDQL,
1281   and DQL. All three methods ran on the same compute instence type (TPU v4), were implemented
1282   in the same codebase. Observe that PA-RL improves at a similar rate per unit amount of wall-clock
1283   time as IDQL, but is able to improve far beyond to a better performance value. DQL largely remains
1284   flat as a function of more unit wall-clock time put into training.
1285
1286   Critic training. In principle, action optimization should increase memory and computation require-
1287   ments to critic training, but it also enables using an action cache to compute ahead of time, even in a
1288   distributed manner, when sufficient numbers of actions from the base policy are available. To make
1289   sure that this cache is not stale and to ensure that the critic models the optimal / on-policy value
1290   function, the actions cache is updated after every epoch of policy training via supervised learning.
1291   When sampling from the base policy is more than T times more expensive than taking T gradient
1292
       steps of the critic (as is the case with OpenVLA or with diffusion policies with a large number of
       denoising steps), PA-RL can be significantly more efficient than alternatives that do not do caching.
1293
1294   Policy distillation. Compared to standard offline RL and online fine-tuning objectives, the super-
1295   vised learning objective PA-RL can be significantly more efficient than policy improvement through
       reparameterization. For example, for a diffusion policy, backpropagating critic gradients through the


                                                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       diffusion chain uses a larger memory footprint than the DDPM objective PA-RL uses, by a factor
1297   equal to the number of denoising steps.
1298
1299   Inference. During inference, PA-RL can optionally also apply action optimization by querying the
1300
       base policy multiple times to sample an action. This can significantly increase the memory require-
       ments of our method. That said, we do note that the number of samples from the base policy during
1301
       inference can be much smaller than during training, as we do with OpenVLA (see Appendix C).
1302
       PA-RL additionally requires taking multiple gradient steps of the critic with respect to the actions.
1303   We note that depending on the architecture used, this can be much cheaper than doing multiple full
1304   forward passes through the Q-function. For example, for image-based domains, the bulk of the com-
1305   putation happens for image encoding, which does not depend on the action. Therefore, the gradient
1306   steps will ignore that part of the network. There is also room for improvement for future work to
1307   investigate reducing the number of gradient steps further into training (as Figure 11 right suggests
1308   local optimization might have diminishing effects as fine-tuning progresses).
