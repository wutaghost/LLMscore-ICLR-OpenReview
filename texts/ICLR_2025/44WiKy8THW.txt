011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Non-autoregressive language models are emerging as effective alternatives to au-
014            toregressive models in the field of natural language processing, facilitating si-
015
               multaneous token generation. This study introduces a novel flow matching ap-
               proach that employs Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence geodesics to interpolate
016
               between initial and target distributions for discrete sequences. We formulate a loss
017
               function designed to maximize the conditional likelihood of discrete tokens and
018            demonstrate that its maximizer corresponds to the flow matching velocity during
019            logit interpolation. Although preliminary experiments conducted on the TinySto-
020            ries dataset yielded suboptimal results, we propose an empirical sampling scheme
021            based on a pretrained denoiser that significantly enhances performance. Addition-
022            ally, we present a more general hybrid approach that achieves strong performance
023            on more complex datasets, such as Fine Web and Lamini Instruction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Non-autoregressive language models have emerged as a promising alternative to traditional autore-
028
      gressive models in natural language processing (NLP) tasks, potentially offering significant advan-
029
      tages during inference by enabling the simultaneous generation of all tokens rather than a sequential
030   process. However, capturing the complex dependencies inherent in discrete textual data without
031   relying on the autoregressive assumption presents substantial challenges.
032
033
      In this paper, we investigate a conditional flow matching approach for text generation, which has
      garnered significant interest recently. Notably, methods such as discrete flow matching Gat et al.
034
      (2024) and Dirichlet flow matching Stärk et al. (2024) have adapted continuous flow-based models to
035
      address the discrete nature of text by representing tokens as one-hot vectors within a V -dimensional
036   simplex, where V denotes the vocabulary size.
037
038   The central concept in flow matching involves initiating a probability path ρt between a known
039
      starting distribution ρ0 and a target distribution ρ1 , which is only accessible through samples. In our
      context, these samples are provided as discrete sequences, represented as sequences of vectors within
040
      a V -dimensional simplex. Previous research has highlighted the limitations of linear interpolation
041
      in this framework Stärk et al. (2024). In response, we propose the utilization of geodesics under
042   the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, which effectively incorporates the inherent geometry of the
043   simplex. These geodesics correspond to linear interpolation in the logit space, expressed as lt =
044   log xt , thereby facilitating a simple closed-form interpolation method.
045
      The loss function within the generation CFM (Conditional Flow Matching) framework can be se-
046
      lected from various options; however, leveraging insights from recent studies, we implement a de-
047
      noiser that maximizes the conditional likelihood log Pθ (x1 |xt , t), which predicts the discrete tokens
048   of x1 . While it is feasible to model the joint conditional distribution, this approach does not confer
049   any advantages over existing GPT models, as setting t = 0 necessitates modeling the unconditional
050   data distribution. Therefore, we focus on modeling the marginal likelihood of each token in x1 con-
051   ditioned on xt , which corresponds to a simplified approximation of the joint distribution. Although
052   the theoretical justification for this loss function has been established in the context of single-token
053   scenarios, its applicability to general sequences has not been comprehensively investigated. We pro-
      vide a theoretical analysis establishing that the exact maximizer of the likelihood yields a precise


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      expression for the flow matching velocity under logit interpolation, thus offering a robust foundation
055   for our training scheme.
056
057   Despite the theoretical rigor of our approach, initial experiments conducted on the TinyStories
058
      dataset Eldan & Li (2023) indicated that the model’s performance was suboptimal. To address this
      issue, we propose an innovative empirical sampling scheme based on the same pretrained denoiser.
059
      In this scheme, given a specific time t and a sample xt , we sample x1 from the distribution P (x1 |xt )
060
      and introduce additional noise to generate a sample xt+h . This process is iteratively repeated to pro-
061   duce the final sequence. Although the theoretical foundations for this method are not yet completely
062   established, our experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements compared to the initial
063   approach.
064
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
065
066          • We introduce the application of Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence geodesics, which cor-
067            respond to linear interpolation in the logit space, for flow matching in discrete sequence
068            modeling.
069
             • We present a theoretical justification demonstrating that the exact maximizer of the likeli-
070            hood function yields the precise flow matching velocity in this context.
071
             • We propose a novel empirical sampling scheme that, while currently lacking comprehen-
072
               sive theoretical justification, exhibits significant performance improvements in experiments
073
               conducted on complex datasets, including the Fine Web and Lamini Instruction datasets.

078   Flow matching Lipman et al. (2023) is a well-established method for determining a flow that con-
079
      nects samples from two distributions with densities ρ0 and ρ1 . This is achieved by solving the
      continuity equation in relation to the time-dependent vector field v(x, t) and the time-dependent
080
      density ρ(x, t), subject to the following boundary conditions:
081
082                                      ∂ρ(x, t)
                                                  = −div(ρ(x, t)v(x, t)),
083                                        ∂t
084                                       ρ(x, 0) = ρ0 (x), ρ(x, 1) = ρ1 (x).
085
      The function ρ(x, t) is referred to as the probability density path. Typically, the initial distribution
086   ρ0 is chosen for convenience, often as a standard normal distribution, ρ(x) = N (0, I). The target
087   distribution ρ1 is unknown; we only possess a set of samples drawn from this distribution. Con-
088   sequently, our objective is to approximate the vector field v(x, t) ≈ v(x, t) using these available
089   samples.
090
      Starting from a defined vector field, we can construct a flow xt , which represents a time-dependent
091
      map satisfying the ordinary differential equation (ODE):
092
093                                                   ∂xt
                                                          = v(xt , t)
094                                                   ∂t
095   with the initial condition
096                                                 xt=0 = x0 .
097   From this process, one can sample a point x0 from the distribution ρ0 and subsequently use the ODE
098   to obtain a point xt=1 = x1 , which will have a distribution approximately equal to ρ1 . While the
099   vector field or path solutions are not unique for given boundaries ρ0 and ρ1 , the identification of any
100   solution facilitates the sampling process from the unknown density ρ1 .

291   3   R ELATED WORK
292
293   In this section, we examine the existing literature on modeling discrete sequences. The authors
294   in Campbell et al. (2024) introduce Discrete Flow Models (DFMs), which integrate discrete and
295   continuous data through the use of Continuous Time Markov Chains. These models enhance tra-
296
      ditional diffusion methods, facilitating multimodal frameworks for protein co-design and achieving
      state-of-the-art results in the generation of protein structures and sequences.
297
298   Moreover, Song et al. (2021) propose a stochastic differential equation (SDE) aimed at transforming
299   complex data distributions by systematically adding and removing noise, leveraging neural networks
300   for accurate score estimation. In addition, the work by Campbell et al. (2022) presents a continuous
301   time framework specifically designed for denoising diffusion models of discrete data, resulting in
302
      the development of high-performance samplers that surpass conventional methodologies.
303   The research conducted by Gat et al. (2024) introduces Discrete Flow Matching, a novel approach fo-
304   cused on generating high-dimensional discrete data, such as language, while emphasizing improve-
305   ments in generative perplexity. Additionally, Ghazvininejad et al. (2019b) utilize masked language
306   modeling techniques to predict target words based on existing input text, and Austin et al. (2021)
307   enhance multinomial diffusion models by incorporating transition matrices tailored for discrete data.
308
      Lastly, Hoogeboom et al. (2021) offer extensions to generative flows and diffusion models specifi-
      cally for categorical data, demonstrating superior efficacy in text modeling and image segmentation
309
      applications.
310
311   Recent advancements have also concentrated on the application of continuous space diffusion meth-
312   ods to discrete datasets Dieleman et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022); Han et al. (2022). Noteworthy
313   contributions by Lin et al. (2023) aim to refine the modeling of diffusion flows, while novel tech-
314
      niques for Continuous Flow Matching have been introduced by Lovelace et al. (2022) and Stärk
      et al. (2024) utilizing Dirichlet paths.
315
316   Autoregressive models have proven to be fundamental in the advancement of natural language pro-
317   cessing Zhao et al. (2023). The influential GPT-2 model Radford et al. (2019) exemplified the
318   potential of autoregressive approaches in producing coherent and contextually relevant text, thereby
319   setting the groundwork for significant progress in a wide range of applications. Subsequent re-
320
      search has further explored these generative capabilities, underscoring the ability of autoregressive
      methodologies to tackle complex linguistic challenges.
321
322   Masked generative modeling has emerged as a promising area, utilizing various techniques to gener-
323   ate content by obscuring portions of the input data and predicting the masked variables Ghazvinine-
      jad et al. (2019a). Research conducted by Savinov et al. (2022) has investigated enhancements to


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      traditional masking techniques within the domain of text generation, leading to developments such
325   as MaskGIT, which employs advanced methodologies for high-resolution image synthesis Chang
326   et al. (2022). Furthermore, Ziv et al. (2024) illustrated the effectiveness of a text-to-music model,
327   demonstrating that the adoption of the MaskGIT framework significantly elevates the quality of
328   generated outputs.

332
      In this study, we undertaken a comprehensive evaluation of our proposed method across both lan-
333
      guage modeling and image generation tasks. For the language modeling component, we compared
334
      our method to existing approaches by employing the established generative metric known as per-
335   plexity. In the context of the image generation task, we utilized the Frechet Inception Distance
336   (FID) as our primary evaluation metric. All experiments were conducted utilizing two H100 GPUs,
337   each equipped with 80 GB of memory.

339   4.1       E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
340
341   Data For our experiments, we conducted evaluations using the Tiny Stories Eldan & Li (2023)
342   and Binarized MNIST LeCun et al. (2010) datasets. The Tiny Stories dataset comprises syntheti-
343   cally generated short narratives produced by the models GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. This dataset employs
344   a limited vocabulary, which significantly reduces the computational resources required for experi-
345   ments. The Binarized MNIST dataset consists of black-and-white images of handwritten digits, each
346
      with a size of 28 × 28 pixels. This can be interpreted as a modeling problem involving sequences of
      length 784 and a vocabulary size of 2.
347
348   Furthermore, we extended our evaluation to more complex datasets, including the Fine Web Penedo
349   et al. (2024) dataset, which contains over 15 trillion tokens of cleaned and deduplicated English web
350   data. For our training purposes, we utilized a smaller subset of this dataset comprising 10 billion
351   tokens, represented as sequences with a length of 1024 and a vocabulary size of 50, 257.
352   Additionally, we assessed the proposed technique in the context of a conditional generation task,
353   utilizing the Lamini Instruction Wu et al. (2023) dataset, which includes 2.58 million pairs of in-
354   structions and responses, each with a length of 256 tokens. In all experimental tasks, we applied a
355   smoothing coefficient of β = 0.01 to facilitate geodesic interpolation.
356
357   Model In our experiments, the architecture utilized for the toy tasks is based on the DiT model Pee-
358   bles & Xie (2022). We conducted extensive experiments on high-dimensional data sets (Fine Web
359   and Lamini Instruction), employing an efficient open-access implementation of GPT-2 1 , which was
360   specifically modified to address the flow matching problem (see Appendix A). The text modeling
361
      architecture consists of 12 layers, 12 attention heads (6 heads for high-dimensional tasks), an em-
      bedding size of 768, and a vocabulary size of 50,257. For the image modeling task, we implemented
362
      a model characterized by 4 layers, 6 attention heads, an embedding size of 96, and a vocabulary size
363
      of 2.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
528
529
530   In this study, we introduced Logit-FM as a robust solution for discrete problems, employing geodesic
531   interpolation within the simplex framework. We validated the use of a denoising objective, enabling
532   the independent resolution of the problem for each token in the sequence. Additionally, we de-
533   veloped a novel inference scheme for the learned denoiser model, which demonstrates significant
534   improvements over traditional inference methods in terms of the quality of generated sequences.
535   Remarkably, our proposed method consistently outperforms autoregressive models in quality, even
536
      with a substantial reduction in the number of function evaluations.
537   Looking forward, we aim to establish a comprehensive theoretical foundation for our empirically
538   effective inference scheme and to assess its performance across a wider array of complex tasks. We
539   contend that the approach presented in this work has the potential to make a substantial contribution
      to the field of discrete data modeling, paving the way for future advancements and applications.
