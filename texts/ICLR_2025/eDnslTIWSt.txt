009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) require post-training methods such
012            as supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction-response pairs to enable instruc-
013            tion following. However, this process can potentially harm existing capabilities
014            learned during pretraining. In this paper, we investigate the loss of context aware-
015            ness after SFT, defined as the capability to extract and understand information
016            from the user-provided context and respond accordingly. We are the first to iden-
017            tify and show that the loss of context-awareness appears on instruction-finetuned
018
               LLMs when the chat template is applied to the input prompts. We identify the per-
               formance decline is partially caused by the bias embedded into the chat template to
019
               focus less on the user-provided context. Based on these observations, we propose
020
               two methods to mitigate the loss of context awareness in instruct models: post-
021            hoc attention steering on user tokens and conditional instruction fine-tuning with
022            a context-dependency indicator. Empirical experiments on 4 context-dependent
023            downstream tasks and 3 pretrained LLMs of different sizes show that our meth-
024            ods effectively mitigates the loss of context awareness without compromising the
025            general ability to follow instructions. Our findings also strongly advocate the ne-
026            cessity to carefully benchmark context awareness after instruction fine-tuning.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) pretrained on large-scale datasets acquire a diverse set of language
032   modeling capabilities in pretraining. To enhance these models’ ability to follow general instructions,
033
      further fine-tuning is typically required such as supervised instruction fine-tuning (SFT) (Wei et al.,
      2021; Ouyang et al., 2022) and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano
034
      et al., 2017) to better understand and respond to human requests. However, the additional fine-tuning
035
      can potentially harm existing capabilities learned in pretraining, as pointed out by several existing
036   works (Lin et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2024).
037
038   In this paper, we particularly investigate the loss of context-awareness after instruction fine-tuning,
039
      which is the capability to understand and retrieve exact information from the user-provided context
      and respond accordingly. Context awareness is crucial for many real-world use cases, including
040
      retrieval augmented generalization (RAG) (Khandelwal et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
041
      2023b), in-context learning (Agarwal et al., 2024), and contextual question-answering (QA) (Ra-
042   jpurkar et al., 2016; Choi et al., 2018; Dua et al., 2019). We first illustrate the loss of context aware-
043   ness in Figure 2 with the Needle-in-a-Haystack test on four popular instruction-tuned models. We
044   demonstrate that the performance degradation is consistent on both long-context and relatively short-
045   context LLMs, which cannot be solely explained by the distribution difference in context lengths
046   between the instruction dataset and the evaluation benchmarks, as suggested by prior works (Dubey
047   et al., 2024).
048   We identify that the bias embedded within the chat template to focus less on the user tokens is a
049   major cause of context-awareness degradation. Normal instruction fine-tuning dataset contains a
050   mixture of both model-dependent and context-dependent queries. The former consists of queries
051   the model can respond to relying mostly on its internal knowledge learned during pretraining. On
052   the other hand, responding to the second type of query requires exact information retrieval and
053   processing from the user-provided context in the input prompt, such as in-context learning, long-
      form instruction-following, and contextual QA tasks. However, a query accompanied by a context

      can still be a model-dependent query as the model may have learned the context during pretraining,
055   and is able to respond without relying on the user given context. Therefore, it is challenging for
056   the model to differentiate between these two type of queries from the prompt only, and incorrect

058
      We validate the bias embedded in chat templates with the Needle-In-a-Haystack test (NIH) (Kam-
059
      radt, 2023), which requires a model to retrieve a given text “needle” from a long paragraph of
060
      irrelevant text. Our experiments show that the needle retrieval performance drops on instruction-
061   finetuned models only when the chat template is applied to the input, which is however crucial for
062   the model to distinguish different roles in conversations. We further show that the performance drop
063   can be attributed to the drop in attention value allocated to the whole user input section. There-
064   fore, the context retrieval capability is not lost in the model, but “inhibited” by the chat-formatted
065   fine-tuning.
066
      Based on these observations, a straightforward approach is to directly steer the attention value during
067   inference time to emphasize the user inputs on instruction-tuned models. This can be achieved by
068   manually intervening attention scores of the user context tokens during generation. Experiments
069   show that while performance on simple retrieval tasks can be significantly boosted by attention
070   steering, manipulating the attention value in the inference stage can harm other capabilities of the
071   model, deteriorating performance on more complex tasks.
072
      To further improve upon the undesirable trade-off of post-hoc techniques, we are motivated to steer
073   the attention allocation in the fine-tuning stage. To achieve this goal, we identify context-dependent
074   conversations before fine-tuning and add a special token to the prompt as a hint to the model. The
075   special token can then be added at inference time when more attention is demanded to be allocated
076   to the user-provided context. Empirical experiments show the effectiveness of our method on several
077   open-source, pretrained LLMs and instruction fine-tuning datasets.
078
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
079
080          • We identify that supervised instruction fine-tuning causes pretrained language models to
081            deteriorate in context awareness (even for short context lengths).
082          • We pinpoint the worsened context awareness is associated with attention allocation bias
083            embedded within the chat template.
084
             • We propose an inference-time technique to partially recover the context-awareness of gen-
085
               eral instruction-tuned language models by manually intervening attention scores.
086
087          • We propose a training-time technique utilizing conditional indicators to mitigate the loss of
088
               context awareness of pretrained language models during instruction-tuning.

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Instruction fine-tuning and chat templates. Large language models only learn language model-
093   ing on general corpus during pretraining. To enable instruction-following, they usually require su-
094   pervised fine-tuning on instruction-following datasets (SFT) (Wei et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022),
095   followed by reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017). In this
096   paper, we mainly focus on the SFT stage. Instruction fine-tuning datasets consist of user instruc-
097
      tion and target model response pairs, which can be collected from modified NLP tasks (Wei et al.,
      2021; Longpre et al., 2023), human annotations (Ouyang et al., 2022; Chiang et al., 2023) or synthe-
098
      sized data from existing LLMs (Ding et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a). Instruction fine-tuning usually
099
      converts training examples into a dialog format with a chat template, which consists of a user role
100   indicator (e.g. [INST] in llama-2 models), an assistant role indicator (e.g. [/INST] in llama-2
101   models) and an optional system role indicator (e.g. <<SYS>> in llama-2 models). Other role indi-
102   cators are also used in more complicated scenarios such as tool using (Schick et al., 2023). However,
103   these role indicators and role partition in the conversation are not sufficiently presented and learned
104   during pretraining, making them prone to bias during fine-tuning.
105
106   Evaluation and improvement of context-awareness The capability to retrieve and understand
107   information from the context and respond accordingly is important for many tasks including in-
      context learning (Agarwal et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2020), retrieval augmented generation (Lewis

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2020) and contextual QA (Dua et al., 2019; Rajpurkar et al., 2016; Choi et al., 2018). These
109   tasks are commonly included in standard evaluation benchmarks of recent LLMs. To improve the
110   performances on context-dependent tasks, a common practice is to collect or synthesize context-
111   dependent data, especially contextual QA data, and mix them into the fine-tuning dataset (An et al.,
112   2024; Dubey et al., 2024). Apart from adding more data, Hsieh et al. (2024) also explores cali-
113   brating the attention weights to compensate for the drop of attention weight in the middle of a long
114   context. However, to date, limited existing works have examined how chat templates affect context-
115   awareness when fine-tuning language models on conversational instruction-following data. The
116   most relevant conclusion from existing works is mentioned by Dubey et al. (2024) that long-context
117
      capabilities learned during pretraining drops significantly after SFT. However, they only attribute
      this performance decline to a lack of long-context data in SFT stage.
118
119
      Conditional Supervised Fine-Tuning Conditional Supervised Fine-Tuning (CSFT) involves fine-
120
      tuning a pretrained model on specific tasks while conditioning on additional information or context.
121   The conditions represent specific attributes or styles of the demonstration response and are concate-
122   nated to the original input as prefixes. The model is expected to associate the condition prefix with
123   the style or attribute such that whenever the condition prefix is added, the model generates responses
124   accordingly. Dong et al. (2023) proposes SteerLM, where they applied CSFT to align models with
125   human values (e.g. helpfulness, humor, and creativity) by conditioning the model on attribute pre-
126   fixes. Korbak et al. (2023) adds two control tokens as indicators for good and bad demonstration
127   responses. As opposed to attribute tags or control tokens, Chain of Hindsight (Liu et al., 2023)
128   conditions demonstration responses on natural language comments on the quality or styling of the
129
      response.

202   3.2   ATTENTION A LLOCATION B IAS FROM C HAT T EMPLATES
203
204   Based on the observation that the performance on NIH only drops when the chat template is present,
205   we hypothesize that the chat templates, which are newly introduced in instruction fine-tuning, may
206
      encode bias to downweight the importance of user inputs when generating responses.
207   The distribution of the dataset adopted for pretraining and instruction-tuning is fundamentally dif-
208   ferent. In the pretraining stage, the model is trained on general-domain texts where all tokens are
209   equally important in the context. However, during instruction fine-tuning, the model learns to distin-
210   guish between user input and its responses through chat templates. This user and model partition is
211   not predominantly present in the pretraining corpus, and therefore difficult for the model to benefit
212
      from pretraining to learn a generalizable mapping. As a result, the model may tend to memorize
      the global bias to attend less on the user input if the instruction finetuning dataset is dominated by
213
      queries that do not require exact information retrieval.
214

      input in the NIH test, with and without the chat template. An input to an instruction-finetuned LLM

       4             M ITIGATING THE L OSS OF C ONTEXT-AWARENESS
255
256
      In the previous sections, we have established that the loss of context awareness to the user input is
257
      apparent when the chat template is applied and is directly reflected in the attention allocated. The
258   next step is to mitigate this loss. The first straightforward approach is to directly intervene and
259   increase the attention weight allocated to the user input to compensate for the attention allocation
260   impacted by the chat template. We call this attention steering in this paper, which can be generally
261   performed at inference time, regardless of how the model is fine-tuned. However, since the attention
262   layers encode additional information beyond merely context dependency, manipulating the attention
263   weights (especially with a larger magnitude) may also introduce unintended artifacts.
264   Therefore, in the second method, we want to encourage the model to focus more on the user input
265   during training for context-dependent queries. Recall from Section 1 that both model-dependent
266   and context-dependent queries can have context provided in the input, the model is unable to reliably
267   distinguish between these two types of queries from the input prompts only. Therefore, we propose a
268   metric to identify context-dependent queries with a seed LLM. We then explicitly inform the model
269   of when to attend more to the user-provided context by appending an indicator token to the selected
      subset of context-dependent instructions during fine-tuning.

      4.1     P OST- HOC ATTENTION S TEERING ON U SER I NPUT
271
272   In this section, we explain in detail how to increase the attention allocation level on the user input
273   with post hoc modification to the attention weight during model inference.
274
      Specifically, on each transformer layer, we modify the self-attention weight:

280
281   We adopt the same implementation of attention steering as Zhang et al. (2024b). They steered the
282   attention of pretrained language models to emphasize a user-specified portion of the user instruction,
283   enabling models to follow user instructions without explicit instruction fine-tuning. In our setting,
284
      we up-weight the attention of instruction fine-tuned models on the whole user input prompt which
      consequently down-weights other tokens (chat template role tokens, BOS/EOS tokens, and partially
285
      generated model responses).
286
287   Although post hoc attention steering to improve context-awareness can be generally applied during
288   inference time after a language model has been instruction fine-tuned, it negatively impacts other
289   capabilities of the model that also depend on the attention score. This is a fundamental constraint of
290
      post-hoc model editing techniques, as components within a language model simultaneously provide
      multiple different functions. The trade-off of compromising the performance of other functions lim-
291
      its the magnitude of steering, which in turn limits the effectiveness of improving context awareness.
292
293   In the next section, we propose a training-stage method to train a model to better respond to context-
294   dependent queries with an indicator token optionally provided by the user.

296   4.2     I NSTRUCTION F INE - TUNING WITH C ONTEXT- DEPENDENCY I NDICATORS
297
      Instead of post-hoc attention steering which introduces negative side effects, it is more desirable to
298
      directly control the attention during the fine-tuning process. Our goal is to nudge the model during
299
      training to teach the model to pay more attention to the user input when an indicator is appended to
300   the user instruction by the user.
301
302   Specifically, we process the instruction fine-tuning examples such that responses that demand more
303
      attention on user instruction context are prepended with a special indicator token in its corresponding
      user instruction. Thus, after training whenever this indicator token is added and the model generation
304
      is conditioned on this token, the model will attend more to the user-provided context.
305

340   With the context-dependency scores calculated for each model response in the instruction fine-tuning

347   the task and input format compared to related works, which synthesize context-relevant examples
348   targeted toward specific tasks (e.g. contextual QA). This makes our method more applicable to
349   different types of user queries that require more attention to the user-provided context.

502   6   C ONCLUSION
503
504   This work highlights the detrimental effects of supervised instruction fine-tuning on the context-
505
      awareness of pretrained language models, even in scenarios involving short context lengths. We
      have identified that this decline is closely linked to inherent attention allocation biases within chat
506
      templates. To combat these challenges, we propose an inference-time technique that allows for
507
      the manual adjustment of attention scores, facilitating a partial recovery of context awareness in
508   instruction-tuned models. Furthermore, we introduce a training-time approach that employs condi-
509   tional indicators to help preserve context awareness during the instruction-tuning process. Together,
510   these contributions aim to enhance the performance of language models in maintaining contextual
511   understanding while benefiting from supervised instruction.
512
513   Limitation Our technique of associating context-dependent user instructions with the indicator
514   token may also encode other unintended styles of the selected subset of instructions. The issue
515   is particularly aggravated if the subset of context-relevant samples is small and significantly dif-
516   ferent from the remaining dataset. Utilizing the indicator token also requires the users to know
517
      which instructions require paying more attention to the user context during inference time. Tasks
      demonstrated in our experiment (e.g. keyword retrieval and closed-book QA) evidently require more
518
      attention to the user context. However, complex tasks may involve multiple language skills which
519
      complicates whether the addition of the indicator token will benefit the performance. A future direc-
520   tion is to automatically determine whether appending the indicator to a user instruction is necessary.
521   Besides, we only validate the effectiveness of our proposed methods on relatively small-size models
522   due to limited computational resources.
