009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) often produce outputs that – though plausible –
012            can lack consistency and reliability, particularly in ambiguous or complex scenar-
013            ios. Challenges arise from ensuring that outputs align with both factual correctness
014            and human intent. This is problematic in existing approaches that trade improved
015            consistency for lower accuracy. To mitigate these challenges, we propose a novel
016            game-theoretic approach to enhance consistency and reliability during the decod-
017            ing stage of LLM output generation. Our method models the decoding process as
018
               a multistage Bayesian decoding game. This ensures consistency through Correct-
               ness Alignment and enhances reliability via Ambiguity Calibration. The model
019
               dynamically converges to a consensus on the most reliable outputs and distin-
020
               guishes {Valid, Specious} outputs without human feedback or additional training.
021            Remarkably, our game design allows smaller models to outperform much larger
022            models through game mechanisms (e.g. 78.1 LLaMA13B vs 76.6 PaLM540B), as
023            well as integrating various LL strategies and models, demonstrating the potential
024            of game-theoretic tools to improve the truthfulness and reliability of LLMs.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated extraordinary capabilities in tasks such as fac-
030   tual question answering, fact-checking, and open-ended text generation (Brown et al., 2020; Radford
031   et al., 2021). However, as these generative models increase in complexity and scale, they not only
032   enhance their generation capabilities but also develop a tendency to produce outputs that, while
033   plausible, may be factually incorrect or subtly misleading (McKenzie et al., 2023). This “specious”
034   behavior – whether an inevitable artifact of the model’s optimization process or an unintended hal-
035
      lucination (Banerjee et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024) – poses a significant challenge, often outpacing
      the ability of human judgment to accurately assess the fidelity and truthfulness of the generated con-
036
      tent (Leike et al., 2018). One direct approach is to optimize model outputs for legibility via human
037
      feedback (e.g., RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017; 2018; Saunders et al., 2022; Markov et al., 2023)).
038   Human feedback, inherently constrained by limitations in interpretability (Singh et al., 2024) and
039   hindered by the illegibility (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024) of AI-generated content, struggles to keep
040   pace with increasingly complex reasoning (Casper et al., 2023; Leike et al., 2018). In light of these
041   challenges, the reliability of generative models in collaborative and high-stakes decision-making
042   remains deeply uncertain and we pose the question:
043
044    How can we efficiently ensure that LLM outputs are not only aligned with human intent but also
045
                 valid, especially when human evaluation may overlook specious errors?
046

047
      approach by introducing a Verifier as a dynamic and rigorous evaluator, serving as a proxy for human

050   alternative to solely relying on human feedback (Bai et al., 2023; Saunders et al., 2022; Markov
051   et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2024); (2) the flexibility to adjust game-theoretic objectives – such as utilities
052   and policies between the generator and verifier – allows us to analyze latent decoding consistency
053   and legibility as a function (Jacob et al., 2024; Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024); and (3) in scenarios
      where human guidance is constrained, structured AI interactions can effectively elicit and refine

072   Focus of the paper. Realistically, neither models nor humans can be expected to be perfectly correct
073   or reliable. Thus, our work focuses on actual consistency in correctness and calibrated ambiguity in
074   reliability. Instead of perfect correctness, we expect the LLM generator to have a high pass rate and
075   the LLM verifier to have a high recall on helpful samples, driving them towards a robust equilibrium
076   of consensus. Instead of absolute reliability, we aim to identify false negative and false positive
077   samples that exhibit fluctuating or unstable behavior under calibrated confidence and disambiguity
078   metrics during the decoding stage, using these elusive cases as focal points for a more precise and
079

080
          1. Valid Output from Generator vs.Verifier. When both the generator and verifier agree on
081          correct results and are reliable to both humans and the LLM.
082
083
          2. Specious Output from Generator vs.Verifier. When the generator and verifier agree on re-
             sults that seem correct but are actually wrong. In these cases, the verifier may recognize the
084
             result, even though a human might not.
085
086   This is analogous to expecting reliable outputs when humans can consistently discern correctness
087   and are not misled by similar-looking but opposite intents. To address the problem, we propose a
088   signaling game setup between a generator and a verifier to resolve the current issues in LLM de-
089   coding. This is motivated by concepts from Equilibrium Consensus Games (ECG) (Jacob et al.,
090   2024) and Prover-Verifier Games (PVG) (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024). Conventional signaling
091   game settings have been successfully deployed for Poker (Brown & Sandholm, 2018; 2019), Strat-
092   ego (Perolat et al., 2022), Diplomacy (, FAIR; Bakhtin et al., 2022; Jacob et al., 2022), and LLM
093
      tasks (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023).
094   Variable correctness and ambiguous reliability requires us to formulate this as a multi-step Bayesian
095   Decoding Game with complex action spaces as shown in Fig. 2. Firstly, the generator’s design is
096   randomly sampled from {Correct, Incorrect} outputs to match latent alignment and consistency,
097   Then, the verifier judges the type of decoding from generators {Valid, Specious} based on a con-
098   vex combination of correctness and reliability. Both the generator and verifier iteratively refine
099
      their policies using improved no-regret optimization until they reach an equilibrium of correctness
      judgement, followed by solving a constrained optimization problem.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES : S IGNALING G AME FOR G ENERATIVE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
132
133   The essence of a signaling game (Gibbons et al., 1992) is that one player (the generator) takes an
134   action, the signal, to convey information to another player (the verifier); in the simplest setup, the
135   final payoff depends on whether the verifier correctly judges the generator’s type based on the gen-
136   erator’s signal. Following this intuition, (Jacob et al., 2024) design a Equilibrium Consensus Game
137   (ECG), without a formal definition of the game. Thus, we provide a comprehensive game-theoretic
138   formulation for generative model decoding, and propose improvements to address limitations.
139   Formally, the signaling game’s components can be defined as: (1) Players: Generator and Verifier;
140   (2) Choice sets: Generator’s choice set is y ∈ CG = Y, with prompt p randomly drawn from
141   {Correct, Incorrect}, and the Verifier’s choice set is v ∈ CV = {Correct, Incorrect}, based on the
142   generator’s choice y ∈ Y; (3) Payoff Function: uG = uV = 1p=v (p, v), where 1 equals 1 if the
143   correctness prompt x matches the verification result, and 0 otherwise. This basic signaling game is
144   illustrated in Fig.2. We are now ready to state the fundamental concept of this signaling game, a
145   Perfect Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (PBNE) (Cho & Kreps, 1987). We use the short form Perfect
146
      Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE). The auxiliary definitions for PBE Defi. 1. are in Appx. C.
147     Definition 1. (Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (Fudenberg, 1991)) A Perfect Bayesian Nash
148     Equilibrium (PBE) is a pair (s, b) of strategy profile and a set of beliefs such that (1) s is
149     sequentially rational given beliefs b, and (2) b is consistent with s.
150
151     Example. For generative model decoding, the generator’s belief is given by its perceived prob-
152     ability distribution, P({correct, incorrect}) = (pi , 1 − pi ), for each yi ∈ Y of the verifier’s
153
        judgment, and with its belief and type, the generator chooses a mixed action that maximizes its
        utility, i.e., if the generator’s type is correct, then its optimal mixed strategy would be allocating
154
        positive possibility only on yi such that pi > 1 − pi and zero possibility to other yi .
155
156   Collusion Avoidance with SE (Sequential Equilibrium). A player in the game is said to be se-
157   quentially rational if and only if, at each information set (the nodes of Generator and Verifier in
158   Fig. 2) they are to move, they maximize their expected utility given their beliefs in the information
159   set. Given any strategy profile s and any information set I on the path of play of s, a player’s beliefs
160   at I are said to be consistent with s if and only if the beliefs are derived using the Bayes’ rule and s.
161   Collusion occurs when the reached equilibrium is uncorrelated with the generator’s type. Thus, we
      further introduce a Separating Equilibrium (SE) to our game to avoid collusion.

473   4     D ISCUSSION
474   Game Design over ECG and PVG. BDG and ECG share the common goal of aligning gener-
475   ative models with human intentions to improve output reliability, yet they differ significantly in
476   their game design, achieving substantial gains with reduced computational overhead. While ECG
477   utilizes moving-average updates to foster consensus, often leading to unstable and fluctuating equi-
478   libria, BDG employs a structured Bayesian framework that drives interactions toward an optimal
479   equilibrium with greater stability. In contrast, Prover-Verifier Games (PVGs), which contribute to
480   ChatGPT4-o1, use a RL-based alignment and focus on adversarial training phases featured by RL
481   and competitive dynamics. This requires intensive training and causes potential deviations from
482
      cooperative strategies. Appx. E and F explore the distinct phases and transitions between these
      frameworks, highlighting BDG’s scalability and its departure from the training-intensive PVG.
483
484   Robustness and Integrative Potential. BDG consistently yields improved results, surpassing or
485   matching the performance of benchmark approaches across various domains and scenarios. This
      robustness is particularly novel, as it demonstrates that BDG is adept at handling diverse scenarios,


                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      even in situations when the initial LLMs are not effective. BDG can also be combined with delib-
487   eration methods like self-consistency or prompting methods like CoT. BDG demonstrates computa-
488   tional efficiency by fast equilibrium convergence, and reliable guidance by human evaluation.
489
490
      Balancing Correctness and Reliability. Reliability (Rastogi et al., 2023) tries to give an account
      of the prover model’s failure modes and sense-making, whether the reasoning is correct or not.
491
      The resulting decoding can be arbitrarily complex (Nanda et al., 2023). In contrast, correctness
492
      allows to verify if a given solution is correct, ignoring how the generator reasoned it to be reliable
493   (consistent with the environment). Consequently, reliability requires model outputs that are coherent
494   and consistent to human understanding (Mökander et al., 2023). We show that it is possible to have
495   both, without sacrificing correctness for reliability (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024), and especially in
496   high-stakes settings reliability is as important as correctness (Casper et al., 2024).
497
      Limitation. One potential limitation arises from the explicit specification of correctness consistency
498
      branches during the game process, as this alignment is primarily intended to match human intent
499   with model outputs, similar to game-based approaches (Jacob et al., 2024; Hendrik Kirchner et al.,
500   2024). While this structure ensures consistency, it may introduce a subtle bias by prioritizing accu-
501   racy. However, in our second stage, this bias can be mitigated through further calibration and other
502   orthogonal strategies, offering more refined guidance to human evaluators. Adding multi-metrics
503   and multiple agents to achieve game-based deliberation will be a future development.
504
505
      5   R ELATED W ORK
506
507
      Multi-Agent Debate Frameworks. Previous work has explored mechanisms where multiple lan-
508   guage model instances “debate” to refine and converge to a final answer (Du et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
509   2023; Khan et al., 2024). It is possible to see our method as a major variant of this multi-agent de-
510   bate in which the interaction occurs within a game-theoretic framework, rather than directly within
511   the language models’ outputs. This structured signaling game enables BDG to enhance the correct-
512   ness and reliability of outputs without relying on human feedback, by dynamically optimizing the
513   generation and verification processes. Additionally, this approach can resolve ambiguity, confusion,
514   and low accuracy caused by inconsistencies, but not by poor reasoning.
515   Decoding Strategies. Top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018), nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019),
516   and typical sampling (Meister et al., 2023) focus on generating high-confidence text but do not ad-
517   dress the correctness of the outputs. Candidates were generated using these methods. Equilibrium-
518   ranking (Jacob et al., 2024) applies an average-moving strategy to the initial distribution. In contrast,
519   BDG integrates a multistage signaling game that inherently balances correctness and consistency
520   during the generation process. BDG can be seamlessly combined with these strategies to enhance
521   the reliability and reliability of generated text.
522   Ranking Techniques. Rranking is a widely used approach to select the correct output from a set of
523   candidates generated by language models. (Thoppilan et al., 2022) use additional human annotations
524   to train a ranking model for response filtering. (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024) trains different provers
525   and verifiers for increasing output legibility. Although our work also utilizes existing language
526   models as discriminators, BDG eliminates the need for additional training and does not impose
527   specific assumptions on the structure of either the generator or discriminator.
528
529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   The Bayesian Decoding Game (BDG) is a novel game-theoretic framework that enhances both the
532   consistency and reliability of LLMs. By framing the decoding process as a multistage signaling
533   game between a generator and verifier, BDG efficiently aligns model outputs with human intent
534   while mitigating the trade-off between correctness and reliability. Our approach achieves superior
535   performance across benchmarks, often surpassing larger models, and demonstrates its adaptability
536   when combined with existing techniques like chain-of-thought prompting. BDG ensures reliable
537
      and robust LLM outputs, offering a scalable, training-free solution to the challenges of ambiguity
      and inconsistency in generative models.

767   B    P OTENTIAL E THICS R ISKS AND S OCIETAL I MPACT
768
769   Bayesian Decoding Game (BDG) is a novel game-theoretic framework that significantly enhances
770   both the consistency and reliability of large language model outputs. By framing the decoding
771   process as a multistage signaling game between a generator and verifier, BDG efficiently aligns
772   model outputs with human intent while mitigating the trade-off between correctness and reliability.
773   BDG ensures reliable and robust LLM outputs, offering a scalable, training-free solution to the
774   challenges of ambiguity and inconsistency in generative models.
775   With the improvement of generation quality, one can imagine more potent disinformation (e.g.,
776   automatic generation of fake news) that may be hard to distinguish from human-authored content. It
777   might be worthwhile to augment current decoding techniques so that the generated outputs will also
778   be watermarked without compromising their quality.

780   C    G AME - THEORETIC F ORMULATION S UPPLEMENTARY
781
782   A generative language model (LM) maps input x to output y according to some distribution PLM (y |
783   x). Here, we do not impose restrictions on the form of input or output, as illustrated in Fig.1.
784   Instead, we address a multi-faceted problem involving a question x and a set of answer candidates
785   Y, generated by pre-trained language models on specific tasks. In the first stage, using this candidate
786   set, we leverage generative LMs in two distinct ways:
787
      Generatively, by supplying as input
788
789    1. a prompt x,
790    2. the set of candidates Y, and
791
       3. a natural language prompt indicating that a correct or incorrect answer is desired. The LM
792
          may be thought of as modeling a distribution PLM (y | x, incorrect), where the token incorrect
793
          denotes the fact that the model was prompted to generate an incorrect answer.
794
795   Verifiably, by supplying as input
796
797    1. the same x and
798    2. a possible candidate answer y ∈ Y, together with
799    3. a prompt indicating that a correctness assessment v ∈ {correct, incorrect} is sought. In this
800       case, the language model acts as a models a distribution PLM (v | x, y) where v ∈ {correct,
801       incorrect}.
802
803   The essence of a signaling game (Gibbons et al., 1992) is that one player (the generator) takes an
804   action, the signal, to convey information to another player (the verifier); in the simplest setup, the
805   final payoff depends on whether the verifier correctly judges the generator’s type based on the gen-
806
      erator’s signal. Based on this intuition from game theory, (Jacob et al., 2024) design a Equilibrium
      Consensus Game (ECG), without a formal definition of the game. Thus, we firstly provide a com-
807
      prehensive game-theoretic formulation for generative model decoding, and propose improvements
808
      to address limitations.
809
      Formally, the signaling game’s components can be defined as follows:


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
        1. Players: Generator and Verifier;
811
812     2. Choice sets: Generator’s choice set is y ∈ CG = Y, with prompt p randomly drawn from
813
           {Correct, Incorrect}, and the Verifier’s choice set is v ∈ CV = {Correct, Incorrect}, based on
           the generator’s choice y ∈ Y;
814
815     3. Payoff Function: uG = uV = 1p=v (p, v), where 1 equals 1 if the correctness prompt x
816        matches the verification result, and 0 otherwise.

      We are now ready to state the fundamental concept of this signaling game, a Perfect Bayesian Nash
818
      Equilibrium (PBNE) (Cho & Kreps, 1987). We use the short form Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium
819   (PBE) with the auxiliary definitions Defi. 6. and 7. for PBE Definition.
820
821       Definition 1. (Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (Fudenberg, 1991)) A Perfect Bayesian Nash Equi-
822       librium (PBE) is a pair (s, b) of strategy profile and a set of beliefs such that
823
            1. s is sequentially rational given beliefs b, and
824
            2. b is consistent with s.
825
826     Example 1. For generative model decoding, the generator’s belief is given by its perceived
827     probability distribution, P({correct, incorrect}) = (pi , 1 − pi ), for each yi ∈ Y of the verifier’s
828     judgment, and with its belief and type, the generator chooses a mixed action that maximizes its
829     utility, i.e., if the generator’s type is correct, then its optimal mixed strategy would be allocating
830     positive possibility only on yi such that pi > 1 − pi and zero possibility to other yi .
831
832   Definition 6. (Sequential Rationality)
833    A player is said to be sequentially rational iff, at each information set he is to move, he maximizes
834   his expected utility given his beliefs at the information set (and given that he is at the information
835   set) - even if this information set is precluded by his own strategy.
836
837   Definition 7. (Consistency on Path)
838    Given any (possibly mixed) strategy profile s, an information set is said to be on the path of play
839
      if and only if the information set is reached with positive probability according to s. Given any
      strategy profile s and any information set I on the path of play of s, the beliefs of a player at I are
840
      said to be consistent with s if and only if his beliefs are derived using the Bayes rule and s.

916   Prover-Verifier Game (PVG) (Hendrik Kirchner et al., 2024), structured as zero-sum games, en-
917   counter substantial challenges that undermine their efficacy in ensuring reliable outputs. The ad-
      versarial nature of zero-sum games inherently prioritizes winning over mutual consistency, which


                                                           17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      leads to strategic behavior focused on exploiting the opposing agent rather than achieving genuine
919   correctness e.g., model collapse. This often results in provers generating outputs that are optimized
920   to mislead the verifier rather than to align with factual truth, thus producing equilibria that favor
921   strategic manipulation over accurate assessment. Such dynamics complicate the training process,
922   requiring extensive tuning and computational resources without guaranteeing robust, interpretable
923   results. Furthermore, the reliance on reinforcement learning in these systems falls short of effec-
924   tively replacing human feedback, as the trained verifier cannot fully replicate the nuanced judgment
925   required to evaluate complex or ambiguous output. These limitations fall into the misalignment be-
926   tween training objectives and practical needs, where models become adept at adversarial optimiza-
927
      tion but lack the reliability and consistency necessary for real-world application. However, Bayesian
      Decoding Game (BDG) can bring the advantages of the game and bridge the purposes with proper
928
      implementations, which bypass the extensive training and adversarial pitfalls by directly modeling
929
      output verification through probabilistic reasoning, enhancing the interpretability and reliability of
930   the generated content without the dependencies on zero-sum competition.

      a brief introduction of PVG.

939
      correctness, whereas the sneaky prover is told to trick the verifier by providing wrong answers that

940

941
