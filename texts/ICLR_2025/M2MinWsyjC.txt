009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present an operator-theoretic framework for time-series forecasting that involves
012            learning a continuous time-shift operator associated with temporal and spatio-
013            temporal problems. The time-shift operator learning paradigm offers a continuous
014            relaxation of the discrete lag factor used in traditional autoregressive models
015            enabling the history of a function up to a given time to be mapped to its future
016            values. To parametrize the operator learning problem, we propose Khatri-Rao
017            neural operators – a new architecture for defining non-stationary integral transforms
018
               which achieves almost linear cost on spatial and spatio-temporal problems. From
               a practical perspective, the advancements made in this work allow us to handle
019
               irregularly sampled observations and forecast at super-resolution in both space
020
               and time. Detailed numerical studies across a wide range of temporal and spatio-
021            temporal benchmark problems suggest that the proposed approach is highly scalable
022            and compares favourably with state-of-the-art methods.

057   In the present work, we propose casting time-series forecasting problems as a supervised learning
058
      problem of the continuous time-shift operator. In contrast to standard autoregressive models based
      on discrete-time (or discrete space-time) representation of the dynamics, the continuous time-shift
059
      operator maps the entire, continuous history of the dynamics over a past time-window into its
060
      future values over a subsequent time-window. Our operator-theoretic approach can be viewed as
061   a continuous relaxation of the discrete lag factor in autoregressive models. This provides several
062   practical advantages such as the ability to learn directly from irregularly sampled observations and
063   to forecast at super-resolution in both space and time while retaining the stability of training neural
064   operators; see Figure 1.
065
      In order to deal with the complexities of learning the time-shift operator for temporal and spatio-
066
      temporal dynamical systems, we propose Khatri-Rao neural operators (KRNOs). KRNOs are a
067   new architecture for operator learning based on non-stationary integral transforms which provides
068   exceptional model flexibility compared to methods based on stationary kernels (Li et al., 2020c),
069   while achieving almost linear scaling. In Section 3 we demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed
070   approach on a suite of challenging test cases including the Darts datasets (Herzen et al., 2022),
071   the M4 datasets (Makridakis et al., 2020), shallow water simulation (Kissas et al., 2022), and a
072   climate modeling problem (Kissas et al., 2022). In total, we consider 27 different test cases and
073   compare performance against 22 modern approaches for temporal and spatio-temporal forecasting to
074   demonstrate the strong generalization capabilities of the proposed approach.

106   Koopman operator (Koopman, 1931a) governing the evolution of observables. However, to the best of
107   our knowledge, the idea of developing a operator-theoretic framework to directly learn the continuous

147   2.2   K HATRI -R AO NEURAL OPERATORS (KRNO S )
148
149   We now introduce KRNOs, a new operator learning architecture based on non-stationary integral
150   transforms, to approximate the time-shift operator of temporal and spatio-temporal dynamical systems.
151   KRNOs offer expressive parametrization of operators using non-stationary integral transform layers
152   which (i) do not require any approximation of the kernel and (ii) scale almost linearly in the number
153   of quadrature nodes. As far as we are aware, ours is the only approach for parametrizing neural
154
      operators which combines these advantages. We will show later that KRNOs provide state-of-the-art
      performance across a number of benchmarks while inheriting the benefits of neural operators such as
155
      being discretization independent and enabling super-resolution in forecasts (Li et al., 2020c).
156

503   4     R ELATED WORK
504
505
506   The nonlinear time-shift operator considered here is distinct from the Koopman operator (Koopman,
507
      1931b), an infinite-dimensional linear operator defined over a space of observables that has been
      extensively studied (Wang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). The time-shift operator corresponding to
508
      a set of sufficiently smooth observables can be viewed as a continuous extension of the Koopman
509
      operator (Zhen et al., 2021). This theoretical connection deserves further study.
510
511   Similar to neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018), the present approach poses time-series forecasting in a
512   continuous setting. However, the presented approach leads to a computationally much more efficient
513
      learning problem since no adjoint solvers are needed to calculate the loss function gradients. Also
      in contrast to neural ODEs, our approach can also deal with spatio-temporal problems in an elegant
514
      fashion – enabling super-resolution in both space and time.
515
516   As discussed earlier, Li et al. (2020a) examined the problem of learning a neural operator that maps
517   the solutions of the Navier-Stokes equations over the time interval [0, 10] to the solution over the
518   interval (10, 50] using FNO and discussed the ability of this strategy to provide super-resolution in
519
      both space and time. This test case can be considered as a special case of the time-shift operator
      formalism proposed in this work with fixed time windows. Our approach is more general since we
520
      use non-stationary kernels to parametrize the time-shift operator and treat (tp , tf ) as hyperparameters.
521
      In addition, as discussed previously the proposed KRNO model is significantly more parsimonious
522   than FNO while providing superior accuracy.
523
524   The use of transformer-based models in time-series forecasting (Chen et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
525
      2022; Wu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; 2024) and neural operators incorporating the attention
      mechanism (Kissas et al., 2022) is becoming increasingly popular. It is worth noting that transformer-
526
      based models developed for time-series forecasting assume that the observations are regularly
527
      sampled; see, for example, FedFormer (Zhou et al., 2022) used in our benchmarking studies.
528

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we showed how the time-shift operator can be learned in a supervised setting for
534   temporal and spatio-temporal problems. We proposed a novel Khatri-Rao neural operator that enables
535   a highly flexible yet parsimonious parametrization of the time-shift operator. Numerical studies on
536   a range of benchmark problems suggest that the proposed approach compares favourably against
537   state-of-the-art methods in time-series forecasting and neural operator learning. Our method achieved
538   top performance on 8/27 test cases and top-3 performance on 19/27 test cases. We hope that the
539   present work will enable further advances in operator-theoretic frameworks for time-series forecasting
      and spatio-temporal modeling.

956   spaced time-series data obtained for the two-dimensional spiral test case from Chen et al. (2018).
957

      high levels of irregularity, alongside one equispaced training trajectory. The equispaced trajectory
958

959

962   time-stamps. This randomization procedure was repeated to create 10 different training datasets.

964   adding random noise ϵt ∼ U[0, 0.156] to the time stamps of the equispaced trajectory. This process
965   was again repeated to create 10 different training datasets where the distribution of the time-stamps
966   are highly irregular making this test-case more challenging. We trained 21 KRNO models in total and
967   the performance of the models are evaluated on a test dataset containing the states at 20 uniformly
968
      spaced time stamps over the interval (15.61, 18.76] seconds.

970   trained on the two sets of irregularly spaced trajectories. For all models, the time-shift operator was
971   trained by fixing the length of the time window corresponding to the input and the output to 1.419

       G        R ESOURCE COMPARISON BETWEEN KRNO AND FNO FOR DIFFERENT
1023            SPATIAL RESOLUTIONS
1024
1025   In this section, we compare the resource usage between KRNO and FNO-3D for different spatial
       resolutions using the shallow water dataset. We utilized the default settings for KRNO as mentioned


                                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       in the shallow water problem, except for the quadrature grid. For all the spatial resolutions, we
1027   employed a quadrature grid of size 32 × 32 × 5 in the hidden KRNO integral transform layers.
1028   It is important to note that for high spatial resolution data, resource usage is primarily driven by
1029   computations in the first and last integral transform layers, which contain the highest number of
1030   quadrature nodes. For the FNO-3D model, the number of Fourier modes in each spatial dimension is
1031   set to the default value of 12. Increasing this value to 64 results in a substantial increase in memory
1032   usage (10,324 MB) and training time per iteration (0.1725 seconds) for a spatial resolution of 160 ×
1033   160. This also increases the parameter count of FNO-3D from 5.5 million to 157 million. In contrast,
1034   KRNO maintains a fixed parameter count of 145319, independent of the size of the quadrature grid
1035
       and the resolution of the dataset.
1036   The results for resource usage during training and inference are shown in Table 8, Table 9, and
1037   Figure 9. It can be noted that even though we use a high-resolution quadrature grid for the first and
1038   the last layers in this numerical study, the time complexity of KRNO is comparable to that of FNO-3D
1039   when the number of Fourier modes are set to 12. As discussed earlier, the memory requirements
1040   and time complexity of FNO-3D will increase dramatically with increase in the number of Fourier
1041
       modes. We observe that the memory usage of KRNO is significantly higher during training when
       compared to inference. We believe that this can be reduced by further optimizing the implementation
1042
       of Khatri-Rao matrix-vector products.
1043
1044   We would like to highlight that our current implementation uses a mid-point quadrature scheme
1045   for the temporal dimension and a trapezoidal quadrature scheme for spatial dimensions to evaluate
1046   the integral transform layers. Additionally, in our current implementation, the quadrature nodes are
1047
       defined over the spatial mesh of the input function. While this approach is reasonable for the problems
       we are considering, it is not the most suitable (and tends to be overly conservative) for high-resolution
1048
       spatio-temporal datasets. For such datasets, the memory requirements can be significantly reduced by
1049
       using quadrature nodes that are defined over a lower-resolution spatial grid which is independent of
1050   the input’s spatial resolution.
1051
1052   Alternatively, we could design a quadrature scheme that uses a low-resolution subsampling of the
1053
       input as nodes and generates quadrature weights on-the-fly to meet a specified target precision. This
       would not only enhance accuracy and efficiency for high-resolution spatio-temporal datasets but also
1054
       improve the performance of KRNO for irregularly spaced observations. We plan to explore this in
1055
       future work.

1101
1102   This section provides additional details on the experimental setup used to generate the results presented
1103   in the main text. For all the numerical studies, the KRNO network architecture had 128 channels in
1104   both the lifting and projection layers and three kernel integral layers. In each test case, we used a
1105   same input and output quadrature grids in each kernel integral layer. For problems involving high
1106   spatial or temporal resolutions, adopting lower-resolution quadrature grids within the internal kernel
1107
       integral layers is recommended as an effective strategy to reduce computational costs. As mentioned
       in the section E, the kernel function used in each integral transform layer is parameterized by a
1108
       neural network containing three hidden layers. Additional hyperparameters used in hyperparameter
1109
       tuning for Darts, M4, Cryto, and Player Trajectory datasets are summarized in Table 11 and Table 13,
1110   respectively. AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017) optimizer is used for training all the models.
1111   All the computations were carried out on a single Nvidia RTX 4090 with 24GB memory.
1112
1113
       In all experiments, we treat the tp and tf as fixed hyperparameters. We would like to mention here
       that further work is needed to explore the possibility of training a single model on a dataset containing
1114
       input/output trajectories for different settings of tp and tf . This would enable the possibility of
1115
       learning a model that can predict the dynamics at different lengthscales.
