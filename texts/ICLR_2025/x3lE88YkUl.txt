009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Noisy labels pose a substantial challenge in machine learning, often resulting
012            in overfitting and poor generalization. Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM),
013            as demonstrated by Foret et al. (2021), improves generalization over traditional
014            Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) in classification tasks with noisy labels by
015            implicitly slowing noisy learning. While SAM’s ability to generalize in noisy
016            environments has been studied in several simplified settings, its full potential
017            in more realistic training settings remains underexplored. In this work, we an-
018
               alyze SAM’s behavior at each iteration, identifying specific components of the
               gradient vector that contribute significantly to its robustness against noisy labels.
019
               Based on these insights, we propose SANER (Sharpness-Aware Noise-Explicit
020
               Reweighting), an effective variant that enhances SAM’s ability to manage noisy
021            fitting rate. Our experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Mini-WebVision
022            demonstrate that SANER consistently outperforms SAM, achieving up to an 8%
023            increase on CIFAR-100 with 50% label noise.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The issue of noisy labels due to human error annotation has been commonly observed in many large-
029   scale datasets such as CIFAR-10N, CIFAR-100N (Wei et al., 2022), Clothing1M (Xiao et al., 2015),
030   and WebVision (Li et al., 2017). Over-parameterized deep neural networks, which have enough
031   capacity to memorize entire large datasets, can easily overfit such noisy label data, leading to poor
032   generalization performance (Zhang et al., 2021). Moreover, the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle &
033
      Carbin, 2019) indicates that only a subset of the network’s parameters is crucial for generalization.
      This highlights the importance of noise-robust learning, where the goal is to train a robust classifier
034
      despite the presence of inaccurate or noisy labels in the training dataset.
035
036   Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), introduced by Foret et al. (2021), is an optimizer designed
037   to find better generalization by searching for flat minima. It has shown superior performance over
038   SGD in various tasks, especially in classification tasks involving noisy labels Baek et al. (2024).
039
      Understanding the mechanisms behind the success of SAM is crucial for further improvements in
      handling label noise. Chen et al. (2024) explain SAM’s generalization within the benign overfitting
040
      framework, showing that SAM outperforms SGD by mitigating noise learning in the early training
041
      stages and facilitating more effective learning of features. In linear models, Baek et al. (2024) show
042   that SAM more effectively resists fitting noisy examples than SGD through an explicit up-weighting
043   mechanism that preserves strong gradient contributions from clean examples, thus slowing the learn-
044   ing of noisy instances.
045
      Although SAM effectively mitigates the impact of noise on learning compared to SGD, it still over-
046
      fits to noisy labels in the later stages of training, as evidenced in Andriushchenko & Flammarion
047
      (2022); Baek et al. (2024). We reconfirmed this phenomenon by evaluating the noisy accuracy
048   metric, which measures how well the model overfits to noisy examples in each epoch as shown in
049   Figure 1(a). Baek et al. (2024) analyze SAM’s explicit up-weighting mechanism under a sample-
050   wise gradient view. However, in neural network settings, Baek et al. (2024) also admitted that this
051   mechanism fails to fully explain SAM’s generalization in tasks with label noise. Motivated by these
052   observations, we aim to investigate why SAM slows down noise learning compared to SGD through
053   a weighting mechanism under a component-wise gradient view in realistic training settings, and how
      to further enhance SAM’s performance in the later stages of training.

      In this work, we investigate SAM’s capacity to mitigate overfitting to noisy labels and propose an
071
      approach to enhance its robustness further, as illustrated in Figure 1. In particular, our investiga-
072   tion reveals two significant findings that motivate our proposed method: (1) During each iteration,
073   specific components in SAM gradient vector contribute significantly to its robustness against label
074   noise. (2) The ratio of these components in noise gradients larger than that in clean gradients, this
075   indicates that further reduction in these components may enhance resistance to noisy label fitting
076   without significantly harming the fitting of clean examples. Building on these insights, we propose
077   a new optimizer, SANER (Sharpness-Aware Noise-Explicit Reweighting), which explicitly controls
078   noisy fitting more effectively than SAM. This is achieved by further reducing the magnitude of the
079   components in SAM’s gradient that correspond to noisy label fitting in each iteration.
080   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
081
082   • We empirically study the behavior of SAM in component-wise gradients. Specifically, in each
083     iteration, we identify components in the SAM gradient vector that significantly contribute to its
084     resistance against fitting noisy labels. These components have lower magnitudes and the same
085     signs as the corresponding components in SGD. We further analyze their impact on slowing down
086
        noisy fitting compared to clean fitting, revealing that reducing the magnitudes of these components
        has the potential to improve resistance to noisy fitting without significantly harming clean fitting.
087
088   • Based on the above idea, we propose SANER, a variant of SAM that has superior resistance to
089     fitting noisy labels compared to SAM. The efficiency of SANER is demonstrated across vari-
090     ous datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Mini-WebVision, under different settings of
091
        noise. SANER consistently outperforms SAM, especially in three challenging overfitting scenar-
        ios: increasing model layer width, training without data augmentation, and limited dataset sizes.
092
093   • We validate the robustness and efficiency of SANER when integrated with various SAM variants,
094     including ASAM (Kwon et al., 2021), GSAM (Zhuang et al., 2022), FSAM (Li et al., 2024), and
095     VaSSO (Li & Giannakis, 2024). This demonstrates that not only SAM but also its other variants
096
        exhibit the characteristics identified in our study.

130   2.2   R ELATED W ORKS
131
132   SAM. In addition to the original SAM, several variants have been developed and have shown em-
133   pirically to improve generalization on datasets with label noise (Kwon et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022;
134   Jiang et al., 2023; Li & Giannakis, 2024; Li et al., 2024). The convergence of SAM has been studied
135
      within the Inexact Gradient Descent framework (Khanh et al., 2023; 2024b), where SAM’s perturbed
      gradient is considered as an approximation of the unperturbed gradient (Khanh et al., 2024a). Sev-
136
      eral efforts have been made to explain SAM’s generalization ability, including investigating SAM’s
137
      implicit bias (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022), examining the oscillations in SAM’s trajec-
138   tory toward flat minima (Bartlett et al., 2023), exploring how SAM regularizes the eigenvalues of
139   the Hessian of the loss (Wen et al., 2023), and analyzing SAM’s generalization through the lens of
140   the bias-variance trade-off (Behdin & Mazumder, 2023).
141
      Shin et al. (2023) examined SAM’s performance in overparameterized classification tasks with noisy
142
      labels, finding that it yields simpler, flatter solutions than SGD. Baek et al. (2024) attributed SAM’s
143
      resistance to noisy fitting to gradient up-weighting via the perturbed step mechanism in linear set-
144   tings. Our work presents a different perspective, showing that SAM also down-weights certain
145   gradient components in realistic settings in each iteration, which helps slow down noisy fitting.
146
147
      Label Noise. Many methods have been developed to enhance noise robustness in deep learning,
      including (1) Designing loss functions that are less sensitive to noisy examples (Zhang & Sabuncu,
148
      2018; Menon et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2023); (2) Implementing a sample weighting
149
      mechanism ensures that the model prioritizes learning from clean data, reducing the impact of noisy
150   examples during training (Liu & Tao, 2015; Ren et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2018; Wei et al., 2020); (3)
151   Utilizing regularization techniques to improve generalization in the presence of label noise (Lukasik
152   et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022); and (4) Adopting training strategies
153   based on semi-supervised learning (Nguyen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020), meta-learning (Ren et al.,
154   2018; Shu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020), or self-supervised learning (Li et al., 2022).
155
      Our gradient-based approach focuses on explicitly identifying and reducing components of the gra-
156   dient vector that contribute more to learning from noisy examples in each iteration. A closely related
157   study is the CDR method by Xia et al. (2021), which isolates noisy (non-critical) and clean (critical)
158   parameters in each iteration based on the magnitude of the product of their gradients and corre-
159   sponding weights to prevent memorization of the noisy labels. The number of critical parameters is
160   determined by estimating the noise rate in the training data. Unlike their approach, our method does
161   not rely on estimating the noise rate but utilizes SAM’s behavior in each iteration to further enhance
      the slowing down of noisy fitting.

358   ResNet18 and CIFAR-10, employing the hyperparameter settings detailed in Appendix A.1. The re-
359
      sults, shown in Figure 5, demonstrate that α is directly proportional to the noisy fitting rate: higher
      values of α (e.g., 2, 1.5, 1.25, 1.1) accelerate noisy fitting compared to SAM (α = 1). Conversely,
360
      using lower values of α reduces the contribution of noise-dominated components, allowing SANER
361
      to better resist noisy fitting and achieve improved generalization performance.
362
363
      Stabilizing clean fitting via a scheduler for α. As shown in Figure 4, the value of pr is relatively
364
      low during the early phase of training. This implies that employing a low α value during this phase
365
      could impede the learning of clean examples. This phenomenon arises from the model’s tendency to
366   prioritize learning clean samples over noisy ones during the early iterations of training, as observed
367   in previous works (Liu et al., 2020; 2023). As a result, during these early iterations, clean-dominated
368   components make up a significant portion of the overall model, including Group B.
369
      To address this issue and ensure robust learning of clean examples, we propose a simple yet effective
370
      solution: a linear scheduler that gradually decreases α from 1 to a predetermined value over k
371
      epochs. This strategy stabilizes the fitting of clean examples in the early training stages, resulting
372   in more consistent performance compared to training without a scheduler. As shown in Figure 5(c),
373   experiments on ResNet34 with CIFAR-100 under 50% label noise demonstrate that SANER without
374   the α scheduler (k = 0) significantly harms clean accuracy, reducing it by approximately 15%.
375   Increasing k improves clean accuracy in the initial phase, and the clean fitting rate remains stable
376   towards the end of training across various value of k, indicating that the performance is not overly
377   sensitive to k. This approach is particularly important in scenarios with high proportions of noisy
      samples or slow clean fitting rate, as shown in Tables 7 and 8 in Appendix C.5.

      6                     C ONCLUSION
521
522
523   In this work, we examine the effectiveness of the SAM optimizer in addressing label noise, noting
524   its advantages over SGD. However, SAM tends to overfit to noisy labels in later training stages. Our
525
      analysis identifies specific down-weighted gradient magnitude components in the SAM’s gradient
      vector that enhance its resistance to label noise. We introduce the concepts of noise-dominated
526
      and clean-dominated components, and analyze the impact of Group B on both noisy and clean
527
      fitting. Our findings suggest that reducing the magnitude of these down-weighted components can
528   further improve resistance to noisy labels. To this end, we propose SANER, a method designed
529   to reduce these components, resulting in enhanced model robustness. SANER outperforms SAM
530   across different architectures, datasets, and noise scenarios, and demonstrates superior performance
531   compared to other SAM variants when combined with them.
532
      Our work utilizes SAM’s perturbed gradient and analyzes it through component-wise gradients to
533
      understand their properties in the context of noisy label tasks. However, our design of Group B does
534   not fully separate noise-dominated components from clean-dominated ones, limiting our ability to
535   specifically target noisy label challenges. This overlap may inadvertently affect the clean fitting rate,
536   a drawback that is only partially mitigated by the scheduler we introduced. Additionally, we were
537   limited by the computational resources required to train on larger datasets, such as ImageNet, which
538   could further validate the scalability of our approach. Future research could explore better isola-
539   tion of noise-dominated components, advanced gradient techniques, or new ways of decomposing
      SAM’s components to enhance label-noise robustness while preserving clean fitting performance.

      A.1   T RAINING D ETAILS
705
706   We train all neural networks from scratch using simple data augmentation techniques, including
707   RandomHorizontalFlip(.) and RandomCrop(.). Specifically, we train the network for
708   200 epochs using SGD with a momentum of 0.9, a weight decay of 0.0005, and a batch size of 128.
709   The initial learning rate is set to 0.1, and it is reduced by a factor of 10 after 100 and 150 epochs,
710   as suggested by Andriushchenko & Flammarion (2022); Shin et al. (2023). For the hyperparameter
711   perturbation radius of SAM, we use ρ = 0.1 in all experiments, following the setting in Foret et al.
712
      (2021). For our SANER method, we experiment with different values for the hyperparameter α
      in the set {0.9, 0.75, 0.5, 0.25, 0.1} and found that α = 0.5 is stable in most cases, making it our
713
      recommended default value. As discussed in Section 5.1, we linearly reduced α from 1 to 0.5 over
714
      the first quarter of the total epochs and maintained α at 0.5 for the remaining epochs. In each
715   experiment, we train a neural network on the training dataset and report the best test accuracy on the
716   test dataset during each epoch. We repeat the experiments three times with different random seeds
717   and report the mean and empirical standard deviation of these best results.
718
719   A.2   T YPES OF N OISE
720
721   In this work, we follows the experimental setup used in previous works for noisy label scenarios. In
722   particular, we used four types of noise as follows:
723
724         1. Symmetric noise: Each label is flipped to any other class with equal probability. In our
725
               experiments, we uniformly flip labels to other classes with probabilities of 25% and 50%.
726         2. Asymmetric noise: Labels are flipped to similar, but not identical classes (Zhang &
727            Sabuncu, 2018). For CIFAR-10, we generate asymmetric noisy labels by mapping spe-
728            cific classes to their most similar counterparts: TRUCK to AUTOMOBILE, BIRD to AIR-
729            PLANE, DEER to HORSE, CAT to DOG, and leaving other labels unchanged, with prob-
730
               abilities of 25% or 50%. For CIFAR-100, each class is shifted circularly to the next class
               with probabilities of 25% or 50%.
731
732         3. Instance-dependent noise: The mislabeling probability of each instance depends on its
733            input features. In our experiments, we use instance-dependent noise from PDN (Xia et al.,
734
               2020) with noisy rates of 25% or 50%, where the noise is synthesized based on DNN
               prediction errors.
735
736         4. Real-world noise: Labels are taken from the mislabeling of real-world human annotations.
737            For CIFAR datasets, we use the ”Worst” label set from CIFAR-10N and the ”Fine” label
738            set from CIFAR-100N (Wei et al., 2022).
