                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal Emotion Recognition (MER) is an important research topic. This pa-
012            per advocates for a transformative paradigm in MER. The rationale behind our
013            work is that current approaches often rely on a limited set of basic emotion labels,
014            which do not adequately represent the rich spectrum of human emotions. These
015            traditional and overly simplistic emotion categories fail to capture the inherent
016            complexity and subtlety of human emotional experiences, leading to limited gen-
017            eralizability and practicality. Therefore, we propose a new MER paradigm called
018            Open-vocabulary MER (OV-MER), which encompasses a broader range of emo-
019            tion labels to reflect the richness of human emotions. This paradigm relaxes the
020
               label space, allowing for the prediction of arbitrary numbers and categories of
               emotions. To support this transition, we provide a comprehensive solution that
021
               includes a newly constructed database based on LLM and human collaborative
022
               annotations, along with corresponding metrics and a series of benchmarks. We
023            hope this work advances emotion recognition from basic emotions to more nu-
024            anced emotions, contributing to the development of emotional AI.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Research on emotions has a history spanning two centuries. As early as the 19th century, Charles
030   Darwin conducted pioneering research about the evolutionary origins and possible purposes of
031   emotions, explaining the emotional expressions of humans and animals (Darwin, 1872). In 1884,
032   William James revealed the process of emotion generation, noting that stimuli trigger activities in
033
      the autonomic nervous system, which in turn produces an emotional experience in the brain (James,
      1884). With the rapid development of AI, emotions have garnered increasing attention. Minsky
034
      (1988) highlighted the importance of emotions: The question is not whether intelligent machines
035
      can have any emotions, but whether machines can be intelligent without any emotions.
036
037   Humans convey emotions through various modalities, which gives rise to the task of Multimodal
038   Emotion Recognition (MER) (Lian et al., 2024b). The basis of MER is how to model emotions.
039
      Currently, emotion models can be broadly classified into two categories: dimensional models and
      discrete models. The former uses multi-dimensional space to describe emotions, with the arousal-
040
      valence dimension being widely adopted; valence describes the pleasantness of a stimulus, while
041
      arousal indicates the intensity of emotion provoked by a stimulus (Warriner et al., 2013). However,
042   these definitions require specialized psychological knowledge, making them abstract and difficult
043   for the ordinary person to understand. This can lead to discrepancies between different annotators,
044   posing challenges for subsequent applications.
045
      Discrete models align more closely with human understanding of emotions. Ekman (1992) proposed
046
      the basic emotion theory, suggesting that there are six basic emotions: anger, disgust, fear, happi-
047
      ness, sadness, and surprise. This theory is widely used in MER, where researchers typically limit
048   the label space to these basic emotions and use multiple annotators to select the most likely label
049   through majority voting. We refer to this task as One-hot MER (OH-MER). Considering that emo-
050   tions can be compound, researchers further propose Multi-label MER (ML-MER), allowing each
051   sample to have multiple labels (Li et al., 2017). However, both OH-MER and ML-MER generally
052   have limited label spaces and numbers. Nevertheless, Plutchik (2001) pointed out that humans can
053   express approximately 34,000 different emotions. Therefore, restricting the label space and number
      will overlook some nuanced emotions.

076   In this paper, we extend traditional MER to Open-vocabulary MER (OV-MER), where we allow the
077   prediction of emotions across any number and categories. Figure 1(a) provides comparisons between
078   different tasks. To facilitate further research, we build an initial dataset, define evaluation metrics,
079   and develop solutions: (1) For the dataset, we propose a human-LLM collaboration strategy. Com-
080
      pared to human-only annotation, our strategy can leverage LLM to enhance the label richness (see
      Section 2); (2) For the metrics, since there is no fixed label space, the model may predict closely
081
      related but differently expressed emotions. For example, the ground truth is joyful and the model
082
      predicts happy. To provide more reliable evaluation results, we first group similar emotions and
083   specifically design metrics for this task (see Section 3); (3) For the solutions, traditional classifiers
084   rely on fixed label spaces. However, OV-MER does not restrict the label space or number of labels,
085   necessitating the definition of new solutions (see Section 4).
086
      A natural question arises: why is open vocabulary so important for MER? The reason is that OV
087
      can generate more nuanced emotions, leading to more accurate and reliable MER. As illustrated
088
      in Figure 1(b), labeling an emotion solely as surprise is not sufficiently informative—we cannot
089   ascertain whether the surprise is positive or negative, which limits its practicality, such as in human-
090   computer interaction (HCI) applications. In contrast, our OV-MER provides emotions like surprise
091   along with additional descriptors such as nervous and dissatisfied, offering a more comprehensive
092   and insightful understanding of the emotional state. Therefore, OV-MER facilitates the transition
093   from basic to nuanced emotion recognition, advancing the development of emotion AI. Appendix A
094   provides more detailed motivation. In summary, we make the following key contributions:
095
096               • Paradigm. We propose a new paradigm in MER called OV-MER. This paradigm transi-
097                 tions from traditional MER to a framework that enables the prediction of any number and
098                 category of emotions, thereby advancing emotion AI toward real-world applicability by
099
                    capturing the full spectrum of human emotions.
100
                  • Groundwork. We lay the groundwork for OV-MER by constructing datasets, defining
101
                    evaluation metrics, and proposing effective solutions. Our dataset enhances label richness
102                 through human-LLM collaboration. Meanwhile, we introduce new evaluation metrics that
103                 leverage emotional relevance to achieve more reliable results.
104
105               • Benchmark. We establish zero-shot benchmarks for OV-MER by conducting extensive
106                 experiments and providing detailed analysis. This task can serve as an important eval-
107                 uation benchmark for multimodal LLMs (MLLMs), challenging their ability to integrate
                    multimodal clues and capture subtle temporal variations in emotional expression.

      2     T HE OV-MERD DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
137
138
139
      Although the concept of OV-MER is intuitive and holds great promise, its practical implementation
      faces significant challenges. The main difficulty lies in the broad and subtle range of human emo-
140
      tions, making comprehensive labeling a complex task. Traditional annotation methods are limited
141
      by their predefined emotion categories, which are often insufficient for the needs of OV-MER. In
142   Figure 2, we propose a human-LLM collaboration strategy that consists of two steps: CLUE-Multi
143   generation and emotion label extraction. Ultimately, we create a dataset called OV-MERD, which
144   offers a richer set of emotions compared to existing datasets (see Table 1). This dataset is an ex-
145   tension of MER2023 (Lian et al., 2023). Specifically, MER2023 is collected from movies and TV
146   series, and we randomly select a portion of data for further annotation to build our OV-MERD.
147
148
      2.1    CLUE-M ULTI G ENERATION
149
150   During the annotation process, we observe that descriptions generated through human-LLM collab-
151   oration are more detailed. This indicates that when annotations are solely performed by humans,
152   they tend to focus on major clues while neglecting minor ones (see Section 5 for more analysis). In
153   this section, we provide a detailed overview of our human-LLM collaboration strategy.
154
155
      Pre-annotation. Initially, we attempt to annotate visual and acoustic clues directly. However, the
156   descriptions obtained in this way cannot cover all information. Therefore, we explore using other
157   models for pre-annotation. (1) For video, given the strong visual understanding capabilities of GPT-
158   4V (“gpt-4-vision-preview”), we use it as video LLM (VLLM) for pre-annotation. Since GPT-4V
159   only supports image input, we uniformly sample three frames from each video and input them into
160   GPT-4V. We discuss the reasons for sampling three frames in Appendix I. (2) For audio, we use the
161   open-source SALMONN (Tang et al., 2023) as audio LLM (ALLM) for pre-annotation, as GPT-4V
      does not support audio input.

      Manual Check. As part of our quality assurance procedures, we perform a detailed examination
175
      of the pre-annotated results. For visual clues, GPT-4V may generate hallucinated responses, i.e.,
176   clues that do not actually exist. Additionally, there are repeated expressions and some temporal
177   association clues are missing. Therefore, we hire annotators to eliminate errors and duplicates, as
178   well as add missing content. For acoustic clues, ALLM struggles to capture emotion-related par-
179   alinguistic features. The main reason is that current ALLM primarily focuses on tasks like ASR or
180   audio event detection (Tang et al., 2023), with less emphasis on paralinguistic information. Hence,
181   we hire multiple annotators to focus on the speaker’s intonation and other emotion-related paralin-
182   guistic clues. To reduce subjective bias, we conduct two rounds of manual checks, each involving
183   different annotators. These annotators are experts in affective computing and are familiar with the
184
      definitions of emotions. Ultimately, these checked clues can accurately reflect the video content.
      Appendix J provides the annotation guideline and layout of the annotation platform.
185
186
187
      CLUE-Multi Generation. Subsequently, we leverage the reasoning capabilities of LLM to merge
      all clues. Specifically, we use GPT-3.5 (“gpt-3.5-turbo-16k-0613”) as the LLM and ask it to merge
188
      textual, acoustic, and visual clues to infer emotional states. The output of this process is an emotion-
189
      related description, denoted as CLUE-Multi (see Figure 2). In Appendix E, we further discuss the
190   details of this merging process and the reasons behind it. Overall, the above annotation pipeline
191   reflects the collaboration between humans and LLMs. In Section 5, we compare human-only anno-
192   tation with this strategy, observing that the latter can generate richer descriptions.
193
194   2.2   G ROUND - TRUTH OV L ABEL E XTRACTION
195
196   Label Extraction. After that, we use the LLM to extract emotion labels from CLUE-Multi. This
197   process relies on GPT-3.5, which we request to identify emotional states based on the provided
198   descriptions without restricting the label space. See Appendix E for more details.
199
200   Language Impact. We further explore the language impact. In Figure 2, we first extract OV
201   labels from English and Chinese descriptions, obtaining YEE and YCC . Then, we translate them
202   into the other language, yielding YEC and YCE . After that, we measure the similarity between
203   different sets and report results in Figure 2. In Appendix K, we detail our experimental design
204
      and similarity metric. We observe that the labels extracted from different languages exhibit some
      differences. For example, the similarity score between YEE and YCE is 0.82, which may be due
205
      to the varying definitions of emotions in different languages. To eliminate language influence and
206
      achieve consensus labels, we merge the labels extracted from both languages and conduct manual
207   checks. These checked labels are regarded as the ground truth.

      2.3   OV-MERD DATASET

      that have been rarely discussed in previous research, such as shy, nervous, and grateful.

      3     E VALUATION M ETRIC
217
218
      Defining evaluation metrics for OV-MER presents significant challenges: (1) OV-MER supports
219   predicting emotions of any category. Thus, the model may predict closely related but differently
220   expressed emotions. To provide more reliable evaluation results, we first group the emotions based
221   on their similarities. (2) OV-MER allows for the prediction of an arbitrary number of labels.
222   Thus, traditional evaluation metrics designed for a fixed number of labels may not be applicable. In
223   this section, we propose set-based evaluation metrics specifically tailored for this task.
224
225   3.1   G ROUPING
226
227   We propose two grouping strategies: one based on GPT and the other based on the emotion wheel
228
      (EW) Plutchik (1980). In the following experiments, we default to using GPT-based grouping.
229
230
      GPT-based Grouping. The most direct approach is to use GPT-3.5 to group all labels based on
      their similarity: Please assume the role of an expert in the field of emotions. We provide a set of
231
      emotions. Please group the emotions, with each group containing emotions with the same meaning.
232
      Directly output the results. The output format should be a list containing multiple lists. However,
233   the evaluation results may be affected by the API version. For example, if OpenAI deprecates an
234   old API, the results based on that API will become difficult to reproduce. Additionally, this process
235   is costly (see Appendix P). Therefore, we attempt to find a replacement for GPT-based grouping.
236
237   EW-based Grouping. EW is a psychological model that catego-
238   rizes emotions in a structured manner. The inner part shows core
239   emotions while moving to the outer part reveals more nuanced emo-
240   tions. Therefore, EW naturally provides emotion grouping informa-
241   tion. Since there is no consensus on EW, we select five representa-
242   tive wheels W1∼W5. See Appendix R for details.

297   4.1   CLUE G ENERATION
298
299   Figure 2 illustrates the generation process of CLUE-Multi, where we combine text with manually
300   checked visual and acoustic clues. In this section, we further introduce the following variants.
301
302
      CLUE-A/T/V. To reveal the modality impact, we propose three variants of CLUE-Multi: CLUE-
      Audio, CLUE-Text, and CLUE-Video. Their generation process is illustrated in Figure 4. (1) CLUE-
303
      Audio: We observe that ALLM cannot fully leverage the text, and using an additional LLM to
304
      emphasize the text can further improve performance, which is also verified in Section 5. Therefore,
305   we merge the checked acoustic clues with text using an additional LLM; (2) CLUE-Text: We only
306   use the text to infer emotional states; (3) CLUE-Video: Since the visual content does not contain
307   audio and text, we only use the checked visual clues. See Appendix M for more examples.
308
309   CLUE-MLLM. MLLMs can address various multimodal tasks. Since emotion recognition relies
310   on temporal information, we choose models that support at least video or audio. To generate CLUE-
311   MLLM, we first use ALLM or VLLM to extract emotion-related descriptions, and then combine
312   these descriptions with text using LLM. Compared with CLUE-Multi, this process does not use
313   manually checked clues. Appendix N provides model cards and relevant prompts. This paper aims to
314
      build a zero-shot benchmark for OV-MER, without the training process. All models are implemented
      in PyTorch, and all inference processes are carried out using a 32G NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU.

517   6   L IMITATIONS
518
519   Firstly, the main contribution of this paper is the definition of a new task and the conduct of foun-
520
      dational research. In the future, we plan to design more effective frameworks to solve OV-MER.
      Specifically, we plan to incorporate more emotion-related instruction datasets to finetune MLLMs,
521
      thereby enhancing their emotion recognition ability. Meanwhile, as mentioned in our paper, how to
522
      integrate subtitle information and fuse multimodal inputs also plays a crucial role in the final perfor-
523   mance. We will also consider these aspects in the framework design. Secondly, we have evaluated
524   some representative MLLMs, but not all models are covered. In the future, we will expand the scope
525   of evaluation to cover more emerging MLLMs to enrich the benchmark. Thirdly, this paper does
526   not involve cultural differences. Specifically, our original data is in Chinese, and the annotators we
527   hired are also native Chinese speakers. In the future, we will also try to extend our method to other
528   cultures and further analyze cultural differences.
529
530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
532   This paper extends traditional MER to OV-MER, allowing for the prediction of arbitrary numbers
533   and types of emotions. To facilitate further research, we construct an initial dataset, define evaluation
534   metrics, and propose solutions. We observe that current MLLMs struggle to achieve satisfactory
535   results, as this task requires consideration of multimodal clues and subtle temporal changes, placing
536   higher demands on MLLMs. Additionally, EW-based metrics can replace GPT-based metrics, thus
537   reducing evaluation costs while ensuring reproducibility. This paper advances current research from
538   basic to nuanced emotion recognition, which is crucial for building emotion AI.

      A    D ETAILED M OTIVATION
811
812
      Limitations of Dimensional Models. Although dimensional models can be used to distinguish
813
      different emotions, they are abstract and difficult for the general public to understand. For instance,
814   the VAD model is one of the most widely used dimensional models. Given a VAD score (V=3.3,
815   A=2.9, D=4.7), it is challenging for people to accurately interpret the corresponding emotion. Ac-
816   cording to previous research (Verma & Tiwary, 2017), this VAD score represents sad. Furthermore,
817   the VAD model attempts to simplify emotions into three linear dimensions. However, emotions are
818   inherently complex and often involve combinations of multiple emotional states, which cannot be
819   fully captured by these three dimensions. Additionally, discrete models align more closely with
820   how humans intuitively understand emotions, making it easier to achieve consensus across different
821   annotators. Therefore, this paper uses discrete models to describe emotions.
822
823
824   OV-MER vs. Existing Emotion Models. OV-MER combines the advantages of both discrete and
825   dimensional models. Compared to one-hot discrete models, OV-MER uses denser discrete labels to
826   capture more subtle emotions, mimicking the continuous attributes of dimensional models. Com-
827   pared to dimensional models, OV-MER uses discrete labels, making it closer to the way humans
828   understand emotions. Therefore, our OV-MER provides a bridge connecting discrete and dimen-
829   sional models.
830
831   Video Emotion vs. Facial Emotion. Video emotion is more complex than facial emotion. This
832   is because, in videos, we need to capture subtle changes in the temporal dimension and integrate
833   multimodal clues. Take Figure 1(b) as an example. In the temporal dimension, we need to infer
834   a person’s nervousness based on his stuttering; in the multimodal dimension, we need to combine
835
      information from different modalities to gain a more comprehensive understanding of emotion. Due
      to the complexity of video emotion, using a single label is limiting, and more discrete labels are
836
      required to better describe video emotion. This is also the motivation behind our OV-MER task.
837
838
839
      Label Importance. In OV-MER, we do not assign different levels of importance to each label. Ev-
      ery emotion holds equal significance, and neglecting anyone can impact the performance of down-
840
      stream tasks. For example, if a human-computer interaction system only captures basic emotions
841
      while overlooking nuanced ones, it may fail to generate appropriate responses.
842
843
844   B    R ELATED W ORK
845
846   Multimodal Emotion Recognition. MER has rapidly developed in recent years (Wu et al., 2014).
847   Current research mainly focuses on building more efficient architectures to achieve higher accuracy
848   on benchmark datasets (Sun et al., 2024). For example, Zadeh et al. (2017) proposed a tensor fu-
849   sion network that addressed the MER task by leveraging interactions among unimodal, bimodal,
850   and trimodal inputs. Tsai et al. (2019) introduced a Transformer-based model that learned implicit
851
      alignment between different modalities and achieved promising results. Lian et al. (2024b) fur-
      ther established MERBench, involving various features, fusion strategies, and datasets. In emotion
852
      recognition, benchmark datasets usually limit the label space to basic emotions and use majority
853
      voting to determine the most likely one or more labels (Lian et al., 2023; Li et al., 2017). However,
854   emotional categories extend far beyond basic emotions. Restricting the label space will inevitably
855   overlook some nuanced emotions. To address this issue, we extend traditional MER to OV-MER,
856   which allows for the prediction of any number and categories of emotions.
857
858   Open Vocabulary Learning. Its main goal is to identify categories beyond the annotated label
859   space (Wu et al., 2024), which has been applied in various fields, such as object detection (Zareian
860   et al., 2021), segmentation (Ghiasi et al., 2022), and scene understanding (Li et al., 2021). For exam-
861   ple, the object detection dataset COCO (Lin et al., 2014) contains 80 categories, while objects in the
862   real world are nearly infinite, highlighting the importance of open vocabulary learning. This paper
863   makes the first attempt to address MER in an open-vocabulary manner. Compared to other tasks,
      MER is more difficult as it requires considering multimodal clues and subtle temporal variations.

       To generate pre-annotated visual clues, we sample three frames from each video and input them
1268
       into GPT-4V. In this section, we discuss the rationale behind the choice of the number of sampled
1269   frames. Specifically, we categorize visual clues into two types: (1) visual clues with relatively long
1270   durations; and (2) visual clues with fast movements, such as eye movements, head movements, and
1271   micro-expressions.
1272
       For the first type, since the duration of most videos is between 1 and 4 seconds (see Appendix H)
1273
       and the video content is usually continuous with only minor differences between adjacent frames
1274
       (see Appendix C), uniformly sampling three frames are sufficient to capture this information; For
1275   the second type, we observe that current MLLMs (including the GPT-4V used in this paper) struggle
1276   to capture these fast movements. Increasing the number of sampled frames does not address this
1277   issue. Previous research has also shown that GPT-4V cannot recognize micro-expressions (Lian
1278   et al., 2024c). To capture these fast movements, we employ multiple professional annotators to
1279   manually add this information.
