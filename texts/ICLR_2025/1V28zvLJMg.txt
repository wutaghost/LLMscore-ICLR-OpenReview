009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Existing Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) models perform well in accuracy but
012
                   often fail to address open-world challenges posed by open-vocabulary queries and
013                out-of-distribution (OOD) videos, which can lead to unreliable predictions. To
014                address uncertainty, particularly with OOD data, we build a VTG baseline using
015                Deep Evidential Regression (DER), which excels in capturing both aleatoric and
016                epistemic uncertainty. Despite promising results, our baseline faces two key biases
017                in multimodal tasks: (1) Modality imbalance, where uncertainty estimation is
018                more sensitive to the visual modality than the text modality; (2) Counterintuitive
019                uncertainty, resulting from excessive evidence suppression in regularization and
020                uneven sample error distribution in conventional DER. To address these, we propose
021
                   an RFF block for progressive modality alignment and a query reconstruction task to
                   enhance sensitivity to text queries. Additionally, we introduce a Geom-regularizer
022
                   to debias and calibrate uncertainty estimation. This marks the first extension of
023
                   DER in VTG tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and
024                robustness of our approach. Our code will be released soon.

      present significant challenges.
089
090   However, due to the complexity of the VTG task and limitations of the vanilla DER method, the
091   baseline exposes two critical biases: (1) Modality imbalance: Uncertainty estimation disproportion-
092   ately favors the visual modality over text. This stems from the nature of VTG, where visual input
093
      dominates tasks like keyframe identification. DER’s loss function, optimized for continuous visual
      signals, further emphasizes this imbalance. Moreover, simple modality concatenation or unimodal
094
      self-attention Lei et al. (2021a); Lin et al. (2023) fails to foster sufficient interaction between text and
095
      visual features, leading to over-reliance on visual input. (2) Counterintuitive uncertainty: Higher-
096   error predictions sometimes receive lower uncertainty due to DER’s regularizer limitations. Unlike
097   classification-based EDL methods, DER lacks a standard KL-divergence term, relying instead on a
098   heuristic regularizer that overly suppresses evidence, especially in low-error samples. This misaligns
099   uncertainty estimates, with low-error samples showing higher uncertainty and vice versa.
100
      To address modality imbalance, we propose a Debiased DER Model for Video Temporal Grounding
101
      (DDM-VTG). Specifically, it corporates with a Reflective Flipped Fusion (RFF) block with dual
102   branches for progressive cross-modal alignment, along with a query reconstruction (QR) task to
103   strengthen the text branch. This enhances the model’s sensitivity to text and mitigates bias in un-
104   certainty estimation. To resolve counterintuitive uncertainty, we introduce a simple yet effective
105   Geom-regularizer that adjusts uncertainty estimation based on prediction accuracy, adaptively sup-
106   pressing overconfidence and debiasing the system. Our contributions are three-fold: (1) This is
107   the first extension of DER to the VTG task, establishing an uncertainty-aware baseline. Leverag-
      ing DER’s uncertainty estimation, we effectively address open-world challenges in VTG. (2) We

      2     R ELATED WORK

116   2.1   V IDEO TEMPORAL GROUNDING
117
      Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) identifies correlated shots in videos based on natural language
118
      queries, which broadly supports various downstream video comprehension tasks, such as video
119   moment retrieval Anne Hendricks et al. (2017); Chen et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2020); Moon et al.
120   (2023b); Li et al. (2024), highlight detection Rui et al. (2000); Sun et al. (2014); Moon et al. (2023b),
121   and video summarization Gygli et al. (2014); Jiang & Mu (2022); Mahasseni et al. (2017); Nalla
122   et al. (2020); Sharghi et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2022). These tasks generally involve formulating
123   the boundaries of significant semantic segments Jiang & Mu (2022); Moon et al. (2023b); Lin
124   et al. (2023). Numerous innovative and effective methods are developed to address the challenges
125   in VTG. For instance, CTRL Anne Hendricks et al. (2017) and MCN Gao et al. (2017) initially
126   generate proposals using sliding windows, which they rank in terms of a cross-modal matching
127
      score. MomentDETR Lei et al. (2021b) applies a transformer to predict potential moments through
      learnable queries. Furthermore, QD-DETR Moon et al. (2023b) employs a cross-attention module
128
      and a negative pair training scheme to enhance multi-modal alignment. MomentDiff Li et al. (2024)
129
      initially sets random boundaries for temporal segments and iteratively refines them to better match the
130   intended semantics. However, existing approaches typically yield deterministic predictions, operating
131   under the assumption that semantic segments are demarcated by clear and precise boundaries. This
132   presumption neglects the inherent ambiguity and uncertainty associated with determining the true
133   extents of these segments. To address this gap, we explicitly models and quantify the semantic
134   uncertainty of video segment boundaries.
135
136   2.2   U NCERTAINTY LEARNING
137
138   Recent studies have highlighted inherent ambiguities and biases in VTG datasets, which significantly
139   impact the integrity and performance of models Zhou et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2023). These
140   uncertainties are categorized into annotation and query uncertainties. Annotation uncertainty stems
141   from varying temporal boundaries assigned by different annotators to the same query, while query
142
      uncertainty arises from the use of differing descriptions for the same video moment, underscoring
      the subjective nature of video interpretation Zhou et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2023). Furthermore,
143
      these datasets exhibit pronounced biases, with common events being overly represented and a small
144
      subset of queries accounting for most actions, leading to a skewed distribution that creates a long
145   tail in ground-truth timestamps Zhang et al. (2023); Otani et al. (2020). These findings underscore
146   the need for meticulous curation of datasets and the adoption of uncertainty-aware modeling ap-
147   proaches Arnab et al. (2020); Malinin & Gales (2018); Zhou & Levine (2021); Gawlikowski et al.
148   (2023). Among the techniques for modeling uncertainty, Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) has shown
149   promise. Originating from the principles of Dempster-Shafer Theory Shafer (1992) and Subjective
150   Logic Sensoy et al. (2018); Jøsang (2016), EDL models uncertainty explicitly through the distribution
151   of "second-order probabilities" over network outputs, finding applications across various classification
152   tasks including action recognition Bao et al. (2021), multi-view clustering Han et al. (2020; 2022),
153
      and semantic segmentation Holmquist et al. (2023) etc. Leveraging DER Amini et al. (2020) for its
      extension, EDL has effectively been applied to regression tasks such as stereo matching Wang et al.
154
      (2022) and emotion attributes estimation Wu et al. (2023). Nevertheless, DER faces challenges like
155
      evidence contraction due to the non-negativity of prior parameters in the Normal Inverse-Gamma
156   (NIG) distribution. New regularizers have been developed to address these issues, enhancing reliability
157   and performance Wu et al. (2024). However, DER often encounters gradient disappearance in high
158   uncertainty areas, necessitating ongoing refinement of its regularization methods Ye et al. (2024);
159   Meinert et al. (2023). In this study, We introduce DER into the VTG task to manage uncertainties in
160   open-world inputs, while further addressing the modality imbalance it presents and improving the
161   structural flaws of the vanilla DER regularizer. To the best of our knowledge, this marks the first
      successful attempt to extend DER in VTG task.

      4.3     D EBIASED DER M ODEL FOR VTG
241
242   To address the biased uncertainty estimation in the baseline in section 4.2, caused by modality
243   imbalance and counterintuitive uncertainty, we propose DDM-VTG. Our model introduces a RFF
244   block for progressive cross-modal alignment, reducing over-reliance on visual features, and a QR
245   task to enhance text sensitivity. As shown in Figure 3 (b), DDM-VTG first encodes an untrimmed
246   video and masked query, reconstructs the masked tokens via the RFF block, and performs VTG. The
247   debiased DER head assesses both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties, while the VTG and QR heads
248   manage task stages. Further details are provided in subsequent sections.
249   RFF block. The RFF block processes inputs from the video and text branches, alternating the roles
250   of video and text as queries and keys/values using shared parameters. Through the cross-attention
251   module, initial features V (1) and Q(1) of video and text branches are respectively updated, reflecting
252   each other’s information. Following cross-attention, each branch refines its features through self-
253   attention to enhance internal feature representation. The outputs of self-attention serve as inputs to
254   the next iteration of the RFF block, progressively enhancing modal alignment. This process is applied
255   sequentially from block 1 to block n in terms of Eq. (7). After completing the n-th layer, the refined
256
      video and query features are output. The specific workflow process is detailed in Appendix E.

      We focus on the following key considerations to conduct convincing experiments: 1) Despite focusing
339
      on robustness and interpretability, does our proposed DDM-VTG model demonstrate competitive
340   performance relative to current state-of-the-art VTG models? 2) Does the proposed QR task and
341   RFF blocks enhance performance in VTG tasks? 3) Does DDM-VTG give low uncertainty when
342   performing high localization accuracy statistically, and vice versa? 4) Is our proposed Geom-
343   regularizer more robust than the vanilla regularizer (i.e., Eq. (3))? 5) Can the model output a high
344   uncertainty score in various OOD scenarios to inform abnormality?

346   5.1    DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
347
348   Datasets. We conducted experiments on several widely used public datasets from diverse scenes:
349   Charades-STA Gao et al. (2017) (in-door activities), QVHighlights Lei et al. (2021b) (untrimmed
350
      daily vlogs & news), TACoS Regneri et al. (2013) (cooking scenes), and TVSum (YouTube videos).
      The detailed information including specific task domains and sizes for different datasets is reported
351
      in Appendix B.1 Table 4 with their different hyperparameters.
352
353   Metrics. For moment retrieval, we use recall@1 with IoU thresholds of 0.5 and 0.7, mean average
354   precision (MAP) with IoU thresholds of 0.5 and 0.75, and MAP avg, which is the average MAP across
355   IoU thresholds from 0.5 to 0.95 in 0.05 increments. For Highlight detection and Video summarization,
356
      we use MAP. Following Lei et al. (2021a), an additional metric HIT@1 is utilized for the Highlight
      detection task in the QVHighlights dataset, representing the hit ratio of the highest-scored clip.
357
358   Experimental Settings. Following previous works Lin et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), we utilize
359   CLIP Radford et al. (2021) (ViT-B/32) and SlowFast Feichtenhofer et al. (2019) (ResNet-50) as a
360   frozen backbone. Unless otherwise specified, the number (n) of RFF blocks is set to 4. The training
361   process is divided into two stages. In the first stage, QR masks and reconstructs noun entities in the
362
      query. Each sentence has 1 noun masked by default. The default epoch for QR is set to 30, with
      a learning rate of 1e-5. We utilize spaCy’s transformer-based parser- Honnibal & Montani (2017)
363
      to extract noun entities from the query text, and the masking is accomplished by zeroing out the
364
      noun entities at the embedding level. In the second stage, DDM-VTG predicts bounding boxed on
365   the visual branch at each video clip. Since our purpose of using DER is to optimize uncertainty
366   without affecting the model’s grounding capability, the gradient of delta in Eq. (10) is set to zero.
367   To avoid similar predictions, we utilize NMS with a threshold of 0.7 at evaluation to achieve better
368   performance. If not stated otherwise, we used the line regularizer on the evidential head. All training
369   for the moment retrieval tasks are conducted on four Tesla V100 GPUs. For the video summarization
370   task, due to the smaller scale of the TVSum dataset, we used only a single V100 GPU.

      Ablation study. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed debiasing method, we design
409
      targeted metrics to quantify the degree of debiasing in the model, and conduct extensive ablation on
410   the validation set of QVHighlights Lei et al. (2021b). For modality imbalance, we define a Varvis ,
411   Vartext and ∆Var to measure the sensitivity of uncertainty under varying levels of noise interference.
412   To be detailed, we add different level of Gaussian noise to video embeddings, and replace a varying
413   proportion of text tokens with irrelevant text, where the noise schedule is the same as in Figure 6.
414   We evaluate the variance of the uncertainty for a video query pair under different noise levels and
415   then take the average across all samples. We use values of Varvis and Vartext calculated in this way
416   to quantify the uncertainty sensitivity for vision and text modality respectively, and the difference
417   between them ∆V ar to describe the modality balance under different model settings. As reported in
418
      Table 3 (a) , our results strongly demonstrate the effectiveness of both RFF block and QR task.
419   For counterintuitive uncertainty, we define a new metric Error-Uncertainty Consistency Measure
420   (EUCM), with more details can be found in Appendix D. Moreover, we also compute the information
421   entropy of different uncertainty distributions, which is used to evaluate the expressive ability of the
422   evidential predictor, as reported in Table 3 (b) . The entropy of predictions with the vanilla regularizer
423   is greater than that with the Geom-regularizer. However, as demonstrated in section 5.3 predictions
424
      made with the vanilla regularizer are prone to be misleading. Consequently, even though it exhibits
      higher information entropy, this entropy may encompass substantial "biased information". In contrast,
425
      the Geom-regularizer not only achieves higher information entropy but also results in a lower EUCM
426
      score, indicating its superior performance.
427

429   gradually increased. We observe that the MAP reaches its optimal value when λgeom is set to 10−2 ,

431   unaffected. However, as λder increases to 1 × 10−2 , MAP begins to decline, reaching its lowest at
      1 × 10−1 . This can be explained by the fact that an excessively high uncertainty constraint weight

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      As the development of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) progresses, increasingly sophisticated
      VTG models are emerging. However, these models often falter when confronted with open-ended user
532
      inputs. Addressing this challenge, this paper introduces a robust VTG model, namely DDM-VTG,
533
      that not only possesses VTG capabilities but also overcomes two types of bias present in proposed
534   baseline methods and enables useful and meaningful quantification of potential uncertainties. This
535   allows the model to provide credible responses to queries that exceed its operational scope. Limited
536   by data quality and scale, the model’s capabilities are not particularly notable. Nevertheless, it offers
537   strategies for enhancing the trustworthiness of AI decisions. Future research will focus on further
538   expanding the decision-making reliability and interpretability of multimodal large language models
539   in video-related downstream tasks.

1012
1013   Figure 7 demonstrates the influence of different regularization strategies on model uncertainty in
1014   relation to prediction error. The top row focuses on aleatoric uncertainty, which is inherent data
1015   uncertainty, whereas the bottom row examines epistemic uncertainty, which stems from model
1016   ignorance. And we can discern the following key information:
1017
1018              • (a) Without DER: This model lacks any form of uncertainty management in the absence of
1019
                    DER, leading to inference results that are difficult to trust due to the complete absence of
                    handling latent uncertainties.
1020
1021              • (b) Only NLL: In this configuration, the model exhibits extremely low uncertainty across
1022                all levels of error rates, indicating overconfidence due to overfitting. This overconfidence
1023
                    suggests a model that is not realistically cautious about its predictions.
1024              • (c) With Vanilla Regularizer: Although the vanilla regularizer in DER measures and
1025                manages uncertainty, it paradoxically induces the model to express higher uncertainty
                    at lower error rates and very low uncertainty at higher error rates. This counterintuitive


                                                            19

1026
                behavior is clearly problematic, as it does not align with rational expectations of uncertainty
1027            behavior.
1028
              • (d) With Geom-Regularizer: Compared to (a), our proposed Geom-regularizer effectively
1029
                measures and manages uncertainty, enabling the model to indicate higher uncertainty at
1030
                higher error rates and vice versa. Relative to (b), it successfully mitigates the model’s
1031            overconfidence, which is beneficial for making prudent decisions. Against (c), it accurately
1032            calibrates the measurement of uncertainty, achieving a more sensible and intuitive assessment
1033            of uncertainty levels.
