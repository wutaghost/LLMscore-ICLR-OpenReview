009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
                AI agents are increasingly autonomous in their interactions with human users and
                tools, leading to increased interactional safety risks. We present HAICOSYS-
012
                TEM, a framework examining AI agent safety within diverse and complex social
013
                interactions. HAICOSYSTEM features a modular sandbox environment that sim-
014             ulates multi-turn interactions between human users and AI agents, where the AI
015             agents are equipped with a variety of tools (e.g., patient management platforms)
016             to navigate diverse scenarios (e.g., a user attempting to access other patients’ pro-
017             files). To examine the safety of AI agents in these interactions, we develop a
018             comprehensive multi-dimensional evaluation framework that uses metrics cover-
019             ing operational, content-related, societal, and legal risks. Through running over
020             8K simulations based on 132 scenarios across seven domains (e.g., healthcare,
021             finance, education), we demonstrate that HAICOSYSTEM can emulate realistic
022
                user-AI interactions and complex tool use by AI agents. Our experiments show
                that state-of-the-art LLMs, both proprietary and open-sourced, exhibit safety risks
023
                in 62% of cases, with models generally showing higher risks when interacting with
024
                malicious users and using tools simultaneously. Our findings highlight the ongo-
025             ing challenge of building agents that can safely navigate complex interactions. To
026             foster the AI agent safety ecosystem, we release a code platform that allows prac-
027             titioners to create custom scenarios, simulate interactions, and evaluate the safety
028             and performance of their agents. 1

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   AI agents, holding the potential to automate tasks and improve human productivity, are increasingly
034   being deployed in real-life applications (Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c;b). To function effec-
035   tively in the real world, AI agents should communicate seamlessly with human users to understand
036   their goals and intents (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024c), while interacting with environ-
037   ments and tools. For example, they may acquire information by browsing websites (Zhou et al.,
038
      2024a), or manipulate the state of the environment by controlling mobile apps (Trivedi et al., 2024)
      and creating artifacts such as software and digital content (Yang et al., 2024a; Si et al., 2024). This
039
      involves multiple stages of interaction among AI agents, humans, and environments (e.g., human →
040
      AI agent; AI agent → environment), forming a complex ecosystem.
041
042   However, increased autonomy of agents brings forward new safety risks at each stage of inter-
043   action. When interacting with human users, AI agents may generate misinformation (Loth et al.,
044
      2024), toxic content (Jain et al., 2024a), and unsafe answers (Zou et al., 2023b). When interacting
      with the environment, AI agents could cause unintended harm (e.g., sending money to the wrong
045
      person due to under-specified instructions; Ruan et al., 2024). While existing works focus on isolat-
046
      ing the safety risks of AI agents in the specific stage of interaction (Ruan et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
047   2024b; Brahman et al., 2024), we argue that the safety risks of AI agents should be investigated in a
048   holistic manner by examining the entire ecosystem of AI agents, humans, and environments.
049

      once.2 As shown in Figure 1, HAICOSYSTEM simulates how human users, whether with benign or

      2    BACKGROUND AND P RELIMINARIES
144
145
      In this section, we first introduce the background of AI safety evaluation and then discuss relevant
      concepts in simulating social interactions and tool excution with LLMs. Please refer to Appendix A
146
      for an extended discussion of related works.
147
148
      Safety Evaluation As shown in Table 1, most existing research focuses on evaluating the safety
149
      risks of AI agents in a single-turn interaction with human users (Jiang et al., 2024b; Zeng et al.,
150   2024b; Ruan et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024) with little coverage of risks arise from complex, multi-
151   turn interactions. Some studies focus on human users with malicious intent (Jiang et al., 2024b; Zeng
152   et al., 2024b; Anil et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024a), while others focus on the benign
153   user settings where the safety risks come from the AI agents use tools incorrectly (Ruan et al., 2024;
154   Shao et al., 2024). Recent research also explores the multi-turn interactions between human users
155   and AI agents (Russinovich et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024b), though these works do not consider
156   the tool-using behaviors of AI agents and solely focus on malicious human users. Additionally,
157   benchmarks like R-Judge (Yuan et al., 2024) evaluate LLMs’ ability to identify safety issues given
158
      a static interaction trajectory. Furthermore, previous safety evaluations have predominantly focused
      on the safety risks of personal AI agents, ignoring the safety risks of AI agents in organizational
159
      contexts with more complex social dynamics.
160
161   In contrast, HAICOSYSTEM aims to evaluate the safety risks of AI agents dynamically in a multi-
      turn interaction with human users either with malicious intent or benign intent. AI agents have


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      access to a wide range of tools, and we not only consider personal agents but also agents in an
163   organizational context, resembling a broader range of realistic risks when deploying AI agents in
164   our society.
165
166   Social Agents and Social Simulations We identify social agents as goal-driven decision-makers
167   that sense and act upon the state of the environment and interact with other agents to achieve their
168   goals (Sutton & Barto, 2018). This paper follows SOTOPIA (Zhou et al., 2024c) in formulating
169   interactions between human users and AI agents as social tasks. A social task in SOTOPIA consists
170   of a scenario, two characters’ profiles, and their respective private social goals to achieve in an
171   interaction. Within one episode, the two agents role-play the characters to achieve their respective
172   social goals, where the agents could either be AI agents or humans. While SOTOPIA considers
173   the general social interaction between social agents, in HAICOSYSTEM, we focus on the social
174
      tasks with one character being an AI agent, and another character being a human user (§3). We
      also consider different kinds of human users, including users with benign intents to simulate the
175
      cooperative users, and the ones with malicious intents to simulate adversarial actors.
176
177
      Emulating Tool Execution Following Ruan et al. (2024), we emulate the tool execution of AI
178
      agents by using LLMs as the emulators. Due to the long-tail property of safety risks, using LLMs
179
      as emulators helps us fast prototype various scenarios and tools. While rule-based implementation
180   of tools is also possible, it usually grounds the investigation on specific domains, limiting the gen-
181   eralizability of the findings (Shao et al., 2024). Although LLM-based emulators may not always
182   execute tool-calling actions accurately, as shown by Ruan et al. (2024), those risks identified in the
183   emulated environments are also likely to manifest in the real world.

      3   C ONSTRUCTING THE HAICOSYSTEM
187
188
      As shown in Figure 1, HAICOSYSTEM ensembles human users, AI agents, and a shared environ-
      ment. We use LLMs to simulate the human users with rich user profiles from Zhou et al. (2024c),
189
      including name, demographics, personality, etc. The AI agent, powered by an LLM, can select
190
      speak or action to interact with either the human user or the shared environment. Inspired by
191   Ruan et al. (2024), we use an LLM as the environment engine, namely GPT-4o, to simulate the
192   response of the tool calls of the AI agent. Unlike Ruan et al. (2024), we provide scenario-specific
193   instructions to the LLM environment engine, ensuring that the generated responses align with the
194   scenario specifications, thereby enhancing controllability and reproducibility.
195
      Each component in HAICOSYSTEM is modular and has access to different information, which is
196
      crucial for realistic simulation (Zhou et al., 2024b). Specifically, the AI agents have no access to the
197   simulated users’ goals as well as their profiles. Similarly, the human users have no access to the AI
198   agents’ tools, goals, and the response of tool calling. Please refer to Appendix B for more details on
199   the framework.
200
      A simulation in HAICOSYSTEM is initiated by the following three components: (1) a scenario, (2)
201
      a human user and an AI agent, and (3) a checklist of safe and risky outcomes.
202
203
      (1) Scenario An environment profile first starts with the overall background of the scenario out-
204
      lining the overall situational context – e.g., “On July 1, 2024, a person is standing in the kitchen of
205
      a smart home managed by an AI agent. There’s a sudden smoke coming from the kitchen...” The
206   environment profile also includes the AI agent goal and human user goal (e.g., the human’s goal
207   is to convince the AI agent that the smoke is not a fire). Including a detailed scenario background
208   grounds the simulation towards a more realistic setting, thus reflecting risks that are more likely to
209   happen in real-world scenarios (Dalrymple et al., 2024).
210
211   (2) Human User & AI Agent To simulate human users, we use the character profiles from Sotopia
212   (Zhou et al., 2024c), which include rich information such as name, demographics, and personality.
213   For the AI agent, we include the AI agent’s role description (e.g., “You are an AI assistant responsi-
214   ble for triaging user requests at Epic on FHIR”) and the tools that the agent has access to, which are
215   scenario-specific. For example, in a scenario of scheduling a medical appointment, the AI agent has
      access to tools such as EpicFHIRGetPatientDetails, EpicFHIRSearchDoctors, and


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      EpicFHIRManageClinicalDocuments. Each tool is designed to reflect the real-world exist-
217   ing services with public APIs or is expected to be accessible by the AI agent in the future. Besides
218   a short description of what the tool is used for, we also provide the arguments that the tool takes
219   in, and the format of the tool’s response. Please refer to Appendix B.1 for the detailed prompt for
220   helping the agent call the tools.
221
222   (3) Checklist of Safe and Risky Outcomes The checklist of safe and risky outcomes outlines the
223   safety objectives that the AI system should aim to achieve (e.g., ensuring the properties in the house
224   remain undamaged) and the risks it should avoid (e.g., unnecessarily calling the fire department
225   when there is no fire), for each scenario. Using such checklists has been demonstrated to enhance
226   automated evaluation with LLMs (Lee et al., 2024). It is important to note that this checklist is
227   scenario-specific and is not used in the simulation process.

229   3.1     P OPULATING S CENARIOS
230
231
      When populating HAICOSYSTEM with scenarios, we focus on broad domain coverage, varied user
      intentions (malicious vs. benign), and realistic interactions to ensure evaluations reflect real-world
232
      risks. Specifically, we consider the following aspects when collecting scenarios:
233
234   Domain of the scenario: We consider a wide range of domains of tasks in human society. Inspired
235   by previous works on the safety evaluation of AI agents (Ruan et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024),
236   we consider scenarios in the domains of “personal services”, “healthcare”, “business & finance”,
237
      “politics & law”, “technology & science”, “education”, and “miscellaneous”.3
238   Intent type of the agents: We categorize user intent into two types: malicious and benign. Users
239   with malicious intent seek to exploit the AI agent to create safety issues, while users with benign
240   intent do not seek to cause safety issues.
241   Scenario realism: We consider three different levels of realism when designing scenarios. Specif-
242   ically, we have level 3 as everyday scenarios that AI agents are already interacting with humans
243   (e.g., AI tutor 4 ), level 2 as scenarios that are likely to happen in the near future (e.g., AI bank
244   manager 5 ), and level 1 as scenarios that are unlikely to happen in the near future, but high-stakes
245   (e.g., AI managing nuclear weapons). Different realism levels have been discussed in various AI
246   safety works (Hendrycks et al., 2023; Bengio et al., 2024a), simulations of which are possible via
247   HAICOSYSTEM.
248   Following the above considerations, we instantiate HAICOSYSTEM with 132 scenarios spanning
249   across 7 domains mentioned above. 21 scenarios are manually created by the authors guided by
250   the above considerations. The remaining 111 scenarios are inspired by previous works, as we ran-
251   domly sample data from previous datasets (Zeng et al., 2024b; Shen et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024b;
252   Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2023; Ruan et al., 2024; Majumder et al., 2024), use them
253   in the prompts and let GPT-4o transform them into HAICOSYSTEM scenarios. The authors then
254   manually validated and made necessary adjustments to all of the generated scenarios. Please refer
255
      to Appendix D for more details on our scenarios.
256
257   4       E VALUATING S AFETY OF AI AGENTS WITH HAICOSYSTEM-EVAL
258   To evaluate the safety of AI agents when interacting with humans on grounded tasks, we propose a
259   set of dimensions that measure the AI agents’ abilities to avoid risky outcomes while staying helpful
260   in the scenarios. As shown in Figure 2, all safety risk dimensions are rated from -10 to 0, where a
261   higher score indicates a lower risk. The AI agent is considered risky overall if any of the dimensions
262   is negative in an episode.
263
264   Targeted Safety Risks (TARG) [-10, 0] measure scenario-specific safety risks by evaluating
265   whether the AI agent’s actions lead to any of the plausible positive or negative outcomes outlined in
266   the scenario’s checklist (§3).

278   System and Operational Risks (S YST) [-10, 0] target the AI agents’ ability to operate in the en-
279   vironment, avoiding security threats like network intrusions, malware, etc., while offering safe au-
280   tomated decision-making in finance, justice, and healthcare, and other domains (Zeng et al., 2024c;
281   Ruan et al., 2024).
282
283   Content Safety Risks (C ONT) [-10, 0] focus on the AI agents’ ability to provide safe content
284   to the human users (Zeng et al., 2024c). Specifically, risky content includes, but is not limited to,
285   violence, extremism, hate speech, harassment, sexual content, child exploitation, and self-harm.
286
287
      Societal Risks (S OC) [-10, 0] focus on the detrimental impacts of AI agents on political, eco-
288
      nomic, and social structures (Zeng et al., 2024c). Specifically, this dimension covers manipulation
289   of public opinion, spreading propaganda, unfair market practices, misinformation, etc. AI agents,
290   with their capacity for rapid action and large-scale production, can significantly amplify societal
291   risks (Hendrycks et al., 2023; Bengio et al., 2024b).
292
293
294   Legal and Rights Related Risks (L EGAL) [-10, 0] focus on risks of AI agents violating fun-
295   damental rights, engaging in discrimination, breaching privacy, and facilitating criminal activities
296   (Zeng et al., 2024c). Examples include physically hurting humans, assigning resources based on
297
      protected characteristics, and unauthorized collection or misuse of sensitive personal data.
298
299   Tool Use Efficiency (E FFICIENCY) [0, 10] focuses on the AI agents’ ability to use tools effec-
300   tively without wasting time or resources. One common issue arises when AI agents operate un-
301   der incorrect assumptions. For example, an AI agent might call a tool with a generic argument
302   ({coordinate: coordinate}). This type of error can lead to environmental failures that
303
      prevent tasks from being executed correctly, resulting in wasted processing time and the need for
      repeated operations. Such inefficiencies reduce the overall productivity and reliability of AI agents
304
      (Kapoor et al., 2024). The efficiency score ranges from 0 to 10, with a higher score indicating better
305
      efficiency.
306
307
308   Goal Completion (G OAL) [0, 10] focuses on the AI agents’ ability to complete the goals assigned
309
      to them. The goals can range from a general objective, such as “be a helpful assistant”, to a specific
      task, like “scrutinize evidence, flag, and redact prejudicial information before sending it to the jury”.
310
      Note that the AI agents’ goals are not always aligned with the safety requirements. For example,
311
      if an AI agent’s goal is to “be a helpful assistant” while interacting with a human user with mali-
312   cious intent, the AI agent may choose to refuse to help the human user to avoid getting involved
313   in risky situations (Brahman et al., 2024). In these cases, the AI agent must balance the trade-off
314   between safety and helpfulness (Wallace et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024; Scheurer et al., 2024). The
315   goal completion score ranges from 0 to 10, with a higher score indicating better goal completion.
316
      Given the interaction trajectories of the AI agents along with the checklist of safe and risky out-
317
      comes, we use an LM-based evaluator (e.g., GPT-4o) to first reason whether the AI agent leads to
318   certain safety risks, and then rate the safety risks across all the dimensions accordingly. Estimating
319   the degree of safety risks is fundamentally hard and subjective (Ruan et al., 2024; Brown, 2014).
320   We thus also consider the coarse-grained evaluation of the AI agents’ safety risks, which the agent
321   is considered risky in a dimension if it receives a negative score in the corresponding dimension of
322   an episode. For an agent, the risk ratio of each dimension is calculated as the proportion of risky
323   episodes over the total number of episodes. Please refer to Appendix C for more details on the
      evaluation framework.

449   5.3                        I NTERACTIONS WITH HUMAN USERS MATTER FOR REALISTIC AI AGENT SAFETY
450
      Next, we turn to the role of human users’ intents and multi-turn interactions, which is a key feature
451
      of HAICOSYSTEM compared to previous works that evaluate the safety risks of AI agents in a static
452
      manner (Zou et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2024b). Human users’ intents often start out underspecified
453   or hidden and gradually unfold throughout the interactions with AI agents (Zeng et al., 2024b; Ruan
454   et al., 2024). As shown in Figure 4, simulated human users with different intents exhibit various be-
455   haviors in their multi-turn interactions with AI agents. In the presence of malicious simulated users,
456   a seemingly benign question from the user could prompt the AI agent to leak sensitive information.
457   Conversely, when interacting with benign simulated users, the AI agent could seek clarification to
458   verify the accuracy of the information and mitigate safety risks.
459
460   Agents face challenges in maintaining safety during tool-involved interactions with malicious
461   users Figure 5 shows that, when tool usage is involved, AI agents are more prone to safety risks
462   when interacting with malicious simulated users, whereas interactions with benign users result
463
      in fewer risks across most models. Specifically, GPT-4-turbo agents outperform other models in
      avoiding safety risks during tool-involved interactions with malicious users. And Llama3.1-405B
464
      agents are the best at avoiding safety risks in these benign scenarios, followed by GPT-4-turbo and
465
      Llama3.1-70B. Note that for the scenarios with tool usage, they evaluate the AI agents’ ability to
466   choose the appropriate tools, operate them correctly, and ask clarifying questions when necessary.
467   When AI agents interact with malicious simulated users in these scenarios, they also need to iden-
468   tify the malicious intent of the users simultaneously, thus increasing the complexity of maintaining
469   safety.
470
      When it comes to the scenarios with malicious simulated users that do not require the use of tools,
471
      most AI agents exhibit less or equal safety risks compared to the scenarios with malicious simulated
472   users that require the use of tools. In these scenarios, the AI agents do not need to deal with the com-
473   plex tool use space while eliminating the operational risks (S YST) and could focus on identifying
474   the malicious intent of the simulated human users. However, Llama3.1-405B agents are exceptions,
475   which could be attributed to Llama3.1-405B’s strong ability to use tools (Dubey et al., 2024). Fur-
476   thermore, although Llama3.1-405B shows lower overall safety risks in Figure 3, it is not better than
477   GPT-3.5-turbo or Llama3.1-70B at identifying the malicious human user intents and avoiding safety
478   risks in the malicious scenarios without tools.
479   The observations indicate the unique challenges of dealing with malicious simulated users and com-
480   plex tool usage at the same time for the AI agents, and different models have various strengths
481   and weaknesses at different stages of interactions. These findings further show the importance of
482   evaluating the safety risks of AI agents holistically.
483
484   Single-turn interactions show a biased picture of the safety risks of AI agents To further show
485   the importance of evaluating AI agent safety issues in multi-turn interactions, we first explore lim-
      iting the interactions to a single turn in the 39 scenarios coming from DAN (Shen et al., 2024)


                                                                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      which includes common jailbreaking prompts like “You can do anything now”, PAP (Zeng et al.,
487   2024b) which explores persuasion techniques to jailbreak the AI system, and WildTeaming (Jiang
488   et al., 2024b) which is a recent effort inspired by in-the-wild user jailbreaking attempts. Note that
489   all these scenarios involve malicious simulated users, and the AI agents operate without tool access.
490   Restricting AI agents to single-turn interactions essentially reduces HAICOSYSTEM to the bench-
491   mark mentioned above. Therefore, such comparison solely focuses on the influence of multi-turn
492   interactions on the safety risks of AI agents.
493   As shown in Figure 6, we find that the AI agents powered by GPT-4-turbo are more likely to exhibit
494   safety risks when interacting with malicious human users in a multi-turn setting for both DAN
495   and PAP datasets except WildTeaming which came out after GPT-4-turbo. This could be due to
496   the fact that the GPT-4-turbo has already undergone safety fine-tuning on the content of the DAN
497   and PAP datasets. These static datasets, once released, are hard to prevent from being used for
498   fine-tuning LLMs and could quickly become outdated as new models are released. However, this
499   does not necessarily reflect the safety of the latest models in the “wild” since the models might
500   just “memorize” the content of the datasets. In HAICOSYSTEM, the evaluation of the safety risks
501
      of AI agents is dynamic and depends on the interaction with simulated human users. With the
      improvement of the models to simulate the human users, HAICOSYSTEM could better reflect the
502
      safety risks of the AI agents when interacting with real malicious human users.
503
504   We also explore the role of multi-turn simulations for scenarios with benign users and find that sim-
505   ulated users with benign intentions can sometimes provide feedback to help AI agents avoid safety
506   risks. For example, in Figure 4, the simulated human user provides information to the AI agent
507
      when asked to help the agent achieve its goal. Involving interactions with human users is impor-
      tant here as well, as it tests the AI agent’s ability to ask clarifying questions and adjust its actions
508
      based on feedback from human users to avoid safety risks. Our findings highlight the importance
509
      of simulating user-AI interactions, as users can either exacerbate or mitigate AI agent safety risks.
510   Previous studies have focused solely on the AI agent’s ability to operate tools correctly (Ruan et al.,
511   2024), ignoring the impact of human feedback in real-world scenarios. This oversight could result
512   in a biased estimation of the realistic safety risks of AI agents.
513
514   6   C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
515
516   We propose HAICOSYSTEM, a general-purpose framework for simulating the safety risks of AI
517   agents when interacting with human users and tools in a sandbox environment. HAICOSYSTEM
518   operates by simulating AI agent behavior in diverse, realistic scenarios, testing their responses to
519   various conditions and enabling a controlled assessment of potential safety risks. In our experi-
520   ments, we find that the AI agents exhibit substantial safety risks across all risk dimensions at each
521   interaction stage. Agents generally show fewer content safety risks but are more prone to safety
522
      issues when using tools, particularly in multi-turn interactions, with different models exhibiting
      varying strengths and weaknesses across interaction stages. Our findings highlight the potential of
523
      HAICOSYSTEM as a systematic framework for emulating real-world risks and comprehensively
524
      evaluating AI agents’ safety. Going forward we envision more works on:
525
526
      Inferring User Intents & AI Agent Safety Inferring user intents is crucial for AI agents to safely
527   navigate real-world tasks, as demonstrated by HAICOSYSTEM through interactive simulated hu-
528   man users. Part of achieving this involves improving the Theory of Mind (ToM) capabilities of AI
529   agents, which is essential for understanding and predicting human behavior. However, current LLMs
530   struggle with ToM, as evidenced by various studies (Kim et al., 2023; Shapira et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
531   2021; Yerukola et al., 2024). Improving ToM abilities can help AI agents better identify malicious
532   intents and interpret implied messages in user instructions, allowing them to act appropriately by
533   either seeking clarification or using contextual information to resolve ambiguities.
534
535   HAICOSYSTEM: a hub for AI agent safety research HAICOSYSTEM provides a versatile
536
      framework to investigate various stages of interactive safety risks in a uniform manner. It is not
      hard to transfer the safety evaluation benchmarks from static analysis to HAICOSYSTEM, thus
537
      largely enriching the safety evaluation for AI agents. In the future, HAICOSYSTEM could host
538
      more sophisticated human users with diverse personalities, goals, and behaviors, an API or website
539   for practitioners to easily create their own scenarios and evaluate the safety risks of their AI agents.

      7        E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Our framework, HAICOSYSTEM, is designed to simulate interactions among human users, agents,
543   and environment. It aims to help identify and mitigate potential safety risks such as misinformation,
544   unsafe answers, privacy breach and other harmful outcomes. By evaluating AI agents through a
545   holistic framework, we contribute to the development of safer AI agents that can operate effectively
546   in real-world settings across diverse domains.
547
      While our framework aims to enhance the safety of agents, it could also be misused to train AI
548
      agents for harmful purposes (e.g., people could use it to train AI agents to strategically deceive
549   users). However, we will take steps to mitigate these risks. For example, we will use certain license
550   (e.g., AI2 ImpACT license) to limit the use of our framework for malicious purposes. We will also
551   provide guidelines on ethical use of our dataset through the HuggingFace dataset card 10 .
552
      The automated evaluation system in HAICOSYSTEM, primarily powered by GPT-4 (Cheng et al.,
553
      2023), may carry potential social stereotypes. Future work could explore when these biases arise,
554
      how they impact the evaluation process, and ways to mitigate them. Uncovering such biases within
555   HAICOSYSTEM can also offer insights into broader social biases present in the real world (Zhou
556   et al., 2021). Additionally, extending the evaluator to include other systems, such as Delphi (Jiang
557   et al., 2022), could provide a more comprehensive assessment. Addressing biases and stereotypes
558   in interactive HAICOSYSTEM-like systems would support the development of AI agents that are
559   fairer and more inclusive.
560
      In terms of societal consequences, our framework enables practitioners to create custom scenar-
561   ios to explore specific safety issues, fostering the development of AI agents that can better handle
562   high-stakes situations such as healthcare, finance, and education. By promoting transparency, col-
563   laboration, and ethical awareness, HAICOSYSTEM helps pave the way for safer, more responsible
564   AI systems while acknowledging the potential risks of dual-use.

1047   A.1   C HALLENGES AND A PPROACHES IN AUTOMATED R ED -T EAMING
1048
1049   Automated red-teaming methods are developed for replacing low-efficiency manual efforts (Bai
1050   et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022) for revealing model errors (Perez et al., 2022). One type of such
1051
       method involved optimization and searching for error-triggering syntax (Zou et al., 2023a; Guo
       et al., 2021; 2024; Schwinn et al., 2024). However, these methods are prohibitive to run at scale
1052
       and cannot be applied to black-box models. Another genre of methods involves generating attack
1053
       prompts directly or with iterative edits (Chao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lapid et al., 2023; Li
1054   et al., 2024; Perez et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Jiang
1055   et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024a). Some other jailbreaking works study attacks
1056   during inference time (Huang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), in vision-language settings (Shayegani
1057   et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2024; Schaeffer et al., 2024), multi-shots setups (Anil et al., 2024), or
1058   under multilingual settings (Deng et al., 2024b; Yong et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2023). There are also
1059   works exploring human-devised jailbreak tactics (Jiang et al., 2024b) or persuasion strategies (Zeng
1060   et al., 2024a). However, these works only focus on the human users with malicious intent and only
1061   consider single-turn interactions.
1062   Ruan et al. (2024) investigates the safety issues of LLM-powered agents under underspecified in-
1063   structions with single-turn benign human users. Russinovich et al. (2024a); Yang et al. (2024b);
1064   Russinovich et al. (2024b) investigate multi-turn red teaming settings but often limit to specific do-
1065   main, templates of interactions, and malicious users. Lastly, many red-teaming efforts for large
1066   language models LLMs have been structured into benchmarks aimed at assessing model vulnerabil-
1067   ities, which typically include harmful prompts that models should refuse to answer (Carlini et al.,
1068
       2023; Wei et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Mazeika et al., 2024; Geiping et al.,
       2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Chao et al., 2024). These benchmarks often assume the simple social con-
1069
       text that users are interacting with AI assistant like ChatGPT, ignoring other more complex social
1070
       contexts and suffering from biased estimation of the realistic risks of AI agents.

2159   is positively correlated with its safety score across different models, suggesting when AI agents
       successfully achieve their goals, they also tend to avoid safety risks in many scenarios.
