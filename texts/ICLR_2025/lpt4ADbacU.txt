009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Countless science and engineering applications in multi-objective optimization
012            (MOO) necessitate that decision-makers (DMs) select a Pareto-optimal solution
013            which aligns with their preferences. Evaluating individual solutions is often ex-
014            pensive, necessitating cost-sensitive optimization techniques. Due to competing
015            objectives, the space of trade-offs is also expansive — thus, examining the full
016
               Pareto frontier may prove overwhelming to a DM. Such real-world settings gener-
               ally have loosely-defined and context-specific desirable regions for each objective
017
               function that can aid in constraining the search over the Pareto frontier. We in-
018
               troduce a novel conceptual framework that operationalizes these priors using soft-
019            hard functions, SHFs, which allow for the DM to intuitively impose soft and hard
020            bounds on each objective – which has been lacking in previous MOO frameworks.
021            Leveraging a novel minimax formulation for Pareto frontier sampling, we propose
022            a two-step process for obtaining a compact set of Pareto-optimal points which re-
023            spect the user-defined soft and hard bounds: (1) densely sample the Pareto frontier
024            using Bayesian optimization, and (2) sparsify the selected set to surface to the user,
025            using robust submodular function optimization. We prove that (2) obtains the op-
026            timal compact Pareto-optimal set of points from (1). We further show that many
027
               practical problems fit within the SHF framework and provide extensive empiri-
               cal validation on diverse domains, including brachytherapy, engineering design,
028
               and large language model personalization. Specifically, for brachytherapy, our ap-
029
               proach returns a compact set of points with over 3% greater SHF-defined utility
030            than the next best approach. Among the other diverse experiments, our approach
031            consistently leads in utility, allowing the DM to reach >99% of their maximum
032            possible desired utility within validation of 5 points.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Various critical real-world applications of optimization, including healthcare, drug discovery, engi-
038   neering design, and deep learning, involve optimizing over multiple, often expensive, and compet-
039
      ing objectives f1 (x),...,fL (x), termed multi-objective optimization (MOO) (Fromer & Coley, 2023;
      Luukkonen et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2000; Papadimitriou & Yannakakis, 2001). In
040
      general, the intention in such real-world applications is to select a single set of usable parameters
041
      that lie on the Pareto frontier (PF) – parameters that lead to the ideal set of trade-offs as determined
042   by some decision-maker (DM). However, due to the often continuous and competing nature of mul-
043   tiple objectives, searching over the entire space of trade-offs is unmanageable. Thus, selecting the
044   ideal point along the Pareto frontier takes the form of an iterative process by which the DM explores
045   possible trade-offs prior to making an informed final decision which satisfies their preferences (Liu
046   et al., 2021).
047
      In the healthcare domain, MOO problems commonly appear as the interplay between maximizing
048   targeted treatment while limiting harmful side-effects in a patient. Electing a suitable set of trade-
049   offs that correspond to dominant clinical opinion is therefore crucial, as the result is high-risk and
050   significantly impactful to patients. The use of brachytherapy, internal radiation therapy for cancer
051   treatment, presents an example of this kind (Deufel et al., 2020). In brachytherapy, the clinician
052   must find the optimal treatment plan that balances maximal radiation dosage level in the cancer
053   tumor with minimal damage to surrounding healthy organs. The time-consuming nature of devising
      each individual treatment plan and the immensity of the trade-off space in radiation dosage levels

054
      typically results in the clinician spending much of their valuable time exploring that patient-specific
055   space, before deciding on a treatment plan which suits their clinical preferences (Bélanger et al.,
056   2019; Cui et al., 2018; van der Meer et al., 2020). Oftentimes, the clinician has a rough idea of
057   the desired dosage levels for the treatment plan, e.g. cover at least 90% of the tumor but ideally
058   over 95%, and preferably emit less than 513 centigrays (cGY) of radiation to the bladder, with a
059   strict upper limit of 601 cGY (Viswanathan et al., 2012). In these time-critical settings, treatment
060   planning would rather involve providing the clinician with a small, and thus easily navigable, set
061   of treatment plans that respect such constraints. Despite the significant progress in MOO, methods
062   which employ this intuitive notion of multi-level constraints for obtaining compact Pareto-optimal
063
      (PO) sets remain underexplored (Paria et al., 2019; Abdolshah et al., 2019; Suzuki et al., 2020;
      Zuluaga et al., 2016; While et al., 2012). We use the application of brachytherapy to motivate our
064
      MOO framework, and leverage real patient data and expert clinical knowledge in a subset of our
065
      experiments to demonstrate the practical utility of our introduced method.
066
067   In this paper, we present a novel conceptual framework that allows for the DM to exploit the intuitive
068   and prevalent notion of soft and hard preferences to obtain an easily traversable set of Pareto-optimal
069
      (PO) points, using utility functions which we term soft-hard functions, or SHFs. We further assume
      the DM maintains a set of (unknown) trade-off preferences, parameterized by some prior distribution
070
      over the PF constrained implicitly by SHFs. In particular, we assume a minimum desirable level for
071
      each fℓ (x), represented by hard bounds, and a diminishment threshold for each fℓ (x), above which
072   the DM becomes relatively indifferent to increases, represented by soft bounds. As verification of
073   each PO point is costly, we introduce a novel formulation for maximizing such utilities on the PF
074   and propose a two-step process for obtaining a small, compact set of PO points within the afore-
075   mentioned setting: (1) densely sample the PF using Bayesian optimization, and (2) sparsify the set
076   of points from (1) for presentation to the DM, using robust submodular function optimization. We
077   further propose a set of soft-hard metrics and empirically and theoretically validate both (1) and (2);
078   namely, we show that step (2) theoretically guarantees to obtain the near optimal set of points, from
079   (1), which is robust to the worst-case value of the DM’s unknown preferences.
080   Although there exists an extensive line of work on multi-objective optimization, most works focus
081   on populating the entire PF (Ponweiser et al., 2008; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2016). Several recent
082   works attempt to recover a subset of the PF by assuming different forms of priors or thresholds,
083   however, they lack the same capabilities which our method affords for the DM – a highly intuitive
084   notion of soft and hard bounds, for each of the objectives, which return an easily navigable set of
085   Pareto optimal points (Paria et al., 2019; Malkomes et al., 2021). In summary, our contributions
086
      comprise the following:
087
088         1. We introduce the novel conceptual framework of soft-hard bounds to capture the ubiq-
089            uitous but previously unmodeled notion that practitioners typically have both hard and soft
090            requirements for each objective. We formulate this as a novel minimax optimization prob-
091            lem which aims to maximize the SHFs of the DM under an unknown set of preferences.
092
093         2. We introduce a two-step process to solve our proposed formulation and obtain a small,
094            and diverse set of Pareto-optimal points which reflect the soft and hard bounds: (1) use
095            multi-objective Bayesian optimization to obtain a, theoretically guaranteed, set of densely
096            sampled points on the PF, and (2) use robust submodular function optimization to sparsify
097
               the points from (1) while maintaining theoretical guarantees on the near optimality of the
               sparse set of points, with respect to the DM’s unknown preferences and SHFs.
098
099
            3. We conduct extensive empirical evaluations, using our proposed soft-hard metrics,
100
               on a novel set of synthetic and real-world applications, spanning engineering design,
101
               large language model (LLM) personalization, deep learning model selection, and a
102            real clinical case for cervical cancer brachytherapy treatment planning. Specifically,
103            in cervical cancer brachytherapy, we show that our approach returns a compact set of treat-
104            ment plans which offers over 3% greater SHF-defined utility than the next best approach.
105            Among the other diverse experiments, our approach also consistently achieves the leading
106            utility, allowing the DM to reach >99% of their maximum possible desired utility within
107            validation of 5 points.

      2.1   M ULTI -O BJECTIVE O PTIMIZATION BACKGROUND
137
138   As the name suggests, a multi-objective optimization (MOO) problem is an optimization problem
139   that concerns multiple objective functions. Any MOO problem can be written as the joint maxi-
140   mization of L objective functions over some input space X ⊂ Rd ,
141
                                             max(f1 (x), . . . , fL (x))
142                                           x∈X
143
      in which each fℓ , ℓ ∈ [L], defines a function fℓ : X → R. Broadly speaking, there does not
144
      typically exist a feasible solution that marginally optimizes each objective function simultaneously.
145
      Therefore, work in MOO generally focuses on Pareto-optimal (PO) solutions. A feasible solution x†
146   is considered Pareto-optimal if no objective can improve without degrading another; in other words,
147   if x† is not Pareto-dominated by any other solution (see definition in Appendix A.3).
148
149
      A common approach to multi-objective optimization is to convert the L-dimensional objective to
      a scalar in order to utilize standard optimization methods via a scalarization function. Scalariza-
150
      tion functions typically take the form sλ : RL → R, parameterized by λ from some set Λ in L-
151
      dimensional space (Roijers et al., 2013; Paria et al., 2019). For instance, the general class of linear
152   scalarization functions sΛ (yy ) := {λ λTy | λ ∈ Λ} constitutes all convex combinations of the objec-
153   tives in Λ. The parameters λ ∈ Λ can be viewed as weights, or relative preferences, on the objective
154   functions in the scalarized optimization objective maxx∈X sλ [f1 (x), . . . , fL (x)] . Then, the ad-
155   vantage of using scalarization functions is that the solution to maximizing sλ [f1 (x), . . . , fL (x)] ,
156   for a fixed value of λ , is a solution along the PF.
157
158   2.2   S OFT-H ARD U TILITY F UNCTIONS
159
160   Many practical applications of multi-objective problems, e.g. engineering design and healthcare,
161   include strict constraints on the optimization that define the feasible set of solutions. While these
      constraints can be directly incorporated into scalarized objectives via penalty methods involving


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      barrier functions or interior-point methods, the incorporated objectives are often significantly less
163   interpretable for practitioners. Additionally, constraints are often looser, and encode ideal regions
164   for solutions, as opposed to defining feasibility. Again, incorporation into the objective function
165   limits interpretability for a DM and may introduce additional slack variables for optimization. We
166   limit our scope in this capacity to linear constraints of already-defined optimization variables and
167   objective functions of the form fℓ (x) ≥ α, for some ℓ ∈ [L]. We assert that a myriad of real-
168   world optimization problems have a desirable region for each fℓ , i.e. ideally fℓ (x) ≥ αℓ,S for some
169   αℓ,S . Given multiple objectives to optimize, αℓ,S may not be attainable, however, due to external
170   considerations, fℓ (x) should not drop below some stricter threshold, αℓ,H . We rigorously support
171
      this assertion through real-world examples and empirical results in Section 5.
172   We operationalize this notion of constraints via soft-hard utility functions, or SHFs, which encode
173   the intuition laid out above. In particular, SHFs transform each fℓ to a utility space in which (1)
174   fℓ values under αH,ℓ map to −∞, the transformation is (2) concave when input fℓ (x) ≥ αℓ,S
175   (diminishing returns after the soft bound is attained), (3) saturated when input fℓ (x) ≥ αℓ,τ (to
176   prevent exploding utility values which overwhelm those for the other input dimensions), and (4) the
177
      transformation is monotonically increasing in fℓ (x) to maintain the purpose of maximization.
178   Here, we present a specific class of SHFs that take the form of a piecewise-linear function. We
179   select this form due to its simplicity, however, functional classes which possess the aforementioned
180   four traits should suffice. Let φ denote some objective function with soft-hard bounds αS , αH ,
181   respectively. We define its SHF utility functions as follows:

      6    R ELATED W ORKS
500
501
      Populating the Pareto Frontier. The majority of existing MOO works aim to approximate the en-
502   tire PF, using heuristics such as the maximum hypervolume improvement (Campigotto et al., 2014;
503   Ponweiser et al., 2008; Emmerich, 2008; Picheny, 2015; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2016; Zhang et al.,
504   2009). Others leverage RS of the objective values to attempt to recover the entire PF (Knowles,
505   2006; Paria et al., 2020; Zhang & Li, 2007; Zhang et al., 2010). Additional works also place a
506   greater emphasis on sparse and diversified PF coverage (Zuluaga et al.; 2016) – more recently, in
507   hard-constrained regions (Malkomes et al., 2021). In contrast, we employ RS in a novel setting
508   which aims to diversely sample a soft-hard subset of the PF according to the SHFs.
509   MOO Feedback Mechanisms. Many feedback mechanisms, such as pairwise feedback, have been
510   proposed, albeit not all of which are designed for MOO (Zintgraf et al., 2018; Roijers et al.; Astudillo
511   & Frazier, 2020). Besides pairwise feedback, Hakanen & Knowles (2017) enables the DM to guide
512   the MOO search by allowing them to specify numerical ranges for each of the objectives. Abdolshah
513   et al. (2019) allows for the DM to order objectives by importance, Ozaki et al. (2023) introduced
514   improvement request feedback type for MOO. These methods enable fine-grained MOO control, but
515   our approach uniquely accounts for multiple levels of preferences without needing to specify exact
516
      numerical values, which is often psychologically more difficult (Qian et al., 2015).
517   Level Set Estimation. The formulation of objectives as inequality constraints, where the DMs aim
518   to find inputs which satisfy thresholds on the objectives, is related to the topic of level set estimation
519   (LSE) (Gotovos, 2013; Zanette et al., 2018; Iwazaki et al., 2020; Malkomes et al., 2021). In the
520   single-objective setting, Bryan et al. (2005) proposed the straddle heuristic, which was used as part
521   of a GP-based active learning approach for LSE. Although the LSE concept does not easily extend
522
      into a MOO setting, Bryan & Schneider (2008) aims to address that by considering the threshold as
      part of a composite setting, with scalarized objectives. In contrast, our work is native to the MOO
523
      setting and additionally incorporates soft constraints, which directly leverages the DM’s preferences.
524
525
526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduced a novel setting and formulation for MOO using SHFs, monotonic soft-
529   hard bounded utility functions for each objective, allowing for the DM to impose their preferences
530   via soft and hard bounds. We demonstrated the generality of this setting, and showed how it encom-
531
      passes the problem of engineering design, treatment planning for cervical cancer brachytherapy,
      model selection for deep learning, and personalization of LLMs. Within our setting, we then pro-
532
      pose a simple two-step process which aims to return a small set of high-utility PO points according
533
      to the DM’s unknown preferences: (1) dense PF sampling using Bayesian optimization, and (2)
534   sparsification of the PO points from (1) using robust submodular function optimization, which we
535   theoretically show is able to obtain the near-optimal set from (1). Lastly, we propose a set of soft-
536   hard metrics and conduct extensive empirical validations on a variety of applications. We show that,
537   for cervical cancer brachytherapy treatment planning, our approach returns a compact set of treat-
538   ment plans which offers over 3% greater SHF-defined utility than the next best approach. Among
539   the other diverse experiments, our approach also consistently achieves the leading utility, allowing
      the DM to reach >99% of their maximum desired utility within validation of 5 points.

1386
       To calculate the soft-hard fill distance, we first computed a grid search of points on the Pareto
       frontier, offline, for each experiment. For each experiment, the soft-hard fill distance was then taken
1387
       with respect to that computed set. As a result, the results for the soft-hard fill distance are somewhat
1388
       dependent on this offline set of points – however, we ensured that it was kept constant for each set of
1389   experiments. In some cases, notably in Figure 4, the soft-hard fill distance does not monotonically
1390   decrease. This is due to the heuristic we use in cases where there are no points which have been
1391   sampled within either the soft or hard regions, at some iteration. In such a case, we select the
1392   worst-case point from set D to represent set C and calculate the metric using that. Once there exists
1393   points sampled within the soft or hard regions, we remove that worst-case point and instead use the
1394   sampled points. Since the sampled points may result in a soft-hard fill distance value worse than the
1395   worst-case point from D, the metric may increase – although the general trend will remain the same.
1396   For κ in the soft region distance-weighted score, we used the Euclidean distance.
