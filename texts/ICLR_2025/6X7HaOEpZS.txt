009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neurons in auto-regressive language models like GPT-2 can be interpreted by ana-
012            lyzing their activation patterns. Recent studies have shown that techniques such as
013            dictionary learning, a form of post-hoc sparse coding, enhance this neuron-level
014            interpretability. In our research, we are driven by the goal to fundamentally im-
015            prove neural network interpretability by embedding sparse coding directly within
016            the model architecture, rather than applying it as an afterthought. In our study, we
017            introduce a white-box transformer-like architecture named Coding RAte Trans-
018
               formEr (CRATE), explicitly engineered to capture sparse, low-dimensional struc-
               tures within data distributions. Our comprehensive experiments showcase sig-
019
               nificant improvements (up to 106% relative improvement) in neuron-level inter-
020
               pretability across a variety of evaluation metrics. Detailed investigations confirm
021            that this enhanced interpretability is steady across different layers irrespective of
022            the model size, underlining CRATE’s robust performance in enhancing neural net-
023            work interpretability. Further analysis shows that CRATE’s increased interpretabil-
024            ity comes from its enhanced ability to consistently and distinctively activate on
025            relevant tokens. These findings point towards a promising direction for creating
026            white-box foundation models that excel in neuron-level interpretation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Representation learning aims to learn a continuous mapping, to transform a random vector in a high
031   dimensional space that is sampled from a dataset, to a feature vector in another (typically lower-
032   dimensional) space (Bengio et al., 2013). Recently, deep learning has witnessed tremendous empir-
033   ical success in modeling massive amounts of high-dimensional data, and the predominant practice
034   has been to learn first a task-agnostic representation by pre-training a large neural network, which
035   is commonly known as the foundation model (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019). Among
036   language foundation models, the transformers architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) with Generative
037   Pre-Training (Radford et al., 2019) (GPT) has recently demonstrated a strong capability of model-
038   ing sequential data and thus predicting subsequent tokens (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022).
039
      Such strong capability has emerged significant success in downstream applications (Lewis et al.,
      2020; Yang et al., 2023), yet the large neural network is known to be black-box, where the represen-
040
      tations in the model are not independently interpretable, introducing difficulty in designing effective
041
      paradigms for major known challenges of (visual) language models like hallucination (Ji et al.,
042   2023; Tong et al., 2024), bias (Garrido-Muñoz et al., 2021; Nadeem et al., 2020), and catastrophic
043   forgetting (Kemker et al., 2018; Zhai et al., 2024).
044
045
      To interpret the functions of individual modules in the language models, mechanistic interpretation
      was proposed to reverse-engineer such models, through identifying meaningful patterns in the data
046
      representations and computational mechanisms of the model components (Olah, 2022; Meng et al.,
047
      2022a). Recent studies on auto-regressive models like GPT-2 have delved into neuron-level inter-
048   pretation, where the focus is on understanding the activations within the model’s MLP layers (Yun
049   et al., 2021). This approach helps to reveal the specific roles of individual neurons, which is cru-
050   cial for precise model editing and control (Meng et al., 2022a;b). Recent research has also proposed
051   that sparse auto-encoder (SAE)-based dictionary learning effectively promotes mono-semanticity of
052   neurons, thus enhancing neuron-level interpretability (Bricken et al., 2023). However, as a post-hoc
053   method, sparse auto-encoders always introduces a non-negative reconstruction loss, which intro-
      duces noise for steering the model and imperfect fidelity when interpreting the neurons (Bricken

      Can we instead build sparsity directly into the language model? In this paper, we develop the
      CRATE language model, a GPT-2-size language model that builds sparse coding into the model with a
083
      mathematically principled way. CRATE handles the problems introduced by sparse encoders at scale:
084
      it (i) escapes the loss introduced in reconstructing the language model representations, enabling loss-
085   free steering, and (ii) escapes the unsteady process of training a sparse auto-encoder. To avoid adding
086   inconsistency into the language model, we develop on top of a mathematically principled white-box
087   model framework, named CRATE (Yu et al., 2023a).1 After encoding the text tokens into numbers,
088   we apply language-domain-specific modifications to the original CRATE architecture and obtain the
089   token representations. The final representations are then used to predict the next token, while the
090   intermediate representations gets interpreted.
091   To this end, the main contribution of this work is to propose a causal language model architecture
092   based on the CRATE model framework that builds sparsity inherently, that achieves significantly bet-
093   ter neuron-level interpretability (106% relative increase) than language models with the GPT archi-
094   tecture under a similar configuration. CRATE forms a family of models from single-layer model up
095   to a 12-layer configuration. Comparative qualitative analysis of neuron activations between CRATE
096   and GPT-2 is provided in Figure 1, alongside extensive quantitative evaluations demonstrating that
097   by explicitly integrating sparse coding into the language model, CRATE achieves markedly improved
098   interpretability across layers compared to GPT-2, applicable across a wide range of model sizes

      under a variety of evaluation metrics.

101   2      R ELATED W ORK
102   Neuron-level Interpretation. Recent studies have provided insights into how auto-regressive mod-
103   els like GPT-2 work at the level of individual neurons, named neuron-level interpretation. These
104   studies focus on analyzing activations, which are the outputs from the activation functions within
105   the model’s multi-layer perceptron (MLP) layers (Yun et al., 2021). Analyzing activations helps

      uncover the roles of language model components, which is significant to applications like precise
109   modifications and control on the model, known as model editing (Meng et al., 2022a;b). Neuron-
110   level interpretation is crucial to understanding the mechanisms in a model, including what concepts
111   are learned in the neurons of the network, whether specific neurons are learning particular concepts,
112   and how localized/distributed and redundantly the knowledge is preserved within neurons of the
113   network. A higher neuron-level interpretability indicates that more neurons are interpretable or neu-
114   rons are more interpretable (Sajjad et al., 2022). As interpretations of the neurons can help localize
115   the knowledge obtained in a neural network, neuron-level interpretation can be used for editing the
116   knowledge in models (Meng et al., 2022a;b), model pruning and distillation (Belinkov et al., 2020),
117
      adapting the model to different domains and steering the output (Erhan et al., 2009; Rimsky et al.,
      2023), and debugging model errors (Hernandez et al., 2021). Improved neuron-level interpretability
118
      increases reliability and performance in the applications above.
119
120   Sparse Auto-encoders. To enhance interpretability, post-hoc sparse coding methods like dictionary
121   learning (Kreutz-Delgado et al., 2003) are used, but these techniques result in imperfect reconstruc-
122   tions and thus always introduces loss when steering the model (Conmy, 2023; Bricken et al., 2023).
123
      Literature also indicates that sparse-autoencoders are hard to scale, i.e., a dramatic drop in inter-
      pretable features can be observed when models becomes deeper (Kissane, 2024). Additionally, tun-
124
      ing SAE models for larger L values involves extensive hyperparameter tuning and time-consuming
125
      training, requiring multiple metrics (reconstruction rate, L1 loss, number of dead neurons) for re-
126   liable judgment, which can’t be easily optimized with automatic engineering tricks (Bricken et al.,
127   2023).
128
129
      Evaluation of neuron-level interpretability. Metrics now exist to evaluate neuron-level inter-
      pretability in language models, examing if neurons trigger on relevant tokens in given contexts (Bills
130
      et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). Recent works have demonstrated that a small number of circuits
131
      in language models are interpretable (Wang et al., 2022; Chughtai et al., 2023), but comprehend-
132   ing each neuron, out of millions, is vital for thorough model safety audits. Given the prohibitive
133   cost of human evaluation on such a scale, OpenAI introduced an automated metric using large lan-
134   guage models for interpretability assessment (Bills et al., 2023), which Anthropic later refined for
135   sparse activations (Bricken et al., 2023). These methods align closely with human judgment and
136   have gained broad acceptance within the research community (Conmy et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023;
137   Burns et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023). These metrics show that neuron-level interpretability
138   in auto-regressive models is limited (Sajjad et al., 2022), where the popular hypothesis is that neu-
139   rons are superpositions of simpler semantics, which makes them fire (produce a high activation) at
140
      multiple semantically distinct sets of tokens (Elhage et al., 2022).
141   White-box models and structured representation learning. In the domain of structured repre-
142   sentation learning, white-box models stand out for their ability to generate explicit, structured data
143   representations that adhere to specific, desirable configurations such as sparsity and piece-wise lin-
144   earity, as discussed by Gregor and LeCun (2010) and Chan et al. (2022). Within this framework, Yu
145   et al. (2023a) introduced an innovative approach to constructing deep networks based on unrolled
146
      optimization. Specifically, Yu et al. (2023a) proposed the CRATE model, utilizing an information-
      theoretic objective aimed at promoting the compression and sparsity of data towards a predefined
147
      statistical structure. Recently, empirical experiments suggest that the white-box design of CRATE
148
      inherently develops segmentation capabilities from the data representations at both holistic and
149   component levels with supervised training in the vision domain (Yu et al., 2023b), which directly
150   motivates us to further explore the data representations within such architecture for language mod-
151   els. Recent work has also shown that the CRATE framework is scalable: it can be effectively scaled
152   up to comparable performance as Vision Transformer (ViT) with careful engineering (Yang et al.,
153   2024). Furthermore, the fine-tuning performance of the pretrained CRATE model is also proven to
154   be comparable in both the language domain (Yu et al., 2023a) and vision domain (Yang et al., 2024).

228   This section introduces the difference between our work and the original CRATE paper (Yu et al.,
229
      2023a), thus introducing what changes we made to the CRATE architecture. We first note that the
      task in this work is different: we apply the CRATE architecture to the next-token prediction task in the
230
      language domain. while the original CRATE paper applies the architecture to the image classification
231
      task in the vision domain. This difference leads to differences in the architecture design: (i) we apply
232   a causal mask to the original CRATE model to avoid the model seeing tokens after the current token,
233   and (ii) we change the embedding layer and heads of the original CRATE model.
234
235
      Second, we’re interested in interpreting the neurons within CRATE on the next-token prediction task
      and making direct comparisons to the GPT architecture. As neuron-level interpretation is commonly
236
      evaluated on the hidden states in the FFN block of the GPT model (Bills et al., 2023; Bricken et al.,
237
      2023), (iii) we increase the hidden dimension of the ISTA block of the original CRATE model to
238   align with the hidden dimension of the FFN block of the GPT model. We thus call the new ISTA
239   block the ISTA-overcomplete block.

      The built-in sparse coding approach introduces consistent and specific neuron-level behaviors.
433   The strong interpretability of CRATE on the OpenAI top-and-random metric and the Anthropic met-
434   ric, as shown in Figure 4, indicates its consistent behavior on relevant tokens. These two methods
435   contain a large portion of top-activated text excerpts, so they are valid for measuring whether the
436   activations are consistent with the summarized explanation (Bills et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023).
437   Additionally, the larger interpretability gap of CRATE and GPT on the OpenAI random-only metric
438   versus the top-and-random metric highlights the specificity of CRATE in avoiding firing on irrele-
439   vant tokens. The random-only metric usually includes highly irrelevant text excerpts, so it effec-
440   tively measures the capability of the language model to avoid activating on semantically irrelevant
441
      tokens (Bills et al., 2023).
442   Qualitatively, we refer back to the qualitative examples shown in Figure 1. We list three neurons
443   from CRATE (row 1) and GPT (row 2), respectively. For each neuron, we show two top-activated
444   text excerpts and one random excerpt. Results show that CRATE is able to consistently activate on
445   sementically similar tokens within the most relevant text excerpts, and does not activate on random
446   tokens that are semantically distinguished from the top tokens. This promotes a more precise ex-
447
      planation given by the explanation model (Mistral in the figure). On the other hand, GPT is much
      worse at distinguishing tokens from different contexts, because it also has high activations on ran-
448
      dom text excerpts where the semantic meanings deviate from the top activations a lot. As a side
449
      note, we also analyze the activation sparsity of CRATE and GPT in Appendix C.

451   Comparing CRATE to GPT with post-hoc sparse auto-encoders. We follow Bricken et al. (2023)
452   and train SAEs for models with layers L ∈ {1, 2, 3}, using output activations from GPT on the
453
      Pile dataset’s training split, leading to the GPT-SAE model. Details on the SAEs’ architecture and
      training are in Appendix E.
454
455   The interpretability scores of GPT-SAE compared to CRATE and GPT, as depicted in Figure 4, reveal
456   that under the long-context OpenAI metrics, GPT-SAE matches GPT but falls short of CRATE. This
457   is attributed to its neuron activations becoming nearly 99% sparse after sparse auto-encoding, dimin-
458   ishing interpretability in long contexts. Conversely, under the Anthropic metric, GPT-SAE surpasses
459
      both GPT and CRATE in interpretability, corroborating findings in Bricken et al. (2023) that post-hoc
      approaches enhance short-context interpretability, often a sign of mono-semanticity. However, the
460
      interpretability of GPT-SAE on the Anthropic metric decreases significantly when ℓ increases, while
461
      CRATE remains steady. Further qualitative comparisons are can be found in Appendix F.

      layer takes 4 hours when h = 512 and a day when h = 3072 on an A100 GPU.

      6    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
527   In this paper, we demonstrated that replacing the standard transformer architecture with the white-
528   box model CRATE as a foundational architecture significantly improves the interpretability. Our
529   empirical findings on the capability of CRATE to be consistent and distinctive on the neuron-level
530   activations underscore the importance of the white-box design in developing better language foun-
531   dation models, fostering optimism that the introduction of built-in sparse coding approaches will
532   catalyze further advancements in neuron-level interpretations.
533   Despite these findings, we acknowledge that the performance of CRATE is not as good as GPT on
534   the next-token prediction task, which is potentially due to the introduction of the ISTA operator
535   that introduces sparsity. This aligns with previous work suggesting that the performance might drop
536   when explicitly introducing sparsity (Bricken et al., 2023). Future work should investigate towards a
537   better trade-off between performance and interpretability of language models with built-in sparsity.
538   It would also be meaningful to research on more qualitative mechanisms in the white-box language
539
      model and how to use these mechanisms for downstream edits.

741   A     D ETAILS ON THE CRATE A RCHITECTURE
742
743   A.1   PARAMETER S IZE OF CRATE AND GPT
744
745   CRATE is smaller than GPT because of the architecture difference. The vanilla GPT architecture
746
      has two main parameterized blocks: Attention block and MLP block.
747   Parameter size of the MSSA Block. In CRATE, the MSSA block resembles the Attention block, but
748   instead of K, Q, V matrices, we only have one matrix. Therefore, compared to standard transformers,
749   CRATE uses 1/3 of the parameters for the multi-head attention part.
750   Parameter size of the ISTA block. The MLP block in vanilla GPT has one parametric matrix that
751   transforms the input representations to the inner space (usually 4x larger), and another parametric
752   matrix that transforms the inner representations back to the output space (as large as the input space).
753   In CRATE, the MLP block is replaced by the ISTA-overcomplete block, which transforms the input
754   representation to the overcomplete basis (4x larger) and transforms back with the same parametric
755   matrix. Therefore, compared to standard transformers, CRATE uses 1/2 of the parameters for the
      MLP part.

962   The OpenAI random-only metric is the easiest to interpret. As noted by Bills et al. (2023), the

967   and tokens with high real activations. Top-and-random scoring addresses the latter, but causes us to
968
      penalize falsely low simulations more than falsely high simulations, and thus tends to accept overly
      broad explanations.

1254   E.6   S TEERING THE LM OR SAE
1255
1256
       In comparison to post-hoc trained SAEs, built-in sparsification processes, such as the one we pro-
       posed in this paper, have the potential to be steered with perfect fidelity. As visualized in Figure 14,
1257
       post-hoc approaches like SAE require steering the model with the decomposed hidden states h,
1258
       whose encoding and decoding processes are both lossy. An imperfect reconstruction systematically
1259   leads to distortions of the steering signal upon the hidden states, and thus affects downstream appli-
1260   cations of the GPT-SAE model. In contrast, CRATE doesn’t include any approximation that distorts
1261   the steering signal, so the signal can be propagated without loss of fidelity. This conclusion does not
1262   change whether the performance of GPT-SAE outperforms CRATE or not.
