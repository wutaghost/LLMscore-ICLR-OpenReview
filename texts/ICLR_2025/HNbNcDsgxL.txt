009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
012            natural language processing tasks. Still, they are prone to generating hallucina-
013            tions—factually incorrect or fabricated content that can undermine their reliabil-
014            ity, especially in high-stakes domains such as healthcare and legal advisory. In
015            response to this challenge, we propose Delta, a novel inference-time approach that
016            leverages contrastive decoding to mitigate hallucinations without requiring model
017            retraining or additional training data. Delta works by randomly masking portions
018
               of the input prompt, then contrasting the original and masked output distribution
               generated by the model, effectively mitigating hallucinations through inference-
019
               only computations. Delta was evaluated on context-rich QA benchmarks like
020
               SQuAD v1.1 and v2, achieving around 3 and 6 percentage points of improve-
021            ment, respectively. It also showed gains of 7 and 2 percentage points on TriviaQA
022            and Natural Question under-sampling decoding. Delta improved SQuAD v2’s no-
023            answer exact match by over ten percentage points. These findings suggest that
024            Delta is particularly effective when hallucinations arise from contextual ambigu-
025            ity. Delta presents a computationally efficient and scalable solution for reducing
026            hallucinations in real-world LLM applications by focusing on inference-time en-
027            hancements.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The rapid development of large language models (LLMs) Brown et al. (2020) has led to significant
033
      advancements in text generation, natural language processing, and a wide range of real-world appli-
      cations OpenAI et al. (2024). These models, powered by vast datasets and complex architectures,
034
      have become essential tools for translation, summarization, and conversational AI tasks McKenna
035
      et al. (2023). Despite these achievements, LLMs face a critical challenge: their probabilistic and
036   nondeterministic nature often generates ”hallucinated” content Xu et al. (2024). These hallucina-
037   tions manifest as text that may sound plausible but is factually incorrect or fabricated. This poses a
038   significant issue, particularly in high-stakes domains such as healthcare, legal advisory, and scientific
039   research, where the accuracy and reliability of the generated content are paramount.
040
      Hallucinations in LLMs arise from their reliance on patterns learned during training, causing them to
041
      occasionally generate outputs unsupported by the input data or real-world facts Huang et al. (2023).
042   Addressing this issue is crucial for improving the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs, especially
043   as they are increasingly integrated into real-time systems and applications where incorrect informa-
044   tion can lead to severe consequences. In response to this challenge, we introduce Delta, a novel
045   approach designed to identify and mitigate text hallucinations on inference-time computation. Un-
046   like traditional methods Ji et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b); Ouyang et al. (2022) focusing on retraining
047   models or requiring access to additional data, Delta operates solely at inference time, making it com-
048   putationally efficient and easily deployable in real-time systems. Delta’s core innovation lies in its
049   use of contrastive decoding Li et al. (2023a); Chuang et al. (2024), which leverages masked versions
050
      of the input text to contrast plausible outputs against potentially hallucinated ones.
051   Delta builds upon previous work in vision-language models, particularly the method introduced in
052   Leng et al. (2024), which mitigates object hallucinations by applying Gaussian noise to the visual
053   input during contrastive decoding. However, adapting this approach to text is more challenging, as
      directly applying noise to textual input is not feasible. To address this, Delta employs a masking


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      strategy Wettig et al. (2023), where tokens in the input sequence are randomly masked to simulate
055   ambiguity. Delta can filter out hallucinated content more effectively during inference by comparing
056   the model’s predictions on masked versus unmasked inputs.
057
058
      Our experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the Delta method, achieving notable im-
      provements in question-answering accuracy. Specifically, Delta delivered gains of approximately 3
059
      and 6 percentage points on SQuAD v1.1 and v2 Rajpurkar et al. (2016), respectively. It achieved a
060
      14.53 percentage point improvement in the no-answer exact match score on SQuAD v2. For more
061   challenging QA datasets like TriviaQA Joshi et al. (2017) and Natural Questions Kwiatkowski et al.
062   (2019), Delta achieved enhancements of 7 and 2 percentage points under-sampling decoding com-
063   pared to the baseline. These results confirm Delta’s robustness and effectiveness in context-rich
064   datasets, showcasing its ability to mitigate hallucinations and enhance performance.
065
      However, a fundamental limitation of Delta is its marginal effectiveness on tasks without explicit
066
      contextual information. For instance, datasets like CommonsenseQA Talmor et al. (2019) and
067   MMLU, which rely heavily on general or implicit knowledge, showed minimal or no improvements,
068   indicating the method’s specific suitability for context-driven scenarios.

071   2   R ELATED W ORKS
072
073   Recent studies have focused on mitigating hallucinations in large language models (LLMs) and large
074   vision-language models (LVLMs) Hinck et al. (2024), where models tend to generate inaccurate or
075
      irrelevant outputs. In vision-language models, such hallucinations often result from over-reliance on
      language priors or biases embedded in the datasets. For example, in LVLMs, object hallucinations
076
      occur when models predict objects not present in the image due to biased object co-occurrences in
077
      the training data. Several approaches have been developed to address this, including Visual Con-
078   trastive Decoding (VCD) and Instruction Contrastive Decoding (ICD).
079
080   The Visual Contrastive Decoding (VCD) method aims to reduce object hallucinations by contrasting
081
      the outputs generated from original and distorted visual inputs. This approach does not require
      additional training or external pre-trained models, making it a computationally efficient solution. By
082
      introducing visual uncertainty, such as Gaussian noise, the method identifies and mitigates instances
083
      where the model overly relies on language priors or statistical biases from the training data, thereby
084   reducing hallucinations Leng et al. (2024).
085
086   Similarly, the Instruction Contrastive Decoding (ICD) technique is employed to tackle hallucina-
087
      tions in multimodal tasks by incorporating instruction disturbances. This method manipulates the
      confidence of multimodal alignment in the model’s visual and textual inputs, helping it differentiate
088
      between hallucinated and relevant tokens. By applying contrastive penalties to tokens influenced by
089
      instruction disturbances, ICD effectively reduces the generation of hallucinated outputs, especially
090   in complex visual contexts Leng et al. (2024).
091
092   In addition, the work context-aware decoding (CAD) has Shi et al. (2024) demonstrated a similar
093
      outcome to our Delta method by adjusting the output probabilities of LMs, amplifying the differ-
      ences between outputs generated with and without the given context. This contrastive approach
094
      encourages models to prioritize contextual information during text generation. Notably, CAD can
095
      be applied to pre-trained LMs without additional training. Unlike our Delta method, the method is
096   mainly based on context-driven datasets, making it less generalizable than the Delta method, which,
097   in theory, could apply to all textual inputs.
098
099
      Both approaches are part of a growing body of work exploring contrastive mechanisms and fine-
      grained multimodal alignment techniques to mitigate hallucinations in models that integrate vision
100
      and language processing. Future research will likely explore more robust mechanisms further to
101
      improve model reliability across different types of multimodal tasks.

106   The work introduces Delta, a novel method that effectively mitigates hallucinations in text-based
107   large-language models. The core idea behind Delta is to address the issue of hallucinations by ma-
      nipulating the inference process itself. Specifically, the method generates output tokens from the

      model using a standard inference procedure, as outlined in Equation 1. Building on hypotheses in-
109   spired by Leng et al. (2024), which suggest that incomplete prompting or missing information tends
110   to amplify hallucination effects, Delta aims to mitigate this by leveraging a contrastive decoding
111   approach.
112
      Delta dynamically adjusts for incomplete information in this approach by comparing the outputs
113
      generated from masked and unmasked input versions. The key concept behind Delta is as follows:
114
      by randomly masking input tokens, the model generates outputs that are more likely to be filled with
115   hallucinated information. Then, by subtracting the hallucinated logits (generated from the masked
116   input) from the original logits, Delta extracts the ”clean” logits—those less influenced by halluci-
117   nated content. This process significantly reduces the likelihood of hallucinations, as demonstrated
118   in Figure 1, leading to more accurate and reliable outputs in context-dependent tasks.

      3.1    I NFERENCE FROM L ANGUAGE M ODEL D ECODER
143
144   In large language models (LLMs), the inference process involves predicting the next token in a
145   sequence based on the previously generated tokens. Given an input sequence x and generated tokens
146   y, we can have z = [x0 , x1 , . . . , xn−1 , y1 , . . . , yt−1 ] where n is the index of the sequence, the
147   conditional probability of the next token yt at time step t is modeled as:
148
149
                                       Pθ (yt | z) = softmax (logitθ (yt | z))                              (1)
150
151
      In this equation, the model generates tokens sequentially, where the logits are computed using the
152   model’s parameters θ. This process is crucial for autoregressive tasks like text generation, where
153   each token is conditioned on all the tokens that came before it.
154

      3.2    E FFECT OF F UZZIFIED (M ASKED ) T EXT ON H ALLUCINATIONS
156
157   Masking portions of input text in large language models can exacerbate hallucinations. For instance,
158   consider the sentence: ”There is a moldy banana on the table. The color of the banana is ””. If the
159   word ”moldy” is masked and replaced with the token ”MASK,” the sentence becomes ”There is a
160   MASK banana on the table. The color of the banana is.” In this case, a model that relies heavily
161   on its pre-trained knowledge may output a high logit value for ”yellow,” a common association for
      bananas, even though ”brown” would be the more accurate color based on the original context. This


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      behavior highlights how the model might default to its prior associations when deprived of important
163   contextual cues, leading to an output that is factually incorrect or ”hallucinated.”
164
165   The issue stems from the model’s tendency to draw upon its pre-trained knowledge to fill the missing
166
      context. Without access to specific context or visual input, the model resorts to typical patterns
      learned during pre-training, such as associating bananas with the color ”yellow,” a more frequently
167
      seen context in training data. This reliance on default associations can result in outputs that are
168
      not grounded in the given input but instead reflect generalized patterns, which may not always be
169   accurate. As illustrated in Figure 1, this effect occurs when the masking introduces ambiguity or
170   removes essential information, leading the model to generate plausible yet erroneous answers that
171   fail to account for the specific context of the prompt.

174
175   In this process, we introduce ambiguity by randomly masking a portion of tokens within the input
176   sequence. Given an input sequence x = [x0 , x1 , . . . , xn−1 ], where n is the length of the sequence,

178   be masked are selected randomly, and the total number of masked tokens is determined by m =
179   ⌊rmask · n⌋, where rmask ∈ [0, 1] represents the masking ratio. The indices of the masked tokens are
180
      randomly selected and gathered into the set Imask = {i0 , i1 , . . . , im }.

192   3.4   C ONTRASTIVE D ECODING
193
194   The Delta method leverages contrastive decoding to enhance inference accuracy and reduce halluci-
195   nations in generated outputs. The key idea is to compare the predictions from masked and unmasked
196   input sequence versions during token generation. At each time step t, the model generates the next
197
      token yt by conditioning on the unmasked sequence z = [x0 , . . . , xn−1 , y1 , . . . , yt−1 ] as well as its
      masked counterpart mask(z) where the n is the index of the sequence x. The contrastive decoding
198
      process can be formalized in equation 3.
199
200
201                Pdelta (yt | z) = softmax [(1 + α) · logitθ (yt | z) − α · logitθ (yt | mask(z))]           (3)
202
203   In this equation, α ∈ [0, 1] (logit ratio) is a tunable hyperparameter that adjusts the relative sig-
204
      nificance of the masked logits. By subtracting the masked logits, which tend to induce stronger
      hallucinations, the method effectively reduces the influence of hallucinated token values in the orig-
205
      inal logits. The non-hallucinated tokens increase through (1 + α) · logitθ (yt | z). As the model
206
      prioritizes more plausible predictions from the unmasked context, this incrementation of probabil-
207   ities for non-hallucinated tokens leads to a more accurate and reliable output. This implies that a
208   higher α value can filter higher levels of hallucinations and amplify the level of non-hallucinated
209   tokens.

223   3.6    C OMPUTING D ELTA FOR C ONTRASTIVE D ECODING
224
      Finally, the Delta method is computed during contrastive decoding. The core idea involves adjusting
225
      the logits for token generation by contrasting predictions from unmasked and masked versions of
226
      the input sequence. The following conditional equation defines how tokens are sampled at each time
227   step t, represented by yt :
228
229
230       yt ∼ softmax [(1 + α) · logitθ (y | z) − α · logitθ (y | mask(z))] , subject to yt ∈ Vhead (z<t )   (5)
231
232   Here, the sequence z, which includes previously generated tokens, is checked against a set of plau-
233   sible sequences Vhead (x<i ). If z is deemed plausible, the model generates a token using modified
234   logits, where the contribution of the unmasked sequence is amplified by a factor of (1 + α), and the
235   contribution of the masked sequence is penalized by a factor of α. The resulting logits are passed
236   through a softmax function to produce a probability distribution over the next possible tokens. If z
237   does not belong to Vhead (x<i ), the probability of generating a token is set to zero. This contrastive
238   decoding mechanism enhances the model’s ability to reduce hallucinations by favoring contextually
239   relevant token predictions over potentially misleading ones.

248   4.1    E VALUATION DATASETS
249
250   To evaluate our method comprehensively, we selected diverse datasets that target various aspects
251   of language model performance. These datasets are categorized based on their inclusion of con-
252   text, question types, and the challenges they pose, providing a robust foundation for assessing the
253   effectiveness of our approach.
254   The Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) Rajpurkar et al. (2016) is widely used for train-
255   ing and evaluating machine reading comprehension models. SQuAD v1.1 contains over 100,000
256   question-answer pairs, with answers found directly in the text, while SQuAD v2 introduces over
257   50,000 unanswerable questions. This additional challenge makes SQuAD v2 particularly valuable
258   for hallucination testing. Unanswerable questions allow us to assess if a model can correctly avoid
259   generating answers when no relevant information is present.
260   TriviaQA is a large-scale question-answering dataset comprising over 650,000 question-answer
261   pairs from trivia quizzes Joshi et al. (2017). It is more challenging than SQuAD because the an-
262   swers can be spread across long and complex documents, including Wikipedia articles and web
263   documents. The dataset evaluates models’ ability to retrieve answers from longer, less structured
264   texts. Google’s Natural Questions (NQ) dataset Kwiatkowski et al. (2019) contains questions that
265   users naturally ask in Google search queries. The answers are retrieved from long Wikipedia docu-
266
      ments, and only a tiny portion of the document is directly relevant to the answer. This dataset tests
      models on open-domain question answering, requiring retrieval and comprehension of long papers
267
      with minimal direct context.
268
269   The four datasets mentioned above all include contextual information, and our method is expected
      to demonstrate significant improvements in them. In contrast, we also prepared two standard

      7   S UMMARY AND F UTURE W ORKS
408
409
      This study introduces Delta, an inference-time method designed to mitigate hallucinations in large
      language models without requiring additional fine-tuning. Delta operates by randomly masking in-
410
      put tokens to identify hallucination-prone logits and then subtracting these from the original logits,
411
      effectively reducing the influence of hallucinations. Experimental results demonstrate Delta’s effec-
412   tiveness in context-rich question-answering tasks, achieving significant performance improvements
413   across datasets such as SQuAD, TriviaQA, and Natural Questions. However, its impact is limited in
414   context-free tasks like CommonsenseQA and MMLU, where the model relies solely on pre-trained
415   knowledge instead of external context. These findings position Delta as a powerful solution tailored
416   for context-dependent tasks, offering valuable insights for reducing hallucinations in real-world ap-
417   plications.
418   Delta employs a straightforward random masking method, which has proven effective but leaves
419   room for improvement. Future research will focus on developing advanced and adaptive masking
420   strategies. One promising direction is targeted masking, prioritizing critical tokens such as proper
421   nouns and key terms rather than applying masking uniformly. Additionally, leveraging techniques
422   like part-of-speech tagging to prioritize tokens of higher informational value, such as nouns and
423   verbs, could refine the method further. These approaches could enhance Delta’s adaptability, making
424   it more robust across diverse QA scenarios.
