009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many tools exist that attempt to detect dependence between random variables, a
012            core question across a wide range of machine learning, statistical, and scientific
013            endeavors. Although several statistical tests guarantee eventual detection of any
014            dependence with enough samples, standard tests may require an exorbitant amount
015            of samples for detecting subtle dependencies between high-dimensional random
016            variables with complex distributions. In this work, we study two related ways
017            to learn powerful independence tests. First, we show how to construct powerful
018
               statistical tests with finite-sample validity by using variational estimators of mutual
               information, such as the InfoNCE or NWJ estimators. Second, we establish a
019
               close relationship between these variational mutual information-based tests and
020
               tests based on the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC), showing that
021            learning a variational bound in the former case is closely related to learning kernels,
022            typically parameterized by deep networks, in the latter. Finally, we show how to
023            find a representation that maximizes the asymptotic power of an HSIC test, proving
024            that this procedure works and demonstrating empirically the practical improvement
025            of our tests (with HSIC tests generally outperforming the variational ones) on
026            difficult problems of detecting structured dependence.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Independence testing, the question of using paired samples to determine whether a random variable
032   X and another Y are associated with one another or if they are statistically independent, is one of
033
      the most common tasks across scientific and data-based fields. Traditional methods make strong
      parametric assumptions, for instance assuming that X and Y are jointly normal so that dependence is
034
      characterized by covariance, and/or operate only in limited settings, for instance the tabular setting
035
      of the celebrated χ2 test or Fisher’s exact test. Applying these approaches to high-dimensional
036   continuous data is difficult at best.
037
038   One characterization of independence is the (Shannon) mutual information (MI): this quantity is zero
039
      if two random variables are independent, and positive if they are dependent. Substantial effort has
      been made in estimating this quantity with a variety of estimators; see e.g. the broad list based on
040
      binning, nearest-neighbors, kernel density estimation, and so on implemented by Szabó (2014). In
041
      high dimensions, however, recent work has focused on estimating variational bounds defined by deep
042   networks (see e.g. Poole et al., 2019), which can ideally learn problem-specific structure. To our
043   knowledge, this class of estimators has not yet been used to construct statistical tests of independence:
044   in particular, if we run the estimation algorithm and estimate a lower bound on the MI of 0.12, does
045   that mean that the variables are (perhaps weakly) dependent, or might that be due to random noise on
046   the samples we saw? The first contribution of this paper is to construct and evaluate tests of this form.
047
      Mutual information, though, is notoriously difficult to estimate (Paninski, 2003). If the question we
048   want to ask is “are X and Y dependent,” we can also consider turning to a different characterization
049   of dependence which may be statistically easier to estimate. The Hilbert-Schmidt Independence
050   Criterion (HSIC), introduced by Gretton et al. (2005), measures the total cross-covariance between
051   feature representations of X and Y , and can be estimated from samples efficiently. The construction
052   supports even infinite-dimensional features in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), equivalent
053   to choosing a kernel function. With appropriate choices of kernel (see Szabó & Sriperumbudur,
      2018), the HSIC is also zero if and only if X ⊥

      Alternatively, we can characterize an independence problem as a two-sample one. Two-sample
      problems are concerned with the question: given samples from P and Q, is P = Q? Under this
076
      framework, when we consider samples from the joint distributions Pxy and Px × Py , the two-sample
077
      problem characterizes an equivalent independence problem between variables X and Y . One way we
078   can measure the discrepancy between Pxy and Px × Py is with a class of distances on probability
079   measures called integral probability metrics (IPM). Defining an IPM using critic functions in an
080   RKHS, we recover the kernel-based Maximum-Mean Discrepancy (MMD) Gretton et al. (2012a).
081
      Any reasonable choice of kernel, such as the Gaussian kernel with unit bandwidth or the distance
082
      kernel that recovers distance covariance, will eventually be able to detect any fixed dependence with
083
      enough samples. In practice, however, this scheme can perform extremely poorly; if the data varies
084   on a very different scale, it will take exorbitant quantities of data to achieve any reasonable test power.
085
086   Thus, tests using Gaussian kernels often rely on the median heuristic to choose a kernel relevant to
087
      the data at hand: choosing a bandwidth based on the median pairwise distance among data points
      (Gretton et al., 2012a). While this is a reasonable first guess for many data types, there exist datasets
088
      where it can be dramatically better to instead select a bandwidth that optimizes a measure of test
089
      power (Sutherland et al., 2017). Beyond that, there exist many distributions where no Gaussian
090   kernel performs well – for instance, many problems on natural image data or involving sparsity – but
091   Gaussian kernels applied to latent representations of such data do. As an extreme example, consider
092   random variables whose twelfth through fifteenth decimal places are always equal. Representations
093   based on Euclidean distance will require an exorbitant number of samples to detect dependence, but a
094   representation that extracts only the relevant decimal places will do so very quickly.
095
      This concept similarly applies to tests based on mutual information. In fact, we can view estimation
096   of a variational MI bound as learning a representation of the data that maximizes its measure of depen-
097   dency. Thus, learning representations of the data that make any dependency more explicit is central to
098   developing more powerful independence tests. Our final contribution involves developing a scheme
099   to learn these optimal representations for both kernel-based and certain MI-based independence tests.
100
      Our kernel-based method method involves learning the representation that maximizes the asymptotic
101
      power of a test based on HSIC. This expression is dominated by the signal-to-noise ratio of HSIC to
102   its standard deviation. We show that we can estimate this quantity from finite samples in quadratic
103   time, and that it is consistent. We then construct a valid test via data splitting and permutation testing
104   (e.g. Rindt et al., 2021). Our overall approach builds on prior work in kernel two-sample testing
105   (Gretton et al., 2012b; Jitkrittum et al., 2016; Sutherland et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020), but translated
106   to the independence testing setting. Our comprehensive experiments also provide empirical evidence
107   that a reframing of the MMD-based two-sample test into an independence test is not optimal; a
      phenomenon we attribute to the statistical properties of both estimators.

      We wish to solve this problem without making strong (parametric) assumptions about the form of Pxy ,
116
      Px , or Py . Most independence tests are based on estimating the “amount” of dependence between
117
      X and Y , or equivalently the discrepancy between between Pxy and Px × Py . Given a nonnegative
118   quantity which is zero if X ⊥  ⊥ Y , we can reject H0 if our estimate is large enough that we are
119   confident the true value is positive.
120
121
      The most famous such quantity is the (Shannon) mutual information. It can be defined as the
      Kullback-Liebler divergence of Pxy from Px × Py , and is zero if and only if X ⊥ ⊥ Y ; equivalently, it
122

123
      based roughly on various forms of density estimation (see e.g. Szabó, 2014), as discussed above
124   these can fail to detect subtle or structured forms of dependence with reasonable numbers of samples.
125   Variational estimators of mutual information give an opportunity for problem-specific representations.
126   As an example, consider the following lower bound (van den Oord et al., 2018; Poole et al., 2019),

      The Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC, Gretton et al., 2005) is also zero if and only if
165   X and Y are independent when an appropriate kernel is chosen. Unlike the mutual information, it is
166   easy to estimate from samples. We will expand on its relationship to mutual information in Section 4.
167
168
      To define HSIC, we first briefly review positive-definite kernels. A (real-valued) kernel is a function
      k : X × X → R which can be expressed as the inner product between feature maps ϕ : X → F,
169
      k(x, x′ ) = ⟨ϕ(x), ϕ(x′ )⟩F , where F is any Hilbert space. A special case is F = Rp and k(x, x′ ) =
170
      ϕ(x) · ϕ(x′ ). For every kernel function, there exists a unique space called the reproducing kernel
171   Hilbert space (RKHS), which consists of functions f : X → R. The key reproducing property of an
172   RKHS F states that for any function f ∈ F and any point x ∈ X , we have ⟨f, ϕ(x)⟩F = f (x).
173
174

      MMD-based independence test. Alternatively to independence tests based on HSIC, we can
394
      reformulate the independence problem into a two-sample one, by asking if paired samples Z = (X, Y )
395
      and Z̃ = (X̃, Ỹ ) drawn from Pxy and Px × Py come from the same distribution. To simulate from the
396
      null distribution Px × Py , we can shuffle the samples Y = (y1 , ..., ym ) by some permutation group
397
      σ : [m] → [m] so that Ỹ = (yσ(1) , ..., yσ(m) ), thereby breaking dependence. With this framing, we
398
      can instead maximize the SNR for the corresponding MMD-based two-sample problem, as done by
399
      Liu et al. (2020). Although HSIC is theoretically identical to MMD for specific choice of kernels
400   (Proposition 2), we find that the statistical properties of these estimators are different, particularly
401   with respect to how estimation under the null distribution Px × Py is performed.
402
403
      In particular, we notice that MMD estimators based on a single shuffling of the samples exhibit
      large variance compared to HSIC, which we detail in Appendix C.4. Moreover, as we consider more
404
      shufflings of the data, the biased MMD estimator converges to the biased HSIC estimator. This
405
      suggests that HSIC estimators handle paired samples more efficiently than its MMD counterpart.
406   Intuitively, this is because HSIC natively considers all possible sample pairings (xi , yj ) of the data
407   when estimating with respect to Px × Py , whereas MMD with a single shuffling is only able to
408   consider m such pairs. We explore this phenomenon in more detail in Appendix C.3.

531      7                    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
         Independence testing aims to see if two paired random variables are statistically independent. We
533
         explored two families of tests to address this problem. The first are tests based on variational mutual
534      information estimators, which, to the best of our knowledge, we are the first to construct. The second
535      are tests based on maximizing the asymptotic power, which was explored for the two-sample problem
536      but not for independence testing. Our findings show that learning representations of the data via our
537      proposed methods lead to powerful tests, with HSIC-based tests generally outperforming MI-based
538      ones. Future work may look to extend this learning scheme to conditional independence testing and
539      apply this to causal discovery. Meanwhile, we may also look into approaches for mitigating, or even
         removing, the data-splitting procedure as done by Biggs et al. (2023) and Kübler et al. (2020).

       D.1   T RAINING & T EST D ETAILS
1243
1244   We design the featurizers ϕω and ϕκ of our deep kernels kω and lκ to be neural networks with ReLU
1245   activations. We avoid using normalization as it may affect test power. Moreover, we make the
1246   Gaussian bandwidth of both kω and lκ a learnable parameter, as well as the smoothing rate ϵ. To
1247   make comparisons as fair as possible, we use similar neural network architectures for each deep
1248   learning based method. In general, we let the featurizer of HSIC-D and MMD-D be identical up to
1249   a concatenation layer which concatenates X and Y to frame the problem as a two-sample test. We
1250
       construct the C2ST-S/L classifier as the MMD-D featurizer plus a linear layer classification head with
       scalar output, and we let both C2ST-S/L and InfoNCE use identical architectures for the classifier
1251
       and critic. Detailed descriptions of each architecture are demonstrated in the following subsections.
1252
1253   All optimization-based methods (HSIC-D/Dx/O, MMD-D, C2ST-S/L, InfoNCE) are first trained on
1254   an identical split of the data, and then tested on the remaining split. In contrast, HSIC-M selects
1255   the median bandwidth based on the entire dataset, and is evaluated on the test set. We train our
1256
       models using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4 over 1,000 epochs for HDGM
       and RatInABox and 10,000 epochs for Sinusoid. We use a batch size of 512. All methods are
1257
       implemented in PyTorch and trained on a NVIDIA A100SXM4 GPU.
1258
1259   Once learned, each methods’ empirical power is evaluated on 100 test sets (SZte1 , ..., SZte100 ). Each
1260   test set contains m test samples SZtei = (Z1tei , ..., ZN
                                                               tei
                                                                   ), which are then used to compute the average
1261   rejection rate under the null via a permutation test. We use 500 permutations for each test and with a
1262   predetermined type-I error rate of 0.05.
