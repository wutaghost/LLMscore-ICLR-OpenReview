009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the dose-response relation between a continuous treatment and the
012            outcome for an individual can greatly drive decision-making, particularly in areas
013            like personalized drug dosing and personalized healthcare interventions. Point
014            estimates are often insufficient in these high-risk environments, highlighting the
015            need for uncertainty quantification to support informed decisions. Conformal
016            prediction, a distribution-free and model-agnostic method for uncertainty quan-
017
               tification, has seen limited application in continuous treatments or dose-response
               models. To address this gap, we propose a novel methodology that frames the
018
               causal dose-response problem as a covariate shift, leveraging weighted conformal
019
               prediction. By incorporating propensity estimation, conformal predictive systems,
020            and likelihood ratios, we present a practical solution for generating prediction
021            intervals for dose-response models. Additionally, our method approximates local
022            coverage for every treatment value by applying kernel functions as weights in
023            weighted conformal prediction. Finally, we use a new synthetic benchmark dataset
024            to demonstrate the significance of covariate shift assumptions in achieving robust
025            prediction intervals for dose-response models.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      How can we determine the optimal dose for a patient to ensure the best therapeutic outcome? What
      is the impact of discounts in an online store on sales? What impact does CO2 concentration have
031
      on local climates? At the core of each of these questions lies a shared causal idea: understanding
032
      the dose-response relation under continuous treatments to inform decision-making. In many cases,
033   these decisions bear significant consequences, where relying solely on point estimates may be insuffi-
034   cient (Feuerriegel et al., 2024). Particularly in high-stakes situations, augmenting predictions with
035   uncertainty quantification (UQ) can significantly improve decision-making processes (Feuerriegel
036   et al., 2024). For instance, while the estimated causal effect of a continuous treatment may appear
037   positive, prediction intervals could suggest a largely negative outcome for a specific individual. Such
038   insights are crucial for deciding interventions. To tackle this, conformal prediction (CP) offers
039   a robust solution for UQ, being both distribution-free and model-agnostic, with formal coverage
040   guarantees (Vovk et al., 2022).
041   In this work, we seek to extend CP to UQ in dose-response models, aiming to aid decision-makers
042   with more informed estimates to tackle such questions. We introduce a novel approach for deriving
043   prediction intervals in the continuous treatment setting using weighted conformal prediction by
044   combining propensity estimation with weighted conformal predictive systems. Furthermore, with the
045   aid of a novel synthetic benchmark, we show how viewing the problem as a covariate shift approach
046   provides coverage across all treatment values to help create more individualized dose-response curves.
047
048   2   BACKGROUND
049
050   In this paper we expand upon the potential outcomes framework introduced in Rubin (2005), otherwise
051   known as the Rubin framework to accommodate continuous treatments. Consider a continuous
052   treatment variable T ∈ [tL , tU ] with a lower bound tL and upper bound tU , observed covariates
053   X, and potential outcomes Y (t) ∈ R representing the outcome that would be observed under
      treatment level t. The Conditional Average Dose-Response Function (CADRF) is defined as ν(x, t) =


                                                        1
054
      E[Y (t)|X = x], the expected value over the Individual Dose-Response Functions (IDRF) for all
055   individuals with observed X. Similar to Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE), to estimate
056   the CADRF we make the following standard assumptions (Rubin, 2005; Hirano & Imbens, 2004):
057
058          • Unconfoundedness: Y (t) ⊥    ⊥ T |X, ∀t ∈ T . This assumption states that, conditional on
059            the observed covariates, the treatment assignment is independent of the potential outcomes.
060            In other words, there are no unobserved confounders that influence both the treatment
061
               assignment and the outcome.
062          • Overlap or positivity: 0 < P (T = t|X = x) < 1, ∀t ∈ T with x ∈ X. The overlap
063            assumption ensures that for every covariate value x, there is a positive probability of
064
               receiving any treatment level. This is crucial for estimating treatment effects across the
               entire range of treatment levels.
065
066          • Consistency: Y = Y (t) with probability 1. This assumption links the observed outcomes to
067            the potential outcomes, stating that the observed outcome is equal to the potential outcome
068
               corresponding to the treatment received.
069   Quantifying the IDRF requires observing the Y (t) for all possible treatment values. These treatment
070   values are all counterfactuals and thus impossible to observe as we only can observe Y for a single
071   treatment value t at a time. Furthermore for estimating the CADRF, likewise with CATE estimation,
072   the distribution of the treatment assignment can bias the estimation (Hirano & Imbens, 2004). This
073   distribution of the treatment assignment is called the propensity distribution, which was initially
074   defined for binary treatments. Hirano & Imbens (2004) introduced the generalized propensity score
075   (GPS) for continuous treatments that aims to unbias the CATE estimation for continuous treatments.
076   The GPS is defined as π(ti |x) = fT |X (T = ti |X = x), which is the evaluation of T = ti on
077   the conditional probability density function T |X (Hirano & Imbens, 2004). If the treatment is
078
      independent of X, i.e. there are no confounders that influence treatment assignment, then fT |X is
      equal for all possible X. Furthermore, the treatment assignment is considered uniformly assigned
079
      between lower tL and upper tU possible treatment if fT |X represents the density function of the
080
      uniform distribution between tL and tU . The GPS can then be used to mimic the randomly assigned
081   treatment to estimate the unbiased CADRF (Wu et al., 2024).
082
083   The simplest method to estimate the CADRF is using an S-learner where a single learner is fit on
084
      both the covariates X and the treatment T to estimate Y . This approach provides a CADRF for each
      specific sample by keeping the covariates X constant and changing T to all different treatment values.
085
      However, if the treatment in the data is not uniformly assigned then the epistemic error can increase
086
      for specific treatment values ti and X = x in low overlap regions or where π(ti |x) becomes very
087   small. Consequently inferring T = ti in these regions would yield unreliable model estimates which
088   should be communicated to ensure correct usage of a CADRF model.
089

      individuals having the same covariates. ϵe,IDRF (x, t) symbolises the epistemic uncertainty coming
      from model specification and finite samples. Estimating both uncertainties creates the opportunity to

105   3    R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Our proposed solution combines three different domains: propensity score methods, conformal
      prediction, and treatment effect or dose-response modelling.


                                                          2
108
      Propensity score methods, introduced by Rosenbaum & Rubin (1983), have become widespread
109   in causal inference, especially in observational studies. These methods aim to balance confounders
110   across treatment groups, reducing bias in treatment effect estimates. Hirano & Imbens (2004) gen-
111   eralized this propensity score to continuous instead of binary treatments, introducing the generalized
112   propensity score and building the foundation for causal inference with continuous exposures. Wu
113   et al. (2024) used the generalized propensity score for matching continuous treatments to debias
114   the treatment assignment and more accurately estimate the average dose-response curve for all
115   treatment values. Other approaches adapt machine learning techniques to dose-response modelling.
116   For instance, Athey et al. (2019) developed generalized random forests for heterogeneous treatment
117
      effect estimation, adaptable to continuous treatments.
118   To provide UQ, this work adapts conformal prediction. Conformal prediction is a model-agnostic
119   method introduced by Vovk et al. (2022) that constructs prediction intervals with guaranteed finite-
120   sample coverage under distribution-free assumptions. Conformal prediction uses conformity scores
121   to assess uncertainty. Various improvements, such as the adaptive version by Romano et al. (2019),
122   have increased the flexibility and applicability to even heteroscedastic settings. Additionally, Lei et al.
123
      (2018) and Papadopoulos et al. (2002) introduced split conformal prediction, significantly improving
      computational efficiency. For scenarios involving covariate or distribution shifts, Tibshirani et al.
124
      (2019) introduced weighted conformal prediction to ensure coverage under mismatched training and
125
      testing data distributions, with additional work by Gibbs & Candes (2021; 2024) and Barber et al.
126   (2023). By reweighting the calibration samples similar to weighted conformal prediction, Guan
127   (2023) introduced localized conformal prediction where the prediction intervals are determined by
128   calibration samples localized around the test sample. Vovk et al. (2019) also introduced conformal
129   predictive systems (CPS); an extension of full conformal prediction that allows extracting predictive
130   distributions instead of prediction intervals. More recently, Jonkers et al. (2024a) combined previous
131   concepts, introducing weighted conformal predictive systems to also account for covariate shifts.
132   In causal inference, conformal prediction has mainly been applied to binary treatments. For instance,
133   Lei & Candès (2021) were among the first to apply conformal prediction to treatment effects estima-
134   tion in randomized experiments and confounded or observational data. Jonkers et al. (2024b) and Alaa
135   & Ahmad (2024) extended this approach to the potential outcomes framework, providing uncertainty
136   to quantify individual treatment effects. However, the use of conformal prediction in continuous
137   treatment settings remains largely unexplored. Schröder et al. (2024) proposed a conformal prediction
138   framework for prediction intervals of treatment effects for continuous treatment interventions. How-
139   ever, their approach mainly covers single-treatment interventions and is computationally intensive,
140
      requiring optimization per confidence level, treatment, and sample where they provide prediction
      intervals for a single treatment value. For a more in-depth analysis of Schröder et al. (2024), see
141
      Appendix E.
142
143   Our goal is to achieve predictive coverage across the entire range of the treatment variable in
144   estimating the dose-response curve. To our knowledge, no existing UQ methods offer conformal
145   prediction guarantees for dose-response models with continuous treatments. To address this gap, we
146
      propose a novel methodology that seeks to provide this coverage by integrating weighted conformal
      prediction with propensity score weighting thereby guaranteeing coverage for any treatment value in
147
      continuous treatment dose-response models.

      4.1.2      W EIGHTED C ONFORMAL P REDICTION
217
218   Evaluating and requiring coverage guarantees for the dose-response model at all possible treatment
219   values changes the test distribution compared to the training distribution. In the training data, all
220   treatment values are sampled according to their (conditional) training distribution, which can be
221   determined by other variables in the case of confounding. However, every treatment value is possible
222
      in testing, and thus, every treatment sample can be sampled. This mimics sampling a new test
      sample with the treatment value from a uniform distribution, which can be vastly different from the
223
      treatment distribution in the training data. Standard conformal prediction only guarantees coverage
224

225   which is called the exchangeability assumption (Vovk et al., 2022; Tibshirani et al., 2019). This issue

      5.1     S YNTHETIC DATA
334
335
      We evaluate the proposed approach on synthetic data as evaluating the true individual dose-response
336   curve requires knowing the counterfactuals which is not feasible in real-world data.
337
338   We used three experimental setups using synthetic data, each having different scenarios that change
339
      specific parameters. Setup 1 is inspired by Wu et al. (2024) and Setup 2 follows the experimental setup
      of Schröder et al. (2024). Both Setup 1 and 2 are clarified in Appendix B. Setup 3 is novel, proposed
340
      by us, which mimics a situation where, for every scenario, two different possible dose-response
341
      functions are possible that each depends on the covariates, resulting in heavy confounding and thus
342   limited overlap.
343
344   For each scenario (over the different setups), 5000 samples were generated using 50 different random
345
      seeds resulting in 50 datasets for each scenario. These datasets were split into 25% test (1250), 25%
      calibration (1250), and 50% training (2500) samples. For each scenario, two different α (significance
346
      values) were evaluated (i.e., 0.1 and 0.05 for a confidence of 90% and 95% resp.). Each sample in the
347
      test set is evaluated using 40 treatment values t0 at equal intervals between the 2% and 98% training

      6       C ONCLUSION
527
      In this work, we have introduced a novel approach to weighted conformal prediction for UQ in dose-
528
      response models, utilizing propensity estimation and kernel functions as weights for the likelihood
529   ratio. Alongside a newly proposed synthetic dataset, our approach highlights the necessity of
530   compensating for the covariate shift in the treatment assignment when evaluating dose-response
531   models across all possible treatment values. This is achieved by assuming uniform treatment
532   sampling during testing, similar to methods used in discrete treatment effect estimation. Additionally,
533   by leveraging conformal predictive systems to estimate propensity distributions, we offer a practical
534   solution to implement UQ in continuous dose-response estimation for various practical use cases.
535   Our contribution not only adds to the field of dose-response modelling but also facilitates delivering
536   reliable, individualized dose-response functions. Our approach has the potential to aid decision-
537   making for personalized dosing in fields such as marketing, policy-making, and healthcare. With this
538   UQ for continuous treatments, we are one step closer to achieving truly personalized interventions
539   that optimize outcomes for individuals.

             :::::::::::::::::::::

      :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::

       D.2    A PPLICATIONS
              :::::::::::::
973
974    The  classic application is in drug dosing, where the goal is to construct a dose-response curve for
       ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
975    every  individual to facilitate decision-making when determining an optimal dose for a new patient.
       ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
976    In a clinical trial, especially phase 1 and phase 2 where the optimal dose is being determined, the
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
977    weighted   conformal    dose-response curve can also act as a tool to analyse the results individually
       ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
978    while  having  an  estimate of the uncertainty estimates that is not biased by the treatment assignment
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
979    distribution.  It quantifies uncertainty for individual predictions, compensating for any treatment
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
980
       distribution  bias. Furthermore, it highlights areas with insufficient data support with infinite
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
       prediction  intervals, guiding decisions about whether further trials or treatments are necessary for
       ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
981
       specific patient  subgroups.  In the regions where there is support, the model predictions provide the
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
982
       CADRF     estimate for this patient and the uncertainty regions show how the outcome would vary.
       ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
983
984    Treatment is not limited to healthcare. Treatment can be generalized as any intervention or action
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
985    which  opens applications in other domains. For example, in predictive maintenance, the model
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
986
       can optimize decisions by estimating the effect of operating pressure on the remaining useful life of
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
       equipment like valves. Similarly, in sales, it can help determine the ideal discount for specific clients
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
987
       to maximize the sold units, demonstrating flexibility in various domains.
       :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
