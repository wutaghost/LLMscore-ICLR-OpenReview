009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable progress in health-
012            care. However, a significant gap remains regarding LLMs’ professionalism in
013            domain-specific clinical practices, limiting their application in real-world diag-
014            nostics. In this work, we introduce Z ODIAC, an LLM-powered framework with
015            cardiologist-level professionalism designed to engage LLMs in cardiological diag-
016            nostics. Z ODIAC assists cardiologists by extracting clinically relevant characteris-
017
               tics from patient data, detecting significant arrhythmias, and generating preliminary
               reports for the review and refinement by cardiologists. To achieve cardiologist-level
018
               professionalism, Z ODIAC is built on a multi-agent collaboration framework, en-
019
               abling the processing of patient data across multiple modalities. Each LLM agent
020            is fine-tuned using real-world patient data adjudicated by cardiologists, reinforcing
021            the model’s professionalism. Z ODIAC undergoes rigorous clinical validation with
022            independent cardiologists, evaluated across eight metrics that measure clinical ef-
023            fectiveness and address security concerns. Results show that Z ODIAC outperforms
024            industry-leading models, including OpenAI’s GPT-4o, Meta’s Llama-3.1-405B,
025            and Google’s Gemini-pro, as well as medical-specialist LLMs like Microsoft’s
026            BioGPT. Z ODIAC demonstrates the transformative potential of specialized LLMs
027            in healthcare by delivering domain-specific solutions that meet the stringent de-
028
               mands of medical practice. Notably, Z ODIAC has been successfully integrated into
               electrocardiography (ECG) devices, exemplifying the growing trend of embedding
029
               LLMs into Software-as-Medical-Device (SaMD).

050   As technology continues to revolutionize healthcare, artificial intelligence (AI) has emerged as a
051   crucial component of medical devices, driving the expansion of digital health in clinical practice
052   (FDA, 2020). Among the most promising AI advancements, large language models (LLMs) are
053   unlocking new possibilities in digital health. With their human-like conversational skills and vast
      pre-trained knowledge, LLMs are increasingly being adopted as clinical support tools by industry


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      leaders, evolving into specialized clinical agents (Boonstra et al., 2024; Gala & Makaryus, 2023; Xu
055   et al., 2024). This evolution has given rise to industrial products such as Microsoft’s BioGPT (Luo
056   et al., 2022), Google’s Med-Gemini (Saab et al., 2024) and Med-PaLM (Tu et al., 2024), as well as
057   a range of open-source medical specialist LLMs (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024b; ContactDoctor, 2024;
058   Wang et al., 2024c) built on Meta’s Llama (Touvron et al., 2023).
059
      Despite these advancements, integrating LLMs into real-world healthcare practice remains in its
060
      early stages, where a significant gap exists in their professionalism (Davenport & Kalakota, 2019;
061   Asan et al., 2020; Weber et al., 2024; Quinn et al., 2022). Bridging these gaps is critical, especially
062   when deploying LLMs in healthcare settings governed by the FDA’s Software-as-a-Medical-Device
063   (SaMD) regulations (FDA, 2018). These regulations require software to demonstrate expert-level
064   proficiency to function as a clinical assistant. However, current LLMs often fall short of this standard
065   due to their general-purpose design, which lacks alignment with the specific standards of clinical
066   practice (Khan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Kerasidou et al., 2022). In the context of SaMD, LLMs
067   need not have universal capabilities but they must perform specialized tasks with the professionalism
068   and accuracy expected in life-critical healthcare environments (Kelly et al., 2019; Yan et al., 2023).
069
      Achieving this alignment is vital to ensure LLMs meet the stringent requirements of real-world
      healthcare deployment.
070
071   Our Work. This study aims to address the challenge of aligning LLMs with the SaMD practice in the
072   field of Cardiology, focusing on the clinical findings and interpretation of electrocardiogram (ECGs)
073   (Yanowitz, 2012). We introduce Z ODIAC, an LLM-powered, multi-agent framework designed to
074   achieve cardiologist-level professionalism. Z ODIAC assists cardiologists by identifying clinically rel-
075
      evant characteristics from patient data, detecting significant arrhythmias, and generating preliminary
      reports for expert review and refinement (details in 3). As illustrated in Figure 1, Z ODIAC combines
076
      advanced data integration with sophisticated technical designs to achieve professional performance.
077
      Specifically:
078
079   I) Data-Driven Professionalism: Z ODIAC is built on real-world data, including (1) patient data
080   collected from clinics, (2) cardiologist-adjudicated texts, and (3) clinical guidelines. This ensures
081
      professionalism in two key aspects: First, Z ODIAC captures real-world cardiological characteristics,
      such as arrhythmias and their contributing factors, rather than relying on benchmarks or synthetic
082
      datasets that may not reflect clinical realities. Second, direct involvement from human experts
083
      (cardiologists) ensures Z ODIAC is fine-tuned to match expert-level performance, while adherence to
084   clinical guidelines mitigates potential biases or errors, enhancing diagnostic accuracy and safety.
085
086   II) Technique-Driven Professionalism: The technical design of Z ODIAC aligns with cardiologist-
087
      level diagnostic practices. Our pipeline is multi-agent, utilizing multiple LLMs to analyze multi-
      modal patient data, including clinical metrics in tabular format and ECG tracings in image format.
088
      This multi-agent framework represents a paradigm cardiologists use to identify key characteristics and
089
      interpret findings for clinically significant arrhythmias. Furthermore, during fine-tuning and inference,
090   we employ cardiologist-adjudicated data, integrating multimodal diagnostic professionalism through
091   instruction tuning and in-context learning. Instruction tuning embeds professionalism into the
092   LLM’s parameters, while in-context learning provides professional demonstrations to further rein-
093   force Z ODIAC’s diagnostics. Finally, we incorporate fact-checking against established cardiological
094   guidelines to ensure the system generates accurate, expert-verified diagnostics.
095
      Clinical Validation. Our experiments aim to bridge the gap between cardiologists and LLM-powered
096   systems in terms of recognizing the professionalism of LLMs. To this end, we conduct a series of
097   clinical validations to assess Z ODIAC’s clinical effectiveness and security. We utilize eight evaluation
098   metrics and collaborate with cardiologists to evaluate Z ODIAC’s performance against leading LLMs,
099   including OpenAI’s ChatGPT-4o, Google’s Gemini-Pro, Meta’s Llama-405B, and specialized medical
100   LLMs like Microsoft’s BioGPT and Llama variants. With cardiologists endorsing Z ODIAC through
101   its application in real patient diagnostics, we underscore its practical utility from the healthcare
102   provider’s perspective.
103   Blueprint and Lifecycle Prospect. Z ODIAC has been successfully deployed on Amazon AWS
104   and integrated with clinical settings to assist in cardiological diagnostics (details in 4.4). The
105   design, development, and deployment of Z ODIAC provide a comprehensive blueprint for introducing
106   professional-grade LLM agents into clinical-grade SaMD, encompassing data utilization, expert-
107   level technical pipelines, and clinical validation. Furthermore, we incorporate human expertise

      2     R ELATED W ORK
126
      LLMs in Clinical Diagnostics. LLMs have shown considerable progress in processing and inter-
127
      preting vast amounts of unstructured medical data, such as patient records, medical literature, and
128
      diagnostic reports. For example, Han et al. (2024) introduced a system that automatically summarizes
129   clinical notes during interactions between patients and clinicians, while Ahsan et al. (2023) explored
130   the role of LLMs in retrieving key evidence from electronic health records (EHRs). Despite these
131   successes, concerns persist regarding LLMs’ domain-specific expertise and professional performance
132   in high-stakes, life-critical clinical settings (Nashwan & AbuJaber, 2023; Jahan et al., 2024; Wang
133   et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024). This work addresses these concerns by designing and validating
134   Z ODIAC through our design and experiments specifically for cardiological diagnostics.
135   Multi-Agent Frameworks. Multi-agent frameworks have been extensively studied to enhance LLM
136   capabilities in handling complex tasks and managing distributed processes (Wang et al., 2024b;
137   Hong et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023). In healthcare, where collaboration across
138   different expertise is essential, multi-agent frameworks have shown their potential in optimizing
139   patient management, coordinating care between various agents (e.g., doctors, nurses, administrative
140   systems), and supporting decision-making processes (Furmankiewicz et al., 2014; Jemal et al., 2014;
141   Shakshuki & Reid, 2015). Recent studies have also focused on leveraging multi-LLM agents to
142   reduce manual tasks in healthcare workflows. For instance, Chen et al. (2024a) employed ChatGPT
143
      in distinct roles within a coordinated workflow, to automate tasks like database mining and drug
      repurposing, while ensuring quality control through role-based collaboration.
144
145   Cardiological Diagnostic Systems. Current cardiological diagnostic systems primarily depend
146   on rule-based algorithms or single-agent approaches for identifying cardiovascular risk factors or
147   predicting cardiac events (Goff Jr et al., 2014; Sud et al., 2022; Olesen et al., 2012). In recent years,
148   deep learning models have been introduced into cardiology (Hannun et al., 2019; Acharya et al.,
149
      2019). However, there remains a significant gap in incorporating recent LLMs into cardiological
      diagnostics—a gap that this work addresses significantly.

      3.2   C ARDIOLOGICAL D IAGNOSTICS WITH M ULTIMODAL DATA
201
202   Cardiological diagnostics follows a process: (B, M, T ) → F → I. In addition, the final interpre-
203   tation is guided by clinical guidelines, denoted as G, which are consensus-based recommendations
204   designed to support healthcare providers in making evidence-based decisions (detailed in D).
205
      A cardiologist begins by reviewing the patient’s data (B, M, T ) to identify clinically relevant
206
      characteristics, such as the PR interval, which are key for diagnosing arrhythmias. These identified
207
      characteristics are then summarized into natural language statements, referred to as findings F, which
208   integrate insights from both tabular metrics M and image-based ECG tracings T . For example,
209   the PR interval is derived from T , while the AF burden is obtained from M. Finally, cardiologists
210   synthesize the findings F with their clinical expertise and the established guidelines G to form the
211   final interpretation I.
212
213   3.3   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION IN Z ODIAC
214
215   As illustrated in Figure 2, Z ODIAC represents the diagnostic process through a multi-agent collabo-
      ration. Each LLM agent is tasked with a specific stage in the diagnostic workflow, enhancing the

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      system’s ability to identify hybrid characteristics across multiple modalities. Z ODIAC is composed of
217   three agents:
218
219   1. Metrics-to-Findings Agent (θM2F ): A table-to-text LLM that extracts key characteristics from
220      tabular metrics (M), while incorporating patient biostatistics from B to generate clinical findings.
221   2. Tracings-to-Findings Agent (θT2F ): An image-to-text LLM that identifies key factors from ECG
222      tracings (T ), integrates relevant information from B, and produces clinical findings.
223   3. Findings-to-Interpretation Agent (θF2I ): A text-based LLM that synthesizes findings (F) from
224      both the θM2F and θT2F , applies clinical guidelines from G, and generates the interpretation (I).
225
226   Formally, the process of Z ODIAC is formulated as:
227                           I ← θF2I (F, G) s.t. F ← θM2F (M, B) ∪ θT2F (T , B)                                     (1)
228
      In this process, θM2F and θT2F independently generate clinical findings based on M and T , respec-
229
      tively, which are then combined to form F. This approach adheres to cardiological diagnostics as
230   each finding in F corresponds to evidence derived from a specific modality – either metrics or ECG
231   tracings.

      4     D ESIGN , D EVELOPMENT, AND D EPLOYMENT OF Z ODIAC
235
      This section details how Z ODIAC achieves cardiologist-level expertise, as well as its compliance with
236
      Software-as-a-Medical-Device (SaMD) regulations. We begin by discussing the process of real-world
237
      data collection and the professionalism-incorporated curation, outlined in Section 4.1. Next, we
238   introduce the instruction fine-tuning in Section 4.2, where the curated data is used to imbue the LLM
239   agents with domain-specific expertise. During inference, as described in Section 4.3, we leverage
240   in-context learning and fact-checking to enhance diagnostic professionalism through collaborative
241   multi-agent interactions. Finally, we present our approach to SaMD-compliant deployment in Section
242   4.4.
243
244   4.1    DATA C OLLECTION AND P ROFESSIONALISM -I NCORPORATED C URATION
245
246   Our data is characterized as real, representative, and professionalism-incorporated.
247   Real-World Patient Data. Instead of relying on public benchmarks from third-party or synthetic
248   data—which often raise concerns about trustworthiness or misalignment with specific clinical appli-
249   cations (Chouffani El Fassi et al., 2024; Fehr et al., 2024; Youssef et al., 2024)—we utilized ECG
250   data sourced from our collaborating healthcare institutions1 under an IRB-approved protocol, with
251   removed patient identifiers to ensure privacy protection. The raw data collection consists of tabular
252   metrics (M) and ECG tracings (T ), as depicted in the left part of Figure 2. To ensure the clinical
253
      relevance, we engaged five independent cardiologists to review the data, resulting in a final dataset of
      2,000+ patients. Of these, 5% were used for clinical validation (Section 5), while the remainder were
254
      used for fine-tuning (Section 4.2).
255
256   Representative Groups. Following FDA’s guideline (Food et al., 2021), it is crucial to include
257   representative data, not simply to amass large volumes. Our dataset encompasses comprehensive
258   arrhythmia types and ensures balanced representation across age and gender demographics, as detailed
259
      in Figure 3-(d).
260   Incorporating Cardiologist-Level Professionalism. When reviewing the raw data, cardiologists are
261   asked to write professional findings (F) and interpretation (I) in accordance with established clinical
262   guidelines (G). This process facilitates fine-tuning the LLMs, embedding cardiologist-level reasoning,
263   evidence-based statements, and a structured format into the models. To optimize the cardiologists’
264   time, medical research assistants first draft the F and I, which are subsequently reviewed and
265
      independently adjudicated by the cardiologists. Additionally, each cardiologist randomly audits
      at least 50% of their peers’ drafts to address issues such as incompleteness, inconsistency, or
266
      diagnostic inaccuracies. This peer-review process not only improves the data quality but also ensures
267
      standardized findings and interpretation with professional accuracy.

299
300   Utilizing cardiologist-adjudicated data, we apply instruction fine-tuning to instill cardiologist-level
301   professionalism into agents θM2F , θT2F , and θF2I . For θM2F , we selected Llama-3.1-8B as the base
302   model, LLaVA-v1.5-13B for θT2F , and another Llama-3.1-8B for θF2I . Each base model is fine-tuned
303   individually, tailored to its specific task using relevant portions of cardiologist-adjudicated data. For
304   instance, as shown in Figure 3-(a)(c), we fine-tune θM2F by utilizing system prompts from (a) and
305   cardiologist-adjudicated texts in the format presented in (c), aligning with the metric-to-findings
306
      task handled by θM2F . Let θAgent denote the trainable parameters of any LLM agent, with X and Y
      representing the instructional input and the corresponding LLM responses for one patient, respectively.
307
      Given the cardiologist-adjudicated data D, the tuning process can be formulated as follows:
308

311   The goal of instruction fine-tuning in Eq. 2 is to minimize the mean of the summed loss by E(L(·, ·))

315   4.3   I NFERENCE WITH I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING AND FACT-C HECKING
316
317   As outlined in Section 3.3, Z ODIAC’s inference process involves a multi-agent approach using the
318   trained agents θM2F , θT2F , and θF2I . First, θM2F processes patient metrics (M) and θT2F handles ECG
319   tracings (T ), together generating findings (F). These findings are then interpreted by θF2I as the
320
      diagnostic interpretation (I). Each agent leverages in-context learning to enhance diagnostic accuracy,
      with fact-checking applied at the final step for backward self-correction.
321
322   In-Context Learning. For each fine-tuned LLM agent, we implement in-context learning using a
323   set of demonstrations (or “demos”) containing cardiologist-adjudicated findings and interpretation.
      The content of each demo is tailored to the specific LLM agent. For instance, demos for θM2F include


                                                                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      cardiologist-adjudicated findings, as shown in Figure 3-(b). To ensure that each demo is relevant to
325   the target patient’s case, we categorize the patient data by gender, age group, and arrhythmia class,
326   following the subgrouping presented in Figure 3-(d). We then select three demos that match the
327   patient’s gender, age group, and arrhythmia class. During inference, the input prompt is a combination
328   of contents shown in Figure 3-(a)(b)(c).
329
      Fact-Checking. Fact-checking occurs after θF2I generates the final interpretation (I). Z ODIAC
330
      applies cardiological guidelines (G) to verify whether the findings (F) correctly lead to the inter-

346   In line with SaMD (FDA, 2018), Z ODIAC represents an important milestone in building professional

348   and connected to the in-hospital frontend to assist cardiologists by providing preliminary reports.
349   As shown in Figure 4, cardiologists or their assistants can upload patient data, including monitored
350   metrics and ECG tracings from wearable patches (Steinhubl et al., 2018) or Holters (Kim et al., 2009).

352

      and interpretation based on the data, and performs fact-checking to ensure accuracy before finalizing
353
      the reports. These reports are then returned to the cardiologists, who can use them as a foundation to
354
      finalize their diagnoses, thus enhancing workflow efficiency and improving diagnostic accuracy.

419
420   Comprehensive evaluations in Table 2 highlight the remarkable capabilities of Z ODIAC. With fewer
421   than 30B parameters (as noted in Section 4.2), Z ODIAC outperforms larger models like Llama-3.1-

423   professionalism (e.g., 4.9 CPH) and security assurance (e.g., 5.0 FFB). Additionally, Z ODIAC

427   prompting a generic model.

429   of BioGPT-Large (1.5B parameters) understandably limits its diagnostic capabilities, a more critical
430   issue is that the data used for fine-tuning models like Meditron-70B appear to be misaligned with
431   real-world clinical practice. Even when aided by in-context learning demos, these specialist LLMs
      struggle to meet the specific requirements and security demands of clinical tasks.

472   For example, GPT-4o presents less accuracy (ACC) and completeness (CPL) when diagnosing

475
      consistent performance across all arrhythmia groups, underscoring the importance of data-driven

      Ablation Study. We evaluate how removing key components from Z ODIAC affects its performance.
480   Figure 6-(d) compares Z ODIAC with three baselines: without fine-tuning, without in-context learning,
481   and without fact-checking. Notably, fine-tuning has the greatest impact on diagnostic performance
482   across all metrics, followed by in-context learning, demonstrating the importance of using fine-tuning
483   to permanently embed domain expertise into the LLMs’ parameters. Without fine-tuning, in-context
484   learning can still guide the model toward proficiency, but with more limited improvements. We also
485   notice that adding fact-checking improves security-related performance (e.g., FFH, FFB), highlighting
      the need to integrate clinical guidelines for safe and responsible diagnostics.

518   6    F UTURE W ORK
519
520   Z ODIAC serves as our minimum viable product (MVP) toward functional completeness. Building
521   on Z ODIAC, future work focused on security, trustworthiness, and transparency is crucial for
522   ensuring long-term success in a competitive market.
523   As emphasized by FDA’s guiding principles (FDA, 2024), securing the development and deployment
524   of LLMs is as important as achieving functional effectiveness. While our current evaluation addresses
525   security-focused metrics, the next phase will prioritize further development of security measures to
526   enhance trust. This will involve investigating third-party adversarial influences in data, identifying
527   inherent weaknesses in LLMs that could lead to vulnerabilities (e.g., backdoors), proposing defensive
528   strategies to safeguard Z ODIAC in life-critical diagnostic applications, and promoting transparency to
529   foster human understanding and effective collaboration in human-machine intelligence.

531   7    C ONCLUSION
532
533   We present Z ODIAC, an LLM-powered, multi-agent framework designed for cardiologist-level
534   diagnostics. Z ODIAC aims to bridge the gap between clinicians and LLMs in the field of cardiology.
535   Leveraging real-world, cardiologist-adjudicated data and techniques including instruction tuning,
536   in-context learning, and fact-checking, Z ODIAC is enhanced to deliver diagnoses with the expertise
537   of human specialists. Through clinical validation, we demonstrate that Z ODIAC produces leading
538
      performance across patients of different genders, age groups, and arrhythmia classes. In conclusion,
      Z ODIAC represent a significant step toward developing clinically viable LLM-based diagnostic tools.

      In this work, we categorize arrhythmias into three subgroups:
844
845   Class I — Normal Arrhythmias: Also known as benign or physiological arrhythmias, these irregular
846   heart rhythms can occur in healthy individuals and typically do not lead to serious health issues.
847   They are generally considered harmless and may not require treatment. In our patient data, Class I
848
      arrhythmias include Sinus Bradycardia, Sinus Tachycardia, and Sinus Arrhythmia.
849   Class II — Clinically Significant Arrhythmias: These arrhythmias involve abnormal heart rhythms
850   that can cause symptoms, lead to complications, or require medical intervention. They may disrupt
851   the heart’s ability to pump blood effectively, increasing the risk of serious events such as stroke,
852   heart failure, or sudden cardiac death. In our patient data, Class II arrhythmias include Pause (¡3s),
853   Ventricular Premature Beat (PVC), and Atrial Fibrillation (AF).
854   Class III — Life-Threatening Arrhythmias: These abnormal heart rhythms can result in severe
855   consequences, such as cardiac arrest, stroke, or sudden cardiac death, requiring immediate medical
856   attention and often emergency intervention. In our patient data, Class III arrhythmias include
857   Ventricular Flutter (VF), Complete Heart Block (Third-Degree AV Block), Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)
858   with Rapid Ventricular Response, Prolonged Pause, Atrial Flutter (AFL), Ventricular Tachycardia
859   (VT), and Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT).
860   In our experiments, we use these arrhythmia classes (I, II, III) for subgroup analysis rather than specific
861   arrhythmias to avoid the limitations of small patient sample sizes for individual conditions. Subgroup
862   analysis based on arrhythmia classes provides a comprehensive view of the LLMs’ diagnostic
863   capabilities across different levels of urgency, offering valuable insights for data collection and
      performance improvement toward more balanced diagnostics.

      Clinical guidelines are systematically developed statements designed to assist healthcare providers
      and patients in making decisions about appropriate health care for specific clinical circumstances.
868
      These guidelines are based on the best available evidence and aim to standardize care, improve the
869
      quality of treatment, and ensure patient safety. For example, a section of clinical guidelines about PR
870   Interval is provided in Figure 10.
871
872
873       PR Interval:

874
          1. Definition of PR Interval
875             The PR interval measures the period from the onset of atrial depolarization (beginning of the P wave) to the onset of ventricular
876       depolarization (beginning of the QRS complex). It reflects the time taken for the electrical impulse to travel from the sinus node through the
          atria, AV node, His bundle, bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers to reach the ventricular myocardium.
877
878       2. Range
879            ● Normal: 120-200 milliseconds
               ● Prolonged: >200 milliseconds, indicating first-degree AV block
880
               ● Shortened: <120 milliseconds, may suggest pre-excitation syndromes like Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
881
882       3. Clinical Relevance
883            ● Normal PR Interval
                     ○ Finding: PR interval within 120-200 milliseconds.
884
                    ○ Interpretation: Indicates normal atrioventricular (AV) conduction. The electrical signal travels from the atria to the ventricles
885       through the AV node and His-Purkinje system within the expected time frame, suggesting healthy cardiac electrical function.

886
               ● Prolonged PR Interval
887
                     ○ Finding: PR interval longer than 200 milliseconds.
888                  ○ Interpretation:
889                       ■ First-Degree AV Block: The prolongation is uniform across all heartbeats. This is often benign but can be associated with
          increased vagal tone, intrinsic AV nodal disease, or effects of certain medications (like beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, or digoxin).
890                        ■ Higher degree AV block predisposition: Indicates potential for progression to higher degree AV block, especially in the
891       setting of structural heart disease or acute myocardial infarction.

892
               ● Short PR Interval
893                  ○ Finding: PR interval less than 120 milliseconds.
894                  ○ Interpretation:
895                      ■ Pre-excitation Syndromes: Such as Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome where there is an accessory pathway (like the
          bundle of Kent) allowing premature ventricular activation.
896                       ■ Junctional Rhythms: If associated with an abnormal P wave morphology or positioning, may indicate that the impulse
897       originates near or within the AV node rather than the atria.

898
               ● Variable PR Interval
899                  ○ Finding: Fluctuating PR intervals across different heartbeats.
900                  ○ Interpretation:
901                     ■ Second-Degree AV Block Type I (Wenckebach): Progressive lengthening of the PR interval until a P wave is not followed by
          a QRS complex.
902                    ■ Atrial Fibrillation with Variable Conduction: If associated with an irregularly irregular rhythm, indicates atrial fibrillation
903       where AV nodal conduction is unpredictably variable.

904
               ● PR Interval with Grouped Beating
905                  ○ Finding: Groups of beats with a consistent PR interval followed by a longer pause.
906                  ○ Interpretation:
907                     ■ Second-Degree AV Block Type II: Typically associated with fixed PR intervals on conducted beats, interspersed with non-
          conducted P waves without prior change in the PR duration.
908                       ■ Mobitz Type II or Advanced Block: Often a precursor to complete heart block, requiring immediate assessment and
909       potentially pacing intervention.

910
               ● Alternating PR Interval
911                  ○ Finding: Alternation in the length of the PR interval from beat to beat.
912                  ○ Interpretation:
                           ■ Electrophysiological Variability: May be due to alternating dominance of different AV nodal pathways, a rare phenomenon
913
          or related to autonomic tone fluctuations.
914                       ■ Underlying Heart Disease: Consider evaluation for ischemic heart disease or infiltrative cardiac conditions that may
          intermittently affect AV nodal conduction.
