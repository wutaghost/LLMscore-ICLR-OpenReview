010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Tractable Function-space Variational Inference (T-FVI) provides a way to estimate
013            the function-space Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between a random prior
014            function and its posterior. This allows the optimization of the function-space KL
015            divergence via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) and thus simplifies the training
016            of function-space Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs). However, function-space
017            BNNs on high-dimensional datasets typically require deep neural networks (DNN)
018            with numerous parameters, and thus defining suitable function-space priors remains
019
               challenging. For instance, the Gaussian Process (GP) prior suffers from scalability
               issues, and DNNs do not provide a clear way to set appropriate weight parameters
020
               to achieve meaningful function-space priors. To address this issue, we propose an
021
               explicit form of function-space priors that can be easily integrated into widely-used
022            DNN architectures, while adaptively incorporating different levels of uncertainty
023            based on the function’s inputs. To achieve this, we consider DNNs as Bayesian
024            last-layer models to obtain the explicit mean and variance functions of our prior.
025            The parameters of these explicit functions are determined using the weight statistics
026            over the learning trajectory. Our empirical experiments show improved uncertainty
027            estimation in image classification, transfer learning, and UCI regression tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Function-space Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) (Sun et al., 2019) have gained significant attention
      within the Bayesian deep learning community, primarily due to their fundamental goal of assigning
034
      prior distributions to the outputs of neural networks directly. Training these BNNs can generally be
035
      conducted by optimizing the function-space Evidence lower bound (ELBO) consisting of the expected
036   likelihood and the function-space KL divergence between a random prior function and its posterior
037   function (Sun et al., 2019). The recent tractable function-space variational inference (T-FVI) (Rudner
038   et al., 2022) presents the closed form of function-space KL divergence using linearized NNs, and thus
039   facilities the optimization of the training objective via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). However,
040   recent function-space BNNs use DNN architecture using many parameters to model high-dimensional
041   dataset and thus raises a challenge in setting the suitable priors for the function-space BNNs.
042   Gaussian process (GP) has been a representative function-space (Rasmussen, 2004). This prior has
043   been used for the small-sized BNNs conducting the regression or low-dimensional classification
044   (Flam-Shepherd et al., 2017; Tran et al., 2022). However, GP prior for modeling high dimensional
045   datasets has scalable issues in training the kernel hyperparameters. Thus, GP prior is rarely used as
046   the function-space prior for the commonly-used DNN architectures, such as ResNet.
047
      Alternatively, mapping weight-space prior to function-space prior through the linearized NNs can
048   be considered for setting the prior of such DNN architectures (Rudner et al., 2022). However, since
049   the derived function-space prior might incorporate largely different prior into the model’s output
050   according to the assigned weight-space prior, it requires to carefully set the weight-space prior and
051   thus limits to practically use of this approach. Additionally, this approach practically restricts the
052   randomness to the last layer for Jacobian computation because it requires a large amount of GPU
053   memory to compute the large-sized Jacobian matrix of NN for each input. This practical usage might
      reduce the flexibility in the resulting BNNs.

054
      Furthermore, function-space VI requires the external dataset for computing function-space KL
055   divergence because this KL term measures the distance between two random functions defined in
056   an infinite-dimensional space (Sun et al., 2019; Rudner et al., 2022). Employing the well-curated
057   external datasets can enhance the model’s uncertainty estimation capabilities (Antorán et al., 2023;
058   Lopez et al., 2023). On the other hand, the arbitrary-chosen external dataset, without considering its
059   relationship with the training set, may adversely impact training.
060
      In this work, we propose an explicit form of function-space prior that can be easily used for the
061   widely-used DNN architectures, and adaptively introduce different levels of uncertainty based on the
062   function’s inputs. To this end, we consider DNNs as Bayesian last-layer models, yielding a closed
063   form of the function-space prior. Then, we devise the explicit mean function and variance functions
064   of our prior to adaptively produce higher uncertainty for each function’s output, similarly to GP. We
065   set the parameters of these explicit mean and variance functions by using the weight statistics over the
066   learning trajectory. Additionally, based on the property of designed prior, we propose an adversarial
067   context feature that can be used for computing the function-space KL divergence without relying on
068   external datasets. We expect this context feature to impose additional uncertainty into the model’s
069
      output on potential Out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs. Our implementation is available here. We
      summarize our contribution as follows:
070
071   • We propose an explicit function-space prior that can be easily used for the common DNN architec-
072     tures as well as adaptively incorporate higher uncertainties for each function’s input.
073   • We propose a context feature to compute the function-space KL without using external datasets.
074   • We showcase the effectiveness of our approach across diverse benchmark tasks. Notably, our prior
075     is more effective in experiments involving large-scale models like vision transformers (Dosovitskiy
076     et al., 2021).

132   3     L IMITATIONS OF THE EXISTING WORKS ON FUNCTION - SPACE BNN S
133
134   In this section, we highlight the limitations of the existing works on function-space BNNs in three
135   perspectives: (1) the choice of priors, (2) computational complexity, and (3) the choice of context
136   sets for KL divergence computation.
137
138
      3.1    T HE CHOICE OF PRIORS
139   Gaussian process prior. Gaussian process (GP) (Rasmussen, 2004) is a stochastic process (SP)
140   assuming that any finite random variables of the SP follow the multivariate Gaussian distribution.
141   The GP has been recognized as a representative function-space prior for BNNs (Sun et al., 2019;
142   Karaletsos & Bui, 2020; Tran et al., 2022). However, using the GP prior is computationally expensive
143   for the large and high-dimensional dataset (Liu et al., 2020a) due to the computational cost of finding
144
      the kernel hyperparameter. Thus, GP priors have been mainly used for regression tasks. It has rarely
      been explored for the BNNs using the commonly-used DNN architecture such as ResNet.
145
146   Function-space prior via linearized neural network. The linearized neural network (NN) yields
147   a tractable function-space prior by specifying the weight-space prior p(θ) and then push-forwarding
148   the weight-space prior to the output of the linearized NN (Rudner et al., 2022), as described in Eq. (4).
149   However, this construction still raises concerns about using the obtained prior as the function-space
150   regularizer KL(q(f )∥p(f )) because it is unclear how the mean µ(x) and variance function Σ(x)
151
      would behave depending on input x. For instance, the mean and variance of the function-space prior
      corresponding to a zero-mean Gaussian weight prior p(θ) = N (θ; 0P , σ 2 IP ×P ) is derived as,
152
153       µ(x) = f (x, 0P ) = 0Q ,   Σ(x) = J(x, 0P )⊤ σ 2 IP ×P J(x, 0P ) = σ 2 J(x, 0P )⊤ J(x, 0P ). (5)
154
      However, one cannot easily interpret the behaviors of these functions. For instance, it is not clear
155
      how the variance Σ(x) changes according to the proximity of an input x to a training set. As shown
156
      in Figs. 1a to 1c, which plots the mean and variance functions for a toy dataset, the variance remains
157   unchanged when transitioning from IND to OOD regions.

      3.3   T HE CHOICE OF CONTEXT SETS
192   As reviewed in Section 2, evaluating the KL divergence between stochastic processes necessitates
193   the use of the context set Xctx . The prior works show that well-curated context sets resembling the
194   original training data yet not identical can enhance the model’s uncertainty estimation capabilities
195   (Antorán et al., 2023; Lopez et al., 2023). However, previous works underscore that the arbitrarily
196   chosen context set without considering its relationship with the training set may have detrimental
197   effects on model training. Indeed, we investigate how varying context set Xctx = (1−α)Xtrain +αXext
198   for α ∈ (0, 1] affects the performance of the function-space VI in Appendix B.1.2 and observe that
199   its performance on IND set tends to degrade as the context set is set as external set Xext .

201   4     A N A DAPTIVE F UNCTION -S PACE P RIORS FROM L EARNING T RAJECTORIES
202
203   In this section, we introduce a novel function-space prior designed to address the limitations discussed
204   earlier. Specifically, we present an explicit form of function-space prior to be widely used for DNN
205   architectures. We consider DNNs as Bayesian Last-layer models, yielding the closed form of the
206   function-space prior, and then devise the explicit mean and variance function of prior to adaptively
207
      produce higher uncertainty as GP prior does. The parameters of mean and variance functions are
      set leveraging the weight and feature statistics obtained from the leaning trajectory. Additionally,
208
      based on our variance function, we propose a straightforward way to compute the context feature
209
      eliminating the need for external datasets as required in previous approaches. Fig. 2a describes the
210   procedure of prior construction and Figs. 2b and 2c describes the effect of the designed function-space
211   prior, which is distinct from the push-forwarded prior in Figs. 1a and 1b.

      5        R ELATED W ORK
393
394   Function-space BNN, VI, and Prior. Our work aligns with prior works (Sun et al., 2019; Rudner
395
      et al., 2022; 2023; Lin et al., 2023) presenting the function-space VI. Our work relieves their limita-
      tions by presenting the explicit form of function-space prior using learning trajectory. Additionally,
396
      our approach aligns with prior works (Hafner et al., 2020; Flam-Shepherd et al., 2017; Tran et al.,
397
      2022) in designing function-space priors. Unlike Hafner et al. (2020), which uses noise perturbation
398   input for prior construction, we consider adversarial perturbation in feature space and clarify its
399   impact on the function-space prior. Furthermore, unlike Flam-Shepherd et al. (2017); Tran et al.
400   (2022), which use GP priors primarily for shallow BNNs, our function-space prior is designed to
401   be feasible with large-scale BNNs using ResNet He et al. (2016) and VIT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020).
402   Unlike Liu et al. (2020b) directly using the approximate GP prior into DNN’s last-layer, our work
403   designs the covariance function motivated from GP predictive variance.
404
      Empirical Bayes for BNNs. Empirical Bayes estimates the parameters of the prior distribution
405   through training. This contrasts with the conventional Bayesian approach where prior parameters
406   are set in advance Casella (1992). For BNNs, Immer et al. (2021) employs marginal likelihood
407   optimization for training the prior. Krishnan et al. (2020) uses the parameters of the pre-trained
408   model as the mean parameters of the weight-space prior. Shwartz-Ziv et al. (2022) uses re-scaled
409   parameters of pre-trained models as the weight-space prior for transfer learning. However, unlike
410   theses work, our work uses the parameter trajectory during training to construct the function-space
411   prior. Furthermore, our prior is developed from scratch training, whereas Krishnan et al. (2020);
412   Shwartz-Ziv et al. (2022) relies on pre-trained parameters on training or upstream datasets as prior.
413   Implicit Process. Our work shares similarities with variants of the variational implicit process
414   (VIP) Ma et al. (2019); Ma & Hernández-Lobato (2021); Rodrguez-Santana et al. (2022); Ortega et al.
415   (2022) in modeling stochastic functions using DNNs. However, while VIP variants aim to enhance
416   modeling capabilities by constructing implicit distributions with stochastic NN generators Ma &
417   Hernández-Lobato (2021) and sparse GPs Rodrguez-Santana et al. (2022), our focus is on building
418   effective function-space prior to improve BNNs.

      7       C ONCLUSION
529
530   We propose an explicit form of function-space prior that can be easily used with the widely-used
531   DNN architectures, as well as to adaptively assign higher uncertainty for each function’s output.
532   We demonstrate that our prior is effective in improving uncertainty estimation, especially for the
533   large-sized model.
534   However, our method has some limitations. As our prior utilizes information from pre-trained epochs,
535   the function-space prior and its variational posterior depend on the selected pre-trained epoch. Thus,
536   tuning the pre-trained epochs is necessary. For the regression task, our prior requires binning to
537   obtain the pseudo-discrete labels from real-valued outputs.
