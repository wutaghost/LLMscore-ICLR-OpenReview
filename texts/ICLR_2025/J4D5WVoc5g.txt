011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We present ViTaM-D, a novel visual-tactile framework for dynamic hand-object
014            interaction reconstruction, integrating distributed tactile sensing for more accurate
015            contact modeling. While existing methods focus primarily on visual inputs, they
016
               struggle with capturing detailed contact interactions such as object deformation.
               Our approach leverages distributed tactile sensors to address this limitation by in-
017
               troducing DF-Field. This distributed force-aware contact representation models
018
               both kinetic and potential energy in hand-object interaction. ViTaM-D first recon-
019            structs hand-object interactions using a visual-only network, VDT-Net, and then
020            refines contact details through a force-aware optimization (FO) process, enhanc-
021            ing object deformation modeling. To benchmark our approach, we introduce the
022            HOT dataset, which features 600 sequences of hand-object interactions, including
023            deformable objects, built in a high-precision simulation environment. Extensive
024            experiments on both the DexYCB and HOT datasets demonstrate significant im-
025            provements in accuracy over previous state-of-the-art methods such as gSDF and
026            HOTrack. Our results highlight the superior performance of ViTaM-D in both
027
               rigid and deformable object reconstruction, as well as the effectiveness of DF-
               Field in refining hand poses. This work offers a comprehensive solution to dy-
028
               namic hand-object interaction reconstruction by seamlessly integrating visual and
029
               tactile data. Codes, models, and datasets will be available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Human manipulates objects with tactile feedback. Reconstructing the manipulation process of hand-
035
      object interaction is an important task that can benefit many downstream tasks, such as VR/AR, robot
036   imitation learning, and human behavior understanding. Previous works (Yang et al., 2021; 2024;
037   Chen et al., 2023a; Tsoli & Argyros, 2018; Hasson et al., 2019b) on dynamic hand-object interaction
038   reconstruction are primarily visual-only. Visual-only methods can recover the global geometry and
039   poses of hand and object, but they struggle with contact details such as object deformation due to a
040   lack of information near contact areas.
041
      Recently, visual-tactile methods (Xu et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2018) have increas-
042   ingly drawn attention in hand-object reconstruction tasks, with the development of tactile sensing
043   techniques (Sundaram et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2017; Yuan et al., 2017; Ren et al., 2023), which can
044   supplement the perceptual ability near contact areas (Fig. 1.a). Among the fast-developing tactile
045   sensors, distributed tactile sensors (Fig. 1.b) (Liu et al., 2017; Yin et al., 2023; Sundaram et al.,
046   2019), which are wearable or attachable to a hand and allow collecting tactile information in a nat-
047   ural human-like hand-object interaction process, are more promising in human manipulation data
048   collection than other types of tactile sensors (Taylor et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2017; Lambeta et al.,
049   2020; Ren et al., 2023). However, how to integrate such sensors with visual perception to reconstruct
050
      the states of hand-object interaction is seldom explored.
051   Due to the conformality between the distributed tactile sensor and the hand, the hand geometry will
052   not be significantly influenced during manipulation. Therefore, we can use the same visual-only
053   approach to reconstruct the hand-object state and integrate the tactile information to refine the result.
      Based on this observation, we propose a novel Distributed Force-aware contact representation, DF-

086

087

088
      objects on the HOT Dataset and the effectiveness of the FO optimization in refining hand poses
089   from penetrations and bad contact states.
090
091   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
092   (1) A visual-tactile learning framework, ViTaM-D. It contains a visual-only dynamic tracking net-
093   work, VDT-Net, for reconstructing hand-object interactions and a force-aware optimization process,
094   FO, to integrate the tactile information into reconstruction refinement based on a novel distributed
095   force-aware contact representation, DF-Field.
096   (2) A new dataset, HOT. It contains 600 RGB-D manipulation sequences on 30 deformable objects
097   from 5 categories with penetration-free hand-object poses and accurate tactile information.

101
102   Hand-object reconstruction has been richly studied because it tries to recover the full details of
103   hand-object interaction, showing potential applicability for downstream tasks.
104   Research in this direction starts with static reconstruction. Earlier works have predominantly relied
105   on RGB inputs to estimate hand pose and object geometry. Hasson et al. (Hasson et al., 2019a)
106   presented a method that learns hand-object interaction using synthetic RGB images with the grasp
107   poses planned by GraspIt! (Miller & Allen, 2004). Doosti et al. (Doosti et al., 2020) proposed a
      graph-based network for hand-object pose estimation. Cao et al. (Cao et al., 2021) introduced a


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      method that operates in-the-wild, estimating the hand pose first and optimizing the solution using a
109   contact representation. The optimization involves push and pull terms to handle the contact, which is
110   purely empirical. Similarly, CPF (Yang et al., 2021; 2024) employs a contact representation using a
111   spring-mass system, adopting an empirical approach to refine the hand-object interaction. ArtiBoost
112   (Yang et al., 2022a) further enhanced the performance of static hand-object reconstruction through
113   data augmentation techniques. AlignSDF (Chen et al., 2022) leveraged RGB inputs to estimate hand
114   pose and combined point cloud data for object geometry, using SDF-based decoders for both hand
115   and object reconstruction.
116   Later, hand-object reconstruction in the dynamic setting draws increasing attention since manipu-
117   lation is naturally dynamic. Approaches have advanced by incorporating temporal information and
118   using more complex representations. Tekin et al. (Tekin et al., 2019) adopted RNN for tempo-
119   ral feature fusion, utilizing egocentric RGB video to capture dynamic hand-object interaction and
120   pose estimation. Hasson et al. (Hasson et al., 2020) improved dynamic reconstruction by enforcing
121   photometric consistency constraints. Ye et al. (Ye et al., 2023) introduced a diffusion model that
122   guides dynamic hand-object reconstruction via score distillation sampling and differentiable render-
123
      ing techniques. Recent advances by Fan et al. (Fan et al., 2024) focused on refining pose estimation
      using SDF-based representations and volumetric rendering, improving the overall dynamic recon-
124
      struction accuracy. Furthermore, gSDF (Chen et al., 2023b) employed transformer architectures and
125
      SDF representations to model complex hand-object interactions dynamically.
126
127   These works focus more on visual-only inputs. However, due to occlusion between the hand and
128   object during the interaction, visual perception usually lacks information near the contact areas, and
129
      such information cannot necessarily be mitigated by cross-frame feature fusion. Therefore, tactile
      perception comes to supplement the near-contact information. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2021)
130
      utilized a tactile glove (Sundaram et al., 2019) to track object movements. However, it focuses
131
      more on the dynamic object trajectory rather than contact geometry. Works such as (Smith et al.,
132   2020; Wang et al., 2018) employed optical tactile sensors, trained the model by synthetic data,
133   and applied to rigid object geometry reconstruction. Later, VTacO (Xu et al., 2023) extended this
134   line of research by using optical tactile sensors to capture object geometry, including deformation,
135   providing a more comprehensive representation of hand-object interactions. Unlike these works,
136   the proposed ViTaM-D inherits the merits from both worlds; it leverages the advanced techniques
137   of visual-only reconstruction and incorporates the distributed tactile readings to enhance the local
138   contact details.

140   3     F ORCE - AWARE C ONTACT R EPRESENTATION
141
142   During manipulation, forces from the hand change both hand and object states. To fully capture
143   dynamic contact behaviors, the representation must encode both contact locations and forces. We
144   introduce a distributed force-aware contact representation, DF-Field (Sec. 3.1), and apply it using
145   distributed tactile sensors (Sec. 3.2).
146
147   3.1   DF-F IELD R EPRESENTATION
148
149   Without loss of generality, we take the object-centric perspective to describe a hand-object interac-
150

151
      nipulation process are driven by the Relative Potential Energy, which is the resulting energy of the
152

155

      4.1   OVERVIEW
200
201   We address the problem of 4D tracking and dynamic reconstruction of hand-object interactions from
202   a visual-tactile perspective and propose a novel method named ViTaM-D. We assume that the visual
203   input consists of a live stream of 3D point clouds, denoted as {Pt ∈ RN ×3 }nt=1 , representing hand-
204   object interactions derived from single-view depth images, where N is the number of input point.
205   The tactile input {Mt }nt=1 contains distributed tactile sensor readings for each frame.
206
      ViTaM-D tracks hand-object states in two stages: Visual Dynamic Tracking (VDT-Net) and Force-
207   aware Optimization (FO). VDT-Net uses visual-only inputs to establish flow features and dynam-
208   ically reconstruct the hand and object, capturing global hand-object geometry but struggling with
209   sparse contact region details. To address this, the second stage, FO, incorporates tactile information
210   to improve contact accuracy and reduce penetration issues. The overall pipeline is given in Fig. 2.

      of 4 and a learning rate of 5e − 5 for 50-100 epochs, depending on the number of objects. The
383
      training process takes 15 hours on an Nvidia A40 GPU.
384
385   For the FO process, we set the threshold distance in the barrier function to ˆl = 2mm and the ball-
386   query radius for finding object vertices to R = 5mm. We minimize the energy function for 100
387   iterations per frame with a learning rate of 2e − 3 using the Adam solver. It takes approximately
388   3.5 ± 0.5 seconds per frame to complete refinement.

      To train and evaluate our method, we first use the dataset DexYCB, which captures hand grasping

393
      against the baselines. In addition, we use the HOT dataset, which contains interactions of hand

      6.3   M ETRICS
399
400   Intersection over Union (IoU) evaluates the intersection percentage between the predicted object
401   mesh and the ground truth.
402
      Chamfer Distance (CD) assesses the reconstruction accuracy of object vertices.
403
404   Mean Per Joint Position Error (MPJPE) evaluates the accuracy of hand joint location tracking.
405   Penetration Depth (PD) measures the physical plausibility of hand-object interaction by reporting
406   the maximum distance of hand penetration into the object.
407
408
      Hand-Object Contact Mask IoU (CIoU) validates the contact recovery of hand-pose optimization
      by measuring the IoU of the contact mask between the optimized hand-object state and the ground
409
      truth. Contacts are defined as distances less than 3mm between hand and object vertices.

413
      For hand-object tracking evaluation, we compare our results with the state-of-the-art methods gSDF
414   (Chen et al., 2023b) and HOTrack (Chen et al., 2023a). gSDF uses RGB images to reconstruct
415   objects with SDF representation, while HOTrack uses segmented point clouds to predict object
416   poses. Our method uses unsegmented point clouds as input and output object meshes, making it
417   more challenging to achieve better results. Additionally, since these baselines only work with rigid
418   objects, we compare them only with the DexYCB object categories. DexYCB does not provide
419   tactile information, so we only use VDT-Net to compare with the baseline methods here. Later, we
420   provide an empirical way to use FO with a fixed force setting, which will be discussed in Sec. 6.5.
421   The quantitative results are shown in Tab. 1. In the DexYCB dataset, our leading scores in IoU and
422   Chamfer distance demonstrate superior object tracking and reconstruction capabilities. Additionally,
423   our higher MPJPE score indicates better hand-tracking performance. gSDF’s better performance on
424   penetration depth is due to occasional incomplete object reconstruction, as illustrated in Fig. 4,
425   where the pitcher is missing its handle. For the HOT dataset, VDT-Net achieves excellent scores.
426   Significant improvements in MPJPE, PD, and CIoU after applying our force-based optimization
427   confirm the effectiveness of our design.
428   Fig. 4 shows the qualitative results of our method. Our tracking and reconstruction outperform state-
429   of-the-art methods in the DexYCB dataset and show excellent performance on deformable objects in
430   the HOT dataset. Our method accurately tracks hand movements and object deformations, thanks to
431   the fusion of flow features and original point cloud features using a transformer, which incorporates
      both the 3D information of the current frame and correspondence features from the previous frame.

      7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we introduce ViTaM-D, a dynamic hand-object interaction reconstruction framework
534   that integrates distributed tactile sensing with visual perception. Featuring the DF-Field represen-
535   tation and force-aware optimization (FO), our approach effectively captures fine contact details and
536   object deformation, and outperforms visual-only methods. We also present the HOT Dataset for
537   benchmarking deformable object manipulation. Evaluations show that ViTaM-D outperforms state-
538   of-the-art methods on both rigid and deformable objects. Future work includes integrating ViTaM-
539   D with real tactile sensors and applying it to robot imitation learning, dexterous manipulation and
      human-robot collaboration.
