009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series forecasting is a critical topic in machine learning. Although existing
012            deep learning methods have demonstrated outstanding performance and currently
013            dominate this field, the latest state-of-the-art (SOTA) models are increasingly en-
014            countering the same limitations: the blockneck of performance. We believe this
015            convergence is due to these models being based on the same mathematical founda-
016            tions. To address this issue, we draw inspiration from the universal approximation
017            theorem (UAT) and show that most commonly used deep learning models for time
018
               series forecasting are specific implementations of UAT. Based on UAT theory and
               the characteristics of time series data, we propose a new forecasting model called
019
               the Multi-Receptive Field Network (MRFNet). This architecture integrates linear,
020
               sparse matrix, convolutional, and Fourier transform modules, resulting in an inter-
021            pretable model with multiple receptive fields that can capture both global and local
022            information. The MRFNet model has been tested extensively on several popular
023            time series forecasting datasets and has achieved superior results.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Time series forecasting is a critical problem with widespread applications in areas such as weather
029   forecasting, traffic prediction, stock market analysis, and power consumption forecasting. To tackle
030   this issue, various analytical and statistical methods have been developed. Early research relied
031   on statistical measures like mean and variance to construct models, such as ARMA (McLeod and
032   Li, 1983) and ARIMA (Ho and Xie, 1998; Zhang, 2003). These models have shown impressive
033
      accuracy and robustness but have limitations in generalization ability and multivariate time series
      forecasting.
034
035   With the advent of deep learning, a series of deep learning-based time series forecasting models
036   emerged, outperforming traditional machine learning methods. The development of deep learning
037   methods for time series forecasting has largely mirrored the evolution of deep learning models them-
038   selves, starting with fully connected neural networks (Kourentzes et al., 2014; Rahman et al., 2015;
039
      Torres et al., 2018), followed by recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Bouktif et al., 2018; Lai et al.,
      2018; Bahdanau et al., 2014; Hewamalage et al., 2021), and subsequently, convolutional neural net-
040
      works, Linear model (Zeng et al., 2023), and Transformer networks (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
041
      2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2022).
042
043   In summary, the three main types of models dominating the time series forecasting field today are
044   linear models, convolutional models, and Transformer models. However, as deep learning continues
045
      to evolve, these models have begun to approach a performance bottleneck on widely used public
      time series forecasting datasets. We believe that the convergence of their performance is due to
046
      their shared mathematical foundations. To delve deeper into this issue, we require a fundamental
047
      mathematical interpretation of these models. The cornerstone of current deep learning theory is the
048   UAT (Cybenko, 2007). Therefore, our goal is to study time series forecasting networks within the
049   framework of UAT. However, UAT traditionally applies only to perceptron networks, while other
050   forms of networks are more complex and seem to diverge from the theorem. Thus, to investigate
051   complex linear, convolutional, and Transformer models within the UAT framework, we need to
052   express them in a mathematical form consistent with UAT. In UAT2LLMs (Wang and Li, 2024a)
053   and UAT2CVs (Wang and Li, 2024b), the ”Matrix-Vector Method” was proposed and used to unify
      2D and 3D convolutional, and Transformer-based models within the UAT theoretical framework.

054
      Building on these studies, we aim to unify deep learning time series forecasting models within the
055   UAT framework.
056
057   Through our derivations, we demonstrate that most deep learning models in the time series fore-
058
      casting domain, whether used independently or as combinations of the three fundamental modules
      - Linear, Convolution, and Transformer - are essentially specific implementations of UAT. This
059
      explains why they ultimately converge towards similar performance blockneck, as they represent
060
      different manifestations of the same mathematical model.
061
062   Based on UAT theory and the characteristics of time series forecasting problems, we propose
063   the MRFNet model, which integrates linear, 1D convolutional, sparse matrix transformation, and
064
      Fourier transform modules. We compare this model with SOTA methods on commonly used time
      series forecasting datasets and our model achieves SOTA performances on many datasets. Addi-
065
      tionally, we performed data preprocessing tailored to the characteristics of time series forecasting
066
      data, further enhancing performance. In conclusion, this paper aims to bridge the theoretical gap and
067   practices in time series forecasting. Our goal is to provide researchers and practitioners with tools to
068   understand and advance the theory and practice of time series forecasting. Our main contributions
069   include:
070
071          • We have demonstrated that most of the current deep-learning-based time series prediction
072            models are specific implementations of the UAT, and explained why the current SOTA
073            models converge to similar performance bottlenecks.
074
             • We proposed the MRFNet model based on the characteristics of time series prediction,
075            which balances both global and local temporal learning. Our model achieved SOTA per-
076            formances across multiple datasets, and we also proved that this model is a specific imple-
077            mentation of UAT for time series prediction.
078
             • We rearranged the data based on the inherent characteristics of time series data, which
079
               further improved the predictive performance of the model.

093   2   R ELATED W ORK
094
095
      RNN: Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) were very popular for time series forecasting a few years
096   ago, emphasizing the importance of sequence dependencies. RNNs incorporate various gating units
097   to learn connections between different sequence positions (Bouktif et al., 2018; Lai et al., 2018; Bah-
098   danau et al., 2014; Hewamalage et al., 2021). RNNs are fundamentally based on the Markov chain
099   process in mathematics. However, key bottlenecks remain, such as vanishing gradients, substantial
100   training workloads, and rapid error accumulation over longer time spans (Ribeiro et al., 2020).
101
      CNN: The use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for audio-related problems was initially
102   proposed by Oord et al. (2016). Later, Bai et al. (2018) introduced the concept of Temporal Convolu-
103   tional Networks (TCNs) as an alternative for time series forecasting (Bai et al., 2018; Vorbach et al.,
104   2021; Aksan and Hilliges, 2019; Luo and Mesgarani, 2019; Hewage et al., 2020). This approach is
105   influenced by the autoregressive Wavenet model (Oord et al., 2016) and incorporates causal convo-
106   lutions to prevent the use of future information. Additionally, TCNs employ dilated convolutions to
107   capture long-term dependencies in time series data. Several similar models have been developed,
      including works by Lara-Benı́tez et al. (2020); Wan et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2021).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Transformer: Transformers have dominated deep learning and shown significant potential in solv-
109   ing time series forecasting problems (Li et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2021; Wen et al.,
110   2022). The multi-head attention architecture can extract information while positional embeddings
111   help retain sequence position information (Kitaev et al., 2020; Zhang and Zhu, 2018; Wu et al., 2021;
112   Shen and Wang, 2022; Madhusudhanan et al., 2021). However, Transformers are computationally
113   complex, and the setting of hyperparameters greatly influences the performance of Transformer-
114   based models. To address these issues, models like Informer, Autoformer, and Fedformer were
115   developed (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022). Liu et al. (2022) tackles time
116   series problems from the perspective of stationarity, while ETSformer (Woo et al., 2022) uses expo-
117
      nential smoothing and frequency attention to replace the self-attention mechanism in Transformers,
      enhancing accuracy and efficiency. Additionally, Zhou et al. (2023) explored using pretrained large
118
      language models fine-tuned for time series prediction.
119
120   Linear: Zeng et al. (2023) argued that Transformers are not the optimal solution for time series
121   forecasting. Instead, they demonstrated that linear methods could achieve SOTA results for both
122   long-range and short-term time series forecasting, outperforming existing models.

184   Currently, deep learning models for time series forecasting can be summarized as multi-layer net-
185   works composed primarily of one or more of the following modules: linear, convolution, and Trans-
186   former. These modules are typically connected using residual connections between successive lay-
187   ers. To demonstrate that such multi-layer networks, composed of these basic modules, are concrete
188
      implementations of the UAT, we first need to show that these modules can be represented in matrix-
      vector form. Once this representation is established, we then need to prove that the mathematical
189
      form of these networks with residual connections aligns with the UAT equation, Eq. (1).
190
191   In UAT2LLMs, it has already been shown that networks composed of multiple Transformer layers
192   have a mathematical form consistent with UAT, and that linear operations can be expressed in matrix-
193   vector form. Therefore, our primary focus here is to prove that 1D convolutional networks can also
194
      be represented in matrix-vector form (see Section 4.1). (Note that we will use a special calculation
      called diamond multiplication: ˛, and W ˛ x “ WT x. More details could be found in UAT2LLMs)
195
      Following that, we demonstrate that the mathematical form of multi-layer networks with residual
196
      connections, expressed in matrix-vector terms, is consistent with UAT (see Section 4.2).

518   6   C ONCLUSION
519
520
      In this paper, we demonstrate that most models in the field of time series forecasting are specific
521   implementations of the UAT, which explains why current SOTA models in time series forecasting
522   tend to converge towards the same performance bottleneck. Based on the principles of UAT and
523   the characteristics of time series data, we have designed a new model, MRFNet, for time series
524   prediction. MRFNet integrates linear modules, sparse matrix modules, convolutional modules, and
525   Fourier transform modules, effectively capturing both global and local receptive field information.
526   Through extensive testing on various common datasets, the MRFNet model has demonstrated its
527   superiority, achieving SOTA-level performance. Additionally, by leveraging the intrinsic properties
528   of time series data, we further refined the data, significantly enhancing the performance on certain
529
      datasets. Finally, we conducted experiments to confirm that time series forecasting models based on
      UAT theory eventually converge to a similar performance bottleneck.

793   C     T HE DATA P ROBLEM OF ILI
794
795   Figure 1 illustrates the data trends for the 7 features in the ILI dataset, labeled as Class 1 to Class
796   7. Time series data generally exhibit two main characteristics: periodicity and trends within each
797
      period. The seven features share a similar period; however, it is evident that Classes 6 and 7 show
      an upward trend over time, while the overall trends of the other features remain stable.
798
799   If there is any correlation between the features, we believe it would be either related to the period
800   (which is the same across features and can thus be disregarded) or to the trend. Since Classes 6 and
801   7 both display a rising trend, while the trends of the other classes remain unchanged, we hypothesize
802   that Classes 6 and 7 should be separated from the other features. Therefore, we grouped the data
803
      into two sets: one containing Classes 6 and 7, and the other with the remaining features. We then
      trained and tested the model on these separate groups. The test results, shown in Table 3, clearly
804
      indicate a significant improvement in model performance after grouping the data for training.
