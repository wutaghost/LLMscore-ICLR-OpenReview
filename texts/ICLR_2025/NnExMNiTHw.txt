009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Speculative decoding reduces the inference latency of a target large language
012            model via utilizing a smaller and faster draft model. Its performance depends on a
013            hyperparameter K — the candidate length, i.e., the number of candidate tokens
014            for the target model to verify in each round. However, previous methods often use
015            simple heuristics to choose K, which may result in sub-optimal performance. We
016            study the choice of the candidate length K and formulate it as a Markov Decision
017            Process. We theoretically show that the optimal policy of this Markov decision
018
               process takes the form of a threshold policy, i.e., the current speculation should
               stop and be verified when the probability of getting a rejection exceeds a threshold
019
               value. Motivated by this theory, we propose SpecDec++, an enhanced version
020
               of speculative decoding that adaptively determines the candidate length on the fly.
021            We augment the draft model with a trained acceptance prediction head to predict
022            the conditional acceptance probability of the candidate tokens. SpecDec++ will
023            stop the current speculation when the predicted probability that at least one token
024            gets rejected exceeds a threshold. We implement SpecDec++ and apply it to the
025            llama-2-chat 7B & 70B model pair. Our adaptive method achieves a 2.04x speedup
026            on the Alpaca dataset (7.2% improvement over the baseline speculative decoding).
027            On the GSM8K and HumanEval datasets, our method achieves a 2.26x speedup
028            (9.4% improvement) and 2.23x speedup (11.1% improvement), respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Current state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated extraordinary capabilities
      in various language tasks and have shown early signs of artificial general intelligence (Achiam et al.,
034
      2023; Anil et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b). As the top-performing LLMs often
035
      have more than a hundred billion parameters, there is an increasing demand for serving such huge
036   models efficiently. To decrease the inference latency, motivated by speculative execution techniques
037   in processors, speculative decoding (Chen et al., 2023a; Leviathan et al., 2023) incorporates a draft
038   model, which is smaller and faster, as the speculator for the target model, which is the large
039   language model we want to accelerate. Given the current prefix, the draft model first auto-regressively
040   generates K tokens, taking substantially less time than it would take the target model. The target
041   model computes their log probabilities in parallel and then sequentially determines whether each
042   token is accepted or not. Following the first rejected token (if any), the algorithm discards the
043   remaining tokens and corrects the rejected token with a fresh sample from a modified distribution. If
044
      all tokens are accepted, a new token is sampled from the next-token probability given by the target
      model and appended to the sequence of accepted tokens, and then the process moves forward. Such
045
      draft-verify-correct loops continue until the desired output is fully generated.
046
047   The speedup effect of speculative decoding depends on two crucial aspects: (1) how well the draft
048   model aligns with the target model, and (2) how fast the draft model gets compared to the target
049   model. The two aspects influence the choice of the hyperparameter K: the number of candidate
050
      tokens generated by the draft model in each loop. When the draft model aligns well and/or runs
      fast, we can choose a larger K, which potentially allows more tokens to be accepted in each loop.
051
      However, a larger K also increases the chances of rejection so that more tokens get discarded.
052
053   Leviathan et al. (2023) studied the problem of choosing the hyperparameter K under the assumption
      that the acceptance rates of all the candidate tokens are constant. The authors showed that there

070   exists one constant K that can maximize the speedup. However, such an assumption is unrealistic
071   and does not approximate real-world cases well. Whether the draft model and the target model
072   align well depends on the hardness of predicting the next token. Intuitively, when the next token
073   is unambiguous from the prefix, the draft model and the target model align well, which means the
074
      acceptance probability of the current candidate token is large compared to other cases.
075   In this work, we aim to boost the performance of speculative decoding by adaptively choosing the
076   candidate length K for each round. We formalize the adaptive decision-making of K for speculative
077   decoding as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). The decision to make at each timestep is whether
078   or not to stop the current speculation round and submit the candidate tokens to the target model for
079   verification and correction. The objective is to minimize the total inference time taken to generate a
080
      full response. Theoretically, we show that the optimal policy takes the form of a threshold policy, i.e.,
      it is optimal to stop the speculation round whenever the probability of existing at least one rejected
081
      token in the candidates exceeds a threshold.
082
083   Inspired by the theory, we propose SpecDec++, an enhanced version of speculative decoding that
084   adaptively determines the candidate length on the fly. First, we train an acceptance prediction head
085   on top of the draft model to predict the acceptance probability of the candidate token. Training such
086
      an acceptance prediction head has two challenges: (1) there will be a severe class imbalance problem,
      e.g., most tokens generated by the draft model will have a high probability of acceptance, depending
087
      on how well the two models align; (2) the input sequence to the model contains mostly tokens from
088
      the target model and only a fraction of tokens generated by the draft model, so the training signal is
089   sparse. To overcome the two challenges, we adopt a weighted Binary Cross-Entropy loss to address
090   the class imbalance problem, and we adapt the random masking idea from BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)
091   to randomly mix tokens from the target model and the draft model to increase training efficiency.
092
      At inference time, we opt to stop the current speculation round when the predicted probability of
093
      the existence of a rejected token exceeds a constant stopping threshold. The procedure is illustrated
094
      in Figure 2. We implement SpecDec++ and apply it to llama-2-chat 7B & 70B model pair.
095   Our adaptive method achieves a 2.04x speedup compared with the 1.90x speedup of the baseline
096   speculative decoding method on the Alpaca dataset (an additional 7.2% improvement). On the easier
097   GSM8K and HumanEval datasets, our method improves the baseline from 2.07x to 2.26x speedup
098   (9.4% improvement) and from 2.00x to 2.23x speedup (11.1% improvement), respectively.
099
      We summarize the contributions below.
100
101   • We formalize the dynamic choice of candidate length in speculative decoding as a Markov Decision
102     Process (MDP). We theoretically show that when the probability that at least one token gets rejected
103     exceeds a threshold, the optimal action is to stop the speculation and submit it for verification.
104   • We propose SpecDec++, an enhanced version of speculative decoding that adaptively determines
105     the candidate length on the fly. We adopt a weighted loss and a token-mixing method to efficiently
106     train the prediction head and use it for dynamic decision-making in the decoding process.
107   • Empirically, our method achieves an additional 7.2%, 9.4%, and 11.1% improvement over the
        baseline speculative decoding on the Alpaca, HumanEval, and GSM8K datasets, respectively.

145   Inference Time of Speculative Decoding.
146   Our objective is to minimize the total inference time, which consists of
147
                                            Ttotal = tdraft Ndraft + ttarget Ntarget ,                         (2.1)
148
149   where tdraft and ttarget are the time needed for one forward pass and Ndraft and Ntarget are the total
150   number of forward passes of the draft model and the target model, respectively. Equation (2.1) holds
151   under the implicit assumption that the forward passes of each of the models take constant time, which
152
      is true when we have enough computational resources to support the increased concurrency when the
      length of the input sequence grows (Leviathan et al., 2023). We empirically verify that Equation (2.1)
153
      holds in our setting; see Section 4.2.
154
155   Let N be the number of the final generated tokens. N is a random variable inherent to the target
156   model and the initial prompt, independent of the draft model and the number of candidate tokens K
157   of each round we choose. Let Ndiscarded be the number of total discarded tokens. Then we have the
158
      following identity for Algorithm 1
159                                         Ndraft + Ntarget = N + Ndiscarded .
160
      Therefore, Ttotal can be written as
161
                                 Ttotal = T0 + tdraft Ndiscarded + (ttarget − tdraft )Ntarget ,                (2.2)

      where T0 = tdraft N is the oracle inference time.
163
164   To minimize the total inference time, we are required to trade-off between two objectives: minimizing
165   the number of the discarded tokens Ndiscarded and minimizing the number of forward passes of the
166
      target model Ntarget . The two objectives conflict with each other, as a larger K will incur more
      discarded tokens but less number of forward passes of the target model. Equation (2.2) states that the
167
      total cost is the weighted sum of the two and the weights are given by tdraft and (ttarget − tdraft ).
168
169   Metrics. To measure the benefit of a speculative decoding pipeline, we divide Equation (2.2) by N
170   and get
171
                  latency = Ttotal /N = tdraft + tdraft · Ndiscarded /N + (ttarget − tdraft ) · Ntarget /N.   (2.3)
172
173   We focus on two metrics: (1) discard rate Ndiscarded /N , which measures the average number of
174   discarded tokens per one generated token, and (1) verification rate Ntarget /N , which measures the
175   average number of the forward calls of the target model per one generated token.

177   2.1   A M OTIVATING E XAMPLE : O RACLE P ERFORMANCES OF G REEDY S PECULATIVE
178         D ECODING
179
180   Let us focus on a simplified deterministic setting of speculative decoding, where we use greedy
181   decoding for the draft model and the target model. In this setting, the draft model deterministically
182   generates a series of greedy tokens (Y1 , . . . , YK ), and the speculative decoding algorithm reduces
183
      to sequentially checking whether Yi is also the greedy token of the target model. The first rejected
      token is replaced by the greedy token of the target model. If all the tokens are accepted, an additional
184
      token is generated by the target model directly.
185
186   For a given prompt xprompt , let (X1 , X2 , . . . , XN ) be the greedy tokens generated by the target model.
187   We ask the following question:
188   What is the oracle performance of the speculative decoding algorithm we can obtain by varying the
189   number of candidate tokens, if we have the knowledge of (X1 , X2 , . . . , XN ) in hindsight?
190
      Let us consider the first speculation round. The draft model generates (Y1 , Y2 , . . . ) greedily. Let Yi be
191
      the first token such that Yi ̸= Xi . The optimal strategy is to stop the speculation at time (i − 1), so the
192
      last candidate token Yi−1 is accepted, and Yi will be generated directly by the target model, because
193   (1) if we stop the speculation earlier, then the shorter candidate tokens will still be accepted, but this
194   induces at least one unnecessary forward pass of the target model; (2) if we stop the speculation later,
195   then we waste at least one candidate token Yi . By repeating the argument, we have the following.
196
      Lemma 2.1. In the greedy decoding setting, for a given prompt xprompt , let (X1 , X2 , . . . , XN )
197
      be the greedy tokens generated by the target model. We define Yi = argmax q(· |
198   xprompt , X1 , X2 , . . . , Xi−1 ) to be the greedy token of the draft model q conditioned on the par-
199   tial generation of the target model. Let S be the set of disagreement between the draft model and the
200   target model: S = {1 ≤ i ≤ N | Yi ̸= Xi }. Then, by optimally stopping at time (i − 1) for every
201   i ∈ S, we obtain the oracle performance with Ndiscarded = 0 and Ntarget = |S| + 1.
202
203   To empirically verify this, we perform a preliminary experiment with the same setting in Section 4,
204   where we use all the prompts in the Alpaca dataset and calculate the set of disagreement S for each
205   prompt with the llama-2-chat-7B/llama-2-chat-70B model pair. The results show that the average
206
      Ntarget /N = 0.164 ± 0.078 and the corresponding oracle throughput is 27.06 ± 4.13 tokens/second
      (2.92x speedup) according to Equation (2.3) with the empirical value of (ttarget , tdraft ) reported in
207
      Section 4.2. In comparison, the average throughput for the target model without speculative decoding
208
      is 9.26 tokens/second, while speculative decoding with the best fixed K gives 17.58 tokens/second
209   (1.90x speedup) (Section 4). We can see a huge potential in adaptively tuning the candidate lengths.

460
461
      Speculative decoding. Since the proposal of speculative decoding, people have been improving
462   the algorithm from different perspectives. Our work is complementary to the works that improve
463   speculative decoding by (1) making the draft model align better with the target model (Zhou et al.,
464   2024; Agarwal et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), (2) building smaller draft models or merging draft
465   models into the target model (e.g. early-exiting) (Miao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
466   2023b; Bae et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Monea et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b), and (3) building
467   a heirachical system of speculative decoding (Spector & Re, 2023; Sun et al., 2024a). Our work is not
468   directly appliable to the methods that do not have the concept of an auto-regressive draft model (Stern
469   et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024b; Bhendawade et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024) and the retrieval-based
470
      methods (He et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Fu et al., 2024). See Appendix B for
      an extended related work about speculative decoding, token trees, and diffusion language models.
471
472   Candidate length selection. Leviathan et al. (2023) make the i.i.d. assumption on the acceptance
473   probabilities of the candidate tokens and theoretically derive the optimal choice of K. Besides,
474   Liu et al. (2024) and Kim et al. (2024) adopt a simple heuristic that ends the speculation if the
475   confidence of the current draft token distribution falls below a threshold. Xu et al. (2023) uses the
476
      cumulative product of the confidences and extends to the token tree version. In comparison, our
      work systematically studies the candidate length selection within the MDP framework and uses the
477
      cumulative product of our trained prediction head to determine the end of the speculation.

480   6                  C ONCLUSION
481
482   We study the determination of the candidate lengths for speculative decoding. We formulate the
483   problem as a Markov Decision Process and provide a theorem that gives a sufficient condition to stop
484   the current speculation. Motivated by the theoretical result, we propose SpecDec++ to adaptively
485   select the candidate length with a trained acceptance prediction head. We demonstrate significant
      speedups over baselines and our method can be seamlessly integrated with other improvements.

      A    L IMITATIONS
703
704
705   Our theoretical result contains a problem-specific constant ∆ which is hard to analyze theoretically or
706   estimate empirically. Nevertheless, the choice of the stopping threshold h can be determined through
707   hyperparameter search; see Appendix C.3. As is the case with all speculative decoding algorithms,
708
      our method relies on the implicit assumption that the draft model and the target model align well. For
      a weak draft model, the acceptance prediction head may perform badly.
709
710
711
712   B    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
713
714   Large language models are mostly based on Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) that
715   auto-regressively predict the probability of the next token given its predecessors. One bottleneck of
716   the inference speed lies in the fact that auto-regressive decoding is an inherently non-parallelizable
717   sequential operation: the probabilities of future tokens depend on the current token and there is no
718   trivial way to skip the current token when predicting future tokens. Therefore, the inference time of
719   auto-regressive decoding scales linearly with the number of the generated tokens.
720   However, the time of a forward pass to compute the log probabilities of the tokens through transform-
721   ers is nearly constant for batched sequences with different lengths within a proper range, thanks to
722   the increasingly powerful parallel computing units (Pope et al., 2023; Vaswani et al., 2017; Chen
723   et al., 2023a; Leviathan et al., 2023).
724
      Therefore, to overcome the bottleneck of the auto-regressive decoding, one can find a fast way to
725   generate K tokens, which often increases FLOPs, and the ask the target model to verify and correct
726   the candidates (Stern et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2023a; Leviathan et al., 2023); see a comprehensive
727   survey (Xia et al., 2024). For those methods to work, we assume that we have enough computational
728   resources (e.g. CUDA memories) to support the increased concurrency. Nevertheless, in the long-
729   context generation regime, the memory issue becomes prominent, which requires additional KV-cache
730   management techniques such as compression or retrieval (Li et al., 2024a; Sun et al., 2024a).
731   Improvements of Speculative Decoding Methods
732
733   The performance of speculative decoding depends on how well the draft model aligns with the target
734
      model, and how fast the draft model is compared to the target model. People have been improving
      speculative decoding in two aspects: (1) making the draft model align better with the target model
735
      via distillation (Zhou et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024) and online learning (Liu et al., 2023); and
736
      (2) making the token generation faster and cheaper, e.g. training multiple smaller draft models from
737   stratch (Miao et al., 2023).
738
739   In addition, the candidate tokens can be generated without a separate draft model (Stern et al., 2018;
740
      Li et al., 2024b; Du et al., 2024; Bhendawade et al., 2024), such as building additional modules that
      predict the next k tokens (Medusa heads (Cai et al., 2024), RNN heads (Zhang et al., 2024), soft
741
      tokens (Monea et al., 2023)), early-exiting methods that reuse the intermediate representations of the
742
      target model (Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023b; Bae et al., 2023), and retrieval-based methods that
743   involve constructing an n-gram datastore and using retrieval to generate candidates (He et al., 2023;
744   Zhao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Fu et al., 2024).
745
746
      Those techniques can be combined, resulting in a heirachical system (Spector & Re, 2023; Zhao
      et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024a).

748   Token Tree Generation, Verification and Pruning.
749
      Paralleling across the batch dimension via token trees is another direction to increase through-
750   puts (Miao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023). For greedy decoding, token tree generation
751   and verification are studied in (Cai et al., 2024). For the stochastic sampling setting, REST (He
752   et al., 2023) proposes a straightforward approach: keeping the token paths that coincide with the
753   stochastic tokens given by the target model. There are also researches extending the stochastic
754   speculative decoding to the token tree setting, which often needs to adjust the drafting and verification
755   probabilities to ensure unbiasedness, e.g. MCSD (Yang et al., 2024a), Recursive SD (Jeon et al.,
      2024), Sequoia (Chen et al., 2024b), EAGLE (Li et al., 2024b), SpecTR (Sun et al., 2024b).

756
      One important problem to study is how to construct and prune the token tree to maximize throughputs
757   and avoid heavy communication overheads, which is studied in (Chen et al., 2024b; Zhong et al.,
758   2024). Our work can serve as a starting point towards the problem, as the candidate length K can be
759   viewed as the depth of a token tree with only one branch.
760
      Diffusion language models. Diffusion language models either in the discrete space (see
761
      D3PM (Austin et al., 2021) and its follow-ups) or in the embedding space (see Diffusion-LM (Li
762
      et al., 2022) and its follow-ups) are non-autoregressive language models, whose generation time can
763   scale sub-linearly with the sequence length. BERT-type encoder-only models and auto-regressive
764   decoder-only models can be also viewed as diffusion model, with mask prediction and next-token
765   prediction being the denoising operation (Austin et al., 2021). Viewing next-token prediction as
766   Jacobi iteration (Santilli et al., 2023) and denoising operation is a powerful idea and it leads to
767   subsequent work such as lookahead decoding (Fu et al., 2024) and consistency LLMs (Kou et al.,
