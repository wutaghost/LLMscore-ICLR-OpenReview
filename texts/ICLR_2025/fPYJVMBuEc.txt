011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-View Representation Learning (MVRL) aims to learn the joint representa-
014            tion from diverse data sources by discovering complex relationships among them.
015            In MVRL, since the downstream task information and the view availability are
016            often unknown a-priori, it is essential for the joint representation to be robust to
017            the partial availability of views. However, existing methods exhibit various limi-
018
               tations, such as discarding potentially valuable view-specific information, lacking
               the ability to extract representation from an arbitrary subset of views, or requiring
019
               considerable computational resources that increase exponentially with the number
020
               of views. To address these challenges, we present a scalable MVRL framework
021            based on contrastive learning. Our approach employs a set of encoders that is able
022            to extract representations from arbitrary subset of views, and jointly trains them
023            with a computation cost that scales linearly with the number of views. We con-
024            ducted comprehensive evaluations across 7 MVRL benchmark datasets ranging
025            from 2 to 8 views, demonstrating that our method robustly handles diverse input
026            view combinations and outperforms strong baseline methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Multi-View Representation Learning (MVRL) focuses on learning the joint representation of in-
033
      stances from various types of views without relying on label or task information (Hwang et al.,
      2021). By uncovering the potentially complex relationships among views, the joint representation
034
      must capture important underlying factors from these views, which is essential for downstream tasks
035
      such as data fusion equipped with multiple sensors (Zhang et al., 2011) and medical diagnoses based
036   on diverse records (Yuan et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018).
037
038   Providing more views of each instance usually helps eliciting more accurate representations, but it
039
      poses three major challenges. Firstly, it requires more sophisticated cross-view association, which
      involves identifying shared and view-specific factors of variation across all views, under the varying
040
      levels of correlations among views. Secondly, increasing the number of views typically increases
041
      the difficulty and cost of collecting the data, necessitating the ability to handle missing views during
042   learning and inference stages. Lastly, it significantly raises the computational cost of learning the
043   representation. For example, to address the scenario where an arbitrary set of views is missing, we
044   could simply learn the representations for every subset of views, but this typically results in compu-
045   tation costs that grow exponentially with the number of views, rendering the approach unscalable.
046
      Recent MVRL approaches leveraging Contrastive Learning (CL) (Tian et al., 2020; Poklukar et al.,
047
      2022) or VAEs (Wu & Goodman, 2018; Shi et al., 2019; Sutter et al., 2021; Hwang et al., 2021)
048   have shown promising results in downstream tasks (e.g., classification) where capturing shared in-
049   formation across views is critical. However, these approaches typically have at least one of the fol-
050   lowing limitations: (1) discarding view-specific information that could be relevant to downstream
051   tasks (Tian et al., 2020; Poklukar et al., 2022), (2) lacking a mechanism to learn representations
052   from any subset of views (Wu & Goodman, 2018; Shi et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020; Poklukar et al.,
053   2022; Hwang et al., 2021), or (3) incurring computational cost that grows exponentially (Sutter et al.,
      2021) or quadratically (Tian et al., 2020) with the number of views.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this work, we propose a scalable, information-theoretic MVRL framework that effectively ad-
055   dresses these three challenges. First, we formulate MVRL as the problem of encoding represen-
056   tations from every subset of views that are informative enough to capture both view-specific and
057   shared factors of variation. We then introduce an information-theoretic objective that jointly trains
058   all subset-view representations with a computational cost that scales linearly with the number of
059   views: by combining the Mahalanobis distance and the InfoNCE (Oord et al., 2018; Poole et al.,
060   2019) objective, we derive a variational lower bound that calibrates each representation to general-
061   ize well in downstream tasks. Through extensive evaluations on 7 MVRL benchmark datasets, we
062   demonstrate that our method robustly encodes representations from various combinations of input
063
      views and outperforms strong baseline methods. Our contributions are three-fold:
064
            1. Theoretical contribution: Proposition 1 in Section 3.2 formally shows that the single Mu-
065            tual Information (MI) term between complete views and the joint representation encoded
066            by Mixture of Experts (Shi et al., 2019) or its variants lower bounds the weighted average
067            of various MI terms. This enables efficient representation learning for all subsets of views.
068
            2. Algorithmic contribution: In Section 3.3, we derive a tractable lower bound for our single
069            MI objective, allowing the MoPoE (Sutter et al., 2021) joint encoder to learn and calibrate
070            exponentially many subset-view representations with a computational cost that scales lin-
071            early with the number of views. Importantly, this represents a significant improvement over
072            prior work (Sutter et al., 2021), which trained the same encoder with a computational cost
073            that increased exponentially with the number of views.
074         3. Empirical contribution: By conducting comprehensive evaluations on 7 MVRL bench-
075            mark datasets spanning 2 to 8 views, we demonstrate the robustness of our method across
076            diverse input-view combinations, consistently surpassing strong baseline methods.

079   2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Multi-View Fusion MVRL methods can be categorized into early fusion and late fusion ap-
082
      proaches (Liang et al., 2021), depending on how they encode multiple views. Early fusion methods
      encode all views into a joint representation by feeding a stack of input views to one joint encoder. For
083
      instance, transformer models are trained with learning objectives such as masked reconstruction (He
084
      et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2022; Georgescu et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022; Mo & Morgado, 2023) or
085   with autoregressive modeling (Ramesh et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; 2023; Wu et al., 2024) on
086   multiple input views. Although these methods benefit from high expressivity in encoding the joint
087   representation, they commonly suffer from heavy computational costs that scale quadratically with
088   the number of views. On the other hand, late fusion methods encode each view into a representation
089   with a dedicated encoder per view and then aggregate these single-view representations into one
090   joint representation. Contrastive Multi-View Representation Learning methods (Tian et al., 2020;
091   Poklukar et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023) and Multi-View VAEs (Wu & Good-
092   man, 2018; Shi et al., 2019; Sutter et al., 2020; 2021; Hwang et al., 2021) fall into this category.
093
      These late fusion approaches are closely related to our work and are further reviewed below.
094   Contrastive Multi-View Representation Learning Contrastive Multi-View Coding (CMC)(Tian
095   et al., 2020) is one of the most representative works in Contrastive MVRL and has been applied to
096   pretraining multimodal foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023). It optimizes
097   the InfoNCE (Oord et al., 2018; Poole et al., 2019) objective between every pair of single-view
098   representations from different views by maximizing their cosine similarity, thereby aligning repre-
099
      sentations from multiple views. However, its single-view representations are encouraged to capture
      only the shared factors of variation since its InfoNCE terms are upper-bounded by the mutual infor-
100
      mation (MI) between two views (Cover, 1999; Wang et al., 2022a), which quantifies the amount of
101
      shared information. Furthermore, the computational cost combinatorially increases with the number
102   of views, making it difficult to apply CMC to a large number of views. In contrast, GMC (Poklukar
103   et al., 2022) employs a complete-view representation to align the single-view representations by
104   maximizing the cosine similarity between the complete-view representation and each single-view
105   representation. Although its computational cost scales linearly with the number of views, it also
106   suffers from discarding view-specific factors due to maximizing the cosine similarities, as observed
107   in our experiments (Sec 4.1). Additionally, GMC employs two backbone encoders for each view,
      doubling the number of encoder parameters.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
109   Multi-View VAEs Multi-View VAEs (Wu & Goodman, 2018; Shi et al., 2019; Sutter et al., 2021;
110   Hwang et al., 2021) learn the joint representation with multiple single-view VAEs by maximizing
111   the evidence lower bound (ELBO). MVAE (Wu & Goodman, 2018) employs the Product of Experts
112   (PoE) as its joint encoder, which effectively aggregates information from all views using an Inverse-
113   Variance Weighted (IVW) average of single-view representations. Since PoE struggles with cali-
114
      brating each single-view encoder, MVTCAE (Hwang et al., 2021) derives Conditional Variational
      Information Bottlenecks (CVIBs) from their Total Correlation objective to calibrate single-view en-
115
      coders toward the PoE joint encoder. In contrast, MMVAE (Shi et al., 2019) employs the Mixture
116
      of Experts (MoE) as its joint encoder, which takes an arithmetic mean of single-view encoders.
117   Although MoE explicitly optimizes each single-view encoder, it potentially fails to aggregate infor-
118   mation from multiple views, just taking each view-specific representation as a separate component
119   in the mixture. To improve MMVAE, MoPoE-VAE (Sutter et al., 2021) introduces a Mixture of
120   Product of Experts (MoPoE) as its joint encoder, which defines the representation of each subset of
121   views by the PoE of views in the subset and combines all subset-view representations using MoE.
122   Although this approach significantly improves MMVAE, it requires computing the density from the
123   MoPoE, resulting in a computational cost that grows exponentially with the number of views. We
124   further investigate these encoder structures in Section 3.1.
125   Since our method is also a late-fusion approach that trains the MoPoE joint encoder with a con-
126   trastive learning objective, it is closely related to both Contrastive MVRL methods and Multi-View
127   VAEs. A detailed comparison between these methods and our approach is provided in Section B.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
304
305
      To evaluate the quality of the representation learned by our method, we conducted evaluations in
306
      linear regression and linear classification tasks in three sets of experiments. In all experiments, we
307   aim to see if our method can robustly perform given any subset of all views.
308
309   Baseline methods We compared our method with strong baseline methods including CMC (Tian
310   et al., 2020), GMC (Poklukar et al., 2022), MoPoE-VAE (Sutter et al., 2021), and MVTCAE (Hwang
311
      et al., 2021). Brief reviews of these baseline methods can be found in Section 2. Since CMC lacks
      a joint representation of multiple views, we computed the average of single-view representations
312
      to aggregate multiple views. Similarly, since GMC learns to aggregate only complete views, we
313
      computed the average of single-view representations when subset-view representations are available
314   in the downstream tasks. In addition to these methods, we included GMCs, a variant of GMC
315   that has only one backbone encoder per view, similar to other comparing methods. All results are
316   averaged over 10 independent runs. Detailed information on the hyperparameter settings of each
317   method can be found in Section D.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we introduced Contrast with Aggregation (CwA), a scalable MVRL framework that ef-
533
      fectively aggregates information from any subset of views. By formulating an information-theoretic
534   objective applicable to existing encoder models, we enabled the optimization of every subset-view
535   representation with a computational cost that increases linearly with the number of views. Ad-
536   ditionally, by integrating the Mahalanobis distance into the InfoNCE objective, we reformulated
537   our method as a CL approach that calibrates each subset-view representation. Extensive evalua-
538   tions on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrated CwA’s superior robustness, significantly
539   outperforming existing methods in leveraging additional views and aggregating information across
      different views.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Our method can be applied in situations where multiple sensors in a multi-sensor system malfunc-
543   tion, potentially posing security risks. For example, in self-driving vehicles with multiple camera
544   inputs, adverse weather conditions like rain can corrupt the sensors. Additionally, this method could
545   decrease the number of views needed for the system to function, which might help reduce the overall
546   carbon footprint. However, there is a potential misuse case where the reduction in the number of
547   views could be exploited to compromise the system’s security. For instance, if the system relies on
548   fewer sensor inputs, it might become more vulnerable to targeted attacks that spoof or manipulate
549   the limited data available. Such vulnerabilities could lead to scenarios where the self-driving vehicle
550   misinterprets its surroundings, potentially causing accidents or unauthorized access to the vehicle’s
551
      control systems. Therefore, while the method offers significant benefits, it is crucial to implement
      robust safeguards to prevent and mitigate any potential security threats arising from reduced sensor
552
      inputs.

       E.4   C OMPARISON TO MULTI - VIEW CLUSTERING METHODS
1351
1352   We additionally evaluated DCP (Lin et al., 2022) and InfoDDC (Trosten et al., 2023), which are
1353   mutli-view clustering methods that support more than two views. Specifically, DCP can learn single-
1354   view representations without labels by applying the three components to every pair of views: (1)
1355   within-view reconstruction, (2) cross-view contrastive learning, and (3) cross-view latent prediction.
1356   InfoDDC also learns single-view representations through cross-view contrastive learning between
1357   every pair of views. Furthermore, it learns the weight of each single-view representation using its
1358
       unsupervised clustering objective, resulting in the weighted average of single-view representations
       as its complete-view representation.
1359
1360   Based on the official implementations of DCP and InfoDDC, we carefully tuned their hyperparam-
1361   eters, including coefficients of loss terms, temperature values related to their contrastive learning,
1362   and the number of clusters (to be equal the number of labels).

1551               Ablation study To assess the impact of the joint encoder choice in CwA, we conducted an abla-
1552               tion study with different encoder choices. Specifically, we trained MoE and PoE joint encoder by
1553               optimizing our objective equation 6. Figure 11b shows the results. While the MoE joint encoder
1554               shows competitive performance with a single view, its performance barely improves with the addi-
1555               tional views. This is because MoE combines only the single-view encoders but not the rest of the
1556               subset-view encoders, thus maximizing MIs only between single views and their representations as
1557               discussed in Section 3.2. Consequently, its ability to extract representations from multiple views is
1558               limited. In contrast, the PoE joint encoder exhibits monotonic improvement by leveraging additional
1559
                   views, though its performance is still limited compared to ours. This is because it learns to extract

1560
                   representation, but does not consider extracting from other subsets of views as discussed in Sec-
1561
                   tion 3.2. The results indicate that the MoPoE joint encoder, which optimizes all combinations of
1562               views by maximizing MI between every subset of views and its representations, is advantageous.
