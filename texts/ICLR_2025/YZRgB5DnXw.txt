009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Small language models, referred to as LLMs with fewer than 10 billion parameters
012            in this work, face critical challenges in problem-solving tasks, often achieving less
013            than 10% accuracy, highlighting the urgent need for effective solutions. While
014            much of the existing research has focused on enhancing the performance of larger
015            models like GPT, an important question remains: Can techniques developed for
016            large models be adapted effectively for smaller ones? Moreover, is it possible to
017            improve these smaller models to the point where they rival, or even outperform,
018            larger models such as GPT-4 in problem-solving tasks?
019            In this paper, we introduce Evaluation-Oriented Problem-Solving (EOP), a novel
020            framework aimed at enhancing the problem-solving capabilities of small LLMs.
021
               Our approach significantly boosts the performance of these models, achieving a
               2% higher accuracy on Python Puzzles compared to standard GPT-4 and a 27%
022
               improvement over state-of-the-art prompting methods using GPT-4 in the Game of
023
               24. Beyond these results, EOP also demonstrates notable accuracy improvements
024            on other tasks. These findings suggest that, with the appropriate strategies, small
025            LLMs can achieve substantial performance gains in problem-solving, challenging
026            the prevailing notion that scaling model size is the primary path to improvement.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have advanced rapidly, demonstrating strong performance across
032   various intelligent tasks such as commonsense reasoning (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Wang, 2018),
033   question-answering (Joshi et al., 2017), scientific knowledge acquisition (Clark et al., 2018; Cobbe
034   et al., 2021), and programming (Chen et al., 2021). The open-source community has also contributed
035   to this growth, offering models of diverse sizes and capabilities. Small LLMs, typically with fewer
036   than 10 billion parameters, are gaining traction due to their advantages in cost and energy efficiency,
037   lower hardware requirements for self-deployment, and reduced domain adaptation costs, all while
038
      maintaining relatively high performance on various benchmarks such as reading comprehension and
      common-sense understanding (Bansal et al., 2024; Javaheripi et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024).
039
040   Despite their strengths in natural language understanding, LLMs face significant challenges in
041   problem-solving (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). Even ad-
042   vanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024a) struggle, consistently scoring poorly on various bench-
043   marks (OpenAI, 2024b), with smaller models performing even worse due to their limited parameters.
044
      Experiments on three problem-solving tasks revealed this gap: GPT-4 scored just 3.0% on Game of
      24 (Yao et al., 2024) and 31.1% on Python Puzzles (Schuster et al., 2021), while smaller models
045
      fared even worse, as shown in Figure 1.
046
047   Various approaches, such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), Tree-of-Thought
048   (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), Graph-of-Thought (GoT) (Besta et al., 2024), and Meta-prompting (Suzgun
049   & Kalai, 2024), have been proposed to enhance the performance of GPT models. These prompting
050
      techniques significantly elevate the reasoning capabilities of GPT-4. In particular, Meta-prompting
      proves to be the most effective method, leading to substantial improvements in accuracy when ap-
051
      plied to GPT-4, as demonstrated in Figure 1.
052
053   Given these contexts, we explored three critical questions to better understand the capabilities of
      small LLMs in reasoning tasks:

084   Q1: Are current methods designed for large models applicable to small LLMs? Although
085   advanced prompting techniques, such as meta-prompting, effectively enhance reasoning in models
086   like GPT-4, our experiments revealed no significant improvement in small LLMs. As shown in
087   Figure 1, methods that succeed with larger models do not appear to be well-suited for their smaller
088   counterparts. The reasons for this discrepancy remain unclear.
089
      Q2: Are small LLMs applicable in problem-solving tasks? Despite their limitations, small LLMs
090   are not entirely ineffective. When using with an oracle evaluator to cherry-pick the correct answer
091   from multiple attempts, they showed improved accuracy, as presented in Figure 2. This indicates that
092   small models can benefit from repeated trials, though they still struggle to identify correct solutions
093   on their own.
094
      Q3: How can small LLMs be made effective in problem-solving tasks? This question forms the
095
      core motivation of our work. We propose that the key to unlocking the problem-solving potential of
096   small LLMs lies in developing a tailored approach that compensates for their weaknesses through
097   evaluation-focused prompting techniques. By leveraging multiple trials and structured evaluations,
098   small models can perform significantly better without requiring extensive fine-tuning. To develop
099   a general framework that is effective across different tasks (i.e. task-agnostic), we chose three
100   problem sets with varying levels of evaluation difficulty: Python Puzzles (Schuster et al., 2021)
101   requires solving Python problems with code comprehension. We have an oracle evaluator (a Python
102   interpreter), making them the simplest to evaluate. Game of 24 (Yao et al., 2024) requires using 4
103   numbers and 3 operators (each from + − ×÷) to get an arithmetic result of 24. It is slightly more
104
      challenging but still straightforward, as it only requires checking if an expression equals 24. Word
      Sorting (Srivastava et al., 2023) requires output an alphabetically sorted list from a list of words. It
105
      poses a great challenge on evaluation, as evaluating the correctness involves checking whether each
106
      pair of words in a list is sorted character by character, which is considerably more complex.
107

149   2   R ELATED WORK
150
151   Recent advancements in prompting methods have significantly enhanced the reasoning capabilities
152   of LLMs. One of the earliest approaches, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), guides LLMs
153   by providing few-shot examples of reasoning steps or by appending instructions like ”Let’s think
154   step by step” to encourage intermediate thinking. This method improves the likelihood of generating
155   correct answers through guided reasoning. Building upon CoT, CoT with Self-Consistency (Wang
156   et al., 2022) further refines this process by performing multiple reasoning attempts and selecting
157   the final answer through result aggregation, which improves accuracy. Based on this method, more
158
      advanced techniques are developed:
159   Tailored and Structured Thoughts: Methods like Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), break
160   down problems into subproblems, structuring reasoning as a tree. Graph-of-Thoughts (GoT) (Besta
161   et al., 2024) extends this concept by transforming the tree into a graph, enhancing performance on
      more complex tasks like sorting. However, both methods require manual task-specific constructions.

250   3.1   M ULTIPLE T RIALS
251
252   As shown in Figure 2, the accuracy of problem-solving continues to increase when conducting re-
253   peated trials, assuming we have an oracle evaluator. In contrast, methods such as ToT, GoT, and
254   Meta-Prompting also use multiple rounds of reasoning. However, these approaches fail to signifi-
255   cantly improve the performance of standard small LLMs, as demonstrated in Figure 4b. We suspect
256   that this is because small LLMs are easily misled by incorrect answers during the refining process.
257
      This issue is discussed further in Section 3.2.
258   Given this, we opt to run small LLMs in multiple independent rounds using a breadth-first approach
259   rather than adopting depth-first answer-refining methods, which are better suited for powerful mod-
260   els like GPT-4. The distinction between these approaches is illustrated in Figure 4a.
261
262   3.2   AGGREGATION E VALUATOR
263
264   As discussed in Section 1, small LLMs have the potential to produce correct answers across multiple
265   trials. However, it is often challenging for these models to identify which of their generated answers
266
      is correct. For tasks that can be transformed into Python problems, a Python interpreter serves as
      a reliable oracle. In such cases, if one of the trial answers is correct, the interpreter will accurately
267
      validate the result.
268
269   For other tasks, the LLM must act as its own evaluator, responsible for assessing the correctness of
      its outputs. For example, in the Game of 24, evaluating whether an expression equals 24 is relatively

292   3.3     R EASONING E VALUATOR
293
294   3.3.1    P YTHON P UZZLES
295   In contrast to previous work (Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024), our goal is to design a framework
296   that is task-agnostic, eliminating the need for custom reasoning processes for each ad-hoc task. For
297   instance, Meta-prompting (Suzgun & Kalai, 2024) introduces a pipeline with complex instructions,
298   refining answers recursively and evaluating with a powerful model. However, applying such tech-
299   niques to small LLMs often fails or even harms performance.
300
      To create a generalizable reasoning process for small LLMs, we use Python Puzzles as a key exam-
301   ple. Python problem-solving is widely applicable, and many other tasks, like Game of 24, can be
302   transformed into Python problems. Instead of merely giving the LLM a wrong answer and expect-
303   ing it to correct itself, EOP leverages the evaluator to provide more detailed feedback on why an
304   answer is wrong, avoiding potential misleading information from the previous response. The prob-
305   lem solver first attempts to solve the task step by step, with the reasoning evaluator then assessing
306   the correctness of each step in the reasoning process.
307   In Python Puzzles, maintaining syntactic correctness while solving a problem step-by-step can be
308   difficult. To address this, we instruct the problem solver to insert assert statements at key points to
309   verify partial results. If an assertion fails, the error, along with any other runtime errors, is reported
310   to the debugger agent, which helps identify both logical and runtime issues.
311
      For example, in Figure 5, we showcase a full reasoning flow from question to final answer. The task
312
      requires returning a list of even indices from a given list, with odd indices assigned a constant value.
313   The assertions first verify the total length of the output list, then check whether the even-indexed
314   elements match the input, and finally confirm that odd-indexed elements are equal to the constant
315   value. The reasoning evaluator identifies an error in the second assertion, enabling downstream
316   agents in the pipeline to refine the solution based on accurate feedback.
317
      By adopting this approach, the LLM can pinpoint the specific part of the reasoning process respon-
318
      sible for the incorrect outcome, significantly reducing the risk of being misled by earlier wrong
319   answers.

      3.3.3    W ORD S ORTING
397
398   For the Word Sorting task, the evaluation can be quite challenging, as demonstrated in Section 4.3,
399   where the accuracy of the aggregation evaluator hovers around 50%. Similar difficulties are also
400   encountered with the reasoning evaluator. Although we could make the reasoning evaluator specific
401   to the Word Sorting task, such as instructing it to compare two adjacent words at a time to simplify
402   the evaluation, this approach introduces two issues: 1) it is not task-agnostic, and 2) even with
403   this modification, the task remains beyond the capabilities of small LLMs, and no improvement in
404   accuracy was observed using this method. Consequently, for Word Sorting and all tasks other than
405
      Python-related problems, we employ a general reasoning evaluator, as described in Section 3.3.2.
406
407   3.4     D EBUGGER
408
      Reasoning evaluators assist in analyzing errors in previous answers. To streamline the incorporation
409   of error analysis into the problem solver, we introduce a debugger that converts error messages into
410   actionable suggestions. These suggestions, along with the original question and the prior answer, are
411   then fed back to the problem solver. In this way, the connection between the errors and the solution
412   refinement is smoother. We use the Debugger specifically for Python-related tasks, because Python
413   interpreters only provide error messages without analysis. Debugger in other tasks simply passes
414   the outputs of the reasoning evaluator.
415
416   3.5     H IERARCHICAL A NSWER F ORMULATOR
417
418
      Due to the reliance on the evaluation process, precise answer formulation is crucial, especially for
      Python tasks, as formatting errors can lead to compilation failures. To ensure correct answer for-
419
      matting, we employ a hierarchical answer formulation unit: first, we attempt to extract the answer
420
      using predefined rules. If this fails, we utilize a small LLM to generate the answer in the specified
