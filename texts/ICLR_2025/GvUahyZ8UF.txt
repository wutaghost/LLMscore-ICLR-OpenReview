                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Designing biological sequences with desired properties is a significant challenge
012
               due to the combinatorially vast search space and the high cost of evaluating each
013
               candidate sequence. To address these challenges, reinforcement learning (RL)
014            methods, such as GFlowNets, utilize proxy models for rapid reward evaluation
015            and annotated data for policy training. Although these approaches have shown
016            promise in generating diverse and novel sequences, the limited training data rela-
017            tive to the vast search space often leads to the misspecification of proxy for out-of-
018            distribution inputs. We introduce δ-Conservative Search, a novel off-policy search
019            method for training GFlowNets designed to improve robustness against proxy mis-
020            specification. The key idea is to incorporate conservativeness, controlled by pa-
021            rameter δ, to constrain the search to reliable regions. Specifically, we inject noise
022
               into high-score offline sequences by randomly masking tokens with a Bernoulli
               distribution of parameter δ and then denoise masked tokens using the GFlowNet
023
               policy. Additionally, δ is adaptively adjusted based on the uncertainty of the proxy
024
               model for each data point. This enables the reflection of proxy uncertainty to de-
025            termine the level of conservativeness. Experimental results demonstrate that our
026            method consistently outperforms existing machine learning methods in discov-
027            ering high-score sequences across diverse tasks—including DNA, RNA, protein,
028            and peptide design—especially in large-scale scenarios. The code is available at
029            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/delta cs-0477.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Designing biological sequences with desired properties is crucial in therapeutics and biotechnology
035
      (Zimmer, 2002; Lorenz et al., 2011; Barrera et al., 2016; Sample et al., 2019; Ogden et al., 2019).
036   However, this task is challenging due to the combinatorially large search space and the expensive
037   and black-box nature of objective functions. Recent advances in deep learning methods for biolog-
038   ical sequence design have shown significant promise at overcoming these challenges (Brookes &
039   Listgarten, 2018; Brookes et al., 2019; Angermueller et al., 2020; Jain et al., 2022).
040
      Among various approaches, reinforcement learning (RL), which leverages a proxy model as a reward
041
      function, has emerged as one of the successful paradigms for automatic biological sequence design
042   (Angermueller et al., 2020). RL methods have the benefit of exploring diverse sequence spaces by
043   generating sequences token-by-token from scratch, enabling the discovery of novel sequences. They
044   employ deep neural networks as inexpensive proxy models to approximate costly oracle objective
045   functions. The proxy model serves as a reward function for training deep RL algorithms, enabling
046   the policy network to generate high-reward biological sequences. The model can be trained in an
047   active learning manner (Gal et al., 2017), iteratively annotating new data by querying the oracle with
048   points generated using the policy; these iterations are called query rounds. There are two approaches
049   for training the policy in this context: on-policy and off-policy.
050   DyNA PPO (Angermueller et al., 2020), a representative on-policy RL method for biological se-
051   quence design, employs Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO; Schulman et al., 2017) within a proxy-
052   based active training loop. While DyNA PPO has demonstrated effectiveness in various biological
053   sequence design tasks, its major limitation is the limited search flexibility inherent to on-policy
      methods. It cannot effectively leverage offline data points, like data collected from previous rounds.

066   Conversely, Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets; Bengio et al., 2021), off-policy RL methods
067   akin to maximum entropy policies (Tiapkin et al., 2024; Deleu et al., 2024), offer diversity-seeking
068   capabilities and flexible exploration strategies. Jain et al. (2022) applied GFlowNets to biological
069   sequence design with additional Bayesian active learning schemes. They leveraged the off-policy
070   nature of GFlowNets by mixing offline datasets with on-policy data during training. This approach
071   provided more stable training compared to DyNA PPO and resulted in better performance.
072   However, recent studies have consistently reported that GFlowNets perform poorly on long-
073   sequence tasks such as green fluorescent protein (GFP) design (Kim et al., 2023; Surana et al.,
074   2024). We hypothesize that this poor performance stems from the insufficient quality of the proxy
075   model in the early rounds. For example, in the typical benchmark, the proxy is trained with 5,000
076   sequences, while GFPs have a combinatorial search space of 20238 , which is bigger than 10309 .
077   GFlowNets are capable of generating sequences from scratch and producing novel sequences be-
078
      yond the data points. However, when the quality of the proxy model is unreliable, novel sequences
      that are out-of-distribution to the proxy model yield unreliable results (Trabucco et al., 2021; Yu
079
      et al., 2021). This motivates us to introduce a conservative search strategy to restrict search space to
080
      the neighborhood of the observed data point during RL training and generating sequences to query
081   for the next round.
082
083   Contribution. In this paper, we propose a novel off-policy search method called δ-Conservative
084
      Search (δ-CS), which enables a trade-off between sequence novelty and robustness to proxy mis-
      specification by using a conservativeness parameter δ. Specifically, we iteratively train a GFlowNet
085
      using δ-CS as follows: (1) we inject noise by independently masking tokens in high-score offline
086
      sequences with Bernoulli distribution with parameter δ; (2) the GFlowNet policy sequentially de-
087   noises the masked tokens; (3) we use these denoised sequences to train the policy. When δ = 1
088   (full on-policy), this becomes equivalent to generating full sequences from scratch, and when δ = 0
089   (fully conservative), this reduces to only showing offline sequences in off-policy training. We adap-
090   tively adjust δ(x; σ) using the proxy model’s uncertainty estimates σ(x) for each data point x. This
091   approach allows the level of conservativeness to be adaptively adjusted based on the prediction un-
092   certainty of each data point. Figure 1 illustrates the overall procedure of the δ-CS algorithm.
093   Our extensive experiments demonstrate that δ-CS significantly improves GFlowNets, successfully
094   discovering higher-score sequences compared to existing model-based optimization methods on di-
095   verse tasks, including DNA, RNA, protein, and peptide design. This result offers a robust and
096   scalable framework for advancing research and applications in biotechnology and synthetic biology.

101   We aim to discover sequences x ∈ V L that exhibit desired properties, where V denotes the vocab-
102   ulary, such as amino acids or nucleotides, and L represents the sequence length, which is usually
103   fixed. The desired properties are evaluated by a black-box oracle function f : V L → R, which
104
      evaluates the desired property of a given sequence, such as binding affinity or enzymatic activity.
      Evaluating f is often both expensive and time-consuming since it typically involves wet-lab experi-
105
      ments or high-fidelity simulations.
106
107   Advancements in experimental techniques have enabled the parallel synthesis and evaluation of
      sequences in batches. Therefore, lab-in-the-loop processes are emerging as practical settings that

184   trollable exploration through a conservative parameter δ. Here, δ defines the Bernoulli distribution
185   governing the masking of tokens in a sequence. Our algorithm is conducted by the following steps:
186

187
188   • Inject noise by masking tokens into x using the noise injection policy Pnoise (x̃ | x, δ).
189   • Denoise the masked tokens using the denoising policy Pdenoise (x̂ | x̃; θ).

193   learning GFlowNets, see Algorithm 1.

      4.1   A DJUSTING CONSERVATIVENESS PARAMETER δ
225
226   Determining the conservative parameter δ is a crucial aspect of the algorithm. We propose and study
227   two variants, constant and adaptive δ.
228
229   Constant. As a simple approach, we set δ as a constant, selecting it to have the noise policy mask
230   4–15 tokens per sequence. Despite its simplicity, this choice effectively enhances policy training and
231   leads to the discovery of high-scoring sequences during active rounds; we provide further studies on
232   δ-CS with a constant δ in Appendix B.1.
233
234   Adaptive. Another intuitive approach is to adjust δ based on the uncertainty of the proxy σ on
235
      each sequence x, that is δ(x; σ). Specifically, we define a function that assigns lower δ values for
      highly uncertain samples and vice versa: δ(x; σ) = δconst − λσ(x). We estimate σ(x), the standard
236
      deviation of the proxy model fϕ (x), via MC dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) or an ensemble
237
      method (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). λ is a scaling factor and related to the influence of the
238   proxy uncertainty on δ; we set it to satisfy λEPD0 (x) σ(x) ≈ L1 based on the observations from the
239   initial round.
240
241   In our main experiments, we use adaptive δ(x, σ) as the default setup.

244
245   5.1   B IOLOGICAL S EQUENCE D ESIGN
246
247   Designing biological sequences using machine learning methods is widely studied. Bayesian op-
248   timization (BO) methods (Mockus, 2005; Belanger et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022) exploit poste-
249
      rior inference over newly acquired data points to update a Bayesian proxy model that can measure
      useful uncertainty. The BO method can be greatly improved in high-dimensional tasks by using
250
      trust-region-based search restrictions (Wan et al., 2021; Eriksson et al., 2019; Biswas et al., 2021;
251
      Khan et al., 2023) and by combining it with deep generative models (Stanton et al., 2022; Gruver
252   et al., 2024). However, these methods usually suffer from scalability issues due to the complexity
253   of the Gaussian process (GP) kernel (Belanger et al., 2019) or the difficulty of sampling from an
254   intractable posterior (Zhang et al., 2022).
255
      Offline model-based optimization (MBO) (Kumar & Levine, 2020; Trabucco et al., 2021; Yu et al.,
256
      2021; Chen et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Yun et al., 2024) also addresses the
257
      design of biological sequences using offline datasets only, which can be highly efficient because
258   they do not require oracle queries. These approaches have reported meaningful findings, such as the
259   conservative requirements on proxy models since proxy models tend to give high rewards on unseen
260   samples (Trabucco et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b). This supports
261   our approach of adaptive conservatism in the search process. Surana et al. (2024) recently noted that
262   offline design and existing benchmarks are insufficient to reflect biological reliability, indicating that
263   settings without additional Oracle queries might be too idealistic.
264   Reinforcement learning methods, such as DyNA PPO (Angermueller et al., 2020) and GFlowNets
265   (Bengio et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2022; 2023b; Hernández-Garcı́a et al., 2024), and sampling with
266   generative models (Brookes & Listgarten, 2018; Brookes et al., 2019; Das et al., 2021; Song & Li,
267   2023) aim to search the biological sequence space using a sequential decision-making process with
268   a policy, starting from scratch. Similarly, sampling with generative models (Brookes & Listgarten,
269   2018; Brookes et al., 2019; Song & Li, 2023) searches the sequence space using generative mod-
      els like VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014). While these approaches allow for the creation of novel


                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      sequences, as sequences are generated from scratch, they are relatively prone to incomplete proxy
271   models, particularly in regions where the proxy is misclassified due to being out-of-distribution.
272
273   An alternative line of research is evolutionary search (Arnold, 1998; Bloom & Arnold, 2009;
274
      Schreiber et al., 2020; Sinai et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2022; Ghari et al., 2023; Kirjner et al., 2024), a
      popular method in biological sequence design. Especially Ghari et al. (2023) proposed GFNSeqEd-
275
      itor, which utilizes GFlowNets as prior distribution to edit biological sequences as an evolutionary
276
      search. Evolutionary search methods iteratively edit given sequences and constrain the new se-
277   quences so as not to deviate too far from the seed sequence; they usually start from the wild-type,
278   which occurs in nature. This can be viewed as constrained optimization, where out-of-distribution
279   for the proxy model can lead to unrealistic and low-score biological sequences. Consequently, they
280   do not aim to produce highly novel sequences. Our method can be seen as a hybrid of off-policy RL
281   and evolutionary search, capitalizing on both the high novelty offered by GFlowNets and the high
282   rewards with out-of-distribution robustness provided by constrained search where they are properly
283   balanced by δ. Our experimental comparison with GFNSeqEditor (Ghari et al., 2023) demonstrates
284   this hybridization balanced by δ enables us to discover sequences with greater novelty and higher
285
      rewards rather than merely using the GFlowNet policy as editing priors.

287   5.2   GF LOW N ETS
288
289   GFlowNets were introduced by Bengio et al. (2021) and unified by Bengio et al. (2023), demon-
290
      strating effectiveness across various domains, including language modeling (Hu et al., 2023), dif-
      fusion models (Sendera et al., 2024; Venkatraman et al., 2024), and scientific discovery (Jain et al.,
291
      2022; 2023a). Several works have aimed to improve their training methods for better credit assign-
292
      ment (Malkin et al., 2022; Madan et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2024) and extensions to
293   multi-objective settings (Jain et al., 2023b; Chen & Mauch, 2024). Orthogonal to this, researchers
294   have investigated better off-policy exploration methods (Rector-Brooks et al., 2023; Shen et al.,
295   2023; ?; Kim et al., 2024c;a;b). Our method is particularly related to these exploration methods,
296   yet the major difference is that they are designed under the assumption that the reward model is
297   accurate, which does not hold in active learning and thus requires conservativeness.

477   6.5              ACHIEVING PARETO FRONTIER WITH BALANCING CAPABILITY OF δ-CS
478
      In this analysis, we demonstrate that δ-CS achieves a balanced search using δ, producing Pareto fron-
479
      tiers or comparable results to the baseline methods: GFN-AL (Jain et al., 2022) and GFNSeqEditor
480   (Ghari et al., 2023). Notably, GFN-AL can be seen as a variant of our method with δ = 1, which
481   fully utilizes the entire trajectory search. This approach is expected to yield high novelty and di-
482   versity, but it is also prone to generating low rewards due to the increased risk of out-of-distribution
483   samples affecting the proxy model. GFNSeqEditor, on the other hand, leverages GFlowNets as
484   a prior, editing from a wild-type sequence. It is designed to deliver reliable performance and be
485   more robust to out-of-distribution issues by constraining the search to sequences similar to the wild
      type. However, unlike δ-CS, GFNSeqEditor does not utilize such obtained samples for training


                                                                                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      GFlowNets in full trajectory level; GFNSeqEditor is expected to have lower diversity and novelty
487   compared to GFN-AL and δ-CS.
488
489   As shown in Fig. 3, GFN-AL generally produces higher diversity and novelty in the RNA and GFP
490
      tasks compared to GFNSeqEditor. However, GFNSeqEditor performs better in terms of reward on
      the large-scale GFP task, whereas GFN-AL struggles due to the lack of a constrained search pro-
491
      cedure in such a large combinatorial space. In contrast, δ-CS achieves Pareto-optimal performance
492
      compared to both methods, clearly outperforming GFNSeqEditor across six tasks, with higher re-
493   wards, diversity, and novelty. For the RNA and GFP tasks, we achieve higher scores than GFN-AL
494   while maintaining similar novelty but slightly lower diversity. In the AAV task, δ-CS shows a distinct
495   Pareto improvement. These results demonstrate that δ-CS provides a beneficial balance by combin-
496   ing conservative search with amortized inference on full trajectories using off-policy GFlowNets
497   training, effectively capturing the strengths of both GFN-AL and GFNSeqEditor. The results with
498   various δ are provided in Appendix B.3.

500   6.6   S TUDY ON PROXY FAILURE AND CONSERVATIVENESS EFFECT
501
502   Task: Hard TF-Bind-8. By modifying the initial dataset distribution and the landscape, we can
503
      make a harder version of TF-Bind-8. Specifically, we collect the initial dataset near a certain se-
      quence (considered as a wild-type) while ensuring that the initial sequences have lower scores than
504
      the given sequence, which is 0.431. The size of D0 is 1,024. Furthermore, we modify the landscape
505
      to give 0 rewards for sequences with scores lower than 0.3. These features are often observed in pro-
506   tein design tasks, where the search space is extremely large, e.g., 20238 previously–with a limited
507   real-world dataset, and the score often falls to 0.
508
509
      Proxy failure. As shown in Figure 2, δ-CS gives slightly better performance than GFN-AL, but
      only marginally. This is because the TF-Bind-8 task is relatively easy to optimize, leading to similar
510
      results across methods. To more clearly assess the effectiveness of δ-CS, we conduct several studies
511
      on the harder TF-Bind-8 task, which is more difficult to optimize. Figure 4a illustrates the proxy
512   values and true scores for all x ∈ X in the first round. While the proxy provides accurate predictions
513   for the initial data points x ∈ D0 (represented by the red dots), it produces unreliable predictions
514   for points outside D0 . This supports our hypothesis that the proxy model performs poorly on out-
515   of-distribution data.
516
      Effect of δ conservativeness. Figure 4b illustrates that the correlation between the oracle f and the
517
      proxy fϕ significantly decreases as data points move farther from the observed dataset. This strongly
518   motivates the use of δ-CS, which constrains the search bounds using δ. By limiting the search to
519   within these constrained edit distances, δ-CS enhances the correlation with the oracle.

524   7     C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   In this paper, we introduced a novel off-policy sampling method for GFlowNets, called δ-CS, which
527   provides controllable conservativeness through the use of a δ parameter. Additionally, we proposed
528   an adaptive conservativeness approach by adjusting δ for each data point based on prediction uncer-
529   tainty. We demonstrated the effectiveness of δ-CS in active learning GFlowNets, achieving strong
530
      performance across various biological sequence design tasks, including DNA, RNA, protein, and
      peptide design, consistently outperforming existing baselines.
531
532   Limitations and future works. The main limitation of our method is that it doesn’t fundamentally
533   resolve the drawbacks of active learning; it serves as a useful tool within the existing framework. In-
534   vestigating robust proxy training and uncertainty measurement techniques remains necessary. These
535   improvements are orthogonal to our approach and can enhance δ-CS when integrated.
536   Future work includes combining δ-CS with existing GlowNet methods. For example, applying
537   improved credit assignment for larger-scale tasks (Jang et al., 2024) and extending to multi-objective
538   settings (Jain et al., 2023b; Chen & Mauch, 2024) could significantly boost its applicability and
539   effectiveness.

842
843
      For training proxy models, we follow the procedure of (Jain et al., 2022). We use Adam (Kingma,
      2015) optimizer with learning rate 1×10−5 and batch size of 256. The maximum proxy update is set
844
      as 3000. To prevent over-fitting, we use early stopping using the 10% of the dataset as a validation
845
      set and terminate the training procedure if validation loss does not improve for five consecutive
846   iterations.

      A.2       P OLICY TRAINING
849
850   As described in Section 6, we employ a two-layer long short-term memory (LSTM; Hochreiter
851   & Schmidhuber, 1997) with 512 hidden dimensions. The policy is trained with a learning rate of
852   5 × 10−4 with a batch size of 256. The learning rate of Z is set as 10−3 . The coefficient κ in
853   Equation (2) is set as 0.1 for TF-Bind-8 and AMP with MC dropout, according to Jain et al. (2022),
854   and 1.0 for RNA and protein design with Ensemble following Ren et al. (2022).

856   A.3       I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS OF BASELINES
857
858   We adopt open-source code from FLEXS benchmark (Sinai et al., 2020).
859
860
       • AdaLead (Sinai et al., 2020): We use a default settings of hyperparmeters for AdaLead. Specif-
         ically, we use a recombination rate of 0.2, mutation rate of 1/L, where L is sequence length, and
861
         threshold τ = 0.05.
862
863    • DbAS (Brookes & Listgarten, 2018): We implement DbAS with variational autoencoder (VAE;
         Kingma & Welling, 2014) as the generator. The input is a one-hot encoding vector, and the


                                                                  16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
         output latent dimension is 2. In each cycle, DbAS starts by training the VAE with the top 20%
865      sequences in terms of the score.
866
       • CbAS (Brookes et al., 2019): Similar to DbAS, we implement CbAS with VAE. The main differ-
867
         ence from DbAS is that we select top 20% sequences with the weights p(x|z, θ(0) )/q(x|z, ϕ(t) ),
868
869
         where p(·; θ(0) ) is trained with the ground-truth samples and q(·; ϕ(t) ) is trained on the generated
         sequences over t training rounds.
870
871    • DyNA PPO (Angermueller et al., 2020): We closely follow the algorithm presented in (Anger-
872      mueller et al., 2020). For a fair comparison, we use CNN ensembles to parameterize the proxy
873
         model instead of suggested architectures.
874    • CMA-ES (Hansen, 2006): We implement a covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy
875      (CMA-ES) for sequence generation. As the generated samples from CMA-ES are continuous,
876      we convert the continuous vectors into one-hot representation by computing the argmax at each
877
         sequence position.
878    • BO (Snoek et al., 2012): We use classical GP-BO algorithm for all tasks. For Gaussian Pro-
879      cess Regressor (GPR), we use a default setting from the sklearn library. For the acquisition
880      function, they use Thompson sampling (Russo et al., 2018).
881
      Furthermore, we employ GFN-AL and GFNSeqEditor. We adopt the original implementation and
882
      setup for TF-Bind-8 and AMP. For newly added tasks, we report better results among the original
883   MLP policy and the LSTM policy. Note that GFP in FLEXS is different from the one employed in
884   GFA-AL; we treat this as a new task based on the observation in the work from Surana et al. (2024).
885
886    • GFN-AL (Jain et al., 2022): We strictly follow hyperparameters of the original code in they
887      conduct experiments on TF-Bind-8 and AMP. The proxy is parameterized using an MLP with
888      two layers of 2,048 hidden. For the policy, a 2-layer MLP with 2,048 hidden dimensions is used,
889      but we also test it with a 2-layer LSTM policy.
890    • GFNSeqEditor (Ghari et al., 2023): We implemented the editing procedure on top of the GFN-
891      AL. Note that GFNSeqEditor does not utilize the proxy model, so the GFlowNets policy is
892      trained using offline data only with the same policy training procedure of GFN-AL. GFNSe-
893      qEditor can also implicitly control the edit percentage with its hyperparameters, which are set
894      δ = 0.01, σ = 0.0001, λ = 0.1 in this study. Note that δ is not the conservativeness parameter.

       B.11   A BLATION S TUDIES ON VARIOUS P ROXY M ODEL Q UALITIES
1459
1460   To further verify that δ-CS is robust to proxy misspecification compared to other GFN methods, we
1461   conducted additional experiments to test whether this hypothesis holds at different levels of proxy
1462   model quality.
1463
       To degrade the proxy model quality, we truncated the initial dataset at different levels—50%, 25%,
1464   and 10% percentiles based on reward values. Proxy models trained on datasets with lower percentile
1465   cutoffs are more misspecified for higher-reward data points, making GFN training and search more
1466   challenging. Under these circumstances, we compared our method with GFN-AL and LS-GFN
1467   (Kim et al., 2024d) as GFN baselines.
1468
       As shown in the figure above, the performance decreases as the percentile decreases, which is ex-
1469
       pected because the proxy quality deteriorates significantly. Among the baselines, our method con-
1470   sistently provides substantially better performance than the others. This demonstrates that our hy-
1471   pothesis—that a conservative search with δ-CS is necessary—holds across different levels of proxy
