009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Cold Posterior Effect (CPE) is a phenomenon in Bayesian Deep Learning
012            (BDL), where tempering the posterior to a cold temperature often improves the
013            predictive performance of the posterior predictive distribution (PPD). Although
014            the term ‘CPE’ suggests colder temperatures are inherently better, the BDL com-
015            munity increasingly recognizes that this is not always the case. Despite this, there
016            remains no systematic method for finding the optimal temperature beyond grid
017            search. In this work, we propose a data-driven approach to select the temperature
018
               that maximizes test log-predictive density, treating the temperature as a model pa-
               rameter and estimating it directly from the data. We empirically demonstrate that
019
               our method performs comparably to grid search, at a fraction of the cost, across
020
               both regression and classification tasks. Finally, we highlight the differing per-
021            spectives on CPE between the BDL and Generalized Bayes communities: while
022            the former primarily emphasizes the predictive performance of the PPD, the latter
023            prioritizes the utility of the posterior under model misspecification; these distinct
024            objectives lead to different temperature preferences.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The last two decades have seen substantial advances in the development of machine learning tools.
030   This has led to deployment across a range of application domains, from image recognition and
031
      natural language processing to autonomous vehicles and medical diagnostics, to name a few. Driven
      in no small part by the technological advances that have enabled large-scale training of high-fidelity
032
      models, these models obtain impressive generalization performance and accuracy that are not readily
033
      explained with classical statistical wisdom.
034
035   One promising avenue that has been explored for explaining and enhancing performance, as well as
036   providing much-needed robustness guarantees, is Bayesian deep learning (BDL). However, extend-
037
      ing classical Bayesian techniques to the machine learning setting is a formidable task—the scale of
      both the data and model size render naı̈ve extensions intractable. Combined with the fact that neural
038
      network models are typically singular (Wei et al., 2022), the mismatch between the size of the data
039
      and the number of parameters makes many of the classical asymptotics inappropriate when applied
040   to problems of interest. Furthermore, a number of phenomena have been observed when analyz-
041   ing deep learning models that were previously considered curiosities or corner-cases in the classical
042   setting.
043
      A prominent example of this is the so-called cold posterior effect (CPE). Named in analogy with
044
      statistical physics, tempered posteriors are obtained by raising the posterior density to the power of
045
      an artificial inverse-temperature parameter. The CPE refers to improved generalization performance
046   of the posterior predictive density (PPD) in both regression (Adlam et al., 2020) and classification
047   (Wenzel et al., 2020) tasks when the temperature T is taken to be cold with 0 < T < 1. This
048   peculiar effect is frequently viewed as a hack to improve generalization performance, and has led to
049   a plethora of works that attempt to explain or ‘fix’ the CPE, see Section 5 for a literature review.
050
051   Contribution. There is growing recognition in the BDL community that, despite the term ‘CPE’,
052   colder temperatures do not always result in better predictive performance for the PPD (Adlam et al.,
053   2020; Zhang et al., 2024). Unfortunately, the common approach of temperature tuning via grid
      search is computationally expensive, as it requires extra posterior sampling for multiple T across

      the grid1 . Sampling from a cold posterior is challenging and requires careful tuning of the sampler
055   with temperature diagnostics (Wenzel et al., 2020). To address these issues, we propose a data-driven
056   method to select an appropriate temperature. To the best of our knowledge, no such dedicated tool
057   exists without appealing to intermediate approximations (e.g., variational inference (Laves et al.,
058   2021)). Our method only requires maximizing a likelihood function to find a suitable temperature.
059   This can usually be done as part of the sampler warm-up phase, and importantly, does not require
060   any extra posterior sampling.

124   It is commonly believed that the PPD (3) at β = 1 is optimal for test LPD when the model is
125   well-specified (Adlam et al., 2020; Aitchison, 2021). However, this is not always the case (see Ap-
126   pendix A for a counter-example). In fact, Zhang et al. (2024) argues that in the likelihood tempering
127
      case, β = 1 can only be optimal if the expected Gibbs training loss remains unchanged with the
      inclusion of new data. We follow the reasoning in Zhang et al. (2024) and reach a similar conclusion
128
      for posterior tempering; see Appendix B for details. This motivates the development of an efficient
129
      method to select the optimal β.

410   5             CPE THROUGH THE LENS OF BAYESIAN D EEP L EARNING
411
412
      Since the publication of Wenzel et al. (2020), there have been numerous attempts to explain CPE,
413   with particular focus on predictive metrics such as test LPD, accuracy (for classification), and MSE
414   (for regression). In addition to the arguments presented in Section 3, we provide a summary of the
415   popular insights into CPE from the BDL literature here.
416
417
      Poor posterior approximation. One explanation for the presence or absence of CPE is inadequate
418
      posterior approximation, e.g., an inappropriate choice of step size, or the omission of Metropolis-
419   Hastings steps in the SGMCMC sampler. However, as shown in Appendices A and B, CPE is not
420   solely an artifact of poor posterior approximation and can be observed theoretically.
421
422
423
      Likelihood corruption due to data augmentation. It has been widely observed that the use of
      data augmentation often amplifies CPE (Izmailov et al., 2021), while being less pronounced when
424
      data augmentation is turned off. It is argued that the augmented data violate the usual independent
425
      and identically distributed (i.i.d.) assumption imposed on the data. However, CPE has been found
426   to persist even after accounting for this assumption violation (Nabarro et al., 2022). Therefore,
427   CPE is unlikely to be a mere artifact of data augmentation. In a separate analysis, Kapoor et al.
428   (2022) argues that the SGMCMC sampler will converge to a tempered posterior in the presence
429   of data augmentation. They concluded that the likelihood is implicitly raised to a power equal
430   to the number of augmentations, and raising the likelihood to a power reciprocal of this number
431   should approximately recover the standard posterior. However, they do not find this adjustment
      alone sufficient to remove CPE completely.


                                                                                      8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Model misspecification. It has been argued that data augmentation and curation may lead to
433   model misspecification, in particular overestimating the aleatoric uncertainty in the data (Aitchison,
434   2021; Kapoor et al., 2022). Therefore, tempering is proposed to be an effective tool for correction.
435   This finding aligns with Bachmann et al. (2022), which showed the optimal temperature dependent
436   on both the aleatoric uncertainty in the data and the ‘invariance’ of the model to augmented data.
437
438   Prior misspecification. While the exact interpretation of a ‘well-specified prior’ is debatable, For-
439   tuin et al. (2022) conducted a large-scale experiment to study the effect of tempering under Gaussian
440   (isotropic or with correlated covariance) and heavy-tailed priors (Student’s-t or Laplace) on four
441   metrics: test LPD, accuracy, expected calibration error (Naeini et al., 2015), and out-of-distribution
442   detection accuracy. They observed that β = 1 is indeed optimal for test LPD and accuracy when us-
443   ing a heavy-tailed prior. However, the standard posteriors derived from heavy-tailed priors also tend
444   to underperform compared to tempered posteriors derived from Gaussian priors. Notably, according
445
      to their experiments, warmer temperatures tend to perform better for expected calibration error, and
      there is no general trend in optimal β for out-of-distribution detection accuracy. Therefore, their
446
      work suggests that the optimal β is not only dependent on the truth-model-prior triplet but also on
447
      the evaluation metric.
448
449
      Attempts to ‘fix’ the CPE. Within much of the CPE literature, tempering is often seen as a ‘hack’
450
      that strays from the Bayesian principle. This has prompted the development of various ‘fixes’ —
451   models and priors that induce standard posteriors with similar predictive performance to tempered
452   posteriors (see, e.g., Fortuin et al., 2022; Kapoor et al., 2022; Marek et al., 2024). However, we
453   argue that this is unnecessary, as tempered posteriors of arbitrary β > 0 are special cases of the
454   generalized Bayes posterior (Bissiri et al., 2016); see Appendix L for a primer. Therefore, posterior
455   tempering does not deviate from the Bayesian principle.
456

      6       CPE THROUGH THE LENS OF G ENERALIZED BAYES
459
460
      In GB, it is a standard practice to tune β to improve the utility of the tempered posterior (Zhang,
      2006a; Grünwald, 2012; Bissiri et al., 2016). Specifically, the likelihood is frequently (but not
461
      always) tempered to a warmer temperature, which contrasts with the cold temperature used in BDL.
462
      In this section, we review some recent developments in GB to clarify the role of tempering in GB
463   and this apparent contradiction.
464
465
      Ensuring posterior concentration on the KL minimizer. Posterior concentration on the KL min-
466
      imizer θ† = arg minθ DKL (q(y|x)|p(y|x, θ))3 as n → ∞ is often a desired property for many
467   statistical applications. Proofs establishing this property (Barron & Cover, 1991; Zhang, 2006a;
468   Grünwald, 2007) typically assume that the following inequality holds for all θ in the parameter
469   space:
470

                                            p(y|x, θ)
471                              Eq(x,y)                     ≤ 1, for all θ ∈ Θ.
472
                                            p(y|x, θ† )
473   It is easy to verify that the inequality holds at β = 1 for well-specified models, where q(x, y) =
474   p(y|x, θ† )q(x). The same cannot be said for misspecified models in general. However, Grünwald
475   (2012) shows that this inequality can still hold for many misspecified models for some warm tem-
476   peratures β ≤ βcritical < 1, and proposed the SafeBayes algorithm to determine βcritical . Therefore,
477   SafeBayes is presented as a tool to achieve posterior concentration on θ† .
478
      However, posterior concentration is not always desirable in the context of maximizing test LPD
479   under model misspecification. Note that taking β ≤ βcritical results in the corresponding PPD con-
480   centrating on the ‘plug-in’ predictive density p(y|x, θ† ) as n → ∞. This may be undesirable if one
481   prioritizes the test LPD of the PPD, since the plug-in p(y|x, θ† ) may not exhibit good test LPD. For
482   example, when the misspecified model is non-convex, the PPD can lie outside the model family and
483   be closer to the truth than the plug-in p(y|x, θ† ). Therefore, avoiding posterior concentration in this
484   situation can actually result in a PPD with a higher test LPD.

486
      Calibrating credible and prediction intervals. Constructing well-calibrated credible and pre-
487   diction intervals with the nominal frequentist coverage probability is known to be challenging, as
488   obtaining the posterior variance of a misspecified model is difficult (Kleijn & van der Vaart, 2012,
489   Example 2.1). These issues can be mitigated by appropriately tuning β, which affects the spread of
490   the posterior. To this end, Syring & Martin (2019) and Wu & Martin (2021) developed algorithms
491   to select β for calibrating credible and prediction intervals, respectively.
492
493   Calibrating prior-to-posterior information gain. In decision-theoretic GB (Bissiri et al., 2016,
494   see Appendix L for a primer), tempered posteriors (with likelihood tempering only) are seen as
495   an update rule that combines data information with prior belief. From this perspective, β can be
496   interpreted as a ‘learning rate’ at which information is ‘transferred’ to the posterior. Hence, it is
497   reasonable to calibrate the information gain at each update. Temperature selection algorithms that
498   follow this approach include Holmes & Walker (2017) and Lyddon et al. (2019).
499
500   Improving test LPD. Concurrent to our work, McLatchie et al. (2024) analyzed the role of β 4 in
501   improving test LPD theoretically. They concluded that, in the moderately large n regime and assum-
502   ing posterior concentration at θ† , the test LPD shows little improvement once β becomes sufficiently
503   large. Although their motivation aligns with ours, their theoretical results do not extend to singular
504
      models (e.g., neural networks) or over-parameterized models (e.g., ResNet20 on CIFAR10).
505
506   6.1       L IMITATIONS OF THE GB FRAMEWORK FOR ANALYZING BDL MODELS
507
508   The existing theory in GB, though elegant, is limited to regular and under-parameterized models
509
      and cannot fully account for CPE observed in BDL. Moreover, the metrics emphasized by GB often
      differ from those prioritized by BDL practitioners, such as test LPD. As a result, GB works do not
510
      provide a prescriptive methodology for selecting an appropriate temperature in BDL. Additionally,
511
      many current temperature selection algorithms (Grünwald, 2012; Syring & Martin, 2019; Wu &
512   Martin, 2021) require repeated posterior computations, making them impractical for modern deep
513   learning models. Finally, the GB literature primarily considers posteriors with likelihood tempering
514   only, i.e., leaving the prior without tempering. However, since many priors p(θ) encountered in BDL
515   are proper and bounded, prior tempering can be regarded as rescaling the prior (Kapoor et al., 2022,
516   Section C.2). Therefore, many of the arguments in GB still apply.
517

      7       C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this work, we proposed a data-driven approach for selecting a good β for use in either of the PPDs
522
      (3) and (8). Our approach circumvents the costly grid search method, i.e., sampling posterior at each
523   β across the grid, by optimizing a likelihood function (7) to obtain a good β. The β̂ ∗ obtained via our
524
      method was shown to achieve comparable test LPD to that obtained from a grid search, all without
      performing any extra posterior sampling.
525
526   Additionally, we presented a detailed discussion to address the seeming contradiction in the opti-
527   mal β recommendations from the BDL and GB communities. We concluded that the optimal β can
528   differ depending on the specific downstream task.
529
530   Limitation. Our method is subject to several limitations that require future attention. Firstly, a
531   poorly-tuned optimization procedure may result in a poor estimation of β̂ ∗ . Secondly, our method
532   has only been empirically tested with the ubiquitous Gaussian priors. Lastly, while our method
533   is empirically effective for modern neural network models and theoretically justified for Gaussian
534   linear regression, a formal guarantee for general neural network models is still lacking.
535
536   Future directions. We hope that this work will inspire further research on data-driven approaches
537   to select β, aimed not only at minimizing test LPD but also at optimizing other metrics, such as
538   expected calibration error.

1199   I.2   R ANK - NORMALIZED SPLIT-R̂
1200
1201   In Table 6, we report the rank-normalized, split-R̂ (Vehtari et al., 2021) on the potential energy (10),
1202   which is invariant to the permutation of neural network weights. This statistic is a strict improvement
1203   over the traditional potential scale reduction factor R̂ (Gelman & Rubin, 1992) and split-R̂ (Gelman
1204   et al., 2013). The R̂ statistic compares between-chain and within-chain variances, thus requiring
1205   multiple independently-initialized Markov chains to compute. It was designed based on the idea
1206   that, for Markov chains that are mixing well, they should converge to the same limiting distribution
1207   regardless of initialization. Therefore, the between-chain and within-chain variances should roughly
1208   be the same, and a R̂ closer to 1 is considered better. The term ‘split’ indicates that the Markov
1209   chains are divided in half, resulting in double the number of Markov chains with half the original
1210   length. The variances are computed across these doubled number of Markov chains to detect poor
1211   convergence within an original chain. The ‘traditional’ R̂ is computed on the original values of the
1212   potential energy, while the ‘rank-normalized’ R̂ is computed on the rank-normalized values of the
1213   potential energy and does not require the limiting distribution to have a finite mean or variance.
1214   In Bayesian deep learning, the posterior distribution is almost always multi-modal (Izmailov et al.,
1215   2021), and we should not expect the independently-initialized Markov chains to always converge to
1216   the same limiting distribution. Therefore, we should not be overly alarmed by the relatively ‘large’
1217   readings of R̂ in Table 6. These values are large by the usual standard in Bayesian statistics but are
1218   common in Bayesian deep learning (Izmailov et al., 2021; Fortuin et al., 2022).

       The classical Bayesian rule (1) updating prior belief p(θ) to the posterior belief p(θ|D) arises from
1407   Bayes’ Theorem. An implicit assumption in this procedure for optimal performance is that the model
1408   is correctly specified: there exists some θ0 such that p(y|x, θ0 ) = q(y|x) for all (x, y). The adage
1409   that “all models are wrong” (Box, 1976) highlights that this assumption should not be expected to
1410   hold. Consequently, it does not make sense to infer on a parameter θ that lacks a connection to the
1411   true data-generation distribution.
1412
       Using a decision-theoretic argument, Bissiri et al. (2016) offers an alternative justification for us-
1413   ing (1), even when the model is misspecified. Suppose that we are interested in a quantity θ0 that
1414   minimizes the expected loss under the truth q(x, y), that is,
1415                                               Z
1416                                θ0 := arg min ℓ(θ, x, y)q(x, y) dy dx                                (16)
1417                                              θ

1418   for some loss function ℓ. Then, we would like to derive an update rule ψ that takes an observation
1419   (x1 , y1 ) and updates our prior belief on θ0 to a posterior belief,
1420
                                         p(θ|x1 , y1 ) = ψ{ℓ(θ, x1 , y1 ), p(θ)}.
1421
1422   Furthermore, the update rule should be coherent. That is, suppose that we would like to update our
1423   posterior with two data points {(x1 , y1 ), (x2 , y2 )}, the update rule should satisfy
1424               ψ{ℓ(θ, x2 , y2 ), ψ{ℓ(θ, x1 , y1 ), p(θ)}} ≡ ψ{ℓ(θ, x2 , y2 ) + ℓ(θ, x1 , y1 ), p(θ)}.
1425
1426

       data are processed. Bissiri et al. (2016) shows that all coherent update rules take the form

1432
       the generalized Bayes posterior, which has been widely analyzed (Zhang, 2006a;b; Jiang & Tanner,
1433   2008; Bhattacharya et al., 2019; Alquier et al., 2016; Alquier & Ridgway, 2020). Note that we must
1434   take care to have a finite normalizing constant to (17),

1439   Under certain regularity conditions, the generalized Bayes posterior will concentrate on θ0 as
1440   n → ∞; a proof can be found in McLatchie et al. (2024, Lemma 5). The key advantage of this
1441   framework in lies in its ability to directly infer the quantity of interest (i.e., θ0 ) using a loss function,
1442   without necessitating any probabilistic assumptions that relate θ0 to the data. For formal proofs, we
1443   direct interested readers to (Bissiri et al., 2016, Section 1).

1445   L.1    C ALIBRATING INFORMATION GAIN
1446
1447
       The tempered posterior, as defined in (2), is a special parameterisation of the generalized Bayes
       posterior achieved by setting the loss to be − log p(y|x, θ) and scaling it by a factor of β > 0,
1448
       i.e. ℓ(θ, x, y) = −β log p(y|x, θ), and concentrating the prior to an equivalent extent. Therefore,
1449
       the tempered posterior has an interpretation of a coherent update rule for the KL minimizer θ† =
1450   arg minθ −β log p(y|x, θ) = arg minθ DKL (q(y|x)∥p(y|x, θ)), as defined in the main text. From
1451   this perspective, the temperature has the role of controlling the amount of information being ‘added’
1452   to the prior in each update step. Holmes & Walker (2017) proposed setting β such that the expected
1453   information gain of our setup (LHS of (18)) matches that of a hypothetical experiment (RHS of (18)),
1454                Z                                     Z
1455                   D(pβ (θ|D), p(θ))q(x, y)dydx = D(p(θ|D), p(θ))p(y|x, θ† )q(x)dydx                (18)
1456
1457   where D is a divergence that measures the information gain from a prior to a posterior, and pβ is a
       tempered posterior (with likelihood tempering only).

1458
       On the LHS of (18), we are measuring the expected information gain in our existing setup,
1459   i.e., q(x, y) is unknown, from a prior to a tempered posterior pβ . Note that this integral is a function
1460   of β. Then, Holmes & Walker (2017) proposed picking a β that matches up the expected informa-
1461   tion gain from a well-specified experiment that targets the same quantity of interest θ† , i.e., having
1462   the truth be p(·|x, θ† )q(x). In the well-specified setting, the optimal β is 1, and thus the information
1463   gain is computed from the prior to a posterior at β = 1. This proposed method is based on the
1464   principle that the expected information gain should match across posteriors that are targeting the
1465   same quantity of interest. In their follow up work, Lyddon et al. (2019) proposed selecting β such
1466   that the asymptotic Fisher information number of the generalized Bayes posterior to match to that
1467
       derived from the “loss-likelihood” bootstrap method, which is a sampling method for general Bayes
       posteriors.

       Accounting for data augmentation in BDL is non-trivial, as the data are no longer i.i.d. This has led
1472   to the development of several techniques to address this issue (Nabarro et al., 2022; Kapoor et al.,

1474   et al., 2016), as the generalized Bayes posterior is derived from iterative belief updates rather than
1475   relying on the independence assumption. We can then regard the posterior as reflecting our belief in
1476   the KL minimizer from the ‘augmented’ truth to our model.
