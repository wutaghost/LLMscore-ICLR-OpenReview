011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Explainability is necessary for tasks that require a clear reason for a given result
014            such as finance or biomedical research. Recent explainability methodologies have
015            focused on attention, gradient, and Shapley value methods. These do not handle
016
               data with strong associated prior knowledge and fail to constrain explainability
               results by relationships that may exist between predictive features.
017
018
               We propose a GraphPINE, a novel graph neural network (GNN) architecture that
               leverages domain-specific prior knowledge for node importance score initialization.
019
               Use cases in biomedicine necessitate generating hypotheses related to specific
020
               nodes. Commonly, there is a manual post-prediction step examining literature
021            (i.e., prior knowledge) to better understand features. While node importance can
022            be obtained for gradient and attention-based methods after prediction, these node
023            importances lack complementary prior knowledge; GraphPINE seeks to overcome
024            this limitation. GraphPINE differs from other GNNs with gating methods that
025            utilize an LSTM-like sequential format such that we introduce an importance propa-
026            gation layer that unifies 1) updates for feature matrix and node importances, jointly
027            and 2) uses GNN-based graph propagation of feature values. This initialization
028            and updating mechanism allows for more informed feature learning and improved
029
               graph representation.
030            We apply GraphPINE to cancer drug response prediction using pharmacogenomics
031
               data (i.e., both drug screening and gene data collected by several assays) for
               5K gene nodes included in a gene-gene input graph with drug-target interaction
032
               (DTI) knowledge graph as initial importance. The gene-gene graph and DTIs
033
               were taken from literature curated prior knowledge sources and weighted by the
034            literature information. GraphPINE demonstrates competitive performance and
035            achieves a PR-AUC of 0.894 and ROC-AUC of 0.796 across 952 drugs. To
036            highlight the interpretability aspect of our work, we provide the ability to generate
037            sub-graphs of node importances. While our use case is related to biology, our
038            work is generally applicable to tasks where information is separately known about
039            feature relationships. Code: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
040            GraphPINE-40DE.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
044
045
      Drug response prediction (DRP) is an open research challenge in personalized medicine and drug
      discovery. DRP and Drug-Target Interaction (DTI) represent distinct but interconnected concepts
046
      in pharmaceutical research. While DTI focuses on predicting the molecular interactions between
047
      drugs and specific proteins or genes, DRP aims to predict if biological systems (e.g., cells) are viable
048   in response to drug treatments. There is no absolute requirement that DRP methods use biological
049   interaction information. However, studies have shown value in the use of molecular interactions
050   for DRP (Sokolov et al., 2016; Costello et al., 2014). Work in this research area seeks to improve
051   treatment outcomes and reduce adverse effects. However, the complex interplay between drug
052   compounds and cellular entities makes this task challenging. Traditional approaches often fail to
053   capture the intricate network of interactions that influence drug response, leading to suboptimal
      predictions with limited interpretability. Despite recent advancements, current DRP methods still


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      face challenges such as data heterogeneity, limited sample sizes, and the need for multi-omics
055   integration (Azuaje, 2017; Lu, 2018; Vamathevan et al., 2019).
056
057   Greater data availability combined with algorithmic improvements have led to an increase in machine
058
      learning (ML) techniques in this research area. GNNs have emerged as a promising approach due to
      their ability to model complex relational data (Kipf & Welling, 2016). Recent GNN variants, such
059
      as Graph Transformer Networks (Yun et al., 2019) and Graph Diffusion Networks (Klicpera et al.,
060
      2019), have shown promise in capturing complex, long-range dependencies in biological networks.
061   However, these advanced architectures often come at the cost of increased complexity and reduced
062   interpretability. This leads to two main limitations in existing GNN models for DRP. First, many
063   models do not incorporate known biological information, such as DTI. This omission can lead to
064   predictions that, while accurate, may not align with known biological mechanisms. Second, the
065   “black box” nature of many deep learning models makes it difficult for researchers and clinicians to
066   understand and trust the predictions. This lack of interpretability is a significant barrier to adopting
067   these models for furthering understanding of drug mechanisms.
068   While some attempts have been made to incorporate biological priors into GNNs (Zitnik et al., 2018)
069   or improve interpretability (Ying et al., 2019), no existing method addresses both challenges in
070   the context of DRP. To address these limitations, we introduce GraphPINE (Graph Propagating
071   Importance Network for Explanation), a novel GNN approach combining the predictive power of
072   deep learning with biologically informed feature importance propagation and interpretability.
073   GraphPINE builds upon recent advancements in GNNs, such as Graph Transformer (Shi et al., 2020)
074   and explainable AI techniques (Ying et al., 2019). The key innovation of GraphPINE lies in its
075   Importance Propagation (IP) Layer, which updates and propagates gene importance scores across the
076   network during the learning process. This mechanism allows GraphPINE to:
077
      1. Integrate known DTI information with the underlying gene network structure, ensuring the model’s
078
         predictions are grounded in known biological interactions.
079
080
      2. Capture drug-gene interactions with N-hops GNN layers, providing a more comprehensive view
         of drug influence on the gene network.
081
082   3. Generate interpretable visualizations of gene-gene interactions under the drug treatment, offering
083      new perspectives on potential drug action mechanisms.

091   2.1   D RUG R ESPONSE P REDICTION
092
093
      Drug response prediction (DRP) refers to the process of forecasting how a particular drug will affect
      the vialbility of a biological system based on various data inputs such as genomic information and
094
      molecular structures (Adam et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2024). The goal is to predict the drug sensitivity,
095
      which can aid in personalized medicine, allowing for more targeted treatments for patients. The basic
096   DRP can be described as the following function y = f (G, D) where y is the drug response value
097   (e.g., IC50: half maximal inhibitory concentration, binary response: drug sensitive/resistance), G
098   is genomic features (e.g., gene expression, mutation), D is drug features (e.g., molecular structure,
099   chemical property information) and f is an ML model.
100
      Recently, Deep Learning (DL) has been applied to DRP, integrating multi-omics data. Several notable
101
      models have emerged: Li et al. (2019) developed DeepDSC which combines an autoencoder for gene
102   expression to obtain hidden embeddings, which are then used as input to a feed-forward network
103   along with drug fingerprint embeddings. Lao et al. (2024) implemented the DeepAEG, including
104   transformer for SMILES and attention for multi-omics data (e.g., mutation, gene expression). Zhao
105   et al. (2023) expands the Similarity Network Fusion (SNF) to the DRP, called Multi-source DRP
106   (MSDRP). They combined multiple data sources (e.g., gene expression, mutation, SMILES, DTI)
107   and used the SNF to fuse multiple information to make 2 interaction embeddings to obtain interaction
      information. A comprehensive review of this topic can be found in (Adam et al., 2020).

      2.2   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS IN C OMPUTATIONAL B IOLOGY
109
110   GNNs have emerged as a powerful tool for modeling complex biological systems. Their application
111   to side effect prediction has demonstrated effectiveness in capturing drug-drug interactions (Huang
112   et al., 2021). GNNs have also been used for molecular property prediction, showcasing the potential
113   of GNNs in cheminformatics (Fu et al., 2021b). For the DRP, GraphDRP (Nguyen et al., 2021)
114   integrates gene expression and protein-protein interaction networks, while MOFGCN (Peng et al.,
115   2021) combines multi-omics data (e.g., gene expression).
116
117   2.3   E XPLAINABLE AI IN B IOLOGICAL A PPLICATIONS
118
      As ML models become complex, there is a growing need for interpretability, especially in biomedical
119
      applications where understanding the rationale behind predictions is fundamental for clinical research.
120
121   Explainable AI methods can be categorized into three main types:
122   1. Gradient-based methods: These techniques utilize gradient information to highlight impor-
123      tant features. For example, Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2020) generates visual explanations
124      for decisions made by convolutional neural networks. Fu et al. (2021a) produces molecular
125      substructure-level gradient to provide interpretability for drug design.
126   2. Attention-based methods: These approaches leverage attention coefficients to identify relevant
127      parts of the input. Abnar & Zuidema (2020) propose methods like attention rollout and attention
128      flow to quantify the propagation of information through self-attention layers, improving the
129      interpretability of Transformer models.
130      For DRP, Inoue et al. (2024) employs Graph Attention Network (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2017)
131      on a heterogeneous network of proteins, cell lines, and drugs, offering interpretability through
132
         attention coefficients. Shi et al. (2024) utilizes directed graph convolutional networks (GCN) (Kipf
         & Welling, 2016) to identify key features of drugs and cells in predicting therapeutic outcomes.
133
      3. Shapley value-based methods: SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) (Lundberg & Lee, 2017;
134      Wang et al., 2024) assigns importance values to input features based on game theory principles,
135      providing a unified measure of feature contributions to model predictions.
136
137
      GraphPINE is most closely related to the attention-based methods, but with key distinctions. Unlike
      typical attention mechanisms that assign importance to edges, GraphPINE uses DTI information to
138
      initialize node importance scores. It propagates this importance throughout the learning process along
139
      with the graph structure. This approach incorporates prior biological knowledge, thereby enhancing
140   biological interpretability.
141
142
      2.4   I NFORMATION P ROPAGATION IN N EURAL N ETWORKS
143
144   Earlier bioinformatic methods such as HotNet and Perturbation Biology were inspired by heat
145   diffusion processes and message-passing algorithms (Leiserson et al., 2015), yet more recent work
146   has focused on applying propagation concepts to neural network-based ML models.
147
      Bach et al. (2015) introduced Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP), a technique for explaining
148
      model decisions. LRP decomposes the prediction by propagating relevance scores from the output
149   layer to the input layer, providing insights into each input feature’s contribution to the final prediction.
150
151   Shrikumar et al. (2017) proposed DeepLIFT (Deep Learning Important FeaTures). This method
152
      computes importance scores, capturing non-linear dependencies that might be missed by other
      approaches. DeepLIFT addresses limitations of traditional gradient-based methods by considering
153
      the difference from a reference input. This approach offers a more nuanced understanding of feature
154
      contributions and provides more interpretable explanations of model outputs.
155
156   More recently, Abnar & Zuidema (2020) introduced Attention Flow, a method designed for Trans-
157   former models. This approach models the propagation of attention through the layers of a Transformer,
158
      quantifying how information flows from input tokens to output tokens. Attention Flow provides a
      more accurate measure of token relationships compared to raw attention weights, offering insights
159
      into how Transformer models process and utilize information across their multiple attention layers.
160
161   These methods can all be viewed as specialized forms of information propagation. In each case, the
      “information” being propagated represents the relevance, importance, or attention associated with


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      different components of the network. These approaches demonstrate how the concept of information
163   propagation can be leveraged to enhance the interpretability of complex neural network models,
164   offering valuable insights into their decision-making processes across various network architectures.
165

      2.5   I MPORTANCE G ATING WITH GNN S
167
168   Recent studies have proposed different approaches for incorporating gating mechanisms into GNNs.
169   Two notable examples are Event Detection GCN (Lai et al., 2020) and CID-GCN (Zeng et al., 2021).
170
      Event detection is a natural language processing (NLP) task that aims to identify specific events
171
      (such as accidents, or business transactions) from documents. Event Detection GCN implements
172
      a gating mechanism utilizing trigger candidate information (e.g., potential event-indicating words:
173   ”attacked”, ”acquired”) to filter noise from hidden vectors. The model incorporates gate diversity
174   across layers and leverages syntactic importance scores from dependency trees, which represent
175   grammatical relationships between words in sentences.
176
      CID-GCN, designed for chemical-disease relation extraction, constructs a heterogeneous graph with
177
      mentions (representing specific entity occurrences), sentences (containing the textual context), and
178
      entities (normalizing multiple mentions) nodes. The model employs gating mechanisms to address the
179   over-smoothing problem in deep GCNs (Li et al., 2018) and enables effective information propagation
180   between distant nodes.
181
182
      GraphPINE advances these concepts through two key ideas. First, it introduces a novel approach
      to importance scoring by leveraging domain-specific prior knowledge for initialization, rather than
183
      relying solely on previous hidden states. Second, it implements a unified importance score updating
184
      mechanism through graph structure learning, departing from the context-based or two-step gating
185   approaches of its predecessors.

      3.2   DATA P REPROCESSING AND N ETWORK C ONSTRUCTION
198
199   We collected multi-omics data (gene expression, methylation, mutation, and copy number variation)
200   from NCI-60 cell lines, gene-gene interaction data from PathwayCommons, and DTI data from
201   various sources including Comparative Toxicogenomics Database (CTD) (Davis et al., 2023), Drug-
202   Bank (Wishart et al., 2018), the Drug Gene Interaction Database (DGIdb) (Freshour et al., 2021), the
203   Search Tool for Interactions of Chemicals (STITCH) (Szklarczyk et al., 2021) and Kinase Inhibitor
204   BioActivity (KIBA) dataset (Tang et al., 2014). For gene-gene network construction, we selected a
205   subset of genes based on three criteria: variance in multi-omics data, network centrality, and DTI
206
      frequency. The final set consisted of 5,181 genes, forming a network with 630,632 interactions. Edge
      information among genes was categorized into seven types and encoded as one-hot vectors. The
207
      detailed explanation is in Appendix A.1.
208
209   In multi-omics data preprocessing, gene expression data was normalized through converting the data
210   to the Transcripts Per Million (TPM), Log2 transformation, and winsorization. A 4-dimensional
211   feature vector was created for each gene in each cell line, incorporating all multi-omics data.
212   For the DTI data, we integrated data from multiple sources (e.g., DrugBank and CTD) and calculated
213   interaction scores based on available literature evidence. In this study, as a simplification, we will
214   refer to both direct physical drug-target interactions (i.e., a chemical binding to a protein) as well
215   as indirect interactions/associations (e.g., “drug results in increased acetylation of a protein” via
      intermediate events) as “DTI”.

325
326   a) Importance Score Propagation: As defined in Equation 5, this mechanism learns a mapping

363   4.1     DATASET
364
365   We processed the IC50 data from the NCI-60 dataset (Shoemaker, 2006) using the rcellminer (Luna
366   et al., 2016) package to create a binary classification. We applied an empirically determined threshold
367   of -4.595 to the log-transformed IC50 values, initially resulting in an equal distribution of labels.
368
      Subsequently, we selected only the drugs that were present in multi-omics datasets. This selection
      process led to an imbalanced dataset, with the resulting dataset consisting of 53,852 entries in total,
369
      comprising 36,171 positive and 17,681 negative instances.
370
371   We established a zero-shot prediction scenario by randomly allocating 70% of cell lines and 60% of
372   NSC identifiers to the training and validation sets (571 drugs and 42 cell lines), with the remaining
373   forming the test set (381 drugs and 18 cell lines). This ensures no overlap between train/validation
374
      and test sets, allowing us to evaluate the model’s ability to generalize to unseen drug-cell line pairs.
375   The data was split into 18,067 training, 4,516 validation, and 6,525 test entries. Class 0 represents
376   drug sensitive, while class 1 indicates drug resistance. The training set has 5,892 sensitive and 12,175
377   resistant cases. The validation set contains 1,487 sensitive and 3,029 resistant cases. The test set
      includes 2,154 sensitive and 4,371 resistant cases. Detailed procedures can be found in Appendix B.

520   It could assist in predicting drug responses in preclinical stages, potentially informing the selection

      A.2     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
779
780   The GraphPINE model was implemented using Python 3.10, PyTorch 2.4.0 and PyTorch Geometric
781   2.5.3, leveraging their efficient deep learning and graph processing capabilities. We employed the
782   Adam optimizer for training, with a learning rate of 0.001 and a batch size of 32. The model
783   architecture incorporates 3 Importance Propagation Layers (L = 3), each containing 64 hidden units.
784   To balance model performance and interpretability, we set the importance regularization coefficient λ
785   to 0.01 and the importance threshold τ to 0.1.
786   All experiments were conducted on NVIDIA Tesla A100 GPUs with 80 GB memory. The average
787   training time for GraphPINE was 0.2 seconds, with an inference time of 0.1 seconds per drug-
788   cell line pair, demonstrating its feasibility for large-scale DRP tasks. To ensure reproducibility
789   and facilitate further research, we have made our code and datasets publicly available at https:
790   //anonymous.4open.science/r/GraphPINE-40DE.
