009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual orientation detection helps navigation, especially without a reliable mag-
012            netic compass or GPS. Inspired by the neural mechanisms of the insect brain,
013            particularly the mushroom body (MB) and the central complex (CX), we propose
014            FlyOrien—a bio-inspired model for object orientation detection. The model mim-
015            ics the MB for random feature extraction, sparse coding and associative learning,
016
               while the CX provides multi-clue sensory integration, enabling interpolation for
               finer orientation representation. FlyOrien’s biologically plausible learning rule
017
               allows one-shot learning, reducing the need for large datasets and repeated train-
018
               ing. We tested FlyOrien on a dataset containing images labeled with orientations,
019            which introduce strong interferences because images of the same object have dif-
020            ferent labels. In this challenging context, FlyOrien achieves competitive perfor-
021            mance compared to convolutional neural networks (CNNs), significantly reducing
022            training time and computational resources. It also has the potential for real-world
023            applications like robotics, where incremental learning is essential.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      In natural environments, various cues like sun direction, skylight polarization, wind direction, and
028
      landmarks help animals navigate (Heinze, 2017). Most of these cues are perceived visually. Even
029
      simple insects can use visual memory to remember the way home after traversing a route once,
030   leveraging mechanisms partly explained by the mushroom body (MB) (Ardin et al., 2016); for a
031   review, see Modi et al. (2020). Their lightweight neural circuits outperform typical artificial neural
032   networks (ANNs) in remembering orientations. Inspired by this, we investigated these circuits to
033   develop an architecture and learning rule for retrieving orientation memory from visual signals.
034
      Assuming an observer always faces an object, with a reference direction which could be true north,
035
      there are three orientations: the angle the observer is facing o, the angle the object is facing o′ , and
036   their relative angle o − o′ . Knowing any two allows computation of the third. If o and o − o′ are
037   known, it is an object-orienting problem; if o′ and o − o′ are known, it is an observer-orienting
038   problem. For simplification, in the object-orienting problem, o is set to 0, and in the observer-
039   orienting problem, o′ is set to 0. Hence, in our dataset, there is only one number as a label for each
040   sample, and the two problems are not explicitly distinguished. By discretizing the range from 0◦
041   to 360◦ to multiple discrete values, the object orientation detection task can be set as a multi-class
042   classification problem.
043   There have been many models for finding objects’ orientation in the image plane but not horizontally
044   on the ground, such as PSC (Yu & Da, 2023), TIOE-Det (Ming et al., 2023), and ReDet (Han
045   et al., 2021). These works extend traditional object detection using rotated bounding boxes and are
046   applied in aerial imagery (Xia et al., 2018), scene text (Ma et al., 2018), and industrial inspection
047   (Wu et al., 2022). These methods typically involve deep networks requiring prolonged training time
048   and random shuffling of many data samples. For near-ground navigation, landmark objects have
049   fixed orientations relative to their surroundings, making the horizontal direction more critical than
050
      in-plane rotation.
051   Insects can remember landmark orientations after a single view without storing image data (Jeffery
052   et al., 2016). This ability is attributed to sparse coding in neural circuits like the MB (Pearce &
053   Bouton, 2001). The MB of Drosophila has been closely observed, 3D reconstructed, and its connec-
      tome analyzed (Li et al., 2020b), revealing how it processes sensory inputs via projection neurons

079   Previous models have explored the MB’s role in olfactory associative learning (Wessnitzer et al.,
080
      2007; Smith et al., 2008; Bennett et al., 2021). Computational neuroscience suggests the MB is
      crucial for insect navigation, such as visual homing (Webb & Wystrach, 2016). Visual inputs to
081
      the MB come from visual projection neurons (VPNs) and local visual interneurons (Ganguly et al.,
082
      2024; Li et al., 2020a). Models by Ardin et al. (2016) and Zhu et al. (2020) demonstrate how insects
083   use the MB for navigation by associating visual scenes with familiar directions.
084
085   The MB’s architecture has inspired computational models like FlyLSH (Dasgupta et al., 2017) for
086
      Locality Sensitive Hashing, which uses random projections and sparse coding similar to PNs and
      KCs (Caron et al., 2013; Baltruschat et al., 2021; Hayashi et al., 2022). The schematic plot of
087
      FlyLSH is presented in the dashed line zone of Figure 1b.
088
089   Another essential navigation circuit is the central complex (CX) (Honkanen et al., 2019), which
090   forms a ring attractor and encodes heading and homing directions (Wu et al., 2016; Zhang, 1996).
091   The CX integrates multiple directional cues to improve navigation accuracy (Heinze, 2017). Neuron
092
      activities predicted by computational models with ring attractors match biological observations.
      The connectome shows extensive connections between MB output neurons (MBONs) and the CX
093
      (Li et al., 2020a), suggesting coordination between familiarity encoding in the MB and continuous
094
      decision-making in the CX.
095
096   Inspired by the MB and CX, we propose FlyOrien, a model for incremental learning of the relative
097   direction between an observer and an object from side-view images. We also propose biologically
098
      plausible learning rules that enable one-shot and incremental learning, reducing training time and
      computational resource requirements. Unlike CNNs, FlyOrien (1) does not have convolutional lay-
099
      ers, (2) employs a wide coding layer with random, untrained weights for sparse coding, and (3) uses
100
      a learning rule minimizing interference during learning.
101
102   We demonstrate FlyOrien’s effectiveness on a modified object orientation dataset and a real-world
103   robotic orientation task. Experiments show that FlyOrien is more efficient than traditional artificial
104
      neural networks, as it only needs a single epoch training to achieve Top-5 accuracy comparable to
      CNNs that typically converge after 100 epochs.

      robot in a real-world environment (Section 3.2).

      2     M ODEL
109
110
      Our model, or FlyOrien, consists of two parts: a simplified MB model with firing-rate neurons and a
111
      modified associative learning rule, and a simplified CX modeled with a modified CANN. The former
112   can learn the orientation of multiple objects, more specifically, associating a view of an object with
113   an orientation angle. The latter merges multiple outputs of the former and provides a finer output.
114   We also proposed a biologically plausible learning rule so that the MB model can learn images by
115   only looking at them once.
116
      For convenience of application, we simplified the MB and CX for a minimal model functioning in
117
      learning object orientation. It ignores neuron’s morphology, uses firing-rate neuron models instead
118   of spiking neuron models, ignores dynamics inside neurons, and treats synapses between neurons as
119   a linear mapping. However, there are still neural dynamics by neuron interactions in the simplified
120   CX and synaptic plasticities by a biologically plausible learning rule from KCs to MBONs in the
121   simplified MB.
122
123   2.1     S IMPLIFIED MUSHROOM BODY MODEL
124
125   The simplified MB has three layers including projection neurons (Figure 1b). The first layer consists
126   of ”PNs” conveying preprocessed images. The second layer consists of ”KCs” encoding images.
127   The third layer consists of ”MBONs” outputting the likelihood of angles.
128
129   2.1.1    DATA PREPROCESSING
130
      Insect sensory inputs are preprocessed before sending to KCs by PNs. The preprocessing can in-
131
      volve dimension reduction, noise reduction, normalization, and gain control (Gopfert & Robert,
132   2002). The actual preprocessing of visual signals in insects can be complex. The neural circuits
133   in the optic lobe play an important role in processing vision in moving (Mauss et al., 2017), then
134   visual information is projected to the MB by posterior lateral protocerebrum PNs (PLP PNs) (Li et al.,
135   2020c). Despite this, previous models suggest that the architecture of the mushroom body (MB)
136   can process and learn from images without the need for complex feature extraction but directly on
137   pixel-level information(Ardin et al., 2016; Dasgupta et al., 2017).
138   As a simple approximation to the optic lobe, which adjusts contrast through lateral inhibition, the
139   first step of our model normalizes inputs. After normalization, the mean pixel intensity of each image
140   is set to 0. The image is then flattened to allow for the model’s use across different modalities. Given
141   a dataset (X, y), where X ∈ Rn×d , each row represents a sample x ∈ Rd , n is the number of sample
142   points, and d is the dimension of a sample point. A sample is shifted by the mean value x̄ of x before
143   being passed to the PNs:

      2.1.3     L EARNING RULE
175
176   The MB is an associative learning center in insects. Associative learning is a type of classic con-
177   ditioning that associates two stimuli or events. In the context of our model, the two stimuli are an
178   image sample and the object orientation on the image. From an aspect of view in machine learn-
179   ing, we can interpret associative learning as supervised learning. Insects can continuously associate
180   sensory stimuli with valences or behaviors, and the connections between KCs and MBONs play an
181
      important role in this process. In our model, learning occurs solely through adjusting the weights
      WKO between these two layers.
182
183   We applied two variations of Hebbian rule (Hebb, 1949) for updating WKO , which are referred to as
184   Method 1 and 2, respectively. Method 1 treats learning as a progress to converge and adjusts a weight
185   multiple times, while Method 2 treats the learning as an instant progress and a weight can only be
186   adjusted once. In both methods, all weights between ”KCs” and the ”MBONs” are initialized to 0.
187
      During training, when an image x and a label y is provided, x is sparsely coded by the “KCs” as b  z,
      and y is presented by corresponding “MBONs” in a one-hot manner.

365
366   The first half of FlyOrien outputs label likelihoods in a multi-class classification setup. However,
367   for real-world applications, we aim for more precise predictions. This experiment evaluates the
368   capability of the full FlyOrien model, combined with CANN, to predict orientations with a finer
369   resolution than that used in training. For ease of evaluation, we divided the data based on object
370   orientations, with 72 evenly distributed orientations, alternating between the training and testing
371   sets.
372   For a fair comparison, we also integrated the baseline models with CANN, resulting in two setups:
373   models with and without CANN. In the first setup, without CANN, orientations in the testing set
374   cannot be predicted directly, so the adjacent angle is used as the correct prediction criterion. In
375   the second setup, although the baseline models only predict orientations in the testing set, with
376   CANN, the orientations in the training set can be predicted, so the Top-5 criterion for multi-class
377   classification is applied. It is important to note that the evaluation criteria differ between these two
      setups, and comparisons are valid only within the same setup.

471   4     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
472
473   Inspired by the neural circuits of insects, particularly the MB and CX, we proposed FlyOrien, a
474   bio-inspired model for incremental learning of object orientation. The model mimics the MB’s
475   sparse coding and associative learning while utilizing the CX to integrate multiple sensory inputs
476
      to refine orientation detection. FlyOrien is designed to learn object orientations efficiently after a
      single exposure, and because it mimics the sparse coding of MB, it has the potential to generalize
477
      to multimodal inputs, such as posture, olfactory, and directional cues, which will be investigated in
478
      further research.
479
480   FlyOrien was tested on open-source datasets and real-world robotic tasks, demonstrating strong
481   performance in estimating object orientations and handling ego motion in complex scenes. Its ability
482
      to learn incrementally, without large datasets or extensive training, highlights its suitability for real-
      time applications.
483
484   Without relying on convolutional layers, FlyOrien learns object orientation efficiently without catas-
485   trophic forgetting, benefiting from its large number of pattern detectors and sparse coding. For in-
      stance, samples in COIL-100-AS with the same label are very similar, so subtle features, such as


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      specific patterns, are crucial for orientation detection, but CNNs are not optimized for this. CNNs
487   generalize by learning from fewer images and using shared weights to capture relationships between
488   local features. Convolutional kernels in the first layer detect low-level features, but this generaliza-
489   tion can overshadow rare or unique patterns, risking them being forgotten. In contrast, MB-like ar-
490   chitectures excel at identifying these subtle features and preventing forgetting by maintaining fixed
491   connections after learning. In our model, many “KCs,” each connected to only a few pixels, act as
492   specialized pattern detectors. Unlike CNNs, which apply the same filters across regions, FlyOrien
493   uses more filters simultaneously, detecting intricate details in a single pass. This key difference
494   enables FlyOrien to perform better and learn faster in our tasks.
495   While FlyOrien offers significant benefits, it is sensitive to pixel-level changes, affecting perfor-
496   mance when objects deform or lighting varies. Addressing these limitations is a key area for future
497   research, particularly by incorporating the optic lobe which is crucial for dynamic vision processing.
498   Extending the CX model to a two-dimensional CANN could also improve navigation in complex,
499   unmapped environments, enhancing FlyOrien’s robustness for more sophisticated spatial tasks.
500   FlyOrien’s lightweight design, free from GPU dependence, allows it to run effectively on small
501   devices like drones and robots, making it ideal for resource-constrained tasks like object tracking,
502   navigation, and surveillance, where low power consumption and computational efficiency are criti-
503   cal.
504
      In practical applications, FlyOrien presents minimal risks. Its use in autonomous robots can improve
505   navigation and object recognition without needing extensive computational resources. However, en-
506   suring transparency and human oversight in deployment is crucial. When used for navigation or
507   surveillance in public spaces, it’s important to respect privacy and operate within ethical guidelines.
508   FlyOrien’s efficiency on small robots makes it ideal for search and rescue, environmental monitor-
509   ing, and industrial automation. With safeguards in place, FlyOrien can positively contribute to these
510   fields without significant risks.
