037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Satellite Image Time Series (SITS) data offer valuable insights into Earth’s surface characteristics
040   and dynamics. It has widespread applications across domains like ecology, agriculture, forestry,
041   land management, disaster monitoring, risk assessment, etc. Deep learning has gained popularity
042   in the remote sensing community due to its ability to learn valuable features from input data with-
043   out feature engineering. In the realm of SITS classification, a combination of convolutional and
044   recurrent neural networks are employed to capture spatiotemporal characteristics from the data. An
045
      alternative to RNNs, transformers, originally proposed for natural language processing tasks, have
      shown promising performance in sequence encoding. A couple of studies Yuan & Lin (2020); Yuan
046
      et al. (2022) used BERT to classify time series for every pixel which limits them from effectively
047
      exploiting the spatial correlations in the image time series. Moreover, these models work only for
048   classification as they segment the image time series which is not suitable for prediction problems
049   like prediction of crop yield, snow cover, cloud cover, etc. The significance of these applications
050   is given in Appendix B.1. In prediction problems, the ground truth is mostly available for coarser
051   granularity than that of pixel e.g., at a county or a district level. Another model TSViT Tarasiou
052   et al. (2023) used ViT for landcover classification. The authors factorize input dimensions into spa-
053   tial and temporal components to reduce the computation. However, the model is not able to identify
      the redundancy in the patches and processes them all.

084
      We propose a transformer model, SaTran, for large size satellite image time series which exploits
085
      spatiotemporal redundancies. SITS data can be characterized by the presence of patches with spa-
086
      tiotemporal redundancy persisting throughout the time series, referred to hereafter as redundant
087   patch tubes. SITS data also contains patches where temporal redundancy lasts only for a few times-
088   tamps, referred to hereafter as non-redundant patch tubes. The pictorial representation of the classi-
089   fication of patch tubes is given in Figure 1. For example, a region of a barren land/water body has
090   spatiotemporal redundancy, and it won’t change even for years (thus is a redundant patch tube); 2)
091   the non-redundant patches (regions of interest) experience changes with time but can still have a tem-
092   poral redundancy for a shorter span, for example, cultivation land experiences changes in the crop
093   cycle duration. However, during harvest time when the crop is fully grown, there can be redundancy
094   for a few time stamps. Removing redundancies reduces the computational requirements thereby
095
      helping in the democratization of satellite image technology. SaTran disentangles spatiotemporal
      and temporal redundancies and makes the SITS processing efficient. Its key features are:
096
097
            1. Dual Redundancy-Handling Mechanism: SaTran introduces two novel modules to address
098
               specific challenges in SITS which were not there in the baseline transformer:
099
100               • PatchTubeSelect: Selects non-redundant patch tubes by leveraging an attention mech-
101                 anism, which reduces input size by focusing only on spatiotemporal hotspots.
102               • TemporalRedundancyHandler: Employs VideoMAE Tong et al. (2022) in distributed
103                 manner to effectively capture and process temporal patterns within these reduced
104
                    hotspots, minimizing temporal redundancies and improves efficiency.
105            Unlike standard Transformers, which operate on complete data and face scalability issues,
106            SaTran’s architecture reduces the computational burden by intelligently selecting and pro-
107            cessing a subset of the data in distributed manner i.e., all the patch tubes are processed in
               parallel without compromising task performance and improves efficiency.

            2. Task-Specific Adaptability: SaTran is designed with inherent flexibility to accommodate
109            the diverse resolutions, temporal frequencies, and spectral characteristics of satellite data
110            from systems like MODIS, Landsat-8, and Sentinel-2. The ability to tune hyperparameters
111            such as patch size, traversal ratio, and attention thresholds allows SaTran to adapt to a wide
112            variety of SITS tasks, such as land cover classification, change detection, etc.
113
            3. Computational Efficiency: By selecting only top k critical patch tubes, SaTran processes
114            significantly fewer tokens than standard Transformers, leading to reduced memory usage

117
            4. Scalability: SaTran’s modular design scales well with higher-resolution data or longer time

            5. We have done extensive experimentation and compared our model with existing video vi-

               models for various downstream earth observation applications like crop yield prediction,
123            snow cover prediction, land cover classification, etc.

192   In this section, we discuss the characteristics of satellite image time series and how it is different
193   from RGB videos, and then describe the architecture of SaTran to handle challenges posed by SITS.
194
195   3.1   C HARACTERISTICS OF SITS
196
197   Satellite image data are stored in a raster format, organized as a tensor with dimensions for height,
198   width, and channels. Temporal aspects can be integrated by arranging spatially-aligned rasters along
199   a fourth dimension. Although this structure resembles images\videos, satellite images are far dif-
200
      ferent from their equivalent in natural images for many reasons including:
201   1) Number of channels in satellite images and size of images from high spatial resolution satellites.
202
      2) Unlike RGB videos, SITS often exhibit spatiotemporal redundancy over time, especially in spe-
203
      cific landforms. For example, water bodies will have consistent patterns year after year. The spatial
204   arrangements of landforms in SITS data do not change drastically over time. In contrast, RGB
205   videos capture dynamic scenes where spatial configurations change frequently. The stable patterns
206   observed in SITS contrasts with the fluid nature of video imagery, especially in urban environments
207   or areas with constant human activity leading to huge spatiotemporal redundancy.
208
      3) The rate of temporal redundancy is different in different spatial landforms. The redundant patch
209
      tubes such as water bodies, urban area, etc. will have temporal redundancy for a longer time. How-
210   ever, non-redundant patch tubes like areas under snow experience different redundancy for a shorter
211   span. In regions where snowfall is a regular occurrence, such as high-altitude areas or northern lati-
212   tudes, temporal redundancy in satellite image time series can be less during initial phase which can
213   eventually be relatively high during winter months once snow settles and decreases as temperature
214   rises. This leads to dynamic changes in the landscape. The once uniform snow cover gives way to
215   patches of melting snow, revealing underlying terrain and vegetation. During this transition period,
      the temporal redundancy in satellite imagery decreases as the spatial patterns evolve rapidly.

      4) SITS do not contain moving objects and thus the orientation of regular RGB videos is of more
217   importance and SITS don’t have any natural orientation Rolf et al. (2024).

239   3.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
240
241   The architecture is given in Figure 2. PatchTubeSelect handles the spatiotemporal redundancy in
242   SITS by selecting and processing the patch tubes using an attention mechanism. Temporal Redun-
243   dancy Handler uses VideoMAE to handle the local temporal redundancy of the selected patch tubes.
244   Both modules are described below:
245
      PatchTubeSelect: A satellite image is gridded virtually into spatial patches. each patch consist
246
      of n × n pixels. A patch tube extends a patch in the temporal dimension. The patch tubes are
247   passed to the Temporal Redundancy Handler which gives the representations of Patch tubes which
248   are then forwarded to the Tube Selection Module (TSM) which is a sub-module in patch tube select.
249   TSM utilizes attention scores to identify the top ’k’ tubes, which are then passed to the Region
250   of Interest Selector (ROI-S) sub-module. ROI-S determines the unprocessed neighboring tubes of
251   the top ’k’ tubes and generates a list of tubes to be processed in the subsequent iteration. Most of
252   the neighboring patch tubes are excluded due to spatiotemporal redundancy and new patch tubes
253   are randomly selected which then become the new regions of interest. This process iterates until a
254   fraction (1/x) of the SITS is processed. The enhanced tube representations obtained from TSM are
255
      subsequently forwarded to the embedding generation module, which outputs the embedding of the
      entire SITS learned by the network across multiple iterations.
256
257   TemporalRedundancyHandler: It adapts VideoMAE for handling temporal redundancy. The
258   patch tubes are divided into tiny tubelets of size (T /t × H/h × W/w) where t, h, w depends
259   on the satellite used and details are given in section 6.1. These tiny tubelets are randomly masked
260   and the remaining tubelets are processed using vanilla Vision Transformer (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al.
261
      (2020). VideoMAE takes these tiny tubelets as inputs and uses the joint space-time cube embedding
      to obtain tubelet tokens. This reduces the volume of input data due to reduced spatial and temporal
262
      dimensions thus making the processing efficient. As the temporal redundancy in SITS persists for
263
      shorter lengths, the original high masking (95%) ratio in VideoMAE cannot be used for SITS. Also,
264   there is less risk of information leakage in SITS, due to which we recommend reducing the masking
265   ratio to 75% which is still significant and thus only a small number of tokens need to be processed by
266   the encoder which further makes the process efficient. The comparative study for different masking
267   ratios is given in the results section. To effectively capture high-level spatiotemporal details within
268   these remaining tokens, we utilize the ViT backbone and incorporate joint space-time attention. This
269   allows all pairs of tokens to interact with each other in the multi-head self-attention layer, enhancing
      the model’s ability to understand complex spatiotemporal relationships.

270
      Embedding Generator: It collects individual embeddings of all the processed patch tubes and uses
271   a feedforward network to give joint representation of entire SITS. It consists of two linear layers.
272
273   Decoder: We pre-train SaTran for two tasks viz. reconstruction and classification (refer section 4)
274
      using different decoders one for each task.
275   The decoder for reconstruction task consists of trans-convolutional layers to perform the upsampling
276   operations and construct the middle frame. Batch normalization is applied to ensure stability and
277   efficiency in training. The decoder is trained using the mean squared error loss function for the
278   model to generate the middle frame reconstructed images.
279   The decoder for the classification task consists of a simple feedforward network with two linear
280   layers and a sigmoid activation for the output. We use binary cross-entropy loss as the loss function.
281
      The decoders are used only in pre-training and are removed during fine-tuning.

284   4   P RE - TRAINING OF S AT RAN
285
286   We optimized SaTran for two objectives in pre-training, given as follows:
287
288
      1) Reconstruction task: We pre-train SatTran using middle frame reconstruction pretext task. The
      embeddings obtained from SatTran are passed to an additional decoder to construct the satellite im-
289
      age at the middle timestamp of the time series. The model learns to capture temporal dependencies
290
      and patterns inherent in the dynamic changes on Earth’s surface. This pre-training task ensures that
291   the model acquires a robust understanding of the temporal and spatial dynamics of SITS.
292
293   2) Classification task: The second pre-training task is a binary classification task to decide whether
294
      the given SITS is ordered or shuffled. SatTran learns whether the given input is temporally in the
      correct order or not. The model is given as input the original HSRSITS and the shuffled time series
295
      by randomly permuting the original time series. The loss function used is binary cross-entropy loss.
296
297   We separately pre-trained SatTran on two SITS datasets viz. MODIS (a moderate spatial resolu-
298   tion satellite) and Landsat-8 (high spatial resolution satellite) image time series, and named them
299   SatTran-M and SatTran-L, respectively. This is due to the properties of the satellite data. The char-
300
      acteristics including height, width, bands, and time stamps (i.e. number of frames) are different for
      different satellites due to the different spatial, temporal, and spectral resolutions. Thus, the model
301
      pre-trained for one satellite data cannot be used for the other satellite data. This also requires opti-
302
      mization for suitable size of patch tubes and tiny tubelets for two satellite data. The details are given
303   in the experimentation section 6.1.
304
305   We have also pre-trained VideoMAE for the reconstruction task in the same way as in the original
306
      paper for MODIS data for different masking ratios. The impact of different masking ratios by
      VideoMAE is given in the results section. VideoMAE did not work for Landsat-8 data because of
307
      the huge size of the images. So we pretrained VideoMAE-R, the resized version of VideoMAE
308
      given in section 6.1
309

      Landsat-8. The total number of instances used for pre-training is 1M for each satellite data and the

362   Preliminary experiments: Pre-training using MODIS data
363   We present preliminary experiments to pre-train SaTran and the existing model VideoMAE for the
364   reconstruction task using MODIS data. The results are captured for different masking ratios. Table
365   1 presents GPU memory and time required for both models using batch size 8 for training. It can
366   be observed from the table that SaTran takes approximately half of the GPU memory than that of
367   VideoMAE. Though VideoMAE needs less time to execute for one epoch in comparison to SaTran,
368   SaTran converges faster due to its attention mechanism of handling spatiotemporal redundancy and
369
      distributed approach of applying VideoMAE to patch tubes. Thus, total time taken by SaTran is
      much less than VideoMAE. Moreover, GPU memory requirements of VideoMAE increase expo-
370
      nentially by reducing the masking ratio. RMSE obtained by SaTran is lower than that of VideoMAE
371

374   masking of tiny tubelets this is unlike what is recommended by VideoMAE (95%) for RGB videos.
375   VideoMAE suggests high masking ratio to avoid data leaks. In our case data leaks are avoided by not
376   processing the redundant patches and 75% masking is sufficient to avoid data leaks in non-redundant
377   patches. We also conducted experiments for batch size 16 where VideoMAE gives out-of-memory
      error when masking ratio is reduced to 60%. The results are given in Appendix D.1.

473   7   C ONCLUSION
474
475   We presented a large SITS model, SaTran, for large size SITS trained in self-supervised learning
476   setup using two-fold data redundancy handling. SaTran introduces two novel designs - automatic
477
      patch tube selection and a distributed approach of applying tube masking on tiny tubelets. SaTran
      reduces the memory requirements by approximately a factor of 2. Experimental results show that
478
      due to short temporal redundancy, it is not recommended to have a very high masking ratio to
479
      achieve better results. Our experiments also demonstrate that the time taken by SaTran increases
480   sublinearly with an increase in image size e.g., we observed an increase of 18% in processing time
481   for 900GB of Landsat-8 data in comparison to 100GB of moderate resolution MODIS data. We
482   have fine-tuned SaTran for various downstream tasks and compared the performance with existing
483   video vision models and existing SITS models. The existing models are unable to process large-
484   size Landsat-8 image time series in its original spatial resolution. It is evident from the results that
485   SaTran efficiently processes the large-size SITS and outperforms all the competitive models for all
      downstream tasks.

625   A.2    DATA P REPROCESSING
626
627   Satellite data is captured as raw multispectral images and needs pre-processing before it can be used
628
      for pertaining or any downstream task. The data preparation steps are given below:
629         1. Handling cloudy pixels: We only consider the scenes having cloud cover of less than 15
630            percentile at every pixel. It is taken care of by using the simple composite algorithm of the
631            Google Earth engine goo.
632         2. Handling missing values: Satellite data often suffers missing data due to clouds on the day
633            of capture, technical failures, and other reasons. We estimate missing data at pixel level
634            using nearest neighbor interpolation and for timestamp level we use linear interpolation
635            paul (2019).
636         3. Bits Precision: Landsat-8 images have float values at every pixel. Float values require 32
637            bits to store a single pixel which leads to the requirement of large storage space for Landsat
638            images. Moreover working with earth observation applications, analysis of historical data
639            is important which increases the number of images to be processed. To save storage space
640            we used the bits precision compression technique Hubara et al. (2016) and converted all
641            the float values to unsigned integer values as done in Goyal et al. (2024).

717            inches. The counties lie in the states of New Hemisphere, Washington, Oregon, California,
718            Colorado, and Utah.
719
             • Soil Moisture Prediction (SMP): The soil moisture data is acquired at the county level
720            for every month using NASA-USDA Enhanced SMAP Global soil moisture data NASA-
721            USDA. This dataset was developed by the Hydrological Science Laboratory at NASA’s
722            Goddard Space Flight Center in cooperation with USDA Foreign Agricultural Services and
723            USDA Hydrology and Remote Sensing Lab. The Soil Moisture Active Passive (SMAP)
724            instrument measures the amount of water in the surface soil everywhere on Earth. The
725            value represents the amount of water in mm. The data is available from 2016 and thus data
726            used in this study is for 5 years from 2016 to 2020. We consider 275 counties from the
727            states- Iowa, Kansas, Illinois, Kentucky, and Indiana.
728          • Solar Energy Prediction (SEP): The information about solar energy produced in a county
729            has been acquired from visual crossing Visual Crossing Corporation (2019). The value
730            represents the total solar energy produced in MJ/m2 for a county in a day. The data used
731            in this work spans the period from 2014 to 2020. The counties considered lie in the states-
732            Iowa, Kansas, Illinois, Kentucky, and Indiana.
733
             • Cloud Cover Prediction (CCP): Cloud cover represents the proportion of the sky covered by
734
               clouds throughout the day. We acquired data captured on a daily basis from visual crossing
735
               Visual Crossing Corporation (2019). The data used in this study spans the period from
736            2014 to 2020. The counties considered lie in the states- Iowa, Kansas, Illinois, Kentucky,
737            and Indiana.
738
739          • Land Cover Classification (LCC): Land cover can be classified into a different number
740            of classes. We used MCD12Q1 modis which classifies land cover into 17 classes. The
741            dataset provides annotations for the land cover at a spatial resolution of 500m and yearly
742
               granularity. The data used in this study spans the period from 2014 to 2020. The counties
               considered belong to states- Iowa, Kansas, Illinois, Kentucky, and Indiana.

764   C     A PPENDIX : M ODELS FOR COMPARISON
765
766   We compared SaTran with two RGB video transformers and two SITS models. These models are
767   incapable of processing Landsat-8 image time series in its original spatial resolution. So we have
768
      used their resize and segmentation variants described in section 6.1. The details of the models used
      for comparison are as follows:
769
770         1. VideoMAE Tong et al. (2022): VideoMAE divides the RGB videos into tubelets and uses
771            a high masking ratio (95%) to handle temporal redundancy in the videos. However, Video-
772            MAE pre-trained on RGB videos is not suitable for SITS data. Thus, for a fair comparison,
773            we tried to pre-train VideoMAE for the Landsat-8 image time series. We are not able to
774            do so for Landsat-8 data in its original fine resolution due to huge size of images and the
775            GPU was out of memory even after reducing batch size to 2. Thus, we pretrain the resize
776            variant of the model mentioned in section 6.1 using 90% and 75% masking ratio and used
777            this variant for all the downstream tasks.
778         2. ViViT Arnab et al. (2021): ViViTArnab et al. (2021) is originally developed for RGB
779            videos. Image time series of dimension T × H × W is converted into multiple tubelets of
780            dimension z × h × w and tokens are extracted from all three dimensions. These tubelet em-
781            beddings efficiently capture spatiotemporal information by representing non-overlapping
782
               spatiotemporal patches. We adapted it for SITS data and used the factorized encoder ver-
               sion of the transformer.
783
784         3. SITSFormer Yuan et al. (2022): SITSFormer uses the neighboring pixels and then predict
785            the label of the center pixel using the BERT model. SITSFormer is originally designed for
786            classification tasks. We have adapted it for prediction tasks by replacing the classification
787
               head with two linear layers and an output layer for prediction.
788         4. TSViT Tarasiou et al. (2023): TSViT is also designed for classification tasks. It splits
789            a SITS instance into non-overlapping patches in space and time which are tokenized and
790            processed by a factorized temporal-spatial encoder. It uses class-specific cls tokens as
791
               inductive bias to improve the model prformance. We have adapted it for prediction tasks
               by replacing the classification head with prediction head consisting of two linear layers and
792
               an output layer for prediction.
