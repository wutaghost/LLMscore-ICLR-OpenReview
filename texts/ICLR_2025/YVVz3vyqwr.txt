                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) poses multifaceted challenges due to its neurodegen-
014            erative nature driven by complex genomic, radiomic, and structural interactions.
015            Understanding these complex relationships is pivotal for advancing diagnostic and
016
               therapeutic approaches. Current models struggle to effectively integrate multi-
               modal data for AD, limiting their predictive accuracy and biological interpretabil-
017
               ity. Thus, there is a pressing need for models that can seamlessly fuse genomic
018
               and radiomic data to provide a holistic understanding of AD pathology. We in-
019            troduce GENRAD, a novel heterogeneous graph neural network (GNN) that in-
020            tegrates multimodal genomic and radiomic data for graph-level classification in
021            AD by representing patients, genes, and brain structures as distinct nodes and im-
022            plementing advanced message-passing techniques. The benefits of GENRAD are
023            fourfold: (1) It enables multimodal fusion of genomic and radiomic data, uncov-
024            ering biologically meaningful insights missed by single-modality models. (2) Its
025            adaptive multi-scale graph representations model interactions at various biological
026            scales, capturing complex relationships essential for understanding AD pathology.
027
               (3) GENRAD incorporates explainable AI techniques, providing detailed analysis
               of key genomic markers and brain regions associated with AD. (4) GENRAD per-
028
               forms unsupervised clustering of genes, allowing the identification of functionally
029
               related biological pathways, thus empowering clinicians with actionable insights
030            for personalized treatment strategies. GENRAD demonstrates superior classifi-
031            cation accuracy in identifying AD-related patterns compared to existing machine
032            and deep learning models, achieving an accuracy of 91.70%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is a complex neurodegenerative disorder influenced by a combination of
039
      genetic, structural, and environmental factors. Early and accurate diagnosis is critical for effective
      intervention, yet the disease’s heterogeneity poses a substantial challenge. Globally, three out of four
040
      individuals with dementia remain undiagnosed and untreated. By 2025, the number of people living
041
      with dementia is projected to reach 139 million—a threefold increase from current levels Nandi et al.
042   (2022). This rise in cases presents not only a health crisis but also an economic burden, as dementia
043   currently costs the global economy over $1.3 trillion annually, a figure expected to increase nearly
044   nine-fold by 2025 Pedroza et al. (2022). Given this urgent situation, there is a pressing need to
045   develop early diagnostic methods to delay disease progression and alleviate the strain on healthcare
046   systems. With the public data, models, and code, GENRAD provides an excellent foundation to
047   help advance the analysis of challenging heterogeneous data.
048   Recent advances in deep learning, particularly Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), offer considerable
049   potential for modeling the convoluted relationships found in biomedical data. However, current
050   GNN models face significant limitations in integrating multimodal data, such as genomic, radiomic,
051   and clinical information. The inherent differences in data types—ranging from genetic sequences to
052   structural MRI scans—pose a significant barrier to their fusion in a unified model. Existing methods
053   often address these modalities separately, leading to suboptimal predictive performance and reduced
      interpretability Venugopalan et al. (2021); Shi et al. (2022). Moreover, many GNN models struggle


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to scale effectively with high-dimensional multimodal data, resulting in computational inefficiency
055   and limited scalability Zhang et al. (2023).
056
057   In this paper, we introduce GENRAD, a novel heterogeneous graph neural network designed to
058
      seamlessly integrate multimodal genomic and radiomic data for graph-level classification in AD.
      GENRAD addresses key shortcomings of existing models by representing patients, genes, and brain
059
      structures as distinct nodes, thus capturing the underlying biological relationships through sophisti-
060
      cated message-passing techniques. Unlike previous approaches that fuse multimodal data late in the
061   process, GENRAD uses a multi-scale graph representation to capture both local and global interac-
062   tions across these modalities, providing a comprehensive view of AD pathology.
063
064
      GENRAD’s unique approach offers significant contributions in four critical areas:
065
            1. Multimodal Fusion for AD: GENRAD integrates genomic data with structural radiomic
066            features from MRI, significantly boosting predictive accuracy and uncovering biologically
067            meaningful insights that single-modality models would miss.
068
            2. Adaptive Multi-scale Graph Representations: By modeling interactions between genes,
069
               brain structures, and patients at multiple biological scales, GENRAD captures complex
070
               relationships that are essential for understanding AD pathology and disease progression.
071
072         3. Enhanced Explainability and Interpretability: GENRAD incorporates explainable AI
073
               techniques, enabling detailed analysis of key genomic markers and brain regions associated
               with AD. This empowers clinicians with actionable insights for personalized treatment.
074
075         4. Unsupervised Clustering of Genes: GENRAD introduces supernodes to perform unsu-
076            pervised clustering of genes, allowing the identification of functionally related biological
077
               pathways and clusters, which improves our understanding of AD-related mechanisms.
078
      Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that GENRAD outperforms state-of-the-art models
079
      while providing interpretable predictions. Our contributions offer a pathway toward early diagnosis
080   and targeted interventions, addressing the growing global burden of AD.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   GNNs have gained prominence for their adeptness in handling graph-structured data, proving espe-
086   cially valuable in domains rich with relational data like medical imaging and genomics.
087   Graph Neural Networks in Medical Domain. In medical applications, GNNs excel in capturing
088   complex spatial and relational patterns. For instance, GNNs have been utilized in MRI-based anal-
089   yses to identify atrophy in specific brain regions, successfully predicting Alzheimer’s Disease (AD)
090   from structural MRI data by exploiting the nuanced spatial relationships within the brain Ktena
091   et al. (2017). Prominent GNN architectures adapted for medical applications include Graph Con-
092   volutional Networks (GCNs), Graph Attention Networks (GATs), and GraphSAGE. These models
093
      have been particularly influential in analyzing brain networks to diagnose neurodegenerative disor-
      ders such as AD, Parkinson’s Disease, and Autism Spectrum Disorder Parisot et al. (2018). Recent
094
      advancements have further demonstrated the utility of GNNs in multimodal neuroimaging and func-
095
      tional MRI data analysis. For example, BrainGNN effectively interprets fMRI data by identifying
096   critical brain regions for AD diagnosis Li et al. (2021), while interpretable temporal graph neural
097   networks leverage longitudinal imaging data for prognostic predictions of AD progression Kim et al.
098   (2021). Similarly, multimodal graph convolutional networks have been employed to combine struc-
099   tural and functional imaging data, offering robust and interpretable tools for clinical diagnosis Zhou
100   et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2022).
101
      Multimodal Data Integration for Disease Prediction. The prediction and diagnosis of complex
102   diseases like AD necessitate the integration of diverse data types, including genetic, radiomic, and
103   clinical data. Multimodal models, designed to capture the multifaceted nature of such diseases,
104   aim to discern patterns unattainable by single-modality approaches. Despite their potential, many
105   existing models struggle to fully leverage the benefits of data integration, often due to challenges
106   in harmonizing spatial information from radiological images with structured genomic data Reym-
107   baut et al. (2021). Recent multimodal GNN approaches, such as those integrating radiomics and
      genomics, have begun addressing these challenges by harmonizing diverse datasets to improve pre-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dictive accuracy and interpretability. For instance, multicenter and multichannel pooling GCNs suc-
109   cessfully merge dual-modality imaging data for early AD diagnosis, while adversarially regularized
110   graph learning models, such as DeepASD, integrate multimodal data for Autism Spectrum Disor-
111   der diagnosis Wang et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2024). Foundational work on anatomical parcellation
112   Tzourio-Mazoyer et al. (2002) and intrinsic functional connectivity mapping Schaefer et al. (2018)
113   supports these models in extracting meaningful spatial features. Benchmarks like NeuroGraph and
114   related data-driven network neuroscience initiatives have further standardized the application of
115   graph machine learning in brain connectomics, promoting reproducibility in multimodal research
116   Said et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023).
117   Explainability in GNNs. Explainability is paramount in healthcare applications of ML, including
118   the use of GNNs, where understanding model predictions is crucial due to the high stakes of medical
119   decision-making. Explainability techniques such as GNNExplainer isolate critical subgraphs by an-
120   alyzing node and edge contributions to the classification score and assign importance scores based
121   on their influence on the final prediction Ying et al. (2019). Recent developments have introduced
122   interpretable graph-based methods that elucidate the influence of multimodal data convergence on
123
      model predictions. Models such as GENRAD enhance these explainability mechanisms by offering
      detailed visualizations of how individual modalities contribute to outcomes. The addition of self-
124
      attention mechanisms in GNN architectures has further improved the interpretability of disease pre-
125
      dictions by focusing on key features in complex brain networks Kazi et al. (2019). Radiogenomics
126   plays a crucial role in these advancements, with applications ranging from cancer diagnostics to
127   neurodegenerative diseases Bodalal et al. (2019); Li & Zhou (2022). Integrative network analyses,
128   such as those identifying molecular signatures underlying regional vulnerabilities in AD, provide
129   valuable insights into selective disease mechanisms Wang et al. (2016).

      3.1     F EATURE E XTRACTION AND P RE - PROCESSING
163
164   Radiomic Features: To derive structural features from brain MRI scans, we first segment the brain
165   into 32 distinct structures using the SynthSeg model, a deep learning tool known for its robustness to
166   variations in MRI resolution and trained using elder and diseased scans Billot et al. (2023). SynthSeg
167   automatically generates segmentation masks, which are validated through a three-step process: (1)
168   automated quality control, (2) comparison of volumes against known ground truths, and (3) manual
169   verification by an expert radiologist. We then use PyRadiomics to extract 107 distinct features
170
      from each segmented brain structure, including shape, first-order statistics, and gray-level attributes,
      providing a detailed radiomic profile for each patient Van Griethuysen et al. (2017).
171
172   Genomic Features: We utilize processed genomic data, including expression levels for 75 key genes
173   highly associated with AD, sourced from Human Protein Atlas (2024). The genomic data is scaled
174   and mapped to the appropriate gene names, ensuring consistent representation across patients. The
175   integration of these distinct features enables GENRAD to model both genetic predisposition and
176
      structural brain changes, offering a holistic view of AD pathology.
177
178   3.2     H ETEROGENEOUS G RAPH S TRUCTURE
179
      GENRAD’s core innovation lies in its heterogeneous graph architecture G = (V, E), which si-
180
      multaneously models patients, genes, and brain structures as distinct node types. This multimodal
181
      graph allows for the capture of complex interactions between these entities, including patient-gene
182   and patient-structure associations, structure-to-structure relationships, and gene co-expression pat-
183   terns. Unlike existing methods, GENRAD adapts to the heterogeneity of these data types through a
184   multi-scale graph structure, which weights interactions at both the local and global levels.
185
186   3.2.1    N ODE R EPRESENTATION
187
188   Patients (Vp ): Each patient node pi ∈ Vp is represented by a feature vector xpi ∈ Rdp , where dp
189   is the dimension of patient-specific features. The features include the encoded APOE genotype, the
190
      gender, the Global Deterioration Scale (GDS), and Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) scores.
      The final feature matrix for patient nodes is Xp ∈ Rnp ×dp , where np is the number of patients,
191
      captures key clinical indicators that are known to correlate with AD progression.
192
193   Genes (Vg ): Each gene node gi ∈ Vg is represented by its corresponding expression values across
194   patients. The feature matrix for genes is denoted as Xg ∈ Rng ×1 , where ng is the number of genes.
195   For each patient, we compute a flattened vector of gene expression values for all associated genes.
196   This feature vector enables GENRAD to capture the molecular aspects of AD by linking patients to
197
      relevant genomic information.
198   Structures (Vs ): Each structure node si ∈ Vs represents a brain region segmented from MRI scans.
199   The radiomic features for each structure are extracted using PyRadiomics, resulting in a feature
200   vector xsi ∈ Rds , where ds represents the number of radiomic features (107 features across 32
201   structures). The final feature matrix for structures is Xs ∈ Rns ×ds , where ns is the number of brain
202   structures. This ensures that structural changes in the brain, which are critical in AD, are directly
203
      incorporated into the graph model.
204
205   3.2.2    E DGE C ONSTRUCTION
206
      The edges between nodes in the graph represent various biological and structural relationships, cap-
207   turing both local interactions (e.g., patient-gene, patient-structure associations) and global patterns
208   (e.g., gene co-expression and structure-to-structure).
209
210
      Patient-Gene Edges (Epg ): We model the association between patients and their gene expression
      data. Each patient pi is connected to all selected 75 gene nodes gj , forming edges (pi , gj ) ∈ Epg .
211
212
      The edge index for these edges is stored in the matrix Epg ∈ R2×|Epg | , where |Epg | is the total
      number of patient-gene connections. This allows the model to directly incorporate individual genetic
213
      variations into the AD prediction task.
214
215   Patient-Structure Edges (Eps ): Patient nodes are connected to structure nodes based on the brain
      regions associated with the patient’s MRI data. The relationship is represented by the edge index

216
      matrix Eps ∈ R2×|Eps | . This enables the model to integrate radiomic features and genetic data,
217   providing a multimodal representation of each patient’s disease profile.
218
219   Gene Co-expression Edges (Egg ): Gene-gene interactions are modeled through a co-expression
220
      network, where edges are established between pairs of genes (gi , gj ) ∈ Egg weighted by their co-
221   expression. The edge weight is stored in Egg ∈ R2×|Egg | . The co-expression score was obtained
222
      from GeneMANIA Warde-Farley et al. (2010). These weighted edges, based on the strength of
      gene co-expression, allow the model to account for gene interaction networks that contribute to AD

      pathology.
224
225   Structure-Structure Edges (Ess ): To capture relationships between different brain structures, these
226   connections form the structure-to-structure edges (si , sj ) ∈ Ess with the edge weight matrix Ess ∈
227   R2×|Ess | . We define edges between each structure and all other 31 structures weighted based on the
228   3D Euclidean distance between the center of mass of each segmented structure. These edges capture
229   spatial relationships between brain regions that are critical for understanding the progression of AD
230
      since there is no functional data available in the dataset.

254
255   A key innovation of GENRAD is its multi-scale integration of genomic and radiomic features. Un-

257   features into a heterogeneous graph from the start, allowing for direct interaction between gene,
258
      structure, and patient nodes. The message-passing mechanism ensures that the information from
      each modality is propagated through the graph, allowing for adaptive weighting of different data
259
      types based on their importance.

      3.4     M ESSAGE PASSING IN GENRAD
263
      Message passing in GENRAD involves propagating information between different node types (pa-
264   tients, genes, and brain structures) in a heterogeneous graph. The goal is to aggregate and update
265   node representations by combining the features of neighboring nodes through learnable aggregation
266   functions. This process is implemented using the SAGEConv layers Hamilton et al. (2017), which
267   are designed to perform efficient neighborhood aggregation by propagating information between
268   heterogeneous nodes. This enables the model to capture complex multimodal relationships in AD
269   pathology by incorporating genomic and radiomic data. The SAGEConv operation ensures that the
      model can efficiently aggregate and learn from high-dimensional multimodal data.

403   5.1   DATASET
404
405   Building a large-scale dataset targeting dementia sub-types is expensive and time-consuming. There
406   is only one public dataset to our knowledge that offers a rich foundation for dementia research, which
407
      is ANMerge Birkenbihl et al. (2021); Table 7 in the appendix outlines the dataset’s key statistics.
      Unlike the ADNI dataset, it encompasses longitudinal MRI scans and comprehensive genomic data.
408
      There are four classes: AD, Vascular Dementia (VaD), Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), and
409
      Control (CTL). Given its recent publication, there are limited papers available on ANMerge, and it
410   only contains one neuroimaging modality, which is structural MRI, and no functional information.

      5.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
413
414   The GENRAD model is trained using a stratified 3-fold cross-validation approach to ensure a bal-
415   anced representation of the classes in each fold. For training, the Adam optimizer with a learning
416   rate of 0.001 and a weight decay of 5∗10−4 was used. Early stopping is employed with a patience of
417   25 epochs to avoid overfitting. The multimodal dataset is built using PyTorch Geometric’s Hetero-
418   Data class, Fey & Lenssen (2019). The class imbalance was handled using class weights computed
419   for the cross-entropy loss function. The hidden channel size for the SAGEConv layers was set to 64.
420
421   5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
422
423
      The ablation study first evaluated the baseline performance of a 3D CNN model using raw MRI,
      establishing a benchmark for the effectiveness of image-based features in isolation. We then ex-
424
      tended our analysis by testing various ML and DL models (Random Forest, Support Vector Ma-
425
      chine, and Feature Generation by Convolutional Neural Network) to gauge their performance rela-
426   tive to GENRAD; the results are provided in the appendix. Our ablation study rigorously evaluated
427   GENRAD’s components, focusing on multimodal integration, graph edges, and message-passing.
428   We showed that combining genomic and radiomic data improves classification accuracy compared
429   to using them independently. Analysis of graph edges (patient-gene, patient-structure, gene co-
430   expression, structure-structure) revealed that removing any edge type reduced performance, under-
431   scoring the importance of capturing both local and global biological relationships. The SAGEConv
      message-passing layers were critical, as detailed in Appendix Table 9.

733   Supernodes were introduced to represent higher-order groupings of genes based on known biological
734   functions, allowing for the categorization of genes into meaningful clusters. These supernodes cap-
735   ture major Alzheimer’s disease-related processes, such as neuroinflammation, mitochondrial dys-
736   function, and APP and Tau pathology. By incorporating supernodes into the graph, the GENRAD
737   GNN model identified these clusters to group genes based on their co-expression and functional
738   roles within these pathways. This allows for more interpretable insights into the complex network
739   of gene interactions.
740   Significant Insights
741
742               • Neuroinflammation Cluster: Genes such as CASP2, SAA1, and C5AR1 demonstrate strong
743                 intra-cluster connectivity, highlighting the role of immune responses in Alzheimer’s pathol-
744                 ogy.
745               • APP and Tau Pathology Cluster: Genes like APP, MAPT, and APBA1 are central to the
746                 Alzheimer’s hallmark proteins, reflecting their pivotal role in disease progression.
747               • Vascular and Blood-Brain Barrier Integrity Cluster: With genes like ACE, LRP1, and NC-
748                 STN, this cluster underscores the critical role of vascular health and blood-brain barrier
749                 integrity in Alzheimer’s. The impairment of these pathways can exacerbate neurodegener-
750                 ative processes by allowing harmful substances to enter the brain, promoting inflammation
751                 and cellular damage.
752               • Synaptic and Neurotransmitter Function Cluster: Genes like SLC30A6 and GPR3 are es-
753                 sential for neurotransmitter signaling and synaptic integrity. This cluster indicates how dis-
754                 ruptions in synaptic function may contribute to cognitive decline in Alzheimer’s Disease.
755                 The presence of SNCA (alpha-synuclein), a gene also implicated in Parkinson’s disease,
                    highlights potential overlapping mechanisms in neurodegenerative disorders.

              • Proteostasis and Protein Aggregation Cluster: The cluster, anchored by genes such as
757             PSEN2 (Presenilin-2) and UBQLN1 (Ubiquilin-1), illustrates the importance of protein
758             homeostasis. Dysregulated proteostasis and protein aggregation are central to Alzheimer’s
