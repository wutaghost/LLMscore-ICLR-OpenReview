009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Decision Trees (DTs) are widely used in various domains for their simplicity and
012            interpretability. However, traditional DTs often suffer from low accuracy and
013            reduced robustness because they rely on fixed splits and a greedy approach to
014            decision-making. While recent approaches combining decision trees with opti-
015            mization seek to balance accuracy, computational efficiency, and interpretabil-
016
               ity, they still fall short in certain aspects. In this paper, we introduce a novel
               Probabilistic univariate Decision Tree (ProuDT), a non-greedy, axis-aligned tree
017
               that aims to address these challenges and achieve significant improvements. By
018
               assigning a single deterministic feature to each decision node, ProuDT ensures
019            univariate splits while preserving the differentiability of soft decision trees for
020            gradient-based optimization. This tree enhances interpretability through trans-
021            parent feature utilization in decision-making. Additionally, ProuDT simplifies the
022            optimization process and reduces computational cost by avoiding complex param-
023            eters. Extensive experiments on tabular datasets demonstrate ProuDT’s superior
024            performance and scalability, particularly in binary and multi-class classification
025            tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Decision trees are among the most widely used and easily understood methods in supervised learn-
030
      ing. The classic decision trees include CART (Breiman et al., 1984) and C4.5 (Quinlan, 2014),
031   which continues to be the most widely recognized trees and are the basis of various advancements.
032   The simplicity and interpretability of decision trees make them a favored choice for many appli-
033   cations. Current approaches to learning decision trees are categorised into greedy and non-greedy
034   methods. Through greedy optimization (Mingers, 1989; Raileanu & Stoffel, 2004), trees are grown
035   following one specific criterion (e.g., Gini impurity) at decision nodes. The process happens recur-
036   sively with further nodes being split without reconsidering previous splits during tree induction. The
037   greedy nature usually leads to sub-optimal solutions (Cormen et al., 2022). To address sub-optimal
038   problems, non-greedy methods have been extensively explored from various perspectives. Global
039
      tree search approaches, such as lookahead methods (Norton, 1989; Kiossou et al., 2024), evolution-
      ary algorithms (Barros et al., 2011), or mathematical programming techniques like mixed-integer
040
      programming (Günlük et al., 2021; Bertsimas & Dunn, 2017; Bertsimas et al., 2022), are all opti-
041
      mization techniques. However, a key limitation of these global search approaches is their lack of
042   scalability. Exploring the entire tree space dramatically increases computational cost, making such
043   methods feasible only for small trees and datasets.
044
045
      Due to the scalability limitations of global search methods and the aim for high classification accu-
      racy, alternative optimization techniques have been explored. Among these alternatives, gradient-
046
      based optimization has gained increasing popularity. Compared to greedy trees with hard split, these
047
      trees demonstrate better learning capability especially with probabilistic splits, i.e., soft splits, and
048   have good prediction performance (Frosst & Hinton, 2017; Wan et al., 2020). However, there are
049   interpretability, accuracy, and computational cost limitations among existing efforts. Probabilistic
050   trees often use multivariate splits, which reduces interpretability by making the roles of features
051   at decision nodes unclear. To address the reduced interpretability caused by multivariate splits,
052   researchers have attempted to maintain univariate splits in soft trees using gradient-based optimiza-
053   tions for hard splits (Marton et al., 2024). However, these efforts negatively impact classification
      accuracy and limit scalability. Moreover, the computational and memory costs of dense feature rep-

054
      resentations are high due to the many learnable parameters involved in gradient-based optimization.
055   For users seeking a simple and transparent model for classification tasks, the complexity can be
056   a significant drawback. This may explain why traditional decision trees remain popular, as their
057   simplicity is appealing despite their potential limitations.
058
      Existing approaches to optimizing decision trees for high accuracy often increase model complexity,
059
      which in turn affects interpretability. Specifically, most gradient-based methods struggle to ensure
060
      sparse features at decision nodes, making it difficult to maintain transparency in the decision-making
061   process. Three commonly explored methods aim to address this challenge. The first approach
062   involves growing an oblique tree to improve accuracy and then applying post-hoc feature removal
063   strategies during prediction. However, this creates a discrepancy between the features used during
064   training and testing, which ultimately degrades the model’s performance. The second approach
065   seeks to learn the position of individual features during tree growth. This is done using a dense
066   matrix to store all features across all decision nodes, aiming to identify the most suitable splitting
067   feature through parameter learning. While this method improves flexibility, it significantly increases
068   memory and computational costs, limiting scalability for higher dimensions and deeper trees. The
069
      third approach employs traditional greedy search algorithms, such as computing information gain.
      While this method easily identifies a deterministic feature for splitting at each decision node, its
070
      performance is often compromised by the inherent limitations of the greedy strategy.
071
072   To address these challenges in gradient-based tree construction, we propose the Probabilistic uni-
073   variate Decision Tree (ProuDT) to jointly improve: (1) accuracy, (2) convergence and inference
074   speed by reducing computational cost through fewer parameters, and (3) interpretability by offering
075
      insights into feature utilization and decision-making process. Specifically, our contributions are:
076         1. We introduce an effective and efficient probabilistic tree learning strategy with fewer learn-
077            able parameters involved during the tree induction. Rather than explicitly focusing on
078            learning the single feature for node splitting, our approach naturally achieves transparent
079            and effective single-feature splits. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first method to
080            directly utilize univariate splitting for probabilistic tree induction.
081         2. We propose a simple yet powerful axis-aligned decision tree that achieves high accuracy
082            and scalability, outperforming leading univariate trees. Moreover, by using the default set-
083            tings for a robust tree learning, users can bypass the complexity of hyperparameter tuning,
084            making the tree easy to deploy.
085         3. We offer insights into feature’s utilization that contribute to the decision-making process.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
088
089
      Greedy trees: Several well-established decision trees have been developed for decades, e.g.,
090   QUEST (Loh & Shih, 1997), CART (Breiman et al., 1984), C4.5 (Quinlan, 2014), CHAID (Kass,
091   1980). The most prominent among them is the CART and C4.5, which construct trees by recursively
092   splitting the data based on feature values that minimize impurity or maximize information gain. Due
093   to the greedy hard splitting nature, these trees suffer from sub-optimal problems that affect accuracy.
094
      Non-greedy trees: To handle the sub-optimal issue, non-greedy works have been proposed from
095
      different aspects. MurTree (Demirović et al., 2022) applies dynamic programming and search al-
096   gorithms to generate optimal decision trees to handle categorical features. Nunes et al. (2020) pro-
097   poses the Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm to facilitate lookahead tree navigation and
098   overcome the sub-optimal problem. Mixed-integer programming is also commonly explored (Bert-
099   simas et al., 2022; Zantedeschi et al., 2021). However, these methods introduce scalibility and
100   computation concerns. Tree alternating optimization (TAO) iteratively optimize node parameters
101   and enable the construction of sparse oblique decision trees (Carreira-Perpinán & Tavallali, 2018).
102   Through gradient-based optimization, DGT (Karthikeyan et al., 2021) achieves an oblique tree with
103   hard splits. In contrast, we aim to optimize a univariate tree, as it can be more interpretable than
104
      multivariate trees.
105   Probabilistic Trees: Probabilistic trees soften the path routing with probabilistic splits, where an
106   instance can be routed along multiple branches with certain probabilities, typically using functions
107   like sigmoid to model the smooth transition (Irsoy et al., 2012; Norouzi et al., 2015; Lee & Jaakkola,
      2019). The prediction is determined either by an aggregation of all leaves, for example through a


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      weighted sum (Hehn et al., 2020; Suarez & Lutsko, 1999; Irsoy et al., 2012), or by the leaf with the
109   highest path probability (Frosst & Hinton, 2017). With the growing interest in explainability in neu-
110   ral networks, the combination of neural networks and decision trees has become increasingly popular
111   (Frosst & Hinton, 2017; Yang et al., 2018; Wan et al., 2020; Rodrı́guez et al., 2024). However, these
112   probabilistic trees often incorporate a linear combination of input features when determining their
113   splits. These multivariate trees preclude users from understanding the feature utilization. Although
114   researchers try to make the tree as interpretable as possible (Xu et al., 2022; Silva et al., 2020), it is
115   still a challenge to clarify the individual feature’s contribution. (Silva et al., 2020) trained a proba-
116   bilistic tree with multivariate splits and then, during prediction, transformed the tree into a univariate
117
      tree by selecting the single feature with the highest weight from the multiple features at each node.
      However, this process led to an inconsistency between the feature splits used in training and testing,
118
      causing accuracy loss during prediction.
119
120   Univariate decision trees: The majority of gradient-based approaches focus on trees with mul-
121   tivariate or more complex splits. This stands in contrast to learning trees with univariate tests.
122   Therefore, constructing a univariate tree at the training stage is a potential solution to overcome the
123
      weakness of multivariate trees. DNDT (Yang et al., 2018) leverages soft binning to make splits and
      yields soft and axis-aligned tree. However, as the author mentioned, DNDT cannot scale well to
124
      datasets with large number of features, i.e., more than 12, due to the limitation of Kronecker prod-
125
      uct. Zantedeschi et al. (2021) utilizes the Argmin Differentiation to optimize tree parameters. This
126   method struggles with scalability to larger feature spaces. Recently, Marton et al. (2024) propose
127   GradTree, a method that enables hard splitting via gradient optimization. In backward propagation,
128   to overcome the non-differentiable process, by leveraging straight-through operator, they bypass the
129   non-differentiable function of hard split derived from forward propagation. However, mismatched
130   forward and backward propagations introduce gaps between training and testing stage, leading to po-
131   tential accuracy loss. In univariate tree induction, a key consideration is selecting the single feature
132   for splitting at each node. GradTree uses all input features at each node, following the probabilistic
133   tree’s method, but introduces the dense matrix to store all feature’s weights (indices) and thresh-
134
      olds across all nodes for single-feature selection. This makes the method computationally expensive
      and memory-intensive, particularly for high-dimensional datasets or deeper trees. The trade-off be-
135
      tween optimization flexibility and scalability limits the model’s applicability to large-scale tasks. In
136
      contrast, our proposed univariate tree induction strategy simplifies the optimization process while
137   maintaining efficiency and interpretability and can yield superior prediction result than the leading
138   univariate trees.

142
143   We introduce ProuDT in this section. Unlike classical decision trees, ProuDT employs a proba-
144   bilistic routing strategy to yield more robust and accurate result. Univariate splitting and feature
145
      selection is introduced in section 3.1. We describe the tree learning in section 3.2.

147   3.1   U NIVARIATE SPLITTING
148
149
      In the probablistic decision tree induction, the sigmoid function is applied to turn hard splits into
      weighted ones (soft splits). The output σ(f (x)(or (1 − σ(f (x)) represents the probability p of a
150
      sample x going left (or right). A commonly adopted strategy involves using a linear combination
151
      of input features for splitting during training, followed by seeking sparse features for prediction.
152   At each decision node, the probability is then computed using a sigmoid function, with weights
153   applied to all input features. The probabilistic trees mentioned above (section 2) treat both weights
154   and thresholds as learnable parameters per node. As show in Eq.1, for a given input feature vector
155   x, the bias bi and corresponding weight vector wi are learned at the splitting node i. GradTree
156   (Marton et al., 2024) improves this strategy by introducing a dense matrix to store each threshold for
157   each feature at each node, instead of using a general bias, with the goal of aligning specific features
158   with their thresholds. However, this method increases memory and computational costs, while also
159   introducing non-differentiable functions.

      3.2   T REE LEARNING
204
205   To simplify the process, ProuDT focuses on fully constructed binary trees for classification prob-
206   lems. During the initialization stage, the threshold value at each decision node is treated as a learn-
207   able parameter and is initialized at the beginning. Additionally, each leaf node contains a vector of
208   class distribution scores, which are also initialized as learnable parameters. Individual features are
209   allocated to each decision node as described in section 3.1.
210   During the training stage, in the forward splitting, each decision node computes a probability for the
211   left branch using the output of a sigmoid function, while the right branch receives the complement of
212   this probability. For the entire tree, each path’s probability is the product of the branch probabilities
213   along that path, denoted as Pl . The path probability is then multiplied by class distribution score λl
214   at the corresponding leaf l to obtain the final value. Finally, with the aggregation of all weighted leaf
215   nodes (Eq.3), the prediction is made. ϕ denotes the weighted sum of class scores across each leaf
      node l, and Qk is the predicted probability for class k after applying the softmax function (Eq.4).

241   The interpretability of our tree stems from the clear ranking and structured positioning of features

      4.1       E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
260   Preliminary Study: For any gradient-based tree, the tree depth should be specified in advance
261   (Frosst & Hinton, 2017; Blanquero et al., 2021; Verwer & Zhang, 2019), as the tree model requires
262   initialization in the beginning. Hence, the depth of a tree is a crucial hyperparameter. Fixing the tree
263   depth, rather than performing an automatic depth search, is more practical for tree training. Since
264   optimizing a fixed structure is already computationally expensive, iterating over multiple structures
265   is not prioritized (Costa & Pedreira, 2023).
266   To gain insights into optimal default depth setting for any given datasets in ProuDT, we conducted
267   a preliminary study on 12 UCI datasets of varying sample size, dimensionalities and class numbers.

      4.3     A BLATION STUDY
343
344   4.3.1    F EATURE POSITIONING
345
346   In our tree design, we assign individual features to each decision node in a cyclic order after pre-
347   ranking them. To evaluate the effectiveness of this feature positioning strategy, we compare the
348   performance of mutual-information ranked feature order against the original feature order from the
349   given datasets.
350   We conducted this experiment to evaluate test accuracy across different depths using the 12 UCI
351   datasets from the preliminary study, analyzing the impact of depth variation on performance. Figure
352   2 shows that after rearranging ranked features to the nodes, with the most important feature being at
353   the root node and the subsequently important features being positioned at deeper levels in sequence,
354   performance across some datasets improves significantly at the very shallow depth such as depth
355   2 when compared to using a non-ranked initial feature sequence. However, as the depth increases,
356
      the performance gap between ranked and non-ranked feature positioning strategies narrows, demon-
      strating that our tree learning in a cyclic order is robust in achieving high accuracy regardless of
357
      different feature sequence at the decision nodes.
358
359   From this result, we gain insights that assigning features to decision nodes after feature ranking
360   can contribute to tree’s convergence. It is plausible as the importance at different decision node is
361   different. The closer a decision node is to the top, the more decision paths it covers, and the greater
362
      its impact on the overall decision-making. Thus, we finalize our learning strategy by positioning
      pre-ranked features, obtained from any simple feature ranking technique, to contribute to the tree’s
363
      performance at shallow depths.

476   5   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      We propose ProuDT, a simple yet powerful learning strategy to construct superior axis-aligned de-
479
      cision trees. In this work, we demonstrate that directly assigning individual features to the deci-
480   sion nodes in a cyclic order enhances both accuracy and efficiency in tree induction and inference.
481   This method provides valuable insights into how individual feature utilization contributes to both
482   interpretability and improved model accuracy. Moreover, experimental results from 12 datasets,
483   complemented by a preliminary study on another set of 12 datasets, confirm the robustness and ef-
484   fectiveness of our default settings, further highlighting the ease of deployment and practical utility of
485   ProuDT. Our exploration paves the way for developing more powerful axis-aligned trees following
      KISS principle.
