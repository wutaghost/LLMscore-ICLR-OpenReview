009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) as a versatile representation paradigm
012            have achieved success in various computer vision tasks. Due to the spectral bias of
013            the vanilla multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), existing methods focus on designing
014            MLPs with sophisticated architectures or repurposing existing training techniques
015            for highly accurate INRs. In this paper, we delve into the linear dynamics model of
016            MLPs and theoretically identify the empirical Neural Tangent Kernel (eNTK) ma-
017            trix as a reliable link between spectral bias and training dynamics. Based on eNTK
018            matrix, we propose a practical inductive gradient adjustment method, which could
019
               purposefully improve the spectral bias via inductive generalization of eNTK-based
               gradient transformation matrix. We evaluate our method on different INRs tasks
020
               with various INR architectures and compare to existing training techniques. The
021
               superiority representation performance clearly validate the advantage of our pro-
022            posed method. Armed with our gradient adjustment method, better INRs with
023            more enhanced texture details and sharpened edges can be learned from the train-
024            ing data by tailored improvements on spectral bias.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The main idea of implicit neural representations (INRs) is using neural networks such as multi-layer
030   perceptrons (MLPs) to parameterize discrete signals in an implicit and continuous manner. Bene-
031   fiting from the continuity and implicit nature, INRs have gained great attention as a versatile signal
032   representation paradigm and achieved state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of computer
033   vision tasks such as signal representation (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2022; Saragadam et al.,
034   2023; Guo et al., 2023), 3D shape reconstruction (Cai et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024)
035   and novel view synthesis (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2022; Fathony et al., 2020).
036   However, obtaining high-precision INRs is non-trivial, MLPs with ReLU activation function often
037   fails to represent high-frequency details. Such tendency of MLPs to represent simple patterns of
038   target function is referred to spectral bias (Rahaman et al., 2019; Xu, 2018). To improve the per-
039   formance of INRs, great efforts have been made to alleviate or overcome this bias of MLPs. These
040   efforts have primarily focused on how MLPs are constructed such as complex input embeddings
041   (Tancik et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2022) and sophisticated activation functions
042   (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Saragadam et al., 2023). Recently, two studies find that training modifica-
043   tions based on existing training techniques, i.e., Fourier reparameterized training (FR) (Shi et al.,
044
      2024a) and batch normalization (BN) (Cai et al., 2024), can improve training dynamics, thereby
      overcoming the spectral bias without altering inference structures.
045
046   Despite these works serendipitously discover that such FR and BN training could alleviate the spec-
047   tral bias and lead to better representation performance, the mechanism behind their improvement
048   is still unclear. Moreover, there is no clear guidance on the choice of Fourier bases matrix for
049   reparameterization or batch normalization layer to confront spectral bias with varying degrees in a
050
      variety of INR tasks. Lack of reliable insight and guidance results in sub-optimal improvements
      in their broader applications. Therefore, how to adjust training dynamics of MLPs to purposefully
051
      overcome spectral bias remains an open and valuable research challenge.
052
053   In this paper, we delve into the linear dynamics model of MLPs and propose an effective training
      dynamics adjustment strategy guided by theoretical derivation to purposefully alleviate the spectral


                                                         1

054
      bias issue. Our strategy allows for tailored impacts on spectral bias based on the spectrum of tar-
055   get signals, thereby achieving more precise INRs. Specifically, these impacts on spectral bias are
056   tailored by purposefully adjusting the spectrum of the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) matrix (Jacot
057   et al., 2018), which closely connects the spectral bias with training dynamics. Given that NTK ma-
058   trix is not available in most cases, we theoretically identify the empirical NTK matrix as a tractable
059   surrogate. Empirical results corroborate this theoretical results. Based on eNTK matrix, we fur-
060   ther propose an inductive gradient adjustment (IGA) method which could purposefully improve the
061   spectral bias via inductive generalization of eNTK-based gradient transformation matrix with mil-
062   lions of data points. We give both theoretical and empirical analysis of our method, demonstrating
063
      how it tailors impacts on spectral bias. Besides, our IGA method can work with previous struc-
      tural improvement methods such as positional encoding (Tancik et al., 2020) and periodic activation
064
      function (Sitzmann et al., 2020). We validate our method in various vision applications of INRs
065
      and compare it to previous training dynamics adjustment methods. Experimental results shows the
066   superiority of training dynamics adjustment strategy. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
067
068          • We connect spectral bias with linear dynamics model of MLPs and derive an training dy-
069            namics adjustment strategy guided by a theoretical standpoint to purposefully improve the
070            spectral bias, i.e., tailored impacts on spectral bias based on the spectrum of target signals.
071          • We propose a practical inductive gradient adjustment method which could purposefully
072            mitigate spectral bias of MLPs even with millions of data points. Theoretical and empirical
073            analyses show how our method tailors impacts on spectral bias.
074
             • We provide detailed experimental analyses across a wide range of implicit neural repre-
075            sentation tasks. Comparing to previous training dynamics methods, our method allows for
076            tailored improvement on spectral bias of commonly MLPs and provides implicit neural
077            representations with more high-frequency details.

      2    RELATED WORK
081
082
      Implicit neural representations. Recently, implicit neural representations (INRs) representing the
      discrete signal as an implicit continuous function by MLPs have gained lots of attention. Comparing
083
      to traditional grid-based signal representation method, INRs have shown the remarkable represen-
084
      tation accuracy and memory-efficient property in a wide range of representation tasks such as 1D
085   audio representation (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022), 2D image representation (Klocek
086   et al., 2019; Strümpler et al., 2022) , 3D shape representation (Park et al., 2019; Martel et al., 2021),
087   novel view synthesis (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Saragadam et al., 2023) and virtual reality (Deng
088   et al., 2022). However, vanilla MLPs with ReLU activation function fails to represent complex sig-
089   nals. Therefore, various modifications have been studied. One category modifications focus on the
090   embedings of inputs. Mildenhall et al. (2021) find that using positional encoding (PE) as inputs en-
091   hances the representation of high-frequency details in neural radiance fields. Further, Takikawa et al.
092   (2021); Martel et al. (2021); Xie et al. (2023) adopt learned features to encode inputs, achieving bet-
093
      ter INRs. A different category of modifications concentrates on activation functions. Sitzmann et al.
      (2020) found that periodic functions, such as Sine function, can obtain more accurate representation
094
      than ReLU activation function. Sine functions are also explored by Fathony et al. (2020) in mul-
095
      tiplicative filter networks. Saragadam et al. (2023) adopt a complex Gabor wavelet activation and
096   achieve robust and accurate representations. Considering a broader framework, the aforementioned
097   work can be summarized as efforts to construct implicit neural representation models or MLPs with
098   more powerful representational capacity (Yüce et al., 2022). Recently, another category, distinct
099   from those previously discussed, focuses on the training process of MLPs. Shi et al. (2024a) find
100   that learning parameters in the Fourier domain, i.e., Fourier reparameterized training (FR), can im-
101   prove approximation accuracy of INRs without altering the inference structure. Cai et al. (2024)
102   repurpose that the classic batch normalization layer (BN) can also improve performance of INRs.
103   Spectral bias. Spectral bias typically refers to a learning bias of MLPs that MLPs tend to learn
104   simple patterns of training data or the low-frequency components of the target function. Great ef-
105   forts have been made to dissect this bias. Rahaman et al. (2019) find that spectral bias varies with
106   the width and depth of MLPs and the complexity of input manifold by experimental and theoretical
107   analyses. Generally, wider or deeper MLPs can learn high frequency information more efficiently,
      and increasing the complexity of input manifolds serves a similar purpose. Xu (2018) attribute this


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      bias of MLPs with Tanh activation function to the uneven distribution of gradients in the frequency
109   domain through Fourier decomposition. From the perspective of linear dynamics of MLPs, spectral
110   bias is induced by the uneven distributed eigenvalues of the corresponding Neural Tangent Kernel
111   (NTK) matrix (Jacot et al., 2018; Arora et al., 2019; Ronen et al., 2019). Inspired by this theoretical
112   result, Tancik et al. (2020); Shi et al. (2024a); Cai et al. (2024) try to elucidate spectral bias of differ-
113   ent models by observing eigenvalue distribution and find that PE, FR and BN help to attain a more
114   uniform eigenvalue distribution of the NTK matrix. Similarly, Geifman et al. (2023) theoretically
115   discuss impacts of modifying the NTK matrix spectrum in infinitely wide networks.
116   In this paper, we adopts the insight of attaining more uniform eigenvalue distribution of the NTK
117   matrix to overcome the spectral bias. For the first time, we show that modifying the eNTK matrix
118   spectrum can have the similar impacts on spectral bias and propose a practical inductive gradient
119   adjustment method for improving the spectral bias of INRs.

126   impractical. By further theoretical analysis, we identify empirical NTK matrix as a tractable proxy,

128   a practical gradient adjustment method, which could purposefully improve the spectral bias via
129   inductive generalization of eNTK-based gradient adjustments.

      7     C ONCLUSION
519
520
      In this paper, we propose an effective gradient
521   adjustment strategy to purposefully improve the spectral bias of multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) for
522   better implicit neural representations (INRs). We delve into the linear dynamics model of MLPs
523   and theoretically identify that the empirical Neural Tangent Kernel (eNTK) matrix connect spectral
524   bias with the linear dynamics of MLPs. Based on eNTK matrix, we propose our inductive gradient
525   adjustment method via inductive generalization of gradient adjustments from sampled data points.
526   Both theoretical and empirical analysis are conducted to validate impacts of our method on spectral
527   bias. Further, we validate our method on various real-world vision applications of INRs. Our method
528   can effectively tailor improvements on spectral bias and lead to better representation for common
529
      INRs network architectures. We hope our study could inspire future works to focus on controlling
      the bias of neural networks by adjusting training dynamics and yield better performance.

       of each iteration.
