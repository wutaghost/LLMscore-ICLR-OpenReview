011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional analyses of gradient descent optimization show that, when the largest
014            eigenvalue of the loss Hessian - often referred to as the sharpness - is below a
015
               critical learning-rate threshold, then training is ‘stable’ and training loss decreases
               monotonically. Recent studies, however, have suggested that the majority of mod-
016
               ern deep neural networks achieve good performance despite operating outside this
017
               stable regime. In this work, we demonstrate that such instabilities, induced by large
018            learning rates, move model parameters toward flatter regions of the loss landscape.
019            Our crucial insight lies in noting that, during these instabilities, the orientation of
020            the Hessian eigenvectors rotate. This, we conjecture, allows the model to explore
021            regions of the loss landscape that display more desirable geometrical properties
022            for generalization, such as flatness. These rotations are a consequence of network
023            depth, and we prove that for any network with depth > 1, unstable growth in
024            parameters cause rotations in the principal components of the Hessian, which
025            promote exploration of the parameter space away from unstable directions. Our
026
               empirical studies reveal an implicit regularization effect in gradient descent with
               large learning rates operating beyond the stability threshold. We find these lead to
027
               excellent generalization performance on modern benchmark datasets.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep neural networks are widely successful across a number of tasks, but their generalization
033   performance is dependent on careful choices of hyperparameters which govern the learning process.
034   Gradient descent (including stochastic gradient descent and ADAM (Kingma & Ba, 2017)) is arguably
035   the most widely-used learning algorithm due to its simplicity and versatility. For such methods, the
036   descent lemma upper-bounds the choice of learning rate by the local curvature (or sharpness) to
037   guarantee stable optimization trajectories and provable decreases for convex training losses.
038   Recently, the ‘unstable’ learning-rate regime has been a focal point for research. Cohen et al. (2022)
039   have demonstrated that in practice, learning rates can go above the stability threshold as determined
040   by the descent lemma. Surprisingly, this appears not to destabilize training trajectories as expected,
041   but instead model sharpness continues near the stability limit while training loss improves. Large
042   learning rates have also been known to improve generalization performance. Our work builds on these
043   findings, showing that training instabilities, caused by large learning rates, drive model parameters
044
      toward flatter regions of the loss landscape to improve generalization.
045   The main contribution of our work, detailed in Section 3, proposes that these instabilities are resolved
046   through rotations in the eigenvectors of the loss Hessian. We demonstrate that, for deep neural
047   networks, unstable training causes these eigenvectors to rotate away from their previous orientations,
048   whereas in the stable regime, the orientations are reinforced. These rotations allow the model to
049   explore regions of parameter space with an in-built bias for flatness. We term the accumulation of
050
      this implicit regularization effect progressive flattening, which we validate empirically.
051   Throughout our experiments, learning rates allow direct control over the magnitude of these regu-
052   larization effects. Our empirical study in Section 4 explores the relationship between learning rates
053   and generalization, revealing a clear phase transition where generalization benefits only emerge for
      learning rates beyond the stability threshold, solidifying the role of instabilities in regularization.

      Additionally, we show that starting with large learning rates have long-term benefits in generalization
055   performance, even when learning rates are reduced later in training. However, our findings also
056   challenge the reliability of sharpness as a metric for generalization, as the degree of eigenvector
057   rotation can, in some cases, be a more effective predictor. The code for reproducing the experiments

      2.1   G RADIENT D ESCENT AND THE D ESCENT L EMMA
063
064   We denote the loss function by L(θ) with parameters θ that are updated with gradient descent using
065   learning rate η, i.e. θt+1 = θt − η∇L(θt ). The so-called sharpness S of the loss landscape is
066   typically estimated with the maximum curvature of the loss Hessian, i.e. S(θ) = λmax (H(θ)) =
067   λmax (∇2 L(θ)). The descent lemma can be stated as:
068                                                                                                 2
      Lemma. For a convex, l-smooth function L(θ), L(θt+1 ) ≤ L(θt ) − η(1 − ηl
                                                                             2 )∥∇L(θt )∥2
069
070   The proof uses the co-coercive property of l-smooth functions using the results of Baillon & Haddad
071   (1977). The decrease in loss is scaled by the quadratic η(1 − ηl
                                                                    2 ), which leads to the optimal learning
072   rate η = 1/l. However, any choice of 0 < η < 2/l guarantees a decrease in the loss function,
073   allowing convergence to the minima in the stable regime of training, leading to a popular rule for η
074   selection. On the other hand, choosing η > 2/l results in so-called instabilities, causing L and θ to
075   grow without bound. Additionally, when η > 1/l, parameters oscillate about the minima.
076   Although these bounds are derived from l, in practice η is chosen without knowing l, leading to
077   an empirical stability threshold of S(θ) given by S(θ) ≤ 2/η, beyond which training is thought to
078   destabilize. However, Cohen et al. (2022) showed that in practice, training is not destabilized as
079   expected. They identified two important phenomena for gradient descent:
080
      Progressive Sharpening: So long as training is stable (S(θ) ≤ 2/η), gradient descent has an
081
      overwhelming tendency to continually increase sharpness.
082
083   Edge of Stability: Once sharpness reaches the stability limit, it sits at, or just above, the stability
084   threshold. Additionally, although the descent lemma does not guarantee a decrease of training loss,
085
      the training loss nonetheless continues to decrease, albeit non-monotonically.
086   In practice, loss functions extend beyond the quadratic and instabilities manifest as spikes in θ, L(θ),
087   and S(θ). Damian et al. (2023) introduced the progressive sharpening factor α = −∇L(θ) · ∇S(θ).,
088   where a positive α indicates that gradient updates promote sharpening. The authors hypothesized that
089   during instabilities, α > 0, meaning progressive sharpening is active and prolongs the instabilities.
090   However, our initial empirical results (see Appendix D) suggest that this effect, in its currently
091
      formulation, plays a limited role during instability, and we posit that the primary driver of instability
      is unstable growth in parameters, driven by S(θ) > 2/η true to the local quadratic approximation.

      2.2   S HARPNESS AND G ENERALIZATION
095
      The notion that solutions with low sharpness, or flat minima, promotes generalization performance is
096   widely accepted. Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) argued, using minimum description length, that
097   flatter solutions are less complex, thereby improving generalization through appeal to Occam’s Razor.
098   More recently, Keskar et al. (2017) and Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018) observed that deep neural networks
099   trained with small learning rates tend to generalize poorly because the minima they converged to
100   were narrow. The width of minima is measured through sharpness, S(θ), often defined as the largest
101   eigenvalue of the loss Hessian. However, Dinh et al. (2017) demonstrated that some output-preserving
102   transformations can lead to arbitrary values of S(θ), revealing a lack of scale-invariance. While this
103   observation weakens the absolute causality from sharpness to generalization, Jiang et al. (2019) found
104
      that sharpness may still serve as a useful indicator of generalization performance.
105   Modern methods for efficiently computing eigenvalue-vector pairs of the Hessian utilize Pearlmutter
106   (1994)’s trick, which allows evaluation of the Hessian vector product without explicitly forming the
107   Hessian. In our work, we present an implementation in Jax (Bradbury et al., 2018) that also employs
      matrix-power-kernel (MPK) re-orthogonalization (Yamazaki et al., 2024) to enhance numerical


                                                         2

108
      stability. Additionally, our study focuses on the similarity between eigenvectors, which can naturally
109   be compared with cosine-similarity in one-dimension. For comparisons between subspaces formed
110   by multiple eigenvectors, we use the cosine-Grassmanian distance (Ye & Lim, 2016). The code to
111   reproduce the results in this paper will be made available on GitHub upon publication.

      3     R EGULARIZATION THROUGH I NSTABILITIES
115
      We study the dynamics of gradient descent during instabilities - a phase of learning driven by unstable
116
      growth in parameters and characterized by spikes in θ, L(θ) and S(θ). In this section, we introduce a
117   toy problem to demonstrate that unstable parameter growth cause rotations in the eigenvectors of
118   the loss Hessian. Adopting a reductionist approach, we attempt to explain the complex behavior of
119   the overall loss function by analyzing individual terms. To model the dynamics of one such term,
120   we utilize a Diagonal Linear Network (DLN), a simple neural network with non-trivial dynamics, as
121   studied in Pesme et al. (2021). The DLN’s multiplicative structure reflects a key feature of depth,
122   which weights are multiplied across vertically-stacked neural layers whenever depth d > 1. We
123   focus on a detailed study of a two-parameter DLN, and the derivation for a general n-parameter
124   model is provided in Appendix B. We then identify phases of η that match up exceedingly well with
125
      established stability bounds, using these insights to conjecture how gradient descent instabilities
      can be resolved through eigenvector rotations. Finally, we show that the cumulative effects of these
126
      dynamics over extended periods of training can result in progressive flattening of the loss landscape.

      3.2   R ESOLUTION OF I NSTABILITIES
232
233
      The DLN model illustrates the gradient descent trajectory for an individual term in the loss function,
234
      modelled as a product of individual weights. Crucially, we identified different directions of rotation
235   among the sharpest Hessian eigenvectors, which we build upon to characterize the empirical behavior
236   of deep neural networks during instabilities. We train a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) on fMNIST
237   (Xiao et al., 2017). The rotation of the sharpest Hessian eigenvectors is tracked in Figure 2, and the
238   landscape along the gradient of L(θ) and S(θ) is shown in Figure 3.
239
      Figure 2 shows the degree of rotation among the sharpest Hessian eigenvectors at three snapshots of
240
      instability, where we compared eigenvector similarities to baselines defined at the beginning of each
241   snapshot. Focusing on the top panel, we observe gradual and monotonic rotations as L(θ) approaches
242   its peak values as predicted by the DLN model. Notably, these rotations do not involve sudden
243   changes in any single eigenvector but reflect a general decrease in similarity across all eigenvectors,
244   conforming to behaviors of the DLN. Interestingly, even after the instability is resolved, the similarity
245   among individual eigenvectors fall while the subspace comparison remain largely similar. As the
246   subspace of top eigenvectors is heavily constrained by the problem, the observation of re-orientations
247   suggest that new combinations of these eigenvectors can be beneficial toward flatness. In the bottom
248   pane, we intervene to enforce stability by setting ηlow = 0.2ηs before the instability is resolved. As
249
      these models have undergone periods of instability, the eigenvectors are rotated away from their
      original orientations, but these rotations are immediately reversed once η reduction takes place.
250
      This reflects an important feature of eigenvectors during instabilities - that stability and instability
251
      encourages monotonic rotations in opposite directions.
252
253   Although the rotation of eigenvectors follows theoretical expectations, the curvature during the
254   resolution of instabilities displays distinct characteristics. In Figure E, we visualize the landscape of
255
      L(θ) and S(θ) along the gradient through a complete instability cycle from epoch 119 to 158. As the
      parameters grow, it moves beyond the region approximated by low-order Taylor Expansions at the
256
      minima and the influence of higher-order terms are revealed.
257
258   At epoch 119, when S(θ) < 2/η, we observe a steady increase in S(θ) while L(θ) decreases. This
259   is progressive sharpening. During this phase, the curvature of both L(θ) and S(θ) sharpens. While
260   sharpening in L(θ) is well-documented (as an increase in S(θ)), the sharpening of S(θ) indicates
261
      the increased influence of higher-order terms in the local Taylor-expansion, and that a higher-order
      derivative is increased (while parameters approach the minima). After epoch 131, when S(θ) > 2/η,
262
      the curvature of both L(θ) and S(θ) remains largely constant, but parameter growth forces the
263
      model to explore wider ridges of the local minima along the unstable directions. We expect this to
264   eventually lead to model divergence, but significant changes to the S(θ) curve are observed after
265   epoch 149, and fortunately a flatter curve is found by epoch 154. At this point, the reduction in
266   S(θ), as a result of a flatter S(θ) curve, outweighs the increased steps as a result of large parameters,
267   resulting in optimization steps toward stability in the following epochs while the orientations of the
268   top eigenvectors are reinforced through stability. We observe these effects as a ‘cooling’ of L(θ) and
269   S(θ), before progressive sharpening eventually drives the model back toward instability to repeat the
      cycle. The full epoch-by-epoch progression is shown in the Appendix E.

286   Theoretical and empirical evidence suggests that rotations play a crucial role in resolving instabilities.
287   In the DLN model, reductions in S(θ) were caused by adjustments in Θ toward the global minimum,
288   which gradually relaxed the stability limit. In contrast, empirical observations showed that S(θ)

293   the model to explore different orientations of eigenvectors, resulting in varying curvatures of S(θ). In
294
      this process, flat (with respect to S(θ)) eigenvectors are reinforced while sharp ones are discarded.

295
      implicitly biases the parameters toward flatter regions of the loss landscape.

298   3.3   P ROGRESSIVE FLATTENING
299
300   While a reduction in S(θ) would be sufficient to achieve stability, our empirical study revealed that
301
      instabilities may also reduce higher order derivatives (the curvature of curvatures), leading to a
      phenomenon we term progressive flattening. This effect manifests through a reduction in progressive
302
      sharpening, whose effects on the S(θ) curve are indirect (see Appendix D). The degree of progressive
303
      sharpening can be tracked over extended periods of stable training and measuring the eventual
304   maximum sharpness S(θ)max that is reached.
305
306   In Figure 4, we track S(θ)max (for MLPs on fMNIST) as η reductions, set to ensure stability, are
307
      applied at different stages. S(θ)max decreases as η reductions are delayed, and larger initial η0 used,
      suggesting that prolonged training with larger learning rates intensifies the regularization effects.
308
309   Our findings highlight a strong link between training with large learning rates and the resulting flatness
310   of local parameter space. In Section 4, we use the connection between S(θ) and generalization to
311   show that large learning rates contribute to improved generalization through progressive flattening.

      4     E FFECT ON G ENERALIZATION P ERFORMANCE
325
326   Section 3 highlighted progressive flattening, the implicit regularization from instabilities which seeks
327   flatter minima, that can be increased with larger learning rates and with longer duration. In this
328   section, our empirical study validates the benefit of large learning rates toward generalization.
329
      Generalization refers to the ability for neural networks to perform well on data not used in training.
330
      The generalization gap is defined as the difference between performance on training data (in-sample)
331
      and on unseen test data (out-of-sample). To standardize measurements, models in this section are
332   trained to completion, defined as achieving > 99% accuracy on the training set. Consequently, test
333   accuracy serves as a direct indicator of the generalization gap.
334
335
      Some of our experiments in this section are conducted on the CIFAR10 image classification dataset
      (Krizhevsky, 2009) using small VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) networks. While the instability
336
      phenomena occur for many choices of error functions (Cohen et al., 2022), we found training to be
337
      more stable with cross-entropy loss compared to mean squared error loss, which motivates the use of
338   cross-entropy loss in our experiments. These experiments are performed in a fully non-stochastic
339   setting, with full-batch gradient descent and eschewing common data augmentation techniques such
340   as random flips and crops (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). Additionally, since batch normalization (Ioffe &
341   Szegedy, 2015) benefits deep convolutional architectures, we use the non-stochastic GhostBatchNorm
342   (Hoffer et al., 2018) computed over fixed batch size 1024, maintaining the default ordering of data in
343   CIFAR10. To ensure the robustness of our study, we trained hundreds of models, but due to budget
344   constraints our experiments in this section are conducted on a reduced 5k (10%) subset of the full
345   dataset. In Section 4.4, we remove these constraints and benchmark our observations on the full 50k
346
      dataset, and introduce non-stochastic augmentations to achieve performance near the state-of-the-art.
      For further details of our network architecture and experimental setup, see Appendix C.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
500
501
      Elements of progressive flattening are observed in the literature. Keskar et al. (2017) and Jastrz˛ebski
      et al. (2018) found that larger learning rates led to more reductions in sharpness which provided
502
      benefits toward generalization, while our builds additionally limits these effects to learning rates that
503
      induce instabilities. Moreover, we found that varying the duration of instability can result in different
504   degrees of flattening, aligning with Andriushchenko et al. (2023b)’s observation of loss stabilization,
505   where phases of large initial learning rates are shown to promote generalization.
506
507
      Mechanisms toward the resolution of instabilities have also been studied. Lewkowycz et al. (2020)
      identified a regime of learning rates, above the stability limit, that can catapult models into flatter
508
      regions. We identify the potential role of rotations in the catapult effect. Damian et al. (2023) showed
509
      that, considering one unstable eigenvalue, gradient descent has a tendency to self stabilize due to
510   the cubic term in the local Taylor expansion. While acknowledging the importance of higher-order
511   terms, the effects we identify are present without requiring sharpening factor α > 0, which was also
512   rarely observed in empirical studies (see Appendix D). Arora et al. (2022) demonstrated that gradient
513   descent can lead to alignment between gradient the sharpest eigenvector of the Hessian v1 . Our
514   model predicts, and we observe, this alignment during stable phases of training when eigenvectors
515   are reinforced, but during instabilities, the orientations of v1 are disrupted.
516   Our empirical work contributes to the growing body of research toward assessing sharpness as a
517   metric for generalization. While the lack of scale-invariance is a weakness for sharpness Dinh et al.
518   (2017), remedies have been proposed (e.g. (Kwon et al., 2021)). Kaur et al. (2023) suggest that the
519   pathological nature of these transforms may not arise with standard optimizers. However, recent
520   results by Andriushchenko et al. (2023a) on modern benchmarks further challenges the sharpness-
521   generalization link, suggesting that the right measure of sharpness may depend on features of the
522   dataset. Our study indicates that once learning rates are controlled and differentiated through weight
523   initialization, sharpness can fail to discriminate between models. Given the crucial role of Hessian
524
      eigenvectors rotations in resolving instabilities, we encourage further exploration of their role in the
      dynamics of gradient descent.
525
526   Lastly, Geiping et al. (2022) demonstrated that state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR10 can be
527   achieved using full-batch gradient descent. Our results, also in a non-stochastic setting, replicate their
528   performance (without explicit regularization), using only learning rates for regularization.
529
530
      6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      Our work highlights a significant implicit bias in gradient descent that favors flatter minima, an
533
      effect frequently conjectured in the deep learning literature. We identify the rotations of Hessian
534   eigenvectors as the primary mechanism driving such exploration, which can lead to progressive
535   flattening of the loss landscape. Using learning rates above the stability threshold, deep neural
536   networks can realize substantial benefits in generalization, reinforcing the already prevalent use of
537   large learning rates among practitioners. Our experiments are conducted in a fully non-stochastic
538   setting, and we encourage further work to determine whether these effects can extend to stochastic
539   gradient descent. We hope our work inspires future efforts toward addressing the role of rotations of
      the Hessian eigenvectors for a better understanding of optimization with gradient descent.

878   D        D RIVERS OF I NSTABILITY
879
880
      We present an exploration of the training dynamics with gradient descent to pinpoint the sources
881   of instability. The Edge of Stability is reached when S(θ) rises to 2/η early in training through
882   progressive sharpening. At this stage, the model exhibits unstable behavior characterized by sudden
883   spikes in training loss and S(θ), dubbed instabilities. To clearly illustrate these dynamics, we train
884   a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) on the fMNIST dataset (Xiao et al., 2017), a simple model on a
885   straightforward task.
886
      Classical theories of stability posits that once past the stability limit, oscillations in parameters become
887   unstable, leading to an increase in magnitude to eventually leads to numerical errors. However, recent
888   findings suggest that deep neural networks can operate at the Edge of Stability for an extended, if not
889   the entire, duration of training. Figure 10 presents a snapshot of a typical training trajectory. Damian
890   et al. (2023) formalised the notion of progressive sharpening by defining a sharpening parameter,
891   α = −∇L(θ) · ∇S(θ).3 This is plotted alongside |θ − θ∗ |, the latter estimating the distance to mean
892   oscillation parameters. In examining the trajectory, we delineate distinct phases of learning through
893   the ascent and descent of |θ − θ∗ |. As gradient descent nears the stability boundary of 2/η, parameter
894   oscillations become more pronounced, driving the system toward instability Notably, the peaks in
895
      L(θ) and S(θ) coincide with peaks in oscillation magnitude. On the other hand, this contrasts with
      the progressive sharpening factor, α, which remains predominantly negative, indicating that minor
896
      gradient adjustments often lead to a reduction in S(θ), contrary to the expected qualitative effects
897
      suggested by progressive sharpening and of instability. This evidence suggests that unstable parameter
898   oscillations, rather than progressive sharpening, are critical drivers of instability.
899
900   A more compelling argument arises when we intervene by suppressing parameter updates along
901
      unstable directions, setting learning rates to ηu = 0 specifically for these directions. The modified
      dynamics, shown in Figure 11, reveal that S(θ) follows a trajectory similar to that when observed
902
      under a globally small learning rate, reflecting a training trajectory completely conducted in the stable
903
      regime. This behavior is characterized by slow and gradual increases to S(θ), which we identify as
904   progressive sharpening, since the latter also exist during stable phases of training. Similarly, reducing
905   the learning rate along unstable directions to achieve effective stability, i.e. ηu < 2/S(θ), reproduces
906   this behavior up until the stability threshold is reached, reinforcing the limited role of progressive
907   sharpening toward the formation of instabilities. Conversely, when gradient updates are restricted
908   solely to the directions of the sharpest eigenvectors, unstable oscillations are re-introduced, though
909   the trajectory of S(θ) significantly changes.
910   These findings underscore the fundamental differences between progressive sharpening and unstable
911   oscillations. Progressive sharpening primarily describes the increases in S(θ), the curvature along
912   the sharpest eigenvector, as a consequence of updates in other directions of the Hessian. In contrast,
913   unstable oscillations are driven specifically by parameter updates along these unstable directions.
914   Our results demonstrate that unstable parameter oscillations play a critical role in the formation of
915   instabilities, challenging the view that progressive sharpening plays the primary role. This distinction
916   is important to why the generalization performance of deep neural networks is improved when trained
