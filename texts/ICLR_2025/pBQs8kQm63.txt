                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013
               Real world evolves in continuous time but computations are done from finite
014
               samples. Therefore, we study algorithms using finite observations in continuous-
015            time linear dynamical systems. We first study the system identification problem,
016            and propose a first non-asymptotic error analysis with finite observations. Our
017            algorithm identifies system parameters without needing integrated observations
018            over certain time intervals, making it more practical for real-world applications.
019            Further we propose a lower bound result that shows our estimator is provably
020            optimal up to constant factors. Moreover, we apply the above algorithm to online
021            control regret analysis for continuous-time linear system. Our system identification
022            method allows us to explore more efficiently,√enabling the swift detection of
023            ineffective policies. We achieve a regret of O( T ) over a single T -time horizon
024
               in a controllable system, requiring only O(T ) observations of the system.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Finding optimal control policies requires accurately modelling the system (Kirk, 2004). However, real-
029
      world environments often involve unknown system parameters. In such cases, estimating unknown
030   parameters from exploration becomes essential to identify the unseen dynamics. This process is
031   recognized as system identification, a fundamental tool employed in various research fields, including
032   time-series analysis (Korenberg, 1989), control theory (Kumar, 1983), robotics (Johansson et al.,
033   2000), and reinforcement learning (Ross & Bagnell, 2012).
034
      The identification of linear systems has long been studied because linear systems, as one of the most
035
      fundamental systems in both theoretical frameworks and practical applications, has wide applications
036   ranging from natural physical processes to robotics. Most classical results provide only asymptotic
037   convergence guarantees for parameter estimation (Åström & Eykhoff, 1971; Ljung, 1998b; Campi &
038   Kumar, 1998b).
039
      On the other hand, with the rapid increase in data scale, there is a growing concern for statistical
040
      efficiency. Consequently, the non-asymptotic convergence of discrete-time linear system identifi-
041
      cation has emerged as another pivotal topic in this field. Investigations into this matter delve into
042   understanding how estimation confidence is influenced by the sample complexity of trajectories (Dean
043   et al., 2018), or the running time on a single trajectory (Simchowitz et al., 2018; Sarkar & Rakhlin,
044   2019). Furthermore, many of these studies operate under the common assumption of stochastic noise,
045   there has been a parallel exploration into the identification of discrete-time linear dynamical systems
046   with diverse setups. This includes scenarios where perturbations are adversarial (Hazan et al., 2020)
047   or when only black-box access is available (Chen & Hazan, 2021).
048   In contrast to studies in discrete time system, there have been relatively fewer non-asymptotic results
049   addressing parameter identification for continuous-time systems. Two problems exist for continuous
050   time analysis. First, nonasymptotic analysis in continuous system without noise can be degenerate,
051   as a short time interval can contain infinite pieces of information. Second, if we consider the non-
052   degenerate case when finite noisy observations are available, then the analyses require concentration
053   results that become known only as in (Simchowitz et al., 2018; Dean et al., 2018; Sarkar & Rakhlin,
      2019). Recently Basei et al. (2022) provides novel analyses for estimating system parameters, which

      relies on continuous data collection and interaction with the environment. Motivated by progress
055   in these works, our first goal is to answer the question below: Can we design a continuous-time
056   stochastic system identification algorithm that provides nonasymptotic error bounds with only a finite
057   number of samples?
058
      We will introduce our system identification algorithms tailored to meet the above requirements. As
059
      expected, we discretize time into small intervals, thereby reducing the problem to a discrete system.
060
      The interesting part involves ensuring that the discretization remains bijective and that the inversion
061   is unbiased. Our algorithm identifies the continuous system using only a finite number of samples
062   from the discrete system. We further propose a information theoretic lower bound that shows our
063   algorithm is optimal.
064

065
      control problem as introduced in (Shirani Faradonbeh & Shirani Faradonbeh, 2023). In this context,
066

067   optimal control policy that narrows the performance gap. The primary challenge involves finding the
068   right balance between exploration and exploitation. Leveraging our identification method for more

088   2   R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   Control of both discrete and continuous linear dynamical systems have been extensively studied
091   in various settings, such as linear quadratic optimal control (Mehrmann, 1991), H2 stochastic
092   control (Dragan et al., 2004), H∞ robust control (Stengel, 1994; Khalil et al., 1996) and system
093   identification (Kumar, 1983; Ljung, 1998b). Below we introduce some of the important results on
094   both system identification and optimal control for linear dynamical systems.
095
096   System Identification Earlier literature focused primarily on the asymptotic convergence of system
097   identification (Campi & Kumar, 1998a; Ljung, 1998a). Recently, there has been a resurgence of
098   interest in non-asymptotic system identification for discrete-time
                                                                p        systems. Dean et al. (2018) studied
099   the sample complexity of multiple trajectories, with O( 1/N ) estimation error on N independent
100   trajectories. For systems with dynamicspxt+1 = Axt + wt (without controllers), Simchowitz et al.
101   (2018) established an analysis for O( 1/T ) estimation error on a single stable trajectory with
102   running time T , while Faradonbeh et al. (2018) and Sarkar & Rakhlin (2019) extended to more
103   general discrete-time systems.
104   Non-asymptotic analyses for continuous-time linear system are less studied. Recently, Basei et al.
105   (2022) examined continuous-time linear quadratic control systems with standard brown noise and
106   unknown system dynamics. Our algorithm is specifically designed for finite observations, achieving
107   an error rate that cannot be attained through the direct discretization of integrals as done in (Basei
      et al., 2022).

109   achieving the optimal control policy in this setup can be straightforward (Stengel, 1994; Yong &

124   3.1   L INEAR DYNAMICAL S YSTEMS
125
126   We first introduce discrete-time linear dynamical systems as follows: Let xk ∈ Rd represent the
127   state of the system at time k, and let uk ∈ Rp denote the action at time k. Then, for some linear
128   time-invariant dynamics characterized by A ∈ Rd×d and B ∈ Rd×p , the transition of the system to
129
      the next state can be represented as:
130                                       xk+1 = Axk + Buk + wk ,                                       (1)
131
132
      where wk ∈ Rd are i.i.d. Gaussian random vectors with zero means and certain covariance.
133   Similarly, a continuous-time linear dynamical system with stochastic disturbance at time t is defined
134   by a differential equation, instead of a recurrence relation:
135
                                        dXt = AXt dt + BUt dt + dWt .                                   (2)
136
137   In this context, we use Xt and Ut to represent the state and action in the continuous-time linear
138   system, distinguishing them from xt and ut in discrete-time systems. Wt denotes the stochastic noise,
139   which is modeled by standard Brownian motion.
140   For a continuous control problem, an important question of a linear dynamical system is whether such
141   system can be stably controlled. Below we define the concepts of stable dynamics and stabilizers.
142   Definition 1. For any square matrix A, define α(A) = maxi {ℜ(λi )|λi ∈ λ(A)}, where ℜ(λ)
143   represents the real part of complex number λ, λ(A) is the set of all eigenvalues of A.
144
      Definition 2. A matrix A ∈ Rd×d is stable if α(A) < 0. A control matrix K ∈ Rp×d is said to be a
145
      stabilizer for system (A, B) if A + BK is stable.
146
147   Under the above definition, a stable dynamic guarantees that the state can automatically go to the
148   origin when no external forces are added, while applying a stabilizer as the dynamic for controller
149   will also ensure that the state does not diverge.

      4.1   I DENTIFYING C ONTINUOUS - TIME S YSTEMS WITH F INITE O BSERVATIONS
217
218   We highlight the finite-sample requirement in our analyses of system identification for continuous
219   systems. Under this setting, not only the running time is finite, but the number of observed states
220   is also limited. In other words, for a trajectory with lasting time T , we can only get access to a
221   finite set of states {Xt1 , Xt2 , ..., Xtk } instead of a continuous uncountable collection
                                                                                               R of trajectory
222   {Xt }, where t is in some interval. One key difference is that quantities such as I ϕ(Xt , Ut )dt
223   with any function ϕ cannot be evaluated without error over an interval I. In addressing the system
224
      identification problem, we underscore a key distinction between our methodology and the traditional
      techniques employed for integral approximation.
225
226   In previous approaches, computations have relied on the approximation Xt+ϵ ≈ (I + ϵA)Xt +
227   ϵBUt + (Wt+ϵ − Wt ). However, this approximation does not accurately reflect the true dynamics as
228   expressed in equation 7, leading to a systematic error between the approximated and actual dynamics.
229   This discrepancy, characterized by the error term ϵ−1 (eϵA − I) − A, is of the order of ϵ. As a result, to
230
      achieve the desired error bound, a super-linear sampling complexity relative to the total running time
      T is required, which significantly increases computational demands. Our method addresses this issue
231
      by utilizing the bijection between domains of S = {X ∥X∥ ≤ 14 } and their matrix exponentials,
232
      thereby overcoming the limitations of direct discretization.

478
479   Our result is closely related to the result in Shirani Faradonbeh

481   and worst cases. We highlight some comparisons below. Both our and their setups assume a previously
482   known stabilizer, which is also common for online control (Abeille & Lazaric, 2018; Ouyang et al.,
483   2019; Faradonbeh et al., 2020; Shirani Faradonbeh & Shirani Faradonbeh, 2023). However, the work
484   of Shirani Faradonbeh & Shirani Faradonbeh (2023) further assumes a known stabilization set for
485   obtaining a stable controller, which is slightly stronger compared with ours. Such difference exists
      because our approach detects divergence and avoids sticking to a controller which is not stable.

      More importantly, our system identification method is different. In Shirani Faradonbeh & Shi-

489   cannot be too small, to ensure that the system can be well identified. This causes an extra log(T )
490   factor to the regret. In contrast, our algorithm follows an explore-then-commit structure, which is
491   enabled by the efficient system identification results presented previously.

527   6     C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
528
529   In this work, we establish a novel system identification method for continuous-time linear dynamical
530   systems. This method only uses a finite number of observations instead of requiring the integration of
531   a consequent√trajectory, and can be applied to an algorithm for online LQR continuous control which
532   achieves O( T ) regret on a single trajectory. Compared with existed works, our work not only eases
533   the requirement for data collection and computation, but achieves fast convergence rate in identifying
534   the unknown dynamics as well.
535   Although our method achieves near-optimal results in system identification and LQR online control
536   for continuous systems with stochastic noise, many questions remain unsolved. First, it is unclear
537   whether our system identification approach can be extended to more challenging setups, such as
538   deterministic or adversarial noise. Additionally, many practical models are non-linear, raising the
539   question of under what conditions discretization methods are effective. We believe these questions
      are crucial for real-world applications.
