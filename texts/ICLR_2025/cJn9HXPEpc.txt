009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The occurrence of bubbles in pipeline parallelism is an inherent limitation that can
012            account for more than 40% of the large language model (LLM) training time and is
013            one of the main reasons for the underutilization of GPU resources in LLM training.
014            Harvesting these bubbles for GPU side tasks can increase resource utilization
015            and reduce training costs but comes with challenges. First, because bubbles are
016            discontinuous with various shapes, programming side tasks becomes difficult while
017            requiring excessive engineering effort. Second, a side task can compete with
018
               pipeline training for GPU resources and incur significant overhead. To address
               these challenges, we propose FreeRide, a system designed to harvest bubbles in
019
               pipeline parallelism for side tasks. FreeRide provides programmers with interfaces
020
               to implement side tasks easily, manages bubbles and side tasks during pipeline
021            training, and controls access to GPU resources by side tasks to reduce overhead.
022            We demonstrate that FreeRide achieves about 8% average cost savings with a
023            negligible overhead of about 1% for typical long training times of LLMs while
024            serving model training, graph analytics, and image processing side tasks.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) are usually trained on GPUs. As these models continue to increase in
030   size, their GPU memory requirements can easily outstrip the capacity of a single GPU (Zhang et al.,
031   2022). Consequently, to accommodate this increase in size and to boost training performance, it is a
032
      common practice to parallelize LLM training across multiple GPUs distributed over several servers.
033   Pipeline parallelism is a prevalent training paradigm for LLMs using multiple GPUs. In this paradigm,
034   the model is divided into multiple stages which are distributed across different GPUs. During training,
035   the forward propagation (FP) and backward propagation (BP) of different input data are carried out
036   in parallel by the pipeline training system at each stage. The pipeline training system schedules these
037   operations in each epoch to train LLMs (Liu et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024).
038   An inherent limitation of pipeline parallelism is bubbles — periods in pipeline training where the
039   GPU stays idle due to unsatisfied dependencies between FP and BP operations (Liu et al., 2023).
040   Experimentally, we observe that bubbles can constitute 42.4% of the pipeline execution time, which
041   results in significant under-utilization of GPU resources used to accelerate pipeline training. Similar
042   levels of under-utilization have also been reported in other studies (Zhang et al., 2022).
043   GPUs are crucial resources, especially those high-end models required for training LLMs (Zhang
044   et al., 2022). To enhance utilization, prior work has explored reducing bubbles by improving how FP
045   and BP operations are interleaved (Fan et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). These approaches are effective
046   for intra-epoch bubbles because they change how operations are interleaved within a pipeline epoch.
047   However, they do not remove the inter-epoch bubbles that occur before and after a pipeline epoch.
048   Prior work has also proposed to decouple the computation of gradients for the input and model weights
049   to mitigate inter-epoch bubbles (Qi et al., 2024). However, they increase the size of activations,
050
      exacerbating GPU memory consumption, a common bottleneck in training LLMs.
051   Given the difficulty and overhead incurred in eliminating these bubbles, an alternative approach is
052   to acknowledge their existence and utilize them by running additional workloads on a GPU. For
053   example, Bamboo and PipeFisher implement procedures that enhance pipeline training and run them
      during bubbles (Thorpe et al., 2023; Osawa et al., 2023). However, they only target specialized

      procedures that are tightly coupled with pipeline training, requiring the training system and the
055   procedures to be highly customized. Consequently, they cannot be used for generic GPU workloads.
056
057   In this paper, we present FreeRide, a holistic system to harvest bubbles in pipeline parallelism to serve
058
      extra GPU workloads as side tasks. There are two main challenges that FreeRide has to overcome.
      First, customizing side tasks for bubbles of various shapes, i.e., their duration and available GPU
059
      memory, requires enormous programming effort. Second, LLM training requires high-end GPUs that
060
      are expensive and in high demand. If side tasks interfere with the main pipeline training workload,
061   e.g., accessing more GPU resources than bubbles can provide, they will slow down pipeline training
062   and significantly increase training costs.
063
064
      Our approach to overcoming the programming complexity is based on the observation that many
      GPU workloads naturally consist of small, repetitive steps, such as the epochs in model training
065
      that repeatedly load data and update model weights. To reduce the programming effort, FreeRide
066
      introduces a framework that abstracts away the implementation details of side tasks, allowing pro-
067   grammers to adapt various side tasks to fit into the bubbles. The key idea is to represent the life cycle
068   of a side task, from its process creation to termination, as states in a state machine. FreeRide provides
069   two sets of unified interfaces — the iterative interface that features lower performance overhead, and
070   the imperative interface that features better versatility. They facilitate the implementation of side
071   tasks as state transitions with little engineering effort. FreeRide manages side tasks through these
072   interfaces and serves them during bubbles.
073   FreeRide limits the GPU resource consumption of side tasks through the automated side task profiler
074   and the side task manager. The side task profiler first captures the key performance characteristics of
075   the newly implemented side tasks. The side task manager coordinates a group of side task workers,
076   one for each GPU in the platform, and assigns each of the side tasks to one of the workers based
077   on the characteristics. During pipeline training, bubbles are reported to the side task manager from
078   FreeRide-instrumented pipeline training system. The side task manager starts side tasks when the
079   bubble period begins and pauses them when the bubble ends. A side task worker deploys each
080   side task on top of CUDA MPS (Nvidia, 2024d) to limit its GPU memory consumption and uses
081
      Docker (Bernstein, 2014) for isolation. These components work collaboratively to serve side tasks
      during bubbles, achieving a low performance overhead on the primary pipeline training workload.
082
083   In summary, FreeRide is a holistic solution that manages and serves the side task by leveraging
084   bubbles in pipeline training, while maintaining minimal performance overhead and requiring low
085   programming effort. We evaluate FreeRide by deploying it to run side tasks alongside DeepSpeed
086   that runs pipeline training (Rasley et al., 2020). We measure the time increase of pipeline training
087
      as the performance overhead caused by side tasks. As the throughput of different side tasks is not
      directly comparable with the pipeline training workload, we use the cost of GPUs as a unified metric,
088
      i.e., the cost of the extra execution time from co-locating side tasks with pipeline training vs. the cost
089
      saved from running side tasks that otherwise would run on dedicated lower-tier GPUs.
090
091   The contributions of this paper are as follows:
092
093   • We study the bubbles in pipeline parallelism, present their various shapes in terms of duration and
094     available GPU memory, and demonstrate their potential for side tasks.
095
096
      • We present the programming framework and interfaces of FreeRide based on a state machine
097
        abstraction to implement generic side tasks with little engineering effort.
098
099
100   • We evaluate FreeRide with model training, graph analytics, and image processing side tasks to
101     demonstrate FreeRide’s effectiveness in harvesting bubbles in pipeline parallelism while reducing
102
        performance overhead.
103
104   • By serving side tasks based on the iterative interface, FreeRide achieves an average cost savings
105     of 7.8% with a low performance overhead of 1.1%. This is significantly better than using CUDA
106     MPS (Nvidia, 2024d) directly to co-locate the tasks, which results in a 4.5% cost increase and
107     48.7% overhead. When handling a mix of these 3 types of side tasks, FreeRide achieves 10.1%
        cost savings with a 1.1% overhead.

125             2                       BACKGROUND AND M OTIVATION
126
127             In this section, we first introduce the underutilization issues due to bubbles in pipeline parallelism
128             and then the motivation for utilizing the bubbles to execute generic workloads.
129
130
                2.1                     P IPELINE PARALLELISM AND B UBBLES
131
132           Pipeline parallelism is a commonly used scheme to train LLMs that exceed the memory capacity of a
133           single GPU (Rasley et al., 2020; Shoeybi et al., 2020). There are periods in pipeline training when the
134           GPU streaming multiprocessor (SM) occupancy is low, as depicted by the green curves in Figure 1(a).
135           We refer to these periods as bubbles in the pipeline, marked as shaded areas. Bubbles inherently
136           exist in pipeline parallelism and occur repetitively throughout training, as they are fundamentally
137           caused by unsatisfied dependencies between FP and BP operations (Liu et al., 2023). In the example
138
              of Figure 1, Stage 1 must wait for input from Stage 0 before executing its first FP operation, creating
              a bubble in Stage 1 that starts from t + 0.
139
140            To study bubbles in pipeline parallelism, we train a 3.6B-parameter LLM adapted from GPT2-
141            XL (Radford et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2023; Karpathy, 2024) using DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020)
142            on a 4-GPU server (detailed setup in Section 4.1). The training is deployed as a 4-stage pipeline, and
143            each stage takes one GPU as shown in Figure 1. Overall, we observe that bubbles exhibit different
144
               characteristics across all 4 stages.
145
146             2.1.1                     B UBBLE C ATEGORIZATION
147
              We categorize the bubbles into 3 types based on their positions in the training pipeline and causes.
148
149            • Type-A bubbles appear at the start and end of each epoch in all stages except for the first stage.
150            They are due to cascading dependencies between operations at the start and end of an epoch. When
151            an epoch starts, the FP operations start at Stage 0, while all other stages wait for input from preceding
152
               stages to start their first FP operation. Likewise, at the end of an epoch, the last BP operation starts at
               Stage 3 and all other stages wait for their succeeding stages to start their last BP operation.
153
154             • Type-B bubbles occur in the middle of each epoch on all stages except the last one. They are
155             caused by dependencies between interleaved FP and BP operations. Once the first FP operation
156             reaches the last stage, all previous stages must wait for the corresponding BP operation before they
157             can proceed with other operations, which causes Type-B bubbles.
158             • Type-C bubbles also occur in the middle of each epoch. Since BP operations typically take longer
159             than FP operations (Zheng et al., 2022), interleaved yet unaligned FP and BP operations create
160             bubbles in each stage except the last. For instance, in Figure 1(a), when Stage 2 finishes its third BP
161             operation, it must wait for input to its fourth BP operation, which is still being processed in Stage 3,
                causing a type-C bubble.

162
      Bubble Duration. In our training setup, the duration of a bubble ranges from 0.22 to 1.04 seconds,
163   depending on its type and stage. The duration increases for Type-A bubbles but decreases for Type-B
164   bubbles from Stage 0 to Stage 3. This is because of the cascading dependency from Stage 3 to Stage 0
165   for Type-A bubbles and from Stage 0 to Stage 3 for Type-B bubbles. For example, a Type-B bubble
166   at Stage 2 is due to an unfinished BP operation at Stage 3, with the same bubble at Stage 1 caused by
167   Stage 2. The accumulated time to satisfy dependencies elongates Type-A or Type-B bubbles at later
168   stages. However, Type-C bubbles are caused by unaligned FP and BP operations. Therefore, they
169   have a short duration and do not exhibit the same stage-dependent variations.
170
171   Available GPU Memory. Determined by the stage, the available GPU memory of a bubble ranges
172
      from less than 3 GB to more than 20 GB in our setup. As shown by Figure 1(b), within a stage, the
      GPU memory consumption of pipeline training remains the same. Thus, the bubbles within the same
173
      stage have the same amount of available GPU memory. Because the later stages consume less GPU
174
      memory to store activations used by BP operations (Liu et al., 2023), the available GPU memory
175   increases from Stage 0 to Stage 3.
176
177   We further study pipeline training of models of different sizes. As shown in Figure 1(c), bubble
178
      shapes differ. Overall, bubbles in larger LLMs have less available memory and shorter duration, but
      the distributions are similar as training follows the same pipeline schedule. Even larger models do
179
      not eliminate bubbles as they inherently exist. Under the same configuration, the characteristics of
180
      bubbles remain the same during training as epochs are repetitive and stable.
181
182   2.1.2    B UBBLE R ATE
183
184   Besides the bubble shape, we evaluate the overall bubble rate, i.e., the total bubble time over pipeline
185   training time. When the model size increases from 1.2B to 6B parameters, as shown in Figure 1(d),
186   both the per-epoch time in pipeline training and the total per-stage bubble time decrease. Therefore,
187   the bubble rate drops only slightly from 42.4% to 40.4%. We also evaluate a larger micro-batch
188
      number, i.e., an increase from 4 (used in Figures 1) to 8. The bubble rate drops to 26.2% as each
      epoch takes longer.
189
190   Prior work has focused on reducing bubbles in pipeline parallelism. One approach is designing
191   different ways of interleaving FP and BP operations (Fan et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). This approach
192   optimizes the scheduling strategies and interleaves FP and BP operations within an epoch. Therefore,
193   they are effective for Type-B and Type-C bubbles that appear inside an epoch but not for Type-A
194
      bubbles. Another approach is to reduce Type-A bubbles by decoupling the computation of gradients
      for the input and the model weights (Qi et al., 2024). This comes at a cost of higher GPU memory
195
      usage due to the extra activation storage, exacerbating the GPU memory bottleneck in LLM training.
196
      Despite these efforts, none of the approaches fully eliminate bubbles in pipeline training.
197
198
      2.2     U TILIZING B UBBLES
199
200   The difficulties in mitigating these bubbles motivate an alternative approach — acknowledging
201   their existence and leveraging their resources for benefits. GPUs used for training are generally
202   compute-rich, with sufficient GPU memory available during the bubbles to accommodate other GPU
203   workloads. Therefore, bubbles can be used to run workloads that otherwise require dedicated GPUs.
204   Previous work attempts to leverages such GPU resources in two ways, (1) by implementing dedicated
205   procedures, and (2) by transparent GPU sharing. Bamboo and PipeFisher implement procedures
206   that enhance pipeline training and run them during bubbles (Thorpe et al., 2023; Osawa et al., 2023).
207   However, they tightly couple the pipeline training system with the specialized procedures that involves
208   complicated implementation especially since such customization should consider various bubble
209   shapes — with durations ranging from 0.22 to 1.04 seconds, and available GPU memory from less
210   than 3 GB to more than 20 GB on each GPU (Section 2.1). Therefore, they cannot be used for generic
211   GPU workloads.
212   Transparent GPU sharing does not require complicated implementation to adapt GPU workloads to
213   bubbles (Nvidia, 2024c;d; Strati et al., 2024). However, they are oblivious of the patterns of pipeline
214   parallel training and bubbles, and can cause significant performance overhead on the high-priority
215   and high-cost pipeline training (Section 4). In addition, some GPU sharing work is tailored for certain
      software toolchain, which significantly limits its versatility (Strati et al., 2024).

      In this work, we aim to make bubble resources available to generic workloads, allowing for a
      programmable and efficient use of bubbles, while minimizing the overhead of side tasks on the
236
      high-priority pipeline training. We identify two major challenges.
237
238   Challenge 1: programming effort required to support generic side tasks. Typically, GPU
239   workloads are implemented based on the assumption that they have access to the full GPU and can
240   run continuously until they finish execution. However, bubbles are intermittent and largely vary
241
      in duration, as in Section 2.1. A side task should be tailored to bubble patterns — the side task
      automatically pauses or resumes when a bubble ends or starts. Customizing the training framework
242
      to embed side tasks is conceptually feasible but limits the flexibility of implementing and executing
243
      generic GPU workloads, much like the limitations from prior work on co-running specialized
244   procedures (Osawa et al., 2023; Thorpe et al., 2023).
245
246   Challenge 2: limiting the impact of side tasks. LLM training can span months on expensive
247
      high-end GPUs and cost millions of dollars (Zhang et al., 2022). Even with side tasks placed in the
      under-utilized bubbles, they may still interfere with pipeline training, significantly increasing the cost
248
      of LLM training and offsetting the benefit of running side tasks. However, limiting the impact of side
249
      tasks is not trivial. As the shape of bubbles varies, naively implementing side tasks may consume
250   more resources than bubbles have — exceeding the duration of bubbles or even crashing the main
251   task due to excessive GPU memory allocation. Ideally, bubbles should be utilized without impacting
252   the more expensive and prioritized LLM training task.
253
254
255   3    D ESIGN OF F REE R IDE
256
257
      FreeRide is our system that addresses the aforementioned challenges in utilizing bubbles in pipeline
258   training to serve generic GPU side tasks. It includes two programming interfaces, an automated
259   profiler, and FreeRide runtime consisting of a side task manager and multiple side task workers. The
260   programming interfaces reduce the engineering effort to implement side tasks that fit into bubbles,
261   and the automated profiler obtains the GPU resource consumption of side tasks, which is used by
262   FreeRide runtime to minimize the overhead of side tasks on pipeline training.
263
      Figure 2 depicts the workflow of FreeRide. First, programmers adapt their side task implementation
264   using FreeRide’s programming interfaces (step ➊). FreeRide then automatically generates a profile of
265   the side task’s characteristics (step ➋), which is submitted with the side task to the side task manager
266   of FreeRide (step ➌). During pipeline training, the side task manager continuously adds bubbles from
267   the instrumented training framework to FreeRide (step ➍). The side task manager assigns side tasks
268   to workers that are deployed in Docker containers (Bernstein, 2014) based on memory allocation of
269   pipeline training and the characteristics of side tasks (step ➎), and starts/pauses side tasks based on
      the available bubbles (step ➏). The side tasks access GPUs through MPS (Nvidia, 2024d) (step ➐).

      In the remainder of this section, we introduce how FreeRide addresses the challenge to implement

273
      3.1   P ROGRAMMING OF S IDE TASKS
274
275   To address the challenge in programming effort required to support generic side tasks, we first
276   make an important observation, that GPU workloads are not monolithic, and that they can be often
277   divided into smaller, repeated steps with largely predictable per-step duration, such as epochs in
278   model training, iterations in graph analytical workloads (Page et al., 1998), and steps to process each
279   image in image-processing workloads (Nvidia, 2019). On the other hand, bubbles also demonstrate
280   repeating and predictable patterns, as discussed in Section 2.1.
281   With these observations in mind, we abstract the life cycle of side tasks using a state machine model.
282   The execution of side tasks within bubbles can be implemented as state transition functions. We then
283   design programming interfaces based on this abstraction. They are discussed below.
284
      As shown in Figure 3(a), we abstract a side task using a state machine model with five states and six
285
      state transitions. The five states capture the life cycle of a side task, from process creation to process
286
      termination, and correspond to different uses of hardware resources, e.g., GPU memory and GPU
287   execution time. The six state transitions are used by the programmer to implement the user-defined
288   logic of a side task, e.g., allocating or releasing hardware resources or performing computation on
289   GPU. Once the side task is implemented, FreeRide automatically handles the state transitions at
290   runtime. Next, using model training as an example, we discuss the states and state transitions.
291
      • SUBMITTED. This state means that FreeRide has profiled a task and submitted it to the side
292
      task manager, but the side task worker has not created the side task process yet. State transition
293   CreateSideTask() happens automatically after the side task manager assigns a side task to a
294   worker and the worker creates the side task process. For a model training side task, this is where the
295   process is first created.
296
      • CREATED. In this state, the worker has created the side task process, and this process has loaded
297
      its context to the main memory but not to the GPU memory. For model training, in this state, the side
298
      task process has already created and initialized variables in CPU memory, e.g., the dataset, the data
299   loader, and the loss function. However, the side task process will not load them into GPU memory
300   until the side task manager initiates the state transition InitSideTask() which indicates the
301   completion of side task initialization.
302
      • PAUSED. This state is where the side task starts to use GPU memory. For model training, the side
303
      task process has loaded its context, e.g., model weights and optimizer states, in the GPU memory.
304
      However, this process waits in the PAUSED state until the side task manager transitions its state to
305   RUNNING through StartSideTask().
306
307   • RUNNING. In this state, the side task executes the step-wise GPU workload. Referring to the
308
      example above of the model training side task, this step involves reading the next batch, computing
      the output and loss, updating the model weights, and resetting the optimizer states. The side task
309
      iteratively enters the RunNextStep() state transition to execute these steps until the side task
310
      manager transitions its state through PauseSideTask(). Therefore, in this state, the side task
311   process uses both the GPU memory and the GPU SMs.
312
313   • STOPPED. This state marks the end of the life cycle of a side task, where the side task process
314
      releases all of its hardware resources and terminates. It can be transited from states CREATED,
      PAUSED, and RUNNING through StopSideTask() initiated by the side task manager.
315
316   Figure 3(b) shows state transitions of a side task in Stage 0 of Figure 1. Initially, the side task is
317   in the PAUSED (P) state. After four FP operations in the main training workload have finished, a
318   bubble starts and the side task manager initiates StartSideTask() to transit the side task to
319   the RUNNING (R) state. After the first bubble ends, the side task manager pauses the side task via
320
      PauseSideTask(). Then, the main training workload has BP operations and bubbles interleaved,
      leading to back-and-forth transitions between PAUSED and RUNNING states of the side task.
321
322   Given the state machine abstraction, the next step is to implement side tasks, which have two
323   requirements. First, the programmer should be able to implement the side task in a way that can
      pause at the end of a bubble and resume at the start of the next bubble. Second, the side task should


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      be able to communicate with the side task manager to receive state transition RPCs for pausing and
325   resuming. To lift programming burdens, FreeRide provides two programming interfaces, the iterative
326   interface, and the imperative interface. The iterative interface is the preferred one for side tasks
327   in FreeRide. It requires the side task to be step-wise, e.g., model training, and provides the lowest
328   performance overhead. For other side tasks that cannot be explicitly implemented step-wise, the
329   imperative interface is the fallback solution. It offers better versatility to support (almost) generic GPU
330   workloads at the cost of higher performance overhead. Both interfaces incorporate the communication
331   of side tasks with other components, and the programmer only has to apply a few lines of changes.
332   We leave the details of programming interfaces as well as examples in Appendix A.1.
333
334   3.2     M INIMIZING THE I MPACT ON P IPELINE PARALLEL T RAINING
335
336
      To address the challenge of limiting the impact of side tasks on the main pipeline training workload
      taking three approaches, FreeRide first leverages offline profiling to understand the shapes of bubbles
337
      and characteristics of newly submitted side tasks (Section 3.2.1). Based on the profiling results,
338
      FreeRide employs one side task manager and multiple side task workers, one for each GPU. The side
339   task manager assigns the newly submitted side task to one of the side task workers with enough GPU
340   memory, and initiates state transitions of side tasks through remote procedure calls (RPCs) at the
341   start and end of each bubble, which are reported by DeepSpeed that we instrument (Section 3.2.2).
342   FreeRide further employs CUDA MPS (Nvidia, 2024d) and a twofold mechanism to prevent side
343   tasks from excessively allocating GPU memory or not pausing correctly (Section 3.2.3).
344
345   3.2.1    P ROFILING B UBBLES AND S IDE TASKS
346
      Bubbles. To know the shapes of bubbles, FreeRide runs DeepSpeed, monitors its estimated SM occu-
347
      pancy and GPU memory consumption through the PyTorch profiler (PyTorch, b), and automatically
348
      measures each bubble’s duration and available GPU memory. Since the pipeline schedule determines
349   bubbles, this offline profiling is done only once for each model and pipeline scheduling on the same
350   hardware platform.
351
352
      Side tasks. After the programmer implements the side task, FreeRide profiles it with the automated
      profiling tool for its performance characteristics of GPU memory consumption and per-step duration,
353
      which FreeRide uses for side task management and GPU resource limit. For side tasks implemented
354
      using the iterative interface, this procedure is fully automated. The profiling tool runs the side
355   task, monitors its GPU memory consumption, and records the timestamps at the start and end
356   of RunNextStep() for the per-step duration. For side tasks implemented using the imperative
357   interface, the tool profiles GPU memory consumption in the same way. However, since the side task
358   is not implemented step-wise, the automated profiling tool does not measure the per-step duration.
359
360   3.2.2    S IDE TASK M ANAGEMENT
361
362   FreeRide’s side task management has two main roles. First, upon receiving a new side task, the side
363
      task manager assigns it to a suitable side task worker. Second, when the pipeline training system
      adds bubbles to the side task manager, the side task manager initiates the state transitions of side
364
      tasks (Figure 3(a)) through RPCs. This way, the side tasks are only served during bubbles and do not
365
      compete for GPU resources with the main pipeline training workload.
366
367   To do so, when the side task manager receives a new side task, it first selects all workers whose
368   bubbles have enough available GPU memory for the side task. Then, it assigns the side task to
369
      worker with the least side tasks waiting to be served. During pipeline training, the side task manager
      periodically checks whether any bubble has just started or expired. If a bubble has just started, the
370
      side task manager starts the execution of the corresponding side task, or adds a new side task to the
371
      bubble’s worker. If a bubble has just expired, the side task manager pauses the corresponding side
372   task. We demonstrate the detailed side task management algorithms in Appendix A.2.
373
374   3.2.3    GPU R ESOURCE L IMIT
375
376   GPU Memory. FreeRide leverages MPS to impose GPU memory limit on side tasks. I.e., when a
377   worker creates a side task, it sets GPU memory limits using MPS. The side task process triggers an
      out-of-memory (OOM) error when its memory consumption exceeds the limit, but other processes


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      remain unaffected. However, FreeRide is also compatible with other mechanisms for limiting GPU
379
      memory, e.g., multi-instance GPU (MIG) (Nvidia, 2024c) or manually implemented accounting
380   through intercepting CUDA kernel calls (Strati et al., 2024).
381
382   GPU Execution Time. FreeRide limits GPU execution time using two mechanisms. (1)
383
      The program-directed mechanism is tailored for the iterative interface. When the side task man-
      ager makes an RPC to initiate StartSideTask() state transition of a side task, it also sends the
384
      end time of this bubble to the side task. After the state transition finishes, the side task enters the
385
      RUNNING state. Before the side task automatically starts RunNextStep(), the program-directed
386   mechanism checks if the remaining time of the bubble is enough for the side task to execute the next
387   step. The side task will only execute the next step if the remaining time exceeds the per-step duration.
388   (2) The framework-enforced mechanism supports side tasks implemented using the imperative inter-
389   face and is also a fallback mechanism for the iterative interface. After the side task manager initiates
390   the PauseSideTask() state transition for a side task, it waits for a short grace period before
391   checking the last paused timestamp — a timestamp maintained by the interface that records the last
392   time the side task was paused. If this timestamp is not updated after the state transition begins, the
393   side task manager assumes that the interface failed to pause the side task correctly and subsequently
394
      instructs the corresponding worker to terminate the side task process using SIGKILL. The side task
      initialization, InitSideTask, which runs only once throughout the life cycle of a side task, is also
395
      protected by this mechanism.

476
      intensity. We conclude that FreeRide effectively utilizes bubbles in pipeline training for serving side
477
      tasks. While the comparative methods can utilize bubbles, unlike FreeRide, they are not designed for
478   this purpose. Thus, they are inefficient in using bubbles, leading to higher costs.

506   5   D ISCUSSION AND R ELATED W ORK
507
508   Security. Prior GPU sharing solutions tend to prioritize efficiency and assume a safe environment.
509   E.g., Orion assumes that co-located GPU workloads are in the same trust domain (Strati et al., 2024).
510   FreeRide provides the same security and isolation guarantees as the lower-level system it is built upon.
511   It incorporates MPS to limit GPU memory which provides separate GPU address spaces (Nvidia,
512   2024b) for pipeline training and side tasks, and Docker for environment isolation (Docker, 2024).
513   Orthogonally, security for co-located GPU workloads is an active research area (Liu et al., 2019;
514   Zhang et al., 2024). We expect future work to co-design security with efficient GPU sharing.
515   Side task management. By implementing different strategies in its side task manager, FreeRide
516   can incorporate more sophisticated management, e.g., co-locating multiple side tasks with various
517   performance characteristics in the same worker to improve the utilization of bubbles (Liu et al.,
518   2022b) or serving side tasks with fairness or performance guarantees (Ghodsi et al., 2011).
519   Scalability. FreeRide can be extended for better scalability. As FreeRide implements communications
520   among its components using RPCs, it can be easily extended to distributed settings with side tasks on
521   multiple servers. FreeRide can also be extended for multi-GPU side tasks, e.g., distributed training
522   and big data processing (Liu et al., 2022a), by launching workers with access to multiple GPUs.
523
      Other ML accelerators. This work targets GPUs due to their widespread accessibility. FreeRide’s
524
      mitigation for bubbles fundamentally applies to other ML accelerators (Jouppi et al., 2017; Meta,
525   2023), provided that the platform has isolation and resource limit options for each process. We
526   anticipate future work to incorporate the approach of FreeRide with other ML platforms.

      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We propose FreeRide, a system to harvest the bubbles in pipeline parallelism to serve generic GPU
      side tasks. It provides programming interfaces that abstract the life cycle of a side task as different
532
      states of a state machine and allows programmers to implement side tasks with little engineering
533
      effort. The side task manager and side task workers manage bubbles and side tasks and reduce the
534   performance overhead of side tasks on pipeline training. Our evaluation shows that, on average,
535   FreeRide achieves 8% cost savings for long-running and expensive pipeline training with a negligible
536   performance overhead of only about 1%.

      side tasks to train ResNet18 and to do LLM inference using the iterative interface of FreeRide in

      Python. As demonstrated by these two examples, the flexibility and versatility of FreeRide enables
757   the user to implement various side tasks with little engineering effort. Less important lines such as
758   importing dependencies and parsing arguments are simplified. Porting this example involves mainly
759   five steps. Step ➀: import FreeRide dependencies and inherit the iterative interface class, which
760   includes an implementation for the state machine abstraction, communication with the side task
761   manager, and the program-directed mechanism to limit the GPU execution time. The programmer
762   only has to migrate the implementation of the original GPU workload to the interface. Steps ➁ and
763   ➂: implement the side task initialization in 2 state transition functions, CreateSideTask() and
764   InitSideTask(), to load the context into main memory and GPU memory respectively. Step ➃:
765
      wrap the original loop implementation with RunNextStep(). Step ➄: the main function handles
      argument parsing and runs the side task interface.
766
767   Most of the modifications are trivial, e.g., wrapping implementations with side task state transition
768   functions in Step ➁, ➂, and ➃, which are required by Python. Aside from this, the programmer can
769   directly copy the important logic, e.g., loading the dataset and training the model, from the original
770   implementation. In addition, if the programmer customizes the model architecture instead of using
771
      the publicly available ones, the model implementation also does not require modification.
772   Imperative programming interface. This interface does not require the programmer to implement
773   the side task in a step-wise way. Therefore, instead of implementing the side task in multiple functions
774   (steps ➁ — ➃), the programmer can merge them in RunGpuWorkload(). However, this approach
775   trades performance for less programming effort, as pausing side tasks through the framework-enforced
776   mechanism incurs more overheads. When the side task manager initiates PauseSideTask() state
777
      transition via an RPC at the end of a bubble, even though the CPU process of the side task is paused
      by the framework-enforced mechanism (Section 3.2.3) after the state transition, CUDA kernels that
778
      have already started cannot be paused because they are asynchronous Nvidia (2024a). As a result,
779
      these CUDA kernels will overlap with pipeline training, causing a higher performance overhead than
780   the iterative interface.

892
893   This section describes the sensitivity study which demonstrates that FreeRide can achieve superior
894   time increase and cost savings compared with MPS in different settings. We change the side task
895   batch size, DeepSpeed model size, and DeepSpeed micro-batch numbers of different side tasks, and
896   study the time increase and cost savings of FreeRide with the iterative interface.
897   (1) Varying batch sizes. Figure 7(a) and (b) include model training side tasks under variable batch
898   sizes. Other side tasks are not included as they do not run with batch sizes. OOM means that the GPU
899   in Server-II does not have enough GPU memory for the configuration, so the cost savings cannot be
900   calculated. FreeRide has low performance overheads, with around 1% increase in execution time,
901   and cost savings of 3.4% – 7.5%.
902   (2) Varying model sizes. In Figure 7(c) and (d), the performance overheads of FreeRide range from
903   -0.7% to 1.9%, and cost savings range from 1.8% to 22.2%. The main reason is the shorter bubble
904   durations when training larger models as the main workload, which was also shown in Figure 1.
905
      (3) Varying micro-batch numbers. In Figure 7(e) and (f), the performance overhead of FreeRide
906
      increases from -0.4% to 1.5%, and cost savings reduces from 2.1% to 11.8%. When the micro-batch
907
      number increases, because of the lower bubble rate (Section 2.1), the cost savings decrease.
