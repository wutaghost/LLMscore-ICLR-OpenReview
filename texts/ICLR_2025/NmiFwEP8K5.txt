009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning (CL) is a research field focused on continuously adapting foun-
012            dation models such as large language models (LMs) to newly emerging informa-
013            tion sources and tasks. While aspects such as parameter efficiency, knowledge
014            transfer, and managing model capacity have recently received attention, the main
015            research focus in CL remains on preventing catastrophic forgetting. Specifically,
016            there is a lack of solutions that address all these aspects simultaneously. We bridge
017            this gap by introducing Gated Expandable Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (GE-
018
               PEFT). Our approach shares knowledge of previous tasks through leveraging a
               single, dynamically expanding PEFT module within LMs while selectively gating
019
               irrelevant previous tasks. Our experiments across multiple task-incremental CL
020
               benchmarks demonstrate that GE-PEFT outperforms existing state-of-the-art CL
021            approaches in both full CL and few-shot settings. Our ablation and parameter sen-
022            sitivity studies highlight the benefit of each proposed component, demonstrating
023            that GE-PEFT offers a more efficient and adaptive solution for CL in LMs.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, pre-trained foundation language models (LMs) trained on vast amounts of textual
029   data have rapidly advanced state-of-the-art performance in a wide range of natural language process-
030   ing (NLP) tasks due to the knowledge inherent in them (Petroni et al., 2019). As the world continues
031   to evolve, the available knowledge changes with existing information becoming outdated or receiv-
032   ing updates, and new information becoming available. To keep up with continuous progress and al-
033
      low LMs to face newly emerging problems, the research domain of continual learning (CL) focuses
      on the continuous adaptation of LMs to new and updated information, as well as newly emerging
034
      tasks. Within this growing field, recent works have identified several desirable properties of CL
035
      approaches, including the prevention of catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), parame-
036   ter efficiency (Omeliyanenko et al., 2023; Wang et al.; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023), and knowledge
037   transfer (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023).
038
039
      While research exists on each individual criterion for CL, to the best of our knowledge, only one
      work partially addresses all criteria simultaneously. Wang et al. (2024) extend the work of Razdai-
040
      biedina et al. (2023) who integrate a weak knowledge transfer mechanism into existing parameter-
041
      efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) strategies for CL, which initializes new and entirely separate PEFT
042   modules for a new task and prepend it with PEFT weights from previous tasks. Wang et al. (2024)
043   further enhance this approach by providing a learned similarity function that identifies relevant prior
044   tasks and reuses PEFT modules only from these tasks for initialization of the current task, thus
045   gating irrelevant tasks through similarity. While this approach achieves state-of-the-art results in
046   CL it only achieves knowledge transfer during initialization of new tasks, leading to the transferred
047   knowledge being overwritten during training, leaving room for further improvements.
048   In this work, we provide an alternative strategy for obtaining all desirable CL criteria while providing
049   knowledge of previous tasks to the current task throughout the entire training and inference. We
050   propose the integration of a single Gated Expandable PEFT (GE-PEFT) module into the pre-trained
051   LM. Within this module, a gating mechanism is leveraged to prevent catastrophic forgetting of
052   previous tasks. By using one GE-PEFT module to train all tasks, knowledge transfer is seamlessly
053   integrated during training and inference. The gating mechanism tracks the weights already allocated
      to previous tasks and ensures that only unused weights are available for updates by subsequent tasks,

      2       R ELATED W ORK

094
      Continual learning CL works can be broadly separated into replay-based, regularization-based,
      and parameter isolation-based Wang et al. (2024) methods. Regularization-based approaches (Kirk-
095
      patrick et al., 2017; Aljundi et al., 2018; de Masson D’Autume et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2021;
096
      Sun et al.) incorporate regularization terms into the loss function to penalize changes to critical
097   parameters for previously learned tasks. Replay-based approaches (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Chaudhry
098   et al., 2018; 2021; Mirzadeh et al., 2020) retain a subset of training data from previous tasks and
099   use it in conjunction with new data when training on subsequent tasks. However, access to prior
100   task data may not always be feasible and storing it can lead to storage overhead. Additionally,
101   while regularization-based and replay-based methods significantly mitigate the issue of catastrophic
102   forgetting, they do not completely eliminate it. In contrast, our approach leverages gating to fully
103   prevent catastrophic forgetting and does not require additional data to be retained for future training.
104   Among parameter isolation-based CL, certain architectures use low-rank factorization to separate
105   neural layers into task-specific and shared parameters Hyder et al. (2022). However, this approach
106   requires significant structural modifications to complex LMs. Early works on LMs update the entire
107

      LM, e.g., with a growing strategy that enlarges the model for each new task and freezes the previous
109   parameters Rusu et al. (2016) or a gating mechanism that reserves neurons for a specific task Serra
110   et al. (2018). Alternatively, Wortsman et al. (2020) learns a binary gating mask for all model pa-
111   rameters, revealing a highly performing sub-network for each task. All these approaches, however,
112   are not parameter efficient as they require updates to an entire model. Wang et al. (2023) propose a
113   PEFT solution that integrates dedicated parameters into a backbone LM to learn new tasks. While
114   this enables parameter efficient CL, PEFT modules are separated which prevents knowledge transfer.
115   Razdaibiedina et al. (2023) introduce progressive prompts, which extends the prefix tuning PEFT
116   approach with a prepending strategy to share knowledge from previous tasks. Wang et al. (2024) fur-
117
      ther add a learned similarity-based weighting scheme to this prepending strategy. This enables the
      gating of irrelevant tasks, but enables knowledge sharing only at training start, as subsequent training
118
      overrides the parameters shared at initialization. In contrast to these works, our GE-PEFT enables
119
      gated expandable and parameter-efficient CL while providing previous knowledge throughout the
120   entire training and inference process.
121
122   PEFT Within PEFT, multiple different strategies have been proposed that integrate a new set of
123   trainable parameters into different parts of the LM while keeping the base parameters frozen to
124   prevent changes. Houlsby et al. (2019) introduce Adapters consisting of multiple layers that are
125   incorporated between intermediate layers of LMs. Hu et al. (2021) propose Low Rank Adapters
126   (LoRA) which provide additive changes to the LM. Li & Liang (2021) introduce prefix tuning where
127   additional weights are added through additional input tokens which are learned during training.
128   These approaches not only enable efficient adaptation of LMs to new knowledge and tasks but also
129   address the problem of catastrophic forgetting of initial model weights. By injecting a dedicated
130
      set of new parameters for each new problem, PEFT methods can also prevent the degradation of
      performance on previously learned tasks. However, this approach prevents any knowledge sharing
131
      between tasks.
132
133
      Growing networks Multiple growing strategies for neural networks have been proposed in related
134   work. Rusu et al. (2016) propose a lifelong learning strategy that enlarges the entire network and
135   copies previous parameters, which results in large numbers of used parameters. Further work focuses
136   on optimizing PEFT module sizes specifically (Zhang et al.; Valipour et al., 2023) but they do not
137   address CL and transfer learning simultaneously.

144   of a small set of additional trainable parameters inserted into a pre-trained backbone LM. During
145   training on new data or tasks, the backbone model remains frozen and only the parameters of the
146   inserted PEFT module are updated. This setup allows the model to learn new knowledge or tackle
147   new tasks while remaining parameter-efficient. In our work, we follow the experimental setup of
148   Wang et al. (2024) and thus apply our GE-PEFT architecture to the PEFT models of Hu et al. (2021);
149
      Li & Liang (2021).

169   3.2   G ATED PEFT
170
171   In PEFT-based solutions, a new PEFT module is inserted into the backbone model to enable training
172   on new data. Each PEFT module operates independently within the model and does not share
173
      weights with other modules. As a result, knowledge transfer between PEFT modules is not possible.
      Inspired by Serra et al. (2018); Ke et al. (2021), we propose to facilitate knowledge transfer using a
174
      single PEFT module that is shared across all tasks and datasets. Since only a single PEFT module
175
      is inserted into the backbone model, a gating strategy with masking is used to prevent catastrophic
176   forgetting that could result from continuously training the PEFT module on new data.
177
178   For each new task or dataset (t), a trainable mask m(t) is computed with the same dimensionality as
179   the activated neurons within the PEFT modules. This binary mask is derived from task embeddings
180   e(t) that take as input a numerical task identifier and transformed into a pseudo-gating function using
181
      a Sigmoid activation and a scaling hyperparameter s by
182                                                     m(t) = σ(se(t) ).                                (3)

185   training. This forces the learned mask m(t) to converge towards 0 or 1 during training. The resulting
186   pseudo-binary masks m(t) are multiplied element-wise with the respective active neurons within the
187   PEFT modules during training.

234   3.3   E XPANDABLE PEFT
235
236   Using a single gated PEFT module across all tasks enables the model to access the knowledge ob-
237   tained from previous tasks while preventing catastrophic forgetting. During training on a new task,
238   a portion of the available neurons is reserved and cannot be updated. Consequently, the capacity
239   to integrate new knowledge into the model with this PEFT solution is limited, as the available neu-
240
      rons may become exhausted over time. To address these limitations, we propose an expandable
      PEFT module that dynamically adjusts its size to meet current needs while still enabling knowledge
241
      transfer and mitigating the catastrophic forgetting effect.

      When to Expand PEFT While several strategies of varying complexity exist for determining
264   when to expand neural networks, such as predefined schedules (Evci et al., 2022), we adopt a simpler
265   approach, using an established strategy that expands the network when the loss reaches a plateau
266   (Wu et al., 2019; Kilcher et al., 2018). To be precise, once the validation loss reaches a plateau,
267   we perform a PEFT expansion step and resume training of the same task with additional trainable
268   parameters. This process is repeated until no further improvement in task validation performance is
269   observed, even after adding more parameters. The validation loss is calculated at the end of each
      epoch and early stopping is applied with a patience of 5 epochs, following Wang et al. (2024).

274
275   Following Wang et al. (2023); Razdaibiedina et al. (2023), we utilize the near-domain benchmarks
276   AfriSenti and Web-of-Science (WOS) (Kowsari et al., 2016), as well as the far-domain benchmark
277   MTL5 (Zhang et al., 2015), for our experiments. The near-domain benchmark consists of closely
278
      related tasks. WOS is a document classification dataset with a hierarchical structure, consisting of
      7 parent classes (biochemistry, civil engineering, computer science, electrical engineering, medical
279
      science, mechanical engineering and psychology), each with 5 closely related child subclasses. In
280
      line with Wang et al. (2024), we structure our experiments into 7 CL tasks based on these high-
281   level parent classes. AfriSenti (Muhammad et al., 2023) is a multilingual sentiment analysis dataset
282   comprising 12 African low-resource languages (Algerian Arabic (dz), Amharic (am), Hausa (ha),
283   Igbo (ig), Kinyarwanda (kr), Moroccan Arabic (ma), Mozambican Portuguese (pt), Nigerian Pidgin
284   (pcm), Swahili (sw), Twi (twi), Xitsonga (ts), and Yoruba (yo)). Following Wang et al. (2024),
285   we conduct our experiments using three different task orders from the AfriSenti dataset. MTL5
286   is a widely used far-domain CL benchmark comprising five distinct text classification tasks. AG
287   News and DBpedia include 4 and 14 classes, respectively, for topic classification. Amazon and Yelp
288   both consist of 5 classes for sentiment classification, while Yahoo Answers contains 10 classes for
289
      question-and-answer classification.
290   For our experiments, we closely follow Wang et al. (2024). We use a train-test split of 115 000
291   and 7 600 samples, respectively, for all experiments involving BERT. We train the model using five
292   different task orders. For the T5 and Llama experiments, in line with prior research, we use 4 of the
293   5 tasks, excluding Yelp, with 16 samples for training while keeping the test set unchanged. These
294   experiments are conducted with three different task orders. Following previous work, we report
295
      macro-F1 score on the AfriSenti dataset and macro-accuracy on WOS and MTL5 datasets (Wang
      et al., 2024; Muhammad et al., 2023). We omit evaluations of backward transfer as all evaluated
296
      methods fully prevent catastrophic forgetting through architectural design. Forward transfer is eval-
297
      uated by comparing the final performance to a model only trained on one task, thus showcasing
298   knowledge sharing potential. Used task orders for all datasets are listed in Appendix A.3.
299
300   To evaluate whether the effectiveness of our method extends to a larger number of tasks, we conduct
301
      an additional experiment on longer sequences. Following Razdaibiedina et al. (2023), we use the
      MTL15 dataset, which consists of 15 classification tasks.

315
316   Sequential Full-FT: The model parameters are fully trainable and the entire model is trained on
317   each task sequentially.
318   PerTask-PEFT: The backbone model parameters are frozen and a dedicated PEFT module is trained
319   for each task separately.

      Sequential PEFT-FT: The backbone parameters are frozen and a single PEFT module is trained

      sequentially on all tasks.

323   EPI (Wang et al., 2023): Trains a dedicated PEFT module and task representation vector for each

      ProgPrompt (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023): A parameter isolation-based method that conducts task-
325   specific prefix tuning. To facilitate knowledge transfer, prefix modules from previous tasks are

349
      Full Continual Learning In this experiment, we compare GE-PEFT integrated into AfroXLMR
350
      and BERT on two near-domain datasets, AfriSenti and WOS, as well as the far-domain dataset
351
      MTL5, against various baselines and state-of-the-art CL approaches, as summarized in Table 1. In
352   the near-domain experiments, non-CL solutions like sequential full finetuning (Seq Full-FT) show
353   a strong negative impact, which can be attributed to catastrophic forgetting. Using separate PEFT
354   modules for each task (PerTask-PEFT) mitigates catastrophic forgetting and outperforms sequential
355   PEFT finetuning (Seq PEFT-FT) across all datasets, as well as ProgPrompt and EPI on AfriSenti.
356   Notably, only MoCL and our GE-PEFT show improvements over this baseline, leveraging knowl-
357   edge sharing across closely related tasks. Overall, across all near-domain datasets, our proposed
358   architecture, GE-PEFT, outperforms MoCL, the current state-of-the-art, by a large margin. In the
359   far-domain experiment on MTL5, we observe similar behavior, though the differences compared to
360
      the per-task baseline (PerTask-PEFT) are smaller than those observed on WOS. This is expected,
      as less knowledge from previous tasks can be leveraged through knowledge sharing. Notably, GE-
361
      PEFT still outperforms all baselines and state-of-the-art approaches, likely due to its ability to gate
362
      irrelevant information as later analyzed in the ablation study.

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this work, we introduced Gated Expandable Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (GE-PEFT), a novel
520   approach for CL in LMs that effectively addresses four key CL criteria: catastrophic forgetting pre-
521   vention, parameter efficiency, knowledge transfer, and managing model capacity. By integrating a
522   single, dynamically expanding PEFT module with a gating mechanism, GE-PEFT enables contin-
523   uous knowledge transfer throughout training and inference while maintaining task separation. Our
524   experimental results across multiple task-incremental CL benchmarks demonstrate that GE-PEFT
525   consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods. Yet, although our GE-PEFT approach is
526   effective for task-incremental learning, it has not yet been evaluated for class-incremental learning
527   scenarios where the task of a given input is not known at inference time. Further, our current version
528
      of GE-PEFT relies on simple heuristics for its expansion strategy. Here, more complex and effective
      expansion strategies are a promising area of future work.

713
714
715   Here we provide further parameter analysis experiments to complement the presented results in
716   Section 5.
717   For the BERT-base model in Table 9, results show slight fluctuations of accuracy independent of the
718   choices of λ and expansion. The model also appears to be insensitive to regularization as increasing
719   the λ parameter shows only a slight trend of decreasing parameter usage with multiple outliers with
720   high λ, e.g., with 25% expansion rate. Despite high λ values, the model maintains a high used-
721   parameter quota, suggesting that the small BERT-base model needs parameters for task-solving,
722   limiting the effect of regularization. Increases in expansion size on the other hand result in more
723   taken parameters, indicated by increased prefix size. However, as high accuracy is also achievable
724
      with low expansion sizes, this indicates that fast growth is not necessary to reach high performance.
725   In contrast to the BERT-base results, the larger T5 model on the low-resource MTL5 dataset in Ta-
726   ble 10 shows a more pronounced effect of regularization that reduces the used-parameter quota with
727   higher λ values. Expansion rate does, however, only show small impacts on this model and dataset
728   combination, as only few expansion steps are made by the model in all configurations. Especially
729   in combination with the regularization, this suggests a behavior where the available parameters are
730
      sufficient to encode all information. Low regularization appears to result in faster saturation of all
      available neurons which does not require growing steps, while high regularization with small expan-
731
      sion size causes multiple growing steps as the regularization prevents the direct use of the available
732
      neurons.
