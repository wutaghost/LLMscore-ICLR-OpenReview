011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Recent advancements in diffusion models have brought new vitality into visual
014                content creation. However, current text-to-video generation models still face chal-
015                lenges such as high training costs, substantial data requirements, and difficulties in
016                maintaining consistency between given text and motion of the foreground object.
017
                   To address these challenges, we propose mask-guided video generation, which re-
                   quires only a small amount of data and is trained on a single GPU. Furthermore, to
018
                   mitigate the impact of background interference on controllable text-to-video gen-
019
                   eration, we utilize mask sequences obtained through drawing or extraction, along
020                with the first-frame content, to guide video generation. Specifically, our model
021                introduces foreground masks into existing architectures to learn region-specific
022                attention, precisely matching text features and the motion of the foreground ob-
023                ject. Subsequently, video generation is guided by the mask sequences to prevent
024                the sudden disappearance of foreground objects. Our model also incorporates
025                a first-frame sharing strategy during inference, leading to better stability in the
026                video generation. Additionally, our approach allows for incrementally generation
027                of longer video sequences. By employing this method, our model achieves effi-
028
                   cient resource utilization and ensures controllability and consistency in video gen-
                   eration using mask sequences. Extensive qualitative and quantitative experiments
029
                   demonstrate that this approach excels in various video generation tasks, such as
030
                   video editing and generating artistic videos, outperforming previous methods in
031                terms of consistency and quality1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037
      In recent years, diffusion-based generative models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a;b) have made
      significant progress in text-to-image generation. Models such as DALLE2 (Ramesh et al., 2022),
038
      Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), and Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022) have demonstrated the
039
      ability to generate diverse and high-quality images guided by text prompts. Given the success of text-
040   to-image generation, some researchers have begun to explore applying these successful experiences
041   to the field of text-to-video (T2V) generation. However, unlike image generation, video creation de-
042   mands not only accurate alignment between text and individual frames, but also coherence between
043   the text and the motion of the foreground object.
044
      Some researchers have attempted to achieve text-to-video generation by training on a large amount
045
      of video data (Blattmann et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2023). While this method may make some
046   progress, it requires a significant amount of computational resources and human effort for data
047   and text annotation, making it impractical for real-world applications. To address this issue, Tune-
048   A-Video (Wu et al., 2023a) introduced a new single-video fine-tuning setup for T2V generation.
049   This approach significantly reduces the training workload, as it only requires the fine-tuning of
050   a single video to complete the training on a consumer-grade GPU. However, Tune-A-Video (Wu
051   et al., 2023a) tends to overfit the given video, making it highly susceptible to the background of the
052   provided video.

      2.1   T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
140
141   In recent years, text-to-image generation technology has made significant progress in producing
142   high-quality and semantically consistent images. Particularly, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020;
143   Song et al., 2020a;b) have been widely favored over GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
144   2017; Xu et al., 2018) and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Sohn et al., 2015; Van Den Oord et al.,
145   2017) for their excellent text consistency, high-quality generated images. Models such as GLIDE
146   (Nichol et al., 2021), Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), DALL·E2 (Ramesh et al., 2022), and LDM
147   (Rombach et al., 2022) enhance the association between text and images using the CLIP (Radford
148
      et al., 2021) model and generate images with fine textures and accurate semantics through powerful
      image generation architectures like Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). Additionally, to achieve a
149
      more personalized generation, researchers have developed methods like DreamBooth (Ruiz et al.,
150
      2023) and textual inversion (TI) (Gal et al., 2022), which expand the tokenizer’s vocabulary by em-
151   bedding user-provided concepts in the text embedding space, and improve image generation through
152   denoising processes. Furthermore, the ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) has brought new vitality to
153   the research on text-to-image generation by introducing more controllable input conditions to Stable
154   Diffusion, such as depth maps, poses, and so on. In our method, we use the first frame mask to
155   create the first frame of the video by ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), which is then used to generate
156   the subsequent frames.
157
158   2.2   T EXT- TO -V IDEO G ENERATION
159
160   The success of text-to-image generation has inspired researchers to explore the field of text-to-video
161   generation, leading to some notable achievements, such as ImagenVideo (Ho et al., 2022) developed
      by Google Research, Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2022) by Meta AI, MagicVideo (Zhou et al.,


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2022), VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2024)], CogVideo (Hong et al., 2022), and AnimateDiff (Guo
163   et al., 2023). These models have achieved a transition from generating static images to dynamic
164   videos through multimodal learning, diffusion models, and keyframe generation, as well as cogni-
165   tion and understanding. However, these models currently focus primarily on generating short videos.
166   To address the challenge of generating long videos, existing methods typically rely on autoregressive
167   models and diffusion models. Autoregressive models like NUWA-Infinity (Wu et al., 2022), Phenaki
168   (Villegas et al., 2022), and TATS (Ge et al., 2022) generate long video content by using generated
169   frames as conditions for subsequent frames. On the other hand, diffusion models like MCVD (Vo-
170   leti et al., 2022), FDM (Harvey et al., 2022), PVDM (Yu et al., 2023), and LVDM (He et al., 2022)
171
      also adopt a similar autoregressive mechanism, generating coherent video sequences by creating
      high-quality intermediate frames and gradually interpolating them. However, these models undeni-
172
      ably require substantial training resources, while methods like Text2Video-Zero (Khachatryan et al.,
173
      2023) and ControlVideo (Zhang et al., 2024b) employ zero-shot techniques, which do not require
174   fine-tuning but depend on pre-trained models and large-scale pre-training data, demanding high data
175   quality and diversity. In specific domains or scenarios, these methods may fail to generate suffi-
176   ciently detailed and accurate content due to the lack of specialized training on domain-specific data.
177   Additionally, methods like Control-A-Video (Chen et al., 2023) and MoonShot (Zhang et al., 2024a)
178   draw on the ideas of ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), using depth maps, edges, and other motion se-
179   quences to generate videos. While these methods can improve generation quality, they also require
180   considerable training resources. To address this issue, we follow the approach of the LAMP (Wu
181   et al., 2023b) model, which can be trained with only a small number of samples and a single GPU.
182
      Different from their approach, we introduce a method that incorporates foreground masks during
      training, allowing for more precise capture of the foreground region. This effectively prevents the
183
      blending of the foreground and background, ensuring clear separation and stable presentation of the
184
      foreground in the video.

203   3.1   T RAINING P IPELINE
204
205   Current text-to-video methods are commonly trained on large-scale datasets. Moreover, they are not
206   only computational expensive. However, this approach is not only computationally expensive but
207   also faces significant challenges in capturing the motion patterns of foreground objects, especially
208   in achieving precise control over motion trajectories. To address these issues and enhance the con-
209   trollability of foreground motion, we optimize the LAMP (Wu et al., 2023b) model and propose a
210   new video generation pipeline.
211   Our model first processes a video set V = {Vi |i ∈ [1, n]} and uses the Segment Anything model
212   (Kirillov et al., 2023) to extract foreground masks from the videos, generating a corresponding set of
213   foreground mask videos. The training data includes n videos and their foreground mask video sets,
214   along with a text Pm describing a shared motion pattern. By fine-tuning a pre-trained T2I model
215   based on the given video set and motion prompt, the model can generate new videos V ′ with motion
      patterns specified by prompt Pm .

219   grained control over motion trajectories.

      3.4   F IRST- FRAME S HARED S AMPLING S TRATEGY
343
344   During inference, most existing methods introduce a random shared noise that is fed into the model
345   throughout the video generation process. This is because reducing the noise variance can narrow the
346   dynamic range of the latent space, which contributes to a more stable generation process. However,
347   we found that if the shared noise is a randomly sampled εs ∼N (0, I), the generated results some-
348   times show a significant color loss in the foreground object and instability in the background ,as
349   shown in Fig 4(a). To address this issue, we convert the first frame image into a latent embedding
350   εs and regard it as shared noise. Then, we sample a noise sequence [ε2 , · · · , εn ] from the same
351   distribution as the base noise of samples. The shared noise is then added to the subsequent frames
352
      at a certain ratio, specifically: εi = αεs + (1 − α)εi , where α is a balance parameter. According to
      our experiments, setting α to 0.2 yields the best results. Adding the first frame’s features to the sub-
353
      sequent frames effectively prevents the loss of certain features during video generation and ensures
354
      continuity between frames. The result is shown in Fig 4(b).

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
487
488   To validate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct ablation experiments by removing key
489   components one by one to observe the impact on the quality of the generated videos. First, we
490   completely remove the conditioning on the first frame and directly generated video frames from
491   random noise. The resulting video, as shown in Fig 6(b), lost the content described by the text,
492   demonstrating the foundational role of the first frame in generating the video. Secondly, we remove
493   the motion sequence mask during inference. The resulting video, as shown in Fig 6(c), exhibited
494
      unclear motion patterns and poor frame-to-frame coherence, highlighting the importance of the mask
      sequence in guiding and capturing motion information. Thirdly, we did not use ControlNet (Zhang
495
      et al., 2023) to generate the first frame, instead generating a random initial frame. As shown in Fig
496
      6(d), the generated first frame did not align with the first frame mask, leading to poor overall video
497   generation quality. This further validates the advantage of using ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023)
498   for generating the first frame based on the mask. Finally, we replace the shared noise with random
499   noise instead of using the latent representation of the first frame. The results, as shown in Fig 6(e),
500   indicated a significant decline in video quality and consistency, with the foreground objects’ colors
501   becoming too bright and losing their original hues. This demonstrates the importance of shared
502   noise. Through these ablation experiments, we demonstrate the crucial role of the motion sequence
503   mask, shared noise, first frame results, and ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) in video generation.
504   These components enable the model to effectively control the motion trajectory of the foreground
505
      text, prevent the issue of the foreground suddenly disappearing, and improve the quality of the
      generated video.

508   5     C ONCLUSION
509
510   In this paper, we propose an innovative mask-guided video generation method that effectively ad-
511   dresses significant challenges in existing technologies, such as difficulties in maintaining consis-
512   tency between foreground text and its motion. By introducing foreground masks during training,
513   this method significantly improves the model’s ability to learn foreground motion, thereby greatly
514   enhancing the quality and controllability of the generated videos. Our approach can be trained on a
515   small number of videos with a single GPU, utilizing ControlNet to generate the content of the first
516
      frame and guiding the generation of subsequent video frames through masks, achieving diversity
      and controllability in the motion of the foreground object. The experimental results show that this
517
      method not only improves the quality and consistency of the generated videos but also efficiently
518
      reduces training resource consumption.

793   D    LIMITATION
794
795   Although our mask-guided video generation method achieves a high degree of consistency between
796   the motion trajectory of the foreground object and the text description, the method still has certain
797   limitations when generating videos beyond the scope of the input mask motion sequence. For exam-
798   ple, when the input motion mask shows a horse walking, even if the text prompt is ”a horse running
799
      in the desert,” the generated video still strictly follows the given mask motion sequence, as shown
      in Fig 8. While the background may display a motion trend, the foreground horse still maintains a
800
      walking posture. Therefore, when there is a clear conflict between the text prompt and the motion
801
      sequence, the generated video tends to prioritize the input motion sequence, ignoring the dynamic
802   information implied in the text prompt. To address this issue, future research will focus on how to
803   adaptively adjust the motion sequence based on the text prompt, allowing the generated video to not
804   only better align with user expectations but also exhibit greater dynamic expressiveness.
