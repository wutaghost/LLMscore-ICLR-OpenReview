009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conventionally trained neural networks excel at prediction but often struggle to
012            model uncertainty in their own predictions. We explore this challenge in a meta-
013            learning bandit decision-making problem for news recommendations; this setting
014            require decision-making algorithms to incorporate pretrained language models to
015            process text data for the best performance. We present a scalable approach to
016            Bayesian uncertainty quantification by posing it as a problem of autoregressive
017            generative modeling of future rewards. First, we use historical data on previously
018
               released news articles to pre-train a generative model to predict sequences of fu-
               ture potential rewards. At inference time, our algorithm makes decisions based
019
               on limited previous rewards and autoregressively generated future rewards. Far
020
               from a heuristic, we synthesize insights from the literature to show our method
021            is a novel implementation of Thompson (posterior) sampling, a prominent bandit
022            algorithm. We prove our pretraining loss directly controls online decision-making
023            performance, and we demonstrate our framework on a news recommendation task
024            where we integrate end-to-end fine-tuning of a pretrained language model to pro-
025            cess news article headline text to improve performance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Settings with recurring interactions between humans and an AI decision-making system are becom-
031   ing increasingly prevalent. Some examples include systems that recommend to users what to buy,
032   and settings in digital health and online education that encourage people towards behaviors aligned
033
      with their goals. In these domains, there is a need for decision-making algorithms that can leverage
      neural networks. Although neural networks excel at making predictions based on intricate text and
034
      visual inputs, effective sequential decision-making requires the ability to comprehend uncertainty—
035
      to recognize when the model is unsure in its predictions and what additional information might
036   benefit future decision-making. Here neural networks often struggle (Gawlikowski et al., 2023).
037
038   In this work we focus on a particular problem setup that is stylized, but which we believe captures
039
      key challenges of real-world human-interactive decision-making problems. As depicted in Figure
      1 we consider a news recommendation problem where each day, a set of news articles is released.
040
      These released articles have no associated historical recommendation data that can be used to learn
041
      about those particular articles. However, a neural network model with a large language model (LLM)
042   component can be trained using historical recommendation data on articles from previous days to
043   predict how likely users are to click on a new article if recommended, based on the article’s text. At
044   the same time, the neural network’s predictions based on the LLM are not perfect, and it is important
045   for the algorithm to know how much to “trust” these predictions. Thus, the challenge is designing
046   a decision-making algorithm that is able to (i) incorporate the LLM’s initial prediction/beliefs, (ii)
047   sharpen its beliefs as it recommends that article throughout the day, and (iii) use the LLM’s belief to
048   make recommendation decisions in a way that balances exploration and exploitation.
049   We frame this problem as a meta-learning multi-armed bandit problem. Effective algorithms must
050   be able to learn from user interactions within each day, as well learn from historical recommendation
051   data collected from previously released news articles. This problem requires algorithms to grapple
052   with uncertainty: with more data we could more precisely learn the effectiveness of a particular
053   action. Is it worth taking that action to learn more? Thompson (posterior) sampling (Russo et al.,
      2020) manages this trade-off by taking actions based on samples from the posterior distribution over

083

      3     P OSTERIOR S AMPLING VIA AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION
136
      Classical Thompson Sampling (TS) samples from a posterior distribution over mean rewards for
137
      each action and selects the action with the highest sample (Russo et al., 2020). TS requires the
138   algorithm designer to (i) specify a meaningful belief (prior) over latent model parameters (e.g.,
139   unknown mean rewards for each action) and (ii) update that belief as the algorithm collects data.
140   Both (i) and (ii) can be significant challenges when using neural networks (Riquelme et al., 2018).
141
142
143
144
145
146
147
148
149
150   Figure 2: Missing data viewpoint. We view uncertainty about unobserved outcomes as the source
151   of uncertainty, avoiding explicit reference to latent parameters or variables. Calibrated generation
152   (imputation) of missing outcomes enables uncertainty quantification and decision-making.
153
      In this work we implement Thompson sampling (TS) without explicitly modeling latent parameters.
154   The impetus of our approach is that unobserved rewards are the source of the decision-maker’s un-
155   certainty (Figure 2): feedback on an article has only been gathered from a subset of users, and there
156   is residual uncertainty in how future users would respond. Inspired by this viewpoint, our method
157   proceeds in two steps. First, we pretrain an autoregressive sequence model to predict successive
158   rewards using historical data Dhist . Then, at decision time the algorithm uses imputed values of the
159   missing rewards (R̂’s) generated autoregressively from the pretrained sequence model to make de-
160   cisions. We show this procedure is an principled implementation of TS. Our main insight applies to
161   general autoregressive sequence models (e.g., transformers), but works well empirically even with
      simpler sequence model architectures (Section 6).

200   The connection between Bayesian inference and sequence modeling suggests that one could imple-
201   ment Thompson sampling in complex settings without closed form posteriors, if one had access to
202   the true posterior predictive distribution. This leads to several natural questions:
203
      1. How can we specify/learn good posterior predictive models when using neural networks?
204
         We learn approximate posterior predictive distributions using autoregressive sequence models
205      by training neural networks models to minimize a sequence prediction loss via gradient descent.
206
      2. What if the learned posterior predictive distribution is imperfect? What if it is not exactly
207
         exchangeable? How does that impact decision-making performance? In practice, it is diffi-
208      cult to ensure that neural network-based sequence models are exactly exchangeable. We prove
209      that the regret of an autoregressive generation version of Thompson sampling is controlled by
210      how well the pretrained sequence model minimizes (the expected analogue of) the sequence loss

      5   R ELATED W ORK
396
397   Meta-Learning in Bandits. There are a variety of meta-learning bandit algorithms (Wan et al.,
398   2023; Cella et al., 2020; Kveton et al., 2021; Bastani et al., 2022); these methods primarily focus on
399
      simpler settings (e.g. Gaussian or linear reward models). There are also deep meta-learning methods
      developed for recommendation systems and the cold-start problem (Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
400
      2021; Zheng et al., 2021). These works primarily focus on more complex recommendation settings
401
      (e.g. tracking one user over time) and not on uncertainty. In contrast, our goal is to showcase our
402   uncertainty quantification method for decision making with foundation models.
403
404   Reinforcement Learning (RL) with Pre-Trained Autoregressive Models. Many recent works in
405
      RL leverage sequence models that are pretrained on a large volume of data collected by an expert
      policy. Some use goal-conditioned sampling of actions to improve over average expert behavior
406
      (Janner et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2019); this works well in some settings but is
407
      provably sub-optimal others (Brandfonbrener et al., 2022; Malenica and Murphy, 2023). In contrast
408   to these works, we do not require data collected by expert policies for training.
409
410   Decision Pretrained Transformers (DPT) (Lee et al., 2023b) relate sampling from a sequence model
411
      that predicts the next expert action to Thompson sampling. Similar to PS-AR, DPT focuses on a
      meta-learning setting and pretrains their sequence models on historical data. However, while PS-
412
      AR sequence models are trained to predict future rewards, DPT sequence models are trained to
413
      predict the next expert action (if an expert action is not available, it is trained to mimic an approxi-
414   mate optimal policy fit from data). We have preliminary experiments comparing to DPT (Appendix
415   E.6) and find that it performs similarly to PS-AR; further investigation is needed to understand the
416   benefits of predicting future rewards versus expert actions.
417
      Autoregressive predictive models are also used in Liu et al. (2023), however they focus on non-
418
      stationary environments and settings with closed-form posterior distributions (like AR processes);
419
      they do not discuss incorporating deep learning models or characterizing model performance when
420   the sequence model is misspecified.
421
422   Thompson Sampling with Neural Networks. Several classes of approaches that have emerged to
423
      scale Thompson sampling to modern large scale decision-making problems with neural networks.
      The first class places a Bayesian prior on the weights of the neural network itself. These methods
424
      include those that form a Bayesian linear regression model from the last layer of a trained neural
425
      network (Riquelme et al., 2018; Snoek et al., 2015), as well as Bayesian neural networks (Zhang
426   et al., 2020). A second class of approaches involves forming using an ensemble of neural networks
427   to simulate samples from a posterior distribution (Osband et al., 2018; Lu and Van Roy, 2017; Qin
428   et al., 2022a). This class also includes algorithms that build on Epinets (Osband et al., 2024; Zhu and
429   Van Roy, 2023; Osband et al., 2023) and HyperModels (Dwaracherla et al., 2020; Li et al.), which
430   attempt to retain the performance of the ensembling with lower computational cost. Notably, Os-
431   band et al. (2024) uses sequence prediction loss to evaluate the quality of (“epistemic”) uncertainty
      quantification, inspiring our efforts to construct bandit algorithms using sequence models.

      7     D ISCUSSION
525
      We formulate a loss minimization problem that implicitly learns an informed prior using historical
526
      data, in order to model the posterior distribution of rewards for decision-making. This connection
527
      enables using modern ML tools to learn rich representations to comprehend uncertainty, in an ac-
528   tionable way. Our formulation introduces a fresh approach to the longstanding challenge of scaling
529   Thompson sampling to incorporate neural networks that incorporate unstructured inputs such as
530   images and text (Riquelme et al., 2018). The main ideas behind our algorithm generalize to contex-
531   tual settings where user-specific contexts Xt can be used to tailor recommendation decisions. We
532   describe generalizing PS-AR to this setting in Appendix B and leave a deeper dive to future work.
533
      Limitations. We assume articles are i.i.d. between pretraining and online evaluation, and user
534   outcomes for each action are exchangeable. Such assumptions may not be appropriate in practice,
535   e.g., if user preferences are nonstationary. In conducting this work, we struggled to find publicly
536   available datasets on which to evaluate our method, which led us to build our news recommendation
537   setting. Building public benchmarks for bandit problems that require using complex inputs (e.g. text
538   and/or images) for best performance is an important open direction. A limitation of this work is we
539   do not provide a thorough answer as to the quality of the historical data (e.g., amount of data and/or
      how data was collected) necessary to ensure learning good sequence models.
