009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph anomaly detection (GAD), which aims to identify nodes in a graph that sig-
012            nificantly deviate from normal patterns, plays a crucial role in broad application
013            domains. Existing GAD methods, whether supervised or unsupervised, are one-
014            model-for-one-dataset approaches, i.e., training a separate model for each graph
015            dataset. This limits their applicability in real-world scenarios where training on the
016
               target graph data is not possible due to issues like data privacy. To overcome this
               limitation, we propose a novel zero-shot generalist GAD approach UNPrompt
017
               that trains a one-for-all detection model, requiring the training of one GAD model
018
               on a single graph dataset and then effectively generalizing to detect anomalies in
019            other graph datasets without any retraining or fine-tuning. The key insight in UN-
020            Prompt is that i) the predictability of latent node attributes can serve as a general-
021            ized anomaly measure and ii) highly generalized normal and abnormal graph pat-
022            terns can be learned via latent node attribute prediction in a properly normalized
023            node attribute space. UNPrompt achieves generalist GAD through two main mod-
024            ules: one module aligns the dimensionality and semantics of node attributes across
025            different graphs via coordinate-wise normalization in a projected space, while an-
026            other module learns generalized neighborhood prompts that support the use of
027
               latent node attribute predictability as an anomaly score across different datasets.
               Extensive experiments on real-world GAD datasets show that UNPrompt signifi-
028
               cantly outperforms diverse competing methods under the generalist GAD setting,
029
               and it also has strong superiority under the one-model-for-one-dataset setting.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph anomaly detection (GAD) aims to identify anomalous nodes that exhibit significant deviations
035   from the majority of nodes in a graph. GAD has attracted extensive research attention in recent years
036   (Ma et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2021; Qiao et al., 2024) due to the board applications in various domains
037   such as spam review detection in online shopping networks (McAuley & Leskovec, 2013; Rayana &
038   Akoglu, 2015) and malicious user detection in social networks (Yang et al., 2019). To handle high-
039
      dimensional node attributes and complex structural relations between nodes, graph neural networks
      (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Wu et al., 2020) have been widely exploited for GAD due to their
040
      strong ability to integrate the node attributes and graph structures. These methods can be roughly
041
      divided into two categories, i.e., supervised and unsupervised methods. One category formulates
042   GAD as a binary classification problem and aims to capture anomaly patterns under the guidance of
043   labels (Tang et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2023). By contrast, due to the difficulty of
044   obtaining these class labels, another category of methods takes the unsupervised approach that aims
045   to learn normal graph patterns, e.g., via data reconstruction or other proxy learning tasks that are
046   related to GAD (Qiao & Pang, 2023; Liu et al., 2021b; Ding et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022).
047
      Despite their remarkable detection performance, these methods need to train a dataset-specific model
048   for each graph dataset for GAD. This one-model-for-one-dataset paradigm limits their applicability
049   in real-world scenarios since training a model from scratch incurs significant computation costs and
050   requires even a large amount of labeled data for supervised GAD methods (Liu et al., 2024; Qiao
051   et al., 2024). Training on a target graph may even not be possible due to data privacy protection
052   and regulation. To address this limitation, a new one-for-all anomaly detection (AD) paradigm,
053   called generalist anomaly detection (Zhu & Pang, 2024; Zhou et al., 2024), has been proposed for
      image AD with the emergence of foundation models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). This new

080   Compared to image AD, there are some unique challenges for learning generalist models for GAD.
081   First, unlike image data where raw features are in the same RGB space, the node attributes in graphs
082   from different applications and domains can differ significantly in node attribute dimensionality and
083   semantics. For example, as a shopping network dataset, Amazon contains the relationships between
084   users and reviews, and the node attribute dimensionality is 25. Differently, Facebook, a social
085   network dataset, describes relationships between users with 576-dimensional attributes. Second,
086   generalist AD models on image data rely on the superior generalizability learned in large visual-
087
      language models (VLMs) through pre-training on web-scale image-text-aligned data (Zhu & Pang,
      2024; Zhou et al., 2024), whereas there are no such foundation models for graph data (Liu et al.,
088
      2023a). Therefore, the key question here is: can we learn generalist models for GAD on graph data
089
      with heterogeneous node attributes and structure without the support of foundation models?
090
091   To address these challenges, we propose UNPrompt, a novel generalist GAD approach that learns
092   Unified Neighborhood Prompts on a single auxiliary graph dataset and then effectively generalizes
093
      to directly detect anomalies in other graph datasets under a zero-shot setting. The key insight in
      UNPrompt is that i) the predictability of latent node attributes can serve as a generalized anomaly
094
      measure and ii) highly generalized normal and abnormal graph patterns can be learned via latent
095
      node attribute prediction in a properly normalized node attribute space. UNPrompt achieves this
096   through two main modules including coordinate-wise normalization-based node attribute unifica-
097   tion and neighborhood prompt learning. The former module aligns the dimensionality of node
098   attributes across graphs and transforms the semantics into a common space via coordinate-wise nor-
099   malization, as shown in Figure 1(a). In this way, the diverse distributions of node attributes are
100   calibrated into the same semantic space. On the other hand, the latter module learns graph-agnostic
101   normal and abnormal patterns via a neighborhood-based latent attribute prediction task. Specifically,
102   we incorporate learnable prompts into the normalized attributes of the neighbors of a target node to
103   predict the latent attributes of the target node. Despite being trained on a small pre-trained GNN
104
      using a single graph, UNPrompt can effectively generalize to detect anomalous nodes in different
      unseen graphs without any re-training at the inference stage, as shown in Figure 1(b)-(d).
105

      method that exhibits effective zero-shot GAD performance across various graph datasets. There is a

118   2   R ELATED W ORK
119
120   Graph Anomaly Detection. Existing GAD methods can be roughly categorized into unsupervised
121   and supervised approaches (Ma et al., 2021; Qiao et al., 2024). The unsupervised methods are typ-
122   ically built using data reconstruction, self-supervised learning, and learnable graph anomaly mea-
123   sures (Qiao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022). The reconstruction-based approaches like DOMINANT
124   (Ding et al., 2019) and AnomalyDAE (Fan et al., 2020) aim to capture the normal patterns in the
125   graph, where the reconstruction error in both graph structure and attributes is utilized as the anomaly
126   score. CoLA (Liu et al., 2021b) and SL-GAD (Zheng et al., 2021) are representative self-supervised
127
      learning methods assuming that normality is reflected in the relationship between the target node and
      its contextual nodes. The graph anomaly measure methods typically leverage the graph structure-
128
      aware anomaly measures to learn intrinsic normal patterns for GAD, such as node affinity in TAM
129
      (Qiao & Pang, 2023). In contrast to the unsupervised approaches, the supervised anomaly detection
130   approaches have shown substantially better detection performance in recent years due to the incor-
131   poration of labeled anomaly data (Liu et al., 2021a; Chai et al., 2022). Most supervised methods
132   concentrate on the design of propagation mechanisms and spectral feature transformations to address
133   the notorious over-smoothing feature representation issues (Tang et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Chai
134   et al., 2022). Although both approaches can be adapted for zero-shot GAD by directly applying the
135   trained GAD models to the target datasets, they struggle to capture generalized normal and abnormal
136   patterns for GAD across different graph datasets. There are some studies working on cross-domain
137   GAD (Ding et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2023) that aim to transfer knowledge from a labeled graph
138
      dataset for GAD on a target dataset, but it is a fundamentally different problem from generalist GAD
      since cross-domain GAD requires the training on both source and target graph datasets.
139
140   Graph Prompt Learning. Prompt learning, initially developed in natural language processing,
141   seeks to adapt large-scale pre-trained models to different downstream tasks by incorporating learn-
142   able prompts while keeping the pre-trained models frozen (Liu et al., 2023b). Specifically, it designs
143   task-specific prompts capturing the knowledge of the corresponding tasks and enhances the compat-
144
      ibility between inputs and pre-trained models to enhance the pre-trained models in downstream
      tasks. Recently, prompt learning has been explored in graphs to unify multiple graph tasks (Sun
145
      et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c) or improve the transferability of graph models on the datasets across
146
      the different domains (Li et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024), e.g., by optimizing the prompts with labeled
147   data of various downstream tasks (Fang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c). Although being effective in
148   popular graph learning tasks like node classification and link prediction, they are inapplicable to
149   generalist GAD due to the unsupervised nature and/or irregular distributions of anomalies.
150
      Generalist Anomaly Detection. Generalist AD has been very recently emerging as a promising
151
      solution to tackle sample efficiency and model generalization problems in AD. There have been a
152
      few studies on non-graph data that have large pre-trained models to support the generalized pattern
153   learning, such as image generalist AD (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhu & Pang, 2024). However, it is a very
154   challenging task for data like graph data due to the lack of such pre-trained models. Recently a
155   concurrent approach, ARC (Liu et al., 2024), introduces an effective framework that leverages in-
156   context learning to achieve generalist GAD without relying on large pre-trained GNNs. Unlike ARC
157   which focuses on a few-shot GAD setting, i.e., requiring the availability of some labeled nodes in the
158   target testing graph dataset, we tackle a zero-shot GAD setting assuming no access to any labeled
159   data during inference stages.
160
161   Inductive Graph Learning. Similar to generalist setting, inductive graph learning (Hamilton
      et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023) also focuses


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      on inference on unseen graph data. However, these methods are not applicable to the generalist
163   setting. Specifically, inductive graph learning trains the model on partial data of the whole graph
164   dataset Hamilton et al. (2017); Ding et al. (2019); Li et al. (2023b) or the previously observed data of
165   dynamic graphs (Fang et al., 2023). Then, the learned model is evaluated on the unseen data of the
166   whole dataset or the future graph. These unseen testing data are from the same source of the training
167   data with the same dimensionality and semantics. In contrast, the unseen data in our method are
168   from different distributions/domains with significantly different dimensionality and semantics. This
169   cross-dataset nature, specifically referred to as a zero-shot problem (Jeong et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
170   2024), makes our setting significantly different from the current inductive graph learning setting.

174   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
175
176   Notations. Let G = (V, E) be an attributed graph with N nodes, where V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN }
177
      represents the node set and E is the edge set. The attributes of nodes can be denoted as X =
      [x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ]T ∈ RN ×d and the edges between nodes can be presented by an adjacency matrix
178
      A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N with Aij = 1 if there is an edge between vi and vj and Aij = 0 otherwise. For
179
      simplicity, the graph can be represented as G = (A, X). In GAD, the node set can be divided into a
180   set of the normal nodes Vn and a set of anomalous nodes Va . Typically, the number of normal nodes
181   is significantly larger than the anomalous nodes, i.e., |Vn | ≫ |Va |. Moreover, the anomaly labels
182   can be denoted as y ∈ {0, 1}N with yi = 1 if vi ∈ Va and yi = 0 otherwise.
183
184   Conventional GAD. Conventional GAD typically focuses on model training and anomaly detec-
185   tion on the same graph. Specifically, given a graph G, an anomaly scoring model f : G → R is
186   optimized on G in a supervised or unsupervised manner. Then, the model is used to detect anoma-
187   lies within the same graph. The model is expected to generate higher anomaly scores for abnormal
188   nodes than normal nodes, i.e., f (vi ) < f (vj ) if vi ∈ Vn and vj ∈ Va .
189
190   Generalist GAD. Generalist GAD aims to learn a generalist model f on a single training graph
191
      so that f can be directly adapted to different target graphs across diverse domains without any fine-
      tuning or re-training. More specifically, the model is optimized on Gtrain with the corresponding
192
      anomaly labels ytrain . After model optimization, the learned f is utilized to detect anomalies within
193                                                    (1)      (n)
      different unseen target graphs Ttest = {Gtest , . . . , Gtest } which has heterogeneous attributes and/or
194
      structure to Gtrain , i.e., Gtrain ∩ Ttest = ∅. Depending on whether labeled nodes of the target graph are
195
      provided during inference, the generalist GAD problem can be further divided into two categories,
196   i.e., few-shot and zero-shot settings. We focus on the zero-shot setting where the generalist models
197   cannot get access to any labeled data of the testing graphs during both training and inference.

199   3.2   OVERVIEW OF T HE P ROPOSED A PPROACH – UNP ROMPT
200
201   The framework is illustrated in Figure 2, which consists of two main modules, coordinate-wise
202   normalization-based node attribute unification and neighborhood prompt learning. For all graphs,
203   the node attribute unification aligns the dimensionality of node attributes and transforms the se-
204   mantics into a common space via coordinate-wise normalization in a projected space. Then, in the
205
      normalized space, the generalized latent attribute prediction task is performed with the neighborhood
      prompts to learn generalized GAD patterns at the training stage. In this prompt learning module,
206
      UNPrompt aims to maximize the predictability of the latent attributes of normal nodes while mini-
207
      mizing those of abnormal nodes. In this paper, we evaluate the predictability via the similarity. In
208   doing so, the graph-agnostic normal and abnormal patterns are incorporated into the prompts. Dur-
209   ing inference, the target graph is directly fed into the learned models after node attribute unification
210   without any re-training or labeled nodes of the graph. For each node, the predictability of latent
211   node attributes is directly used as the normal score for final anomaly detection.

267   must capture the generalized normal and abnormal patterns across graphs. Otherwise, the model
268   would overfit the dataset-specific knowledge of the training graph which can be very different from
269   that in target graphs. In this paper, we reveal that the predictability of latent node attributes can

      patterns can be learned via latent node attribute prediction in the normalized node attribute space
271   with the neighborhood prompts. The key intuition of this anomaly measure is that normal nodes
272   tend to have more connections with normal nodes of similar attributes due to prevalent graph ho-
273   mophily relations, resulting in a more homogeneous neighborhood in the normal nodes (Qiao &
274   Pang, 2023); by contrast, the presence of anomalous connections and/or attributes makes abnormal
275   nodes deviate significantly from their neighbors. Therefore, for a target node, its latent attributes
276   (i.e., node embedding) is more predictable based on the latent attributes of its neighbors if the node
277   is normal node, compared to abnormal nodes. The neighborhood-based latent attribute prediction is
278   thus used to measure the normality for GAD. As shown in our experiments (see Figures 1(b)-(d) and
279

403   Implementation Details. To ensure a fair comparison, the common dimensionality is set to eight
404   to unify the node attribute across graphs for all methods, and SVD is used for feature projection. The
405   number of layers in GNNs is set to one and the number of hidden units is 128. The transformation
406   layer is also implemented via a one-layer MLP with the same number of hidden units. The size
407   of the neighborhood prompt is set to one by default. For all baselines, we adopt their official code
408
      and follow the recommended optimization and hyperparameter settings to conduct the experiments.
      UNPrompt and all its competing methods are trained on Facebook and then directly tested on the
409
      other six GAD datasets without any further training or additional knowledge of the target graphs.

      Main Results. The AUROC and AUPRC results of all methods are presented in Table 1. From
412
      the table, we can have the following observations. (1) Under the proposed generalist GAD scenario
413   where a model is trained on a single dataset and evaluated on six other datasets, all the compet-
414   ing baselines fail to work well, demonstrating that it is very challenging to build a generalist GAD
415   model that generalizes across different datasets under zero-shot setting. (2) For supervised methods,
416   the simple GCN achieves better performance than the specially designed GAD GNNs. This can be
417   attributed to more dataset-specific knowledge being captured in these specialized GAD models, lim-
418   iting their generalization capacity to the unseen testing graphs. (3) Unsupervised methods perform
419   more stable than supervised methods across the target graphs and generally outperform supervised
420   ones. This is because the unsupervised objectives are closer to the shared anomaly patterns across
421
      graphs compared to the supervised ones, especially for TAM which employs a fairly generalized
      local affinity-based objective to train the model. (4) The proposed method UNPrompt demon-
422
      strates strong and stable generalist GAD capacity across graphs from different distributions and
423
      domains. Specifically, UNPrompt achieves the best AUROC performance on 5 out of 6 datasets and
424   the average performance outperforms the best-competing method by over 9%. In terms of AUPRC,
425   UNPrompt outperforms all baselines on 4 out of 6 datasets and also achieves the best average per-
426   formance. The superiority of UNPrompt is attributed to the fact that i) the proposed coordinate-wise
427   normalization effectively aligns the features across graphs, and ii) the shared generalized normal
428   and abnormal patterns are well captured in the neighborhood prompts.

528   5      C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose a novel zero-shot generalist GAD method, UNPrompt, that trains one
531   detector on a single dataset and can effectively generalize to other unseen target graphs without any
532   further re-training or labeled nodes of target graphs during inference. The attribute inconsistency and
533   the absence of generalized anomaly patterns are the main obstacles for generalist GAD. To address
534   these issues, two main modules are proposed, i.e., coordinate-wise normalization-based attribute
535   unification and neighborhood prompt learning. The first module aligns node attribute dimensionality
536
      and semantics, while the second module captures generalized normal and abnormal patterns via the
      neighborhood-based latent node attribute prediction. Extensive experiments on various real-world
537
      GAD datasets from different distributions and domains demonstrate the effectiveness of UNPrompt
538
      for generalist GAD. Besides, the experiments conducted on the unsupervised GAD with UNPrompt
539   further support the rationality of the learned anomaly patterns in the generalist model.

      To demonstrate the wide applicability of the proposed method UNPrompt, we further perform unsu-
867   pervised GAD with UNPrompt which focuses on detecting anomalous nodes within one graph and
868   does not have access to any node labels during training. Specifically, we adopt the same pipeline
869   in the generalist GAD setting, i.e., graph contrastive pertaining and neighborhood prompt learning.
870   Since we focus on anomaly detection on each graph separately, the node attribute unification is dis-
871   carded for unsupervised GAD. However, the absence of node labels poses a challenge to learning
872   meaningful neighborhood prompts for anomaly detection. To overcome this issue, we propose to
873   utilize the pseudo-labeling technique to guide the prompt learning. Specifically, the normal score
874   of each node is calculated by the neighborhood-based latent attribute predictability after the graph
875
      contrastive learning process:

880   learning neighborhood prompts. To achieve this, the normal score si is transformed into the loss
881
      weight wi = Sigmoid(α(si − t)) where t is a threshold and α is the scaling parameter. In this way,

942   Table 5. The more detailed description of each dataset is given as follows
943
             • Facebook (Xu et al., 2022). Facebook is a social network where each node represents a

946            in (Ding et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021b).

             • Reddit (Kumar et al., 2019). Reddit is a forum-based network derived from the social
948
               media platform Reddit, where nodes represent users, and the embeddings of post texts serve
949
               as attributes. Users who have been banned from the platform are labeled as anomalies.
950
951
             • Weibo (Kumar et al., 2019). Weibo is a social network and their associated hashtags are
               obtained from the Tencent Weibo platform. Users who engaged in at least five of these
952
               activities are labeled as anomalies while the others are classified as normal samples. Sus-
953
               picious activities are defined as two posts made within a specific timeframe, such as 60
954            seconds. The attributes of each node include the location of a micro-blog post and bag-of-
955            words features.
956
             • Amazon (McAuley & Leskovec, 2013). Amazon is a graph dataset that captures the re-
957
               lations between users and product reviews. There are 25 handcrafted features used as the
958            node attribute (Zhang et al., 2020). The users with more than 80% helpful votes are labeled
959            as normal entities and users with less than 20% helpful votes as anomalies. Amazon is con-
960            structed by extracting the Amazon-UPU dataset that connects the users who give reviews
961            to at least one common product.
962          • YelpChi (Rayana & Akoglu, 2015). YelpChi includes hotel and restaurant reviews filtered
963            (spam) and recommended (legitimate) by Yelp. There are 32 handcrafted features used as
964            node attributes (Rayana & Akoglu, 2015). The users with more than 80% helpful votes are
965            labeled as benign entities and users with less than 20% helpful votes as fraudulent entities.
966            The YelpChi is constructed by extracting YelpChi-RUR which connects reviews posted by
967            the same user.
968          • Amazon-all (McAuley & Leskovec, 2013). Amazon-all includes three types of relations:
969            U-P-U (users reviewing at least one same product), U-S-U (users giving at least one same
970            star rating within one week), and U-V-U (users with top-5% mutual review similarities).
971            Amazon-all is formed by treating the different relations as a single relation following Chen

       H    I NDUCTIVE L EARNING VS . Z ERO - SHOT G ENERALIST L EARNING
1160
1161
       Despite our method and inductive graph learning (Hamilton et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2018) are both
1162
       focused on evaluating the learned models on unseen graph data during inference, there are funda-
1163   mental differences between our zero-shot learning and the inductive graph learning. We clarify the
1164   differences as follows:
1165
1166         • For inductive graph learning, the training dataset and testing dataset come from the same
1167           graph source. For example, for the graph classification task in Xu et al. (2018), 20 graphs
1168           of protein-protein interaction (PPI) datasets are used for training, and 2 other graphs are
1169           used for testing. These graphs both belong to the same protein-protein interaction graph
1170           dataset with the same attribute distribution and semantics. Therefore, the learned model
1171
               can be easily generalized to the test graphs.
1172         • In our zero-shot setting, the training dataset and testing dataset are from different domains
1173           and distributions. They differ in the dimensionality of node attributes and graph semantics.
1174           For example, as a shopping network dataset, Amazon contains the relationships between
1175
               users and reviews, and the node attribute dimensionality is 25. Differently, Facebook, a so-
               cial network dataset, describes relationships between users with 576-dimensional attributes.
1176
               This is one fundamental difference between the inductive setting and our zero-shot setting.
1177
               Moreover, our zero-shot setting requires the learned models to be directly applied to other
1178           graphs from different domains without any further tuning/training or labeled nodes of the
1179           target graphs. This requires the learned model to capture the more generalized patterns
1180           for anomaly detection based on only one training graph, resulting in a task being more
1181           challenging than the mentioned inductive learning.
1182         • There are also studies (Ding et al., 2021a) on inductive graph anomaly detection, but the
1183           problem setting is also fundamentally different from our setting. In particular, to allow the
1184           evaluation of inductive detection, Ding et al. (2021a) samples nodes from the same graph
1185           to construct two graph datasets, with one graph used for training and another used for
1186           testing, leading to the fact that the training and test datasets are essentially from the same
1187           distribution. This is fundamentally different from our settings, where training and testing
               datasets are separately from highly heterogeneous distributions and domains.
