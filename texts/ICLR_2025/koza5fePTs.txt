009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Classical and natural language planning tasks remain a difficult domain for modern
012            large language models (LLMs). In this work, we lay the foundations for improving
013            planning capabilities of LLMs. First, we construct a comprehensive benchmark
014            suite encompassing both classical planning benchmarks and natural language
015            scenarios. This suite includes algorithms to methodically generate instances of tasks
016            with varying levels of difficulty, allowing for rigorous and systematic evaluation of
017            LLM performance. Next, we investigate the use of many-shot in-context learning
018
               to enhance LLM planning, exploring the relationship between increased context
               length and improved planning performance. In addition, we demonstrate the
019
               positive impact of fine-tuning LLMs on optimal planning paths. We also probe
020
               the efficacy of chain-of-thought reasoning methods to improve LLM planning
021            performance. Moreover, we probe the performance of the proposed methods in
022            out-of-distribution scenarios, assessing the ability to generalize to novel and unseen
023            planning challenges. Finally, we investigate model’s failure modes and reveal
024            insights that hold true across different benchmarks.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Intelligent agents require the ability to plan to proactively chart a course of action to achieve their
030   objectives. This capacity for strategic foresight is considered fundamental to intelligent behav-
031   ior (Russell & Norvig, 2016). While classical search algorithms have long been the cornerstone of
032   planning studies, machine learning techniques, particularly Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and
033
      reinforcement learning, have emerged as useful additions, significantly expanding the capabilities of
      modern planning systems.
034
035   With the advent of powerful large language models (LLMs), there are new opportunities to both
036   revisit classical planning problems, and to further explore new problems through natural language
037   specification that reflects the ambiguity and uncertainty of real-world domains. Planning capability
038   is important for many tasks such as game playing, meeting scheduling and trip planning. Research
039
      is already underway to leverage the commonsense knowledge of LLMs in real-world tasks (Huang
      et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023) and to generate sensible plans (Valmeekam et al.,
040
      2023; Hao et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2024). This research has shed some light on LLMs’ struggle with
041
      planning tasks. Even state-of-the-art LLMs may produce ineffective or even incorrect plans, even in
042   straightforward scenarios. Our paper focuses on analyzing and improving the planning capability of
043   LLM systems.
044
045
      We provide a scalable benchmark suite in both PDDL and natural language to measure planning
      capability of LLMs. Specifically, we explore two distinct planning representations: the formal
046
      Planning Domain Definition Language (PDDL) (McDermott et al., 1998), which provides a stan-
047
      dardized representation for classical planning problems and allows for rigorous plan validation; and
048   natural language, which offers a more flexible and intuitive representation better reflecting real-world
049   scenarios. For both scenarios, we provide a code for generating as many instances with a degree of
050   difficulty of choice. We also provide a mapping method for translating PDDL benchmarks to natural
051   language and measure the performance of the generated benchmarks. The generated planning tasks
052   are scalable and can grow to examine and assist stronger models. Figure 1 depicts a simple sample
053   of BlocksWorld benchmark with description both in PDDL and natural language. Each instance in
      BlocksWorld consists of a set of blocks, a table and a robot hand, where the goal is to move from

072

073

074   using ICL leads to a significant boost in planning performance, which can be further improved by

076   thought, debate-as-reasoning) allow smaller models to perform closer to SoTA frontier models in the
077   natural language task domain. Our results show that fine-tuning with the optimal plan can lead to
078   near-perfect accuracy, even when using relatively small models.
079
      Next, we investigate both in-domain and out-of-domain generalization and note that the proposed
080   plans demonstrate excellent in-domain generalization: instances with similar complexity are solved
081   with similar levels of accuracy. For ICL, prompting with easier instances leads to better performance
082   on hard instances compared to prompting with hard instances. Fine-tuning leads to better performance
083   for both in-domain and out-of-domain scenarios, even with a much smaller model, compared to ICL
084   with long-context.
085
      Lastly, we probe the failure modes of the model. We categorize the failure modes into three categories:
086   failure to satisfy environmental constraints, failure to meet the goal and failure to generate legal
087   actions in a given state. We note that not all of these failure modes are present across all benchmarks
088   and methods. Moreover, as the instance complexity increases the model success rate decreases.
089   Additionally, we pinpoint failure modes that are result of biases in training data emphasizing the
090   importance of data curation during training.

      1.1     R ELATED W ORK
094
095
096   Prior works investigations the planning capabilities of LLMs, have found that these models struggle
097   to solve planning tasks (Hao et al., 2023; Valmeekam et al., 2023; 2024; Kambhampati et al., 2024),
098
      In contrast, we show that LLMs can be capable of solving such tasks and one can reach near-perfect
      accuracy for some scenarios, with certain methods. We run experiments on the same BlocksWorld
099
      benchmark as these prior works, and generate additional difficult cases (i.e., adding more blocks).
100
      Similar to these works, we also use PDDL verifiers to compute accuracy. See Section 3.4 for details.
101
102   Xie et al. (2024) proposed a TravelPlanner benchmark and showed GPT4-Turbo can solve some of
103   the benchmark tasks with a success rate of 0.6%. The TripPlanner benchmark used in our work has
104
      two main differences: it is not an agent-based environment and rather a natural language benchmark,
      and it has unique answers due to carefully designed constraints.
105
106   Stechly et al. (2024) suggests that LLMs are not capable of generalizing their plans on BlocksWorld
107   if one uses chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022). In this work we show positive results in terms
      of generalization performance.

      There is another line of work that uses a hybrid approach, meaning that they either use an external
109   tool to solve the planning tasks (Kambhampati et al., 2024; Hirsch et al., 2024), or reformulate the
110   problem as another task such as SMT (Hao et al., 2024) and use an external tool to solve it. Lehnert
111   et al. (2024) use A∗ as search mechanism and a specific transformer architecture to achieve planning
112   capability for that specific architecture. We differ from this line of work in that we focus on teaching
113   LLM itself to perform the planning task.
114
      In addition to standard few-shot prompting, we also make use of inference time techniques that
115   construct a chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022), namely tree-of-thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2023) and
116   Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Hao et al., 2023) and debate-as-reasoning (Du et al., 2023) We
117   demonstrate that these methods can considerably improve an LLMs planning capabilities such that
118   smaller open-source models that use these approaches can outperform larger foundational models.

      2       B ENCHMARK
122
      To evaluate the planning capabilities of LLMs, we assemble a benchmark suite that appropriately
123
      represents various classical and natural-language planning tasks. On these benchmarks, we assess
124
      LLM performance using In-Context Learning (ICL), Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), and chain-of-
125   thought (CoT) methods for planning.
126
127   PDDL (Planning Domain Definition Language): PDDL McDermott et al. (1998) is a standardized
128
      language used in artificial intelligence for representing planning problems. PDDL provides a formal
      way to describe the initial state of an environment, a goals, the space of valid actions, and the state-
129
      transition properties of actions in the environment. PDDL has two main components (1) Domain:
130
      Describes the general characteristics of the planning problem, including the types of objects, actions,
131   and predicates (conditions that can be true or false). (2) Problem: Defines a specific instance of
132   the planning problem within the domain, including the initial state of the world and the goals to be
133   achieved.
134
      We select three datasets that use PDDL, generated additional subsets of them, and additionally
135
      map these datasets to natural language for an additional evaluation task. Additionally, we select two
136
      native natural language datasets, containing Trip Planning and Calendar Scheduling tasks (Zheng
137   et al., 2024). For the PDDL-based datasets, we select BlocksWorld, Logistics, and Mini-Grid. We
138   then translated the PDDL problem descriptions from these datasets into natural language to compare
139   performance when using formal and informal representations.
140
141   2.1      PDDL B ENCHMARKS
142
143   The creation of all PDDL datasets follows a three-step procedure. (1) Initially, the process involves
144   the creation of an initial state and a goal (target state). (2) Subsequently, the initial state and goal are
145   utilized to formulate a problem in PDDL. (3) Finally, the problem is solved using a classic planner
146   Fast-Downward1 .
147   This procedure is repeated with increasingly difficult configurations for a select number of problems.
148   The result of this procedure are additional datasets that comprise a set of PDDL problems and solutions
149   of various difficulty. Importantly, this procedure enables us to create datasets with increasingly
150   difficult problems and any number of samples, which are appropriate for assessing the ability to plan
151   using different methods, such as in-context learning versus Supervised Fine-Tuning. Moreover, we
152   can scale the dataset generation and create as many instances as needed for different investigations.
153
      We provide the details of our benchmark suite below.
154   We perform planning for BlocksWorld, Logistics and Minigrid both for PDDL and Natural Language.
155   For the mapping to natural language, we use a slot filling technique which maps each predicate of
156   the initialization and goal as well the action to sentences (Appendix B.2). For the verification of the
157   plans, we use regular expressions to map the plan in Natural Language back to PDDL.
158   BlocksWorld: BlocksWorld is a standard planning problem from International Planning Conference
159   (IPC)-2000 2 . This domain consists of a set of blocks, a table and a robot hand, where the goal is to

      Logistics: Logistics is an AI planning problem from IPC-1998 3 expressed in PDDL that involves

      capacities and locations, showcasing model’s ability to manage multi-step logistics efficiently.

170   Mini-Grid: Mini-Grid is a task from Artificial Intelligence Planning Systems (AIPS)-1998 4 , also

      key shapes. The goal then is for a robot to navigate from an initial position to a designated goal cell.

174   2.2      NATIVE NATURAL L ANGUAGE P LANNING B ENCHMARKS
175
      Trip Planning: Trip Planning is a task from NaturalPlan (Zheng et al., 2024) benchmark focusing
176
      on planning a trip itinerary under given constraints. The goal of the task is to find an itinerary
177   satisfying constraints such as the order of visiting N cities. It includes enough constraints for each
178   instance such that there is only one solution to the task, which makes the evaluation of the predictions
179   straightforward.
180
      Calendar Scheduling: Calendar Scheduling from the NaturalPlan (Zheng et al., 2024) benchmark
181
      represents the task of scheduling a meeting of either 30 minutes or an hour among up to 7 attendees.
182
      The attendees may have a busy schedule or a light schedule with less than half of the working hours
183   spent in meetings.

186   3       E XPERIMENTS
187
188   Previous works demonstrated that, without intervention, LLMs often struggle with even simple
189   planning tasks (Hao et al., 2023; Valmeekam et al., 2023; 2024; Kambhampati et al., 2024). LLMs
190
      often lack the information on how to structure their plan constructively, and struggle to plan around
      the enumerated constraints. In this section we present our experimental results for the interventions
191
      we investigate, demonstrating that they lead to significant improvements to the LLMs planning
192
      capability. We also investigate plan generalization (i.e., the ability to generalize to unseen instances)
193   in several scenarios.
194
195   For PDDL experiments, we measure accuracy of the generated plan with a verifier (Fox & Long).
196
      For natural language experiments we rely on either recasting the task to PDDL and verifying with
      a verifier, or extracting the answer and comparing it to expected results (Zheng et al., 2024). In all
197
      experiments, GPT-4 refers to GPT-4 Turbo and we may omit "Turbo" for space constraints, also if we
198
      do not mention Pro or Flash Gemini 1.5 refers to Gemini 1.5 Pro.

      3.1      I N - CONTEXT LEARNING
201
202   For in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020), we adhere to the standard procedure, employing a prompt
203   containing several examples for the task. Each example comprises a planning problem statement and
204   its corresponding solution, referred to as a shot. Following the examples, the test problem is added
205   without the corresponding solution. Subsequently, an LLM receives this prompt and is expected to
206   generate a plan following the format and logic of the examples in the prompt. See Appendix A for
207   examples of prompts.
208

      3.3    P LAN GENERALIZATION
360
361   For any LLM application, the question of how well the method generalizes to out-of-training-
362   distribution (OOD) inputs is always present. Here, we investigate how LLM planning generalizes.
363   Plan generalization has three main categories: (1) Generalize to unseen instances of the same environ-
364   ment (2) Generalize to renaming of actions and objects (3) Generalize to unseen plan environments.
365   We focus on the first category which sits at the core of the desired capability.
366   For these generalization experiments, we consider BlocksWorld and Logistics benchmarks with
367   various difficulty levels (as described in Section 3.2). We look into performance of both SFT and
368   ICL approaches. Tables 2 shows generalization performance for BlocksWorld in SFT setting for
369   BlocksWorld 3-7, 8-20 split and Table 3 considers generalization between BlocksWorld 3-7 and 8-9
370   blocks split for both SFT and ICL with different models and number of shots. Table 4 depicts plan
371   generalization for Logistics benchmark for splits of 1-2 and 3-5 packets.
372   Our analysis reveals several key findings: (1) Superiority of SFT: SFT consistently outperforms ICL
373   across both benchmarks, even when utilizing a smaller model for SFT. This suggests that SFT’s
374   explicit training process, focused on the specific task, leads to more effective learning and better
375   generalization. (2) In most ICL scenarios, training the model on easier instances first results in
376   improved performance on harder examples, e.g., see Table 3, rows 1-4 and 6. (3) Limitations of hard
377   example training: Contrary to some expectations, training the model exclusively on hard examples
      does not always translate to better performance on easier ones (for example, see Table 3 rows 2, 4-6).

      4   I NVESTIGATING FAILURE C ASES
450
451
      In this section, we analyze the outcome of various approaches to improving LLM planning perfor-
      mance, and dive into a more detailed categorization of failure modes.
452
453   The LLM planning failure modes we observed can be classified into the following three categories:
454
      (1) Constraint Violation: The model fails to satisfy one of the explicitly declared environment
455   constraints. i.e., it ignores one of the conditions required to be able to do an action. Even if this
456   happen only for one instance of an action and not for all instances of the same action. For example
457   model wants to put block b1 on block b2 while block b2 is not empty.
458
      (2) Failure to meet the goal: The model makes a plan that meets the environment constraints but does
459
      not reach the goal state at the end of the trajectory.
460
461   (3) Out-of-vocabulary actions: The model proposes actions which are not in the environment’s action
462   space.
463   PDDL benchmarks Here we examine the failure modes for both the Blocksworld and Logistics
464   domains and SFT, ICL setting. Due to space constraints we report Blocksworld SFT settings here
465   and refer the reader to Appendix C for the rest of plots and analysis. First we look at the effect
466   of distribution of number of blocks in the planning instance. We compare Figure 5a that includes
467   successful eval cases of BlocksWorld 8-20 blocks to, to all its eval data in Figure 5b respectively.
468   We note that the model failure cases increases as the number of blocks in the problem increases.
469   We also separate the reasons of failure in BlocksWorld 8-20 instances as seen in Figure 5c and
470
      Figure 5d respectively.Note that failure mode (3) does not happen for BlocksWorld benchmark in
      SFT or ICL settings and for Logistics in ICL setting, but for Logistics benchmark in SFT settings all
471
      three categories are present (see Appendix C for details).
472
473   Next, We study the distribution of number of blocks in out-of-domain cases focusing on BlocksWorld
474   3-7 to 8-20 blocks generalization. Comparing the successful cases in Figure 6a to all the eval samples
475   in Figure 5b, we observe that the generalization performance of the model that is trained on 3-7
476
      blocks drops as the number of eval blocks increases from 8 to 20.
477   To probe the models even further, we look into the category where the generated plan fails to satisfy
478   one of the environment constraints. We study the step at which the plan fails to meet the constraint.
479   Considering Figure 6b and comparing it to Figure 6c, the model seems to have trouble generalizing
480   from the beginning, having the majority of its failures concentrated on earlier steps. This trend stays
481   true for Logistics benchmark as well and is due to the fact that models go deep before they go wide
482
      when they want to produce the plan output. In other terms, this gives the intuition that the model
      seems to get overwhelmed when encountering difficult unseen examples, leading to failure from the
483
      beginning by not satisfying the constraints.
484

      that the model has learned a correlation between the step number and the action it chooses, leading

      5     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   We examined planning capabilities of LLMs through benchmark development, generalization assess-
531
      ment and analysis of failure modes. Our observations for ICL setting has implications for the future
      development and training of LLMs, potentially informing strategies to enhance their capacity to
532
      process and leverage extended contextual information. It also points to the opportunity for followup
533
      work investigating ways to further improve the inference time reasoning procedures, and their scaling
534   properties. Our investigation of plan generalization reveals three key findings: superiority of SFT,
535   curriculum learning effectiveness and limitations of hard example training; suggesting that a balanced
536   approach, incorporating both easy and hard examples, might be optimal for achieving well-rounded
537   performance. Our analysis of failure modes point to future work on dataset design and reasoning
538   procedures to directly address the discussed failure modes. These enhancements can unlock new
539   levels of versatility and robustness in LLM-based planning systems, paving the way for their broader
      adoption in real-world applications.

       B.1       DATASET C REATION
1029
1030   In these experiments BlocksWorld dataset for 3 to 7 blocks consists of 40000 samples.
1031   In the creation of the BlocksWorld dataset as outlined in Algorithm 1, the key parameters include the maximum
1032   number of blocks num_blocks and the quantity of examples n to be generated for each block count. Here, the
1033   maximum number of blocks is a number greater than 3. As we use uniform sampling, this results in a linear
1034   increase in the number of more complex examples. However, it’s important to note that as the number of blocks
       increases, the simpler combinations are exhausted since all possible combinations might be included. The
1035
       methods CreateStacks generates random stacks of blocks, iteratively sampling from the available blocks to
1036   determine stack heights until all blocks are utilized. The method CreatePro denotes a simple method to translate
1037   the block configuration into PDDL which is python reimplementation of functionality in 4ops-Blockworld
1038   code}5 .
1039
1040
       Algorithm 1 Create BlocksWorld Dataset
1041       function C REATE DATASET BW(num_blocks, n)
1042          dataset ← []                                                                    . Initialize an empty list
              for problem_id ← 1 to n do
1043
                  b ← R ANDOM U NIFORM(3, num_blocks)
1044              initStacks ← C REATE S TACKS(b)
1045              goalStacks ← C REATE S TACKS(b)
1046              if initStacks == goalStacks then
1047                  continue                                                                     . Skip equal stacks.
                  end if
1048
                  problem ←C REATE P RO(initStacks, goalStacks)
1049              plan ←FAST D OWNWARD(problem, domain)
1050              dataset ← dataset + [(problem, plan)]
1051          end for
1052          return dataset
           end function
1053
1054   Algorithm 1, we generate 28k unique samples. From these, we randomly select 25500 of the for training set and
1055            2500 for validation set. This procedure yields a problem distribution as shown in Figure 8.
