009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the significant advancements made by Diffusion Transformer (DiT)-based
012            methods in video generation, there remains a notable gap with camera-pose per-
013            spectives. Existing works such as OpenSora do not adhere precisely to antic-
014            ipated trajectories, thereby limiting the utility in downstream applications such
015            as content creation. Therefore, we introduce a novelty approach that achieves
016            fine-grained control by embedding sparse camera-pose information into the tem-
017            poral self-attention layers. We employ LoRA to minimize the impact on the orig-
018
               inal attention layer parameters during fine-tuning and enhance the supervision of
               camera-pose in the loss function. After fine-tuning the OpenSora’s ST-DiT frame-
019
               work on the RealEstate10K dataset, experiments demonstrate that our method
020
               outperforms LDM-based methods for long video generation, while maintaining
021            optimal performance in trajectory consistency and object consistency.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The rapid evolution on video generation has been marked by the rise of DiT method Peebles &
027   Xie (2023), which is well-suited for generating long video sequences. Despite these advances, DiT
028   models often struggle with controllability, particularly in the precise control of camera movements,
029   which is essential for many creative applications.
030
      The recent video generation methods such as AnimateDiff Guo et al. (2023), Lumiere Bar-Tal et al.
031   (2024), and SVD Blattmann et al. (2023a) have advanced from text-to-image (T2I) to text-to-video
032   (T2V) domains by modifying the U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015). Currently, the guidance by cam-
033   era motion and object motion information, like MotionCtrl Wang et al. (2024b) and CameraCtrl He
034   et al. (2024), takes more possibility to video content creation. However, these methods are mainly
035   constrained by the Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022), which imposes strict
036   limitations on the latent space, resulting in videos generated by U-Net fail to adjust resolution and
037   duration. With the release of Sora Brooks et al. (2024) earlier this year, researchers start to focus on
038   DiT-based methods. Recent works such as Kling, OpenSora Zheng et al. (2024), and Open-Sora-
039
      Plan Lab & etc. (2024) have conducted extensive explorations on 3D-VAE and spatial-temporal DiT
      (ST-DiT). These methods have achieved promising results in the T2V task. For applications involv-
040
      ing motion manipulation, Tora Zhang et al. (2024b) has implemented the extraction of trajectory data
041
      into motion-guided fusion. However, there is currently no effective solution for the enhancement of
042   controllable video generation with camera-pose sequences.
043
044   Therefore, we propose a camera-pose controllable method for DiT-based video generation (CCM-
045
      DiT), which effectively embeds camera-pose sequences into DiT and generates videos according to
      the corresponding camera-pose sequence. Our method utilizes the OpenSora-v1.2 framework and
046
      extracts inter-frame motion sequences from reference videos in camera perspectives. First, each
047
      frame is annotated with a 12-dimension motion matrix, including a 3 × 3 rotation matrix and a
048   3 × 1 translation matrix. Effectively capturing the precision of camera-pose remains a challenge.
049   We propose the Sparse Motion Encoding Module for converting a pixel-wise motion field based on
050   Plücker coordinates into a sparse motion field. Second, compared to the U-Net, the DiT framework
051   compresses the temporal dimension to reduce VRAM usage, making it difficult to align frame-based
052   motion information with the temporal attention layer, thus complicating the embedding of camera-
053   pose motion. Inspired by Tora, we train a VAE Kingma (2013) for the latent space of camera-pose
      sequences, improving its alignment with the temporal attention layer.

086
               function related to camera-pose.
087          • Our method achieves SOTA during long video generation with camera-pose sequences.

091   2.1   V IDEO G ENERATION
092
093   With diffusion models being proven as an effective method for creating high-quality images, re-
094   search on dynamic video generation has gradually emerged. Make-a-video Singer et al. (2022)
095   and MagicVideo Zhou et al. (2022) use 3D U-Net in LDM to learn temporal and spatial attention,
096   though the training cost is relatively expensive. VideoComposer Wang et al. (2024a) expands the
097
      conditional input forms by training a unified encoder. Other methods (Align Your Latents Blattmann
      et al. (2023b), VideoElevator Zhang et al. (2024a), AnimateDiff, Direct a Video Yang et al. (2024a),
098
      Motioni2v Shi et al. (2024), Consisti2v Ren et al. (2024)) improve the performance by reusing T2I
099
      models and make adjustments in the temporal and spatial attention parts to reduce issues such as
100   flicker reduction. Video generation models based on DiT or Transformer Vaswani (2017) adopt
101   spatial-temporal attention from LDM, such as Latte Ma et al. (2024), Vidu Bao et al. (2024),
102   CogVideoX Yang et al. (2024b) and SnapVideo Menapace et al. (2024), which have significant
103   advantages in terms of resolution and duration compared to LDM methods.

      3.2   CCM-D I T
155
156   As depicted in Fig. 1, the proposed CCM-DiT consists of two modules: the Sparse Motion Encoding
157   Module and the Temporal Attention Injection Module. Previous works describe camera motion in
158   various ways, such as using Plücker coordinates He et al. (2024); Bahmani et al. (2024) or directly
159   based on motion matrices Wang et al. (2024b). Methods based on Plücker coordinates calculate the
160   Plücker embedding for each pixel in the image coordinate space, with the corresponding equation:

177   matrix and intrinsic parameters of the camera-pose. RK−1 [x, y, 1] + t forms the direction vector
178   from the camera center to the pixel point (x, y). For the method of directly using motion matrices,
179   camera poses are serialized frame-by-frame into RT ∈ RL×12 , where L denotes the frame num-
180   ber. During motion injection, the parameters are replicated in spatial dimension to align temporal
181   attention layer. However, this approach may encounter problems for DiT-based method that exists
182
      time-dimensional compression.
183   Sparse Motion Encoding Module. In this work, we propose a method for converting a pixel-wise
184   motion field based on Plücker coordinates into a sparse motion field, as shown in Fig. 2. Although
185   Plücker coordinates can precisely describe the motion trajectory for each pixel in the image, we
186   perform sparse sampling of the motion field to enhance computational efficiency and adapt to spatial
187   domain feature representations. Specifically, the image is sampled at regular intervals and Plücker
188
      motion vectors are calculated on these sparse points, forming a sparse motion vector field. Assuming
      the image resolution is W × H, we sample every sx pixels in the x direction and every sy pixels in
189
      the y direction to obtain a sparse point sequence {(xi , yj )}, with the corresponding sparse motion
190
      trajectory given by:
191
192
                                                                        T
193                                Pxi ,yj = [oc , 1] RK−1 [xi , yj , 1] + t                             (4)
194
      where xi = i · sx and yj = j · sy , with i and j being the sampling indices. Here, we get a sparse
195
      motion field Fs ∈ RL×M ×N , the M = W/sx , N = H/sy .
196
197   We trained a VAE to compress the sparse motion field, aligning it with the temporal sequences
198   in OpenSora. MegViT-v2 Yu et al. (2023) is selected to maintain consistency with the temporal
199
      attention layers and the reconstruction loss of the camera-pose motion is calculated.
200   Temporal Attention Injection Module. We consider three typical embedding methods, including
201   channel-dimension concatation, adaptive normalization, and cross-attention, as shown in Fig. 3.
202   Direct channel-dimension concatation adds the camera-pose motion latent to the temporal layers,
203   which is used in MotionCtrl. Adaptive normalization uses multi-layer perceptron (MLP) for latent
204   motion alignment with temporal layers. β, γ are used for shift & scale during linear projection,
205
      respectively. For cross attention, temporal layers represents query, while latent motion represents
      key and value, calculates the hidden layers. We experiment three injection methods, and adaptive
206
      normalization gives the best performance and consistency during video generation.

212   reconstruction loss and KL loss. The reconstruction loss aims to minimize the gap between the
213   predicted result and the ground truth, while the KL loss minimizes the divergence between the
214   VAE’s output distribution and the standard normal distribution. During the fine-tuning of the latent
215   motion using MLP, we freeze the ST-DiT parts except for the temporal attention layer, and introduce
      LoRA during the update of the self-attention to reduce VRAM usage. Additionally, a novelty loss

      4     E XPERIMENTS
253
254
      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
255
256
      We use the weights and network structure following OpenSora-v1.2. When training the Sparse
257   Motion Encoding, only the parameters of the motion-relative part and the temporal-attention part
258   are trained, while the backbone parameters are frozen to retain the original capabilities. During
259   training, the approach of MotionCtrl is followed. We extract 16-frame camera pose information and
260   convert it into a RGB sparse representation (as shown in Fig. 4), and feeding it into the VAE for
261   reconstruction. The guidance scale is set to 7.0. We fine-tunes on 4 Nvidia L40s with the learning
262   rate of 5 × 10−5 , requiring 100k steps and with the batch size of 1, which takes approximately 2.5
263   days.

298   4.4   Q UANTITATIVE AND Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS
299
300   We evaluate the performance of several video generation models on both short video (16 frames)
301
      and long video (72 frames) generation tasks. The methods include LDM-based approaches such as
      SVD, AnimateDiff, MotionCtrl, and CameraCtrl, and DiT-based methods like EasyAnimate, VD3D,
302
      and OpenSora, as shown in Table 1. The resolution for LDM-based methods is mainly 256 × 256
303
      or 384 × 256, while DiT-based methods use a unified resolution of 640 × 360. For short video
304   generation tasks, MotionCtrl shows an advantage, achieving the best results in video consistency
305   metrics (FVD and CamMC). However, in long video generation tasks, CCM-DiT demonstrates sig-
306   nificant advantages in consistency metrics. This is mainly attributed to CCM-DiT’s more precise
307   camera-pose sequences input during long video generation, which allows for fine-grained control
308   over each frame. Additionally, it outperforms previously proposed methods in the CLIPSIM metric
309   as well, which demonstrates that CCM-DiT effectively retains reference image. This is because we
310   freeze other irrelevant parameters as much as possible when introducing camera-pose sequences,
311   preserving the model’s original video generation capabilities.
312   We also present the visualized performance of video generation using CCM-DiT (Fig. 5 and 6).
313   For simple camera-pose, such as zoom in and zoom out, CCM-DiT performs excellently on these
314   basic camera movement tasks, accurately following the camera motion poses. For complex tasks,
315   such as camera movement with rotation, CCM-DiT achieves smooth transitions while maintaining
316   the object pose effectively.

      4.6   D ISCUSSIONS
388
389   CCM-DiT demonstrates excellent performance in maintaining camera consistency for long video
390   generation, but there are still the following challenges and limitations:
391
392          • The performance of the main object is relatively weak. We focus on maintaining the
393            consistency of camera-pose motion. Although object consistency is also preserved, due to
394            the conservative nature of motion estimation, the object movement tends to be limited to
395
               small-scale motions, making large-scale motion generation more challenging.
396          • There is limited support for camera-pose motion trajectories. To ensure consistency in
397            our study, we use camera-pose condition based on 16 frames. More frame requirements
398            rely on frame interpolation for completion. Currently, generating more complex motion
399
               videos remains a challenge.
400
401   5     C ONCLUSION
402
403   We propose a novelty method for camera-pose controllable video generation based on DiT archi-
404   tecture. To effectively inject camera-pose sequences into the temporal-attention layer, we introduce
405   a Sparse Motion Encoding Module that transforms motion into sampling points in the RGB space
406   and use a VAE to achieve latent motion feature embedding. Our method achieves SOTA in camera
407   motion control for long video scenarios. We believe this work will find valuable applications in the
408
      future of controllable video creation.
