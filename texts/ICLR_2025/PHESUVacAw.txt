009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Single-cell omics technologies capture molecular snapshots of cells, while most
012            biological processes unfold over time. Accurately predicting single-cell gene ex-
013            pression at unmeasured time points enhances our understanding of these processes,
014            reducing costs and experimental effort by enabling the interpolation and extrap-
015            olation of observed data. This helps study continuous development, response to
016            perturbations, and disease progression. To address this problem, we propose an
017            encoder-decoder transformer architecture for Temporal Reconstruction of Cellular
018            Events (TRACE). TRACE models gene expression generation as a sequence-to-
019
               sequence generation task by learning to transform a sequence of genes from a
               source condition (e.g., previous time) into a sequence of genes in a target condition
020
               (e.g., next time point). TRACE decoder learns to generate gene tokens of the target
021
               condition by iteratively unmaking tokens in the target sequence, overcoming the dis-
022            cordance between autoregressive modeling and the non-sequential nature of gene
023            expression data. We evaluate TRACE both quantitatively and qualitatively on three
024            datasets, covering a range of tasks and biological scenarios. TRACE outperforms
025            existing models in generalizing across in-distribution and out-of-distribution tasks
026            for temporal prediction. Furthermore, we demonstrate the biological relevance of
027            the cell embeddings learned by TRACE by delineating activation-dependent cell
028            stages in immune cells, measured across multiple time points. Our findings suggest
029            that TRACE can enhance in silico hypothesis generation, improving our under-
030
               standing and prediction of cellular changes over time. This ultimately facilitates
               disease understanding and supports the design of cost-effective experiments for
031
               biological discovery.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Investigating how cells and tissues respond to external perturbations (i.e., interventions) such as drugs,
038   biochemical stimuli, or gene editing is central to understanding (patho-)physiology and developing
039
      efficient therapeutics. In this context, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) provides a pivotal
      tool for transcriptomic profiling of cells at unparalleled resolution and scale (Svensson et al., 2020).
040
      However, scRNA-seq experiments are expensive and complex (Huang et al., 2024). Additionally, the
041
      destructive nature of the technology prevents repeated sampling from the same cell, which poses a
042   challenge for studying continuous biological processes. Thus far, time-resolved single-cell studies are
043   limited in the number of sampled time points and throughput due to the associated cost and logistical
044   overhead (i.e. performing 24h time course experiments, limited availability of clinical samples).
045   Generative machine learning methods have emerged as a promising avenue for inferring perturbation
046   responses across time. Such in silico temporal predictions can support experimental design, scientific
047   discovery and ultimately drug development.
048   Multiple computational frameworks have been developed to predict single-cell condition-specific
049   gene expression. For example, generative modeling using variational auto-encoders (VAEs)(Kingma
050   & Welling, 2014) combined with vector arithmetics (Lotfollahi et al., 2019) or disentanglement
051   learning (Hetzel et al., 2022; Lopez et al., 2023). Optimal transport(Bunne et al., 2023; Huguet et al.,
052   2022; Tong et al., 2024; Schiebinger et al., 2019; Klein et al., 2023a) and dynamical modeling (Tong
053   et al., 2024; Huguet et al., 2022; Yeo et al., 2021) based methods have also yielded promising results.
      More broadly, these models predict single cell gene expression counts for missing conditions in time-


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      series prediction settings, or in response to perturbations such as drugs, diseases, and endogenous
055   physiological stimuli (e.g., cytokines).
056
057   In parallel, large-scale masked language modeling (Devlin et al., 2018b; Achiam et al., 2023; Raffel
058
      et al., 2020a) has been applied to train single-cell foundation models (Cui et al., 2024; Theodoris
      et al., 2023). By analogy to natural language processing, cells (sentences) are treated as sequences
059
      of genes (words). In terms of perturbation response prediction, Geneformer examines the impact of
060
      removing genes from the cell sequence (analogous to an experimental knock-out) on cell embeddings
061   (Theodoris et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) while scGPT has been specifically fine-tuned to predict
062   unseen multi-gene perturbations (Cui et al., 2024).
063
064
      In this work, we propose TRACE, the first sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) encoder-decoder single-
      cell generative model designed to predict temporal changes in cells (Fig. 1), inspired by advances in
065
      seq2seq modeling in language and multi-modal learning (Raffel et al., 2020b; Yu et al., 2022; Chang
066
      et al., 2022). TRACE addresses the challenging task of predicting temporal changes in single-cell
067   data. The model takes a sequence of gene tokens from a source condition (e.g., time point t) as
068   input and generates a transformed sequence for a target condition (e.g., time point t′ ). This differs
069   from existing methods (Bunne et al., 2023; Huguet et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2024; Schiebinger et al.,
070   2019; Klein et al., 2023a), which rely on low-dimensional cell embeddings (e.g., PCA of the data)
071   to directly generate gene-level embeddings for unseen time points. TRACE, on the other hand,
072   generates gene-level embeddings, which allow for gene space analysis or easy conversion back to
073   the original count space. More importantly, operating at the gene level enables the model to directly
074   learn gene-gene relationships across time points.
075   TRACE functions as both a generative and an embedding model, unlike current encoder-only single-
076   cell transformer models Cui et al. (2024); Theodoris et al. (2023). Its flexibility allows for the
077   modeling of high-dimensional single-cell data without relying on dimensionality reduction, unlike
078   recent innovations using flow matching and optimal transport, which primarily operate in low-
079   dimensional spaces (Huguet et al., 2022; Yeo et al., 2021). TRACE’s learned embedding space
080   enables seamless transformation from token space to gene expression count space through a count
081
      decoder. Additionally, TRACE can be easily integrated into the foundation model pre-training stack
      and scales to large-scale pre-training, leveraging transformers’ efficient and parallelizable training
082
      strategies developed in the NLP and LLM communities (Dao et al., 2022), while avoiding challenges
083
      in VAE training, such as posterior collapse (Dai et al., 2020) and provides an alternative to the
084   promising flow matching and diffusion models in this space.
085
086   We demonstrate TRACE’s abilities to predict condition-specific changes and support downstream
087
      analyses across comprehensive experiments. TRACE outperforms existing methods in both in-
      distribution and out-of-distribution prediction tasks for time-specific changes. TRACE effectively
088
      captures biological signals in cell embeddings, such as cell types and populations, and achieves
089
      superior performance for modeling count distributions. Finally, we highlight another use case showing
090   how TRACE can capture known gene markers for T cell activation through gene embedding analysis,
091   underpinning its potential to uncover novel biological processes.

094   2    R ELATED WORKS
095
096   Modeling cells as a sequence The first model to represent cells as a sequence of tokens (genes) was
097   scBERT(Yang et al., 2022), which used bidirectional encoder pretraining by masking gene labels,
098   similar to BERT(Devlin et al., 2018a). Geneformer(Theodoris et al., 2023) introduced rank value
099   encoding, where each genes’ expression is normalized based on the median expression across a
100   corpus of 30M cells, and then ranked within each cell. scGPT(Cui et al., 2024) uses autoregressive
101   masking to predict gene expression binned values. However, no existing work represents the single-
102   cell generation problem as a full seq2seq task using a transformer encoder-decoder formulation.
103   The power of encoder-decoder architectures for generative modeling has been demonstrated in text
104
      generation(Raffel et al., 2020b), text-to-image(Yu et al., 2022), and audio(Borsos et al., 2023) models,
      motivating our current work.
105
106   Modeling temporal dynamics We are interested in the task of predicting gene expression at time
107   point t′ given single cell gene expression at time point t (and a potential perturbation). The temporal
      coupling between cell populations at time points t and t′ has been leveraged by multiple methods

136   treating this as an optimal transport (OT) problem. For example, developmental processes can be
137   approximated by OT couplings, with transitions between progenitor and differentiated cell states
138   modeled as locally linear transitions between probability distributions (Schiebinger et al., 2019). Cel-
139   lOT combines OT and input convex neural networks to learn OT maps in a fully paramterized manner,
140   yielding improvements in scalability, stability and performance (Bunne et al., 2023). To account
141   for non-linear trajectories in biological systems, TrajectoryNet incorporates continuous normalizing
142   flows and a dynamic OT system (Tong et al., 2020). Conditional flow matching (CFM) generalizes
143   this approach to arbitrary transport maps, avoiding the limitations of continuous normalizing flows
144
      (such as the assumption of deterministic process and a Gaussian starting distribution), and uses
      minibatch approximations to efficiently estimate the OT map in a simulation-free manner (OT-CFM)
145
      (Tong et al., 2023). MIOFlow uses neural ordinary differential equations (ODE) solver to learn an OT
146
      plan in a latent space which preserves geodesic distances between time points (Huguet et al., 2022).
147   While further improvements to OT-based methods have been reported(Eyring et al., 2023), their
148   applicability to the high-dimensional gene expression single-cell prediction tasks benchmarked here
149   has not been demonstrated. Alternatively, PRESCIENT is a generative model which uses stochastic
150   differential equations to model cellular differentiation as a diffusion process.
151
      Here, we adopt a novel approach, modeling the time series task as a seq2seq problem. The advantage
152
      of this approach is that the model simultaneously learns the gene-level transformation of cells between
153   time points and learns biologically-meaningful gene and cell embeddings. Additionally, unlike flow-
154   based models, the seq2seq approach learns the transformation between the input distribution and
155   target distribution (i.e., between the initial and final states) without requiring the explicit definition of
156   closed-form conditional flows.

161   TRACE is an encoder-decoder transformer designed to generate a sequence of genes and their
      expression for a cell under a desired target condition, given a sequence of genes in the source

      3.2   C ELL SEQUENCE G ENERATION
217
218   As in text generation, autoregressive decoding predicts tokens conditioned on the previously gen-
219   erated sequence. However, gene expression does not follow this unidirectional logic, as genes act
220   together in non-sequential gene regulatory networks. Instead, the iterative decoder proposed in the
221   bidirectional MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022) transformer is more suitable to infer "cell sentences".
222   While theoretically, this method could generate all tokens simultaneously, tokens are iteratively
223   inferred as this approach yields superior results (Chang et al., 2022). The sequence starts blank with
                                     (0)
224   all unpadded tokens masked x̂M̄ . At each iteration step r, a mask scheduling function γ determines
      the number of masked tokens n = γ Rr N . As the number of iteration steps r increases, the number

225
226                                                                                        (r)
      of mask tokens decreases. The probabilities p(r) ∈ RN for the masked tokens x̂M̄ are predicted
227                                                                                                      (r)
      based on the bidirectional context of unmasked tokens. For each masked position, a token xi
228
      is sampled based on predictive probabilities pi , wherein temperature annealing can be adjusted to
229
      modulate diversity. Moreover, gene tokens cannot occur multiple times within the same sequence,
230   thus unmasked tokens are excluded from the possibilities. The remaining tokens undergo the same
231   prediction cycle until the total step R is reached, and all tokens are predicted.
232
233
      Interpolation and Extrapolation We introduce two positional encodings. The first one captures
      the rank of gene tokens in a cell, and the second one determines the order of time points. We add
234
      the positional encodings P E1,i = {pei,t }N                            T
                                                   i=1 and P E2,t = {pei,t }t=1 based on the position of
235
      each token within the cell’s sequence, and the timepoint, respectively. We interpolate between two
236   time points ti−1 and ti+1 by introducing new time points ti to the time positional encoding P E2,t
237   between P E2,t−1 and P E2,t+1 during training. During testing, we also provide all time points as the
238   context during generation to generate the interpolated time points. For extrapolation, we follow the
239   training mode described above and provide all time points as the context during the generation. We
240   can decide the sequence length and the number of time points by adjusting the positional encoding
241   for both extrapolation and interpolation cells. We investigate the effect of using different types of
242   positional encoding on the model’s performance. (See the details and results at ablation 5.3.)

248   Metrics We use Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD, Mean kernel (Gretton et al., 2006)), Earth
249   Moving Distance (1-Wasserstein, EMD (Cuturi, 2013)), Pearson correlation (PearsonR), and Rouge
250   score(See et al., 2017). EMD is calculated for each gene separately based on (Lotfollahi et al., 2023),
251   and the mean value over genes is reported. All metrics except the Rouge score are reported on
252   log-normalized counts.
253   Implementation For the main model, we use a 6-layer transformer encoder-decoder. For the count
254   decoder, we use a multi-layer perceptron with a GELU activation layer. We use Adam Optimizer
255   (Kingma & Ba, 2015). We use NVIDIA A100 80 GB and H100 80 GB for all the experiments. See
256   Appendix A.1 for more information about hyperparameters. To recreate other methods’ results, we fol-
257   low their respective repositories. We compute PCA on log-normalized counts to reduce the dimension
258   to 100 dimensions for OT-CFM and 50 dimensions for other methods. For OT-CFM, we scaled the PC
259   values as recommended. The predicted PC values are inverse-transformed to project back to the count
260   space. You can find the repository here: https://anonymous.4open.science/status/TRACE-ICLR-3316

      Discussion In this paper, we introduce TRACE, a seq2seq transformer model designed for single-cell
480   temporal prediction. We compare our approach with the state-of-the-art (SOTA) models in single-cell
481   temporal data generation, and TRACE shows promising results across the tasks. We evaluate our
482   model across three different studies in developmental biology, T cell activation, and response to
483   infection. TRACE can generate embeddings of cells and genes at unseen time points, enabling
484   analysis in embedding space while also demonstrating recoverability of original gene expression
485   counts. These applications highlight the generative potential of TRACE. We envision that TRACE
      can facilitate temporal analysis of single-cell data and guide experimental design and cell engineering.

496   Limitation The effectiveness and performance of TRACE depend on how the data is paired. To
497   capture cellular heterogeneity and cellular processes (i.e., temporal effects), having paired data is
498   essential. However, the one-to-one mapping of a source cell in time point t − 1 to a cell in time point
499   t does not capture biological processes such as cell growth and death which have been addressed in
500   unbalanced OT (Schiebinger et al., 2019).
501   Future work Leveraging recent developments in NLP for fine-tuning (Zhao et al., 2024; Dettmers
502   et al., 2024) and continual learning (Wang et al., 2024) of LLMs can improve the generative power of
503   TRACE to enhance generation quality for unseen and rare cell types. Furthermore, this work can be
504   applied to other areas of biology, such as developmental biology (Schiebinger et al., 2019; Klein et al.,
505   2023b) or disease progression to study genes driving cellular changes or differentiation trajectories.
