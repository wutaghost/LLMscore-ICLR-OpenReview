011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Multivariate Time Series (MTS) forecasting plays a vital role in various practical
014            applications. Current research in this area is categorized into Spatial-Temporal
015            Forecasting (STF) and Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF). While these
016
               tasks share similarities, the methods and benchmarks used differ significantly.
               Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks (STGNNs) excel at modeling interre-
017
               lationships in STF tasks but face difficulties with long sequence inputs due to
018
               inefficient training. In contrast, LTSF models handle long sequences well but
019            struggle with capturing complex variable interrelationships. This paper proposes
020            the Spectral Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Network (S2GNN) to address these
021            challenges, unifying short- and long-sequence spatio-temporal forecasting within
022            a single framework. S2GNN leverages a decoupled GNN along with an MLP ar-
023            chitecture to ensure efficiency. Specifically, it employs spectral GNNs for global
024            feature extraction on an adaptive graph structure and uses MLP to process mul-
025            tiple feature embeddings, enabling it to handle varying sequence lengths. Ad-
026            ditionally, we introduce scale-adaptive node embeddings and cross-correlation
027
               embeddings for better differentiation between similar temporal patterns. Exten-
               sive experiments on eight public datasets, including both STF and LTSF datasets,
028
               demonstrate that S2GNN consistently outperforms state-of-the-art models across
029
               diverse prediction tasks. Code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
030            science/r/S2GNN-B21D.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Current Multivariate Time Series (MTS) prediction methods can be broadly categorized into Spatio-
036   Temporal Forecasting (STF) and Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) (Shao et al., 2023).
037   STF focuses on capturing both spatial and temporal dependencies, with Spatio-Temporal Graph
038   Neural Networks (STGNNs) being a prominent approach (Jiang et al., 2023). These models are
039   commonly evaluated on spatio-temporal datasets, such as those from the PEMS (Chen et al., 2001)
040   traffic series. In contrast, LTSF emphasizes learning patterns like seasonality or trends over longer
041   input sequences and utilizes long-sequence datasets that cover a wide range of scenarios to ensure
042   robust model generalization (Qiu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2021).
043   The key distinction between these two research areas lies in their ability to process input sequences.
044   STF models are typically designed for short-term inputs and may struggle with longer sequences,
045   partly due to the high training costs associated with some models (Han et al., 2024). On the other
046   hand, LTSF models are better suited for handling longer input sequences but often face challenges in
047   capturing spatio-temporal relationships and tend to incur high computational complexity, especially
048   when using transformer-based architectures (Huang et al., 2023). Figure 1 shows the performance of
049   several STF models on short-term and long-term inputs. As the figure indicates, the FLOPs of most
050
      models increase significantly with input length, making long-term training nearly impossible. Thus,
      there is a clear need for an STGNN model capable of handling both short and long input sequences
051
      efficiently.
052
053   As a crucial part of STGNN models, most existing approaches rely on GCN-based methods (Kipf
      & Welling, 2016). However, current research in GNNs mainly follows two directions: spatial-based

          and spectral-based approaches (Guo & Wei, 2023). Spatial GNNs capture node representations
055       through message passing and aggregation among neighboring nodes, often acting as low-pass fil-

145    2.1             S PECTRAL G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK
146
147    Spectral GNNs work in the frequency (spectral) domain using graph Laplacian, which enables them
148    to apply various filters to the graph signal (node features) (Bruna et al., 2013). The graph filters
149    could be fixed (Kipf & Welling, 2016) (Klicpera et al., 2018) (Wu et al., 2019a) or approximated
150    with polynomials (Defferrard et al., 2016) (Chien et al., 2020) (He et al., 2021). Spectral graph
151    models naturally excel at learning heterophilic relationships. Since the homophilic/heterophilic re-
152
       lationships in adaptive graphs are uncertain, spectral models are well-suited for use on adaptive
       graphs if we can ignore their dynamic changes. Among existing spectral GNNs, ChebNetII (He
153
       et al., 2022) can approximate an arbitrary spectral filter h(λ) with an optimal convergence rate, and
154
       the learnable parameter γj allows the model to learn an arbitrary spectral filter via gradient descent.
155    Additionally, it offers strong interpretability. It is formulated as:

      2.2   M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
163
164   Multivariate time series forecasting covers a wide range of methods. Solely from the perspective
165   of explicitly leveraging inter-variable relationships, models can be broadly categorized into GNN-
166   based models, cross-variable interaction models and cross-time interaction models. First, we provide
167   some definitions to facilitate a quicker understanding of the relevant concepts.
168   Time series. A time series X ∈ RN ×T is a time oriented sequence of N -dimensional time points,
169   where T is the number of time points, and N is the number of variables. When N = 1, a time series
170   is called univariate. When N > 1, it is called multivariate.
171
      Spatial-Temporal Forecasting (STF) primarily focuses on modeling the temporal and spatial pat-
172
      terns of multivariate time series. Let G = (V, E) be a network as an undirected graph with V = |V|
173
      nodes and E = |E| edges. It involves learning a model f that, given observations of historical look-
174   back observations X ∈ RV ×P of P timesteps on a graph G, outputs predictions Y ∈ RV ×F for the
175   future horizon of length F . The process is defined as:
176                                                       fθ
177
                            [xt−P +1 , · · · , xt−1 , xt ; G] −→ [xt+1 , xt+2 , · · · , xt+F ]
178   where xt ∈ RV is a graph signal vector at time step t, θ is a collection of learnable parameters. In
179   this paper, the number of nodes is equal to the number of variables, i.e., N = V .
180   Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) primarily focus on modeling the long-term depen-
181   dencies of multivariate time series. The definition of LTSF is similar to STF, except that it does not
182   explicitly use a graph structure and typically involves a longer input data sequence.
183
      With the development of GCN (Defferrard et al., 2016) (Kipf & Welling, 2016), models begin to
184   utilize GCNs to model spatial dependencies based on a pre-defined prior graph and further combine
185   them with sequential models (Yu et al., 2017) (Li et al., 2017) (Wu et al., 2019b). However, many
186   recent works argue that the pre-defined prior graph might be biased, incorrect, or even unavailable in
187   many cases. Thus, they propose to jointly learn the graph structure (i.e., adaptive graph) (Bai et al.,
188   2020) (Jin et al., 2022) (Shang et al., 2021) or use graph-free methods to do STF tasks (Shao et al.,
189   2022a) (Liu et al., 2023b) (Deng et al., 2021).
190   Cross-variable interaction models are also critical for time series forecasting. Recently, (Zhang
191   & Yan, 2023) and (Liu et al., 2023c) both adopted channel-wise Transformer-based frameworks,
192   and extensive experimental results have demonstrated the effectiveness of channel-wise attention for
193   time series forecasting.
194
      Cross-time interaction models focus on extracting information along the time dimension, such as
195
      patch-wise patterns (Nie et al., 2022), multi-scale information (Wang et al., 2024) (Shabani et al.,
196   2022), or frequency-domain features (Wu et al., 2021) (Piao et al., 2024).

206   3.1   I NDISTINGUISHABILITY OF T IMES S ERIES S AMPLES
207
208   Simply speaking, sample indistinguishability occurs when dynamics from different factors overlap,
209   making input sequence features too similar to distinguish. (Deng et al., 2021) points out that this
210   issue hinders deep neural networks from capturing spatial and temporal differences, while (Shao
211   et al., 2022a) offers a simple framework to mitigate it. In this paper, we introduce a scale-adaptive
212
      node embedding method to adapt the model to varying node numbers and a cross-correlation embed-
      ding method to extract distinguishable features directly from the input sequence, addressing cases
213
      where temporal information is unavailable or ineffective.

339   4     E XPERIMENTS
340
341   In this section, our primary goal is to demonstrate the effectiveness and feasibility of S2GNN in han-
342   dling both long and short sequence inputs. We first introduce the basic experimental setup, followed
343   by presenting the results of S2GNN on real-world data. Afterward, we compare the computational
344
      cost of training S2GNN and baseline models for one epoch across various datasets. Although dif-
      ferent models require varying total epochs for training and have different memory demands, the
345
      comparison of FLOPs still provides an intuitive reflection of the challenges faced during model
346
      training. Finally, we visualized the prediction results of S2GNN and the baseline models on cer-
347   tain datasets. From these visualizations, we were able to identify some of the reasons why existing
348   models fail in specific scenarios.
349
350   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
351
352   Datasets. We conducted experiments on eight real-world datasets, including five traffic flow datasets
353   commonly used in spatio-temporal forecasting tasks (PEMS03, PEMS04, PEMS07, PEMS08, and
354   SD) and three benchmark datasets typically employed for long-term time series forecasting tasks
355   (Electricity, Weather, and Traffic).
356   Baselines. We selected several existing spatiotemporal models and compared their performance on
357   both short-term and long-term inputs in Figure 1. Among these, D2STGNN (Shao et al., 2022b) re-
358   quire pre-defined graph structures, while StemGNN (Cao et al., 2020) and MTGNN (Wu et al., 2020)
359   employs an adaptive graph structure. Although STID (Shao et al., 2022a) and STAEformer (Liu
360   et al., 2023a) do not utilize graph structures, they have demonstrated effectiveness in short-term
361   STF tasks. Detailed results are in the appendix (Table 3).
362   For the LTSF tasks, we selected various types of models from the perspective of explicitly leverag-
363   ing inter-variable relationships as benchmark baselines. DLinear (Zeng et al., 2022) is one of the
364   simplest and most effective recent models. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) processes time series data
365   by segmenting it into patches, which is widely adopted nowadays. MTGNN (Wu et al., 2020) has
366   proven effective for both STF and LTSF. TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022) and iTransformer (Liu et al.,
367   2023c) focus on leveraging relationships between variables for forecasting. CrossGNN (Huang
368
      et al., 2023) is a recently proposed spatial GNN model with linear complexity that balances both
      efficiency and predictive performance in LTSF tasks.
369
370   Evaluation metrics. Following previous work, for STF tasks, we compared the MAE, RMSE, and
371   MAPE using the re-normalized prediction results, benchmarking against other models. For LTSF
372   tasks, we used the normalized prediction results and compared the models based on MAE and MSE.
373   To simplify the presentation of results across both STF and LTSF datasets, we omitted MAPE from
374
      the main text since the other two metrics used are sufficient to support the conclusions of the paper.
375   Implementation details. Our experiments were conducted on a 16GB V100 and a 40GB NVIDIA
376   A100 GPU, averaging results over three random seeds. Due to the training efficiency of existing
377   models and our computational resource limits, we reproduced only a subset of them. Our repro-
      duced results on long-sequence data were slightly worse than those in the CrossGNN paper, so we

      spatiotemporal forecasting tasks. Our proposed model not only achieves state-of-the-art prediction

422
423
      Moreover, Figure 3 illustrates the shape of the graph filter learned by S2GNN. It can be observed
      that across all datasets (more results are in Figure 9), the graph filter exhibits a band-rejection filter
424
      shape, which strongly indicates that the adaptive graph contains both homophily and heterophily.
425
      This observation highlights that current methods using spatial GNNs limit the expressive capacity
426   of the temporal adaptive graph. More specifically, on spatiotemporal datasets, the learned graph
427   filter retains both low-frequency and high-frequency components while suppressing mid-frequency
428   components, suggesting a strong polarization between homogeneity and heterogeneity. Therefore,
429   we suspect that in typical spatiotemporal forecasting tasks, such as traffic flow prediction, ignoring
430   odd-hop neighbors could theoretically enhance the robustness of spatial-GNN models (Lei et al.,
431   2022). On multivariate datasets, however, the presence of mid-frequency components indicates that
      the polarization in the adaptive graph structure is less pronounced.

         5                  C ONCLUSION
526
527
528      In this paper, our research is motivated by the inefficiency of existing Spatio-Temporal Graph Neu-
529      ral Networks (STGNNs), which also struggle to handle long-sequence inputs. Through observing
530      the training process of adaptive graphs in existing models, we found that their graph structures tend
531
         to stabilize after a few epochs. Based on this, we propose the Spectral Spatio-Temporal Graph
         Neural Network (S2GNN) to unify short-term and long-term STF while maintaining training effi-
532
         ciency. The decoupled GNN along with an MLP architecture ensures overall efficiency. Specifically,
533
         S2GNN incorporates learnable filters that adaptively capture both short-term and long-term features,
534      improving the model’s expressiveness. To keep the model simple yet effective, we enhance its abil-
535      ity to handle indistinguishable samples by introducing a Scale-Adaptive node embedding method
536      and a Cross-Correlation embedding method. These methods allow the node embeddings to adjust to
537      varying numbers of nodes and learn distinguishable features directly from input sequences. Experi-
538      ments on eight public datasets show that our model effectively addresses spatio-temporal prediction
539      for both short and long sequences across different scenarios. It also suggests the importance of
         modeling both homophilic and heterophilic relationships in MTS forecasting.
