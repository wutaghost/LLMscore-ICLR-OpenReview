011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Nonconvex minimax problems have received intense interest in many machine
014            learning applications such as generative adversarial network, robust optimization
015            and adversarial training. Recently, a variety of minimax optimization algorithms
016
               based on Lipschitz smoothness for finding first-order or second-order stationary
               points have been proposed. However, the standard Lipschitz continuous gradient
017
               or Hessian assumption could fail to hold even in some classic minimax prob-
018
               lems, rendering conventional minimax optimization algorithms fail to converge
019            in practice. To address this challenge, we demonstrate a new gradient-based
020            method for nonconvex-strongly-concave minimax optimization under a general-
021            ized smoothness assumption. Motivated by the important application of escap-
022            ing saddle points, we propose a generalized Hessian smoothness condition, under
023            which our gradient-based method can achieve the complexity of O( −1.75 log n)
024            to find a second-order stationary point with only gradient calls involved, which
025            improves the state-of-the-art complexity results for the nonconvex minimax opti-
026            mization even under standard Lipschitz smoothness condition. To the best of our
027
               knowledge, this is the first work to show convergence for finding second-order
               stationary points on nonconvex minimax optimization with generalized smooth-
028
               ness. The experimental results on the application of domain adaptation confirm
029
               the superiority of our algorithm compared with existing methods.

      In recent years, minimax optimization problems, under various assumptions on the objective func-
035   tions, has been a major focus of research in machine learning fields, with various applications includ-
036   ing adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018), generative adversarial networks (GAN) (Goodfellow
037   et al., 2014), and multi-agent reinforcement learning (Omidshafiei et al., 2017). A general formula-
038   tion of Minimax optimization problem can be written as

041
042   In this paper, we focus on the nonconvex-strongly-concave case where the objective function f :
043   Rm × Rn 7→ R is nonconvex in x and strongly-concave in y.
044   Historically, Nouiehed et al. (2019) was the first work providing non-asymptotic convergence rates
045   for nonconvex-strongly-concave minimax problems without assuming special structure of the objec-
046   tive function. They use the notion of  -first-order stationary point to measure the rate of convergence
047   of their algorithm. Using this notion, they showed that their algorithm finds an  -first-order station-
048   ary point in O( −2 ) gradient evaluations.
049   Another way to measure the convergence rate of an algorithm for solving (1) is to define the primal
050   function Φ(x) = maxy∈Y f (x, y) and measure the first-order optimality in terms of the nonconvex
051   problem minx∈X Φ(x). In this context, Thekumparampil et al. (2019) proposed the proximal dual
052   implicit accelerated gradient (ProxDIAG) algorithm for smooth and nonconvex-strongly-concave
053   minimax problems and proved that this algorithm finds an  -first-order stationary point of Φ with
      the rate of O( −2 ).

126   Compared to first-order methods, there has been significantly less research on the second-order
127   methods for minimax optimization problems with global convergence rate estimation. However,
128   a significant body of recent work shows that first-order stationary points cannot guarantee the lo-
129   cal optimality in nonconvex-(strongly)concave settings and the global optimality in convex-concave
130   settings. Lin et al. (2022) proposed newton-based methods and obtained global rates of conver-
131   gence within O( −2/3 ) iterations using Hessian-vector information, matching the theoretically es-
132   tablished lower bound in convex-concave settings. For nonconvex-strongly-concave settings, Luo
133   et al. (2022) presented Minimax Cubic-Newton, obtaining a second-order stationary point of Φ with
134   calling O(κ1.5  −1.5 ) times of Hessian oracles and Õ(κ2  −1.5 ) times of gradient oracles, while the
135   inexact version obtaining a second-order stationary point with Õ(κ1.5  −2 ) Hessian-vector oracle
136   calls and Õ(κ2  −1.5 ) gradient calls. Yang et al. (2023) proposed a Perturbed Restarted Acceler-
137   ated HyperGradient Descent algorithm, improved the complexity bound to Õ(κ1.75  −1.75 log6 n)
138   with only gradient iterations. But none of these algorithms are proved efficient under generalized
139   smoothness assumption. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first work to study the convergence
140
      for finding second-order solutions in nonconvex-strongly-concave minimax optimization problems
      beyond bounded Lipschitz smoothness assumption.

142   Generalized smoothness. The convergence analysis of most existing minimax algorithms needs
143   to assume the gradient or hessian is Lipschitz. However, such assumptions are fail to hold in an
144   important class of neural networks such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Elman, 1990), long-
145   short-term memory networks (LSTMs) (Graves & Graves, 2012) and Transformers (Vaswani, 2017)
146
      which are shown to have unbounded smoothness (Pascanu, 2012; Zhang et al., 2019; Crawshaw
      et al., 2022). For minimization optimization, Zhang et al. (2019) proposed a relaxed smoothness
147
      assumption that bounds the Hessian by a linear function of the gradient norm, that is, a function f
148
      is said to be (l0 , l1 )-smoothness if there exists some constants l0 > 0 and l1 ≥ 0 such that

151   Under the same condition, Zhang et al. (2020a) considers momentum in the updates and improves
152   the constant dependency of the convergence rate for SGD with clipping derived in Zhang et al.
153   (2019). Qian et al. (2021) studies gradient clipping in incremental gradient methods, Zhao et al.
154   (2021) studies stochastic normalized gradient descent, and Crawshaw et al. (2022) studies a gen-
