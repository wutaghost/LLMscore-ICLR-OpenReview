009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We tackle the 2-norm (Euclidean) k-Hyperplane Clustering problem (k-HC2 ),
012            which asks for finding k hyperplanes that minimize the sum of squared 2-norm
013            (Euclidean) distances between each point and its closest hyperplane. We solve
014            the problem to global optimality via spatial branch-and-bound techniques (SBB)
015            by strengthening a mixed integer quadratically-constrained quadratic program-
016
               ming formulation with constraints that arise when formulating the problem in p-
               norms with p ̸= 2. In particular, we show that, for every (appropriately scaled)
017
               p ∈ N ∪ {∞}, one obtains a variant of k-HC2 whose optimal solutions yield
018
               lower bounds within a multiplicative approximation factor. We focus on the case
019            of polyhedral norms where p = 1, ∞ (which admit a disjunctive-programming re-
020            formulation), and prove that strengthening the original formulation by including,
021            on top of the original 2-norm constraints, the constraints of one of the polyhedral-
022            norms leads to an SBB method where nonzero lower bounds are obtained in a
023            linear (as opposed to exponential) number of SBB nodes. Experimentally, we
024            show that our strengthened formulations lead to speedups from 41 to 1.5 orders of
025            magnitude, drastically improving the problem’s solvability to global optimality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Given m points {a1 , . . . , am } in Rn , the k-Hyperplane Clustering problem, or k-HC2 , asks for
030   identifying k hyperplanes which minimize the sum of the squares of the distances between each
031   point and the hyperplane closest to it in Euclidean (2-norm) distance.
032
033
      k-HC2 arises when relationships of co-linearity (in R2 ) or co-(hyper)planarity (in Rn ) are sought.
      One of the problem’s most natural applications is line/surface detection in digitally-sampled images
034
      and in 3d environments Amaldi & Mattavelli (2002). More applications are found in diverse areas
035
      such medical prognosis Bradely & Mangasarian (2000), linear facility location Megiddo & Tamir
036   (1982), discrete-time piecewise affine hybrid system identification Ferrari-Trecate et al. (2003), prin-
037   cipal/sparse component analysis Washizawa & Cichocki (2006); He & Cichocki (2007); Tsakiris &
038   Vidal (2017), nonlinear regression He & Qin (2010), dictionary learning Zhang et al. (2013), LiDAR
039   data classification Kong et al. (2013), and sparse matrix representation Georgiev et al. (2007).
040
      k-HC2 was first introduced by Bradely & Mangasarian (2000), where it is shown that, with k = 1,
041
      the problem is solved by computing an eigenvalue-eigenvector pair of a suitably defined matrix built
042   as a function of the data points. k-HC2 is N P-hard in any norm since fitting m points in Rn with k
043   hyperplanes with 0 error is N P-complete even for n = 2, as shown by Megiddo & Tamir (1982). To
044   tackle k-HC2 (without optimality guarantees) when k ≥ 2, Bradely & Mangasarian (2000) proposed
045   an adaptation of the popular k-means heuristic by MacQueen et al. (1967). An exact Mixed Integer
046   Quadratically Constrained Quadratic Programming (MI-QCQP) formulation for k-HC2 which is
047   solvable with a spatial branch-and-bound method (SBB) is proposed by Amaldi & Coniglio (2013),
048   together with a heuristic for larger-scale instances. Works addressing variants of k-HC2 asking for
049   the smallest number of hyperplanes with a distance no larger than a given ϵ > 0 are found in Dhyani
050
      & Liberti (2008); Amaldi et al. (2013).
051   In this work, we aim at solving k-HC2 to global optimality via a spatial branch-and-bound (SBB)
052   method. For this, we strengthen a classical mixed-integer quadratically-constrained quadratic pro-
053   gramming (MI-QCQP) formulation for k-HC2 by including constraints (and variables) that arise
      when formulating the problem in another p-norm (p ̸= 2). In particular, we show that, under mild

261   4.1   S PATIAL BRANCH - AND - BOUND
262
263   The basic idea of the spatial branch-and-bound (SBB) method is of building a dual bound by op-
264   timizing over a convex (typically polyhedral) envelope conv(F ) of the feasible region F of the
265   problem. F is then split into two sub-regions F1 and F2 with tighter bounds on at least a variable.
266
      This allows for constructing tighter convex envelopes of F1 and F2 in such a way that the optimal
      solution over conv(F ) is cut off due to not belonging to conv(F1 ) ∪ conv(F2 ). F1 and F2 are then
267
      recursively optimized in a classical branch-and-bound fashion.
268
269   Let us consider the case of k-HC(2,1) . We assume (as done by most of the state-of-the-art solvers

      4.5   S OLVING THE STRENGTHENED FORMULATIONS VIA SBB
406
407   We extend the analysis in Proposition 2 to the strengthened formulations with the following two
408   propositions (their proofs of both are contained in the appendix):
409
410   Proposition 3. Assume that the constraint ∥wj ∥1 ≥ 1, j ∈ [k], is imposed and that branching takes
411
      place on the sjh variables first. Then, a nonzero global lower bound is calculated after generating
412
      Θ(2k(n−1) ) nodes; after this, no further branching on w takes place.
413   Proposition 4. Assume that ∥wj ∥∞ ≥ √1n , j ∈ [k], is imposed and that branching takes place on
414   the ujh variables first. Then, O(nk) nodes suffice to obtain a nonzero lower bound; after this, no
415   further branching on w takes place.
416
417   Propositions 3 and 4 show the crucial advantages of strengthening formulation (k-HC(2,1) ) via
418   the two (scaled) polyhedral-norm constraints we considered. Proposition 3 indicates that, if the
419   ||wj ||1 ≥ 1, j ∈ [k], constraints are imposed and branching takes places on the 0-1 variables of
420   such norm constraints, in a complete SBB tree of depth Θ(2k(n−1) ) the polyhedral-norm constraint
421   is satisfied in every leaf node. While this number can be large, this is in stark contrast to the 2-norm
422   case, where the same number of branching operations only suffices to obtain the first nonzero global
423   lower bound, and the number of branchings needed to completely describe the feasible region of
424   the problem in the w space depends on the solver’s feasibility tolerance (since, for each j ∈ [k],
425
      the complement of the feasible region is a sphere). More interestingly, Proposition 4 shows that,
      when the ∥wj ∥∞ ≥ √1n , j ∈ [k], constraints are imposed and branching takes places on their 0-1
426
427   variables, the size the SBB tree is extremely small—only polynomial in k and n. The difference
428
      between the two results is obviously due to the geometry of the 1- and ∞-norm balls, since the
      former has 2n facets while the latter only 2n.
429
430   When included in a formulation for k-HC2 on top of the ||wj ||2 ≥ 1, j ∈ [k], constraints, the
431   polyhedral-norm constraints accelerate the computation of a nonzero global lower bound, thus lead-
      ing to more pruning and, overall, a faster SBB method. This is better shown in the next section.

      6    C ONCLUDING REMARKS
475
      We have focused on solving the 2-norm k-Hyperplane Clustering problem with spatial branch-and-
476
      bound (SBB) techniques by strengthening the classical formulation with constraints that arise from
477
      (scaled) p-norm formulations of the problem, with p ̸= 2. Focusing on the 1- and ∞-norms, we have
478   theoretically shown that including the constraints stemming from the 1-norm version of the problem
479   leads to computing nonzero lower bounds in a linear (rather than exponential) number of SBB nodes.
480   Our experimental results show speedups from 14 to 1.5 orders of magnitude, substantially improving
481   the problem’s solvability to global optimality.
482
      Future works include addressing the problem’s combinatorial (assignment) aspect, which, as the
483
      number of data points increases, may become a limiting factor when solving k-HC2 to global opti-
484   mality, and extending our techniques to other problems featuring nonconvex p-norm constraints.
