                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Zero-shot composed image/video retrieval is a challenging task that involves using
012            a combination of a reference visual input and a relative caption as a query to search
013            for target visual data. Earlier studies have treated composed image retrieval and
014            composed video retrieval methods separately, potentially neglecting the benefits of
015            integrating image-video-text representation learning. In this paper, we consolidate
016            these tasks into a single Composed Visual Retrieval (CVR) task, which requires
017            the composition of image and video samples with textual modifications using a
018            unified retrieval model. Our principal insight is that the video modality can be
019            effectively added to existing vision-language pretrained models. When integrated
020
               with the Spherical Linear Interpolation (Slerp) method previously proposed for
               Composed Image Retrieval (CoIR), we found that it results in an effective approach
021
               for solving the CVR task, which we called Slerp+ . Extensive experiments demon-
022
               strate Slerp+ ’s superiority across various composed image and video retrieval
023            benchmarks, including our newly proposed video benchmark. Notably, Slerp+
024            mutually enhances image and video retrieval performance over single-modality
025            models, underscoring its potential to transform the field of compositional visual
026            retrieval.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Composed Image Retrieval (CoIR) and composed Video Retrieval (CoVR) tasks take visual data and
032   user-provided textual instructions as queries to retrieve relevant data samples from the gallery. Given
033
      that certain characteristics are more accurately described through language while others are more
      effectively conveyed visually, the multi-modality of the query enhances the quality of search results.
034
      For example, a customer may combine text descriptions with images to find specific products, such
035
      as searching for “white shirts similar to this red one”. The potential of composed retrieval in a wide
036   range of practical applications has led to a significant rise in the level of interest within the retrieval
037   community.
038
039
      In the supervised setting, the task of composed retrieval requires expensive annotations of triplets
      for model training, each of which consists of a reference visual input, a relative caption, and target
040
      data Liu et al. (2021); Baldrati et al. (2022); Ventura et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024). As a result,
041
      zero-shot learning-based methods Saito et al. (2023); Baldrati et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2023); Du
042   et al. (2024) as well as pseudo-triplet based methods, as proposed in Jang et al. (2024b); Ventura
043   et al. (2023), have gained popularity in recent years. However, both approaches require a complex
044   additional layer in their designs, complicating the process and restricting their applicability to specific
045   contexts. For instance, zero-shot methods require an additional projection module to transform
046   images into pseudo-word tokens. In the case of pseudo-triplet methods, the generation of pseudo-
047   triplets necessitates costly tuning of a Large Language Model (LLM), and potentially suffers from
048   the inherent hallucinations that LLMs are especially prone to. Lastly, it is important to note that the
049   CoIR and CoVR tasks have so far been isolated from each other, potentially overlooking the overall
050
      effectiveness that a unified retrieval system could offer.
051   In this paper, we propose an extension of the Spherical Linear Interpolation (Slerp) Jang et al. (2024a)-
052   based CoIR method for the unified image-video-text Composed Visual Retrieval (CVR) task, which
053   we call Slerp+ . Our method provides a simple yet effective solution that integrates image and video
      representations with text, improving both image and video composed retrieval performances. As

115   Composed Retrieval with Supervised Triplets Supervised learning-based methods for Composed
116
      Image Retrieval (CoIR) Liu et al. (2021); Baldrati et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2021); Goenka et al.
      (2022); Delmas et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2024) have garnered significant interest due to their decent
117
      performance and domain-specific applicability. These methods utilize human-annotated triplets,
118
      comprising reference images, textual descriptions of desired modifications, and target images, to train
119   CoIR models for natural images Liu et al. (2021) and fashion images Wu et al. (2021); Goenka et al.
120   (2022). An intriguing approach to compose triplets follows a semi-supervised paradigm. Here, Visual
121   Delta Generation (VDG) Jang et al. (2024b) uses both supervised triplets and unlabeled data samples
122   to enhance the performance of the CoIR model. Ventura et al. (2023) further expanded the composed
123   retrieval task to the video modality, proposing the CoVR task. They construct triplets using videos
124   instead of images, after which they train a CoVR model that shows promising performance. However,
125   approaches utilizing supervised triplets face significant challenges when there is a need to scale up
126   the training set due to the high labeling cost. Additionally, domain-specific supervised triplets often
127
      result in low generalization capability. In this work, we propose a unified method for both images
      and videos that doesn’t rely on any human-annotated triplets, thereby reducing domain-specific bias
128
      and offering flexibility for various composed retrieval use cases.
129
130
      Zero-shot Composed Retrieval Zero-shot CoIR models, which use large-scale image-caption
131
      pairs instead of annotated triplets, have also been proposed Saito et al. (2023); Cohen et al. (2022);
132   Baldrati et al. (2023); Du et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2023); Jang et al. (2024a). These zero-shot methods
133   leverage vision-language pretraining models Radford et al. (2021); Li & et al. (2022); Li et al.
134   (2023) to provide image-text joint representation. The ease of large scale data collection through
135   web-crawling has accelerated the development of these vision-language pretrained models, and in
136   turn more generalized zero-shot CoIR models. However, zero-shot learning has not yet been extended
137   to the video modality. This is primarily due to the greater difficulty in gathering video-caption pairs
138   compared to image-caption pairs and the increased computational cost. Additionally, the complexity
139   of video data may require more data samples to achieve satisfactory zero-shot composed retrieval
140
      performance. To address these challenges, we propose a simple and effective unified CVR method,
      Slerp+ , that utilizes both image-caption and video-caption pairs for training. This approach not only
141
      allows both modalities to complement each other, it is also more practical due to its capability to
142
      perform image or video composed retrieval with an unique integrated model.

145   3     M ETHOD
146
147   In this section, we detail our unique, unified Slerp+ framework for the Composed Visual Retrieval
148   (CVR) task. Similar to zero-shot CoIR methods Saito et al. (2023); Cohen et al. (2022); Baldrati
149   et al. (2023); Levy et al. (2023); Du et al. (2024); Jang et al. (2024a), Slerp+ utilizes image-caption
150
      pairs for training while also incorporating video-caption pairs to achieve a seamless understanding of
      image, video, and text modalities. We first discuss the embedding-based learning scheme that trains
151
      a transformer-based vision-language encoders. Subsequently, we describe the retrieval inference
152
      process, which employs linear interpolation between visual and textual embeddings to generate a
153   composed embedding.

      3.3     I NFERENCE
254
255   After training the model, it can be utilized to process images, videos, and text, generating embeddings
256   for retrieval. However, executing composed retrieval requires a method to combine visual and text
257   embeddings. One such approach is the early-fusion strategy. This strategy simultaneously feeds the
258   text encoder of the model with visual patch tokens and text modifications, using the output [CLS]
259   token as the composed embedding. However, this approach may not be entirely accurate. The model,
260   trained to handle multi-modal inputs with VTM - a system designed to classify whether visual data
261
      and text match, is not intended to compose visual and text elements to project a composed output.
262   In light of this, an alternative late-fusion approach that utilizes interpolation between visual and
263   text embeddings, also known as Slerp Shoemake (1985); Jang et al. (2024a), can be considered.
264   This strategy employs individual modality encoders to generate separate visual and text embeddings,
265   which are then linearly interpolated to obtain a composed embedding c as:
266
267                          c : Slerp(v, w; t) = (sin((1 − t)α) · v, + sin(tα) · w) / sin(α)                                           (5)
268
269   where t is balancing scalar value and α is the angle between embeddings which is computed as
      α = cos−1 (v · w). This process can be seamlessly applied to the model trained in Section 3.2. The

      reason lies in the contribution of both VTC and VTM losses to the discovery of a fine-grained semantic
271   alignment among the embeddings of images, videos, and text simultaneously. Such alignment leads to
272   densely distributed embeddings, covering the intermediate compositional representation successfully.

      Finally, we can construct a image-video-text unified retrieval system using the Slerp process, which
      we call Slerp+ . The retrieval gallery is established using visual embeddings v obtained from image
275
      and video samples. Users can input a query image or video, along with a text modification, to create a
276
      composed embedding c using the Slerp method. The system then searches relevant images or videos
277   from the gallery by calculating the cosine similarity between the composed query embedding and
278   each individual image or video embedding.

288   4.1   S ETUP
289
290   Datasets. To train the Slerp+ model, we utilize two types of datasets: (1) image-caption pairs and
291
      (2) video-caption pairs. Specifically for image-caption pairs, we use CC3M dataset Sharma et al.
      (2018), which was collected through web crawling. This is a common dataset for training zero-shot
292
      composed IR methods Saito et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2023); Du et al. (2024), and we use a subset of
293
      2.3M pairs that was accessible to us to train the model.
294
295   For video-caption pairs, we use a subset of the WebVid dataset Bain et al. (2021). Following the
296   setup proposed in CoVR Ventura et al. (2023), we select its training split, which consists of a total of
297
      130K video-caption pairs, ensuring no overlap between the training and test sets. Furthermore, we
      clean up the dataset by removing noisy samples with too few frames or exceptionally long videos,
298
      resulting in 94K video-caption pairs for the training set of the Slerp+ model.
299
300   To evaluate composed retrieval performance, we utilize two image-based benchmarks and two video-
301   based benchmarks. For images, first, we utilize CIRR Liu et al. (2021), which deals with natural
302   images, where the test split consists of 503 subgroups of 2,178 images. Second, we utilize FashionIQ
303
      Wu et al. (2021), which focuses on fashion domain images of three categories: Dress, Shirt, and
      Toptee. Following the literature, we employ the validation split for evaluation, which consists of
304
      15,415 images.
305
306   For videos, first, we utilize the CoVR test splits named WebVid-CoVR-Test set. A high-quality set
307   of 2,556 triplets is carefully selected and curated to form the separate corpus of the WebVid dataset.
308   Second, to further evaluate the performance of models on composed VR tasks, we establish a new
309
      experimental protocol of more complex text modifications, Activitynet-CoVR, based on the validation
      split of the Activitynet-captions dataset Krishna et al. (2017). This dataset labels each video with
310
      corresponding sentences (captions) and timestamps. We create reference and target video pairs using
311
      two methods: (1) Intra-pair, selecting two video clips from the same video, with the earliest caption
312   and the corresponding video clip as the reference and the last clip as the target, and (2) Inter-pair,
313   selecting two video clips from different videos by calculating the similarity between all possible
314   video clips and choosing the closest two with cosine similarity scores over 0.8, measured with the
315   VideoMAE-Large model Tong et al. (2022). This results in a total of 1,260 pairs. Similar to the
316   annotation steps for the CoVR test set, we use the instruction-tuned LLM, LLaMA3 LLa (2024).
317   We prompt it directly with [Please give an imperative that makes video A to video B ], including
318   the caption of each video clip. Finally, annotators carefully filter out noisy triplets, resulting in 800
319   composed VR triplets for evaluation.
320   Evaluation Metrics. In line with the protocols adopted in benchmarks Saito et al. (2023); Ventura
321   et al. (2023), we evaluate the model’s performance using recall scores at the top K retrieval results
322   (R@K) for ranks 1, 5, 10, and 50. The metric Recall at rank k (R@k) measures the frequency at
323   which the correct image or video appears among the top k results. A higher recall score indicates
      superior performance.

      configured as: LoRAα = 16, rank = 16, and dropout = 0.1. Throughout the training process, we
349
      maintain the entire set of parameters for Ev and Et as fixed. Only the parameters for LoRA, the
350
      text projection linear layer, and the matching head θm are updated. The models are trained using
351   8 × A100-80GB GPUs. For video training, we use a batch size of 4, sampling 8 equally-spaced
352   frames per video, resulting in a total of 32 frames. For image training, we use a batch size of 32.
353   Therefore, each GPU processes a total of 64 samples (frames + images). The initial temperature
354   τ for scaling is set to 1/0.07 and is continuously updated during training. We employ the AdamW
355   optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2018) with a fixed learning rate of 3e-5 and a weight decay of 0.01.
356   The training is conducted for single epoch, with the trainable parameters constituting less than 0.32%
357   of the total parameters, ensuring efficiency.
358   During the evaluation, we adopt the same frame sampling strategy as in the training stage, sampling
359   8 equally-spaced frames per video for both query and gallery videos. To compose the visual and text
360   embeddings with late-fusion (Slerp), we set t to 0.6 for videos and 0.7 for images by default. For
361   comparison, we also utilize the X-CLIP Ni et al. (2022) model, a CLIP-style video-text pretraining,
362   with the same Slerp inference. Additionally, in line with the experiments conducted in CoVR Ventura
363   et al. (2023) that evaluate the performance of image-text models in a video-text scenario, we input the
364   selected frames separately into the vision encoder of BLIP. We then either average (Avg) the image em-
365
      beddings or apply an early-fusion strategy with the pretrained Cross-Attention (CA) as used in CoVR.
      We use the pretrained checkpoint from Huggingface: microsoft/xclip-large-patch14,
366
      and the pretrained checkpoint provided by the official implementation of the CoVR model.

      Ablation Study. To validate our approach, we perform an ablation study on Slerp+ regarding training
473
      schemes and report the results in Table 5. In (a, b), we vary the number of frames extracted from
474   videos during training. Compared to the baseline, which uses 8 frames per video, we find that using
475   more frames slightly improves performance. However, the increase is marginal, so we choose to
476   use 8 frames per video by default for efficiency. In (c), we try averaging all frame embeddings with
477   text embedding instead of using the Slerp to obtain composed embedding, but this result in poorer
478   performance. In (d, e), we examine the impact of each training loss. We observe that both have a
479   decent effect on performance improvement, and they complement each other to achieve the best
480   performance when combined. Lastly, in (f, g), we examine the joint training of image-video-text by
481   excluding video in (f) and image in (g). The results show that utilizing both image and video samples
482
      simultaneously with the proposed training scheme improves both image and video composed retrieval
      accuracy.

522   5        D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
523
524   Potential Impact. By combining visual data with textual modifications, our Slerp+ system addresses
525   the limitations of both supervised and zero-shot learning approaches, providing a unified framework
526   that improves retrieval accuracy without relying on costly supervised triplets. The potential societal
527   impact includes significantly improved search functionalities across various applications, from
528   e-commerce to multimedia management, by providing accurate and contextually relevant results.
529   Limitation. A potential limitation of Slerp+ system is its dependency on the quality and diversity
530   of the image-caption and video-caption pairs used during training. While the zero-shot learning
531   approach alleviates the need for supervised triplets, the model’s performance may still be constrained
532   by the representational richness and variety of the training data.
533
      Conclusion. In this paper, we introduced the CVR task and Slerp+ , a novel unified framework for
534   composed image and video retrieval that seamlessly integrates visual and textual modalities. By
535   employing a vision and text encoder which are fine-tuned with both image-caption and video-caption
536   pairs, Slerp+ overcomes the limitations of traditional supervised learning and zero-shot approaches.
537   Our method’s effectiveness is demonstrated through significant improvements on existing CoIR and
538   CoVR benchmarks and a new and more complex video evaluation benchmark that we introduce in
539   this paper. The results underscore the potential of Slerp+ to advance compositional vision-language
      retrieval, offering a robust and scalable solution for diverse real-world applications.
