                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, there has been a surge of interest in proving discretization bounds
014            for sampling under isoperimetry and for diffusion models. As data size grows,
015            reducing the iteration cost becomes an important goal. Inspired by the great success
016
               of the parallel simulation of the initial value problem in scientific computation, we
               propose parallel Picard methods for sampling tasks. Rigorous theoretical analysis
017
               reveals that our algorithm achieves better dependence on dimension d than prior
018
               works in iteration complexity (i.e., reduced from O(poly(log
                                                                       e            d)) to O(log
                                                                                           e     d)),
019
               which is even optimal for sampling under isoperimetry with specific iteration
020
               complexity. Our work highlights the potential advantages of simulation methods in
021            scientific computation for dynamics-based sampling and diffusion models.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   We study the problem of sampling from a probability distribution with density ПҖ(x) вҲқ exp(вҲ’f (x))
028   where f : Rd вҶ’ R is a smooth potential. We consider two types of setting. Problem (a): the
029   distribution is known only up to a normalizing constant (Chewi, 2023), and this kind of problem
030   is fundamental in many fields such as Bayesian inference, randomized algorithms, and machine
031   learning (Marin et al., 2007; Nakajima et al., 2019; Robert et al., 1999). Problem (b): known as the
032   score-based generative models (SGMs) (Song & Ermon, 2019), we are given an approximation of
033
      вҲҮ log ПҖt , where ПҖt is the density of a specific process at time t. The law of this process converges to
      ПҖ over time. SGMs are now the state-of-the-art in many fields, such as computer vision and image
034
      generation (Ho et al., 2022a; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), audio and video generation (Ho et al., 2022b;
035
      Yang et al., 2023), and inverse problems (Song et al., 2021).
036
037   For Problem (a), specifically log-concave sampling, starting from the seminal papers of Dalalyan
038   & Tsybakov (2012), Dalalyan (2017), and Durmus & Moulines (2017), there has been a flurry of
039
      recent works on proving non-asymptotic guarantees based on simulating a process which converges
      to ПҖ over time (Wibisono, 2018; Vempala & Wibisono, 2019; Altschuler & Talwar, 2022; Mou et al.,
040
      2021). Moreover, these processes, such as Langevin dynamics, converge exponentially quickly to ПҖ
041
      under mild conditions (Dalalyan, 2017; Bernard et al., 2022; Mou et al., 2021). Such dynamics-based
042   algorithms for Problem (a) share a common feature with the inference process of SGMs that they are
043   actually a numerical simulation of an initial-value problem of differential equations (Hodgkinson
044   et al., 2021). Thanks to the exponentially fast convergence of the process, significant efforts have
045   been conducted on discretizing these processes using numerical methods such as the forward Euler,
046   backward Euler (proximal method), exponential integrator, mid-point, and high-order Runge-Kutta
047   methods (Vempala & Wibisono, 2019; Wibisono, 2019; Oliva & Akyildiz, 2024; Shen & Lee, 2019;
048   Li et al., 2019).
049   Furthemore, in recent years, there have been increasing interest and significant advances in under-
050   standing the convergence of inherently dynamics-based SGMs (De Bortoli, 2022; Lee et al., 2023;
051   Chen et al., 2024b; 2022; Tang & Zhao, 2024; Pedrotti et al., 2023; Li & Yan, 2024). Notably,
052   polynomial-time convergence guarantees have been established (Chen et al., 2022; 2024b; Benton
053   et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024), and various discretization schemes for SGMs have been analyzed (Lu
      et al., 2022a;b; Huang et al., 2024).

068   The algorithms underlying the above results are highly sequential. However, with the increasing
069   size of data sets for sampling, we need to develop a theory for algorithms with limited iterations.
070   For example, the widely-used denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020) may take
071   1000 denoising steps to generate one sample, while the evaluations of a neural network-based score
072   function can be computationally expensive (Song et al., 2020).
073
      As a comparison, recently, the (naturally parallelizable) Picard methods for diffusion models reduced
074   the number of steps to around 50 (Shih et al., 2024). Furthermore, in terms of the dependency on the
075   dimension d and accuracy Оө, Picard methods for both Problems (a) and (b) were proven to be able to
076   return an Оө-accurate solution within O(poly(log d)) iterations, improved from previous O(da ) with
077   some a > 0. However, for Problem (a), a large gap remains relative to the recent lower bound shown
078   in Zhou et al. (2024), and the O(poly(log d)) iteration complexity is not yet optimal for diffusion
079   models.

236   3     T ECHNICAL OVERVIEW
237
238   We adopt the time splitting for the time horizon used in the existing parallel methods (Gupta et al.,
239
      2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Anari et al., 2024; Yu & Dalalyana, 2024; Shen & Lee, 2019). Our
      algorithm, however, depart crucially from prior work in the design of parallelism across the time
240
      slices, and the modification for controlling the score estimation error. Below we summarize these
241
      notion contributions and technical novelties.
242
243   Recap of existing parallel sampling methods. Existing works for parallel sampling apply the
244   following generic discretization schemes (Gupta et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Anari et al., 2024;
245   Yu & Dalalyana, 2024; Shen & Lee, 2019). At a high level, these methods divide the time horizon
246   into many large time slices and each slice is further subdivided into grids with a small enough
247   step size. Instead of sequentially updating the grid points, they update all grids at the same time
248   slice simultaneously using exponentially fast converging Picard iterations (Alexander, 1990), or
249   randomized midpoint methods (Shen & Lee, 2019; Yu & Dalalyana, 2024; Gupta et al., 2024). With
250   O(log
       e     d) Picard iterations for O(log
                                      e     d) time slices, the total iteration complexity of their algorithms
               2
251   is O(log d). However, while sequential updating of each time slice is not necessary for simulating
         e
252   the process, it remains unclear how to parallelize across time slices for sampling to obtain O(log d)
253   time complexity.
254
255   Algorithmic novelty: parallel methods across time slices. Naively, if we directly update all the
256   grids simultaneously, the Picard iterations will not converge when the total length is T = O(log
                                                                                                    e    d).
257   Instead of updating all time slices together or updating the time slice sequentially, we update the
258   time slices in a diagonal style as illustrated in Figure 2. For any j-th update at then-th time slice
259
      (corresponding the rectangle in the n-th column from the left and the j-th row from the top in Figure
      2), there will be two inputs: (a) the right boundary point of the previous time slice, which has been
260
      updated j times, and (b) the points on the girds of the same time slice that have been updated j вҲ’ 1
261
      times. Then we perform P times Picard iterations with these inputs, where the hyperparameter P
262   depends on the smoothness of the score function. The main difference compared to the existing
263   Picard methods is that for a fixed time slice, the starting points in our method are updated gradually,
264   whereas in existing methods, the starting points remain fixed once processed.
265

269   components: (i) convergence error of the continuous process, (ii) discretization error, and (iii) score
      estimation error. For (i) the convergence error of the continuous process, it is rather straightforward

293
      (ii) Discretization error: Discretization error mainly arise from the truncation errors on discrete
      grids with the grids gap as O(1/d). In existing parallel methods, the sequential update across time
294
      slices benefits the convergence of truncation errors along the time direction. Assuming the truncation
295
      errors in the previous time slice have converged, its right boundary serves as the starting point for all
296   grids in the current O(1)-length time slice which results in an initial bias of O(d). Subsequently, by
297   performing O(log d) exponentially fast Picard iterations, the truncation error will converge. However,
298   in our diagonal-style updating scheme across time, the truncation error interacts with inputs from both
299   the previous time slice and prior updates in the same time slice. Consequently, the bias-convergence
300   loop that holds in sequential updating no longer holds.
301
      (iii) Score estimation error: If the score function itself is Lipschitz continuous (Assumption 5.3 for
302   Problem (b)), no additional score matching error will arise during the Picard iterations. This allows
303   the total score estimation error to remain bounded under mild conditions (Assumption 5.1). However,
304   for Problem (a), since it is the velocity field вҲҮf instead of the score function s that is Lipschitz,
305   additional score estimation errors will occur during each update. For the sequential algorithm, these
306   additional score estimation errors are contained within the bias-convergence loop, ensuring the total
307   score estimation error remains to be bounded. Conversely, for our diagonal-style updating algorithm,
308   the absence of convergence along the time direction causes these additional score estimation errors to
309   accumulate exponentially over the time direction.
310
311   Technical novelty. Our technical contributions address these challenges by the appropriate selection
312   of the number of Picard iterations within each update P and the depth of the Picard iterations J. We
313   outline the details of the choices below.
314

315   with Ljn , and that the additional score estimation error for each update scales with Оҙ 2 .
316
317   To address the initial challenge related to the truncation error, we choose the Picard depth as
318

320
      slices, we can ensure that b < 1 along the Picard iteration direction. Consequently, the truncation
      error will converge if the iteration depth J is sufficiently large, such that aN bJ is sufficiently small.
321
      This requirement implies that J = O(N + log d).

334   Balance between time and Picard directions. We note that the Picard method, despite being
335   the simplest approach for time parallelism, has achieved optimal performance in certain specific
336   settings. On the one hand, the continuous processes need to run for at least O(log d) time. To ensure
337
      convergence within every time slice, the time slice length have to be set as O(1), resulting in a
      necessity for at least O(log d) iterations. On the other hand, with a proper initialization O(d), Picard
338
      iterations converge within O(log d) iterations. Our parallelization balances the convergence of the
339
      continuous diffusion and the Picard iterations to achieve the improved results.

      proposed in scientific computation (Gear, 1991; Ong & Schroder, 2020; Gander, 2015), especially
343
      for parallel Picard iterations (Wang, 2023). Compared with prior work in scientific computation, our
344   approach exhibits several significant differences. Firstly, our primary objective differs from that in
345   simulation. In sampling, we aim to ensure that the output distribution closely approximates the target
346   distribution, whereas simulation seeks to make each point on the discrete grid closely match the true
347   dynamics. Second, our algorithm differs significantly from that of Wang (2023). In our algorithm,
348   each update takes the inputs without the corrector operation. Furthermore, we perform P Picard
349   iterations in each update to prevent error accumulation over time T = O(log
                                                                               e      d). In comparison, the
350   algorithm proposed in Wang (2023) performs a single Picard iteration in each update for simulation
351   on a finite time interval. However, these two fields are connected through the sampling strategies that
352   ensure each discrete point closely approximates the true process at every sampling step.
