                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) is crucial in unravelling decision-making
               processes in complex machine-learning models. LIME (Local Interpretable
013
               Model-agnostic Explanations) is a well-known XAI framework for image anal-
014
               ysis. It utilizes image segmentation to create features to identify relevant areas
015            for classification. Consequently, poor segmentation can compromise the con-
016            sistency of the explanation and undermine the importance of the segments, af-
017            fecting the overall interpretability. To address these challenges, we introduce
018            DSEG-LIME (Data-Driven Segmentation LIME), featuring: i) a data-driven seg-
019            mentation for human-recognized feature generation by foundation model integra-
020            tion, and ii) a user steered granularity in the hierarchical segmentation procedure
021            through composition. We evaluate DSEG-LIME on pre-trained models using Im-
022            ageNet classes, explicitly targeting scenarios without domain-specific knowledge.
023
               Our findings demonstrate that DSEG outperforms most of the XAI metrics and
               enhances the alignment of explanations with human-recognized concepts, signifi-
024
               cantly improving interpretability.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Why should we trust you? The integration of AI-powered services into everyday scenarios, with or
031   without the need for specific domain knowledge, is becoming increasingly common. For instance,
032   consider AI-driven systems that assist in diagnosing diseases based on medical imaging. In such
033
      high-stakes scenarios, accuracy and alignment with expert knowledge are paramount. To ensure
      reliability, stakeholders, including medical professionals and regulators, frequently seek to evaluate
034
      the AI’s performance post-deployment. For example, one might assess whether the AI correctly
035
      identifies anomalies in medical scans that could indicate early-stage cancer. The derived question
036   - ”Why should we trust the model?” - directly ties into the utility of Local Interpretable Model-
037   agnostic Explanations (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016). LIME seeks to demystify AI decision-making
038   by identifying key features that influence the output of a model, underlying the importance of the
039   Explainable AI (XAI) research domain, particularly when deploying opaque models in real-world
040   scenarios (Barredo Arrieta et al., 2020; Linardatos et al., 2021; Garreau & Mardaoui, 2021).
041
      Segmentation is key. LIME uses segmentation techniques to identify and generate features to de-
042   termine the key areas of an image that are critical for classification. However, a challenge emerges
043   when these segmentation methods highlight features that fail to align with identifiable, clear con-
044   cepts or arbitrarily represent them. This issue is particularly prevalent with conventional segmen-
045   tation techniques. These methods, often grounded in graph- or clustering-based approaches (Wang
046   et al., 2017), were not initially designed for distinguishing between different objects within images.
047   However, they are the default in LIME’s implementation (Ribeiro et al., 2016).
048   Ambiguous explanations. The composition of the segmentation has a significant influence on the
049   explanation’s quality (Schallner et al., 2020). Images with a large number of segments frequently
050   experience significant stability issues in LIME, primarily due to the increased number of sampled
051   instances (Section 2). This instability can lead to the generation of two entirely contradictory ex-
052   planations for the same instance, undermining trust not only in LIME’s explanations but also in
053   the reliability of the model being analyzed (Garreau & Mardaoui, 2021; Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola,
      2018; Zhou et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2024). Moreover, humans often struggle to

      This work. In this paper, we address the challenges above by introducing DSEG-LIME (Data-
085   driven Segmentation LIME), an adaptation of the LIME framework for image analysis across do-
086   mains where specialized knowledge is not required. We replace the conventional segmentation al-
087   gorithm with foundation models, such as SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), and often refer to these mod-
088   els as data-driven to emphasize their capability to generate features that more effectively capture
089   human-recognizable concepts, leveraging insights derived from extensive image datasets. Given the
090   great segmentation ability of such models, we implement a compositional object structure, adapting
091   LIME’s feature generation with a novel hierarchical segmentation. This adaptation provides flex-
092   ibility in the granularity of concepts, allowing users to specify the detail of LIME’s explanation,

097   distinguishing it from other methods.

      Contribution. The key contributions of our paper are summarized as follows: (i) We present DSEG-
      LIME, an enhanced version of the LIME framework for image analysis, leveraging foundation mod-
100
      els to improve image segmentation. (ii) DSEG extends LIME by incorporating compositional object
101
      structures, enabling hierarchical segmentation that offers users adjustable feature granularity. (iii)
102   We rigorously evaluate our approach with other segmentation methods and LIME enhancements
103   across multiple pre-trained image classification models. Our evaluation includes a user study for
104   qualitative insights and distinguishes between explaining (quantitative) and interpreting (qualita-
105   tive) aspects. We acknowledge that explanations considered intuitive by users may not always re-
106   flect the AI model’s operational logic, which can diverge from human perception (Molnar et al.,
107   2022; Freiesleben & König, 2023). To address this, we complement our evaluation with several
      quantitative performance metrics widely used in XAI research (Nauta et al., 2023).

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Region-based perturbation XAI techniques. LIME is among several techniques designed to ex-
111   plain black box models through image perturbation. Fong & Vedaldi (2017) introduced a meta-
112   predictor framework that identifies critical regions via saliency maps. Subsequently, Fong et al.
113   (2019) developed the concept of extremal perturbations to address previous methods’ limitations.
114   Additionally, Kapishnikov et al. (2019) advanced an integrated-gradient, region-based attribution
115   approach for more precise model explanations. More recently, Escudero-Viñolo et al. (2023) have
116   highlighted the constraints of perturbation-based explanations, advocating for the integration of se-
117   mantic segmentation to enhance image interpretation.
118   Instability of LIME. The XAI community widely recognizes the instability in LIME’s explana-
119   tions, which stems from LIME’s design (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Zhou et al., 2021; Zhao
120   et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2024). Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola (2018) handled this issue by showing the
121   instability of various XAI techniques when slightly modifying the instance to be explained. A di-
122   rect improvement is Stabilized-LIME (SLIME) proposed by Zhou et al. (2021) based on the central
123   limit theorem to approximate the number of perturbations needed in the data sampling approach to
124   guarantee improved explanation stability. Zhao et al. (2020) improved stability by exploiting prior
125
      knowledge and using Bayesian reasoning - BayLIME. GLIME (Tan et al., 2024) addressed this is-
      sue by employing an improved local and unbiased data sampling strategy, resulting in explanations
126
      with higher fidelity - similar to the work by Rashid et al. (2024). Recent advancements include
127
      Stabilized LIME for Consistent Explanations (SLICE) Bora et al. (2024), which improves LIME
128   through a novel feature selection mechanism that removes spurious superpixels and introduces an
129   adaptive perturbation approach for generating neighbourhood samples. Another hierarchical-based
130   variation, DLIME Zafar & Khan (2021), utilizes agglomerative hierarchical clustering to organize
131   training data, focusing primarily on tabular datasets. In contrast, DSEG-LIME extends this concept
132   to images by leveraging the hierarchical structure of image segments.
133
      Segmentation influence on explanation. The segmentation algorithm utilized to sample data
134   around the instance x strongly influences its explanation. It directly affects the stability of LIME
135   itself, as suggested by Ng et al. (2022). This behaviour is in line with the investigation by Schall-
136   ner et al. (2020) that examined the influence of different segmentation techniques in the medical
137   domain, showing that the quality of the explanation depends on the underlying feature generation
138   process. Blücher et al. (2024) explored how occlusion and sampling strategies affect model explana-
139   tions when integrated with segmentation techniques for XAI, including LRP (Layer-Wise Relevance
140   Propagation) (Montavon et al., 2019) and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). Their study highlights
141   how different strategies provide unique explanations while evaluating the SAM technique in image
142
      segmentation. Sun et al. (2023) used SAM within the SHAP framework to provide conceptually
      driven explanations, which we discuss in Appendix B.4.
143
144   Segmentation hierarchy. The work of Li et al. (2022) aimed to simulate the way humans structure
145   segments hierarchically and introduced a framework called Hierarchical Semantic Segmentation
146   Networks (HSSN), which approaches segmentation through a pixel-wise multi-label classification
147   task. HIPPIE (HIerarchical oPen-vocabulary, and unIvErsal segmentation), proposed by Wang et al.
148
      (2023), extended hierarchical segmentation by merging text and image data multimodally. It pro-
      cesses inputs through decoders to extract and then fuse visual and text features into enhanced repre-
149
      sentations.

154   In this section, we introduce the LIME framework (Ribeiro et al., 2016), providing its theoretical
155   foundation and functionality to establish the context for our approach.
156   Notation. We consider the scenario where we deal with imagery data. Let x ∈ X represent an
157   image within a set of images, and let y ∈ Y denote its corresponding label in the output space
158   with logits Y ⊆ R indicating the labels in Y. We denote the neural network we want to explain by
159   f : X → Y. This network functions by accepting an input x and producing an output in Y, which
160   signifies the probability p of the instance being classified into a specific class.

      3.1   L OCAL I NTERPRETABLE M ODEL - AGNOSTIC E XPLANATIONS
163
164   LIME is a prominent XAI framework designed to explain the decisions of a neural network f in
165   a model-agnostic and instance-specific (local) manner. It applies to various modalities, including
166   images, text, and tabular data (Ribeiro et al., 2016). In the following, we will briefly review LIME’s
167   algorithm for treating images.
168   Feature generation. The technique involves training a local, interpretable surrogate model g ∈ G,
169   where G is a class of interpretable models, such as linear models or decision trees, which approxi-
170   mates f ’s behavior around an instance x (Ribeiro et al., 2016). This instance needs to be transformed
171   into a set of features that can be used by g to compute the importance score of its features. In the
172   domain of imagery data, segmentation algorithms segment x into a set of superpixels s0 ...sd ∈ S D ,
173   done by conventional techniques (Hoyer et al., 2019; Achanta et al., 2012; Felzenszwalb & Hut-
174   tenlocher, 2004; Neubert & Protzel, 2014). We use these superpixels as the features for which we
175   calculate their importance score. This step reflects the problematic process mentioned in Section 1,
176
      which forms the basis for the quality of the features that influence the explanatory quality of LIME.
177   Sample generation. For sample generation, the algorithm manipulates superpixels by toggling
178   them randomly. Specifically, each superpixel si is assigned a binary state, indicating this feature’s
179   visibility in a perturbed sample z. The presence (1) or absence (0) of these features is represented in
180   a binary vector z′i , where the i-th element corresponds to the state of the i-th superpixel in z. When
181   a feature si is absent (i.e., si = 0), its pixel values in z are altered. This alteration typically involves
182
      replacing the original pixel values with a non-information holding value, such as the mean pixel
      value of the image or a predefined value (e.g., black pixels) (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Tan et al., 2024).
183
      Consequently, the modified instance z, while retaining the overall structure of the original image x,
184
      exhibits variations in its feature representation due to these alterations.
185
186   Feature attribution. LIME employs a proximity measure, denoted as πx , to assess the closeness
187   between the predicted outputs f (z) and f (x), which is fundamental in assigning weights to the
188
      samples. In the standard implementation of LIME, the kernel πx (z) is defined as follows:

      In this section, we will present DSEG-LIME’s two contributions: first, the substitution of traditional

      feature generation with a data-driven segmentation approach (Section 4.1), and second, the estab-

      4.1   DATA - DRIVEN SEGMENTATION INTEGRATION
210
211   DSEG-LIME improves the LIME feature generation phase by incorporating data-driven segmenta-
212   tion models, outperforming conventional graph- or cluster-based segmentation techniques in creat-
213   ing recognizable image segments across various domains. Specifically, our approach mainly uses
214   SAM (Segment Anything) (Kirillov et al., 2023) due to its remarkable capability to segment im-
215   ages in diverse areas. However, as the appendix shows, it can also be applied to other segmentation
      models. Figure 2 illustrates the integration of DSEG into the LIME framework, as outlined in


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Section 3. Specifically, DSEG impacts the feature generation phase, influencing the creation of su-
217   perpixels/features S D , and subsequently affecting the binary vector z′ and the feature vector z. This
218   modification directly impacts the loss function in Equation (2) and the proximity metric for per-
219   turbed instances in Equation (1), leading to an improved approximation of the interpretable model
220   g, which is used to explain the behaviour of the original model f for a given instance x. How-
221   ever, the effect of DSEG aligns with that of other segmentation methods like SLIC, as the surrogate
222   model primarily leverages the resulting segments without incorporating additional elements from
223   the segmentation foundation models underlying DSEG. This argument is further substantiated in the
224   discussion of our experimental results.

332   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
333
      Segmentation algorithms. Our experiment encompasses, along with SAM (vit h), four conven-
334
      tional segmentation techniques: Simple Linear Iterative Clustering (SLIC) (Achanta et al., 2012),
335
      Quickshift (QS) (Hoyer et al., 2019), Felzenszwalb (FS) (Felzenszwalb & Huttenlocher, 2004) and
336   Watershed (WS) (Neubert & Protzel, 2014). We carefully calibrate the hyperparameters of these
337   techniques to produce segment counts similar to those generated by SAM. This calibration ensures
338   that no technique is unfairly advantaged due to a specific segment count – for instance, scenarios
339   where fewer but larger segments might yield better explanations than many smaller ones. In the
340   Appendix, we demonstrate the universal property of integrating other segmentation methods within
341   DSEG by presenting additional experiments with DETR (Carion et al., 2020) and SAM 2 (Ravi
342   et al., 2024) in Appendix B.5, Appendix B.6.
343   Models to explain. The models investigated in this paper rely on pre-trained models, as our primary
344   emphasis is on explainability. We chose EfficientNetB4 and EfficientNetB3 (Tan & Le, 2019) as the
345   ones treated in this paper, where we explain EfficientNetB4 and use EfficientNetB3 for a contrastiv-
346   ity check (Nauta et al., 2023) (Section 5.2.1). To verify that our approach works on arbitrary pre-
347   trained models, we also evaluated it using ResNet-101 (He et al., 2015; maintainers & contributors,
348   2016) (Appendix B.1) and VisionTransformer (ViT-384) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) (Appendix B.2).
349   Furthermore, we demonstrate the applicability of our approach on a zero-shot learning example of
350
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) using a new dataset with other classes (Appendix B.7).
351   Dataset. We use images from the ImageNet classes (Deng et al., 2009), on which the covered models
352   were trained (Tan & Le, 2019; He et al., 2015; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). Our final dataset consists of
353   50 carefully selected instances (Appendix D.1), specifically chosen to comprehensively evaluate the
354   techniques quantitatively. However, we want to emphasize that the selection of images is not biased
355   toward any model. We also test the approach for another dataset in Appendix B.7. Additionally, our
356
      code (including documentation) is available in the supplementary material, allowing us to verify our
      claims. We will also make the code publicly available upon acceptance.
357
358   Hyperparameters and hardware setup. The experiments were conducted on an Nvidia RTX
359   A6000 GPU. We compare standard LIME, SLIME (Zhou et al., 2021), GLIME (Tan et al., 2024),
360   and BayLIME (Zhao et al., 2020), all integrated with DSEG, using 256 samples per instance, a batch
361   size of ten and mean superpixel value for perturbation. For each explanation, up to three features are
362
      selected based on their significance, identified by values that exceed the average by more than 1.5
      times the standard deviation. In BayLIME, we use the ’non-info-prior’ setting. For SAM, we con-
363
      figure it to use 32 points per side, and conventional segmentation techniques are adjusted to achieve
364
      a similar segment count, as previously mentioned. In SLIC, we modify the number of segments
365   and compactness; in Quickshift, the kernel size and maximum distance; in Felzenszwalb, the scale
366   of the minimum size parameter; and in Watershed, the number of markers and compactness. Other
367   hyperparameters remain at default settings to ensure a balanced evaluation across methods.

372   covering three domains: content, presentation, and user experience. In the content domain, we eval-
373   uate correctness, output completeness, consistency, and contrastivity. Presentation domain metrics
374   like compactness and confidence are assessed under content for simplicity. We will briefly describe
375   each metric individually to interpret the results correctly. The user domain, detailed in Section 5.3,
376   includes a user study that compares our approach with other segmentation techniques in LIME.
377   We use quantitative and qualitative assessments to avoid over-emphasizing technical precision or
      intuitive clarity (Molnar et al., 2022).

      5.2.1   Q UANTITATIVE METRICS DEFINITION
379
380   Correctness involves two randomization checks. The model randomization parameter check (Ran-
381   dom Model) (Adebayo et al., 2020) tests if changing the random model parameters leads to different
382   explanations. The explanation randomization check (Random Expl.) (Luo et al., 2020) examines
383   if random output variations in the predictive model yield various explanations. For both metrics, in
384   Table 1 we count the instances where explanations result in different predictions when reintroduced
385   into the model under analysis. The domain also utilizes two deletion techniques: single deletion
386   (Albini et al., 2020) and incremental deletion (Hoyer et al., 2019; Goyal et al., 2019). Single dele-
387
      tion serves as an alternative metric to assess the completeness of the explanation, replacing less
      relevant superpixels with a specific background to evaluate their impact on the model predictions
388
      (Ramamurthy et al., 2020). After these adjustments, we note instances where the model maintains
389
      the correct image classification. Incremental deletion (Incr. Deletion) entails progressively elimi-
390   nating features from most to least significant based on their explanatory importance. We observe the
391   model’s output variations, quantifying the impact by measuring the area under the curve (AUC) of
392   the model’s confidence, as parts of the explanation are excluded. This continues until a classification
393   change is observed (not ground truth class), and the mean AUC score for this metric is documented
394   in Table 2.
395
      Output completeness measures whether an explanation covers the crucial area for accurate classi-
396   fication. It includes a preservation check (Preservation) (Goyal et al., 2019) to assess whether the
397   explanation alone upholds the original decision, and a deletion check (Deletion) (Zhang et al., 2023)
398   to evaluate the effect of excluding the explanation on the prediction outcome (Ramamurthy et al.,
399   2020). This approach assesses both the completeness of the explanation and its impact on the clas-
400   sification. The results are checked to ensure that the consistency of the classification is maintained.
401   Compactness is also considered, highlighting that the explanation should be concise and cover all
402   the areas necessary for prediction (Chang et al., 2019), reported by the mean value.
403   Consistency assesses explanation robustness to minor input alterations, like Gaussian noise addi-
404   tion, by comparing pre-and post-perturbation explanations for stability against slight changes (Noise
405   Stability) (Zhang et al., 2021; Bhatt et al., 2021), using both preservation and deletion checks. For
406   consistency of the feature importance score, we generate explanations for the same instance eight
407   times (Rep. Stability), calculate the standard deviation σi for each coefficient i, and then average all
408   σi values. This yields σ̄, the average standard deviation of coefficients, and is reported as the mean
409   score.
410   Contrastivity integrates several previously discussed metrics, aiming for target-discriminative ex-
411   planations. This means that an explanation ex for an instance x from a primary model f1 (Ef-
412   ficientNetB4) should allow a secondary model f2 (EfficientNetB3) to mimic the output of f1 as
413   f1 (x) ≈ f2 (ex ) (Schwab & Karlen, 2019). The approach checks the explanation’s utility and trans-
414   ferability across models, using EfficientNetB3 for preservation and deletion tests to assess consis-
415   tency.

501   5.4   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
502
503   DSEG-LIME performs the feature generation directly on images before inputting them into the
504   model for explanation. For models like ResNet with smaller input sizes (He et al., 2015), the quan-
505
      titative advantages of DSEG are less evident (Appendix B.1). Experiments have shown that better
      results can be achieved with a lower stability score threshold of SAM. Furthermore, substituting
506
      superpixels with a specific value in preservation and deletion evaluations can introduce an inductive
507
      bias (Garreau & Mardaoui, 2021). To reduce this bias, using a generative model to synthesize re-
508   placement areas could offer a more neutral alteration. Additionally, future work should thoroughly
509   evaluate feature attribution maps to ensure that methods assign significant attributions to the correct
510   regions, like in appendix C.5. This comprehensive assessment is essential for verifying the inter-
511   pretability and reliability of such methods. Lastly, our approach, like any other LIME-based method
512   (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2024), does not assume a per-
513   fect match between the explanation domains and the model’s actual domains since it simplifies the
514   model by a local surrogate. Nonetheless, our quantitative analysis confirms that the approximations
515   closely reflect the model’s behaviour. Future work could focus on integrating the foundation model
516
      directly into the system through a model-intrinsic approach, similar to (Sun et al., 2023).
517   No free lunch. Although DSEG provides promising results in many domains, it is not always univer-
518   sally applicable. When domain-specific knowledge is crucial to identify meaningful features or the
519   feature generation task is inherently complex, DSEG might not perform as effectively as traditional
520   segmentation methods within LIME (Khani et al., 2024) (Appendix C.4). However, future explo-
521   ration could involve testing alternative segmentation techniques, such as integrating HSSN (Wang
522
      et al., 2023) or HIPPIE (Li et al., 2022) instead of SAM (or DETR) to overcome this limitation.

524   6     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this study, we introduced DSEG-LIME, an extension to the LIME framework, incorporating a
527   data-driven foundation model (SAM) for feature generation. This approach ensures that the gen-
528   erated features more accurately reflect human-recognizable concepts, enhancing the interpretability
529   of explanations. Furthermore, we refined the process of feature attribution within LIME through
530   an iterative method, establishing a segmentation hierarchy that contains the relationships between
531   components and their subcomponents. In Appendix C.2, we show that our idea also helps explain a
532   model’s wrong classifications. Through a comprehensive two-part evaluation, split into quantitative
533   and qualitative analysis, DSEG emerged as the superior method, outperforming other LIME-based
534   approaches in most evaluated metrics. The adoption of foundational models marks a significant step
535
      towards enhancing the post-hoc and model-agnostic interpretability of deep learning models.

      A    DSEG-LIME ALGORITHM
865
866
867   Algorithm 1 DSEG-LIME framework pseudocode
868   Require: f (black-box model), ζ (segmentation function), x (input instance), g (interpretable
869     model), d (maximum depth), hp (segmentation hyperparameters), θ (minimum segment size),
870     k (top segments to select)
871   Ensure: g approximates f locally around x
872     1. Initial segmentation:
873
        S ← ζ(x, hp)                                                            ▷ Segment the input instance
        2. Small cluster removal:
874
        S ′ ← {si ∈ S | size(si ) ≥ θ}                                               ▷ Remove small clusters
875
        3. Hierarchical ordering:
876     H ← BuildHierarchy(S ′ )                                            ▷ Build hierarchical segmentation
877     for l ← 1 to d do
878         4. Empty space removal:
879         H[l] ← NearestNeighbor(H[l])                                            ▷ Fill unsegmented space
880         if l = 1 then
881              Sl ← H[l]                                                              ▷ Segments at depth 1
882         else
883              Sl ← {si ∈ H[l] | parent(si ) ∈ top ids}              ▷ Select child segments of top parents
884
            end if
            Z ← Perturb(x, Sl )                                         ▷ Create neighborhood perturbations
885
            w ← Proximity(Z, x)                             ▷ Compute sample weights based on proximity
886
            preds ← f (z) for all z ∈ Z                                              ▷ Get predictions from f
887         g ← InitializeModel(g)                         ▷ Initialize a new interpretable model for depth l
888         g ← Fit(g, Z, preds, w)                                               ▷ Train interpretable model
889         top ids ← {id(si ) | si ∈ Sl , si is among top k features in g}        ▷ Update top segment IDs
890     end for
891     return g                                                           ▷ Return the local surrogate model
892
893
      We present the pseudocode of our DSEG-LIME framework in Algorithm 1. To construct the hierar-
894
      chical segmentation within our framework, we start by calculating the overlaps between all segments
895   in S. We build a hierarchical graph using this overlap information through the following process.
896
897   First, we identify the top-level segments, which do not occur as subparts of any other segments.
898
      These segments serve as the highest-level nodes in the hierarchical graph. Starting from these top-
      level segments, we apply a top-down approach to identify child segments recursively. For each
899
      parent segment, we check for segments that are contained within it; these fully contained segments
900
      are designated as child nodes of the parent in the graph.
901
902   This recursive process continues for each subsequent level, ensuring that every parent node encom-
903   passes its child nodes. The hierarchical graph thus formed represents the structural relationships
904
      between segments, where parent-child relationships indicate that child segments are complete parts
      of their respective parent segments. By constructing the hierarchy in this manner, we capture the
905
      nested structure of segments, which supports multi-level interpretability within the DSEG-LIME
906
      framework.
