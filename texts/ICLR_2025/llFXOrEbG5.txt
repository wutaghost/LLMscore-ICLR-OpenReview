009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012
013            Gradient-based multi-step iteration has been widely used to enhance attack ef-
014            ficiency of adversarial examples. In this work, we propose a Fixed Strength
015
               Optimization (FSO) method to accelerate the convergence of adversarial examples
               with a fixed preset attack strength. FSO can be easily combined with existing attack
016
               techniques to achieve fast convergence and well-controlled attack strength. We
017
               further introduce a combined norm based on L2 and L∞ norms to modulate the
018            attacking direction. This combined norm can help to balance the attack strength in
019            the directions of semantic information and noise components in the model gradients.
020            By incorporating the combined norm into FSO, our numerical experiments show
021            improved attack transferability and high imperceptibility of perturbations.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   Adversarial examples Szegedy et al. (2013); Goodfellow et al. (2014); Bai et al. (2019) and the
032   transferability of adversarial perturbations Goodfellow et al. (2014); Liu et al. (2016) initially raised
033   significant security concerns Sharif et al. (2016); Eykholt et al. (2018); Ma et al. (2021) of deep neural
034   networks (DNNs). However, following studies and applications have demonstrated various benefits
035   derived from adversarial examples. For instance, training with adversarial examples enhances DNN
036   robustness Madry et al. (2017); Ma et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2021b); Ilyas et al. (2018a); Duan et al.
037   (2020); Wu et al. (2020b); Ma et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2019), allowing stable performance against
038   unknown or malicious inputs. Recent studies also showed that adversarial examples can protect
039
      intellectual property by preventing diffusion models from generating painting imitations Liang et al.
      (2023). In these applications, it is essential to develop methods that generate highly transferable
040
      adversarial examples in an efficient way.
041
042   This paper considers generating adversarial examples with a constant fixed attack strength. The
043   motivation arises from observations regarding existing attack methods during multi-step attacks. To
044   illustrate our observations, we conducted an experiment using black-box attacks across four methods
045
      (Projected Gradient Decent (PGD) Madry et al. (2017), Skip Gradient Method (SGM) Wu et al.
      (2020a), Variance Reduction Method (VR) Wu et al. (2018), Interaction Reduction Method (IR) Wang
046
      et al. (2021a)). As we can see from Figure 1 (a), (1) Given an attack method, the actual perturbation
047
      strength (perturbation radius) gradually increases during the multi-step process until it reaches the
048   preset upper limit, or never reaches the upper limit until the end; (2) For each attack method, the
049   increase in transferability is accompanied by the increase in attack strength; (3) Methods having
050   larger perturbation strength achieve higher transferability. These observations lead us to the question:
051   Is the "poor" transferability of a few methods simply due to their low perturbation strength of the
052   adversarial examples? For instance, the PGD method exhibits the lowest transferability, yet it also
053   maintains the lowest perturbation strength, indicating the potential of improving its transferability by
      simply increasing the perturbation strength.

069
      Motivated by this observation, we propose the Fixed Strength Optimization (FSO) method to directly
070   optimize adversarial examples on the ε-neighboring sphere of the original sample, where the attacking
071   strength ε is the norm of the perturbation (as depicted in Figure 1 (b)). In the optimization, we
072   simply use the tangential component of the gradient (or the variants of gradient as proposed in SGM,
073   VR, IR, etc.) to update the adversarial example and use projection operation to keep it on the sphere.
074   Compared to previous multi-step methods, FSO can achieve both faster convergence and higher
075   transferability under the same attack strength. Furthermore, FSO allows for a fairer comparison of
076   different attack methods since the perturbation strength is fixed.
077   In defining adversarial examples, a distance metric is required to quantify similarity. A common
078   choice of the metric would be Lp norm. Here we point out a few facts about the L2 and L∞ norms,
079   which are the most used metric: (1) The optimization in FSO is difficult if the L∞ norm is used,
080   because the tangential component is always zero; (2) A perturbation obtained with a multi-step
081   L∞ attack method is not an optimized perturbation based on L∞ norm. Namely, the perturbation
082   on many pixels are not maximized because the sign of the perturbation on these pixels changes
083   in different steps; (3) Adversarial examples obtained by L2 attack method, while achieve higher
084
      transferability compared to those obtained by L∞ attack method, also maintain greater visual
      impairment. As a general assumption of gradient-based post-hoc interpretability methods Simonyan
085
      et al. (2013); Smilkov et al. (2017), the magnitude of the input-gradient highlights task-relevant
086
      features. Coordinates with larger input-gradient magnitude contain more semantic information, thus
087   being more relevant to model predictions than those with smaller magnitude.
088
089   In this work, we propose a combined norm, the L2_∞ norm, to quantify the strength of perturbations.
090
      The combined norm is determined by both the L2 norm and L∞ norm. As a consequence, the
      combined norm helps to inhibit perturbations on large input-gradient directions compared to attacks
091
      based on L2 norm. This is helpful to suppress attacks in the direction of semantic information. The
092
      new norm also helps to inhibit strong perturbations on the small input-gradient directions compared
093   to attacks based on L∞ norm. This prevents from introducing strong noise into adversarial examples.
094   As a side benefit, the new norm can be smoothly incorporated in FSO.
095
096
      Our contributions are summarized as follows.
097   - We introduce FSO to generate adversarial examples in an optimization fashion under fixed attacking
098    strength. FSO generates adversarial examples in only a few iteration steps, whereas improves the
099    state-of-the-art transferability benchmarks.
100   - We introduce combined norm that suppresses the shortcomings of L2 and L∞ norms in generating
101    adversarial examples. By incorporating the combined norm into FSO, perturbations in our numerical
102    experiments demonstrate enhanced attack transferability and high imperceptibility.

      2.1   M ULTI - STEP ATTACKS AND A DVERSARIAL TRANSFERABILITY.
111
112   Given a clean example xori with class label y and a target DNN model f , the goal of an adversary is
113   to find an adversarial example xadv that fools the network into making an incorrect prediction (i.e.
114   f (x adv ) ̸= y), while still remaining in the ε-ball centered at x ori (i.e. ∥x adv − x ori ∥ ≤ ε ). Existing
115   adversarial attacks can be broadly categorized into two types: white-box attacks Goodfellow et al.
116   (2014); Madry et al. (2017); Kurakin et al. (2018); Papernot et al. (2016); Su et al. (2019); Carlini &
117   Wagner (2017); Szegedy et al. (2016); Chen et al. (2018); Modas et al. (2019), where the adversary
118
      has full access to the target model, and black-box attacks Liu et al. (2016); Ilyas et al. (2018a); Chen
      et al. (2017a); Bhagoji et al. (2018); Papernot et al. (2017); Bai et al. (2020), where the adversary
119
      has no information about the target model. A specific type of black-box attack leverages adversarial
120
      transferability Wu et al. (2020a; 2018); Xie et al. (2019); Dong et al. (2018), where adversarial
121   perturbations generated on a source DNN are transferred to other target DNNs.
122
123    Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) Goodfellow et al. (2014). FGSM lays the groundwork for
124
      gradient-based adversarial attacks by perturbing clean example xori by the amount of ε along the
      gradient direction:

209   The generation of adversarial examples can be obtained by the following optimization equation:

      Multi-step gradient-based attack methods are not efficient in performing the optimization in Equation
217   (2). First, as shown in Figure 1 (a), although the optimized adversarial example is usually on the
218   ε-sphere, multi-step methods requires many iteration steps to progressively increase the perturbation
219   strength2 ; second, the tangential component (to the ε-sphere) of the perturbation direction g t is
220   usually small compared to the normal component, which lead to slow convergence of the multi-step
221   methods. In particular, when the L∞ norm is used, adversarial examples obtained by multi-step
222   methods usually do not stay on the ε-sphere, since the sign of the perturbation direction g t on many
223   pixels changes from step to step.
224   Next, we propose a method, Fixed Strength Optimization (FSO), to directly optimize adversarial
225   examples on the ε-sphere, i.e., ∥x adv − x ori ∥ ≡ ε. Existing attack methods can be naturally
226   incorporated to determine the tangential perturbation direction to update adversarial examples. FSO
227   significantly reduces the number of iterations while improving the transferability.

394
      1. Each curve increases with the iteration step and converge to a plateau in a few (≤ 10) steps.
      This shows the fast convergence rate of FSO. As a comparison, the multi-step attack shown in
395
      Figure 3 requires more than 20 iteration steps to converge to the plateau. Thus FSO can accelerate the
396
      convergence (by 2 ∼ 3 folds) and reduce iteration steps. Compared to results shown in Figure 1 (a),
397   where the attack strength may increases slowly, the acceleration rate can be even larger.
398
399   2. The transferability increases with m for all test networks. For the first three target networks,
400
      m = 2 is sufficiently large that further increment of m only leads to insignificant increase of the
      transferability. Since larger m means larger allowed L∞ norm of the perturbation while the largest
401
      possible L2 norm remains unchanged, the monotonically increasing property is natural. It is important
402
      to see that m ∼ 2 or 3 is sufficient to achieve nearly best performance. This means that we can still
403   control the L∞ norm of the perturbation well (≤ mε∞ ), thus suppressing strong attack of semantic
404   information. The small saturation number of m allows us to achieve balanced attack–with both weak
405   semantic attack and weak noise introduction.
406
      In summary, we can achieve fast convergence and balanced attack using FSO under the L2_∞ norm.

514   6   CONCLUSION
515
516   We developed a Fixed Strength Optimization (FSO) method to generate adversarial examples. In
517   FSO, the optimization of adversarial examples are directly performed under the constraint of fixed
518   perturbation strength, where the strength is defined as the norm of the perturbation. FSO can
519   significantly improve both the convergence speed and transferability. The perturbation is well
520   optimized in a few (≤ 10) steps. We also propose a combined norm, the L2_∞ norm, for adversarial
521   examples to balance the attack on semantic information and introduction of noise. By incorporating
522
      the combined norm into FSO, our numerical experiments show improved attack transferability and
      high imperceptibility of perturbations.
