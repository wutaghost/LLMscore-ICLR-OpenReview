009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present CycleVTON, a cycle-consistent diffusion-based virtual try-on frame-
012            work. Unlike existing methods that rely on a single try-on network, our model
013            consists of two conjugated networks. In addition to the regular try-on network,
014            we design a clothing extraction network that extracts the clothing worn by the
015            person and standardizes it into a front-facing format. These two networks are
016
               symmetrical, enabling alignment between the generated dressed human and real
               images of dressed human, as well as between the extracted clothing and its front-
017
               facing ground truth. This cycle-consistent optimization strategy allows for en-
018
               hanced retention of clothing textures and structures, ensuring a more realistic and
019            accurate clothing generation in virtual try-on scenarios. Moreover, the conjugated
020            network structure not only supports traditional virtual try-on but also allows flex-
021            ible clothing extraction and clothing exchange between different individuals. The
022            experiments on VITON-HD demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In recent years, with the rapid development of online shopping, virtual try-on technology has gar-
      nered significant attention as an important technological application. The goal of this technology is
028
      to realistically synthesize given clothing images onto the corresponding regions of a person’s body.
029
      This task poses significant challenges due to the need to maintain the individual characteristics of
030   the person, retain the style of the clothing along with its details, and ensure that the virtual try-on
031   appears natural and well-fitted.
032
033
      Early virtual try-on systems (Han et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018) primarily relied on Thin-Plate
      Spline (TPS) transformations to achieve clothing deformation. While this approach could roughly
034
      fit clothings to the human body and worked for simple poses, it struggled to handle more complex
035
      human poses. To more accurately predict clothing deformation, subsequent research (Xie et al.,
036   2023) introduced appearance flow estimation to model non-rigid deformations, addressing complex
037   poses by predicting dense pixel-level correspondences. Although such warping-based virtual try-on
038   methods have achieved impressive results, particularly in retaining pattern details due to pixel-level
039   predictions, they often produce unnatural results and artifacts when dealing with occlusions and
040   intricate human poses, see Figure 1 (a).
041
      Due to remarkable capabilities in visual generation demonstrated by diffusion models (Ho et al.,
042   2020; Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), they have been increasingly
043   applied to virtual try-on tasks. Compared to warping-based methods, methods (Gou et al., 2023;
044   Morelli et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Choi et al.,
045   2024; Zhang et al., 2024) based on diffusion models can handle complex human poses, achieving
046   more natural try-on effects. However, diffusion models generate the dressed person progressively
047   from noise, and their supervision signal is limited to the original clothing, rather than using the
048   deformed clothing. Instead, the model implicitly transforms the original clothing, enabling an adap-
049   tive modification. This often results in the loss of fine details, as shown in Figure 1(b). While many
050
      efforts (Xu et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2024) have sought to preserve clothing details, challenges like
      texture duplication and layout alterations persist, particularly with intricate patterns. Consequently,
051
      maintaining the structural layout and texture fidelity of clothing remains a critical challenge.
052
053   In this work, we observe that virtual try-on based on diffusion models performs better when the
      clothing is flat or presented in a near-frontal view, accurately restoring the clothing’s detailed texture

089   virtual try-on with clothing take-off networks, allowing not only traditional virtual try-on but also

093
      In summary, our contributions are as follows:
094
095   (1) We propose a new cycle-consistent diffusion-based framework for virtual try-on, named Cy-
096   cleVTON. This framework not only enhances the accuracy of clothing generation and improves
097   robustness to clothing deformation and human pose variation but also provides greater flexibility to
098
      meet diverse clothing generation needs.
099   (2) We introduce two complementary networks in CycleVTON. Alongside the conventional try-
100   on network, we innovatively incorporate a clothing extraction network that extracts clothing from
101   the human body and normalizes it to a frontal view. We propose a cycle-consistency optimization
102   strategy, which aligns the generated dressed human with real images and the extracted clothing with
103   its true frontal appearance. This approach expands the model’s supervision signals and enhances its
104
      performance.
105   (3) We conduct extensive experiments between the proposed method and state-of-the-art virtual try-
106   on approaches. The results demonstrate that CycleVTON achieves superior performance on the
107   widely used VITON-HD benchmark. Furthermore, we showcase the model’s capability to swap
      clothing between different individuals through the cycle design.

      2.1   WARPING -BASED V IRTUAL T RY- ON
111
112   arping-based virtual try-on methods typically involve two main stages: clothing warping and try-
113   on synthesis. Early approaches, such as those introduced in (Han et al., 2018), utilized Thin Plate
114   Spline (TPS) for deforming clothing to fit the human body. This method employed a coarse-to-fine
115   strategy, initially generating a rough try-on result with an encoder-decoder architecture, which was
116   then refined using TPS to accurately map the clothing onto the target person. Subsequent methods
117   have focused either on enhancing TPS’s deformation capabilities (Fincato et al., 2021; 2022; Li
118   et al., 2023) or addressing the limitations within the try-on workflow (Wang et al., 2018; Yang et al.,
119
      2020; Ge et al., 2021a). To improve TPS performance, Minar et al. (2020) redesigned the process
      with optimized input representations and refined training loss. Similarly, Wang et al. (2018) refined
120
      the TPS training process by introducing a new Geometric Matching Module (GMM), moving away
121
      from calculating key point correspondences and instead matching in-store clothing to the target
122   body shape. Other approaches have sought to address specific challenges, such as misalignment and
123   occlusion. Ge et al. (2021a) introduced a disentangled cycle-consistent try-on network to mitigate
124   misalignment between clothing and the body, avoiding the need for paired clothing-body data. For
125   occlusion issues, Yang et al. (2020) proposed predicting the semantic layout of the reference image
126   post-try-on to identify which parts should be retained.
127
      However, the limited deformation capabilities of TPS make it challenging to handle complex poses.
128   To address this, many optical flow-based virtual try-on methods (Bai et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2021b;
129   Han et al., 2019; He et al., 2022; Shim et al., 2024) have been proposed, which calculate pixel-level
130   deformation fields for clothing, enabling them to handle complex deformations effectively. Despite
131   these advancements, these methods still suffer from misalignment caused by explicit deformations,
132   often producing unnatural visual try-on results when dealing with complicated human poses. Addi-
133   tionally, explicit deformation fails to account for real occlusion during the try-on process, leading to
134   artifacts like clothing textures incorrectly appearing behind the neck.
135
136   2.2   D IFFUSION -BASED V IRTUAL T RY- ON
137
138   With the remarkable success of diffusion models in visual generation (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
139
      2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), their application in virtual try-on has garnered
      increasing research attention. Models like LaDI-VTON (Morelli et al., 2023) and DCI-VTON (Gou
140
      et al., 2023) leverage diffusion models to virtually try on clothing that has been explicitly deformed
141
      to fit the human body. TryonDiffusion (Zhu et al., 2023), utilizing two parallel UNet networks,
142   demonstrated on large-scale datasets that diffusion models can implicitly warp clothing foregrounds
143   for virtual try-on. OOTDiffusion (Xu et al., 2024) and IDM-VTON (Choi et al., 2024) fine-tune
144   and denoise clothing networks with the same UNet structure to preserve more accurate clothing
145   details. MMTryon (Zhang et al., 2024) combines diffusion models in virtual try-on to consider
146   more modality information and clothing details. Despite these advancements, diffusion model-based
147   approaches still face challenges in preserving fine clothing details and largely depend on front-view
148   clothing. In this work, we revisit the diffusion-based framework for virtual try-on, aiming to enhance
149   detail preservation through cyclically extracting features from the generated results.

209   3.2   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
210
211   Our work aims to optimize the performance of diffusion-based virtual try-on framework, improving
212
      the retention of texture and structure in virtual try-on systems. To achieve this, we propose a new
      framework called CycleTryon. Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of the framework, which
213
      consists of two main modules: the virtual try-on network and the clothing extraction network.
214
215   The virtual try-on network uses standard frontal-view clothing as input, rendering it onto the corre-
      sponding regions of the human body. The clothing extraction network, on the other hand, extracts

216
      clothing data from images of dressed humans and standardizes it into a frontal-view format. These
217   two networks complement each other: the virtual try-on network can use the output of the clothing
218   extraction network as input, and vice versa, forming a cycle structure that flexibly supports various
219   clothing-related applications. Leveraging this cyclic nature, we introduce a cycle-consistent opti-
220   mization strategy. This strategy not only allows for supervision using dressed human images, as
221   in traditional virtual try-on models, but also enables the use of frontal clothing images for supervi-
222   sion. This enhances the ability of both networks to retain information from the original clothing and
223   human images, thereby improving the overall performance of the model.

225   3.3   V IRTUAL T RY- ON N ETWORK
226
227   As illustrated in Figure 2, the virtual try-on network aims to integrate clothing information into
228   the masked clothing area of the human body image while preserving the original body pose and
229   image background. This network comprises two main components: ReferenceNet1 and denoising
230   UNet1. The ReferenceNet1 takes standard frontal-view clothing images as input, combined with
231
      CLIP features, to provide more refined clothing details to the denoising UNet1, thereby enhancing
      the preservation of these details. The denoising UNet1 primarily receives conditional noise con-
232
      taining human information as input and uses both the CLIP features and the clothing features from
233
      ReferenceNet1 to progressively denoise and incorporate the clothing details into the corresponding
234   regions of the human body.
235
236   For the CLIP features used as conditions, we employ CLIP’s image encoder to convert the frontal-
237
      view clothing images into image embeddings. These embeddings are then injected into the diffusion
      model through cross-attention to provide color and texture information.
238
239   For the conditional noise, we generate it by combining the human image, the masked human body
240   image, the body pose image obtained via OpenPose (Cao et al., 2017), and the human parsing image
241   obtained using Densepose (Güler et al., 2018). Specifically, the human image, masked human body
242   image, and human parsing image are encoded using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma,
243
      2013) to generate latent variables. The latent variable from the human image is further perturbed
      with noise to create latent noise. These latent variables are then concatenated with the mask image
244
      of the clothing area, which is resized through interpolation, forming a 13-channel tensor. Here,
245
      each latent variable has 4 channels, and the mask image has 1 channel. The OpenPose image is
246   processed using PoseGuider (Hu, 2024) to extract 4-channel human body pose features. Finally, the
247   13-channel tensor is passed through a convolutional layer and then added to the body pose features,
248   producing the final conditional noise with 4 channels.
249
250
      3.4   C LOTHING E XTRACTION N ETWORK
251
252   The goal of our clothing extraction network is to capture clothing information from human images,
253   thereby creating a standardized clothing representation across various virtual try-on tasks, includ-
254   ing traditional cloth-to-human try-on and more flexible human-to-human try-on. This network also
255   includes both ReferenceNet2 and denoising UNet2. However, unlike the virtual try-on network,
256   ReferenceNet2 uses dressed human images as input, providing denoising UNet2 with more accu-
257   rate and detailed information about distorted clothing. Denoising UNet2 takes conditional noise
258   containing frontal-view posture information as input, leveraging CLIP features with distorted cloth-
259   ing information as a condition. It then progressively removes the noise to extract the frontal-view
260
      clothing details from the distorted clothing.
261   For the CLIP features used as conditions, we utilize CLIP’s image encoder to transform the distorted
262   clothing image into an image embedding. For the conditional noise, we generate it by combining
263   the frontal-view clothing image with the clothing pose image. Specifically, the frontal-view clothing
264   image is first encoded using a VAE to obtain latent variables, which are further perturbed to generate
265   latent noise. At the same time, the clothing pose image is processed by PoseGuider (Hu, 2024) to
266
      extract pose features. The latent noise is then passed through a convolutional layer and added to the
      clothing pose features to produce the final conditional noise.
267
268   For clothing pose estimation, the focus is capturing key point information specific to the clothing.
269   To obtain training data for clothing pose, we utilize existing human pose estimation methods (such
      as (Cao et al., 2017)) to predict ten key points on the body that correspond to the upper clothing.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      These key points include the neck, two shoulders, two elbows, two wrists, and four hip points. By
271   extracting the positions of these key points from human images, we can derive the pose information
272   of the clothing worn. Next, we segment the clothing region from the human body to generate
273   an image corresponding to the identified clothing pose. We then pair the clothing image with its
274   associated pose and use an existing clothing landmark predictor (Chen et al., 2023) for training. By
275   providing the predictor with normal clothing images, we can effectively acquire the corresponding
276   pose information.
277   By introducing the clothing extraction network, our model can not only utilize the paired clothing-
278   human images provided in the dataset but also extract additional clothing images from humans,
279   thereby enhancing the training of the virtual try-on network. This approach provides extra super-
280   vised training, significantly improving the model’s performance in terms of accuracy, robustness,
281   and generalization. These improvements enable the virtual try-on network to achieve higher accu-
282   racy and better clothing fitting, resulting in more effective and precise clothing rendering.

354   4.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETTING
355
356   Dataset: Our experiments primarily utilize the publicly available virtual try-on dataset, VITON-
357   HD (Choi et al., 2021), which comprises high-resolution in-shop clothing images that include both
358
      paired and unpaired model images. Derived from VITON, the VITON-HD dataset comprises 13,679
      pairs of frontal female and upper-body clothing images, divided into training and testing sets with
359
      11,647 and 2,032 pairs, respectively.
360
361   Evaluation Metrics: Similar to previous works (Choi et al., 2021), virtual try-on involves paired
362   and unpaired settings. In the paired setting, the original clothing image is used to reconstruct the
363   original person image, while in the unpaired setting, the person’s clothing is changed to the given
364
      clothing. To assess the performance of our method, we employ distinct evaluation metrics for each
      setting, as indicated in the existing literature. For the paired setting, we utilize two widely-used
365
      metrics: Structural Similarity (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Sim-
366
      ilarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018). In contrast, for the unpaired setting, we evaluate our model
367   using Frechet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) and Kernel Inception Distance (KID)
368   (Bińkowski et al., 2018).

506   5   CONCLUSION
507
508   In this paper, we present the CycleVTON architecture, a significant advancement in virtual try-on
509
      technology that effectively addresses the challenges of maintaining the identity of the model while
      accurately representing clothing details. We design a clothing extraction network that standard-
510
      izes various clothing conditions into a unified format, and enhances both texture preservation and
511
      structural integrity during the try-on process.
512
513   Additionally, the cycle-consistent optimization strategy developed for this framework promotes mu-
514   tual learning between the clothing extraction and virtual try-on networks, allowing for improved
515
      retention of the texture and structure in the final synthesized images. Our experiments demonstrate
      that CycleVTON outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, as evidenced by superior qualitative
516
      and quantitative results on the VITON-HD dataset.
517
518   Overall, CycleVTON not only contributes to the ongoing advancements in virtual try-on technology
519   but also sets the stage for further research aimed at refining these methods. Future investigations
520   could explore integrating additional data sources and enhancing the model’s adaptability to diverse
521
      clothing types and poses, thereby pushing the boundaries of realistic virtual try-on experiences in
      online shopping.
