009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Confounders are extraneous variable that affect both the input and the target, re-
012            sulting in spurious correlations and biased predictions. Learning feature represen-
013            tations that are invariant to confounders remains a significant challenge in contin-
014            ual learning. To remove the influence of confounding variables from intermediate
015            feature representations, we introduce the Recursive Metadata Normalization (R-
016            MDN) layer, which can be integrated into any stage within deep neural networks
017            (DNNs). R-MDN performs statistical regression via the recursive least squares
018
               algorithm to maintain and continually update an internal model state with respect
               to changing distributions of data and confounding variables. Since R-MDN op-
019
               erates on the level of individual examples, it is compatible with state-of-the-art
020
               architectures like vision transformers. Our experiments demonstrate that R-MDN
021            promotes equitable predictions across population groups, both within static learn-
022            ing and across different stages of continual learning, by reducing catastrophic for-
023            getting caused by confounder effects changing over time.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Confounders are extraneous variables that influence both the input and the target, resulting in spuri-
029   ous correlations that distort the true underlying relationships within the data (Greenland & Morgen-
030   stern, 2001; Ferrari et al., 2020). These spurious correlations introduce bias into learning algorithms,
031   causing the feature representations learned by models, such as deep neural networks (DNNs), to be
032   skewed (Buolamwini & Gebru, 2018; Obermeyer et al., 2019; Oakden-Rayner et al., 2020; Chen
033
      et al., 2021; Seyyed-Kalantari et al., 2020).
034   This problem is particularly prevalent in medical studies, such as those related to brain development
035   (Casey et al., 2018), biological and behavioral health (Petersen et al., 2010; Brown et al., 2015), and
036   dermatoscopic images (Tschandl et al., 2018), which are often confounded by demographic factors
037   like age, sex, and socioeconomic background, and factors related to data acquisition. For example,
038   a DNN trained to diagnose neurodegenerative disorders from brain MRIs might disproportionately
039
      rely on age instead of the underlying pathology. This may occur either due to the disease causing
      accelerated aging or in cases where there is a selection bias, i.e., having different distributions in the
040
      diseased cohort versus the control group. This can lead to models that are inequitable and inaccurate
041
      for certain populations (Rao et al., 2017; Seyyed-Kalantari et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Adeli et al.,
042   2020b; Lu et al., 2021; Vento et al., 2022). Given these challenges, it is crucial to develop techniques
043   that enable DNNs to focus on task-relevant features while remaining invariant to confounders, which
044   are often available as auxiliary information or metadata in such datasets.
045
      Methods such as BR-Net (Adeli et al., 2020a), MDN (Lu et al., 2021), P-MDN (Vento et al., 2022),
046
      and RegBN (Ghahremani Boozandani & Wachinger, 2024) have been previously proposed to ad-
047
      dress the challenges posed by confounders when training DNNs. There are, however, a multitude of
048   situations where some of them cannot be applied, such as MDN, that requires estimating batch-level
049   information, in association with vision transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), and within the context
050   of continual learning where one cannot look at future data for training. This continuum of data
051   may arise in various contexts. For example, in a cross-sectional study (Tschandl et al., 2018), the
052   training process is divided into distinct stages, with each stage featuring different data distributions.
053   Conversely, in a longitudinal study (Petersen et al., 2010; Brown et al., 2015; Casey et al., 2018),
      the system does not have access to all data at the outset; instead, new data—such as patient visits

075   creates a gap, as there is a need for algorithms that effectively and explicitly remove the influence of
076   confounding variables under changing data or confounder distributions.
077
      To this end, we propose Recursive Metadata Normalization (R-MDN) to remove (normalize) the
078
      effects of the confounding variables from the learned features of a DNN through statistical regres-
079   sion. Specifically, R-MDN leverages the recursive least squares (RLS) algorithm (Albert & Sittler,
080   1965), which has been widely utilized in adaptive filtering, control systems for reinforcement learn-
081   ing, and online learning scenarios (Xu et al., 2002; Gao et al., 2020). R-MDN is a layer that can
082   be inserted at any stage within a DNN. The use of statistical linear regression is motivated by their
083   success in de-confounding learned feature representations (McNamee, 2005; Brookhart et al., 2010;
084   Pourhoseingholi et al., 2012; Adeli et al., 2018). The assumption of an underlying linear relation-
085   ship between confounders and learned features arises from two key considerations: (1) decisions
086   made by nonlinear models are often challenging to interpret, and (2) sufficiently powerful nonlinear
087
      models can extract almost any arbitrary variable from the information present in the features, even
      if those variables are not explicitly represented. R-MDN operates by iteratively updating its internal
088
      parameters—consisting of regression coefficients and an estimated inverse covariance matrix, which
089
      together form an internal model state—based on previously computed values whenever new data is
090   received. This state represents the current understanding of the relationship between the learned fea-
091   tures and the confounders, enabling the model to adapt dynamically as new data flows in. R-MDN,
092   therefore, applies to static learning, where such a sequence of uninterrupted examples (minibatches)
093   come from a single stationary distribution.
094
      By design, a key advantage of R-MDN is in the context of continual learning, when each training
095
      stage consists of data drawn from different stationary distributions. This continuum of data can again
096   be understood as a sequence of uninterrupted examples that a model learns from over time. Here, R-
097   MDN does not need to train a stage-specific network. Instead, the internal state can be continuously
098   updated over time as the model progresses through successive training stages. Therefore, only a
099   single network equipped with R-MDN layers needs to be trained on the entire dataset, with the model
100   being able to generalize across stages (data or confounder distributions)—both in performance and
101   the ability to remove the effects of the confounders (see figure 1).
102   In summary, we propose R-MDN—a flexible normalization layer that is able to residualize the ef-
103   fects of confounding variables from learned feature representations of a DNN by leveraging the
104   recursive least squares closed-form solution. It can do so under varying data or confounder distribu-
105   tions, making it an effective algorithm for both static and continual learning (sections 4.1, 4.2). We
106   provide a theoretical foundation to our approach (section 3), and empirically validate it in different
107   experimental setups and DNN architectures (sections 4.1.1, 4.1.2, 4.2.1, 4.2.2). We find that R-MDN
      helps in making equitable predictions for population groups (such as boys and girls) not only within a

      single cross-sectional study (section 4.1.2), but also across different stages of training during contin-
109   ual learning, by minimizing catastrophic forgetting of confounder effects over time (section 4.2.2).
110   Moreover, R-MDN generalizes well to examples where the influence from confounding variables is
111   absent (section 4.2.1).

114   2   R ELATED W ORKS
115
116   Widely used techniques such as batch (Ioffe, 2015), layer (Ba et al., 2016), instance (Ulyanov,
117   2016), and group (Wu & He, 2018) normalization standardize intermediate feature representations
118   of DNNs, i.e., they normalize them to have zero mean and unit standard deviation across different
119   dimensions of the data. They do not explicitly remove the effects of confounding variables from
120   these features.
121   Prior works have proposed methods for learning confounder-invariant feature representations based
122   on domain-adversarial training (Liu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018; Sadeghi et al., 2019; Adeli et al.,
123   2020a), closed-form statistical linear regression analysis (Lu et al., 2021), penalty-approach to gra-
124   dient descent (Vento et al., 2022), regularization (Ghahremani Boozandani & Wachinger, 2024),
125   disentanglement (Liu et al., 2021; Tartaglione et al., 2021), counterfactual generative modeling
126   (Neto, 2020; Lahiri et al., 2022), fair inference (Baharlouei et al., 2020), and distribution match-
127   ing (Baktashmotlagh et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2018). Among these, distribution matching techniques
128   do not particularly remove the influence of individual confounders from learned features. Adver-
129
      sarial training, on the other hand, typically involves a confounder-prediction network applied to
      pre-logits feature representations, with an adversarial loss used to minimize the correlation between
130
      features and confounders. However, adversarial approaches struggle to scale effectively when faced
131
      with multiple confounding variables. Likewise, disentanglement, fair inference, and counterfactual
132   generative modeling techniques only partially remove confounder effects from a single layer of the
133   network (Zhao et al., 2020; Vento et al., 2022).
134
135

      tistical regression analysis, is a popular technique. MDN is a layer that can be inserted into the DNN
136
      to residualize confounder effects from intermediate learned features. It does so through the ordinary
137
      least squares algorithm, wherein it computes a closed form solution for the expression z = Xβ + r

      matrix, β are the regression coefficients, and r is the component in the learned features invariant to
140
      the confounder. To work with minibatches  of data, MDN re-formulates the closed-form solution as

      ing. Not only does this pre-computation step require a space and computation overhead, employing
144   batch-level statistics during training precludes it from being used with vision transformers, where
145   computation is parallelized over individual examples. In the context of continual learning, where
146   we might not have all data at the outset of training, MDN would have to repeatedly re-calculate Σ−1
147   whenever new data comes in. Even if we did have all data at the outset, as in a cross-sectional study,
148   a “look-ahead” operation would be required to have MDN compute Σ−1 with respect to data from
149   all stages of training.
150   To alleviate issues around the use of batch statistics, a penalty-approach to MDN (P-MDN) was
151   proposed (Vento et al., 2022). The authors of P-MDN observe that MDN solves a bi-level nested
152   optimization problem by having the network learn task-relevant features while also being invariant
153   to the confounder. The authors suggest to solve a proxy objective minβ,W L(φ(z − Xβ), y) +
154   γL∗ (z; X), where φ is the non-linear computation to be performed within the network after the
155   current layer, y are the target labels, and γ is a penalty parameter that trades off task learning with
156   confounder-free feature learning. Now, P-MDN is able to work with arbitrary batch sizes. However,
157   as we see in this work, γ becomes very difficult to tune, and optimizing the proxy objective often
158
      leads to non-robust results with high variance across different seed runs.
159   For continual learning, methods such as those based on regularization (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017),
160   knowledge distillation (Li & Hoiem, 2017), and architectural changes (Rusu et al., 2016; Mallya &
161   Lazebnik, 2018; Bayasi et al., 2024) have been proposed to overcome catastrophic forgetting—the
      phenomenon where DNNs forget information learned in prior training stages when acquiring new

163   by learning task (domain) general features (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2019; Creager et al.,

165   features. While domain-adversarial training and P-MDN still apply to the continual learning setting,

      Say we have N training samples, where the input matrix A ∈ RN ×d , for some dimension d, is
171
      associated with target labels y ∈ RN and information about the confounding variable x̃ ∈ RN . Let
172   the output after a particular layer of a deep network be the features z ∈ RN . Our goal is to obtain the
173   residual r from the expression z = x̃β˜x + y β˜y + r = Xβ + r, where X = [x̃ y] and β = [β˜x ; β˜y ]
174   is a set of learnable parameters. In other words, the learned features z are first projected onto the
175   subspace spanned by the confounding variable and the labels, with the term x̃β˜x corresponding to
176   the component in z explained by the confounder and y β˜y to that explained by the labels. We want
177   to remove the influence of x̃ from z while preserving the variance related to the labels. We thus
178   compute the composite β as explained below, but obtain the residual r = z − x̃β˜x ; i.e., only with
179   respect to β˜x . This residual explains the components in the intermediate features irrelevant to the
180   confounder but relevant to the labels, and thus for the classification task.

      3.2     F ROM BATCH TO LAYER STATISTICS
217
218   Remember that one of the drawbacks of MDN is that it has to compute and store batch-level statistics
219   Σ with respect to the entire training data prior to training. Then, it uses this information along with
220   computing batch-level estimates for each minibatch to residualize the features. The requirement of
221   such batch-level estimates makes MDN unsuitable for modern SOTA architectures like vision trans-
222   formers, wherein computations happen in parallel over all examples in a mini-batch. Incorporating
223   an MDN module will inherently require an aggregation step for batch-level statistics to be com-
224   puted, resulting in a significant computational overhead. R-MDN, on the other hand, operates on the
225
      level of individual examples in a minibatch. That is why it works in a purely online regime, as well
      as can be inserted in vision transformers to residualize intermediate learned features of the system.

228   3.3     R EGULARIZATION
229
230
      Notice that R-MDN can adapt quickly to changing data distributions over time due to its iterative
      nature, being especially helpful for continual learning where a continuum of data comes from several
231
      different stationary distributions. However, this iterative nature of the method might sometimes lead
232
      to it being too sensitive to small changes in the data. Random fluctuations, or data noise, can lead to
233   unstable updates to R-MDN parameters. Therefore, we add a regularization term λI to P (N + B).
234   λ is a hyperparameter that is tuned during training (ablation in suppl. F). This has the effect of
235   smoothing out the updates and stabilizing the residualization process, resulting in some robustness to
236   noise. Additionally, adding this regularization term helps to ensure numerical stability by preventing
237   the computation of an inverse for a matrix that might be singular or ill-conditioned.

358

359
      before, we insert a residualization module after every layer except the last one. In addition to this
360   approach, we establish two additional baselines—one where we use BR-Net, and adversarial training
361   framework, with the same base model as the encoder, and another where we pre-process the input
362   images prior to training by regressing out the influence of confounders directly from the pixel space
363   (hereafter referred to as Pixel-Space Residualization). We set the batch size to be 128, which is the
364   largest that can be fit in GPU memory (see additional details in suppl. D).

367   boys, and it also has a higher true negative rate (table 2). This is because the base model makes
368   use of the pubertal development scores to drive its predictions. On the other hand, R-MDN incurs a
369   modest decrease in performance, but significantly drives down the correlation between the learned
370   features and the confounder for both boys and girls. Moreover, it has the lowest mean difference
371   between the true positive and true negative rates among all evaluated methods (quantified in table
372   2, visualized in figure 9a,b,c), signifying that it is not biased toward children of either sex. Other
373   methods like MDN and P-MDN have a higher prediction accuracy, but either fail to drive down the
374
      correlation between the features and the confounder due to requiring relatively larger batch sizes, or
      remain biased towards girls despite driving the correlation down.
375

377   the model does not use the cerebellum—which is the region mostly confounded by PDS (Adeli et al.,

      4.2        C ONTINUAL L EARNING
406
407
      Here, we move from training on data sampled from a single stationary distribution to training on
408
      a continuum of data by slightly modifying the setting described by Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017):
409   Given a 4-tuple (ai , xi , yi , si ) for i ∈ [N ], where ai ∈ A is the input, xi ∈ X is the confounder,
410                                                                                                                   iid
      yi ∈ Y is the label, and si ∈ S is the training stage descriptor, it satisfies local iid, i.e., (ai , xi , yi ) ∼
411
      Psi (A, X , Y). Our goal is to learn a classifier g : A × S → Y, that is able to predict the label y
412
      associated with an unseen 2-tuple (a, s), where (a, y) ∼ Ps at any point during or after training on
413   the S stages, in a way such that it does not make use of confounder information in x, if any.
414
                                                                                                                                            38

415
416   4.2.1        A C ONTINUUM OF S YNTHETIC DATASETS
417
      We transform the synthetic data from section 4.1.1 into a continuum of data with varying distri-
418
      butions of the confounding variable and main effects. Specifically, we design 3 different datasets,
419
      each with 5 stages of training that arrive sequentially. For stage 1 across all 3 datasets, we start out
420   with the parameter controlling the main effects σA ∈ U(3, 5) for group 1, and ∈ U(4, 6) for group
421   2. We use the same distributions for σB (that controls the magnitude of the confounding variable).
422   This means that predictions kernels (i.e., those associated with true discrimination cues) are more
423   “intense” (have a higher magnitude) for images in group 2 than in group 1. Over “time”, the distribu-
424   tions of either the confounding variable, main effects, or both change in a way that emphasize biased
425   learning of a classifier that makes uses of confounder information for discrimination. A complete
426   description of the 3 datasets is presented in suppl. C.
427   To quantify knowledge transfer, we define the Backward Transfer distance (BWTd) and Forward
428   Transfer distance (FWTd) metrics. These metrics are adapted from BWT and FWT defined in
429   Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017) to work with the setting where a model is expected to achieve certain
430   theoretical accuracies on data from both previous and future stages of training. Say we have a total
431   of S stages. Let Ri,j denote the classification accuracy of the model on stage sj after learning stage
      si . And let Ai denote the theoretical maximum accuracy for stage si . Then,

440   The smaller these metrics, the better the model. While all methods are very good at backwards
441   transfer, R-MDN is better at forward transfer as well (table 3 and figure 8b). This means that even
442   with changing distributions of the confounding variable, R-MDN only “looks at” the main effects for
443   classification, allowing it to learn features that transfer well to later tasks while remaining invariant to
444   the confounder itself. Other methods make use of confounder information to various degrees, pulling
445   their classification accuracy away from A. This is also reflected in R-MDN consistently achieving
446   an accuracy near the theoretical maximum for the test sets of each stage, while also showing the
447   lowest correlation with the confounding variable (figure 8c).
448   We also quantify how different methods generalize to unseen images where the confounder is absent
449   (figure 5). This issue arises in situations where, for example, a model is trained on data from multiple
450   hospitals, with the machine type acting as a confounder, and then tested on data from a completely
451   different hospital with a uniform machine type (Zech et al., 2018). In this scenario, the base model
452   experiences a sharp drop in performance when the distribution of the confounder changes in the
453   test data. Both BR-Net and P-MDN show some resistance to the distribution shift but fail when
454
      the confounder is entirely absent. In contrast, R-MDN maintains consistent performance across all
      distributions.

      4.2.2   HAM10000 D ERMATOSCOPIC S KIN L ESION C LASSIFICATION
457
458   Lastly, we use the HAM10000 dataset to classify 2D dermatoscopic images of pigmented skin le-
459   sions into seven distinct diagnostic categories (Tschandl et al., 2018). The dataset consists of 10015
460   images, which we divide into five training stages. In each stage, the age distribution—the confound-
461   ing variable for this study (read suppl. C)—varies, with younger populations represented in the
462   earlier stages and older populations in the later stages. For each stage, we randomly allocate 80% of
463   the images for training and the remaining 20% for evaluation.
464   In this experiment, we transition from a CNN to a vision transformer as the base architecture, and
465   as the encoder for BR-Net. For R-MDN, we explore three different variants: (A) inserting the R-
466   MDN layer after the self-attention layer in every transformer block, as well as after the pre-logits
467   layer; (B) inserting it at the end of every transformer block and after the pre-logits layer; and (C)
468   inserting it only after the pre-logits layer. For P-MDN, we place the P-MDN layer right after the
469   pre-logits layer. Additionally, we establish three continual learning frameworks as baselines: elastic
470   weight consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) as a regularization method, learning without
471
      forgetting (LwF) (Li & Hoiem, 2017) for knowledge distillation, and PackNet (Mallya & Lazebnik,
      2018), an architectural method that applies iterative pruning.
472
473   Results are summarized in table 4 and visualized in figure 10. While the base model performs
474   decently on the classification task, it exhibits significantly lower backward transfer on earlier stages
475   of training. In contrast, R-MDN not only effectively removes the confounder’s influence from the
476   features, as indicated by a low dcor2 value, but also demonstrates significantly better backward
477
      transfer than the base model. We try to understand why this is by looking at the t-SNE visualizations
      of their features (figure 6). When the base model is trained on the final stage, it learns to clearly
478
      separate feature clusters for each of the seven diagnostic categories. However, it is possible that
479
      this separation is influenced by the stage-specific distribution of the confounding variable, leading
480   to spurious correlations driving cluster separation, and thus poor transfer to previous data. On the
481   other hand, R-MDN also forms feature clusters for the different categories but without introducing
482   the same level of separation. This might be because R-MDN relies on task-relevant information,
483   rather than the confounder, to discriminate between the categories. As a result, R-MDN is able to
484   apply the knowledge learned in the current stage to previous stages of training, improving its overall
485   backward transfer performance. Such backward transfer seems logical, since the classification task
      is the same across all stages, and only the confounder distribution changes.

520       5               C ONCLUSION
521
522       In this work, we presented Recursive Metadata Normalization (R-MDN)—a flexible layer that can
523       be inserted at any stage within deep neural networks to remove the influence of confounding vari-
524
          ables from feature representations. R-MDN leverages the recursive least squares algorithm to op-
          erate at the level of individual examples, enabling it to adapt to changing data and confounder
525
          distributions in continual learning. It also promotes equitable outcomes across population groups                                                                                                48


526
          and mitigates the catastrophic forgetting of confounder effects over time. As a direction for future
527       work, R-MDN could be adapted and evaluated on datasets beyond medical contexts, such as video
528       streams and audio signals, where confounding variables like environmental noise, lighting condi-
529       tions, camera angles, or speaker accents might introduce spurious correlations in the data and bias
530       the learning algorithm.

      B    C OMPUTATIONAL AND M EMORY C OMPLEXITY
919
920
      MDN, P-MDN, and R-MDN each have their tradeoffs in terms of computational complexity, mem-
921   ory complexity, and the extent to which the influence of the confounder is removed from the learned
922   feature representations. As demonstrated in this work, R-MDN empirically works better than both
923   MDN and P-MDN. The asymptotic complexity of each is presented here.
924
      Say there are N training examples, broken into batches of size B. Let the confounder matrix X
925
      have a shape N × p, where p is associated with the number of confounders, the target, and a bias of
926
      1, and the intermediate learned feature representations have a size of N × h.
927
928   Firstly, MDN internally uses the linear least squares estimator, which requires pre-computing the
929   matrix Σ = X ⊤ X in O(N p2 ) steps. Inverting this p × p matrix further requires O(p3 ) steps. Then,
                                                                                 ⊤
930   for every batch of information during training, a batch level estimate X z is produced, where the
931   (·) operation refers to a batch instead of the entire training data. This takes O(Bph) steps. Post-
932   multiplying this p × h matrix with Σ−1 requires O(p2 h) steps. If computations over batches of
933   information occur E times, the total computational complexity becomes O(p3 + N p2 + E(p2 h +
934   Bph)). In terms of memory complexity, a p × p Σ−1 needs to be stored, along with the residual
935
      model parameters β of size p × h.
936   For R-MDN, computations only occur over batches of information. In memory, residual model
937   parameters β of size p × h and an estimate of the inverse covariance matrix P of size p × p are
938   required. For every processing iteration, computing the Kalman gain K requires O(Bp2 ) steps
939   for P X ⊤ , O(B 2 p + Bp2 ) steps for XP X ⊤ , O(B 3 ) for inverting this latter matrix, and O(B 2 p)
940   steps for multiplying the matrices together. Updating P using KX requires O(B 2 p) steps. And
941
      finally, updating β requires computing Ke in O(Bph) steps. The total computational complexity
      turns out to be O(E(B 3 + B 2 p + Bp2 + Bph)) steps. Empirically, R-MDN works best with small
942
      batch sizes B, showing very fast convergence rates, and having a computational complexity that
943
      is independent of the size of the training dataset. This becomes important for continual learning,
944   especially longitudinal studies, where data collected over several years or decades can prohibit the
945   use of MDN.
946
947
      P-MDN does not use a closed-form solution to linear statistical regression. Instead it uses gradient
      descent to optimize a proxy objective. Thus, the only memory complexity stems from storing β
948
      parameters of size p × h. The computational complexity is dominated by the number of iterations
949
      required to navigate the proxy loss landscape, with results that are not often robust with high variance
950   across runs.

       Here, we quantify how task performance and the ability to learn confounder-free feature represen-
1550
       tations change with different number of training epochs per stage; i.e., with the number of times
1551
       every example from the training data is presented to the system. We observe that R-MDN is the
1552   only method that is able to remove the influence of the confounder from the learned features for

1557   BR-Net and P-MDN are decent methods for continual learning, but they require the same training
1558   examples to be seen multiple times in order to drive high prediction scores and remove confounder
1559   influence from learned features.
