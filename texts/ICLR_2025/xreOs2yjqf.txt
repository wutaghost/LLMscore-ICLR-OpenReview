011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The recent advancements in text-to-image generative models have been remarkable.
014            Yet, the field suffers from a lack of evaluation metrics that accurately reflect the
015            performance of these models, particularly lacking fine-grained metrics that can
016
               guide the optimization of the models. In this paper, we propose E VAL A LIGN, a
               metric characterized by its accuracy, stability, and fine granularity. Our approach
017
               leverages the capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) pre-
018
               trained on extensive data. We develop evaluation protocols that focus on two
019            key dimensions: image faithfulness and text-image alignment. Each protocol
020            comprises a set of detailed, fine-grained instructions linked to specific scoring
021            options, enabling precise manual scoring of the generated images. We supervised
022            fine-tune (SFT) the MLLM to align with human evaluative judgments, resulting in a
023            robust evaluation model. Our evaluation across 24 text-to-image generation models
024            demonstrate that E VAL A LIGN not only provides superior metric stability but also
025            aligns more closely with human preferences than existing metrics, confirming its
026            effectiveness and utility in model assessment. We will make the code, data, and
027
               pre-trained models publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Text-to-image models, such as DALL·E series (Ramesh et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023), Imagen (Sa-
033
      haria et al., 2022), and Stable Diffusion (Podell et al., 2023), have significantly impacted various
      domains such as entertainment, design, and education, by enabling high-quality image generation.
034
      These technologies not only advance the field of text-to-image generation but also bloom applications
035
      such as video generation (Blattmann et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023d; Tan et al., 2024b), image edit-
036   ing (Song et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023c) and human image generation (Wang
037   et al., 2024). Despite achieving incredible progress, the evaluation methods in this area are far from
038   flawless and suffer heavily from data bias, as they are mainly trained on real images but are employed
039   to evaluate synthesized images.
040
      Since human-based evaluations are considerably costly in money and time, existing evaluation
041
      methods are primarily based on pretrained models, which are trained on real images. However,
042   the trained real images are generated by humans and high in image faithfulness and text-image
043   alignment because of their generation essence. Meanwhile, the evaluated images are synthesized by
044   text-to-image models and encounter problems such as low image faithfulness or text-image alignment,
045   constrained by the performance of generative models.
046
      We dub the gap between the training data and the evaluated data as data bias, which may cause the
047
      evaluation models perform ill-suited on text-to-image evaluation. Because of the data bias, existing
048   text-to-image evaluation methods performs poorly in synthesized image evaluations. Unfortunately,
049   during our preliminary observation, nearly every synthesized images contain visual elements with low
050   image faithfulness or text-image alignment, emphasize their significance on evaluation performance.
051   Notably, there are also some works such as HPSv2 (Wu et al., 2023b) and PickScore Kirstain et al.
052   (2024), where their evaluation models are trained synthesized images. However, in their evaluation
053   settings, the utilized synthesized images are treated as real images as they don’t explicitly recognize
      the problem of synthesized images with low image faithfulness.

054
      In view of these issues, we propose E VAL A LIGN, a comprehensive, fine-grained and interpretable
055   metric on text-to-image model assessing with low cost but high accuracy. To build E VAL A LIGN,
056   we first curate a dataset composed of fine-grained human feedback scores on synthesized images,
057   with consideration of the corresponding prompts. The granularity of the feedback covers 11 skills
058   categorized into two aspects: image faithfulness and text-image alignment. After that, we Supervised
059   finetune (SFT) a Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) on the annotated dataset, aligning it
060   with human prior on detailed and accurate text-to-image evaluation.
061   Owing to extensive pre-training and large model capacity, MLLMs demonstrate excellent image-text
062   understanding and generalization capabilities. However, since the pre-training data does not include
063   synthesized images with low image faithfulness or evaluation-related text instructions, using MLLMs
064   directly for model evaluation may yield non-optimal results. Especially, we want to use MLLMs
065   to support comprehensive and detailed evaluations, encompassing 11 skills and 2 aspects. The
066   definitions and nuances of these may not be fully understood by the MLLM. Therefore, we employ
067   SFT on a small amount of high-quality annotated data to align the MLLM with human judgement
068   on evaluating synthesized images in criteria of 11 skills and 2 aspects. Notably, since the main
069
      intelligence of E VAL A LIGN stems from the annotated dataset and the utilized MLLM, we will make
      them accessible to the public.
070
071   In summary, our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
072
       • We build a detailed human feedback dataset specifically designed to address the aforementioned
073
         challenges of text-to-image model evaluations. The annotated dataset is thoroughly cleaned,
074      carefully balanced in topics, and systematically annotated by human. The dataset is composed by
075      fine-grained human prior on evaluating synthesized images in criteria of 11 skills and 2 aspects.
076    • We propose E VAL A LIGN, a text-to-image evaluation method which accurately aligns evaluation
077      models with fine-grained human prior using the annotated dataset. E VAL A LIGN exclusively
078      supports an accurate, comprehensive, fine-grained and interpretable text-to-image evaluations.
079      Besides E VAL A LIGN is cost-effective in terms of annotation and training and computationally
080      efficient.
081    • With E VAL A LIGN, we conduct evaluations over 24 text-to-image models and compare
082      E VAL A LIGN with existing evaluation methods. Quantitative and qualitative experiments demon-
083      strate that E VAL A LIGN outperforms other methods in evaluating model performance.

087   2.1   B ENCHMARKS OF TEXT- TO - IMAGE GENERATION
088
089   Despite the incredible progress achieved by text-to-image generation Zhang et al. (2023a); Tan et al.
090   (2024a), evaluations and benchmarks in this area are far from flawless and contain critical limitations.
091   For example, the most commonly used metrics, IS (Salimans et al., 2016), FID (Heusel et al., 2017),
092   and CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021) are broadly recognized as inaccurate for their inconsistency with
093
      human perception. To address, HPS series (Wu et al., 2023b;a), PickScore (Kirstain et al., 2024),
      and ImageReward (Xu et al., 2024) introduced human preference prior on image assessing to the
094
      benchmark, thereby allowing better correlation with image quality. However, with varying source
095
      and size of training data, these methods merely score the evaluated images in a coarse and general
096   way, which cannot serve as an indication for model evolution. Meanwhile, HEIM (Lee et al., 2024)
097   combined automatic and human evaluation and holistically evaluated text-to-image generation in 12
098   aspects, such as alignment, toxicity, and so on. As a consequence, HEIM relies heavily on human
099   labour, limiting its application within budget-limited research groups severely. Otani et al. (2023)
100   standardized the protocol and settings of human evaluation, ensuring its verifiable and reproducible.
101   Considering the issues of existing benchmarks, we propose E VAL A LIGN to offer a cost-efficient,
102   comprehensive and fine-grained text-to-image model evaluation. Through our observations, we found
103   that image faithfulness and text-image alignment are two key factors for comprehensive evaluation.
104
      Image faithfulness requires the model to generate visual elements that are consistently faithful to the
      real-world. For example, visual elements such as distorted body. Meanwhile, text-image alignment
105
      measures how the generated images are aligned with their corresponding prompts.

      et al., 2024) and LLMScore (Lu et al., 2024) also formulate the evaluation as a set of visual question

133   2.2     M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (MLLM S )
134
      Pre-trained on massive text-only and image-text data, MLLMs have exhibited exceptional image-text
135
      joint understanding and generalization abilities, facilitating a large spectrum of downstream applica-
136
      tions. Among the works major in MLLMs, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b; 2023) and MiniGPT4 (Zhu
137   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a) observed that multimodal SFT is sufficient to align MLLMs with
138   human preferences and enable them to accurately answer fine-grained questions about visual content.
139   Besides, Video-LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023b) and VideoChat (Li et al., 2023) utilized MLLMs
140   for video understanding. VILA (Lin et al., 2023) quantitatively proved that involving text-only
141   instruction-tuning data during SFT can further ameliorate model performance on text-only and
142   multimodal downstream tasks. LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024a) extracted visual tokens for both the
143   resized input image and the segmented sub-images to provide more detailed visual information for
144   MLLMs, achieving significant performance bonus on tasks with high-resolution input images.
145   However, due to the data bias, existing MLLMs cannot perfectly quantify for text-to-image evaluations.
146   Thus, we meticulously curate a SFT dataset to align MLLMs with detailed human feedback on
147   synthesized images.

      3.2   DATA A NNOTATION
217
218
      Prompt annotation. For text prompts focused on text-image alignment, we begin by annotating
219
      the entities and their attributes within the text, as illustrated in Figure 1. Our annotators extract
220   the entities mentioned in the prompts and label each entity with corresponding attributes, including
221   quantity, color, spatial relationships, and actions. During the annotation, we also ask the annotators to
222   annotate the overall style of the image if described in the corresponding prompt and report prompts
223   that contain toxic and NSFW content. These high-quality and detailed annotations facilitate the
224   subsequent SFT training and evaluation of the MLLM. The prompt annotation procedure ensures
225   that the MLLM can accurately align and respond to the nuanced details specified in the prompts,
226   enhancing both the training process and the model’s performance in generating images that faithfully
227   reflect the described attributes and style.
228
      Image annotation. The images generated by text-to-image models often present challenges such as
229   occluded human body parts, which can impede the effectiveness of SFT training and evaluation of
230   the MLLM. To address these challenges and enhance the model’s training and evaluative capabilities,
231   specific annotations are applied to all images depicting human and animals. These annotations include:
232   presence of human or animal faces; visibility of hands; visibility of limbs. By implementing these
233   annotations, we ensure that the MLLM can more effectively learn from and assess the completeness
234   and faithfulness of the generated images. This structured approach to annotation not only aids in
235   identifying common generation errors but also optimizes the model’s ability to generate more accurate
236   and realistic images, thereby improving both training outcomes and the model’s overall performance
237
      in generating coherent and contextually appropriate visual content.
238   Instruction-finetuning data annotation. To align the MLLM with human preference prior on
239   detailed synthesized image assessing, we can train the model on a minimal amount of fine-grained
240   human feedback data through SFT training. As a consequence, we devise two sets of questions,
241   each is concentrated on a specific fine-grained skill of image faithfulness and image-text alignment.
242   Human annotators are required to answer these questions to acquire the fine-grained human preference
243   data. To aid them to understand the meaning and principle of each question, thereby ensuring high
244   annotation quality, we employ a thorough and comprehensive procedure of annotation preparation.
245   First, we write a detailed annotation guideline and conduct a training for the annotators to explain the
246
      annotation guideline and answer their questions about the annotation. Then, we conduct a multi-turn
      trial annotation on another 50 synthesized images. After each trial, we calculate the Cohen’s kappa
247
      coefficient and interpret annotation guidelines for our annotators. In total, we conduct nine turns
248
      of trial annotation, and in the last turn of the trial, the Cohen’s kappa coefficient of our annotators
249   reaches 0.681, indicating high inter-annotator reliability and high annotation quality.
250
251   After completing the aforementioned preparations, we delegate the images filtered during image
252
      annotation to 10 annotators and ask them to complete the annotation just as how they did in the
      trial annotation. Furthermore, during the whole annotation procedure, four experts in text-to-image
253
      generation conduct random sampling quality inspection on the present annotated results, causing
254
      a second and a third re-annotation on 423 and 112 inspection-failed samples. Overall, owing to
255   the valuable work of our human annotators and our fastidious annotation procedure, we get quality-
256   sufficient instruction-tuning data required for the SFT training of the MLLM. More details of the
257   annotation procedure will be introduced in supplementary files.

290
291   4.1   S UPERVISED F INETUNING THE MLLM
292
      As we mentioned above, we use MLLMs as the evaluation models and let it to answer a set of
293
      carefully-designed instructions, thereby achieving quantitative measurement of fine-grained text-
294
      to-image generation skills. Due to data bias, zero-shot MLLMs perform poorly when it comes to
295   evaluation on generated images, particularly in term of image faithfulness. To solve this problem,
296   we apply SFT training on the detailed human annotation to align the MLLM with human preference
297   prior. Formally, the SFT training sample can be denoted as a triplet: question (or the instruction),
298   multimodal input and answer. During SFT training, the optimization objective is the autoregressive
299   loss function utilized to train LLMs, but calculated only on the answer, the loss function can be
300   formulated as follows:
301                                             N
                                                X
302                                    L(θ) =         log p(Ai |Q, M, A<i ; θ),                                (1)
303                                             i=1
304
      where N is the length of the ground truth answer, Q is a fine-grained question of the generated image
305
      and its available answer, M is the image and textual description, while A is the human annotated
306   answer selected from the given options. Notably, we expand each option to make it more detailed
307   and descriptive, thereby benefiting SFT performance by allowing the MLLM to better understand the
308   meaning of each option.

310   4.2   E VALUATION AND M ETRICS
311
312   To evaluate synthesized images with consideration of its synthetic nature, E VAL A LIGN is designed
313   to evaluate image faithfulness and text-image alignment in a fine-grained way. Notably, image
314   faithfulness and text-image alignment are two common errors occurred in synthesized images, whereas
315   real images inherently exhibit high levels of both image faithfulness and text-image alignment.
316
      Image Faithfulness measures whether synthesized images are faithful to real-world commonsense.
317
      With higher image faithfulness, the visual elements of generated images more closely resemble
318   their real-world counterparts. Unfortunately, text-to-image models often generate images with low
319   faithfulness, such as distorted body structures and human hands. This is also a critical reason
320   why we set image faithfulness as one of the benchmarking aspects in E VAL A LIGN. Additionally,
321   evaluating image faithfulness requires considering the input prompts, as prompts may describe
322   unreal or impossible scenarios that inherently affect the faithfulness of the generated images. For
323   example, when prompts like "a dog walking like a human" or "a man on Mars without a spacesuit"
      are provided, the generated images may naturally deviate from real-world image faithfulness. Under


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      such circumstances, the synthesized images cannot be regarded as low in image faithfulness since the
325   generative models are merely following prompts that contain super-reality scenarios.
326
327   Text-Image Alignment evaluates whether generated images are aligned with their conditioned
328   prompts. In the inference settings of text-to-image models, the image generation process is condi-
329   tioned on textual prompts, requiring alignment between the text prompts and the synthesized images.
330   However, through our observations, text-to-image models cannot consistently follow input prompts,
331
      often yielding images with visual elements misaligned with the input prompts. For example, models
      may generate images featuring an orange cat when conditioned on the text prompt "a blue cat."
332
333   During inference, the multimodal large language model (MLLM) is required to generate an appropri-
334   ate response given a specific question Q and multimodal input M in an autoregressive manner:
335                                          Ri = f (Q, M, R<i ; θ),                                      (2)
336
      where Ri is the i-th generated token, R<i represents the sequence of tokens generated before step
337
      i, and θ denotes the parameters of the fine-tuned MLLM. This autoregressive generation process is
338
      considered complete once the model generates an end-of-sequence (EOS) token or the generated
339   response exceeds a preset maximum generation length. After generation, we employ rule-based
340   filtering and regular expressions to extract the option chosen by the MLLM. Each option is assigned
341   a unique predefined score to quantitatively measure a fine-grained skill specified by the question Q:
342
                                      Score(Q) = g(R) = g(f (Q, M ; θ)),                                  (3)
343
344   where g(·) represents the procedure of option extraction and score mapping.
345   We devise two holistic and detailed question sets, Sf and Sa , that encompass every aspect of image
346   faithfulness and text-image alignment, respectively. Consequently, our metric, EvalAlign, can be
347   defined by averaging the scores of the questions in the two sets:
348
                                                      1 X
349                                  EvalAlignf =               Score(Qi ),                            (4)
                                                    |Sf |
350                                                          Qi ∈Sf
351                                                 1 X
352
                                      EvalAligna =       Score(Qj ),                                      (5)
                                                   |Sa |
                                                             Qj ∈Sa
353
354   where EvalAlignf and EvalAligna indicate the image faithfulness score and the text-image alignment
355   score evaluated by our method, respectively.

359
      For details about the SFT training, we apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) finetuning on LLaVA-NeXT (Liu
      et al., 2024a) models to align them with the E VAL A LIGN dataset. Additionally, we merely adapt
360
      LoRA finetuning on the Q and K weights of the attention module, as extending the finetuning to the
361
      ViT (Dosovitskiy, 2020) and projection modules will lead to overfitting. The entire training process
362   is conducted on 32 NVIDIA A100 GPUs for 10 hours, with a learning rate of 5×10−5 . As for the
363   ablation study, we evaluate the finetuned LLaVA-NeXT 13B model on the validation dataset. In the
364   final experiment, we apply SFT to the LLaVA-NeXT 34B model on the testing dataset to testify its
365   generalization ability.

554   We are committed to conducting this research with the highest ethical standards. Our goal is to
555   contribute positively to the fields of evaluation benchmarks on artificial intelligence generated content,
556   emphasizing transparency and reproducibility in our design. Similar with other MLLMs, E VAL A LIGN
557   may potentially generate responses contain offensive, inappropriate, or harmful content. Since the
558   base MLLMs of E VAL A LIGN are pretrained on large datasets scraped from the web that might
559
      contain private information and harmful content, they may inadvertently generate or expose sensitive
      information, raising ethical and privacy concerns. MLLMs are also susceptible to adversarial attacks,

561
      to manipulate model outputs, posing security and ethic risks. To alleviate these safety limitation
562   and our fulfill our social responsibility as artificial intelligence researchers, we create dedicated
563   evaluation sets for bias detection and mitigation, and conducted adversarial testing through hours of
564   redteaming. Besides, E VAL A LIGN is designed for fine-grained, human-aligned automatic text-to-
565   image evaluations, which can serve as a stepping stones toward revealing the inner generation nature
566   of text-to-image generative models, thereby lowering the ethical hazard of these models. We believe
567   that with appropriate use, it could provide users with interesting experiences for detailed synthesized
568   image evaluation, and inspires more appealing research works about text-to-image generation.
