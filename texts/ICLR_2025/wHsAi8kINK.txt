009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In federated learning, the non-identically distributed data affects both global and
012            local performance, while clients with small data volumes may also suffer from
013            overfitting issues. To address these challenges, we propose a federated learning
014            framework called Fed3+2p. In Fed3+2p, we divide the client neural network into
015            three parts: a feature extractor, a filter, and classification heads, and to train these
016            parts, we present two types of coordinators to train client sets with a two-phase
017            training strategy. In the first phase, each Type-A coordinator trains the feature
018
               extractor of partial clients, whose joint data distribution is similar to the global
               data distribution. In the second phase, each Type-B coordinator trains the filter
019
               and classification heads of partial clients, whose data distributions are similar to
020
               each other. We conduct empirical studies on three datasets: FMNIST and CIFAR-
021            10/100, and the results show that Fed3+2p surpasses the state-of-the-art methods
022            in both global and local performance across all tested datasets.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In Federated Learning, the non-identically distributed nature of data leads to some challenges,
028   such as unstable model parameter aggregation and inefficient communication Alamgir et al. (2022);
029   Kairouz et al. (2021); Tan et al. (2023). Personalized Federated Learning (pFL) has attracted much
030   attention in mitigating feature distribution inconsistency and label distribution imbalance issues in
031   recent years Tan et al. (2023); Dinh et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023b;c), and is also the focus of our
032   research.
033   In pFL, the performance of the global model plays a crucial role in ensuring robustness across di-
034   verse datasets, while local optimization allows each client to improve its model based on its specific
035   data, adapting to the unique characteristics of its own dataset Konečný et al. (2015). However,
036   existed pFL approaches pose a problem on existed clients: no or limited improvement of global
037   performance and local performance Zhang et al. (2023a); Hu et al. (2020); Dinh et al. (2022), such
038   as FedPerArivazhagan et al. (2019), FedProx Li et al. (2020b) and pFedMe Dinh et al. (2022). Re-
039
      cently, shared feature extractors combined with personalized classification heads P-Heads Collins
      et al. (2023); Chen & Chao (2022) or personalized feature extractor Li et al. (2021b); Tan et al.
040
      (2022) are proposed to promote global and local performance, respectively. The core idea of this
041
      method is to train different parts of the network with proper data samples, so we extend this idea
042   here, by reorganizing the neural network from the ”feature extractor - personalized classification
043   head” to ”feature extractor - filter - personalized classification head”, and training the three parts
044   separately.
045
      On the one hand, a high-quality feature extractor is essential for these methods, but it will bias the
046
      shared feature extractor’s parameters away from the global optimum when averagely aggregating
047
      Duan et al. (2021). For example, when a client’s data dominates the data space or are more concen-
048   trated, the feature extractor tends to learn more prominent features from the client, which will cause
049   the feature extractor to reflect the data characteristics of this client more, posing the risk of overfitting
050   on specific client Wang et al. (2020); Karimireddy et al. (2021). Thus, an underperforming feature
051   extractor may mislead the feature extraction process, causing a decline in global performance Chen
052   et al. (2023). In addition, through mathematical proof Duan et al. (2021), we know that when the
053   local data distribution is consistent with the global data distribution, the risk of the model overfitting
      to specific clients is reduced. Here, to train a high-quality feature extractor, we partition all clients

054
      into several sets whose joint data distributions are similar to the global distribution, and then use
055   coordinators to manage the training of clients.
056
057   On the other hand, the personalized classification head P-Head plays a crucial role in the personal-
058
      ized classification ability of each client’s local model, which is independently trained on each client’s
      local dataset Sattler et al. (2019); Konečný et al. (2015); Chen & Chao (2022). However, due to the
059
      typically simple structure of the P-Head and the lack of external information during independent
060
      training, it is prone to overfitting on clients with smaller data volumes Liu et al. (2019); Wang et al.
061   (2020). Here, to address the overfitting issue, we partition all clients into several groups with similar
062   data distributions, designate a part of the neural network as a shared filter part for each client group,
063   and then use coordinators to manage the training of clients.
064
      In corresponding to the three reorganized parts of the neural network, we design a two-phase train-
065
      ing strategy. In the first phase, we train the feature extractor shared by all clients. In the second
066
      phase, we train the filter shared by clients with similar data distributions, along with the classifi-
067   cation heads unique to each client. Overall, we constructed a federated learning framework named
068   Fed3+2p, which features a three-part neural network architecture and a two-phase training strategy,
069   introducing coordinators to manage the client training process.
070
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
071
072
             • We propose a federated learning method Fed3+2p, which divides a neural network archi-
073
               tecture into three parts: a feature extractor, a filter, and classification heads, and adopts a
074            two-phase training strategy with two types of coordinators managing the clients.
075
076
             • To train the shared feature extractor of all clients under conditions similar to the global data
               distribution, we present Type-A coordinators to partition all clients into several sets whose
077
               joint data distributions are similar to the global distribution, and then update the parameters
078
               of the feature extractor on coordinators and the central server.
079
080          • To train the shared filter of some clients under conditions of similar data distributions,
081
               we propose Type-B coordinators to cluster clients with similar data distributions, and then
               update the parameters of the filter on their corresponding coordinator.

084   2     R ELATED W ORK
085
086   In this section, we provide a brief overview of imbalanced data learning and introduce various per-
087   sonalized federated learning algorithms.
088
089   2.1   I MBALANCED DATA L EARNING
090
091   The primary solutions to address this issue involve sampling and ensemble learning Yang et al.
092   (2019); Li et al. (2020a).
093   Sampling includes undersampling and oversampling. Undersampling involves sampling from im-
094   balanced datasets to obtain a balanced subset, which is easy to implement but requires large
095   datasets.Unfortunately, in FL, the datasets of each client are typically small. Oversampling gen-
096   erates minority class samples to balance the dataset, but generating a large number of duplicate
097   minority class samples on small client datasets may lead to severe overfitting. Ensemble methods
098   are sensitive to noise and outliers, which are common in distributed datasets. While these meth-
099   ods manipulate the dataset to alleviate the non-identically distributed problem, they have limited
100
      effectiveness due to aforementioned issues.
101
102   2.2   P ERSONALIZED F EDERATED L EARNING
103
104
      Existing personalized Federated Learning (pFL) algorithms can be categorized into several main
      types, each with its unique characteristics.
105
106   Fine-Tuning. Algorithms in this category, such as Per-FedAvg Fallah et al. (2020), learn a global
107   model through fine-tuning. They perform minor local fine-tuning on each client to adapt to specific
      client data distributions and features.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      P-Heads. Including algorithms like FedPer Arivazhagan et al. (2019), FedRep Collins et al. (2023),
109   and FedRoD Chen & Chao (2022), which partition the neural network into a feature extractor and P-
110   Heads. The feature extractor is shared, while each client independently owns P-Heads. This allows
111   for personalized model training based on the specific requirements of each client.
112
      Regularization Methods. Algorithms in this category use regularization techniques to balance the
113
      relationship between the global model and personalized models, such as FedProx Li et al. (2020b),
114
      pFedMe Dinh et al. (2022), and Ditto Li et al. (2021a). They introduce additional regularization
115   terms to control the distance between the global model and personalized models, achieving a balance
116   between global performance and personalized performance.
117
118
      An ideal pFL is a type of FL with two objectives: (1) aggregating information for collaborative
      learning and (2) training reasonable personalized models Zhang et al. (2023a). However, existing
119
      pFL methods often focus on only one of these objectives on clients. FedPer Arivazhagan et al.
120
      (2019) and FedRep Collins et al. (2023) rely solely on local data for personalized training, lacking
121   the learning of global knowledge, which makes them prone to overfitting when client data is limited.
122   FedRoD Chen & Chao (2022) trains the feature extractor to capture global features but overlooks
123   personalized feature extraction.

      5     E XPERIMENT
315
316   To verify the global and local performance of Fed3+2p, we compare it with the SOTA methods on
317   widely used datasets: FMNIST and CIFAR-10/100. Additionally, to assess the effectiveness of the
318   coordinator, filter, and two-phase training method in Fed3+2p, we conduct ablation studies.
319
320   5.1   S ET U P
321
322   Datasets. We evaluate our proposed Fed3+2p on the following three datasets: FMNIST Xiao et al.
323   (2017) and CIFAR-10/100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009). The method for splitting the training set and
      test set is also consistent with that of Xiao et al. (2017) and Krizhevsky et al. (2009).


                                                             6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Backbones. Based on the work of McMahan et al. (2023); Chen & Chao (2022); Acar et al. (2021),
325   we use a ConvNet LeCun et al. (1998). For the FMNIST datasets, the model consists of 2 con-
326   volutional layers and 2 fully connected layers. The convolutional layers have 32 and 64 channels,
327   respectively. The fully connected layers have 50 neurons as the hidden size and 10 neurons as the
328   output for 10 classes. For the CIFAR-10/100 datasets, the model consists of 3 convolutional layers
329   and 2 fully connected layers. The convolutional layers have 32, 64, and 64 channels, respectively.
330   The fully connected layers have 64 neurons as the hidden size and 10/100 neurons as the output for
331   10/100 classes.
332   Statistically heterogeneous settings. To simulate non-identically distributed data distribution on
333   CIFAR-10/100 and FMNIST, we create heterogeneous data partitions for K clients following work
334   Hsu et al. (2019). Suppose the dataset has R classes. For each class r, a K-dimensional probability
335   vector qr is generated from the Dirichlet distribution Dir(α), where qr = [qr [1], qr [2], . . . , qr [K]].
336   Each element qr [k] of this vector represents the probability of assigning data from class r to client
337   k. The data of each class r is allocated to client k according to the probability qr [k]. Specifically, if
338   there are Nr samples in class r, then client k will receive approximately qr [k] × Nr samples. Since
339
      α < 1, the generated probability vectors qr are typically very imbalanced, meaning that the data for
      some classes may be concentrated on a few clients, while other clients may have fewer or none of
340
      these class samples.
341
342   Baselines. Specifically, we compare Fed3+2p with twelve federated learning algorithms, including
343   FedAvg McMahan et al. (2023), FedProx Li et al. (2020b), Per-FedAvg Fallah et al. (2020), pFedMe
344   Dinh et al. (2022), Ditto Li et al. (2021a), FedPer Arivazhagan et al. (2019), FedRep Collins et al.
345
      (2023), FedRoD Chen & Chao (2022), FedDYN Acar et al. (2021), CFL Sattler et al. (2019), PACFL
      Vahidian et al. (2022), and FedClust Islam et al. (2024). Additionally, we conduct ablation experi-
346
      ments to demonstrate the effectiveness of each module.
347
348   Metric. To evaluate global performance, we test the global model (GM) on the global test set
349   (G-Test) and also on the local test set (P-Test) using the global model (GM). To assess local perfor-
350   mance, we test the local model (LM) on the local test set (P-Test). For each local test set, its data
351
      distribution is consistent with each local training set.
352   Hyperparameters. We conduct 100 training rounds for each method. Consistent with Li et al.
353   (2020b), the local learning rate is set to decay over communication rounds. For FMNIST, the initial
354   learning rate is set to 0.01, and for CIFAR-10/100, the initial learning rate is set to 0.001, which
355   is reduced by a factor of 0.99 in each round, similar to the approach in Acar et al. (2021). We
356   reset the learning rate at the beginning of each training phase in Fed3+2p. We use M = 100 clients
357
      for FMNIST and M = 20 for CIFAR-10/100, and sample 20%/40% clients at every round. For
      the duration of the experiments, we utilize the SGD optimizer with a weight decay of 1e-5 and a
358
      momentum of 0.9. The mini-batch size is 40. During each round, clients perform local training over
359
      5 epochs. The results presented are the mean values from five separate experiments conducted with
360   different random seeds.

451   Effectiveness of Filter: In the first phase of the ablation experiments, removing the filter leds to a
452   decline in local performance. In the FMNIST dataset, local performance decreased slightly by 0.3%,
453   while in CIFAR-10, it dropped by 0.4%. In CIFAR-100, there was almost no change, with only a
454   0.1% decrease. These results show that the filter enhances the model’s local performance, especially
455   on simpler datasets like FMNIST and CIFAR-10, where it helps capture local data features and
456   mitigate overfitting issues that may arise in clients with smaller datasets. However, on more complex
457   datasets like CIFAR-100, the filter has relatively less impact, with the model relying more on other
458   components to improve performance.
459   Effectiveness of Coordinator: In the second phase of the ablation experiments, removing both the
460   Type-A and Type-B coordinators results in a significant decline in global and local performance.
461   In the FMNIST dataset, global performance dropped by 4.8% and local performance by 2.1%. In
462   the CIFAR-10 dataset, global performance decreased by 17.4% and local performance by 2.4%. In
463   the CIFAR-100 dataset, global performance fell by 5.2% and local performance by 7.0%. These
464   results demonstrate that the coordinators enhance both global and local performance. Specifically,
465
      the Type-A coordinator mitigates model bias caused by non-identically distributed client data by
      grouping clients into sets that are similar in joint data distribution to the global data distribution and
466
      managing their training, which allows each group’s model updates to reflect a more comprehensive
467
      global knowledge, thereby enhancing global performance. Similarly, the feature extractor also learns
468   richer feature representations in this process, which can be directly utilized during the training of
469   the personalized parts in the second phase, allowing them to better adapt to their respective data
470   distributions and improve local performance.
471
      Two-phase Strategy: In the third phase of the ablation experiments, we remove the two-phase
472
      training strategy and disable all coordinators, resulting in a further decline in local performance. In
473
      the FMNIST dataset, local performance decreased by 2.7%; in the CIFAR-10 dataset, local perfor-
474   mance dropped by 3.6%; and in the CIFAR-100 dataset, local performance fell by 8.4%. These
475   results indicate that the two-phase training strategy can enhance the model’s local performance.
476   By separating the training of the feature extractor from that of the classification head, the feature
477   extractor can learn more comprehensive global features in the first phase, thereby providing more
478   effective feature representations for the classification head, especially when local data distributions
479   are diverse.
480   Through progressive ablation experiments, we demonstrated the cumulative impact of the filter,
481   coordinator, and two-phase training strategy on the model’s global and local performance. The ex-
482   perimental results indicate that the filter primarily affects the model’s local performance as expected,
483   while the coordinator enhances the performance of both the global and local models. The two-phase
484   training strategy further improves both global and local performance by facilitating staged train-
485   ing. Therefore, maintaining the synergy among these modules is key to the success of the Fed3+2p
      framework.

526   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, we propose a novel federated learning framework called Fed3+2p to address the chal-
529   lenges posed by overfitting issues faced by non-identically distributed data and clients with small
530   data volumes, affecting both global and local performance. Fed3+2p divides the neural network
531   into three parts: a feature extractor, a filter, and classification heads, and introduces two types of
532   coordinators to implement a two-phase training strategy for these components. Experimental results
533   demonstrate that Fed3+2p significantly outperforms all SOTA methods in both global and local
534   performance.
535   In future research, we aim to further enhance the flexibility and adaptability of the Fed3+2p frame-
536   work. One potential improvement is to establish a threshold mechanism for automatically switching
537   between the two-phase training process, which would autonomously decide when to use Type-A or
538   Type-B coordinators. This would make the training process more efficient and better suited to differ-
539   ent types of clients. Additionally, we plan to explore using other metrics to group clients, enhancing
      privacy by collecting the minimum necessary information from clients.
