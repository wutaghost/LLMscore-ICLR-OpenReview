009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, research on point-based architectures has advanced rapidly, show-
012            casing their competitive performance. However, the unstructured nature of point
013            clouds limits the application of effective operators such as convolutions in fea-
014            ture extraction. Although many works have attempted to address the issues of
015            unstructured data and introduce convolutions or transformers, the complex spa-
016
               tial mappings of point clouds and cumbersome convolution implementations in
               these methods limit real-time performance of the model. Furthermore, excessive
017
               structural mapping ignores the independence of point cloud position representa-
018
               tion and fails to capture finer-grained features. To tackle these challenges, we
019            serialize point clouds to provide them with structure and introduce AdaConv to
020            directly utilize 2D convolutions, which simplifies the process and better preserves
021            the relative positional relationship. Additionally, we propose a novel dynamic
022            refinement approach for point cloud positions, continuously modifying the coor-
023            dinates of points within the convolutional neighborhood to enhance the flexibility
024            and adaptability. We also integrate local and global features to compensate for the
025            loss of point cloud features during downsampling. Finally, we propose DSConv
026            based on PointNeXt, maintaining scalability and inference speed. By combining
027
               DSConv with new architectural designs, we outperform the current state-of-the-art
               methods on ScanObjectNN, Scannet V2, and S3DIS datasets.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the realm of dense point cloud analysis, there are currently two main approaches: one represented
033   by models like Qian et al. (2022) and Thomas et al. (2019), which utilize MLP and convolutional
034   structures; the other employing self-attention mechanisms (Guo et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021; Wu
035   et al., 2022; 2024) for point cloud analysis. The former relies on unstructured data or complex
036   mappings, where the MLPs focuses on point-wise feature analysis, while point cloud convolutional
037   structures perform complex equivalent convolutions over neighborhoods, failing to achieve the fine-
038   grained neighborhood feature extraction based on spatial positions like 2D convolutions. The latter
039
      struggles to effectively capture local features. Hence, neither method fully leverages the local rela-
      tive positional relationships among points. Redundant MLP computations and complex 3D convo-
040
      lutions also introduce computational burdens. Moreover, the richer degrees of freedom and structure
041
      in three-dimensional objects makes addressing issues such as object rotation and deformation more
042   challenging. To address these challenges, we propose a flexible sequential convolution method to
043   extract richer local features with strong real-time performance and leverage the independence of
044   point cloud positions to cope with changes in object posture.
045
      Recent advancements, such as PointGPT (Chen et al., 2024) have addressed the unstructured nature
046
      of point clouds by employing serialization, allowing for the extraction of features based on rela-
047
      tionships in one-dimensional space. Inspired by this, the core motivation of our work is to utilize
048   serialization techniques to transform unstructured point cloud data into sequences with structured
049   spatial features, followed by the extraction of local features through designed convolution. To ex-
050   plore these methods, we propose Dynamic Sequence Convolution (DSConv) built upon Inverted
051   Residual MLP (InvResMLP) module of PointNeXt (Qian et al., 2022) for Local Aggregation. We
052   map points within groups across multiple directions and compute sliding windows along the cor-
053   responding sequence to extract local features, which is a fine-grained convolution without complex
      mappings, while improving the reduction method to ensure global features. We couple point posi-

      tions with convolution parameters to enable dynamic adjustments of these parameters based on the
055   distribution of points within the convolution neighborhood. Additionally, we propose an efficient
056   method for point position refinement to handle target pose variations, dynamically adjusting point
057   positions based on the features of neighbors to enhance flexibility while maintaining high through-
058   put. Specifically, our contributions are summarized as follows:
059
060   • We introduce point cloud serialization and adaptive convolution (AdaConv), which replace the
061     reliance on unstructured point clouds or complex spatial mapping, enabling better utilization of
062
        relative positional relationships among point clouds and less computational burden.
063   • We propose a dynamic point position refinement method to address challenges with object pose
064     variation in three-dimensional space.
065   • We adopt a local-global structure that enables network to flexibly combine global MLP and local

067     cloud positions with convolution parameters to improve the generalization of DSConv.
068   • Our proposed DSConv shares the same scalability and inference speed as Qian et al. (2022),
069     with fewer parameters. DSConv-XL outperforms PointNeXt-XL by a 4.8% mean IoU (mIoU)
070     increase on S3DIS (Armeni et al., 2016) (6-fold cross-validation). Additionally, through further
071     architecture designs, DSConv-XXL achieves state-of-the-art performances across multiple tasks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075
      Point-based Networks. Point-based networks provide greater flexibility and preserve the original
076   information more effectively compared to voxel-based methods (Zhou & Tuzel, 2018; Yan et al.,
077   2018). PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a) introduce MLPs for handling unordered point cloud data. Point-
078   Net++ (Qi et al., 2017b) introduce a hierarchical structure for feature aggregation at different scales.
079   Wang et al. (2019b;a); Qian et al. (2021a) utilize graph neural networks for point cloud analysis. Yu
080   et al. (2022); Pang et al. (2022) utilize self-supervised algorithms for pre-training. Transformer-like
081   networks (Zhao et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; 2024) extract local features via self-attention. Ma et al.
082   (2022); Qian et al. (2022); Deng et al. (2023) improve model performance through residual connec-
083   tions and different feature preprocessing techniques. However, the use of unstructured point clouds
084
      in these methods impedes the exploitation of relative position information, while the serialization
      and convolution strategies in DSConv effectively utilize this information.
085
086
      Convolution in Point Clouds. Convolution, as one of the effective methods for processing local
087
      features, has always been a focus in point cloud analysis. SpiderCNN (Xu et al., 2018) defines con-
088
      volution kernels as Taylor-expansion-based polynomials, encoding geometric information via step
089   functions within local neighborhoods. PointCNN (Li et al., 2018) learns an X-transformation from
090   input points to reassign associated features and feature weights, enabling convolution to process
091   regularized input features. PointConv (Wu et al., 2019) generates convolution kernels by applying
092   MLPs and kernel density estimation to points, convolving them with neighborhood points. KPConv
093   (Thomas et al., 2019) introduces rigid kernel point deformable convolution, where the parameters of
094   kernel points are used to process the points mapped to that position. These convolution methods in
095   point clouds rely on complex mappings that affect the algorithm speed, while our serialized mapping
096   approach is simpler and reduces the computational cost.
097
098   Utilization of Point Cloud Serialization. Recent works have begun exploring serialization meth-
099
      ods to process raw point clouds, aiming to overcome their inherent disorder and non-structural na-
      ture. OctFormer (Wang, 2023) applies Morton-like sorting to octree-based point cloud data and
100
      performs windowing along the resulting sequence. PointGPT (Chen et al., 2024) utilizes Z-order
101
      (Morton, 1966) sorting on points during self-supervised pre-training, leveraging remaining points
102   to predict missing patches. Point Transformer V3 (Wu et al., 2024) alternates between Z-order and
103   Hilbert curves (Hilbert & Hilbert, 1935) to convert point clouds into patches, aiming to mitigate
104   the impact of K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) search on speed. These methods suggest that serialized
105   raw point cloud data still maintains a good spatial proximity relationship. However, the aforemen-
106   tioned serialization methods can only ensure compactness within the sequence, with relatively poor
107   ability to preserve relative positional information among point clouds. In contrast, our approach to
      serialization leveraging positional relationships avoids this limitation.

      Point cloud collection methods and data storage formats often result in sparse and unordered point
156   clouds. To address this issue, point clouds are typically converted into voxels (Zhou & Tuzel, 2018)
157   or pillars (Lang et al., 2019). However, these approaches not only incur significant computational
158   overhead but also lead to the loss of information in dense regions. In contrast, point cloud se-
159   rialization, while imparting structure, can preserve all point cloud information intact and achieve
160   higher efficiency. By designing appropriate mapping methods, points in multi-dimensional space
161   can be mapped to a one-dimensional continuous sequence, preserving the local relationships be-
      tween points. We propose a multi-directional point cloud serialization method and compare it with

      existing methods. Mapping methods based on distance relationships, such as those shown in Fig. 2a
163   and Fig. 2b, can better ensure the compactness of adjacent points in the sequence, but are limited
164   in representing the relative positional relationships of points. By comparison, our proposed method,
165   based on mapping along three coordinate axes (as shown in Fig. 2c and Fig. 2d), can directly repre-
166   sent the relative positional relationships of points in the point cloud, thereby preserving local relative
167   positional features of objects to a certain extent and providing better geometric representation capa-
168   bilities. Additionally, our approach does not require encoding, leading to greater efficiency.
169   In most cases, simply serializing along coordinate axes may map points that are far apart to nearby
170   positions in the sequence (as shown in Fig. 2c). Therefore, we map the point clouds after grouping
171   module, and by leveraging the compactness of the grouped point clouds, we can effectively avoid
172   the issue of distant points being mapped to close positions due to non-encoded mapping. With the
173   constraint of grouping, our serialization method achieves similar results to those based on encoded
174   distance serialization, and the order within each sequence also reflects the relative positional rela-
175   tionships between point clouds, as shown in Fig. 2d. The serialized point cloud, which can directly
176   reflect the spatial physical distribution, simplifies the implementation of 3D spatial convolution and
177
      enables finer-grained point cloud convolution.

181   application of 2D convolution, there is still a drawback in using convolution for point cloud pro-

246
      vious section to compute the required offsets for each point within the receptive field when convolv-
247   ing at any position. Then, these offsets are applied to the position channels (as shown in Fig. 4a),

249   original positions. We attempt to implement this operation using Deformable Convolutional Net-
250   work (DCN) (Dai et al., 2017b; Zhu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023). However, DCNs result in
251   significant memory consumption and considerably slow down the algorithm. Therefore, we propose

      3.4   N ETWORK D ETAILS
271
272   Local-global structure. Local Aggregation (LA) structure employs saturation sampling during
273   the downsampling process, resulting in significant redundant computations. Additionally, its max
274   pooling excessively emphasizes the features of individual points within a group, neglecting others
275   and missing out on holistic features. Therefore, we retain the original LA module in DSConv module
276   but replace its max pooling with sum pooling to enhance the speed and improve its ability to extract
277   global features, reducing over-reliance on individual key points. A MLP is used to mix them.
278
279
      Coupling position with convolution parameters. We improve the flexibility of DSConv by cou-
      pling the positional information of local point cloud with convolution parameters, providing different
280
      computation parameters for points at different locations within the local space. We propose using
281
      positional encoding to implement the coupling process, for each channels as follows:
282
                                      w(f + P E(p)) = wf + biascoupled ,                                 (5)
283
284   where f and p represent the features and positional coordinates of each point within each group after
285
      coordinate decentralization, respectively. P E() is positional encoding. This approach introduces a
      bias that couples position with convolution parameters for each channel computation result. Unlike
286
      Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Carion et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021) model, we use
287
      pointwise convolution to encode relative positional information into each feature channel, avoiding
288   complex trigonometric operations. Notably, experiment shows that simply adding PE to the LA
289   module does not lead to a significant performance improvement.
290
291   Feature preprocess. We calculate the feature differences between neighboring points and center
292   point, thereby highlighting important features in local region. With the coupling step, we process
293   the input features as follows:
294                              f ′ = f − fcenter + Relu(BN (Conv1×1 (p))),                         (6)
295   where Conv1×1 denotes a mapping from coordinate dimensions to feature dimensions, and fcenter
296   corresponds to the features of the central point in groups. Therefore, we can use Eq. 6 to couple
297   positional features and capture salient changes in local features.
298
299   Grouping strategy. Excessive summation of points may result in large feature values that affect
300   parameter convergence. On the other hand, the traditional grouping method is overly redundant in
301   sampling, while DSConv only requires less points that can reflect the spatial structure for extracting
302   geometric features, so we reduce the number of sampled points. Although this may lead to uneven
303
      sampling and produce different results between consecutive samplings, it significantly improves
      speed and resolves convergence issues. To enhance sampled point quality, we modify the sampling
304
      method: firstly, N points are sampled by ball-query (Qi et al., 2017b) or KNN, then Farthest Point
305
      Sampling (FPS) is used to resample N/4 points, ensuring consistent and representative sampling.

308
309   We adjust the original architecture (Qian et al., 2022), with the improvements primarily reflected in
310   the XXL scale model. We refer to PointNeXt for model scaling and further increase the number of
311   DSConvs in each stage. In DSConv-XXL, to fully leverage the advantages of DSConv, we remove
312   the initial MLP stem and the first downsampling, directly operating on the input point cloud, effec-
313   tively increasing the number of processed points by × 4. To handle the large point cloud data, we
314
      adopt grid-sampling for faster downsampling, inspired by Thomas et al. (2019); Wu et al. (2022).
      Additionally, due to the increased complexity of feature extraction in DSConv, residual connections
315
      preprocess the original input with an MLP to better adapt to the feature changes. Furthermore, we
316
      retain the inverted bottleneck structure, using it after every two DSConv layers. We represent the
317   channel of the initial embedding MLP as C and the number of DSConv modules as B, and designed
318   four sizes of models as follows:

      4     E XPERIMENTS
344
345
      To validate the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct experimental evaluations on the semantic
346
      segmentation datasets ScanNet V2 (Dai et al., 2017a) and S3DIS (Armeni et al., 2016), as well as the
347   point cloud classification dataset ScanObjectNN (Uy et al., 2019). To ensure fair contrast, except for
348   the hyperparameters of the method proposed in this paper, we maintain the same data augmentation
349   and hyperparameter settings as Qian et al. (2022), and use the same evaluation criteria. For the new
350   DSConv-XXL, we use larger point cloud input and independent hyperparameter settings.
351
352   Experimental setups. We train DSConv using CrossEntropy loss with label smoothing (Szegedy
353   et al., 2016), AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer, and cosine decay. For semantic
354   segmentation task, we set the initial learning rate lr=0.01, weight decay 10−4 , and train for 100
355   epochs. In classification task, DSConv is trained with an initial lr=0.002, weight decay of 0.05,
356
      and the number of input points is set to 1024 for 400 epochs. For DSConv-XXL, we set lr to 0.006
      and warmup epoch to 10, while employing dropout to enhance generalization. For segmentation
357
      tasks, the voxel size is set to 0.02m and 0.04m for ScanNet V2 and S3DIS. In ScanNet V2, we adopt
358
      the latest training strategy from Chen et al. (2023) and expand the input points to 80,000. For fair
359   comparison, we utilize the testing scheme provided by PointNext (Qian et al., 2022) for our tests.
360   Model parameter size and GFLOPs are provided, and we also compare the model inference speed
361   using throughput (TP) following Qian et al. (2021b; 2022). Throughput for segmentation models
362   is measured with 8×15000 points, while for classification models, it is measured with 32×2048
363   points. We evaluate model performance using an RTX 4070 12GB GPU.
364
365   4.1   3D S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION ON S3DIS AND S CAN N ET
366
367   Segmentation on S3DIS. S3DIS (Stanford Large-Scale 3D Indoor Spaces) (Armeni et al., 2016)
368   comprises laser-scanned data from 271 rooms across six different large-scale indoor areas, encom-
369
      passing 13 categories. Results for both Area 5 and 6-fold cross-validation are presented in Tab. 1
      and Tab. 2. With more modules and updated training strategies, DSConv-XXL achieves 75.5%
370
      mIoU on S3DIS Area 5 surpassing KPConvX by 2.0% mIoU. By removing the stem MLP from
371
      DSConv-XXL, the number of points processed by DSConv in each stage increases, while the num-
372   ber of feature channels is halved. This reduces parameters but increases GFLOPs. On S3DIS 6-fold
373   cross-validation, DSConv-XL also achieves state-of-the-art performance, with a 4.8% improvement
374   in mIoU compared to PointNeXt and outperforming the state-of-the-art PointVector (Deng et al.,

450   utilized by convolution. The inferior performance of 3rd-order Z-order method compared to ours
451   shows that the improvement of our algorithm is not solely due to an increase in model parameters.
452
453   Point cloud position refinement methods. We explore the effectiveness of different point cloud
454   position refinement methods proposed in Sec. 3.3 and their impact on model throughput. As shown
455
      in Tab. 6, our SPR method achieves performance close to D-SPR (a SPR based on DCN method,
      which samples neighborhoods for each sequence to generate intermediate feature maps of size 9
456
      × the original feature size), demonstrating a certain level of flexibility and significantly improving
457
      throughput. Despite having less flexibility, the PPR method still enhances performance. This result
458   indicates that incrementally refining point cloud positions as needed during feature extraction is
459   effective, and the higher flexibility, the more pronounced the performance improvement.
460
461   Convolution strategies. We evaluate different receptive field shapes and multi-sequence fusion
462   algorithms. As shown in Tab. 7, the receptive field of 3×1×5 is limited by the number of sampling
463   points, resulting in a lower mIoU. In contrast, the 3×1×3 receptive field achieves higher perfor-
464   mance with fewer parameters and computational cost. Using BEV (Yang et al., 2018) perspective
465   (xy sequences) for receptive fields performs poorly due to the loss of information in the Z-axis di-
466   rection. Additionally, using different convolution parameters for sequences in different directions is
467
      beneficial. Using addition for sequence fusion has fewer parameters and a higher mIoU than others.
468
469

      pling (PPC), Local-Global structure, sequence adaptive convolution and point position refinement.
470
      The results are illustrated in Tab. 8. PointNeXt-L (Qian et al., 2022) is our baseline. Simply adding
471
      PPC or global path in baseline do not significantly improve performance. However, removing them
472   from DSConv leads to a significant performance decline, indicating that sequence convolution re-
473   lies on global information and is more sensitive to position. Additionally, it shows that the cou-
474   pling of position and parameters enhances the flexibility of convolution. By gradually adding each
475   component we propose, we improve the baseline results to 72.4% mIoU. The increase in mIoU
476   demonstrates the effectiveness of each component. More experiments are provided in Appendix B.

478   5     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we introduce DSConv, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on S3DIS, Scan-
481   NetV2 and ScanObjectNN. We utilize coordinate-based serialization to preserve the relative posi-
482   tional information of point clouds, followed by feature extraction using convolution. Additionally,
483   we propose adaconv and multi-sequence fusion strategies to achieve finer-grained sequential convo-
484   lution. Local-global structure ensures the global features within the groups. Position refinement and
485   parameter coupling enhances the flexibility in processing point clouds. By adjusting input features
      and optimizing sampling methods, our model still maintains excellent scalability and speed.

848   C     V ISUALIZATION
849
850   As shown in Fig. 7, our algorithm is able to perform better at the boundary planes, with more distinct
851   segmentation lines for planar regions. This indicates that our algorithm is more sensitive to edge
852   information, similar to 2D convolution algorithms. The utilization of local-global features gives
853
      us an advantage in both large complex scenes and simple scenarios. However, like most methods,
      our algorithm also exhibits limitations in segmenting small embedded components, such as small
854
      windows on walls.

859   We visualize output features of the last module in DSConv using Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017)

      allowing it to extract more fine-grained features. Additionally, the high flexibility of DSConv make

      it easier to learn complex shapes, such as airplane tail fins and computer brackets.
