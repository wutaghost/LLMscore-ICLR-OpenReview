009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown great promise in processing graph-
012            structured data, but they often require large amounts of labeled data and are sensi-
013            tive to noise. In this paper, we propose a novel node-level data augmentation ap-
014            proach that leverages a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) within a dual-task learning
015            framework to address these challenges. Our method utilizes the VAE to gener-
016            ate enriched node representations that capture both structural and feature-related
017            information, which are then combined with the original node features for clas-
018
               sification by a Graph Attention Network (GAT). Experiments conducted on the
               Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed datasets show that our approach outperforms base-
019
               line models, achieving up to 7.3% higher accuracy in Pubmed, and surpassing
020
               recent state-of-the-art data augmentation techniques. This work highlights the
021            effectiveness of dual-task learning for robust feature enhancement and advances
022            data augmentation strategies in GNNs.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Graph-structured data is increasingly prevalent across domains, including social networks, biolog-
028   ical systems, and recommendation engines. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become central
029   tools for analyzing such data due to their success in tasks like node classification, link prediction,
030   and community detection (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2018). Despite this success,
031   GNNs often require extensive labeled data and can be sensitive to noise or structural perturbations,
032
      limiting their applicability in settings where high-quality labeled data is scarce or noisy.
033   Traditional augmentation techniques, such as edge manipulation or node feature masking, aim to in-
034   crease data diversity and robustness but may fail to fully capture the complex dependencies in graph
035   structures. These methods risk introducing unrealistic modifications that disrupt graph integrity, thus
036   necessitating more refined augmentation approaches (You et al., 2020; Rong et al., 2020).
037   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) offer a probabilistic framework for
038   learning expressive latent representations and have been adapted for graph tasks like link prediction
039   and graph generation (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Salha et al., 2019). However, their potential for node-
040   level data augmentation, particularly in supervised learning, remains underexplored. Leveraging
041   VAE-generated latent representations within a GNN framework may enrich node features in a way
042   that maintains structural coherence and improves robustness to noise.
043   In this work, we propose a novel node-level data augmentation method that combines a VAE with
044   a dual-task learning framework to generate enriched node representations. Unlike traditional ap-
045   proaches, our method uses a multi-channel encoder that treats various GNN architectures as comple-
046   mentary filters. Each GNN channel—such as GCN, GAT, SAGE, or GIN—extracts unique structural
047   patterns, effectively decomposing data into multi-faceted representations. This modular, filter-based
048   design allows our framework to flexibly incorporate additional GNN variants, enhancing feature
049   diversity and task adaptability.
050   Our approach simultaneously trains the VAE for both data reconstruction and node classification,
051   creating latent representations that are both structurally informative and task-relevant. This study
052   is constrained by limited resources, which directs our focus towards methods that can demonstrate
053   robustness and scalability within these constraints. In this way, the VAE serves as a core innovation
      in generating new features that improve robustness against noise and enriches the original feature

      set. By using this combination of VAE-driven feature augmentation and a multi-channel encoder,
055   our framework is not only robust to noisy environments but also highly adaptable to different graph

058
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
059          • VAE-based Node-Level Augmentation: We introduce a VAE framework that produces

078   2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
079
      Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become the standard approach for learning on graph-
080
      structured data (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2018). Key architectures, including
081
      Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017), Graph Attention Networks (GAT)
082   (Veličković et al., 2018), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), and Graph Isomorphism Networks
083   (GIN) (Xu et al., 2019), have shown effectiveness in tasks such as node classification, link pre-
084   diction, and community detection. Each of these architectures captures different aspects of graph
085   structure: GCNs focus on local aggregation, GATs use attention mechanisms for adaptive neighbor
086   importance, SAGE aggregates neighborhood information to capture long-range dependencies, and
087   GIN improves expressive power for isomorphism properties in graphs.
088   However, GNNs often suffer from limitations like over-smoothing—where node features become
089   indistinguishable in deeper layers—and the need for substantial labeled data to achieve high perfor-
090   mance (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Zhao et al., 2023). Moreover, single-architecture approaches may be
091   insufficient to fully capture diverse structural information in complex graph data. Our multi-channel
092   encoder addresses these limitations by treating each GNN architecture as a distinct filter, combining
093   their unique strengths in a modular framework to enhance feature diversity and adaptability.
094
095   2.2   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS FOR G RAPHS
096
097   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) provide a probabilistic approach to
098   learning latent representations and have been leveraged in graph learning for tasks such as link
099   prediction and graph generation. Notable VAE-based models, such as VGAE (Kipf & Welling,
100   2016) and GraphVAE (Simonovsky & Komodakis, 2018), primarily focus on unsupervised learning
101   and graph generation by modeling distributions over adjacency matrices and node features. While
102   these models contribute to generative tasks, their potential for direct node-level data augmentation
103
      in supervised learning remains underexplored.
104   The VAE model is typically trained by minimizing a combined objective of reconstruction loss and
105   Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence:
106
107
                            LVAE = Eqϕ (z|x)[log pθ (x|z)] − KL(qϕ (z|x)||pθ (z))                       (1)

      where x represents the input node features, and z is the latent representation learned by the encoder.
109   In our work, this objective is adapted to create task-relevant, augmented node representations for
110   supervised node classification, enhancing feature richness and robustness against noise.

      2.3   DATA AUGMENTATION IN G RAPHS
114
      Data augmentation techniques in graph learning aim to improve model generalization by artifi-
115
      cially increasing data diversity. In graph settings, common methods like DropEdge (Rong et al.,
116   2020)—which randomly removes edges—and GraphMix (Verma et al., 2021)—which creates mixed
117   node features—have been proposed to address issues like over-smoothing and overfitting. GraphCL
118   (You et al., 2020) further introduces contrastive learning with augmentations like node dropping and
119   edge perturbation to encourage model robustness.
120
      However, these techniques often rely on random perturbations, which may inadvertently disrupt
121
      essential structural information. Our approach differs by generating structured, task-relevant repre-
122   sentations using VAE to maintain graph integrity, offering a more refined augmentation strategy that
123   preserves important structural dependencies for node classification.
124
125
      2.4   M ULTI -TASK L EARNING IN GNN S
126
127
      Multi-task learning (MTL) (Caruana, 1997) is commonly used to enhance model generalization by
128   simultaneously training on related tasks, as seen in applications like node classification combined
129   with link prediction (Zhang & Chen, 2018) or community detection (Sun et al., 2019). Dual-task
130   learning, a subset of MTL, enables GNNs to learn more robust and discriminative representations
131   by balancing information across tasks. In our framework, we integrate dual-task learning within the
132   VAE, simultaneously training for both data reconstruction and node classification. This approach
133   improves feature representation quality and robustness, as it allows the model to learn a latent space
134   that benefits both reconstruction and task-specific objectives.
135
136   2.5   O UR C ONTRIBUTION
137
138   While VAEs, data augmentation, and multi-task learning have each been explored within GNN
139   frameworks, their combined potential in a modular framework for node-level data augmentation
140   is less explored. By introducing a VAE with a dual-task learning framework and a filter-based
141   multi-channel encoder, we bridge this gap, enabling the generation of enriched, task-relevant node
142   representations. This method is not only effective for node classification but also provides a highly
143
      adaptable framework for diverse graph tasks by allowing the selection of different GNN channels
      based on the data characteristics and task requirements.

      Our proposed method consists of two main components: a VAE with a multi-channel encoder for
149   node-level data augmentation, and a GAT for node classification using the augmented features
150   to evaluate the effectiveness of the augmentation process. The dual-task learning framework
151   trains the VAE simultaneously for both data reconstruction and node classification, ensuring that the
152   learned representations are both robust and task-relevant.

      3.1   BASELINE M ODEL : D OUBLE - LAYER GAT
155
156   To establish a benchmark in our experimental study, we implemented a two-layer GAT as the base-
157   line, capturing relational dynamics within the graph structure for progressive refinement of node
158   representations. After systematic hyperparameter tuning, we identified optimal settings: a learning
159   rate of 0.01, a hidden layer size of 1, 28 attention heads in the first layer, and 12 in the second, using
160   the Adam optimizer with a weight decay of 0.001. Our baseline model achieved a node classification
161   accuracy of 82.8%, with precision, recall, and F1 scores of 0.810, 0.841, and 0.822, respectively,
      providing a strong foundation for comparisons with our proposed framework.

186   3.2     U PSTREAM TASK : VAE WITH AUXILIARY N ODE C LASSIFICATION TASK
187
188   For the upstream task, we designed a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to capture latent represen-
189   tations of the Cora dataset through a dual-task training approach. The VAE encoder maps the input
190   graph data to a latent space, while the decoder reconstructs the data from these latent representa-
191
      tions. Notably, the encoder’s reparameterized output (specifically the mean µ) serves a dual role: it
      contributes to the data reconstruction and simultaneously acts as input for an auxiliary node clas-
192
      sification task through two stacked linear layers. This dual-task setup allows the VAE to produce
193
      embeddings that are robust to noise while enhancing downstream classification performance.
194
195   3.2.1    E NCODER A RCHITECTURE
196
197   The encoder design, shown in Figure 1, captures features and structures through several key stages:
198   - Initial Linear Layer Node features are first projected into a higher-dimensional space, providing
199   an enriched representation that supports subsequent convolutional operations.
200   - Multi-channel Convolutional Layers The encoder’s core component is the multi-channel con-
201   volutional layer, comprising multiple parallel graph convolutional operations. By treating GCN,
202   GAT, SAGE, and GIN layers as unique filters, each capturing different structural properties, the
203   model gains a more comprehensive understanding of the graph:
204           • GCN Layer: Aggregates local connectivity patterns to capture neighborhood features.
205           • GAT Layer: Uses an attention mechanism to prioritize important nodes, enhancing rela-
206             tional representation.
207
              • SAGE Layer: Aggregates information for long-range dependencies, offering a broader
208
                graph perspective.
209
              • GIN Layer: Emphasizes isomorphism properties, maintaining nuanced node feature rep-
210
                resentations.
211
212   - Feature Fusion The outputs of the convolutional layers are concatenated, merging the strengths
213   of each graph convolutional operation into a single, enriched feature representation.
214   - Output Linear Layers This concatenated representation is then fed into two parallel linear lay-
215   ers to estimate the latent space parameters µ (mean) and log σ 2 (log variance), defining the latent
      distribution for reconstruction.

      3.2.2     VAE T RAINING
237
238   VAE Loss The VAE’s loss function combines reconstruction loss and a KL divergence term:
239
                                LVAE = Eqϕ (z|x) [log pθ (x|z)] − KL(qϕ (z|x)||pθ (z))                    (2)
240
241   where x represents the input node features, and z is the latent representation.
242
243   Dual-task Training In our training framework, the encoder output serves both reconstruction and
244
      node classification. By training the encoder on both tasks, it learns to generate latent representations
      beneficial for both, enhancing classification accuracy and retaining high-quality reconstructions.
245
246
      Loss Weight Adjustment The dual-task loss function incorporates weighted contributions from
247
      reconstruction and classification losses:
248
249                                         Ltotal = a · Lrecon + b · Lclass                              (3)
250   where a and b are weight parameters for each loss. In practice, the classification loss tends to be 10
251   to 100 times smaller than the reconstruction loss. To balance these, we amplify the classification
252   loss by approximately 4500 times, achieving a scale alignment that prevents dominance by either
253   task, optimizing overall performance and accuracy.

287   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL F RAMEWORK
288
289   Our study adopts a two-stage framework to enhance GNN performance for node classification tasks.
290   In the first stage, a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) is used to learn latent representations of the
291   graph data, capturing both structural and feature information. In the second stage, these latent
292
      representations are combined with raw features to serve as inputs for a Graph Attention Network
      (GAT), enabling enriched feature-based classification. The VAE is trained under a dual-task learning
293
      framework to ensure that the learned representations are both task-relevant and structurally coherent.

464   catenating raw features with task-specific latent representations, our approach preserves node-level
465
      distinctions, enabling GAT to achieve higher classification accuracy and improved robustness. This
      enhancement is particularly evident in models incorporating multi-channel convolutional layers,

      which capture diverse local and global structural patterns.
