009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning representations with minimally dependent embedding dimensions can
012            have many potential benefits such as improved generalization and interpretability.
013            This work provides a differentiable and scalable algorithm for dependence min-
014            imization, moving beyond existing linear pairwise decorrelation methods. Our
015            algorithm involves an adversarial game where small networks identify dimension
016            relationships, while the main model exploits this information to reduce dependen-
017            cies. We empirically verify that the algorithm converges. We then explore depen-
018
               dence reduction as a proxy for maximizing information content. We showcase the
               algorithm’s effectiveness on the Clevr-4 dataset, both with and without supervi-
019
               sion, and achieve promising results on the ImageNet dataset. Finally, we propose
020
               an algorithm modification that gives more control over the level of dependency,
021            sparking a discussion on optimal redundancy levels for specific applications. Al-
022            though the algorithm performs well on synthetic data, further research is needed
023            to optimize it for tasks such as out-of-distribution detection.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In representation learning (Rumelhart et al., 1986b; Hinton et al., 2006; Bengio et al., 2013), al-
029   gorithms learn to extract lower-dimensional representations from input data. The quality of rep-
030   resentations is typically evaluated by measuring performance in targeted applications. However,
031   the significance of a representation goes beyond enhancing downstream performance: considering
032   properties like fairness, interpretability, and generalization is crucial for any real-world application.
033   Take for instance the application of autonomous driving: it is safety-critical to deploy recognition
034   algorithms that can not only accurately detect samples from the training classes but also identify
035
      when a sample comes from an unknown class. Failing to do so may lead the autonomous agent to
      make poor decisions. We motivate why current recognition algorithms could be inadequate in this
036
      scenario with a simple example (illustrated in Figure 1):
037
038   Example 1. Consider a dataset with images of colored shapes with samples from three training
039
      classes: ”red squares”, ”green triangles”, and ”blue triangles”. A classifier could reach a minimum
      loss value of zero and perfect accuracy by only extracting the color in the output representation.
040
      Nevertheless, if one introduced during inference examples from a ”red triangles” class, the model
041
      would predict with high confidence that those examples are ”red squares”, despite having a shape
042   shared by none of the objects from the ”red squares” class.
043
044   In Example 1, the model relies only on a subset of the features that are relevant to discriminate
045   the training classes, which is problematic when out-of-distribution (OOD, Ben-David et al. (2010);
046   Nguyen et al. (2015)) is required. In this work, we advocate that minimizing the dependence between
047   the embedding dimensions, and thereby minimizing redundancy, could push the model to encode
048   additional features (e.g. both shape and color features in this example) to increase generalization
049   and the robustness of predictions in the presence of OOD samples.
050   Learning representations with uncorrelated dimensions has a long history in machine learning. Re-
051   cent methods include applications in self-supervised representation learning (Huang et al., 2018;
052   Zbontar et al., 2021; Ermolov et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2021) which minimize the pairwise linear
053   correlation between embedding dimensions and an adversarial approach that decorrelates dimen-
      sions beyond linearity (Brakel & Bengio, 2017), but that is prone to instability during training.

070   Still, there is to date no stable method for mutual and non-linear dependence reduction. Indeed,
071   finding a training objective for data independence that would be at the same time differentiable and
072
      scale to high-dimensional data without making assumptions about the underlying data distributions
      is notably difficult.
073
074   This paper presents a training algorithm to reduce the dependence between learned embedding di-
075   mensions using neural networks. The algorithm involves an adversarial game between two types of
076   players: (1) a series of small neural networks are trained to predict one embedding dimension of a
077   representation given the other dimensions, and (2) an encoder is trained to counter reconstruction by
078
      updating the representations’ distribution.
079   Experimentally, we show that the game systematically converges to an equilibrium where the depen-
080   dence between the embedding dimensions is minimal. We then investigate minimizing dependence
081   as a proxy for information maximization and demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness both with
082   and without the help of supervision. Specifically, experiments suggest that the algorithm helps a
083   classification model learn concepts beyond label supervision, demonstrating great generalization
084
      capabilities. We also show that the method can learn rich representations without supervision by
      training a self-supervised method on both synthetic data and the large-scale ImageNet dataset.
085
086   The main contribution of this paper is to introduce a stable algorithm for nonlinear mutual depen-
087   dence minimization and to verify its convergence empirically. We further propose a modification
088   of the algorithm that enables networks to keep some level of redundancy when required and study
089   its impact on generalization. Finally, we discuss the implications of the algorithm on information
090
      maximization and demonstrate its effectiveness on the Clevr-4 dataset. In particular, the algorithm
      improves generalization in supervised learning and is also effective without supervision.

093   2   R ELATED WORK
094
095
      Dimensionality reduction has a long history in machine learning, with early works already focusing
096   on finding a representation with uncorrelated variables. Notably, the Principal Component Analysis
097   (PCA, Pearson (1901); Hotelling (1933)) transforms a large set of random variables into a smaller
098   set of uncorrelated variables, known as principal components. This transformation is achieved while
099   preserving the maximal variance in the original dataset, thereby reducing dimensionality without
100   significant loss of information. More recently, approaches in self-supervised representation learning
101   (SSL) also exploited decorrelation (Huang et al., 2018; Zbontar et al., 2021; Ermolov et al., 2021;
102   Bardes et al., 2021) to minimize the dependence between the output dimensions of a deep neural
103   network and as a means to avoid collapsed representations, a common issue in SSL (Jing et al., 2022;
104
      Hua et al., 2021). In this work, we investigate decorrelation beyond pairwise linear dependencies.
105   Autoencoders. The PCA algorithm is closely related to autoencoders (AEs, Kramer (1991);
106   Rumelhart et al. (1986a)). An autoencoder consists of two neural networks: an encoder that com-
107   presses the input into a latent space and a decoder that reconstructs the input from this representation.
      Both networks are trained jointly with a reconstruction error. Interestingly, the optimal solution of a


                                                                  2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      linear AE corresponds to performing PCA. However, unlike PCA, an autoencoder can learn nonlin-
109   ear dimensionality reductions, but its latent space is not guaranteed to have uncorrelated dimensions.
110   The variational autoencoder (VAE, Kingma (2013)) is an important extension of the autoencoder.
111   It uses a probabilistic approach where the encoder maps the input to a distribution over latent vari-
112   ables and the decoder reconstructs data by sampling from this distribution. Its training involves a
113   criterion minimizing the divergence between the predicted and prior distributions. This extension
114   of AEs allows the generation of new samples similar to the training data. Shortly after its introduc-
115   tion, multiple works (Higgins et al., 2017; Burgess et al., 2018; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al.,
116   2018) proposed variations to the VAE objective to encourage disentanglement between the latent
117
      variables. Intuitively, disentanglement implies learning representations where changes in one factor
      of variation correspond to changes in a single feature, but disentangled concepts may be dependent.
118
      However, while disentanglement is appealing, the problem is ill-defined (Locatello et al., 2019) and
119
      is not the focus of this study.
120
121   Input-output mutual information maximization. Another important line of work on dimension-
122   ality reduction relies on the infomax principle (Linsker, 1988; Bell & Sejnowski, 1995), which
123
      suggests maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the input data and the output of a neural
      network to learn informative representations. Similar to our work’s objective, MI is an information-
124
      theoretic measure of the information shared by two random variables. Mutual Information Neu-
125
      ral Estimation (MINE, Belghazi et al. (2018)) provided a first estimate of the MI between high-
126   dimensional continuous random variables using neural networks. Built upon MINE, DeepInfo-
127   Max (Hjelm et al., 2019) learns representations by optimizing three criteria: (1) maximizing the MI
128   between the input and the output, (2) maximizing the MI between global and local representations,
129   and (3) matching the output distribution to a uniform prior with the help of adversarial learning.
130   Different from methods derived from the infomax principle, our algorithm minimizes redundancy
131   directly within the representation instead of maximizing a proxy for the input-output MI.
132   Adversarial learning. We now discuss the core training paradigm behind our algorithm and its
133   most notable extensions. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs, Goodfellow et al. (2014)) are
134   the first approach to train neural networks jointly with an adversarial objective: a generator is trained
135   to create realistic synthetic data, while a discriminator is trained to predict whether a sample came
136   from the training dataset or the generator. Inspired by GANs, Makhzani et al. (2015) introduced
137   Adversarial Autoencoders. This method trains adversarial networks to match the aggregated poste-
138   rior distribution of an autoencoder’s latent space with an arbitrary prior distribution. Thus, sampling
139   from any point of the distribution results in meaningful data generation. InfoGANs (Chen et al.,
140
      2016) is another related extension of GANs: they incorporated an information-theoretic criterion
      into GANs to learn disentangled and interpretable representations. Specifically, the method maxi-
141
      mizes a lower bound of the MI between the latent variables and the generated data. Our approach
142
      differs from both as it neither matches the representation’s distribution to a prior distribution nor
143   maximizes a proxy for MI. Furthermore, our algorithm is not bound to generative networks.
144
145   Most similar to our work, Brakel & Bengio (2017) used adversarial networks to decrease dependence
146
      by training an encoder to produce samples from a joint distribution that are indistinguishable from
      samples of the product of its marginals. However, this training objective is unstable and requires
147
      careful tuning, while ours systematically converges to the desired equilibrium.

284   4     A PPLICATIONS
285
286   Encoding input data into representations with independent embedding dimensions does not neces-
287   sarily create a useful structure for downstream tasks. For instance, assume an optimal representa-
288   tion with independent features. Research in nonlinear independent component analysis (NLICA)
289   demonstrated that there are countless ways to transform the representation while maintaining statis-
290   tical independence (Darmois, 1951; Jutten & Karhunen, 2004). These transformations can involve
291   complex mixing functions that result in representations that may be challenging to interpret or ex-
292   hibit undesirable properties for certain applications. For examples of mixing transformations, we
293   refer to (Taleb & Jutten, 1999).
294   Therefore, an additional objective function is required to guide the network towards a practical so-
295   lution. This work focuses on the application of minimizing dependence as a proxy for information
296   content maximization. We investigate the combination of the adversarial loss with a source of super-
297   vision (Section 4.1) and as a self-supervised objective (Section 4.2). In the following, we define the
298   learning objective of the encoder to be a weighted combination of the adversarial loss of maximizing
299   the reconstruction error Ladv and a task-specific loss function Ltask .
300
301   4.1   I NFORMATION MAXIMIZATION FOR CLASSIFICATION
302

304   imum. To counter this limitation, we encourage a model to minimize redundancy as a proxy for

306   representation of a classifier, zstd be its standardized version and l = W z + b be the logits vector
307
      with W ∈ Rnc ×d and b ∈ Rnc where nc is the number of training classes. We formulate the loss
      function as a weighted combination of the adversarial reconstruction loss applied to zstd and the
308
      softmax cross-entropy loss:

339   4.2                       S ELF - SUPERVISED LEARNING
340
341   Our algorithm also finds applications in self-supervised representation learning. In a similar spirit
342   to decorrelation SSL techniques (Huang et al., 2018; Zbontar et al., 2021; Ermolov et al., 2021;
343   Bardes et al., 2021), our adversarial objective pushes representations to be minimally redundant and
344   prevents collapse to a trivial solution (Jing et al., 2022). However, different from those approaches,
345   ours is not bound to pairwise linear decorrelation.
346   We add an invariance loss term that enforces consistency between the input and the output by push-
347   ing two augmentations of the same image to be close in the embedding space, i.e. a small change
348   in the input should not lead to a completely different output. Following the methodology from
349   SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), we sample a minibatch of n images and duplicate every image. We
350   then apply different data augmentations to the two views of each image and enforce invariance by
351   minimizing the MSE between the representations z and z ′ from corresponding augmented views:

      dataset (Johnson et al., 2017). The dataset comprises 100,000 synthetic images representing 3D
406
      objects of various shapes, colors, textures, and counts. Each taxonomy has 10 different classes. The
407
      label for one taxonomy is sampled uniformly and independently from the other ones, which means
408   that knowing the label for one taxonomy provides no information about the other taxonomies.
409
410   Evaluation protocols. We investigated generalization capabilities by training a classifier on one
411
      taxonomy and evaluating its accuracy on the remaining taxonomies to assess if representations en-
      code features beyond the ones relevant to the training classes. The model is compared with a baseline
412
      classifier trained without the adversarial game. We evaluate the accuracy with a simple weighted-
413
      nearest neighbor (kNN) classifier trained on top of frozen features following common practice in
414   SSL (Wu et al., 2018; Caron et al., 2021). The kNN algorithm classifies predictions based on the
415   majority class of their nearest neighbors in the embedding space, providing an easy way to assess
416   the clustering quality for every taxonomy. Similarly, the SSL adversarial model is evaluated with a
417   kNN classifier on the four taxonomies.
418
      Implementation details. Both the supervised and SSL models are trained on the Clevr-4 dataset
419
      for 200 epochs. We apply two data augmentations during training: random horizontal flipping with
420   p = 0.5 and random cropping by keeping at least 60% of the image area, followed by resizing to
421   224 × 224 pixels. We train ResNet-18 encoders and two-layer dependence networks. The networks
422   are trained alternately, with one step for each network per iteration. We set the task-specific loss
423   coefficient to λ = 0.2 in both settings. More details are provided in Appendix C.2. We trained
424   the adversarial networks of the classification and SSL models with respectively an l1 reconstruction
425   loss with a margin of 0.4 and an MSE reconstruction loss on the standardized representations. The
426   different loss formulations are compared in Section 5.3.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we introduced a representation learning algorithm to minimize the dependence be-
526
      tween the embedding dimensions of a representation. Our method involves an adversarial game
527
      where small dependence networks identify dimension relationships, while the encoder exploits this
528   information to reduce dependencies. Our problem formulation leads to stable training and em-
529   pirically converges to minimally dependent representations. Furthermore, we observed that some
530   applications may benefit from representation with a small level of redundancy. Consequently, we
531   introduced an alternative formulation where the encoder only maximizes the reconstruction error of
532   dependence networks up to a set limit. We empirically verified the benefits of our algorithm on the
533   Clevr-4 dataset. Our method significantly improves generalization in supervised learning. It is also
534   effective without supervision, both on synthetic and natural images.
535   Our study suggests that the optimal level of redundancy varies depending on the application. How-
536   ever, additional research is necessary to gain a deeper understanding of this phenomenon. Another
537   promising area for future work is to explore how our approach’s generalization capabilities can be
538   leveraged for out-of-distribution detection or domain adaptation. Lastly, the best architecture for
539   the dependence networks remains to be studied, one may for instance consider Kolmogorov-arnold
      networks (Liu et al., 2024) for improved interpretability and convergence speed.

887   This section details the hyper-parameter tuning of the SimCLR and VICReg baselines.
888   We implemented the models following the original papers from SimCLR Chen et al. (2020) and
889   VICReg Bardes et al. (2021). We trained ResNet-18 backbones and trained each model with and
890   without a projection head to find which setup works best for each technique when applied to the
891   Clevr-4 dataset. For a fair comparison, we followed the same experimental setup as for our SSL
892   method: we used the SGD optimizer with a momentum of 0.9, and a weight decay of 2 · 10−5 . The
893   learning rate follows a cosine decay schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016) with 10 epochs of linear
894   warmup and is scaled with a linear scaling rule (Goyal, 2017).
895   We ran a grid search on the projection head choice, the learning rate, and the batch size. The models
896   were trained for 80 epochs and the best-performing model was then re-trained for 200 epochs. Its
897   results are reported in Table 2 from Section 5.2.
