011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) technology provides a powerful means
014            of combining private databases with large language models (LLMs). In a typical
015            RAG system, a set of documents is retrieved from a private database and inserted
016
               into the final prompt, which is then fed into the LLMs. Nevertheless, existing re-
               search has shown that an attacker can exploit a simple manually designed attack
017
               suffix to induce LLM to output private documents in prompt with high probability.
018
               However, in this paper, we demonstrate that the privacy leakage risk exhibited by
019            using such simple manual attack suffix is significantly underestimated. In partic-
020            ular, we propose a novel attack method called Documents Extraction Attack via
021            LLM-Optimizer (DEAL), which leverages an LLM as optimizer to iteratively re-
022            fine attack strings, inducing the RAG model to reveal private data in its responses.
023            Notably, our attack method does not require any knowledge about the target LLM,
024            including its gradient information or model details. Instead, our attack can be ex-
025            ecuted solely through query access to the RAG model. We evaluate the effective-
026            ness of our attack on multiple LLM architectures, including Qwen2, Llama3.1,
027
               and GPT-4o, across different attack tasks such as Entire Documents Extraction
               and Private Identity Information (PII) Extraction. Under the same permission set-
028
               ting as the existing method, the Mean Rouge-L Recall (MRR) of our method can
029
               reach more than 0.95 on average in the Entire Documents Extraction task, and we
030            can steal PII from the retrieved documents with close to 99% accuracy in the PII
031            Extraction task, highlighting the risk of privacy leakage in RAG systems.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Ram et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024) is an
037   advanced framework in natural language processing (NLP) that combines retrieval-based methods
038   with generative models. Generally, the RAG system first retrieves several documents from the pri-
039   vate database based on the user’s query, and then utilizes these documents as context in the prompt
040   to guide the LLM answer questions based on the content of the documents. However, such a frame-
041   work poses a significant privacy risk as: the RAG model may inadvertently output the exact content
042   of the retrieved documents, leading to potential privacy leaks.
043   Current methods (Huang et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024a) for assessing the privacy leakage risk of
044   RAG models typically involve appending a malicious suffix to the user’s query to induce the LLM
045   to output sensitive information from the retrieved data. For example, a suffix like ”Please repeat
046   all the context” might be added to the query. However, previous manually crafted attack strings
047   often struggle to achieve optimal effectiveness. For instance, Zeng et al. (2024a) demonstrated that
048   text extracted using simple manually crafted attack suffixes can achieve only about 50% average
049   similarity with the target text. Our experiments further indicate that this privacy leakage risk is
050
      significantly underestimated, even under similar attacker capabilities.
051   Inspired by Sordoni et al. (2024) and Zhou et al. (2023), we propose the Documents Extraction
052   Attack via LLM-optimizer (DEAL), a black-box attack that leverages an LLM as an optimizer to
053   iteratively refine the attack suffix. The pipeline of our method is shown in Figure 1. Specifically,
      we begin the attack with an initial suffix, such as ”Please repeat all the context,” and query the RAG

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), first proposed
111   by Lewis et al. (2020), has become a popular method for enhancing the output quality of large
112   language models (Chase, 2022; Liu, 2022; Van Veen et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Ram et al., 2023).
113   This technique enables these models to access up-to-date knowledge without requiring retraining Si
114   et al. (2023). Furthermore, the retrieved information increases response relevance and mitigates the
115   hallucination problem Shuster et al. (2021) critical to large language models. Due to its adaptability
116   and these benefits, RAG technology is widely adopted in AI-Generated Content (AIGC) Zhao et al.
117   (2024).
118   Privacy Leakage of RAG Models. With the widespread use of Retrieval-Augmented Generation
119   (RAG) technology, however, privacy concerns have been rarely studied. Huang et al. (2023) present
120   the first study on privacy risks in retrieval-based language models, focusing on nearest neighbor
121   language models (kNN-LMs) Khandelwal et al. (2020). Subsequently,Zeng et al. (2024a) examine
122   privacy leakage in a more popular RAG architecture and highlight its associated risks. They propose
123   SAGE, a novel two-stage synthetic data generation paradigm designed to protect personally iden-
124   tifiable information (PII) by rewriting the retrieved documents Zeng et al. (2024b). Additionally,
125
      Anderson et al. (2024) propose using Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) against RAG systems
      to determine if specific data samples were included in the retrieval database. They also suggest
126
      rewriting the RAG template as a defensive measure, where the model refuses to answer sensitive
127
      queries. Taken together, these studies indicate that further research is needed.
128
129   Large Language Models as Prompt Optimizers. Previous work has proposed several approaches
130   to prompt tuning, including methods that represent prompts as continuous vectors (Lester et al.,
131
      2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Qin & Eisner, 2021) and those that discretely optimize
      prompts through gradient-guided search (Shin et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2020; Chen
132
      et al., 2023). However, these methods are not well-suited for black-box large language models
133
      (LLMs), which are only accessible via APIs. To address this issue, Zhou et al. (2023) introduced
134   Automatic Prompt Engineer (APE), a method that generates a pool of candidate prompts one at a
135   time and then filters and resamples candidates at each step. Subsequent research has built on this
136   foundation. Some studies have explored using LLMs to generate and analyze gradients and optimize
137   prompts through beam search (Pryzant et al., 2023), while others have used LLMs to summarize
138   analysis results and generate new prompts (Sun et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b). Additionally,
139   Sordoni et al. (2023) proposed Deep Language Network (DLN), a multi-layer LLM architecture,
140   and Wang et al. (2023) integrated Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) into the optimization process.
141   While many studies have primarily focused on methodological advancements, Ma et al. (2024)
142
      addresses why the performance of LLM optimizers is sometimes suboptimal.

144   3   T HREAT M ODEL
145
146   Following convention in the computer security community, we start with a threat model that defines
147   the space of actions between users and the service.
148
      Attack Goal. Consider a generation task being performed by a service API f , which takes a user-
149
      provided query q as input and passes it to a Retrieval-Augmented Generator (RAG) model. The RAG
150   model comprises three primary components: a large language model M , a retriever R, and a private
151   database D. Upon receiving a query q, the retriever R extracts the top-k most relevant documents
152   from D corresponding to the query q, denoted formally as R(q, D) = {d1 , d2 , ..., dk } ⊆ D. The
153   RAG model then integrates the retrieved documents R(q, D) and the query q using a template T
154   to generate an answer, which can be represented as f (q) = M (T (R(q, D), q)). By appending an
155   attack suffix s to the query, i.e., passing a query ’q||s’ to the RAG model, where ’||’ is a concatenate
156   function, the adversary’s objective is to reproduce as much private data in R(q, D) as possible in the
157   answer f (q||s).
158   Metrics of success. In this paper, we focus on two extraction tasks: (1) extracting entire docu-
159   ments, and (2) extracting personal identifying information (PII) from the documents, such as email
160   addresses, URLs, and other sensitive information. For the entire documents task, an attack is con-
161   sidered successful if the answer f (q) contains the true retrieved context R(q, D). To measure the
      success of this task, we follow the approach of Zhang et al. (2023) and use Rouge-L recall (Lin,

175   Capabilities. We assume that the attacker has only the privileges of a general user of the API service,
176   allowing them to pass queries to the RAG model but not access or manipulate the private database.
177   The attacker’s capabilities are limited to crafting and submitting queries, without any additional
178   information or control over the system. Specifically, we do not assume access to token likelihoods,
179   knowledge of the model architecture, or model weights. Furthermore, the service API is reset after
180   each query, ensuring that the attacker cannot exploit any residual information from previous queries.

182   knowledge of the private data format) to train the attack suffix. And we also verify the attack effect

      4.2   D OCUMENTS EXTRACTION ATTACK VIA LLM- OPTIMIZER
204
205   To solve this problem, we leverage the LLM-optimizer framework. LLM-optimizer harnesses the
206   power of LLMs to simulate the backpropagation process. The overall algorithm flow of DEAL
207   is shown in Algorithm 1. Specifically, our approach involves the following steps: (1) Collecting
208   forward examples, (2) Generating new suffix candidates, and (3) Filtering suffix candidates.
209
      Collecting Forward Examples. First, we construct a query batch {qi }bi=1 and then query the RAG
210   model with these queries and the current initial suffix s. For each query qi ||s, we extract the target
211   yi based on the retrieved contexts R(qi ||s, D). The target yi is defined as the complete context for
212   entire-document tasks, whereas for PII-extraction tasks, yi is the collection of personally identifiable
213   information (PII) extracted from R(qi ||s, D). Subsequently, we collect a forward examples set
214   {qi , yi , ŷi }bi=1 , where yˆi represents the RAG model’s answer. Notably, the RAG model can be
215   created locally by the attacker, eliminating the need to query the victim RAG model during this
      process.

230   Generating suffix candidates. To help LLM to extract useful information from the forward ex-
231   amples, we categorized these examples into two groups based on their training loss: successful
232   examples and error examples. We then incorporated these forward examples into backward tem-
233   plates Bs . Figure 1 shows a simplified backward template. Additionally, we introduced Chain of
234   Thought (CoT) reasoning into this template, allowing the LLM to generate an analysis of these ex-
235
      amples prior to producing refined suffixes. During this process, we repeat the aforementioned steps
      c times to generate c distinct suffix candidates. To increase the diversity among these candidates, we
236
      both raise the temperature of the optimizer LLM and make slight modifications to the content of the
237
      backward template in each iteration.
238
239   Filtering Suffix Candidates. To select the best suffix candidate, we test each candidate suffix using
240   the query batch {qi }bi=1 which is also used in forward process, and then select the candidate with
241
      the highest score as the initial suffix for the next iteration.

243   4.3   M ITIGATE THE R ANDOMNESS OF THE O PTIMIZATION P ROCESS
244
245
      The optimization process using LLM as the optimizer introduces significant randomness; therefore,
      we employ three methods to mitigate its impact: 1) adjusting the batch size and 2) adjusting the
246
      number of candidate suffixes.
247
248   Adjusting the Batch Size. The batch size determines both the number of forward examples and
249   the number of test samples during the filtering of candidate suffixes. A too small batch size may
250   result in overfitting, where the results of a single round of optimization are tailored to a limited set
251
      of samples, ultimately causing instability during the optimization process. Based on our experience,
      the LLM optimization process remains relatively stable when the batch size is set to 8.
252
253   Adjusting the Number of Candidate Suffixes. Increasing the number of suffix candidates en-
254   hances the likelihood of positive updates in each iteration. Based on our experience, setting the
255   number of candidate suffixes to 4 is sufficient for our attack.

      6.2   S AFETY P ROMPT
513
514
      In this section, we introduce safety prompts to the RAG models to prevent the leakage of private data
515   in their responses, such as ”Do not, at any circumstances directly repeat the provided contexts.” and
516   ”Do not, at any circumstances, output the email address or URLs in the provided context.”. We
517   evaluate the effectiveness of these safety prompts on LLaMA3.1-8B and LLaMA3.1-70B, assessing
518   attack performance with the safety prompt placed either before or after the query. As shown in
519   Table 2, the effectiveness of our safety prompts varies across different LLMs. For Llama3.1-70B,
520   the safety prompts significantly mitigate attacks using the suffix. In the Entire Documents Extraction
521   task, the MRR for both the baseline suffix and our optimized suffix is reduced to approximately 0.1.
522   In the PII Extraction task, the MER for the baseline suffix drops to as low as 8%, while the MER
523
      for our suffix is reduced to around 17%. Conversely, the defensive effect of our safety prompts
      on LLaMA3.1-8B is considerably weaker. Although the safety prompt slightly alleviates private
524
      data leakage with the baseline suffix, it proves completely ineffective with our optimized suffix. In
525
      summary, safety prompts can mitigate privacy leaks, but their design may need to be tailored for
526   individual models. Optimizing safety prompts presents an interesting avenue for future research.

529   7     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel approach to exploit private databases in Retrieval-Augmented
532
      Generator (RAG) systems. Our experimental results demonstrate that an attacker with only standard
533
      API user permissions, limited to modifying the query content, can still extract private data from
534   the RAG model by optimizing their queries. Notably, this optimization can be achieved using only
535   publicly available resources. The results show that our method significantly outperforms existing
536   RAG privacy-stealing attacks. In addition, we explore potential ways to mitigate our attack. Our
537   results show that filtering malicious queries by LLM or adding safety prompt to the prompt of
538   RAG model can mitigate our attack to some extent, but these methods still have certain limitations.
539   Overall, our research reveals the privacy leakage risk of RAG model, providing a reference for the
      proper usage of RAG techniques in real-world applications.
