                                                             A BSTRACT
008
009            Although recommender systems (RS) have been well-developed for various fields of applications,
010            they suffer from the crisis of platform credibility with respect to RS confidence and fairness, which
011            may drive users away from the platform and result in the failure of the platform’s long-term suc-
012            cess. In recent years, a few works have tried to solve either the model confidence or fairness issue,
013            while there is no statistical guarantee for these methods. Therefore, there is an urgent need to solve
014
               both issues with a unifying framework with statistical guarantees. In this paper, we propose a novel
               and reliable framework called Guaranteed User Fairness in Recommendation (GUFR) to dynami-
015            cally generate prediction sets for users across various groups, which are guaranteed 1) to include
016            the ground-truth items with user-predefined high confidence/probability (e.g., 90%); 2) to ensure
017            user fairness across different groups; 3) to have the minimum average set size. We further design
018            an efficient algorithm named Guaranteed User Fairness Algorithm (GUFA) to optimize the pro-
019            posed method, and upper bounds of the risk and fairness metric are derived to help speed up the
020            optimization process. Moreover, we provide rigorous theoretical analysis concerning risk and fair-
               ness control and the minimum set size. Extensive experiments also validate the effectiveness of the
021
               proposed framework, which aligns with our theoretical analysis. The code is publicly available at
022            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GUFR-76EC.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Recommender Systems (RS) (Aggarwal, 2016; Fan et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2024)are a type of information filtering
028
      system designed to provide suggestions to users based on their preferences. Decades of effort have been devoted to
      improving the accuracy of these recommendation models, However, less attention has been paid to model confidence,
029
      affecting users’ trust in the platform’s credibility. In recent years, few recommendation approachesNaghiaei et al.
030   (2022); KWEON et al. (2024) are developed for model confidence. However, these methods are heuristic modeling
031   without statistical guarantee. Meanwhile, fairness is another critical issue that could lead to poor user experience on
032   the platform’s reliability. Some fairness-based recommendation models Li et al. (2023) have been developed in recent
033   years Han et al. (2023; 2024b). While these papers effectively alleviate fairness issues in recommendation systems,
034   they are typically empirically validated without statistical guarantees for both the performance and fairness.
035   As a result, we are motivated to develop a complete and statistically guaranteed recommendation framework that con-
036   siders both model confidence and fairness issues as a whole in this paper. Our overall goal is to construct set predictors
037   that can generate the minimum prediction set for each user while being guaranteed to provide model confidence and
038   ensure user fairness among different groups. Thus, the objectives of our framework are threefold: (1) to construct
039
      prediction sets that cover the true item with a high user pre-defined probability, say 90% (i.e., confidence level); (2) to
      guarantee user fairness across different groups; and (3) to guarantee the minimum average set size while ensuring (1)
040
      and (2).

081   2.1   R ECOMMENDATION
082
      Recommender systems (Ko et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2015), which are used to help users make decisions via personalized
083   content or product recommendations, have been thoroughly studied over the past decades by developing recommen-
084   dation models for different fields of application, such as e-commerce (Schafer et al., 1999), media streaming (Chang
085   et al., 2017), social networks (He et al., 2024) etc. To ensure the long-term success of digital platforms, credibility
086   and fairness of recommendation are two crucial factors today that are urgently needed to ensure the satisfaction of
087   customers. Traditional recommendation models primarily focused on accuracy (Adomavicius & Tuzhilin, 2005; Ricci
088
      et al., 2010), but there is an increasing recognition that model confidence—quantifying the reliability of the recom-
      mendations—is equally important. Researchers try to develop recommendation approaches that provide the model
089
      confidence. For example, Kweon et.al. (2024) enhances model confidence by dynamically adjusting recommendation
090   strategies based on performance metrics, ensuring the model adapts to user interactions and maintains high predictive
091   accuracy. Naghiaei et al. (2022) proposes a confidence-aware optimization-based re-ranking algorithm that accounts
092   for calibration confidence based on user profile size. However, these methods are heuristic modeling without statisti-
093   cal guarantee. Meanwhile, some fairness-based recommendation models have been developed in recent years, which
094   usually focus on a particular fairness issue in specific fields of application. Fairness in recommendation systems can
095
      be viewed from diverse perspectives Li et al. (2023). One such perspective is Individual fairness and Group fairness.
      Individual fairness requires that similar individuals receive comparable treatment. However, defining this similarity
096
      is challenging due to disagreements over task-specific similarity metrics Dwork et al. (2011). Group fairness, on the
097   other hand, ensures that protected groups receive treatment comparable to that of advantaged groups or the general
098   population Pedreschi et al. (2009), thus ensuring equitable treatment across predefined groups. Group fairness can be
099   further classified from the user side or item/platform side. Focusing on User-Side group Fairness, it can be defined
100   based on sensitive features like age, gender, race, etc. Yao & Huang (2017) utilized gender to distinguish between
101   advantaged and disadvantaged user groups and measured prediction discrepancies. Another approach utilizes differ-
102
      entiating groups based on user interactions as defined by Li et al. (2021a) and Abdollahpouri et al. (2019). They
      defined unfairness between users based on their interaction levels or popular item interactions, labeling the top 5% as
103
      active or advantaged. To ensure fairness, existing works apply several techniques, most commonly in regularization
104   and constrained optimization Li et al. (2021a); Islam et al. (2021), where fairness is given as a regularization param-
105   eter. Furthermore, Han et al. (2024a) mitigate unfairness by sharing training samples within and across user groups,
106   effectively addressing data sparsity. Another work by Han et al. (2024b) proposes a hypergraph-based framework
107   that models high-order correlations among users to improve training for disadvantaged users. Some other approaches
      use Reinforcement Learning by formulating the problem as a Constrained Markov Decision Process Ge et al. (2021;

108
      2022). To evaluate the fairness, Yao & Huang (2017) introduced four group metrics to evaluate collaborative filtering
109   recommender models. Fu et al. (2020) employed the Group Recommendation Unfairness (GRU) metric to assess
110   disparities across these user groups based on performance metrics. They depicted it balances fairness with utility—a
111   dual improvement noted by Rahmani et al. (2022), under certain conditions. However, to the best of our knowledge,
112   none of these approaches ensured a statistical guarantee of the fairness problem.

114   2.2   RISK - CONTROLLING PREDICTION SETS
115
      We develop uncertainty quantification for the model confidence and fairness based on Risk-Controlling Prediction
116
      Sets (RCPS) Bates et al. (2021b). RCPS is a general framework, not a specific algorithm, for producing predictive sets
117   that satisfy the risk control in Definition 2.1. Different contexts require different designs of risk or other estimators to
118   achieve the best performance. For example, in the context of medical diagnosis, if the set S(X) represents plausible
119   diagnoses based on patient features X and R(S) can be defined as the expected risk of the loss from missing true
120   diagnoses, then RCPS ensures this risk to remain below α with confidence 1 − δ. This enables doctors to automatically
121   screen for many diseases (e.g., via a blood sample) and refer the patient to relevant specialists. We will apply the
122   framework of RCPS to the designed risk and fairness in the context of recommendation.
123   Definition 2.1 (Risk-controlling prediction sets (RCPS) Bates et al. (2021b)). Let S be a random function taking values
124   in the space of functions X → Y ′ (e.g., a functional estimator trained on data). We say that S is a (α, δ) − RCP S if,
      with probability at least 1 − δ, we have R(S) ≤ α.

      6.3.3    T IME E FFICIENCY C OMPARISON
509   We analyze the computational cost (training time) of the GUFR framework in comparison with other fairness baselines.
510   Specifically, for in-processing fairness baselines such as NFCF and MFCF, we consider the fine-tuning step to calculate
511   the training time. For post-processinng fairness baselines such as NeuMF-UFR and GMF-UFR, we take the re-
512   reranking step as the training time. For proposed GUFR framework, we take the calibration step as the training time.
513   We measure the time of GUFR, averaged on top of all the base models. Our experiments are conducted via 10-fold
      cross validation to ensure statistical reliability. The results are presented in Table 5.
514
515   From the results, we can observe that our proposed framework GUFR is significantly more time-efficient than the
516   other fairness baselines, which indicates the scalability of our method. This is because in-processing methods like
517   NFCF and MFCF involves model refitting which substantially increases the computational cost. By contrast, GUFR
      operates independently of the training phase, eliminating this overhead. Additionally, GUFR is substantially faster than
518
      NeuMF-UFR and GMF-UFR, other post-processing methods, because these models involve solving a constrained and
519   complex optimization problem, whereas GUFR employs a simple yet effective greedy-based algorithm.

      7   C ONCLUSION
523
      This paper investigates two principle issues that affect the credibility of RS with respect to confidence and fairness.
524
      We integrate the two factors into a unified framework called Guaranteed User Fairness in Recommendation (GUFR),
525   which dynamically outputs prediction sets that are guaranteed to have the risk and fairness below a threshold with pre-
526   specified high confidence, such as 90%, while retaining the minimum average size. We conduct theoretical analysis
527   and empirical studies, which are consistent in validating the effectiveness. It is noteworthy that the efficiency of
528   optimizing the GUFR also depends on the tightness of the derived upper bounds for our risk and fairness, thus, we
529   leave the question whether there exists tighter upper bounds for the future work. Moreover, the proposed framework
530
      can work on top of any recommendation model by taking them as black-box, which offers a robust foundation for
      advancing fairness and reliability in RS, paving the way for future research and development in this field.

794   A.3.1     DATASETS AND G ROUPING M ETHODS
795
      In the main paper, we introduced four user grouping strategies to evaluate the fairness and performance of our frame-
796
      work: (1) grouping based on interaction count with items, (2) grouping based on user age, (3) grouping based on
797   user gender, and (4) grouping based on geographic categorization into developed and other countries. These strategies
798   were applied to the AmazonOffice, Book-Crossing, MovieLens, and Last.fm datasets, respectively. Below, we provide
799   further details on the grouping methodology:
800
801           • Grouping by interaction count: Following Li et al. (2021a), users were initially evenly split into two groups,
802             with 50% assigned to each group. The groups were then dynamically adjusted to ensure that the minimum
803
                interaction count in the advantaged group exceeded the maximum count in the disadvantaged group by at
                least one.
804
805           • Grouping by age: Users were divided into two age groups: younger users (≤ 60 years) and older users (> 60
                years).
806
807           • Grouping by gender: Users were grouped into binary categories based on identified gender (male and
808
                female).
809           • Geographic categorization: Users were categorized based on their country of origin into developed (e.g.,
                USA, UK, Europe, Japan etc.) and other countries.


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Furthermore, we conducted an additional grouping experiment on the Last.fm dataset. We extended the interaction
811   count-based grouping to incorporate interactions with popular items, following Abdollahpouri et al. (2019). The
812   results of this experiment are provided in Appendix A.4.2.
813
814   A.3.2    S AMPLING AND DATA S PLITTING
815
816   We followed the following sampling and splitting method:
817
              • Negative sampling: Following Ma et al. (2024), we selected 50 non-interacted items per user through nega-
818
                tive sampling for training, validation, and testing.
819
820           • Data splitting: We employed the Leave-One-Out (LOO) strategy (He et al., 2017; Han et al., 2023) to
                partition the dataset into training, calibration, and testing sets. Specifically, for each user, one interaction was
821
                isolated for calibration and testing, while the remaining interactions were used for training.
822
823           • Multiple trials: To account for variability in sampling and splitting, we repeated the experiments over 20
                independent trials. For each trial, random negative samples were drawn for training, validation, and testing.
824
                The results were averaged across all the trials.

828   To evaluate the effectiveness of our framework, we implemented it on top of the five base recommender models

              • LightGCN: Configured with an embedding size of 8 and 3 graph convolution layers.

848

      This is particularly useful in real-world scenarios, where different applications may have different definitions of fair-
      ness. By allowing any grouping method, the framework can support dynamic fairness criteria that can evolve with
947   changing societal norms or organizational policies, thereby allowing practitioners to define user groups based on the
948   specific biases or fairness concerns relevant to their context.
949
950
      A.5   P RACTICAL A PPLICABILITY OF THE F RAMEWORK
951
952
      We now analyze the practical applicability of our framework. In real-world recommendation systems, prediction sets
953   are often fixed to a specific size k and applied uniformly across all users. This fixed size is typically determined
954   heuristically or through trial and error, aiming to maximize the likelihood of including items that users may interact
955   with while prioritizing and ranking items by relevance. However, this heuristic approach has several limitations:
956
957          • Fixed-size sets can lead to cognitive overload for users when the size is too large or fail to meet individual
958            user needs when the size is too small.
959          • They do not account for disparities in user engagement or group fairness, potentially disadvantaging certain
960            user groups.
961
             • Recommending unnecessary items results in resource inefficiencies for platforms.
962
963
      Our framework addresses these challenges by dynamically determining the minimum prediction set size for each user,
964   satisfying fairness and performance guarantees with statistical confidence (e.g., 95%). This complements the existing
965   recommender systems as we can employ an appropriate aggregation method (for example, mean) to compute global
966   k. This global k, obtained with the theoretical guarantees, can then be applied to unseen users, ensuring that fairness
967   and performance guarantees hold across the system.
968   For example, in e-commerce platforms such as Amazon, instead of heuristically fixing k = 10 for all users, our frame-
969   work identifies an optimal k (e.g., k = 7) that balances fairness and accuracy, reducing unnecessary recommendations
970   and enhancing user satisfaction while optimizing platform resources. Similarly, in streaming services like Netflix,
971   dynamically adjusting k in cold-start scenarios ensures concise and personalized recommendations, preventing user
      overwhelm and aligning with platform resource constraints.
