009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) are increasingly developed to meet
012            diverse deployment needs, varying in scale and computational demand. While
013            recent research has focused on building MLLMs from Small Language Models
014            (SLMs), these efforts remain limited in flexibility and are still data- and compute-
015            intensive. In this paper, we present the first comprehensive study on flexibly
016            compressing and recovering existing MLLMs in a data-efficient manner. Hence,
017            we address a critical gap in the literature by empirically analyzing best prac-
018            tices for adapting to specific hardware or resource limitations. Our study inves-
019            tigates pruning and knowledge distillation techniques, examining their impact on
020
               downstream performance across various model compression strategies, including
               pruning paradigms, recovery training schemes, and data requirements. Key find-
021
               ings reveal that widthwise pruning is particularly effective in resource-constrained
022
               scenarios. For smaller compression ratios, finetuning the multimodal projector
023            alone can restore most performance, while combining finetuning with hidden state
024            knowledge distillation proves most effective across all compression levels. No-
025            tably, we demonstrate efficient model downsizing using as little as 5% of the orig-
026            inal dataset for moderate compression. Our analysis suggests best practices for
027            compressing MLLMs for resource-efficient deployment. With our best practices,
028            Bunny-v1.0-3B retains over 95% of its original performance, while LLaVA-v1.5-
029            7B maintains more than 97%, with compression ratios below 30%.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      State-of-the-art multimodal large language models (MLLMs) (Liu et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023;
035
      Chen et al., 2024b) based on Large Language Models (LLMs) require substantial resources. For
036   instance, the LLaVA family (Liu et al., 2023) includes models with parameter counts ranging from 7
037   to 34 billion. Even those designed to be more memory-efficient, such as Bunny-v1.0-3B (He et al.,
038   2024), still require significant storage, with 3 billion parameters. Reducing the size of these models
039   without compromising performance is crucial for adapting them to diverse deployment scenarios
040   with varying resource constraints.
041
      Despite the growing need for efficient MLLMs, most existing research has focused on building
042   MLLMs on SLMs (Zhu et al., 2024a; He et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2023). While these approaches
043   successfully reduce the overall model size, their flexibility is constrained by the fixed size of the
044   underlying SLM. Furthermore, training an SLM from scratch to meet desired specifications is com-
045   putationally expensive (Chu et al., 2023). Meanwhile, efforts to compress multimodal models have
046   largely focused on task-specific tuning (Wang et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023). To the best of our
047   knowledge, no previous work has investigated general-purpose model compression for MLLMs.
048   In this work, we aim to uncover the key practices for obtaining effective and compressed MLLMs.
049   We evaluate several techniques designed for compressing LLMs on MLLMs and investigate how
050   different design choices for performance recovery affect the downstream performance of the com-
051   pressed MLLMs. Our comprehensive empirical study explores several key dimensions: the pruning
052   paradigms applied, the objectives used to restore initial performance, and the amount of data required
053   for effective recovery. Specifically, we examine two MLLMs: a large-scale model (LLaVA-v1.5-7B
      (Liu et al., 2024)) and a model already optimized for efficiency (Bunny-v1.0-3B (He et al., 2024)).

084

089

091                    model even without recovery training.

                     • With recovery training, layerwise pruning is better for small ratios, while widthwise

095                  • Finetuning only the multimodal projector is sufficient at small compression ratios,

      Pruning. Unstructured pruning (Dong et al., 2017; Frankle & Carbin, 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Park
      et al., 2020; Sanh et al., 2020b; Farina et al., 2024) removes individual weights or neurons. While
112
      such approaches can achieve strong compression rates with minimal accuracy trade-offs, they usu-
113
      ally require specialized hardware or software for effective acceleration. Structured pruning (Ding
114   et al., 2019; Li et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2021; You et al., 2019) eliminates entire groups of parameters
115   to reduce both the model’s size and its computational overhead. Semi-structured pruning offers a
116   middle ground between structured and unstructured methods by selectively pruning certain model
117   parts. In the context of LLMs, Fang et al. (2023) and Ma et al. (2023) have successfully applied
118   structured pruning, achieving significant sparsity with minimal performance degradation. Xia et al.
119   (2024) targets transformer layers and demonstrates that some layers can be pruned more aggres-
120   sively without compromising accuracy. Meanwhile, Dery et al. (2024) propose a dynamic pruning
121   strategy that adjusts pruning throughout the training process. In this work, we focus on structure
122
      pruning as well as the recovery strategies for MLLMs.
123   Further Compression Methods. In addition to pruning, techniques like quantization and low-rank
124   factorization are also widely used for model compression. Quantization (Bai et al., 2021; Yao et al.,
125   2022; Zafrir et al., 2019) reduces model size and computational cost by lowering the precision of
126   model parameters, enabling efficient inference with minimal performance loss. Low-rank factoriza-
127   tion (Hsu et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2021b; Lan et al., 2020; Ashkboos et al., 2024) compresses models
128
      by approximating large weight matrices through the product of smaller matrices, effectively reduc-
      ing the number of parameters while maintaining most of the model’s capacity. While these methods
129
      can offer significant compression, we focus on pruning techniques, which allow for more granular
130
      control over the architecture by directly targeting and removing redundant components.
131
132   Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is a standard method for compressing LLMs by
133   transferring knowledge from a large teacher model to a smaller student model (Gou et al., 2021;
134
      Sanh et al., 2020a). In NLP classification settings, KD is often applied by having the student model
      replicate the teacher’s output distribution (Liang et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023),
135
      hidden states Jiao et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2019), or attention patterns (Wang et al., 2020; 2021),
136
      allowing the student to learn from the teacher’s internal representations effectively. For text gener-
137   ation tasks, Xu et al. (2024) provides a comprehensive survey of the role of knowledge distillation
138   in language models. Hsieh et al. (2023) introduce multi-stage distillation, transferring intermediate
139   representations to help the student model capture more detailed features. Gu et al. (2023) propose to
140   replace the forward Kullback-Leibler divergence with a reverse Kullback-Leibler divergence to pre-
141   vent the student model from overestimating the low-probability regions of the teacher distribution.
142
      Efficient MLLMs. Recent studies (Jin et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024; Wei et al.,
143   2024) have explored Multimodal Small Language Models (MSLMs). Models such as LLaVA-Phi
144   (Zhu et al., 2024b) utilize pretrained small language models to lower computational costs, while
145   MobileVLM (Chu et al., 2023) concentrates on projector designs to enhance MSLM performance.
146   The Bunny model (He et al., 2024) explores the effects of training data size on performace. Al-
147   though these approaches reduce model size, they are constrained by the fixed dimansions of the
148   base SLM. Our study specifically addresses methods for customizing the size of existing MLLMs
149   through structured pruning and recovery strategies.
150   While most multimodal structured compression efforts, such as EfficientVLM (Wang et al., 2023)
151   and UPOP (Shi et al., 2023), focus on task-specific tuning for tasks like visual question answer-
152   ing and image captioning, general-purpose model compression for MLLMs remains underexplored.
153   Our work addresses this gap by investigating structured pruning techniques and recovery strategies
154   applicable across a variety of multimodal tasks. Unlike previous approaches optimizing models for
155   specific tasks, our study provides general-purpose compression guidelines for MLLMs.

      Notation. Given a triplet X = {xv , xp , xr }, the objective of an MLLM mθ , parameterized by
163   θ = {ψ, ϕ, W}, is to generate a response xr based on an input image xv and a text prompt xp , such
164   that mθ (xv , xp ) = xr . The MLLM typically consists of a vision encoder gψ (·), an LLM fϕ (·), and
165   a multimodal projector W aligning the two modalities. The prompt xp is tokenized into Tp , while
166   the vision encoder processes the image xv to extract visual features, which are then converted into
167   language embedding tokens Tv via the multimodal projector:
168                                Tv = W · gψ (xv ) and fϕ (Tv ⊙ Tp ) = xr .                                (1)
169
170   The concatenated visual tokens Tv and prompt tokens Tp are fed into the LLM’s M layers, produc-
171   ing hidden states {Hi ∈ RT ×d }M i=1 , where T is the number of tokens and d is the hidden dimension.
172   Finally, the probabilities pmθ (xr |xv , xp , τ ) are computed by passing the final hidden state through
173   the classification head with softmax temperature τ .

175   3.1     P RUNING
176
177   Pruning seeks to reduce the number of parameters in a model, thus decreasing its computational
178   cost. In MLLMs, the majority of parameters θ are concentrated in the LLM fϕ , so downsizing it can
179
      significantly reduce the overall computational burden. The LLM is typically structured along two
      main dimensions: depth and width. Depth refers to the number of stacked transformer layers, while
180
      width pertains to the internal structure of each layer, including the multi-head attention mechanism
181
      and the multi-layer perceptron (MLP). In this paper, we explore two pruning strategies to reduce
182   the parameter count in LLMs: layerwise pruning, which removes entire transformer layers, and
183   widthwise pruning, which eliminates the least important components within each layer.
184
185
      To determine which layers or components to prune, we draw a small subset of n samples from
      the the original visual instruct-tuning dataset as the calibration dataset D = {xjv , xjp , xjr }nj=1 . The
186
      importance of each layer or component is assessed, and those with the lowest importance are pruned.
187
188

193   2024), which quantifies the importance of layer i through the cosine distance between input Hi and
194
      output hidden states Hi+1 . The key assumption is that layers that cause larger changes in hidden
      states have a greater influence on model performance. The BI score of layer i is then calculated by

231   3.2     R ECOVERY T RAINING
232
233   Pruning a large multimodal language model results in performance degradation, affecting both lan-
234   guage modeling and cross-modality alignment. To mitigate this, we investigate two recovery training
235
      methods: supervised finetuning (Sec. 3.2.1) and knowledge distillation (Sec. 3.2.2). We consider
      the orginal teacher model mTθ , the pruned student model mSθ′ , and a recovery dataset D.

      3.2.1    R ECOVERY T RAINING WITH SUPERVISED FINETUNING
238
239   A simple yet effective approach to recovery training is supervised finetuning on the original dataset.
240   This method helps counteract performance degradation by allowing the model to adapt its parame-
241   ters to the modified architecture while taking advantage of the detailed annotations in the original
242   dataset. Here, we first focus on training only the multimodal projector to realign the vision and lan-
243   guage spaces. Second, we jointly finetune both the projector and the language model while keeping
244   the vision encoder fixed, as finetuning the vision encoder does not improve performance (Karam-
245
      cheti et al., 2024). We use the cross-entropy loss for supervised finetuning, denoted as

248   3.2.2    R ECOVERY T RAINING WITH KNOWLEDGE DISTILLATION
249
250   Knowledge distillation (KD) is a method used to transfer knowledge from a large, well-trained model
251
      (the teacher) to a smaller or pruned model (the student) (Hinton et al., 2015). This approach allows
      the pruned model to regain lost performance by mimicking the decision-making process of the more
252
      capable teacher. In our setup, the uncompressed model acts as the teacher, while the pruned model
253
      serves as the student. We explore two main strategies, logits-based KD and hidden state based KD,
254   evaluating different loss functions and their trade-offs.
255
256   Logits-based KD focuses on aligning the output probability distributions of the pruned model with
257
      those of the teacher model. The logits-based KD loss is defined as
                                               h                                                       i

260   We leverage two distinct KD losses to evaluate the differences between these logit distributions.
261   Given the teacher distribution pθ and the student distribution qθ′ , the standard KD objective mini-
262   mizes the approximated forward Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between these two distributions,
263   denoted as LKD (pθ , qθ′ ) = DKL (pθ ∥qθ′ ). This approach encourages the student distribution to
264   match all the modes of the teacher distribution.
265   However, minimizing forward KL can lead qθ to assign excessively high probabilities to areas where
266   p has little or no probability mass (Malinin & Gales, 2019). In contrast, Reversed Kullback–Leibler
267   divergence (RKL) minimizes LKD (pθ , qθ′ ) = DKL (qθ′ ∥pθ ), encouraging qθ′ to focus on the major
268   modes of pθ while assigning low probabilities to its less significant regions. This helps the student
269   model avoid learning unnecessary long-tail variations of the teacher distribution and instead focus
      on generating more accurate responses (Gu et al., 2023; Holtzman et al., 2019).

291   Experimental setup. We evaluate pruning and knowledge distillation strategies on both a large-
292   scale MLLM model (LLaVA-v1.5-7B (LLaVA) (Liu et al., 2024)) and a smaller-scale MLLM model
293   (Bunny-v1.0-3B (Bunny) (He et al., 2024)). LLaVA is built upon Vicuna-v1.5 (Chiang et al., 2023)
294   with 6.7 billion parameters, and Bunny is based upon Phi-2 (Javaheripi et al., 2023) with 2.8 billion
295   parameters. We provide a detailed overview of the model architectures in Appendix A. For both
296   models, we exclusively use their visual instruction tuning datasets: LLaVA-v1-5-mix665k (Liu et al.,
297   2024) for LLaVA and Bunny-695K (He et al., 2024) for Bunny. During pruning, we randomly
298   select 10 samples as the calibration dataset to compute layer importance. For recovery training,
299
      we experiment with various portions of the original dataset (i.e. 5%, 10%, 20%, and 100%) for
      finetuning and knowledge distillation. We set the distillation temperature to 2.0 for logits-based
300
      distillation and use the final layer representation for hidden state matching (see Appendix B).
301
302   We evaluate the pruned and recovery-trained models on visual question-answering tasks using GQA
303   (Hudson & Manning, 2019) and SQA-I (Lu et al., 2022), as well as instruction-following tasks
304   with POPE (Li et al., 2023), MME-Cognition, MME-Perception (Yin et al., 2023) and MMMU
305
      (Yue et al., 2024). To ensure consistency, we use the lmms-eval suite (Bo et al., 2024) for all
      evaluations. For clearer comparisons, we calculate the relative performance as a percentage of the
306
      original (uncompressed) model’s performance on each benchmark.

497   4.5   M ODEL COMPRESSION RESULTS FOLLOWING OUR BEST PRACTICES
498
499   In this section, we summarize our key findings as a set of best practices and highlight model per-
500   formance achieved by following them. Based on the empirical results from the previous section, we
501   outline the following best practices for compressing MLLMs:
502
503
       Best Practices for MLLM Compression and Recovery
504    • Widthwise pruning is more effective in low-resource settings, yielding an efficient model
505      even without the need for recovery training.
506    • With recovery training, layerwise pruning excels for smaller compression ratios (below
507      40%), while widthwise pruning performs better at higher ratios (above 40%).
508    • For small compression ratios, fine-tuning just the multimodal projector is often sufficient
509      to restore performance, with minimal impact from pruning.
510
       • For recovery training, combining finetuning with knowledge distillation of the intermedi-
511      ate representations using L2 loss consistently achieves the highest performance across all
512      compression ratios.
513
       • Data efficiency can be significantly boosted, requiring only 5% of the original data to match
514
         full-data training results, though full datasets are still needed for high compression ratios.
515
516
      These guidelines provide insights for researchers aiming to develop new techniques for deploying
517
      MLLMs, enabling more effective model customization for specific deployment needs.
518
519   To illustrate the performance at different compression ratios, Table 3 offers a detailed comparison
520   of results for both Bunny and LLaVA across various multimodal benchmarks. The results show
521
      that, with compression ratios below 30%, Bunny retains over 95% of its original performance, while
      LLaVA maintains more than 97%. Even at higher compression ratios, up to 60%, our best practices
522
      preserve an average performance of 83% for Bunny and 78% for LLaVA. These findings underscore
523
      the feasibility of compressing MLLMs without incurring significant performance degradation. We
524   provide qualitative results for the compressed models in Appendix G. Additionally, other compres-
525   sion techniques, such as quantization, could be layered on top of the current framework to further
526   reduce inference time and memory usage, see Appendix H. We also include InternVL (Chen et al.,
527   2024a) in Appendix I to show our best practices generalize to other model.

531
      In this work, we investigated efficient compression techniques for MLLMs, focusing on two key
532
      pruning strategies: width and layerwise. Our study assessed the impact of these strategies on model
533   performance, both before and after recovery training, across various compression ratios. We further
534   explored recovery methods such as supervised finetuning and knowledge distillation to address per-
535   formance degradation caused by pruning. We formulate our findings as best practices, which offer
536   practical guidelines for optimizing MLLMs, enabling a balance between model size, performance,
537   and data efficiency to meet specific deployment resource constraints. Due to computational con-
538   straints, in this work we focused on two representative pruning techniques on two different models.
539   Future work could extend these best practices to include a broader range of pruning techniques and
      models to further refine these strategies.
