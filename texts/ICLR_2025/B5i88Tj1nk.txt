010
011
012            We identify significant gaps in the existing frameworks for assessing the faithful-
013            ness of post-hoc explanation methods, which are essential for interpreting model
014            behavior. To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel adversarial infor-
015            mation masking (AIM) approach that enhances in-distribution information mask-
016
               ing techniques. Our study conducts the first quantitative comparison of faith-
               fulness assessment frameworks across different architectures, datasets, and do-
017
               mains, facilitating a comprehensive evaluation of post-hoc explanation methods
018
               for deep learning of human electroencephalographic (EEG) data. This work lays
019            a foundation for further developments of reliable applications of explainable arti-
020            ficial intelligence (XAI). The code and sample data for this work are available at
021            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/EEG-explanation-faithfulness-5C05.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent advances in deep learning (DL) have strengthened the discussion around eXplainable Artifi-
029
      cial Intelligence (XAI) (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Samek et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017). Since
030
      most deep neural networks operate as ”black boxes” that lack direct interpretability (Samek et al.,
031   2016; Ancona et al., 2017), XAI is essential for three main reasons. First, it enables effective eval-
032   uation of AI-assisted decision-making processes (Goodman & Flaxman, 2017). Second, it assists
033   researchers in debugging and improving DL models (Cadamuro et al., 2016; Adebayo et al., 2020;
034   Krishna et al., 2024). Third, XAI reveals information that may be hidden from human perception
035   (Shrikumar et al., 2017). Among various XAI methods, model-agnostic approaches that provide in-
036   sight into what a model has learned are referred to as post-hoc explanations. These explanations can
037   be categorized based on the level of features they address, ranging from human-interpretable high-
038   level representations to low-level input features, with the categories termed training point ranking,
039
      concept activation, and feature attribution (Adebayo et al., 2022). In the field of electroencephalo-
      gram (EEG) analysis, the use of DL for decoding task-related patterns has shown significant success,
040
      leading to increased interest in recent years (Roy et al., 2019). In EEG-DL research, feature attribu-
041
      tion methods enhances our understanding of both the EEG data and the models employed through
042   visualizing saliency of input features (Tjoa & Guan, 2020; Pan et al., 2022; Bilodeau et al., 2024).
043
044   As the number of feature attribution methods expands, new concerns emerge regarding the quality of
045
      the explanations generated. Quantitative assessment of explanation quality remains a challenge, as
      it is often difficult to differentiate between model misbehavior and flaws inherent in the attribution
046
      methods (Sundararajan et al., 2017). Nonetheless, criteria for evaluating quality have become more
047
      widespread over the past decade. We summarize the related research in Table 1, which provides
048   a general overview of the current landscape. Recent efforts have primarily focused on assessing
049   the effectiveness of explanations in accurately representing the features that significantly influence
050   model decisions, a quality we will refer to as ”faithfulness”. As Shah et al. (2021) suggested, a
051   larger feature attribution indicate a higher relevance to model prediction. This can be understood
052   in two key ways: 1) model decisions can reflect the presence or absence of an input feature, and 2)
053   perturbations to important features tend to have a more pronounced impact on model decisions, and
      vice versa.

076   Building on the two key points, we categorize existing evaluation strategies for faithfulness into
077   two main types: ”fidelity analysis” (Yeh et al., 2019) and ”robustness analysis” (Hsieh et al., 2020;
078   Fang et al., 2024). Fidelity analysis quantifies the discrepancy between perturbations of input fea-
079
      tures based on explanations and the expected changes in model output. Since the absence of an
      important feature should result in a significant decline in model performance, a smaller discrep-
080
      ancy between actual degradation and expected change reflects higher fidelity or greater faithfulness
081
      of the explanation. Conversely, robustness analysis examines whether attribution magnitudes are
082   positively correlated with a feature’s susceptibility to adversarial attacks. Features that are less in-
083   fluential to model decisions should show greater tolerance to adversarial perturbations, and a faithful
084   explanation should accurately represent this by identifying such features as unimportant.
085
      However, existing frameworks for assessing faithfulness face several challenges:
086
087          • Current evaluations of post-hoc explanation methods rely on suboptimal information mask-
088            ing techniques, which can result in out-of-distribution imputations when applied to real-
089            world data.
090
             • Although multiple frameworks have been proposed, there is no standardized methodology
091            that enables a quantitative comparison among these explanation methods.
092
093   The challenges are elaborated in Section 2.1. To address these gaps in the context of EEG-DL
094   analysis, given the number of studies that have adopted XAI (Tjoa & Guan, 2020; Sujatha Ravindran
095   & Contreras-Vidal, 2023), our study proposes comprehensive faithfulness evaluation frameworks
096   incorporating multi-domain information masking techniques.
097   Our primary objective is to determine which explanation methods are most suitable for elucidating
098   EEG-DL models. Our contributions include:
099
100         1. We expand the leading in-distribution information masking method, Remove and Debias,
101            to accommodate multiple domains, including spatial, temporal, and spectral dimensions.
102         2. We introduce an adversarial information masking (AIM) approach to circumvent issues
103            related to hand-crafted distribution selection and to enhance in-distribution information
104            masking for multivariate time series data.
105         3. We assess the effectiveness of in-distribution information masking through a novel Multi-
106            Domain Adversarial Robustness (mdAR) framework that includes new normalized faith-
107            fulness metrics and an evaluation result consistency-based methodology for framework
               validation.

      2.2   Fidelity analysis EVALUATION FRAMEWORKS
163
164   RemOve And Retrain (ROAR) Hooker et al. (2018) introduced the ROAR evaluation framework
165   to address the issue of distribution shift. In this approach, after features are removed through fixed-
166   value imputation, the model is retrained to adapt to the altered data distribution. The faithfulness of
167   the model is then assessed based on the decline in accuracy of the retrained model.
168
      RemOve And Debias (ROAD) Building on ROAR, Rong et al. (2022) identified additional chal-
169
      lenges, including information leakage and ranking inconsistency, arising from fixed-value imputa-
170
      tion. Using mutual information theory, they proposed the ROAD framework, which employs Noisy
171   Linear Feature Imputation. This method minimizes the revelation of class-relevant information with-
172   out necessitating retraining, resulting in a consistent ranking of explanation faithfulness.
173
174   Geometric RemOve And Retrain (GOAR) Park et al. (2023) critically examined the ROAR and
175   ROAD frameworks from a geometric standpoint, highlighting their lack of invariance to coordinate
176
      transformations and neglect of directional information in the data’s geometric structure. To address
      these limitations, they proposed the GOAR framework, which incorporates a diffusion model into
177
      the ROAR process to purify the modified dataset, offering a coordinate-independent solution.
178
179
180   2.3   Robustness Analysis EVALUATION FRAMEWORKS
181
182
      Adversarial Robustness (AR) Deep neural networks are known to be vulnerable to adversarial
      perturbation (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and the EEG-DL models are no exception (Zhang & Wu,
183
      2019). As the common objective of an attack is to maximize the model failure while minimizing
184
      the perturbation scale, Hsieh et al. (2020) leveraged this idea and resorted to nuanced adversar-
185   ial perturbation as an alternative to the brute-force value imputations. Their faithfulness metric is
186   ”Robustness − S”, denoting the maximum perturbation tolerance on the feature subset S that was
187   attributed higher importance by the explanation. Although Hsieh et al. did not directly address the
188   abovementioned issues, we argue that this framework is a capable workaround by exploiting the
189   imperceptible and model parameter-related nature of adversarial perturbation.
190
      OOD-resistant Adversarial Robustness (OAR) Expanding on the adversarial robustness (AR)
191
      framework, Fang et al. (2024) introduced a novel approach that explicitly accounts for data dis-
192
      tribution by incorporating an out-of-distribution (OOD) reweighting block. This block employs a
193   variational graph autoencoder (VGAE) that is trained independently on the unmodified data. The
194   VGAE generates OOD scores for adversarial examples, enabling the reweighting of faithfulness
195   assessments for each explanation method based on its respective OOD score. However, it is im-
196   portant to note that the effectiveness of the VGAE training can be compromised in the presence of
197   significant data variability.
198
199   2.4   E XPLANATION EVALUATION IN EEG-DL MODEL INTERPRETATION
200
201   With the growing understanding of post-hoc explanations in computer vision, there has been a re-
202   cent expansion into other fields exploring this topic (Turbé et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024). To the
203   best of our knowledge, there are currently a few peer-reviewed studies that proposed systematic
204   quality evaluations on the subject of post-hoc explanations for DL-based EEG decoders. Apicella
205   et al. (2022) conducted a removal-based study with a 3-layered fully connected network trained for
206   an individual subject from an emotion EEG dataset. In the study, they experimented on MoRF and
207
      LeRF removal order from spatial (EEG electrode), spectral (frequency band), and temporal (time
      sample) perspectives. Cui et al. (2023) also conducted a removal-based study, this time on real
208
      EEG datasets and benchmark EEG-DL models. They designed experiments on spatial domain and
209
      different scaled temporal domain. Torres et al. (2023) applied trial-level ROAR framework on an
210   autism EEG dataset and its customized CNN. Finally, instead of using removal based method, Su-
211   jatha Ravindran & Contreras-Vidal (2023) utilize an EEG generation toolbox with the concept of
212   SNR, which is incompatiable with the idea of domain-specific and removal orders. They designed
213   sensitivity experiments on the synthetic EEG dataset using a toy DL model. However, a fully repro-
214   ducible explanation evaluation that incorporated the suboptimal evaluation strategies and validated
215   on open EEG datasets and benchmark EEG decoders is still absent, making it difficult to establish
      trust in previous evaluation outcomes (Singh et al., 2021; Rajpura et al., 2024).

      The latest auxiliary model-based frameworks (GOAR, OAR) are underpinned by the auxiliary
219   model’s ability to impose distribution constraint on the perturbed data. Preserving distribution for
220   natural multivariate time-series is a non-trivial task, dataset variability for one, the preparatory work
221   can be computationally exhaustive while remaining biased to the limited dataset on which the model
222   was trained (Rong et al. (2022) Appendix B, Fang et al. (2024)). Given these difficulties in justify-
223   ing such framework design on multivariate EEG, we take the ROAD and AR (SimOAR) frameworks
224   as the cornerstones to develop our explanation faithfulness evaluation framework for EEG, termed
225   multi-domain ROAD (mdROAD) and multi-domain AR (mdAR).

227   spectral (frequency band), and temporal (time sample) domain. In this work, we denote the multi-

236   3.1   M ULTI - DOMAIN ROAD FRAMEWORK
237
238   In the original ROAD study, Rong et al. (2022) stated that neighboring features are highly correlated,
239   thus a subtle imputation for a pixel can be constructed using the linear interpolation of its neighbors.
240   To ensure the linear relationship to not leak class-related information, a small noise ϵ is added to
241
      the computed interpolation, hence the name ”Noisy Linear Imputation”. We devised domain-wise
      feature imputation methods in the spirit of 1) use neighboring features to ensure in-distribution
242
      imputation and 2) introduce noise when the imputation is at risk of information leakage.
243
244   For spatial domain, the target are k electrodes ranked top/last in Sc . The imputation result is gen-
245   erated using weighted interpolation of the target’s neighboring channels according to the actual
246   electrode montage, the equation can be written as x′c∈Φc ,t = W xc∈Φ   / c +ϵ, where W stands for a
247
      weight matrix for mixing the signals of remaining channels. Since a complete set of neighboring
      channels includes four direct and four indirect neighbors and the weights should sum up to one, we
248
      set wd = 1/6 and wid = 1/12 as a Laplacian spatial filter commonly used in channel-wise impu-
249
      tation of EEG data (Banville et al., 2022). In addition, although Φc does not necessary to fall into a
250   connected region, it is often the case that the target electrodes become connected and the solution is
251   solved together as a sparse system.
252
253
      For spectral domain, the target is a consecutive frequency band that takes k % of sample power and
      is ranked most/least important in Sf . The bandwidth is determined exhaustively and differs for each
254
      configuration, details are provided in the appendix B.4. In addition to the basic concepts of noisy
255
      linear imputation, the spectral feature imputation is also inspired by the ”spectrum interpolation”
256   method developed for power line noise removal (Leske & Dalal, 2019). Empirical evidence (He,
257   2014) suggest that the EEG spectrum has a 1/f-like aperiodic scale-free background component
258   caused by the co-fluctuations of different frequencies (Donoghue et al., 2020), with a power law
259   exponent” falls in [0-3]. To ensure the 1/f-like spectrum structure, the imputation is generated by
260   a real polynomial of degree 3: P (f ) = Σ3i=0 ai f −i fitted onto the sample power spectrum, the
261   imputation equation can be written as x′c,t = Fc−1 (x′c,f ) where x′c,f ∈Φf = P (f ). Notably, since
262   the amplitude and phase cannot be reconstructed from the power spectrum and there is no knowing
263   how the phase of neighboring frequencies correlate to each other (?), only the frequency amplitude
264   is consciously maintained as a means of corruption of the linear relationship.

291

355   4.3   E XPLANATION FAITHFULNESS MEASUREMENTS
356

509   6   C ONCLUSION
510
511   We introduce a novel adversarial information masking (AIM) approach to enhance in-distribution
512   information masking, addressing key gaps in the assessment of faithfulness for post-hoc explana-
513   tions in deep learning. To validate the AIM method, we conduct the first quantitative comparison
514   of faithfulness assessment frameworks across various architectures, datasets, and domains. Through
515   these efforts, we successfully identify effectiveness of post-hoc explanation methods in EEG-DL,
516   thus furthering our understanding of model behavior and improving their explainability. Future re-
517   search could focus on refining these frameworks and exploring their applicability to a wider range
518
      of multivariate time series and sequential data contexts.

861   Sensory Motor Rhythm (SMR) Motor imagery (MI) reflects endogenous activity in the senso-
862   rimotor cortex induced by imagined movement. The SMR dataset in this study comes from BCI
863   Competition Dataset 2A (Brunner et al., 2008), it consisted of 22 channel EEG data recorded at
      250Hz sampling rate from 9 subjects performing 4 MI tasks (left hand, right hand, feet and tongue).


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      The dataset contains one training session and one evaluation session for each subject recorded on
865   different dates. A session includes 72 trials for each of the 4 tasks. The preprocessing followed Wei
866   et al. (2019) by downsample to 125 Hz and epoch to [-0.5, 4] seconds post cue onset.
867
868
      Feedback Error-Related Negativity (ERN) Feedback Error-Related Negativity (ERN) is a time-
869   locked amplitude component that can be observed after the subject encounters an erroneous event.
870   The dataset comes from (Jérémie Mattout & Kan, 2014) and is available as the early stage release in
871   the ”BCI challenge” on Kaggle. The dataset consisted of 56 channel EEG data recorded at a 600Hz
872   sampling rate from a total of 16 subjects executing P300 speller task. The experiment had a total of
873   340 trials from 5 sessions, where we split the first 300 trials as the training set and the remainder as
874   the test set. The preprocessing followed Pan et al. (2022) by downsample to 128 Hz, bandpass filter
875   to 1-40 Hz and epoch to [0, 1.25] seconds post cue onset.
876
877   Steady state visual evoked potential (SSVEP) Steady state visual evoked potential (SSVEP)
878   are quasi-periodic oscillatory responses that occur in the occipital cortex when a person is visually
879   stimulated by flickering of a specific frequency (Wang et al., 2016). The dataset in this study comes
880   from MAMEM SSVEP experiment 2 (Martinez et al., 2007), it consisted of 256 channel EEG data
881
      recorded at 250Hz sampling rate from 11 subjects, with stimuli in 5 frequencies (6.66, 7.50, 8.57,
      10.00, and 12.00 Hz). The experiment has 5 sessions, we used the first 4 sessions as our training set
882
      and the last as the test set. The preprocessing followed Pan et al. (2022) by downsample to 125Hz,
883
      bandpass filter to 1-50 Hz, and epoch the original trials into 1 second segments.

903   Spectral domain target search For spectral domain target frequency band B, firstly we assume
904   for each center frequency bc we can find frequency band BL (blef t –bc Hz) and BR (bc –bright Hz)

906   gradually adding up the power from each side of bc . Since the datasets were bandpass filtered in the

908   under the BL or BR meet k2 % of sample power, our solution is to continue the search in the opposite
909   direction.
