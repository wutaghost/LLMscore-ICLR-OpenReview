009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent work characterized shifts in preparatory activity of the motor cortex dur-
012            ing motor learning. The specific geometry of the shifts during learning, washout,
013            and relearning blocks was hypothesized to implement the acquisition, retention,
014            and retrieval of motor memories. We sought train recurrent neural network (RNN)
015            models that could be used to study these motor learning phenomena. We built an
016            environment for a curl field (CF) motor learning task and trained RNNs with rein-
017            forcement learning (RL) with novel regularization terms to perform behaviorally
018
               realistic reaching trajectories over the course of learning. Our choice of RL rather
               than supervised learning was motivated by the idea that motor adaptation to a
019
               novel environment, in the absence of demonstrations, is a process of reoptimiza-
020
               tion. We find these models, despite lack of supervision, reproduce many behav-
021            ioral findings from human and monkey CF adaptation experiments. Relearning
022            is faster than initial learning, indicating formation of motor memories. Optimal
023            reaches under a CF are not straight, but rather curved, which is optimal and has
024            been observed in humans and macaques. These models also captured key neu-
025            rophysiological findings. We found that the model’s preparatory activity existed
026            in a force-predictive subspace that remained stable across learning, washout, and
027            relearning. Additionally, preparatory activity shifted uniformly, independently of
028            the distance to the CF trained target. Finally, we found that the washout shift be-
029
               came more orthogonal to the learning shift, and hence more brain-like, when the
               RNNs were pretrained to have prior experience with CF dynamics. We argue the
030
               increased fit to neurophysiological recordings is driven by more generalizable and
031
               structured dynamical motifs in the model with more prior experience. This sug-
032            gests that the near-orthogonality of learning-washout neural geometry underlying
033            motor memory may be influenced by structured dynamical motifs in the motor
034            cortex circuitry developed from prior experience. Together, our work takes a step
035            towards elucidating the factors that support motor memory, acquisition, retention,
036            and retrieval during motor learning.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   How does the brain learn? One approach to this question is to view the brain under the framework
042   of deep learning (Richards et al., 2019). The authors argue that with the tremendous success of deep
043   learning in many tasks, it may benefit systems neuroscience to view the brain through the lens of
044   deep learning, seeking to identify the brain’s objective functions, learning rules, and architectures,
045
      in the context of the set of tasks it is optimized for. Parallel with the growth of deep learning, neu-
      roscience has experienced exponential, Moore’s-law-like, growth in the number of simultaneously
046
      recorded neurons as animals perform a range of tasks (Urai et al., 2022), creating a need for mod-
047
      eling and theoretical methods that scale with recording growth. One consequence of this parallel
048   growth in deep learning and systems neuroscience is growing evidence that artificial neural net-
049   works reproduce key features of neural activity recorded from various brain areas in animals across
050   a broad array of tasks (Sussillo et al., 2015; Mante et al., 2013; Rajan et al., 2016). In systems neu-
051   roscience, these trained or designed recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are then analyzed to explain
052   either the ”why” or ”how” of various cognitive or motor neural computations. These models have
053   been successful in elucidating novel mechanistic insight in many tasks, including decision-making
      (Mante et al., 2013; Barak et al., 2013), movement generation (Sussillo et al., 2015), timing (Rajan


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018), working memory (Orhan & Ma, 2019) and exploration (Banino
055   et al., 2018).
056
057   In this paper, we don’t seek to characterize or hypothesize how a biological network expertly per-
058
      forms a cognitive, motor, or navigational task, but rather seek to match underlying neural activity
      implementing the task over the course of learning and hypothesize how ecologically relevant prior
059
      experience influences neural activity underpinning learning. Critically, motor learning has important
060
      differences compared to prior studies on decision-making, movement generation, working memory,
061   and timing. These studies have been primarily concerned with the dynamical mechanisms within
062   a static network that implement these behaviors, and aimed to explain neural computations at a
063   snapshot in time, instead of how the dynamical mechanisms are organized across learning.
064
      The task we model is a motor adaptation experiment conducted with macaques (Sun et al., 2022).
065
      This study characterized the systematic changes in neural activity underlying the learning, unlearn-
066
      ing (”washout”), and relearning of a curl field perturbation on delayed reaches to targets. It also
067   uncovered structured relationships between the activity changes underlying the acquisition of dif-
068   ferent trained target configurations. The highly systematic geometric relationship between these
069   changes were hypothesized to implement motor memory in the brain. These results will serve as a
070   detailed guide for evaluating the behavioral and neural characteristics of RNNs trained with RL.
071
      To create a model of, and generate a hypothesis regarding the learning process in (Sun et al., 2022),
072
      we argue that it is natural and important that reinforcement learning (RL) is used to train the net-
073   works. In Sun et al. (2022), the monkey learns to reach under a curl field in the absence of demon-
074   strations. Not knowing, a priori, the optimal behavior makes supervised learning and imitation
075   learning an unnatural choice. Since, the monkey is instead rewarded with a small dose of fruit juice
076   at the end of a successful reach to a target, it is more natural to train our RNN model of motor
077   learning with RL (Botvinick et al., 2020) Also, in similar motor learning tasks to the one we seek to
078   model (Sun et al., 2022), the learning process is driven by performance reoptimization (Izawa et al.,
079   2008). We therefore use PPO (Schulman et al.) to train our RNN models and evaluate whether the
080   model exhibits similar behavioral and neural adaptation to animals.
081   Our contributions are as follows. 1) Artificial models can generate physically unrealistic accelera-
082   tions, resulting in non-physiological behavior. We therefore develop action distribution regulariza-
083   tion terms that lead to realistic accelerations during reaches. 2) We build an artificial environment
084   that replicates the curl field adaptation experiment in Sun et al. (2022), and train an RNN to learn this
085   task using deep RL. We show the trained RNN replicates key behavioral features of motor learning.
086   3) We show that our model reproduces several key neurophysiological features of motor adaptation,
087
      including a stable, reused, force-predictive subspace for preparatory activity, and uniform shift of
      preparatory states. 4) Finally, we show that providing the model with prior experience reproduces
088
      additional neurophysiological features such as near-orthogonality of learning and washout shifts,
089
      demonstrating that matching the task set between artificial and animal models (Richards et al., 2019)
090   can improve neural similarity. We believe this study demonstrates that RNNs trained with RL are
091   a useful source of hypothesis generation that can support progress in the theoretical understand-
092   ing of why systematic brain activity emerges during motor adaptation, and how motor memory is
093   implemented.

173   2.2   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE AND TRAINING
174
175   We implemented our agent policy πθ with RNNs and trained them to maximize rewards in the
176   environment using proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al.).
177
178   Model architecture: PPO is an actor-critic method, where the actor is the agent policy, πθ , and
179   the critic is the value function, which is used to compute clipped advantages. We used a shared actor
180   and critic network, which is a single layer LSTM with a hidden and cell size of 256. The actor and
181   critic are two separate linear readouts of the LSTM hidden state. For the actor, a linear layer after
182
      the LSTM outputs the mean and logarithmic standard deviation of Gaussian distributions for the
      cursor’s x and y acceleration. For the critic, a linear layer after the LSTM outputs the state value.
183
      As discussed in Section 2.1, the model input is the 9D environment state.
184
185   We chose to use a LSTM for scientific and practical reasons. Primarily, we use a recurrent architec-
186   ture as these architectures have reproduced many key features of neural activity in a range of motor
187   (Sussillo et al., 2015) and non-motor (Mante et al., 2013; Rajan et al., 2016; Orhan & Ma, 2019)
188
      tasks when trained in a purely task driven manner, sometimes with minimal explicit constraints to
      improve match to neural activity. We emphasize that we chose a recurrent architecture not due to
189
      performance considerations, but in order to investigate the unit population activity across stages of
190
      learning in the motor adapation task from Sun et al. (2022). We use LSTMs instead of ”vanilla”
191   RNNs, because we found that ”vanilla” RNNs did not converge reliably in training.
192

221   Curriculum Learning (during pretraining): When the monkey began experiments, it could,
222   due to prior experience, already reach along straight trajectories towards radial targets, without a

226

228

229   window is reduced by 10%. The curriculum continues until the acceptance window is of area 1 cm2 .

      3.1     P OLICY REGULARIZATION INDUCES REALISTIC REACH PROFILES
241
242   We first found that the policy regularization we introduced in Section 2.2 resulted in realistic reach
243   profiles. Models learned to hold still until go cue presentation. At go cue presentation, models
244   generated a modest acceleration to initiate the reach, then subsequently decelerated. Models then
245   held the cursor still over the goal target. Example trajectories during delayed reaches to all 8 targets
246   are shown in Fig. 2d.
247   We performed an ablation of the policy regularization terms in the no-CF pretraining condition.
248   As Fig. 2a shows, without regularization, peak acceleration increases nearly linearly with training
249   beginning from when the RNN model successfully learns the curriculum, reflecting the reward fo-
250   cused optimization. With the smoothness constraint, the peak acceleration drops substantially. With
251   the zeroness constraint or both constraints, max acceleration saturates at around 2 m/s2 at or before
252   completion of the curriculum. The impact of these regularization terms on the acceleration profile is
253
      shown in Fig. 2b. Note the qualitative similarity between the RNN’s acceleration profiles with the
      monkey’s in Fig. 2c. With this, we have shown that regularization terms can control model behavior
254
      without direct supervision. These terms can be used to impose more biologically plausible behav-
255
      ior on the RNN models when modeling motor tasks. For the rest of model training, the zeroness
256   and smoothness coefficients were both set to 0.001. This was selected empirically for approximate
257   behavioral match, but can be fine tuned for more exact match of the acceleration profile.

368   3.3   RNN S TRAINED WITH RL REPLICATE SEVERAL NEUROPHYSIOLOGICAL
369         CHARACTERISTICS
370
371   Since the RNN model replicated many of the behavioral characteristics of monkeys engaging in mo-
372   tor adaptation, we investigated the similarity between RNN model preparatory activity and biologi-
373   cal neural activity over the different blocks of learning. We characterized the RNN model’s ”neural
374
      geometry” by the following 4 neurophysiological features observed in monkeys: (1) preparatory
      state geometry, (2) stability of the force-predictive subspace, (3) uniformness of preparatory activity
375
      shifts after CF learning, and (4) orthogonality of learning-washout shifts.
376
377   Preparatory states: It is well known that neurons in the motor cortex fire in preparation for a
      reach to a target (Sun et al., 2022; Churchland et al., 2006b;a; Santhanam et al., 2006; Churchland


                                                                 7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      et al., 2010). When neural activity during movement preparation is collectively visualized in a
379   2D projection (found via PCA, factor analysis, or TDR (Mante et al., 2013)), they are organized
380   according to the topology of the physical locations of the reaching targets (Santhanam et al., 2009).
381   We visualized the preparatory activity in the RNN model by first constructing time series of the
382   artificial neuron activations during the delay period of the task. We then performed dimensionality
383   reduction using TDR (Mante et al., 2013). We identified two orthogonal dimensions where neural
384   activity predicts cursor acceleration and projected the preparatory activity into these dimensions.
385   We found that the models, across all 4 seeds, possess preparatory states (Fig. 3b) and that the
386   preparatory states are organized in a circle, mirroring the geometry of the targets. These results
387
      therefore demonstrate that RL-trained RNN models exhibit similar preparatory state geometry to
      trained animals.
388
389
390   Stable force predictive subpsace One of the discoveries of Sun et al. (2022) is that the force-
391   predictive subspace found by TDR remains force-predictive throughout subsequent phases of learn-
392   ing, washout, and relearning. The stability of this subspace across learning for the monkey can be
393
      seen in Fig. 3c (see Fig.1 for all seeds), where the before and after learning preparatory states for
      any given target remains stable in the force-predictive subspace. Likewise, Fig. 3b shows that the
394
      preparatory states remain largely stable in this force-predictice subspace found before learning for
395
      the RNN as well.
396
397
398   Uniform preparatory state shifts The most surprising result of Sun et al. (2022) is that even
399
      though the CF is behaviorally trained for one target (the down target), the preparatory states of all
      eight targets shift, reflecting newly learned motor cortex neural population states to perform the task.
400
      Further, this shift is not larger for the trained target, but uniform across all targets. In sum, Sun et al.
401
      (2022) reported that preparatory activity evolves over adaptation outside of the force (acceleration)-
402   predictive subspace, with all eight preparatory states shifting by approximately the same distance
403   and direction.
404
405
      We performed this analysis on RNN models after they were also trained to reach through the curl
      field towards the down target. We first computed the learning axis, which is the vector that points
406
      from the average of the preparatory states before learning to the average preparatory state after
407
      learning. Projecting the preparatory states onto this learning axis, we then compared the average
408   shift of the three targets near the trained target (down, down left, and down right targets, Fig. 3a),
409   compared to the three far targets (up, up left, and up right targets, Fig. 3a). Surprisingly, even
410   in RNN models, we found that the shifts are uniform for both pretraining conditions (Fig. 3f,
411   shifts not significantly different, p > 0.05, Wilcoxon rank sum test), reproducing the findings from
412   neurophysiological experiments.
413
      Together, these results demonstrate that RL-trained RNN models, with no direct supervision, exhibit
414   a key hallmark of cortical motor learning: a uniform shift in preparatory state geometry across all
415   targets during CF learning, even though the CF was applied to only one target.

      Nearly orthogonal learning and washout shifts During CF washout, Sun et al. (2022) quantified
      a washout axis by substracting the mean preparatory state after washout with the mean preparatory
424
      state after learning. The before learning, after learning, and after washout states can then be viewed
425
      in the 2D space spanned by the learning and washout axes. For monkeys, the learning and washout
426   shifts were found to be close-to-orthogonal in this space across days (Sun et al., 2022) (Fig 3e).

428   new preparatory states that are distinct from preparatory states before learning, in spite of identi-
429   cal behavior. It is thought that these new preparatory states represent a motor memory of the CF,
430   enabling faster relearning (also observed in our RNN models in Fig. 2g). We therefore quantified
431   if RNN preparatory states after washout returned to pre-learning states or new states, and whether
      these shifts were orthogonal to the learning axis.

453   We observed that while the RNN model exhibited washout shifts that did not return to before learning

456

457
      leveraged to reach under a CF. That is, these networks had to learn how to reach through a CF de
458   novo, unlike monkeys that have a lifetime of prior experience.
459
460   We hypothesized that if the RNN model was pretrained to reach under curl fields, it would develop
461
      more structured dynamical motifs corresponding to various curl field intensities; The model would
      then exhibit more brain-like neural geometry during learning and washout as a consequence of lever-
462
      aging (Driscoll et al., 2022) the additional recurrent circuitry created from richer prior experience.
463
464
      Pretraining with prior experience with curl fields We thus constructed another pretraining pro-
465
      cedure (CF-pretrained RNN model) that differs from the original procedure in the following ways
466
467
            1. Pretraining occurs with a random curl field perturbation. The curl field coefficient k from
468            Eq. 1 is set to a random value from (−K, K) after every successful reach, where K grows
469            from 0 to 0.4 in steps of 0.01 with the curriculum.
470
            2. The curl field coefficient k is also provided to the network. We provide k to the network to
471
               encourage the network to develop structured dynamical motifs that can deal with a range
472
               of curl fields. In nature, a monkey moves their hands under a range of dynamics. For ex-
473            ample, a monkey may throw rocks. By sight, the monkey can estimate the weight of the
474            rock. Internally, it thus has an estimate of the parameters characterizing the dynamics of
475            the environment, before any physical interaction. The monkey likely arrived to the experi-
476            mental room with a vast range of such prior experience, where the underlying parameters
477            characterizing environment dynamics are observed. We reason that providing the curl field
478            coefficient to the model imitates this sort of prior experience.
479
480   After the pretraining, the model input corresponding to the curl field coefficient is zeroed out. The
481   CF-pretrained RNN model does not differ from the baseline model in input after pretraining.
482   We quantified preparatory state shifts during learning and washout for RNN models with CF-
483   pretraining (Fig. 4a). We observed that they closely replicate close-to-orthogonal shifts also ob-
484   served in Sun et al. (2022) (Fig. 3e). We emphasize that CF pretraining replicated these results with
485   no explicit additional constraints on model weights or model activity, resulting in washout shifts
      that were more orthogonal to the learning shifts (Fig. 4b). As shown in Fig. 4b, we found that the

      mean angle between the learning and washout shift was significantly closer to orthogonal for the CF
487   pretrained model, compared to the model without prior experience with the curl field.
488
489   The added prior experience has not detrimentally affected any of the other key features of neural
490
      activity. The preparatory states are still organized circularly, and the force-predictive subspace re-
      mains stable (Fig.2). The preparatory state shifts remain uniform (Fig 4c). Together, these results
491
      demonstrate that RNN models exhibit many neurophysiological features of biological motor mem-

495   4   D ISCUSSION
496
497   We found that our KL-divergence based policy regularization terms could induce realistic reach tra-
498   jectories in our artifical motor adaptation experiment. We then showed that both of our models nat-
499   urally replicated an array of behavioral findings from motor adaptation Sun et al. (2022), including
500   overcompensated reaches in a curl field and evidence of motor memory through increased relearning
501
      speed. In addition to behavioral replication, we also found activity resembling neurophysiological
      recordings from monkey motor cortices Sun et al. (2022). In particular, the preparatory states exist
502
      in a stable force-predictive subspace, and shifted uniformly over the course of learning.
503
504   Finally, we explored the impact that prior experience has on the activity underlying adaptation to
505   a curl field. We found that CF-pretrained models replicated the more orthogonal relationship be-
506   tween learning and washout shifts observed in monkey experiments. From this, we hypothesize
507
      prior experience may have induced the development of structured dynamical motifs Driscoll et al.
      (2022)that then shape the neural geometry observed through learning and washout (Sun et al., 2022).
508
      Future work should also seek to characterize properties of the weight changes underlying the activity
509
      changes.
510
511   Our findings around pretraining parallel suggestions from a number of researchers that accurate
512   models for cognition may require pretraining (Yang & Molano-Mazón, 2021). Humans, for exam-
513
      ple, enter each situation with significant prior knowledge. Similarly, before beginning the motor
      adaptation experiment (Sun et al., 2022), the monkeys had developed motor skill in a wide array
514
      of tasks, and their motor cortices likely encoded significant priors about the relationships between
515
      different magnitudes and types of perturbing forces, as well as generated movements.
516
517   Future work ought replicate additional neurophysiological phenomena. Sun et al. (2022) character-
518   ized relationships between learning shifts of different trained targets, or opposite curl fields, as well
519
      as a ”retrieval” of motor memory, where relearning caused the preparatory states to return to the
      learning state. While behaviorally, we found that relearning is faster than learning, indicating some
520
      form of motor memory, our RNN models may not be using the same underlying mechanisms.
521
522   Future work may further investigate the algorithms, objective functions, architectures, or task-set
523   priors that implement motor memory in the brain. It may be interesting to investigate the impacts
524   of continual learning algorithms inspired by neurobiological observations (Zenke et al., 2017; Kirk-
525
      patrick et al., 2017) on model activity, to expand the model’s pretraining set to contain multiple
      tasks (Yang & Wang, 2020), or more ecologically relevant tasks (Molano-Mazón et al., 2023). Ad-
526
      ditionally, it may be interesting to investigate the impact of reward shaping, curriculum learning, or
527
      different RNN architectures on learning-related model activity.
528
529   Our work involved simple comparisons of angles or distances in neural space. Future work could
530   also develop or include additional methods of specifying salient or important features of neural
531
      activity, and quantifying the similarity between these features in artificial models and animal models.
532
533   5   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We have demonstrated that artificial models can learn curl field reaching in a way that reproduces
536   several key behavioral and neural features in animal learning. Our method constrains motor adapta-
537   tion behavior by using action distribution regularization terms while training RNNs with PPO. Our
538   models, regardless of prior experience, replicated key behavioral findings in monkey motor adap-
539   tation experiments, and several neurophysiological features. We also show that pretraining with
      randomized curl fields leads to an increase in brain-like neural geometry, suggesting that previous


                                                         10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      motor memories influenced the neural geometry observed in Sun et al. (2022). Previous work train-
541   ing RNNs with RL for neuroscience was limited to cognitive and value-based tasks with discrete
542   action spaces (Song et al., 2017) or reaching tasks without consideration for the learning process
543   (Marin Vargas et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first that investigates the
544   similarity between RL-trained RNN model activity during learning to the monkey motor cortex dur-
545   ing motor learning, and puts forward the hypothesis that near orthogonal aspects of neural geometry
546   underlying learning and washout may be a product of structured dynamical motifs formed through
547   prior experience.
