011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The development of Machine Learning (ML) methods for Computational Fluid
014            Dynamics (CFD) is currently limited by the lack of openly available training
015            data. This paper presents a new open-source dataset comprising of high fidelity,
016
               scale-resolving CFD simulations of 500 geometric variations of the Ahmed Car
               Body - a simplified car-like shape that exhibits many of the flow topologies that
017
               are present on bluff bodies such as road vehicles. The dataset contains simulation
018
               results that exhibit a broad set of fundamental flow physics such as geometry
019            and pressure-induced flow separation as well as 3D vortical structures. Each
020            variation of the Ahmed car body were run using a high-fidelity, time-accurate,
021            hybrid Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) - Large-Eddy Simulation (LES)
022            turbulence modelling approach using the open-source CFD code OpenFOAM. The
023            dataset contains boundary, volume, geometry, and time-averaged forces/moments
024            in widely used open-source formats. In addition, the OpenFOAM case setup is
025            provided so that others can reproduce or extend the dataset. This represents to
026            the authors knowledge, the first open-source large-scale dataset using high-fidelity
027
               CFD methods for the widely used Ahmed car body that is available to freely
               download with a permissive license (CC-BY-SA).

      I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The past decade has seen major efforts to improve the accuracy and computational efficiency of CFD
      approaches by utilizing the latest High-Performance Computing (HPC) hardware (e.g., GPUs, arm64-
034
      based processors (3; 24; 68; 20)) as well as designing new algorithms and methods to match this
035
      emerging new HPC hardware (51; 41; 26; 73; 70; 59). Alongside this code and solver development,
036   there has also been considerable work to explore different turbulence modelling approaches that
037   can achieve greater accuracy.g., wall-modeled Large-Eddy-Simulation (WMLES) (39; 15), Hybrid
038   RANS-LES (10; 19; 66; 57; 56; 8; 54) using a range of underlying numerical approaches.
039
      This combination of higher fidelity methods coupled with advances in HPC has enabled CFD methods
040
      to correlate better to experimental data for cases such as complete aircraft simulations (8; 39; 27)
041
      or realistic road cars (34; 5). However, whilst these advances have improved simulation accuracy,
042   lowered run times and often reduced the cost of a CFD simulation, there still exists a fundamental
043   link between these three factors. A coarser mesh or time-step may help to reduce the runtime or cost,
044   but it would most likely worsen accuracy due to insufficient spatial or temporal resolution. Likewise
045   to improve accuracy, the additional mesh or temporal resolution will increase the computational
046   cost. This link is particularly important when it comes to developing tools that can provide real-time
047   predictions, as reducing the prediction time to seconds with state of the art methods ends up worsening
048   the accuracy or requiring very large number of HPC resources, which ultimately increases the cost.
049   Machine Learning (ML) methods, in particular, surrogate models, have emerged as a potential method
050   (71; 16; 49; 72) that once trained can provide a rapid prediction tool at a time and cost much lower
051   than traditional methods (2; 37; 11). However, ML methods available in the literature today, do
052   not directly improve the accuracy compared to traditional CFD methods, so there still exists a need
053   for traditional CFD approaches where high accuracy is needed, as well as the tool to provide the
      underlying training data.

054
      The field of surrogate models via machine learning is broad but in general, two main approaches
055   have emerged: physics-driven and data-driven (49). Physics-driven approaches (58; 32; 62; 47; 61)
056   typically add the Partial Differential Equation (PDE) e.g Navier-Stokes, as a soft constraint or
057   regularizer to the loss function in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (61) and Physics-
058   Informed Neural Operators (PINOs) (47). Data-driven approaches on the other hand typically use
059   machine learning techniques to learn the solutions or solution operator of PDEs (22), e.g., program
060   the ML model in a supervised manner to minimize the difference between the ‘true’ solution and
061   the predicted solution. The ‘true’ solution can become available by using traditional solvers or
062   by experimental measurements. Examples of these data-driven approaches that are trained on
063
      supervised simulation data include message-passing graph neural networks (GNNs) (14; 25), e.g.,
      MeshGraphNets (60; 23; 42) and pure data-driven Neural Operators (43; 44; 29; 45).
064
065
066   T HE NEED FOR HIGH QUALITY TRAINING DATA AND RELATED WORK
067
068   The training dataset is a key ingredient to the development and validation of any ML method, whether
069   they are data-driven or physics-driven. To date, there are orders of magnitude less open-source CFD
070   data than in other communities such as natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision (18).
071   Recently, several groups have generated large-scale training data to demonstrate the potential of
072   their ML methods (e.g Jacob et al. (35), Li et al. (46), Baque et al. (12)) however none were made
073
      open-source and freely available, and therefore cannot be used by the community to develop their own
      methods. More recently, Bonnet et al. (13) released the AirfRANS dataset that includes more than
074
      1000 2D airfoils simulated using a steady-state RANS setup using OpenFOAM, that is freely available
075
      to download under a permissive open-source license. This dataset is useful for the initial development
076   of ML models, however there are numerous limitations including the 2D nature of the geometries,
077   the steady-state lower fidelity method (RANS) and the large gap in complexity between this and a
078   3D plane or car. To address these limitations, Elrefaie et al. (21) developed the DrivAerNet dataset
079   that includes 4000 geometric variants of the DrivAer geometry, also simulated using a steady-state
080   RANS formulation, using OpenFOAM on meshes of between 8 and 16 million cells. This dataset
081   has the benefits of the realism of a road-car but lacks the high-fidelity CFD simulation approach i.e
082   steady-state RANS have been shown in numerous studies to not be accurate for large-scale bluff
083   body flows (5; 6; 4) and the mesh sizes of 8-16M cells are an order of magnitude lower than typically
084
      used within industry (addressed by our upcoming DrivAerML dataset (9)). Finally the license that
      DrivAerNet dataset is provided under (CC-BY-NC) does not permit the use for commercial methods
085
      which limits it’s use by the emerging eco-system of startups, software providers and tech companies.
086
087
088   F OCUS OF THIS WORK
089
090   In this work (and the associated WindsorML (7) and DrivAerML datasets (9), that were created in
091   tandem with this AhmedML dataset) we focus on addressing the limitations of this previous work by
092   firstly using higher-fidelity scale-resolving methods (WMLES or Hybrid RANS-LES), that are the
093   state of the art for the CFD community and provide better correlation to ground-truth experimental
094
      data (5; 6; 4). And secondly, making the data freely available, in a consistent data format that
      is common across each dataset, and available under a permissive license (CC-BY-SA). Choosing
095
      meshes and CFD approaches that are closer to what industry would use for the chosen test-case is
096
      key, because a method that works for a 2D airfoil with 500k nodes (13) may not work for a 3D 20M
097   cell case - due to both the underlying computational efficiency of the method and/or a limitation in
098   the method itself e.g., MeshGraphNet (60) vs. multi-scale MeshGraphNet (23) that may not show
099   differences at small mesh sizes but would for larger.
100
      In addition, one of the main interests for ML-enhanced CFD is whether these models can provide a
101
      cheaper and faster prediction tool - thus testing it on realistically sized datasets is important to also
102
      accurately measure the time to train and do inference.

125   BACKGROUND
126
      The Ahmed body (1) is a generic car geometry, shown in Figure 1; comprising a flat front with
127
      rounded corners and a sharp slanted rear upper surface. It has been subject to extensive wind-tunnel
128   (48; 1; 53), as and CFD testing ((36; 50; 30; 52; 30; 31; 63; 17; 40; 33; 55; 63; 4)). This has been
129   typically done for different slant back angles (25o and 35o ) that represent both largely geometry and
130   pressure-induced flow separation, together with 3D vortical structures. For that reason, this test case
131   has proven popular amongst the CFD community (especially the turbulence modelling community),
132   partly because of its car-like shape and because it maintains a challenging flow to predict even with
133   higher-fidelity methods (4).

      G EOMETRY
179
180   The geometry, as defined by Ahmed (1) is illustrated in Figure 2; the body has a length of L = 1.044m
181   with a height, H = 0.288m and a reference area of Aref = 0.112. In the experimental study, the
182   body was mounted on four stilts, at a height of 0.05m, to permit flow under the car which is important
183   for ‘ground effect’. To avoid additional mesh complexity, the stilts were here neglected, in line with
184   previous numerical studies in the literature (4). Thus the body for the baseline geometry is fixed at
185   the same height, 0.05m, above the floor. We discuss the variations of this geometry in Section
186
187   B OUNDARY CONDITIONS
188
189
      The flow is at a Reynolds number of Re = 7.68 × 105 based on the body height H (0.288m) (or
      2.78 × 106 if based upon the body length (1.044m)), dynamic viscosity (ν = 3.75 × 10− 7) and the
190
      free-stream velocity U∞ = 1ms−1 . At this Reynolds number the flow is largely turbulent and similar
191
      to those of a realistic road-car. An inlet condition is imposed 3m upstream of the body and an outlet
192   condition is imposed 6m downstream. A no-slip wall condition is imposed on the ground floor and
193   car body, with slip walls applied to the wind tunnel walls. The nutUSpaldingWallFunction (based
194   upon the work of Spalding (67)) is used to model the near-wall given the high y + grid. The time
195   step is set to ∆tU∞ /L = 6 × 10−4 (where L is the length of the body), which ensures a maximum
196   convective CFL number of less than one in areas of key flow separation. Each simulation was run
197   for a total of 40 convective transit times (=T U∞ /L); time-averaging began after the initial 10 transit
198   times.
199
200   C OMPUTATIONAL M ESH
201
202   An unstructured mesh of approximately 15-20M prismatic and hex-dominant cells were used (depend-
203
      ing on the input geometry) using the OpenFOAM internal meshing tool; snappyHexMesh (Figure
      3). A high-y+ approach was taken with 3 prism layers, resulting in a y + range of approximately 50
204
      over the top surface of the body, which is exceeded at areas of significant flow acceleration and much
205
      lower where flow separation occurs. Uniform volumetric refinement was placed around the vehicle to
206   reduce variation of the grid between geometry variations.
207
208
      S OLVER S ETUP
209
210   OpenFOAM v2212 was used for all the simulations, run in double-precision, compiled with OpenMPI
211   4.1. The pimpleFoam solver was used, which is a segregated, transient solver for incompressible,
212   turbulent flow of Newtonian fluids. The Spalart-Allmaras based Delayed Detached-Eddy Simulation
213   (SA-DDES) (65) model was used for all simulations in order to capture the large-scale geometry
214   and pressure-induced flow separation. A hybrid 2nd-order upwind/central differencing spatial
215   discretization scheme was used based upon the work of Travin et al. (69) for the momentum
      equations and a second-order upwind scheme for the turbulent variables. A second order upwind


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      scheme was used for temporal discretization. The Geometric AMG (GAMG) solver was used for the
217   pressure variables and smoothSolver was used for the velocity and turbulent variables.
218
219
      HPC SETUP
220
221   All simulations were run on Amazon Web Services, using a dynamic HPC cluster provisioned by
222   AWS ParallelCluster v3.9. Amazon EC2 hpc6a.48xlarge nodes were used for each case, which
223   contain a dual-socket AMD Milan 96 core (in-total across both sockets) chip with 384Gb RAM.
224   These are connected using a 100Gbit/s Elastic Fabric Adapter (EFA) interconnect (64). A 40TB
225   Amazon Fsx for Lustre parallel file-system was used as the main data location during the runs, which
226   were later transferred to object storage by Amazon S3. Each simulation was run on 288 cores using
227   4 hpc6a.48xlarge nodes that were selectively under-populated to 72 cores per node to obtain the
228
      optimum memory bandwidth. Each case took approximately 30mins to mesh and 48hrs to solve.

230   VALIDATION
231
232   The primary aim of this work is to provide a dataset that can be used to develop and validate ML
233   approaches, where the training data is based upon a CFD setup that can provide the best correlation
234   to experimental data for the baseline geometry and have the best chance of also correlating to it’s
235   geometry variants. The CFD approach (i.e., meshing, turbulence model, numerical methods) used
236   for this work represents the high fidelity approaches that would be used within academia and more
237   importantly industry for bluff body shapes (5; 6; 4). As will be discussed in the next section, the
238
      Ahmed car body is a challenging test-case for CFD because the rear slant angle goes through an
      abrupt change in flow separation between 25o and 35o and thus only Direct Numerical Simulation
239
      (DNS) would have the potential to confidentially predict every geometric variant. Thus the validation
240
      within this paper is to show rigour and a sound methodology but ultimately it’s not possible to achieve
241   perfect correlation to the ultimate ground truth without extensive mesh sizes and computational
242   expense that would provide diminishing returns.
243
244
      P RIOR WORK
245
246   The choice of a transient scale-resolving method i.e., Delayed Detached-Eddy simulation (DDES)
247   (65) is based upon decades of work studying bluff body shapes, where RANS models has consistently
248   failed to capture the correct flow physics (31; 38; 63; 28; 33; 55; 63). However in these works it
249   was also shown that even though high-fidelity hybrid RANS-LES methods consistently provide
250   better correlation to experimental data than lower-fidelity RANS methods, they still suffer from some
251   inaccuracies. The source of these inaccuracies are mesh resolution, turbulence model formulation
252
      and numerical schemes used. Thus, the next section shows the validation work for our specific CFD
      approach for the baseline Ahmed car body geometry.

450   L IMITATIONS OF THE DATASET
451
452   Whilst the dataset has numerous benefits over prior work e.g high-fidelity, large-scale, freely available
453   it has several shortcomings:
454
455          • The dataset has purely geometrical differences with no variation in boundary conditions.
456            Expanding the dataset to include boundary condition changes (i.e inflow velocity) would
457            help ML developers to use this dataset for more than just geometry prediction.
458          • Whilst the Ahmed car body has become a standard test-case to assess CFD methods for
459            bluff body separation (such as road car aerodynamics) it lacks the complexity of a real-life
460            vehicle (addressed by the upcoming related DrivAerML dataset (9))
461          • The CFD modelling approach still does not exactly match experimental data and even
462            higher-fidelity methods on finer meshes can yield even closer correlation to the experimental
463            ground truth (e.g Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS)
464
             • The dataset only includes time-averaged data rather than time-series. This is because industry
465            typically focuses on time-averaged data can be be inspected for engineering usefulness. In
466            addition the data requirements for capturing the full time-history (e.g 80,000 time-steps)
467            which significantly increase the size of the dataset. However future work could be to include
468            this for a limited number of runs to help develop models to capture the time-history.

      C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
472
      This paper outlines the open-source AhmedML dataset that contains outputs from the Computational
473
      Fluid Dynamics simulations of 500 geometric variants of the Ahmed car body. These simulations
474
      were conducted using a high-fidelity, transient scale-resolving methodology in the open-source code;
475   OpenFOAM. This comprehensive dataset includes volume and boundary data in commonly used
476   open-source formats as well as time-averaged forces and the underlying geometry STLs. In addition
477   2D slices of the flowfield through x,y & z are provided as well as images of flow-field variables in
478   those slice locations. This dataset alongside two further datasets released in tandem (WindsorML (7)

       The Navier-Stokes equations represent the fundamental motion of a fluid. They are derived from the
813   conservation of mass and momentum, and can be expressed in Cartesian form for an incompressible
814   Newtonian flow with constant density and variable viscosity (which are the assumptions used in the
815   AhmedML dataset simulations) as follows:
816
817
818
                                                ∂ui
                                                            =     0                                         (1)
819                                             ∂xi
820                                    ∂ui      ∂ui                   1 ∂p    ∂τij
                                           + uj             =     −         +                               (2)
821                                    ∂t       ∂xj                   ρ ∂xi   ∂xj
822
823   where the viscous stress τij is defined as:
824
825                                                         ∂ui   ∂uj
                                              τij = µ           +                                           (3)
826                                                         ∂xj   ∂xi
827
828   While these equations fully represent any fluid flow, an analytical solution is only possible for
829
      basic flows that are subject to many assumptions. When an analytical solution is not possible,
      numerical methods may be used to achieve an approximate solution. In order to obtain a solution,
830
      these differential equations must be discretized to form integral equations which can be solved by a
831
      numerical procedure.
832
833   By solving these equations directly without further modelling or assumptions, the fluid velocity and
834   pressure can be obtained, this method is called Direct Numerical Solution (DNS). Whilst it is the
835
      most complete numerical method as it involves no further assumptions or modelling, it is not practical
      for industrial applications using the current generation of computational resources. Three alternative
836
      approaches are discussed below; Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS), Large Eddy Simulation
837
      (LES) and hybrid RANS-LES - the final which is used to generate this dataset.

      A.1    R EYNOLDS AVERAGED NAVIER -S TOKES (RANS)
840
841    This method relies on providing a statistical representation of the turbulence via mathematical models,
842   in which case none of the turbulence is resolved. All such methods are based on the Reynolds temporal
843   decomposition; this involves applying an ‘ensemble-averaging’ process to the whole turbulent flow
844   field, φ(x, t), which, for the case where the flow is statistically stationary, is given by:
845
                                                                       0
846                                          φ(x, t) = Φ(x) + φ (x, t)                                      (4)

       A.3    H YBRID RANS-LES
1027
1028    The choice between RANS and LES methods is dependant on many factors, such as the accuracy
1029   required or the physical quantities that are of interest. For flows that are largely steady and for which
1030   only mean quantities are of interest (such as an airfoil at a low angle of attack), RANS modelling is
1031   often a suitable and cheap choice. However for complex flows that contain regions of large separation
1032   or strong anisotropy (such as the Ahmed body), then traditional RANS techniques often fail. For
1033   many cases their inability to capture the large scale unsteadiness and a tendency to under-predict
1034
       the shear-stress in the separated layer (which leads to a longer separation length) mean they are
       unsuitable for these types of flows (9). Even more advanced Reynolds Stress models that are able to
1035
       account for the anisotropy in the flow are often unable to provide an good enough description of the
1036
       unsteady flow field for highly separated flows (8).
1037
1038   Whilst LES models can in general provide a much better alternative to RANS modelling for unsteady
1039   flows, they do so at a much higher cost, so much higher that for high-Reynolds numbers flow these
1040
       costs are too great for general purpose calculations. As the Reynolds number increases, the size of
       the smallest eddies decrease and therefore so must the grid and time step to account for this. For
1041
       many engineering problems in the aerospace and automobile industry the computational resources
1042
       are simply too great.
1043
1044   An alternative approach that balances computational cost with accuracy are hybrid RANS-LES
1045   methods. These methods solve a RANS model in regions where the flow is attached, more simple to
1046
       model and thus requires a coarser grid and then switch to a LES simulation in the regions where the
       RANS model would perform poorly such as the separation region behind the body. Arguably one of
1047
       the first methods to pioneer this hybrid approach was Detached-Eddy Simulation (DES) (22). This
1048
       approach is based on splitting the domain into RANS and LES regions based on the grid resolution.
1049   DES can be seen as a three-dimensional, unsteady model based on an underlying ‘off-the-shelf’
1050   RANS model. It seamlessly acts as a sub-grid scale model in regions where the grid is fine enough to
1051   support a LES, and as a RANS model in areas where it is not. The main advantage of this approach
1052   is that it is conceptually easy to understand and mathematically simple because it only requires the
1053   modification of a single term. In DES, the RANS turbulent length scale is adapted to Equation 29
1054   which effectively changes the turbulent length scale to one based on the grid resolution. For the
1055   original SA-DES model this results in every instance of the turbulent length scale being substituted
1056   for the new definition, as shown in Equation. 31.

       B.13       G EOMETRY VARIANTS
1459
1460   500 geometric variations of the Ahmed car body (as per Table 7) were created to produce a broad
1461   range of shapes that would produce differing flow physics e.g geometry-induced separation, pressure-
1462   induced separation and 3D vortical structures. The challenge then being for ML methods to be able
1463   to train and ultimately predict unseen examples across these broad dataset. Figures 8 & 9 show the
1464   Total Pressure Coefficient for the first 50 runs of the dataset at y = 0 and z = 0. They illustrate the
1465   broad range of flow separation patterns i.e complete flow separation from the rear surface, to a fully
1466
       attached flow.
1467   In order to efficiently create the range of geometries, the open-source parametric 3D CAD tool
1468   SolveSpace 4 was used. First, a base parametric model was created by hand in SolveSpace GUI
1469   according to Ahmed specifications and saved to file in the SolveSpace text format (.slvs). A custom
1470   python script randomised each parameter for each variant using Latin hypercube sampling before
1471   writing each variant as a separate .slvs text file. Next, using python script, for each variant the
1472
       SolveSpace command-line executable was invoked to load the variant .slvs and its geometries
       exported as a triangulated mesh in STL format with 20,000 vertices and chord tolerance of 0.005mm.
1473
1474   To illustrate the large differences in flow physics, Figures 7a & 7b shows the total pressure coefficient
1475   for a low and low drag result, which shows the large range of flow features that are produced in this
1476   dataset i.e run205 has fully separated flow over the rear-window, whereas run238 has it fully attached.
1477   There is even more of a dramatic effect when looked in the z slice, where the vortical structures create
1478
       a much wider wake, which in part contributes to the much higher drag.

       C     ML EVALUATION
1675
1676
       C.1   S UMMARY
1677
1678   We have conducted preliminary analysis on our dataset using a modified version of one of the
1679   state-of-the-art scientific machine learning (SciML) methods, MeshGraphNet (7) on various tasks to
1680   illustrate the practicality of the dataset for ML evaluation. We utilize the encoder-processor-decoder
1681   architecture in MeshGraphNets and modify the method to enable it to make time-averaged predictions
1682   (2).
1683   The entire Ahmed dataset is split into training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%) sets. For each
1684   use case, the model is trained on the training set, and the checkpoint that had the best validation error
1685   was used to obtain the inference results on the test set.
1686
       For the Ahmed dataset, using the predicted surface pressure and wall-shear stress on the 1.1M node
1687   vtp surface mesh, we obtain predictions (shown in Figure 10) for the drag coefficient with a mean
1688   absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 0.101 and a mean absolute error (MAE): 0.034. For the lift
1689   coefficient the mean absolute error (MAE): 0.085. The surface contours of the actual, predicted
1690   and error for the mean pressure and wall-shear stress are shown in Figure 11. Training time is
1691   approximately 36 hours on x8 NVidia L40s GPUs and the inference time is less than a minute on
1692   the same hardware. Please note that these runs are preliminary and further work to optimize the
1693   methodology and hyperparameters is on-going which will published in future papers.
