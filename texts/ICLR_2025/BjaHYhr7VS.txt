011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Differential learning rate (DLR), a technique that applies different learning rates
014            to different model parameters, has been widely used in deep learning and achieved
015
               empirical success via its various forms. For example, parameter-efficient training
               (PET) applies zero learning rates to most parameters so as to significantly saves
016
               the computational cost; adaptive optimizers such as Adam apply the coordinate-
017
               wise learning rate to accelerate the convergence.
018
               At the core, DLR leverages the observation that different parameters can have
019
               different loss curvature, which is hard to characterize in general. We propose
020            the Hessian-informed differential learning rate (Hi-DLR), an efficient approach
021            that captures the loss curvature of parameters for any model and optimizer adap-
022            tively. Given a proper grouping of parameters, we empirically demonstrate that
023            Hi-DLR can improve the convergence by dynamically determining the learning
024            rates during the training. Furthermore, we can quantify the influence of different
025            parameters and freeze the less-contributing parameters, which leads to a new PET
026            that automatically adapts to various tasks and models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      DLR by parameter groups We term the differential learning rate (DLR) as a technique that as-
      signs different learning rates to different parameter groups. Here the parameter groups are partitions
032
      of model parameters w ∈ RD such that w = [w(1) , ..., w(K) ] and the gradient g = [g(1) , ..., g(K) ],
033
      and we defer the notations to Section 2.1.
034
035   When K = 1, this reduces to the uniform learning rate (ULR) and we update with ηt ∈ R such that
036                                            wt+1 = wt − ηt gt .
037
038   When K > 1, we have multiple learning rates η(k) for k ∈ [K] such that
039                     wt+1 = wt − [η(1) g(1),t , ..., η(K) g(K),t ] := wt − η[K],t g[K],t
040
041   Motivation of DLR. At high level, DLR can be beneficial because the loss curvature can be very
042   different for different parameters. The loss curvature is captured by the Hessian information matrix
043   and its eigen-spectrum, as demonstrated by Figure 1-5 in Ghorbani (2019), Figure 1 and 3 in Yao
044   et al. (2020), Figure 1 and 6 in Sankar et al. (2021), and Figure 1-2 in Zhang et al. (2024). We
045   visualize in Figure 1 that, by grouping the parameters into biases and weights, the two groups have
046   significantly different curvatures and prefer different learning rates. We give further motivation from
047   an optimization perspective in Section 2.2 and Section 2.3.
048
049   Related works to DLR. In fact, DLR has been widely used in deep learning, including parameter-
050
      efficient training (PET), layer-wise learning rate, and adaptive optimizers.
051   For example, PET methods include Adapter Houlsby et al., BitFit Zaken et al. (2022), LoRA and its
052   variants Hu et al. (2022); Hayou et al. (2024) and others (see more in Han et al. (2024)) are special
053   cases of two-group DLR, because the majority of parameters is frozen and non-trainable (i.e. using
      a learning rate of 0) and a small portion of parameters uses a non-zero learning rate. These methods

071   Another example is the layer-wise learning rate. Howard & Ruder (2018) proposed a depth-wise
072   DLR where deeper layers use larger learning rate with η(l) = η · 2.6l and l is the layer index. Similar
073   ideas have been proposed not only in fine-tuning but also in pre-training You et al. (2019); Zheng
074   & Kwok (2019); You et al. (2017); Singh et al. (2015); Ginsburg et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2022);
075   Sun et al. (2019); Ioannou et al. (2023). Some variants have extended the depth-wise DLR in a
076   block-wise manner, by grouping multiple layers into a block for faster computation.
077   From a different angle, we can view the adaptive optimizers including Adam Kingma & Ba (2014)
078   as SGD with coordinate-wise DLR (K = D where D is the model size), since each parameter uses

085
086   Despite the success of DLR in many areas, there are some challenges for its wider application which
087   we introduce in the following.
088
089   Potential challenges in DLR. Naively implemented, DLR will introduce K hyperparameters in-
090
      stead of 1 hyperparameter by ULR. This leads to the challenge of hyperparameter tuning, which can
      be prohibitively expensive, especially when model size D or number of learning rates K is large.
091
092   One approach that reduces the number of effective hyperparameters in DLR, which is adopted by
093   the aforementioned works, is to incorporate a heuristic structure among η(k) . As we have discussed,
094   different PET methods freeze different parameters; the depth-wise learning rate uses a fixed ratio
095   2.6 to scale η(l) ; SignSGD or Adam uses the coordinate-wise gradient norm to scale ηi , so that K
096
      hyperparameters have only 1 degree of freedom in η.
097   Nevertheless, such heuristic structure may fail to work in some cases. For example, the fixed ratio in
098   depth-wise learning rate is expected to vary for different models and tasks. We also see in first two
099   rows of Table 2 that LoRA is comparable to full model training (FMT) on SST-2, MNLI and QNLI,
100   but not so on MRPC and ColA. Further experiments in Figure 6 and Figure 7 lend strengths to our
101   observation that no one PET method fits all cases.
102   We consider an orthogonal approach that preserves the K degrees of freedom and adaptively adjusts
103   η(k) with minimal overhead, which can be used in combination with PET and adaptive optimizers.
104
105   Automatic ULR. In the ULR regime, recent advances have proposed automatic learning rate
106   schedule (or parameter-free, or learning-rate-free methods), including but not limited to D-
107   Adaptation Defazio & Mishchenko (2023), Prodigy Mishchenko & Defazio (2023), DoG Ivgi et al.
      (2023), and GeN Bu & Xu (2024), among which GeN uniquely leverages the Hessian information.

108
      However, these methods cannot easily optimize the DLR in general. In this work, we extends GeN
109   from ULR to DLR in Algorithm 1. We term our method as Hessian-informed DLR (Hi-DLR) and
110   highlight that its subcase Hi-ULR is equivalent to GeN.
111
112
      We note there are more learning rate techniques that can leverage Hessian information, such as
      line-search Drori & Taylor (2020); Goujaud et al. (2022); Armijo (1966); Bertsekas (1997), but an
113
      extra overhead is incurred for a fine-grained search. We have developed some efficient tricks so that
114
      Hi-DLR can be almost as fast as ULR in Section 3.
115
116   Contribution.
117
118    • We introduce Hi-DLR in equation 2.4 to enrich the approximation of Hessian information, in
119      addition to the pre-conditioning of any optimizer, so as to leverage the different loss curvature of
120      different parameters through different learning rates.
121    • We propose Algorithm 1 to efficiently compute Hi-DLR, with a novel diagonalization trick in
122      equation 3.1, which not only significantly reduces the computation cost, but also separates the
123      contribution of different parameter groups in equation 5.1.
124    • We demonstrate that Hi-DLR is favorable for various tasks like image/text classification, regres-
125      sion, multi-task learning, as well as parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
126    • We develop a meta-framework of PET as an application of Hi-DLR, where we use the per-
127      parameter influence to select trainable parameters and thus an adaptive PET method for general
128      models and tasks.

352   NAM Agarwal et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2023) is a special neural network architecture, which has mul-

      For Hi-DLR, the lr0 (black lines) is the learning rate for the bias. lr1, lr2 · · · is the learning rate

      5.2   A META - FRAMEWORK OF ADAPTIVE PET
487
488   Our meta-framework is flexible and model-agnostic: given a number of PET methods and the corre-
489   sponding parameter groups, we leverage Algorithm 1 to select the parameter groups with high PPI
490   and freeze the others if PPIk < ψ · mink PPIk . Here ψ is an adjustable hyperparameter, with ψ = 1
491   meaning full model training (FMT) and ψ > 1 meaning PET. We note that higher ψ leads to fewer
492   trainable parameters and likely worse performance but better computation efficiency).
493   In particular, we can determine ψ and thus the PET method by experimenting on a small model, and
494   then transfer to larger models, since we empirically observe that different model sizes (within the
495   same architecture) have similar PPI by parameter groups in Figure 7.

716
      Sum of Beale and Rosenbrock        Beale is a convex function and Rosenbrock is a non-convex func-
      tion.
717
718                  Beale(x, y) = (1.5 − x + xy)2 + (2.25 − x + xy 2 )2 + (2.625 − x + xy 3 )2
719
              Rosenbrock(x, y) = 100(y − x2 )2 + (1 − x)2
720
721   The unique minimizer for Beale is (3, 0.5), for Rosenbrock is (1, 1). The optimization problem is a
722   sum of Beale and Rosenbrock:
723
724                           L(w0 , w1 ) = Beale(w0 , 0.5) + Rosenbrock(w1 , 1).
725
726
      So the minimizer of this new L is (3, 1). We optimize from the initialization at (4, 3).

981    B     C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
982
983    We follow the same analysis as in Bu & Xu (2024) and it follows that Hi-DLR has the same peak
984    memory cost as a base optimizer. For time complexity, we consider three operations: the forward
985    pass F , the back-propagation B and other costs C. Therefore, the base optimizer takes F + B + C
986    whereas Hi-DLR takes (1 + 4K Φ )F + B + C. Here the additional computation is from extra forward
987    passes. In a full-parameter training on a single GPU, C is negligible and B ≈ 2F , the relative
988    training speed of Hi-DLR is 1+14K . For instance, when K = 3, Φ = 10, Hi-DLR is roughly 70% as
                                       3Φ
989    fast as a base optimizer. While training with PET methods, the B ≈ F , the relative speed becomes
          1
990    1+ 4K
              . When K = 3, Φ = 10, Hi-DLR is roughly 62.5% as fast as a base optimizer.

994
       The success of DLR depends on the grouping of parameters: a sub-optimal grouping strategy might
995
       lead to a less effective learning rate adaptation. It remains an interesting future direction on how to
