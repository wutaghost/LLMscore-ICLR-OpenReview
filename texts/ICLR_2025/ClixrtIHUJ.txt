                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper addresses the challenges of class incremental learning (CIL) within
012
               the broader context of continual learning. In CIL, a system learns a sequence of
013            tasks or classes incrementally. The resulting classifier can categorize test sam-
014            ples into any learned class thus far without relying on task-specific information
015            during testing. CIL presents two significant challenges: catastrophic forgetting
016            (CF) and inter-task class separation (ICS). ICS occurs because the system lacks
017            data from previous tasks when learning new ones, making it harder to establish
018            decision boundaries between classes, reducing accuracy. This paper proposes a
019            novel method to overcome both CF and ICS. The basic classifier is based on the
020            statistical technique Mahalanobis distance (MD), which measures the distance of
021
               a data point to a normal distribution. In the proposed approach, each class is rep-
               resented by a normal distribution with the mean and covariance derived from the
022
               features of its training data, which are extracted from a language model (LM).
023
               To reduce storage, all classes share a common covariance matrix. Two additional
024            techniques are also proposed to enhance the accuracy: (1) using a kernel function
025            to expand the feature space, and (2) incorporating an ensemble mechanism. Our
026            experiments show that the proposed method achieves accuracy comparable to the
027            upper bound accuracy of joint fine-tuning, which, to our knowledge, has not been
028            achieved before.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The objective of continual learning (CL) is to enable an AI agent to incrementally acquire knowledge
034   by learning a sequence of tasks over time (Chen & Liu, 2018; Wang et al., 2023a). One of the major
035   challenges in CL is catastrophic forgetting (CF), where updating the model’s parameters to learn
036
      new tasks can lead to a decline in performance on previously learned tasks (McCloskey & Cohen,
      1989). In this work, we focus on the class incremental learning (CIL) setting of continual learning
037
      (CL) (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019).
038
039   In CIL, each task introduces a unique set of classes, and the goal is to train a single unified model
040   capable of recognizing all the classes encountered across tasks. Since learning has to be done in-
041   crementally, learning a task has no or limited access to the data of previous tasks. A defining char-
042
      acteristic of CIL is that no task identification information is provided during testing, meaning the
      model does not know which task a test sample belongs to.1 An additional challenge to CIL is inter-
043
      task class separation (ICS) (Kim et al., 2022), which refers to the problem that without access to
044
      previous task data when learning a new task, it is difficult to establish decision boundaries between
045   new and previously learned classes. While many CIL methods have been proposed (De Lange et al.,
046   2021; Zhou et al., 2024), they still suffer from significant performance degradation as more tasks are
047   learned, largely due to the combined challenges of CF and ICS.
048
      This paper proposes a novel technique, called Kernel Mahalanobis Distance (KMD), to address
049
      both challenges and can achieve an accuracy level comparable to that obtained by joint training of
050
      all classes/tasks together, which is regarded as the accuracy upper bound of CIL, with no replay data.
051
052       1
            Two other common CL settings are task incremental learning, where task information is provided during
053   testing, and domain-incremental learning, which involves learning tasks from different domains but with the
      same set of classes (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019).

054
      KMD leverages a frozen language model (LM) as a fixed feature extractor, meaning that dur-
055   ing learning, the LM’s parameters remain unchanged, and no additional structures like learnable
056   prompts (Wang et al., 2022b; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) or adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019) are
057   added to tailor it for each new task. KMD operates under the assumption that each class follows
058   a Gaussian distribution characterized by a mean and covariance. It further assumes that all classes
059   share the same covariance but differ in their means. Throughout the CIL process, KMD simply cal-
060   culates and updates the shared covariance and class-specific means based on the features extracted
061   by the LM for each training sample. For classification, it employs Mahalanobis Distance (MD)–a
062   function that measures the distance between a data point and a Gaussian distribution–to compare
063
      the test sample to the distribution of each class and select the nearest match. Since the approach
      involves no training or updates to the LM’s parameters, it avoids CF caused by parameter updates.
064
065   Two additional enhancements are proposed to further improve the results. First, employing the
066   Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel (Scholkopf & Smola, 2018) to improve the features extracted
067   from the LM. The RBF kernel computes the dot product in an implicit higher-dimensional space,
068   giving higher similarity to closer points and lower similarity to those further apart. This creates
069
      a localized effect, where the influence of each data point diminishes as you move further away
      from it, which is desirable for our distance-based classification approach. However, the RBF kernel
070
      requires storing the pairwise similarities for all data points, which is infeasible. To address this, We
071
      approximate the kernel using Random Fourier Features (RFF) (Rahimi & Recht, 2007), making it
072   feasible for CIL. When a new task arrives, KMD uses the kernalized features to compute the class
073   mean of each class and updates the shared covariance matrix for all classes learned thus far. Second,
074   instead of relying on a single kernel transformation, we create a few parallel models and perform an
075   ensemble for the final classification, which gives the final system KMD-Ensemble. In summary, this
076   paper makes three key contributions:
077
      (1) It presents a novel CIL method called KMD, which leverages the rich features extracted from
078   an LM, further enhancing them through a kernel technique and an ensemble strategy. To our knowl-
079   edge, this approach has not been previously explored.
080
081
      (2) KMD addresses both CF and ICS challenges by (i) using a shared covariance matrix and class
      feature means to define Gaussian distributions, effectively addressing ICS by creating clear class
082
      boundaries, and (ii) gathering only statistical information during CIL without updating the LM or
083
      training new networks, thus avoiding CF at the LM level. Although incrementally updating the
084   covariance matrix could introduce CF, our results show even a slight positive knowledge transfer.
085
086   (3) Our experiments show that KMD-Ensemble significantly outperforms existing baselines, achiev-
087
      ing accuracy comparable to joint Fine-tuning of the LM on all tasks, the upper bound for CIL. We
      further validate this using various LMs. This is notable, as existing CIL methods have consistently
088
      below this upper bound–often by a considerable margin–posing a significant barrier to their practical
089
      use in real-world applications.

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   There is a large body of literature on continual learning (CL). Most of it focuses on mitigating
095   catastrophic forgetting (CF). Existing methods generally fall into several categories: Regularization-
096   based methods use regularizers to penalize changes to important parameters of previous tasks, (Kirk-
097   patrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2019a). Replay-based methods
098   store some samples from previous tasks and learn a new task using both the new and stored data to
099
      maintain performance across tasks (Liu et al., 2021a; Scialom et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2022; Huang
      et al., 2021). Some methods learn data generators instead of storing actual data to generate samples
100
      similar to those from previous tasks (Shin et al., 2017; He & Jaeger, 2018). Architectural-based
101
      methods include many approaches. Some expand the network as new tasks are introduced (Wang
102   et al., 2022a; Yan et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023). Some use parameter isolation, where sub-networks
103   are trained for each task via mechanisms like masking or orthogonal projection (Serra et al., 2018;
104   Gururangan et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2020;
105   Liu et al., 2023). Yet, some class incremental learning (CIL) methods employ a task predictor to
106   identify the appropriate model for the predicted task classifier (Rajasegaran et al., 2020; Abati et al.,
107   2020; Wang et al., 2023b; 2024a). They may utilize strategies like separate networks, entropy, or
      out-of-distribution (OOD) detection to determine the task and to deal with inter-task class separa-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tion (ICS) problem. Most CL methods operate in a batch setting, where all task data is available
109   at once. There are also online CL methods that process data incrementally in a stream (Mai et al.,
110   2022). While we do not focus on streaming, our approach shares similarities with (Hayes & Kanan,
111   2020), which uses streaming linear discriminant analysis with class means and a covariance matrix.
112   However, our work differs by employing Mahalanobis distance. Additionally, we enhance feature
113   representations with a kernel and utilize an ensemble technique.
114
      In natural language processing (NLP), continual learning has been applied to a wide range of prob-
115   lems, e.g., text classification (Sun et al., 2020; Chuang et al., 2020), sentiment analysis (Ke et al.,
116   2021), topic modeling (Gupta et al., 2020), slot filling (Shen et al., 2019), question answering (Greco
117   et al., 2019), language acquisition (Li et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024), and the pre-
118   training of language models (Qin et al., 2022; Ke et al., 2021). Employing pre-trained models is a
119   standard approach in many NLP-related CL scenarios as leveraging their capabilities can improve
120   performance (Shao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). For further insights and overview, please refer
121   to the surveys (Ke & Liu, 2022; Wang et al., 2023a).
122   The rise of large foundation models has led to a growing interest in integrating CL with pre-trained
123   models (Yang et al., 2024). While prior work incorporates pre-trained models, they do so within the
124   framework of the three main CL strategies discussed above, which still suffer from CF. In contrast,
125   we explore the full potential of the pre-trained large language models as fixed feature-extractors for
126   CIL, i.e., leveraging only their latent features for downstream tasks. However, our experiments show
127   that using latent features directly is sub-optimal. To address this, we propose KMD, which enhances
128   features with kernel functions, improving class separability in the kernelized space. KMD is distinct
129
      in being replay-free, without relying on regularizers or architectural changes.

133   This section presents the main background information for the proposed method.
134   Class Incremental Learning (CIL): In CIL, a model is trained on a sequence of tasks
135   {T1 , T2 , . . . , TT }, where each task Tt introduces a disjoint set of classes with its associated train-
136                            (i) (i)
      ing data Dt = {(xt , yt )}N         t

137   previous tasks T1 , . . . , Tt−1 is not accessible while learning the current task Tt . The model must
138   learn new classes without forgetting previously learned ones, despite the absence of earlier training
139   data. The goal is to learn a unified model F : X → Y capable of classifying samples from any of
140   the classes encountered across the T tasks. During inference, the task identity is unknown, and the

186   4     P ROPOSED M ETHOD : KMD
187
188   We propose enhancing MD with a kernel function, resulting in the KMD method. The core idea
189
      is to improve the separability of feature representations obtained from the pre-trained LM using a
      kernel function. This allows us to maintain a robust representation of the data as new classes/tasks
190
      are added, without suffering from CF.

      4.1   K ERNEL F UNCTIONS
193
194   While MD works well for separable Guassian distributions, it may struggle when class boundaries
195   are not well-separated in the original feature space. A powerful approach to overcome this limitation
196   is through the use of kernel functions, which implicitly map the input data (features from the LM
197   in our case) into a higher-dimensional space where the data becomes more separable. In this high-
198   dimensional space, MD can provide better class separation, even when the original feature space
199   lacks clear boundaries.

201   infinite-dimensional feature space, a kernel function K(xi , xj ) computes the inner product in this

296
297   Four text classification datasets are used in our experiments: 1. CLINC: This dataset has 150
298   classes, which are dialogue intents, from many different application domains (Larson et al., 2019).
299   We used the train/test split of 10,000/750 samples, and the classes were randomly divided into 10
300   disjoint tasks. 2. Banking: This dataset has 77 classes of dialogue intents in the banking domain
301
      (Casanueva et al., 2020). We employed a 10,000/1,000 train/test split and divided the classes into

302
      (Liu et al., 2021b). We used a train/test split of 10,000/1,000 samples and divided the classes into
303
      7 disjoint tasks. 4. HWU: Another dialogue intent classification dataset with 20 domains and 64
304   classes (Auer et al., 2007). We used a train/test split of 9,000/1,000 samples and partitioned the
305   classes into 8 disjoint tasks.
306
307
      We adhere to the standard CIL protocol, where the classes are partitioned into disjoint tasks. The
      classes within each dataset are randomly shuffled and assigned to these tasks, ensuring that each
308
      task introduces new classes not seen in previous tasks. To account for the variability in performance
309
      due to different task splits, we perform multiple runs with different random shuffles and report the
310   average results.

      5.2   BASELINES
313
314   We compare KMD against a range of baselines, categorized into existing CIL methods, class-
315   prototype based methods, and joint training. These diverse baselines allow us to thoroughly evaluate
316   the performance of KMD.
317
      – Existing CIL Baselines: These systems use various existing popular approaches. 1. Vanilla:
318   Sequentially fine-tunes the model on each task with no mechanism to mitigate CF. 2. EWC (Elas-
319   tic Weight Consolidation): A popular regularization-based method that adds a penalty to preserve
320   important parameters from previous tasks, balancing new learning with retention (Kirkpatrick et al.,
321   2017). 3. KD (Knowledge Distillation): Uses knowledge distillation to help the model retain infor-
322   mation from old tasks by learning from softened output probabilities of previous versions of itself
323   (Hinton et al., 2015). 4. L2P (Learn to Prompt): Freezes the LM and learns trainable prompts to
      guide inference, adapting to new tasks without altering the LM (Wang et al., 2022b). 5. LAMOL


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      (Language Modeling for Lifelong Language Learning): Employs pseudo-replay by generating
325   pseudo-examples of previous tasks to mix with new task data, maintaining past performance while
326   learning new tasks (Sun et al., 2019). 6. VAG (Vocabulary-Aware Label Generation): Lever-
327   ages vocabulary sparsity to selectively activate relevant outputs for each task, mitigating forgetting.
328   Instead of traditional classification, VAG focuses on generating labels (Shao et al., 2023).
329
      – Class-Prototype Based Baselines: 7. NCM (Nearest Class Mean): Maintains a mean feature
330
      vector for each class, updated incrementally. Classification is based on the nearest class mean to
331   the test sample’s feature vector. 8. MD (Mahalanobis Distance): Uses the original feature space
332   without our RBF kernel extension.
333
334
      – Upper Bound Baseline: 9. Joint Fine-tuning: Fine-tuning the full LM by adding a classifier head
      on top of the latent features and training on all classes simultaneously as a single task. The result
335
      from this is considered the upper-bound performance of CIL.

339
      For all experiments–except the ablation on different LMs–we use the BART-base model (Lewis
340
      et al., 2019), which features a 6-layer encoder-decoder architecture with a 768-dimensional hidden
341
      state. We chose this model because many of our baselines employ a generative objective or require
342   generating pseudo-replay data during training, making the decoder component essential. Addition-
343   ally, the state-of-the-art baseline VAG (Shao et al., 2023) also utilizes BART-base.
344
345
      To study the generalization of our method to other LMs, we also evaluate it using the following
      models: 1. paraphrase-MiniLM-L3 (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) (3 layers, 384 dimensions), 2.
346
      BERT-base (Devlin, 2018) (12 layers, 768 dimensions), 3. RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019b)
347
      (24 layers, 1024 dimensions), 4. T5-3b (Raffel et al., 2020) (24 layers, 1024 dimensions), and 5.
348   Mistral-7b Jiang et al. (2023) (32 layers, 4096 dimensions).
349
350   LAMOL and VAG were executed using their official codes and configurations. For the remaining
351
      existing baselines, we used implementations from (Shao et al., 2023) repository. The class-prototype
      based baselines were implemented using our own code, adhering to the same update rules applied
352
      in KMD to ensure consistency in comparison.
353
354   The Joint Fine-tuning model, regarded as the upper bound, is trained for 50 epochs with a batch
355   size of 128, using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-3 for the classifier head and 1e-4 for
356   the LM parameters. Additionally, we experimented with various configurations, including different
357
      learning rates, batch sizes, and epoch numbers, to ensure the models were thoroughly trained and
      optimized. We also compared our Joint Fine-tuning results with those reported in (Shao et al., 2023)
358
      and found that our configuration actually achieves better performance.
359
360   For KMD-Ensemble, we always use an ensemble of 5 models, but we also provide an ablation study
361   on the impact of the number of models in the ensemble. KMD itself has two hyperparameters:
362   the transformation dimension D and the RFF σ. Given the CIL setup, where tasks are learned
363
      incrementally, the system does not see all tasks at the same time, and validation sets are not typically

365
      The σ parameter is also empirically determined within range [10−2 , 10−6 ] for each LM and remains
366   fixed across different datasets. We will show the results of different parameter settings later. Our
367   implementation is built using PyTorch, with all LMs sourced from the Hugging Face Transformers
368   library. All experiments are conducted on a NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80GB of VRAM.

457   5.5.3   A NALYSIS OF F ORGETTING R ATE
458
459   Our method has no forgetting at the LM level since we do not fine-tune it or add any additional struc-
460   ture to it for adaptation. However, the incremental updates to the shared covariance can introduce
461   some forgetting. Therefore, we measured the forgetting rate after all tasks were learned and found
462
      it to be negative across all datasets, as shown in Table 3. This indicates slight positive knowledge
      transfer, meaning the accuracy on earlier tasks improved after learning new ones.
463

465
466   5.5.4   M EMORY U SAGE C OMPARISON
467
468   We compare the methods in terms of memory usage. The Joint Fine-tuning only adds a classifier
469   head on top of the LM features, introducing approximately 0.1M additional parameters for typical
470   values of M = 150 classes and d = 768 hidden dimensions. Existing method baselines, particularly
471
      those requiring the model to operate in the generation mode, significantly increase the memory
      usage. For instance, an LM head required for text generation adds approximately 38.5M parameters
472
      for a vocabulary of 50,265 tokens, although this number does not increase with the number of
473
      classes.
474
475   Class Prototype (CP) methods are more memory-efficient as they only require storing the class
476   prototypes. NCM requires M ×d parameters for the mean vectors, similar to the classifier head of the
477
      Joint model. MD adds an d × d covariance matrix, increasing the parameter count by approximately
      0.6M. KMD introduces D × (d + 1) fixed non-trainable parameters for the RFF transformation.
478
      With D set to 5000, this adds around 3.8M parameters. KMD also scales the parameters required
479
      for CPs by a factor of D/d, leading to an additional 0.75M parameters. The covariance matrix for
480   KMD is D × D, resulting in an additional 25M parameters. In total, KMD’s memory footprint
481   is approximately 29.5M parameters. This memory requirement is still significantly lower than the
482   LM head needed for text generation alone. Our KMD-Ensemble utilizes 5 models, resulting in a 5x
483   increase in memory usage, which remains within a reasonable limit. For reference, the BART-base
484   model used in our experiments has 139.5M parameters. We highlight that a large portion of KMD’s
485   parameters are associated with the fixed RFF transformation and the shared covariance matrix, which
      do not increase as more classes are added in the CIL process.

500   5.5.5   E FFICIENCY AND RUNTIME A NALYSIS
501
502   Our method is highly efficient, as it bypasses the need to update LM parameters or compute gradients
503   during training. Instead, it simply computes class means and the covariance matrix. On the CLINC
504   dataset with a BART-base LM, KMD and KMD-Ensemble train in approximately 10 and 30 seconds,
505
      respectively, on our GPU setup–comparable to the time required to extract latent features from the
      LM. In contrast, Joint Fine-tuning takes about 4 minutes to train. Existing CL method baselines take
506
      much longer to train, as they involve updating the model incrementally on each task. They often
507
      require additional computations too, e.g., computing the output of their previous versions (KD) or
508   generating pseudo-replay data (LAMOL and VAG), leading to training times ranging from 11 to 23
509   minutes.

      5.5.6   A NALYSIS OF H YPERPARAMETERS
512
513   For KMD-Ensemble, we used 5 models in the ensemble, as we found this to provide good perfor-
514   mance without significantly increasing space or computation requirements. Further details on the
515   effect of different ensemble sizes (number of models) on performance can be found in Appendix A.
516   KMD itself has two key hyperparameters: transform dimension D and the kernel scale σ. Figure 1
517   shows how these hyperparameters affect accuracy across different datasets. D controls the balance
518   between the memory usage and the accuracy of kernel approximation. We found that setting D to
519   5000 provides a good balance, offering sufficient accuracy without excessive memory usage. σ af-
520   fects the scale of the RBF kernel and thus influences the separation of the transformed features. We
521
      fixed σ for all the datasets after determining it for each backbone (see Apendix A). KMD performs
      well across all datasets with this parameter setting. This indicates that KMD can learn various tasks
522
      incrementally without the need for major adjustments to its configuration.

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527
      A large body of literature exists on class incremental learning (CIL). Most existing methods focused
528   on mitigating the CF by Fine-tuning an LM through direct parameter updates or by learning prompts
529   or adapters, but these approaches are still prone to CF and limited attentions have been paid to
530   ICS. The proposed method KMD deals with both problems and is fundamentally different from the
531   traditional approaches. KMD only uses a fixed LM as a feature extractor. It leverages the Radial
532   Basis Function (RBF) kernel to enhance the feature representation through Random Fourier Features
533   approximation. This kernelized representation is then used to compute class means and a shared
534   covariance matrix. The final classification is based on Mahalanobis distance. Our experiments show
535   that KMD-Ensemble significantly outperforms existing baselines and, more importantly, achieves
536
      accuracy on par or better than joint Fine-tuning, which is regarded as the upper bound of CIL.
537   Limitations: The proposed method relies on the assumption that the LM contains sufficiently rich
538   features for the CIL tasks in the target domain. If the LM’s features are not well-suited to a specific
539   domain, the accuracy of our method may suffer. A standard approach to address this is to fine-tune
      the general-purpose LM using a large domain-specific corpus before applying it to CIL.
