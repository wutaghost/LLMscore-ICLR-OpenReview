009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent diffusion has demonstrated promising results in image generation and per-
012            mits efficient sampling. However, this framework might suffer from the problem
013            of posterior collapse when applied to time series. In this paper, we first show that
014            posterior collapse will reduce latent diffusion to a variational autoencoder (VAE),
015            making it less expressive. This highlights the importance of addressing this is-
016            sue. We then introduce a principled method: dependency measure, that quantifies
017
               the sensitivity of a recurrent decoder to input variables. Using this tool, we con-
               firm that posterior collapse significantly affects time-series latent diffusion on real
018
               datasets, and a phenomenon termed dependency illusion is also discovered in the
019
               case of shuffled time series. Finally, building on our theoretical and empirical
020            studies, we introduce a new framework that extends latent diffusion and has a sta-
021            ble posterior. Extensive experiments on multiple real time-series datasets show
022            that our new framework is free from posterior collapse and significantly outper-
023            forms previous baselines in time series synthesis.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) has achieved promising performance in image generation
029   and offers significantly higher sampling speeds than standard diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020).
030
      However, we find that, when applied to time series data, this framework might suffer from posterior
      collapse (Bowman et al., 2016), an important problem that has garnered significant attention in the
031
      literature on autoencoders (Baldi, 2012; Lucas et al., 2019), where the latent variable contains little
032
      information about the data and it tends to be ignored by the decoder during conditional generation.
033   In this paper, we aim to provide a systematic analysis on the impact of posterior collapse on latent
034   diffusion and improve this framework based on our analysis.
035
036   Impact analysis of posterior collapse. We first show that a strictly collapsed posterior reduces the
037   latent diffusion to a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013), indicating that this
038   problem makes the framework less expressive, even weaker than a vanilla diffusion model. We then
039   introduce a principled method termed dependency measure, which quantifies the dependencies of
040   an autoregressive decoder on the latent variable and the input partial time series. Through empirical
041   estimation of these measures, we find that the latent variable has an almost exponentially vanishing
042   impact on the recurrent decoder during the generation process. An example (i.e., the green bar chart)
043
      is shown in the upper left subfigure of Fig. 1. More interestingly, the upper right subfigure illustrates
      a phenomenon we call dependency illusion: Even when the time series is randomly shuffled and thus
044
      lacks structural dependencies, the decoder of latent diffusion still heavily relies on input observations
045
      (instead of the latent variable) for prediction.
046
047
      New framework to solve the problem. We first point out that the root cause of posterior collapse
048   lies in the improper design of latent diffusion, which leads to avoidable KL-divergence regularization
049   and a lack of mechanisms to address the insensitive decoder (Bowman et al., 2016). Building on
050   these findings, we propose a novel framework that extends latent diffusion, allowing the diffusion
051   model and autoencoder to interact more effectively. Specifically, by treating the diffusion process as
052   a form of variational inference, we can eliminate the problematic KL-divergence regularization and
053   permit an unlimited prior distribution for latent variables. To let the decoder be more sensitive to
      the latent variable, we also apply the diffusion process to simulate a collapsed posterior, imposing

097   maps high-dimensional or structured data into low-dimensional latent variables; 2) a diffusion

141   3     P ROBLEM A NALYSIS
142
143   In this section, we first formulate the problem of posterior collapse in the framework of time-series
144   latent diffusion and show its significance. Then, we define proper measures that quantify the impact
145   of posterior collapse on the models. Finally, we conduct empirical experiments to confirm that
146   time-series diffusion indeed suffers from this problem.
147
148   3.1   F ORMULATION OF P OSTERIOR C OLLAPSE AND I TS I MPACTS
149
150
      Let us focus on time series X = [x1 , x2 , · · · , xT ], where every observation xt , t ∈ [1, T ] is a
      D-dimensional vector and T denotes the number of observations. A potential risk of applying the
151
      latent diffusion to time series is the posterior collapse (Bowman et al., 2016), which occurs to
152
      some autoencoders (Bahdanau et al., 2014), especially VAE (Lucas et al., 2019). Its mathematical
153   formulation in the framework of latent diffusion is as follows.
154
155   Problem formulation. The posterior of VAE: q VI (z | X), is collapsed if it reduces to the Gaussian
156   prior pprior (z) = N (z; 0, I), irrespective of the time-series conditional X:
157
158
                                        q VI (z | X) = pprior (z), ∀X ∈ RT D .
159   In this case, the latent variable z contains no information about time series X, otherwise the pos-
160   terior distribution q VI (z | X) would vary depending on different conditionals. Above is a strict
161   definition. In practice, one is mostly faced with a situation where q VI (z | X) ≈ pprior (z) and it is
      still appropriate to say that the posterior collapses.


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Implications of posterior collapse. A typical symptom of this problem is that, since the latent
163   variable z carries very limited information of time series X, the trained decoder f dec tends to ignore
164   this input variable z, which is undesired for conditional generation pgen (X | z). Besides this empir-
165   ical finding from previous works, we find that posterior collapse is also significant in its impact on
166   the expressiveness of latent diffusion. Let us first see the below conclusion.
167   Proposition 3.1 (Gaussian Latent Variables). For standard latent diffusion, suppose its posterior
168   q VI (z | X) is collapsed, then the distribution q latent (z) of latent variable z will shape as a standard
169   Gaussian N (0, I), which is trivial for the diffusion model to approximate.
170
171   Proof. The proof is fully provided in Appendix A.
172
173   In other words, latent variable z is just Gaussian in the case of posterior collapse. The diffusion
174   model, which is known for approximating complex data distributions (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Li
175   et al., 2024), will in fact become a redundant module. Therefore, posterior collapse reduces latent
176   diffusion to a simple VAE, which also samples latent variable z from a standard Gaussian N (0, I).
177   We conclude that the problem makes latent diffusion less expressive.
178
        Takeaway: The problem of posterior collapse not only lets the decode f dec tend to ignore the
179     latent variable z for conditional generation pgen (X | z), but also reduces the framework of
180     latent diffusion to VAE, making it less expressive.

307   4     P ROBLEM R ETHINKING AND N EW F RAMEWORK
308
309   In this section, we first analyze how the framework design of latent diffusion makes it tend to suffer
310   from posterior collapse. Then, based on our analysis, we propose a new framework, which extends
311   latent diffusion but addresses the problem.
312
313   4.1    R ISKY D ESIGN OF L ATENT D IFFUSION
314
      Previous works (Semeniuta et al., 2017; Alemi et al., 2018) have identified two main causes of the
315
      problem: KL-divergence term and strong decoder. For time-series latent diffusion, we will explain
316   as below that those causes indeed exist, but are in fact avoidable.
317
318   Unnecessary regularization. The KL-divergence term DKL (q VI (z | X) || pprior (z)) in Eq. (2)
319   moves the posterior q VI (z | X) towards prior pprior (z), which has the side effect of posterior
320   collapse by definition. In essence, this term is tailored for VAE, such that it is valid to sample latent
321   variable z from the Gaussian prior pprior (z) for inference. However, for latent diffusion, the variable
322   z is sampled from the diffusion model, which can approximate a non-Gaussian prior distribution.
323   Hence, the interaction between the VAE and diffusion model is not properly designed, which incurs
      a limited prior pprior (z) and a risky KL-divergence term DKL (·).

358
      diffusion model more effectively than latent diffusion. With this better framework design, we can

402   Diffusion process for collapse simulation. Then, we apply the last few iterations of the diffusion

531
      In this paper, we provide a solid analysis of the negative impacts of posterior collapse on time-series
      latent diffusion and introduce a new framework that is free from this problem. For our analysis, we
532
      begin with a theoretical insight, showing that the problem will reduce latent diffusion to VAE, ren-
533
      dering it less expressive. Then, we introduce a useful tool: dependency measure, which quantifies
534   the impacts of various inputs on an autoregressive decoder. Through empirical dependency esti-
535   mation, we show that the latent variable has a vanishing impact on the decoder and find that latent
536   diffusion exhibits a phenomenon of dependency illusion. Compared with standard latent diffusion,
537   our framework gets rid of the risky KL-divergence regularization, permits an unlimited prior distri-
538   bution, and lets the decoder be sensitive to the latent variable. Extensive experiments on multiple
539   real-world time-series datasets show that our framework has no symptoms of posterior collapse and
      notably outperforms the baselines in terms of time series generation.

901   F.2   S TUDY ON RUNNING T IMES
902
      Our framework only incurs a minor increase in training time and enjoys the same inference speed
903
      as the latent diffusion. For training, while our framework will run the decoder a second time for
904   collapse simulation LCS , it can be made in parallel with the first run of decoder f dec for likelihood
905   computation LVI . Therefore, the training is still efficient on GPU devices. Our framework also has
906   a different way of variational inference to infer latent variable z from data X. However, it admits a
907   closed-form solution and is thus as efficient as the reparameterization trick of latent diffusion. For
908   inference, our framework has no difference from the latent diffusion: sampling the latent variable z
909   with the reverse diffusion process and running the decoder f dec in one shot.
910   To show the running times in practice, we perform an experiment on the MIMIC dataset as shown
911   in Table 3. We can see that our framework indeed only has a minor increase for training. Given
912   that our framework is free from posterior collapse and delivers better generation performances, this
913   slight time investment is well worth it.

945   sequential structure similar to time series, it is intuitive that the posterior of text latent diffusion might
946   also collapse. This intuition is supported by many evidences from previous works (Bowman et al.,
947   2015; Fu et al., 2019b). To verify that our framework is capable of improving text latent diffusion,
948   we have conducted an experiment using two publicly available text datasets: ATIS (Hemphill et al.,
949   1990) and SNIPS (Coucke et al., 2018).
950   The numbers in this table represent BLEU scores (Papineni et al., 2002), a widely used metric
951   for evaluating text generation models. Higher scores indicate better performance. As the results
952   shown in Table 5, we can see that our framework has significantly improved the text latent diffusion
953   and notably outperformed a strong baseline—Skip Connections—across all datasets and backbones.
954   Therefore, our framework also applies to text data.

957   paper: Image latent diffusion is rarely affected by posterior collapse due to its non-autoregressive

      decoder. To confirm this claim in practice, we conduct an experiment comparing latent diffusion
      with our framework on the widely used CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009).
