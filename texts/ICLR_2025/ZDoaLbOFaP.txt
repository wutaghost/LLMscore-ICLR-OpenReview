                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Covariance Neural Networks (VNNs) perform graph convolutions on the covariance
               matrix of tabular data and achieve success in a variety of applications. However,
011
               the empirical covariance matrix on which the VNNs operate may contain many
012
               spurious correlations, making VNNs’ performance inconsistent due to these noisy
013            estimates and decreasing their computational efficiency. To tackle this issue, we
014            put forth Sparse coVariance Neural Networks (S-VNNs), a framework that applies
015            sparsification techniques on the sample covariance matrix before convolution.
016            When the true covariance matrix is sparse, we propose hard and soft thresholding
017            to improve covariance estimation and reduce computational cost. Instead, when
018            the true covariance is dense, we propose stochastic sparsification where data
019            correlations are dropped in probability according to principled strategies. We
020            show that S-VNNs are more stable than nominal VNNs as well as sparse principal
021
               component analysis. By analyzing the impact of sparsification on their behavior,
               we provide novel connections between S-VNN stability and data distribution. We
022
               support our theoretical findings with experimental results on various application
023
               scenarios, ranging from brain data to human action recognition, and show an
024            improved task performance, stability, and computational efficiency of S-VNNs
025            compared with nominal VNNs.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Covariance-based data processing is key to machine learning pipelines due to its ability to whiten data
031   distributions, identify principal directions, and estimate the interdependencies among features. Such
032   advantages have been shown in several applications including brain connectivity estimation (Bessadok
033   et al., 2022; Qiao et al., 2016), financial data (de Miranda Cardoso et al., 2020; Wang & Aste, 2022)
034   and human action recognition through motion sensors measurements (Liao et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
035   2023). One prominent approach is Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which maximizes the
036   variance of data points by projecting them onto the eigenvectors of their covariance matrix (Jolliffe &
037   Cadima, 2016). However, PCA-based approaches are unstable to errors in the covariance estimation,
038   i.e., a poor estimate of the covariance matrix and its eigenvectors may lead to unpredictably bad results,
039
      especially in high-dimensional and low-data regimes or when the covariance matrix eigenvalues are
      close to each other, and these limitations also appear in more advanced PCA variants such as kernel
040
      PCA (Jolliffe, 2002; Paul, 2007; Baik et al., 2005; Schölkopf et al., 1997). To overcome this issue,
041
      coVariance Neural Networks (VNNs) have been proposed (Sihag et al., 2022). VNNs apply graph
042   convolutions to the covariance matrix, an operation that, similarly to PCA, learns the importance
043   of the principal directions of the data. Due to their graph convolution operation, they inherit the
044   stability property of GNNs (Gama et al., 2020b) and extend it to errors in covariance estimation,
045   which guarantees more reliable performance even in the presence of poor covariance estimates
046   since their output difference when operating on the sample and true covariance is bounded (Sihag
047   et al., 2022; 2024; 2023). Nevertheless, VNNs operate on the sample covariance matrix, which
048   results in two limitations. First, VNNs are sensitive to settings in which covariance estimation is
049   problematic, especially in high-dimensional and low-data regimes (Paul, 2007; Baik et al., 2005;
050
      Féral & Péché, 2007). Second, VNNs are computationally expensive and memory inefficient as the
      sample covariance matrix is typically dense no matter whether the true covariance matrix is dense or
051
      sparse due to sample estimation errors, which is especially problematic on high-dimensional datasets.
052
053   To overcome these limitations, we propose sparsification-based covariance regularizers for VNNs to
      improve the covariance matrix estimation. We perform stability analysis for sparse VNNs (S-VNNs)

054
      to investigate the effects of covariance sparsification techniques on the performance of VNNs. When
055   true covariance matrices are sparse, we propose hard and soft thresholding strategies and show that
056   S-VNNs are more robust to both the finite-data estimation error and the covariance sparsification
057   thresholding. For generic scenarios where true covariance matrices are not necessarily sparse, we
058   propose a stochastic sparsification approach and prove the stability of S-VNNs in this setting. Our
059   findings quantify a trade-off between stability and sparsification (i.e., efficiency). That is, dropping
060   covariances according to their absolute values leads to higher stability but, in some cases, low
061   sparsification, whereas dropping a fixed percentage of covariance values improves sparsification
062   at the expense of stability. We corroborate our findings with experiments on synthetic and real
063
      datasets highlighting the benefits of sparsity on covariance-based neural processing both in terms of
      performance and computation. Our specific contributions are as follows.
064
065   (C1) Sparse covariance neural networks. We propose covariance sparsification techniques for
066   VNNs to improve performance and reduce computation. When the true covariance is sparse, we
067   perform hard and soft covariance thresholding. When the true covariance is dense, we propose a
068   stochastic sparsification that allows controlling the sparsification level and the desired stability.
069   (C2) Stability to covariance sparsification. We characterize the effects of covariance sparsification
070   on the VNN through stability analysis. We show that S-VNNs can not only reduce computation but
071   also improve stability under appropriate settings, compared to the nominal VNN and the sparse PCA.
072
      (C3) Empirical validation. We validate our theoretical findings with experiments on real and syn-
073   thetic datasets, demonstrating the importance of sparsification in low-data settings and the improved
074   efficiency of S-VNNs on brain data and human action recognition use cases.

311   Lipschitz if there exists a constant P s.t. ∥∇L h(λ1 , λ2 )∥2 ≤ P and ∥λ1 ⊙ ∇L h(λ1 , λ2 )∥2 ≤ P.
312
313   The Lipschitz gradient characterizes the variability of h(λ) since, for two multivariate frequency
314   vectors λ1 , λ2 , we have h(λ2 )−h(λ1 ) = ∇T    L h(λ1 , λ2 )(λ2 −λ1 ). The generalized integral Lipschitz
315   filter limits this variability to be at most linear in the multidimensional space and to decrease as the
316
      frequency λ is specified at large values, which extends the standard integral Lipschitz property to the
      multivariate frequency domain.

355   4.3   S TOCHASTIC SPARSIFICATION STRATEGIES
356
357   Following our theoretical analysis, we propose two strategies to assign the sparsification probabilities
358   to covariance values and assess their impact on the stability of VNN.
359
      Absolute covariance values (ACV). Despite being dense, the covariance matrix may contain some
360
      values of lower magnitude due to spurious correlations. We want to drop these small values with a
361
      high probability to save computational cost while keeping large covariance values to maintain the
362   useful dependencies. Thus, we define the probability as pij = |ĉij |/ĉmax , where ĉmax = maxi,j |ĉij |.
363   Fig. 2 (left) shows that the stability term qin within Q [cf. Thm. 4] is small when the covariance
364   value ĉin approaches zero or the maximal value ĉmax . This is because small covariance values have
365   little impact on the VNN stability even if they are more likely to be dropped, and large covariance
366   values (close to ĉmax ) are less likely to be dropped though their removal would affect stability more.
367
368   Ranked covariance values (RCV). While ACV improves efficiency by preserving stronger correla-
369
      tions, it does not allow any control over the amount of sparsification (e.g., if all covariance values
      are high, very few are dropped). To overcome this, we define a set of probability values with a
370
      desired mean p and assign them to covariance values based on their positions in this absolute ranking.
371
      Formally, consider an ordered set of probabilities P = {p′1 , . . . , p′N ′ }, where p′i ≤ p′i+1 , and each
372   p′i is sampled from N (p, σ) with σ = min((1 − p)/3, p/3)) (such that the number of values not in
373   [0, 1], which we clip to the interval, is negligible) and N ′ is the number of probability values for
374   assignment. We set pij = p′k where k = |{clm : |clm | < |cij |; l, m = 1, . . . , N }| is the position
375   of cij in the ordered ranking of absolute covariances. Consequently, the expected percentage of
376   dropped covariances is 1 − p, which allows controlling the sparsification level at the risk of removing
377   useful covariances. In practice, the value of p can be treated as a hyperparameter and tuned through
      cross-validation or on a validation set based on the desired accuracy and stability.

490   6    R ELATED W ORKS
491
      Sparse PCA. The instability of the finite-sample covariance matrix is widely studied for PCA (Jolliffe,
492   2002; Paul, 2007; Baik et al., 2005) and various regularized estimators have been proposed including
493   covariance shrinkage (Ledoit & Wolf, 2003; 2012), lasso penalties (Bien & Tibshirani, 2011; Han Liu
494   & Zhao, 2014), and thresholding (Bickel & Levina, 2008b; Deshpande & Montanari, 2016). We
495   leverage here hard and soft thresholding given their benefits in PCA in low-data sparse-covariance
496   settings and study their impact on VNN stability.
497
      Stability of GNNs-VNNs. VNNs can be seen as graph convolutional neural networks operating on
498   the covariance graph (Sihag et al., 2022), which draws analogies with PCA, but have been shown
499   to be more stable extending the small perturbation analysis of GNNs (Gama et al., 2020b; Kenlay
500   et al., 2021b;a; Levie et al., 2021; Maskey et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023) to covariances. Due to their
501   robustness, they have proved successful in brain data processing (Sihag et al., 2024), transferability
502   to large-dimension data (Sihag et al., 2023), temporal settings (Cavallo et al., 2024b) and biased
503   datasets (Cavallo et al., 2024a). All these results, however, hold for the dense covariance matrix which
504   is suboptimal in low-data regimes and computationally heavy. Here, we propose S-VNNs and study
505   their stability when the true covariance matrix is sparse and dense. This merits a different treatment
506
      than the perturbation assumption studied in (Sihag et al., 2022) and overcomes the limitations of
      dense VNNs. Our stochastically sparsified VNN draws analogies with the stability of GNNs to
507
      random link drops (Gao et al., 2021a;b) but we generalize those findings to the more challenging case
508
      where all sparsified probabilities differ rather than being identical. We also propose design strategies
509   for the sparsified probabilities based on our theoretical analysis.
510
511   Graph sparsification in GNNs. Graph sparsification techniques have been used for multiple purposes
512
      in GNNs but predominantly to improve: (i) scalability in large and dense graphs (Hamilton et al.,
      2018; Zhang et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Srinivasa et al., 2020; Ye & Ji, 2021);
513
      (ii) GNN expressiveness in graph classification tasks and alleviate oversmoothing (Papp et al., 2021;
514
      Rong et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2020; Morris et al., 2021); and (iii) enhance
515   interpretability (Rathee et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Naber et al., 2024). Our S-VNNs aim on the
516   one hand to improve scalability and on the other to preserve the tractability and theoretical links
517   with covariance-based data processing. While our sparsification approaches can be applied also to
518   weighted graphs (see Appendix H), in this work we focus on covariance sparsification to characterize
519   theoretically how this affects stability w.r.t. covariance estimation errors and spurious correlations.
520
521   7    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we develop sparse covariance neural networks (S-VNNs) and study the effects of
524   sparsification techniques on covariance neural networks. We show that S-VNNs are more stable
525   to the finite-data sample effect and more computationally efficient. In particular, when the true
526   covariance is sparse, we propose S-VNNs with hard/soft thresholding and show that they transfer
527   to the nominal VNN with a rate inversely proportional to the square root of the number of data
528   points. When the true covariance is dense, we put forth a stochastic sparsified approach to reduce the
529   computational complexity while maintaining mathematical tractability and stability. In the framework
530   of stochastic sparsification, we propose principled strategies, i.e., ACV and RCV, that align with
531
      the theoretical observations to tune sparsification impact and computation efficiency. Experimental
      results on four real datasets show that the proposed S-VNNs improve substantially on the computation
532
      time and achieve competitive or better performance compared with nominal VNNs, thus validating
533
      that spurious correlations are present and damaging in real datasets. Although VNNs can be applied to
534   several other regularized covariance estimators (Ledoit & Wolf, 2003; Bickel & Levina, 2008a; Bien
535   & Tibshirani, 2011; Friedman et al., 2007) to reduce the impact of spurious correlations, we here focus
536   on thresholding since, unlike other estimators, it allows to control the sparsity of the covariance matrix
537   (fundamental for time and memory efficiency), and allows for theoretical tractability. Future work
538   will investigate other covariance estimators and characterize the regularization effect of stochastic
539   sparsification, which allows the model to observe multiple instances of the same covariance with
      potential benefits for robustness, expressiveness and reduced oversmoothing.

      A     BACKGROUND ON PCA AND C ONNECTION WITH VNN S
811
812   PCA is a change of basis that aims at minimizing the redundancy of the transformed data while
813   maintaining their statistical properties of interest (Shlens, 2014; Jolliffe, 2002). PCA achieves
814   this by projecting the data onto a space where the covariance matrix of the transformed data is
815   diagonal, i.e., the off-diagonal elements, which characterize the redundancy of the data, are all zeros.
816   More formally, given a dataset X ∈ RN ×t , where each column is a data sample x ∈ RN , PCA
817   applies a transformation X̃ = TT X with T ∈ RN ×N such that the covariance of the transformed
818   data X̃X̃T is diagonal. This goal is achieved by setting T = V̂, where Ĉ = XXT = V̂Λ̂V̂T
819   is the eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix of the data, since X̃X̃T = V̂T XXT V̂ =
820   V̂T (V̂Λ̂V̂T )V̂ = Λ̂ due to the orthonormality of the covariance eigenvectors. PCA is also commonly
821   used for dimensionality reduction by selecting only some of the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix
822   to define the new basis, and several techniques exist to select the number of components, such as
823   looking at the eigenvalues of the covariance and selecting the number corresponding to the largest
824   eigenvalue drop. PCA is equivalent to the graph Fourier transform (GFT) for a graph defined by the
825   covariance Ĉ (Gama et al., 2020c; Isufi et al., 2024). Indeed, the GFT projects data on the space
826   defined by the eigenvectors of the graph shift operator S ∈ RN ×N (a matrix describing the graph
827   structure, e.g., adjacency or Laplacian), i.e., X̃ = UT X given the eigendecomposition S = UWUT .
828   If we consider the graph described by the sample covariance matrix, i.e., S = Ĉ, then PCA and GFT
829   perform the same operation.
830   The connection between PCA and VNNs emerges by looking at the action of the covariance filter in
831   the spectral domain. For a data sample x, taking the GFT of the covariance filter leads to ũ = V̂T u =

1276
1277   Consider a hard-thresholded sample covariance matrix C̄ as per Def. 2. Given the sample covariance
1278   matrix Ĉ, the following is a sufficient condition for C̄ to be positive semidefinite (PSD) (Bickel &
1279
       Levina, 2008b, Section 2): C̄ is PSD if ∥C̄ − Ĉ∥ ≤ ϵ and λmin (Ĉ) > ϵ for an ϵ > 0, where λmin (·)
1280   computes the smallest eigenvalue. That is, if the true covariance matrix is sparse, then the term
1281   ∥C̄ − Ĉ∥ decreases as the number of samples t increases and the sparse estimate is more likely to be
1282   PSD.

       H    E XTENSION OF C OVARIANCE S PARSIFICATION T ECHNIQUES TO G RAPHS

1287
       The proposed sparsification techniques can also be applied to weighted graphs. Hard and soft
       thresholding of a graph consists of removing all the edges whose absolute weight is below a given
1288
       threshold, and decreasing the weight of the others in case of soft thresholding. Stochastic sparsification
1289
       can also be performed on graphs, where each edge aij (where aij is the absolute weight of the edge
1290   between nodes i, j or 0 if the edge does not exist) is removed with a probability 1 − pij . In the case of
1291   ACV, pij = |aij |/ maxi,j |aij |. For RCV, instead, we can control the level of sparsification through a
1292   parameter p by defining an ordered set of probabilities P = {p′1 , . . . , p′N ′ }, where p′i ≤ p′i+1 , and
1293   each p′i is sampled from N (p, σ) with σ = min((1 − p)/3, p/3) (such that the number of values
1294   not in [0, 1], which we clip to the interval, is negligible) and N ′ is the number of probability values
1295   for assignment. We then set pij = p′k where k = |{alm : |alm | < |aij |; l, m = 1, . . . , N }| is the
       position of aij in the ordered ranking of absolute edge weights. This allows to increase the efficiency

1323            the brain, equally split before and after an epileptic seizure event. We use the same data

1331            and contains resting-state fMRI time series of patients with attention deficit hyperactivity
1332            disorder (ADHD) and neurotypical controls (NC). Each time series consists of 122 graph
1333            signals recorded in 200 brain locations (nodes) per patient. The available training set
1334            contains 200 patients and the test set 40 patients. We therefore consider a VNN with input
1335            feature size 122 over 200 nodes and we perform classification to predict whether the patient
1336            is ADHD or NC.

1338            the chest, right wrist and left ankle of 10 subjects performing 12 different actions. Since
1339            random data splits introduce contaminations due to overlapping windows (Tello et al., 2023),
1340            we use recordings from different subjects for train, validation and test. Specifically, we use
1341            subjects 6,10 for validation, 2,9 for test and the rest for training. We do not use the ECG
1342            measurements placed in the chest. The goal is to classify the action performed by the subject.
1343            The sampling rate is 50 Hz.
1344          • Realdisp (Banos et al., 2014b) contains data of 17 subjects performing 33 actions. We use
1345            subjects 4, 6, 10 and 11 for validation; subjects 1, 7, 8, 9, 12 and 14 for test and the remaining
1346            for training. We use the 10 most common activity labels: walking, jogging, running, cycling,
1347            elliptic bike, trunk twist (arms outstretched), rowing, knees (alternatively) bend forward,
1348            waist bends forward, trunk twist (elbows bended). We segment the data in MHEALTH and
1349            Realdisp creating sliding windows of size 128 (2.56 seconds) for measurements relative to
                the same activity with 50% overlap.
