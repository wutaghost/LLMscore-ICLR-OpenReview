009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Person search aims to simultaneously detect and re-identify a query person within
012            an entire scene, involving detection and re-identification as a multi-task problem.
013            While existing studies have made significant progress in achieving superior per-
014            formance on clean datasets, the challenge of robustness under various corruptions
015            remains largely unexplored. To address this gap, we propose two benchmarks,
016            CUHK-SYSU-C and PRW-C, designed to assess the robustness of person search
017            models across diverse corruption scenarios. Previous studies on corruption have
018
               been conducted independently for single tasks such as re-identification and detec-
               tion. However, recent advancements in person search adopt an end-to-end multi-
019
               task learning framework that processes the entire scene as input, unlike the com-
020
               bination of single tasks. This raises the question of whether independent achieve-
021            ments can ensure corruption robustness for person search. Our findings reveal
022            that merely combining independent, robust detection and re-identification mod-
023            els is not sufficient for achieving robust person search. We further investigate the
024            vulnerability of the detection and representation stages to corruption and explore
025            its impact on both foreground and background areas. Based on these insights, we
026            propose a foreground-aware augmentation and regularization method to enhance
027            the robustness of person search models. Supported by our comprehensive robust-
028            ness analysis and evaluation framework our benchmarks provide, our proposed
029
               technique substantially improves the robustness of existing person search models.
               Code will be made publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Person search is a task that involves detecting individuals in complex scenes and subsequently re-
035
      identifying the same individuals from a gallery of scene images. Significant advancements in this
036   task have been made through the discriminative capabilities of deep neural networks (DNNs). Since
037   person search requires both pedestrian detection and person re-identification simultaneously, earlier
038   studies have adopted a two-step framework (Lan et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Han et al., 2019;
039   Wang et al., 2020), where independent detection and re-identification models are applied sequen-
040   tially. More recent models have adopted a one-step approach (Li & Miao, 2021; Lee et al., 2022;
041   Cao et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022), which processes the entire scene in a single pass and leverages
042   joint end-to-end multi-task learning, leading to significant improvements.
043   Despite these advancements, DNNs have shown vulnerability to common corruptions such as noise,
044   blur, and compression artifacts, often resulting in significant performance degradation (Liu et al.,
045   2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024; He et al., 2023; Schiappa et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2021).
046   This vulnerability leads to the necessity for models to maintain robustness under such challenging
047   conditions, driving numerous studies focused on enhancing corruption robustness (Mintun et al.,
048   2021; Kar et al., 2022; Dooley et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023). This line of research has been
049   explored in the fields of detection (Michaelis et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024) and
050
      re-identification (Chen et al., 2021). However, to our knowledge, the impact of data corruption on
      person search models remains largely unexplored, highlighting the need for further investigation in
051
      this area.

122   2    R ELATED W ORKS
123
      Person Search. Existing person search works are typically categorized into two approaches: the
124   two-step and the one-step methods. Two-step approaches involve two independent detection and
125   reID models, where a detection model first detects people appearing in the scene, and then a reID
126   model recognizes the detected individuals. The primary goal of this two-step approach (Han et al.,
127   2019; Dong et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020) is to specialize these models, which were initially
128   designed for independent tasks, for the person search task by employing techniques like adapting the
129   prediction by a detector to facilitate the reID process. On the other hand, the one-step approach (Xiao
130   et al., 2017; Munjal et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; Han et al., 2021; Li & Miao,
131   2021; Yu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022; Yan et al.,
132
      2023), which performs both sub-tasks with a single model, has recently gained popularity due to its
      simplicity and efficiency, reducing the number of parameters by nearly half.
133
134   One-step approaches take the entire scene as input and jointly train the detection and reID heads in
135   an end-to-end manner. This is commonly achieved by employing a Faster R-CNN (Ren et al., 2016)
136   detection head and reID head with the OIM (Xiao et al., 2017) loss. The one-step approach focuses
137   on addressing challenges that arise from the joint learning of the two tasks. Chen et al. (2020)
138
      address conflicting learning objectives, and SeqNet (Li & Miao, 2021) and OIMNet++ (Lee et al.,
      2022) incorporate the detection quality on reID head training and search performance. PSTR (Cao
139
      et al., 2022) and COAT (Yu et al., 2022) enable recent one-step person search frameworks to leverage
140
      the advantages of transformers. Another challenge unique to person search, unlike conventional
141   reID, is the influence of background information on extracting person representations from larger
142   receptive fields. GLCNet (Qin et al., 2023) mitigates this by extracting features from the entire
143   global scene, including both the background and foreground, to improve the final person embedding.
144   While these studies have made significant advancements in the person search field, they have not
145   been sufficiently investigated for corruption robustness.
146
147
      Benchmarking Robustness to Corruption. Deep neural networks are often considered general-
      izable, but they are not as robust to corruption as humans are (Leveque et al., 2022). The pioneering
148
      study by Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) introduced a benchmarking paradigm, where synthetic
149
      corruptions are used to evaluate model robustness. This benchmark, ImageNet-C, employs algorith-
150   mically generated corruptions, revealing the unexpected vulnerability of networks to even simple
151   perturbations. Following this paradigm, robustness research has expanded into broader areas of vi-
152   sion tasks, including object detection (Michaelis et al., 2019), pose estimation (Wang et al., 2021),
153   semantic segmentation (Kamann & Rother, 2020), person re-identification (Chen et al., 2021), and
154   depth estimation (Kong et al., 2024).
155
      While significant progress has been made in improving the robustness of specific vision tasks, ex-
156   isting research primarily focuses on individual tasks (Wang et al., 2021; Kamann & Rother, 2020;
157   Kong et al., 2024). Person search, however, is a multi-task problem involving both detection and
158   re-identification, each requiring different input types and resulting in conflicting optimization (Lin
159   et al., 2021). Although previous studies (Michaelis et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021) have contributed
160   substantially to our understanding of how models for individual tasks perform under various cor-
161   ruption scenarios, the robustness of the multi-task framework in person search against corrupted
      environments has yet to be sufficiently explored.

      Benchmark Dataset Design. To evaluate the robustness of person search models, we propose two
187   benchmarks: CUHK-SYSU-C and PRW-C. These benchmarks are built upon the widely adopted
188   CUHK-SYSU (Xiao et al., 2017) and PRW (Zheng et al., 2017) datasets, which have been exten-
189   sively used in numerous person search studies (Han et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Li & Miao, 2021;
190   Kim et al., 2021). CUHK-SYSU dataset offers a diverse range of backgrounds from various ur-
191   ban scenarios, CUHK-SYSU-C retains this diversity while introducing corrupted scenes captured in
192   those environments. The PRW dataset includes images captured from six distinct viewpoints at each
193   location, and PRW-C preserves this multi-view feature, adding corruption across various angles. For
194   evaluation using our benchmarks, we utilize the same test splits from these datasets.
195   We extend the corruptions used in the pioneering study by Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) to the
196   person search task. Considering the characteristics of urban outdoor environments in person search,
197   we incorporate rain (Chen et al., 2021) and dark (Kong et al., 2024) as corruption types of our bench-
198   marks. The corruption types of our benchmark include: ‘gaussian noise’, ‘speckle noise’, ‘defocus
199   blur’, ‘glass blur’, ‘motion blur’, ’gaussian blur’, ‘snow’, ‘frost’, ‘fog’, ‘brightness’, ‘spatter’, ‘rain’,
200   ‘dark’, ‘contrast’, ‘elastic’, ‘pixelate’, ‘jpeg compression’, and ‘saturate’. Each corruption is applied
201
      at five distinct severity levels, with higher severity indicating greater image degradation (i.e., more
      severe corruption). The severity levels are established based on the traits of corruption scenarios ob-
202
      served in real-world. As an example, the rain corruption depicted in Figure 1 has various attributes
203
      including slope of rain droplets, color of rain droplets, drop length & width, overall blurriness and
204   brightness to simulate different severity. Details for all corruptions are available in Appendix.
205
206   In person search, the goal is to match a given query image to its corresponding gallery image in
207
      a database; corruption can be applied to either the query or the gallery image. Query and gallery
      images may not always exhibit the same type or severity level of corruption. We construct the bench-
208
      marks by randomly applying different corruption types and severity levels to query and gallery im-
209
      ages, similar to prior work in instance retrieval (Chen et al., 2021). We repeat the evaluation process
210   five times and report the mean performance for experimental results. For benchmark statistics and
211   further discussion of this design choice, see Appendix C and B, respectively.
212
213   Evaluation Models. To explore the corruption robustness of person search models, we employ six
214   seminal state-of-the-art models including both CNN and transformer architectures: OIMNet (Xiao
215   et al., 2017), NAE (Chen et al., 2020), OIMNet++ (Lee et al., 2022), SeqNet (Li & Miao, 2021),
      COAT (Yu et al., 2022), and PSTR (Cao et al., 2022). More details of these models are provided

      3.2   S ENSITIVITY TO C ORRUPTION IN D ETECTION AND R EPRESENTATION S TAGES
289
290   The experiments in the previous section demonstrate that existing person search models are vulner-
291   able to corruption. In a typical person search framework, the detection head first identifies person
292   candidates, and then the reID head extracts representations from the detected regions. Therefore,
293   we assess the individual sensitivity of both the detection and representation stages to corruption.
294
      To evaluate detection sensitivity to corruption, we first apply the detection head to corrupted images
295
      to obtain predicted bounding boxes. We then extract features from the corresponding regions in
296   clean images using these box coordinates and perform the search process. Conversely, to evaluate
297   the representation sensitivity to corruption, we perform detection on clean images to obtain predicted
298   boxes, then extract features from the corresponding regions in corrupted images, followed by the
299   search process. We evaluate the model across five severity levels, providing a separate benchmark
300   for each to observe how the model responds to increasing corruption severity.
301
      The results of these individual sensitivity studies are in Table 2. The ‘Oracle’ row represents per-
302   formance using ground truth bounding boxes, while the ‘Clean’ row refers to the results where both
303   detection and search are performed on clean images. The five remaining rows (Level 1 through Level
304   5) show the impact of increasing the severity level of corruption. The results reveal that performance
305   degrades more significantly when person representations are extracted from corrupted images. At
306   level 5 of ‘Detection against Corruption’, although detection performance declines, search perfor-
307   mance remains relatively stable, with 70% of R@1, indicating that the model retains the search
308   capability despite noisy detection results. However, when person representations are corrupted (i.e.,
309   ‘Representation against Corruption’), search performance drops significantly, even with relatively
310
      strong detection performance. These findings reveal that both detection and representation stages are
      sensitive to corruption, further highlighting that the representation stage is particularly vulnerable.

      to the foreground – directly impacting the person’s appearance – there is a notable drop in both

      4.1   F OREGROUND - AWARE AUGMENTATION
347
348   Data augmentation has proven to be a valuable approach for boosting model robustness in computer
349   vision (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Rebuffi et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024b). However, as shown
350   in Figure 5 (b), applying data augmentation to the entire scene results in severe semantic corruption
351   and unreliable bounding boxes (e.g., overlapping pedestrians). Our analysis highlights that search
352   performance remains largely unaffected when corruption is restricted to the background. To this end,
353   we introduce a foreground-aware augmentation to generate appropriate augmented counterparts for
354   given input images. We define the areas containing people as foreground and determine these using
355
      ground truth bounding boxes during training. To minimize the role of ground truth bounding boxes
      in generating shortcuts during training, we apply a translation to the box coordinates of a person
356
      before applying augmentation to the corresponding region.
357
358   In our training process, we incorporate both clean scenes and their augmented counterparts. Let the

      6   C ONCLUSION
528
      In this work, we present two benchmarks for evaluating the robustness of person search, CUHK-
529
      SYSU-C and PRW-C, to assess and analyze the robustness of person search models. We explore
530
      how various features of person search influence robustness with the expectation where our findings
531   will be valuable lessons to the research community in related fields. From the findings obtained
532   through our experiments, we propose a solution for robust person search that not only achieves
533   competitive performance on clean datasets, but also demonstrates effective robustness enhancement
534   in corrupted environments.
535   Potential Limitations. While our method performs well in simulated environments, we have not
536   extensively tested its performance under real-world corruption scenarios. To mitigate this gap, we
537   collect and evaluate our method on real corruption images, which shows the validity of our proposed
538   approach in the real world. We have not considered scenarios with multiple simultaneous corrup-
539   tions. Additionally, while we categorize corruption severity into five levels, this discretization may
      not fully capture the continuous nature of real-world corruption.

      7   S OCIAL I MPACT
546
      The development and deployment of person search systems require careful consideration due to their
547
      potential impact on individual privacy and societal norms. While these systems offer substantial ben-
548
      efits for security and surveillance by enhancing the ability to locate and identify individuals across
549   diverse environments, they simultaneously pose a profound risk to personal privacy. The ability to
550   track and identify individuals without their consent can lead to a range of privacy violations, from
551   the unwarranted monitoring of public movements to the potential for misuse in stalking and harass-
552   ment. It is, therefore, imperative that developers, implementers, and policymakers involved in the
553   creation and use of person search systems consider these ethical implications from the outset. The
554   development of these systems should be guided by ethical principles that prioritize the well-being
555   and privacy of individuals, incorporating mechanisms for accountability and oversight to prevent
556   misuse. It is our collective responsibility to balance innovation with the imperative to safeguard
557
      human dignity and privacy.
558   By doing the above, we can harness the benefits of person search systems while mitigating the risks,
559   ensuring these technologies enhance societal welfare without compromising individual freedoms.
560   For instance, person search technology can expedite victim identification and rescue efforts in natu-
561   ral disasters or accidents, improving the effectiveness of emergency responses and potentially saving
562   lives. Disaster environments often involve challenges like heavy rainfall or snowfall, while accident
563
      scenes may present issues such as varying lighting conditions or spatter-covered camera lenses.
      Our study aims to optimize person search techniques to function effectively under these varied and
564
      adverse conditions.

      In this section, we describe person search models that we examine in Section 3.1. These seminal
838
      state-of-the-art works have contributed to various aspects of the person search field. OIMNet (Xiao
839   et al., 2017) firstly proposes an end-to-end person search framework by jointly training the detection
840   and reID head. NAE (Chen et al., 2020) addresses the issue of conflicting learning objectives, a
841   common challenge in person search and related fields (Xu et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2021b; Lin
842   et al., 2021), that arise during the joint learning of the detector and reID head. SeqNet (Li & Miao,
843   2021) improves the quality of detection results through a stronger detection head by considering
844   that the detection result influences the training of the reID head. This simple yet effective concept
845   has prompted subsequent studies to adopt its design (Li et al., 2022; Jaffe & Zakhor, 2023; Li
846   et al., 2023). OIMNet++ (Lee et al., 2022) improves the widely used OIM (Xiao et al., 2017) loss
847
      and considers the quality of detection results in the training of the reID head. PSTR (Cao et al.,
      2022) and COAT (Yu et al., 2022) enable recent one-step person search frameworks to leverage the
848
      advantages of the Transformer (Vaswani, 2017).

1034   C.3   C ORRUPTION I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1035
1036   In creating the benchmarks for the person search, we consider the typical attributes of scenes used
1037
       in person search, where both prominent subjects and multiple small background figures coexist in
       the same scene. We conduct the data quality check to ensure that the persons in the images are
1038
       still detectable and re-identifiable by humans, even after corruption is applied. The implementation
1039
       details of the corruption in CUHK-SYSU-C and PRW-C are as follows:
1040
1041
       Snow. We use the method proposed in Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) to implement the snow
1042
       corruption. To represent the diverse features of the snowy scene in the real world, we express
1043   various attributes of snowy scenes and adjust their intensity for different severity levels. Flake Size:
1044   Determines the average size or thickness of the snowflakes. As this value increases, the size of the
1045   snowflakes increases. We set the parameters (0.1, 0.2, 0.55, 0.55, 0.55) to adjust its intensity. Size
1046   Variation: Represents the standard deviation of the size distribution of snowflakes. A larger value
1047   results in greater variation in the sizes of snowflakes. We set 0.3 as a parameter for the intensity.
1048   Snowfall Intensity: Indicates the degree of snowfall intensity applied to the image. Higher values
1049   simulate heavier snowfall. We set the parameters (3, 2, 4, 4.5, 2.5) to adjust its intensity. Snow
1050   Coverage Threshold: Sets the minimum value for snow generation. Snow will not be generated
1051
       below this threshold, simulating areas with less snow coverage. We set the parameters (10, 12, 12,
       12, 12) to adjust its intensity. Wind Effect: Determines the radius of motion blur. This simulates
1052
       the direction and speed of falling snow, affected by wind. We set the parameters (0.5, 0.5, 0.9, 0.85,
1053
       0.85) to adjust its intensity. Blurriness: Determines the intensity of motion blur. Higher values
1054   make the snowflakes appear more blurred, simulating faster-falling snow or stronger wind. We set
1055   the parameters (4, 4, 8, 8, 12) to adjust its intensity. Snow Opacity: Determines the mixing ratio
1056   between the original image and the snow effect layer. Values closer to 1 show more of the original
1057   image, while values closer to 0 intensify the snow effect, simulating denser snowfall. We set the
1058   parameters (0.8, 0.7, 0.7, 0.65, 0.65) to adjust its intensity.
1059
1060   Frost. We use the method proposed in Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) to implement the frost
1061   corruption. This simulates the effect of frost or ice forming on the surface of the image, giving the
1062   appearance of a cold and frosty environment. Frost Intensity: This determines the strength of the
1063   frost effect applied to the image. Higher values result in more pronounced frost, simulating thick ice
1064   or frost on the surface. We set the parameters (1, 0.8, 0.7, 0.65, 0.6) to adjust its intensity. Blending
1065
       Ratio: Controls how much the frost image is blended with the original image. A lower value results
       in more frost coverage, while a higher value reveals more of the original image beneath the frost
1066
       layer. We set the parameters (0.4, 0.6, 0.7, 0.7, 0.75) to adjust the blending ratio.
1067
1068
       Fog. We use the method proposed in Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) to implement the fog cor-
1069
       ruption. This simulates the effect of fog or mist, reducing visibility and softening the details in the
1070
       image. Fog Density: This determines the density of the fog applied to the image. Higher values
1071   result in denser fog, which obscures more of the image. We set the parameters (1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 2.5,
1072   3.0) to adjust the density. Fog Smoothness: Controls the rate of decay for the fractal noise used
1073   to generate the fog. Lower values create fog with sharper, more defined transitions, while higher
1074   values produce smoother fog with more gradual transitions. We set the parameters (2, 2, 1.7, 1.5,
1075   1.4) to adjust its smoothness.
1076
1077   Rain. We use the method proposed in Saxena (2023) to implement the rain corruption. To simu-
1078   late the rain effect on images, we introduce various attributes related to rain and adjust their intensity
1079   at different severity levels. Slope of Rain Droplets: Determines the inclination of rain droplets. This
       simulates how much the rain is tilted by the wind. At lower intensities, droplets are lighter and more


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       easily tilted by wind, forming steeper slopes. At higher intensities, droplets are heavier, causing rain
1081   to fall more vertically and be less affected by wind. We randomly select the slope from within the
1082   range (-20, 20) to adjust its intensity. Color of Rain Droplets: Determines how the rain droplets
1083   reflect light. As the severity increases, the color of the droplets changes from light gray to dark gray,
1084   simulating the visual effect of increasing rain density. This change reflects the reduction of light
1085   reflection and transmission through the air due to heavier rain. The default color is set to light gray
1086   (200, 200, 200), while for heavy rain, it is defined as medium gray (150, 150, 150), and for torrential
1087   rain, it is represented as dark gray (80, 80, 80). Blur Value: Indicates the degree of blur effect ap-
