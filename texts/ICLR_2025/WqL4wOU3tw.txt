                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               In this work, we build a simple but strong baseline for sounding video generation.
               Given base diffusion models for audio and video, we integrate them with addi-
016
               tional modules into a single model and train it to make the model jointly generate
017
               audio and video. To enhance alignment between audio-video pairs, we introduce
018            two novel mechanisms in our model. The first one is timestep adjustment, which
019            provides different timestep information to each base model. It is designed to align
020            how samples are generated along with timesteps across modalities. The second
021            one is a new design of the additional modules, termed Cross-Modal Condition-
022            ing as Positional Encoding (CMC-PE). In CMC-PE, cross-modal information is
023            embedded as if it represents temporal position information, and the embeddings
024            are fed into the model like positional encoding. Compared with the popular cross-
025            attention mechanism, CMC-PE provides a better inductive bias for temporal align-
026
               ment in the generated data. Experimental results validate the effectiveness of the
               two newly introduced mechanisms and also demonstrate that our method outper-
027
               forms existing methods. The source code will be released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Diffusion models have made great strides in the last few years in various generation tasks across
      modalities including image, video, and audio (Yang et al., 2023). Although these models are often
034
      large-scale and require a huge amount of computational resources for training, several prior studies
035
      such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) have made their trained models publicly available,
036   which substantially accelerates the progress of research and development on generative models.
037   However, these models have mainly focused on a single modality, and it is still challenging to
038   construct a model that is capable of generating multi-modal data.
039
      In this work, we focus on audio-video joint generation, which is also known as sounding video
040
      generation (Liu et al., 2023b). Although sounding videos are one of the most popular types of multi-
041
      modal data, their generation has been addressed by only a few recent studies (Liu et al., 2023b; Ruan
042   et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023) due to the extremely high difficulty of handling heterogeneous and
043   high-dimensional data for generative modelling. This challenge makes the training of multi-modal
044   generative models much more expensive than that of single-modal models, and it creates a barrier to
045   the research and development of sounding-video generation technologies.
046
      In this paper, we present a simple baseline method for sounding video generation. We utilize the
047
      latest generative models in both the audio and video domains, and our method effectively integrates
048   these models for audio-video joint generation. Specifically, we basically train only additional mod-
049   ules introduced during model combination, which reduces the cost for training. To enhance align-
050   ment within a generated pair of audio and video, we introduce two novel mechanisms: timestep
051   alignment and Cross-Modal Conditioning as Positional Encoding (CMC-PE). Experimental results
052   with several datasets validate the effectiveness of these mechanisms and also demonstrate that the
053   proposed method performs on par with or better than existing methods in sounding video generation
      in terms of video quality, audio quality, and cross-modal alignment.

095   2.1.2    A PPLICATION TO SINGLE - MODAL GENERATION
096
097   Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable performance across various modalities, particu-
098   larly in vision and audio domains. In the vision domain, the initial attempt was limited to generating
099
      low-resolution images (Ho et al., 2020), but it was soon extended to handle high-resolution im-
      ages (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) and videos (Ho et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023;
100
      Blattmann et al., 2023). To reduce the computational cost due to the high dimensionality of data,
101
      the latest diffusion models are often trained in the space of latent features (Rombach et al., 2022)
102   obtained by an encoder such as VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014) or VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021).
103   A similar trend can be found in the audio domain: diffusion models were initially used to directly
104   generate waveforms (Chen et al., 2020b; Kong et al., 2020) and were then extended to generate
105   compressed representation or latent features of audio signals (Liu et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023).
106   In this work, we utilize the latest publicly available models in both domains, specifically, Animate-
107   Diff (Guo et al., 2023) and AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023a), to efficiently construct an audio-visual
      generative model that is capable of jointly generating video and audio well aligned with each other.

      2.2     AUDIO - VIDEO GENERATIVE MODELS
109
110   2.2.1    C ROSS - MODAL CONDITIONAL GENERATION
111
112   Video-conditioned audio generation (V2A) has been extensively explored in the literature of audio-
113
      visual generative models. Pioneering works adopted regression models (Owens et al., 2016; Chen
      et al., 2020c; Ghose & Prevost, 2020) and GANs (Hao et al., 2018; Ghose & Prevost, 2022), but
114
      auto-regressive models (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021; Du et al., 2023) and diffusion models (Luo et al.,
115
      2023; Mo et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Comunità et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) have become
116   popular choices recently due to their scalability and capability of generating diverse data. To apply
117   these models for V2A, we additionally need a mechanism to feed video conditional information into
118   audio generation models. Such cross-modal conditioning has typically been achieved by a cross-
119   attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), where the conditional information is used to compute
120   keys and values in the attention process. In this work, we propose a new module for the cross-modal
121   conditioning that is simple but effective for obtaining higher alignment between modalities.
122   Compared with V2A, audio-conditioned video generation (A2V) has not been as intensely addressed
123   in the literature, as high-quality video generation itself is already challenging. Given the success of
124   large-scale autoregressive models (Weissenborn et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2021) and diffusion mod-
125   els (Ho et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023) in video generation, audio-to-video
126   generation has also been addressed by extending these models to accept audio conditions (Ge et al.,
127   2022; Yariv et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). In this paper, we also extend existing diffusion models
128   of video generation, but our goal is to enable joint generation of audio and video, which is substan-
129   tially more challenging than audio-to-video. For this purpose, we propose a new mechanism to
130
      adjust timesteps across modalities during the generation process. It is particularly required for joint
      generation to effectively handle noisy multi-modal data at each timestep for higher alignment, be-
131
      cause any clean data is not accessible during the generation process differently from the situation of
132
      V2A or A2V.
133
134
      2.2.2    AUDIO - VIDEO JOINT GENERATION
135
136   As mentioned above, audio-video joint generation, namely, sounding video generation, is challeng-
137   ing compared to single-modal generation, and few studies have tackled it (Liu et al., 2023b; Ruan
138   et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023). SVG-VQGAN (Liu et al., 2023b) adopts a novel tokenizer for audio
139   and video to obtain suitable representation for multi-modal generation with auto-regressive models.
140   MM-Diffusion (Ruan et al., 2023) and TAVDiffusion (Mao et al., 2024) are multi-modal diffusion
141
      models specifically designed for audio-video paired data. CoDi (Tang et al., 2023) integrates several
      single-modal dedicated diffusion models and additionally adopts environment encoders to extract
142
      modality-specific features to condition the generation process in the other modalities. All these
143
      models incur a large computational cost for training due to their new model architectures special-
144   ized for the joint generation. In this work, we aim to construct sounding video generation models
145   with minimal effort by effectively transferring state-of-the-art models in both the audio and video
146   domains. A very recent work by Xing et al. (2024) shares a similar motivation to ours, but it adopts
147   guidance based approach, which heavily limits the capability of the model to generate temporally
148   aligned samples. In contrast, we introduce an efficient adaptation method that significantly enhances
149   temporal alignment across modalities.

197   3.2     T IMESTEP ADJUSTMENT
198
199   3.2.1    W HY DO WE NEED TO ADJUST TIMESTEPS ACROSS MODALITIES ?
200
201
      The necessity of the timestep adjustment stems from a discrepancy in the noise schedules between
      modalities. As described in Eq. (1), the timestep information is closely related to the noise schedule
202
      {βt }, and this schedule is pre-determined at each modality in our setting. Therefore, how samples
203
      are collapsed as the timestep progresses (or equivalently, how samples are generated as the timestep
204   reverts) is not necessarily aligned between modalities.
205
206   To visualize this discrepancy, we plot the loss distribution over the timestep in Fig. 2a. The loss
207
      values are measured in the experiment (described in Section 4.1) and are normalized by their value
      at t = 0 for each modality. Here, we choose the loss instead of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) as a
208
      proxy to observe how samples are generated, as SNR is not suitable for comparisons between data
209
      with a different number of dimensions (Hoogeboom et al., 2023). Obviously, the loss on video data
210   is heavily skewed towards t = 0, which implies that the noise schedule for videos is set to more
211   rapidly collapse data into noise as the timestep progresses. Such a noise schedule is often adopted
212   to address the high dimensionality of video data (Hoogeboom et al., 2023). However, if we directly
213   re-use this schedule in the joint generation setting, video information will not be very informative
214   for audio generation at the intermediate timesteps, which makes the generation process more like
215   audio-to-video than joint generation. To solve this problem, we need to adjust the timesteps across
      modalities.

233
234   We adopt a global timestep t and local timesteps, denoted by m(t) and n(t), for video and audio
235   modality, respectively. The global timestep is set to control the noise level of all modalities and is
236   evenly sampled in the generation process as usual timesteps. On the other hand, the local timesteps
237   are set to adjust the noise level of each modality for higher alignment. We introduce a simple strategy
238   to set the local timesteps, as follows:
239                                           √γ !                                       √1γ !

      where γ is a hyperparameter for the timestep adjustment, and Tv and Ta are the maximum timestep
      in the base video and audio models, respectively. This definition is designed to make m(t)/Tv
244

245
      adjusted to be much smaller than that in audio generation. This leads to reducing the gap mentioned

247   the original schedule (as we will show in the experiments). When γ is set to one, both the local
248   timesteps are set to be equal to the global timestep t, so nothing is adjusted in this setting.
249

250
      axis represents the global timestep, and the vertical axis represents the normalized loss at each local
251
      timestep corresponding to the global one. Compared with Fig. 2a, the loss distributions become
252   much more similar to each other. This indicates that how samples are generated along with the
253   timestep becomes more aligned between video and audio. Consequently, through the joint genera-
254   tion, we can expect higher alignment between the generated pair of data. In this paper, we set γ to
255   1.5, unless otherwise noted. How to automatically set this hyperparameter remains as future work.

258
259   3.3.1    T HE STANDARD CHOICE : CROSS - ATTENTION AND ITS LIMITATION
260
261
      In the literature, the cross-attention mechanism has been extensively used for cross-modal condi-
      tioning in diffusion models. Figure 3a shows the simplest design in this approach adopted in our
262
      baseline method (Tang et al., 2023). For brevity, we discuss the case of audio-conditioned video
263
      generation, but it can also be applied to the case of video-conditioned audio generation. In this de-
264   sign, the conditional audio information is embedded into a single feature vector by an encoder, and
265   it is used to compute keys and values in the cross-attention taken with the intermediate features in
266   the video generation model. By training the encoder and the cross-attention block with audio-video
267   paired data, we can make the model generate videos aligned with given audio information. Although
268   this design is simple and widely applicable, it is quite challenging to achieve higher temporal con-
269   sistency between the audio condition and generated video, since the single vector does not have
      sufficient capability to represent every piece of temporally local information in the audio condition.

      3.3.2         C ROSS -M ODAL C ONDITIONING AS P OSITIONAL E NCODING (CMC-PE)
298
299
      To achieve higher temporal alignment, we propose Cross-Modal Conditioning as Positional Encod-
300   ing (CMC-PE), a simple but effective method of cross-modal conditioning. Figure 3b depicts how
301   CMC-PE works. First, the conditional audio is encoded to a sequence of feature vectors along with
302   time frames that work as if representing temporal position information. The extracted features are
303   then added to the intermediate features in the video U-Net to function as positional embedding. To
304   make this addition process valid, the features are interpolated and broadcast in advance to match
305   their shape with that of the video features. Finally, the updated features are processed with a self-
306   attention block. The features used for CMC-PE are extracted from current noisy latents xt by the
307   connector. We adopt the self-conditioning technique here (Chen et al., 2022), where the estimated
308
      data x̂0|t at each timestep shown in Eq. (6) is concatenated to the input.
309   CMC-PE has several advantages as follows. First, as the audio information is embedded into a
310   sequence of vectors arranged in the time-frame direction, CMC-PE can utilize temporally local in-
311   formation that is suitable to temporally align the generated video with the conditional audio. Second,
312   it has a strong inductive bias for higher temporal alignment, as the extracted temporally-local audio
313   information is explicitly tied to the corresponding temporally local video information. Lastly, it is
314
      widely applicable to existing model architectures and conditional generation tasks. Once a target
      axis or axes of the intermediate features for desired alignment across modalities are given, CMC-PE
315
      can be extended in a straightforward manner.

      4.1     E XPERIMENTS WITH A DEDICATED DATASET FOR EVALUATING TEMPORAL ALIGNMENT
352
353   4.1.1     DATASET AND EVALUATION METRICS
354
355   We extended the GreatestHits dataset (Owens et al., 2016) for our experiments. It contains 977
356   videos of humans hitting various objects with a drumstick in the scene. As the hitting sound and
357   motion are dominant in the video, this dataset is suitable for evaluating the temporal alignment
358
      between the generated video and audio. We created video captions using LLaVA-NeXT (Zhang
      et al., 2024b) and utilized them as text conditions in our method. The details of this process are
359
      described in the appendix A.3.
360
361   We evaluated the quality of the generated data from three perspectives: video quality, audio quality,
362   and temporal alignment. For the former two, we used FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2018) and FAD (Kil-
363   gour et al., 2019), both of which are widely utilized in the literature. To measure how much the
364
      generated video and audio are aligned with each other in term of temporal dynamics, we used the
      AV-Align score proposed by Yariv et al. (2023). This score is defined as Intersection-over-Union
365
      between onsets detected from the audio and peaks obtained from the optical flow. It is especially
366
      useful to measure the temporal alignment in the GreatestHits dataset, as hitting sounds make clear
367   onsets, and hitting motions have correlating and distinct peaks in the optical flow.
368
369   We slightly modified how to compute AV-Align score from the official implementation. Specifi-
370
      cally, we tuned hyper-parameters of the optical flow estimation and those of the onset detection to
      accurately estimate hitting timing using annotated timestamps in the Greatest Hits dataset. In addi-
371
      tion, we compute IoU after rewriting it with precision and recall to mitigate an issue caused by the
372
      difference of temporal resolution between video and audio. Details are provided in the appendix A.4.
373

377   per second, and the size of each frame is 256 × 256. The sampling rate of the audio is 16 kHz. We

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we have built a simple but strong baseline method for sounding video generation. For
532   efficient training, we only add small modules to a pair of existing audio and video diffusion models
533   and train them with audio-video paired data for joint generation. In our method, we introduced two
534   novel mechanisms, timestep adjustment and CMC-PE, to boost cross-modal alignment of the gener-
535   ated data. The timestep adjustment provides a modality-wise timestep schedule to align the speed at
536
      which samples are generated along with the timesteps at each modality. CMC-PE provides a better
      way to feed each modal feature into another-modal diffusion model in terms of inductive bias for
537
      higher temporal alignment compared with a popular cross-attention mechanism. The experimental
538
      results demonstrated that our method achieves high cross-modal alignment as well as high quality
539   of the generated video and audio.
