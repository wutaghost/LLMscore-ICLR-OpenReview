009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We focus on improving the visual understanding capability for boosting the vision-
012            language models. We propose Arcana, a multiModal language model, which in-
013            troduces two crucial techniques. First, we present Multimodal LoRA (MM-LoRA),
014            a module designed to enhance the decoder. Unlike traditional language-driven
015            decoders, MM-LoRA consists of two parallel LoRAs – one for vision and one for
016            language – each with its own parameters. This disentangled parameters design
017
               allows for more specialized learning in each modality and better integration of mul-
               timodal information. Second, we introduce the Query Ladder adapter (QLadder)
018
               to improve the visual encoder. QLadder employs a learnable “ladder” structure
019
               to deeply aggregates the intermediate representations from the frozen pretrained
020            visual encoder (e.g., CLIP image encoder). This enables the model to learn new
021            and informative visual features, as well as remaining the powerful capabilities of
022            the pretrained visual encoder. These techniques collectively enhance Arcana’s
023            visual perception power, enabling it to leverage improved visual information for
024            more accurate and contextually relevant outputs across various multimodal scenar-
025            ios. Extensive experiments and ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness and
026            generalization capability of our Arcana.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) Wang et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023);
031   Liu et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2023a) have made significant advancements. These models amalgamate
032   image representations into large language models (LLMs) through an adaptor Touvron et al. (2023a);
033   Zheng et al. (2024). Various methods Dai et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023); Dong
034   et al. (2024) leverage the capabilities of the powerful LLM to excel in various multimodal tasks.
035
      While existing MLLMs showcase remarkable proficiency in multimodal tasks, they still face chal-
036   lenges in visual perception that is crucial for further tasks, such as reasoning or creation Chen et al.
037   (2023a); Liu et al. (2023a). Fig. 1 (a) presents several examples that clearly highlight this issue. We
038   observe deficiencies in current MLLMs regarding low-level visual perception, such as color and
039   quantity, as well as high-level visual perception, such as small object detection and localization.
040   Consequently, there is a pressing necessity to bolster the comprehension capabilities of existing
041   MLLMs, specially for vision.
042   The insufficient visual perception capabilities of MLLMs can mainly be attributed to two factors:
043   decoder and visual encoder. As depicted in Fig. 1(b), existing language driven decoder structures
044   directly couple visual and language modalities. Such design not only disregards their unique char-
045   acteristics but also may lead to information confusion, thus impairing the accurate understanding
046   and processing of visual information. On the other hand, freezing visual encoder directly limits the
047   ability to learn and represent visual information. Therefore, improving the visual perception requires
048   rethinking the decoder design and optimizing the use of the visual encoder to better capture and
049   process visual features.
050   As shown in Fig. 1(c), previous multimodal large language models (MLLMs) typically relied on
051   CLIP as the visual encoder. However, research Tong et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024) has revealed
052   limitations in CLIP’s ability to capture complex visual features. To address this, recent methods
053   have incorporated self-supervised learning (SSL) pretrained models, such as DINOv2 Oquab et al.
      (2024), and fused their outputs with CLIP’s features to enhance the visual encoder’s representation

098   on traditional multimodal benchmarks, such as MMbench Liu et al. (2023b) and TextVQA Singh

102   the robustness of MM-LoRA and QLadder across different benchmarks, its ability to generalize in
103   diverse scenarios, and its performance in comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

      sequence length of visual features. Similar designs are used by mPLUG-OWL2 Ye et al. (2023a), and
134   MiniGPT-4 Zhu et al. (2023). However, these methods Anil et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023); Chen et al.
135   (2023a) couple visual and language modalities in the same space using language-guided decoders,
136   overlooking the uniqueness of different modalities. This oversight may result in interference between
137   modalities, potentially affecting performance. To this end, we employ MM-LoRA to implement a
138   multimodal decoder, aiming to mitigate the impact of modality interference on the model.
139
      Improve visual perception for MLLMs. Currently, MLLMs are the most popular approach in VL
140   community Alayrac et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022), and enhancing their visual recognition capabilities
141   has become a prominent research trend. Integrating visual features into large language models
142   (LLMs) via a linear layer has become the mainstream approach Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al.
143   (2023). However, this approach often relies on frozen vision encoders to provide visual features,
144   which limits the visual recognition capabilities of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). To
145   address this issue, existing methods enhance visual recognition in two ways. The first method Luo
146   et al. (2024); Tong et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024) introduces new high-resolution vision encoders,
147   significantly improving visual recognition by increasing the number of visual tokens. For example,
148
      LLaVA-HR Luo et al. (2024) achieves this by incorporating ConvNeXt Liu et al. (2022) to handle
      high-resolution images. However, these methods significantly increases the number of visual tokens.
149
      Therefore, we propose QLadder, which can significantly enhance the model’s visual perception
150
      capability with the introduction of a small number of visual tokens. The second method Wang et al.
151   (2023); Dong et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2023a) expands the learning space for visual tokens within the
152   large language model to accelerate visual-language alignment, thereby enhancing visual perception.
153   For instance, Internlm-Xcomposer2 Dong et al. (2024) introduces Partial-LoRA, adding a LoRA to
154   visual tokens to strengthen their representation. However, experiments with MM-LoRA have shown
155   that directly increasing the learning space for visual tokens in the decoder does not improve the
156   model’s performance.

188   3.2   A RCHITECTURE
189
190   Our approach Arcana (illustrated in Fig. 2(a) consists of three main components: visual encoder,
191   vision-language adapter and large language model. Each component is described in the following.
192   Visual Encoder. The primary objective is to extract visual features from the image. The encoder
193   learned with language-supervision, e.g., the CLIP Radford et al. (2021) visual model, is widely
194   adopted. The CLIP encoder is often fixed during fine-tuning, e.g., in LLaVA, for keeping the
195   representation capability of the original CLIP encoder. We propose to improve the visual encoder
196   through a query ladder adaptor (QLadder) from the fine-tuning data that may contain new visual
197   semantics. The structure is shown in Fig. 3(b). This adapter enhances the visual feature representation
198
      of the visual encoder by adding a small number of query visual tokens while retaining the pretrained
      image encoder. It improves Arcana’s visual perception capability.
199
200   We extract visual features Fc ∈ RNI ×Cv through the CLIP encoder, where Cv represents the channel
201   of visual feature, NI indicates the number of image patch. A set of learnable vectors xq is fed into
202   QLadder to acquire additional visual features Fq ∈ RNq ×Cv , where Nq << NI . The two kinds of
203   visual features are concatenated: Fv = concat(Fc , Fq ). As shown in Fig 2(b), QLadder comprises
204   multiple layers composed of cross-attention and feed-forward networks (FFNs).
205   Vision-Language Adapter. We map the output of visual encoder to the same space as the language
206   embedding space through an Vision-Language adapter. The adapter g consists of two MLP layers.
207   The output visual features are denoted as FI = g(Fv ).
208
      Large Language Model. For multimodal tasks Goyal et al. (2017); Hudson & Manning (2019),
209
      leveraging pre-trained large language models (LLMs) can provide valuable linguistic priors. Through
210   multimodal instruction tuning, LLMs learn to comprehend visual features within images, enabling
211   comprehensive understanding and processing of multimodal data. Typically, this process is accom-
212   plished through full fine-tuning or LoRA Hu et al. (2021). However, these methods overlook the
213   unique characteristics of modalities, leading to modality confusion. This not only damages MLLMs’
214   accurate understanding and processing of visual information but also affects natural language under-
215   standing. Therefore, a multimodal decoder that provides separate learning spaces for each modality
      is a better choice for MLLMs.

      3.3   M ULTIMODAL L O RA
217
218   To implement a multimodal decoder based on a large language model, we propose a multimodal
219   LoRA. This approach projects visual and language features into separate semantic spaces to decouple
220   their representations, thereby avoiding modality interference. This allows Arcana to retain the unique
221   characteristics of each modality, enhancing its visual perception without compromising natural
222   language understanding. Next, we detail the MM-LoRA process.
223   MM-LoRA, as illustrated in Fig. 3, consists of visual LoRA and language LoRA. In comparison
224   to LoRA, we introduces two parameters, β and γ, to control the rank size of (R) visual LoRA and
225   language LoRA. It’s worth noting that β+γ = 1 to ensure that no additional parameters are introduced
226   compared to LoRA. Specifically, given a sequence of visual-language features F ∈ R(Nv +Nt )×C and
227   a multimodal mask M ∈ {0, 1}(Nv +Nt ) , where C represents the hidden dimension in LLMs, Nv and
228   Nt indicates the number of visual and language tokens, respectively. We define a modality separation
229   function Θ to separate the tokens of different modalities within F .

248   3.4   T RAINING PARADIGM
249
250   Following prior work Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023), we adopt a two-stage approach involving
251   pretraining and multimodal instruction fine-tuning to train Arcana, as illustrated in Fig. 2(b). The
252
      purpose of the pretraining stage is to align the visual encoder with the language model, while
      multimodal instruction fine-tuning aims to adapt the model better to specific tasks through fine-tuning.
253
      We found that freezing the visual encoder limits the MLLM’s ability to capture complex visual
254
      information, such as scene text and visual knowledge. To address this issue, we introduce Qladder
255   and enable it to be trained in both the pretraining and instruction fine-tuning stages. This strategy
256   allows the model to more effectively capture both low-level and high-level semantic visual information.
257   Additionally, we introduce MM-LoRA fine-tuning as an alternative to full fine-tuning and LoRA
258   fine-tuning, enabling a multimodal decoder that minimizes modality interference. Specifically, in
259   the pretraining stage, we train Qladder and the vision-language adapter, while in the instruction
260   fine-tuning stage, we train Qladder, the vision-language adapter, and MM-LoRA. Furthermore, to
261   ensure the linguistic capabilities of Arcana, we employ joint training, adjusting the entire model
262   during instruction fine-tuning, integrating textual and multimodal instructions.

464   the integration of DINOv2 significantly increased the model’s training time. In contrast, Q-Ladder
465
      enhances both visual grounding and visual understanding through adaptive learning of distinguishing
      features. This dual improvement allows Q-Ladder to maintain or boost performance across a wide
466
      range of benchmarks, even when using a smaller dataset (over 2 million samples from Arcana).

      This is why Q-Ladder continues to achieve performance gains across various benchmarks, including

525   5        C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we introduce a new multimodal large language model, Arcana, which incorporates two
528   novel techniques. Unlike current mainstream methods, Arcana employs MM-LoRA for a multimodal
529   decoder, enabling more efficient information processing and integration across different modalities.
530   MM-LoRA effectively combines data from various modalities without significantly increasing compu-
531
      tational complexity, reducing information interference between modalities. Secondly, we present the
      QLadder structure, which demonstrates for the first time that with limited multimodal training data,
532
      retaining the capabilities of a pre-trained model and adding a small number of visual encoders can still
533
      enhance the performance of multimodal language models. This hierarchical structure progressively
534   refines and enhances the expression of visual information, resulting in improved adaptability and gen-
535   eralization in multimodal tasks. With these two key techniques, Arcana not only excels in multimodal
536   tasks but also shows potential for performance improvement even in data-constrained environments.
537   Additionally, the severe lack of visual information in the image captions of open-source data limits
538   the visual perception capabilities of multimodal large language models. To address this, we designed
539   a data engine that uses diverse visual annotation models and large language models to generate
      captions rich in visual information.
