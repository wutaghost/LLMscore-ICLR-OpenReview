009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge graphs accumulate information about more and more entities of the
012            world. Much research is conducted to improve embedding models that capture
013            this information and give useful node features in many downstream applications.
014            However, most current methods are hard to scale to large knowledge graphs,
015            partly because GPU memory is too small to hold the embeddings of many en-
016
               tities –YAGO4 has 67M entities. To scale existing embedding models on modest
               hardware, we introduce SEPAL: Scalable Embedding Propagation Algorithm for
017
               Large knowledge graphs. The key idea of SEPAL to reduce compute is to only
018
               optimize embeddings on a core subset of entities, those that come with much more
019            information than others. Then SEPAL propagates these embeddings to the rest of
020            the graph with message passing, but no explicit optimization. To enable efficient
021            message passing, we break down large graphs into well-connected subgraphs that
022            fit in GPU memory using a new algorithm called BLOCS: Balanced Local Over-
023            lapping Connected Subgraphs. We evaluate SEPAL on five different knowledge
024            graphs for four downstream regression tasks. We show that SEPAL outperforms
025            alternative on downstream tasks, while providing a 43× speedup to its base em-
026            bedding algorithm. Moreover, outside the core subgraph, embeddings obtained by
027
               message passing are not degraded compared to traditional methods, demonstrating
               the validity of SEPAL’s propagation.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Relational data gathers various information on different objects across multiple tables. Modern
033   general-purpose knowledge graph push the agenda to describe an increasingly large fraction of the
034   entities of the world: Wikidata (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014) describes as of 2024 109M entities,
035   and YAGO4 gives a curated view on 67M entities (Pellissier Tanon et al., 2020). For machine
036   learning and artificial intelligence, capturing general knowledge opens an old promise of making
037   tasks easier via this knowledge (Lenat & Feigenbaum, 2000). Integrating this information into
038   machine learning does raise the challenge of assembling features from multiple tables. For this
039
      purpose, graph-embedding methods provide node features that can improve downstream learning
      task (Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Cvetkov-Iliev et al., 2023; Robinson et al., 2024). Increasingly
040
      sophisticated embeddings models (Bordes et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2014; Balazevic et al., 2019, ...)
041
      help produce embeddings that better capture the relational aspect of the data, which is important for
042   downstream tasks (Cvetkov-Iliev et al., 2023).
043
044   The size of large knowledge graphs is exploding: Wikidata gains 20M entities yearly (Wikimedia).
045
      This enables the exciting prospect of general feature enrichment: given a downstream table, enti-
      ties can be automatically linked to the knowledge graph (Mendes et al., 2011; Foppiano & Romary,
046
      2020; Delpeuch, 2019), and node features could be inserted in the table to facilitate machine learning
047
      task. The more entities in the knowledge graph, the more this process provides value to the down-
048   stream analysis. And yet, there can be a disconnect between the growth of knowledge graphs, and
049   the fact that the increasingly complex embedding models tend to be less tractable, and are typically
050   demonstrated on comparatively small graphs, often subsets of real-world graphs such as FB15k (15k
051   entities from Freebase) or WN18 (40k entities from WordNet), 3 orders of magnitude smaller than
052   modern general knowledge graphs, or industrial knowledge graphs (Sullivan, 2020). One roadblock
053   to scaling knowledge-graph embeddings is that, with many entities, the embeddings no longer fit in
      the memory of GPUs. The typical answer to this challenge is distributed computation across GPUs,


                                                        1
054
      explored by PyTorch-BigGraph (Lerer et al., 2019) and many more (Zheng et al., 2020; Mohoney
055   et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2022, ...). This comes with sizeable
056   engineering and computational cost, as the graph is fit piece by piece. The multi-GPU requirement
057   is a challenge for non-profits such as the Wikimedia foundation, in charge of Wikidata, given that
058   the embeddings should be recomputed regularly to incorporate newly-added entities.
059
      Here we show how to scale most knowledge-graph embedding methods with little computational
060
      resources. Our goal is to bridge the gap between advanced embedding approaches and huge general-
061   purpose knowledge graphs that strive to gather all human knowledge at once. For this, we leverage
062   fundamental structure in knowledge graphs: a small set of “core” entities come with much more
063   information than the others. Our method, SEPAL (Scalable Embedding Propagation Algorithm for
064   Large knowledge graphs), is ‘pluggable’ to any embedding model that, at triple-level, models the
065   tail embedding as a relation-specific transformation of the head embedding. There are two technical
066   contributions that enable SEPAL to bring scalability. 1) We show that good embeddings on a small
067   core subset of entities can be propagated to give good embeddings for the full graph. The challenge
068   here is to maintain the relational geometry. 2) We devise an algorithm called BLOCS to break down
069
      a huge knowledge graph into overlapping subsets that fit in GPU memory. Here, the challenge lies
      in the scale-free and connectivity properties of a large knowledge graph: some nodes are connected
070
      to a significant fraction of the graphs, while others are very hard to reach.
071
072   We start by reviewing related work. Then, section 3 describes our contributions. In section 4 we
073   evaluate SEPAL’s performance on knowledge graphs of increasing size between YAGO3 (Mahdis-
074   oltani et al., 2014) and YAGO4 (67M entities, Pellissier Tanon et al., 2020); we study the use of
075
      the embeddings for feature-enrichment on four downstream machine learning tasks, showing that
      SEPAL makes embedding methods much more tractable while generating better embeddings for
076
      downstream tasks. Empirical findings show that:
077
078   1. propagating embeddings to outer entities with message passing does not lead to a performance
079      loss and gives orders of magnitude speed-ups compared to full optimization;
080   2. this approach can be scaled to very large knowledge graphs on modest hardware.

      2     R ELATED WORK : EMBEDDING AND SCALABILITY IN KNOWLEDGE GRAPHS
083
084
      Knowledge graphs are multi-relational graphs describing knowledge in an entity-relation model.
085   Knowledge graphs store information as triples (h, r, t), where h is the head entity, r is the relation,
086   and t is the tail entity.
087
088
      2.1   G RAPH - EMBEDDING METHODS
089
090   Graph embedding methods learn low-dimensional (typically d = 100 or 200) vector representations
091   for the entities and relations. Knowledge-graph embeddings are directly related to the more general
092   graph embedding literate, learning representations for graph nodes but not for the relations. Different
093   embedding methods naturally lead to different structures of the embedding space.
094
095   Global methods Global methods can be formulated at the graph level, typically using the adja-
096   cency matrix A. A first family of methods performs explicit matrix factorizations on matrices
097   derived from the adjacency matrix, for instance GraREP (Cao et al., 2015) or NetMF (Qiu et al.,
098
      2018). These methods output close embedding vectors for nodes with similar neighborhoods.
099   As the adjacency matrix does not represent the edge-type information of multi-relational graphs, it
100   may be preferable to use a {0, 1}-valued third-order tensor, and correspondingly tensor factoriza-
101   tion approaches such as canonical polyadic decomposition (Hitchcock, 1927), Tucker decomposi-
102   tion (Tucker, 1966), or more recently RESCAL (Nickel et al., 2011). Unlike other global methods,
103   these methods compute embeddings for both entities and relations. However, they limit the relational
104
      model to multiplicative interactions between entities and relations embeddings.
105   To avoid relying on –potentially costly– optimization, another strategy is to compute random pro-
106   jections. Indeed these give very cost-effective approximations of the pairwise distances (Dasgupta
107   & Gupta, 2003). FastRP (Chen et al., 2019) proposes a scalable approach, with a few well-chosen
      applications of the adjacency matrix on a random projection matrix.


                                                        2
108
      Local methods Local methods formulate an optimization on triples rather than on the matrices
109   or tensors. They define a scoring function f (h, r, t) to represent the plausibility of a triple given
110   the embeddings θh , θr , θt of the entities and relation. The embeddings are optimized by stochastic
111   gradient descent to maximize the score of positive triples, and minimize that of negative ones.
112
      Skip-gram negative sampling (SGNS), behind word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013), has been shown
113
      to perform an implicit factorization (Levy & Goldberg, 2014). It has been adapted to graphs: Deep-
114
      Walk (Perozzi et al., 2014) and node2vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016) perform random walks on the
115   graph to generate “sentences” fed to word2vec. Here the scoring function between two nodes h and
116   t is simply f (h, t) = θh · θt . RDF2vec adapts this framework to multi-relational graphs by adding
117   the relations to the generated sentences (Ristoski & Paulheim, 2016).
118
      Triple-based methods design scoring functions to model the relations as geometric transformations
119
      in the embedding space. A seminal model is TransE (Bordes et al., 2013), modeling relations as
120
      translations in the embedding space. Many of these models can be framed as:
121

125   ding ϕ(θh , θr ). The challenge is to design a clever ϕ operator to model complex patterns in the data
126   –hierarchies, compositions, symmetries... Indeed some relations are one-to-one (people only have
127   one biological mother), well represented by a translation, while others are many-to-one (for instance
128   many person were BornIn Paris), calling for ϕ to be a contractive operation (Wang et al., 2017).
129   Many models explore different parametrizations, among which MuRE (Balazevic et al., 2019), Ro-
130   tatE (Sun et al., 2019) or QuatE (Zhang et al., 2019) have good performance (Ali et al., 2021b). This
131   framework also includes models like DistMult (Yang et al., 2014), ComplEX (Trouillon et al., 2016)
132   or TuckER (Balažević et al., 2019), that implicitly perform tensor factorizations.

148   2.2   S CALING GRAPH ALGORITHMS
149
      Various tricks help scale graph algorithms to the sizes we are interested in –millions of nodes.
150
151
      Graph partitioning Scaling up computation on graph, for graph embedding or more generally,
152
      often relies on breaking down graphs in subgraphs. For this, the partitioning, clustering, and
153   community-detection literatures are relevant. METIS (Karypis & Kumar, 1997), is a greedy node-
154   merging algorithm heavily used to scale all types of graph algorithm. A variety of algorithms have
155   also been developed to detect “communities”, groups of nodes more connected together, often with
156   applications on social networks: the Label Propagation Algorithm (LPA) (Raghavan et al., 2007),
157   spectral clustering (SC) (Shi & Malik, 2000), Louvain method (Blondel et al., 2008), the Leading
158   Eigenvector (LE) method (Newman, 2006), the Infomap method (Rosvall & Bergstrom, 2008), and
159   the Leiden method (Traag et al., 2019) which guarantees connected communities.
160
161   Local subsampling Other forms of data reduction can help to scale graph algorithms (e.g. based
      on message passing). Algorithms may subsample neighborhoods, as GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al.,


                                                        3
162
      2017) that selects a fixed number of neighbors for each node on each layer, or MariusGNN (Wal-
163   effe et al., 2023) that uses an optimized data structure for neighbor sampling and GNN aggrega-
164   tion. Cluster-GCN (Chiang et al., 2019) restricts the neighborhood search within clusters, obtained
165   by classic clustering algorithms, to improve computational efficiency on graphs with a community
166   structure. GraphSAINT (Zeng et al., 2019) creates overlapping subgraphs through random walks.
167
168
169   Multi-level techniques. Multi-level approaches, such as HARP (Chen et al., 2018), GraphZoom
170
      (Deng et al., 2019) or MILE (Liang et al., 2021), coarsen the graph, compute embeddings on the
      obtained smaller graph, and project them back to the original graph.
171
172
173   2.3   S CALING KNOWLEDGE - GRAPH EMBEDDING
174
175   The multi-relational aspect of knowledge graphs, captured e.g. in the ϕ detailed above, calls for
176   scaling tailored methods.
177
178
179   Parallel training. Many approaches speed up triple-level stochastic solvers by distributing train-
180   ing across multiple workers, starting from the seminal PyTorch-BigGraph (PBG) (Lerer et al., 2019).
181
      The challenge is then to limit overheads and communication costs, as the embeddings of the relations
      are global trainable parameters, and thus require moving data between workers. For this, DGL-KE
182
      (Zheng et al., 2020) reduces data movement by using sparse relation embeddings and a min-cut-
183
      based graph partitioning algorithm (Karypis & Kumar, 1997, METIS) to distribute the triples across
184   workers. HET-KG (Dong et al., 2022) further optimizes distributed training by preserving a copy of
185   the few most frequently used embeddings on each worker, to reduce communication costs. These
186   ‘hot-embeddings’ are periodically synchronized to minimize inconsistency. SMORE (Ren et al.,
187   2022) leverages asynchronous scheduling to overlap CPU-based data sampling, with GPU-based
188   embedding computations. Algorithmically, it contributes a bidirectional rejection sampling strategy
189   to generate the negatives at a very low cost. GraphVite (Zhu et al., 2019) accelerates SGNS for graph
190   embedding by both parallelizing random walk sampling on multiple CPUs, and negative sampling
191   on multiple GPUs. Finally, Marius (Mohoney et al., 2021) optimizes data movement with 1) a data
192
      flow architecture that maximizes resource utilization of the entire memory hierarchy, including disk,
      CPU, and GPU memory, 2) Partition caching and a buffer-aware data ordering to minimize disk IO.
193
194
195   Bags of entities. Other attempts to scale knowledge graphs include StarSpace (Wu et al., 2018),
196   that models some entities as bags of other entities rather than giving them explicit embeddings,
197   or NodePiece (Galkin et al., 2021) that embeds a subset of entities called anchors, and learns an
198   aggregation function to compute embeddings for all the other entities.

201   3     SEPAL: EXPANDING FROM A CORE SUBGRAPH
202
203   The work on scaling knowledge-graph embedding has mainly focused on efficient parallel comput-
204   ing to speed up stochastic optimization. We introduce a complementary approach, SEPAL, which
205   changes how the embeddings are computed, avoiding much of the optimization cost. To extract rich
206   node features from very large knowledge graphs, SEPAL allocates more computation time to the
207   more frequent entities. To that end, SEPAL proceeds in two steps (Figure 1):
208
      1. compute connected overlapping subgraphs that cover the full graph;
209
210   2. propagate the embeddings from the core to the outer subgraphs, with a message-passing strategy
211      preserving the relational geometry.
212   SEPAL’s key idea is to propagate embeddings to regions of the graph where they have not been
213   computed yet, departing from embedding propagation methods (Vashishth et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
214   2022) that use propagation as a post-processing to smooth pre-trained knowledge-graph embeddings.
215   SEPAL is compatible with any embedding model whose scoring function has the form given by
      Equation 1, some examples of which are provided in Table 1.

232   3.1   S PLITTING LARGE GRAPHS WITH BLOCS
233
234   Breaking up the graph into subgraphs is key to scaling up our approach memory-wise. Specifically,
235
      we seek a set of subgraphs that altogether cover the full graph but are individually small enough to
      fit on GPUs, to enable the subsequent GPU-based message passing.
236
237
238
      Core subgraph. SEPAL first defines the core of a knowledge graph as the subgraph induced by
      its most central entities. To build it, SEPAL focuses on entities, selecting the top η% entities by
239
      degree and keeping the largest connected component of the induced subgraph. The parameter η is
240
      chosen large enough to ensure that the core subgraph contains all the relation types present in the
241   graph (typically η varies between 2 and 5%).
242
243
      Outer subgraphs. The next class of subgraphs that we generate –the outer subgraphs– aim at
244
      covering the rest of the graph. The purpose of these subgraphs demands the following requirements:
245
246   R1: connected the subgraphs must be connected, to propagate the embeddings
247   R2: bounded size the subgraphs must have bounded sizes, to fit their embedding in GPU memory
248   R3: coverage the union of the subgraphs must be the full graph, to embed every entities
249   R4: scalability extraction must run with available computing resources, in particular memory
250
      Extracting such subgraphs is challenging on large knowledge graphs. These are scale-free graphs
251
      with millions of nodes exhibit no well-defined clusters (Leskovec et al., 2009) and pose difficulties
252   to existing partitioning algorithms. For instance, algorithms based on propagation, eigenvalues,
253   or power iterations of the adjacency matrix (Raghavan et al., 2007; Shi & Malik, 2000; Newman,
254   2006) struggle with the presence of extremely high-degree nodes that make the adjacency matrix
255   ill-conditioned. To satisfy our requirements despite these challenge, we allow subgraphs to overlap.
256
      We contribute BLOCS, an algorithm designed to break large graphs into Balanced Local
257
      Overlapping Connected Subgraphs. The name summarizes the goals: 1) Balanced: BLOCS pro-
258   duces subgraphs of comparable sizes. m, the upper bound for subgraph sizes, is a hyperparameter.
259   2) Local: the subgraphs have small diameters. The essence of BLOCS minimizes the intrasubgraph
260   mean shortest path length using a diffusion step. This locality property is important for the efficiency
261   of SEPAL’s propagation phase, as it intuitively reduces the number of propagation iterations needed
262   to converge to the global embedding structure. 3) Overlapping: a given node can belong to several
263   subgraphs. This is beneficial to our purpose because it facilitates information transfer between the
264   different subgraphs during the propagation. 4) Connected: all generated subgraphs are connected.
265   BLOCS uses three base mechanisms to grow the subgraphs: diffuse (add all neighboring entities
266   to the current subgraph), merge (merge two overlapping subgraphs) and dilate (add all unassigned
267   neighboring entities to the current subgraph). There are two different regimes during the generation
268   of subgraphs. First, few entities are assigned, and the computationally effective diffusion quickly
269   covers a large part of the graph, especially entities that are close to high-degree nodes. However,
      once these close entities have been assigned, the effectiveness of diffusion drops because it strug-

360   4.2   E VALUATING NODE FEATURES ON DOWNSTREAM REGRESSION TASKS
361
      We evaluate the embeddings as node features, used to facilitate learning in downstream tasks (Grover
362
      & Leskovec, 2016; Cvetkov-Iliev et al., 2023; Robinson et al., 2024). This task enables to compare
363
      the value of knowledge graphs of different sizes. Indeed, for a user, a suboptimal embedding of a
364   larger knowledge graph may be more interesting than a high-quality embedding of a smaller knowl-
365   edge graph because the larger graph brings information on more entities. We benchmark 4 down-
366   stream regression tasks (adapted from Cvetkov-Iliev et al., 2023): Movie revenues, US accidents,
367   US elections, and housing prices (see Appendix C).
368
      Figure 2 gives the prediction performance on the downstream tasks. SEPAL not only scales well to
369
      very large graphs (computing times markedly smaller than Pytorch-BigGraph), but also create more

521   Insights brought by our experiments go further than SEPAL. First, the method successfully exploits
522   the asymmetry of information between “central” entities and more peripheral ones. Power-law distri-
523   butions are indeed present on many types of objects, from words (Piantadosi, 2014) to geographical
524
      entities (Giesen & Südekum, 2011) and should probably be exploited for general-knowledge repre-
      sentations such as knowledge-graph embeddings. Second, and related, breaking up large knowledge
525
      graphs in communities is surprisingly difficult: some entities just belong in many (all?) communi-
526
      ties, and others are really hard to reach. Our BLOCS algorithm can be useful for other knowledge-
527   graph engineering tasks, such as scaling message-passing algorithms or simply generating partitions.
528   Finally, the embedding propagation in SEPAL appears powerful and we conjecture it will benefit
529   further approaches. First, it can be combined with much of the prior art to scale knowledge-based
530   embedding. Second, it seems a natural solution for link prediction semi-inductive settings: link
531   prediction on nodes newly connected to the graph (Ali et al., 2021a; Galkin et al., 2021), that thus
532   could be easily embedded by propagation. Finally, embedding propagation could naturally adapt to
533   continual learning settings (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019; Hadsell et al., 2020; Biswas et al., 2023)

       A.2               E XECUTION TIME BREAKDOWN
830
831   Here, we present the contribution of each part of the pipeline to the total execution time. Specifically,
832   we break down our method into four parts:
833
834                      1. core subgraph extraction;
835                      2. outer subgraphs generation (BLOCS);
836                      3. core embedding;
837
                         4. embedding propagation.
838
839   Figure 5 shows the execution time of the different components of SEPAL. It includes our five largest
840   knowledge graphs, for which the execution times have the same order of magnitude. The results
841   reveal that most of the execution time is due to the core embedding and embedding propagation
842   phases, while the core extraction time is negligible.
843   Three key factors influence SEPAL’s execution time during the three main steps of the pipeline:
844
845                      1. The core subgraph size: the more triples in the core subgraph, the longer the core embed-
846                         ding. This explains the wide disparities between the core embedding times on Figure 5,
847                         despite all the core subgraphs having roughly the same number of entities: YAGO4 core
848                         subgraph is more dense (33M triples), compared to YAGO4.5 (7M triples) for instance. Of
849
                            course, the core embedding time also depends on hyperparameters such as the number of
                            training epochs.
850
851                      2. The diameter of the knowledge graph: graphs with large diameters call for more dilation
852                         steps during BLOCS’ subgraph generation, and dilation is more costly than diffusion be-
853
                            cause it requires checking node assignments. This explains why adding the taxonomies to
                            YAGO4 and YAGO4.5 drastically reduces the time required to run BLOCS, as shown on
854
                            Figure 5.
855
856
                         3. The total number N of entities in the graph: this number determines the size of the
                            embedding matrix. The communication cost of moving embedding matrices from CPU
857
                            to GPU, and vice versa, accounts for most of the propagation time, and increases with
858
                            N . It also increases with the amount of overlap between the outer subgraphs produced by
859                         BLOCS, explaining the differences in propagation time between YAGO4.5 and YAGO4.5
860                         + taxonomy for instance.
861
862   Interestingly, the number of propagation steps K as little impact on the embedding propagation
863   time. The reason for this is that much of this time stems from the communication cost of loading the
      embeddings onto the GPU, and not from performing the propagation itself.

      A.4        C OMPARISON WITH N ODE P IECE
899
      SEPAL shares with NodePiece the fact that it embeds a subset of entities. Parallels can be drawn
900   between: a) the anchors of NodePiece and the core entities of SEPAL; b) the encoder function of
901   NodePiece and the embedding propagation of SEPAL. Yet, our approach differs from NodePiece in
902   several ways.
903
904
905
906   Neighborhood context handling. Both methods handle completely differently the neighborhood
907   of entities. NodePiece tokenizes each node into a sequence of k anchors and m relation types,
908
      where k and m are fixed hyperparameters shared by all nodes. If the node degree is greater than
      m, NodePiece downsamples randomly the relation tokens, and if it is lower than m, [PAD] tokens
909
      are appended; both seem sub-optimal. In contrast, SEPAL accommodates any node degree and
910
      uses all the neighborhood information, thanks to the message-passing approach that handles the
911   neighborhood context.
912
913   Additionally, NodePiece’s tokenization relies on an expensive BFS anchor search, unsuitable for
914
      huge graphs. On our hardware, we could not run the vanilla NodePiece (PyKEEN implementation)
      on graphs bigger than Mini YAGO3 (129k entities). For YAGO3 and YAGO4.5, we had to run an
915
      ablated version where nodes are tokenized only from their relational context (i.e., k = 0, studied in
916
      the NodePiece paper with good results), to skip the anchor search step.

945   A.5     OTHER CORE SELECTION STRATEGIES
946
      SEPAL selects the core entities based on degree. This is convenient for two reasons:
947
948
            1. Degree is inexpensive to compute, ensuring the core extraction phase to be fast (see Fig-
949            ure 5);
950
951
            2. It yields very dense core subgraphs. Indeed, while they contain η% of the entities of the full
               graph, they gather around 4η% of all the triples (Table 4). This allows the training on the
952
               core to process a substantial portion of the graph triples, resulting in richer representations.
953
954   However, a problem is that some relation types may not be included in the core subgraph.
955
956   A.5.1    R ELATION - BASED CORE SELECTION STRATEGY
957
958   To deal with the relational coverage issue, we introduce another method for selecting the core sub-
959   graph. It is based on the relations: for each relation type, select the edges with the top η% degree
960   (sum of degrees of head and tail) and keep the corresponding entities, then select the largest con-
961   nected component of the induced subgraph.
962   This approach enhances relational coverage but, as a counterpart, yields a sparser core subgraph
963   (Table 4). For YAGO4 and Freebase, the datasets on which the problem arises, switching from
964   degree-selection to relation-selection increases the number of relation types in the core from 61 to
965   75 (over 76) on YAGO4, and from 5,363 to 14,266 (over 14,665) on Freebase. Some relation types
966   are still missing because they were not present in the largest connected component of the induced
967   subgraph.
968   Performance-wise, Figure 8 shows the impact of the core selection strategy. Simply using the degree
969   appears to consistently give better than relation-based core selection results on downstream tasks.
970   This is probably due to a richer core subgraph, containing more triples to train good core embeddings
971   (Table 4). However, the sparser core resulting from the relation selection strategy also makes it faster,
      as there are fewer triples during core training.

998    A.5.2    M IXED CORE SELECTION STRATEGY
999
1000   The relation-based core selection strategy still presents two significant drawbacks: it triggers a per-
1001   formance drop compared to the degree-based approach, and some relations are still missing in the
1002   core. For this reason, we introduce a ”mixed” core selection strategy, aiming to get the best of both
1003   worlds.
1004   The mixed core selection strategy proceeds in four main steps:
1005
1006         1. Degree selection: Sample the nodes with the top ηn degrees.
1007
1008         2. Relation selection: Sample the edges with the top ηe degrees (sum of degrees of head and
1009
                tail) for each relation type, and keep the corresponding entities.
1010         3. Merge: Take the union of these two sets of entities.
1011
1012         4. Reconnect: If the induced subgraph has several connected components, add entities to
1013            make it connected to the core. This is done using a breadth-first search (BFS) with early
1014            stopping from the node with the highest degree of each given connected component (except
1015
                the largest) to the largest connected component. For each connected component (except the
                largest), a path linking it to the largest connected component is added to the core subgraph.
1016
1017
       This way, each relation type is guaranteed to belong to the core subgraph, by design. Table 4 shows
1018
       that it is indeed the case experimentally, even for Freebase which has 14,665 relation types.
1019
1020   This method features two hyperparameters ηn and ηe , the proportions for node and edge selections,
1021   controlling the size of its output subgraph. The values we used are provided in Table 4 for each
1022
       dataset.
1023   Regarding performance, Figure 8 reveals that the mixed strategy helps bridge the gap between the
1024   degree and relation selection strategies. It consistently performs better than the simple relation-based
1025   strategy and concedes little to the degree-based approach. Furthermore, one can adjust the values of
       ηn and ηe to control the trade-off between downstream performance and relation coverage.

1158            performing model on this dataset. This makes the scores more comparable between the
1159            different evaluation datasets.
1160         3. Cumulative: for each model, we sum its scores across every evaluation dataset. As there
1161            are 4 evaluation datasets, the highest possible score for a model is 4. Getting a score of 4
1162            means that the model beats every model on every evaluation dataset.

1224   D.2    E XPERIMENTAL STUDY OF HYPERPARAMETER EFFECT
1225
1226   Now we study experimentally the effect of varying the proportion η of entities in the core. Figure 9
1227   shows that increasing η tends to improve embedding quality for downstream tasks. However, the
1228   effect seems to be plateauing relatively fast for YAGO3 (not much improvement between η = 5%
1229   and η = 10%). For other datasets (YAGO4.5, YAGO4), it is not possible to explore larger values
1230   of η because the core subgraph would not fit in the GPU memory. Moreover, decreasing η makes
1231
       SEPAL run faster, as the core embedding phase accounts for a substantial share of the total execution
       time (Figure 5). There is, therefore, a trade-off between time and performance.
