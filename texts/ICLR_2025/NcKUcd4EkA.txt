009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) significantly enhance the capabilities of AI
012            systems, the increasing volume of query processing requests presents challenges
013            for cost-effective inference, particularly due to repetitive queries that lead to un-
014            necessary resource consumption and increased costs. Caching strategies are em-
015            ployed to store a small set of previous queries, enabling direct retrieval of repet-
016            itive queries without reprocessing by the LLMs. However, existing caching al-
017            gorithms often assume uniform query lengths, simplifying cache selection to a
018            top-K problem, which is inadequate for real-world scenarios with heterogeneous
019
               lengths. To address this issue, we propose a bandit learning algorithm for proac-
               tive query caching in LLMs, specifically considering variable-sized queries. We
020
               cast the optimal cache query cache problem as a knapsack problem. Since the
021
               repetitive pattern and processing cost are unknown and has uncertainty, we cast
022            the learning-to-cache problem as a bandit learning problem. Compared to conven-
023            tional bandit learning frameworks, a new technical challenge is that the reward of
024            an arm would not be observed if it is pulled. To tackle this, we propose
                                                                                      √ an Lower
025            confidence bound (LCB)-type algorithm, which we prove has a Õ( T ) order of
026            regret and show that our regret does not deteriorate compared to previous results
027            when incorporating a variable size setting. Furthermore, we demonstrate that our
028            online cache policy effectively reduces the additional computational overhead typ-
029            ically associated with calculating the optimal cache.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) have gained increasing popularity across various fields (Zhang
035   et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023b;a). However, as the capabilities of LLMs have increased, the challenge
036   of resource consumption during LLM deployment has become increasingly significant and cannot
037   be ignored. This leads to higher inference costs and longer latency compared to traditional models.
038
      As a result, when a large number of queries must be processed simultaneously, LLMs often prove to
      be inefficient or unsuitable for such tasks (Chen et al., 2023).
039
040   In the real-world marketplace of LLMs, it is inevitable for online large model systems to handle
041   some repetitive tasks (Chen et al., 2023). For the same task, the large model needs to be called
042   repeatedly multiple times to obtain the same result, which will inevitably lead to a waste of resources.
043   As the scale of LLMs grows larger, the cost of repeated queries becomes increasingly significant.
044
      Developing methods to reduce these redundant costs and resource waste would be highly beneficial
      for the efficient deployment of large models, significantly lowering the overall operational costs
045
      associated with them.
046
047   In the field of computer memory access (Belady, 1966), caching is an effective method for opti-
048   mizing data access speed and reducing the overhead of resource redundancy by establishing a fast
049   retrieval mechanism within the system. When applied to large model systems, caching allows re-
050
      peated queries to directly retrieve results from the cache without requiring complex computations
      from the LLMs, as the results of these repeated queries are assumed to remain largely unchanged.
051
052   Recent research (Stogiannidis et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024) involve a cache to store and reuses LLM
053   API’s response when similar queries are repeatedly asked. These works have successfully integrated
      caching strategies into the deployment of large models, but to cope with more complex real-world

054
      online environments, there is still much room for improvement in the field of cache combined with
055   LLMs. Incorporating cache into LLMs presents several challenges: 1) how to determine which
056   query to cache appropriately, 2) managing incomplete information when updating the cache in large-
057   scale, dynamic user environments. For the first challenge, in order to reduce overhead, we need to
058   cache those queries that may be popular and incur high costs. However, the cost associated with
059   each query may be unknown and variable; it may vary depending on different users or changes
060   in the computation process. From a statistical perspective, the cost of processing each query is
061   a random variable that depends on the query itself and fluctuates due to variations in the auto-
062   regressive generation process, as well as the length and quality of the generated responses (Zhu
063
      et al., 2024). For the second challenge, since the cost of each query may depend on the preferences
      of different users or other unpredictable factors, and the popularity of queries shifts over time, it is
064
      impossible to know all relevant information for each query in advance. This requires a strategy that
065
      dynamically collects specific statistics for each query through feedback in an online environment to
066   update the cache.
067
068   To deal with the selection of queries, Zhu et al. (2024) takes into account both the expected cost
069
      and popularity of queries as criteria for cache selection. This natural approach has proven effective
      in experiments and is relatively easy to implement. However, Zhu et al. (2024)’s online algorithm
070
      performs well only when each query has the same and fixed size in the cache, meaning the number
071
      of queries that can be stored in the cache remains constant. In the case where different queries
072   have variable size, although they make a simple discussion in appendix, there still exists room for
073   improvement. In variable size case, in addition to considering the cost and popularity of a query, the
074   length of the query must also be taken into account. At this point, the number of queries that the
075   cache can store will no longer be constant.
076
      In this paper, we consider the aforementioned issue from the perspective of bandit with knapsack
077
      constraint. We propose an online algorithm Online Stream Cache Bandits, an extension variant of
078   Zhu et al. (2024) to solve the problem. Our main contributions are as follows:
079
080   Algorithmic Framework. The proposed Online Stream Cache Bandits algorithm contains four key
081
      components. 1) Selecting cache with knapsack constraints: We consider the issue of variable size as
      a 0-1 knapsack problem. Here, the cost and popularity of a query represent the value, while the size
082
      of the query corresponds to its weight. Thus, selecting an optimal cache is equivalent to solving for
083
      the optimal knapsack configuration. 2) Lower confidence bound bandits: Different from traditional
084   bandit literature using UCB-type algorithms, we use lower confidence bound for estimation. This
085   choice is driven by the fact that, in our environment, the selected arm is not always observed in each
086   round due to cahce hit, which incurs zero cost (Zhu et al., 2024). On the other hand, this lower
087   confidence bound acts like a pessimistic strategy, helping to avoid our cache policy trapping into
088   sub-optimal solution. 3) Double scheduling strategy: The introduction of the knapsack problem into
089   our policy incurs additional computational overhead. If the knapsack problem is trivially computed
090   in each round, this additional computational overhead will grow linearly. To mitigate this, we use a
091   double scheduling strategy, where we only invoke the knapsack problem solver to update the whole
092
      cache at rounds that are integer powers of 2, reducing the additional cost to logarithmic order. 4)
      Recommended cache: Using the trivial method mentioned above, extra memory space is required
093
      to store all historical information and result of each query so that it can be selected in a timely
094
      manner when updating the cache. However, this extra memory space is often impractical in real-
095   world scenarios. To address this, we introduce a recommended cache that temporarily stores the
096   optimal cache selecting identified by the knapsack problem and “waits” for the relevant query. This
097   approach eliminates the need for additional memory space, and we show that this modification does
098   not affect the regret bound.
099
      Theoretical Analysis. The involving of the knapsack problem presents several challenges in our
100
      theoretical analysis, such as we can not determine how many queries are currently stored in the cache
101   or to find the regular of value of the queries stored in the current cache. Despite
102
                                                                                      √ these challenges, we
      demonstrate that our Online Stream Cache Bandits algorithm achieves a Õ( T ) regret bound with
103   a O(log T ) additional computational cost. This result aligns with the findings of Zhu et al. (2024)’s
104   work, which is the most relevant work  √ to ours, and the order of T in our regret bound aligns with
105   their established lower bound of Õ( T ). Furthermore, we show that the work of Zhu et al. (2024)
106   can be seen as an special case of ours when all queries are set to the same length.

      Experiments Evaluation. Zhu et al. (2024) proposed a heuristic method that replaces one query
109   with the smallest expected cost per-size, defined as P (q)C ∗ (q)/S(q), each round to solve variable
110   size case. However, their method requires a restrictive condition: the length of the query with the
111   result must be much smaller than the length of the cache, i.e. S(q) << M . Our analysis shows that
112   their method will fail without this condition. Using Zhu et al. (2024) as our baseline, we conduct
113   experiments on both synthetic and real data. Our results show that the Online Stream Cache Bandits
114   algorithm consistently achieves superior performance in terms of regret across both simulation and
115   real-world datasets.

118   2   R ELATED WORK
119
120   In the field of LLM, cache technology can address the issue of repeated inquiries, thereby reduc-
121   ing the cost of the LLM marketplace (Chen et al., 2023). Recently, caching strategies tailored for
122   LLMs have emerged (Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), focusing on content reuse during the
123   inference phase. These strategies aimed to optimize the internal data flow within LLMs, minimizing
124
      delays and lowering resource consumption. Bang (2023) is recognized in the industry for its sim-
      plicity and flexibility, providing various options for integration with different LLMChat services.
125
      However, it does not offer advanced cache performance enhancements. In contrast, Li et al. (2024)
126
      focuses on semantic-oriented enhancement, they improve cache efficiency by leveraging the seman-
127   tic understanding of queries. Zhu et al. (2024) propose a Caching in Online Learning algorithm to
128   avoid resource wasting. In their further discussion, they mention that generalizing their work to a
129   variable-size cache, in which distinct queries have heterogeneous lengths. However, they did not
130   conduct detailed research or provide a rigorous analysis. Inspired by Zhu et al. (2024), we general-
131   ize their problem with a variable size constraint. Furthermore, we consider involving the knapsack
132   problem to solve this variable size issue and propose the algorithms with theory guarantees.
133   Cache is a key technology that enhances system performance by reducing the overhead of redundant
134   computations. Traditional cache replacement algorithms delve into the most effective strategies
135   for caching queries that vary in frequency, cost, and required cache space. To address the issue
136   of varying frequencies, Lee et al. (2001) propsed a standard approach which use a Least Frequently
137   Used (LFU) or Least Recently Used (LRU) cache eviction strategy, and these work have been proven
138   to be optimal for both adversarial and stochastic queries (Bura et al., 2022). When varying costs
139   and varying frequencies exist simultaneously, Jin & Bestavros (2000), Arlitt et al. (2000) propose
140   and study the Greedy Dual-Size with Frequency (GDSF) replacement algorithm, which takes both
141
      frequency and cost into consideration. Our problem setting may provide inspiration for solving
      cache problems that involve uncertainty.
142
143   The Knapsack Problem (Kellerer et al., 2004) is a classic problem in computer science and opti-
144   mization theory. In the algorithms we propose, the knapsack problem will determine how to select
145   the cache efficiently. We can regard our problem as a 0-1 knapsack problem and use dynamic pro-
146   gramming (Bertsekas, 2005), a classical method to address the knapsack problem, as our Oracle
147
      to update our policy. The online knapsack problem (Zhou et al., 2008) is an online variant of the
      knapsack problem, which requires making decisions on the spot without knowing all the inputs.
148
      The challenge of the online knapsack problem lies in making optimal or near-optimal decisions un-
149
      der incomplete information, which often requires designing effective algorithms to approximate the
150   optimal solution.
151
152   The multi-armed bandit problem is a reinforcement learning problem that has broad applications
153
      in finance (Shen et al., 2015), medicine (Liu et al., 2018), chemistry (Wang et al., 2024) and rec-
      ommendation systems (Zhou et al., 2017). In the classic stochastic formulation, a casino player is
154
      faced with a set of slot machines, each with a fixed but different reward distribution that is initially
155
      unknown. With a limited budget, the player’s goal is to maximize the total payout by identifying
156   and playing the slot machines that offer better returns. To this end, the player effectively allocates
157   limited resources to balance exploration of the machines that have been played less and exploita-
158   tion of the current best options. In our work, we can see each query as an arm, and its cost is an
159   unknown distribution. Under this setting, the UCB-type method can deal with the uncertainty of
160   each query. Compared to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK), which is a problem that combines bandit
161   and knapsack constraints, our problem setting is different. The BwK problem (Badanidiyuru et al.,
      2018; Liu et al., 2024) assumes that there are K resources consumed over time, each with distinct

      budgets B1 , ..., BK . Every resource i ∈ [K] is associated with a consumption, The goal of policy
163   is to maximizes the accumulated reward subject to the budget constraints that each consumption of
164   resource i is less than Bi . In our work, we utilize the knapsack problem to address heterogeneous
165   size queries, treating the cache as a knapsack constraint: the lengths of distinct queries represent

      4.1   BASIC BATCH C ACHE BANDITS
280
      Batch setting. We begin our algorithm with a batch setting, which means that the algorithm can
281
      record all the queries along with their results and can obtain any query with its result at any time. In
282   this case, a straightforward policy for our model is that we can naturally use the Oracle at the end of
283   each round, and the agent selects the cache according to the results provided by the Oracle. We first
284   propose a batch version of our policy (Algorithm.1), this version does not consider the storage costs
285   of all queries and their results, and it assumes that the results of the required queries can be obtained
286   at any time. In this algorithm, the cache will be reselected in each round. At the end of each round,
287   the Oracle will provide the optimal solution based on the current estimation of all queries; then the
288   policy will select queries according to the output of the Oracle and add them to the cache. Under
289   Algorithm.1, the current cache will ultimately converge to the optimal cache when the estimations
290
      converge to the true values. On the other hand, if the estimations of all queries become precise
      enough, the current cache will be updated to the optimal cache immediately.
291
292   Lower confidence bound. Similar to Zhu et al. (2024), we also use a lower confidence bound in
293   the estimation of the average cost of each query; this differs from traditional UCB-based methods.
294   The reason we use the lower bound is that the pessimistic policy in cost will prevent the algorithm
295   from getting trapped in a sub-optimal solution. In our setup, the optimal cache always selects the
296
      query set with the largest value sum, which is the sum of C ∗ (q)P (q) in our model. If we use an
      upper confidence bound for estimation, once the Oracle outputs a sub-optimal solution for cache
297
      selection, it will maintain the estimation of queries included in this sub-optimal cache as greater
298
      than that of other queries with high probability. This prevents the algorithm from correcting the
299   solution selected by the Oracle, leading to a trap in the sub-optimal cache. This is akin to adding
300   a penalty to the estimation of queries that remain in the cache for a long time, making them more
301   likely to be replaced, similar to an enforcement of exploration.
302
      Replacement strategy. In Zhu et al. (2024), they proposed a method that replacing one query with
303
      the smallest expected cost per-size P (q)C ∗ (q)/S(q) each round. This method is natural and does
304
      not incur any additional cost. However, in certain situations related to the knapsack problem, due
305   to the size constraint, the current cache may need to replace multiple queries at once to update to
306   the optimal solution. If we use the LFU policy and allow only one query to be replaced at a time, it
307   may prevent the algorithm from updating to the optimal cache. Unlike Zhu et al. (2024), the Oracle
308   we use here allows the policy to update more than one query each round, which can prevent the
309   algorithm from getting trapped in a sub-optimal solution.
310
      Practical limitations. Although Algorithm 1 can effectively select the optimal solution, it has sig-
311   nificant limitations. Algorithm 1 requires a record of all queries, and their results must be obtainable
312   at any time to ensure it can use the appropriate queries to update the cache promptly. Thus, we
313   need a large amount of additional storage space to keep all the information about queries and their
314   results; however, this is not feasible in an online setting. In fact, this additional storage requirement
315   is unreasonable in real-world scenarios. On one hand, if we could store all the results of queries, the
316   cache would become unnecessary. On the other hand, Algorithm 1 needs to call the Oracle in each
317   round, which incurs a significant additional computational cost as the number of rounds increases,
318   especially in solving the NP-hard problems like the knapsack problem, such a high computational
319
      cost is unsustainable.

358   Stream-Based Cache Updates. In the online model, the algorithm operates in a stream-based
359   manner, meaning it does not store all previous queries and their results. Instead, it maintains only the
360   sequence numbers and estimated values of the queries. Whenever the cache is updated, any queries
361   and their corresponding results that are removed from the cache are permanently cleared. As a result,
362   the algorithm can only access the queries currently in the cache and any newly arriving queries. It
363   may not be able to update the cache immediately based on the output of the oracle because the
364
      required queries may have already been cleared due to replacement, necessitating the re-acquisition
      of their results when they arrive next. This resembles a data stream, where the algorithm can only
365
      see the content of newly arrived queries and cannot access the content of all queries in each round.
366
      To solve this issue, we involve a recommended cache denoted by M̂ to temporarily store the results
367
      output by our Oracle at the current time.
368
369   When Algorithm 2 call the Oracle, our policy will not update cache immediately, instead it will first
370   get a recommended cache M̂t , and clear out the current cache. In the following rounds, if a query
371   that belongs to the recommended cache arrives, it will miss the current cache and be input into the
372   LLM to get its result. Then, this query will be put into the current cache; otherwise, no update is
373   made to the cache, regardless of whether the current query hits. This process will repeat until the
374
      next recommended cache update occurs. Under this replacement method, we do not need to store
      all results of each queries, which satisfy the online model and be more reasonable.
375
376   Reducing computation. To reduce the additional computational cost, we establish a special pro-
377   tocol which only call the Oracle in the round that is the power of 2, this protocol can reduce the
      computational cost of calling Oracle from O(T ) to O(log T ).

      Although the number of cache misses seems to increase due to the lack of timely updates in the
379   online mode and the reduction of Oracle calls to minimize computational overhead, we can show
380   that the deterioration of the overall algorithm is not significant. Unlike traditional bandit algorithms,
381   the update for the estimation of a query cost occurs only when the query does not hit the cache.
382   Therefore, if the number of misses increases in the previous round, the estimation of the query will
383   become more precise afterward, leading the knapsack returned by the oracle to gradually approach
384   the optimal knapsack. In other words, the more misses there are, the more precise the query estima-
385   tion will be. In the same time, the times of clear operation will be bound by O(log T ), which further
386   bound the number of missing.
387   Algorithm 2 balances the computational cost and the convergence speed. In the batch version,
388   the current cache updates immediately to the optimal cache when the oracle provides the optimal
389   knapsack. However, it needs to call the oracle in each round to ensure the algorithm knows the
390   optimal solution in a timely manner, which leads to an additional computational cost of O(T ). In
391   the online version, we do not√need to call the Oracle in each round, and we can demonstrate that the
392   regret is still bounded by Õ( T ).

498   significant additional costs.
