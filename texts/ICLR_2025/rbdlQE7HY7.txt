011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           This paper presents novel contributions to the field of online optimization, particu-
014           larly focusing on the adaptation of algorithms from concave optimization to more
015           challenging classes of functions. Key contributions include the introduction of
016           uniform wrappers, establishing a vital link between upper-quadratizable functions
017           and algorithmic conversions. Through this framework, the paper demonstrates su-
018
              perior regret guarantees for various classes of up-concave functions under zeroth-
              order feedback. Furthermore, the paper extends zeroth-order online algorithms
019
              to bandit feedback counterparts and offline counterparts, achieving a notable im-
020
              provement in regret/sample complexity compared to existing approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      The optimization of continuous DR-submodular functions has become increasingly prominent in
      recent years. This form of optimization represents an important subset of non-convex optimization
027
      problems at the forefront of machine learning and statistics. These challenges have numerous real-
028
      world applications like revenue maximization, mean-field inference, and recommendation systems,
029   among others (Bian et al., 2019; Hassani et al., 2017; Mitra et al., 2021; Djolonga & Krause, 2014;
030   Ito & Fujimaki, 2016; Gu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023).
031
032
      A natural staring point to for DR-submodular maximization is to start from a convex optimization
      algorithm and adapt it to the setting of DR-submodular functions. Online Convex Optimization
033
      (OCO) is extensively utilized across various fields due to its numerous practical applications and
034
      robust theoretical underpinnings. The tools from the area of online convex optimization have been
035   applied to many online non-concave optimization algorithms, e.g., to converge to stationary points
036   in online non-concave optimization (Yang et al., 2018), or algorithms with approximation guaran-
037   tees for DR-submodular optimization (Chen et al., 2018; Niazadeh et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022;
038   Pedramfar et al., 2023).
039
      In this paper, we focus on a large class of functions, namely the class of quadratizable functions,
040
      first introduced in (Pedramfar & Aggarwal, 2024a). Quadratizable functions includes special sub-
041   classes of non-convex/non-concave functions where the offline constrained optimization problem
042   is NP-hard to solve but we can find an α-approximation of the optimal value in polynomial time.
043   Indeed, it is shown that this class of online upper quadratizable optimization includes up-concave
044   optimization (a generalization of DR-submodular and concave optimization) in the following cases:
045   (i) monotone γ-weakly µ-strongly DR-submodular functions with curvature c over general convex
046   sets, (ii) monotone γ-weakly DR-submodular functions over convex sets containing the origin, and
047   (iii) non-monotone DR-submodular optimization over general convex sets.
048   Even though the tools from OCO have proven effective in more challenging classes, much of past
049   work along these lines involve taking inspiration from OCO and manually designing new algorithms
050   and analyzing them specific to each problem setting. This raises the following question
051
052           When and how can we adapt algorithms from the (simpler) setup of online convex
053           optimization into algorithms for online optimization over more general classes of
              functions?

      In this paper, we try to provide partial solutions to this question for adapting OCO algorithms to
055   algorithms for online quadratizable optimization. The notion of quadratizability is built upon a gen-
056   eralization of the defining condition f (x) − f (y) ≥ ⟨∇f (y), x − y⟩ of convex functions. This sim-
057   ilarity with convex functions is a starting point which allows us to define a class of meta-algorithm
058   called “uniform wrappers”. Uniform wrappers provide a straightforward way to convert OCO al-
059   gorithms into algorithms that can handle quadratizable functions. We also develop a guideline to
060   convert the existing proofs for regret bounds of the base algorithms in the convex setting into regret
061   bounds of the new algorithms over the quadratizable functions.
062   We note that, for a specific class of algorithms, this question was partly addressed in (Pedramfar &
063   Aggarwal, 2024a). Specifically, as we will discuss in Appendix B, their result can be formulated as
064   a special case of ours, where they assume that the starting algorithm is a first order online algorithm
065   with semi-bandit feedback that obtains sub-linear regret against fully adaptive adversaries. This
066   condition is too restrictive to allow for adapting many of the ideas in OCO literature. In this paper
067   we take a step further and can handle broader classes of algorithms, including the more challenging
068   setting of zeroth order feedback.
069   As an application of our framework, we propose a variant of a bandit convex optimization algorithm
070   that was introduced in (Saha & Tewari, 2011) as the base algorithm, namely Zeroth Order Follow the
071   Regularized Leader (ZO-FTRL) and demonstrate how it can be converted using uniform wrappers
072   (denoted by W) to obtain 3 algorithms for function classes (i)-(iii) mentioned above. See Tables 1
073   and 2 for details. Note that ZO-FTRL and W(ZO-FTRL) are zeroth order, but they are not bandit
074   feedback algorithms. We also extend the results to those with bandit feedback, as well as derive
075
      sample complexity guarantees for the offline algorithm.
076   The main contributions in this work include:
077
078   1. We develop a general framework for converting algorithms and their regret guarantees from on-
079      line convex concave optimization to online quadratizable optimization. Conversion of the algo-
080      rithm could be applied to any online optimization algorithm, and the conversion of the proof is
081
         described using a general guideline.
082   2. Our framework obtains or matches the state of the art algorithm in all online optimization settings
083      considered. (See Table 1) Note that our framework also recovers all known results for non-
084      stationary DR-submodular maximization. (See Remark 6 and Table 3 in (Pedramfar & Aggarwal,
085
         2024a))
086   3. Except for deterministic first order feedback and the special case of γ-weakly non-monotone
087      functions with γ < 1, our framework obtains or matches the state of the art algorithm in all
088      online optimization settings considered. (See Table 2)
089   4. We obtain superior regret guarantees for several classes of weakly DR-submodular functions un-
090      der zeroth order feedback, specifically (i) monotone γ-weakly µ-strongly DR-submodular func-
091      tions with curvature c over general convex sets, (ii) monotone γ-weakly DR-submodular func-
092      tions over convex sets containing the origin, and (iii) non-monotone DR-submodular optimization
093
         over general convex sets. (See Table 1 and Theorem 6)
094   5. Those results can be extended to the bandit setting yielding improved results for bandit feedback.
095      (See Table 1 and Theorem 7)
096   6. The results for zeroth order online algorithms can be specialized to offline algorithms, resulting in
097      three new algorithms with a sample complexity of 1/ϵ3 in different settings, which is significantly
098      better than the state of art 1/ϵ4 . (See Table 2 and Theorem 8)

158

173   A query oracle is a function that provides the observation to the agent. Formally, a query oracle for a
174
      function f is a map Q defined on K such that for each x ∈ K, the Q(x) is a random variable taking
      value in the observation space O. The query oracle is called a stochastic value oracle or stochastic
175
      zeroth order oracle if O = R and f (x) = E[Q(x)]. Similarly, it is called a stochastic up-super-
176
      gradient oracle or stochastic first order oracle if O = Rd and E[Q(x)] is a up-super-gradient of f

178   a deterministic oracle. Note that, given a function, there is at most a single deterministic gradient

180   deterministic gradient oracle. We say an oracle is bounded by B if its output is always within the
181   Euclidean ball of radius B centered at the origin. We say the agent takes semi-bandit feedback if
182   the oracle is first-order and the agent query function is trivial. Similarly, it takes bandit feedback
183   if the oracle is zeroth-order and the agent query function is trivial1 . If the agent query function is
184   non-trivial, then we say the agent requires full-information feedback.

186   is a sequence (B1 , · · · , BT ) of functions where each Bt maps a tuple (x1 , · · · , xt ) ∈ Kt to a tuple
187   (ft , Qt ) where ft ∈ F and Qt is a query oracle for ft . We say an adversary Adv is oblivious if
188   for any realization B = (B1 , · · · , BT ), all functions Bt are constant, i.e., they are independent of

284   algorithm A. However, even if the original algorithm has a sublinear regret over concave functions
285   and F is a function class that is upper quadratizable with W, this does not guarantee that the resulting
286   algorithm W(A) has a sublinear regret over F. In this section we discuss how we might convert the
287   proofs of the regret bound for A over concave functions into a proof of a similar regret bound for
288
      W(A) over F. We will refer to algorithms A where the regret bounds could be be converted as
      wrappable algorithms.
289
290   The core idea for converting proof for concave optimization into proofs for upper-quadratizable
291   optimization can be informally summarized in a few steps:
292
293
            (0) Sometimes, if the algorithm A is the result of application of a meta-algorithm to another
                algorithm B, e.g. A = SFTT(B) (the meta-algorithm SFTT converts algorithms that
294
                require full-information feedback to ones that work with (semi)-bandit feedback; see Ap-
295
                pendix J), we may need to consider the base algorithm instead. For example, in the example
296             of SFTT, we might want to consider SFTT(W(B)) instead of W(SFTT(B)) = W(A).
297
            (1) Rewrite the parts of proof (after possibly adapting the algorithm) of the original regret
298
                bound without assuming that the function class in concave, in order to isolate the use on
299
                concavity in the proof. In this step, we hope to obtain a result that would only require a
300             single use of an inequality of the type f (y) − f (x) ≤ ⟨∇f (x), y − x⟩ − µ2 ∥y − x∥2 to
301             complete the proof for the concave case. See Theorems 1 (as an example of a family of
302             zeroth order results) and 9 (as an example of a family of first order results) for examples of
303             this step.
304         (2) Verify that the results of the previous step could be adapted to upper-quadratizable setting.
305             See the proof of Theorems 2 and 10 for examples of this step.
