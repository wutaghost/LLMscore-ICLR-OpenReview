009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel prompt design for Large Language Models (LLMs) tailored to
012            Asynchronous Time Series. Unlike regular time series, which assume values at
013            evenly spaced time points, asynchronous time series consist of events occurring at
014            irregular intervals, each described in natural language. Our approach effectively
015
               utilizes the rich natural language of event descriptions, allowing LLMs to benefit
               from their broad world knowledge for reasoning across different domains and
016
               tasks. This allows us to extend the scope of asynchronous time series analysis
017
               beyond forecasting to include tasks like anomaly detection and data imputation.
018
               We further introduce Stochastic Soft Prompting, a novel prompt-tuning mecha-
019
               nism that significantly improves model performance, outperforming existing fine-
020            tuning methods such as QLORA. Through extensive experiments on real-world
021            datasets, we demonstrate that our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance
022            across different tasks and datasets.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      An asynchronous time series (also named temporal event sequence or continuous-time event se-
      quence) is a temporally ordered set of events that describe the progression of actions or occurrences.
028
      Asynchronous time series are ubiquitous in daily life, such as healthcare (Lorch et al., 2018; Rizoiu
029
      et al., 2018), finance (Bacry et al., 2015; Jin et al., 2020), e-commerce (Hernandez et al., 2017), and
030   social media (Zhang et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2023). In each of those domains, predicting the next
031   events plays a crucial role.
032
033
      Unlike regular time series, which consist of values at evenly spaced time intervals (like weather mea-
      surements), asynchronous time series consist of multiple types of discrete events occurring sporadi-
034
      cally over time. For example, in the context of social media platforms like Twitter, user interactions
035
      (likes, comments, shares, and follows) happen sporadically and at irregular intervals.(Zhao et al.,
036   2015) Each such type of interaction with a user’s profile represents an event type, and together with
037   their timestamps, form an asynchronous time series. (Xue et al., 2024) Modeling such asynchronous
038   time series is challenging due to the irregular timing and the diversity of event types, which contrasts
039   with the uniformity and regularity of traditional time series data.Schirmer et al. (2022) (Horn et al.,
040   2020) (Zhang et al.)
041
      Traditionally, to model asynchronous time series, events are grouped into a fixed, small number of
042   categorical types. (Xue et al., 2024) Separate stochastic processes—such as Poisson processes or
043   Hawkes processes—are then modeled for each event type to predict which event will occur next
044   and when. (Mei et al., 2022) (Hawkes, 1971) However, this approach presents several significant
045   drawbacks. Firstly, it inherently limits research to datasets with a small number of event types
046   because modeling each event type separately becomes increasingly computationally intensive as the
047   number of event types grows (Zuo et al., 2020). Secondly, events can vary widely and may not
048   fit neatly into predefined categories. Thirdly, this method leads to the loss of meaningful natural
049   language descriptions associated with the events. Fourthly, these methods treat each event type
050
      independently, ignoring any interactions between them - for example, likes and shares of a tweet are
      not independent events. Lastly, extending these methods to other tasks require significant theoretical
051
      development. (Shchur et al., 2021)
052
053   Deep learning models have significantly revolutionized techniques for time series modeling, and
      even more so with the introduction of transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). However, there are

      often limitations due to the scarcity of training data, overfitting in specific domains, and the highly
055   specialized architectural designs. In response to those challenges, Large Language Models (LLMs)
056   have emerged as a powerful and promising direction to model time series data. For example, (Gruver
057   et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Xue & Salim, 2023; Jin et al., 2024) have illustrated how LLMs can

062   on regular time series with evenly spaced numerical observations and cannot be directly applied to
063
      asynchronous time series due to their irregular intervals and diverse event types described in natural
      language. While LLMs have recently been explored for action recognition and action forecasting
064
      from videos Zhao et al. (2024) Wang et al. (2024), applying LLMs to textual asynchronous time
065
      series over multiple tasks (like anomaly detection and imputation) remains largely unexplored.

      This paper presents LASTS (Language-modeled-Asynchronous Time Series), a novel prompting
078
      based framework to adapt LLMs to asynchronous time series data while keeping the backbone model
079   intact. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explore the capabilities of LLMs to
080   process textual asynchronous time series data and works on multiple tasks as shown in Figure 1.
081   Our framework overcomes the drawbacks presented by traditional approaches for modeling asyn-
082   chronous time series- It can handle datasets with large number of event types easily, it does not need
083   to group events into predefined categorical bundles, it retains the natural language descriptions of
084   event types and utilizes them, and it is able to leverage the rich interactions between different event
085   types.
086   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
088          • We introduce LASTS (Language-modeled Asynchronous Time Series) which is a novel
089            framework that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to model asynchronous time
090            series data, while effectively handling datasets with a large number of event types without
091            the need for predefined categorical groupings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
092            work to explore the capabilities of LLMs to process textual asynchronous time series data
093
               across multiple tasks such as forecasting, anomaly detection, and data imputation.
094          • We introduce Stochastic Soft Prompting (StoP) which is an innovative prompt-tuning
095            mechanism that serves as a parameter-efficient method to adapt LLMs to asynchronous
096            time series data. StoP learns soft prompts that significantly improve model performance
097
               and enhance adaptability by randomly truncating the prompts during training to learn more
               diverse representations.
098
099          • We conduct comprehensive evaluations on real-world datasets across multiple tasks to
100            demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method. Our approach achieves state-of-
101
               the-art performance, outperforming existing methods, and highlights the potential of LLM-
               based models to effectively process and analyze asynchronous time series data.

106   Temporal Point Processes (TPPs). TPPs (Hawkes, 1971; Daley & Vere-Jones) have emerged as
107   the standard method to model asynchronous time series data. Over the last decade, a large number
      of neural temporal point processes have been proposed to capture complex dynamics of stochastic

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      processes in time by using neural networks. Du et al. (2016); Mei & Eisner (2017) proposed to use
109   models based on Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to model the sequence of events. Then, more
110   advanced models (Mehrasa et al., 2019; Lüdke et al., 2023) were proposed to better model the un-
111   certainty when predicting the future. Recently, several neural TPP models incorporate Transformers
112   in order to improve the performance by using attention to better model long-term dependencies:
113   Self-attentive Hawkes process (SAHP) (Zhang et al., 2020), Transformer Hawkes process (THP)
114   (Zuo et al., 2020), and Attentive Neural Hawkes Process (A-NHP) (Mei et al., 2022).
115
116   Transformers for Time Series. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have become popular to
117   model regularly-sampled time series because of their ability to capture long-range dependencies
118   and to extract semantic correlations among the elements of a long sequence. Informer (Zhou et al.,
119
      2021) introduced a novel self-attention architecture to reduce the quadratic complexity of the orig-
      inal self-attention. Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) used a novel decomposition architecture with an
120
      auto-correlation mechanism to identify more reliable temporal patterns. Crossformer (Zhang & Yan,
121
      2023) proposed a novel architecture to model both the cross-time and cross-dimension dependencies
122   multivariate time series forecasting. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) tokenizes the time series in patches,
123   and proposes a channel-independent patch time series Transformer to improve the long-term fore-
124   casting accuracy.
125
      Due to the space limitations, we only review some popular models and invite the reader to check out
126
      (Wen et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023) for a more complete literature reviews of Transformer models
127
      for regularly-sampled time series. Most of the time series Transformer models are designed for
128   specific tasks, and cannot be easily extended to asynchronous time series data or other tasks like
129   anomaly detection or imputation.
130
131   Foundation Models (FMs) for Time Series. FMs (Bommasani et al., 2021) are a family of deep
132   models that are pretrained on vast amounts of data, and have caused a paradigm shift due to their
133   unprecedented capabilities for zero-shot and few-shot generalization. FMs have revolutionized nat-
134   ural language processing (Brown et al., 2020; BigScience Workshop et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024;
135   Dubey et al., 2024) and computer vision (Radford et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023). The availabil-
136   ity of large-scale time series datasets has opened the door to pretrain a large model on time series
137   data. ForecastPFN (Dooley et al., 2024) proposed the first zero-shot forecasting method trained
138
      purely on synthetic data. Lag-Llama (Rasul et al., 2023) introduced a univariate probabilistic fore-
      casting model that was pretrained on a large corpus of diverse time series data. TimeFM (Das et al.,
139
      2024) pretrained a decoder style attention model with input patching, using a large time series corpus
140
      comprising both real-world and synthetic datasets. Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024) introduced a frame-
141   work for pretraining on tokenized time series data, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot forecasting
142   performance and simplifying forecasting workflows. MOIRAI (Woo et al., 2024) is an enhanced
143   Transformer architecture pretrained in the Large-scale Open Time Series Archive, that achieves
144   competitive performance as a zero-shot forecaster.
145
146   LLMs for Time Series LLMs pretrained on large amounts of text data have emerged as a promis-
147   ing direction to model time series data. GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023), LLM4TS (Chang et al., 2023),
148   and TEMPO (Cao et al., 2023) fine-tuned a pretrained GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019) on some time
149   series downstream tasks to capture intrinsic dynamic properties. TimeLLM (Jin et al., 2024) pro-
150   posed a reprogramming framework to repurpose LLMs for general time series forecasting with the
151
      backbone language models kept intact. PromptCast (Xue & Salim, 2023) introduced a new prompt-
      based forecasting paradigm, where the numerical input and output are transformed into prompts
152
      and the forecasting task is framed in a sentence-to-sentence manner. LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023)
153
      showed that LLMs can zero-shot extrapolate time series if the numerical values of the time series are
154   well represented. LLM Processes (Requeima et al., 2024) explores various prompt configurations
155   for using LLMs for time series forecasting condiitoned on a textual context. We refer the reader to
156   (Zhang et al., 2024) for a more detailed survey on the topic.
157
158   Vision Models for Time Series. Several works started to explore the use of FMs pretrained on
159   images because of the better intrinsic similarities between images and time series such as trend,
160   stationarity, seasonality/periodicity, and sudden change. (Zhou et al., 2023) tried to fine-tune a BEiT
161   (Bao et al., 2022) trained on images for time series forecasting, but it falls short of the leading text-
      based and time series-based FMs. Recently, VisionTS (Chen et al., 2024) proposes to use a vision

162
      Transformer pretrained on ImageNet to reduce the cross-domain gap or in-domain heterogeneity
163   between time series and text.
164
165   Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT). PEFT (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) is a paradigm to adapt
166   pretrained LLMs to various domains without fine-tuning all of a model’s parameters, which can
167   be costly and require large amount of training data. LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) methods freeze the
168   pretrained model weights and injects trainable rank decomposition matrices into each layer of the
169   Transformer architecture, greatly reducing the number of trainable parameters for downstream tasks.
170   QLoRA (Dettmers et al.) advances finetuning by significantly reduces memory usage while preserv-
171   ing task performance.
172
173   Soft Prompt Tuning. Soft prompts have emerged as a compute efficient method for adapting a
174
      pretrained LLMs to new domains without altering their core architectures. Brown et al. (2020) was
      among the first to demonstrate the power of prompting for task adaption of pretrained language
175
      models, but automatically finding suitable sets of text prompts remains an open challenge. Li &
176
      Liang (2021); Qin & Eisner (2021) proposed the prefix tuning technique that preprends a few task
177   specific soft tokens to the input and hidden states of each Transformer layer. During the training,
178   the parameters of soft prompts are updated by gradient descent while the model parameters keep
179   frozen. Liu et al. (2021) showed the prefix tuning technique could be effectively applied to natural
180   language understanding with different scales of models. Lester et al. (2021) simplified the prefix
181   tuning technique that only adds soft prompts to the input layer and is now considered as the standard
182   soft prompt-tuning.

184   3    BACKGROUND
185
186   Notations. We observe n events over a fixed time interval [0, T ), with each event being denoted
187   as (e, t), where e ∈ E is the event type (or attributes) and E represents the space of event types.
188   An asynchronous time series is a sequence of events x1:n = ((e1 , t1 ), (e2 , t2 ), . . . , (en , tn )) where
189   ti is an increasing sequence in [0, T ) that does not necessarily observe any periodicity. A common
190   alternative to the event time ti is the inter-arrival time τj := tj −tj−1 , they are considered isomorphic
191   and often used interchangeably. In our work there is very little constraint on E and in principle, our
192   model still works even if E is infinite. We only need to be able to compute a vectorial representation
193
      of the event type/attributes, which is achieved through the LLM’s learned input embeddings in our
      work.
194
195
      Language modeling. Language modeling is a widely used task to train LLMs where the goal is
196
      predicting the next word or character in a document. Language models are designed to work on a
197   sequence of m tokens, where each token belongs to a vocabulary. Tokenizer transforms the input text
198   data into a sequence of tokens. The tokenization process is important and can impact significantly
199   performances, for it directly influences how patterns form within tokenized sequences and the types
200   of operations that language models can learn.
201
202   Tasks We propose a new approach to model asynchronous time series with LLMs which solves
203   three different tasks (see Figure 1):
204
205
             • Forecasting (also known as next event prediction) Given a history of events x1:m from an
               asynchronous time series, the model is tasked with predicting the next event xm+1 .
206
207
             • Data imputation. One of the events xj of the series is randomly chosen and masked, the
               model is tasked with filling in the gap.
208
             • Anomaly detection. One event xj of the series is randomly chosen and its event type ej
209
               is replaced randomly by another event type e′ . The model is tasked with identifying this
210
               out-of-place element.
211
212   To find the right recipe for the model to solve these tasks, we innovated in two major directions:
213   first, we studied various representations of the asynchronous time series as inputs to LLMs (Section
214   4.1) for zero shot completion of these tasks; and secondly, we study different parameter efficient
215   techniques to adapt an LLM backbone for working with asynchronous time series, while leveraging
      its knowledge of the world and its understanding of natural language (Section 4.2).

241   4.1     LASTS - P ROMPTING LLM S WITH A SYNCHRONOUS T IME S ERIES DATA
242
243   Unlike ordinary time series, often represented as sequences of numerical values (Gruver et al., 2023),
244   asynchronous time series are represented as sequences of events xi = (ei , ti ), where ei is the event
245
      type, and ti is a representation of the timestamp of this event. Normally, ti is expressed as inter-
      arrival time, which is the time elapsed between event xi−1 and xi .
246
247   In prior work on modeling asynchronous time series (Du et al., 2016; Mehrasa et al., 2019; Zhang
248   et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022), events are typically reduced to categories from a small set of options.
249   In contrast, we retain the event types ei as natural language descriptions. We introduce LASTS,
250   which specifies how to input an asynchronous time series as part of a prompt to effectively leverage
251
      LLMs for various tasks on such data.
252
      LASTS Prompt Structure The LASTS prompt consists of three parts that can be mapped to
253
      the system-user-assistant structure when using an instruction fine-tuned LLM (see Figure 2). The
254
      system prompt introduces what an asynchronous time series is, provides a description of the task
255   to be performed, and includes details about the underlying dataset. The user prompt represents the
256   input series as a comma-separated sequence of tuples (ei , ti ), where ei is the textual description of
257   the event type and ti is the inter-arrival time. The assistant prompt contains the correct event if
258   performing LLM adaptation training, or is left to be generated by the LLM during inference.
259
      More details about the exact prompts used in our experiments can be found in Appendix A.2.
260

      4.2     PARAMETER E FFICIENT LLM A DAPTATION WITH LASTS REPRESENTATION
262
263   Having established a representation of asynchronous time series for use with LLMs via LASTS, we
264   further enhance the model’s adaptability to various tasks using three different adaptation techniques:
265
266   Low Rank Adaption LoRA is a family of low-rank adaptation techniques that reduce the number
267   of trainable parameters by learning small, low-rank updates to selective model weights, allowing for
268   efficient fine-tuning of large models. We adapt the LLM backbone for our tasks by applying low-
269   rank adaptations using the LASTS representation as inputs to encode both the task and the input
      asynchronous time series.

      Soft Prompting (SP) SP involves prepending a continuous prompt to the LASTS representation,
271   which is trained through gradients from next token prediction loss. This guides the model towards
272   task-specific behavior without altering the model weights directly. (See Figure 2)
273
274
      Stochastic Soft Prompting (StoP) We propose Stochastic Soft Prompts - an enhancement of SP
275
      which learns more robust prompts by imposing a coarse-to-fine structure on the prompt tokens. (See
276   section 5.3). Similar to SP, we prepend a continuous prompt to the LASTS representation which is
277   trained through gradients from next-token prediction loss. However, in SP, the entire soft prompt
278   P of length L is used during training, while in StoP, we randomly select a prefix of the prompt P
279   for each training batch. Specifically, for each batch, we choose a prefix length l from a probability
280   distribution p(l), where l ≤ L. The soft prompt used for that batch is then represented by:
281
282
                                         Pbatch = P [: l] with l ∼ p(l)                                  (1)
283
      In our experiments, we use a uniform distribution as p. Both the forward pass and the backward pass
284   are conducted using only the selected prefix Pbatch . During inference, we use the entire learned soft
285   prompt of length L:
286
                                             Pinference = P [1 : L]                                      (2)
287
288   See figure 2 for more details. Our approach is inspired by techniques like dropout (Srivastava et al.,
289   2014) and stochastic depth (Huang et al., 2016), as well as audio models like SoundStream (Zeghi-
290   dour et al., 2021), where randomly selecting the first k codebooks during training enables better
291   generalization.
292   These adaptation techniques enable an LLM backbone to handle a variety of asynchronous time
293   series tasks, including forecasting, imputation, and anomaly detection, while maintaining parameter
294   efficiency. Details on the exact prompt representation are provided in Appendix A.4.

      Datasets. We perform experiments on two different sets of datasets: three text-based action
      datasets and five standard temporal point process datasets. The main difference is that actions are
302
      represented by words in the action datasets, whereas they are represented by indices in temporal
303
      point process datasets. The text-based action datasets are built from the action annotations of ac-
304   tivity videos. Breakfast (Kuehne et al., 2014) contains 1712 videos with 177 action classes related
305   to breakfast preparation. Each video has a sequence of events to prepare breakfast, with each event
306   containing the timestamp and the action. EPIC-KITCHENS-100 (Damen et al., 2022) is a large-
307   scale dataset in egocentric vision capturing daily activities in the kitchen over multiple days with a
308   total of 100 hours of recording. It presents more complex activity than Breakfast dataset, with rich
309   annotations of sequences of actions comprising of 97 verb classes and 300 noun classes, with 20K
310   unique narrations. MultiTHUMOS (Yeung et al., 2018) contains 400 videos with 65 action classes
311   related to human activities. Each video has a sequence of human activity events, with each event
312
      containing the timestamp and the activity. For the temporal point process datasets, we use the five
      benchmarks introduced in (Xue et al., 2024): Amazon (Ni et al., 2019) where the goal is to predict
313
      the timestamp and category (among 16 categories) of the next reviewed product, Retweet (Zhou
314
      et al., 2013) where the goal is to predict the timestamp and category (among 3 categories) of the
315   next user to retweet a post, Taxi1 where the goal is to predict the timestamp and category (among
316   10 categories) of the next pick-up or drop-off of a taxi driver, Taobao (Xue et al., 2022) where the
317   goal is to predict the timestamp and category (among 20 categories) of the item clicked by a user,
318   and StackOverflow2 where the goal is to predict the timestamp and category (among 22 categories)
319   of the next badges for a given user. We follow the same data preprocessing as in (Xue et al., 2024).
320   For each of these datasets, the semantic meaning of the event type is unknown, and only the index
321   of the event type is available. We use the index of the event type as input of our model.

      Metrics. Due to bi-modality nature of the asynchronous time series, we report separate metrics
335
      for the event type and time. We report the Macro-F1 (M-F1) (Yang, 1999) for event type prediction

      as Macro-F1 is better suited for multi-class classification tasks with skewed class distributions (see

341   Implementation details We use Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) as our LLM backbone.
342   For zero-shot experiments, we disable sampling during response generation, ensuring deterministic
343   outputs. For LLM adaptation experiments, we use QLoRA as the low rank adaptation algorithm,
344   Adam as the optimizer, and a constant learning rate of 2e−4 for QLoRA and 1e−4 for prompt tuning.
345   Following Xue et al. (2024), we split our datasets into a train/validation/test ratio of 70/10/20. Both
346   SP and StoP training are conducted for the same number of epochs. We employ early stopping based
347   on the Macro-F1 on the validation set. We report performance on the test set.
348   We use a prompt length of 400 for prompt tuning in both SP and StoP experiments. This value was
349   selected through hyperparameter tuning across all datasets and tasks, striking a balance between
350   model capacity, performance, and the compute resources available to us. Given that Llama-3-8B-
351   Instruct has a hidden dimension of 4096, this configuration results in approximately 1.6M trainable
352   parameters, which corresponds to only 0.02% of the LLM parameters. For QLoRA, we use a rank
353   of 4, resulting in a comparable number of trainable parameters (1.7M )

466   Comparison of SP and StoP learned token representations. The tokens learned by Stochastic
467   Soft Prompt (StoP) and Soft Prompt (SP) have distinct characteristics due to differences in their
468   training paradigms. To illustrate this difference, we plot the t-SNE projections of the first 100
469
      tokens from a prompt of length 400 for both StoP and SP in Figure 4. We observe that the tokens
      learned through StoP training are more spread out, indicating greater diversity, while those learned
470
      through SP training tend to cluster more closely. StoP uses a coarse-to-fine approach, where the
471
      first embeddings are more diverse to cover a large part of the space than the first embeddings trained
472   with SP. This difference is further highlighted by the cosine similarity between adjacent tokens in
473   the last plot of Figure 4: the adjacent tokens in StoP prompts have lower similarity compared to SP.
474   It allows StoP to work better than SP, even when only the first soft tokens are used (see Figure 5).
475   Using more soft tokens further improves StoP, as it gains access to more fine-grained information.
476
477   All prefixes are valid prompts in StoP The training paradigm of StoP forces all prefixes of StoP
478
      to act as valid standalone prompts, as they are used as prompts during training for some batches (if
      trained for long enough). (see Figure 5). This further strengthens our belief that tokens in StoP are
479
      arranged from coarse, independent tokens at the beginning to tokens with tokens containing finer
480
      information towards the end. See Appendix A.10 for further discussion on StoP structure.

522    Understanding StoP prompts through probing While prior work such as Lester et al. (2021)
523    attempts to interpret learned prompts by mapping them to the closest input embeddings—often
524    yielding incoherent results—we instead explore probing the LLM using the learned prompt. By
525    appending the learned prompt with a simple instruction, such as ”Tell me in as much detail as pos-
526    sible what task you are supposed to do,” we encourage the LLM to generate an output that reflects
527
       its understanding of the task. This approach allows us to gain some insight into what the model has
       summarized from the tasks and datasets it has been trained on. We present multiple model responses
528

533    We explored a novel approach to building an asynchronous time series model using an LLM, offer-
534    ing a new perspective distinct from traditional TPP methods. The method we proposed to encode an
535    asynchronous time series in a prompt suggests that the model can leverage an LLM’s world knowl-
536
       edge to perform various downstream tasks such as forecasting, anomaly detection, and imputation.
537    Furthermore, Stochastic Soft Prompt (StoP), an interpretable adaptation of soft prompt, seems to be
538    efficient in adapting a LLM to asynchronous time series data. We believe this approach could be
539    extended to other data genres like image or natural language texts. We also hope that our findings
       will open new avenues for research on asynchronous time series models.

      A.1   DATASET P REPARATION
814
      We remove any sequence in the dataset that is very small (< 4 elements). We split the dataset in a
815
      random 70/10/20 train, validation and test split. Each sequence is expanded into multiple sequences
816
      based on the task:
817
818
             • Forecasting: We convert a sequence into multiple prediction tasks. For each element of the
819            series, the prediction task is to predict the element given the preceding elements. We impose
820            a minimum and maximum length requirements on the number of preceding elements used.
821
822          • Imputation: For every element in the series, we replace the element by a mask, and the
823            imputation task is to predict the masked element given the remaining sequence.
824
             • Anomaly Detection: For every element in the sequence, we replace the action by a random
825
               different action. the anomaly detection task is to identify the element of the sequence that
826            has been tampered with.
827
828   For the three test based datasets - Breakfast, MultiTHUMOS and EPIC-KITCHENS, the event types
829   are already represented as text. The remaining 5 datasets from the temporal point processes domain
830   lack a textual component, and the event types are represented by integers. For these datasets, we
831   simply treat each integer event type as a string, allowing the LLM to process it similarly to text-based
832   data.

       A.5   BASELINES
977
978    Random Baseline To evaluate our methods on the three text-based datasets and the three tasks,
979    we establish a random baseline simulating random guesses. For forecasting and imputation, given
980    an input asynchronous time series, the baseline predicts the inter-arrival time as the average of all
981    inter-arrival times in the sequence and selects a random event type from the valid event descriptions.
982    For anomaly detection, it randomly labels an event from the series as anomalous (see Table 1).
983
984    Foundation Models for Time Series Baseline We adapted Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024), a state-
985    of-the-art foundation model designed for zero-shot forecasting on time series data, as a baseline for
986    forecasting and imputation tasks on asynchronous time series datasets. We use the largest model ver-
987    sion (amazon/chronos-t5-large) available which contains 710M model parameters. Since Chronos
988    exclusively handles numerical data, we converted our event descriptions into categorical represen-
989
       tations. Each asynchronous time series of length n was transformed into a sequence of 2n integers,
       alternating between inter-arrival times and event categories.
990
991    For forecasting, the task was framed as predicting the next two elements in this sequence given the
992    historical context. Adapting Chronos for imputation, however, required additional considerations
993    since it is inherently designed for forecasting. We reformulated the imputation task as a forecasting
994    problem: if the prefix leading up to the missing element is longer than the suffix following it, we
995
       treated imputation as forecasting the missing element using the prefix as context. Conversely, if the
       suffix is longer, we reversed the suffix and used it as context to forecast the missing element. This
996
       approach ensures the longest possible context is utilized for predicting the missing value.
997
998    It is worth noting that adapting Chronos for anomaly detection is not straightforward, as anomaly
999    detection involves identifying a single anomalous event within the series, which does not align with
1000   Chronos’ forecasting capabilities. Consequently, Chronos is provided as a baseline exclusively for
1001
       forecasting and imputation tasks.
1002
1003   LLMs for Time Series Baselines We adapted two LLM-based methods for time series: LLM-
1004
       Time (Gruver et al., 2024) and LLMProcesses (Requeima et al., 2024), as baselines. Since both
       methods are designed for numerical time series, we converted textual event descriptions into cate-
1005
       gorical representations.
1006
1007
       LLMT IME In this method, each data point is represented as a pair: (inter-arrival-time, event-
1008
       categorical). We modified the default next-token prediction behavior of the model using simple
1009
       task-specific prompts:
1010
1011          • Forecasting: Predict the next time and event.
1012
1013
              • Imputation: Find the element marked as ’MISSING.’
1014          • Anomaly Detection: Find the anomalous time and event.
1015
1016   LLMP ROCESSES This method uses in-context learning with (x, y) examples derived from a se-
1017   quence, treating the sequence as a real-valued function on a 2D space as domain. In this setup, x
1018   represents a point in 2D space (x1 , x2 ), where x1 denotes the sequence position, and x2 indicates
1019   the output type: 0 for inter-arrival time and 1 for event categorical. For a given sequence, we crafted
1020   two distinct prompts: one for predicting the event categorical and another for predicting the inter-
1021   arrival time, based on the corresponding value of x. We followed the recommended settings from
1022
       the original paper for prompt construction.
1023   However, anomaly detection does not align with this framework, as it involves identifying a single
1024   anomalous time point where the function output is 0 everywhere except at the anomaly. This makes it
1025   unsuitable for predicting function values at unseen points based on prior observations. Consequently,
       we adapted this approach exclusively for forecasting and imputation tasks.

1026
       TPP Models as Baselines We compare our best fine-tuned model configuration, LAST S +StoP ,
1027   against current state-of-the-art methods for forecasting on asynchronous time series. These methods
1028   are adapted from the benchmark study in (Xue et al., 2024). The evaluation spans eight datasets, five
1029   of which—Amazon, Retweet, Taxi, Taobao, and StackOverflow contain event categoricals without
1030   textual descriptions and are regarded as standard benchmarks for asynchronous time series analysis.
1031
       We benchmark the TPP models covered in the EasyTPP benchmark (Xue et al., 2024) on the three
1032
       textual datasets considered in our work: Breakfast, MultiTHUMOS, and EPIC KITCHEN. Since
1033   these datasets represent events as text and TPP models are not equipped to handle text directly, we
1034   converted the event names into event categoricals to make them compatible with these models.
1035

1326   A.10                       F URTHER ANALYSIS ON S TOCHASTIC S OFT P ROMPTS (S TO P)
1327
1328   In this section, we comment on the structure learned by StoP prompts and discuss the practical
1329   benefits of Stochastic Soft Prompts.
1330
1331
       Evidence for Coarse-to-Fine Structure The prompts learned through Stochastic Soft Prompts
1332   (StoP) suggest the presence of a structured coarse-to-fine hierarchy. In this structure, the first few
1333   tokens appear to encode broader task-level information, while later tokens may refine predictions by
1334   adding more detailed nuances. Below, we provide observations that support this behavior:
1335
1336                       1. t-SNE Projections: Visualizations of t-SNE projections (see Figure 10) suggest that the
1337                          first few tokens in StoP prompts may encode more diverse or independent representations,
1338                          as indicated by their wider spread in the projection space. In contrast, the later tokens tend
1339                          to cluster more closely together, potentially reflecting the refinement of previously encoded
1340                          information.
1341
                           2. Cosine Similarity: Adjacent tokens at the beginning of the StoP prompt tend to exhibit
1342
                              lower cosine similarity compared to tokens later in the prompt (see Figure 10). This pattern
1343
                              suggests more diverse information being captured at the beginning of the prompt. Standard
1344                          soft prompts, however, show uniformly high cosine similarities across all tokens, lacking
1345                          this structure.
1346
1347                       3. Prefix Validity: Figure 5 indicate that any prefix of a StoP prompt serves as a valid stan-
1348                          dalone prompt, with additional tokens refining the predictions. This behavior suggests that
1349
                              early tokens convey broad task-level information, while later tokens refine and add finer-
                              grained details.

1369   Practical Benefits of StoP    We observe that StoP offers many benefits over standard soft prompt-
1370   ing:
1371
1372         1. Improved Generalization: StoP prompts achieve better generalization compared to stan-
1373            dard soft prompts, with an average improvement of 12.69% in Macro-F1 across all
1374            datasets (Breakfast, MultiTHUMOS, and EPIC KITCHENS) and tasks (Forecast, Impu-
1375            tation, Anomaly Detection) (see Table 6)
1376         2. Faster Training: The stochastic nature of StoP reduces training time by approximately
1377            25%, making it more efficient than standard soft prompting.
1378         3. Resource Efficiency: StoP enables flexible deployment in resource-constrained environ-
1379            ments. Longer trained StoP prompts can be truncated to prefixes as needed, allowing for
1380            adaptable inference without compromising performance.
