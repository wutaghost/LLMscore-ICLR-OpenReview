009                                                                             A BSTRACT
010
011               Dataset distillation has demonstrated strong performance on simple datasets like
012               CIFAR, MNIST, and TinyImageNet but struggles to achieve similar results in more
013               complex scenarios. In this paper, we propose EDF (emphasizes the discriminative
014               features), a dataset distillation method that enhances key discriminative regions in
015               synthetic images using Grad-CAM activation maps. Our approach is inspired by a
016               key observation: in simple datasets, high-activation areas typically occupy most of
017               the image, whereas in complex scenarios, the size of these areas is much smaller.
018
                  Unlike previous methods that treat all pixels equally when synthesizing images,
                  EDF uses Grad-CAM activation maps to enhance high-activation areas. From a
019
                  supervision perspective, we downplay supervision signals that have lower losses,
020
                  as they contain common patterns. Additionally, to help the DD community better
021               explore complex scenarios, we build the Complex Dataset Distillation (Comp-DD)
022               benchmark by meticulously selecting sixteen subsets, eight easy and eight hard,
023               from ImageNet-1K. In particular, EDF consistently outperforms SOTA results in
024               complex scenarios, such as ImageNet-1K subsets. Hopefully, more researchers
025               will be inspired and encouraged to improve the practicality and efficacy of DD.
026               Our code and benchmark will be made public.

047   1    I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Dataset Distillation (DD) has been making remarkable progress since it was first proposed by Wang
050   et al. (2020). Currently, the mainstream of DD is matching-based methods (Zhao et al., 2021; Zhao &
051   Bilen, 2021b;a; Cazenavette et al., 2022), which first extract patterns from the real dataset, then define
052   different types of supervision to inject extracted patterns into the synthetic data. On several simple
053   benchmarks, such as CIFAR (Krizhevsky, 2009) and TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015), existing
      matching-based DD methods can achieve lossless performance (Guo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).

054
      However, there is still a long way to go before DD can be practically used in real-world applications,
055   i.e., images in complex scenarios are characterized by significant variations in object sizes and the
056   presence of a large amount of class-irrelevant information. To show that current DD methods fail to
057   achieve satisfying performance in complex scenarios, we apply trajectory matching (Guo et al., 2024)
058   on a 10-class subset from ImageNet-1K extracted by selecting similar classes of CIFAR-10, called
059   IN1K-CIFAR-10. As depicted in Figure 1a, under three compressing ratios, DD’s performances1 on
060   IN1K-CIFAR-10 are consistently worse than those on CIFAR-10.
061   To figure out the reason behind the above results, we take a closer look at the ImageNet-1K and
062   CIFAR-10 from the data perspective. One key observation is that the percentage of discriminative
063   features in the complex scenario, which can be visualized by Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2016),
064   is much lower. From Figure 1b, CIFAR-10 images are mostly sticker-like, and activation maps
065   have higher means and larger highly activated areas. By contrast, activation maps of the IN1K-
066   CIFAR-10 subset exhibit much lower activation means and smaller highly activated areas. Previous
067   methods (Du et al., 2022; Khaki et al., 2024) treat all pixels of synthetic images equally. Therefore,
068   when applying these methods to more complex scenarios, the large ratio of low-activation areas leads
069
      to non-discriminative features dominating the learning process, resulting in a drop in performance.

      2     M ETHOD
138
      Our approach, Emphasize Discriminative Features (EDF), enhances discriminative features in
139
      synthetic images during distillation. As shown in Figure 3, EDF first trains trajectories on real images
140
      T and synthetic images S and computes the trajectory matching loss. Then, Common Pattern Dropout
141   filters out low-loss supervision signals, retaining high-loss ones for backpropagation. After obtaining
142   gradients for the synthetic images, Discriminative Area Enhancement uses dynamically extracted
143   Grad-CAM activation maps to rescale pixel gradients, focusing updates on discriminative regions.
144
145   2.1   C OMMON PATTERN D ROPOUT
146
147   This module reduces common patterns in supervision by matching expert and student trajectories on
148   real and synthetic data, then removing low-loss elements. This ensures only meaningful supervision
149   enhances the model’s ability to capture discriminative features.
150
      Trajectory Generation and Loss Computation. To generate expert and student trajectories, we first
151   train agent models on real data for E epochs, saving the resulting parameters as expert trajectories,
152   denoted by {θt }E0 . At each distillation iteration, we randomly select an initial point θt and a target
153   point θt+M from these expert trajectories. Similarly, student trajectories are produced by initializing
154   an agent model at θt and training it on the synthetic dataset, yielding the parameters {θ̂t }N  0 . The
155   trajectory matching loss is then computed by comparing the final student parameters θ̂t+N with the
156   expert’s target parameters θt+M , normalized by the initial difference:
157

175   2.2   D ISCRIMINATIVE A REA E NHANCEMENT
176
      After the pruned loss from Common Pattern Dropout is backpropagated, this module amplifies the
177
      importance of discriminative regions in synthetic images. Grad-CAM activation maps are dynamically
178   extracted from the synthetic data to highlight areas most relevant for classification. These activation
179   maps are then used to rescale the pixel gradients, applying a weighted update that prioritizes highly
180   activated regions, thereby focusing the learning process on key discriminative features.
181

183   gradients that flow into the final convolutional layer, highlighting key areas relevant for predicting
184

194   et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024) on the complex scenario is that they treat each pixel

236   3     C OMPLEX DD B ENCHMARK
237
238
      We introduce the Complex Dataset Distillation (Comp-DD) benchmark, which is constructed by
239
      selecting subsets from ImageNet-1K based on their complexity. This benchmark represents an
240
      early and pioneering effort to address dataset distillation in complex scenarios. Although there are
241   numerous benchmarks (Krizhevsky, 2009; Le & Yang, 2015; Cui et al., 2022b) for simpler tasks,
242   there is a notable absence of benchmarks designed specifically for complex scenarios. This gap
243   presents a significant challenge to advancing research in this area and limits the practical application
244   of dataset distillation. To bridge this gap, we propose the first dataset distillation benchmark explicitly
245   built around scenario complexity, aiming to promote further exploration within the DD community.
246
247
      Complexity Metrics. We evaluate the complexity of an image by measuring the average size of
      high-activation regions of the Grad-CAM activation map. Using a pre-trained ResNet model, we first
248
      generate Grad-CAM activation maps for all images, class by class. For each image, we calculate the
249
      percentage of pixels with activation values above a predefined threshold (set to 0.5 in our case), with
250   higher percentages indicating lower complexity (more clarifications
                                                                   P P can bei found in Appendix D.2).
251   Formally, the complexity of the i-th image is computed as h w 1[Mh,w            ≥ 0.5] where 1 is the
252   indicator function. The complexity of each class is then determined by averaging the complexity
253   scores across all images within that class.
254
255   Subset Selection. To reduce the influence of class differences, we select subsets from each category,
256   where a category consists of classes representing visually similar objects or animals of the same
257
      species. This approach allows us to focus on complexity while controlling for inter-class variability.
258   Specifically, we first manually identify representative categories in ImageNet-1K with sufficient
259   numbers of classes (≥ 20). For each category, we rank the classes by complexity in descending order.
260   Following established practice, we construct two ten-class subsets for each category: the easy subset,
261   comprising the ten least complex classes, and the hard subset, comprising the ten most complex
262   classes. The subset-level complexity is determined by averaging the complexity scores across all
263
      classes in each subset.
264   Statistics. We carefully selected eight categories from ImageNet-1K: Bird, Car, Dog, Fish, Snake,
265   Insect, Round, and Music. Each category contains two ten-class subsets: one easy and one hard, with
266   difficulty determined by the complexity metrics outlined above. Figure 4a summarizes the number of
267   training images in each subset, while all subsets contain 500 images in the validation set. To illustrate
268   the difference between easy and hard subsets, Figure 4b compares the subset-level complexity for
269   each category. As expected, the hard subsets display significantly higher complexity than the easy
      subsets. For a detailed breakdown of the classes in each subset, please refer to Appendix D.1.

      (ConvNetD5) as both the distillation and the evaluation architecture. For cross-architecture evaluation

      SRe2L (Yin et al., 2023) and RDED (Sun et al., 2023). The results for subsets not covered in these

404   Frequency of Activation Map Update. To accurately capture the evolving discriminative features in
405   synthetic images, EDF dynamically updates the Grad-CAM activation maps at a predefined frequency.
406   The choice of update frequency should be adjusted based on the IPC to achieve optimal performance.
407   As shown in Table 5b, larger IPCs benefit from a lower update frequency, as the pixel learning rate is
408   set lower for more stable distillation. In contrast, smaller IPCs require a higher update frequency to
409   effectively adapt to the faster changes in the synthetic images during training.
410   This trend is influenced by the pixel learning rate: larger IPCs can use lower rates to ensure smooth
411   convergence, making frequent updates unnecessary. Smaller IPCs, with limited data capacity, require
412   higher learning rates and more frequent updates to quickly adapt to changes in discriminative areas.
413   Thus, selecting the appropriate update frequency is essential for balancing stability and adaptability
414   in the distillation process, depending on dataset size and complexity.

      Strategies for Discriminative Area Enhancement. The Discriminative Area Enhancement (DAE)
416
      component involves two key factors: the enhancement factor β and the threshold for activation maps.
417
      Ablation studies (Table 6a) show that the best performance is achieved when β is between 1 and
418   2. When β < 1, some discriminative areas are diminished rather than enhanced, as their gradient
419   weights become < 1. Conversely, excessively large β values (≥ 10) lead to overemphasis on certain
420   areas, distorting the overall learning process (see Appendix C.3 for examples of this distortion).
421   Therefore, β should be reasonably controlled to balance the emphasis on discriminative regions.
422
      Regarding the threshold for activation maps, using the mean activation value as a dynamic threshold
423
      results in better performance compared to using a fixed threshold. This is because the mean adapts
424   to the evolving activation maps during training, whereas a fixed threshold risks either emphasizing
425   low-activation areas if set too low or omitting key discriminative features if set too high.

490   6    R ELATED W ORK
491
492   Approaches. Dataset Distillation (DD) aims to create compact datasets that maintain performance
493   levels comparable to full-scale datasets. It can be applied in practical fields such as continual learning
494   (Masarczyk & Tautkute, 2020; Rosasco et al., 2021), privacy preservation (Dong et al., 2022; Yu et al.,
495   2023), and neural architecture search (Jin et al., 2018; Pasunuru & Bansal, 2019). Approaches in DD
496
      can be categorized into two primary approaches: matching-based and knowledge-distillation-based.
497   1) Matching-based approaches are foundational in DD research, focusing on aligning synthetic
498   data with real datasets by capturing essential patterns. Landmark works like gradient matching
499   (DC) (Zhao et al., 2021), distribution matching (DM) (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a), and trajectory matching
500   (MTT) (Cazenavette et al., 2022) extract critical metrics from real datasets, then replicate these
501   metrics in synthetic data. Subsequent research has refined these methods, improving the fidelity
502
      of distilled datasets (Zhao & Bilen, 2021b; Wang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2022b;
      Liu et al., 2023a;b; Cazenavette et al., 2023; Sajedi et al., 2023; Khaki et al., 2024). Data selection
503
      techniques have been integrated to synthesize more representative samples (Xu et al., 2023; Sundar
504
      et al., 2023; Lee & Chung, 2024). Recent advancements optimize distillation for different image-per-
505   class (IPC) settings, balancing dataset size and information retention (Du et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
506   2023; Guo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Lee & Chung, 2024). Moreover, soft labels have been widely
507   applied to improve the performance (Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021; Cui et al., 2022a; Qin et al.,
508   2024; Yu et al., 2024). Despite these improvements, most matching-based approaches treat all pixels
509   uniformly, failing to emphasize discriminative regions and often overlooking distinctions between
510   supervision signals, limiting their effectiveness on complex datasets like ImageNet-1K.
511   2) Knowledge-distillation-based approaches take an alternative route by aligning teacher-student
512   model outputs when evaluating distilled datasets. Notable examples include SRe2L (Yin et al., 2023)
513   and RDED (Sun et al., 2023), where the student model is trained by aligning outputs with outputs of
514   a teacher model on the same batch of synthetic data, specifically by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler
515   (KL) divergence between the student’s predictions and the teacher’s output. In our work, we adopt
516   knowledge distillation as a validation strategy for fair comparisons.
517
      Benchmarks. DD research has mainly focused on simpler datasets such as CIFAR (Krizhevsky,
518
      2009), TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015), and DC-BENCH (Cui et al., 2022b). These datasets contain
519
      a high proportion of class-specific information, enabling DD methods to extract and synthesize dis-
520   criminative features more easily. However, research in more complex scenarios has been limited. To
521   address this, we propose the Comp-DD benchmark, which systematically explores dataset distillation
522   complexity by curating subsets from ImageNet-1K with varying degrees of difficulty. This benchmark
523   provides a more rigorous evaluation framework, facilitating deeper exploration of DD in complex,
524   real-world settings and encouraging further advances in the field.
525
526
      7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   We introduced Emphasize Discriminative Features (EDF), a dataset distillation method that enhances
529   class-specific regions in synthetic images. EDF addresses two key limitations of prior methods:
530   i) enhancing discriminative regions in synthetic images using Grad-CAM activation maps, and ii)
531   filtering out low-loss signals that embed common patterns through Common Pattern Dropout (CPD)
532   and Discriminative Area Enhancement (DAE). EDF achieves state-of-the-art results across ImageNet-
533   1K subsets, including lossless performance on several of them. We also proposed the Comp-DD
534   benchmark, designed to evaluate dataset distillation in both simple and complex settings.
535
      Limitations and Future Work. EDF dynamically updates Grad-CAM activation maps of synthetic
536
      images according to an update frequency. This may introduce extra computation, especially when the
537   IPC is large. Also, we only use Grad-CAM to evaluate discriminative areas of an image in this work.
538   In the future, other indicators that can identify discriminative features of an image can be used jointly
539   to include more perspectives.

997    D.2    C OMPLEXITY M ETRICS
998
999
       We use the percentage of pixels whose Grad-CAM activation values exceed a predefined fixed
       threshold to evaluate the complexity of an image. In our settings, the fixed threshold is 0.5. The
1000
       reasons for fixing the threshold at 0.5 are twofold. Firstly, when selecting subsets, images are static
1001
       and won’t be updated in any form (this is different from EDF’s DAE module, which updates synthetic
1002   images). Thus, using a fixed threshold is sufficient for determining the high-activation areas.
1003
1004   Secondly, values of a Grad-CAM activation map range from 0 to 1, with higher values corresponding
1005
       to higher activation. We present the distribution of the activation map of a random image from
       ImageNet-1K in Figure 13b, where the majority of pixels have activation values between 0.25 and
1006
       0.75. Subsequently, if the threshold is too small or too large, the complexity scores of all classes
1007
       will be close (standard deviation is small), as shown in Figure 12 and 13. This results in no clear
1008   distinguishment between easy and hard subsets. Finally, we set 0.5 as the threshold, which is the
1009   middle point of the range. Complexity distribution under this threshold is shown in Figure 10.
1010
1011
       Our complexity metrics are an early effort to define how complex an image is in the context of
       dataset distillation. We acknowledge potential biases or disadvantages and encourage future studies
1012
       to continue the refinement of complex metrics.
