011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a novel method for Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), incorpo-
014            rating the convex property of the value function over the belief space in Partially
015
               Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs). We introduce hard- and soft-
               enforced convexity as two different approaches, and compare their performance
016
               against standard DRL on two well-known POMDP environments, namely the
017
               Tiger and FieldVisionRockSample problems. Our findings show that including
018            the convexity feature can substantially increase performance of the agents, as well
019            as increase robustness over the hyperparameter space, especially when testing on
020            out-of-distribution domains.
021

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) have become the standard formalism for solving sequential
026   decision making problems (Van Otterlo & Wiering, 2012). For applications in which perfect ob-
027
      servability cannot be assumed, MDPs can be extended to model the probabilistic decision process as
      a Partially Observable MDP (POMDP), which provides an efficient framework for optimal decision
028
      making under uncertainty (Kochenderfer, 2015; Kaelbling et al., 1998; Shani et al., 2013; Braziunas,
029
      2003; Walraven & Spaan, 2019; Oliehoek et al., 2008; Cassandra et al., 1994).
030
031   Classical dynamic programming (DP) and reinforcement learning RL are the two established solu-
032   tion methods for MDP and POMDPs. RL approaches are used to overcome the curse of dimension-
033
      ality of DP methods, and additionally do not require a model of the environment (Kochenderfer,
      2015). Neural network (NN) based deep RL (DRL) applied to MDPs has been particularly success-
034
      ful, even in high dimensional problem settings (e.g., Mnih et al., 2013; 2015; Silver et al., 2016;
035
      2018). Solving POMDPs is a more difficult task, but one approach is to directly handle the noisy
036   observation-action history (or a variant thereof) (Koutas et al., 2024; Hettegger et al., 2023; Arcieri
037   et al., 2023). In cases, where no environment model is available, this is the only option. If one
038   has an environment model, then beliefs, i.e., a probability distribution over all system states, can
039   be computed. Using beliefs can be computationally beneficial (Arcieri et al., 2023; Koutas et al.,
040   2024); however, most current approaches do not use DRL with beliefs. In this work, we propose
041   to extend belief-based DRL solutions for POMDPs, by taking into account the theoretical property
042   that the optimal value function is convex over the belief space. We hypothesize that introducing this
043   informative property in the training process enables faster learning, and leads to better performance
044
      in out-of-distribution (OOD) domains.
045   We propose two approaches for convexity-enforcement of the value function in the training process,
046   namely soft-enforced and hard-enforced convexity. The performance of convexity-informed DRL
047   is examined for both convexity approaches applied to the Dueling Q-Network (Wang et al., 2016)
048   value-based architecture. Two benchmarks are used for performance comparison, which are the
049   classic Tiger (Kaelbling et al., 1998) and FieldVisionRockSample (FVRS) (Ross et al., 2007) prob-
050
      lems. We show that when trained with the convexity modification, the involved NNs have stronger
      generalization performance compared to the standard training schedule and, in some cases, better
051
      training performance.
052

      Learning convex functions has a rich history in machine learning. However, the techniques devel-

158   4.1   C ONVEXITY CONDITIONS FOR MULTI - DIMENSIONAL FUNCTIONS
159
      A function f : Rn −→ R is convex if its domain is a convex set and for all u, v in its domain, and
160   all t ∈ [0, 1], we have (Ahmadi & Hall, 2016)
161
                                   f (tu + (1 − t)v) ≤ tf (u) + (1 − t)f (v)                            (11)

162
      If f is differentiable, one can define an alternative condition of convexity, which is equivalent to
163   Equation 11 (Ahmadi & Hall, 2016):
164
165
                          f (u) + ∇u f (u)T (v − u) ≤ f (v), for all u, v ∈ dom(f ).                    (12)
166   If f is twice differentiable, the one can define yet another condition of convexity which is equivalent
167   to Equations 11 and 12 (Ahmadi & Hall, 2016):
168                                 0 ⪯ H(f )(u) = ∇2u f (u), for all u ∈ dom(f ),                      (13)
169   i.e., the Hessian matrix H(f )(u) must be positive semi-definite. Equation 11 defines convexity in
170   terms of the function value at different points, whereas Equations 12 and 13 define convexity via the
171   first and second derivatives.

268
269   With numerical experiments, we test if enforcing convexity in the value function can improve the
      performance of DRL. Specifically, we test the following two hypotheses:

271
272   H2: Convexity-informed DRL performs better in out-of-distribution domains due to improved ex-

315   We test the Tiger problem for all versions of enforced convexity (hard, point, grad and hess) as well

321
      is outlined in Section B.1.

323   The test of H1 for the best hyperparameters yields no difference between the standard DRL approach
      and our proposed methods. This is due to the simplicity of the problem, where the majority of agents

      finds the optimal policy in the given amount of steps; thus, the mean performance over multiple seeds

326
327   To test H2 for the best hyperparameters, we train the agents on a specific observation accuracy
328
      and cross-evaluate their performance on different observation accuracies. Since again, the major-
      ity of the hyperparameters converge to the optimal solution, we perform the cross-evaluation over
329
      all optimal agents. We observe that for agents trained on pobs = {0.6, 0.8, 0.9} there was no no-
330
      ticeable difference in the cross-evaluation performance between the individual convexity methods.
331   Our explanation for this is that the optimal policy is characterized by two transition points from the
332   action ’listen’ to ’open-left’/’open-right’. Since these transition points lie very close to each other
333   for pobs ∈ (0.5, 1.0) the methods do not have to perform a large extrapolation, which leads to al-

335   and b = 1.0 during training; thus, the agent does not know the location of the policy transition.

339
      enforced methods. This is reflected by lower medians (blue lines) and/or worse interquartile ranges.

381   Furthermore, for FVRS we use the LReLU activation functions, as we noticed that during training
382
      when the method converges to a stable policy (e.g., always go left), the weights of the hidden layers
      become high and negative. This ensures that the output is always -1 after passing through multiple
383
      ELU layers which yields the same Q-value for every possible combination of inputs. As a result,
384
      the method is stuck in this local minimum. To avoid this saturation, we switch to LReLU activation
385   functions for the FVRS problem.
386
387   Moreover, we do hyperparameter searches for all convexity methods for the default (def) and heavi-
388
      side (heavi) observation functions. We report the performances on the originally trained environment
      as well as the cross-evaluations on other observation functions in the same figures. The results over
389
      all samples are reported in Section B.2, whereas the performances of the hyperparameters over 10
390
      runs for are shown in Figures 3 and 4, respectively. When trained on the default setting (Figure
391   3), both convexity approaches perform better than standard DRL, both on the original and all OOD
392   domains. On the other hand, when trained on the heaviside observation function, the gradient-based
393   approach emerges as the single clear winner over all observation functions.

421   7   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
422
423   In this work, we propose to extend DRL by enforcing the belief-convexity of the value function in
424   the training process. We have shown that convexity-enforced DRL can yield notable improvements
425   compared to the standard approach, such as better robustness over the hyperparameter space, as
426   well as better mean performance of the best hyperparameters. Our approach performs particularly
427   well when trained on edge case problems (pobs = 1.0 for Tiger and pobs = heavi for FVRS) and
428
      applying the policy to the standard problem formulation counterpart. This suggests that a well-
      behaved extrapolation of the value function leads to better policies when encountering OOD-data.
429
430   Based on the results in this work, we recommend the usage of the gradient-based enforcement, as
431   it was better or at least equally good compared to the standard and point-based approach in every
      investigated setting.

787
788   The Tiger problem (Kaelbling et al., 1998) consists of an agent standing in front of two doors.
789
      Behind one of the doors there is a tiger (T ), behind the other there is no tiger (T̄ ), and the agent does
      not know where the tiger is. At each timestep the agent can decide whether he wants to open one of
790
      the doors with the actions ’open-right’ / ’open-left’ (OR / OL ) or perform a listening (L) action. By
791
      listening, the agent hears the tiger roaring behind its true location with probability pobs ∈ [0.5, 1.0].
792
793   Opening the door with the tiger behind yields a reward of r(T ) = −100, opening the other door
794   incurs r(T̄ ) = 10. Listening on the other hand, costs r(L) = −1. After opening a door, the
795
      environment resets with a new random tiger location.
796   The environment state of the Tiger problem can be fully described with a scalar value denoting, e.g.,
797   the belief of the tiger being behind the left door b ∈ [0.0, 1.0]. In general, the optimal policy depends
798   on the horizon h, i.e., the number of times the game is played. For the special cases of h = 1 and
799   h → ∞, the optimal policy collapses to a relatively simple one. It consists of listening at every
800   timestep until the belief that the tiger is at a given door falls below a certain optimal belief threshold
801
      bopt , or the belief surpasses 1 − bopt . For h = 1, the associated optimal belief threshold b1opt can be
      obtained by considering only the immediate expected rewards. The agent should open the door if
802

      D     FVRS PROBLEM
865
866
      D.1    FVRS DESCRIPTION
867
868   The FieldVisionRockSample (Smith & Simmons, 2012; Ross et al., 2007) problem is defined by
869   a tuple (n, k), where n defines the width of a square grid and k the number of rocks distributed
870   randomly in the grid. The state of each rock is either good (GR) or bad (BR). The actions available
871   to the agent at each timestep are to move north (MN), south (MS), east (ME), west (MW), or to
872   perform a sampling (C) action. The starting point of the agent is randomly sampled along the west
873
      boundary of the grid, and there is an exit zone situated along the east boundary of the grid.
874   Upon reaching the exit zone the agent receives the reward rE = 10. When the agent is located in
875   the same grid cell as a rock, it can sample it. If the rock is good, the agent receives rGR = 10; if
876   it is bad, the agent receives rBR = −10. Otherwise, performing the sampling action with no rock
877   present incurs a reward of 0. In addition, we punish the agent if it wants to move out of the grid at the
878   west, north and south boundary. These illegal moves are associated with a reward of rIM = −10.
879

939   E     T RAINING SPECIFICATIONS
940
941   We first start with a large number of hyperparameters and a broad range (i.e., multiple orders of
942   magnitude) of possible values. For each convexity method, we then sample this space of possible
943   hyperparameters with a fixed number of samples, which we call training runs, and let the agents
944   train for a fixed number of timesteps (no early stopping). The sampling of the hyperparameter space
945   is conducted via via Bayesian hyperparameter tuning (Shukla, 2023), which empirically finds better
946   hyperparameters compared to their more prominent counterparts, namely grid and random search
947   (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012). At the end of each run, we evaluate the policies of the respective agents.
948
      In the general case, we estimate the achieved expected sum of discounted rewards with Monte Carlo
      simulation. For the Tiger problem, an optimal solution is available with classical methods; hence, we
949
      can additionally use the Kullback-Leibner divergence to filter for agents which achieved the optimal
950
      policy (e.g., in Figure 2).
951
952   Since the agents usually find the optimal solution when trained for a sufficiently large number of
953   steps, we heavily restrict the number of available steps for each training run. A few training runs
954
      were used for an initial estimate of the speed of convergence and a fraction of that was used for the
      cutoff. Based on this small pre-analysis, the maximum number of training steps is chosen as 5,000
955
      and 50,000 for the Tiger and FVRS problems, respectively.
956
957   For the Tiger problem, we consider the infinite-horizon stationary policy. With a discount factor of
958   γ = 0.9, the rollout depth dT was chosen as 150, where the reward’s contribution to the sum of
959   discounted rewards is in the order of 10−5 . On the other hand, for the FVRS problem, we search for
960
      the policy which maximizes the expected rewards over a game instance, i.e., from the starting point
      until the robot reaches the end zone. We restrict the maximum rollout depth to dF V RS = n2 · k.
961
      Hence, this defines a limit policy of visiting every grid cell and sampling every rock.
