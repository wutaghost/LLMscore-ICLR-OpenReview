009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The accurate forecasting of financial time series remains a significant challenge
012            due to the stochastic nature of the underlying data. To improve prediction accu-
013            racy, feature engineering has become a vital aspect of forecasting financial assets.
014            However, engineering features manually often requires domain expertise. We pro-
015
               pose to utilise an automated feature generation architecture, Contrastive Predictive
               Coding (CPC), to generate embeddings as input to improve the performance of
016
               downstream financial time series forecasting models. To benchmark the effective-
017
               ness of our approach, we evaluate forecasting models on predicting the next day’s
018            log return on various foreign exchange markets with and without embeddings. Fi-
019            nally, we assess our CPC architecture by employing the same trained encoder on
020            different currency pairs and calculating the Sharpe ratio of our strategies.
021
022
023   1     I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Analysing financial time series is an integral component of stock trading, risk management and
026
      econometrics. Auto-regressive integrated moving averages and linear regression are traditionally
      used and serve as the foundation for many modern forecasting techniques. However, these methods
027
      often falter when confronted with the highly volatile and stochastic nature of financial datasets,
028
      where the assumptions of stationarity and linearity do not hold (1).
029
030   Advancements in machine learning have allowed for more complex temporal patterns to be captured
031   for both analysis and forecasting of time series (2). Deep Learning (DL) and neural networks enable
032
      the extraction of complex patterns and representations which have the potential to enhance predictive
      accuracy. Notably, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks have been shown to be able to
033
      forecast time series accurately to a degree (3).
034
035   LSTMs still struggle when attempting to forecast non-deterministic time series. This is partially
036   due to the large amount of data required for the LSTM to learn more complex patterns. Feature
037   engineering is typically utilised to supplement models with further data but this is a time-consuming
038
      step that requires extensive domain knowledge and can introduce bias.
039   We propose to implement Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) architecture as presented by Aaron
040   van den Oord et al. to extract features from financial time series (4). To assess the performance of
041   this approach, linear regression and LSTM models are trained with and without the CPC-generated
042   embeddings and compared based on accuracy metrics. Several baselines models such as the persis-
043   tence model and naive mean model are implemented as a baseline. By conducting this comparative
044
      analysis, we aim to evaluate the relative performance and potential advantages of CPC in high-
      quality embeddings based unpredictable and highly variable stochastic financial time series.
045

      1.1   L ITERATURE OVERVIEW
047
048   (4) introduces CPC as an innovative approach for unsupervised learning. CPC leverages a self-
049   supervised learning framework that predicts future observations in a latent space while contrasting
050   correct futures against negative samples. This approach enables the extraction of meaningful pat-
051   terns from large datasets without the need for extensive labelled data. CPC’s strength lies in its
052   ability to learn compact and informative representations, making it particularly useful for applica-
053   tions where labelled data is scarce. However, CPC was experimented on a deterministic environment
      and has yet to be applied successfully to financial time series that exhibit stochastic behaviour.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (5) proposes a novel deep learning framework, LSTNet, for multivariate time series forecasting.
055   LSTNet attempts to capture both short-term local dependencies and long-term patterns in time se-
056   ries data utilising convolutional neural networks and recurrent neural networks in combination. In
057   addition, a recurrent-skip mechanism along with an autoregressive component is implemented for
058   the handling of long-term dependencies and scaling issues. Despite the model outperforming bench-
059   marks on certain non-stochastic datasets, it failed to statistically outperform baselines on the one
060   financial dataset it was tested on.
061   (6) introduces a novel approach called Time-series Representational Learning through CPC (TRL-
062   CPC) for detecting anomalies in multivariate time series data. The proposed method presents an
063   effective way to learn representations using the CPC architecture and a non-linear transformation
064   function. The datasets utilised in this study are not similar to financial time series data, which
065   are notoriously more difficult to predict due to having higher levels of randomness and volatility.
066   Furthermore, the dataset used includes more than one feature, which makes evaluating the CPC
067   architecture’s impact on the results more difficult.
068   The lack of evaluating automated features generated by the CPC architecture in a stochastic envi-
069   ronment is what forms the basis of our research.

222   2.5     C ONTRASTIVE P REDICTIVE C ODING (CPC)
223
224   CPC is an unsupervised representation learning approach designed to “learn the representations
225
      that encode the underlying shared information between different parts of the (high-dimensional)
      signal” (4). Unlike traditional supervised learning methods, CPC generates embeddings that capture
226
      the temporal structure and dependencies inherent in time series data.
227
228
229
      2.5.1    OVERVIEW OF C ONTRASTIVE P REDICTIVE C ODING
230   The CPC’s architecture’s objective is to maximise the mutual information between a context vector
231   and future observations in a latent space. The model consists of an encoder network that transforms
232   raw inputs into a lower-dimensional representation and a context network that aggregates these
233   representations over time to predict future sequences (4).
234
      The CPC architecture implemented in this study is tailored for financial time series data and utilises
235
      windows as data points. The flow of data through the CPC model can be described as follows:
236
237           • Encoder Network: denoted as fθ , maps each window of raw input data,
238             Xi = (xi,1 , xi,2 , . . . , xi,T ), where i denotes the window index and T is the window length,
239             into a lower-dimensional latent representation zi . This process is represented by the equa-
240             tion:
241                                                           zi = fθ (Xi )                                  (7)
242
243             where zi ∈ Rd is the latent representation of window i, and fθ is a function parameterised
244             by θ (in our case a series of one-dimensional convolutional layers followed by a dense
245             layer).
246           • Context Network: denoted as gϕ , aggregates the sequence of window embeddings
247             {z1 , z2 , . . . , zn } to produce a single context vector c defined as:
248
249                                                 c = gϕ (z1 , z2 , . . . , zn )                          (8)
250
251             where c ∈ Rh and gϕ is a function parameterised by ϕ, implemented using a Gated Re-
252             current Unit (GRU). The GRU only returns the final hidden state to output a single context
253
                vector that has aggregated information from all window embeddings.
254           • Contrastive Objective: is to maximise the similarity between the context vector c and the
255             true future latent representation zn+k , while minimising the similarity with the negative
256             sample. We do this by minimising the binary cross-entropy loss function where the pre-
257             dicted probability ŷ for the positive sample being the correct future latent representation is
258             computed as:
259                                                 ŷ = σ (mean(c · zn+k ))                                (9)
260
261
                where σ denotes the sigmoid function. The binary cross-entropy loss, L, is then computed
                as:
262
                                              N
263                                        1 X
                                   L=−           (yi log(ŷi ) + (1 − yi ) log(1 − ŷi ))           (10)
264                                       N i=1
265
266             where yi represents the binary label (1 for positive samples, 0 for negative samples), and ŷi
267             represents the predicted probability obtained from the sigmoid activation.
268

      2.5.2   DATA G ENERATION FOR CPC
271
272   The generation of negative samples is arguably the most important aspect of the CPC architecture. If
273   negative samples are too easy to differentiate between positive samples, the model will fail to create
274   relevant and predictive embeddings. In contrast, if negative samples are too similar to the positive
275   samples, the model will fail to learn and high-quality embeddings will not be generated. Therefore,
276   a custom data generator was developed to create batches of data specifically for contrastive learning.
277   This generator produces sequences of time windows, with each batch containing both positive and
278   negative pairs, along with corresponding labels indicating whether a given pair is a true or false
279
      match.
280
281   Positive Sample Generation: For each index or window in the time series data, the positive sam-
282   ple represents the next consecutive window of the time series.
283
      The following pseudo-code illustrates the process for generating positive samples:
284
285
      FOR each position i IN data LENGTH - (timesteps * (n_windows + 1)):
286
          SET context_windows TO empty list
287
          FOR each w IN range(n_windows):
288           SET window TO data SLICE from
289               (i+(w*timesteps)) TO (i + (w + 1) * timesteps)
290           APPEND window TO context_windows
291       SET y_positive TO data SLICE from
292           (i+(n_windows*timesteps)) TO (i+(n_windows+1)*timesteps)
293   END FOR
294
295
      Negative Sample Generation: Conventional CPC methodologies typically involve selecting ran-
296   dom samples from different sequences or distant parts of the same sequence. In our approach,
297   negative samples are generated using a normal distribution of random noise based on the parameters
298   of the input window. This is based on the Black-Scholes framework which models financial time
299   series as a stochastic process (10). Generating negative samples in this way allows for the model to
300   learn to differentiate from random noise, therefore creating high-quality embeddings.
301
      To generate a negative sample, a random normal distribution is created:
302
303
                                          ynegative ∼ N (µynegative , σynegative )                     (11)
304
305
306   where µynegative and σynegative are the mean and standard deviation of the input window.
307   The pseudo-code for generating negative samples is as follows:
308
309
      FOR each position i IN data LENGTH - (timesteps * (n_windows + 1)):
310       SET context_windows TO empty list
311       FOR each w IN range(n_windows):
312           SET window TO data SLICE from
313               (i + w * timesteps) TO (i + (w + 1) * timesteps)
314           APPEND window TO context_windows
315       SET y_negative_base TO data SLICE from
316           (i + (n_windows - 1) * timesteps) TO (i + n_windows * timesteps)
317       GENERATE y_negative USING Gaussian noise WITH
318
              mean(y_negative_base) AND std(y_negative_base)
      END FOR
319
320
      This negative sampling strategy introduces noise that mimics random characteristics of financial
321
      time series, forcing the CPC model to learn patterns that are more predictive than randomness.

      3.2   LSTM M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
389
      Despite the consensus that LSTMs are designed to avoid the gradient vanishing problem and should
390   be able to capture temporal dependencies (9) , the results suggest that the LSTM model struggles to
391   learn meaningful features from the USDJPY time series data. This can be attributed to the following
392   factors:
393
394          • Financial time series are inherently noisy and are highly stochastic. This is due to a variety
395            of factors (geopolitical events, macroeconomic indicators, etc.) that together contribute to
396            an almost random behaviour. LSTM models require a substantial amount of data or features
397            to learn effectively and therefore struggled with our task.
398          • LSTM architectures have more parameters than their non-neural network counterparts. If
399            the number of parameters is significantly larger than the number of data points, overfitting
400            has a high probability of occuring. This concern is exacerbated further when an LSTM is
401            trained on noisy data.
402
403   3.3   L INEAR R EGRESSION M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
404
405
      The LR model, despite its simplicity and significantly fewer parameters compared to the LSTM
      model, outperforms the LSTM in forecasting the USDJPY. This surprising result can be explained
406
      by several reasons:
407
408
             • Linear Regression, being a simpler model with fewer parameters, is less prone to overfitting
409            compared to an LSTM. It does not attempt to capture complex non-linear patterns in the
410            data, which can be beneficial due to the inherent noisy nature of the our input.
411
             • The LR model provides stable parameter estimates even with relatively small datasets. This
412
               can be beneficial as the relationship between input and output might not be highly complex
413            or might vary frequently due to external market conditions.
414
415
             • The financial time series of exchange rates often exhibit linear trends or mean-reverting be-
               haviour over short periods. The LR model, which inherently assumes a linear relationship
416
               between input variables and the output, can effectively capture these linear or near-linear
417
               trends without the need for complex model architectures.

      3.4   M EAN AND Z ERO M ODELS : E VIDENCE OF M EAN -R EVERTING B EHAVIOUR
420
421   Both the Mean and Zero models perform close to each other and have the lowest error rate among
422   the other models. This outcome strongly suggests that the USDJPY rate exhibits a mean-reverting
423   behaviour, a common characteristic in many financial time series.
424
425          • The Mean model, which predicts future values as the mean of the historical data, works
426            well in scenarios where the time series is mean-reverting. The near-zero error indicates
427            that the exchange rate often returns to its average return over time, making the Mean model
428            a reliable predictor.
429          • Similarly, the Zero model, which assumes that all future log returns will be zero (implying
430            no change in price), performs well, further supporting the hypothesis of mean reversion. A
431            zero prediction effectively suggests that the current price level is expected to persist, which
               aligns with a market that lacks strong directional trends and frequently reverts to a mean.
