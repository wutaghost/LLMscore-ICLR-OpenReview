009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the recent paradigm of Federated Learning (FL), multiple clients train a shared
012            model while keeping their local data private. Resource constraints of clients and
013            communication costs pose major problems for training large models in FL. On
014            the one hand, addressing the resource limitations of the clients, sparse training
015            has proven to be a powerful tool in the centralized setting. On the other hand,
016            communication costs in FL can be addressed by local training, where each client
017            takes multiple gradient steps on its local data. Recent work has shown that lo-
018
               cal training can provably achieve the optimal accelerated communication com-
               plexity (Mishchenko et al., 2022). Hence, one would like an accelerated sparse
019
               training algorithm. In this work we show that naive integration of sparse train-
020
               ing and acceleration at the server fails, and how to fix it by letting the clients
021            perform these tasks appropriately. We introduce Sparse-ProxSkip, our method
022            developed for the nonconvex setting, inspired by RandProx (Condat & Richtárik,
023            2022), which provably combines sparse training and acceleration in the convex
024            setting. We demonstrate the good performance of Sparse-ProxSkip in extensive
025            experiments.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Federated learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning approach that enables multiple edge de-
031   vices to collaboratively train a shared model while keeping their data local (McMahan et al., 2017;
032   Konečný et al., 2016; Bonawitz et al., 2017). This paradigm addresses significant privacy concerns
033
      by avoiding the need to transfer potentially sensitive data to a central server and thus can enable
      access to huge datasets. Instead, local models are trained on each client’s device, and only the
034
      model updates are aggregated at the server to train a shared global model. However, one of the main
035
      challenges in FL is the limited computational and communication resources of edge devices (Caldas
036   et al., 2018b).
037
038   Pruning is a well-known technique in the centralized setting for reducing the computational and
039
      memory costs of model training and inference (Han et al., 2015; Evci et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2024).
      There are two major directions: dense-to-sparse or sparse-to-sparse training (Liu & Wang, 2023).
040
      Dense-to-sparse (DTS) training starts with a dense network and proceeds by systematically remov-
041
      ing redundant or less important parameters and reduces the model size without substantially sacrific-
042   ing performance. Sparse-to-sparse (STS) training starts with a sparse network and usually proceeds
043   by sparsifying and regrowing weights but keeping the sparsity constant. Both lead to computational
044   savings at inference time as the final model is sparse (Srinivas et al., 2017). But sparse-to-sparse
045   training also leads to substantially reduced training costs as the model is sparse throughout the whole
046   process. Hence, a sparse-to-sparse algorithm for FL would address the resource limitation of edge
047   devices for efficient training and inference.
048   However, a key issue during training in FL are communication costs, as for every step of the opti-
049   mizer the clients have to share the model updates with the server or with each other. Local training
050   has emerged as the key paradigm for efficient learning which allows the participating clients to take
051   multiple update steps before communicating with each other. It first appeared in the popular al-
052   gorithm FedAvg and showed great empirical success in applications (McMahan et al., 2017). In a
053   recent breakthrough, Mishchenko et al. (2022) introduced ProxSkip, the first algorithm to be prov-
      ably more communication-efficient than FedAvg by employing control variates and randomization.

073   In a follow-up work, Condat & Richtárik (2022) were able to generalize the acceleration guaran-
074   tees of ProxSkip to allow for multiple proxs in an algorithm called RandProx. In the convex setting
075
      with l1 regularization, RandProx allows to obtain a sparse model while employing acceleration, al-

076
      outperformed by nonconvex techniques based on the l0 seminorm.
077
078   Challenge. To achieve an efficient algorithm for FL, sparse-to-sparse training and the recent the-
079   oretical advances on acceleration need to be combined. Hence, we address the following research
080   question:
081   Is it possible to incorporate acceleration with nonconvex techniques usually found in sparse-to-
082   sparse training algorithm?
083
      Contributions. A common approach in the FL literature is to apply pruning at the server (Stripelis
084
      et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024). First, we show that this naive approach fails in the case of ProxSkip.
085   Then, inspired bt RandProx, we derive a new algorithm, Sparse-ProxSkip, which addresses the
086   problems by pruning at the clients instead of the server. We show that this is necessary through a
087   combination of theory and experiments. Finally, we validate our algorithm in extensive experiments.
088   Figure 1 shows how our proposed algorithm outperforms baselines for convex and deep learning
089   experiments.
090
      Hence, the paper starts with an overview of the theoretical background of RandProx and its applica-
091   tion for pruning through l1 regularization in section 3. Section 4.1 then shows the superiority of this
092   algorithm for regression on the Blog Feedback dataset. Regression was chosen, as the theoretical
093   guarantees hold only in convex scenarios like this and centralized STS regression is a well estab-
094   lished field known as Subset Selection (Hastie et al., 2017). Section 4.2 establishes the superiority in
095   logistic regression on FEMNIST (Caldas et al., 2018a). Finally, Section 4.3 deals with deep learning
096   experiments.

      Despite some existing studies on deriving sparse models in federated learning, the topic remains in-
101
      sufficiently understood. The most similar STS approach is given by Tong et al. (2020), who combine
102   FedAvg and TopK to yield FedHT and FedIHT. Their approach does not integrate acceleration or
103   control variates. Hence, this will be considered a baseline for our work. Furthermore, only FedIHT
104   prunes the model before sending it to the server and thus uses the major communication efficiency
105   of training a sparse model instead of a dense one (Yi et al., 2024). Subsequent works do not incor-
106   porate acceleration or address client drift either (Lin et al., 2022; Bibikar et al., 2022; Horvath et al.,
107   2021; Isik et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2022), or they are not fully STS (Jiang et al.,
      2022; Qiu et al., 2022; Munir et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021).

108
      In the DTS regime, the most simple approach is given by FedSparsify, which applies Gradual Magni-
109   tude Pruning in FedAvg at the server (Stripelis et al., 2022). The main difference between FedHT and
110   FedSparsify is that the latter starts with a dense model and ramps up the sparsity by a cubic schedule
111   during the training as is usual in centralized pruning. Another recent DTS work takes the approach
112   of applying further centralized training approaches at the server (Lee et al., 2024). Here, one gathers
113   up the local updates (usually with a fixed learning rate) and treats them as the gradient at the server.
114   Then one can apply both centralized optimizers and centralized pruning techniques. In particular,
115   Lee et al. (2024) apply the DTS techniques of random pruning, saliency pruning (Molchanov et al.,
116   2016), GMP (Zhu & Gupta, 2017) and Straight Through Estimation (Bengio et al., 2013) and for
117
      STS they apply static sparse training, dynamic sparse training (Mocanu et al., 2018) and RigL (Evci
      et al., 2020). We will show that acceleration and pruning at the server fail and need to be applied
118
      at the clients instead. Hence, our work enables integrating all of the aforementioned centralized
119
      pruning techniques with ProxSkip or Scaffold (Karimireddy et al., 2020).

      3.1   BASELINE M ETHODS
157
158   Additionally to FedHT and FedIHT discussed in the Section 2, we consider the following simple
159   baselines of how to address the research question of incorporating pruning, acceleration and tackling
160   client drift. A simple approach is to employ an accelerated algorithm like ProxSkip to obtain the
161   dense solution w∗ and then take TopK(w∗ ) of it for the desired sparsity, where the TopK operator
      keeps the K largest elements of a vector unchanged and sets the other ones to zero. This approach


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      does not address resource constraints of the clients or take advantage of training a sparse model to
163   reduce communication cost. We will call this approach Final-TopK. The experiments will show that
164   Sparse-ProxSkip addresses client resources and outperforms this method, showing that it provides a
165   valuable contribution.
166
      Another approach would be to consider pruning at the server, i.e. applying TopK after averaging the
167
      model and before sending it back to the clients. Applying optimization techniques at the server is a
168
      common approach in FL (Lee et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2022; Stripelis et al., 2022). When applied to
169   ProxSkip, we refer to this variant as Accelerated-Server-Pruning and it can be found in Algorithm 1.
170   A major drawback is that this method does not benefit from compression for saving on uplink com-
171   munication costs. As pruning is done before downlink communication, the models uploaded to
172   the server are dense, incurring full communication cost. Furthermore, we show in the experiments
173   that Accelerated-Server-Pruning violates a key invariant of control variates, so that it is essentially
174   inappropriate for FL.

261
      We start with convex experiments for the following reasons. First, the convex setting is well un-
262
      derstood and the theoretical guarantees of ProxSkip and RandProx hold only in this case. From a
263
      theory point of view, TopK is not nonexpansive and hence might lead to divergence. Hence, we
264   start with the convex setting to clearly investigate the effects of the mechanisms. Second, convex
265   models are still surprisingly widespread in industrial applications. Third, many successful methods
266   for the nonconvex case were designed for the convex case and then adapted to the nonconvex case.
267   And lastly, ProxSkip and related accelerated methods are even without pruning still underexplored in
268   the deep learning case. Hence, adapting these methods for sparse deep learning is challenging, but
269   we provide experiments and general insights for this setting as well. General experimental details
      can be found in Appendix A.

478   5                                      C ONCLUSION
479
480   We investigated whether it is possible in FL to combine the recent theoretical advances of accelera-
481   tion and client drift mitigation via local training, with sparse training. We showed that 1) the naive
482   combination of these techniques fails; 2) it is theoretically and empirically crucial to keep the sum
483   of the control variates to zero; 3) pruning should be done at the clients, not the server. Based on
484   these important findings, we developed a theoretically-motivated method, Sparse-ProxSkip, which
485   integrates the successful mechanism of TopK for sparse training in FL. Our experiments confirm
      its efficiency.

      F         O UTLOOK AND L IMITATIONS
904
905
      Sparse training might prove crucial for training large models in FL, which offer architectural benefits
906
      over small models. Here, sparse training enables larger models to respect the resource requirements
907   of edge devices. Furthermore, these findings might be invaluable for combining centralized sparse
908   training and pruning methods with acceleration. We provided a general invariant that pruning has to
909   take place at the clients but future work might address the details of this integration. Additionally, in
910   its current form, the method provides inference benefits and communication cost savings but would
911   need further development for reducing the computational costs during training. In particular, our
912   current gradients and control variables are dense, requiring further modification before yielding a
913   sparse-to-sparse training method with the computational and memory footprint of a small model. In
914   the pruning literature, masking is usually employed for this aspect. Here, one could apply masking to
915
      the control variates as well and combine gradient calculation and pruning as to decrease the memory
      cost of the full gradients.
