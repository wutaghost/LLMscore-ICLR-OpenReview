009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Although likelihood-based methods are theoretically appealing, deep generative
012            models (DGMs) often produce unreliable likelihood estimates in practice, particu-
013            larly for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. We reinterpret variational autoen-
014            coders (VAEs) through the lens of fast and slow weights. Our approach is guided
015            by the proposed Likelihood Path (LPath) Principle, which extends the classical
016            likelihood principle. A critical decision in our method is the selection of statistics
017
               for classical density estimation algorithms. The sweet spot should contain just
               enough information that’s sufficient for OOD detection but not too much to suffer
018
               from the curse of dimensionality. Our LPath principle achieves this by selecting
019
               the sufficient statistics that form the "path" toward the likelihood. We demonstrate
020            that this likelihood path leads to SOTA OOD detection performance, even when
021            the likelihood itself is unreliable.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Independent and identically distributed (IID) samples during training and testing are key to much
027   of machine learning’s (ML) success. However, as ML systems are deployed in the real world,
028   encountering out-of-distribution (OOD) data is inevitable and poses significant safety risks. This is
029
      particularly challenging in the most general setting where labels are absent, and test input arrives in a
      streaming fashion. The objective of general unsupervised OOD detection is to develop a scalar score
030
      function, trained on PID (in-distribution (ID) samples), that assigns higher scores to data from POOD
031
      (out-of-distribution samples) than to data from PID .
032
033   Naïve approaches, such as using pθ (x), the likelihood of deep generative models (DGMs), are
034   attractive in theory but have proven ineffective due to unreliable likelihood estimates, often assigning
035
      high likelihood to OOD data (Nalisnick et al., 2018). Furthermore, even with perfect density
      estimation, likelihood alone is insufficient to detect OOD data (Le Lan & Dinh, 2021; Zhang
036
      et al., 2021) when the ID and OOD distributions overlap. Compounding this, recent theoretical
037
      works (Behrmann et al., 2021; Dai et al.) show that perfect density estimation may be infeasible for
038   many DGMs.
039
040   Research Question (RQ) 1: Can we achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) unsupervised OOD detection
041   without relying on accurate likelihood estimation?
042
      We take a step towards answering this question by developing a principled method for unsupervised
043
      OOD detection. Our algorithm is inspired by a reinterpretation of Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
044   from the fast and slow weights perspective, originally proposed in the context of adaptive neural
045   networks and meta-learning (Hinton & Plaut, 1987; Munkhdalai & Trischler, 2018; Ba et al., 2016).
046   Our algorithm has two stages. In the first stage (neural feature extraction), we train VAEs and
047   extract key statistics contributing to the likelihood function. In the second stage (classical density
048   estimation), these statistics are used as training data to fit a classical statistical density estimation
049   algorithm (COPOD (Li et al., 2020) or MD (Lee et al., 2018; Maciejewski et al., 2022)) for OOD
050   detection.
051   The key design decision in our algorithm is the choice of statistics, which leads to our second research
052   question:
053
       RQ 2: How do we select key statistics for the classical density estimation algorithm?


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The desired statistics should strike a balance: including too many activations leads to the curse of
055   dimensionality, while including too few fails to capture enough information. Our approach is to
056   select the minimal sufficient statistics of the main components on the computational graph leading to
057   the likelihood function. These anchoring statistics define the computational path of the likelihood
058   function, which we term the Likelihood Path (LPath) Principle.
059
060
       Under imperfect likelihood estimation, there is more information in the computational path leading
       to the marginal likelihood function pθ (x) relative to pθ (x) alone. Information can be optimally
061
       extracted by the minimal sufficient statistics of the individual components of the factorization of the
062
       likelihood function.
063
064   Although the LPath principle has independent interest in representation learning and can be applied
065   to other DGMs, this work focuses on a thorough case study of applying the LPath principle to the
066   OOD detection problem using Gaussian VAEs. We take the sufficient statistics of the VAE encoder
067
      and decoder as key statistics for our two-stage algorithm, achieving SOTA performance on common
      benchmarks (Table 1). Compared to other SOTA methods, we used a much smaller model (DC-VAEs
068
      from Xiao et al. (2020)’s architecture) with a parameter count of 3M, compared to 44M for Glow in
069
      DoSE (Morningstar et al., 2021) and 46M for the diffusion model (Liu et al., 2023). We believe this
070   “achieving more with less” phenomenon demonstrates our method’s potential.
071
072   To summarize, our main contributions are:
073   Empirical contribution: We achieved SOTA unsupervised OOD detection performance on common
074   benchmarks (Table 1) using a much smaller model compared to other SOTA methods, addressing
075   RQ1.
076
      Methodological contribution: We proposed the LPath Principle, which generalizes the classical
077   likelihood principle1 for instance-dependent inference (e.g., OOD detection) under imperfect density
078   estimation, addressing RQ2.

      3     OOD D ETECTION WITH FAST AND S LOW W EIGHTS
193
194   In this section, we reinterpret a classical prior OOD detection method from the slow weight perspective
195   and introduce our method from the fast weight perspective. We then detail our algorithm. In the next
196   section, we provide a thorough analysis of our method’s statistical and combinatorial foundations.
197
198   3.1    OOD D ETECTION WITH VAE S LOW W EIGHTS
199
200   Reinterpreting the Likelihood Regret Method The likelihood regret method for OOD detec-
201   tion (Xiao et al., 2020) can be reinterpreted as detecting OOD samples using the information update
202   in slow weights. At a high level, after obtaining θtrained from training, they fine-tune VAEs by
203   maximizing likelihood on a test sample xTest to get θonline , and track the following likelihood regret:
204                                       log p(θonline | xTest ) − log p(θtrained | xTest ).                                    (15)
205
      In other words, their work involves two inferential procedures. First, (XTrain , pθ ) −→ θtrained ;
206
      second, (XTrain , xTest , pθ ) −→ θonline , where they do not maximize pθ jointly on (XTrain , xTest ), but
207
      sequentially on XTrain first and xTest next. However, likelihood regret is empirically outperformed
208   by alternative approaches (Morningstar et al., 2021) which did not involve any fine-tuning. This
209   is probably because training neural networks on one sample is challenging. Optimizing for a few
210   iterations changes θtrained very little, while training for many iterations results in overfitting quickly.
211   Furthermore, in streaming OOD detection, such computational overhead is formidable.
212
213   3.2    OOD D ETECTION WITH VAE FAST W EIGHTS
214
215   Given that OOD detection with slow weights induces formidable computational overhead during test
      time and poses optimization challenges, we propose to perform OOD detection with fast weights.

236   3.3   T HE LPATH A LGORITHM FOR FAST W EIGHTS OOD D ETECTION

238
      We use Eqs. 16–19 as the scoring metrics for our OOD detection algorithm. We call it the Likelihood
      Path (LPath) algorithm because it is based on minimal sufficient statistics of the individual components
239
      of the factorization of the likelihood function; we provide a detailed description and analysis in
240
      Section 4.3.
241
242   Our algorithm is detailed in Algorithm 1. It first trains a VAE and extracts statistics in Eqs. 16–19
243   in the first stage (neural feature extraction). Then it fits a classical statistical density estimation
244
      algorithm (COPOD (Li et al., 2020) or MD (Lee et al., 2018; Maciejewski et al., 2022)) in the second
      stage (classical density estimation).
245
246   Our algorithm can be used with a single VAE model (LPath-1M) or a pair of two models (LPath-2M).
247   For LPath-1M, we use the same VAE to extract all of u(x), v(x), w(x), s(x). When used with a pair
248   of two models (LPath-2M), we train two VAEs: one with a very high latent dimension (e.g., 1000)
249   and another with a very low dimension (e.g., 1 or 2). In the second stage, we extract the following
250
      statistics: (u(x)lowD , v(x)highD , w(x)highD , s(x)lowD ), where u(x)lowD , s(x)lowD are taken from the
      low-dimensional VAE and v(x)high D , w(x)high D from the high-dimensional VAE. Appendix D.1.2
251
      explains the reasoning behind this combination.

267    Under imperfect likelihood estimation, there is more information in the computational path leading

269    extracted by the minimal sufficient statistics of the individual components of the factorization of the

      4.1    T HE L IKELIHOOD P RINCIPLE
297
298
      The maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) approach to unsupervised learning focuses on finding
299   parameters ψ to maximize the likelihood ℓ(ψ | x) := p(x | ψ) given training data x, so that we
300   transfer information from x to ψ. MLE is a special case of the likelihood principle (Berger & Wolpert,
301   1988):
302
303   The likelihood principle states that all the evidence in an observed sample x relevant to model
304
      parameters is contained in the likelihood function ℓ(ψ | x).
305   MLE satisfies the likelihood principle because inferring the most likely parameter depends only on
306   ℓ(ψ | x). Many OOD detection works (Nalisnick et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020) satisfy this principle
307   as well.
308
      In summary, the likelihood principle postulates that ℓ(ψ | x) (as a function of ψ) tells us everything
309
      about x. If we make our decisions (e.g., OOD detection) based only on the likelihood function, our
310   decision satisfies the likelihood principle.
311
312
313   4.2    T HE S UFFICIENCY P RINCIPLE
314
315   While the likelihood principle suggests that all information is contained in ℓ(ψ | x), it is a complex
316
      function and does not directly tell us what statistics to include for RQ2. To better process such
      overwhelming information, we seek to reduce our selection to the simplest set that still contains
317
      sufficient information about ℓ(ψ | x). How do we formalize such information trimming in the context
318
      of unsupervised learning?
319
320
              • The information reduction procedure T should be a function of x, a statistic.
321
322           • T should be sufficient for describing p(x | ψ) or ψ: p(x | T (x), ψ) = p(x | T (x)).
323
              • T should be minimal: F (T ) is no longer sufficient for ψ, for any non-invertible function F .


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
       In summary, a minimal sufficient statistic T tells us everything about ψ that we can possibly learn
325    from observing x, and if we attempt to trim T further by any irreversible process, we would lose some
326    information for inferring ℓ(ψ | x) 2 .
327
328   Alternatively, we can view sufficient statistics from an information-theoretic perspective. Let I denote
329   the mutual information. T (x) is sufficient for ψ if:
330                                           I(ψ; T (x)) = I(ψ; x).                                     (20)
331
      In other words, the data processing inequality I(ψ; T (x)) ≤ I(ψ; x) becomes an equality if T is
332
      sufficient. This is useful for answering RQ2. Given a new sample x, the encoder and decoder neural
333
      nets would produce millions of activations, all of which could be useful for OOD detection. However,
334   this is clearly overwhelming. The minimal sufficient statistic T (x) gives us the set of statistics that
335   cannot be reduced further without losing some information.
336
337
      The sample mean vectors and sample covariance matrices (Eqs. 7 and 9) parameterizating the standard
      Gaussian VAE’s encoder and decoder are minimal sufficient statistics (Wasserman, 2006).

      4.3   L IKELIHOOD PATH P RINCIPLE
340
341   Our proposed LPath principle states that:
342
343    Under imperfect likelihood estimation, there is more information in the computational path leading
344    to the marginal likelihood function pθ (x) relative to pθ (x) alone. Information can be optimally
345    extracted by the minimal sufficient statistics of the individual components of the factorization of the
346
       likelihood function.
347   For VAEs, this entails applying the likelihood principle twice in the VAE’s encoder and decoder
348   conditional distributions and tracking their minimal sufficient statistics: T (x, z). Here, minimal
349   sufficient statistics represent two optimal conditions for inference: They are sufficient because once
350   (µz (x), σz (x)) and (µx (z), σx (z)) are known, the conditional likelihood functions can be defined.
351   They are minimal because any other parameterization of a Gaussian will involve no fewer parameters.
352   Recall the VAE formulation:

405   4.4       C OMBINATORIAL C ANCELLATION
406
407
      We analyzed the LPath Principle for OOD detection from the statistical perspective. We can gain more
      concrete insights on why the LPath Principle works if we take a combinatorial perspective, which
408
      can act as an empirical method to select statistics, answering RQ2. The key insight is that factors
409
      in the likelihood function risk getting canceled in the likelihood itself, and the signals they contain
410   for OOD detection will be drowned out. This is how information is lost in Eq. 24. To address this,
411   we could separate each factor out and capture the signal they contain with their sufficient statistics,
412   arriving at our LPath Principle.
413
      In the case of VAEs, the encoder and decoder contain complementary information for OOD detection,
414

      but hthey could be

417   (evaluated at samples from the encoder qϕ (z | x)) being too small both suggest x could be an
418   anomaly, but their scalar product can be well-ranged, which drowns out the signal for OOD discovery.
419   A more concrete interpretation of this cancellation phenomenon from the pixel texture vs. semantics

      they do not address RQ2 and provide a principled method to select such statistics.

480   We presented the Likelihood Path Principle applied to unsupervised, one-sample OOD detection. We
481   provided in-depth analyses from the neural (fast-slow weights), statistical (likelihood and sufficiency

      Prior works have approached OOD detection from various perspectives and with different data
651   assumptions, e.g., with or without access to training labels, batches of test data, or single test data
652   points in a streaming fashion, and with or without knowledge and inductive bias of the data. In the
653   following, we give an overview organized by different data assumptions with a focus on where our
654   method fits.
655
      The first assumption is whether the method has access to training labels. There has been extensive
656
      work on classifier-based methods that assume access to training labels (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016;
657   Frosst et al., 2019; Sastry & Oore, 2020; Bahri et al., 2021; Papernot & McDaniel, 2018; Osawa et al.,
658   2019; Guénais et al., 2020; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2016; Pearce et al., 2020). Within this category,
659   there are different assumptions as well, such as access to a pretrained network or knowledge of OOD
660   test examples. See Table 1 of Sastry & Oore (2020) for a summary of such methods.
661
      When we do not assume access to the training labels, the problem becomes more general and also
662
      harder. Under this category, some methods assume access to a batch of test data where either all
663   the data points are OOD or not (Nalisnick et al., 2019). A more general setting does not assume
664   OOD data would come in batches. Under this setup, there are methods that implicitly assume prior
665   knowledge of the data, such as the input complexity method (Serrà et al., 2019), where the use of
666   image compressors implicitly assumes an image-like structure, or the likelihood ratio method (Ren
667   et al., 2019), where a noisy background model is trained with the assumption of a background-object
668   structure.

720   D.1.1    D IMENSIONALITY T RADE - OFF
721
722
      In this section, we discuss heuristics for training VAEs in the context of OOD detection, focusing on
      the trade-offs involved in selecting the latent dimension.
723
724
725
      Balancing the Trade-off in Latent Dimension A single VAE encounters a trade-off when selecting
      the latent dimension for effective OOD detection:
726
727
              • Higher Latent Dimension Benefits the Encoder: Increasing the latent dimension enhances
728
                the encoder’s ability qϕ to discriminate between in-distribution (ID) and OOD data. A
729
                higher-dimensional latent space allows the encoder to map ID and OOD data to more
730             distinct regions, reducing overlap and improving separability. This increased capacity
731             enables the encoder to capture complex features of the data, improving its discriminative
732             power.
733
              • Lower Latent Dimension Benefits the Decoder: Decreasing the latent dimension en-
734
                hances the decoder’s ability pθ to identify OOD data through reconstruction errors. A
735
                lower-dimensional latent space constrains the decoder, making it less capable of accurately
736             reconstructing OOD data that it hasn’t seen during training. This constraint leads to larger
737             reconstruction errors u(x) = ∥x − x b∥2 for OOD samples, providing a useful signal for
738             detection.
739
740   This trade-off poses a challenge: adjusting the latent dimension to favor one component (encoder or
741   decoder) may compromise the performance of the other. Increasing the latent dimension benefits
742   the encoder but may reduce the decoder’s effectiveness in generating meaningful reconstruction
743   errors. Conversely, decreasing the latent dimension enhances the decoder’s ability to produce larger
744   reconstruction errors for OOD data but may impair the encoder’s discriminative capacity.
745
746   Implications for VAE Design When designing a single VAE for OOD detection, it’s essential to
747   consider this trade-off:
748
749           • For the Encoder: Aim for a higher latent dimension to improve the separation between ID
750
                and OOD data in the latent space.
751           • For the Decoder: Consider a lower latent dimension to increase reconstruction errors for
752             OOD data, enhancing detection based on reconstruction discrepancies.
753
754   However, finding an optimal latent dimension that satisfies both requirements within a single VAE
755   can be challenging. Adjusting the latent dimension to favor one aspect inherently affects the other,
      leading to suboptimal performance in at least one component.

      Two VAEs Face No Such Trade-off To overcome this trade-off inherent in a single VAE, we
757   propose using two VAEs with different latent dimensions, as discussed in the next section. By pairing
758   a high-dimensional VAE with a low-dimensional one, we can leverage the strengths of both models
759   without being constrained by the conflicting requirements of a single latent dimension.
760
761   D.1.2    PAIRING VAE S : L EVERAGING D UAL L ATENT D IMENSIONS
762
763   Two VAEs Overcome the Trade-off To resolve the trade-off in latent dimension selection, we
764   propose training two VAEs with different latent dimensions:
765
            1. High-Dimensional VAE: This VAE has an overparameterized (large) latent dimension. Its
766
               encoder qϕ is capable of capturing complex features and provides informative statistics such
767
               as v(x) and w(x) that help discriminate between ID and OOD data.
768
769         2. Low-Dimensional VAE: This VAE has an underparameterized (small) latent dimension. Its
770
               decoder pθ is constrained, leading to higher reconstruction errors u(x) for OOD data due to
               its limited capacity to represent unfamiliar inputs.
771
772
      By combining the strengths of both VAEs, we can effectively detect OOD data. The high-dimensional
773   VAE’s encoder excels at distinguishing ID from OOD data in the latent space, while the low-
774   dimensional VAE’s decoder amplifies reconstruction errors for OOD samples.

      D.3    W IDTH AND H EIGHT OF A V ECTOR I NSTEAD OF I TS l2 N ORM T O E XTRACT
865          C OMPLEMENTARY I NFORMATION
866
867   In our visual inspection, we find that the distribution of the scalar components of (u(x), v(x), w(x))
868   can be rather uneven. For example, the visible space reconstruction x − x   b error can be mostly low
869   for many pixels, but very high at certain locations. These information can be washed away by the l2
870   norm. Instead, we propose to track both lp norm and lq norm for small p and large q.
871   For small p, lp measures the width of a vector, while lq measures the height of a vector for big
872   q. To get a sense of how they capture complementary information, we can borrow intuition from
873   lp ≈ l0 , for small p and lq ≈ l∞ , for large q. ∥x∥0 counts the number of nonzero entries, while ∥x∥∞
874   measures the height of x. For x with continuous values, however, l0 norm is not useful because it
875   always returns the dimension of x, while l∞ norm just measures the maximum component.
876   Extreme measures help screen extreme data. We therefore use lp norm and lq norm as a continuous
877   relaxation to capture this idea: lp norm will “count” the number of components in x that are unusually
878   small, and lq norm “measures” the average height of the few biggest components. These can be more
879   discriminitive against OOD than l2 norm alone, due to the extreme (proxy for OOD) conditions they
880   measure. We observe some minor improvements, detailed in Table 2’s ablation study.
