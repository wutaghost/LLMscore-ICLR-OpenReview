009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Are Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) stochastic parrots? Do they
012            genuinely understand? This paper aims to explore the core cognitive abilities that
013            human intelligence builds upon to perceive, comprehend, and reason in MLLMs.
014            To this end, we propose CogDevelop2K, a comprehensive benchmark that spans
015            12 sub-concepts from primitive knowledge like object permanence and boundary
016
               to more complex abilities like intentionality understanding, structured via the de-
               velopmental trajectory of a human mind. We evaluate 46 MLLMs on our bench-
017
               marks. Surprisingly, we observe a reversed cognitive developmental trajectory
018
               compared to humans. Comprehensively, we further evaluate the influence of eval-
019            uation strategies and prompting techniques.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Building on the foundation of advanced large language models (LLMs), multi-modal large language
025   models (MLLMs) have recently demonstrated human-level performance in complex tasks involving
026   high-level reasoning, perception, and cognition (Li et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024; Gemini, 2023; Fu
027   et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023), such as Spatial Reasoning (Chen et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024), OCR
028
      (Mori et al., 1999), Scene Understanding (Cordts et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017), Action Recogni-
      tion (Jhuang et al., 2013; Herath et al., 2017) and Prediction (Lan et al., 2014; Kong & Fu, 2022).
029
      The progress in MLLMs has reignited hopes for achieving Artificial General Intelligence (AGI).
030
      However, recent studies have shown that even state-of-the-art MLLMs face critical limitations as
031   compared to human intelligence. On the one hand, they perform poorly on rudimentary reasoning
032   tasks like counting Paiss et al. (2023), compositional reasoning Yuksekgonul et al. (2022) and spatial
033   reasoning Hoehing et al. (2023) despite their excellence at high-level reasoning tasks on similar do-
034   mains (Paiss et al., 2023; Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024). On the other hand, said excellency often
035   does not appear to translate to more generalized and real-world contexts (Shiffrin & Mitchell, 2023;
036   Zhang et al., 2024). To explore the underlying reason for these limitations, we draw inspirations
037   from human cognitive development.
038   Past research has shown that humans exhibit a series of rudimentary yet robust abilities in domains
039   such as object, number, space, action, and social cognition at a very young age. Such abilities,
040   often known as ”core” cognition, grounds the set of diverse and complex abilities of human intel-
041   ligence that develop later (Spelke et al., 1992; 1994; 1995; Spelke & Kinzler, 2007; Baillargeon &
042   Carey, 2012; Mitchell, 2020; 2021). From infancy to early adulthood, human cognition develops
043   along a structured trajectory, with interdependent relations between early, simple abilities and late,
044   complex abilities. For instance, the ability to imagine the perspectives of others typically develops
045
      between the ages of 3 and 6 (Piaget & Inhelder, 1969), while the capacity to fully comprehend oth-
      ers’ intentions matures around age 12 (Wimmer & Perner, 1983; Wellman et al., 2001; Liu et al.,
046
      2008). At the same time, the ability to understand other people’s intentions largely depends on the
047
      the ability to understand other people’s perspectives (Iacoboni, 2009; De Waal & Preston, 2017;
048   Liu et al., 2017; Caviola et al., 2021; Ninomiya et al., 2020). An influential account of human
049   learning has suggested that cognitive development is fundamentally driven by the increase of com-
050   putational/representational power of the system, which allows for more complex mental operations
051   to be performed on external data (Fodor, 1975; Pylyshyn, 1980; Halford et al., 1998; Fodor, 2008).
052   However, while high-level abilities emerge directly due to enhanced operational resources, these op-
053   erations are critically guided by the ”core” cognition system that has enabled the system to possess a
      rudimentary understanding of each cognitive domain. This early-stage grounding not only empow-

054
      ers humans to achieve a reliable performance at basic yet widely-applicable tasks starting from very
055   young ages, but is also precisely what supports high-level abilities to robustly direct task-relevant
056   behaviors despite the nuanced signals exist in the environment (Mitchell, 2021).
057
058
      Given this line of evidence, we suggest that the absence of a core cognition system may provide
      a joint account of the two limitations faced by current MLLMs: poor performance on basic rea-
059
      soning and the lack of robustness with high-level reasoning. In order to assess this hypothesis, We
060
      draw on theoretical and empirical approaches from developmental science to create benchmarks
061   that evaluate simple and complex cognitive abilities in large vision-language models that are in-
062   terrelated along the developmental trajectory. On a high level, we follow Jean Piaget’s theory of
063   cognitive development, which identifies four stages in children: sensorimotor, preoperational, con-
064   crete operational, and formal operational (Piaget, 1950; Piaget & Inhelder, 1969; 1974). During the
065   sensorimotor stage, infants acquire knowledge through sensory experiences and actions, developing
066   an understanding of basic object properties, such as permanence, continuity, and boundaries. In the
067   preoperational stage, symbolic representation emerges, along with a grasp of basic physical proper-
068   ties. The concrete operational stage is characterized by the development of logical thinking and an
069
      understanding of intuitive physics. Finally, the formal operational stage introduces more advanced
      cognitive abilities, including abstraction, hypothetical reasoning, counterfactual thinking, and tool
070
      use. The interdependence and developmental trajectories of these abilities can be mapped in terms
071
      of a tree-like structure (as illustrated in Fig. 1).
072
073   To evaluate the performance of MLLMs on the core cognitive abilities, we curate the first-ever vision
074   cognitive development benchmark, termed as CogDeveop2K, which consists of a total of 2519 ques-
075
      tions with 2517 images and 455 videos. Then, we evaluate 46 MLLM models on our benchmark that
      spans all four stages of cognitive development. We introduce a novel multi-frame question format
076
      to evaluate models’ co-reference, cognitive reasoning and temporal understanding capability simul-
077
      taneously. Forty-seven models are compared against a human baseline under zero-shot conditions
078   using 11 different prompts (including no prompt). Surprisingly, while prompts can boost model per-
079   formance by 8.1%, models still demonstrate reversed trends in cognitive development against those
080   observed in children.

      2.2   H UMAN C OGNITIVE D EVELOPMENT
131
      The sensorimotor stage is the first stage of cognitive development proposed by Jean Piaget (Piaget,
132
      1952; Piaget & Inhelder, 1974). Spanning from birth to approximately 2 years of age, this stage is
133   characterized by infants’ understanding of the world through their sensory experiences and motor
134   actions. Several prominent features of human intelligence develop during this period. First, infants
135   develop object permanence, that they realize objects and people continue to exist even when not in
136   direct sight, or being heard or touched (Baillargeon et al., 1985). They start to understand that there is
137   a sense of continuity for the ways that objects exist, and the inductive bias of continuity is essential,
138   e.g., for recognizing objects when occluded or for continuously tracking objects (Spelke et al., 1995;
139   Le Poidevin, 2000). Infants also develop the sense of boundary during this stage, namely, the ability
140   to recognize where one object ends and another begins (Kestenbaum et al., 1987; Jackendoff, 1991).
141
      Lastly, infants develop spatial and perceptual constancy by the end of sensorimotor stage. Spatiality
      refers to the ability to perceive the position and distance of objects relative to oneself and each other,
142
      and recognize the spatial invariance between them when presented by various sensory experiences
143
      (Hermer & Spelke, 1996; Bell & Adams, 1999).
144
145   Preoperational and concrete operational stage are the second and third stage of Piaget’s cognitive
146   development. Typically spanning over 2 to 7 years of age, preoperational stage is the transitional
147
      stage to concrete operational stage, which children enter around 7 years of age. During this period,
      children begin to develop internalized mental actions supported by organized structures that can be
148
      manipulated and reversed in systematic ways, known as mental operations (Janet, 1905; Kirkpatrick,
149
      1908; Piaget, 1950; Piaget & Inhelder, 2014; Miller, 2016). Through mental operations, children
150   are then able to rigidly perform tasks that are previously unreachable, such as thinking from other
151   people’s perspectives, understanding hierarchical relations of objects, and reasoning about physical
152   events in the world. These tasks require not only rudimentary understandings of physical con-
153   cepts, which gradually became in place during preoperational stage, but also relational and trans-
154   formational reasoning that can only be done through mental operations (Piaget & Inhelder, 1974;
155   Church & Goldin-Meadow, 1986; Houdé, 1997). Since preoperational stage is mostly meaningful as
156   the transitional period preceding concrete operational stage, we do not have evaluation dimensions
157   specifically targeting the stage. However, tasks targeting concrete operational stage could assess the
158
      existence of knowledge associated with preoperational stage, such as law of conservation (Piaget,
      1952; Halford, 2011; Houdé, 1997).
159
160   The formal operational stage is the fourth and final stage in Piaget’s theory of cognitive development,
161   typically emerging around 11 or 12 years of age and continuing into adulthood (Inhelder & Piaget,
      1958). Starting in this stage, one is able to systematic and flexibly apply mental operations to

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      not only concrete, physical domains but also abstract, formal domains (Kuhn & Angelev, 1976;
163   Shayer, 1979; Huitt & Hummel, 2003). Foremost, this stage is characterized by the development of
164   complex thinking and reasoning abilities, such as abstraction, pattern recognition, the employment
165   of logic, and hypothetical and counterfactual reasoning (Piaget, 1950; Inhelder & Piaget, 1958).
166   These cognitive advancements pave the way for more sophisticated abilities to interact with the
167   physical world, marked by mechanical reasoning and tool use (O’Brien & Shapiro, 1968). Together,
168   there is the advancement in social cognition, characterized by a deeper understanding of intentions,
169   actions, and the reasoning behind them (Meltzoff, 1999).

171   2.3   E VALUATION D IMENSION
172
173
      Boundary Boundary refers to the cognitive understanding of where one object ends and another
      begins, an essential aspect of perceiving and understanding the physical world (Kestenbaum et al.,
174
      1987). Without understanding boundary, it seems very hard to construct a concept of object (Berke-
175
      ley, 1709; Jackendoff, 1991).
176
177
      Spatiality Spatiality, particularly demonstrated through the A-not-B task, involves a child’s under-
178   standing of the location of objects in relation to their environment (Bell & Adams, 1999). In a classic
179   A-not-B task, an object is hidden at location A (such as under a cup) and the child successfully finds
180   it several times. Then, the object is visibly moved to a different location B (under a different cup), in
181   full view of the child. Younger infants often make the error of searching for the object at the original
182   location A, indicating a developmental stage where their understanding of object spatiality is still
183   forming.
184
185   Perceptual Constancy Perceptual constancy is the cognitive ability to perceive objects as being
186   constant in their properties, such as size, shape, and color, despite changes in perspective, distance,
187   or lighting (Rutherford & Brainard, 2002; Khang & Zaidi, 2004; Green, 2023). For instance, con-
188
      sider a red ball being thrown in a park. To an observer, the ball appears smaller as it moves farther
      away, yet the observer understands it remains the same size throughout its trajectory.
189
190
      Object Permanence Permanence, or specifically object permanence, is the idea that objects con-
191
      tinue to exist even when they are not visible (Baillargeon, 1986; Spelke et al., 1992). Imagine a
192
      simple scene: a small child playing peek-a-boo. In the beginning, when the caregiver covers their
193   face with their hands, the child might seem surprised or even distressed, thinking the person has
194   disappeared. However, as children’s understanding of permanence develops, they begin to realize
195   that just because they can’t see the person’s face, it doesn’t mean the person is gone.
196
197   Continuity Continuity is the cognitive prior in humans that in our world, objects usually exist in a
198   consistent and continuous manner, even moving out of sight (Spelke et al., 1995; Le Poidevin, 2000;
199   Spelke et al., 1994; Yantis, 1995; Yi et al., 2008; Bertenthal et al., 2013). Picture a train moving
200   through a tunnel: as it enters one end, yet we naturally expect it to emerge from the other end, if the
201   train is long enough. This expectation demonstrates our understanding of object continuity. Even
202   though the train is not visible while it’s inside the tunnel, we know it continues to exist.
203
204   Conservation Conservation refers to the ability to understand that certain properties of physical
205
      entities are conserved after an object undergoes physical transformation (Piaget & Inhelder, 1974).
      This is instantiated in their ability to tell that quantities of physical entities across different domains,
206
      such as number, length, solid quantity and liquid volume, will remain the same despite adjustments
207
      of their arrangement, positioning, shapes, and containers (Halford, 2011; Craig et al., 1973; Piaget
208   & Inhelder, 1974; Houdé et al., 2011; Poirel et al., 2012; Marwaha et al., 2017; Viarouge et al.,
209   2019). For example, when a child watches water being poured from a tall, narrow glass into a short,
210   wide one, a grasp of liquid conservation would lead them to understand that the amount of water
211   remains the same even though its appearance has changed.
212
213   Perspective-taking Perspective-taking is the ability to view things from another’s perspective.
214   This ability has seminal importance both to the understanding of the physical world as well as to
215   the competence in social interactions (Wimmer & Perner, 1983; Wellman, 1992; Liu et al., 2008;
      Barnes-Holmes et al., 2004). The Three Mountain Task first invented by Jean Piaget is widely used

295   Hierarchical Relation Hierarchical relation refers to the ability to organize objects or concepts
296   into structured categories and subcategories, which are supported by the development of mental
297   operations marked by class inclusion and transitivity (Shipley, 1979; Winer, 1980; Chapman &
298   McBride, 1992). Class inclusion refers to the ability to recognize that some classes or groups of
299   objects are subsets of a larger class. For example, a child in the concrete operational stage is able to
300   understand that all roses are flowers, but not all flowers are roses (Borst et al., 2013; Politzer, 2016).
301
      This concept is essential for one’s systematic and logical organizations of conceptual knowledge.
      Transitivity refers to the ability to understand logical sequences and relationships between objects
302
      (Andrews & Halford, 1998; Wright & Smailes, 2015). For instance, if a child knows that Stick A is
303
      longer than Stick B, and Stick B is longer than Stick C, they can deduce that Stick A is longer than
304   Stick C.
305
306
      Intuitive Physics Intuitive physics refers to the ability of humans to predict, interact with, and
307
      make assumptions about the physical behavior of objects in their world (Michotte, 1963). As chil-
308
      dren grow, they transition from simplistic understandings, such as expecting unsupported objects to
309   fall, to more complex theories, such as grasping the principles of inertia (Spelke et al., 1994; Kim &
310   Spelke, 1999) and gravity (Vasta & Liben, 1996; Kim & Spelke, 1999; Li et al., 1999).
311
312
      Intention Understanding Intention understanding involves recognizing and interpreting the ac-
313   tions of others (Searle, 1979; Rosenthal, 1991). This process is not just about observing a behavior
314   but also about understanding the goal behind it (Baker et al., 2009; Gandhi et al., 2021). For ex-
315   ample, seeing someone reaching for a cup is not just about recognizing the physical action but
316   understanding the intention behind it (e.g., they want to drink).
317
318   Mechanical Reasoning Mechanical reasoning refers to the ability to understand and apply me-
319   chanical concepts and logical principles to solve problems (Allen et al., 2020). This cognitive con-
320   cept first involves the ability to interpret and predict the behaviors of complex physical systems and
321   understand how different mechanisms of the systems work. Second, mechanical reasoning requires
322   the ability to apply logic rules, such as induction, abduction, syllogism (O’Brien & Shapiro, 1968;
323   Cesana-Arlotti et al., 2018), and reasoning forms, such as hypotheticals and counterfactual (Byrne,
      2016), to figure out how to manipulate these systems to achieve a desired outcome (Hegarty, 2004).

345   CogDevelop2K comprises 2517 images and 445 videos with multimodal options and questions,
346   crawled primarily from networks as well as self-recorded content. Our targeted platforms in-
347   clude Wikipedia, Reddit, Twitter, Quora, and TieBa. Some of the captions were adapted from

354   2.5      DATASET D ESIGN
355
356   Existing datasets typically support only one question-answer format or single modality type, which
357   hinders the assessment of reasoning capabilities across different modalities within the same domain.
358
      For instance, current interleaved image understanding and video understanding models cannot be
      effectively compared on the same question. To address this limitation, we include multiple Q&A
359
      formats (e.g., multiple-choice, true/false, and numeric question-answer) and complicate question-
360
      answering by incorporating a new image-video-text interleave format as shown in Fig. 2. To further
361   explore the cognitive development capabilities of models across these modalities, we optimize our
362   formality as follows:
363
364   Addressing Weak Image-Text Correlation and Imbalance In existing interleaved image-text
365   datasets, the correspondence between images and text is often loose, and text provides marginal
366   information for image modeling. This imbalance can cause models to over-rely on textual informa-
367   tion, especially when text segments are lengthy (Lin et al., 2023) . To address this issue and focus
368   on the image understanding abilities of the model, we eliminate sentences that describe the image.
369   This ensures that the textual information is highly relevant to but does not overlap with the image
370   content.
371
372
      Testing Co-Reference, Reasoning, and Temporal Understanding with novel Multi-Frame
      Questions Multi-frame questions can simultaneously evaluate a model’s three inference ability:
373
      Co-Reference, Reasoning, and Temporal Understanding (Jiang et al., 2024). Co-reference involves
374
      linking natural language descriptions with specific image inputs (e.g., ”the first image” or ”A.<img
375   1>”). Reasoning requires models to make decisions based on cognitive knowledge, such as describ-
376   ing spatial relationships. Temporal Understanding, on the other hand, tests the model’s capability to

      2.6   E VALUATION S TRATEGY
399
400   We comprehensively evaluate models’ capability of cognitive reasoning using 46 multi-image in-
401   terleave MLLMs with 11 different promopts. The two evaluation baselines are outlined as follows:
402
403   Human baseline We recruit 22 participants, all of whom are college students proficient in En-
404   glish. Participants are instructed to skip a question if the question is phrased ambiguously or is too
405   complicated to answer in 90 seconds. This question is marked as failed if the human participant
406   does not provide an answer.
407
408   Zero-Shot-4482 -Circular Baseline Similar to previous studies (Lu et al., 2022), the zero-shot
409   setup follows the format of Q(M )T → A, where the input includes the question text (Q), task
410
      description (T), and multiple options (M) concatenated as tokens, with the output being the pre-
      dicted answer (A). Given that model predictions can exhibit bias in multiple-choice settings, we
411
      implemented circular evaluation as baseline. In circular evaluation, all answer options are shifted
412
      one position at a time, ensuring that the correct answer appears in each option slot. Only when the
413   model correctly predicts all shifted answers is it considered accurate (Liu et al., 2023). All images
414   and videos were resized to 4882 .
415
416   Prompts Strategically crafted prompts can enhance model performance, regardless of whether
417   fine-tuning is applied (Bsharat et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). To mitigate this, we use image-
418   independent contexts, such as relevant concept introductions and character assignments, which en-
419   courage models to reason beyond the provided textual information. The prompts we used can be
420   categorized into leading words, deeper thing, role assignment, reward or penalty, and explanation.

      4     D ISCUSSION
476
477
      Our results demonstrate that MLLMs exhibit an intriguing reverse cognitive development pattern.
478
      Namely, they are systematically proficient at complex tasks that are typically understood to require
479
      abilities underlying simple tasks that they perform poorly. This finding supports the hypothesis that
480   MLLMs lack a ”core” cognition system, which, as we suggested, could appear as challenge to the
481   current foundational architecture of MLLMs as a long-term solution to achieve human-like general
482   intelligence (Summerfield, 2022). Specifically, the inability to implement core knowledge in artifi-
483   cial intelligence models prevent them from achieving human-level robustness in performances, even
484   if such models seem to excel at certain complex cognitive reasoning tasks (Mitchell, 2020; 2021;
485   Shiffrin & Mitchell, 2023; Palmarini & Mitchell, 2024). MLLMs’ poor performances on founda-
      tional concepts like spatiality, permanence, continuity, and perspective-taking, which directly reflect


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      upon grasps of core knowledge, while achieve proficiency in complex concepts like tool using and
487   intention understanding exactly exemplifies this concern.Specifically, the developmental trajectory
488   of human cognition is marked by complex cognitive abilities being grounded upon extremely robust
489   understandings of a series of foundational concepts, namely core knowledge (Spelke, 2000; Spelke
490   & Kinzler, 2007). Through early stages of development, children exhibit rudimentary yet stable
491   understandings of objects, actions, number, space, and social partners, each dimension laying the
492   foundations for the acquisitions of complex abilities in later life.
493   While a straightforward explanation of this intriguing reverse cognitive development pattern is the
494   absence of ”core” cognitive abilities, further research is needed in order to arrive at a comprehen-
495   sive explanation for the mechanistic details of why such abilities fails to emerge as opposed to the
496   case in humans. At the same time, existing theories may provide some preliminary insights into
497   this question. In particular, it is likely that scaling of parameters and training data mainly enhanced
498   the operational resources available to MLLMs but not necessarily allow the grasp of core knowl-
499   edge (Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Kello et al., 2010; von der Malsburg, 2024). This is because neural
500   networks do not themselves contain built-in domain-specific information but are only capable of
501
      representing such knowledge through the topological structures of the connection units and the val-
      ues of their corresponding weights. Therefore, while MLLMs may have gathered all the information
502
      needed for constructing a system of core knowledge from the training data , it is likely represented
503
      in an extremely distributed fashion that prevents reliable tracing and retrieval (Hinton et al., 1986;
504   Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Yang et al., 2022). If the task condition do not offer enough cues to trigger
505   the required aspect of the system, then MLLMs may fail at the task even if they ”know” the concept
506   that is needed for providing the correct answer.
507
      An initial validation of this explanation is that the best model performance on our benchmarks are
508
      achieved when the prompts contain an explanation of the concept tested by the experiment (see Table
509
      2). Such an effect is notable given that unlike regular in-context learning prompts, concept expla-
510   nations contain mainly high-level, abstract information that has no direct relevance to the context of
511   the task. Opposingly, it may be seen as a rather immediate attempt of conceptual pre-training that
512   facilitates the retrieval and synthesis of ”core” conceptual resources distributed throughout the net-
513   works. The effectiveness of such an attempt calls for the attention to training measures for MLLMs
514   aiming at bettering the implementation and use of core cognition, which appears to be a promising
515   approach toward more robust and comprehensive foundational models.

      5   C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we explored the cognitive capabilities of Multi-modal Large Language Models
521
      (MLLMs) through the lens of core cognitive abilities that underpin human intelligence. By introduc-
      ing CogDevelop2K, a novel benchmark that spans 12 subconcepts across developmental stages, we
522
      aimed to assess the fundamental understanding and reasoning capacities of MLLMs. Our evaluation
523
      of 46 models revealed intriguing insights, including a reversed cognitive developmental trajectory
524   compared to humans. This finding raises questions about whether MLLMs truly comprehend tasks
525   or simply exhibit performance without genuine understanding. These results underscore the need
526   for further investigation into the cognitive foundations of MLLMs, as well as the influence of evalu-
527   ation strategies and prompting techniques in shaping their outcomes. Ultimately, this study serves as
528   a step toward unraveling the nature of MLLM intelligence and their potential limitations in mirroring
529   human cognition.
