009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to predict future outcomes given control actions is fundamental for
012            physical reasoning. However, such predictive models, often called world mod-
013            els, have proven challenging to learn and are typically developed for task-specific
014            solutions with online policy learning. We argue that the true potential of world
015            models lies in their ability to reason and plan across diverse problems using only
016
               passive data, without requiring online interactions with the environment. Con-
               cretely, we require world models to have the following three properties: 1) be
017
               trainable on offline, pre-collected trajectories, 2) support test-time behavior op-
018
               timization, and 3) facilitate task-agnostic reasoning. To realize this, we present
019            DINO World Model (DINO-WM), a new method to model visual dynamics with-
020            out reconstructing the visual world. DINO-WM leverages spatial patch features
021            pre-trained with DINOv2, enabling it to learn from offline behavioral trajectories
022            by predicting future patch features. This design allows DINO-WM to achieve ob-
023            servational goals through action sequence optimization, facilitating task-agnostic
024            behavior planning by treating desired goal patch features as prediction targets. We
025            evaluate DINO-WM across various domains, including maze navigation, tabletop
026            pushing, and particle manipulation. Our experiments demonstrate that DINO-WM
027
               can generate zero-shot behavioral solutions at test time without relying on expert
               demonstrations, reward modeling, or pre-learned inverse models. Notably, DINO-
028
               WM exhibits strong generalization capabilities compared to prior state-of-the-art
029
               work, adapting to diverse task families such as arbitrarily configured mazes, push
030            manipulation with varied object shapes, and multi-particle scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
      Robotics and embodied AI have seen tremendous progress in recent years. Advances in imitation
035
      learning and reinforcement learning have enabled agents to learn complex behaviors across diverse
036   tasks (Lee et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Hafner et al., 2024; Hansen et al., 2024;
037   Agarwal et al., 2022; Haldar et al., 2024) (Jia et al., 2024). Despite this progress, generalization
038   remains a major challenge (Zhou et al., 2023). Existing approaches predominantly rely on policies
039   that, once trained, operate in a feed-forward manner during deployment—mapping observations to
040   actions without any further optimization or reasoning. Under this framework, successful generaliza-
041   tion inherently requires agents to possess solutions to all possible tasks and scenarios once training
042   is complete, which is only possible if the agent has seen similar scenarios during training (Brohan
043   et al., 2023b;a; Reed et al., 2022; Etukuru et al., 2024). However, it is neither feasible nor efficient
044
      to learn solutions for all potential tasks and environments in advance.
045   Instead of learning the solutions to all possible tasks during training, an alternate is to fit a dynam-
046   ics model on training data and optimize task-specific behavior during runtime. These dynamics
047   models, also called world models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018), have a long history in robotics and
048   control (Sutton, 1991; Todorov & Li, 2005; Williams et al., 2017). More recently, several works
049   have shown that world models can be trained on raw observational data (Hafner et al., 2019; 2024;
050
      Micheli et al., 2023; Robine et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2024). This enables flexible use of model-
      based optimization to obtain policies as it circumvents the need for explicit state-estimation. Despite
051
      this, significant challenges still remain in its use for solving general-purpose tasks.
052
053   To understand the challenges in world modeling, let us consider the two broad paradigms in learning
      world models: online and offline. In the online setting, access to the environment is often required

077   in the dataset. However, when required to solve a task, methods in this domain require strong
078   auxiliary information to overcome the lack of dense coverage on the task-specific domain. This
079   auxiliary information can take the form of expert demonstrations (Pathak et al., 2018), structured
080   keypoints (Ko et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2024), access to pretrained inverse models (Du et al., 2023;
081   Ko et al., 2023) or dense reward functions (Ding et al., 2024), all of which reduce the generality of
082   using offline world models. The central question to building better offline world models is if there
083   is alternate auxiliary information that does not compromise its generality?
084   In this work, we present DINO-WM, a new and simple method to build task-agnostic world models
085   from an offline dataset of trajectories. DINO-WM models the world dynamics on compact em-
086   beddings of the world, rather than the raw observations themselves. For the embedding, we use
087   pretrained patch-features from the DINOv2 model, which provides both a spatial and object-centric
088   representation prior. We conjecture that this pretrained representation enables robust and consistent
089   world modeling, which relaxes the necessity for task-specific data coverage. Given these visual em-
090   beddings and actions, DINO-WM uses the ViT architecture to predict future embeddings. Once this
091
      model is trained on the offline dataset, planning to solve tasks is constructed as visual goal reaching,
      i.e. to reach a future desired goal given the current observation. Since the predictions by DINO-
092
      WM are high quality (see Figure 4), we can simply use model predictive control with inference-time
093
      optimization to reach desired goals without any extra information during testing.
094
095   DINO-WM is experimentally evaluated on four environment suites spanning maze navigation, slid-
096   ing manipulation, and particle manipulation tasks. Our experiments reveal the following findings:
097
098
      • DINO-WM produce high-quality future world modeling that can be measured by improved visual
        reconstruction from trained decoders. On LPIPS metrics for our hardest tasks, this improves upon
099
        prior state-of-the-art work such as RSSM by 56% (See Section 4.7).
100
101   • Given the latent world models trained using DINO-WM, we show high success for reaching
102     arbitrary goals on our hardest tasks, improving upon prior work by 83% (See Section 4.3).
103   • DINO-WM can be trained across environment variations within a task family (e.g. different
104     maze layouts for navigation or different object shapes for manipulation) and achieve higher rates
105     of success compared to prior work (See Section 4.5).

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      We build on top of several works in building world models, optimizing them, and using compact
111
      visual representations. For conciseness, we only discuss the ones most relevant to DINO-WM.
112
113   Model-based Learning: Learning from models of dynamics has a rich literature spanning the fields
114   of control, planning, and robotics (Sutton, 1991; Todorov & Li, 2005; Astolfi et al., 2008; Holkar
115   & Waghmare, 2010; Williams et al., 2017). Recent works have shown that modeling dynamics and
116
      predicting future states can significantly enhance vision-based learning for embodied agents across
      various applications, including online reinforcement learning (Hafner et al., 2024; Micheli et al.,
117
      2023; Hansen et al., 2024; Robine et al., 2023), exploration (Mendonca et al., 2021; 2023a; Sekar
118
      et al., 2020), planning (Watter et al., 2015) (Finn & Levine, 2017; Ebert et al., 2018; Hafner et al.,
119   2019), and imitation learning (Pathak et al., 2018). Several of these approaches initially focused
120   on state-space dynamics (Deisenroth & Rasmussen, 2011; Chua et al., 2018; Lenz et al., 2015;
121   Nagabandi et al., 2019), and have since been extended to handle image-based inputs, which we
122   address in this work. These world models can predict future states in either pixel space (Finn &
123   Levine, 2017; Ebert et al., 2018; Ko et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023) or latent representation space (Yan
124   et al., 2021). Predicting in pixel space, however, is computationally expensive due to the need for
125   image reconstruction and the overhead of using diffusion models (Ko et al., 2023). On the other
126   hand, latent-space prediction is typically tied to objectives of reconstructing images (Hafner et al.,
127
      2019; 2024; Micheli et al., 2023), which raises concerns about whether the learned features contain
      sufficient information about the task. Moreover, many of these models incorporate reward prediction
128
      (Hafner et al., 2024; Micheli et al., 2023; Robine et al., 2023), or use reward prediction as auxiliary
129
      objective to learn the latent representation (Hansen et al., 2024; 2022), inherently making the world
130   model task-specific. In this work, we aim to decouple task-dependent information from latent-space
131   prediction, striving to develop a versatile and task-agnostic world model capable of generalizing
132   across different scenarios.
133
      Generative Models as World Models: With the recent excitement of large scale foundation mod-
134
      els, there have been initiatives on building large-scale video generation world models conditioned on
135
      agent’s actions in the domain of self-driving (Hu et al., 2023), control (Yang et al., 2023; Bruce et al.,
136   2024), and general-purpose video generation (Liu et al., 2024). These models aim to generate video
137   predictions conditioned on text or high-level action sequences. While these models have demon-
138   strated utility in downstream tasks like data augmentations, their reliance on language conditioning
139   limits their application when precise visually indicative goals need to be reached. Additionally, the
140   use of diffusion models for video generation makes them computationally expensive, further re-
141   stricting their applicability for test-time optimization techniques such as MPC. In this work, we aim
142   to build a world model in latent space rather than in the raw pixel space, which enables more precise
143   planning and control.
144   Pretrained Visual Representations: Significant advancements have been made in the field of
145   visual representation learning, where compact features that capture spatial and semantic information
146   can be readily used for downstream tasks. Pre-trained models like ImageNet pre-trained ResNet (He
147   et al., 2016), I-JEPA (Assran et al., 2023), and DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024) for
148   images, as well as V-JEPA (Bardes et al., 2024) for videos, and R3M (Nair et al., 2022), MVP (Xiao
149   et al., 2022) for robotics have allowed fast adaptation to downstream tasks as they contain rich spatial
150
      and semantic information. While many of these models represent images using a single global
      feature, the introduction of Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) has enabled the
151
      use of pre-trained patch features, as demonstrated by DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024).
152
      DINO employs a self-distillation loss that allows the model to learn representations effectively,
153   capturing semantic layouts and improving spatial understanding within images. In this work, we
154   leverage DINOv2’s patch embeddings to train our world model, and demonstrate that it serves as a
155   versatile encoder capable of handling multiple precise tasks.

188   3.1     DINO- BASED W ORLD M ODELS (DINO-WM)
189
190
      We model the dynamics of the environment in the latent space. More specifically, at each time step
      t, our world model consists of the following components:
191
192                                       Observation model: zt ∼ encθ (zt | ot )
193                                          Transition model: zt+1 ∼ pθ (zt+1 | zt−H:t , at−H:t )
194
                     Decoder model (optional for visualization): ôt ∼ qθ (ot | zt )
195
196   where the observation model encodes image observations to latent states zt , and the transition model
197   takes in a history of past latent states of length H. The decoder model takes in a latent zt , and
198   reconstruct the image observation ot . We use θ to denote the parameters of these models. Note
199   that our decoder is entirely optional, as the training objectives for the decoder is independent for
200   training the rest part of the world model. This eliminates the need to reconstruct images both during
201
      training and testing, which reduces computational costs compared to otherwise coupling together
      the training of the observational model and the decoder, as in (Hafner et al., 2024; Micheli et al.,
202
      2023).
203
204   DINO-WM models only the information available from offline trajectory data in an environment,
205   in contrast to recent online RL world models that also require task-relevant information, such as
206   rewards (Hansen et al., 2022; 2024; Hafner et al., 2020), discount factors (Hafner et al., 2022; Robine
207
      et al., 2023), and termination conditions (Hafner et al., 2024; Micheli et al., 2023).

      3.1.1     O BSERVATION M ODEL
210   With the goal of learning a generic world model across many environments and the real world, we
211   argue that the observation model should 1) be task and environment independent, and 2) contain
212   rich spatial information which is crucial in navigation and manipulation tasks. Contrary to previous
213   works where the observation model is always learned for the task at hand (Hafner et al., 2024), we
214   argue instead that it is not always possible for world models to learn an observation model from
215   scratch when facing a new environment, as perception is a general task that can be learned from
      the large corpus of internet data. Therefore, we choose the out-of-the-box pre-trained DINOv2


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      model as our world model’s observation model, as it has been shown to excel at object detection,
217   semantic segmentation, and depth estimation tasks which require substantial spatial understanding.
218   The observation model is kept frozen throughout both training and testing time. At each time step t,
219   it encodes an image ot to patch embeddings zt ∈ RN ×E , where N denotes the number of patches,
220   and E denotes the embedding dimension. This process is visualized in Figure 2.

222   3.1.2    T RANSITION M ODEL
223

      We adopt the ViT architecture (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) for the transition model due to its suitability
      for processing patch features. We modify the model by removing the tokenization layer, as it oper-
225
      ates on patch embeddings, effectively transforming it into a decoder-only transformer. We further
226
      make a few modifications to the architecture to allow for additional conditioning on proprioception
227   and controller actions.
228
229   Our transition model takes in a history of past latent states zt−H:t−1 and actions at−H:t−1 , where
230
      H is a hyperparameter denoting the context length of the model, and predicts the latent state at next
      time step zt . To properly capture the temporal dependencies, where the world state at time t should
231
      only depend on previous observations and actions, we implement a causal attention mechanism in
232
      the ViT model, enabling the model to predict latents autoregressively at a frame level. Specifically,
233                                                              i

236   We argue that predicting at a frame level and treating patch vectors of one observation as a whole

238   observation rather than isolated tokens, leading to improved temporal generalization.
239
      To model the effect of the agent’s action to the environment, we condition the world model’s pre-
240   dictions on these actions. Specifically, we concatenate the K-dimensional action vector, mapped
241   from the original action representation using a multi-layer perceptron (MLP), to each patch vector

243   concatenating it to the observation latents, thereby integrating it into the latent states.

301   Our experiments are designed to address the following key questions: 1) Can we effectively train
302   DINO-WM using pre-collected offline datasets? 2) Once trained, can DINO-WM be used for
303   visual planning? 3) To what extent does the quality of the world model depend on pre-trained visual

306
      state-of-the-art world models that model the world both in latent space and in raw pixel space.

309

311   and manipulation environments with varying dynamics complexity. For all environments, the obser-

321
         serves as a visual reference but not a goal. A variant with multiple object shapes is also explored.
322   c) Wall: This custom 2D navigation environment featuring two rooms separated by a wall with a
323      door opening. The task requires the agent to navigate from a randomized starting location in one
         room to a goal in the other room, which requires the agent to pass through the door. We introduce

      4.2   BASELINES
333
334   We compare DINO-WM with the following state-of-the-art models commonly used for control:
335
336   a) DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2024): DreamerV3 learns a world model to interpret visual inputs
337      into categorical representation. It predicts future representations and rewards based on given
338
         action and trains an actor-critic policy from its imagined trajectories. In our experiments, we
         train Dreamerv3 agents with our offline datasets without any reward or task information, and
339
         perform MPC on the learned world model for solving downstream tasks.
340
341
      b) TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al., 2024) : TD-MPC2 learns a decoder-free world model in latent space
         and uses reward signals to optimize the latents. It serves as a strong baseline for reconstruction-
342
         free world modeling. In our experiments, we train TD-MPC2 agents with our offline datasets
343
         without any reward or task information, and perform MPC on the learned world model for
344      solving downstream tasks.
345
      c) AVDC (Ko et al., 2023): AVDC leverages a diffusion model to generate an imagined video
346
         of task execution based on initial observation and a textual goal description. It then estimates
347      optical flow between frames to capture object movements and generates robot arm commands.
348      Since this model generates an entire sequence of observations conditioned on a given goal, we
349      provide qualitative evaluations of the method, and provide MPC planning results for an action-
350      conditioned variant of the method in Appendix A.7.

504   4.7     D ECODING AND I NTERPRETING THE L ATENTS
505
506   Although DINO-WM operates in latent space and the observation model is not trained with pixel
507
      reconstruction objectives, training a decoder is still valuable for interpreting the model’s predictions.
      We evaluate the image quality of predicted futures across all models and find that our approach
508
      outperforms others, even those whose encoders are trained with environment-specific reconstruction
509
      objectives. This demonstrates the robustness of DINO-WM despite the lack of explicit pixel-level
510   supervision. We report two key metrics: Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) and
511   Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018) on the reconstruction of
512   world models’ predicted future states. SSIM measures the perceived quality of images by evaluating
513   structural information and luminance consistency between predicted and ground-truth images, with
514   higher values indicating greater similarity. LPIPS, on the other hand, assesses perceptual similarity
515   by comparing deep representations of images, with lower scores reflecting closer visual similarity.

528   5     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
529
530   In this work, we introduce DINO-WM, a simple yet effective technique for modeling visual dy-
531   namics in latent space without the need for pixel-space reconstruction. We have demonstrated that
532   DINO-WM captures environmental dynamics and generalizes to unseen configurations, indepen-
533   dent of task specifications, enabling visual reasoning at test time and generating zero-shot solutions
534   for downstream tasks through planning. DINO-WM takes a step toward bridging the gap between
535   task-agnostic world modeling and reasoning and control, offering promising prospects for generic
536   world models in real-world applications. For limitations, DINO-WM still relies on the availability
537
      of ground truth actions from agents, which may not always be feasible when training with vast video
      data from the internet. Additionally, while we currently plan in action space for downstream task
538
      solving, an extension of this work could involve developing a hierarchical structure that integrates
539
      high-level planning with low-level control policies to enable solving more fine-grained control tasks.

816
               The agent’s dynamics incorporate physical properties such as velocity, acceleration, and

822
               shaped block to a designated target position. It has a two-dimensional action space with

823
               and color of the object to assess the model’s capability to adapt to novel tasks. We generate
824
               a dataset of 18500 samples replaying the original released expert trajectories with various
825            levels of noise and evaluate the model’s performance across all different shapes to assess
826            its adaptability.
827
828         c) Wall: This custom 2D navigation environment features two rooms separated by a wall with
829            a door. The agent’s task is to navigate from a randomized starting location in one room to
830            a goal in the other, passing through the door. We present a variant where wall and door
831
               positions are randomized, testing the model’s generalization to novel configurations. For
               the fixed wall setting, we train on a fully random dataset of 2000 trajectories each with 50
832
               time steps. For the variant with multiple training environment configurations, we generate
833
               10240 random trajectories.
834
835         d) Rope Manipulation: Described in Zhang et al. (2024), this task is simulated with Nvidia
836            Flex and consists of an XArm interacting with a soft rope placed on a tabletop. The objec-
837            tive is to move the rope from an arbitrary starting configuration to a specified goal config-
838            uration. For training, we generate a random dataset of 1000 trajectories of 20 time steps of
839
               random actions from random starting positions, while testing involves goal configurations
               set from varied initial positions, incorporating random variations in orientation, length, and
840
               spatial displacement.
841
842         e) Granular Manipulation: This environment uses the same simulation setup as Rope Ma-
843            nipulation and involves manipulating about a hundred particles to form desired shapes. The
844            training data consists of 1000 trajectories of 20 time steps of random actions starting from

846            verse starting positions, along with random variations in particle distribution, spacing, and
