009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Imitation learning (IL) enables agents to acquire skills directly from expert
012            demonstrations, providing a compelling alternative to reinforcement learning.
013            However, prior online IL approaches struggle with complex tasks characterized
014            by high-dimensional inputs and complex dynamics. In this work, we propose a
015            novel approach to online imitation learning that leverages reward-free world mod-
016
               els. Our method learns environmental dynamics entirely in latent spaces without
               reconstruction, enabling efficient and accurate modeling. We adopt the inverse
017
               soft-Q learning objective, reformulating the optimization process in the Q-policy
018
               space to mitigate the instability associated with traditional optimization in the
019            reward-policy space. By employing a learned latent dynamics model and planning
020            for control, our approach consistently achieves stable, expert-level performance in
021            tasks with high-dimensional observation or action spaces and intricate dynamics.
022            We evaluate our method on a diverse set of benchmarks, including DMControl,
023            MyoSuite, and ManiSkill2, demonstrating superior empirical performance com-
024            pared to existing approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Imitation learning (IL) has garnered considerable attention due to its broad applications across vari-
029
      ous domains, such as robotic manipulation (Zhu et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023) and autonomous driv-
030
      ing (Hu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021). Unlike reinforcement learning, where agents learn through
031   reward signals, IL involves learning directly from expert demonstrations. Recent advances in offline
032   IL, including Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) and Implicit BC (Florence et al., 2022), highlight
033   the advantages of leveraging large datasets in conjunction with relatively straightforward behav-
034   ioral cloning (BC) methodologies. However, despite its wide applicability, IL methods that do not
035   incorporate online interaction often suffer from poor generalization outside the expert data distribu-
036   tion, especially when encountering out-of-distribution states. Such limitations make these methods
037   vulnerable to failure, as even minor perturbations in state can lead to significant performance degra-
038   dation. This is often reflected in issues such as bias accumulation and suboptimal results (Reddy
039
      et al., 2019). These challenges stem from BC’s inability to fully capture the underlying dynamics of
      the environment and its inherent lack of exploration capabilities (Garg et al., 2021).
040
041   To address these shortcomings, methods like GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016), SQIL (Reddy et al., 2019),
042   IQ-Learn (Garg et al., 2021), and CFIL (Freund et al., 2023) have introduced value or reward esti-
043   mation to facilitate a deeper understanding of the environment, while leveraging online interactions
044   to enhance exploration. Nevertheless, these approaches continue to face substantial challenges,
045
      particularly when applied to tasks with high-dimensional observation and action spaces or com-
      plex dynamics. Additionally, framing online IL as a min-max optimization problem within the
046
      reward-policy space, often inspired by inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) techniques, introduces
047
      instability during training (Garg et al., 2021). Recent advancements in world models have demon-
048   strated exceptional performance across a wide range of control tasks, underscoring their potential
049   in complex decision-making and planning scenarios (Hafner et al., 2019a;b; 2020; 2023; Hansen
050   et al., 2022; 2023). Specifically, world models offer advantages over model-free agents in terms of
051   sampling complexity and future planning capabilities, resulting in superior performance on complex
052   tasks (Hansen et al., 2022; 2023; Hafner et al., 2019a). Notably, decoder-free world models, which
053   operate exclusively in latent spaces without reconstruction, have proven to be highly effective and
      efficient in modeling complex environment dynamics (Hansen et al., 2022; 2023).

054
      Motivated by these insights, we explore the application of world models in the context of online
055   imitation learning without rewards, enabling IL agents to develop a deeper understanding of en-
056   vironmental dynamics and improve their performance in tasks characterized by high-dimensional
057   observations and complex dynamics. In this work, we present a novel approach to online imitation
058   learning that leverages the strengths of decoder-free world models, specifically designed for com-
059   plex tasks involving high-dimensional observations, intricate dynamics, and vision-based inputs. In
060   contrast to conventional latent world models, which rely on reward and Q-function estimation, our
061   approach completely eliminates the need for explicit reward modeling. We propose a framework for
062   reward-free world models that redefines the optimization process within the Q-policy space, address-
063
      ing the instability associated with min-max optimization in the reward-policy space. By utilizing an
      inverse soft-Q learning objective for the critic network (Garg et al., 2021), our method derives re-
064
      wards directly from Q-values and the policy, effectively rendering the world model reward-free.
065
      Moreover, by performing imitation learning online, our model addresses key challenges in IL, such
066   as out-of-distribution errors and bias accumulation.
067
068   Through online training with finite-horizon planning based on learned latent dynamics, our method
069
      demonstrates strong performance in complex environments. We evaluate our approach across a
      diverse set of locomotion and manipulation tasks, utilizing benchmarks from DMControl (Tunya-
070
      suvunakool et al., 2020), MyoSuite (Caggiano et al., 2022), and ManiSkill2 (Gu et al., 2023), and
071
      demonstrate superior empirical performance compared to existing online imitation learning meth-
072   ods.
073
074   Our contributions are as follows:
075
             • We introduce a novel, robust methodology that leverages world models for online imitation
076
               learning, effectively addressing the challenges posed by complex robotics tasks.
077
078          • We propose an innovative gradient-free planning process, operating without explicit reward
079            modeling, within the context of model predictive control.
080          • We showcase the model’s effectiveness in inverse reinforcement learning tasks by demon-
081            strating a positive correlation between decoded and ground-truth rewards.

      Our work builds upon literature in Imitation Learning (IL) and Model-based Reinforcement Learn-

088
      Imitation Learning Recent works regarding IL leveraged deep neural architectures to achieve
089
      better performance. Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL) (Ho & Ermon, 2016) formu-
090
      lated the reward learning as a min-max problem similar to GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Model-
091   based Adversarial Imitation Learning (MAIL) (Baram et al., 2016) extended the GAIL approach to
092   incorporate a forward model trained by data-driven methodology. Inverse Soft Q-Learning (Garg
093   et al., 2021) reformulated the learning objective of GAIL and integrated their findings into soft
094   actor-critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018) and soft Q-learning agents for imitation learning. CFIL (Freund
095   et al., 2023) introduced a coupled flow approach for reward generation and policy learning using
096   expert demonstrations. ValueDICE (Kostrikov et al., 2019) proposed an off-policy imitation learn-
097   ing approach by transforming the distribution ratio estimation objective. Das et al. (2021) proposed
098   a model-based inverse RL approach by predicting key points for imitation learning tasks. SQIL
099
      (Reddy et al., 2019) proposed an online imitation learning algorithm with soft Q functions. Diffu-
      sion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) is a recent offline IL method using a diffusion model for behavioral
100
      cloning. Implicit BC (Florence et al., 2022) discovers that treating supervised policy learning with
101
      an implicit model generally improves the empirical performance for robot learning tasks. Hybrid in-
102   verse reinforcement learning (Ren et al., 2024) proposed a new methodology leveraging a mixture of
103   online and expert demonstrations for agent training, achieving robust performance in environments
104   with stochasticity. Prior works (Englert et al., 2013; Hu et al., 2022; Igl et al., 2022) explored the
105   potentials of model-based imitation learning on real-world robotics control and autonomous driv-
106   ing. EfficientImitate (Yin et al., 2022) combined EfficientZero (Ye et al., 2021) with adversarial
107   imitation learning, achieving excellent results in DMControl (Tassa et al., 2018) imitation learning
      tasks. V-MAIL (Rafailov et al., 2021) introduced a model-based approach for imitation learning

108
      using variational models. CMIL (Kolev et al., 2024) proposed an imitation learning approach with
109   conservative world models for image-based manipulation tasks. Ditto (DeMoss et al., 2023) devel-
110   oped an offline imitation learning approach with Dreamer V2 (Hafner et al., 2020) and adversarial
111   imitation learning. DMIL (Zhang et al., 2023) utilized a discriminator to simultaneously evaluate

115   Model-based Reinforcement Learning Contemporary model-based RL methods often learn a

      allowing an agent to learn and plan effectively. TD-MPC series (Hansen et al., 2022)(Hansen et al.,
121   2023) learns a scalable world model for model predictive control using temporal difference learning
122   objective.

      Our approach employs a model-based methodology to address challenges in online imitation learn-
      ing. By integrating a data-driven approach for latent dynamics learning with planning for control,
125
      the agent is able to effectively capture and leverage the underlying environment dynamics. Empirical
126
      evaluations demonstrate that our model achieves superior performance on complex online imitation
127   learning tasks compared to existing methods.

187   4     M ETHODOLOGY
188
189   In reinforcement learning, world models typically regress explicit reward signals provided by the
190   environment. In imitation learning, prior approaches (Kolev et al., 2024; DeMoss et al., 2023) aim
191   to train a reward model through adversarial objectives alongside a separate critic network trained
192
      on temporal difference objectives. In contrast, we eliminate the need for a separate reward model
      by retrieving rewards directly from the learned critic. To this end, we propose a reward-free world
193
      model that learns exclusively from reward-free expert demonstrations and environment interactions,
194
      without training a dedicated reward model. Furthermore, since our model can decode dense rewards
195   from the critic, it can solve inverse reinforcement learning tasks using reward-free interactions and
196   a limited set of expert demonstrations that include only states and actions.

      where µ, σ ∈ RH×m , m = dim A. (zt , ..., zt+H ) are computed by unrolling with (at , .., at+H )
361
      using dynamics model dθ . Eq.15 is solved by iteratively computing soft expected return ϕΓ of
362
      sampled actions from N (µ, σ 2 ) and update µ, σ based on weighted average with ϕΓ . We describe the
363   detailed planning procedure in Algorithm 1. Eq.15 is an estimation of the soft-Q learning objective
364   (Haarnoja et al., 2017) for RL with horizon H. After iteration, we execute the first action sampled
365   from the normal distribution at ∼ N (µ∗t , (σt∗ )2 I) in the environment to collect a new trajectory for
366   behavioral buffer Bπ .

      We propose an online imitation learning approach that utilizes reward-free world models to ad-
      dress tasks in complex environments. By incorporating latent planning and dynamics learning,
531

532
      stable, expert-level performance on challenging tasks, including dexterous hand manipulation and

534   real-world applications in manipulation and locomotion, particularly for tasks that involve visual
535   inputs and complex environment dynamics.
