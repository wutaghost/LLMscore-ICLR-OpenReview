009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural speech models build entangled internal representations, which capture a
012            variety of features (e.g., pitch, loudness, syntax, or semantics of an utterance)
013            in a distributed encoding. This complexity makes it difficult to track how such
014            representations rely on textual and acoustic information when used in downstream
015            applications, limiting their interpretability. In this paper, we build upon the Infor-
016            mation Bottleneck principle to propose a disentanglement framework that separates
017            speech representations learned by pre-trained neural speech models into two dis-
018
               tinct components: one encoding content (i.e., what can be transcribed as text) and
               the other encoding acoustic features relevant to a downstream task. We apply and
019
               evaluate our framework to emotion recognition and speaker identification target
020
               tasks, quantifying the contribution of textual and acoustic features at each model
021            layer. We also apply our disentanglement framework as an attribution method to
022            identify the most salient speech frame representations from both the textual and
023            acoustic perspectives.
024
025
026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The internal activation vectors of most modern deep learning systems, including Neural Speech
029   Models (NSM) such as Wav2Vec2 (Baevski et al., 2020), HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) are highly
030   entangled. This means that distinct characteristics (such as pitch, loudness, syntax and semantics)
031   of a spoken utterance are not separated into individual dimensions within the model’s latent space,
032   but are instead mixed together. Entanglement is a major obstacle for our ability to interpret and to
033
      intervene; disentanglement, to the extent that it is possible and even if imperfect, is therefore often
      highly desirable.
034
035   In this paper, we investigate to what extent we can learn to disentangle speech representations,
036   focusing on the following desiderata:
037            • The method must disentangle representations already learned by large pre-trained spoken
038              language models, with minimal extra data and computation.
039            • The method must be general, and applicable to representations from pre-trained models with
040              varying architectures, sizes, and learning objectives.
041            • The approach should allow for acoustic features to emerge based on their relevance to a
042              target downstream task, rather than being based on pre-specified static features such as pitch
043              and timbre: if acoustic dimensions are defined in advance, some task-relevant aspects may
044              be lost.
045   The above properties put strong constraints on our approach and distinguish our study from most
046   work on learning disentangled representations for speech (Qian et al., 2019; 2020; Qu et al., 2024; Ju
047   et al., 2024).
048   Although our proposed disentanglement framework is not modality-specific, in this paper we focus
049   on two speech-related downstream tasks. As our first case study, we choose emotion recognition as
050   the target task: while the content of an utterance is a strong cue for detecting emotion, the acoustic
051   features provide additional information. For example, consider the utterances “I’m so happy” and
052   “I’m fine,”: where the former explicitly conveys happiness through its content, the latter can represent
053   a wide range of emotions depending on prosody and tone. Our second case study closely follows the
      setup of the first, but we choose speaker identification as the target task. We hypothesize that content

054
      offers only limited information, and the acoustic information will play an almost exclusive role in
055   revealing the identity.
056
057   In Section 2, we propose a two-stage disentanglement framework (sketched in Figure1) based on the
058
      Information Bottleneck principle (Tishby et al., 2000; Alemi et al., 2016) to disentangle textual and
      acoustic features encoded in NSMs. In stage 1, we train a decoder with two objectives: to map the
059
      internal representation of an existing speech model to text, but also minimize the presence of irrelevant
060
      information in these representations. The goal is to ensure that the latent representation preserves
061   only the speech features necessary for accurate transcription, while filtering out any extraneous
062   characteristics. In stage 2, we train a second decoder on the same speech representations. This
063   decoder also has access to the latent ‘textual’ representation learned in stage 1, and is again trained
064   with 2 objectives: to predict our target labels (emotion or speaker IDs), and to minimize the amount
065   of information encoded in the vector. This objective should ensure that the representation learned
066   in stage 2 avoids encoding textual information – since the decoder already has access to it and the
067   information minimization term discourages redundancy. Instead, it is expected to capture additional
068   acoustic characteristics that are beneficial for the specific task.
069   In Section 3 we describe the models, dataset and training regime we use. In Sections 4 and 5 we
070   evaluate our framework. We obtain highly compressed latent representations, that yield strong
071   performance on both the standard transcription and our two target tasks. Moreover, our probing
072   (Alain & Bengio, 2016) experiments show that the representations we obtain are almost perfectly
073   disentangled from each other: while textual latent representations can predict transcriptions as
074   effectively as the original speech representations, they exhibit random performance when predicting
075
      acoustic features (e.g., pitch or speaker identity). In contrast, acoustic representations excel at
      predicting these features but show random performance for transcriptions, validating the effectiveness
076
      of our disentanglement approach. The textual latent representations produced in the first stage are
077
      independent of the target task and can be easily applied to new downstream tasks.1
078
079   Finally, we analyze the emergence of the two types of representations in the original speech model.
080   In Section 6, we trace back the contributions of the different layers of pre-trained and fine-tuned
081
      versions of the Wav2Vec2 (Baevski et al., 2020) to the representation of emotion. We find that
      as we progress through the layers, the acoustic contribution to emotion recognition significantly
082
      decreases in models fine-tuned on ASR, while the textual contribution increases, as they benefit
083
      from more accurate transcription and understanding of word polarity. Additionally, in Section 7,
084   we qualitatively demonstrate that our disentanglement framework can serve as a feature attribution
085   method to highlight the most significant frame representations for a given target task. Unlike existing
086   gradient-based methods (Sundararajan et al., 2017), our approach allows us to determine whether a
087   frame’s contribution is textual or acoustic. Such disentangled attribution techniques can have many
088   applications, e.g., in psychiatric research where the mismatch between textual and acoustic emotion
089   expression was shown predictive of mood and disorders (Niu et al., 2023) or in bias control within
090   speech agents.

098   2.1       VARIATIONAL I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
099
      The core idea of an Information Bottleneck (IB, Tishby et al., 2000) is to learn a stochastic encoding
100
      Z that maximizes the prediction of a target variable Y , while minimizing the information retained
101   about the input H. Accordingly, the loss function to be optimized based on this principle can be
102   defined as follows:
103
                                         LIB = I(Z, Y ) − βI(H, Z)                                        (1)
104
105   where I(., .) represents mutual information that measures the dependence between two variables.
106   The coefficient β ≥ 0 controls the trade-off between retaining information about either H or Y in Z.

143

148   Σ: pθ (z|h) = N (z|µθ (h), Σθ (h)). The optimization is performed using the reparameterization
149   trick (Kingma & Welling, 2013).

      2.2    O UR P ROPOSED M ETHOD
152
153   Consider a sequence of speech representations (h1 , ..., hT ), produced by a pre-trained speech model,
154   where ht represents the speech frame representation at timestamp t, and T denotes the total number
155   of frames in a given utterance. Given a target task, our goal is to decompose each frame representation
156   into two distinct latent representations: zttextual and ztacoustic . Our approach to disentangling these
157   two latent representations involves two stages, sketched in Figure 1. Both stages use frozen speech
158   representations obtained from the same speech model as input.

      2.2.1    S TAGE 1
163
164   In the first stage, we aim to distill the textual content from speech frame representations, while
165   discarding other non-textual features. The textual capability of a speech model is typically evaluated
166   using automatic speech recognition (ASR), and measured by the word error rate (WER) metric, which
167   assesses how accurately the model can transcribe spoken utterances based on their representations.
168   We extract all speech frame representations for a given audio from a pre-trained speech model
169   and compute a weighted average across model layers, with the weights learned during training.
170   These frame representations are then given as input to a bottleneck encoder, which compresses the
171   information into low-dimensional latent frame representations. To decode transcriptions from the
172   latent frame representations, we employ the Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC, Graves
173   et al., 2006) loss as the task loss term in Equation 2, thus minimizing the following loss function:
174                                              LCTC − βLInformation                                              (3)
175
176   In this way, we force the bottleneck encoder p(z textual |h) to retain only the information necessary
177   for transcription (as encouraged by the CTC loss), while discarding irrelevant features in the original
178   representation (h) (constrained by the information loss4 ). We refer to the latent frame representation
179   for frame t learned at this stage as zttextual . Intuitively, these latent representations capture the
180   minimum statistics of speech representations needed for decoding transcriptions.
181
182   2.2.2    S TAGE 2
183
      Our goal in the second stage is to capture acoustic features that complement the textual features
184
      learned in stage 1 and contribute to the downstream task. To achieve this, we replace the task loss
185
      in Equation 2 with the Cross-Entropy loss (CE) over the target class labels, thus, minimizing the
186   following loss function:
187                                          LCE − βLInformation                                      (4)
188
189   Using labeled data for our target task in this stage, we extract speech frame representations from
190   the same speech model and again learn a weighted average over layers. These representations are
191   then fed into a bottleneck encoder p(z acoustic |h) — with the same architecture as the encoder in
192   stage 1 — to form the complimentary latent representations (ztacoustic ). We apply the information
193   loss to the frame-wise latent representations at the output of the encoder. Subsequently, we pass
194
      these latent representations through an attention layer5 to have latent representations at the utterance
      level, since labels for our target tasks are assigned to the whole utterance. Finally, the pooled latent
195
      representation z acoustic is concatenated with the frozen textual latent representations z textual
196
      (previously trained in Stage 1) to decode the target task. This conditional setup encourages the
197   trainable latent representations to retain only non-textual features, particularly those absent in the
198   textual latent representations. During the training process in this stage, no gradient updates are
199   directed back to the zttextual learned in stage 1.

224
225   To obtain speech representations, we use two prominent self-supervised speech models: Wav2Vec2

      3.3       DATA
235
236   Transcription. For training in stage 1, we use subsets of two widely used read speech corpora:
237   LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) and Mozilla’s Common Voice 17.0 (Ardila et al., 2019). The
238   former is derived from audiobooks, while the latter is recorded by contributors reading sentences
239   displayed on a screen. We randomly select 4,000 examples from each corpus, ensuring an equal
240   representation of gender and speaker ID, with each sample having a maximum duration of 14 seconds.

242
      we use the entire test-clean set of the LibriSpeech dataset. Following (Baevski et al., 2020), we
      remove non-spoken special characters (e.g., commas and periods) from transcriptions, as these are
243
      not included in our target vocabulary.
244
245
      Target tasks. For emotion data in stage 2, we utilize IEMOCAP (Busso et al., 2008) database,
246
      which consists of five dyadic sessions involving ten speakers (5 male, 5 female). Following prior
247
      research (Li et al., 2021; 2022), we exclude utterances without transcripts and combine Happy and
248   Excited labels to form a 4-way classification task. We then undersample the dataset to balance
249   emotion classes, resulting in 4,064 utterances (∼ 5 hours of audio, with an average duration of 4.4
250   seconds per segment). Each utterance is assigned one emotion from the label set: {Angry, Happy,
251   Neutral, Sad}. For Speaker Identity as our target task, we utilize a subset of Mozilla’s Common Voice
252   17.0 dataset (Ardila et al., 2019), consisting of 4,000 training and 1,000 test samples, stratified by
253   two genders (male and female) and 24 speaker identities. All audio files in this study are resampled
254   to 16 kHz to match the sampling rate used for the pre-training data of the target models.

456   7           L OCALIZING SALIENT TEXTUAL AND ACOUSTIC FRAMES
457
458
459
      In this section, we use our disentanglement framework to localize salient input features for the target
      tasks. The attention layer in stage 2 of our framework (see Figure 1) can be used to identify those
460
      frames in the original audio input whose latent representations contribute most to our target tasks.
461
      Crucially, the disentanglement mechanism allows us to clearly separate the contributions of acoustic
462   features from those of textual features. This disentangled attribution could be particularly useful in
463   fields like psychiatry, where differences between textual and acoustic emotional expressions can aid
464   in the diagnosis of disorders (Niu et al., 2023), or in detecting bias in speech agents’ responses to
465   user requests.
466
      Finding salient input features for model decisions is often done by computing the gradient of the
467
      model’s output with respect to the inputs (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Ancona et al., 2018; Yuan et al.,
468   2019; Samek et al., 2019). To compare our attention scores with gradient-based attribution scores, we
469   train another classifier for the target task on the original hidden states of the Wav2Vec2 model and
470   use Integrated Gradients (IG) to compute attribution to individual frames (Sundararajan et al., 2017).
471   We normalize the IG scores to sum to 1.
472
      We then obtain frame-by-frame distributions of both acoustic and sentiment features to compare them
473
      directly with attribution results. For acoustic features, we focus on intensity and pitch, identifying
474   and representing peaks and valleys in their temporal patterns. To analyze sentiment, we use the
475   spaCy toolkit (Honnibal & Montani, 2017) to annotate word-level polarity (positive, negative, or
476   neutral) within the utterances. Using Montreal Forced Aligner (McAuliffe et al., 2017), we map these
477   word-level labels to frames (see details in Appendix J). As a result, each feature vector for a speech
478   frame includes the following dimensions: (1) the presence of a peak or valley in intensity, (2) the
479   same for pitch, and (3) the presence of a ‘sentiment-laden’ word, all normalized to the range [0, 1].
480   We then compute the dot product between the frame-wise attribution scores and the corresponding
481   feature vectors. Figure 5 presents these results, averaged across all examples in the IEMOCAP
482   test set, and demonstrates that acoustic attention effectively captures peaks and valleys in acoustic
483   features (intensity and pitch), while textual attention focuses on word polarity. Both have higher
484   agreement with features than Integrated Gradient scores (see Appendix K for qualitative examples).
485   Note that textual attention exhibits fairly high similarity with acoustic features as polar words are
      often pronounced with emotional emphasis.

      8   R ELATED W ORK
501
502   Learning disentangled representations such that distinct factors are separable and controllable has
503   been the focus of many studies. Bengio et al. (2013) motivate this approach within the context
504   of deep learning, while Wang et al. (2024) provide a general up-to-date overview. For speech,
505   disentanglement has been used for controllable style transfer in voice conversion, where the goal is to
506
      modify para-linguistic information while preserving linguistic content (van den Oord et al., 2017;
      Polyak et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2019; 2020; Choi et al., 2021; 2022; Qu et al., 2024). In our study, we
507
      specifically posit the following desiderata which distinguish our study from most work on learning
508
      disentangled representations for speech:
509
             • The approach disentangles representations learned by pre-trained models, with minimal
510            data or computation. This contrasts with methods (such as AutoVC (Qian et al., 2019),
511            SpeechSplit (Qian et al., 2020), Prosody2Vec (Qu et al., 2024), FACodec (Ju et al., 2024))
512            that rely on learning disentangled representations from scratch, or require large amounts of
513            fine-tuning data: training of Prosody2Vec takes around three weeks. In contrast, we train
514            our proposed method directly on the target task, and it takes only a few minutes.
515
             • The approach applies to pre-trained models with different architectures. This contrasts with
516            methods tailored to a specific architecture: for example, Prosody2Vec (Qu et al., 2024)
517            builds on top of HuBERT’s (Hsu et al., 2021) quantization module to encode content.
518
519
              • The approach should allow for acoustic features to emerge based on a target task. In contrast,
                an approach such as SpeechSplit (Qian et al., 2020) splits audio into pre-specified factors
520
                (content, timbre, pitch, rhythm).
521
      From the perspective of employing VIB to maximize the mutual information between latent represen-
522
      tations and target labels (as opposed to using Autoencoders and reconstruction loss), our work relates
523   to (Gao et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2020) in computer vision. Regarding feature attribution, our work
524   relates to (Wang et al., 2023), which proposes a multi-modal information bottleneck approach aimed
525   at disentangling relevant visual and textual features to enhance the interpretability of vision-language
526   models.
527
528
529   9   C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      We present a disentanglement framework which separates entangled representations of NSMs into
532
      textual and acoustic components, while retaining only features relevant to the target tasks. In ex-
533
      periments with the emotion recognition and speaker identification tasks, we demonstrated that the
534   framework can effectively isolate key features, improving interpretability, whilst maintaining the
535   performance of the original model. The framework holds potential for applications where disentan-
536   glement is key, such as ASR systems where privacy-preservation is key for responsible deployment
537   (and sometimes even a legal requirement); separating sensitive speaker-specific information from
538   task-relevant information is essential in such systems. We also showed that the the framework is
539   useful for disentangled feature attribution, revealing the most significant speech frames from both
      textual and acoustic perspectives.

      In the input of both VIB and probing training setups in Section 4, we train weights for layer averaging

731   probing setup.
732
      In stage 1 we observe that, in pre-trained models, the upper-middle layers are more capable of
733
      decoding transcription. In fine-tuned models, however, this information shifts to and becomes
734   concentrated at the final layer. This pattern is consistent across both Base and Large model sizes as
735   well as for different bottleneck dimensions.

737
      For emotion recognition and speaker identity tasks in stage 2, however, there is a notable difference
      between Base and Large models. In Base models, the earlier layers contribute more in both pre-trained
738
      and fine-tuned setups. In contrast, layers in Large models contribute uniformly, suggesting that the
739
      acoustic information useful for these target tasks is distributed across layers in Large models.

742   F    S ANITY CHECK PROBING EXPERIMENT FOR OTHER EXISTING METHODS
743
744   In this section, we compare the disentanglement effectiveness of our proposed method with FACodec
745   (Ju et al., 2024) and SpeechSplit (Qian et al., 2020), two existing methods mentioned by one of the
746
      reviewers. We extracted the embeddings of the disentangled components from their framework and
      trained a linear classifier to probe them for various types of textual and acoustic information (similar
747
      to the probe in Section 5.1).
748
749   Figures F.1 and F.2 demonstrate the results for FACodec and SpeechSplit, respectively. We can see
750   that their acoustic components perform poorly in linearly decoding acoustic features compared to
751   our acoustic latent representation (Figure 2). Moreover, it is not possible to linearly decode the
752
      transcription (content) of the audio from their content vectors (in contrast to ours).
753   This suggests that while existing work on speech disentanglement has mainly focused on reconstruct-
754   ing input audio with different styles or speakers, the disentanglement quality of their components has
755   not been properly evaluated. For instance, in Section 4 in (Ju et al., 2024), it is stated: ”For Word
      Error Rate (WER), we use an ASR model (footnote: HuBERT-Large fine-tuned on the 960 hours of


                                                           14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      LibriSpeech for ASR) to transcribe our generated speech.” However, this approach is not an accurate
757   way of assessing the quality of content vectors since the HuBERT model here acts as a confounding
758   variable that significantly contributes to the reported WER. In contrast, our CTC probing method
759   directly evaluates the ease of decoding transcriptions from these content vectors, providing a more
760   reliable measure of their disentanglement quality.
