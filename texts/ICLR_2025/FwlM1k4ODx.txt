011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The information-bottleneck principle suggests that the foundation of learning lies
014            in the ability to create compact representations. In machine learning, this goal
015
               can be formulated as a Lagrangian optimization problem, where the mutual infor-
               mation between the input and latent representations must be minimized without
016
               compromising the correctness of the model’s predictions. Unfortunately, mutual
017
               information is difficult to compute in deterministic deep neural network classifiers,
018            which greatly limits the application of this approach to challenging scenarios. In
019            this paper, we tackle this problem from a different perspective that does not in-
020            volve direct computation of the mutual information. We develop a method that
021            induces the collapse of latent representations belonging to the same class into a
022            single point. This point collapse not only significantly reduces the entropy of the
023            latent distribution, thereby creating an information bottleneck that correlates with
024            improved generalization, but also makes the network Lipschitz, offering guarantees
025            for enhanced robustness. Our method is straightforward to implement. We demon-
026
               strate that it substantially improves the network’s robustness, provides a small
               yet statistically significant increase in generalization, and enhances the network’s
027
               ability to detect misclassifications.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Information-Bottleneck (IB) theory provides a theoretical framework for representation learning in
033   deep neural networks (DNNs) Tishby et al. (2000); Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015). The underlying
034   idea of IB is that, to promote more generalizable and robust learning, DNNs must discard irrelevant
035   information. This concept is formalized by minimizing the mutual information between the input
036   and latent representations while still retaining the information necessary for accurate prediction.
037   This principle leads to a Lagrangian optimization problem, which has been shown to enhance the
038   network’s generalization and robustness Alemi et al. (2019). The connection between generalization
039
      and robustness is in fact well-established in the literature Xu & Mannor (2010); Szegedy et al. (2014);
      Achille & Soatto (2018); Novak et al. (2018). Furthermore, empirical evidence supports that IB
040
      improves network performance Hu et al. (2024), and recent theoretical work provides rigorous
041
      arguments for IB’s role in controlling generalization errors Kawaguchi et al. (2023).
042
043   The IB principle suggests that DNNs aim to transform high-dimensional input data into more compact
044   yet sufficiently informative latent representations. In fact, this transformation is automatically
045
      enforced within DNNs, as demonstrated in Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby (2017). Their findings primarily
      identify two dynamic phases: empirical error minimization (ERM) and compression. During the
046
      ERM phase, which occurs over a limited number of epochs, the mutual information between the
047
      layers and the input/output increases; this phase is also referred to as the fitting phase. This is followed
048   by the compression phase, which extends over a much greater number of epochs. In this phase, the
049   mutual information between the layers and the input decreases. During the compression phase, the
050   network progressively constructs more compact embeddings of the input data layer by layer. In
051   DNN classifiers, this process can be expressed as a data separation law, which demonstrates that
052   each layer improves the separation of different classes at a constant geometric rate He & Su (2023).
053   Such evolving separation results in the fascinating phenomenon of Neural Collapse (NC) Papyan
      et al. (2020); Han et al. (2022), which can be observed in the latent space of DNN classifiers. NC

054
      spontaneously occurs in the penultimate layer of overparameterized DNNs during the terminal phase
055   of training (TPT), i.e., when training continues beyond the point of zero training error. Interestingly,
056   it has been documented that during the TPT, the network improves its generalization and robustness,
057   implicitly suggesting a connection with the IB principle. During this terminal phase, the network
058   continues to find more compact representations by increasing the relative distance between latent
059   representations belonging to different classes, eventually developing a highly symmetric structure.
060   In practice, this entails that the class means in the penultimate layer collapse to the vertices of an
061   equiangular tight-frame simplex (ETFS).
062   The occurrence of NC has been widely investigated theoretically in the context of unconstrained
063   feature models, that is when the topmost layers of the classifiers are treated as free optimization
064   variables Mixon et al. (2020); Fang et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2021); Graf et al. (2021); Ji et al. (2022);
065   Tirer & Bruna (2022); Ergen & Pilanci (2020); Zhou et al. (2022a). In these idealized models, the
066   ETFS are shown to be the only global minimizers. Nonetheless, in practical applications, perfect
067   convergence to an ETFS is not always observed, as highlighted in Tirer et al. (2023), indicating a
068   discrepancy between theoretical models and real-world neural networks. The phenomenon of NC has
069
      also been recently explored using information-theoretic metrics such as matrix mutual information
      ratio and matrix entropy difference ratio Zhang et al. (2024); Song et al. (2024b;a).
070
071   Besides improved generalization and robustness, subsequent research has revealed additional benefits
072   of NC. For instance, in Galanti et al. (2021; 2022); Li et al. (2024) NC has been linked to transfer
073   learning. In these works it is shown that the NC properties also emerge for classes not seen during
074   training, provided these come from the same distribution as the training set. This implies that
075
      only a few examples are needed to train a linear classifier on these new classes. Following this
      trajectory, other works Wang et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023) utilize NC-based metrics to enhance the
076
      transferability of models. Another application has been in connecting NC with out-of-distribution
077
      (OOD) detection. Haas et al. (2023) show that the appearance of NC facilitates OOD detection and
078   subsequently Ammar et al. (2023) developed a method that leverages the NC geometric properties to
079   enhance OOD detection.

081   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
082
083   In this paper, we present a method to induce the formation of an information bottleneck in DNN
084   classifiers. Rather than directly solving the IB Lagrangian optimization problem, we achieve this by
085   inducing a collapse of all same-class latent representations into a single point, thereby reducing the
086   entropy of the latent representations. This collapse is accomplished through the implementation of a
087
      penultimate layer with the following properties:
088
            1. The layer is linear.
089
            2. A loss function that compresses the layer’s latent representations is added.
090
091         3. The dimensionality of the layer is kept low to create an information bottleneck.
092
      A penultimate layer incorporating the three mentioned properties is referred here as IB layer.
093
094   An IB layer creates an information bottleneck and induces a type of collapse that substantially
095   differs from the traditional concept of NC, which emerges spontaneously as observed in Papyan et al.
096   (2020). Using our method, data points belonging to the same class precisely converge to a single
097   point, and each class-specific point is located at a different vertex of a hypercube, thus developing a
098
      binary structure. This differs from NC, where such single-point convergence does not manifest, and
      convergence occurs toward an ETFS.
099
100   To our knowledge, this represents the first technique that forces the collapse of all latent representa-
101   tions of a given class into a single point, significantly enhancing class separability in the latent space.
102   The point collapse induced by our method ensures that the network satisfies a Lipschitz condition
103   while significantly reducing the entropy of the latent distributions, thereby enforcing an information
104
      bottleneck. Our method has practical applications, combining the benefits of Lipschitz continuity
      and the information bottleneck, such as improved robustness and generalization. Additionally, as
105
      demonstrated by our experiments, it enhances the network’s capability to detect misclassifications.
106
107   The primary innovation of this work lies in the use of a loss function to compress the latent represen-
      tations in the penultimate layer, specifically applied to a linear layer. Notably, a penalization on latent

      features was already employed in the unconstrained feature model in Zhu et al. (2021) to analyze
109   the global optimization landscape of the cross-entropy loss function, which also included penalties
110   on both the weights and features of the layer-peeled model. However, in that theoretical study the
111   feature penalty was used as a condition to derive a global optimizer and does not address the network
112   enhancements that arise only under a significant penalty applied to a linear layer. We emphasize that
113   the use of a linear layer is essential for applying a significant penalty; without it, all representations
114   would collapse to zero, rendering them indistinguishable. In contrast, the linear layer enables the
115   formation of binary encoding and latent point collapse—a phenomenon documented and explained
116   with principled arguments for the first time in this work. We also demonstrate the utilization of a
117
      bottleneck layer as a penultimate layer, which has a lower dimensionality than the final classifier and
      the previous layer.

      1.2   R ELATED WORKS
121
      The idea of utilizing an IB optimization in DNNs was first outlined in Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015).
122   In the context of DNN classification, this concept has been implemented using variational approx-
123   imations Alemi et al. (2019); Kolchinsky et al. (2019); Chalk et al. (2016), through injection of
124   multiplicative noise Achille & Soatto (2017), and with efficient mutual information estimators Bel-
125   ghazi et al. (2021); Butakov et al. (2024); Gabrié et al. (2019); Goldfeld et al. (2019). However,
126   minimization of the IB functional in deterministic DNNs remains ill-posed for most optimization
127   problems Amjad & Geiger (2020). In contrast, we do not aim to minimize mutual information
128   directly; instead, we induce the collapse of latent representations into a single point, which in turn
129   lowers the entropy of the latent distribution and consequently the mutual information.
130   Sparse coding methods in deep neural networks, such as L0 regularization Louizos et al. (2018), aim
131   to sparsify the network by removing some weights or nodes Bellec et al. (2017), or input features
132   via L1 regularization (LASSO) Lemhadri et al. (2021), thereby reducing complexity and enhancing
133   computational efficiency. These approaches, including network pruning Han et al. (2015), focus
134   on permanently minimizing the number of active parameters to prevent overfitting and improve
135   generalization. A conceptually different regularization technique is dropout Srivastava et al. (2014),
136
      which temporarily disables nodes during training to avoid overspecialization, but does not result in a
      sparse network at inference time. In contrast, our method compresses latent representations without
137
      altering the network’s architecture during either training or evaluation.
138
139   The implementation of loss functions on intermediate layers of DNNs has already been utilized in
140   the context of deep supervision Lee et al. (2014); Li et al. (2022). However, in these cases, the loss
141   function provides additional intermediate supervision to guide representations towards the correct
142
      solution, whereas in our case, the loss function is used to compress the volume occupied by latent
      representations.
143
144   In the context of the NC phenomenon, the work of Haas et al. (2023) imposes L2 normalization on the
145   latent representations, projecting them onto the surface of a hypersphere. This method has been shown
146   to accelerate convergence to NC, but it does not induce the collapse of all latent representations into a
147   single point. Furthermore, no performance enhancement of the trained networks was documented,
148
      aside from improved OOD detection.

152   Given a labeled dataset {xi , yi } , i = 1 . . . N , where N is the number of data points, we address the
153   problem of predicting the labels using a classifier. We utilize a deep neural network that produces a
154   nonlinear mapping of the input f (x), aiming to approximate the distribution represented by the data.
155   Deep neural networks comprise multiple layers stacked together. Each layer produces an internal
156   latent representation. The final output of the network can be expressed as a composition of the
157   functions represented by these layers f (x) = f (M ) ◦ f (M −1) ◦ . . . f (1) (x) where M denotes the
158
      number of layers in the network.

160   sake of this exposition, into two main steps. Firstly, the nonlinear components of the neural network
161   transform the input into a latent representation, denoted as h(x). This representation is the output
      of the last hidden layer before classification. The final output is then obtained by applying a linear

      2.1   L ATENT P OINT TO COLLAPSE AND I NFORMATION BOTTLENECK
171
172   Our objective is to design a loss function that promotes the collapse of all same-class latent represen-
173   tations into a single point, thereby enforcing an information bottleneck. To achieve this, we begin by
174   formulating the optimization of the IB Lagrangian, which aims to maximize the following objective:
175
176
                                            LIB = I(z; y) − βI(z; x),                                        (1)
177
178   where I(z; y) denotes the mutual information between the latent representation z and the labels y
179   and I(z; x) represents the mutual information between z and the input data x. The parameter β
180   controls the trade-off between compression and predictive accuracy. In App. B, we demonstrate
181   that minimizing this quantity in deterministic DNN classification is equivalent to minimizing the
182   following quantity:
183
184                                      LIB = LCE (f (x), y) − βH(z),                                       (2)
185
186   where H(z) is the entropy associated with the latent distribution z. Minimizing the cross-entropy
187   loss function is a canonical approach for optimizing DNN classifiers. What remains is to minimize
188   the entropy H(z), which we propose to achieve indirectly by inducing the collapse of all same-class
189   latent representations into a single point. To this end, we introduce an additional linear layer prior to
190
      the classifier, defined as z = WH h(x) + bH . This layer serves as the penultimate step in the network
      architecture, with classification being subsequently determined through another linear operation,
191
      f (x) = W z +b. In addition to the cross-entropy loss applied to the network’s output, we incorporate
192
      an L2 loss function into the penultimate layer defined as: LH (z) = ∥z∥2 , where ∥ · ∥ is the Eucledian
193   norm. The resulting loss function is thus composed of two terms L = LCE + γ LH , where γ is a
194   positive scalar. The latent point collapse emerges as a result of balancing two conflicting tendencies
195   coming from the two components of the loss function
196
197

200   The squared loss function encourages latent representations to be closer to zero, whereas the cross-
201   entropy loss necessitates that the latent representations of different classes be linearly separable in the
202   penultimate layer. This configuration generates two opposing tensions: on the one hand, the squared
203   loss drives representations towards each other, potentially making them numerically inseparable or
204   even overlapping; on the other hand, the cross-entropy loss enhances separability and increases the
205   relative distance between latent representations of different classes.
206   In App A we provide principled arguments that describe how the interplay between these opposing
207   tensions induce the collapse of same-class latent representations into a single point, where all collapse
208   points are located at the same distance from the origin. Single point collapse of latent representations
209   ensures Lipschitz continuity of the network defined as
210
                                        ∥f (x1 ) − f (x2 )∥ ≤ L∥x1 − x2 ∥.                                   (4)
211
212   Considering that the final output representations are obtained as a linear combination of the latent
213   representations z which are located in the sorrounding of the origin, we can conclude that latent point
214   collapse renders networks Lipschitz. More specifically, in Zhang et al. (2022); Li et al. (2019), it is
215   shown that the minimum amount of perturbation required to induce misclassification in a Lipschitz
      network is proportional to the Lipschitz constant L, and inversely proportional to the margin of

219   same reasoning cannot be done in case of networks whose penultimate latent representations are not
220   bounded close to the origin.

      2.2   B INARY ENCODING
244
245   The collapse points are located on a hypersphere, and our experiments demonstrate that they align
246   with the vertices of a hypercube inscribed within this hypersphere. More precisely, we find that at
247   each node of an IB layer, latent representations can approximately assume one of two values, thereby
248   forming a binary encoding. However, we do not provide an explanation for why these collapse
249   points specifically correspond to the vertices of a hypercube. One possible argument is that as latent
250   representations approach the origin, it becomes increasingly difficult for the network to accurately
251
      position all representations within such a confined space without mistakenly placing a representation
      at a vertex associated with a different class. A practical solution to this challenge, potentially
252
      discovered by the optimizer, is to maximize the relative distance between different collapse points in
253
      each dimension of the latent space. This can be achieved by arranging opposing groups of collapse
254   points symmetrically around the origin, as illustrated in Fig. 1. We note that such a symmetric
255   arrangement can be realized using a linear layer, which allows for the symmetric displacement of
256   latent representations relative to the origin. However, if non-linearities such as ReLU are introduced,
257   the symmetric configuration becomes infeasible. In this case, the compression term would drive all
258   representations toward the origin, rendering them indistinguishable.

367   LeakyReLU) prevents the network from converging, as demonstrated by our tests. The issue arises
368   because all latent representations are pushed close to the origin. Without access to the negative semi-
369   axis—since the activation functions produce outputs equal to or near zero for negative inputs—the
370   network cannot distinguish between different classes and develop binary encodings. For this reason,
371
      we exclude this specific architecture from our ablation study.
372   Experiments were performed on the SVHN Netzer et al. (2011), CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets
373   Krizhevsky et al. (2009) utilizing the ResNet architectures He et al. (2016). Code to reproduce the
374   results presented in this work is available online in the linked repository 1 . All experimental details,
375   alongside a summary with the different architectures employed, are provided in Appendix C. We
376   remark that L2 regularization was always used during training for all architectures.

403   3.2   ROBUSTNESS , GENERALIZATION , AND RELIABILITY
404
405
      In our experiments, we observe that the induction of a latent point collapse correlates with several sig-
      nificant benefits, including increased robustness, enhanced misclassification detection and improved
406
      generalization, as evidenced by the results presented in Table 2.
407
408   The most remarkable result is the dramatic improvement in the network’s robustness. Results in Table
409   2 provide the magnitude of the minimal perturbation on the input data sufficient to trigger a change in
410   the label classification. To obtain this value, we employed the DeepFool algorithm Moosavi-Dezfooli
411
      et al. (2016). Interestingly, we note that the intermediate number of nodes employed in the IB
      architecture provides the best results. We can explain the improved robustness with the fact that
412
      networks implementing an IB layer are Lipschitz.
413
414   The best accuracy results on the testing set at the last epoch are obtained using an IB layer, as shown
415   in Table 2. More specifically, the best results are achieved with the architecture IB, featuring an
416   intermediate number of nodes, that is in line with the robustness results. We can note that the addition
417
      of a penultimate layer with low dimensionality, as in the L IN P EN and N ONLIN P EN architectures,
      tends to decrease the network’s accuracy, and that the implementation of dropout or L1 does not
418
      provide significant improvements. This suggests that the performance improvement observed with the
419
      IB architecture is not due to an increase in the network’s number of parameters nor to a regularization
420   effect, but rather is attributed to the formation of an information bottleneck.

430   on this metric, we can evaluate the network’s confidence. The farther a point is from other points

      4     D ISCUSSION
493
494
      The implementation of an IB layer assists the network in finding a compact geometry that enhances
495
      overall performance. According to the IB principle, DNNs autonomously seek progressively compact
496   representations layer by layer to facilitate classification, eventually developing NC. However, this
497   behavior is not consistently observed, particularly before the TPT. Following the IB principles, we
498   design a loss function comprising two terms: one that maximizes the information about the input’s
499   label and another that encourages compression of the latent representations on the penultimate layer.
500   The critical use of a low-dimensional linear layer enables the development of highly symmetric and
501   compact representations, leading to the collapse of same-class latent representations into single points
502   located at the vertices of a hypercube.
503   Latent point collapse renders the network Lipschitz, which proves the observed improvements in
504   robustness. Additionally, it reduces the entropy of the latent distribution, effectively creating an
505   information bottleneck. This phenomenon is well-documented in the literature as being associated
506   with improved generalization, that is also evidenced in this work. These enhancements in robustness
507   and generalization are also present in the TPT and are associated with the NC phenomenon. However,
508   our experimental design ensures that a significant portion of the training are conducted in the
509   TPT, ensuring that our documented benefits are supplementary to those naturally arising from NC.
510
      Beyond robustness and generalization, we also observe an improved ability of the network to detect
      misclassifications.
511
512   The phenomenon of single-point collapse that we have demonstrated results from the interplay be-
513   tween the cross-entropy loss and L2 regularization on the penultimate layer. The differing asymptotic
514   behaviors of these two functions give rise to an equilibrium point, leading to the emergence of point
515   collapse. As NC has been shown to develop with various types of loss functions beyond cross-entropy
516
      Han et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022b), it would be interesting to study this type of interplay with
      these other loss functions.
517
518
519
      4.1   C ONCLUSION
520
      In this paper, we have introduced a straightforward method for inducing a point collapse of latent
521   representations into the vertices of a hypercube. We have shown that this method dramatically
522   enhances the robustness of the network, while also providing a small, but statistically significant,
523   improvement of the generalization and reliability of the network. The simplicity of implementing this
524   method is particularly striking, as it requires only the addition of an additional layer to an existing
525   backbone architecture and an additional loss function. Given the ease of implementation and the
526   significant benefits it brings to the network, we expect that this methodology will find widespread
527   applications.

      To understand how our method induces the collapse of same-class latent representations into a single
759
      point, we analyze the behavior of the latent representations z in the penultimate layer under the effect
760   of the cross-entropy loss and the compression term.
761
762   The cross-entropy loss is defined as:
763

      where z ∈ Rd is the latent representation, W ∈ RK×d is the weight matrix of the classifier, b ∈ RK
767   is the bias vector, y is the true class index, and K is the number of classes.
768
769   In the terminal phase of training, where all training samples are already well-classified, the latent
770
      representation z is closer to the weight vector of the true label, Wy , than to the weight vectors of
      other classes. Minimizing LCE encourages the logit corresponding to the true class, (W z + b)y , to
771
      increase relative to other logits. Since the logits are computed as a linear transformation of z, this
772
      process implicitly drives z to adopt larger magnitudes and align more closely with the weight vector
773   Wy . When z is already well-aligned with Wy , an increase in magnitude ∥z∥ has a larger relative
774   effect on the projection onto Wy than on projections onto other weight vectors. This is because the
775   change in projection scales with cos θ, where θ is the angle between z and the respective weight
776   vector. As cos θ is largest for Wy in this phase, increasing ∥z∥ further amplifies the true class logit
777   more significantly than the logits for other classes. This results in a sharper separation of classes in
778   the softmax probabilities and a reduction in the cross-entropy loss. It is worth noting that increasing
779   the y element in the bias vector also contributes to softmax minimization. While this observation is
780   accurate, it applies to all classes equally and is thus averaged out when latent representations with
781
      diverse labels are present in the same batch during optimization. Our primary focus in this study is
      on analyzing the alignment of latent representations with the prototype vectors of the linear classifier
782
      W . Furthermore, the simplification in our analysis, where we focus solely on the behavior of the
783
      numerator, is justified by the assumption that we are already in the terminal phase of training. At this
784   stage, latent representations are well-aligned with their respective class prototype vectors. Since the
785   summation in the denominator involves exponentials, where one term (corresponding to the dominant
786   class) significantly outweighs the others, changes in the total loss can be approximated by considering
787   only the dominant term.

870   mutual information between the latent representations and the labels, I(z; y). This mutual information

1089   The emergence of binary encoding is made possible because all latent representations collapse into
1090   one of two possible points. This collapse reflects a natural tendency of DNNs to seek increasingly
1091   compact embeddings of latent representations. The phenomenon of NC can also be viewed as an
1092   expression of this inclination, though it significantly differs from the binary encoding induced by
1093   our method. More specifically, NC involves the convergence of class means to the vertices of an
1094   ETFS, but this is not possible in a low-dimensional manifold. In fact, an ETFS constructed in a
1095

       possible to assign each class to a different vertex when P < K − 1. In this case of large number of
1096
       classes, NC converges to a structure where class means are at equidistant points on the surface of a
1097
       hypersphere Jiang et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023). In our experiments, this condition was always met.
1098   More specifically, for the CIFAR10 dataset, the embedding utilized with NARROW IB had the lowest
1099   possible dimensionality to preserve information about different classes using a binary encoding, i.e.,
1100   P = ⌈ log2 K ⌉. We also note that, a hypercube constructed in a P -dimensional space contains 2P
1101   distinct vertices, with each of the K different classes occupying one of those vertices. All other
1102   2P − K vertices remain unoccupied, suggesting that they could potentially be utilized to learn new
1103   classes in a transfer-learning setting.
1104   Collapse into the vertices of a hypercube creates a latent binary encoding of the input representations
1105   onto the penultimate layer. In a classification task that includes K different classes, a dimensionality
1106   of ⌈log2 K⌉ is required in the penultimate layer to create the minimum amount of information for a
1107   binary encoding to encode the input signal. Thus, if the penultimate layer has a larger dimensionality,
1108   in a number of nodes all representations could collapse to zero to minimize the compressing term
1109   in the loss function while maintaining the same accuracy. However, this scenario is never verified
1110   in our case studies where a binary structure is observed in each node, even when a redundant
1111   penultimate layer (i.e., with a dimensionality larger than ⌈log2 K⌉) is utilized. We could explain this
1112
       with the consideration that through gradient-based optimization methods, such a dramatic change
       of configuration—i.e., all latent representations in one node collapsing to zero without altering
1113
       classification performance of the network—cannot be reached in practice. Still, in our case studies,
1114
       we note that more performant networks, in terms of generalization, implement a dimensionality of
1115   the penultimate layer that is larger than the minimum value that would be required only considering
1116   the total number of classes. In these cases some nodes are redundant for a basic encoding of the class.
1117   One possible reason for utilizing more dimensions than necessary in the penultimate layer could be
1118   that, during training, the network progressively learns to employ each dimension in the penultimate
1119   layer for signaling specific features that are extracted from the input data, thus overcoming the idea
1120   that the binary encoding is only meant to differentiate between the different classes. According to
1121   this hypothesis, the binary encoding would entail that each dimension in the penultimate layer signals
1122   the presence or absence of a specific feature that is identified by the network in the different classes.
1123
       This hypothesis, if verified, would then pave the way for future applications of binary encoding for
       explainability in deep neural networks.
