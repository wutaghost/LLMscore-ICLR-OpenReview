031   where P is the population distribution from which we sample a dataset D = (ξ1 , . . . , ξn ) ∼ P n ,
032   and the component functions f ( · ; ξ) : Rd → R may be neither smooth nor convex. Such problems
033   are ubiquitous throughout machine learning, where losses given by deep-learning based models give
034   rise to highly nonsmooth nonconvex (NSNC) landscapes.
035
      Due to its fundamental importance in modern machine learning, the field of nonconvex optimization
036   has received substantial attention in recent years. Moving away from the classical regime of con-
037   vex optimization, many works aimed at understanding the complexity of producing approximate-
038   stationary points, namely with small gradient norm (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Fang et al., 2018; Car-
039   mon et al., 2020; Arjevani et al., 2023). As it turns out, without smoothness, it is impossible to
040   directly minimize the gradient norm without suffering from an exponential-dimension dependent
041   runtime in the worst case (Kornowski & Shamir, 2022). Nonetheless, a nuanced notion coined as
042   Goldstein-stationarity (Goldstein, 1977), has been shown in recent years to enable favorable guar-
043   antees. Roughly speaking, a point x ∈ Rd is called an (α, β)-Goldstein stationary point (or simply
044
      (α, β)-stationary) if there exists a convex combination of gradients in the α-ball around x whose
      norm is at most β.1 Following the groundbreaking work of Zhang et al. (2020), a surge of works
045
      study NSNC optimization through the lens of Goldstein stationarity, with associated finite-time guar-
046
      antees (Davis et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Cutkosky et al., 2023; Jordan et al., 2023; Kong & Lewis,
047   2023; Grimmer & Jia, 2023; Kornowski & Shamir, 2024; Tian & So, 2024).
048
049   In this work, we study NSNC optimization problems under the additional constraint of differential
050
      privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006). With the ever-growing deployment of ML models in various
      domains, the privacy of the data on which models are trained is a major concern. Accordingly, DP

448   6   D ISCUSSION
449
450   In this paper, we studied nonsmooth nonconvex optimization, and proposed differentially private
451   algorithms for this task which return Goldstein-stationary points, improving the previously known
452   sample complexity for this task.
453                                                                           √
454
      Our single-pass algorithm reduces the sample complexity by at least a Ω( d) factor (and sometimes
      more, depending on the parameter regime of interest), compared to the previous such result by
455
      Zhang et al. (2024). Furthermore, our result has a dimension-independent “non-private” term, which
456
      was previously claimed impossible. Moreover, we propose a multi-pass algorithm which preforms
457   sample-efficient ERM, and show that it further generalizes to the population.
458
459   It is interesting to note that our guarantees are in terms of so-called “approximate” (ε, δ)-DP, whereas
460
      Zhang et al. (2024) derive a Rényi-DP guarantee (Mironov, 2017). This is in fact inherent to our
      techniques, since we condition on a highly probable event in order to substantially decrease the
461
      effective sensitivity of our gradient estimators. Further examining this potential gap in terms of
462
      sample complexity between approximate- and Rényi-DP for nonsmooth nonconvex optimization is
463   an interesting direction for future research.
464
465   Another important problem that remains open is establishing tight lower bounds for DP nonconvex
466
      optimization and perhaps further improving the sample complexities obtained in this paper. We
      note that the current upper and lower bounds do not fully match even in the smooth setting. In
467
      Appendix D, we provide evidence that our upper bound can be further improved, by proposing
468
      a computationally-inefficient algorithm, which converges to a relaxed notion of stationarity, using
469   even fewer samples than the algorithms we presented in this work.

       D     E VEN BETTER SAMPLE COMPLEXITY VIA OPTIMAL SMOOTHING
973
974
       In this Appendix, our aim is to provide evidence that the sample complexities of NSNC DP op-
975    timization obtained in our work are likely improvable, at least with a computationally inefficient
976    method. This approach is inspired by Lowy et al. (2024), which in the context of smooth optimiza-
977    tion, showed significant sample complexity gains using algorithms with exponential runtime. As
978    as we will show, a similar phenomenon might hold for nonsmooth optimization. To that end, we
979    propose a slight relaxation of Goldstein-stationarity, and show it can be achieved using less samples
980    via an exponential time algorithm.
981
982    D.1    R ELAXATION OF G OLDSTEIN - STATIONARITY
983
984
       Recall that x ∈ Rd is called an (α, β)-Goldstein stationary point of an objective F (x) =
       EξP[f (x; ξ)] if there exist y1 , . . . , yk ∈ B(x, α) and convex coefficients (λi )ki=1 so that
985
       ∥ i∈[k] λi Eξ [∇f (yi ; ξ)]∥ ≤ β. Arguably, the two most important properties satisfied by this defi-
986
       nition are that
987
988           (i) If f (x; ξ) are L-smooth, any (α, β)-stationary point is O(α + β)-stationary.
989
990          (ii) If ∂ α F (x) ̸= 0, then F x − ∂ Fα(x) ∂ α F (x) ≤ F (x) − α ∂ α F (x) .
                                                    ∥ α      ∥
991
992    The first property shows that Goldstein-stationarity reduces to (“classic”) stationarity under smooth-
993    ness. The second, known as Goldstein’s descent lemma (Goldstein, 1977), is a generalization of the
994    classic descent lemma for smooth functions.
995
       It is easy to see that Goldstein-stationarity is equivalent to the existence of a distribution P supported
996
       over B(x, α), such that ∥Eξ, y∼P [∇f (y; ξ)]∥ ≤ β. We will now define a relaxation of Goldstein-
997
       stationarity that is easily verified to satisfy both of the aforementioned properties.
998
