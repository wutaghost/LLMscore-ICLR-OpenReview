009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Missing data poses significant challenges for machine learning and deep learning
012            algorithms. In this paper, we aim to enhance post-imputation performance, mea-
013            sured by machine learning utility (MLu). We introduce a nearest-neighbor-based
014            imputation method, DrIM, designed for heterogeneous tabular datasets. However,
015            calculating similarity in the data space becomes challenging due to the varying
016            presence of missing entries across different columns. To address this issue, we
017            leverage the representation learning capabilities of language models. By trans-
018
               forming the tabular dataset into a text-format dataset and replacing the missing en-
               tries with mask (or unk) tokens, we extract representations that capture contextual
019
               information. This mapping to a continuous representation space enables the use of
020
               well-defined similarity measurements. Additionally, we incorporate a contrastive
021            learning framework to refine the representations, ensuring that the representations
022            of observations with similar information in the observed columns, regardless of
023            the missingness patterns, are closely aligned. To validate our proposed model,
024            we evaluate its performance in missing data imputation across 10 real-world tab-
025            ular datasets, demonstrating its ability to produce a Complete dataset having high
026            MLu.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Handling missing data is challenging, as machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) methods
032   typically require complete datasets (Tan et al., 2013; Kingma & Welling, 2014; Goodfellow et al.,
033
      2014; Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Ke et al., 2017; Vaswani et al., 2017; Ho et al., 2020; An & Jeon,
      2023). Rubin says that the goal of imputation tasks is to provide complete statistical inference (Rubin
034
      & Schenker, 1986). However, many recent imputation methods have focused on providing complete
035
      datasets that can yield high post-imputation performance, including machine learning utility (MLu).
036   Ivanov et al. (2019); Yoon et al. (2018); Mattei & Frellsen (2019); Ipsen et al. (2021); Zhao et al.
037   (2023); Du et al. (2024a); Wen et al. (2024) emphasize the importance of imputing missing data in
038   terms of MLu. In this paper, we also focus on imputing incomplete datasets to provide a complete
039   dataset that enables effective training of many ML and DL algorithms while achieving high MLu.
040
      We empirically observed that nearest-neighbor-based imputation methods, such as k-nearest neigh-
041
      bors imputation (kNNI) (Troyanskaya et al., 2001) and its variants (Jiang & Yang, 2015; Thomas
042   & Rajabi, 2021; Du et al., 2024b), can achieve high MLu. Moreover, Anil Jadhav & Ramanathan
043   (2019); Keerin & Boongoen (2022); Ding et al. (2024) have demonstrated that these methods of-
044   ten outperform other imputation techniques. These nearest-neighbor-based methods impute missing
045   values by leveraging the similarities between observations and utilizing the values of their near-
046   est neighbors, thereby preserving local data characteristics (Troyanskaya et al., 2001; Tarsitano &
047   Falcone, 2011).
048   However, nearest-neighbor-based imputation methods face two key challenges: 1) They struggle to
049   effectively represent or address missing values. While various distance or similarity metrics have
050   been proposed for missing data, their performance depends on how they handle these missing values
051   (Juhola & Laurikkala, 2007; Muñoz & Hernández-González, 2012; AbedAllah & Shimshoni, 2016;
052   Santos et al., 2020). For instance, assigning a distance of 0 when both values are missing can
053   be inflexible and adversely affect the imputation process (Santos et al., 2020). 2) Applying these
      methods to heterogeneous (i.e., mixed-type) tabular data presents challenges. Jerez et al. (2010);


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Garcı́a-Laencina et al. (2010) have demonstrated that continuous and categorical variables exhibit an
055   imbalance when measuring distance, with continuous variables having a more significant influence
056   on the distance metric, affecting the results of imputation.
057
058
      Therefore, we introduce a nearest-neighbor-based imputation method termed DrIM (Contextual-
      Driven Missing IMputer) to address these challenges. Our proposed method includes taking advan-
059
      tage of the language model’s ability to generate contextualized representations, which are learned
060
      from a large collection of text datasets. We utilize textual encoding to convert tabular data into text-
061   format data and apply language models to obtain representations (Yin et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2021;
062   Borisov et al., 2023; Radford & Narasimhan, 2018; Devlin et al., 2019; Nazir et al., 2023).
063
064
      Our representation-based nearest-neighbor imputation method provides solutions to address chal-
      lenges 1) and 2), respectively: 1) Missing values are represented as [MASK] (or [UNK]) tokens,
065
      allowing language models to utilize their representation capabilities and leverage contextual hints
066
      from the other columns. 2) By mapping both continuous and categorical columns into a continuous
067   space using their representation vectors, we enable the use of well-defined similarity measures, such
068   as cosine similarity. Furthermore, we incorporate fine-tuning using a contrastive learning frame-
069   work to further enhance the language model’s representational capacity. This allows it to build more
070   meaningful neighbors, which in turn improves the post-imputation performance.
071
      Our primary contributions can be summarized as follows:
072
073         1. By adopting language models and transforming each record into a representation vector, we
074            propose a nearest-neighbor-based imputation method that is applicable to heterogeneous
075            tabular datasets.
076         2. We achieve state-of-the-art imputation performance in terms of MLu without any model
077            training.
078         3. We provide an intuitive explanation while empirically confirming that our fine-tuning using
079            contrastive learning enhances the MLu of the imputed dataset.
080
081   We validate the effectiveness of our proposed method by evaluating its imputation performance
082   across 10 real-world tabular datasets, accounting for four missing data mechanisms and five miss-
083   ingness rates. We employ four MLu performance metrics, including classification tasks, model se-
084
      lection, and feature selection performance. Additionally, we demonstrate that applying a contrastive
      learning framework enhances imputation performance in terms of MLu.

087   2   R ELATED W ORKS
088
089   Imputation methods. The effectiveness of missing imputation ultimately lies in reducing bias and
090   enhancing estimation efficiency in inference after imputation (Little & Rubin, 2019). This always
091
      depends on the missing data mechanism, and in general, a single imputation has the limitation of
      not accounting for the uncertainty induced by the imputation (van Buuren, 2012). Nevertheless,
092
      single imputation is widely used because of its convenience and its ability to improve estimation
093
      efficiency in downstream tasks (Álvarez Verdejo et al., 2021). In particular, a single imputation based
094
      on estimating conditional expectation performs well when the bias introduced by the imputation
095
      does not significantly affect the performance of the specific downstream task (Troyanskaya et al.,
096   2001). Deep learning has garnered significant attention for its ability to flexibly model conditional
097   expectations of observed data and effectively learn metrics in the observed data space (Yoon et al.,
098   2018; Ivanov et al., 2019; Kyono et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2024). These two tasks are closely related
099   to the nonparametric kNNI. kNNI is a method for calculating local conditional expectations and
100   involves dimension reduction and metric learning to compute conditional expectations effectively.
101
      Distance measures for missing data. kNNI is a widely used imputation method because it effec-
102   tively utilizes the similarity between patterns to generate accurate estimates (Wilson & Martinez,
103   1997) and maintains the overall data distribution (Santos et al., 2017). However, its performance
104   heavily relies on the chosen distance function. Several distance functions have been developed to
105   handle heterogeneous tabular data and account for missing values. The HEOM (Aha et al., 1991;
106   Wilson & Martinez, 1997) calculates distances based on feature types, using normalized Euclidean
107   distance for continuous features and an overlap metric for categorical ones. Missing values are as-
      signed a distance of 1, and if both are missing, 0. Juhola & Laurikkala (2007) refined HEOM to


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      maintain this approach. The HVDM (Stanfill & Waltz, 1986; Wilson & Martinez, 1997) functions
109   similarly to HEOM but requires class target information for categorical features. SIMDIST (Muñoz
110   & Hernández-González, 2012) introduces a heterogeneous similarity function incorporating prior
111   knowledge, with similarity based on the presence or absence of values. Additionally, redefined ver-
112   sions of HVDM treat missing values as special cases (Santos et al., 2020), and the Mean Euclidean
113   Distance (AbedAllah & Shimshoni, 2016) has been used for managing incomplete data in clustering.
114   In contrast, our approach utilizes metric learning for imputing missing values. Instead of relying on
115   existing distance functions, our method learns a distance (similarity) metric on a manifold using lan-
116   guage models, specifically by computing the Euclidean distance between the representation vectors
117
      obtained from language models.

121   xj is a value of the jth column having its column name with Cj . Here, subscript j refers to the
122   jth element. g(·; θ) is a function that extracts the representation vector from the text input, where θ

      is its trainable parameter. The missingness pattern of an observation is defined by a corresponding
      missingness indicator vector m ∈ {0, 1}p , where mj = 0 if xj is missing. An incomplete dataset

      is given {(x(i) , m(i) )}ni=1 comprising n i.i.d. realizations of x and m.

127   3       METHODOLOGY
128
129   DrIM1 is a single imputation method based on the kNNI employing representations for each tubular
130   record using language models. The main difference between the DrIM and the conventional kNNI
131   approach lies in the way the distance metric between data points is learned using an underlying
132   model for imputing missing values. This distance metric is defined as the Euclidean distance be-
133
      tween the representation vectors of [CLS] tokens output by the BERT model (Devlin et al., 2019)2 .
      We will elucidate its theoretical properties in this section.

249   convert each observation into a sentence-like format, deriving representations from this sentence.

339   4.1     OVERVIEW
340
341   We propose two versions of our model, DrIM: 1) DrIMBASE , which utilizes the pre-trained BERT
342
      directly, and 2) DrIMFINE , which incorporates contrastive learning framework3 . While any BERT
      model can be used, we employ the BERT-base model 4 to extract representation vectors.
343
344   Datasets. Similar to several recent studies (Mattei & Frellsen, 2019; Nazábal et al., 2020; Muzellec
345   et al., 2020; Ipsen et al., 2021; Jarrett et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023), we utilize 10 real tabular
346   datasets from the UCI repository5 and Kaggle6 , which vary in sample sizes. The datasets are split
347   into training and testing sets, with an 80% training and 20% testing ratio. Detailed statistics of these
348
      datasets are provided in Appendix A.4.
349   Additionally, following the approach in Muzellec et al. (2020); Jarrett et al. (2022); Zhao et al.
350   (2023), we generate missing value masks for each dataset using three mechanisms (MCAR, MAR,
351   MNAR) across four settings (MCAR, MAR, MNARL, MNARQ). Refer to Appendix A.7.1 for
352   detailed descriptions and implementations of these mechanisms. We generate missing values at
353   rates of 0.2, 0.3, 0.4, 0.6, and 0.8 for each mechanism.
354   Baseline models. In this experiment, we investigate our proposed method, DrIM, alongside 13
355   imputation methods, categorized into statistical imputation, ML-based imputation, and DL-based
356   imputation as follows:
357
358            • Statistical imputation: Mean (Farhangfar et al., 2007), and kNNI (Troyanskaya et al., 2001)
359            • ML-based imputation: EM (Nelwamondo et al., 2007), SoftImpute (Mazumder et al.,
360              2010), MICE (van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), missForest (Stekhoven &
361              Bühlmann, 2012), and Sinkhorn (Muzellec et al., 2020)
362            • DL-based imputation: GAIN (Yoon et al., 2018), VAEAC (Ivanov et al., 2019), MIWAE
363              (Mattei & Frellsen, 2019), not-MIWAE (Ipsen et al., 2021), MIRACLE (Kyono et al.,
364              2021), and ReMasker (Du et al., 2024a).
365
366
      Detailed experimental settings for the reproducibility of these baseline models and our proposed
      model are provided in Appendix A.5.
367
368   Evaluation Metrics. To assess the imputation performance, we use two types of evaluation criteria:
369   1) imputation fidelity and 2) machine learning utility. The performance of imputers is reported
370   by averaging the metrics across 10 real tabular datasets. Imputation fidelity is measured by the
371   sum of the SMAPE (symmetric mean absolute percentage error) for continuous columns and the

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
487
488   This paper introduces DrIM, a context-driven nearest-neighbor-based imputation method specif-
489   ically designed to handle incomplete heterogeneous tabular datasets. Our proposed method lever-
490   ages the representation learning capabilities of language models and integrates a contrastive learning
491   framework for fine-tuning. In other words, in our proposed method, BERT performs dimensionality
492   reduction on (numerical) tabular datasets in text format through the bidirectional attention mecha-
493   nism, and we perform metric learning to maximize mutual information between observed and miss-
494   ing data entries. Our extensive experiments across 10 real-world datasets demonstrate that DrIM
495   consistently outperforms existing imputation methods, achieving state-of-the-art post-imputation
496
      performance in terms of MLu across various missing data mechanisms, including MCAR, MAR,
      MNARL, and MNARQ.
497
498   In this paper we have not explicitly demonstrated how the neighbors using a BERT model for kNNI
499   specifically improves downstream task performance. However, we believe our proposed method
500   provides insights into the relationship between learned metrics obtained through contrastive learning
501   and conditional independence among variables by emphasizing the enhancement of machine learn-
502
      ing utility rather than statistical fidelity. This connection between contextual embeddings derived
      from BERT and downstream task performance may serve as a critical clue for further exploration,
503
      which we leave for future research.

1189
1190   A.7.1    M ISSING M ECHANISM
1191
1192   Descriptions. Data missingness is a common challenge in research and practical analysis, cate-
1193
       gorized into three primary missing mechanisms: 1) Missing Completely at Random (MCAR), 2)
       Missing at Random (MAR), and 3) Missing Not at Random (MNAR).
1194
1195   Under the MCAR mechanism, the reason for missingness has no relationship with any data, neither
1196   observed nor unobserved. In other words, the likelihood of data being missing is equal across all
1197   observations. The primary advantage of MCAR is that it does not introduce bias into the data
1198   analysis. However, despite this advantage, data missingness can still reduce the statistical power of
1199
       the study because of the reduced sample size.
1200   MAR occurs when the probability of missingness is related to the observed data but not the unob-
1201   served missing data. Essentially, even though the data is missing, the mechanism assumes that the
1202   missingness is explainable by other variables in the dataset. That is, the missingness can be modeled
1203   and imputed using the information available in the data, allowing for more accurate analyses despite
1204   the missingness.
1205   Lastly, if the missingness is not specified by either MCAR or MAR, it becomes MNAR. The MNAR
1206   is the most challenging mechanism, as it implies that the missingness is related to the unobserved
1207   data itself. In this case, the missing data is systematically different from the observed data, which
1208   introduces bias if not properly accounted for. For example, patients with severe symptoms may
1209   be less likely to report their health status, making their data missing. MNAR requires sophisticated
1210   statistical methods to address, as ignoring or improperly handling it can lead to biased and unreliable
1211
       results.
1212   Implementations. Following Muzellec et al. (2020); Jarrett et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2023), we
1213   generate the missing value mask for each dataset with three mechanisms in four settings. (MCAR) In
1214   the MCAR setting, each value is masked according to the realization of a Bernoulli random variable
1215   with a fixed parameter. (MAR) In the MAR setting, for each experiment, a fixed subset of variables
1216   that cannot have missing values is sampled. Then, the remaining variables have missing values
1217
       according to a logistic model with random weights, which takes the non-missing variables as inputs.
       A bias term is fitted using line search to attain the desired proportion of missing values. (MNAR)
1218
       Finally, two different mechanisms are implemented in the MNAR setting. The first, MNARL, is
1219
       identical to the previously described MAR mechanism, but the inputs of the logistic model are then
1220   masked by an MCAR mechanism. Hence, the logistic model’s outcome depends on missing values.
1221   The second mechanism, MNARQ, samples a subset of variables whose values in the lower and upper
1222   pth percentiles are masked according to a Bernoulli random variable, and the values in-between are
1223   left not missing.
