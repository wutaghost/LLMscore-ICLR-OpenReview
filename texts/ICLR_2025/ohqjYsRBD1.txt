009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language models (LLMs) suffer from forgetting of upstream data when
012            fine-tuned. Despite efforts on mitigating forgetting, few have investigated whether,
013            and how forgotten upstream examples are dependent on and associated with newly
014            learned tasks. Insights on such associations enable efficient and targeted mitigation
015            of forgetting. In this paper, we empirically analyze forgetting (measured in log-
016            perplexity increase) that occurs in N upstream examples of language modeling
017            or instruction-tuning after fine-tuning LLMs on one of M new tasks, visualized
018
               in M × N matrices. We demonstrate that the matrices display simple low-rank
               patterns, often well-approximated with multiplicative scalar effects of upstream
019
               examples and newly learned tasks. We also examine fine-grained associations with
020
               visualization and statistics. Leveraging the low-rank nature of the associations, we
021            predict forgetting of upstream examples when fine-tuning on unseen tasks with
022            matrix completion over the empirical associations. This enables fast identification
023            of most forgotten examples without expensive inference on the entire upstream
024            data. The approach, despite simplicity, outperforms prior approaches that learn
025            semantic relationships of learned tasks and upstream examples with LMs for
026            predicting forgetting. We demonstrate the practical utility of our analysis by
027            showing statistically significantly reduced forgetting as we upweight predicted
028            examples for replay at fine-tuning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      There has been growing need for promptly updating LLMs to mitigate harmful behaviors, update
      outdated knowledge, and expand the set of application domains. Although fine-tuning allows efficient
034
      and incremental updates of models, it risks catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989;
035
      Goodfellow et al., 2014) of upstream knowledge learned in the pre-training or instruction-tuning
036   phase, causing unintended prediction changes over known information. This is problematic for
037   the stability of online deployed LLM systems, limiting the feasibility of continual fine-tuning in
038   practice (Raffel, 2023; Shi et al., 2024).
039
      While extensive works have developed algorithms to mitigate forgetting (Wu et al., 2024), the
040
      increasing scale of the model and data necessitates more efficient and targeted approaches. A key
041
      aspect of the challenge is understanding when and what LLMs forget, prompting analysis into the
042   patterns of frequently forgotten examples (Toneva et al., 2019; Maini et al., 2022; Zhang & Wu, 2024),
043   and the impacts of models and hyperparameters on forgetting (Mirzadeh et al., 2022; Kalajdzievski,
044   2024; Que et al., 2024; Ibrahim et al., 2024). However, how the associations between learned tasks
045   and upstream examples inform forgetting remains under-explored. Understanding such associations
046   creates potential for targeted mitigation of forgetting over specific upstream examples when a new
047   task is learned. While theoretical and empirical study indicate associations between learned and
048   forgotten tasks in shallower neural networks (Lee et al., 2021; Goldfarb et al., 2024; Ramasesh
049   et al., 2021), the associations are under-explored for LLMs, or measured regarding upstream data of
050
      language modeling or instruction-tuning.
051   In this paper, we empirically study the associations between learned tasks and forgotten upstream
052   examples of language modeling or instruction-tuning. We experiment with OLMo-1B, OLMo-7B,
053   OLMo-7B-Instruct (Groeneveld et al., 2024) and MPT-7B (Team, 2023) models where upstream data
      is open-source. We analyze forgetting (in log perplexity increase) over N upstream examples, after

084   To summarize, the contributions of this paper are (1) an empirical analysis on how forgotten examples
085   are associated with learned tasks in representative 1B and 7B language models, and (2) a novel
086   approach of predicting example forgetting by solving a matrix completion problem over the empirical
087
      associations, and (3) a practical and efficient algorithm to mitigate forgetting during LLM fine-tuning
      by upweighting upstream examples for replay according to the predicted forgetting.

091
092   In this section, we start by formally defining the metrics of forgetting and set up the problem
093   formulation of analyzing the associations between learned tasks and forgotten examples. We then
094   introduce models and datasets used for collecting the statistics.
095
096   2.1    C OLLECTING S TATISTICS OF F ORGETTING
097
098   Upstream examples and learned tasks. Large Language models (LLMs) are commonly pre-trained
099   with language modeling objectives over a massive collection for corpora, with some LLMs further
100   post-trained (instruction-tuned) to better follow human instructions. We use upstream data to refer to
101   language modeling or instruction tuning training data used at the pre-training or post-training phase
102   of LLMs. For upstream data of language modeling, we define each upstream example xj ∈ x1..N as
103   a chunk of document (e.g., a Wikipedia article) of a model-specific maximum number of tokens. For
104
      instruction tuning, each xj ∈ x1..N corresponds to a pair of instructions and ground truth responses.
105   Measuring forgetting. We fine-tune an LLM (or an instruction-tuned LLM) on one unseen
106   instruction-tuning task Ti from a collection of tasks T1..M . This results in M separately fine-tuned
107   models f1..M . We then evaluate performance degradation on each upstream example xi ∈ x1..N . We
      measure log perplexity as the performance metric as they are applicable to both language modeling


                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and instruction tuning, and are known to correlate well with other dataset-specific metrics (Hoffmann
109   et al., 2022). For language modeling, log perplexity of an example xi is averaged over all tokens in the
110   example; for instruction tuning, we average log perplexity over the ground truth response tokens only.
111   We measure forgetting zij that occurs on an upstream example xj ∈ x1..N as increase (degradation)
112   in log perplexity after fine-tuning the LM on a new task Ti ∈ T1..M . We record forgetting zij in an
113   association matrix Z ∈ RM ×N .

115   2.2   M ODELS AND DATASETS
116
117   Our analysis requires access to upstream data of LLMs for measuring their log perplexity changes
118   occurred after fine-tuning. We experiment with OLMo-1B, OLMo-7B, OLMo-7B-Instruct, and
119   MPT-7B where upstream data of language modeling and instruction-tuning is open-source.
120   OLMo-1B and 7B. OLMo models are pre-trained on Dolma (Soldaini et al., 2024), a massive
121   collection of text covering diverse domains such as Wikipedia and news articles. We fine-tune LMs
122   over 66 tasks from FLAN-V2 (Longpre et al., 2023), 11 tasks from Tulu V2 (Ivison et al., 2023),
123   and 8 tasks from Dolly (Conover et al., 2023), obtaining 85 fine-tuned models. The collections
124   cover diverse instruction-tuning tasks such as reading comprehension, math reasoning, and safety
125   alignment. We then evaluate log perplexity increase on a 1% subset of Dolma-v1.6-Sample. Each
126   upstream example is a maximum 2,048-token document from Dolma, resulting in 141,816 examples.
127
      For 7B models, We perform either full-parameter fine-tuning or LoRA adapter tuning (Hu et al.,
      2022) only, noted as OLMo-7B (Full FT) or OLMo-7B (LoRA) respectively.

130   iments, we few-shot fine-tune OLMo-7B-Instruct over new task data from MMLU (Hendrycks

132   perplexity increase over a stratified sample of 10,718 examples from Tulu v2 as upstream examples.
133
      The number of training examples in these datasets are smaller (on the scale of tens to hundreds of
      examples), making the setup closer to LLM error editing (Yao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). We
134
      perform either full-parameter fine-tuning or LoRA fine-tuning.
135
136   MPT-7B. MPT models are pre-trained on a diverse collection of corpora. We fine-tune the LM over
137   the same 85 instruction-tuning tasks as OLMo models and evaluate forgetting on Redpajama (Com-
138   puter, 2023), involved as a part of the pretraining corpora of MPT. We sample 10,000 maximum
139
      2,048 token documents from Redpajama as upstream examples.

141   include the other training details in Appendix A.

            C OMPLETION
298
299   We utilize our findings in Sec. 3 to predict example forgetting as the model learns a new task, a
300
      problem also studied in prior works (Jin & Ren, 2024). Finding out the most forgotten examples
      allows better spot of the behavior changes of the models, enabling efficient and targeted approaches
301
      to mitigate forgetting, e.g., by replaying these examples (Aljundi et al., 2019a; Wang et al., 2024).
302
      Although the ground truth forgetting can be directly obtained by running inference with the fine-tuned
303   model over the upstream data, this requires extensive computational resources. We restrict the
304   prediction methods to be computationally efficient.
305
306
      Our analysis in Sec. 3 suggests that (1) the associations between learned tasks and forgotten examples
      display simple statistical patterns, while (2) the associations correlate poorly with many similarity
307
      metrics. Therefore, we hypothesize that leveraging the statistics of forgetting allows more effective
308
      prediction of forgetting than leveraging the contents of the tasks and examples. Following this
309   intuition, we formulate prediction of example forgetting as a matrix completion problem over the
310   empirical associations Z, analogical to collaborative filtering in recommender systems (Sarwar
311   et al., 2001), where scalable approaches are studied extensively. We start by setting up the problem
312   formulation of predicting example forgetting, and evaluate the performance of prediction of different
313   approaches. We present reduced forgetting by utilizing the prediction outcomes during fine-tuning.
314
315

395   As we have discussed in Sec. 4.1, predicting forgetting with matrix completion requires seed forgetting
396   z S to be evaluated. We consider an offline and an online variant of the approach. The offline variant
397   performs a replay-free run of fine-tuning on the task Ti , after which the seed forgetting will be
398   evaluated. We then perform another run of fine-tuning while replaying examples with the predicted
399   forgetting. This creates computational overhead equivalent to one extra run of fine-tuning, but is still
400
      efficient when the training set of fine-tuning is considerably smaller than the upstream data. The
      online variant instead replays random examples for first 10% of fine-tuning steps, after which it
401
      evaluates seed forgetting and determine examples to be replayed in the rest of 90% steps. Compared
402
      to the offline variant, this mitigates the extra overhead of fine-tuning by trading off the prediction
403   accuracy of forgetting.
404
405   Baselines of mitigating forgetting. We compare with diverse strategies of selecting upstream
406
      examples for sparse replay. We primarily examine whether weighted sampling with predicted
      forgetting statistically significantly improves over random sampling of upstream examples (Random).
407
      We also compare with Maximally Interfered Retrieval (MIR) (Aljundi et al., 2019a), a selection
408
      strategy sharing the similar notion of importance that forgotten examples should be selected for
409   replay. The approach performs bi-level sampling by selecting the most forgotten examples from a
410   small random subset of upstream data (set as |S| = 30). In addition, we apply strategies that consider
411   different definitions of upstream example importance. We examine an approach based on perplexity
412   thresholds (PPL) (Marion et al., 2023), which samples upstream data of which the perplexity is around
413   the median of the distribution. For OLMo-1B, we also sample replayed examples proportional to the
414   gradient inner products (Grad-Prod) we evaluated in Sec. 3.3, in a similar vein to coreset selection
415   approaches that utilize gradient information (Park et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). As a reference,
416   we also experiment with upweighting upstream examples with ground truth forgetting zij , which,
417
      however, can face computational efficiency issues in practice.
418    Metrics. We measure log-perplexity increase over a held-out subset of 10,000 examples from the
419    upstream data. This ensures none of the test examples are selected for replay by any of the example
420    selection strategies.

      5    R ELATED W ORKS
476
477
      Factors that affect forgetting. In this paper, we primarily studied how the associations between
478
      learned and forgotten examples inform forgetting. Prior works have studied various factors that affect
479
      forgetting of the models, such as (1) type and size of the LM (Mehta et al., 2021; Scialom et al., 2022;
480   Kalajdzievski, 2024; Mirzadeh et al., 2022) (2) trainable parts of the model (e.g., LoRA, soft prompts,
481   or full-model tuning) (Biderman et al., 2024a; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) (3) hyperparameters such
482   as learning rate (Ibrahim et al., 2024; Winata et al., 2023), dropout (Goodfellow et al., 2014), number
483   of training steps (Biderman et al., 2024b; Kleiman et al., 2023) (4) optimizer (Lesort et al., 2023) and
484   training algorithms (e.g., various continual learning algorithms) (Shi et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), (5)
485   the upstream examples or the knowledge themselves (Toneva et al., 2019; Zhang & Wu, 2024). Future
      works can study how various factors affect the associations between learned tasks and forgotten


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      examples. We consider empirical and theoretical study on the effect of task similarity on forgetting
487   to be most relevant to ours. Ostapenko et al. (2022) empirically study relationships between task
488   similarity and forgetting in foundation models over a sequence of newly learned tasks; our work
489   instead focuses on forgetting of upstream data of LLMs. Theoretical study by Doan et al. (2020);
490   Ding et al. (2024); Evron et al. (2022) dissects effects of the learned tasks on forgetting in linear
491   models or around model initialization. We believe research on interpretations of forgetting (Tao et al.,
492   2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Kotha et al., 2024) is complementary to ours and can potentially explain in
493   the future why the associations in Z are often simple, and in which circumstances the associations
494   become more complicated.
495   Data selection and data attribution. Related to our work, data attribution studies faithful algorithms
496   to find training examples that account for a prediction (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ilyas et al., 2022) from a
497   pool of training examples. Park et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024) study
498   the problem of selecting a subset of training data that maximizes performance on a given domain
499   or task at a fixed budget for LLMs. Feldman & Zhang (2020); Tirumala et al. (2022); Biderman
500   et al. (2024b); Swayamdipta et al. (2020) identify memorized, important, or forgetful training data.
501
      However, the notion of data importance in these works is different from how likely the upstream
      examples will be forgotten during fine-tuning. Furthermore, a systematical study on how such
502
      importance is dependent on newly learned tasks is still absent. Prior works represented by Aljundi
503
      et al. (2019a); Wang et al. (2024); Aljundi et al. (2019b) study selection strategies of examples for
504   replay-based continual learning algorithms.
505
506   Predicting model behaviors. A number of works show LLMs can display a hybrid pattern of
507
      unpredictable to highly predictable behaviors (Ganguli et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). Ye et al.
      (2023); Xia et al. (2020); Schram et al. (2023) study prediction of task performance across datasets
508
      and training setups. We perform prediction at the example level which is more fine-grained and
509
      under-explored.

512   6    C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   In this paper, we empirically analyzed the associations between learned and forgotten examples in
515   LM fine-tuning. We showed the association displays a low rank pattern across different setups. We
516   showed the example associations alone offer useful information to predict example forgetting when
517
      fine-tuning LMs on new tasks. We demonstrated the practical utility of our analysis by showing
      reduced forgetting as we reweight examples for replay with predicted forgetting. Future works can
518
      extend the study to a continual learning setup where new domains or tasks are sequentially learned
519
      while predicting forgetting of upstream examples online.
520
521   Limitations. In our research process, we tried to control confounding factors such as model size,
522   learning rate, or the number of training steps. However, we did not systematically study how the
523
      associations between learned tasks and forgotten upstream examples depend on these factors. For
      example, although we presented results of 1B and 7B models from the same family (OLMo) and
524
      observe differences in their association statistics (such as R2 scores in Sec. 3.1), it is unclear how the
525
      associations will appear in smaller or larger models. Besides, our empirical findings raise questions
526   about interpretability of the associations, i.e., why certain examples are more prone to forgetting
527   while learning a new task, and whether there exist more human-interpretable patterns behind the
528   associations. Finally, we limited our experiments to fine-tuning on a single task at a time. Predicting
529   and mitigating forgetting in sequentially learned tasks is left as future work.

       We evaluate downstream task performance of LMs before and after fine-tuning on 8 tasks from Dolly
1007
       with LM-Evaluation-Harness framework (Gao et al., 2024). For OLMo-7B models, we evaluate
1008   on the same set of downstream tasks in OLMo technical report (Groeneveld et al., 2024). For
1009   OLMo-7B-Instruct models, we evaluate on Open LLM Leaderboard tasks4 . For fine-tuned models,
1010   we compare no replay, replaying random examples, and replaying with forgetting predicted by offline
1011   KNN. Tables 4 and 5 summarize the results.
1012
       We notice that fine-tuning OLMo-7B on Dolly improves downstream performance on most of the
1013
       downstream tasks. This aligns well with the purpose of fine-tuning a LM that is not instruction-tuned.
1014   Nevertheless, we notice performance degradation on two of the tasks, namely ARC-Easy and Sciq,
1015   which indicates forgetting. Although offline KNN achieves higher accuracy scores on these two tasks
1016   compared to no-replay (67.48 to 67.34, 84.91 to 84.09), we do not find the improvement statistically
1017   significant. For OLMo-7B-Instruct, fine-tuning on Dolly only improves performance on MUSR. The
1018   models clearly suffer from forgetting on the other tasks such as IFEval. Offline KNN achieves higher
1019   scores than random or no replay on IFEval (18.28 compared to 18.19 and 17.72), but we could not
1020   conclude about the significance of the improvement.
1021   To summarize, we do not see clear performance improvement in downstream task performance
1022   (evaluated with task-specific metrics) by replaying random or chosen examples. We conjecture that
1023   replay-based approaches are not sufficient to mitigate forgetting on their own, and can be combined
1024   with other approaches such as careful learning rate scheduling or parameter regularization. We leave
1025   more effective algorithms to mitigate downstream task forgetting with predicted forgetting as future
       works.
