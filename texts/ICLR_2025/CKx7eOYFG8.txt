009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning strives to train a model in a sequential manner by learning from
012            new tasks while retaining information about old tasks. Treating this as a common
013            classification problem leads to catastrophic forgetting, especially in deep learning
014            settings, where knowledge of old tasks is forgotten as soon as a model is opti-
015            mized on new tasks. Existing solutions tackle this problem by imposing strict
016            assumptions, such as the availability of exemplars from previously seen classes or
017            a warm start of a model on many classes before starting the continual learning.
018
               While effective on known benchmarks, such assumptions can be impractical and
               do not directly address the stability-plasticity dilemma in continual learning. In
019
               this paper, we follow a recent push in the field to tackle continual learning in the
020
               exemplar-free cold-start setting. We propose Model-in-the-Middle (MITM). The
021            idea behind MITM is to separate the learning of new classes and retention of past
022            class knowledge by using two distinct models. We propose a learner with sym-
023            metric distillation from both models, enabling us to learn evolving representations
024            as new tasks arrive. We show that explicitly separating and balancing old and new
025            tasks through symmetric distillation helps absorb large distribution shifts, mitigat-
026            ing the stability gap. Our approach is simple yet outperforms the state-of-the-art
027            in the challenging exemplar-free cold-start continual learning setting.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Continual representation learning addresses a fundamental limitation in deep learning: when a neu-
033
      ral network is trained on a new task, it immediately forgets most information about old tasks (French,
      1999). This catastrophic forgetting results from the stability-plasticity trade-off, as identified by
034
      Grossberg (2012). An overly stable model may fail to learn new tasks, while an overly plastic model
035
      risks forgetting previous tasks. Thus, the central challenge in continual learning is to strike the right
036   balance between stability and plasticity, allowing models to learn representations for both old and
037   new tasks (De Lange et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024).
038
039
      Continual learning methods approach the stability-plasticity dilemma from various angles. Some
      focus on stability; Starting from a strong initialization point and attempting to change the model
040
      as little as possible for the new classes (Zhang et al., 2023; McDonnell et al., 2024). Dynamic
041
      network approaches, for instance, expand the model’s architecture as new data emerges, based on
042   the assumption that model capacity is limited and adapting to new features will overwrite old ones,
043   leading to forgetting (Rusu et al., 2016; Yan et al., 2021). For models with fixed or limited capac-
044   ity, regularization-based approaches control plasticity by either adjusting the optimization direction
045   (data regularization) (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Buzzega et al., 2020) or evaluating the importance of each
046   parameter and regularizing the parameters (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020).
047
      Another group of methods emphasizes plasticity, enabling the model to adapt to new patterns while
048   remembering old patterns (Wu et al., 2019; Buzzega et al., 2020). Exemplar-based methods aim to
049   preserve the knowledge of previous tasks by replaying exemplars stored in memory (Chaudhry et al.,
050   2019; Rebuffi et al., 2017) or by generating synthetic examples using generative models (Smith et al.,
051   2021). On the other hand, prototype-based approaches use exemplars to prevent semantic drift by re-
052   calculating class prototypes after each stage (Rebuffi et al., 2017; De Lange & Tuytelaars, 2021; Zhu
053   et al., 2021). Model rectification methods assume that catastrophic forgetting will occur, and they at-
      tempt to mitigate it by correcting the biases introduced (Wu et al., 2019). Similarly, prototype-based


                                                         1

054
      approaches that do not use exemplars or pre-trained models correct the biases by drift estimation
055   and feature consolidation (Gomez-Villa et al., 2024; Magistri et al., 2024). Knowledge distillation
056   techniques transfer information from an expert model, trained on previous tasks, to a student model
057   that is learning the current task (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Buzzega et al., 2020).
058
      The need for exemplars remains central in most methods to avoid bias toward recent tasks. Storing
059
      previous-task samples, however, has multiple practical limitations, such as memory constraints,
060
      legal concerns, and dealing with sensitive and private data that should be safeguarded (Venkatesan
061   et al., 2017). Moreover, exemplar-based solutions do not generalize to vision-language domains,
062   as keeping exemplars of all possible textual descriptions is not tractable. This has prompted a shift
063   towards exemplar-free continual learning (Wang et al., 2024), where many methods use prototypes
064   to replace exemplars. A downside of exemplar-free prototype-based methods is that they are prone
065   to semantic feature drift (Yu et al., 2020). To mitigate the drift, these methods work under warm-start
066   settings, beginning with a large first task or a pretrained model (Zhu et al., 2021; Petit et al., 2023) to
067   start with good representations that stay unchanged. Recent works advocate studying exemplar-free
068   cold-start continual learning (Magistri et al., 2024; Gomez-Villa et al., 2024), as this setup provides
069
      the most challenging setting for continual learning. In the absence of exemplars and warm-start,
      models employ complex techniques to correct the bias (Gomez-Villa et al., 2024) or consolidate
070
      features (Magistri et al., 2024).
071
072   The goal of this paper is to perform continual learning without the need for exemplars, prototypes,
073   warm starts, or additional corrections. We strive for a simple solution that stays close to a canonical
074   supervised objective, without the need to balance information from old and new tasks through careful
075
      hyperparameter tuning. To that end, we introduce Model-in-the-Middle (MITM). MITM balances
      plasticity and stability by separating the learning of new representations from the integration of
076
      past and current knowledge. Given a network to be optimized, we take a leading, middle, and
077
      trailing version. The trailing model retains past knowledge, while the leading model learns new
078   information. The middle model distills knowledge from both, achieving a natural balance between
079   stability and plasticity, effectively mitigating task-recency bias. To the best of our knowledge, this
080   is the first exemplar-free cold-start method that addresses task-recency biases without relying on
081   complex correction or consolidation mechanisms. Empirical results show that MITM provides state-
082   of-the-art results in the exemplar-free cold-start setting. Supporting analysis shows that our approach
083   comes without a significant bias toward initial or most recent tasks while mitigating forgetting.

      2    R ELATED WORK
086
087
      Warm-start methods In the exemplar-free setting, most methods use regularization to prevent
088   forgetting. LwF (Li & Hoiem, 2017) applies regularization to class probabilities, while EWC (Kirk-
089   patrick et al., 2017) applies it to model weights. Others approach regularization by estimating pa-
090   rameter importance (Aljundi et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017). However, these methods often suffer
091   from significant semantic drift (Yu et al., 2020), leading to further research aimed at reducing this
092   issue. FeTrIL (Petit et al., 2023) addresses this by generating pseudo-features based on the differ-
093   ence between old and new prototypes, helping the model adapt to new classes. Panos et al. (2023)
094   freeze the feature extractor after the first task, using pseudo-features from the initial task to handle
095   subsequent ones. In FeCAM (Goswami et al., 2024), the authors leverage the heterogeneous fea-
096
      ture distribution in class-incremental learning, using an anisotropic Mahalanobis distance instead
      of the standard Euclidean metric for better classification. These methods are typically evaluated in
097
      warm-start scenarios, which begin with a large initial task or pretrained models. Starting from strong
098
      representations allows these models to focus more on stability by freezing the feature extractor (Petit
099   et al., 2023; Panos et al., 2023; Goswami et al., 2024), limiting plasticity or relying on pre-trained
100   models (Zhang et al., 2023; McDonnell et al., 2024). Recently, the shortcomings of warm-start set-
101   tings have sparked renewed interest in exemplar-free cold-start class-incremental learning (Magistri
102   et al., 2024; Gomez-Villa et al., 2024).
103
104   Correction-based methods Works addressing the difficult problem of exemplar-free cold-start
105   continual learning must manage the effects of increased plasticity. High plasticity can cause feature
106   drift, leading to a bias toward the most recent tasks, as the model adapts quickly but risks losing
107   information from earlier tasks (Masana et al., 2022). In earlier approaches, this bias was corrected
      by using exemplars (Wu et al., 2019) or applying cosine normalization (Hou et al., 2019). In the


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      exemplar-free setting, methods such as Zhu et al. (2021; 2022) mitigate this bias by employing
109   prototypes, which approximate class means in the feature space and help calibrate the classifier.
110   Prototypes are also used to model semantic drift, as in Gomez-Villa et al. (2024), and can even
111   eliminate the need for a classification layer. Direct prevention of semantic drift is addressed in
112   Magistri et al. (2024) by consolidating features and balancing them through prototype rehearsal.
113   While prototype-based methods are promising, they require assumptions about class distributions,
114   and the subsequent parameters must be carefully estimated for effective use.
115
116   Disentangling past and present task representations Another way to balance stability-plasticity
117   without using exemplars is by separating what is relevant for past and present tasks. One group of
118   methods (Mittal et al., 2021; Ahn et al., 2021) addresses this separation by identifying the Softmax
119
      operation as a major cause of catastrophic forgetting. They show that applying Softmax separately
      to the previous and current task classes can mitigate this issue. This separated Softmax also helps
120
      reduce the stability gap (De Lange et al., 2023), as demonstrated in Caccia et al. (2022). On one
121
      side, dynamic networks explicitly separate the past and present knowledge by expanding the model’s
122   backbone, or neurons, as new data emerges (Rusu et al., 2016; Yan et al., 2021), based on the as-
123   sumption that the model’s capacity is limited in representing both old and new tasks effectively.
124   However, these methods require expandable memory budgets, making them unsuitable for incre-
125   mental learning on edge devices. On the other side, distillation-based methods, such as Javed &
126   Shafait (2019); Buzzega et al. (2020); Mittal et al. (2021), compress past and present knowledge
127   into a single student model by using a previous copy of the model as a teacher. Our approach strikes
128   a balance between these extremes, where we use three separate models, each an expert in the current
129   task, past tasks, and a combination of the two. The idea of using three separate models for exemplar-
130
      free incremental learning is also explored in Lee et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2020). In contrast, we
      jointly train all models on the current task data, preventing abrupt shifts in target distributions.

      Our motivation for choosing logit distillation builds on the observations on feature distillation in
221   Magistri et al. (2024), who argue that feature distillation avoids feature drift, a key factor in for-
222   getting, but does so by constraining plasticity in all directions. In contrast, by distilling based on
223   past logits, we constrain only the feature subspace relevant to these logits and allow for plasticity
224   in the underlying feature representations. This is crucial, as this allows the underlying features to
225   recombine to accommodate the new classes while retaining logit activation’s on past classes.
226
      In summary, the leading model allows for a few simplifications compared to other approaches. Due
227   to the separation of learning new representations from merging existing ones, we allow for uncon-
228   strained optimization of the current task. Additionally, there is no need to explicitly balance this
229   plasticity, as the symmetric distillation naturally introduces this balance. The lack of hyperparame-
230   ters for balancing and additional codebooks, memory banks, or pretraining makes for a simple yet
231   effective approach.

235   4.1   T RAINING SETUP
236
237   We train a slimmed ResNet18 model based on Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017), as is commonly used in
238   continual learning literature (Buzzega et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; Magistri et al., 2024). Following
239   Buzzega et al. (2020), we apply random crops and horizontal flips to both the input stream and buffer
240   exemplars. All introduced methods are trained for 100 epochs using SGD with a momentum of 0.9
241
      and no weight decay. The learning rate is set to 0.01 and is reduced by a factor of 0.1 at epochs 50
      and 85. The order of classes is fixed to ensure reproducibility.
242
243   Our experiments are conducted on S-CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009; Rebuffi et al., 2017),
244   which consists of 10 incremental steps, each containing 10 classes, in a cold-start setting following
245   Magistri et al. (2024); Gomez-Villa et al. (2024). We also perform experiments on TinyImageNet
246   (Wu et al., 2017) under the same cold-start configuration and with 10 steps. Unlike the warm-start
247
      setting, where the model is pretrained on a portion of the data (typically half of the tasks) before
      incremental learning, the cold-start setting is more challenging as models must learn incrementally
248
      from scratch.

      4.2   E VALUATION METRICS
251
252   To provide insight into the performance of the method both during training and the resulting model,
253   we report multiple metrics. We report Final Average Accuracy (FAA), the accuracy averaged over all
254   tasks after training on the final task. Additionally, we report Average Incremental Accuracy (AIA)
255   as in Magistri et al. (2024), which averages the evaluation accuracy after every task, providing
256   some insight into the performance during training. As in De Lange et al. (2023), we report average
257   minimum accuracy (min-ACC) and average forgetting (Forgetting) to provide insight into how
258   well knowledge is retained. Specifically, to compute the average minimum accuracy, we evaluate
259   the entire test set after every training iteration and retain the per-task minimum observed value, as
260
      specified in De Lange et al. (2023). This provides insight into the transient forgetting, known as the
      stability gap, which is hypothesized to contribute to overall forgetting.
261
262   We introduce a new metric Final Accuracy standard deviation (FAσ) to provide further insight into
263   task bias, as many methods exhibit a bias towards the most recent task (Masana et al., 2022). To this
264   end, we report the standard deviation of the per-task final accuracy. The intuition is that a method
265   with significant bias towards any task has a larger variance in final task accuracies than an unbiased
266
      method with equal accuracy for all tasks. FAσis not independent of FAA, and it should always be
      considered alongside it.

299
300
      In our first experiment, we compare MITM to baselines and state-of-the-art exemplar-free ap-
301   proaches in the cold-start setting. Additionally, we consider forgetting and task bias metrics to
302   provide insight. In Table 1, MITM outperforms other methods by a margin in S-CIFAR100, while
303   Table 2 shows the same improvements for S-TinyImageNet. We observe that FeTrIL, tailored for
304   the warm-start setting, struggles in the cold-start setting. This is also the case for other warm-start
305   methods such as Zhu et al. (2021); Goswami et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2022) as previously highlighted
306   by Magistri et al. (2024). EFC and LwF+LDC perform better in the cold-start setting and represent
307   the state-of-the-art. MITM outperforms these methods in both accuracy metrics. In terms of final
308   average accuracy we improve by more than 9% over LwF+LDC and EFC, while reducing forgetting
309
      by 42% compared to the latter, indicating we do not prevent forgetting by simply not learning. The
      average minimum accuracy observed for past tasks is close to the final average accuracy, indicating
310
      we do not suffer from the transient forgetting observed in De Lange et al. (2023). This benefit in
311
      minimum accuracy is also observed for EFC, which also employs a separated softmax.
312
313   Mitigating the stability gap prevents potential degradation of past task performance, contributing to
314   preventing a bias to certain tasks, and allowing EFC to improve over the baselines in terms of task
315
      bias. MITM further improves in this metric, obtaining a result close to joint training performance.
      This indicates it does not compromise in task bias to inflate final average accuracy, which can be the
316
      case when biased to the most recent task. We attribute this additional balance shown by MITM to
317

498   5   C ONCLUSION
499
500   In this paper, we introduce MITM, a simple symmetric distillation method for continual represen-
501   tation learning that closely resembles canonical supervised learning. Our method does not depend
502   on exemplars, warm start, or additional bias correction. We motivate the separation of learning new
503   representations and merging existing ones, and show that this approach naturally mitigates the sta-
504
      bility gap and reduces task bias. Building on these properties, our approach obtains state-of-the-art
      results for exemplar-free cold-start class-incremental learning. Additionally, we introduce a new
505
      metric for task bias and investigate MITM’s ability to balance past and current tasks. Finally, we
506
      perform ablations on the components of our method regarding the disentanglement strategy and the
507   stability-plasticity balance to highlight their contribution.
508
509   We employ logit distillation to allow underlying features to adapt, providing increased plasticity
510
      over feature distillation. It is possible that insights from regularization methods can further refine
      the allowed plasticity to avoid forgetting. This paper focuses on offline continual learning without
511
      exemplars and in low-memory settings. Extending our method to be complementary to exemplar-
512
      based settings and online continual learning scenarios might be exciting directions to investigate.
