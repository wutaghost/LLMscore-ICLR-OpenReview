009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we describe the creation and use of synthetic datasets based on var-
012            ious partial differential equations to support spatio-temporal graph modeling in
013            machine learning for different applications. More precisely, we showcase three
014            equations to model different types of disasters and hazards in the fields of epi-
015            demiology, atmospheric particles, and tsunami waves. Further, we show how such
016
               created datasets can be used by benchmarking several machine learning models
               on the epidemiological dataset and, additionally, by showing how pre-training
017
               on such synthetic datasets can improve model performance on real-world epi-
018
               demiological data. The presented methods enable others to create datasets and
019            benchmarks customized to individual requirements. The source code for our
020            methodology and the three created datasets can be found on github.com/github-
021            usr-ano/Temporal Graph Data PDEs.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Spatio-temporal graph modeling in machine learning has recently seen some developments (Chen
027
      & Eldardiry, 2024). Despite the existence of few regularly used data sources, we identify a lack
      of larger high-quality datasets for different tasks. A noteworthy source of data for spatio-temporal
028
      graph machine learning is traffic data from the California PeMS system (Li et al., 2018; Yu et al.,
029
      2018; Cini et al., 2023) in different settings. Also, COVID-19 data has been used in e.g. theoreti-
030   cal work classifying different architectures (Gao & Ribeiro, 2022). Although the existent datasets
031   are valuable for specific tasks, they have limitations in quality (high noise), scope (few samples),
032   accessibility (individually curated but unpublished), and adaptability (high individual preprocess-
033   ing effort). These limitations hinder the comprehensive benchmarking and development of spatio-
034   temporal graph architectures for different tasks. Therefore, large and more diverse high-quality
035   datasets that are freely accessible are crucial.
036   The utilization of synthetic data allows the fast creation and adaption of datasets while avoiding
037   barriers regarding privacy or data availability. Furthermore, they are less error-prone regarding
038   measurement flaws and easier to control, as all involved dynamics are known in contrast to real-
039   world data. Some examples of (partially) synthetic datasets that find application in machine learning
040   are weather datasets (Muñoz Sabater et al., 2021), which are extensively used, along with turbulence
041   data Li et al. (2008), PDE-data Takamoto et al. (2022) or ground motion data (Lehmann et al., 2024).
042   Numerous scientific processes can be described with partial differential equations (PDEs). While
043   in many fields alternative modeling approaches exist, the versatility of PDE-based modeling is out-
044   standing. Over the past centuries, considerable research efforts have been dedicated to advancing
045   mathematical modeling with PDEs. Through our publication, we intend to make these significant de-
046   velopments accessible to the spatio-temporal graph learning community. Given their omnipresence
047   in countless applications, we outline a method for generating synthetic datasets based on PDEs in
048   this paper. Even though there exists a significant amount of work on PDEs and their solutions (Evans,
049   2022), the practical implementation of a PDE solver remains a demanding task.
050   Throughout this work, we solve three exemplary PDEs related to different types of disasters, each
051   describing the movement of entities over space and time. We solve these PDEs on an irregular
052   domain, and evaluate them on irregularly distributed points on this domain, as this is the realistic
053   scenario for the presented applications. Subsequently, we form time-dependent graphs from the
      obtained values in combination with the underlying spatial structure, to which we grant researchers

      direct access. While some prominent ML research focuses on the study of PDEs itself, such as
055   Raissi et al. (2019); Li et al. (2021) where an analytic description of the dynamics has to be known,
056   we hereby intend to mainly address data-centric studies of spatio-temporal graphs. Notably, we
057   facilitate the adoption of our code to other domains, dynamics, PDEs, or even applications. Our
058   work distinguishes itself from prior work (Takamoto et al., 2022) not only through the selected
059   PDEs, but also the fundamental methodology, the the Finite Element Method (FEM). Specifically
060   the FEM allows for complex domains with complex boundary conditions, and our overall outcome is
061   a temporal graph, not a grid. We further emphasize the interchangeability of the (complex) domain,
062   dynamics, or underlying equation throughout the utilization of the FEM and our published code.
063   The first equation we employ is inspired by the structure of real-world epidemiological data (e.g.
064   COVID-19 incidences), which suggests the use of spatio-temporal graph machine learning (Nguyen
065   et al., 2023; Gao & Ribeiro, 2022). We create a comparably large graph dataset containing long
066   records of infectious data with different epidemiological settings but consistent measurements over
067   time, absence of noise, and unconstrained accessibility to all determining information. This or any
068   similar epidemiological PDE, based on the SIR-ODE (Kermack & McKendrick, 1991), has never
069
      been solved numerically. Especially, there are no accessible simulations for other practitioners. Fur-
      thermore, we want to enable epidemiological researchers, to use individual spatial domains (e.g.
070
      country) by adapting our published code to specifically train and test models on other geometries.
071
      Additionally, a broad collection of similar PDEs with slight adaptations can easily extend the model-
072   ing by features such as vaccination status (Schlickeiser & Kröger, 2021), exposed (He et al., 2020),
073   or disease vectors (Collins & Duffy, 2022). Naturally, similar adaptations can also be applied to the
074   following two equations.
075
      Another prominent simulation technique in epidemiology for the spatio-temporal spread of infec-
076
      tious diseases are Agent-Based models (ABMs). AMBs often incorporate the SIR-ODE (Kermack
077
      & McKendrick, 1991) and simulate the spatial spread through the actions of agents, where an agent
078   represents an individual entity, such as a person or animal, which stochastically interact with each
079   other and their environment. While ABMs are commonly used to model macroscopical results of
080   microscopical actions (Ferguson et al., 2006), they depend on many epidemiological assumptions,
081   are stochastical and are limited to epidemiology. Modeling the spatial spread of a pathogen through
082   a diffusion process via PDEs is much easier and more stable regarding skewed assumptions. We
083   further illustrate our method of data-generation, alongside two more PDEs. Their resulting datasets
084   are less applied to real-world problems but help researchers in development, exploration and bench-
085   marking of new models.
086   The second equation, an advection-diffusion (or convection-diffusion) equation, models the move-
087   ment of particles (e.g. dust, nuclear fallout, smoke) or other quantities in the atmosphere. The
088   arrangement as a spatio-temporal graph as the underlying datatype is canonical, as real-life mea-
089   surements of those quantities with a sensor network likewise have an irregular geometry. Besides a
090   further benchmarking of temporal graphs, the creation of such a dataset could be used to pre-train
091   either risk-prediction models or to pre-train or test specific parts of ML-based weather models such
092
      as ClimODE (Verma et al., 2024).
093   As a third exemplary PDE, we choose the wave equation, which is very common in physics and
094   describes, for example, water, sound, or electromagnetism. In our case, we model a tsunami wave
095   approaching some coastline, where an interesting task could be to predict the next few steps of the
096   wave.
097   To demonstrate the utility of synthetic data, we lastly present a benchmarking of prominent spatio-
098   temporal prediction models on the epidemiological data. Alongside, we test the expressivity-based
099   classification concept of Gao & Ribeiro (2022), to understand whether it is sufficient to estimate
100   the performance of different architectures and their quality in practice. We further underline the
101   importance of synthetic data by evaluating if a transfer of knowledge into real-world data shows any
102   benefit.

      2     T EMPORAL PDE S TO SIMULATE SPATIO - TEMPORAL MOVEMENT
109
110   To simulate the proposed propagations through space and time through the three exemplary PDEs,
111   we first generally outline the numerical approximation of the solution. In Section 3, we describe the
112   generations of the datasets more explicitly.
113
      We solve the PDEs on (different) 2D-domains Ω ⊂ R2 for the three proposed scenarios. The
114
      PDEs are time-dependent on an interval [0, T ], and their solutions ν are of dimension d ∈ {1, 2},
115
      depending on the equation. Generally, our PDEs have the form ∂ν    ∂t = F (ν, ∇ν, ∆ν) with some
116
      additional boundary conditions. F is some (general) functional which will be specified later, ∆ the
117
      Laplace operator and ∇ the Gradient, both only along the spatial dimensions.
118
119   The solution ν : [0, T ] × Ω → Rd will be evaluated on a set of points within the domain Ω.
120   These points mimic geographical regions and their administrative units, or any unevenly distributed
121
      network of sensors. We equip this set of points with adjacencies and inverse distances to create a
      spatio-temporal graph dataset.
122
123
      Numerical solution sketch In the following paragraphs, we describe the numerical approximation
124
      of different time-dependent PDEs. This part can be skipped by a reader looking for a spatio-temporal
125   graph dataset but does not want to bother with the details of its generation. However, to understand
126   and adapt the published code and the data generation process, a short sketch of the methods is
127   inevitable to us and important to a reader looking to expand our set of equations and parameters.
128   Note, that although the outlined methodology can be applied to countless processes and PDEs, the
129   complexity of the synthesized data is limited by the amount and complexity of given dynamics.
130
      We solve the equations iteratively in the time domain and solve the occurring PDE for each time-
131   step with the FEM, this order is called Rothe method. We therefore first discretize alongside the
132   temporal dimension with an Euler scheme, i.e. approximate the time-derivative with a difference
133   quotient. We use the Crank-Nicolson method, which is a mixture of the implicit and explicit Euler
134   scheme for time-stepping. With θ = 0.5, a small temporal Euler stepsize h, and ν = ν(x, t) and
135   ν+ = ν(x, t + h) the approximation has the form
136                          ν+ − ν
                                     = θF (ν, ∇ν, ∆ν) + (1 − θ)F (ν+ , ∇ν+ , ∆ν+ ).
137                             h
138   Since for the timestep t the solution ν is known, we thereby obtain a PDE that is not time-dependent
139   anymore. We solve this PDE for ν + with the FEM, which is the most flexible, reliable, and therefore
140   standard numerical approach on PDEs. We only give a sketch of the FEM here, as details can be
141   found in numerical textbooks for PDEs. The FEM discretizes the domain Ω into a mesh, on which
142   a set of test functions ϕ as well as trial functions ν̃ (approximation of the solution ν) are defined.
143
      Throughout this work, we use first-order (linear) Lagrange Elements as test and trial functions. To
      obtain the so-called weak formulation, we rearrange the PDE into an implicit and an explicit part,
144
      then multiply it from the left with the test functions and integrate over the full domain. This forms
145
      an inner product, which allows an underlying Sobolev space to have the structure of aRHilbert space.
146   We will not dive deeper into this, but use the notation of an inner product ⟨ϕ, ν⟩ = Ω ϕν dx for a
147   better readability.
148
149
      Replacing additionally ν, ν+ with the corresponding trial functions ν̃, ν˜+ , we obtain the formulation:
150              ⟨ϕ, ν̃+ ⟩ − h(1 − θ)⟨ϕ, F (ν̃+ , ∇ν̃+ , ∆ν̃+ )⟩ = ⟨ϕ, ν̃⟩ + hθ⟨ϕ, F (ν̃, ∇ν̃, ∆ν̃)⟩.
151   The left-hand-side (implicit side) can be written as A(ν̃+ ), the right hand (explicit side) as b. We
152   then seek to solve A(ν̃+ ) = b for (the parameters of) ν̃+ . Note, that technically ϕ is a vector of test
153   functions, but we wanted to keep the notation simple.
154
      One often employed identity for compact ϕ is ⟨ϕ, ∆ν⟩ = −⟨∇ϕ, ∇ν⟩ due to integration by parts,
155   which we apply when Dirichlet or zero-flow Neumann boundary values are imposed. Note that
156   we omit plenty of information on which space this inner product is defined and details of such
157   transformations for the sake of simplicity, but again refer to any textbook covering the FEM.

247
248   To compute the solutions of the three presented PDEs, we generate a mesh of the domain Ω with the
249   meshing software gmsh (Geuzaine & Remacle, 2009). The implementation of the FEM is done with
250   deal.ii (Arndt et al., 2023), which is written in C++ and abstracted the full implementation around
251   the FEM, but this can be done with any other FEM library.
252
      As an underlying domain for both the epidemiological PDE and the advection-diffusion PDE, we
253
      use the shape of Germany. We evaluate the solutions of the PDEs on a set of points that have
254   the coordinates of administrative regions in Germany: 400 NUTS-3 regions. The intuition is the
255   reporting of COVID-19 cases in the same spatial granularity. We additionally manually create an
256   adjacency list of neighboring regions and weight them with the inverse of the distance of the centers
257   of the regions, More details on this can be found in the appendix A.1. We ultimately have created a
258   graph

263   with 400 = |V | nodes, 2088 = |E| edges and edge features Xi ∈ Rd for every time-step i. The

      4.1   (S PATIO -) TEMPORAL MODELS
365
366   We want to test solely data-centric models and do not take any knowledge of the underlying PDE
367   into account, such as its structure or parameters. We test both temporal and spatio-temporal models.
368   More specific details can be found in the supplementary material A.2 and the published material
369   online.
370
      Repetition The repetition model is a naive baseline that simply repeats the last given value.
371
372   RNN Recurrent-Neural Networks based on GRUs (Cho et al., 2014) have proven to be successful
373   for sequence prediction. During training, teacher forcing is applied. During validation forecasts are
374
      produced autoregressively.
375   TST Time Series Transformer (TST) is a Transformer-based (Vaswani et al., 2023) architecture
376   designed for temporal forecasting. It utilizes encoder and decoder layers to capture temporal de-
377   pendencies, and a positional encoding depending purely on the time-step. During training, teacher
      forcing is applied. During validation forecasts are produced autoregressively.


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      MP-PDE Given that the underlying data is based on solutions of a PDE, we also test a model based
379   on a Message-Passing PDE-solver (MP-PDE) from Brandstetter et al. (2022), but will not pass any
380   information of the underlying PDE to our model. The model consists of an encoder, a processor, and
381   a decoder. The encoder creates node-wise embeddings of the context data. The processor consists of
382   an MP-GNN, operating on a single graph with embedded features. The decoder is a 1D convolution
383   applied node-wise, and a special update rule, that propagates the last value through the next time-
384   step.
385   RNN-GNN-Fusion Motivated by the PDE itself, we aimed to separate the time from the space
386   dimensions by building an RNN (analogously to the abovementioned model) to encode the under-
387   lying ODE and an MP-GNN to emulate the diffusion. RNN and GNN run parallel, their outputs are
388   combined in a convex combination. During training, teacher-forcing is applied. During validation
389   forecasts are produced autoregressively. In the classification of Gao & Ribeiro (2022) this would be
390   classified as time-and-graph.
391   GraphEncoding We recreated a model from a recent contribution from the field of ML-based epi-
392   demiological forecasting (Nguyen et al., 2023). This model encodes the contextual time-steps sep-
393   arately in a shared GNN. The encoded graphs are then propagated through an LSTM (Hochreiter
394   & Schmidhuber, 1997) network. To achieve a forecast for multiple days, this network is applied
395   autoregressively. In the classification of Gao & Ribeiro (2022) this would be classified as graph-
396   then-time.

400
401   To benchmark the presented architectures from section 4.1, we define three tasks on the synthetic
402   epidemiological dataset, created from the SI Eq. 1, that are motivated by forecasting tasks with
403
      real-world data. While we wanted to present thorough experiments on this dataset, results with a
      similar experimental setup, but on the other two datasets from Eq. 2 and Eq. 3, can be found in the

406   We will proceed with the epidemiological dataset which we split 80/20 along the time axis into a
407   train and a test dataset, and omit any information about the Susceptible S, as this reflects real-world
408   data. On this data, we define the following tasks for machine learning models.
409   Forecasting on clean data The most straightforward task is a simple forecast of the next n
410   timesteps, based on the last m timesteps of inputs. We set m = n = 14. The input data into
411   the models therefore are 14 consecutive graphs, sharing the same adjacency, or one graph with 14
412   node features: (V, E, Xi,..,i+13 ). The targets are simply Xi+14,..,i+27 . As a test- and training loss
413   we use the RMSE over all samples, nodes in V , and forecasted timesteps m.
414   Stability: Noise on test data Since usually there is heavy noise on real-world input data, it is a
415   highly relevant and interesting scenario to test the robustness of the tested machine learning models.
416   We adopt the experimental setting from the previous forecasting benchmarking, but add noise on the
417   nodes features Xi,...,i+13 of the test dataset, but not onto training data. This enables us to study some
418   aspects of the models’ robustness in a controlled setting. Further, this reflects real-world data, on
419   which noise exists but is variable due to changed measurements, or delayed reporting. We studied
420   two different types of noise:
421   We found the Gaussian noise with distribution N (0, 0.01) to be an interesting setting for the nor-
422   malized dataset. A plot of the noisy data can be found in the appendix A.2.1 Fig 9.
423
      We also studied noise which reflects the failure of sensors/reports and therefore the occurrence of
424
      reported zeros instead of real values. Guided by this scenario, we replace 10% of the test data with
425   zeros to further study the models’ robustness.
426
427   Denoising: noise on context data We seek to study the abilities of different models to implicitly
428
      denoise input data, by both training and testing on noisy context data. We use the same Gaussian
      noise and dropout noise as in the previous experiments.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
494
495   We demonstrated how time-dependent PDEs can be used to create synthetic data for machine learn-
496
      ing on graphs. In particular, we have created and published three exemplary datasets that can be used
      by the community for further research questions. Notably, the numerical solution of any epidemi-
497
      ological PDE constitutes a novelty. The described method enables researchers to create synthetic
498
      temporal graph datasets for individual use cases to support the development of new machine learn-
499   ing methods, in particular, for use cases where data is scarce. Even though the methodology can be
500   easily adapted to many other use cases by using our published code, the more complex the PDEs
501   become, the more difficult it will be to solve them, while on the other hand, simpler PDEs may
502   lack the flexibility to model complex scenarios. However, we demonstrate through transfer-learning
503   experiments that pre-training on our synthetic datasets can lead to improvements in real-world data
504   performance. Despite the underlying PDE being relatively limited in flexibility and parameters,
505   these experiments showed significant performance increases for some models. Additionally, as an
506   application of our datasets, we have demonstrated a benchmarking of various models, showcas-
507
      ing that rigorous testing of common architectures was a substantial research gap due to a lack of
      sufficient data.
508
509   In summary, this paper presents a method for generating individual PDE-based graph datasets, of-
510   fers three readily available synthetic datasets, provides a benchmarking, and highlights significance
511   through drastic improvements of performance in real-world data. This work lays the foundation
512   for the development of further datasets and research problems, inviting others to build upon our
513
      findings.

774
775   The wave equation (3) also consists of a single consecutive simulation. There are two waves through-
776   out the simulation, that start at the boundaries. The boundary conditions are usuallyRobin boundary
777   conditions αu + βη · ∇u = 0 three exceptions: first during the interval 0 < t < 50000and on the
778   boundary with x1 > 6200000, x0 < 3400000, second during the interval 1000000 < t < 1100000,
779   and on the boundary with x1 > 6200000, x0 > 3800000, and third during the interval 1800000 <
780   t < 1900000, and on the boundary with x1 > 6200000, 3500000 < x0 < 3600000.
781   On these intervals and regions, the Dirichlet boundary g are respectively a constant factor
782   (0.8, 1, 0.9) for the wave’s amplitude, that can easily be adapted for further simulations. we set
