009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            A major barrier to the practical deployment of large language models (LLMs) is
012            their lack of reliability. Three situations where this is particularly apparent are
013            correctness, hallucinations when given unanswerable questions, and safety where
014            responses are harmful or offensive. In all three cases, models should ideally ab-
015            stain from responding—much like humans refrain from answering questions when
016            uncertain. Inspired by analogous approaches in classification, this study explores
017            the feasibility and efficacy of LLMs abstaining when uncertain in the domain of
018
               question-answering. We investigate two kinds of uncertainties, statistical uncer-
               tainty metrics and a distinct verbalized measure, termed as In-Dialogue Uncertainty
019
               (InDU), measuring hedge words such as ‘I don’t know’ in responses. Using these
020
               uncertainty measures combined with models with and without reinforcement learn-
021            ing with human feedback (RLHF), we show in all three situations, abstention based
022            on the right kind of uncertainty measure can boost the reliability of LLMs. By
023            abstaining for a few highly uncertain samples we improve correctness by up to 8%,
024            avoid 50% of hallucinations by correctly identifying unanswerable questions, and
025            in particular increase safety by 70-99% with almost no additional computational
026            overhead.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Team, 2023), LLaMA
032   (Touvron et al., 2023), and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) have shown remarkable potential across a
033
      spectrum of real-world applications. However, a major barrier to their increased practical deployment
      is their lack of reliability. Language models often answer simple questions incorrectly, and make
034
      up answers when they do not know — a phenomenon known as hallucination. Moreover, they can
035
      be manipulated into providing harmful or unsafe responses when prompted suitably, which leads to
036   safety issues.
037
038   In all three situations—when answers are incorrect, unknown, or unsafe — the language model
039
      should ideally refrain from answering the question, a concept termed abstention. In machine learning,
      pioneering work by Chow (1970) proposes classifiers should refrain from making a prediction
040
      when they are uncertain. A corpus of literature has further explored methods for implementing
041
      uncertainty-based abstention effectively in classification tasks (El-Yaniv et al., 2010; Geifman &
042   El-Yaniv, 2017; 2019). In this study, we explore the feasibility and efficacy of analogous approaches –
043   abstaining when uncertain – in the context of large language models, particularly in the domain of
044   question-answering.
045
      How can we effectively assess uncertainty in large language models (LLMs)? Neural network classi-
046
      fiers typically predict outcomes by rounding a probability vector over classes. Hence, classification
047
      uncertainty is naturally measured by how spread out these probabilities are, often quantified through
048   entropy. Similarly, when provided with a context or question, LLMs generate a probability vector for
049   each token, suggesting that uncertainty can be evaluated by measuring some form of entropy over
050   these probabilities. We call this type of uncertainty statistical uncertainty. However, unlike classifiers,
051   LLMs also exhibit a different form of uncertainty due to their text output. They may opt for responses
052   such as "I don’t know" or incorporate hedge-words like "perhaps" or "maybe" in their answers. We
053   refer to this form of uncertainty as In-Dialagoe Uncertainty. Given that this form closely mirrors
      human expressions of uncertainty, it becomes crucial to examine this particular aspect of uncertainty

069   In this study, we investigate the potential of uncertainty-based abstention to enhance performance
070   in question answering tasks. Specifically, we analyze three distinct scenarios. First, we consider
071
      correctness, wherein the model provides incorrect responses in standard question answering tasks.
      Second, we explore the phenomenon of hallucinations in the context of unanswerable questions,
072
      where the questions posed lack a correct answer, leading the model to fabricate responses, if it does
073
      not abstain from answering them. Finally, we explore safety concerns arising when the model is
074   presented with questions that may lead to unsafe or harmful answers. Across all three scenarios,
075   we examine two different types of uncertainties, namely statistical uncertainty and In-Dialogue
076   Uncertainty, and examine the impact of RLHF on these uncertainties.
077
      Specifically, our findings are:
078
079            • For correctness, we find abstaining using statistical uncertainty improves correctness across
080              a range of question-answering tasks from 2% to 8%. Additionally, although RLHF alters
081              the predictive distribution of the LLMs, it still preserves the model’s uncertainty awareness
082              and can be used to enhance correctness.
083            • For hallucination given unanswerable questions, we find thresholding abstention by In-
084              Dialogue Uncertainty (InDU) can effectively indicate unaswerable questions and thereby
085              reduce hallucinations by 50%.
086
               • For safety, we show abstaining using statistical uncertainty for RLHF fine-tuned models can
087
                 improve safety by filtering out 70-99% of unsafe responses.
088
089   Our conclusions underscore the importance of uncertainty for abstention. Abstaining based on the
090   right form of uncertainty can improve the reliability of language models by boosting correctness,
091   reducing hallucinations, and improving safety.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
094
095
      Uncertainty quantification has become a prominent field of research across various machine learning
096   domains, including Natural Language Processing (NLP). A relatively new area of research within
097   NLP is Natural Language Generation (NLG), which presents unique challenges for uncertainty
098   estimation. Researchers such as Jiang et al. (2021); Malinin & Gales (2021); Kuhn et al. (2023);
099   Huang et al. (2023) have adapted probability-based methods to NLG tasks. Jiang et al. (2021)
100   and Malinin & Gales (2021) compute predictive entropy by focusing on token-wise conditional
101   probabilities, thereby measuring lexical confidence. In contrast, Kuhn et al. (2023) propose a more
102   sophisticated approach that estimates uncertainties using semantic likelihood probabilities associated
103   with the meanings of text, as opposed to standard sequence likelihoods.
104   Another research direction involves prompting or finetuning models to express uncertainty. Kadavath
105   et al. (2022), for instance, allow LLMs to assess their own uncertainty based on their response to
106   a given prompt. This is achieved by sampling candidate answers from an LLM and feeding them
107   back into the model to predict the uncertainty of these samples. Lin et al. (2022) finetune a model to
      express discrete uncertainty values via predefined labels, while Xiong et al. (2023) prompt the model


                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to express its uncertainty as an integer from 0 to 100. Conversely, Tian et al. (2023) use specifically
109   designed prompts to make the model judge its own uncertainty and respond with a predefined list
110   of words indicating confidence. However, these approaches entail additional computational effort
111   and can be sensitive to distribution shifts (Kuhn et al., 2023). Moreover, these approaches require
112   explicitly prompting the model for uncertainty estimates, which feels unnatural to humans and
113   is therefore suboptimal for chat-based applications. Our approach eliminates the need to request
114   verbalized uncertainty from the model, ensuring a natural interaction. Instead, we utilize human-like
115   uncertainty expressions through In-Dialogue Uncertainty (InDU), making our setup seamlessly
116   applicable to real-world chat scenarios.
117   Model awareness of its knowledge boundaries has been a longstanding challenge in machine learning,
118   and recent efforts have begun to address this issue in the field of NLG. Slobodkin et al. (2023)
119   delve into the behavior of LLMs when faced with unanswerable questions, finding evidence of
120   unanswerability encoding directly in the embeddings. Complementing this, Yin et al. present a
121   unique dataset composed of unanswerable questions across five diverse categories, along with their
122   answerable counterparts. Their research demonstrates that self-knowledge can be further improved
123
      by in-context learning and instruction tuning. Amayuelas et al. (2023) contribute to this field by
      curating a dataset featuring new unanswerable questions and devising a semantic evaluation method to
124
      quantify the uncertainty of the responses. However, these studies do not take into account uncertainty
125
      measures for distinguishing between answerable and unanswerable questions, a strategy that has
126   proven effective in other machine learning domains (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2018; Liang et al., 2020;
127   Liu et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2022).
128
129
      Beyond enhancing the helpfulness of LLMs, RLHF finetuning is also employed to induce safety
      into these models (Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022). However, it is widely recognized that the
130
      guardrails established via RLHF finetuning can be bypassed through adversarial attacks, particularly
131
      by crafting specialized prompts (Ganguli et al., 2022; Kour et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). While there
132   has been research on identifying harmful prompts and responses using a separate model (Inan et al.,
133   2023), we use uncertainty metrics and their ability to predict unsafe responses in RLHF finetuned
134   models solely via investigating the model’s outputs.

139   3.1     S TATISTICAL U NCERTAINTY M EASURES
140
141   In this study, our focus is on uncertainty measures that can be directly extracted from the model
142   without the need for specific prompting or finetuning, making them suitable for chat-based scenarios.
143   These probabilistic measures are widely used and have been proven to be highly effective in recent
144   research (Kuhn et al., 2023; Malinin & Gales, 2021). These methods compute a model’s confidence
145
      directly based on the probability distribution of the prediction. There are two types of methods:
      single inference, where confidence is derived directly from the response by summing the negative log
146
      likelihoods, and multiple inference, where multiple responses are sampled from the model to estimate
147
      the predictive or semantic entropy of the output distribution. We employ a singular inference method,
148   specifically negative log-likelihood, alongside two distinct multiple inference techniques: predictive
149   entropy and semantic entropy.
150

188   3.2     I N -D IALOGUE U NCERTAINTY
189
190   Hedging is a widely used strategy in human language to convey uncertainty and has been extensively
191   studied (Ferson et al., 2015; Fraser; Islam et al.; Theil et al., 2018; Raphalen et al., 2022; Ulinski
192   et al., 2018). While our objective is not to devise a perfect metric for measuring degrees of hedging in
193   natural language, we are primarily interested in whether LLMs inherently utilize hedging as a means
194
      to implicitly signal uncertainty, without the need for explicit prompting. We also aim to explore
      whether this verbalized implicit uncertainty gives further insights into uncertainty awareness of LLMs
195
      besides statistical measures. To this end, we employ a straightforward and interpretable method that
196
      quantifies the number of hedge words in a response. A higher count of hedge words in a response
197   indicates a higher level of In-Dialogue Uncertainty (InDU), and conversely, a lower count suggests
198   less uncertainty. For our experiments, we employ the same hedge word list as used by Islam et al..
199

201
      3.3     A SSESSING THE QUALITY OF UNCERTAINTY MEASURES
202
      To assess the quality of an uncertainty measure we gauge how well uncertainty can distinguish the
203
      correctness of a response. In line with previous work (Kuhn et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b; Band et al.,
204   2022), we compute the area under the receiver operator characteristic (AUROC) for each uncertainty
205   measure. AUROC captures how well uncertainty ranks correct versus incorrect responses. We use
206   AUROC as prior work (Kuhn et al., 2023) confirmed AUROC serves as a more suitable gauage of
207   uncertainty quality compared to calibration measures such as the Brier score for free-form question
208   answering. We compute AUROC as a gauge of quality for hallucination and safety settings in a
209   similar fashion with the corresponding ground truth label. For example, for safety the ground truth
210   label corresponds to whether a responses is safe or unsafe.
211   Next to assess how well abstention based on uncertainty can improve correctness, we compute
212   Accuracy-Rejection Curves (ARC) (Lin et al., 2023a). ARC shows the change in accuracy when a
213   model abstains for low-confidence responses given an uncertainty threshold. To gauge the overall
214   quality across thresholds, we compute the Area Under the Accuracy-Rejection Curve (AUARC). We
215   compute ARC and AUARC for both hallucinations and safety in a similar fashion by assessing how
      abstention based on uncertainty can reduce hallucinations or boost the safety of model responses.

217
218
      We divide our experiments based on 3 settings: correctness, hallucinations and safety. Correctness
219   refers to scenarios were the model is required to answer questions truthfully in the case of answerable
220   questions. Hallucinations corresponds to settings where the model is required to abstains from
221   responding when faced with unanswerable questions. Lastly, safety relates to the model’s ability to
222   differentiate between safe and unsafe responses.
223
224   4.1   C ORRECTNESS S ETTINGS
225
226   Models Throughout this study, we utilize models from the Llama2 family Touvron et al. (2023)
227   since these models are open source and let us directly access log probabilities. These models, available
228
      in both pretrained (Base) and RLHF finetuned versions, offer direct access to log probabilities and
      come in various sizes, with the number of parameters being 7B, 13B and 70B. To ensure maximum
229
      reproducibility, we leverage open-source libraries from Hugging Face.
230
231
      Datasets We employ a diverse range of Q&A datasets. These include closed book question-
232
      answering datasets such as TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) and SciQA (Auer et al., 2023), as well as the
233   open book conversational question-answering dataset CoQA (Reddy et al., 2019), which provides
234   a supporting paragraph to aid in answering a question. We also utilize StrategyQA (Geva et al.,
235   2021), an implicit reasoning dataset requiring Yes or No answers, and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021),
236   a mathematical dataset comprising grade school math word problems created by human problem
237   writers.
238
239   Determining correctness To derive a ground truth label, we use fuzzy exact match. Unlike the
240   traditional exact match, which requires a response to match the reference answer word for word,
241   fuzzy exact match assesses whether the reference answer is encompassed within the response. This
242
      approach is more robust to variations in model response styles, while maintaining the interpretability
      of exact match. We show the validity of fuzzy exact match by comparing it with human evaluations
243
      in Appendix G.
244
245
      4.2   H ALLUCINATION S ETTINGS
246
247   Models We use the same models as we use in correctness settings.
248
249   Datasets We employ the SelfAware dataset (Yin et al.), which is divided into two subsets: unanswer-
250   able questions from five diverse categories and their semantically closest answerable counterparts.
251   The unanswerable questions are designed to be intrinsically unsolvable, where the expected response
252   is abstention rather than a hallucinated answer. Therefore, the model’s objective is to distinguish
253   between answerable and unanswerable questions. This setup enables a fair assessment of the model’s
254   ability to distinguish between knowing and not knowing the answer to a question.
255
256   Determining hallucinations via unanswerable questions We examine the model’s ability to
257   differentiate between answerable and unanswerable questions, using this distinction as our ground
258
      truth labels for computing AUROC.

282   5     R ESULTS
283
284   Can uncertainty reveal when a model should abstain from responding with incorrect, hallucinated, or
285   unsafe answers? To explore this question, we evaluate statistical and In-Dialogue forms of uncertainty
286   for pretrained and RLHF finetuned models across three scenarios eliciting incorrect, hallucinated, or
287   unsafe answers.
288   First for correctness, we find statistical uncertainty for both pretrained and RHLF models can reveal
289   incorrect responses. Second for hallucinations, we find In-Dialogue uncertainty can distinguish
290   answerable from unanswerable questions, particularly for RLHF finetuned models. Third for safety,
291   we show statistical uncertainty for RLHF models can surface unsafe responses. Finally, we opera-
292   tionalize the right form of uncertainty for each scenario to determine when the model should abstain.
293   In doing so, we show uncertainty—at a minimal computational cost—can boost correctness, reduce
294   hallucinations, and improve safety in language models.
295
296   5.1   S TATISTICAL UNCERTAINTY CAN INDICATE WHEN TO ABSTAIN FROM RESPONDING
297         INCORRECTLY
298
299   Can uncertainty distinguish correct from incorrect model responses? In Table 1, we compare statistical
300   uncertainty measures across nine Q&A datasets. Specifically we measure AUROC as an indicator of
301   how well a particular uncertainty measure ranks correct responses. We find that statistical measures of
302   uncertainty are able to discriminate correct from incorrect responses. For RLHF fine-tuned language
303
      models semantic entropy performs the best on the majority of the datasets with an average AUROC
      of 0.69. In-Dialogue Uncertainty on the other hand cannot effectively differentiate between correct
304
      and incorrect answers with an average AUROC of 0.53. For the reasoning based GSM8K dataset it is
305
      notable that both, statistical uncertainty measures as well as In-Dialogue Uncertainty, exhibit close to
306   random detection performance.
307
308   Can statistical measures also distinguish incorrect responses for RHLF finetuned models? To study
309
      this, we compare the same statistical measures of uncertainty as well as diversity of responses for
      RLHF finetuned models (measured by the number of semantic sets). As shown in Figure 2, we
310
      find that RLHF finetuning leads to less diversity and notably higher confidence for model responses.
311
      These findings align with prior work indicating that RLHF finetuning increases misscalibration in
312   LLMs OpenAI (2023); Kadavath et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2023); He et al. (2023);
313   Zhou et al. (2024). Nevertheless, our findings demonstrate that RHLF finetuning actually preserves
314   how well uncertainty can distinguish incorrect from correct responses.
315
316
      Improving correctness by abstaining with statistical uncertainty. Given uncertainty can effec-
317
      tively distinguish incorrect responses, can we use uncertainty to improve the overall accuracy on QA
318   tasks? We analyze Accuracy-Rejection Curves (ARCs), which measure accuracy on QA tasks given
319   an uncertainty threshold for abstaining, in Figure 3 a. Our analysis reveals that by rejecting the 5%
320   most uncertain samples on TriviaQA based on semantic entropy, we can boost the accuracy of the
321   RLHF finetuned model from 84.4% to 86.0%. With a higher rejection rate of 25% we can increase
322   the accuracy further by 8.2% to 92.6%. We show additional results for SciQA in Appendix H. These
323   results underscore how uncertainty, at a minimal computation cost, can improve models’ accuracy on
      QA tasks.

482   6    D ISCUSSION
483
484   Uncertainty can be complicated in large language models. On the one hand, similar to classification,
485   there is statistical uncertainty induced by the vector of token probabilities – together with its multiple
      versions, such as predictive and semantic entropy. On the other, there is In-Dialogue Uncertainty


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      induced by the presence of hedge words in responses. In addition, there are different forms of the
487   models – with or without RLHF – that have different kinds of uncertainty profiles.
488
489   The main message of our study is that despite these complications, selecting the right kind of
490
      uncertainty together with the right form of the model can lead to better abstention decisions for
      question answering, in the process promoting correctness and safety, and reducing hallucinations for
491
      unanswerable questions. The form of uncertainty and the form of the model differ from one use-case
492
      to the next – statistical uncertainty works for correctness and safety, while in-dialogue uncertainty
493   works for unanswerable questions. We have also introduced a new perplexity analysis to guide
494   practitioners in selecting the appropriate uncertainty regime, enhancing the practical applicability of
495   our findings for improving reliability. Specifically, we recommend leveraging statistical uncertainty
496   in low-perplexity scenarios and in-dialogue uncertainty in high-perplexity settings.
497
      Limitations of our work include that relying on the model’s internal sense of uncertainty is inherently
498
      imperfect. Furthermore, we only discuss applications of hallucinations within the datasets available
499   for unanswerable questions, but do not tackle all forms of possible hallucinations. Overall, our study
500   underscores the importance of uncertainty in the context of LLMs, and we hope that it will contribute
501   to the recognition of uncertainty as a critical factor in working with LLMs, as has been the case in
502   other fields of machine learning for a long time (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2018; Liang et al., 2020; Liu
503   et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2022).

      To provide deeper insights into the impact of uncertainty scores on the performance and safety of
709   models, we present further Accuracy-Rejection Curves (ARCs) in the following sections. Generally,
710   ARCs measure the accuracy on a subset of the dataset that is retained when samples are progressively
711   rejected based on an uncertainty measure, from the most uncertain to the least uncertain. A higher
712   Area Under the Accuracy-Rejection Curve (AUARC) indicates a more effective uncertainty measure,
713   as it can better differentiate between incorrect and correct samples, or between safe and unsafe
714   samples.
715
716
      A.1                C ORRECTNESS S ETTINGS
717
718
      In the context of correctness settings, accuracy is defined as the ratio of correct samples to all
719   samples in the remaining dataset. In Figure 4 we present ARC curves for both TriviaQA and SciQA,
720   comparing the pretrained and RLHF finetuned models. For TriviaQA, the AUARC is identical for
721   both models at 0.94. For SciQA, the RLHF finetuned model outperforms the pretrained model with a
722   score of 0.83 versus 0.77. As we reject more samples, the accuracy increases, demonstrating that the
723   use of uncertainty measures to reject samples effectively enhances the model’s performance. This
724   approach is particularly beneficial when the goal is to deploy a highly accurate model. While such a
725   model may abstain more frequently, when it does provide an answer, it is more likely to be accurate.
726

992    G    VALIDITY OF F UZZY E XACT M ATCH
993
994    To evaluate the correctness of model responses, we derive a ground truth label via utilizing fuzzy
995    exact match. This method, unlike the traditional exact match metric that necessitates a word-for-word
996    match between the response and the reference answer, determines if the reference answer is contained
997    within the response. This approach is more resilient to variations in model response styles, while
998    preserving the interpretability inherent in exact match.
999    We validate the effectiveness of fuzzy exact match by comparing it with human evaluations on 200
1000   samples each from TriviaQA and SciQA. We juxtapose the accuracy derived from these human
1001   evaluations with that obtained from fuzzy exact match, as well as the widely used exact match and F1
1002   score metrics based on RougeL. For TriviaQA, the accuracy based on human evaluations is 87.5%,
1003   closely mirrored by the 85.0% accuracy of fuzzy exact match. In contrast, the accuracies based on
1004   exact match and F1 score are significantly lower, at 14.5% and 27.8%, respectively. A similar trend
1005
       is observed for SciQA, with human evaluations yielding an accuracy of 77.0%, fuzzy exact match
       65.0%, and exact match and F1 score only 2.5% and 7.0% respectively. These findings indicate that
1006
       the commonly used metrics of exact match and F1 score diverge significantly from human evaluations
1007
       when assessing the correctness of responses. On the other hand, fuzzy exact match aligns much more
1008   closely with human evaluations and is still equally interpretable. Consequently, we adopt fuzzy exact
1009   match as our metric for correctness throughout our study.

       We employ two metrics to compute the safe response rate of models given a dataset: one is based on
       Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023), and the other utilizes the keyword-based approach described in Zhu
