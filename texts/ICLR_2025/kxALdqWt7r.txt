009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            CLIP, despite its robust zero-shot capabilities, often suffers from spurious correla-
012            tions that can lead to prediction errors, especially when deployed in environments
013            different from their training data. This paper addresses the challenge of correcting
014            errors in CLIP, particularly when only limited data is available and the underlying
015            biases causing errors are unknown. To tackle this issue, we introduce a novel
016
               two-phase model editing framework. In the first phase, we propose to utilize a
               data-driven approach to identify the spurious features that directly contribute to
017
               errors without prior knowledge of the biases and nullify the corresponding com-
018
               ponents in the model, creating a spurious-feature-ablated model. In the second
019            phase, we edit the original model by aligning the model’s outputs with those of the
020            spurious-feature-ablated model for misclassified samples to correct errors, while
021            also aligning with the original model for the remaining data to maintain locality.
022            Our experiments on the synthetic dataset and real-world datasets demonstrate the
023            effectiveness of our method in both identifying the causes of errors and rectifying
024            the model to significantly improve model performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) has emerged as a ground-
029
      breaking visual-language model, garnering substantial attention due to its remarkable zero-shot
030   performance across various downstream tasks (Zhou et al., 2022; Lüddecke & Ecker, 2022; Rom-
031   bach et al., 2022; Mokady et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022). By employing a contrastive learning
032   framework, CLIP aligns image features with corresponding textual descriptions within a unified
033   embedding space. It is trained on a vast corpus of image-text data from the web, enabling it to learn
034   robust visual representations that contain rich semantic information. These representations facilitate
035   zero-shot predictions by classifying images into categories based on the closest embedding similarity
036   between the image and the text descriptions of category names. This method has demonstrated strong
037   performance, even in out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks.
038   Despite its impressive generalization capabilities, CLIP is not without its limitations. It may inadver-
039   tently learn spurious correlations between visual features and text descriptions. When downstream
040   data distributions significantly deviate from the pre-training distributions, these correlations can
041   change, leading to failures in prediction. For instance, CLIP might rely on background or other
042   context attributes in images for classification tasks (Zhang & Ré, 2022; Ma et al., 2024), which can
043   lead to incorrect predictions when the context shifts.
044   In practical scenarios, when prediction failures in the CLIP model are observed after deployment,
045   there is an urgent need to correct these errors, particularly when only limited data is available.
046   Numerous studies (Gao et al., 2024; Zhang & Ré, 2022; Kumar et al., 2022; Dehdashtian et al., 2024;
047   Chuang et al., 2023; Seth et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022) have proposed methods to fine-tune the
048   pre-trained CLIP or to adapt the image representations, aiming at reducing spurious correlations and
049   enhancing the robustness of visual features. However, these methods typically require a large number
050
      of training images, which is not feasible when available data for correcting errors is scarce.
051   Model editing (Mitchell et al., 2021; De Cao et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023) offers a promising solution
052   in such scenarios. Model editing focuses on rectifying mistakes not just in the error samples but
053   also in all related samples that have the same underlying cause of the error (edit success) without
      affecting other unrelated data (edit locality). Previous studies (Gandelsman et al., 2024; Bhalla et al.,

070
      latent and unidentified in real-world settings. Consequently, it remains a significant challenge to
      correct errors in CLIP with limited data and without prior knowledge of the underlying biases.
071
072   To address this challenge, our approach involves identifying the components of the model that
073   contribute to errors in a data-driven manner. We start by following (Gandelsman et al., 2024) to break
074   down the image embedding into individual MLP and attention layers, and further decompose the
075   features of the attention layer into individual attention heads. We focus on attention heads, because,
076
      according to findings in (Gandelsman et al., 2024), MLP layers usually have a negligible direct effect
      on the prediction. We then performed a causal analysis (McGrath et al., 2023) using a small number
077
      of misclassified and correctly classified samples from the same category to identify the attention heads
078
      that contribute to prediction errors. This involves measuring the change of prediction after replacing
079   the attention head of a misclassified sample with the average feature of the same head from correctly
080   classified samples, and vice versa. Based on the causal analysis, we obtain the heads that are directly
081   responsible for incorrect predictions, without prior knowledge of the spurious correlations or the
082   specific role of the heads. Finally, we eliminate the effect of these misleading heads by zero-ablating
083   their contribution to the image embedding.
084
      While our initial identify-then-ablate editing strategy achieves the desired edit success in rectifying
085   CLIP, it may fail to achieve locality especially when the identified spurious features are also causal
086   features for unrelated data. Moreover, this strategy requires an additional step of zero-ablating the
087   identified head feature which could complicate the deployment. In real-world applications, adding
088   new modules post-deployment can be challenging. To address these issues, we propose a two-stage
089   model editing framework, as outlined in Fig. 1. In the first stage, we apply our identify-then-ablate
090   editing strategy to obtain a spurious-feature-ablated model. In the second stage, we fine-tune the
091   original model. We use a KL divergence loss to align the output logits of the model with those of
092   spurious-feature-ablated model for misclassified data and a KL divergence loss between the output
093
      logits and those of the original model for the remaining data. The former loss encourages the model to
      learn the knowledge from the spurious-feature-ablated model such that the error in the original model
094
      is corrected, ensuring edit success. The later loss encourages the model to preserve the knowledge of
095
      the original model such that the effect of rectification is limited to the target samples, maintaining
096   edit locality.
097
098   Summary of contribution: In this paper, we tackle the challenge of rectifying the CLIP model when
099
      only limited data is available and the biases causing errors are unknown and propose a two-phase
      framework for model editing. In the first phase, we identify which parts of the model (specifically,
100
      attention heads) are most responsible for errors by analyzing their direct contributions to the erroneous
101
      predictions using the available data. We then nullify these parts to create a spurious-feature-ablated
102   model that is less influenced by misleading features. In the second phase, we edit the model
103   by learning the error-corrected knowledge from the spurious-feature-ablated model to ensure edit
104   success, and learning the error-unrelated knowledge from the original model to ensure edit locality.
105   We conduct extensive experiments on the Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020) dataset with known
106   spurious correlations to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying the cause
107   of error. We also verify the superior performance of the proposed CLIP model editing method on
      real-world datasets with misclassified samples.

      2.1   CLIP-V I T I MAGE R EPRESENTATION D ECOMPOSITION

112   CLIP comprises a text encoder Etext and an image encoder Eimage , both of which learn representa-
113   tions in a shared vision-language space. The CLIP model is pre-trained by maximizing the similarity
114   for matched pairs and minimizing that for all unmatched pairs. During inference, CLIP generates
115   representations for both the input image and the textual descriptions of each class. It then calculates
116   the similarity between the image representation and each text representation, selecting the class with
117
      the highest similarity as the predicted class.

159   2.2   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION : M ODEL E DITING FOR CLIP
160
161   In this study, we address a common issue in the CLIP model, where it incorrectly predicts the label ŷe
      for an image xe , despite the ground truth being ye . This error is not isolated to a single instance but


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is indicative of a broader, underlying bias affecting a specific subset of data. We denote this subset as
163
      Ixe ,ye ,ŷe = {x| arg maxy fθ (x) = ŷe }, where fθ represents the CLIP model parameterized by θ.
164
165   To correct this bias, model editing refines the model, aiming to correct the error not only for the
166   image xe but also for all similar instances in Ixe ,ye ,ŷe . This correction should achieve two main
167
      objectives: edit success and edit locality. Edit success refers to the model’s ability to accurately
      predict the correct labels for the problematic data after the modifications, measured by the accuracy
168
      of the post-edit model θe on Ixe ,ye ,ŷe . Edit locality ensures that these changes minimally impact the
169
      model’s performance on unrelated data, maintaining its general accuracy. It can be examined by the
170   post-edit model’s accuracy on an unrelated dataset, defined as Oxe ,ye ,ŷe = {x|x ∈   / Ixe ,ye ,ŷe }.
171
172   Typically, the failures in CLIP are discovered after using a small set of data from each class to validate
173
      the model’s performance. These data, with both ground-truth labels and predicted labels, form the
      data basis for performing editing. In many cases, not all the data points are misclassified. Therefore,
174
      we assume that the dataset available for editing contains K1 correctly predicted samples and K2
175
      misclassified samples for each targeted class.

178   3     R EFINE CLIP: M ODEL E DITING FOR CLIP
179
180   This section presents our proposed two-phase model editing framework, RefineCLIP, designed
181   to rectify biases in CLIP that cause incorrect predictions. In the first phase, we employ a data-
182   driven method to identify the biases in the attention heads responsible for errors, followed by a
183
      straightforward nullifying strategy to eliminate these biases and correct the errors. In the second
      phase, our algorithm adapts the model to learn the error-correcting knowledge gained from the first
184
      phase, enabling successful edits while preserving the predictions for unrelated data to achieve edit
185
      locality.
186
187

      3.1   R EMOVE S PURIOUS F EATURE BY Z ERO -A BLATION
189
      By pre-training on vast diverse image-text pair data collected from web, CLIP learns rich visual and
190   language features. Moreover, as demonstrated by Gandelsman et al. (2024), certain attention heads in
191   CLIP-ViT capture specific image properties such as texture, shape, color, object count, location, etc.
192   However, CLIP also learns spurious correlations that can lead to incorrect predictions. For example,

198   mitigate their effect, we employ zero ablation, which modifies the image representation by effectively

333   4.1   D EBIASING CLIP
334
      Studies have shown that CLIP models suffer from various biases (Agarwal et al., 2021) including
335
      image background (Zhang & Ré, 2022; Ma et al., 2024) and demographic attributes (Wang et al.,
336
      2021; 2022; Dehdashtian et al., 2024). Addressing these biases is crucial for improving the model’s
337   performance in zero-shot prediction tasks. Research efforts can be broadly categorized into two
338   approaches. The first approach involves fine-tuning the CLIP model using novel data construction
339   strategies to enhance robustness (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang & Ré, 2022; Berg et al., 2022). For
340   instance, Wang et al. (2021) introduced a fair sampling strategy to balance data concerning biased
341   attributes like gender. Similarly, Zhang & Ré (2022) developed an adapter training method with
342   conservative sampling aimed at improving group robustness. These strategies, however, often require
343   extensive data for training. The second approach focuses on manipulating the features directly to
344   reduce bias. This includes techniques like feature reweighting or projection (Chuang et al., 2023;
345
      Wang et al., 2022; 2021; Adila et al., 2023; Dehdashtian et al., 2024). For example, RoboShot (Adila
      et al., 2023) employs a projection method to eliminate harmful information and enhance beneficial
346
      information in features by referring to harmful and helpful representations. Additionally, Wang
347
      et al. (2022) uses a re-presentation matrix to adjust features, minimizing representation divergence
348   for target attributes while maximizing it for bias attributes. These methods are potent in reducing
349   spurious information but require prior knowledge of the biases.
350
351   4.2   M ODEL E DITING
352
353   Model editing techniques (Mitchell et al., 2021; De Cao et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023) aim to
354   refine the behavior of LLMs for specific input-output pairs, while preserving their performance on
355   other data. These methods fall into three main categories: classifier-based, meta-learning-based,
356   and locate-then-edit methods. Classifier-based model editing works by retaining the pre-trained
357
      parameters and using a classifier to determine behavioral modifications. This method ensures
      that the original model predictions remain unchanged for unrelated samples outside the edited
358
      scope, while modifications are applied only to targeted samples. Locate-then-edit methods involve
359
      identifying specific model parameters linked to particular knowledge through causal tracing. Once
360   these parameters are pinpointed, they are directly updated to achieve the desired edits. Meta-learning-
361   based model editing utilizes a hyper-network, known as an editor, to update parameters. This editor
362   is meta-trained across multiple editing tasks to learn how to generate the necessary updates based on
363   the provided edit samples. For a detailed review of these methods for language models, please refer
364   to Yao et al. (2023).
365
      Despite its strides in large language models, adapting similar techniques to visual models like CLIP
366   and Vision Transformers (ViTs) remains largely untapped. Santurkar et al. (2021) adapted classifiers
367   in convolutional neural networks to mitigate concept-level spurious features by mapping misleading
368   visual concepts to correct targets. However, this requires prior knowledge of the erroneous visual
369   concept, its location, and the target concept, which may not always be available. Another work
370   (Gandelsman et al., 2024) proposes to ablate the spurious heads to rectify the errors, but it also
371   demands prior knowledge of which visual concept triggers the error and the specific role of the head
372   corresponding to the concepts.
373   Our proposed framework is partially similar to each of these approaches. In the first phase, similar to
374   Gandelsman et al. (2024), we perform ablation to edit, targeting spurious correlations. In the second
375   phase, akin to Santurkar et al. (2021), we adjust the classifier. However, our method distinguishes
376   itself by identifying spurious features through a data-driven approach, eliminating the need for prior
377   bias knowledge. This aspect is crucial for practical applications where such prior knowledge is
      unavailable.

533    6                    C ONCLUSION
534
535    In this paper, we introduce a two-phase model editing framework for rectifying the prediction errors in
536    CLIP which are caused by unknown spurious features. We show that the proposed measure effectively
537    identifies the heads causing incorrect predictions and removing these identified features from the
538    image representation repairs the model’s performance. We further propose using representation
539    adaption to refine CLIP features such that it reduces the influence of spurious features for incorrectly
       predicted data while preserving the prediction of unrelated data.

       F    L IMITATIONS AND D IRECTIONS FOR F UTURE R ESEARCH
1235
1236   In this work, we focus on identifying the model components related to spurious correlations by
1237   analyzing the direct effects of attention heads within the image encoder of CLIP-ViT. Specifically,
1238   we examine how these attention heads impact the image representation. However, this analysis does
1239   not account for the indirect effects of attention heads, which could also influence model behavior
1240   in subtle ways. The exclusion of these indirect effects represents a key limitation of our current
1241   approach. Future work could explore methods to incorporate or approximate the indirect relationships
       between attention heads, potentially improving the robustness of spurious correlation identification.
