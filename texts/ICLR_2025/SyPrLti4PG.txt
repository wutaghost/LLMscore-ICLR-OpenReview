009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent variable models serve as powerful tools to infer underlying dynamics from
012            observed neural activity. Ideally, the inferred dynamics should align with true
013            ones. However, due to the absence of ground truth data, prediction benchmarks
014            are often employed as proxies. One widely-used method is co-smoothing, which
015            involves jointly estimating latent variables and predicting observations along held-
016            out channels to assess model performance. In this study, we reveal the limitations
017            of the co-smoothing prediction framework and propose a remedy. Utilizing a
018
               student-teacher setup with Hidden Markov Models, we demonstrate that the high
               co-smoothing model space encompasses models with arbitrary extraneous dynam-
019
               ics within their latent representations. To address this, we introduce a secondary
020
               metric— few-shot co-smoothing. This involves performing regression from the
021            latent variables to held-out channels in the data using fewer trials. Our results
022            indicate that among models with near-optimal co-smoothing, those with extrane-
023            ous dynamics underperform in the few-shot co-smoothing compared to ’minimal’
024            models that are devoid of such dynamics. We also provide analytical insights into
025            the origin of this phenomenon. We further validate our findings on real neural data
026            using two state-of-the-art methods: LFADS and STNDT. In the absence of ground
027            truth, we suggest a novel measure to validate our approach. By cross-decoding
028            the latent variables of all model pairs with high co-smoothing, we identify models
029
               with minimal extraneous dynamics. We find a correlation between few-shot co-
               smoothing performance and this new measure. In summary, we present a novel
030
               prediction metric designed to yield latent variables that more accurately reflect the
031
               ground truth, offering a significant improvement for latent dynamics inference.
032            Code available here.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In neuroscience, we often have access to simultaneously recorded neurons during certain behaviors.
038   These observations, denoted X, offer a window onto the actual hidden (or latent) dynamics of the
039   relevant brain circuit, denoted Z (Vyas et al., 2020). Although, in general, these dynamics can be
040   complex and high-dimensional, capturing them in a concrete mathematical model opens doors to
041   reverse-engineering, revealing simpler explanations and insights (Barak, 2017; Sussillo & Barak,
042   2013). Inferring a model of the Z variables, also known as latent variable modeling (LVM), is part
043   of the larger field of system identification with applications in many areas outside of neuroscience,
044
      such as fluid dynamics (Vinuesa & Brunton, 2022) and finance (Bauwens & Veredas, 2004).
045   Because we don’t have ground truth for Z, prediction metrics on held-out parts of x are commonly
046   used as a proxy (Pei et al., 2021). However, it has been noted that prediction and explanation are
047   often distinct endeavors (Shmueli, 2010). For instance, Versteeg et al. (2023) use an example where
048   ground truth is available to show how different models that all achieve good prediction neverthe-
049   less have varied latents that can differ from the ground truth. Such behavior might be expected
050
      when using highly expressive models with large latent spaces. Bad prediction with good latents is
      demonstrated by Koppe et al. (2019) for the case of chaotic dynamics.
051
052   Various regularisation methods on the latents have been suggested to improve the similarity of Z to
053   the ground truth, such as recurrence and priors on external inputs (Pandarinath et al., 2018), low-
      dimensionality of trajectories (Sedler et al., 2022), low-rank connectivity (Valente et al., 2022; Pals


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2024), injectivity constraints from latent to predictions (Versteeg et al., 2023), low-tangling
055   (Perkins et al., 2023), and piecewise-linear dynamics (Koppe et al., 2019). However, the field lacks
056   a quantitative, prediction-based metric that credits the simplicity of the latent representation—an
057   aspect essential for interpretability and ultimately scientific discovery, while still enabling compar-
058   isons across a wide range of LVM architectures.
059
      Here, we characterize the diversity of model latents achieving high co-smoothing, a standard
060
      prediction-based framework for Neural LVMs, and demonstrate potential pitfalls of this framework.
061   We propose a few-shot variant of co-smoothing which, when used in conjunction with co-smoothing,
062   differentiates varying latents. We verify this approach both on synthetic toy problems and state-of-
063   the-art methods on neural data, providing an analytical explanation of why it works in a simple
064   setting.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
068
069
      Our work builds on recent developments in Neural LVMs for the discovery of latent structure in
      noisy neural data on single trials. We refer the reader to Pei et al. (2021) supplementary table 3
070
      for a comprehensive list of Neural LVMs published from 2008-2021. Central to our work is the
071
      co-smoothing procedure, which evaluates models based on the prediction of activity from held-out
072   neurons provided held-in neuron activity from the same trial. Co-smoothing was first introduced in
073   Yu et al. (2008) and Macke et al. (2011) for the validation of GPFA as a Neural LVM.
074
075
      Pei et al. (2021) curated four datasets of neural activity recorded from behaving monkeys and es-
      tablished a framework to evaluate co-smoothing among other prediction-based metrics on several
076
      models in the form of a standardized benchmark and competition.
077
078   In contrast to prediction approaches, a parallel line of work focuses on explaining and validat-
079   ing Neural LVMs on synthetic data, enabling direct comparison with the ground truth (Sedler
080   et al., 2022; Brenner et al., 2022; Durstewitz et al., 2023). Versteeg et al. (2023) validated their
081
      method with both ground truth and neural data, demonstrating high predictive performance with
      low-dimensional latents.
082
083   A concept we introduce is cross-decoding across a population of models to find the most parsi-
084   monious representation. Several works compare representations of large model populations (Mah-
085   eswaranathan et al., 2019; Morcos et al., 2018). They apply Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA),
086   a symmetric measure of representational similarity, whereas we use regression, which is not sym-
087
      metric. The application to Neural LVMs may be novel.
088   One related approach comparing representations in deep neural networks is stitching components of
089   separately trained (and subsequently frozen) models into a composite model using a simple linear
090   layer (Lenc & Vedaldi, 2015; Bansal et al., 2021).
091   Central to our work is the concept of few-shot learning a decoder from a frozen intermediate rep-
092   resentation. Sorscher et al. (2022) developed a theory of geometric properties of representations
093   that enables few-shot generalization to novel classes. They identified the geometric properties that
094   determine a signal-to-noise ratio for classification, which dictates few-shot performance. While this
095   setting differs from ours, links between our works are a topic for future research. To our knowl-
096   edge, the use of few-shot generalization as a means to identify interpretable latent representations,
097   particularly for Neural LVMs, is a novel idea.

146   It is common to assume that being able to predict held-out parts of X will guarantee that the inferred
147   latent aligns with the true one (Macke et al., 2011; Pei et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2018; Meghanath et al.,
148   2023; Keshtkaran et al., 2022; Keeley et al., 2020; Le & Shlizerman, 2022; She & Wu, 2020; Wu
149   et al., 2017; Zhao & Park, 2017; Schimel et al., 2022; Mullen et al., 2024; Gokcen et al., 2022; Yu
150
      et al., 2008; Perkins et al., 2023). To test this assumption, we use a student-teacher scenario where
      we know the ground truth. To compare how two models (u, v) align, we infer the latents of both
151
      from X test , then do a regression from latents of u to v. The regression error is denoted Du→v (i.e.
152
      DT→S for teacher to student decoding). Contrary to the above assumption, we hypothesize that good

154   vice versa (Fig. 1C). It is possible that the inferred latents possess additional features, unexplained
155   by the true latents (high DT→S ).

      5    F EW- SHOT PREDICTION SELECTS BETTER MODELS
254
255
256   Because our objective is to obtain latent models that are close to the ground truth, the co-smoothing
257   prediction scores described above are not satisfactory. Can we devise a new prediction score that
258
      will be correlated with ground truth similarity? The advantage of prediction benchmarks is that they
      can be optimized, and serve as a common language for the community as a whole to produce better
259
      algorithms (Deng et al., 2009).
260
261   We suggest few-shot co-smoothing as a complementary prediction score to co-smoothing, to be
262   used on models with good scores on the latter. Similarly to standard co-smoothing, the functions g
263   and f are trained using all trials of the training data (Fig. 3A). The key difference is that a separate
264
      group of N k-out neurons is set aside, and only k trials of these neurons are used to estimate a mapping
      g ′ : Zt,: 7→ Rt,k-out (Fig. 3B), similar to g in equation 2. The neural LVM (f, g, g ′ ) is then evaluated
265
266
      on both the standard co-smoothing Q using g ◦ f and the few-shot version Qk using g ′ ◦ f (Fig. 3C).
267   This procedure may be repeated several (s) times independently on resampled sets of k trials, giving
268   s estimates of g ′ , each yielding a score Qk for each k-set. For small k, the Qk s tend to be highly
269   variable. Thus we compute and report the average score ⟨QkS ⟩ over the s resamples for each student
      S. Practical advice on how to choose the value of k and s is given in appendix G.

320   good models. The students differ in that the bad student has more than one state corresponding

322   co-smoothing. In the few-shot setting, however, the output of all states needs to be estimated using
323   a limited amount of data. Thus the information from the same amount of observations has to be
      distributed across more states. This data efficiency argument can be made more precise.

492   8   D ISCUSSION
493
494   Latent variable models aim to infer the underlying latents using observations of a target system. We
495   showed that co-smoothing, a common prediction measure of the goodness of such models cannot
496   discriminate between certain classes of latents. In particular, extraneous dynamics can be invisible
497
      to such a measure.
498   We suggest a complementary prediction measure: few-shot co-smoothing. Instead of directly re-
499   gressing from held-in to held-out neurons as is done to evaluate co-smoothing, we distinguish the
500   encoder and the decoder. To evaluate the trained model we substitute the decoder with a new decoder
501   estimated using only a ‘few’ (k) number of trials. The rate predictions provided by the few-shot de-
502   coder are evaluated the same as in standard co-smoothing. Using synthetic datasets and HMM mod-
503
      els, we show numerically and analytically that this measure correlates with the distance of model
      latents to the ground truth.
504
505   We demonstrate the applicability of this measure to real world neural datasets, with SOTA architec-
506   tures. This required developing a new proxy to ground truth – cross decoding. For each pair of SOTA
507   models that we obtained, we performed a linear regression across model latents, provided identical
508   input data. Models with extraneous dynamics showed up as a bad target latent on average, and vice
509
      versa. Finally we show that these two characterisations of extraneous dynamics are correlated. An
      interesting extension would be to use this new metric as another method to select good models.
510
      The computational cost is high, because it requires training a population of models and comparing
511
      between all of them. It is also less universal and standardised than few-shot co-smoothing, as it is
512   dependent on a specific ‘jury’ of models. The HMM results, however, show that it is more correlated
513   to ground truth than the few-shot.
514
515
      While the combination of student-teacher and SOTA results put forth a compelling arguement, we
      address here a few limitations of our work. Firstly, our SOTA results use only one of the datasets in
516
      the benchmark suite (Pei et al., 2021). With regard to the few-shot regression, while the bernoulli
517
      HMM scenario has a closed form solution: the maximum likelihood estimate, the poisson GLM
518   regression for the SOTA models is optimised iteratively and is sensitive to the l2 hyperparameter
519   alpha. In our results we select k and α that distinguish models in our candidate model sets giving
520   moderate/high few-shot scores for some models and low scores to others. This is an empirical choice
521   that must be made for each dataset and model-set. The few-shot training of g ′ is computationally
522   inexpensive and may be thus can evaluated over a range of values to find the ideal ones.
523
      Overall, our work advances latent dynamics inference in general and prediction frameworks in par-
524   ticular. By exposing a failure mode of standard prediction metrics, we can guide the design of
525   inference algorithms that take this into account. Furthermore, the few-shot prediction can be incor-
526   porated into existing benchmarks and help guide the community to build models that are closer to
527   the desired goal of uncovering latent dynamics in the brain.
