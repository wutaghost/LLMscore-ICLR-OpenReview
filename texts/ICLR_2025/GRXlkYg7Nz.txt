009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The sparsity of team rewards poses a significant challenge that hinders the ef-
012            fective learning of optimal team policies in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement
013            learning. One common approach to mitigate this issue involves augmenting sparse
014            rewards with individual rewards to guide policy training. However, a significant
015            drawback of such approaches is that modifying the reward function can potentially
016            alter the optimal policy. To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel multi-agent
017
               policy optimization approach that ensures consistency between the mixed policy
               (learned from a combination of individual and team rewards) and the team pol-
018
               icy (based solely on team rewards), through a new policy consistency constraint
019
               that aligns the returns of both policies in policy optimization model. We fur-
020            ther develop an iterated policy optimization procedure to solve the formulated
021            problem, deriving an approximate optimization objective for each iteration of the
022            mixed and team policies. Experimental evaluation conducted in the StarCraft II
023            Multi-Agent Challenge Environment (SMAC), Multi-Agent Particle Environment
024            (MPE), and Google Research Football (GRF) environments demonstrate that our
025            proposed approach effectively addresses the policy inconsistency problem, i.e., it
026            evenly outperforms strong baseline methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has attracted significant interest due to its
031
      potential in solving complex decision-making tasks (Yan & Xu, 2020; Chen et al., 2024). Despite
032
      advancements in MARL algorithms, the issue of sparse team rewards remains a major obstacle, lim-
033   iting the practical application of these algorithms in real-world scenarios such as power grids (Titta-
034   ferrante & Yassine, 2021), aerial vehicles (Du et al., 2021), and robotics (Sun et al., 2020).
035
036
      Previous research addressing the issue of sparse rewards commonly relies on additional individual
      dense rewards. These approaches can be categorized into three main types: utilizing expert knowl-
037
      edge (Kurach et al., 2020; Lowe et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2022; Zhu & Zhao, 2021), exploring
038
      the state space (Strehl & Littman, 2008; Bellemare et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2023; Jeon et al., 2022;
039   Liu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2024), and action exploration (Li et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023a). While
040   incorporating individual rewards have shown promise in addressing sparse rewards, recent studies
041   highlight a critical issue: learned policies may deviate from optimal policies due to modifications
042   in the reward function, especially in multi-agent environments (Wang et al., 2022). For instance, in
043   a cooperative battle simulation, agents incentivized by individual rewards may prioritize individual
044   skills (such as shooting or escaping) over the collective goal of winning the battle.
045   The motivation of the our research is illustrated in Figure 1. While team rewards are expected to
046   guide agents towards the optimal policy (orange dashed line), the sparsity of the reward function
047   often hinders the policy learning process (orange solid line). Shaping mixed rewards (through a
048   combination of team and individual rewards) can facilitate more efficient policy learning but may
049   lead to suboptimal policies due to alterations in the reward function (green line). This highlights
050   the need to maintain consistency between the learned policy and the optimal team policy when
051   incorporating mixed rewards.
052   While IRAT (Wang et al., 2022) mitigates the policy inconsistency issue through improving policy
053   similarity between learned and team policies, our approach completely eliminates this issue by deriv-
      ing exact policy objectives from a constrained Lagrangian dual optimization model. By maximizing

087   2    R ELATED WORK
088
089
      Individual Rewards in MARL: Introducing individual rewards has become one of the most preva-
090   lent and effective strategies to mitigate the sparse reward issue in MARL. Existing research in this
091   area can be broadly categorized into three groups: expert knowledge-based, state space exploration-
092   based, and action space exploration-based approaches.
093
      External expert knowledge plays a crucial role in formulating individual rewards by leveraging prior
094
      understanding of environmental dynamics (Kurach et al., 2020; Lowe et al., 2017; Huang et al.,
095
      2022; Zhu & Zhao, 2021). For example, a simple design rewards the elimination of enemies and
096   the health of teammates in SMAC (Samvelyan et al., 2019). Further, MAPPER algorithm utilizes
097   expert knowledge to decompose tasks and construct sub-tasks with dense rewards (Liu et al., 2020).
098   However, relying solely on external knowledge can be impractical, as obtaining such knowledge is
099   prohibitively expensive in real-world environments (Zhang et al., 2021a; Ryu et al., 2022).
100
      To address the aforementioned challenge, some works incorporate individual rewards based on ac-
101
      quired transactional information. One straightforward approach is to explore novel states by count-
102   ing visited states (Strehl & Littman, 2008; Bellemare et al., 2016). However, this approach faces
103   difficulties in complex environments with vast state spaces. Recently, methods such as EDTI/EITI
104   have been developed to promote the exploration of novel states that significantly influence agents’
105   actions (Wang et al., 2019). LAIES partitions the state space into internal and external states, con-
106   structing individual rewards to promote exploration of external states (Liu et al., 2023). MASER
107   formulates individual rewards based on the distance between the current state and a target state
      chosen by the Q-value of visited states and actions (Jeon et al., 2022). Furthermore, DIFFER de-


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      composes team experience into individual experience for constructing individual rewards (Hu et al.,
109   2023). Additionally, Liu et al. (2021) and Xu et al. (2024; 2023b) exploit prior structural knowledge
110   to encourage agents to explore subsets of the state space.
111
112
      In contrast to state exploration, another line of research focuses on constructing individual rewards
      by exploring the action space. One example is the CDS approach (Li et al., 2021), which aims
113
      to maximize the mutual information between agent identities and trajectories, thereby encouraging
114
      more diverse actions. Xu et al. (2023a) introduce individual rewards based on the concept of joint
115   policy diversity, which quantifies the disparity between the current policy and previous policies.
116
117   Policy consistency in RL: While previous studies have shown promising results in addressing re-
118
      ward sparsity, the policy inconsistency issue (resulting from the introduction of individual rewards)
      is often overlooked. To the best of our knowledge, there are two works focusing on this inconsis-
119
      tency issue. The first work proposes a constrained policy optimization method within a single-agent
120
      environment (Chen et al., 2022). This method iteratively updates the extrinsic policy and the actual
121   policy using lower bounds of dual objectives as optimization objectives. In contrast, our approach
122   leverages transformed dual optimization objectives to train policies directly, thereby avoiding the
123   bias introduced by lower bounds of the objective function.
124
      The work closest to ours is IRAT (Wang et al., 2022). While IRAT focuses on reshaping the opti-
125
      mization objective to enhance the policy similarity between individual and team policies, Figure 4
126
      in Appendix A shows that merely maximizing policy similarity does not necessarily lead the learned
127   policy to converge towards an optimal team policy. Besides, the oversight of mixed rewards prevents
128   IRAT from achieving a balance between individual skill execution and collaborative team objec-
129   tives. This work therefore derives the optimization objective precisely by solving the Lagrangian
130   dual optimization problem under a policy consistency constraint, which ensures the equivalence of
131   cumulative team rewards obtained by the learned mixed policy and the optimal team policy.

      4.3     I MPLEMENTATION
339
      CMT is implemented within the CTDE framework, with MAPPO algorithm as the backbone. The
340                                                                 i
      implementation involves two types of policies: mixed policy πE+i   and team policy πEi
                                                                                             . Each policy
341   is parameterized by separate networks. During policy execution phase, each agent utilizes the actor
342   network of mixed policy to interact with environment, while the policy information for mixed policy
343   and team policy are stored into data buffer Dm and Dt , respectively.
344
      When training, CMT algorithm iteratively updates the policies and the Lagrange multiplier λ. In
345                                                  i                                  i
      each iteration, it optimizes the mixed policy πE+i while keeping the team policy πE fixed. It then
346                                i
      updates the team policy πE based on the optimized mixed policies of all agents. The pseudo-
347   code of CMT algorithm is summarized in Algorithm 1. For more details about the CMT algorithm
348   implementation, please refer to Appendix C.

442
443   In the MPE environment, we compare our approach with IRAT, MAPPO, QMIX, and MAPPO
444   (Sparse) across three types of environments: Spread, Attack, and Predator-Prey.
445
      In the Attack environment, three agents collaborate to reach and attack a single landmark, earning
446
      a positive reward 20 upon successful completion. Each agent incurs a penalty based on their distance
447
      to the landmark, and a penalty −1 when colliding with other agents. As shown in the first column
448   of Figure 3, CMT outperforms all other algorithms, achieving a team reward exceeding 20. IRAT
449   ranks second with a team reward around 7, while MAPPO, QMIX, and MAPPO (Sparse) perform
450   worse, with team rewards below 5. These results indicate that algorithms assisted by individual
451   rewards tend to overlook policy inconsistency, which adversely affects their performance. Although
452   IRAT partially addresses this issue, it fails to achieve consistency between the mixed policy learned
453   from mixed rewards and the team policy derived from team rewards. In contrast, CMT successfully
454   establishes this consistency.
455   In the Spread environment, four agents collaborate to locate two landmarks, receiving a sparse
456   positive reward 10 when multiple agents simultaneously discover a landmark. Additionally, each
457   agent earns an individual reward based on its minimum distance to undiscovered landmarks. Ex-
458   perimental results are presented in the second column of Figure 3. These results demonstrate that
459   CMT once again achieves the highest team rewards, around 10. IRAT and MAPPO follow closely
460   with a team reward of 8. MAPPO (Sparse) and QMIX perform the worst among all algorithms.
461   These findings further show that CMT can identify the sub-optimal policy trap caused by additional
462
      individual rewards.
463   In the Predator-Prey environment, five predators work together to capture two prey, receiving a
464   sparse positive reward 20 when multiple agents successfully capture the same prey. Each agent
465   also earns an individual reward 5 when it successfully hits a prey. The results in the Predator-
466   Prey environment differ slightly from those in the previous two environments. As shown in the
467   third column of Figure 3, the CMT and IRAT algorithms perform similarly, with CMT achieving
468
      a slightly higher final team reward. This is because in the Predator-Prey environment, individual
      rewards play a more significant role than in the other two environments. The IRAT algorithm focuses
469
      on improving policy performance by fully utilizing individual rewards, whereas the CMT algorithm
470
      also considers the consistency between the team policy and the mixed policy.

      5.4     A BLATION STUDIES
494
495
      It is noteworthy that CMT incorporates three crucial design elements: the extended TD error, policy
496
      approximation based on the similarity assumption, and optimization objective reconstruction with
497   KL divergence. To assess the impact of all components on CMT’s performance, we conduct an
498   ablation study in MPE environments. We conduct three ablation studies: i) CMT-TD(w) as CMT
499   with the extended TD error replaced by a standard TD error (defined as Eq. 15) used by MAPPO
500   and IRAT for policy optimization; ii) CMT-SP(w) is CMT with the policy approximation removed
501   by initially using distinct parameters between mixed and team policy networks; iii) CMT-KL(w)
502   represents CMT without the KL terms-based reconstruction module.
503   Table 3 demonstrates that the inclusion of the extended TD error exerts the most significant impact,
504   followed by the KL divergence-based optimization objective reconstruction model, with the policy
505   approximation technique showing the least influence on algorithm performance. This finding aligns
506   our core innovation outlined in Section 1. The extended TD error, which originates from resolving
507   the policy consistency constraint in the Lagrangian dual problem and the incorporation of mixed
508   rewards, notably contribute to CMT’s enhanced performance. Moreover, the minimal disparity be-
509   tween the outcomes of CMT-SP(w) and CMT suggests that eliminating the similarity assumption
510   does not dramatically affect algorithm performance.
511   The influence of scaling individual rewards on the algorithm’s performance is also investigated. In
512   Table 3, CMT(3×) denotes the CMT developed with individual rewards amplified three times, while
513   CMT(5×) indicates the CMT developed with individual rewards amplified five times. Comparing
514   CMT(3×) and CMT(5×) with baseline CMT across three scenarios, we observe that the perfor-
515   mance variation remains within 30%, indicating CMT’s robustness to individual reward scaling.
516   Finally, we examine CMT’s robustness to reward design by testing with random individual rewards
517   sampled from [−1, 1] (denoted as CMT(RD) in Table 3). The results show that with inappropriate
518   individual rewards, CMT(RD) performs similarly to MAPPO(Sparse), both showing limited effec-
519   tiveness. This indicates that even with poorly designed individual rewards, our approach maintains
520   a performance floor equivalent to methods without additional rewards. Additional ablation studies
521   investigating the impact of the initial Lagrangian multiplier λ on CMT’s performance can be found
522   in Appendix D.3.

525   6     C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
526
527
      In this paper, we focus on addressing the challenge of deviation in optimal policies in MARL due
528   to the introduction of individual rewards. To tackle this problem, we propose a novel multi-agent
529   constrained policy optimization procedure, which maximizes the cumulative rewards while ensur-
530   ing the consistency between the team policy and the mixed policy learned from the sum of team and
531   individual rewards. Leveraging the min-max dual objective presented in the constrained policy opti-
532   mization model, our approach iteratively updates the mixed and the team policies under the proposed
533   policy consistency constraint. Experimental results validate that CMT successfully overcomes the
534   policy inconsistency issue, gaining superior performance across 27 tasks on MPE, SMAC, and GRF
535   environments, compared to SOTA MARL algorithms.
536   One limitation of the proposed approach lies that we utilize the similarity assumption when simpli-
537   fying the optimization objectives. Although we can develop policy networks with the same parame-
538   ters to make this assumption hold during implementation, there may still be divergence between the
539   mixed and team policies during the policy learning process. Relaxing this assumption leads to an
      interesting direction that is worth further exploration in future work.
