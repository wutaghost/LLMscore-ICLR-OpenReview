009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) demonstrates significant progress
012            in solving cooperative and competitive multi-agent problems in various environ-
013            ments. One of the principal challenges in MARL is the need for explicit prediction
014            of the agents’ behavior to achieve cooperation. To resolve this issue, we pro-
015            pose the Shared Recurrent Memory Transformer (SRMT) which extends memory
016            transformers to multi-agent settings by pooling and globally broadcasting individ-
017            ual working memories, enabling agents to exchange information implicitly and
018            coordinate their actions. We evaluate SRMT on the Partially Observable Multi-
019
               Agent Pathfinding problem in a toy Bottleneck navigation task that requires agents
               to pass through a narrow corridor and on a POGEMA benchmark set of tasks. In
020
               the Bottleneck task, SRMT consistently outperforms a variety of reinforcement
021
               learning baselines, especially under sparse rewards, and generalizes effectively
022            to longer corridors than those seen during training. On POGEMA maps, includ-
023            ing Mazes, Random, and MovingAI, SRMT is competitive with recent MARL,
024            hybrid, and planning-based algorithms. These results suggest that incorporating
025            shared recurrent memory into the transformer-based architectures can enhance co-
026            ordination in decentralized multi-agent systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Multi-agent systems have significant potential to solve complex problems through distributed intel-
032   ligence and collaboration. However, coordinating the interactions between multiple agents remains
033   challenging, often requiring sophisticated communication protocols and decision-making mecha-
034   nisms. We propose a novel approach to address this challenge by introducing a shared memory as
035   a global workspace for agents to coordinate behavior. The global workspace theory (Baars, 1988)
036   suggests that in the brain, there are independent functional modules that can cooperate by broad-
037   casting information through a global workspace. Inspired by this theory, we consider the agents
038   in Multi-Agent Pathfinding (MAPF) as independent modules with shared memory and propose a
039
      Shared Recurrent Memory Transformer (SRMT) as a mechanism for exchanging information to im-
      prove coordination and avoid deadlocks. SRMT extends memory transformers (Burtsev et al., 2020;
040
      Bulatov et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Cherepanov et al., 2024) to multi-agent settings by pooling
041
      and globally broadcasting individual working memories.
042
043   In this study, we test the shared recurrent memory approach on Partially Observable Multi-agent
044   Pathfinding (PO-MAPF) (Stern et al., 2019), where each agent aims to reach its goal while observing
045
      the state of the environment, including locations and actions of the other agents and static obstacles,
      only locally. Multi-agent pathfinding can be considered in the decentralized manner, where each
046
      agent independently collects rewards and makes decisions on its actions. There are many attempts
047
      to solve this problem: in robotics (Van den Berg et al., 2008; Zhu et al., 2022), in machine and
048   reinforcement learning field (Damani et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2023; Sartoretti
049   et al., 2019; Riviere et al., 2020). Such methods often involve manual reward-shaping and external
050   demonstrations. Also, several works utilize the communication between agents to solve decentral-
051   ized MAPF (Ma et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). However, the resulting solutions
052   are prone to deadlocks and poorly generalizable to the maps not used for training. In this work, we
053   compare SRMT to a range of reinforcement learning baselines and show that it consistently out-
      performs them in the Bottleneck navigation task. Tests on POGEMA benchmark Skrynnik et al.

      (2024a) show that SRMT is competitive with numerous recent MARL, hybrid, and planning-based

060   2.1   S HARED MEMORY AND COMMUNICATION IN MULTI - AGENT REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
061
062   Shared memory is designed to help agents coordinate their behavior, and it is closely related to ex-
063
      isting approaches in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), particularly those involving inter-
      agent communication. Compared to a single-agent reinforcement learning, providing agents with
064
      communication protocol presents a significant challenge in MARL (Foerster et al., 2016; Iqbal &
065
      Sha, 2018; Zhang et al., 2018). Common strategies to address this problem include (1) a centralized
066   setting, where a central controller aggregates information from all agents; (2) a fully decentralized
067   setting, where agents make decisions based solely on local observations; and (3) a decentralized
068   setting with networked agents, allowing agents to share local information with each other (Zhang
069   et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023; Nayak et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2019).
070
      In multi-agent pathfinding (MAPF) with reinforcement learning, various methods fit within these
071
      three categories. Decentralized methods without communication include approaches such as
072   IQL (Tan, 1993), VDN (Sunehag et al., 2018), QMIX (Rashid et al., 2020), QPLEX (Wang et al.,
073   2021), Follower (Skrynnik et al., 2024b), and MATS-LP (Skrynnik et al., 2024c). These meth-
074   ods propose to implement the agent decision-making based only on local information. Notably,
075   VDN, QMIX, and QPLEX adopt centralized training with a joint Q-function but operate in a de-
076   centralized manner during execution with individual Q-functions. In contrast, centralized methods
077   such as LaCAM (Okumura, 2023) and RHCR (Li et al., 2021) employ a centralized search-based
078   planner. Finally, decentralized methods with communication, such as DCC (Ma et al., 2021b),
079   MAMBA (Egorov & Shpilman, 2022), and SCRIMP (Wang et al., 2023), allow agents to share
080
      information to enhance coordination and avoid collisions. These methods utilize various commu-
      nication strategies, including selective information sharing (DCC), discrete communication pro-
081
      tocols (MAMBA), and scalable communication mechanisms based on transformer architectures
082
      (SCRIMP), all aimed at improving agent cooperation in complex and dynamic environments.
083
084   MAMBA (Egorov & Shpilman, 2022) is a pure MARL approach that uses communication and
085   centralized training within a Model-Based Reinforcement Learning framework, featuring discrete
086
      communication and decentralized execution. A 3-layer transformer serves as the communication
      block with its outputs used by the agents to update their world models and make action predic-
087
      tions. Each agent maintains its own version of the world model, which can be updated through the
088
      communication block.
089
090   QPLEX (Wang et al., 2021) is a pure multi-agent reinforcement learning method that employs
091   multi-agent Q-learning with centralized end-to-end training, providing inter-agent communication.
092
      QPLEX learns to factorize a joint action-value function to enable decentralized execution. The
      approach uses a duplex dueling mechanism that connects local and joint action-value functions,
093
      allowing agents to make independent decisions while benefiting from centralized training.
094
095   Follower (Skrynnik et al., 2024b) is a learnable method for lifelong MAPF without communication
096   that uses a centralized path planning with a modified A* heuristic search to reduce agents’ colli-
097   sions. MATS-LP (Skrynnik et al., 2024c) is also a learnable method for lifelong MAPF without
098
      communication that uses a learnable policy combined with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to
      reason about possible future states.
099
100   RHCR (Li et al., 2021) is a purely search-based centralized planner that does not require training.
101   It decomposes the lifelong MAPF into a sequence of windowed MAPF instances, with re-planning
102   occurring according to a predetermined schedule, resolving collisions within the current planning
103   window only.
104   The proposed shared recurrent memory approach fits into a decentralized setting with networked
105   agents. Unlike other methods, SRMT allows agents to communicate indirectly through a shared
106   memory. Each agent uses a recurrently updated memory and learns to read and write its individ-
107   ual memory representations to a shared space. This allows agents to retain information over time
      steps in the episode and enables the effective individual and collective decision-making process in


                                                       2

108
      pathfinding tasks. Furthermore, unlike approaches that rely on a joint Q-function for centralized
109   training, our method maintains decentralization throughout training and execution. The fully de-
110   centralized setting of the multi-agent system might be preferable over the centralized one in many
111   real-world applications.

      2.2   S HARED MEMORY AND MEMORY TRANSFORMERS
114
115
      SRMT extends the memory transformers (Burtsev et al., 2020; Bulatov et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022;
116   Cherepanov et al., 2024) to multi-agent settings by pooling and globally broadcasting individual
117   memories of each agent.
118
119
      Memory Transformer (Burtsev et al., 2020) augments the standard Transformer architec-
      ture (Vaswani et al., 2017) by introducing special memory tokens appended to the input sequence,
120
      providing the additional operational space for the model. These memory tokens are trainable and
121
      are used by the model as working memory. In the Recurrent Memory Transformer (RMT) (Bulatov
122   et al., 2022), memory tokens transfer information between segments of a long input sequence. In
123   this case, multiple memory tokens act as a recurrent state, effectively turning the transformer into a
124   recurrent cell that processes each segment as input at each time step.
125
      Agent Transformer Memory (ATM) (Yang et al., 2022) introduces a transformer-based working
126
      memory into multi-agent reinforcement learning. Each ATM agent maintains a memory buffer of
127
      the last N memory states, sequentially updated by a transformer network. This approach is similar
128   to the RMT, but instead of using only the latest memory state, ATM uses the several most recent
129   memory states. Additionally, each memory state in ATM consists of a single vector, whereas in
130   RMT, multiple memory vectors work together as a recurrent hidden state.
131
      Recurrent Action Transformer with Memory (RATE) (Cherepanov et al., 2024) extends the De-
132
      cision Transformer (Chen et al., 2021) by incorporating memory tokens and a dedicated memory
133
      update module, the Memory Retention Valve, which updates memory states to effectively handle
134   long segmented trajectories.
135
136   Relational Recurrent Neural Network (RRNN) (Santoro et al., 2018) utilizes the multi-head dot
137
      product attention to update the memory state given the new input data. Then it employs the modifi-
      cation of a standard LSTM (Hochreiter, 1997) treating the memory matrix as a matrix of recurrent
138
      cell states to predict the model outputs.
139
140   Approaches such as ATM, RATE, and RRNN are focused on maintaining individual memory states
141   for each agent. In contrast, SRMT extends recurrent memory to multi-agent RL and facilitates
142   the shared access to individual agents’ memories, enabling coordination and joint decision-making
143
      among all agents in the environment.

      3     S HARED R ECURRENT M EMORY T RANSFORMER
146
147
      Multi-agent pathfinding task is set as follows. The agents interact in the two-dimensional environ-
148
      ment represented as graph G = (V, E) with the vertices corresponding to the locations and the
149
      edges to the transitions between these locations. The timeline consists of discrete time steps. The
150   predefined final step of the agent interaction episode is called the episode length. At the beginning
151   of the episode, each agent i is given a start location si ∈ V and a goal location gi ∈ V to be reached
152   until the end of the episode. At each time step, an agent performs an action to stay in its current
153   location or move to an adjacent one. The task of multi-agent pathfinding is to make each agent reach
154   its goal until the end of the episode without colliding with the other agents.
155
      In this study, we work with a decentralized Partially Observable Multi-agent Pathfinding (PO-
156   MAPF) (Stern et al., 2019). Decentralization of MAPF means that the multi-agent system has
157   no global controller, each agent performs actions and collects rewards independently of the oth-
158   ers. Also, each agent observes the environment obstacles, other agents, and their goals only locally,
159   from a squared window of fixed size centered at the agent’s current location. Formally, the partially
160   observable multi-agent Markov decision process M is defined (Bernstein et al., 2002):

      We approximate the policy with a deep neural network depicted in Figure 1. The policy consists of
      the spatial encoder to process agent observation and the actor-critic network. The agents are assumed
205
      to be homogeneous, so the policy is shared between agents during training. The spatial encoder uses
206
      ResNet (He et al., 2016) with an additional Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) in the output layer. The
207   Action Decoder and the Critic Head are Dense layers. The policy neural network is presented in
208   Figure 1. We adopt the Skrynnik et al. (2024b) approach for configuring the policy-approximating
209   model and input-output data pipelines. The model input is the agent’s local observation tensor of
210   shape 3 × m × m tensor, where m is the observation range. The channels of the tensor encode the
211   static obstacle locations combined with the current path, the other agents, and their targets.
212
      SRMT is used as a core subnetwork of the actor-critic model. As the main mechanism in SRMT, we
213   consider the attention block implemented in the Huggingface GPT-21 model. In SRMT, the input
214   sequence for each agent at each time step is constructed by combining three key components: the

      4.1       C LASSICAL MAPF ON B OTTLENECKS
229
230   We use the POGEMA (Skrynnik et al., 2024a) framework for our experiments. In POGEMA the
231   two-dimensional environment is represented as a grid composed of obstacles and free cells. At each
232   time step each agent can perform two types of actions: moving to an adjacent cell or remaining at
233   their current position. Agents have limited observational capabilities, perceiving other agents only
234   within a local R × R area centered on their current position. The episode ends when the predefined
235   time step, episode length, is reached. The episode can also end before this time step if certain
236   conditions are met, i.e. all agents reach their goal locations.
237   As an initial test of our approach, we selected a simple two-agent coordination task where the agents
238   must navigate through a narrow passage. The environment consists of two rooms connected by a
239   one-cell-wide corridor, as illustrated in Fig. 2a. Each agent has a 5 × 5 field of view and starts in
240   one room, and their goal is located in the opposite room, requiring both agents to pass through the
241   narrow corridor to complete the task. To train the model, we utilized 16 maps with corridor lengths
242   uniformly selected between 3 and 30 cells. To evaluate the performance, we utilize three metrics:
243
244
                 • Cooperative Success Rate (CSR) – a binary measure indicating whether all agents reached
                   their goals before the episode’s end;
245
246              • Individual Success Rate (ISR) – the fraction of the agents that achieved their goals during
247                the episode;
248              • Sum-of-Costs (SoC) – the total number of time steps taken by all agents to reach their
249                respective goals (the lower the value the better).
250
251   The policy function is approximated with a deep neural network (see Fig. 1, left). To benchmark the
252   effectiveness of SRMT, we compared it against MAMBA, QPLEX, ATM, RATE, and RRNN mod-
253   els. As additional baselines that also serve as ablations of SRMT, we evaluate the following core
254   subnetwork architectures: the recurrent memory transformer that allows agents to process individ-
255
      ual memory representations without sharing them (RMT), the memoryless transformer (Attention),
      the empty core that uses the direct connection of spatial encoder to the actor-critic action decoder
256
      (Empty), and GRU RNN (RNN) to assess the difference between the attention-based and RNN-
257
      based observation processing. Additional details regarding the training procedure can be found in
258   Appendix A.1. For the Bottleneck task, we apply no advanced heuristics or methods for path plan-
259   ning to test the impact of memory addition. Our path-planning strategy is simple: each agent aims
260   to follow the shortest path to the goal at each time step. If according to the planned movements, the
261   agents may collide, their final decision will be to retain their current positions until the next step.
262   We hypothesize that a shared memory mechanism will help to solve such bottleneck problems.
263
      We first evaluated SRMT against the baseline models using three variations of the reward function:
264   Directional, Moving Negative, and Sparse.2 In the Directional setting, the agent was rewarded for
265   reaching the goal and for every step that brought it closer to the goal. In the Moving Negative
266   strategy movements are slightly penalized to minimize the path to the goal but no information about
267   goal location is provided. In contrast, the Sparse reward function only gives a reward when the agent
268   successfully achieves the goal cell, offering no intermediate rewards.

      4.2                        L IFELONG MAPF

380   Lifelong multi-agent pathfinding (LMAPF) is an extension of MAPF, where agents receive new
381   destinations upon completing their current goals. The main quality metric for this task setting is
382   the average throughput calculated as the average number of goals reached by all agents per episode
383   step.

385   65 × 65 following the (Skrynnik et al., 2024b) training procedure. The same architecture as depicted
386   in Fig. 1 is used for the policy approximation model with a larger number of layers compared to
387   the classical MAPF on bottleneck environments. The detailed listing of hyperparameters can be
388   found in the Appendix A.1 Table 1. We consider the following reward strategy for lifelong SRMT
389   experiments: if the agent follows the planned path to the goal, it receives a small positive reward
390   r = 0.01. Otherwise r = 0. To define the path from the current location to the goal, we consider
391   the A* shortest path algorithm and more advanced Heuristic Path Decider method presented in the
392
      Follower (Skrynnik et al., 2024b). A* plans the shortest individual path to the goal, while heuristic
      search finds evenly dispersed paths to alleviate the congestion problems in environments with large
393
      populations of agents.

      FlwrPlan) surpasses that of MAMBA, MATS-LP, QPLEX, and even the centralized planning method
433   RHCR. This indicates that integrating heuristic planning into the SRMT framework enhances per-
434   formance in highly congested settings.

436
      To further evaluate the performance of SRMT, we measured its effectiveness using high-level MAPF
      metrics from the POGEMA benchmark (Skrynnik et al., 2024a). These metrics provide an assess-
437
      ment of different aspects of multi-agent coordination and scalability:
438
439         • Performance shows the relation of the average throughput of each method to the best value
440           among the considered methods. The metric is averaged over the results obtained on the
441           Random (20 × 20 grids with randomly placed obstacles) and Mazes (maze-like grids of
442           size 21 × 21) sets of maps
443         • Pathfinding is a binary value that demonstrates if the path of a single agent on a large map
444           is optimal. To measure it, the methods were evaluated on 256 × 256 city maps from the
445           MovingAI (Stern et al., 2019) benchmark.
446
            • Congestion is calculated as the average density of the agents present in the observations of
447
              each agent compared to the overall density of the agents on the Warehouse map.
448
449
            • Cooperation metric is dedicated to the ability of the method to resolve complex situations.
              It is obtained as the relative average throughput with respect to the best-performing classical
450
              LMAPF solvers measured on a small set of hand-crafted 5 × 5 Puzzles maps. The maps
451
              were constructed to contain difficult patterns requiring agents’ cooperation.
452
453         • Out-of-Distribution demonstrates the Performance metric values on the maps unseen during
454
              training. For evaluation, the MovingAI-tiles 64×64 patches of MovingAI maps were used.
455         • Scalability describes how the runtime of each method changes with the growing number
456           of agents on the Warehouse map. The metric is calculated as the ratio between the relative
457           runtimes and relative numbers of agents.
458
      Figure 6 presents the results of a comparison of SRMT and the other methods across these key
459
      metrics. The bar chart illustrates that SRMT and its variants demonstrate competitive performance,
460
      particularly in Scalability and Pathfinding. When integrated with the Follower planning, SRMT
461   performs best in Congestion management. The centralized planning method RHCR leads in several
462   metrics, notably Cooperation, Out-of-distribution, Performance, and Pathfinding, reaching nearly
463   100% in these categories, but is performing worse in Scalability. MAMBA shows strong perfor-
464   mance in Congestion management and Scalability.
465
      In terms of Performance, SRMT achieves high average throughput on Random, Mazes, and Movin-
466
      gAI environments, indicating effective goal-reaching behavior in diverse settings. For the Pathfind-
467   ing metric, SRMT and its variants perform exceptionally well, suggesting that the agents find near-
468   optimal paths even on large-scale maps.
469
470
      Regarding Congestion, MAMBA slightly outperforms SRMT, showcasing better handling of high-
      density agent scenarios on the Warehouse map. However, SRMT maintains competitive perfor-
471
      mance, demonstrating its ability to navigate congested areas effectively. Furthermore, SRMT per-
472
      forms best in Congestion management when integrated with Follower planning.
473
474   The Cooperation metric highlights the centralized method RHCR’s superiority in resolving complex
475   situations requiring intricate coordination. While SRMT does not reach RHCR’s level in this aspect,
476
      it outperforms the other decentralized methods except MATS-LP with MCTS planning, indicating
      reasonable cooperative capabilities without centralized control.
477
478   For Out-of-Distribution generalization, SRMT variations exhibit strong performance, maintaining
479   high throughput on unseen maps. This emphasizes SRMT’s robustness and ability to generalize
480   beyond the training environments.
481   Our experiments demonstrate that incorporating shared memory into transformer-based architec-
482   tures significantly enhances coordination in decentralized multi-agent systems. SRMT effectively
483   enables agents to implicitly share information, enabling better decision-making and conflict avoid-
484   ance. The results indicate that SRMT not only outperforms traditional MARL baselines in complex
485   environments but also generalizes well to unseen maps and scales efficiently with an increasing
      number of agents.

521
522   In this paper, we introduced a novel Shared Recurrent Memory Transformer (SRMT) architecture for
523   enhancing coordination in multi-agent systems. SRMT enables agents to exchange information im-
524   plicitly and coordinate actions without explicit communication protocols. Our experimental results
525   on the bottleneck navigation task demonstrate that SRMT consistently outperforms baseline models,
526   especially in challenging scenarios with sparse rewards and extended corridor lengths. The shared
527   memory mechanism allows agents to generalize their learned policies to environments with signifi-
528   cantly longer corridors than those seen during training, demonstrating the scalability and robustness
529
      of our approach. On POGEMA maps including Mazes, Random, Moving-AI, and Warehouse SRMT
      is competitive with various recent MARL, hybrid, and planning-based algorithms. These findings
530
      highlight the potential of incorporating shared memory structures in transformer-based architectures
531
      for multi-agent reinforcement learning.

534   L IMITATIONS
535
536   As in the majority of research related to Multi-Agent Pathfinding (MAPF), in this work, we as-
537   sume that the agents have flawless localization and mapping abilities. Our primary focus is on the
538   decision-making aspect of the problem. We also consider that the agents execute actions accurately
539   and that their moves are synchronized. Additionally, we treat obstacles as fixed elements of the
      environment.
