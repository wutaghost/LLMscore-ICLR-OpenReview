011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited exceptional performance across
014            a broad range of tasks and domains. However, they still encounter difficulties in
015
               solving mathematical problems due to the rigorous and logical nature of mathe-
               matics. Previous studies have employed techniques such as supervised fine-tuning
016
               (SFT), prompt engineering, and search-based methods to improve the mathemati-
017
               cal problem-solving abilities of LLMs. Despite these efforts, their performance
018            remains suboptimal and demands substantial computational resources. To ad-
019            dress this issue, we propose a novel approach, BEATS, to enhance mathemati-
020            cal problem-solving abilities. Our method leverages newly designed prompts that
021            guide the model to iteratively rewrite, advance by one step, and generate answers
022            based on previous steps. Additionally, we employ a pruning tree search to opti-
023            mize search time while achieving strong performance. Furthermore, we introduce
024            a new back-verification technique that uses LLMs to validate the correctness of
025            the generated answers. Notably, our method improves Qwen2-7b-Instruct’s score
026
               from 36.94 to 61.52 (outperforming GPT-4’s 42.5) on the MATH benchmark.
               The code is made available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   LLMs have demonstrated exceptional performance across diverse tasks and domains (Touvron et al.,
033   2023; meta llama, 2024; Bai et al., 2023a), excelling in zero-shot and few-shot scenarios. Recent
034   advancements in scaling laws and fine-tuning have further enhanced their capabilities, enabling their
035   application in complex real-world tasks such as natural language understanding and multimodal
036   processing.
037   Among the various capabilities of LLMs, mathematical proficiency is crucial, as it reflects not only
038   logical reasoning but also the model’s capacity for structured problem-solving. Mastery of mathe-
039   matical tasks necessitates precision, adherence to complex rules, and the application of algorithms,
040   all of which are essential indicators of an LLM’s overall reasoning and cognitive abilities. There
041   are generally two approaches to enhance mathematical capability. The first set of methods trains
042   LLMs to improve their mathematical skills. Models such as Mammoth (Yue et al., 2023; 2024) and
043
      Internlm-math (Ying et al., 2024), along with DeepSeek (Shao et al., 2024), utilize vast amounts
      of data to develop robust mathematical models. The second set of methods employs tree search
044
      and self-correction techniques to enhance mathematical abilities. Techniques like ToT (Yao et al.,
045
      2024), RAP (Hao et al., 2023), ReST-MCTS* (Zhang et al., 2024), and LiteSearch (Wang et al.,
046   2024) leverage tree structures and search methods such as BFS, DFS and Monte Carlo Tree Search
047   (MCTS). However, both approaches still encounter suboptimal results. They face the following
048   challenges:
049
050   Suboptimal Prompts Self-improving models (Yao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) typically ad-
051   dress problems by either decomposing them into subproblems or rewriting them, followed by solv-
052   ing through methods CoT or Process of Thought (PoT). However, they tend to overlook the issue
053   of ambiguous problem statements. As illustrated by the root node in Figure 1(a), vague expressions
      can mislead the LLM’s understanding.

      et al., 2023; 2024; Ying et al., 2024) often suffer from insufficient amounts of data and high com-
      putational costs. Search-based approaches enhance mathematical reasoning during the inference
074
      stage, thus avoiding the pressure of additional training. However, due to the vast search space, a
075
      naive search algorithm can lead to a significant increase in inference time(Yao et al., 2024). Al-

078
079
      Ineffective Verification Method When selecting among multiple candidate answers to a problem,
080
      previous works like Yao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024) typically employ voting-based verification
081
      methods. However, they overlook the fact that LLMs can make the same mistakes across multiple
082   routes.
083
084   To address these challenges, we propose BEATS, a novel method for efficient search aimed at en-
085
      hancing mathematical performance. Our method guides the model to answer problems instructed
      by clarified question, thereby avoiding ambiguities in problem statements. We meticulously design
086
      prompts that instruct the model to disambiguate, solve one step at a time, and directly generate an-
087
      swers based on preceding steps. Additionally, traditional verification methods in tree search, such
088   as majority voting, may be unreliable, as LLMs can perpetuate the same mistakes across multiple
089   branches. To overcome this, we introduce a back-verification technique that re-submits both the
090   answer and the problem to the model for a judgment of correctness, leveraging the model’s capa-
091   bilities while reducing its reasoning difficulty. Furthermore, we employ a pruning tree search to
092   optimize search time while achieving strong performance. It is worth noting that with our meticu-
093   lously designed pruning tree, we can control search expenses; simultaneously, compared to MCTS,
094   the pruning tree is able to search through every leaf node, ensuring promising performance, while
095   MCTS is more likely to search based on prior experience.
096   The core contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
097
098            • Meticulously Designed Prompt We developed three newly curated prompts designed to
099              solve mathematical problems step-by-step, provide final answers, and, most importantly,
100              avoiding ambiguities in problem statements.
101
102
               • Pruning Tree Search for Controllable Inference Time We implement a pruning strategy
                 for the tree by imposing constraints on the search steps. Specifically, we restrict the rewrit-
103
                 ing of the question to once and terminate the tree construction when answer is achieved.
104
105            • New Effective Verification Method We propose a new back-verification method that re-
106              submits both the answer and the problem to the model for a judgment of correctness, as
107              shown in Figure 1. This approach enhances the performance of searching in LLMs com-
                 pared to majority voting.

      2.1   M ATH L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
116
117   LLMs have demonstrated significant capabilities across various tasks, including mathematical
118   problem-solving, which is a critical skill for these models. However, learning to solve mathematical
119   problems poses challenges for LLMs, often requiring large amounts of training data and substantial
120   computational resources. In this paper, we review several state-of-the-art (SOTA) models specifi-
121   cally designed to tackle mathematical problems.
122
      Llemma (Azerbayev et al., 2021) integrates both code and mathematical data to train mod-
123   els, resulting in strong performance. InternLM2 (Ying et al., 2024) utilizes a vast amount of
124   math-related pre-training corpus to achieve high performance. Mammoth (Yue et al., 2023) col-
125   lected Chain-of-Thought (CoT) data for fine-tuning language models and achieved impressive re-
126   sults. Mammoth2 (Yue et al., 2024) builds on Mammoth by collecting WebInstruct, one of the
127   largest open-source math datasets, and uses it to fine-tune LLMs, resulting in SOTA performance.
128   DeepSeek (Shao et al., 2024) employs preference-based mathematical data to perform an additional
129   stage of reinforcement learning, achieving SOTA results.
130   In addition to models explicitly trained for mathematics, a few foundation models exhibit excep-
131   tional mathematical proficiency. Llama3 (Touvron et al., 2023) has shown remarkable performance
132   in solving mathematical problems. Qwen2 (Bai et al., 2023b), another series of outstanding models,
133   is one of the SOTA open-source models. Furthermore, closed-source models like Claude and GPT
134   also demonstrate strong capabilities in mathematical problem solving.
135
136   2.2   P ROMPT E NGINEERING FOR L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
137
138   The effectiveness of large language models in various applications largely depends on the quality
139
      of the prompts used. There are already many designed prompts that can significantly enhance the
      performance of LLMs (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024;
140
      Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a). However, these methods that rely on manual prompt engi-
141
      neering are far less scalable. In the field of mathematical logical reasoning for LLMs, the Chain of
142   Thought and its derived strategies are widely popular due to their effectiveness. Zero-shot CoT (Ko-
143   jima et al., 2022) is adding a simple sentence like “Let’s think step by step” at the end of questions
144   to assist LLMs in generating reasoning steps. Instead of Zero-shot CoT, Manual-Cot (Wei et al.,
145   2022) provides reasoning steps as few shots. Self-Consistency further improves language models’
146   reasoning performance by generating a diverse set of reasoning paths and choosing the most consis-
147   tent answer in the final answer set. Tree of Thought (Yao et al., 2024) and GOT (Besta et al., 2024)
148   extend the reasoning pathway from linear to non-linear data structures by leveraging multiple LLM
149   queries to elicit different plausible reasoning paths (Yang et al., 2024). Buffer of Thought (BOT)
150
      (Yang et al., 2024) designs a series of thought-template for tasks, and for each problem, it retrieve
      a relevant thought-template to prompt LLMs. PS prompting (Wang et al., 2023a) improves COT by
151
      encouraging LLMs to devise a plan before attempting to solve a problem. In this paper, we employ
152
      meticulously designed prompts to enhance the model’s mathematical capabilities.

197   3.1     P ROMPT D ESIGN
198
199   We design three actions for the tree search, illustrated in Figure 7. The three options are: One Step
200
      Forward, Giving Final Answer, and Disambiguation.
201
202
      One Step Forward The prompt is summarized in Figure 7(a). It encourages the model to progress
      through the search tree by evaluating the next logical step based on the current context and informa-
203
      tion. Given that mathematical problems often require multi-step reasoning, splitting a problem into
204
      individual steps reduces the complexity of the LLM’s response. By addressing each step sequen-
205   tially, we enhance the likelihood of arriving at the correct answer, as the model can focus on one
206   aspect of the problem at a time, thereby improving accuracy and clarity in reasoning.
207
208   Giving the Final Answer The prompt is summarized in Figure 7(b), this option directs the model
209   to provide a conclusive answer after considering all relevant information, ensuring clarity and pre-
210   cision in responses. At the appropriate moment, this prompt assists in summarizing the reasoning
211   behind multi-step answers, allowing the model to draw a definitive conclusion. By integrating in-
212   sights from each step, it helps ensure that the final answer accurately reflects the cumulative logic
213   and reasoning process.
214
215   Disambiguation The prompt is illustrated in Figure 7(c). This prompt emphasizes reformulating
      the initial query to enhance clarity and specificity, thereby facilitating a more effective search pro-

      cess. This approach is necessary, as many problem descriptions are frequently ambiguous or unclear,
217   leading to incorrect answers. For example, the query, Josh decides to try flipping
218   a house. He buys a house for $80,000 and then invests $50,000 in
219   repairs. This increased the value of the house by 150%. How much
220   profit did he make?, can introduce ambiguity. By incorporating a step to rewrite questions,
221   we aim to eliminate such ambiguities, ensuring that the model fully comprehends the problem
222   before attempting to solve it. This helps prevent errors that result from misinterpretations of the
223   initial query.

274   3.3    BACK -V ERIFICATION
275
276   After constructing the tree, we apply a depth-first search (DFS) to identify the leaf nodes. From
277   these, we select only those that contain the phrase The answer is as candidate answers for back
278   verification. For a candidate answer A, we concatenate it with the question Q for back verification
279   using LLMs:
280                                        Correct = LLM (Q ⊕ A)                                       (3)
281
282   Back verification involves leveraging both the answer and the question to allow the LLM to confirm
283   the correctness of the answer. It is well-established that verifying an answer is typically easier
284   than solving the original problem. Thus, we employ back verification to enhance the accuracy of
285   validation. After the back-verification, we utilize majority voting based on the back-verification
286   results. The impact of back verification is further examined in Section 4.3.

      5    C ONCLUSION
474
475
      In this paper, we introduced BEATS, a new method designed to enhance the mathematical problem-
476
      solving capabilities of LLMs. By addressing critical challenges such as suboptimal prompts, in-
477   effective verification methods, and high computational costs, our approach offers a significant im-
478   provement in performance. The meticulously crafted prompts facilitate step-by-step reasoning, re-
479   ducing ambiguities in problem statements and enabling the model to generate accurate answers. Our
480   innovative back-verification technique enhances the reliability of results by ensuring that answers
481   are thoroughly validated. Additionally, the pruning tree search strategy allows for controlled infer-
482   ence time while maintaining state-of-the-art performance. Through extensive experimentation, we
483   demonstrated that BEATS notably outperforms existing methods, marking a solid foundation for ad-
484   vancing mathematical reasoning in LLMs. This work represents an excellent starting point, paving
485
      the way for future research to explore more effective verification methods and their applicability
      across a broader spectrum of complex problem domains.
