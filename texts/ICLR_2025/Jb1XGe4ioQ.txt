009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Flow matching (FM) is a family of training algorithms for fitting continuous nor-
012
               malizing flows (CNFs). Conditional flow matching (CFM) exploits the fact that
013            the marginal vector field of a CNF can be learned by fitting least-square regres-
014            sion to the so-called conditional vector field specified given one or both ends of the
015            flow path. We show that viewing CFM training from a Bayesian decision theoretic
016            perspective on parameter estimation opens the door to generalizations of CFM al-
017            gorithms. We propose one such extension by introducing a CFM algorithm based
018            on defining conditional probability paths given what we refer to as ”streams,” in-
019            stances of latent stochastic paths that connect pairs of noise and observed data.
020            Further, we advocate the modeling of these latent streams using Gaussian pro-
021
               cesses (GPs). The unique distributional properties of GPs, and in particular the
               fact that the velocity of a GP is still a GP, allows drawing samples from the result-
022
               ing stream-augmented conditional probability path without simulating the actual
023
               streams, and hence the ”simulation-free” nature of CFM training is preserved. We
024            show that this generalization of the CFM can substantially reduce the variance
025            in the estimated marginal vector field at a moderate computational cost, thereby
026            improving the quality of the generated samples under common metrics. Addi-
027            tionally, we show that adopting the GP on the streams allows for flexibly linking
028            multiple related training data points (e.g., time series) and incorporating additional
029            prior information. We empirically validate our claim through both simulations and
030            applications to image and neural time series data.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Deep generative models aim to estimate and sample from an unknown probability distribution. Con-
036
      tinuous normalizing flows (CNFs, Chen et al. (2018)) construct an invertible and differentiable map-
037   ping, using neural ordinary differential equation (ODE), between a source and the target distribution.
038   However, traditionally, it has been difficult to scale CNF training to large datasets (Chen et al., 2018;
039   Grathwohl et al., 2019; Onken et al., 2021). Recently, Lipman et al. (2023); Albergo & Vanden-
040   Eijnden (2023); Liu et al. (2023b) showed that CNFs can be trained via a regression objective, and
041   proposed the flow matching (FM) algorithm. The FM exploits the fact that the marginal vector field
042   inducing a desired CNF can be learned through a regression formulation, approximating per-sample
043   conditional vector fields using a smoother such as a deep neural network (Lipman et al., 2023). In
044   the original FM approach, the training objective is conditioned on samples from the target distribu-
045
      tion, and the source distribution has to be Gaussian. This limitation was later relaxed, allowing the
      target distribution to be supported on manifolds (Chen & Lipman, 2024) and the source distribution
046
      to be non-Gaussian (Pooladian et al., 2023). Tong et al. (2024a) provided a unifying framework
047
      with arbitrary transport maps by conditioning on both ends. While their framework is, in principle,
048   general, it does require the induced conditional probability paths be readily sampled from, and as
049   such they considered a few Gaussian probability paths. Moreover, most existing FM methods only
050   consider the inclusion of two endpoints, and hence cannot accommodate data involving multiple
051   related observations, such as time series and other data with a grouping structure. Recently, Albergo
052   et al. (2024) proposed a multimarginal stochastic interpolants, which can jointly learn a multivariate
053   distribution by generalization of the stochastic interpolant framework. However, this approach may
      be too restrictive for path design, and hence may limit it usage to such as time series data.

      In this paper, we first view FM from the perspective of Bayesian estimation under squared error loss,
055   which motivates us to go one level deeper in Bayesian hierarchical modeling and specify distribu-
056   tional assumptions on streams, which are latent stochastic paths connecting the two endpoints. This
057   leads to a class of CFM algorithms that conditions at the “stream” level, which broadens the range
058   of conditional probability paths allowed in CFM training. By endowing the streams with Gaussian
059   process (GP) distributions, these algorithms provide wider sampling coverage over the support of
060   the marginal vector field, leading to reduced variance in the estimated vector field and improved
061   synthetic samples from the target distribution. Furthermore, conditioning on GP streams allows for
062   flexible integration of related observations through placing them along the streams between two end-
063
      points and for incorporating additional prior information, all while maintaining analytical tractability
      and computational efficiency of CFM algorithms.
064
065   In summary, the main contributions of this paper are:
066
067         1. We present a Bayesian decision theoretic perspective on FM algorithms, which provides
068
               an additional justification for FM algorithms beyond gradient matching and serves as the
               foundation for extensions to these algorithms by latent variable modeling on the streams.
069
070         2. We generalize CFM training by augmenting the specification of conditional probability
071            paths through latent variable modeling on the streams. We show that streams endowed
072            with GP distributions lead to a simple stream-level CFM algorithm that preserves the
073
               “simulation-free” training.
074         3. We demonstrate that adjusting the GP streams can reduce the variance of the estimated
075            marginal vector field with moderate computational cost.
076         4. We demonstrate how to use GP streams to integrate related observations, thereby taking
077            advantage of the correlation among related samples to enhance the quality of the generated
078            samples from the target distributions.
079         5. These benefits are illustrated by simulations and applications to image (CIFAR-10, MNIST
080            and HWD+) and neural time series data (LFP), with code for Python implementation in the
081            supplementary materials.

      2   A BAYESIAN D ECISION T HEORETIC P ERSPECTIVE ON F LOW M ATCHING

      We start by viewing FM training from a Bayesian decision theoretic perspective. Consider i.i.d.
086
      training observations from an unknown population distribution q1 over Rd . A CNF is a time-
087   dependent differomorphic map φt that transforms a random variable x0 ∈ Rd from a source dis-
088   tribution q0 into a random variable from q1 . The CNF induces a distribution of xt = φt (x0 ) at each
089   time t, which is denoted by pt , thereby forming a probability path {pt : 0 ≤ t ≤ 1}. This probabil-
090   ity path should (at least approximately) satisfy the boundary conditions p0 = q0 and p1 = q1 . It is
091   related to the flow map through the change-of-variable formula or the push-forward equation
092
                                                   pt = [φt ]∗ p0 .
093
094   FM aims at learning the corresponding vector field ut (x), which induces the probability path over
095   time by satisfying the continuity equation (Villani, 2008).
096
      The key observation underlying FM algorithms is that the vector field ut (x) can be written as a
097   conditional expectation involving a conditional vector field ut (x|z), which induces a conditional
098   probability path pt (·|z) corresponding to the conditional distribution of φt (x) given z. Here, z is
099   the conditioning latent variable, which can be the target sample x1 (e.g. Ho et al. (2020); Song et al.
100   (2021); Lipman et al. (2023),) or a pair of (x0 , x1 ) on source and target distribution (e.g. Liu et al.
101   (2023b); Tong et al. (2024a)). Specifically, Tong et al. (2024a), generalizing the result from Lipman
102   et al. (2023), showed that
103                                  Z
                                               pt (x|z)q(z)
104                         ut (x) = ut (x|z)                 dz = E (ut (x|z)|xt = x) ,
                                                   pt (x)
105
106   where the expectation is taken over z, which one can recognize is the conditional expectation of
107   ut (x|z) conditional on the event that xt = x. The integral is with respect to the conditional distri-
      bution of z given xt = x.

      It is well-known in Bayesian decision theory (Berger, 1985) that under squared error loss, the
109   Bayesian estimator, which minimizes both the posterior expected loss (which conditions on the
110   data and integrates out the parameters) and the marginal loss (which integrates out both the param-
111   eters and the data), is exactly the posterior expectation of that parameter. This implies immediately
112   that if one considers the conditional vector field ut (x|z) as the target of “estimation”, and the cor-
113   responding “data” being the event that xt = x, i.e., that the path goes through x at time t, then the
114   corresponding Bayes estimate for ut (x|z) will be exactly the marginal vector field ut (x), as it is now
115   the “posterior mean” of ut (x|z). We emphasize again that here the “data” differs from the actual
116   training and the generated noise observations, which in fact help form the “prior” distribution.
117   The FM algorithm is motivated from the goal of approximating the marginal vector field ut (x)
118   through a smoother vtθ (typically a neural network), via the flow matching (FM) objective
119
                                 LFM (θ) = Et∼U (0,1),x∼pt (x) kvtθ (x) − ut (x)k2 ,
120
121   which is not identifiable due to the non-uniqueness of the marginal vector fields that satisfy the
122   boundary conditions without further constraints. In the following, we presume t ∼ U (0, 1) and only
123   show random variables to save notations. FM algorithms address this by fitting vtθ to the conditional
124
      vector field ut (x|z) after further specifying the distribution of q(z) along with the conditional proba-
      bility path pt (x|z), through minimizing the finite-sample version of the marginal squared error loss.
125
      This approach was referred to as the conditional flow matching (CFM) objective
126
127                           LCFM (θ) = Et,z∼q(z),x∼pt (x|z) kvtθ (x) − ut (x|z)k2 .
128
129   Traditionally, optimizing the CFM objective is justified because it has the same gradients w.r.t. θ
130
      to the corresponding FM loss (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024a). The Bayesian decision-
      theoretic perspective provides a further validation because approximating the conditional vector field
131
      by minimizing the marginal squared error loss can be interpreted as approximating the “posterior
132
      expectation” of ut (x|z), which is exactly ut (x).
133
134   Moreover, this is true for any coherently specified probability model q(z). So long as the conditional
135   probability path pt (x|z) is tractable, a suitable CFM algorithm can be designed. Therefore one can
136
      enrich the specification of q(z) using Bayesian latent variable modeling strategies. This motivates
      us to generalize CFM training to the stream level, which we describe in the next section.

141   3.1   A PER - STREAM PERSPECTIVE ON FLOW MATCHING
142
      A stream s is a stochastic process s = {st : 0 ≤ t ≤ 1}, where each st is a random variable in the
143
      sample space of the training data. We focus on streams connecting one end x0 in the source to the
144
      other x1 in the training data. From here on, s will take the space of the latent quantity z.
145
146   Instead of defining a conditional probability path and vector field given one endpoint at t = 1
147   (Lipman et al., 2023) or two endpoints at t = 0 and 1 (Tong et al., 2024a), we shall consider
148
      defining it given the whole stream connecting the two ends. In order to achieve this, we need to
      specify a probability model for s. This can be separated into two parts—the marginal model on the
149
      endpoints π(x0 , x1 )and the conditional model for s given the two ends. That is
150
151                        (x0 , x1 ) ∼ π   and    s|s0 = x0 , s1 = x1 ∼ ps (·|x0 , x1 ).
152   Our model and algorithm will generally apply to any choice of π that satisfies the boundary condi-
153   tion, including all of the examples considered in Tong et al. (2024a). We defer the description of
154   specific choices of ps (·|x0 , x1 ) to the next section and for now focus on the general framework.
155
      Given a stream s, the “per-stream” vector field ut (x|s) represents the “velocity” (or derivative) of
156
      the stream at time t, conditional on the event that st = x, i.e, the stream s passes through x at time
157   t. Assuming that the stream is differentiable with in time, the per-stream vector field is
158
159
                                             ut (x|s) := ṡt = dst /dt,
160   which is defined only on all pairs of (t, x) that satisfy st = x. The per-stream view extends previous
161   CFM conditioning on endpoints and provides more flexibility. See Appendix A for more detailed
      discussion on how the per-stream perspective relates to the per-sample perspective on FM.

      While the endpoint of the stream s1 = x1 is an actual observation in the training data, for the task
163   of learning the marginal vector field ut (x), one can think of our “data” as the event that a stream s
164   passes through a point x at time t, that is st = x. Under the squared error loss, the Bayes estimate for
165   the per-stream conditional vector field ut (x|s) will be the “posterior” expectation given the “data”,
166   which is exactly the marginal vector field
167
                                   ut (x) = E(ut (x|s)|st = x) = E(ṡt |st = x).                             (1)
168
169   Following Theorem 3.1 in Tong et al. (2024a), we can show that the marginal vector ut (x) indeed
170   generates the probability path pt (x). (See the proof in the Appendix H.1.) The essence of the proof
171   is to check the continuity equation for the (degenerate) conditional probability path pt (x|s).
172   A general stream-level CFM loss for learning ut (x) is then
173
174                       LsCFM (θ) = Et,s kvtθ (st ) − ut (x|s)k2 = Et,s kvtθ (st ) − ṡt k2
175   where the integration over t is again U (0, 1) and that over s is with respect to the marginal distri-
176   bution of s induced by π(x0 , x1 ) and ps (·|x0 , x1 ). As in previous research such as Lipman et al.
177   (2023); Tong et al. (2024a), we can show that the gradient of LsCFM equals that of LFM with details
178   of proof in Appendix H.2. However, the stream-level CFM can be justified from a Bayesian decision
179   theoretic perspective without gradient matching (Section 2). Because the (population-level) mini-
180   mizer for the sCFM loss is ut (x), minimizing the sCFM loss provides a reasonable estimate for the
181   marginal vector field ut (x). To see this, rewrite the sCFM loss by the law of iterated expectation as
182
                                     LsCFM (θ) = Et Es kvtθ (st ) − ṡt k2 |t .

183
184   The inner expectation can be further written in terms of another iterated expection:
185
                             Es kvtθ (st ) − ṡt k2 |t = Est Es kvtθ (st ) − ṡt k2 |t, st .

186

187   For any x, Es kvtθ (st ) − ṡt k2 | t, st = x = Es kvtθ (x) − ṡt k2 | t, st = x , whose minimizer is the
188   conditional expectation of ṡt given st = x, which is exactly ut (x). Hence, one can estimate ut (x)
189   by minimizing LsCFM (θ). This justifies training ut (x) through the sCFM loss without regard to any
190   specific optimization strategy.

192   3.2   C HOICE OF THE STREAM MODEL
193
194   Next, we specify the conditional model for the stream given the endpoints ps (·|x0 , x1 ). This model
195
      should emit streams differentiable with respect to time, with readily available velocity (either ana-
      lytically or easily computable). Previous methods such as optimal transport (OT) conditional path
196
      (Liu et al., 2023b; Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024a) can provide rather poor coverage of
197
      the (t, x) space, resulting in extensive extrapolation of the estimated vector field vt (x). It is thus
198   desirable to consider stochastic models for the streams that ensure the smoothness while allowing
199   streams to diverge and provide more spread-out coverage of the (t, x) space. Previous research sug-
200   gested that, compared to ODEs, SDEs (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) can be more robust in
201   high-dimensional spaces (Tong et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), likely due to the
202   robustness arising from the regularization induced by the additional stochasticity.
203
      To preserve the ”simulation-free” nature of CFM, we consider models where the joint distribution
204   of the stream and its velocity is available in closed form. In particular, we further explore the
205   streams following Gaussian processes (GPs). A desirable property of GP is that its velocity is
206   also a GP, with mean and covariance directly derived from original GP (Rasmussen & Williams,
207   2005). This enables efficient joint sampling of (st , ṡt ) given observations from a GP in stream-
208   level CFM training. By adjusting covariance kernels for the joint GP, one can fine-tune the variance
209   level to control the level of regularization, thereby further improving the estimation of the marginal
210   vector field ut (x) (Section 4.1). The prior path constraints can also be incorporated into the kernel
211   design. Additionally, GP conditioning on the event that the stream passes through a finite number of
212
      intermediate locations between two endpoints again leads to a GP with analytic mean and covariance
      kernel (Section 4.2). This is particularly useful for incorporating multiple related observations.
213
214   Specifically, given M time points t = (t1 , t2 , . . . , tM ) with t1 = 0 and tM = 1, we let st =
215   (st1 , st2 , . . . , stM ), and consider a more general conditional model for ps (· | st = xobs ), where
      xobs = (xt1 , xt2 , . . . , xtM ) are a set of “observed values” that we require the statistic process s to

256   the training set (and hence more regularization), one could consider using a kernel producing larger
257   variance as t approaches to ends with finite training samples (Figure 2 and 7). Moreover, because
258   the GP model for s is specified given the two endpoints, both its mean and covariance kernel can
259   be specified as functions of (x0 , x1 ). For example, if x1 is an outlier of the training data, e.g., from
260   a tail region of q1 , then one may incorporate a more variable covariance kernel for ps (·|x0 , x1 ) to
261
      account for the uncertainty in the “optimal” transport path from x0 to x1 .

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527   We have presented a Bayesian decision theoretic perspective to CFM training, which motivates an
528   extension to CFM algorithms based on latent variable modeling. In particular, we adopt GP models
529   on the latent streams. Our GP-CFM algorithm preserves the “simulation-free” feature of CFM
530
      training by exploiting distributional properties of GPs. This generalization not only reduces the
      sampling variance by expanding coverage of the sampling space in CFM training, but also allows
531
      easy integration of multiple related observations to achieve borrowing of strength.
532
533   There are some potential improvements either under GP-CFM frameworks or generally motivated
534   by Bayesian decision theoretic perspective. For example, under GP-CFM framework, current im-
535   plementations require the complete observations for all time points, which can be rare in time series
536
      applications. To deal with the missingness as well as the potential high-dimensionality of the train-
      ing data, we may fit the GP-CFM in some latent space as in latent diffusion models (Rombach et al.,
537
      2022) and latent flow matching (Dao et al., 2023).
538
539   We believe that the Bayesian decision theoretic perspective and GP-CFM generalization so moti-
      vated open the door to various further improvements of CFM training of CNFs.

      A      D ISCUSSION ON P ER - STREAM P ERSPECTIVE ON F LOW M ATCHING
665
666
      It is helpful to recognize the relationship between the per-stream vector field and the conditional
667   vector field given one or both endpoints introduced previously in the literature. Specifically, the
668   per-sample vector field in Lipman et al. (2023) corresponds to marginalizing out s given the end
669   point x1 , that is, ut (x | x1 ) = E (ut (x | s) | st = x, s1 = x1 ). Similarly, the conditional vector
670   field of Tong et al. (2024a), corresponds to marginalizing out s given both x0 and x1 , that is ut (x |
671   x0 , x1 ) = E (ut (x | s) | st = x, s0 = x0 , s1 = x1 ). Furthermore, when ps (· | x0 , x1 ) is simply
672   a unit-point mass (Dirac) concentrated on the optimal transport (OT) path, i.e., a straight line that
673   connects two endpoints x0 and x1 , then ut (x | s) = ut (x | x1 ) = ut (x | x0 , x1 ) for all (s, t, x)
674   tuples that satisfy s0 = x0 , s1 = x1 , st = x. Intuitively, when the stream connecting two ends is
675
      unique, conditioning on the two ends is equivalent to conditioning on the corresponding stream s.
      In this case, our stream-level FM algorithm (Section 3.2) coincides with those previous algorithms.
676
      More generally, however, this equivalence does not hold when ps (· | x0 , x1 ) is non-degenerate.
677
678   The per-stream view affords additional modeling flexibility and alleviates the practitioners from
679   the burden of directly sampling from the conditional probability paths given one (Lipman et al.,
680   2023) or both endpoints (Tong et al., 2024a). While the per-stream vector field induces a degenerate
681
      unit-point mass conditional probability path, we will attain non-degenerate marginal and conditional
      probability paths that satisfy the boundary conditions after marginalizing out the streams. Sampling
682
      the streams in essence provides a data-augmented Monte Carlo alternative to sampling directly from
683
      the conditional probability paths, which can then allow estimation of the marginal vector field ut (x)
684   when direct sampling from the conditional probability path is challenging. Additionally, as we will
685   demonstrate later, by approaching FM at the stream level, one could more readily incorporate prior
686   knowledge or other external features into the design of the stream distribution ps (· | x0 , x1 ).
