009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Portrait animation, particularly audio-driven portrait animation, requires flexibility
012            in facial expressions, head movement, and dynamic contexts. However, existing
013            diffusion-based methods rely heavily on the design of ReferenceNet, leading to
014            increased training complexity and incompatibility with other custom base models or
015            adapters, also limiting face position, view changes, and animated context generation.
016
               To address these challenges, we propose AnyExpress, a lightweight, modular
               framework that eliminates the need for ReferenceNet, reducing the number of
017
               trainable parameters by 7 times. By training one plug-and-play audio-motion
018
               adapter, it allows freeform, expressive audio-driven portrait animation with any
019            face pose and any animated context, while supporting text-driven modifications.
020            In the context of character generation, there are two primary methods to control
021            the desired character attributes. First, if a specific ID needs to be assigned, this
022            can be achieved through ID controls (e.g., IP-Adapter-Face). Alternatively, the
023            character’s attributes can be controlled through textual descriptions. Through
024            comprehensive qualitative and quantitative analyses, AnyExpress demonstrates
025            unprecedented freedom in generating videos with dynamic background, lower
026            training demand, and seamless integration with evolving custom models and control
027
               adapters, providing a flexible solution for diverse generation needs. The demo is
               available at https://anyexpress-alpha.github.io/Any, and we will
028
               release our code, encouraging further improvement.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The human face talking video is not merely a static object in the context of multimedia and commu-
034   nication; rather, it is a dynamic, vibrant object in which the background changes and the speaker’s
035   face can appear from front-facing to side profiles and other poses (Wang et al., 2021a;b; Zhong
036   et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023a). Recently, advanced methods built on ReferenceNet-based dif-
037   fusion frameworks (Hu, 2024) have demonstrated superior performance in audio-driven portrait
038
      animation (Chen et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Wei et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a).
039   However, we argue that this design has two limitations. First, as shown in Fig. 1 middle top, reliance
040   on a ReferenceNet increases the model’s parameter count and complicates the training procedure.
041   This added 2D-UNet results in a highly coupled framework, additional training stages, and greater
042   resource requirements, making the approach less scalable and generalizable under other control
043   adapters. Second, as shown in Fig. 1 right top, current methods overly constrain video outputs,
044
      with the pose tightly bound to the reference image, limiting view change through large angles or
      outpainting generation. Additionally, these methods can only produce a static background as the
045
      reference image, which fails to reflect the dynamic environments of the real-world.
046
047   Considering this, we argue that highly flexible, audio-driven talking face generation is crucial for
048   real-world applications. We term this Freeform Portrait Animation, which aims to generate talking
049   faces in any configuration (Fig. 1 right): ❶ Any face pose, allowing for faces to be generated
050
      from any angle or position, extending beyond the reference image; ❷ Any animated context,
      seamlessly integrating talking faces with animated elements and backgrounds; ❸ Any control via
051
      text instructions, enabling users to modify background and identity attributes through text prompts.
052
053   To address these limitations and achieve Freeform Portrait Animation, we design a solution cen-
      tered on the principle of “Low Coupling, High Cohesion”, enabling seamless integration with other

      Diffusion-Based Portrait Animation. Recent diffusion-based portrait animation methods focus
111   on different control modalities—audio, visual signals, or multi-modal combinations. Audio-driven
112   methods synchronize lip movements and head motions but struggle with large-scale head movements,
113   limiting expressiveness (Wang et al., 2021a; Yu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c).
114   Visual-driven methods use facial landmarks and poses to guide animations but fail to incorporate audio
115   dynamics and often introduce distortions when driving signals differ significantly from the reference
116   image in identity or proportions (Xie et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). Several multi-modal methods
117   attempt to combine audio and visual signals for more nuanced control (Drobyshev et al., 2024; Wang
118   et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024a). However, these existing methods primarily rely on strong control
119
      via ReferenceNet, resulting in video generation being overly constrained by the reference image. This
      not only limits flexibility in adapting to dynamic external conditions, such as backgrounds or facial
120
      poses, but also complicates the training processes and prevents adaptive evolution alongside new
121
      developments in diffusion models (Chen et al., 2023a; Sauer et al., 2023; 2024; Esser et al., 2024). In
122   this work, we address these limitations by introducing a lightweight adapter that supports Freeform
123   Portrait Animation. Our approach enables face generation from various angles and positions within
124   dynamic environments, providing a more flexible and natural animation experience.
125
      Adapter. Adapters were introduced to make fine-tuning large pre-trained models more efficient,
126
      enabling transfer learning with compact modules instead of full-model fine-tuning (Houlsby et al.,
127
      2019; Li et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2021). As large-scale datasets have grown
128   (Schuhmann et al., 2022), diffusion models now have billions of parameters. Fine-tuning all these
129   for each task (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Podell et al., 2024; BlackForestLabs,
130   2024; Yang et al., 2024c) is computationally expensive and can cause issues like catastrophic
131   forgetting (Smith et al., 2024; Gao & Liu, 2023), where models lose previously learned knowledge.
132   To address this, adapter-based methods (Mou et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhong et al., 2023a;
133   Xing et al., 2024) insert lightweight modules into diffusion models for task-specific adaptation.
134   However, current portrait animation methods heavily rely on fine-tuning the entire diffusion model,
135   with some approaches even replicating the UNet, which dramatically increases computation and
136
      hinders transferability, highlighting the necessity of lightweight and scalable solutions.

      To overcome these limitations, we propose Progressive Prefix Conditioning (Fig. 4 right). This
286
      method builds consistency among windows by conditioning each new window on prefix frames taken
287
      from the end of the previous window. This is similar to how, in life, each moment informs the next,
288   creating a cohesive and natural result. The prefix frames serve as a “guiding thread,” informing the
289   generation of upcoming frames and ensuring seamless continuity. However, even with prefix frames,
290   deviations in texture, color, and other attributes can still arise across windows. To mitigate this, we
291   further introduce anchor alignment. During the denoising of the first window (the anchor), we store
292   the mean and variance of its latents across U-Net blocks at each timestep. In subsequent windows,
293   we align their latents with the stored anchor’s statistics at every timestep, ensuring that the generated
294   frames remain consistent with those of the first window. Unlike Progressive Fusion, which focuses
295   on static averaging, Progressive Prefix Conditioning allows for dynamic refinement and adjustment,
296
      promoting smooth transitions. Refer to Algorithm C.1 for the formulated procedure.

      Training. In our design, we fine-tune only the audio and motion modules, using a two-stage strategy
300
      to prevent overfitting in the motion module. Since the temporal module is pretrained and requires less
301
      training time than the randomly initialized audio module, fine-tuning both with the same learning
302   schedule can cause the motion module to overfit the downstream training datasets. To address this,
303   in stage I, we perform longer fine-tuning of both modules to enable the audio module to drive
304   lip synchronization and ensure smooth transitions. In stage II, we restore the motion module from
305   pretrained weights and apply shorter fine-tuning with the audio module from stage I, achieving
306   proper integration of these two modules without identity overfitting.
307
      Inference. During inference, a single reference image and driving audio are taken as input to generate
308   an audio-driven portrait animation video. To ensure visual consistency in long videos, we use the last
309   4 frames of the previous window as the prefix frames for the next (Sec. 3.3). Additionally, the base
310   model parameters remain unchanged throughout. After fine-tuning, the audio-motion adapter can
311   animate any personalized T2I models without needing extra data collection or further training. As
312   shown in Fig. 6b, 11, 12, our design seamlessly supports various functionalities already present in the
313   base model and the broader adapter ecosystem. It can integrate with identity-image control (Ye et al.,
314   2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Guo et al., 2024c; Song et al., 2024), pose control (Zhang et al., 2023b;
315   Mou et al., 2024), and text control. This adaptability allows for versatile multi-modal control without
316
      the need for retraining large diffusion models, making it highly scalable for portrait animation tasks.

322   Implementation Details. The training process used 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs over two stages: 180,000
323   steps in stage 1 and 30,000 steps in stage 2, with a batch size of 16 and a video resolution of 512
      × 512. Each instance generated 16 video frames, and noise latents are concatenated with the first 4

324
      ground truth frames for prefix conditioning. Both stages had a learning rate of 1e-6, with the motion
325   module initialized using Animatediff weights (Guo et al., 2024b). A 0.1 dropout was applied to
326   reference images, text instructions, and audio during training. For inference, sequence continuity was
327   ensured by concatenating noisy latents with the last 4 motion frames from the previous window.
328
      Datasets. AnyExpress is trained by HDTF (Zhang et al., 2021) (28k clips, 42.58 hours), and other
329
      collected videos (171k clips, 263.23 hours). The facial regions in these videos are cropped and
330
      resized to 512×512. The total training dataset comprises approximately 300 hours of video. We
331   cleaned the data by retaining single-person speaking videos with strong lip-audio consistency while
332   excluding those with scene changes, significant camera movements, or excessive noise. Moreover,
333   the quantitative evaluation (Table. 2) was performed on the HDTF, CelebV (Zhu et al., 2022) datasets.
334
      Evaluation Metrics. Unlike ReferenceNet-based methods that mainly reconstruct the reference
335
      image with minor modifications and rely on metrics like FID and FVD, our Freeform Portrait
336
      Animation focuses on personalizing or re-contextualizing the talking face identity. Thus, we introduce
337   evaluation metrics tailored to measure the effectiveness for this task. For portrait animation quality,
338   Pose Diversity Score (∆P) measures head motion intensity , with higher scores indicating more diverse
339   movements and flexibility (Xu et al., 2024a); and Sync-C and Sync-D evaluate lip synchronization
340   with the audio, with higher Sync-C and lower Sync-D indicating more natural lip movements (Chung
341   & Zisserman, 2017). For video quality, DOVER Score (Wu et al., 2022; 2023) represents the overall
342   quality of videos from aesthetic and technical perspectives. For identity preservation, FaceID
343   Consistency calculates cosine similarity between generated faces and the reference image using a
344   face recognition model, while CLIP-I Score (Radford et al., 2021) measures structural similarity to
345
      ensure consistent facial features and alignment. Refer to Appendix A.2 for details.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduced AnyExpress, a scalable framework for audio-driven portrait anima-
535   tion that eliminates the need for ReferenceNet, reducing computational complexity and enhancing
536   compatibility with custom models. Key innovations include a flexible Face-ID control for identity
537   consistency and an Audio-Motion Adapter for motion dynamics without retraining the entire U-Net
538   architecture. A Progressive Prefix Conditioning strategy also ensures smooth transitions in long video
539   sequences. Comprehensive analyses show AnyExpress’s ability to generate dynamic, expressive
      animations with lower training demands.

867
868   All primary experiments are conducted using Stable Diffusion v1.51 , with an image size of
869   512x512x3 and a latent space of 64x64x4. For personalized models (Fig. 6b), Realistic V62
870   and TWingShadow3 are employed. Inference are conducted on a single Nvidia V100 GPU with 30
871   timesteps. Considering large guidance scale (Ho & Salimans, 2021) often leads to overly saturated
872   and unnatural images. We employ dynamic thresholding (Saharia et al., 2022) to handle color issues
873   by clipping outof-range pixel values, with guidance scale set to 5 and a mimic guidance scale set to
874   3.5. The learning rate is 1e-6 in two stages, and we optimize overall framework using Adam (Kingma
875   & Ba, 2015).

877   A.2     D ESIGN OF E VALUATION M ETRICS
878
879            • Portrait Animation Quality. These metrics measure how well the generated animations
880              capture natural motion, head movement diversity, and lip synchronization.
881              Perspective Diversity Score (∆P) measures head motion intensity by calculating the average
882              pose angle differences (yaw, pitch, roll) between adjacent frames. It provides an indication
883              of the overall diversity and range of head movements generated in the video. Higher ∆P
884              scores reflect the ability to generate a wider range of head motions, indicating flexibility in
885
                 animation.
886              Sync-C and Sync-D measures the synchronization between audio input and lip movements
887              in the generated videos. Higher Sync-C scores and lower Sync-D scores indicate better
888
                 alignment with the audio, reflecting natural and accurate lip movements.
889            • Video Quality. These metrics assess the technical and aesthetic quality of the generated
890              videos, focusing on aspects like facial structure similarity and overall visual appeal.
891              Dover Score represents the overall quality of the generated video, considering both technical
892              aspects (sharpness, frame continuity) and aesthetic elements (visual appeal). A higher Dover
893              score indicates superior video quality, reflecting a smooth, sharp, and visually pleasing
894              animation.
895
               • Identity Preserving Ability. These metrics evaluate how well the model retains the facial
896              identity of the subject across various frames and conditions.
897
                 Face Similarity uses a face recognition model (e.g., VGG Simonyan & Zisserman (2015),
898
                 FaceNet (Schroff et al., 2015), ArcFace (Deng et al., 2019)) to compute the cosine similarity
899
                 between the generated faces and the reference image’s face across frames. A higher score
900              indicates consistent preservation of the subject’s identity, even when generating faces from
901              different perspectives.
902
                 CLIP-I Score also serves to measure facial feature similarity across frames, ensuring that
903              the generated faces align with the reference identity. It measures the structural similarity
904              between the face in the reference image and the faces in each frame of the generated
905              video using a CLIP encoder pretrained on the LAION-Face dataset. A higher CLIP-I score
906              indicates that the facial structure in the generated video remains consistent with the reference
907              image, contributing to higher video quality.

       E     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1158
       Since AnyExpress relies on a highly scalable and modular audio-motion adapter, it provides a flexible
1159
       foundation that can easily integrate with more advanced models and techniques in future work. This
1160
       modularity ensures that only lightweight modules are trained, allowing for seamless adaptation to
1161   new developments without the need for extensive re-training. This scalability makes AnyExpress
1162   ideal for incorporating state-of-the-art models and methodologies. As the field of diffusion models
1163   continues to evolve, this architecture can be enhanced with new capabilities while maintaining
1164   efficiency and adaptability. These following aspects underscore where future research can refine and
1165   augment the methodology presented in AnyExpress: (1) Incorporating Advanced Base Models:
1166   The architecture of AnyExpress is currently built on SD1.5. However, emerging advanced models
1167   like SDXL, DiT, SD3, and FLUX offer enhanced capabilities for more complex tasks, holding the
1168   potential to significantly improve the quality and flexibility of portrait animation. (2) Enhancing
1169
       Identity Control: Integrating advanced identity controllers like PuLID (Guo et al., 2024c) and
       MoMA (Song et al., 2024) can improve identity consistency. Techniques like InstantID (Wang
1170
       et al., 2024b), which offers finer identity control, are promising, though currently incompatible due
1171
       to the difference in base models (SDXL vs. SD1.5). Resolving these compatibility issues would
1172   unlock significant gains in identity preservation. (3) Improved Audio-Visual Synchronization:
1173   Incorporating more sophisticated synchronization techniques, such as advanced audio analysis
1174   and cross-modal learning, could further enhance the alignment of facial movements with audio,
1175   especially in nuanced or emotionally expressive contexts. (4) Enhancing Temporal Coherence:
1176   Advanced temporal coherence mechanisms are required to address inconsistencies in fast or intricate
1177   sequences. Leveraging long-term dependencies or recurrent neural networks could help achieve
1178   smoother transitions and eliminate flickering. (5) Boosting Computational Efficiency: Optimizing
1179   computational efficiency and reducing the steps (Song et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
1180
       2023) would make AnyExpress more suitable for real-time applications.

1185   particularly when driven by audio inputs. One significant concern is the potential misuse of such

       risks, it is crucial to establish clear ethical guidelines and promote responsible usage of the technology.
