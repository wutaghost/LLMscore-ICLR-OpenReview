009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Affect computing is crucial in fields such as human-computer interaction, health-
012            care, and market research, yet emotion’s ambiguity and subjectivity challenge
013            current recognition techniques. We propose Emotion Interpretation (EI), a task
014            that interprets the reasons behind emotions, and create the Emotion Interpretation
015            Benchmark (EIBench) using a VLLM-assisted dataset construction method,
016
               Coarse-to-Fine Self-Ask (CFSA), with carefully human in-the-loop annotation.
               EIBench includes 1,615 basic and 50 multi-faceted complex emotion interpretation
017
               samples. Experiments show limited proficiency of existing models in EI, with
018
               the best achieving 62.41% accuracy in the zero-shot setting and some performing
019            lower than the text-only LLaMA-3 model (6.26%) in the caption-provided set-
020            ting. Different personas assigned also differ the benchmark results. Overcoming
021            the challenges posed by EI can result in more empathetic AI systems, thereby
022            enhancing human-computer interaction and emotion-sensitive applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

027
      Affect computing plays a crucial role across diverse domains (Khare et al., 2024), such as human-
      computer interaction (HCI) (Jain et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2022; Parviainen & Søndergaard, 2020;
028
      Yang et al., 2019), healthcare (Dahl & Harvey, 2007; Saarni et al., 2007; Tronick, 2018), and market
029
      research (Cambria et al., 2017; Caruelle et al., 2022; Srivastava & Bag, 2024). Recent research
030   mainly focuses on recognizing emotions by categorizing them into basic types. However, the inherent
031   complexity and subjectivity of emotions make it difficult for individuals to accurately identify
032   their own emotions in complex situations. Despite this, these applications share a common goal:
033   understanding the triggers of emotions. The variability of emotional experiences across individuals
034   and contexts underscores the need to move beyond simple categorization and focus on the triggers
035   and circumstances that lead to emotions, known as “Emotional Triggers”.
036   In response to this motivation, we propose the Emotion Interpretation (EI) task, which focuses on
037   interpreting emotional triggers rather than categorizing emotions. As shown in Figure 1, this task
038   involves identifying the specific causes of emotional states for given individuals or scenes, aiming
039   to better understand human’s emotion by try to think at their own position. Overcoming the EI task
040   can result in a more empathetic AI system and applications. The Vision Large Language Models
041   (VLLMs), known for their extensive world knowledge and explanatory abilities, are well-suited for
042   emotion interpretation tasks (Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b;
043   Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; 2023; Li et al., 2023b) But due to the lack of existing benchmark,
044
      the performance still can not be measured.
045   To advance research in this area, we established the Emotion Interpretation Benchmark (EIBench)
046   (Figure 1), which includes 4 primary emotions of 1615 basic EI samples, and 50 complex multifaceted
047   EI samples, such as combinations of happiness and sadness (Figure 1 (e)). We also proposed an
048   VLLM-assisted data annotation scheme, the Coarse-to-Fine Self-Ask (CFSA) method, which employs
049   the Chain of Thought (CoT) approach (Press et al., 2022; Madaan et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Besta
050
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; 2022) to guide LLMs in preliminary annotation.
051   We conducted a comprehensive assessment of commonly used open-source and closed-source
052   models to thoroughly evaluate different models’ abilities in the EI task. There are 4 evaluating
053   settings: 1) zero-shot to user questions, 2) combining image captions with user questions, and 3)
      reasoning with CoT, 4) LLM under different personas. The experiments indicate that existing models

      2     R ELATED W ORK
085   We review the relevant literature in emotion recognition, emotion cause extraction, humor studies,
086   and chain-of-thought prompting techniques, providing context for our work on interpreting emotion.

088   2.1     C ONTEXT AWARE E MOTION R ECOGNITION
089
090   Context Aware Emotion Recognition (CAER) goes beyond Facial Expression Recognition (FER),
091   which focuses solely on perceiving emotion via the face (Wang et al., 2020b;a; Vo et al., 2020; Zheng
092   et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023c; Cheng et al., 2023). CAER considers the emotional
093
      cues provided by background contexts, integrating facial and body language in a joint and boosting
      manner (Kosti et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2023a; Xenos et al., 2024; Bhattacharya et al., 2020; Ruan
094
      et al., 2020; Mittal et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019). Various methods
095
      have contributed to this area. For example, (Kosti et al., 2017) established the EMOTIC dataset and
096   proposed a baseline that combines the body region and the whole image as the context. (Lee et al.,
097   2019) proposed a dataset derived from movies with human social context. (Yang et al., 2023a) built a
098   context dictionary based on clusters of visual features to identify meaningful patterns using visual
099   context. Additionally, (Xenos et al., 2024) explored CAER with the commonsense knowledge from
100   VLLMs, achieving SOTA on EMOTIC (Kosti et al., 2017) and CAER-S (Lee et al., 2019) datasets.
101
      2.2     E XPLAINABLE E MOTION R ECOGNITION WITH LLM S
102
103   Large Language Models (LLMs) have been increasingly used for emotion recognition due to their
104   world knowledge (Ouyang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Fei et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023). In NLP
105   study, (Fei et al., 2023) used Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting to recognize implicit emotions,
106   while (Lei et al., 2023) designed a retrieval-based system for conversational emotion recognition.
107   The development of Vision LLMs (VLLMs) (Liu et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a) has
      further expanded the application of LLMs in emotion recognition (Cheng et al., 2024b). However,


                                                                                                   2

108
      due to the lack of emotion-related datasets, the capability of LLMs in this area is limited. To
109   address this, (Xie et al., 2024) employed visual instruction tuning at various emotion data for better
110   performance, (Xenos et al., 2024) used VLLMs to extract commonsense context, combining it
111   with image data to train a transformer model, and (Fei et al., 2023) utilized the CoT approach
112   to incrementally guide models through emotion tasks. While these works focus on identifying
113   emotion types, this research aims to interpret the implicit emotion triggers behind human emotions,
114   comprehending the formation of emotions.

      2.3   H UMOR S TUDY
116
117   Humor is an integral part of human life and has been the focus of extensive research (Chandrasekaran
118   et al., 2016; Hwang & Shwartz, 2023; Hessel et al., 2023; Hyun et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b;
119   Hasan et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2015; Annamoradnejad & Zoghi, 2020; Hasan et al., 2021). For
120   instance, (Chandrasekaran et al., 2016) examined the elements or characteristics within cartoon
121   scenes that contribute to humor. Similarly, Memecap (Hwang & Shwartz, 2023) compiled a dataset
122   of 6.3K visual memes with visual metaphors to facilitate meme interpretation. (Hessel et al., 2023)
123
      tested the ability of large language models (LLMs) to understand humor using a subset of the New
      Yorker Cartoon Caption Contest. Additionally, (Hyun et al., 2023) introduced the Video Laugh
124
      Reasoning task to explain why people laugh in specific videos. (Chen et al., 2023b) investigated
125
      pretrained LLMs’ ability to understand Chinese humor, which may also aid future research in humor
126   generation. Humor study delves into the elements that evoke laughter or amusement, interpreting the
127   specific triggers of humor. Our EI task aims to interpreting the triggers behind a broader spectrum of
128   emotional responses, not limited to amusement but extending to various emotional states.
129
130
      2.4   E MOTION C AUSE E XTRACTION
131   Emotion Cause Extraction aims to identify the triggers lead to emotions was first proposed in text
132   domain (Lee et al., 2010). For a more accurate identification, researchers propose Emotion Cause
133   Pair Extraction (ECPE) that use multi-task learning frameworks to simultaneously predict emotions
134   and their causes (Xia & Ding, 2019). Wang et al. (2024) further push it into multi-modal domain at
135   SemEval challenge in conversation, for using multimodal input to identify the corresponding emotion
136   trigger with current speaker. Zhang et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2024b) won the challenge by using
137   powerful LLM based method InstructERC (Lei et al., 2023) for the context understanding. Our task
138   builds upon ECE for we not only looking at what can be found in the input explicitly, but more
139
      behind the picture. The EI task not only identify the triggers but also interpret them, needing deeper
      understand of the whole context, as well as the common sense knowledge.
140
141   2.5   C HAIN OF T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
142
143   Chain of Thought (CoT) Prompting enhances problem-solving by breaking complex tasks into
144   manageable, sequential steps, improving both accuracy and transparency (Press et al., 2022; Madaan
145
      et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; 2022). (Press et al., 2022) proposed
      the Self-Ask method, where LLMs generate and answer their own sub-questions to solve a larger
146
      problem. (Zhang et al., 2023) extended CoT to multimodal tasks, using a two-step approach where
147
      LLMs first generate a rationale and then use it for reasoning. (Zhang et al., 2022) introduced a method
148   for LLMs to solve tasks step-by-step in a one-by-one manner. We propose a Coarse-to-Fine Self-Ask
149   method to guide VLLMs in assisting the EIBench annotation. This method progresses from general
150   to scenario-specific perspectives, gradually deepening the understanding of emotional triggers, thus
151   extending the application of CoT techniques to the domain of emotion interpretation.

      Emotional Trigger We define an emotional trigger to stimulus that provokes an emotional response
200
      in an individual (Table 2). These triggers can include atmospheres, such as a dimly lit room, and
201   social interactions like arguments. Body movements and facial expressions, although not triggers
202   themselves, play a crucial role in communicating emotions and can amplify the effect of other
203   triggers. Objects with sentimental value, performances in music or theater, outdoor activities like
204   hiking, clothing choices, and sports events all play significant roles in triggering emotions.
205
      While many of these triggers can be visually depicted, there are also implicit triggers that can evoke
206
      emotions beyond the image, such as an athlete’s adrenaline rush or the special feelings associated with
207   people and scenes. We categorize these as “other” triggers. These varied triggers can deeply influence
208   an individual’s emotional state, often subconsciously, shaping their reactions and interactions.

318
319   Emotional Trigger Recall and Long-term Coherence Given the subjective nature of emotions,
320   multiple triggers could elicit a particular response, and some may be missed despite thorough reviews.
321   Therefore, we use Recall as one evaluation metric. If the model’s interpretation overlaps with our

      6    C ONCLUSION
512
513
514   In this paper, we introduce the Emotion Interpretation (EI) task, which focuses on interpreting
515   emotional triggers rather than merely labeling emotions. We establish the Emotion Interpretation
516   Benchmark (EIBench) using a VLLM-assisted construction method, Coarse-to-Fine Self-Ask (CFSA),
517   consisting of 1,615 basic EI samples, and 50 well-annotated multifaceted complex EI samples.
518
      Extensive experiments evaluating commonly used open-source and closed-source models demonstrate
      that these models have limited proficiency in the EI task. Some are even performing lower than the
519
      text-only LLaMA-3 model in the caption-provided setting. This task not only enhances EI but also
520
      provides a metric for evaluating the emotional intelligence of VLLMs. By considering emotions
521   from multiple perspectives, it aids in analyzing implicit emotions and advances the field of emotion
522   recognition. Additionally, our annotation method facilitates emotion reasoning dataset development
523   and provides a valuable resource for multi-turn dialogue in emotion research.
524
525
526   7    L IMITATION AND S OCIAL I MPACT
527
528
529   Interpreting emotions is inherently challenging due to the subjective nature of personal experiences.
530   It is an open-world problem that requires a nuanced understanding, which is difficult to achieve
531
      with current methodologies. Therefore, we encourage the research community to explore innovative
      approaches to enhance our collective comprehension of emotional diversity. Our annotation process,
532
      which is augmented by VLLM-assisted tools, is conducted by a team of four annotators. While this
533
      approach provides valuable insights, it may also introduce potential biases. The significant costs
534   associated with large-scale annotation in EIBench have constrained the range of emotions we can
535   explore as well. Besides, EIBench at this time is only for the purpose of assessment, not fine-tuning.
536   To mitigate these issues, we would expand our pool of annotators to include a more diverse group
537   of individuals from various backgrounds and underrepresented minority groups, as well as increase
538   the scale of EIBench for support training. This expansion will not only enrich our dataset but also
539   ensure that our understanding of emotions is more inclusive and representative of the broader human
      experience.

      MiniGPT-v2 Chen et al. (2023a) is a sophisticated multimodal language model that stands out for
      its unified interface capability for diverse vision-language tasks such as image description, visual
837
      question answering, and visual grounding. Its architecture simplifies the integration of high-resolution
838
      visual inputs with a large language model, utilizing a technique that concatenates four neighboring
839   visual tokens, significantly reducing the sequence length and enhancing training efficiency.
840
841   The model’s excellence is attributed to its unique three-stage training strategy. Initially, it undergoes
842
      pretraining with a broad mix of datasets to establish a robust foundation in vision-language under-
      standing. This is followed by a multi-task training phase, where the model refines its capabilities on
843
      specific tasks using fine-grained datasets, excluding weakly-supervised data to focus on high-quality
844
      image-text alignment. The final stage involves multi-modal instruction tuning and chatbot enhance-
845   ment, integrating complex datasets to improve the model’s conversational skills and its ability to
846   handle diverse instructions, thus preparing it for real-world applications.
