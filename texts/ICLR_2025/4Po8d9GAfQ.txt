011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities, but still strug-
014            gle with complex reasoning tasks requiring multiple steps. While prompt-based
015            methods like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) can improve LLM reasoning at inference
016
               time, optimizing reasoning capabilities during training remains challenging. We
               introduce LaTent Reasoning Optimization (LaTRO), a principled framework
017
               that formulates reasoning as sampling from a latent distribution and optimizes it
018
               via variational approaches. LaTRO enables LLMs to concurrently improve both
019            their reasoning process and ability to evaluate reasoning quality, without requiring
020            external feedback or reward models. We validate LaTRO through experiments
021            on GSM8K and ARC-Challenge datasets using multiple model architectures. On
022            GSM8K, LaTRO improves zero-shot accuracy by an average of 12.5% over base
023            models and 9.6% over supervised fine-tuning across Phi-3.5-mini, Mistral-7B, and
024            Llama-3.1-8B. Our findings suggest that pre-trained LLMs possess latent reasoning
025            capabilities that can be unlocked and enhanced through our proposed optimization
026            approach in a self-improvement manner.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The development of large language models (LLMs) with enhanced reasoning capabilities has emerged
032   as a crucial area of research. Despite their impressive advances, the inherent next-token prediction
033
      mechanism of LLMs makes it challenging for these models to solve complex problems requiring
      multiple reasoning steps (Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023). For instance, LLMs often struggle
034
      to directly provide accurate solutions to mathematical problems or even simple puzzles like counting
035
      specific letters in a word. Consequently, researchers have explored various prompting strategies
036   that guide LLMs to generate reasoning trajectories or rationales—sequences of tokens that build a
037   step-by-step progression toward an answer. Techniques such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al.,
038   2022), Tree-of-Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), and Program-of-Thought (PoT) (Chen et al., 2023)
039   prompting methods exemplify this approach.
040
      Recent progress has also focused on inference-time techniques to enhance the reasoning abilities of
041
      LLMs (Wu et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2024), as observed in the OpenAI o1 model (OpenAI, 2024).
042   These methods have demonstrated remarkable performance in diverse reasoning tasks, including
043   mathematics (Cobbe et al., 2021b; Trinh et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024), coding (Jimenez et al., 2023;
044   Guo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), and scientific problem-solving (Rein et al., 2023). Notable
045   inference-time methods, such as CoT with Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) (Wang et al., 2023) and
046   CoT-Decoding (Wang & Zhou, 2024), extend the CoT approach by generating multiple reasoning
047   paths and selecting the most consistent one. Additionally, techniques like ReAct (Yao et al., 2023a)
048   and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) integrate reasoning into LLM agent loops, further enhancing their
049   problem-solving capabilities.
050   Despite the promising results at inference time, improving the reasoning abilities of LLMs during their
051   training phase remains a challenging problem. Several obstacles impede progress in this area. Firstly,
052   there is a scarcity of high-quality reasoning data for complex problems, limiting the applicability
053   of traditional supervised fine-tuning (SFT) approaches (Zelikman et al., 2022). Moreover, when
      such data is available, SFT on deterministic reasoning paths may result in a lack of diversity in

080
081   Self-improvement approaches like STaR (Self-Taught Reasoner) (Zelikman et al., 2022) and Quiet-
082   STaR (Zelikman et al., 2024) have shown promise in enhancing language models’ reasoning ca-
083   pabilities without external feedback. However, STaR relies on task-specific few-shot examples to
084   bootstrap its reasoning process, which can limit its generalizability across diverse tasks. While
085
      Quiet-STaR attempts to overcome this by inferring implicit rationales across arbitrary text, it does not
      directly optimize the reasoning process itself. Through these findings, we observe that pretrained
086
      LLMs already possess innate reasoning capabilities but just have not been fully activated or utilized,
087
      inspiring us to propose our approach.
088
089   Our proposed method, LaTent Reasoning Optimization (LaTRO), addresses the limitations of
090   previous approaches by formulating reasoning as sampling from a latent distribution and optimizing
091
      it through a principled variational framework. As illustrated in Fig. 1, LaTRO enables language
      models to concurrently improve both their reasoning process and ability to evaluate reasoning quality,
092
      without requiring task-specific few-shot examples or external reward models. Key contributions of
093
      LaTRO include:
094
095
       1. A theoretical formulation connecting LLM reasoning optimization to latent variable models;
096
097
098    2. A self-rewarding mechanism leveraging the model’s own probability estimates;
099
100    3. Significant performance gains across multiple model architectures and reasoning tasks, demon-
101       strating LaTRO’s effectiveness in unlocking latent reasoning capabilities of language models.
102
103   Our findings suggest that pre-trained LLMs are not only capable reasoners but also possess the
104   potential to act as explicit reward models for evaluating reasoning paths. We term this approach of
105   utilizing explicit reward functions induced by LLMs themselves as "self-rewarding." Empirically,
106   LaTRO outperforms both baseline models and supervised fine-tuning approaches on reasoning tasks
107   like GSM8K, while also demonstrating the capacity to compress reasoning processes and shift
      computational burdens from inference to training time.

      2       R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Prompt-based LLM Reasoning Prompt-based reasoning methods prove to be effective across
111   various domains, such as math problem-solving (Polu & Sutskever, 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2021;
112   Cobbe et al., 2021a), logical reasoning (Sprague et al., 2024) and agentic tasks (Yao et al., 2023a;
113   Shinn et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023b). Chain-of-Thoughts or CoT (Wei et al., 2022) is the pioneering
114   work that prompts LLMs to decompose challenging tasks into smaller reasoning steps. After that, two
115   primary research directions further improved reasoning capabilities during inference. One direction
116   searched over the reasoning trajectories against a process-based verifier, or reward model (Yao et al.,
117   2024; Besta et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2023). For example, tree-of-thoughts (Yao et al., 2024)
118   explored over thoughts by depth-first search (DFS), breadth-first search (BFS) or beam search. The
119
      other approach used a critic model to provide verbal feedback, iteratively refining the responses with
      that feedback (Saunders et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023b; Madaan et al., 2023).
120
121
      Self-Rewarding for LLM Reasoning Reasoning capabilities in LLMs can be enhanced in post-
122
      training through self-rewarding and reinforcement learning. The Self-Taught Reasoner, or STaR (Ze-
123
      likman et al., 2022) introduced a bootstrapping technique that allows LLMs to generate rationales
124   and fine-tune itself with self-generated reasoning paths. Quiet-STaR (Zelikman et al., 2024) extended
125   this by training LLMs to infer implicit rationales across arbitrary text, enhancing both reasoning
126   and predictive abilities without task-specific fine-tuning. Reinforced Fine-Tuning, or ReFT (Trung
127   et al., 2024) took this further by leveraging reinforcement learning to improve generalization in
128   reasoning tasks like math problem-solving, enabling LLMs to learn from multiple reasoning paths.
129   Self-correction capabilities in LLMs can also be reinforced through self-generated data (Kumar et al.,
130   2024). Lastly, Hoffman et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2023) formulated the reasoning process as latent
131   variable models, aligning LLMs towards more accurate reasoning with fewer annotated data.

296   4.3   P RACTICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
297
298   To reduce computation overhead and better control the sampling of reasoning rationales during
299   training, we limit their maximum token length to L. The rationale ends either at the EOS token or at
300   the start of a predefined answer template (e.g., "The answer is"). We then use the truncated rationale
301   z , along with the query x and the answer z , for further computation.
302   We also encourage the LLM to finish its reasoning process with L tokens. Inspired by the implementa-
303   tion of the RLOO trainer in the TRL library (von Werra et al., 2020) , we introduce a constant penalty
304   for rationales truncated by the maximum token length L. This penalty encourages the generation of
305   rationales that fit within the specified token limit.

462       twice as high as Binkie’s. If Binkie bowled a score of 90, what was Frankie’s score? Let’s
463       think step by step.
464       Response (Base model): Let’s break this problem down into steps:
465       1. We know that the difference between Frankie’s score (F) and Binkie’s score (B) is 15. So
466       we can express Frankie’s score as F = B + 15
467       2. We also know that Frankie’s score is twice as high as Binkie’s score. So we can write
468       another equation: F = 2B
469       3. Now, we have two equations with two unknowns. Let’s solve for Frankie’s score (F) by
470       setting the two equations equal to each other: - 2B = B + 15 - Multiply both sides by 1 to get
471       rid of the fraction: 2B - B = 15 - Simplify: B = 15 (since subtracting B from 2B results in 15)
472       4. Now that we know Binkie’s score, we can find Frankie’s score using either of the equations
473       we created earlier. Let’s use the first one (F = B + 15): - F = 15 + 15 - Frankie’s score is 30
474       more than Binkie’s score, which is 15 in this case. So Frankie’s score is 15 + 15 = 30 + 15 =

497   6     C ONCLUSION
498
499   In conclusion, this work introduces LaTRO, a principled framework for optimizing language models’
500   reasoning capabilities without external feedback or reward models. By formulating reasoning as
501   sampling from a latent distribution and leveraging self-rewarding, LaTRO enables models to concur-
502   rently improve both their reasoning process and ability to evaluate reasoning quality. Our extensive
503
      experiments across multiple model architectures and tasks demonstrate significant performance gains,
      with LaTRO outperforming baseline models and supervised fine-tuning approaches. These findings
504
      suggest that pre-trained LLMs possess latent reasoning capabilities that can be unlocked through our
505
      proposed optimization approach, representing a significant step towards creating more intelligent
506   systems that can self-evolve their problem-solving capabilities.
507
508   While LaTRO shows promising results, there are some limitations to consider. The computational
509
      cost of sampling multiple rationales during training could be prohibitive for very large models. Future
      work could explore ways to reduce this computational overhead, such as using more efficient sampling
510
      techniques or adaptive rationale generation. Other promising directions include investigating the
511
      applicability of LaTRO to a wider range of reasoning tasks beyond math and science problems, and
512   exploring how to conduct multi-step reasoning learning to to enhance reasoning capabilities further.
513   Despite these limitations, our contributions advance both the state-of-the-art in LLM reasoning
514   capabilities and provide valuable insights into the nature of LLM alignment and its potential for
515   self-improvement.

889       Step 2: Calculate the total number of regular and discounted glasses. Kylar wants to buy 16
890       glasses in total. Since every second glass is discounted, we can divide the total number of

899       4. Since he needs 16 glasses, he buys 8 pairs. 5. Therefore, Kylar needs to pay 8 pairs * $8
900       per pair = $64. So, Kylar needs to pay $64 for the 16 glasses. The answer is 64.

928      coldest to hottest?
929      Options: [’ice, water, steam’, ’ice, steam, water’,’steam, ice, water’,’steam, water, ice’]
930      Response (Base model): To determine the order from coldest to hottest, let’s evaluate each
931      option.
932      - Ice: It is the solid form of water and is typically around 0°C (32°F).
933      - Water: It is the liquid form of water and is usually around 100°C (212°F) or 0°C (32°F) at
934      standard pressure and temperature conditions, respectively.
935      - Steam: It is the gaseous form of water and is typically at a temperature equal to or greater
936      than 100°C (212°F) at standard pressure conditions, depending on the pressure and other
937      factors.
938      Option 1: ’ice, water, steam’ - This order seems incorrect since steam is typically hotter than
939      water at standard conditions.
940      Option 2: ’ice, steam, water’ - This order is incorrect because ice is the coldest, but the relative
941      order of water and steam is opposite of what we know.
942      Option 3:’steam, ice, water’ - This order is incorrect since ice is colder than steam.
943      Option 4:’steam, water, ice’ - This order seems the most plausible, considering that steam is
944      hotter than water and water is hotter than ice.
945      The answer is ’steam, water, ice’.
946      Response (SFT): water, ice, steam
947      Response (LaTRO): To determine the order from coldest to hottest, let’s analyze each option:
948      1. Ice has a solid form and a fixed temperature, which is typically around 0°C (32°F) at
949      standard pressure.
950      2. Water is liquid and exists at a temperature of 0°C (32°F) at standard pressure, melting point
951      of ice.
952      3. Steam is the gaseous state of water and is typically around 100°C (212°F) at standard
953      pressure.

         The answer is [’ice, water, steam’].
