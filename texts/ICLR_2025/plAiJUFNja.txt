011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a groundbreaking unified theory for drug-drug interaction (DDI) aware
014            domain adaptation (DA) in the context of drug synergy prediction. Our frame-
015            work seamlessly integrates concepts from optimal transport, information geome-
016
               try, and quantum information theory within the setting of abstract Banach spaces.
               We introduce a novel DDI-aware optimal transport problem, formulated as a
017
               geodesic equation on an infinite-dimensional Finsler manifold that encodes both
018
               DDI structure and optimal transport costs. This geometric formulation provides
019            a unified perspective on DDI-aware domain adaptation, interpreting the process
020            as the evolution of a transport map along a geodesic in a space that captures
021            both domain discrepancy and drug interaction patterns. Our approach extends
022            to a stochastic gradient flow on the space of probability measures, combining
023            ideas from information geometry and stochastic analysis. We prove the exis-
024            tence of a unique invariant measure for this flow and establish its convergence
025            properties using techniques from infinite-dimensional Markov processes and con-
026            vergence. Our comprehensive mathematical framework not only unifies exist-
027
               ing approaches to domain adaptation and DDI prediction but also opens new av-
               enues for research at the intersection of these fields. By bridging the gap be-
028
               tween abstract mathematical theories and practical drug synergy prediction, our
029
               work paves the way for more effective and theoretically grounded algorithms
030            in drug discovery and personalized medicine. The proposed unified theory has
031            far-reaching implications, potentially revolutionizing our understanding of cross-
032            domain adaptation in complex biochemical systems and inspiring novel com-
033            putational methods in pharmaceutical research. Our anonymous gitHub link:
034            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CGSP-F518

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   The advent of combination therapies has revolutionized the treatment landscape across a wide spec-
040   trum of medical conditions, including cancer Crystal et al. (2014), infectious diseases Zheng et al.
041   (2018), cardiovascular disorders Giles et al. (2014), and autoimmune diseases Smilek et al. (2014).
042   By synergistically combining drugs with distinct mechanisms of action, these therapies offer the po-
043   tential for enhanced efficacy and reduced side effects. However, the identification of effective drug
044
      combinations remains a formidable challenge, given the astronomical number of possible pairings
      and the complexity of drug-drug interactions (DDIs).
045
046   Traditional approaches to drug combination discovery, relying heavily on clinical intuition and em-
047   pirical trials, are insufficient to explore the vast combinatorial space of potential therapies. This
048   limitation has spurred interest in computational methods for predicting effective drug combinations.
049   However, existing computational approaches often struggle with the high-dimensional nature of
050
      the problem, the scarcity of reliable negative samples, and the challenge of transferring knowledge
      across different disease domains.
051
052   In this paper, we present a groundbreaking unified theory that addresses these challenges by seam-
053   lessly integrating drug-drug interaction (DDI) awareness into the domain adaptation (DA) frame-
      work. Our approach represents a paradigm shift in computational drug discovery, leveraging ad-

      vanced mathematical concepts from optimal transport, information geometry, and quantum infor-
055   mation theory to create a robust and flexible framework for predicting drug synergies across diverse
056   disease domains. At the heart of our theory lies a novel formulation of the DDI-aware optimal trans-
057   port problem. We extend the classical optimal transport framework to incorporate DDI information,
058   defining a cost function that not only measures the discrepancy between drug distributions in dif-
059   ferent domains but also accounts for the preservation of DDI structures. This formulation allows
060   us to capture the complex interplay between drug interactions and domain-specific characteristics, a
061   crucial aspect often overlooked in previous approaches. Building upon this foundation, we introduce

064
      resulting geodesic equation encapsulates both the optimal transport dynamics and the DDI preserva-

      tion constraints, offering a powerful tool for analyzing and optimizing drug combination predictions
066   across domains.

068   To address the inherent uncertainty and variability in biological systems, we develop a stochastic
069

      dimensional Markov processes, allows us to explore the space of potential transport maps while
070
      simultaneously minimizing domain discrepancy and preserving DDI structure. We prove the exis-

      tence of a unique invariant measure for this flow and establish its convergence properties, providing

073
074   Furthermore, we establish connections between our DDI-aware optimal transport formulation and
075
      quantum information theory. By defining a quantum version of the DDI-aware optimal transport
      problem, we gain insights into the fundamental limits of domain adaptation in the presence of drug-
076
      drug interactions. This quantum perspective opens new avenues for exploring the role of entan-
077
      glement and non-locality in drug combination prediction, potentially leading to quantum-inspired
078   algorithms for pharmaceutical research. Our unified theory culminates in the derivation of a non-
079   linear integro-differential equation that governs the evolution of drug distributions across domains.
080   This PDE formulation unifies concepts from optimal transport, DDI preservation, and domain adap-
081   tation within a single evolutionary equation, connecting our work to the rich theory of nonlinear
082   transport equations and mean-field games. Additionally, we establish a large deviation principle for
083   the DDI-aware optimal transport cost, offering asymptotic guarantees on its concentration around
084   the population limit and providing insights into the sample complexity of our domain adaptation
085   framework.
086   The comprehensive mathematical framework we present not only unifies existing approaches to
087   domain adaptation and DDI prediction but also opens new avenues for research at the intersection of
088   these fields. By bridging the gap between abstract mathematical theories and practical drug synergy
089   prediction, our work paves the way for more effective and theoretically grounded algorithms in drug
090   discovery and personalized medicine. Our approach addresses several key limitations of previous
091   computational methods for drug combination prediction:
092   1. Reliable negative samples: Unlike previous studies that often rely on randomly generated negative
093   samples, we leverage existing DDI data as a credible source of negative examples, enhancing the
094   robustness of our predictions.
095
      2. Domain adaptation: Our framework explicitly addresses the challenge of transferring knowledge
096   across different disease domains, a critical aspect for developing broadly applicable drug combina-

103   underlying model architectures, making it applicable to diverse drug combination prediction scenar-
104   ios.

117   1.1.1    M ETHODOLOGIES FOR S CREENING D RUG S YNERGIES

119
      High-throughput screening (HTS) methodologies depend on HTS datasets, which primarily assess
      cellular viability in response to anti-cancer drug treatments. The degree of therapeutic synergy can
120
      be evaluated using various metrics, such as Loewe additivity Loewe (1953), Bliss independence
121
      Bliss (1939), the highest single agent Berenbaum (1989), or zero interaction potency Yadav et al.
122   (2015). In studies focusing on drug combinations utilizing HTS data, the inputs typically consist of
123   triplets in the form of (drug1, drug2, cell line). Several HTS databases are publicly accessible. For
124   example, O’Neil et al. O’Neil et al. (2016) introduced a cancer drug combination dataset encom-
125   passing 22,737 experiments, which includes 583 pairwise drug combinations tested across 39 cancer
126   cell lines. Another significant resource is NCI-ALMANAC Holbeck et al. (2017), a large-scale HTS
127   dataset that provides synergy measurements for pairwise combinations of 104 FDA-approved drugs
128   across 60 cancer cell lines. Additionally, DrugCombDB Liu et al. (2020) offers an extensive repos-
129   itory containing HTS assay data for 448,555 drug combinations, covering 2,887 unique drugs and
130
      124 human cancer cell lines.

      Utilizing DDIs as a negative dataset enhances the precision of combination therapy predictions, as
135   DDIs indicate scenarios where the efficacy or toxicity of one drug is altered when administered
136   alongside another, typically to avoid adverse safety outcomes Paltun et al. (2021). Moreover, SCL is
137   a contrastive learning technique that utilizes label information to draw embeddings of the same class
138   closer while repelling those of different classes Khosla et al. (2020). Studies leveraging knowledge
139   graphs (KGs) utilize databases such as DCDB Liu et al. (2010), DCDB 2.0 Liu et al. (2014), and CD-
140   CDB Shtar et al. (2022). These databases compile lists of drug combinations curated from diverse
141   literature sources and clinical studies, which serve as labeled data for KG-based drug combination
