009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Cognitive maps are internal world models that enable adaptive behavior including
012            spatial navigation and planning. The Cognitive Map Learner (CML) has been re-
013            cently proposed as a model for cognitive map formation and planning. A CML
014            learns high dimensional state and action representations using local prediction
015            learning. While the CML offers a simple and elegant solution to cognitive map
016            learning, it is limited by its simplicity, applying only to fully observable envi-
017
               ronments. To address this, we introduce the Partially Observable Cognitive Map
               Learner (POCML), extending the CML to handle partially observable environ-
018
               ments.
019
               The POCML employs a superposition of states for probabilistic representation and
020
               uses binding operations for state updates. Additionally, an associative memory is
021
               incorporated to enable adaptive behavior across environments with similar struc-
022            tures. We derive local update rules tailored to the POCML’s probabilistic state
023            representation and associative memory. We demonstrate a POCML is capable of
024            learning the underlying structure of an environment via local next-observation pre-
025            diction learning. In addition, we show that a POCML trained on an environment
026            is capable of generalizing to environments with the same underlying structure
027            but with novel observations, achieving good zero-shot next-observation predic-
028            tion accuracy, significantly outperforming sequence models such as LSTMs and
029            Transformers. Finally, we present a case study of navigation in a two-tunnel maze
030
               environment with aliased observations, showing that a POCML is capable of ef-
               fectively using its probabilistic state representations for disambiguation of states
031
               and spatial navigation.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Cognitive maps are central to the adaptive behavior of intelligent agents, enabling capabilities rang-
037   ing from spatial navigation to planning to reasoning and abstraction; they are internal world models
038   that allow agents to predict the consequences of their actions (Behrens et al., 2018). Known to be
039   responsible for spatial representations in the brain (Eichenbaum, 2017; Behrens et al., 2018), the
040   hippocampal formation (HF), consisting of the hippocampus and the entorhinal cortex, is a primary
041   source of inspiration for models of cognitive map learning. While we do not know how exactly the
042   brain implements cognitive maps, there are various theoretical models of how this might be done at
043   an algorithmic level (Whittington et al., 2020; George et al., 2021; Stöckl et al., 2024).
044   Recently, Stöckl et al. (2024) proposed the Cognitive Map Learner (CML), a simple model that can
045   learn high-dimensional representations of states and actions that reflect the structure of the underly-
046   ing environment using local synaptic plasticity rules that minimize next-state prediction error. Using
047   these learned representations, a CML can subsequently perform online planning to reach a goal state
048   via a simple action selection heuristic; i.e. by choosing the action most similar to the vector formed
049   by the difference between the goal state and current state. Compared to probabilistic models, such
050
      as the Tolman-Eichenbaum machine (TEM) (Whittington et al., 2020) and clone-structured cog-
      nitive graph (CSCG) (George et al., 2021), CML is more computationally efficient, geometrically
051
      interpretable, and only requires local learning rules to train.
052
053   However, CML is limited by its simplicity, applying only to fully observable environments where
      observations unambiguously distinguish different states. In comparison, models such as TEM and


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      CSCG are more expressive and can handle partially observable environments due to their probabilis-
055   tic nature; that said, they are limited by their computational cost and limited interpretability.
056
057   To fill in this research gap, we introduce the Partially Observable CML (POCML), an extension of a
058
      CML to partially observation environments. A POCML leverages random Fourier features to enable
      the representation of a state in superposition. The superposition of states is used to represent states in
059
      a probabilistic manner, to which a binding operation is used for updating these states simultaneously
060
      and independently. To decouple the underlying environment structure and state-observation relation,
061   we endow the POCML with a memory storing state-observation associations to enable adaptive
062   behavior in different environments with the same underlying structure and derive corresponding
063   local update rules that take the new probabilistic state representation and associative memory into
064   consideration.
065
      We demonstrate that when presented with sequences of actions and observations of random walks in
066
      an environment, a POCML is capable of learning the underlying structure via local next-observation
067   prediction learning. In addition, we show that a POCML trained on an environment is capable of
068   generalizing to environments with the same underlying structure but with novel observations, achiev-
069   ing good zero-shot next-observation prediction accuracy, significantly outperforming sequence mod-
070   els such as LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017).
071   Finally, we present a case study of navigation in a two-tunnel maze environment with aliased obser-
072   vations, showing that a POCML is capable of effectively using its probabilistic state representations
073   for disambiguation of states and spatial navigation.

079   Given a sequence of observations ot and actions at produced by an agent acting in an environment,
080   a CML learns to predict the next observation based on the current observation and action taken by
081
      the agent (Stöckl et al., 2024).
082   In a CML, observations ot ∈ Rno and actions at ∈ Rna are represented as one-hot vectors and
083   are embedded into a common high dimensional state space S ⊆ Rn via embedding matrices Q ∈
084   Rn×no and V ∈ Rn×na , respectively. Here, no is the number of observations, na is the number of
085   actions, and n is the dimension of the state
                                             √ space. Both Q and V have entries sampled i.i.d. from
086   N (0, 1) and are then normalized by 1/ n.

130   3    CML S IN PARTIALLY O BSERVABLE E NVIRONMENTS
131
132   A CML operates within a fully observable environment; i.e. it assumes that one can exactly infer
133   which state one is in just from a given observation. This assumption is reflected as a bijection
134   between observations ot and states st : each observation corresponds exactly to one column of Q.
135   However, most environments do not have this property; they are partially observable. In this section,
136   we introduce an extension of a CML that can operate in partially observable environments, the
137   Partially Observable CML (POCML).
138
139   T WO L EVELS OF R EPRESENTATION
140
141   The POCML model considers two levels of representation: (1) the “standard” level as in a regular
142   CML, and (2) the “Fourier” level using random Fourier features. This extension of coupled repre-
143
      sentation is motivated by the need to maintain the straightforward geometric interpretation of CML
      while enabling the superposition of states.
144
145   In our model, we wish to represent uncertainty as a superposition of states. As a naive attempt, we
146   first consider directly superposing the states. If the model is unsure whether it is in state s1 or s2 ,
147   it can represent its estimated state as ŝt = s1 + s2 . However, if we apply Eq. 2 to predict the next
148   state, we obtain
149                                    ŝt+1 = ŝt + Vat = s1 + s2 + Vat .                                  (8)
150
      This causes an issue both in learning and in interpretation because the superposition of states shares
151
      the same operation as a state transition. To address this issue, we propose to use binding instead of
152
      addition to predict the next state, i.e.
153
154                                                ŝt+1 = st ⊙ Vat ,                                       (9)
155   then we can exploit the distributivity of the binding operation:
156
157
                                   ŝt+1 = ŝt ⊙ Vat = s1 ⊙ Vat + s2 ⊙ Vat .                               (10)
158
      This approach does not guarantee the same geometric properties intrinsic to the standard represen-
159
      tation.
160
161   To have the best of both worlds, we apply the random feature map ϕ to the standard representation s
      to obtain a Fourier representation s. The state superposition and state update of Eq. 10 is applied to

      N EXT OBSERVATION PREDICTION IN GRID AND TREE ENVIRONMENTS

311   We test the POCML model on both grid and tree environments. An environment can be represented
312   as a directed graph whose nodes are states and edges are actions. Thus, the underlying structure
313   of an environment is defined by its state-action transitions. Here, we consider environments in
314   which actions in general have the same effect across states (e.g. going up does the same thing in
315   every state of a grid). When traversing the environment, an agent receives different observations in
316   different states defined by a state-observation mapping. Two instances of an environment have the
317   same underlying structure but can have different state-observation mappings. We sample trajectories
                           −1
318   {(xt , at , xt+1 )}Tt=1 from the environment by choosing an initial state in the environment uniformly
319   randomly, then choosing uniformly random actions. Figure 1A and 1B visualize a 3-by-3 grid
320
      environment and a 3-level tree environment respectively along with example trajectories.
321   Given a collection of trajectories from an environment, we train the POCML model by applying the
322   update rules given in Eqs. 33 and 34. As mentioned in the previous section, doing so minimizes
323   the cross entropy between the predicted and actual observations, effectively performing local next
      observation prediction.

411   POCMLs generalize to environments with different observations in a zero-shot manner We
412   test the zero-shot performance of POCML in environments with novel observations. To do this,
413   we generate multiple instances of the environment, where each instance has the same underly-
414   ing structure (e.g. grid, tree) but with observations that are randomly sampled from a uniform
415   distribution with replacement at each state. For example, in a 3-by-3 grid, we have possible ob-
416
      servations x1 , . . . , x9 . For each state in the grid, we choose the observation by sampling from
      Unif(x1 , . . . , x9 ). Thus, the model initially has no information about what observations to expect
417
      in these instances. The model must traverse the environment to gain information about the state-
418
      observation mapping.
419
420   For each instance of an environment, we generate two trajectories. The model uses the first trajectory
421   for exploration in order to populate its memory about the novel environment. The second trajectory
422
      is used to test the next observation prediction accuracy of the model. The initial state is provided in
      both trajectories to ground the model. Note that there is zero training on these new instances so the
423
      task is zero shot. Figure 3 describes the zero-shot experimental setup.

467   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
468
469   In this work, we introduced the Partially Observable Cognitive Map Learner (POCML), an extension
470   of the Cognitive Map Learner (CML) framework to partially observable environments. The POCML
471   distinguishes between two levels of representation and represents states probabilistically through a
472   superposition of states, which are updated via the binding operation. Additionally, we incorporate
473   an associative memory mechanism to support adaptive behavior across environments with shared
474   underlying structures. We derive local update rules for next-observation prediction that account for
475   both the probabilistic state representation and the associative memory.
476   We demonstrate that a POCML can successfully learn the underlying structure of an environment
477   using these update rules. Furthermore, we show that a POCML trained in one environment gen-
478   eralizes well to new environments with similar structures but novel observations, achieving high
479   zero-shot next-observation prediction accuracy and significantly outperforming models like LSTMs
480   and Transformers. Finally, we perform a case study on spatial navigation in a two-tunnel maze
481   with aliased observations. We show that the POCML effectively leverages its probabilistic state
482
      representations for state disambiguation and spatial navigation.
483   For future work, it is of interest to extend the POCML framework to the continuous domain and
484   adapt the update rules to support a trainable observation encoding component. Given that a POCML
485   effectively learns a cognitive map of the environment, we would also like to investigate its integration
      with model-based reinforcement learning methods.

590   Comparison against other cognitive map models TEM is a computational model of the
591   hippocampal-entorhinal system (Whittington et al., 2020). Given sequences of sensory inputs and
592   actions in different environments, it can extract the shared structure between the environments. TEM
593   has two types of latent variables: g, which represents the abstract location at a particular point in the
      sequence, and p, which represents a conjunction between sensory observation and abstract location.


                                                         11
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      TEM and POCML are similar in that both have a separation between abstract state and observation,
595   but TEM uses the conjunctive representation p in its memory component, an auto-associative Hop-
596   field network (Hopfield, 1982). In contrast, POCML uses a hetero-associative memory that asso-
597   ciates state and observation implemented as a table of expected counts. In addition, while TEM uses
598   recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to predict the next abstract state, POCML uses the binding oper-
599   ation. While RNNs are more expressive, the binding operation over RFF admits a non-parametric
600   kernel density estimation interpretation of the model. Moreover, the specific choice of Gaussian
601   kernel enables a geometric interpretation of the underlying learned state and action representations:
602   next state prediction can be performed by adding the action representation to the state representation
603
      in this space. That said, TEM does learn grid-cell representations that reflect biology and explain
      the remapping phenomenon; thus, it is valuable from a neuroscience perspective.
604
605   From a computational perspective, in both learning and inference, TEM requires iterative processing
606   to perform memory retrieval and state and observation inference, which produces a sizable compu-
607   tational overhead. POCML does not have this limitation.
608   Algorithmically, there is a comparatively greater difference between CSCG (George et al., 2021)
609   and POCML. Both models can be considered action-augmented hidden Markov models (HMMs)
610   though actions are treated slightly differently in both models. Moreover, CSCG uses a variant of an
611   HMM called a cloned HMM (CHMM). While CSCG represents state-transition probabilities via a
612   state-transition matrix, POCML does this through kernel density estimation (based on RFF). Given
613   that POCML uses vector representations for states and actions, it can easily be extended to larger
614   environments with the same regularity structure simply by keeping the action representations fixed.
615
616   Relation to reinforcement learning It is important to note that POCML learns the structure of
617
      the environment in a reward-free manner, which sets it apart from value-based reinforcement learn-
      ing (RL) techniques. That said, the structure of the environment learned by the POCML encodes
618
      environment dynamics p(s′ |s, a) ∝ δ(ϕ(s′ ), ϕ(s) ⊙ ϕ(a)), which can subsequently be used for
619
      model-based RL. The tunnel-maze experiment in the results section demonstrates a way in which
620   this model of the environment can be used given a policy. It is of interest to investigate the conse-
621   quence of a tighter coupling between model and policy in future work.

      This work has several limitations. First, evaluation was performed on simplistic environments.
      These experiments illustrate the properties and functionality of the POCML model but further work
