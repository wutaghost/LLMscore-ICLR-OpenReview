011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The widespread use of AI-generated content from diffusion models has raised
014            significant concerns regarding misinformation and copyright infringement. Wa-
015
               termarking is a crucial technique for identifying these AI-generated images and
               preventing their misuse. In this paper, we introduce Shallow Diffuse, a new wa-
016
               termarking technique that embeds robust and invisible watermarks into diffusion
017
               model outputs. Unlike existing approaches that integrate watermarking through-
018            out the entire diffusion sampling process, Shallow Diffuse decouples these steps
019            by leveraging the presence of a low-dimensional subspace in the image generation
020            process. This method ensures that a substantial portion of the watermark lies in
021            the null space of this subspace, effectively separating it from the image generation
022            process. Our theoretical and empirical analyses show that this decoupling strategy
023            greatly enhances the consistency of data generation and the detectability of the
024            watermark. Extensive experiments further validate that our Shallow Diffuse out-
025            performs existing watermarking methods in terms of robustness and consistency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b) have recently become a new dominant family
030
      of generative models, powering various commercial applications such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach
031   et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024), DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023), Imagen (Saharia
032   et al., 2022) Stable Audio (Evans et al., 2024) and Sora (Brooks et al., 2024). These models have sig-
033   nificantly advanced the capabilities of text-to-image, text-to-audio, text-to-video, and multi-modal
034   generative tasks. However, the widespread usage of AI-generated content from commercial diffu-
035   sion models on the Internet has raised several serious concerns: (a) AI-generated misinformation
036   presents serious risks to societal stability by spreading unauthorized or harmful narratives on a large
037   scale (Zellers et al., 2019; Goldstein et al., 2023; Brundage et al., 2018); (b) the memorization of
038   training data by those models (Gu et al., 2023; Somepalli et al., 2023a;b; Wen et al., 2023b; Zhang
039
      et al., 2024a) challenges the originality of the generated content and raises potential copyright in-
      fringement issues; (c) Iterative training on AI-generated content, known as model collapse (Fu et al.,
040
      2024; Alemohammad et al., 2024; Dohmatob et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024; Gibney, 2024) can
041
      degrade the quality and diversity of outputs over time, resulting in repetitive, biased, or low-quality
042   generations that may reinforce misinformation and distortions in the wild Internet.
043
044   To deal with these challenges, watermarking is a crucial technique for identifying AI-generated con-
045
      tent and mitigating its misuse. Typically, it can be applied in two main scenarios: (a) the server
      scenario: where given an initial random seed, the watermark is embedded to the image during the
046
      generation process; and (b) the user scenario: where given a generated image, the watermark is
047
      injected in a post-process manner; (as shown in the left two blocks in Figure 3). Traditional water-
048   marking methods (Cox et al., 2007; Solachidis & Pitas, 2001; Chang et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2019)
049   are mainly designed for the user scenario, embedding detectable watermarks directly into images
050   with minimal modification. However, these methods are vulnerable to attacks. For example, the wa-
051   termarks can become undetectable with simple corruptions such as blurring on watermarked images.
052   More recent methods considered the server scenario (Zhang et al., 2024c; Fernandez et al., 2023;
053   Wen et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024; Ci et al., 2024), where they improve robustness by integrating
      watermarking into the sampling process of diffusion models. For example, the work (Ci et al., 2024;

087   marking from the sampling process (see Figure 3 for an illustration). Our theoretical and empirical

089   termark. With better consistency as well as independence from the initial random seed, Shallow
090   Diffuse is flexible for both server and user scenarios.
091
      Our contributions. The proposed Shallow Diffuse offers several key advantages over existing
092   watermarking techniques (Cox et al., 2007; Solachidis & Pitas, 2001; Chang et al., 2005; Liu et al.,
093   2019; Zhang et al., 2024c; Fernandez et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024; Ci et al.,
094   2024) that we highlight below:
095
096   • Flexibility. Watermarking via Shallow Diffuse works seamlessly under both server-side and user-
097     side scenarios. In contrast, most of the previous methods only focus on one scenario without a
098     straightforward extension to the other; see Table 1 and Table 2 for demonstrations.
099
100   • Consistency and Robustness. By decoupling the watermarking from the sampling process, Shal-
101     low Diffuse achieves higher robustness and better consistency. Extensive experiments (Table 1
102     and Table 2 ) support our claims, with extra ablation studies in Figure 5a and Figure 5b .
103
104   • Provable Guarantees. Unlike previous methods, the consistency and detectability of our ap-
105     proach are theoretically justified. Assuming a proper low-dimensional image data distribution
106     (see Assumption 1), we rigorously establish bounds for consistency (Theorem 1) and detectability
107     (Theorem 2).

339   4   T HEORETICAL J USTIFICATION
340
341   In this section, we provide theoretical justifications for the consistency and the detectability of Shal-
342   low Diffuse introduced in Section 3 for unconditional diffusion models. First, we make the following
343   assumptions on the watermark and the diffusion model process.
344   Assumption 1. Suppose the following hold for the PMP fθ,t (xt ):
345
346   • Linearity: For any small t and ∆x ∈ Sd−1 , we always have
347                                fθ,t (xt + λ∆x) = fθ,t (xt ) + λJθ,t (xt )∆x.
348
349   • L-Lipschitz continuous: we assume that fθ,t (x) is a L-Lipschiz continuous at every t:
350                                        ||Jθ,t (x)||2 ≤ L,    ∀x ∈ Rd .
351
352   It should be noted that our assumptions are mild. The L-Lipschitz continuity is a common as-
353   sumption for analysis. The approximated linearity have been shown in (Chen et al., 2024) with the
354   assumption of data distribution to be a mixture of low-rank Gaussians. Here, we assume the linearity
355   to be exact for the ease of analysis, and it can be generalized to approximate linear case.
356   Now consider injecting a watermark λ∆x in Equation (3), where λ > 0 is a scaling factor and ∆x
357   is a random vector uniformly distributed on the unit hypersphere Sd−1 , i.e., ∆x ∼ U(Sd−1 ). Then

      5     E XPERIMENTS
386
      In this section, we present a comprehensive set of experiments to demonstrate the robustness and
387
      consistency of Shallow-Diffuse across various datasets. We begin by highlighting its performance in
388   terms of robustness and consistency in both the server scenario (Section 5.1) and the user scenario
389   (Section 5.2). Additionally, we compare Shallow Diffuse with other related works in the trade-
390   off between robustness and consistency, as detailed in Section 5.3. Moreover, we investigate the
391   effect of timestep t on both robustness and consistency, with results presented in Section 5.4. We
392   further explore the multi-key identification experiments in Appendix C.2. Lastly, we provide an
393   ablation study on watermark pattern design (Appendix C.3), channel averaging (Appendix C.4),
394   watermarking embedded channel (Appendix C.5), and sampling method (Appendix C.6).
395   Baseline For the server scenario, we select the following non-diffusion-based method: DWtDct
396   Cox et al. (2007), DwtDctSvd Cox et al. (2007), RivaGAN Zhang et al. (2019), StegaStamp Tancik
397   et al. (2020); and diffusion-based method: Stable Signature Fernandez et al. (2023), Tree-Ring
398   Watermarks Wen et al. (2023a), RingId Ci et al. (2024), and Gaussian Shading Yang et al. (2024).
399   In the user scenario, we adopt the same baseline methods, except for Stable Signature and Gaussian
400   Shading, as these methods are not suitable for this setting.
401   Datasets We use Stable Diffusion 2-1-base (Rombach et al., 2022) as the underlying model for
402   our experiments, applying Shallow diffusion within its latent space. For the server scenario (Sec-
403   tion 5.1), all diffusion-based methods are based on the same Stable Diffusion, with the original
404   images x0 generated from identical initial seeds xT . Non-diffusion methods are applied to these
405   same original images x0 in a post-watermarking process. A total of 5000 original images are gen-
406
      erated for evaluation in this scenario. For the user scenario (Section 5.2), we utilize the MS-COCO
      Lin et al. (2014), WikiArt Tan et al. (2019), and DiffusionDB datasets Wang et al. (2022). The first
407
      two are real-world datasets, while DiffusionDB is a collection of diffusion model-generated images.
408
      From each dataset, we select 500 images for evaluation. For the remaining experiments in Sec-
409   tion 5.3, Section 5.4, Appendix C, we use the server scenario and sample 100 images for evaluation.
410
411
      Metric To evaluate image consistency under the user scenario, we use peak signal-to-noise ratio
412
      (PSNR) Jähne (2005), structural similarity index measure (SSIM) Wang et al. (2004), and Learned
413
      Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) Zhang et al. (2018), comparing watermarked images to
414   their original counterparts. In the server scenario, we assess the generation quality of the water-
415   marked images using Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining Score (CLIP-Score) Radford et al.
416   (2021) and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) Heusel et al. (2017). To evaluate robustness, we vary
417   the threshold η0 and plot the true positive rate (TPR) against the false positive rate (FPR) for the
418   receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. We use the area under the curve (AUC) and TPR
419   when FPR = 0.01 (TPR @1% FPR) as robustness metrics. Robustness is evaluated both under clean
420   conditions (no attacks) and with various attacks, including JPEG compression, Gaussian blurring,
421   Gaussian noise, and color jitter, Resize and restore, Random drop, median blurring, diffusion pu-
422
      rification Nie et al. (2022), VAE-based image compression models Cheng et al. (2020); Ballé et al.
      (2018) and stable diffusion-based image regeneration Zhao et al. (2023b). We report the average ro-
423
      bustness of these attacks in the main paper. Detailed settings and experiment results of these attacks
424
      are provided in Appendix C.1.

      5.4   R ELATION BETWEEN INJECTING TIMESTEP, CONSISTENCY AND ROBUSTNESS
519
      Figure 5 shows the relationship between the watermark injection timestep t and both consistency
520   and robustness 1 . Shallow Diffuse achieves optimal consistency at t = 0.2T and optimal robustness
521   at t = 0.3T . In practice, we select t = 0.3T . This result aligns with the intuitive idea proposed
522   in Section 3.1 and the theoretical analysis in Section 4: low-dimensionality enhances both data
523   generation consistency and watermark detection robustness. However, according to Chen et al.
524   (2024), the optimal timestep rt for minimizing rt satisfies t∗ ∈ [0.5T, 0.7T ]. We believe the best
525   consistency and robustness are not achieved at t∗ due to the error introduced by DDIM-Inv. As t
526   increases, this error grows, leading to a decline in both consistency and robustness. Therefore, the
527   best tradeoff is reached at t ∈ [0.2T, 0.3T ], where Jθ,t (xt ) remains low-rank but t is still below
528
      t∗ . Another possible explanation is the gap between the image space and latent space in diffusion
      models. The rank curve in Chen et al. (2024) is evaluated for an image-space diffusion model,
529
      whereas Shallow Diffuse operates in the latent-space diffusion model (e.g., Stable Diffusion).

533
534   We proposed Shallow Diffuse, a novel and flexible watermarking technique that operates seamlessly
535   in both server-side and user-side scenarios. By decoupling the watermark from the sampling pro-
536   cess, Shallow Diffuse achieves enhanced robustness and greater consistency. Our theoretical anal-
537
      ysis demonstrates both the consistency and detectability of the watermarks. Extensive experiments
      further validate the superiority of Shallow Diffuse over existing approaches.

779   A.1   I MAGE WATERMARKING
780
781
      Image watermarking has long been a crucial method for protecting intellectual property in computer
      vision (Cox et al., 2007; Solachidis & Pitas, 2001; Chang et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2019). Traditional
782
      techniques primarily focus on user-side watermarking, where watermarks are embedded into images
783
      post-generation. These methods (Al-Haj, 2007; Navas et al., 2008) typically operate in the frequency
784   domain to ensure the watermarks are imperceptible. However, such watermarks remain vulnerable
785   to adversarial attacks and can become undetectable after applying simple image manipulations like
786   blurring.
787
      Early deep learning-based approaches to watermarking (Zhang et al., 2024c; Fernandez et al., 2023;
788
      Ahmadi et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2018) leveraged neural networks to embed water-
789
      marks. While these methods improved robustness and imperceptibility, they often suffer from high
790   computational costs during fine-tuning and lack flexibility. Each new watermark requires additional
791   fine-tuning or retraining, limiting their practicality.
792
793
      More recently, diffusion model-based watermarking techniques have gained attraction due to their
      ability to seamlessly integrate watermarks during the generative process without incurring extra
794
      computational costs. Techniques such as Wen et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2024); Ci et al. (2024)
795
      embed watermarks directly into the initial noise and retrieve the watermark by reversing the diffusion
796   process. These methods enhance robustness and invisibility but are typically restricted to server-side
797   watermarking, requiring access to the initial random seed. Moreover, the watermarks introduced
798   by Wen et al. (2023a); Ci et al. (2024) significantly alter the data distribution, leading to variance
799   towards watermarks in generated outputs (as shown in Figure 1).
800
      In contrast to Wen et al. (2023a); Ci et al. (2024), our proposed shallow diffuse disentangles the
801
      watermark embedding from the generation process by leveraging the high-dimensional null space.
802   This approach, both empirically and theoretically validated, significantly improves watermark con-
803   sistency and robustness. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first method that supports water-
804   mark embedding for both server-side and user-side applications while maintaining high robustness
805   and consistency.
806
807   A.2   L OW- DIMENSIONAL SUBSPACE IN DIFFUSION MODEL
808
809   In recent years, there has been growing interest in understanding deep generative models through
      the lens of the manifold hypothesis (Loaiza-Ganem et al., 2024). This hypothesis suggests that


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      high-dimensional real-world data actually lies in latent manifolds with a low intrinsic dimension.
811   Focusing on diffusion models, Stanczuk et al. (2024) empirically and theoretically shows that the
812   approximated score function (the gradient of the log density of a noise-corrupted data distribution) in
813   diffusion models is orthogonal to a low-dimensional subspace. Building on this, Wang et al. (2024);
814   Chen et al. (2024) find that the estimated posterior mean from diffusion models lies within this low-
815   dimensional space. Additionally, Chen et al. (2024) discovers strong local linearity within the space,
816   suggesting that it can be locally approximated by a linear subspace. This observation motivates our
817   Assumption 1, where we assume the estimated posterior mean lies in a low-dimensional subspace.
818   Building upon these findings, Stanczuk et al. (2024); Kamkari et al. (2024) introduce a local in-
819   trinsic dimension estimator, while Loaiza-Ganem et al. (2024) proposes a method for detecting
820   out-of-domain data. Wang et al. (2024) offers theoretical insights into how diffusion model training
821   transitions from memorization to generalization, and Chen et al. (2024); Manor & Michaeli (2024b)
822   explores the semantic basis of the subspace to achieve disentangled image editing. Unlike these pre-
823   vious works, our approach leverages the low-dimensional subspace for watermarking, where both
824   empirical and theoretical evidence demonstrates that this subspace enhances robustness and consis-
825
      tency.

1059   We evaluate Shallow Diffuse with channel averaging enabled (γ = 1.0) and disabled (γ = 0.0),
1060   as shown in Table 8. Unlike the adaptive image enhancement techniques proposed in Zhang
1061
       et al. (2024c), our approach embeds the watermark in a single channel while averaging the non-
       watermarked channels. This design takes advantage of the fact that many image processing opera-
1062
       tions, such as color jittering or Gaussian blurring, tend to affect all channels uniformly. By isolating
1063
       the watermark in one channel, it will be less vulnerable to those attacks. Thus, applying channel
1064   averaging slightly enhances robustness against certain attacks while maintaining comparable con-
1065   sistency. Therefore, we set γ = 1.0 for Shallow Diffuse.
