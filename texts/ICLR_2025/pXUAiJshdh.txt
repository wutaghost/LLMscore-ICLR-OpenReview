009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have gained increasing prominence in scientific
012            research, but there is a lack of comprehensive benchmarks to fully evaluate their
013            proficiency in understanding and mastering scientific knowledge. To address this
014            need, we introduce the SciKnowEval benchmark, a novel framework that system-
015            atically evaluates LLMs across five progressive levels of scientific knowledge:
016            studying extensively, inquiring earnestly, thinking profoundly, discerning clearly,
017            and practicing assiduously. These levels aim to assess the breadth and depth of
018
               scientific knowledge in LLMs, including memory, comprehension, reasoning, dis-
               cernment, and application. Specifically, we first construct a large-scale evaluation
019
               dataset encompassing 70K multi-level scientific problems and solutions in the
020
               domains of biology, chemistry, physics, and materials science. By leveraging this
021            dataset, we benchmark 26 advanced open-source and proprietary LLMs using
022            zero-shot and few-shot prompting strategies. The results reveal that despite the
023            state-of-the-art performance of proprietary LLMs, there is still significant room for
024            improvement, particularly in addressing scientific reasoning and applications. We
025            anticipate that SciKnowEval will establish a standard for benchmarking LLMs in
026            science research and promote the development of stronger scientific LLMs.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated an impressive capabil-
032   ity in storing and recalling world knowledge, continuously expanding the boundaries of artificial
033
      intelligence. Their exceptional performance has permeated diverse specialized domains, including
      the scientific domain, leading to the emergence of scientific LLMs, such as Galactica (Taylor et al.,
034
      2022), SciGLM (Zhang et al., 2024a), and ChemLLM (Zhang et al., 2024b). To steadily advance
035
      scientific research, it is crucial to establish reliable benchmarks that comprehensively evaluate these
036   models’ capability in handling scientific knowledge.
037
038   While several existing LLM benchmarks (Li et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2018) have
039
      incorporated scientific questions into their evaluations, and some benchmarks (Sun et al., 2024; Wang
      et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024; Welbl et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023) are specifically
040
      tailored for the scientific domain, we argue that the current benchmarks do not fully evaluate the
041
      potential of LLMs in scientific research due to their inherent limitations. Firstly, many existing
042   benchmarks, such as AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023), SciQ (Welbl et al., 2017), and ScienceQA (Lu
043   et al., 2022), include science questions only up to the high school level, failing to tap into the deeper
044   capability of LLMs. Secondly, recent scientific domain benchmarks like ChemLLMBench (Guo
045   et al., 2023), SciBench (Wang et al., 2023), and SciAssess (Cai et al., 2024), despite involving
046   more specialized scientific tasks, lack a comprehensive evaluation system, resulting in a limited
047   understanding of capabilities. Lastly, most benchmarks overlook the assessment of safety issues in
048   scientific research, even those attempting a multi-dimensional comprehensive evaluation such as
049   SciEval (Sun et al., 2024).
050   In response to these deficiencies, in this study, we adopt a distinctive perspective, “LLMs as Scientists”,
051   to revisit the evaluation in the scientific domain. We draw inspiration from the profound principles of
052   Confucius outlined in the ancient Chinese philosophy “Doctrine of the Mean” , and present a novel
053   Scientific Knowledge Evaluation benchmark, referred to as SciKnowEval, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
      This benchmark aims to assess LLMs based on their proficiency in five progressive levels: studying

151   3.1        D ESIGN P HILOSOPHY
152
153   The design philosophy of SciKnowEval is inspired by the profound principles of Confucius elucidated
154   in the ancient Chinese book “Doctrine of the Mean”: Studying extensively, Enquiring earnestly,
155   Thinking profoundly, Discerning clearly, and Practicing assiduously. This principle reflects the five
156   progressive levels in the human learning process. In this study, we regard LLMs as Scientists and
157   utilize this concept to evaluate them. Specifically, each level provides a perspective to assess the
158   proficiency of LLMs, as described below.
159
160              • L1: Studying extensively (i.e., knowledge memory). This dimension evaluates the breadth
161                of an LLM’s knowledge across various scientific domains. It measures the model’s ability
                   to remember a wide range of scientific concepts.


                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • L2: Enquiring earnestly (i.e., knowledge comprehension). This aspect focuses on the
163            LLM’s capacity for inquiry and exploration within scientific contexts, such as analyzing
164            scientific texts, identifying key concepts, and questioning relevant information.
165
             • L3: Thinking profoundly (i.e., knowledge reasoning). This criterion examines the model’s
166
               capacity for critical thinking, logical deduction, numerical calculation, function prediction,
167            and the ability to engage in reflective reasoning to solve problems.
168
             • L4: Discerning clearly (i.e., knowledge discernment). This aspect evaluates the LLM’s
169
               ability to make correct, secure, and ethical decisions based on scientific knowledge, includ-
170
               ing assessing the harmfulness and toxicity of information, and understanding the ethical
171            implications and safety concerns related to scientific endeavors.
172
             • L5: Practicing assiduously (i.e., knowledge application). The final dimension assesses
173
               the LLM’s capability to apply scientific knowledge effectively in real-world scenarios, such
174
               as solving complex scientific problems and creating innovative solutions.
175
176   Building upon the above design philosophy, we develop the SciKnowEval benchmark specifically tai-
177   lored for assessing multi-level scientific knowledge in LLMs. In particular, we undertake meticulous
178   designs in terms of data scale, diversity and quality when constructing the evaluation dataset:
179
180          • Large-scale. We architect our dataset to be large-scale, enabling a more accurate and robust
181
               assessment of LLMs.
182          • Multi-level. We design and construct our datasets to encompass a wide range of tasks,
183            spanning multiple levels of scientific knowledge, to comprehensively assess the breadth and
184            depth of knowledge in LLMs.
185          • High-quality. We prioritize the quality of our data through rigorous quality control measures,
186            ensuring the reliability of the proposed dataset.

188   3.2   DATA C OLLECTION M ETHODS
189
190
      Fig. 2 illustrates three data collection approaches employed in SciKnowEval, including generating
      questions&answers (QAs) from the literature or textbooks, refactoring the existing QAs, as well as
191
      transforming the traditional scientific datasets into textual formats suitable for LLMs. We elaborate
192
      on these methods as follows.
193
194
      I. Generating New QAs from Literature Corpus. Literature and textbooks cover a broad range
195   of scientific knowledge, and leveraging this data will facilitate a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs’
196   capabilities in the scientific domains. We collect massive papers from article preprint platforms (e.g.,
197   BioRxiv), literature databases (e.g., PubMed), and textbook databases (e.g., LibreTexts). We utilize
198   LLMs to automate the procedures of QA pair generation. Specifically, following domain experts’
199   advice, we carefully design effective prompts for literature QA tasks. These prompts exhibited in
200   Appendix A5 guide the LLM to extract relevant professional knowledge from literature and textbook
201   paragraphs, enabling it to generate new QA pairs around this expertise. To ensure quality assessment
202   of the generated questions, we emphasize in the prompts that answers must be explicitly found in the
203
      original text without introducing any external information.
204
205
      II. Refactoring the Existing QAs. We sample additional QAs from existing open-source scientific
      benchmarks, including MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022), SciEval (Sun et al., 2024), MMLU (Hendrycks
206
      et al., 2020), XieZhi (Gu et al., 2023), PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019), and HarmfulQA (Bhardwaj &
207
      Poria, 2023). To mitigate the risk of data contamination and leakage in these benchmarks, we employ
208   LLMs to refactor these QAs in various forms, such as question rewriting and option reordering.
209   Moreover, in cases where some QAs lack explicit annotations indicating their corresponding levels in
210   SciKnowEval, LLMs are utilized to automatically categorize the data into distinct levels.
211
212   III. Transforming the Scientific Databases. To enhance the variety and scope of tasks in our
213   dataset, we select several structured databases and transform them into textual formats suitable for
214   evaluating LLMs. These databases mainly include molecular (e.g., PubChem (Kim et al., 2021)),
215   protein (e.g., UniProtKB (Consortium, 2023)), and cellular-related (e.g., SHARE-seq (Ma et al.,
      2020)) sequence information, which contain annotations related to structure, properties, and functions.

245   3.3      DATA Q UALITY C ONTROL
246
247
      The primary concern of data quality is the QAs generated from literature corpus. To ensure the
      generated data with high quality, we employed a three-stage data screening process:
248
249
               • Initial screening by LLMs. We first explicitly instructed LLM during data generation
250
                 that the correct options must be clearly identifiable from the provided literature snippets.
251              After data generation, we prompt LLMs to simulate an open-book exam task to determines
252              whether each question’s answer can be found in the corresponding snippet.
253
254            • Human evaluation. We randomly selected approximately 5% of the generated questions
255              and provided them with two domain experts. During the evaluation, we used the instructions
256
                 in Table A11 to guide the evaluators, asking them to thoroughly assess the data and classify
                 it into binary categories of “Yes” and “No” for quality.
257
258            • Post-screening by LLMs. We employed LLMs to summarize the failure types of the
259              identified low-quality entries, and added them into the prompt to conduct a full dataset
260              quality assessment, discarding similar types of low-quality questions.
261
262   More details of data quality control are provided in Appendix A7.

311
312   Models. We select 26 widely-used and high-performing LLMs. These models are categorized into
313   three types based on their accessibility and purpose. The details about the implementation of models
314   can be found in Appendix A2.
315
316               • Proprietary LLMs: This category generally represents state-of-the-art. Included models are
317                 OpenAI’s GPT series (Ouyang et al., 2022), specifically OpenAI’s GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini,
318                 GPT-4-Turbo, GPT-3.5-Turbo, Anthropic’s Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Claude3-Sonnet (Anthropic,
319                 2024), Google’s Gemini1.5-Pro (Reid et al., 2024), and Alibaba’s Qwen-Max (Yang et al.,
320
                    2024).
321               • Open-Source General-Purpose LLMs: These LLMs excel in general domains and serve as
322                 a foundation for further research into scientific LLMs. We selected seven LLMs from diverse
323                 sources, ranging in size from 7B to 72B, including Qwen2-7/72B-Inst (Yang et al., 2024),
                    Llama3-70B-Inst (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen1.5-7/14B-Chat (Bai et al., 2023), Llama3-

               8B-Inst (Dubey et al., 2024), Llama2-13B-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023), ChatGLM3-6B (Du
325            et al., 2021), Gemma1.1-7B-Inst, and Mistral-7B-Inst (Team et al., 2024).
326
327          • Open-Source Scientific LLMs: These models have acquired specialized knowledge by
328            training on scientific domain data. We selected models focused on biology and chemistry,
329
               including ChemDFM-13B (Zhao et al., 2024), Galactica-6.7B/30B (Taylor et al., 2022),

332
333   Evaluation Mode. In our experiments, the input begins with a system prompt that describes the
334
      types and categories of questions. We employ two evaluation settings: zero-shot and few-shot. The
      zero-shot setting evaluates models’ problem-solving capabilities without any prior examples, testing
335
      their ability to solve problems based on their own inherent knowledge. In the few-shot setting, models
336
      are provided with a limited number of examples before the test example to assess their capability to
337   acquire and incorporate new information into their problem-solving processes.
338
339
340
      Evaluation Criteria. We adopt diverse evaluation metrics, tailoring our assessment to different task
      types. When evaluating True/False, classification and multiple-choice questions, we use accuracy
341
      as the performance metric. For relation extraction questions, we use the F1 -score that combines
342
      precision and recall. For generative questions, we adopted different evaluation methods tailored to the
343   characteristics of each task. Specifically, following the existing works (Edwards et al., 2022; Yu et al.,
344   2024; Guo et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023; Edwards et al., 2024), we calculate the average scores using
345   BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) metrics for the tasks of molecule captioning,
346   protein captioning, and single cell analysis. For molecule generation, we compute the average of the
347   exact match rate and fingerprint-based Tanimoto similarity (Yu et al., 2024). For protein generation,
348   we leverage the Smith-Waterman algorithm (Smith et al., 1981) to perform local sequence alignment
349   of two protein sequences. For other generative tasks, we designed meticulous prompts for GPT-4o
350   to evaluate the responses of LLMs. The scoring prompt templates are exhibited in Appendix A9.
351
      Considering the challenge of aggregating different metrics, we report the average rankings of LLMs
      in each task as the final score.

356   In this section, we report the performance of LLMs in the SciKnowEval dataset. Table 4 and Table
357
      A1 summarize the zero-shot performance rankings of LLMs at each level, offering valuable insights
      into the strengths and weaknesses exhibited by each model. We emphasize our key observations as
358
      follows, and the illustrative examples can be found in Appendix A10.
359
360
361   Overall Performance. Proprietary LLMs, such as the GPT-4 series and Claude-3.5, have consis-
362
      tently demonstrated superior performance across these four domains, securing their highest overall
      rankings. Notably, Claude3.5-Sonnet has exhibited exceptional capability and adaptability in scien-
363
      tific domains. Open-source LLMs with larger scales, including Llama3-70B and Qwen2-72B, also
364
      exhibited comparable performance. In contrast, scientific LLMs performed moderately and only
365   showcased strengths in a few tasks.
366
367
368
      Performance on Each Level. We then analyze the performance of LLMs on the five levels. Table
      A3, A4, A5, and A6 show the scores of LLMs on each task at each level.
369
370   L1 reflects the model’s memory of scientific knowledge. Proprietary LLMs, such as GPT-4o
371   demonstrated the best capabilities in four domains, showcasing its extensive knowledge coverage.
372   In the medium-scale (∼10B) LLMs, ChemDFM emerged as one of the top open-source models

      4.3      D ISCUSSIONS
454
455   SciKnowEval exhibits Sufficient Difficulty and Challenge. Firstly, our results indicate that in
456   zero-shot setting, proprietary models consistently outperform other open-source models. Moreover,
457   there is a noticeable positive correlation between model size and performance, e.g., Galactica-
458   30B outperforms Galactica-6.7B, and Qwen1.5-14B-Chat exceeds the performance of Qwen1.5-
459   7B. Secondly, by examining the detailed scores of GPT-4o across various tasks, it is evident that
460   SciKnowEval spans multiple levels of difficulty. For most tasks at the L1 and L2 levels, GPT-4o
461   achieves accuracies above 85%. However, GPT-4o struggles with tasks at the L3 and L5 levels,
462   particularly those involving molecular SMILES and protein sequences. Lastly, our carefully designed
463
      L4 level, aimed at evaluating the safety of LLMs, introduces a novel challenge compared to other
      benchmarks such as SciEval and SciAssess. We observed that GPT-4o often failed to reject harmful
464
      questions in the Harmful QA task, presenting a potential risk of misuse.
465
466
      Few-shot Setting enhances Model Performance across Tasks. For the few-shot setting, we
467
      selected two competitive models from each of the three categories of LLMs, and evaluated them on
468   most of the tasks in our datasets. Table 5 summarizes the task performance shifts of these LLMs
469   under a 3-shot setting. For most tasks involving multiple-choice and true/false questions, we observed
470   significant performance gains, especially for tasks at the L3 level. For instance, GPT-4o and Claude3-
471   Sonnet saw accuracy increases of 27.10% and 28.58%, respectively, in the fluorescence prediction
472   task. This indicates that few-shot settings can substantially enhance models’ scientific reasoning and
473   computational abilities.
474   Incremental Pre-training or Fine-tuning on Scientific Corpus show Promise. We compared
475   two pairs of models: 1) Llama2-13B vs. ChemDFM-13B, and 2) Mistral-7B-Inst vs. LlaSMol-
476   Mistral-7B. We can observe that ChemDFM-13B, built on the Llama-13B framework and further
477   pre-trained and fine-tuned on a corpus of 34 billion tokens from scientific literature, significantly
478   outperformed Llama2-13B-Chat. Similarly, fine-tuning with instruction data for molecule generation
479   and description enabled LlaSMol-Mistral-7B to surpass Mistral-7B-Inst at the L1 and L5 levels in the
480   chemical domain.
481   Evaluating OpenAI o1 with SciKnowEval. Recently, OpenAI released a new LLM, o1 (OpenAI,
482   2024), which excels at complex task reasoning, especially in the fields of science, mathematics, and
483   programming. In a series of challenging benchmarks, o1 delivered outstanding results and even
484   surpassed human experts in PhD-level scientific Q&A sessions. To further assess o1’s capabilities
485   in scientific domains, we used a subset of the SciKnowEval dataset to evaluate the performance
      of o1-preview and o1-mini. The subset consists of 1,775 challenging questions that GPT-4o-mini


                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      fails to answer correctly. The evaluation results are presented in Figure A1. Through analyzing
487   the quantitative results and several cases, we have three key findings: (1) By generating hidden
488   chain-of-thoughts (CoT) during model inference, o1 has a significant improvement in answering
489   questions related to scientific computation and reasoning, however, it occasionally falls into the
490   traps of reasoning problems, especially those involving complex physical principles and laws. (2)
491   o1 has integrated safety rules into its CoT, improving the model’s ability to align with safety, but it
492   still lacks sufficient knowledge regarding certain substances (such as uncommon toxic compounds
493   and viruses) and may output harmful responses. (3) In addition to scientific reasoning and safety
494   alignment, the improvement of o1 in scientific knowledge memory, knowledge understanding, and
495
      knowledge application is limited. See Appendix A11 for more evaluation results and case analyses.
496
497   5   C ONCLUSION
498
499   In this paper, we introduce the SciKnowEval benchmark, a novel framework designed to compre-
500   hensively and systematically evaluate the scientific knowledge of LLMs. SciKnowEval defines five
501   progressive levels, aimed at deeply reflecting the breadth and depth of LLMs’ scientific knowledge.
502   It focuses on biology, chemistry, physics and materials as four representative domains, encompassing
503   70K multi-level problems and answers. We employed this SciKnowEval dataset to conduct extensive
504
      benchmarking and thorough analysis of 26 advanced LLMs. Our findings indicate that even the most
      advanced LLMs struggle to effectively address tasks related to scientific reasoning and application.
505
506   In the future, we aim to broaden the scope of SciKnowEval by encompassing additional scientific
507   domains and incorporating more domain-specific tasks. Additionally, due to the large scale of
508   SciKnowEval datasets and the involvement of some tasks that require scoring based on GPT-4o, there
509   are some costs associated with the assessment. In future efforts, we aim to optimize the assessment
510
      methods, such as by substituting GPT-4o with an open-source scientific LLM evaluator. We anticipate
      that SciKnowEval will become a standard for evaluating LLMs in scientific research and discovery,
511
      thereby promoting the development of scientific LLMs.

       A7     DATA Q UALITY
1891
1892
       To ensure the generated data quality, we employed a three-stage data screening process:
1893
1894   1. Initial screening by LLMs. Our primary concern is the ”Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)”
1895   tasks entirely generated by LLMs, such as the Literature QA task. To ensure the correctness of
1896
       LLM-generated answers, we first explicitly instructed LLM during data generation that the correct
       options must be clearly identifiable from the provided literature snippets. After data generation, we
1897
       prompt GPT-4o (Table A10) to simulate an open-book exam task, where it determines whether each
1898
       question’s answer could be found in the corresponding literature snippet, and if so, GPT-4o could
1899   provide answers to questions based on text snippets (for example, identifying the correct option for
1900   multiple-choice questions from a snippet). By comparing these answers with the previously generated
1901   answers, we can verify the accuracy of the original answers. Otherwise, we consider the answers to
1902   the questions unverifiable, and we simply delete them.
1903
       2. Human evaluation. We randomly selected approximately 5% of the questions from each task
1904
       and provided them with two experts in biology and chemistry, with the assistance of five graduate
1905   students from related fields. It took a week to complete the quality evaluation. During the evaluation,
1906   we used the instructions in Table A11 to guide the evaluators, asking them to thoroughly assess the
1907   data and classify it into binary categories of ”Yes” and ”No” for quality. Only data that fully met the
1908   requirements was rated ”Yes.” Ultimately, we identified 2.1% instances of data that were rated ”No”
1909   after the first human evaluation stage.
1910
