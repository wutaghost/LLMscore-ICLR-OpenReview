009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large visual language models like CLIP have demonstrated impressive perfor-
012
               mance on various downstream tasks involving common data, e.g., natural images,
013
               by leveraging prompt learning. However, these models often falter when applied
014            to tasks involving rare data, e.g., medical images. We provide an experimental
015            insight into this phenomenon: CLIP is insensitive to the class names of rare im-
016            ages. For instance, replacing the class name “medulloblastoma” (a type of brain
017            tumor) with “dog” in prompts has minimal impact on performance, a phenomenon
018            not observed with common images. This highlights the disparity in representation
019            learning between common and rare data. To realign prompt learning with rare
020            image recognition, we propose a novel prompt learning strategy, termed prompt
021            reverse learning (PeLen). Different from the existing methods that adapt CLIP’s
022
               representations to downstream tasks, PeLen adapts task-specific representations
               to CLIP’s representations. Built upon the insensitivity to the class names of rare
023
               images, PeLen designates common images and their class names to represent a
024
               specific class of rare images and class names, e.g., allowing the image and text
025            of a dog to correspond to the image and text of medulloblastoma. Consequently,
026            PeLen learns prompts to align the representations between the rare images and the
027            visual and textual representations of common images. Our experiments on three
028            types of rare images demonstrate the efficacy of PeLen for rare image recognition.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) Radford et al. (2021) stands out as a significant
035   milestone in large-scale language vision models, drawing considerable attention for its exceptional
036   performance in various tasks. CLIP intricately integrates images and text, embedding them into a
037   shared representation space through extensive training on large-scale datasets. In this representation
038   space, the correlations between images and text are precisely captured and encoded, enabling CLIP
039
      to simultaneously comprehend the meaning of both images and text. This makes it possible to per-
      form zero-shot inference, where CLIP leverages the rich knowledge learned during pre-training to
040
      make judgments by comparing and measuring the embeddings of images and text in the representa-
041
      tion space. This multi-modal alignment capability empowers CLIP to excel not only in traditional
042   visual tasks such as image classification Zhou et al. (2022a); Gao et al. (2024) and object detection
043   Yao et al. (2022) but also in more complex image generation Ramesh et al. (2022) and language
044   understanding Li et al. (2022a) tasks, demonstrating remarkable universality and adaptability.
045
      Advanced research has extensively explored CLIP’s alignment capabilities, particularly focusing on
046
      tuning the context (embeddings) of prompts, i.e., the text input of the text encoder. Specifically,
047
      since small changes in manual prompts can bring huge performance differences, it is particularly
048   important to optimize the prompts. In this context, CoOp Zhou et al. (2022b) introduces learnable
049   vectors into prompts, essentially learning a text embedding that better matches visual features ex-
050   tracted by the image encoder, thereby enhancing CLIP’s performance and efficiency. Futher research
051   such as MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023) further aligns the visual-language (V-L) modalities through
052   multi-modal prompts to achieve deeper fusion and synergy. These emerging approaches contribute
053   to strengthening CLIP’s adaptability to various domains and tasks, sparking further exploration and
      innovation in prompt learning methods.

      However, prompt learning methods would face challenges in downstream tasks with rare images,
077
      such as medical, remote sensing, and infrared images. This is because these images rarely appear
078
      in the training dataset of CLIP, compared with common data, e.g., natural images. Accordingly,
079   it could be difficult for CLIP to align rare images and the corresponding text in the shared repre-
080   sentation space. Our experiments, as shown in Figure 1a, verify this point, where the zero-shot
081   inference ability of CLIP slightly outperforms random guessing on rare images. Meanwhile, learn-
082   ing prompt Zhou et al. (2022b;a) for rare images brings limited performance gain, which is not as
083   exciting as that on natural images Zhou et al. (2022b;a). While rare images occur less frequently in
084   CLIP training data due to privacy or security concerns, adapting pre-trained vision-language models
085   to tasks involving rare images remains crucial.
086   To re-enable prompt learning for rare image recognition, we perform an experimental exploration,
087   motivated by the poor ability to align rare images and their captions. In the existing prompt learning
088   methods, class names mainly serve as the class priors in the optimizing process to guide model
089   learning. In the rare image scenario, the class name priors may hardly contribute to optimizing
090   prompts due to the poor ability to align visual and textual features. Thus, replacing the class name of
091   rare images with a dummy word would cause limited performance change, which distinguishes rare
092   images from natural images. Fortunately, our experimental results provide a consistent conclusion
093
      with the point, as depicted in Figure 1b. Specifically, when optimizing context, we replace the class
      names of rare images in prompts with those of common images. For instance, we leverage “dog”
094
      as the class name for the MRI image of “medulloblastoma” (a type of brain tumor) to optimize
095
      prompts. Our results show that even dummy words can achieve performance comparable to the
096   official class names. Namely, the performance of prompt learning is insensitive to the class names
097   when processing rare images. By contrast, performance on natural images drops significantly when
098   replacing “water lily” with “dog.”
099
      These experimental explorations motivate us to leverage the CLIP’s insensitivity to class names of
100
      rare images to promote prompt learning. We propose prompt reverse learning (PeLen) for prompt
101
      learning with rare images. The insight is that PeLen adapts task-specific representations to CLIP’s
102   representations, which is different from existing methods adapting CLIP’s representations to down-
103   stream tasks. Specifically, PeLen first assigns a class of common images to each class of rare images
104   to establish a mapping relationship, e.g., letting images and text of “dogs” correspond to images and
105   text of “medulloblastoma”. Subsequently, PeLen optimizes prompts to approach representations of
106   rare images to those of common images. Meanwhile, PeLen predicts labels of rare images using the
107   classification weight generated by prompts of common images. CLIP lacks prior knowledge of rare
      images, but has sufficient prior knowledge of common images. Consequently, PeLen promotes the

      3.1   V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODELS
273
274   Recently, pre-trained visual language models (VLM) have made significant progress in joint learning
275   of images and text. Models like CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and ALIGN Jia et al. (2021)utilize
276   extensive image-text pairs to train multimodal networks, demonstrating impressive performance
277   in various downstream tasks Gao et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2021); Du et al. (2022); Wu et al.
278   (2023) through contrastive learning. In the development of VLM, some of their variants further
279
      optimize the performance of the model. For instance, GLIP Li et al. (2022b) unifies object detection
      and phrase grounding tasks, incorporating image and text prompts simultaneously into the object
280
      detection network. CLIPSeg Lüddecke & Ecker (2022) and CRIS Wang et al. (2022) use CLIP for
281
      segmentation. Although the VLM model learns a wide range of general knowledge, it still faces
282   challenges in the huge distribution differences between medical images and natural images. In this
283   work, we propose a prompt reverse learning technique based on CLIP to effectively apply CLIP to
284   rare image recognition.
285
286   3.2   P ROMPT L EARNING
287
288   Prompt learning is initially introduced in NLP Jiang et al. (2020); Tsimpoukelli et al. (2021); Shin
289   et al. (2020). By designing specific prompts, the model is guided to perform text understanding,
290   generation or classification in the desired way without modifying the large-scale pre-trained model.
291   Inspired by prompt learning in NLP, the VL model begin to replace manually designed context by
292
      automatically optimizing context. CoOp Zhou et al. (2022b) trains a set of learnable vectors for the
      language branch end-to-end to achieve better downstream performance and efficiency. Co-CoOp
293
      Zhou et al. (2022a) trains specific prompts for image instances, addressing the overfitting issue
294
      of CoOp on base classes and improving performance on new classes. Beyond language prompts,
295   Bahng et al. (2022) learns perturbations on images to explore the effectiveness of visual prompts for
296   large-scale Models. VPT Jia et al. (2022) introduces a small amount of image tokens as learnable
297   prompts, enabling Vision Transformer Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) to adapt to downstream visual tasks.
298   MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023) proposes multi-modal prompts, coupling visual and language branches
299   to promote synergy and enhance the generalization ability of VLM. However, these prompt learning
300   methods do not fully consider the problem of insufficient perception when rare images as the role of
301   the downstream tasks. In this work, we leverage prompts to align the representations of rare images
302   with the visual and textual representations of common images, thus taking full advantage of CLIP’s
303
      powerful perceptual capabilities.

305   4     E XPERIMENTS
306
307   CLIP’s training data is sourced from Internet, which means it rarely encounter data from certain
308   specific domains. In order to verify Pelen’s generalizability, we collect five datasets on three distinct
309   image domain: medical, remote sensing and infrared images. To validate the effectiveness of the
310   proposed prompt reverse learning (PeLen) method, we conduct two tasks using these five datasets.
311
312   4.1   S ETUP AND BASELINES
313
314
      We employ two highly representative recognition tasks and five datasets from three rare image do-
      main to evaluate the effectiveness of different prompt learning methods.
315
316   Image classification task. This task aims to assign images to predefined categories. We divide the
317   dataset into training, validation, and test set. Current prompt learning methods usually encounter
318   challenges in rare image classification tasks because these images are rarely appear in the training
319   set of CLIP. Therefore, the proposed PeLen method primarily focuses on image classification tasks.
320   Domain adaptation task. It is particularly important to adapt a pre-trained vision language model,
321   e.g., CLIP, to a target domain with different characteristics, e.g., rare iamges. In the context of
322   few-shot learning, the target domain has limited labeled data. We use 16 shots for training while
323   test models on the full test set. Few-shot learning demonstrates the ability to transfer cross-domain
      knowledge, which is used to verify the effectiveness of PeLen.

337
338   Metric. Our experiment choose mean class accuracy as evaluation metric for ISIC2018 dataset, and
339   accuracy for the other dataset. Due to the ISIC2018 dataset has severe long-tail distribution problem,
340   the mean class accuracy considers the accuracy within each class, ensuring the final results is not
341   biased by the varying number of samples across different classes.
342
      Data details. The modality of medical images is different from natural images, so we need to
343   process the data to be consistent with natural images. The images in MURA are single-channel X-
344   rays, we copy the single channel three times. Besides, each patient has a different number of X-rays,
345   we use a single sample when training the model, and vote on the results of multiple X-rays to get
346   the final result for one patient during testing. Each patient in the pediatric brain tumor dataset has
347   three modalities, we concatenate the three modalities, corresponding to the RGB channels of natural
348   images. Additionally, since each modality has 24 frames of images, we intercept 8 consecutive
349   frames of images starting from the third frame according to the location of the brain tumor to obtain
350   the average feature vector for classification. The images in other datasets are consistent with natural
351
      images and do not require any special processing. The data augmentation strategies are employed
      during training including random horizontal flipping and random vertical flipping, randomly resize
352
      crop to 224 × 224, mean and standard deviation normalization.

355   RSSCN7 and pediatric brain tumor classification, the depth of the prompt is 12, the length is 10. In

357
      the length is 8. And the learning rate is 0.0005 in ISIC2018 and MURA dataset, 0.001 in other. All

363
      Image classification experiments. Table 1 shows the performance of PeLen on five rare image
      classification tasks, and PeLen achieves better performance on all five tasks. Although CoOp and
364
      Co-CoOp have powerful generalization capabilities for downstream tasks on natural images, their
365
      performance on rare image tasks is unsatisfactory. This also proves the huge gap between rare
366   images and natural images, emphasizing the necessity of utilizing visual prompts to bridge this gap.
367   Compared with RLM-VP and VPT, PeLen achieves huge performance gains. RLM-VP trains a box
368   attached around the image as a visual prompt, and VPT uses an inserted token as a visual prompt.
369   Although they both use visual prompts with additional label mapping in RLM-VP, PeLen allows
370   the distribution of rare images downstream to be calibrated to a fixed upstream image distribution,

477   5     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      Through prompt learning, large-scale vision-language models such as CLIP demonstrate strong gen-
480   eralization performance on downstream tasks with common images. But challenges arise when fac-
481   ing rare image tasks. We observe an intriguing phenomenon: CLIP lacks sensitivity to rare image
482   class names. Leveraging this insight, we propose Prompt Reverse Learning. Differing from pre-
483   vious works, we align the representation of rare images with the fixed upstream representations of
484   common images at both the visual and textual levels. To validate our approach’s effectiveness, we
485   conduct experiments on five datasets containing rare images. Our results demonstrate that Prompt
      Reverse Learning enhances the generalization ability of CLIP for rare image recognition tasks.

652
      Melanoma detection. Melanoma is a malignant tumor that accounts for 90% of skin cancer deaths
653
      Garbe et al. (2016). Current diagnostic methods for melanoma detection present several limitations
654   and challenges. To address these problems, deep learning methods have emerged in melanoma
655   detection in recent years. Here, we use the ISIC2018 datasets Codella et al. (2019); Tschandl et al.
656   (2018). This dataset including 7 types of skin cancer. The training set has 10015 images, the
657   validation set has 193 images, and the test set has 1512 images.
658
      Musculoskeletal abnormality detection. Musculoskeletal abnormalities are the main cause of pain
659
      and disability Woolf & Pfleger (2003). Musculoskeletal abnormality detection is a binary task of
660   whether X-ray images of the musculoskeletal system are normal. The MURA Rajpurkar et al.
661   (2017) dataset contains a large number of X-ray images of multiple body parts, including elbow,
662   finger, forearm, hand, humerus, shoulder, and wrist. The training set contains 8280 normal and
663   5177 abnormal, and the validation set contains 661 normal and 538 abnormal. Since the test set is
664   not public, we test on the validation set.
665
      Remote sensing image classification. This task has important applications in many fields, includ-
666   ing agriculture, urban planning, environmental monitoring, resource management, etc. RSSCN7
667   Zou et al. (2015) dataset contains 2800 remote sensing images, which come from 7 typical scene
668   categories: grass, field, industry, riverlake, forest, resident, parking.
669
      Infrared image classification. Infrared image has significant applications in various fields such as
670
      military, security, meteorology, environmental monitoring, and medical imaging. For example, it
671
      can be used for night surveillance and border patrol to improve security and monitoring efficiency.
672   RGB-NIR Brown & Süsstrunk (2011) dataset consists of 477 images in 9 categories captured in
673   RGB and Near-infrared. The scene categories include: country, field, forest, indoor, mountain,
674   oldbuilding, street, urban, water. We use infrared images in the dataset for training and testing.
675
      Pediatric brain tumor classification. The incidence of pediatric brain tumors have jumped to
676
      the first place in the common childhood cancer Ostrom et al. (2020), seriously threatening chil-
677
      dren’s lives and health. Pediatric brain tumors can be divided into different types according to their
678   pathological properties, such as glioma and medulloblastoma. There are different clinical treatment
679   options for different types of brain tumors. At present, the diagnosis and treatment of brain tumors
680   are mainly based on pathological slices after surgery. This invasive detection method carries a high
681   risk to patients as well as high financial and time costs. Therefore, it is of great significance to
682   establish a non-invasive, convenient and accurate tumor classification method. We use the pediatric
683   brain tumor dataset collected from the hospital. This dataset contains preoperative MRI images of
684   623 pediatric patients with posterior fossa tumors and the corresponding tumor types, including 201
685   medulloblastomas, 101 choroid plexus tumors, 177 ependymomas, and 144 gliomas. Each patient’s
686
      preoperative MRI image had three modalities: T1-weighted, T1-weighted contrast-enhanced, and
      T2-weighted. Each modality consists of 24 frame images, each frame corresponding to a brain slice
687
      at a different location. The tumor is located in the posterior fossa, so only 8 frames are used during
688
      training. We preprocess the data as follows, applied the N4ITK Song et al. (2017) bias field correc-
689   tion method to remove the inhomogeneity of the bias field, and then applied HD-BET Schell et al.
690   (2019) for brain extraction.
