011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Autoformalization, the process of transforming informal mathematical language
014           into formal specifications and proofs remains a difficult task for state-of-the-art
015
              (large) language models. Existing works point to competing explanations for the
              performance gap. On one hand, large language models exhibit exceptional per-
016
              formance on translation tasks, suggesting their significant potential for autofor-
017
              malization. On the other hand, the quantitative reasoning capabilities of standard
018           language models remain limited, leading to suboptimal performance on autofor-
019           malization and the subsequent task of formal theorem proving. To this end, we
020           introduce a novel methodology that leverages backtranslation with hand-curated
021           prompts to enhance the mathematical capabilities of language models, particu-
022           larly addressing the challenge posed by the scarcity of labeled data. Specifically,
023           we evaluate three primary variations of this strategy: (1) on-the-fly (online) back-
024           translation, (2) distilled (offline) backtranslation with few-shot amplification, and
025           (3) line-by-line proof analysis integrated with proof state information. Each vari-
026
              ant is designed to optimize data quality over quantity, focusing on the high fidelity
              of generated proofs rather than sheer data scale. Our findings provide evidence
027
              that employing our proposed approaches to generate synthetic data, which priori-
028
              tizes quality over volume, improves the autoformalization performance of LLMs
029           as measured by standard benchmarks such as ProofNet. Crucially, our approach
030           outperforms pretrained models using a minimal number of tokens. We also show,
031           through strategic prompting and backtranslation, that our approaches surpass the
032           performance of finetuning with extensive multilingual datasets such as MMA on
033           ProofNet with only 1/150th of the tokens. Taken together, our methods show a
034           promising new approach to significantly reduce the resources required to formal-
035           ize proofs, thereby accelerating AI for math.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Neural machine translation has been a focal point of research since the early development of ma-
040
      chine learning (Dong et al., 2015) (Wu et al., 2016). Autoformalization can be viewed as a spe-
041
      cialized application of this approach, where the objective is to translate theorems from informal,
042   human-readable markup languages like LaTeX into formal languages such as Lean4 — a program-
043   ming language specifically designed to encode complex mathematical constructs (de Moura & Ull-
044   rich, 2021). In principle, the development of an agent capable of precise autoformalization would
045   significantly reduce the prohibitive costs of manually formalizing proofs. This would have pro-
046   found implications; such an advancement could render all mathematical knowledge, much of which
047   is presently recorded in natural language, programmable: substantially enhancing the usability of
048   interactive theorem proving systems and accelerating the expansion of human mathematical under-
049   standing (Klein et al., 2009).
050   Autoformalization first gained traction in 2018 when researchers used long short-term memory net-
051   works to generate statements in Mizar (Wang et al., 2018). More recently, experiments demonstrated
052   promising results using a naive few-shot learning approach to translate English into Isabelle code
053   (Wu et al., 2022). Historically, researchers have focused on merging automated theorem provers
      with language models, which often struggle with quantitative reasoning, limiting the types of theo-

054
      rems that can be expressed and solved and reducing the models’ effectiveness in complex, real-world
055   scenarios. Our study adopts a novel approach by integrating interactive theorem provers with large
056   language models, leveraging the sequential processing capabilities of both. By fine-tuning LLMs
057   with intermediary statements from the LeanDojo dataset (Yang et al., 2023), we aim to enhance
058   our understanding of formal theorem provers’ reasoning processes and successfully incorporate this
059   signal into an LLM.
060
      While LLMs have performed well on benchmarks related to various natural language understanding
061   tasks as evaluated by benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), they have continued to
062   struggle with tasks that require deeper quantitative reasoning (Lewkowycz et al., 2022). Moreover,
063   while strides have been made in using deep learning for symbolic mathematics (Lample & Charton,
064   2020), propositional logic (Hahn et al., 2021), and differential systems (Charton et al., 2021), the
065   field of autoformalization and theorem proving has generally not seen proportional advances. This
066   disparity can largely be attributed to two main factors:
067
            1. Complexity: Current models exhibit a gap in their ability to translate intricate mathemati-
068
               cal concepts from natural language to formal logic.
069
070
            2. Scarcity: The available training data for fine-tuning models in formal languages like Lean
               is significantly limited. Given the specialized nature of these programming languages, the
071
               sum total of formal-math language data is a minuscule fraction of the size of modern LLMs’
072
               training datasets. Thus, except for hand-curated benchmarks such as ProofNet (Azerbayev
073            et al., 2023a) and MiniF2F Zheng et al. (2021), there is virtually no paired formal-informal
074            data.

      We present a new autoformalization dataset, AI4Math, which pairs statements from natural lan-
092
      guage proofs with their corresponding translations in Lean, as shown in Figure 1. Our approach to
093
      generating this dataset leverages backtranslation: a method in neural machine translation for creat-
094   ing synthetic training data (Liu et al., 2021). Given a monolingual dataset in the target language,
095   backtranslation proceeds as follows:
096
097         1. Utilize a pretrained model to generate translations in the reverse direction, i.e., from the
098            target language to the source language.
099         2. Assemble a parallel corpus consisting of these synthetic source and ground-truth target
100            pairs.
101         3. Fine-tune or train on the generated dataset, enabling the model to translate the synthetic
102            source examples back to the target language.
103
104
      Our dataset, AI4Math, serves two primary purposes: first, to train an autoformalizer on individual
      statement pairs, and second, to fine-tune an LLM on the state of the proof before and after a specific
105
      tactic is applied. This approach aims to closely mimic the reasoning process in theorem proving,
106
      enhancing the LLM’s ability to understand and generate formal proofs.

121   2   T RAINING DATA G ENERATION VIA BACKTRANSLATION
122
123   Backtranslation has been a pivotal method in neural machine translation (NMT), with its demon-
124   strated utility for enhancing training efficacy in the absence of sufficient labeled data (Poncelas
125   et al., 2018). This technique was further refined in transformer models (Han et al., 2021) and in
126
      preliminary experiments involving distilled backtranslation within the domain of autoformalization
      (Azerbayev et al., 2023a). We chose this methodology because it is one of the most prominent
127
      and validated methods for NMT tasks (Liu et al., 2021), and has been shown to be analytically and
128
      empirically suitable for neural model training (Jiang et al., 2023).
129
130   Our approach is to start with data in formal language (FL) and translate it into informal language
131   (IL). We refer to this process as informalization. We demonstrate that each of these three dataset
132
      generation methodologies can improve model performance:
133
            • On-The-Fly Backtranslation: This strategy for data augmentation utilized a unique back-
134
              translation approach integrated within a custom training loop. Specifically, the training loop
135           itself has four main steps, as outlined in Figure 2: (1) translating a batch of FL examples to
136           IL (2) translating the synthetic IL examples back to FL, using the (synthetic IL, FL) pairs
137           as labeled training data (3) computing the loss of the generated FL translations compared
138           to the original FL examples and (4) backpropogating to update the model weights.
139           This method diverged from traditional practices by not employing separate teacher and
140           student models; instead, it trained a single model to simultaneously manage both directions
141           of the translation task. This setup was chosen because it enabled the model to iteratively
142           generate its own training data at each step, and this self-sufficient nature allowed us to avoid
143           the problem of limited labeled data without incurring large API costs.
144           The relatively small size of the model used in our experiments, GPT-2 with 124M param-
145           eters, suggested that it would not be very effective at generating high-quality synthetic
146           informalizations. Despite this, the on-the-fly backtranslation method led to a significant
147           reduction in evaluation loss after just a few hundred training steps, eventually leading to
148
              a substantial improvement compared to the baseline as seen in Table 2. Nevertheless, we
              eventually reached a performance plateau due to the inherent limitations of the baseline
149
              model. Due to resource constraints, we were unable to validate this method with a larger,
150
              more capable model such as Llemma-7B (Azerbayev et al., 2023b). We hypothesize that
151           employing a larger pretrained model could yield even more promising results. This method
152           thus serves as a baseline for comparison, and can highlight the potential benefits of using
153           more powerful models in future work.
154
            • Distilled Backtranslation: Considering the limitations of our initial approach, we pivoted
155
              to a more robust technique known as distilled backtranslation. For this method, we utilized
156           a more powerful pretrained model, GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), as the teacher model by feeding
157           it a dataset of FL examples and utilizing it to generate corresponding synthetic IL text, as
158           outlined in Figure 3. We capitalized on the strong in-context learning capabilities of LLMs
159           (Brown et al., 2020) by employing a few-shot prompt that included six labeled examples
160           of FL-to-IL translations. This few-shot amplification approach allowed us to develop a
161           comprehensive labeled dataset for training, rather than generating data on-the-fly. The full
              technique and prompt can be found in 5, with a formal example in Appendix A.

397   The Multilingual Mathematical Autoformalization (MMA) dataset also uses powerful models GPT-4
398   to translate from formal language (FL) to informal language (IL) via zero-shot instruction prompting
399   on a large, diverse corpus of mathlib4 data (Jiang et al., 2023). Our approach enhances the efficiency
400   of this process by applying a more intense six-shot prompting strategy on mathlib4 statements; the
401   fine-tuning performance of the resulting dataset not only surpasses that of the MMA dataset on the
402   ProofNet benchmark but does so using only 1/150th of the tokens: significantly optimizing resource
403
      use while enhancing output quality. Utilizing a regex, we parsed Lean files from mathlib into over
      100,000 tactic proof scripts, and we call this methodology using the “Full-Proof” in pregenerating
404
      informal-formal pairs for informalization via GPT-4. Despite the high number of parsed scripts, at
405
      an estimated $0.05 per informalization we were only able to informalize a subset of this collection.
406
407   Our dataset also utilizes LeanDojo, an open-source platform that offers a comprehensive dataset of
408   over 90,000 Lean theorems. Existing models like ReProver, a retrieval-augmented language model,
409
      have demonstrated superior performance compared to established models such as Tidy and GPT-
      4 (Yang et al., 2023). However, while retrieval-augmented models like ReProver are promising in
410
      leveraging formal corpora to enhance LLMs, they rely heavily on automated theorem provers. These
411
      models often skip over the detailed intermediate steps that are critical for understanding complex
412   mathematical logic, thereby restricting the systems’ flexibility and explainability.
413
414   Moreover, the prevalent use of first-order logic in these models constrains their capacity to articulate
415
      more sophisticated proofs. Our approach in GPT-4 LeanDojo (Individual Tactics) addresses this lim-
      itation by directly modeling the incremental deduction characteristic of interactive theorem provers.
416
      While this approach involves higher computational costs, Table 2 demonstrates its effectiveness in
417
      significantly enhancing baseline performance. Our method achieves notable improvements over the
418   baseline with a very small number of tokens.

421   6   D ISCUSSION
422
423   Our paper introduces a novel dataset that can be used for enhancing the autoformalization capabili-
424   ties of large language models. We demonstrate evidence that models can attain superior performance
425   on autoformalization benchmarks such as ProofNet by training on AI4Math, and our generation
426   strategies can significantly reduce the number of tokens required compared to MMA.
427   We also note several limitations with our method. Firstly, our fine-tuning was exclusively conducted
428   using GPT-2. In future research iterations, we anticipate that employing more powerful models
429   —– such as Mistral 7B or Llemma 7B for fine-tuning could lead to even better performance. Our
430   ”On-The-Fly” backtranslation process was especially constrained by the smaller model size used,
431   and using more powerful models for fine-tuning in future iterations could enhance the quality of
      informalizations.

      Cost constraints were another factor that limited our dataset’s expansion, as more sophisticated
433   prompt engineering with GPT-4, particularly for translating and segmenting entire proofs, proved
434   costly. Despite the higher expense, these backtranslation methods surpassed baseline performance
435   with fewer tokens, indicating superior data quality. Given its potential, this approach merits further
436   exploration if sufficient funding can support the additional costs.

      7   F UTURE W ORK
439
440
      In our current research, we concentrated on autoformalizing theorem statements rather than gener-
441
      ating complete formal proofs. This focus was primarily driven by our goal to streamline the process
442   of translating natural language into formal language theorems, which are integral to the groundwork
443   of interactive theorem proving (ITP). However, a significant next step in our exploration involves as-
444   sessing whether the entire proofs or tactic scripts, when generated by our model, can be successfully
445   compiled within the Lean4 environment.
446
      Additionally, leveraging Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs) to extract datasets of intermediate proof
447
      steps presents a novel opportunity for advancing the task of autoformalization. By sequentially
448   informalizing intermediate statements within a proof, we can generate a dataset that captures the
449   individual steps undertaken by ITPs. This data can subsequently be used to train an LLM, effectively
450   replicating the sequential reasoning performed by ITPs. Since this approach looks to model the
451   incremental deduction used by interactive theorem provers directly, we predict that this could enable
452   language models to develop richer capabilities for reasoning tasks and enable generalization beyond
453   their training proofs.
454   The ability for a model to generate not just the theorem statements but also the accompanying proofs
455   would mark a substantial leap forward in automating the theorem proving process. It would not
456   only enhance the efficiency and accessibility of formal verification but also potentially transform
457   how complex mathematical and logical reasoning is conducted in computational settings. Thus, the
458   next phase of our research will be dedicated to testing the compatibility and correctness of model-
459   generated proofs with Lean4, aiming to bridge the gap between automated theorem generation and
460   its practical application in ITP systems. This endeavor will necessitate a deeper integration of our
461   model’s outputs with the stringent syntax and logical framework of Lean4, posing a challenging yet
462
      essential milestone towards fully automated theorem proving.

465
466   Our paper has several larger implications for the field of autoformalization. By focusing on aut-
467   oformalizing theorem statements, we streamline the process of translating natural language into
468   formal language, which is critical for the groundwork of interactive theorem proving. Successfully
469   training an agent for precise autoformalization could drastically reduce the resources needed to con-
470
      vert extensive natural-language mathematical proofs into formal language. This progress would
      enhance the accessibility and efficiency of automated theorem proving and potentially quicken ad-
471
      vancements in related fields like formal verification and program synthesis. Looking forward, a
472
      skilled autoformalizer could make all mathematical knowledge, currently documented primarily in
473   natural language, programmable —– significantly broadening the usability of interactive theorem
474   proving systems and accelerating the growth of human mathematical knowledge.
