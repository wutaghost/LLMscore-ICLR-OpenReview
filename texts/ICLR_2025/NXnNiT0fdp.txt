009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) is computationally intensive because
012            it requires updating all parameters. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) improves effi-
013            ciency by modifying only a subset of weights but introduces a trade-off between
014            expressivity and computational cost: lower ranks reduce resources but limit ex-
015            pressiveness, while higher ranks enhance expressivity at increased cost. Despite
016            recent advances in adaptive LoRA techniques, existing methods fail to provide a
017            theoretical basis for optimizing the trade-off between model performance and effi-
018
               ciency. We propose Geometric Low-Rank Adaptation (GeLoRA), a novel frame-
               work that computes the intrinsic dimensionality of hidden state representations
019
               to adaptively select LoRA ranks. We demonstrate that the intrinsic dimension
020
               provides a lower bound for the optimal rank of LoRA matrices, allowing for a
021            principled selection that balances efficiency and expressivity. GeLoRA dynami-
022            cally adjusts the rank for each layer based on the intrinsic dimensionality of its in-
023            put and output representations, recognizing that not all model parameters equally
024            impact fine-tuning. Empirical validation on multiple tasks shows that GeLoRA
025            consistently outperforms recent baselines within the same parameter budget.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   LLMs are currently at the forefront of natural language processing tasks, yet achieving effective
031   personalization requires additional fine-tuning. Pretraining an LLM on a diverse corpus enables it to
032   learn general linguistic patterns and representations, which can be further refined through fine-tuning
033
      on task-specific datasets. However, fine-tuning the entire model is computationally expensive, both
      in terms of time and memory. To address this, a more efficient approach involves adjusting only
034
      a subset of the model’s parameters, known as Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Han et al.,
035
      2024). PEFT methods include techniques such as adapter layers (Houlsby et al., 2019), which in-
036   troduce new trainable layers into the model’s backbone, and approaches like BitFit (Zaken et al.,
037   2022), which modify a subset of the model’s original weights (e.g. bias weights). Low-rank adapta-
038   tion methods, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), decompose update matrices into low-rank components
039   and are particularly prominent in reducing computational costs, while maintaining comparable per-
040   formance to full fine-tuning.
041
      LoRA and its variants operate under the assumption that pre-trained language models possess a
042   low “intrinsic dimension” (Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Li et al., 2018), suggesting that weight updates
043   should similarly exhibit low rank. However, a key challenge with these techniques lies in determin-
044   ing the optimal rank values, which involves balancing expressivity and computational efficiency.
045   Expressivity refers to the model’s ability to capture complex patterns in the data, while computa-
046   tional efficiency pertains to the speed and resource requirements for fine-tuning. The trade-off is
047   evident: lower ranks reduce expressivity but enhance memory efficiency and computational speed,
048   whereas higher ranks increase expressivity at the cost of greater memory usage, longer computation
049   times, and most likely more data to learn weights reliably. Typically, ranks are set uniformly across
050
      all layers, with practitioners relying on trial-and-error to achieve a balance between expressivity and
      efficiency. This process is time-consuming and may not always yield optimal results.
051
052   On the other hand, using random projection to reduce the dimensionality of the parameter space
053   until achieving 90% of the full fine-tuning performance may not be ideal, as it inherently limits
      the model’s potential to achieve higher performance. Recent studies on the geometry of hidden

      representations (Valeriani et al., 2023) reveal that these representations also exhibit low intrinsic
055   dimensionality, reflecting the compression occurring at each layer of the model. This raises a natural
056   question:
057
058             Is there a connection between the manifold of data representations and the
059             manifold of model parameters?
060
      We theoretically investigate the relationship between the intrinsic dimensionality of data represen-
061
      tations and the ranks of weight updates in language models, deriving a lower bound for the optimal
062   rank based on the intrinsic dimensionalities of the input and output of each transformer block. Build-
063   ing on this foundation, we propose a novel approach, Geometric Low-Rank Adaptation (GeLoRA),
064   to address the trade-off between expressivity and computational efficiency by exploiting the geomet-
065   ric properties of the model’s hidden representations. GeLoRA leverages intrinsic dimensionalities to
066   provide a more principled mechanism for adjusting ranks, thereby achieving an optimal balance be-
067   tween model expressivity and computational constraints. Our method dynamically adjusts the ranks
068   for low-rank adaptation by considering both the compression occurring at each transformer block
069   and the specific characteristics of the model and dataset, offering a more precise and theoretically
070
      motivated balance between performance and resource efficiency.
071   Determining the ground truth intrinsic dimension of each hidden state is impractical; however, vari-
072   ous techniques can provide reliable estimates. Among these, we will adopt the Two Nearest Neigh-
073   bors (TwoNN) method (Facco et al., 2017), which has proven to be an effective estimator. It is robust
074   to variations in curvature and density within the data and has been widely used to analyze representa-
075   tions in deep neural networks in previous studies (Ansuini et al., 2019; Doimo et al., 2020; Valeriani
076
      et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Kvinge et al., 2023; Basile et al., 2024).
077   Contributions. The contributions of our work are as follows:
078

088            demonstrate that GeLoRA outperforms existing baselines while maintaining the same pa-

               rameter budget.

091   2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093   LLMs have achieved state-of-the-art performance in a wide range of natural language processing
094   (NLP) tasks across diverse domains. Models such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020) and BERT (Devlin
095   et al., 2019) have demonstrated exceptional proficiency in tasks including language modeling, sen-
096   timent analysis, machine translation, and question answering, which showcases their versatility in
097   natural language understanding and generation.
098   However, developing a more personalized model requires additional fine-tuning, which must be
099   handled efficiently due to the substantial computational costs involved. This is where PEFT (Han
100   et al., 2024) comes into play. It aims to balance the fine-tuning performance with the need to reduce
101   computational overhead by selectively adjusting a small subset of the model’s parameters, thereby
102   minimizing resource consumption, as compared to the more resource-intensive process of full fine-
103   tuning.
104   Within this framework, different lines of research in model fine-tuning explore various approaches
105   to optimizing efficiency. One such approach focuses on parameter tuning techniques, where only a
106   subset of model parameters is trained while others remain fixed. An example is BitFit (Zaken et al.,
107   2022), which exclusively adjusts the bias terms and the task-specific head within the model, leaving
      the remaining parameters unchanged. Another research direction involves the use of adapter layers


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      by introducing small trainable layers, known as “adapters” (Houlsby et al., 2019), into the model,
109   which enable adaptation to new tasks without altering the model’s original weights. Moreover,
110   context-based fine-tuning methods (Petrov et al., 2024) are used to influence model outputs through
111   input representation modification. Prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), for instance, appends task-
112   specific parameters to the input’s embedding, guiding the model’s responses without altering its core
113   parameters. Finally, LoRA (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023; Hayou et al., 2024) represents
114   a significant line of research that involves decomposing update matrices into the product of two
115   low-rank matrices to reduce the number of trainable parameters, while maintaining comparable
116   performance to full fine-tuning. Despite its advantages, LoRA faces challenges in determining the
117
      appropriate rank for the low-rank matrices. Typically, the rank is set uniformly across layers through
      a trial-and-error process, which is often suboptimal.
118
119   More recently, several LoRA variants have been developed to address the issue of setting uniform
120   rank values by dynamically adjusting the rank for each layer. These variants compute importance
121   scores or prune unnecessary ranks based on budget constraints, thereby optimizing rank alloca-
122   tion. Notable examples include AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023), SaLoRA (Hu et al., 2023), SoRA
123
      (Ding et al., 2023), and ALoRA (Liu et al., 2024), each offering strategies to improve fine-tuning
      efficiency. AdaLoRA dynamically allocates the parameter budget across weight matrices during
124
      fine-tuning using singular value decomposition (SVD). It adjusts the rank of matrices by assign-
125
      ing higher ranks to critical singular values and pruning less important ones, resulting in a sparse
126   selection of ranks. However, its heuristic criterion for sparsity selection lacks strong theoretical jus-
127   tification. Additionally, the computational complexity is increased due to operations like computing
128   moving averages for importance scores and handling gradients from orthogonality regularization
129   during training. On the other hand, SaLoRA dynamically learns the intrinsic rank of each incremen-
130   tal matrix using a binary gating mechanism and a differentiable relaxation method, which selectively
131   removes non-critical components. While this improves efficiency, removing these components may
132   introduce instability during training. To mitigate this, orthogonality regularization is applied to the
133   factor matrices, improving training stability and generalization. However, the optimization process,
134
      which involves Lagrangian relaxation and orthogonal regularization, increases the computational
      overhead. SoRA also adjusts the intrinsic rank dynamically during training by employing a sparse
135
      gating unit, which is learned through the minimization of the l0 norm via the proximal gradient
136
      method. Despite its promise, the sparsifying process lacks a strong theoretical foundation and may
137   struggle to generalize to new domains effectively. Lastly, ALoRA enables dynamic rank adjustment
138   during the adaptation process through two key steps: first, estimating the importance scores of each
139   LoRA rank, and then pruning less important or negatively impactful ranks while reallocating re-
140   sources to critical transformer modules that require higher ranks. However, the computational cost
141   of performing adaptive budget LoRA (AB-LoRA) can be high, which may hinder its practicality in
142   certain settings.

146   3.1   I NTUITION
147
148   Consider a linear map f : x 7→ W x, where the matrix W has low rank r. The low rank of W
149   implies that f compresses the semantic information of x into a lower-dimensional space, such that
150   dim ℑmf = r. While the functions approximated by transformer blocks are far more complex than
151
      a linear map, we will later show that intrinsic dimension profiles can provide valuable insight for
      selecting appropriate ranks for each layer of a language model. Specifically, they offer a lower bound
152
      on the number of parameters required to effectively encode information. To rigorously examine how
153
      the rank of hidden states correlates with the number of parameters needed for effective fine-tuning
154   in a transformer block, we present a formal theoretical framework in the next section.

      Ideally, we aim to compute the local dimensionality of the parameter space at each gradient step.
217   However, two primary challenges hinder this approach. Firstly, the information matrix behaves as a
218   random matrix, typically maintaining full rank with probability 1 (Feng & Zhang, 2007). Secondly,
219   the computational feasibility poses a significant obstacle, as computing the FIM at each step requires
220   extensive computational resources.
221
      While the FIM is almost surely of full rank, it often has very small eigenvalues, on the order of
222
      ϵ ∈ R+ . According to the Cramér-Rao bound, the variance of the parameter estimates is greater
223   than or equal to 1/ϵ. Therefore, parameters associated with such small eigenvalues provide negli-
224   gible information about the model and can be considered effectively uninformative. Disregarding
225   parameters with very small eigenvalues leads us to the concept of intrinsic dimension. The intrinsic
226   dimension is defined as the minimum number of parameters required to capture the local variance
227   of the data points effectively. Consequently, the intrinsic dimension represents a lower bound on the
228   local dimensionality.

234   Several significant challenges persist. First, the computation of the FIM and the determination of
235
      its rank are prohibitively expensive in terms of computational resources. Second, estimating the
      intrinsic dimension of the neuromanifold is also infeasible. Furthermore, the required number of
236
      parameters to optimize (i.e. the rank of the FIM) pertains to the entire model rather than to each
237
      independent matrix, resulting in a high lack of granularity.

239   However, we have access to the input data and its representations across different transformer blocks
240   within the large language model. Consequently, we can shift our focus to the data manifold, which
241

242
      sionality, we will begin by defining the components of the transformer blocks. Each transformer
243
      block comprises two primary components: a multi-head attention mechanism and a feed-forward
244   network. Additionally, it incorporates skip connections, which are essential for mitigating the rank
245   collapse problem, and a normalization layer.

326   We evaluate the performance of our GeLoRA technique across several natural language processing
327   tasks. First, we assess its performance on the GLUE benchmark for natural language understanding
328   (Wang et al., 2019), using tasks such as CoLA (Warstadt et al., 2019), SST-2 (Socher et al., 2013),

332
      instruction-following tasks by fine-tuning the model on the Airoboros dataset (Durbin, 2024) and
      evaluating on MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023a). For natural language understanding and question
333
      answering, we use the DeBERTaV3 model (He et al., 2021), following established practices in the
334
      literature. For instruction-following tasks, we fine-tune using Phi-2 (Li et al., 2023). We compare
335   GeLoRA’s performance against several fine-tuning techniques, including weight update tuning (Za-
336   ken et al., 2022), adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2021), and LoRA and
337   its variants (Hu et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).

413   Moreover, using dataset-specific ranks aligns with a common practice used to enhance performance
414   during fine-tuning across various benchmarks, which is intermediate task tuning. This approach
415   involves fine-tuning a model on a different task from the target task as a preliminary warm-up step.
416   While this methodology is primarily intuitively motivated—rooted in the idea of learning common
417
      features and fostering common-sense reasoning—its theoretical justification remains less clear. In
      this regard, we aim to provide a plausible explanation for the effectiveness of this approach. We
418
      focus on three tasks: MRPC (Dolan & Brockett, 2005), STS-B (Cer et al., 2017), and RTE (Dagan
419
      et al., 2006; Bar-Haim et al., 2006; Giampiccolo et al., 2007). Although each dataset has a specific
420   focus, they all assess semantic relationships between pairs of texts, presenting a strong case for a
421   sequential fine-tuning strategy. MRPC targets the identification of paraphrases, where two sentences
422   convey the same idea using different wording. STSB evaluates the degree of semantic similarity
423   between sentences on a continuous scale ranging from 0 to 5. RTE determines whether one sentence
424   entails another, reflecting a distinct aspect of semantic relationships. These tasks require the model
425   to comprehend nuanced semantic properties, including synonyms, paraphrases, and entailment. As a
426   result, the underlying language representations across these datasets exhibit significant similarities.
427   Consequently, we hypothesize that fine-tuning on MRPC can facilitate the subsequent fine-tuning
428
      processes for STSB and RTE.
429   We posit that the main reason for this enhanced performance stems from data compression, as the
430   model learns features relevant to the target tasks during intermediate training. To evaluate this
431   hypothesis, we theorize that the lower bound of intrinsic dimensions will become looser after com-
      pression. Our experimental results support this hypothesis. For instance, we observe a decrease in


                                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the mean intrinsic dimension for RTE (from 13.47 to 12.97) as shown in Figure 3a, while Figure
433   3b shows that the mean intrinsic dimension for STS-B remains consistent (from 13.19 to 13.01),
434   albeit with a change in their profiles as shown in Figure 3a and Figure 3b. Additionally, we note
435   similarities in the behavior of different layers: the lower layers, responsible for basic features (such
436   as syntax and grammar), remain largely unchanged; however, the higher layers, which capture more
437   complex features, exhibit significant compression. The intermediate layers, as indicated by recent
438   studies on the geometry of hidden representations, show a slight increase in their capacity due to the
439   model’s specialization in the semantics of the intermediate task. Thus, the decrease in the mean in-
440   trinsic dimensions corresponds to a reduction in the lower bounds presented in Corollary 3.2.1. This
441
      loosening of the bounds indicates that the number of parameters required for optimal performance
      has decreased, leading to more efficient training.

506   4   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
507
508   In this work, we introduced GeLoRA, a theoretically grounded technique designed for the efficient
509   fine-tuning of large language models. GeLoRA effectively addresses the expressivity-efficiency
510   trade-off inherent in low-rank adaptation techniques. Our approach is straightforward yet pow-
511
      erful, supported by theoretical analyses that ensure an optimal balance between expressivity and
      computational efficiency. We theoretically demonstrated that the number of parameters requiring
512
      optimization per transformer block is lower bounded by the difference in the intrinsic dimensions
513
      of the corresponding input and output hidden representations. This finding provides a method for
514   estimating the optimal ranks for low-rank adaptation techniques, and connecting the manifold of
515   data representations to the manifold of model parameters. Empirically, our methodology surpasses
516   current state-of-the-art approaches on the GLUE benchmarks while maintaining computational ef-
517   ficiency. Additionally, GeLoRA offers a potential theoretical justification for the effectiveness of
518   intermediate task tuning in certain scenarios. However, we acknowledge that our technique shifts
519   some computational overhead to the preprocessing step and relies on a local estimator of intrinsic
520   dimensions, specifically the Two Nearest Neighbors (TwoNN) method. We believe this aspect can
521   be further improved through the application of persistent homology dimensions to estimate intrinsic
522
      dimensions, as this approach considers both local and global topological features of the manifold.
      Moreover, it can be computed efficiently on GPUs by leveraging parallelization.

766   dimensional ambient space. The intrinsic dimensionality (ID) of M is defined as the smallest num-

768

974    B.2.1    P ROOF OF T HEOREM 3.1 – I NTRINSIC D IMENSION AS A L OWER B OUND
975
                                                                                     ˆ
       Theorem B.1 (Intrinsic Dimension as a Lower Bound). The intrinsic dimension idim(ϕ) is a
976
977
       lower bound to the local dimensionality d(ϕ).
978                                                          ˆ
                                                    d(ϕ) ≥ idim(ϕ).
979
980
       Proof. The local dimensionality d(ϕ) of a neuromanifold is defined as the rank of the Fisher In-
981    formation Matrix (FIM), which corresponds to the number of non-zero eigenvalues of the FIM.
982    However, in practice, while the FIM is almost surely of full rank, many of its eigenvalues can be
983    exceedingly small, on the order of ϵ ∈ R+ , where ϵ is a small positive threshold.
984
       According to the Cramér-Rao bound, the variance of parameter estimates is inversely proportional to
985
       the eigenvalues of the FIM. Specifically, for an eigenvalue on the order of ϵ, the variance of the cor-
986
       responding parameter is at least 1/ϵ. Parameters associated with such small eigenvalues contribute
987    negligible information about the model and can therefore be considered effectively uninformative.
988
989    By disregarding parameters associated with small eigenvalues, we obtain the definition of the intrin-
990
       sic dimension idim(ϕ), which represents the minimal number of parameters necessary to describe
       the structure of the manifold. The specific value of the intrinsic dimension depends on the threshold
991
       ϵ used to exclude eigenvalues below a certain magnitude. This threshold determines the uninfor-
992
       mative directions that are discarded, yielding an estimate of the ground truth intrinsic dimension.
993                                                         ˆ
       Consequently, the estimated intrinsic dimension idim(ϕ)       is always less than or equal to the local
994    dimensionality d(ϕ):
995                                                ˆ
                                                 idim(ϕ)  ≤ d(ϕ).
996
997                                              ˆ
       Thus, the estimated intrinsic dimension idim(ϕ) provides a lower bound for the local dimensionality
998    d(ϕ), completing the proof.
