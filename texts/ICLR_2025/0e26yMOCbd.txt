011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Over-smoothing is regarded as a key issue affecting the performance of deep
014            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). As the number of GNN layers increases, model
015
               performance degrades significantly, due to node embeddings converging into in-
               distinguishable vectors. This phenomenon stems from the recursive aggregation
016
               of neighbor node representations, which impairs the distinguishability of node
017
               embeddings. From an energy perspective, this is associated with the convergence
018            of node embeddings to a fixed point solution during the minimization of Dirich-
019            let energy, hindering the model’s ability to learn underlying geometric structures.
020            While Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) have achieved success in modeling
021            graph-structured data, there is still insufficient understanding of how the under-
022            lying geometry contributes to the trainability of deep GCNs. In this paper, we
023            present a novel geometric perspective to understand the poor performance of deep
024            GCNs during training, a method called Charge Dirichlet Energy (CDE-GNN).
025            We argue that maintaining a healthy geometric structure can significantly enhance
026
               the trainability of GCNs and enable state-of-the-art performance, even in base
               GCN architectures. Subsequently, we analyze the importance and feasibility of
027
               learning geometric shapes, demonstrating the critical role of geometric informa-
028
               tion in training deep GNNs. Extensive empirical validation on multiple bench-
029            mark datasets shows that our method improves the geometric shape of deep base
030            GCNs, significantly enhancing their performance and outperforming many state-
031            of-the-art methods in competitive settings. Our contributions include not only a
032            new approach to mitigating over-smoothing and over-compression but also com-
033            prehensive theoretical and empirical verification of the importance of geometric
034            structures for the trainability of deep GNNs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      GNNs have recently emerged as a hot topic in computer science and artificial intelligence Gori et al.
      (2005); Scarselli et al. (2008); Duvenaud et al. (2015); Hamilton et al. (2017); Xu et al. (2018a).
040
      GNNs have found widespread applications in fields such as computer vision and graphics Monti
041
      et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2018); Eliasof & Treister (2020), social network analysis Kipf & Welling
042   (2016); Defferrard et al. (2016), and bioinformatics Jumper et al. (2021). Most GNNs adopt the
043   message passing paradigm Gilmer et al. (2017), where learnable nonlinear functions propagate in-
044   formation across the graph Kipf & Welling (2017); Veličković et al. (2018). Specifically, information
045   from neighboring nodes is iteratively aggregated and used to update central node representations,
046   making GNNs well-suited for modeling complex relational structures (nodes and edges) in graph-
047   structured data. Many real-world domains naturally exhibit graph structures, and tasks based on
048   graph structures, such as social analysis Qiu et al. (2018), traffic forecasting Guo et al. (2019); Li
049   et al. (2019b), biology Fout et al. (2017); Shang et al. (2019), recommendation systems Ying et al.
050
      (2018), and computer vision Zhao et al. (2019), are commonly modeled using GNNs.
051   However, GNNs generally follow a common message-passing paradigm Gilmer et al. (2017), which
052   has significant limitations. These include restricted expressiveness Xu et al. (2019); Morris et al.
053   (2019), over-compression Alon & Yahav (2020); Di Giovanni et al. (2023), and the inability to
      capture long-range dependencies Li et al. (2018). Additionally, the propagation operators in most


                                                        1

054
      common architectures are constrained to be non-negative, leading to a smoothing effect in the prop-
055   agation process, which can result in over-smoothing Li et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2020a). As more
056   layers are stacked, node features become indistinguishable, and the performance of deep GNNs de-
057   grades significantly Zhao & Akoglu (2020b); Nt & Maehara (2019); Oono & Suzuki (2020); Cai
058   & Wang (2020). This phenomenon corresponds to the excessive shrinkage of Euclidean distances
059   between nodes, resulting in the loss of distinguishing information. Consequently, in practice, most
060   tasks only require a few layers (two or three) Qu et al. (2019).
061   Recent studies have analyzed the training of GNNs from the perspective of Dirichlet energy Cai
062   & Wang (2020); Zhou et al. (2021), showing that as the network depth increases, Dirichlet energy
063   decays to zero, limiting the expressive power of GNNs.
064
      In particular, during the process of over-smoothing, the node representations’ feature magnitudes
065
      diminish Oono & Suzuki (2019), high-frequency features are filtered out, and low-frequency features
066
      are diffused into noise Wang & Leskovec (2020). Geometrically, the norms of node representations
067   contract, converging toward a fixed point Gu et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2022);
068   Liu et al. (2022). This leads to edge-space contraction, structural collapse, and the loss of geometric
069   information. To increase model capacity, researchers have employed residual connections and initial
070   connections Xu et al. (2018c); Li et al. (2019a); Chen et al. (2020b) to alleviate over-smoothing and
071   improve model depth and capacity. However, model performance does not always improve with
072   increased depth.
073   To address these challenges, we propose a geometry-driven framework that designs learnable propa-
074   gation mechanisms based on a parameterized graph Laplacian operator. We define Hilbert spaces on
075   both vertices and edges, leveraging Dirichlet energy defined on edge space to measure the smooth-
076   ness on the graph. These parameterized methods provide flexibility in learning the geometric shapes
077   of vertex and edge spaces from data. To prevent Dirichlet energy from collapsing to zero, we impose
078   a minimum Dirichlet energy ω on node representations, effectively preventing unreasonable contrac-
079   tion of edge space and mitigating the homogeneity of node features. From the perspective of edge
080   space, the learnable ω allows the operator to flexibly adjust the distances between nodes, avoiding
081
      distance collapse caused by over-smoothing and enhancing the robustness of node representations
      by preserving geometric structure.
082
083   We validate the effectiveness of our model and theoretical results on various benchmark datasets.
084   Experimental results show that in most cases, our model outperforms both explicit and implicit
085   GNN baselines in two types of tasks, demonstrating significant advantages in addressing the issues
086   of over-smoothing and over-compression. Our main contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We propose a geometric framework based on a parameterized graph Laplacian operator,
089
               aimed at mitigating the problems of over-smoothing and over-compression in deep GNNs.
090
             • We theoretically analyze the importance of geometric shape learning and its profound im-
091            pact on the trainability of deep GNNs, rigorously proving the critical role of geometric
092            information in enhancing overall model performance and effectiveness.
093
094          • Through comprehensive and extensive empirical validation on diverse benchmark datasets,
095            we conclusively show that our innovative approach significantly improves the performance
096            of deep GCNs across various real-world scenarios, consistently outperforming numerous
097            state-of-the-art methods in comparative evaluations.

188   smoothing.

      4     M ETHOD
257
      In this section, we propose a novel GNN architecture, CDE-GNN, designed to effectively miti-
258
      gate the over-smoothing problem prevalent in deep GNNs. Building on the theoretical analysis of
259
      Dirichlet energy and graph geometry provided earlier, CDE-GNN introduces an ”initial Dirichlet
260   energy” term to preserve the original topological information throughout the layers. This approach
261   prevents excessive Dirichlet energy decay and maintains the discriminability of node embeddings.
262   The initial Dirichlet energy is designed as a lower bound, ensuring that the geometric structure of
263   the embeddings does not collapse during training.
264
265   4.1   N UMERICAL B EHAVIOR OF OVER - SMOOTHING AND T OPOLOGICAL C OLLAPSE
266
267   As discussed earlier, over-smoothing is characterized by the continual decay of Dirichlet energy. As
268   the number of GNN layers increases, the Dirichlet energy E(X (l) ) of node embeddings diminishes
269   and may eventually approach zero. This trend leads to node embeddings becoming indistinguish-
      able in high-dimensional space, exacerbating the over-smoothing problem. Furthermore, when the


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Dirichlet energy E(X (l) ) approaches zero, the distances between node embeddings shrink, causing
271   the entire network’s topology to collapse, with all node embeddings converging to the same fixed
272   point. This not only erases the discriminative information between nodes but also prevents deep
273   GNNs from effectively capturing and leveraging the underlying geometric structure of the graph,
274   severely limiting the model’s expressiveness and performance. Specifically, topological collapse re-
275   sults in a lack of diversity in node embeddings, making it impossible to differentiate between nodes
276   of different classes or structures, thereby negatively impacting downstream tasks.
277
278
      4.2     I NTRODUCING I NITIAL D IRICHLET E NERGY AS A S OLUTION
279
280   To prevent Dirichlet energy from approaching zero during training and causing topological collapse,
281   we propose incorporating the original graph topology as ”initial Dirichlet energy.” This energy is
282   continuously injected into each layer. Specifically, CDE-GNN updates node embeddings in each
283   layer by combining the feature aggregation of the current layer with the topological information
284   from the initial layer to maintain the geometric structure of the embeddings. The initial Dirich-
285   let energy serves as a lower bound for the Dirichlet energy, ensuring that even in deep networks,
286   the geometric diversity of node embeddings is preserved, thereby preventing the embeddings from
287
      becoming overly homogeneous and the topology from collapsing.
288

290

295
296
      where L̃ is the symmetrically normalized Laplacian matrix responsible for propagating and aggre-
297
      gating node features across the graph, W(l) is the trainable weight matrix at layer l, and σ(·) is a
298
      non-linear activation function (e.g., ReLU). Einit is the initial Dirichlet energy of the original graph.
299
300   The parameter α controls the contribution of the initial Dirichlet energy. By incorporating the term

      Dirichlet energy Einit captures the geometric information of the original graph and, when multiplied
304
      by the initial node embeddings X (0) , ensures that each layer’s update process retains the topological

305
      geometric diversity even in deep networks, avoiding the tendency to collapse into a single fixed
306
      point. The initial Dirichlet energy acts as a lower bound during the training process, providing the
307   necessary geometric constraints to ensure the model’s stability and trainability in deep architectures.

479
      This paper presents a geometric perspective on the over-smoothing and compression issues in deep
480   GNNs, revealing how increasing depth leads to indistinguishable node embeddings and adversely
481   affects model performance. We propose a geometric framework based on a parameterized graph
482   Laplacian operator, which controls the lower bound of Dirichlet energy to prevent geometric collapse
483   and mitigate over-smoothing. Both theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that this
484   method significantly enhances the trainability and performance of deep GNNs, particularly in node
485   classification tasks, outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods. Future work could explore
      extending the application of geometric information to heterogeneous and dynamic graphs.
