009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The automatic assembly problem has attracted increasing interest due to its complex
012            challenges that involve 3D representation. This paper introduces Jigsaw++, a novel
013            generative method designed to tackle the multifaceted challenges of reconstructing
014            complete shape for the reassembly problem. Existing approach focusing primarily
015            on piecewise information for both part and fracture assembly, often overlooking
016
               the integration of complete object prior. Jigsaw++ distinguishes itself by learning
               a category-agnostic shape prior of complete objects. It employs the proposed
017
               “retargeting” strategy that effectively leverages the output of any existing assembly
018
               method to generate complete shape reconstructions. This capability allows it to
019            function orthogonally to the current methods. Through extensive evaluations on
020            Breaking Bad dataset and PartNet, Jigsaw++ has demonstrated its effectiveness,
021            reducing reconstruction errors and enhancing the precision of shape reconstruction,
022            which sets a new direction for future reassembly model developments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The challenge of object reassembly spans numerous applications from digital archaeology to robotic
      furniture assembly, and even to the medical field with fractured bone restoration. Object reassembly
028
      problems are classified into part assembly, which deals with semantically significant parts (Zhan
029
      et al., 2020; Schor et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Dubrovina et al., 2019), and fractured
030   assembly, which handles pieces broken by substantial forces (Huang et al., 2006; Lu et al., 2023;
031   Wu et al., 2023b). However, existing approaches face a critical limitation: they lack comprehensive
032   understanding of the complete object when working with fragmentary inputs. This limitation is
033   particularly acute in real-world scenarios where only a subset of fragments is available, and current
034   reconstruction methods heavily rely on category-specific templates, c.f. (Thuswaldner et al., 2009;
035   Papaioannou et al., 2017). It underscores the need for a new approach that could address these gaps
036   and provide a complete shape prior to future research.
037   To address this fundamental challenge, we introduce Jigsaw++, a novel framework that bridges the
038   gap between partially assembled pieces and the complete object prior. Rather than replacing existing
039   assembly algorithms, our approach learns to synthesize plausible complete shape priors that can
040   guide the reassembly process. While previous methods have attempted to compose shape priors (Yin
041   et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2015; Deng et al., 2023) , they typically impose restrictive constraints, such
042   as requiring specific object categories or pre-existing complete shape templates. In contrast, Jigsaw++
043   learns to generate complete shape directly from partial assemblies, which enables our method to
044
      support a broader range of assembly scenarios.
045   Our approach draws inspiration from the recent success of 3D shape generators employing diffusion
046   models, which map Gaussian noise to instances on the data manifold. Based on this principle, we
047   propose to learn a complete shape prior through the generative model, then optimize the mapping
048   from the partially assembled input towards this complete shape space. Ideally, this method will
049   provide a realistic representation of what the complete object would look like based on the given
050
      input. Among many 3D representations, we focus on the point-cloud representation, due to its tight
      connection to the data acquisition devices and problem settings (Lu et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2020).
051
052   Learning a point cloud generative model for fractured object reassembly is difficult. Most approaches
053   require a fixed number of points and are also restricted to specific categories or need class conditioning.
      Another challenge is the scale of training data for learning shape priors. We overcome these challenges

079    In summary, our main contributions are as follows.
080
       • We introduce Jigsaw++, a novel method that imagine the complete shape prior through retargeted
081      rectified flow. The method generates comprehensive complete objects to serve as guides for the
082      assembly process.
083    • We develop an object-level point cloud generation module capable of adapting to a large or

091    • Jigsaw++ is orthogonal to the existing object reassemble methods. Our experiments on both the
092      Breaking Bad dataset and PartNet demonstrate its adaptability to various assembly challenges and
093      its ability to achieve significant improvements over baseline inputs.

       2.1       O BJECT R EASSEMBLY
099
       Object reassembly problem falls into two primary categories: part assembly and fractured assembly.
100
       In part assembly, semantic-aware learning methods have emerged in recent years. Specific tools
101
       designed for the assembly of CAD mechanics have been developed (Jones et al., 2021; Willis et al.,
102    2022). For the assembly of categorical everyday objects, research efforts (Schor et al., 2019; Li et al.,
103    2020; Wu et al., 2020; Dubrovina et al., 2019) have concentrated on generating missing parts based
104    on an accumulated shape prior to completing the entire object, although this approach can lead to
105    shape distortions relative to the input parts. More recent works (Zhan et al., 2020; Harish et al., 2022;
106    Li et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024) learns the part positions directly through regression or generative
107    methods. However, these methods require the input objects to be semantically decomposed in a
       consistent manner and necessitate specific training for each object category.


                                                          2
108
      The fractured assembly problem specifically addresses objects broken by extreme external forces.
109   Previous research in this area typically falls into two categories: assembly based on fracture surface
110   features or complete shape template. The former approach focuses on detecting fractured surfaces
111   and extracting robust descriptors, with early work (Ruiz-Correa et al., 2001; Gelfand et al., 2005;
112   Salti et al., 2014; Huang et al., 2006) employing hand-crafted features for assembly. More recent
113   learning-based techniques have introduced methods (Chen et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023b; Lu et al.,
114   2023; Scarpellini et al., 2024) using learned features for matching local geometries, or predicting or
115   generating piece positions. Another significant limitation of existing approaches is that they require
116   that most of the fragments be available as input. However, this assumption is violated in real settings
117
      where a significant potion of fragments is missing (Thuswaldner et al., 2009; Papaioannou et al.,
      2017), in which prior knowledge of the complete object is critical.
118
119   Existing approaches that use information of complete shapes are template-based methods (Yin et al.,
120   2011; Zhang et al., 2015; Deng et al., 2023). However, they often assume a specific complete shape
121   for assembly, but are typically constrained by specific categories or challenges in generating accurate
122   shape priors. Such settings do not apply to general-purpose fracture object reassembly.
123
124
      2.2   3D O BJECT G ENERATION
125
126
      The field of 3D shape generation has witnessed significant progress, driven by the application of
127   various generative models that produce high-quality point clouds and meshes. Techniques such as
128   variational autoencoders (Yang et al., 2018; Gadelha et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2021) and generative
129   adversarial networks (GANs) (Valsesia et al., 2018; Achlioptas et al., 2017) have been widely
130   implemented to process 3D data. Further enhancements have been achieved through the integration
131   of normalizing flows and diffusion models, which have spurred the development of state-of-the-art
132   approaches (Yang et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Luo & Hu, 2021; Zeng et al., 2022;
133   Lyu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a; Mo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Gao et al., 2022). People also
134   studied using 2D images and implicit neural fields to create text-guided 3D shapes (Xu et al., 2023;
135
      Ruiz et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023). Some approaches (Zhou et al., 2021; Lyu
      et al., 2021) also explored the generative shape completion which is highly relative to our task. These
136
      techniques strive to generate point clouds, SDFs, and meshes with both high fidelity and diversity,
137
      with some employing latent-based generation to even support multimodal 3D generation.
138
139   Our approach adopts comparable results in this space and addresses two fundamental challenges in
140   point cloud generation. The first challenge is limited paired 3D data we have for learning a shape
141
      prior. Our approach develops a mapping between point clouds and RGB images, allowing us to use
      pretrained models that take 2D images as the input. The second challenge is point clouds with varying
142
      number of points. We again address this issue using the mapping between RGB images and point
143
      clouds, which enable us to generate 3D point clouds with many more points than prior approaches.
144
145
146   2.3   D IFFUSION M ODEL AND R ECTIFIED F LOW
147
148   Our approach uses state-of-the-art diffusion-based techniques for learning the shape prior and the
149   mapping from inaccurate input to complete object output. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song
150   et al., 2021a; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b; Podell et al., 2023; Song et al., 2021b)
151   have demonstrated their versatility and effectiveness in a variety of generative tasks, including image,
152   audio, and video generation (Saharia et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022). These models
153
      operate via a forward process that incrementally adds Gaussian noise, coupled with a reverse process
      that gradually restores the original data, thus achieving high fidelity in the generated outputs. Beyond
154
      stochastic differential equation (SDE)-based approaches (Song et al., 2021b;a), recent efforts have
155
      emerged (Liu et al., 2023; Liu, 2022; Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo et al., 2023) focusing on directly
156   learning probability flow ordinary differential equations (ODEs) between two distributions. This
157   shift has led to improvements in generative efficiency and quality. Specifically, the introduction of
158   Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2023; Liu, 2022) implements a reflow process that significantly speeds
159   up the generation process, which is effective in large-scale image generation (Esser et al., 2024; Liu
160   et al., 2024). These collective advances highlight the transformative impact of diffusion models in
161   various generative modeling tasks. This work focuses on developing a fractured object reassembly
      approach that uses these generative models under novel 2D-3D representations.

197   3.2   A PPROACH OVERVIEW
198
      Jigsaws proceeds in two stages. The first stage learns a generative model to capture the shape space of
199
      complete objects. The second stage focuses on“regargeting” which reconstructs the complete shape
200
      from partially assembled inputs. Below we highlight the main characteristics of each stage.
201
202
      Learning Complete Shape Priors. The first stage learns a generative model of point clouds that
203
      capture shape prior of the underlying objects. There are many available point cloud generative
204   models (Zhou et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023). However, there are two fundamental
205   challenges in adopting them for our setting. First, most point cloud generative models are category
206   specific and use a fixed number of points. Therefore, it is difficult to adopt them to learn a category
207   agnostic model that requires different numbers of points capture geometric details of different
208   categories of objects. Second, 3D data is sparse, which is insufficient to learn a category agnostic
209   model to encode the shape space of objects in diverse categories.
210   Jigsaw++ adopts LEAP (Jiang et al., 2024), a pretrained multi-image-2-3D model to learn shape
211   priors. LEAP uses DINOv2 features, which are trained from massive image data. In doing so, our
212   generative model uses not only 3D data, but also 2D large-scale data. We introduce a bidirectional
213   mapping between uncolored point clouds and RGB images. This mapping addresses the domain
214   gap between raw 3D geometry and colored inputs to LEAP (as well as many other image-based 3D
215   reconstruction model). It also nicely addresses the issue of having a limited number of 3D points. We
      will discuss details in Sec. 4.


                                                          4
216
      Reconstruction through Retargeting. The second stage learns the reconstruction model that takes
217   the assembly result of an off-the-shelf method as input and outputs a complete 3D model. A standard
218   approach is to formulate this procedure as inversion-based methods (Song et al., 2021a; Mokady
219   et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). In the image generation setting, the input is first
220   inverted or mixed with noise and then re-generated.
221
      The difference in our setting is that the inputs are biased partially assembled objects, and we do not
222
      have quantification of which part of the input is correct and which is not. In contrast, image-based
223   conditions in existing approaches are unbiased complete objects. Due to this distribution shift, if we
224   naively condition the learned generative model on the biased inputs, the resulting 3D shape is also
225   biased. This is because not all latent codes in standard latent spaces correspond to valid 3D shapes.
226   Addressing this issue requires a “retargeting” phase where the model is fine-tuned to understand the
227   disparities between the partially assembled and complete objects.
228
      In addition to fine-tuning, the typical approach for guidance-based generation in diffusion models
229   involves performing reverse sampling, mixing the latent representation with a certain level of noise,
230   and then executing forward sampling (standard generation). As diffusion-based models often require
231   extensive sampling steps, we opt for the rectified flow (Liu et al., 2023) formulation, which allows
232   for skip-over of steps during inverse sampling, thereby accelerating the fine-tuning process. This
233   necessitates the use of rectified flow as the formulation for our generative model in the first stage. We
234   will discuss details in Sec. 5.

241   Bi-directional Mapping between Point Clouds and Images Our generative framework is built
242   upon a bi-directional mapping between point clouds and 2D images. Specifically, consider a nor-
243   malized point cloud represented as o ∈ [0, 1]N ×3 . Each point oi ∈ [0, 1]3 within this cloud, is
244   associated with a function f : [0, 1]3 → [0, 255]3Z . This function maps each point oi to a color vector
245   ci ∈ [0, 255]3Z in the RGB space, where the mapping process is described by ci = f (oi ) = ⌊255ci ⌋.
246   Please note that, although the color space is treated with integer values in this context, for applications
247
      involving image-to-3D reconstruction models, the color values can be maintained as fractional,
      thereby preserving accuracy throughout the transformation process. While similar coordinate-to-color
248
      mappings have been explored in pose estimation and reconstruction tasks (Wang et al., 2019; Sridhar
249
      et al., 2019), our work presents its first application to 3D generation.
250
251   The forward mapping from point cloud to image space is achieved through rasterization under
252   specified camera poses. Conversely, the inverse mapping f ′ reconstructs 3D coordinates from color
253
      values as oi = f ′ (ci ) = 255

                                     ci . This enables the recovery of point clouds from colored images
      encoded under our scheme. We further refine the reconstructed points through camera-ray alignment,
254
      projecting each decoded 3D point onto the ray connecting its corresponding pixel to the camera
255
      center. By aggregating multiple views from strategically selected camera poses, we can reconstruct a
256   complete object point cloud with controllable point density.
257
258   This bi-directional mapping establishes a cyclic relationship: given a set of camera poses, we can
259
      render a sequence of images from a colored point cloud, and conversely, reconstruct the original point
      cloud from these images and camera parameters with high fidelity.

265   Jigsaw++ uses LEAP as the image encoder backbone. It provides a global embedding g from the

362   number of steps required during the reverse sampling phase - to just 1/25 of the original steps - while
363   preserving a faithful latent representation. This efficiency is key to decreasing the fine-tuning cost.

476   performs best when k = 1/10. A full reverse sampling phase tends to overly mimic the input,
477   which is suboptimal for reconstruction. Moreover, setting k too low can cause the latent to deviate

      7      C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      In this study, we present Jigsaw++, a novel framework developed to tackle the challenge of complete
      shape reconstruction in object reassembly tasks. Jigsaw++ utilizes a novel point cloud generative
532
      model that reimagines the complete object shape from partially assembled inputs. By incorporating
533
      image-to-3D reconstruction techniques, Jigsaw++ adeptly navigates the challenges of scale and
534   diversity in training data. Additionally, we show the rectified flow formulation enhances our proposed
535   “retargeting” phase, establishing a more robust connection between the latent space and the complete
536   object space. Experimental results demonstrate Jigsaw++’s superior reconstruction performance,
537   marking a significant improvement over existing methods. Although we have achieved successful
538   reconstructions, we have yet to devise methods to effectively leverage our outputs as guidance for
539   further reconstructions. This limitation opens up new avenues for research in the field of object
      reassembly.

834
835   Generating a complete shape prior based on a partially assembled object is a relatively new problem
836   that is often underestimated in its complexity. We explored several intuitive solutions during the de-
837   velopment of our method to demonstrate the challenges involved. While it is impossible to enumerate
838
      all potential solutions, we have selected representative approaches to highlight the uniqueness of
      our task. The difficulty in providing an accurate shape prior stems from two main challenges: (1)
839
      Lack of quantification of assembly errors: We do not know which pieces are correctly assembled
840
      and which are not. (2) Balancing shape alteration: The algorithm must adapt to varying degrees of
841   assembly accuracy, from minor adjustments for nearly perfect assemblies to significant corrections
842   for misplaced or incomplete pieces.
843
844
      We tested four representative algorithms using state-of-the-art models and evaluated their performance
      on four test cases. Figure 7 illustrates the results of these experiments.
845
846
      Point Cloud Completion We adopted AdaPoinTr (Yu et al., 2023) using their open-sourced code
847
      and model trained on the ShapeNet dataset. We provided the algorithm with a subset of correctly
848
      placed pieces from Jigsaw’s result. The algorithm exhibited the following limitations: (a) It interpreted
849   the subset of parts as a complete shape, resulting in no additional completion as in the first bottle. (b)
850   With more parts in the second bottle, it completed the top slightly, but the was sparse and limited
851   in range. (c) It produce a resonable result for the plate which most closely resembled a typical
852   completion task, while for the vase, it over-correct the given input.
853
854   Point Cloud Generative Model with Editing We employed LION (Zeng et al., 2022) with the
855   SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022) model using their open-sourced code. The results showed that (a) the
856   generated shapes with similar overall forms (e.g., thin long shape for bottle input, flat shape for plate),
857   but (b) unable to to consistently maintain the correct object category.
858
859   Point Cloud Auto-encoders We utilized LION’s (Zeng et al., 2022) VAE to assess the effectiveness
860
      of reconstruction. Results showed that the output was mostly identical to the input, with only minor
      changes towards the desired shape. This behavior is consistent with the VAE’s objective of accurate
861
      shape reconstruction.
862
863   While these methods excel in their designed tasks, they fall short in addressing the specific challenges
      of inferring complete shape prior for the reassembly problems.


                                                         16
864
865
866   Inversion-then-Generate We evaluate the effectiveness of direct inversion-then-generate pipeline
867   to show how “retargeting step” influence the result. Using the same generator parameters and inversion
868   settings as Jigsaw++ experiments, we observe that this baseline approach yields improvements on
869   simpler cases (e.g., bottles and vases with minor variations). However, it demonstrates significant
870   limitations when substantial modifications are required, exhibiting failure patterns similar to direct
871   VAE reconstruction. These results suggest that the inversion-then-generate approach alone lacks the
872   flexibility to accommodate major structural changes, underscoring the importance of our retargeting
873   mechanism in handling complex shape difference.
874
875
876   Conditional Generation One potential solution is to train a conditional generative model by
877   finetuning our first-stage model with partially assembled inputs as conditions, similar to techniques
878   used in recent 2D generation tasks (Ho et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023; Valevski et al., 2024). We
879   implement this by incorporating partial assembly point clouds as additional input tokens during the
880   finetuning process.
881   This conditional approach achieves stronger baseline performance with a Chamfer Distance of
882   4.8 × 10−3 , 46.3% precision, and 50.6% recall. While its Chamfer Distance matches our retargeting
883   method, the precision falls below input level despite achieving higher recall. Qualitative analysis
884   reveals the underlying behavior: the model excels at smoothing input geometry and completing
885   missing regions (hence higher recall) but struggles to correct misplaced parts (resulting in lower
886   precision). In contrast, our retargeting approach achieves a better balance among the three key
887
      challenges of this task: correcting misplaced parts, completing missing regions, and maintaining valid
      shape structure. This comparison validates the effectiveness of our retargeting strategy in handling
888
      the unique requirements of assembly-guided shape prior generation.

       E    A PPLY J IGSAW ++ T O A SSEMBLY P ROBLEM
1027
1028
       To clarify how Jigsaw++ enhances practical assembly tasks, we present a complete pipeline overview
1029   in Fig. 10. Our method serves as an intermediate step that provides complete shape prior as an
1030   additional level of information to improve assembly accuracy.
1031
1032
       Given fragment point clouds, the pipeline operates in three phases:
1033
            1. Initial Assembly: Existing methods (e.g., Jigsaw, SE(3)) compute initial fragment place-
1034
               ments using local geometric features, producing a partially assembled object.
1035
1036
            2. Shape Prior Generation: Jigsaw++ processes this partial assembly to generate a complete
               shape prior in the same point cloud representation as the input fragments.
1037
1038        3. Assembly Refinement: The shape prior guides fragment placement optimization (through
1039           geometric matching in our example) between fragments and the complete shape. This
1040           produces refined transformation matrices for each fragment, improving the final assembly
1041
               accuracy.
1042
       Importantly, this pipeline can be extended in future work by developing more sophisticated matching
1043   algorithms between fragments and shape priors, or by incorporating the shape prior directly into
1044   existing assembly optimization objectives.
