015                                               A BSTRACT
016
017            In this paper, we explore semantic clustering properties of deep reinforcement
018            learning (DRL) to improve its interpretability and deepen our understanding of the
019            internal semantic organization. In this context, semantic clustering refers to the
020
               ability of neural networks to cluster inputs based on their semantic similarity in the
               internal space. We propose a DRL architecture that incorporates a novel semantic
021
               clustering module, which includes both feature dimensionality reduction and online
022
               clustering. This module integrates seamlessly into the DRL training pipeline,
023            addressing the instability of t-SNE and eliminating the need for extensive manual
024            annotation in the previous semantic analysis methods. Through experiments, we
025            validate the effectiveness of the proposed module and demonstrate its ability to
026            reveal semantic clustering properties within DRL. Furthermore, we introduce
027            new analytical methods that leverage these properties to provide insights into the
028            hierarchical structure of policies and the semantic organization within the feature
029            space. These methods also help identify potential risks within the model, offering
030            a deeper understanding of its limitations and guiding future improvements.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been widely applied in various domains such as robotics, au-
035
      tonomous systems, game playing, and healthcare, due to its ability to solve complex decision-making
036   tasks (Ernst & Louette, 2024; Li, 2023; Shakya et al., 2023). However, the black-box nature of DRL
037   models makes the decision-making process difficult to understand, potentially leading to unforeseen
038   risks. In this study, we explore semantic clustering properties to improve the interpretability of
039   DRL models. Semantic clustering refers to the process of grouping information based on underlying
040   semantic similarities in the feature space of neural networks. Studying semantic clustering in DRL
041   helps uncover the model’s internal knowledge structure and reveals semantic relationships between
042   states, enhancing both the interpretability and transparency of DRL models.
043   Although semantic clustering has been thoroughly investigated in natural language processing
044   (NLP) (Rong, 2014; Pennington et al., 2014) and computer vision (CV) (Long et al., 2023; Saha
045   et al., 2023; Park et al., 2021; Zhou & Wei, 2020), it remains underexplored in DRL, largely due to
046   the complexity introduced by temporal dynamics and the absence of direct supervised signals. The
047   sequential nature of decisions in DRL further complicates the task of capturing evolving semantics.
048   Early work introduced external constraints—e.g., bisimulation (Kemertas & Aumentado-Armstrong,
049   2021; Zhang et al., 2021) and contrastive learning (Eysenbach et al., 2022; Agarwal et al., 2021;
050
      Laskin et al., 2020)—to shape feature spaces conducive to semantic clustering. In contrast, we focus
      on investigating whether DRL can intrinsic exhibit semantic clustering capabilities.
051
052   Mnih et al. (2015) and Zahavy et al. (2016) analyzed the semantic distribution of the DRL feature
053   space for Atari games using t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) (van der Maaten
      & Hinton, 2008). Nevertheless, these studies are limited in multiple ways: (i) they are limited to a


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      small set of Atari games with fixed scenes, making it difficult to distinguish whether clustering arises
055   from pixel similarity or semantic understanding, (ii) Zahavy et al. (2016) manually define features
056   for specific games, limiting the adaptability of their method to other environments, and (iii) both
057   studies rely on t-SNE visualization for semantic analysis, which tends to produce unstable results
058   and lacks an automated clustering mechanism. Consequently, these approaches require significant
059   manual effort for feature space annotation and analysis, hindering comprehensive semantic analysis
060   and integration into downstream tasks.
061   Specifically, we make the following key contributions:
062
063          • We perform a comprehensive exploration of semantic clustering properties in DRL, advanc-

065            that uses a small set of Atari games with fixed scenes, we use Procgen 1 (Cobbe et al., 2020),
066            which offers rich semantic diversity and dynamic, procedurally generated environments,
067            making it well-suited for investigating semantic clustering in DRL.
068          • We introduce a novel architecture that addresses the limitations in previous semantic analysis
069            methods based on t-SNE, providing a more stable and effective approach for studying
070            semantic properties in DRL.
071
072
             • We present new methods for model and policy analysis, which help to understand the internal
               semantic distribution, the hierarchical structure of policies, and identify potential risks in
073
               the DRL model.

078   Semantic Clustering in NLP and CV Prior work in NLP has shown that the spatial arrangement
079   of word embeddings reflects semantic similarities, with semantically-related terms forming clusters
080   in the embedding space (Pennington et al., 2014; Rong, 2014). Similarly, in computer vision, images
081   with similar content are positioned closely in the learned feature space (Long et al., 2023; Saha et al.,
082   2023; Park et al., 2021; Zhou & Wei, 2020).
083
084   Semantic Clustering in DRL Mnih et al. (2015) and Zahavy et al. (2016) have previously explored
085   visualizing the DRL feature space using t-SNE. In these studies, t-SNE visualizations show that
086   features of states with close pixel distances tend to cluster together. However, due to the fixed nature
087   of the scenarios they used (Atari games), semantic clustering could not be conclusively verified. This
088   limitation motivates our use of Procgen to validate our approach.
089
090   Interpretability of DRL DRL interpretability research often focuses on video games due to their
091   controlled environments and clear rules, which make analyzing decision-making processes easier.
092   PW-Net (Kenny et al., 2023) enhances interpretability by using human-friendly prototypes to explain
093   the model’s decision-making. DIGR (Xing et al., 2023) improves interpretability by generating
094   saliency maps that highlight the most relevant features influencing the agent’s decisions. Concept
095
      policy models integrate expert knowledge into multi-agent RL, enabling real-time intervention and
      clearer interpretation of agent behavior (Zabounidis et al., 2023). MENS-DT-RL (Costa et al.,
096
      2024) applies decision trees to provide a transparent, rule-based explanation of the learning process.
097
      Furthermore, attention mechanisms and symbolic reasoning frameworks have also been applied to
098   enhance interpretability (Shi et al., 2022; Mott et al., 2019; Lyu et al., 2019). Our work explores
099   how DRL models internally organize information, offering new insights into the structure of learned
100   representations.

130   Background Vector-quantized variational autoencoders (VQ-VAE) (van den Oord et al., 2017) are
131   a family of successful generative models that combine the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework
132   with discrete latent representations through a novel parameterization of the posterior distribution. The
133   VQ-VAE workflow begins with an encoder network Ê that maps an input x to a latent representation
134   Ê(x). This representation is then quantized by mapping it to the nearest embedding in a codebook
135   {ek |k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , K}}. The quantized representation is then passed into a decoder network D̂ to
136   reconstruct the input x. The loss function for VQ-VAE is defined as:
137                                          2                        2                         2

                 LVQ-VAE = x − D̂(ek ) + sg Ê (x) − ek + β sg (ek ) − Ê (x) ,                             (1)
                                                    2                                2                         2
139   where sg is a stop-gradient operator and β weights the distance reduction between the encoded output

      Ê(x) and its closest embedding ek .
141
142   In this paper, we modify VQ-VAE to achieve the following objectives: (i) Assign features to
143   the nearest VQ embedding for clustering. (ii) Seamlessly integrate with DRL training, enabling
144
      simultaneous clustering and policy learning. (iii) Enhance clustering and interpretability through
      joint training with additional losses. Further details are provided in § 3.2.

      3.1   S EMANTIC C LUSTERING M ODULE
148   To overcome the limitations of previous t-SNE-based semantic analyses (see § 1), we propose a novel
149   semantic clustering module, which includes dimensionality reduction and online clustering.
150
151   Dimensionality Reduction Given the complexity of states in most DRL applications, their features
152   are often high-dimensional. For example, DQN (Mnih et al., 2015) uses 512-dimensional features
153   when trained on Atari games. Clustering high-dimensional features is challenging due to the “curse
154   of dimensionality,” which diminishes the effectiveness of distance metrics as dimensions increase,
155   making it difficult to assess data point similarity accurately (Beyer et al., 1999; Aggarwal et al., 2001).
156   To mitigate these issues, we reduce feature dimensionality before clustering, resulting in more robust
157   and interpretable clustering outcomes. This not only simplifies the clustering process but also enables
158
      human-interpretable visualizations, typically in 2D.
159   The instability of t-SNE arises from its non-convex objective function, making it highly sensitive
160   to initialization and leading to varied visualization outcomes (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008;
161   Wattenberg et al., 2016). To overcome these challenges, we propose the Feature Dimensionality
      Reduction (FDR) network. This network remaps high-dimensional features to 2D using policy


                                                                     3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      training data for online training, ensuring stable and efficient mappings. The FDR network’s loss
163   function is designed to preserve the consistency of distance relationships between high-dimensional
164   and 2D feature spaces, measured by pairwise similarities as described in § 3.2.
165
166   Online Clustering To reduce the labor involved in extensive manual annotation from previous
167   work and to facilitate the integration of downstream tasks, we propose using online clustering to
168   segment the feature space and assist in semantic analysis. We modify the VQ-VAE framework to
169   achieve online clustering, as detailed in § 3.2.

      Improved Clustering Our loss design not only achieves dimensionality reduction and clustering
217   but also enhances the clustering properties of the feature space, making states within each cluster
218   more compact and the cluster boundaries more separable. This improvement is crucial for clearly
219   distinguishing the semantics of states at the cluster boundaries, further enhancing the model’s
220   interpretability. We demonstrate the improved clustering in § 4 and provide more experimental
221   evidence of this enhanced clustering property and the intrinsic nature of semantic clustering in DRL
222   in Appendix C.
223   This enhanced clustering arises from the fact that although the stop-gradient operation (sg) in L′VQ-VAE
224   prevents FDR features from moving towards the closest VQ embeddings, when the FDR features
225   become denser under the influence of LFDR , it indirectly reduces L′VQ-VAE and, consequently, Ltotal .
226   In other words, training with both LFDR and L′VQ-VAE decreases the sum of distances between FDR
227   features and their corresponding VQ embeddings within the same cluster. Additionally, since LFDR
228   aims to bring p and q closer, the improved cluster density in the FDR feature space is also transferred
229   to the state feature space.

340   4.2   S EMANTIC C LUSTERING IN DRL
341
342   In this section, we illustrate semantic clustering analysis using the Ninja game, in which the agent
343   goes from left to right, jumping over various ledges and scores points by touching the mushroom on
344   the far right. In Appendix D, we analyze additional games, reaching similar conclusions.
345
346
      Mean Image Analysis We performed a qualitative analysis of the mean images of states within
347   each cluster. Figure 3 presents state examples from the FDR space of Ninja along with the mean
348   images of each semantic cluster, and Table 1 contains natural language descriptions of the clusters as
349   well as notable features of the mean images corresponding to each cluster. Corresponding videos can
350   be found in the supplementary material.
351
      Unlike static semantic clustering in some CV and NLP tasks, where clustering is based on a single
352
      image or word, DRL’s semantic clustering is dynamic in nature—state sequences with similar
353   semantics are grouped into the same semantic cluster. Notably, this semantic clustering goes beyond
354   pixel distances and operates on a semantic understanding level of the environment, as illustrated in
355   figures 3 and 4. This generalized semantic clustering emerges from the DRL model’s inherent ability
356   to learn and summarize from changing scene dynamics, independent of external constraints like
357   bisimulation or contrastive learning, and without the need for supervised signals. The neural network’s
358   internal organization of policy-relevant knowledge indicates clustering-based spatial organization
359   based on semantic similarity. Furthermore, we find that video sequences within clusters can be
360   summarized using natural language, akin to the ‘skills’ humans abstract during learning processes.
361
362   Human Evaluation In addition to qualitatively analyzing the mean images, we hired 15 human
363   evaluators to validate the semantic clustering properties. Specifically, video sequences from each
364   episode are segmented into multiple clips based on the cluster each frame belongs to, and these clips
365   are grouped by cluster for evaluators to review. Each evaluator watched these grouped clips and
366   responded to three interpretability-related statements for two out of a set of three games (Jumper,
367   Fruitbot, and Ninja). The response for each question was chosen from a five-point Likert scale with
368   the following options: Strongly Disagree (1), Disagree (2), Neutral (3), Agree (4), and Strongly
369
      Agree (5). Further details on the human evaluation procedure are provided in Appendix G. The
      statements and the results of the human evaluation are provided in Table 2. The mean scores for all
370
      statement-environment combinatiosn are greater than 4, with the exception of statements 1 and 3 for
371
      FruitBot, for which the lower bounds on the mean set by the standard error of the mean (SEM) are
372   3.99 and 3.92 respectively. One explanation for the slightly lower score on FruitBot is that in the
373   description of behaviors within clusters in FruitBot, the agent’s relative distance to the wall ahead
374   (far/near) and the agent’s relative position on the screen (left/center/right) require a higher degree of
375   subjective judgment—in contrast, Jumper has a clear radar for direction and position information,
376   and Ninja has more explicit behavioral reference objects, e.g., ledges and mushrooms. These results
377   suggest that humans generally agree that our model possesses semantic clustering properties and
      supports interpretability.

      5    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
512
      Our approach has the following limitations that we plan to address in future work. First, semantic
513
      clustering depends on clear semantic distributions in the FDR space, which can become unstable when
514   policies deviate significantly from optimal behavior, leading to ambiguous groupings. To mitigate
515   this, more robust clustering methods may be needed to ensure stability even under suboptimal policies.
516   Second, as our approach is unsupervised, determining the optimal number of clusters is crucial. Too
517   few clusters can result in mixed semantic interpretations within a cluster, reducing clarity, while
518   too many clusters may lead to fragmented and incoherent groupings. We selected eight clusters to
519   balance interpretability and granularity, but future work could explore adaptive clustering techniques
520   that adjust cluster numbers dynamically based on task complexity. Finally, we plan to extend our
521   approach to additional DRL algorithms, benchmarks, and practical applications, which will help
522
      evaluate its generalizability and robustness in more complex environments, and multi-agent settings.
523
524   6    C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we investigated the semantic clustering properties of DRL. Using a novel approach
527   that combines dimensionality reduction and online clustering, we analyzed the internal organization
528   of knowledge within the feature space. Our method provides a stable mapping of feature positions
529   and enhances semantic clustering, revealing meaningful structures in continuous sequences of video
530   game states. We demonstrate that semantic clustering in DRL arises dynamically as the agent
531
      interacts with its environment. As the agent explores diverse states during reinforcement learning,
      it naturally clusters semantically related states based on spatial and temporal relationships. This
532
      dynamic clustering exploits regularities in the environment, offering a unique approach compared to
533
      the static clustering observations in NLP and CV.

792   C    S EMANTIC C LUSTERING AS AN I NTRINSIC P ROPERTY OF DRL
793
794
795
      We conducted a stop gradient experiment to further investigate whether semantic clustering is an
      inherent property of DRL. In this experiment, we applied a stop gradient operation to Equation 4
796
      and removed the connection between the VQ codes and the original state features. This was done to
797
      prevent the semantic clustering module from influencing the feature space and to observe whether
798   semantic clustering would still occur. The results, as shown in Figures C.1 and C.2, demonstrated
799   that states within the same semantic cluster continued to exhibit similar semantic interpretations, even
800   without the influence of the semantic clustering module. However, the boundaries between clusters
801   became less clear, making it more difficult to distinguish the semantics of states near the edges of
802   clusters.
803
      These observations suggest that semantic clustering is indeed an intrinsic property of DRL, driven
804   by the agent’s interaction with its environment during training. The proposed semantic clustering
805   module enhances this natural clustering behavior by increasing the density of clusters, thus improving
806   the separability between them. To fine-tune the influence of the module, we introduced a control
807   factor. At the beginning of training, the control factor is kept low, allowing the DRL training to shape
808   the feature space independently. As the policy becomes more optimized and the semantic distribution
809   of states becomes more organized, the control factor is gradually increased to further enhance the
      clarity and separability of clusters.

1225   I.2   I NTERPRETABILITY A NALYSIS
1226
1227   Figures I.2a and I.2b illustrate the FDR results for K = 4 and K = 12, respectively. Our method
1228   effectively produces clusters that are clearly separable, regardless of the VQ embedding number.
1229
       However, interpretability is influenced by the choice of K. When K = 12, the semantic clusters
1230   become overly fragmented, making it difficult to form coherent semantic explanations for clusters.
1231   Conversely, when K = 4, Table 6 shows that clusters contain multiple distinct semantic explanations,
1232   which negatively impacts interpretability.
1233
       Clusters with incomplete or incoherent semantic descriptions hinder interpretability by introducing
1234
       ambiguity in understanding the agent’s behavior. This lack of clarity complicates policy analysis
1235
       and makes it challenging to draw meaningful insights. Conversely, when a single cluster contains
1236   multiple interpretable behaviors, it increases the cognitive load for users who must disambiguate
1237   between these behaviors. Such a many-to-one mapping between behaviors and clusters undermines
1238   the straightforward identification of the agent’s current strategy, reducing the utility of clustering
1239   as a tool for decision-making. To address these challenges, it is essential to ensure a one-to-one
1240   mapping between clusters and explanations. When each cluster is associated with a single, coherent
1241   explanation, it eliminates the need for further distinctions within clusters, facilitating clear policy
       analysis and enhancing human understanding of the agent’s behavior.
