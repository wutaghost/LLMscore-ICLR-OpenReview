011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep generative models for structure-based drug design (SBDD), where molecule
014            generation is conditioned on a 3D protein pocket, have received considerable in-
015            terest in recent years. These methods offer the promise of higher-quality molecule
016
               generation by explicitly modelling the 3D interaction between a potential drug and
               a protein receptor. However, previous work has primarily focused on the quality of
017
               the generated molecules themselves, with limited evaluation of the 3D poses that
018
               these methods produce, with most work simply discarding the generated pose and
019            only reporting a “corrected” pose after redocking with traditional methods. Little
020            is known about whether generated molecules satisfy known physical constraints
021            for binding and the extent to which redocking alters the generated interactions.
022            We introduce POSECHECK, an extensive benchmarking suite for state-of-the-art
023            SBDD methods and find that generated molecules have significantly more physi-
024            cal violations and fewer key interactions compared to baselines, calling into ques-
025            tion the implicit assumption that providing rich 3D structure information improves
026            molecule complementarity. We make recommendations for future research tack-
027
               ling identified failure modes and hope our benchmark will serve as a springboard
               for future SBDD generative modelling work to have a real-world impact.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Structure-based drug design (SBDD) Blundell (1996); Ferreira et al. (2015); Anderson (2003) is a
      cornerstone of drug discovery. It uses the 3D structures of target proteins as a guide to designing
034
      small molecule therapeutics. The intricate atomic interactions between proteins and their ligands
035
      shed light on the molecular motifs influencing binding affinity, selectivity, and drug-like properties.
036   Employing computational methods such as molecular docking Trott & Olson (2010); Alhossary et al.
037   (2015), molecular dynamics simulations Klepeis et al. (2009), and free energy calculations Chipot
038   & Pohorille (2007), SBDD aids in the identification and optimization of potential drug candidates.
039
      Deep generative models for SBDD have recently attracted considerable attention in the ML com-
040
      munity Du et al. (2022); Isert et al. (2023). These models learn from vast compound databases to
041
      generate novel chemical structures with drug-like properties Gómez-Bombarelli et al. (2018). By
042   explicitly integrating protein structure information, these models aim to generate ligands with a
043   higher likelihood of binding to the target protein. In particular, advancements in geometric deep
044   learning Bronstein et al. (2017); Atz et al. (2021); Jing et al. (2020) have led to a new suite of gener-
045   ative methods, enabling the design of 3D molecules directly within the confines of the target protein
046   Masuda et al. (2020); Luo et al. (2021); Peng et al. (2022); Guan et al. (2023a); Schneuing et al.
047   (2022). These methods, which concurrently generate a molecular graph and 3D coordinates, pro-
048   vide the significant advantage of obviating the need for determining the 3D pose post hoc through
049   traditionally slow molecular docking programs – at least in theory.
050   Assessing the quality of molecules generated by these methodologies is not straightforward, with
051   little work on experimental validation, especially for de novo design Baillif et al. (2023). Typical
052   evaluation metrics (Figure 1a) focus primarily on the 2D graph of the generated molecules them-
053   selves, measuring their physicochemical properties (e.g. QED Bickerton et al. (2012)) and adher-
      ence to drug discovery heuristics (e.g. Lipinski’s Rule of Five Lipinski et al. (2012)). For effective

105   2   BACKGROUND
106
107   Deep Generative Models for 3D Structure-based Drug Design Many works have recently tried
      to recast the SBDD problem as learning the 3D conditional probability of generating molecules given

108
      a receptor, allowing users to sample new molecules completely de novo inside a pocket. Common
109   methods utilize Variational AutoEncoders (VAEs) Kingma & Welling (2013), Generative Adversar-
110   ial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014), Autoregressive (AR) models and recently Denoising
111   Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) Ho et al. (2020). LiGAN Masuda et al. (2020) uses a 3D
112   convolutional neural network combined with a VAE model and GAN-style training. 3DSBDD Luo
113   et al. (2021) introduced an autoregressive (AR) model that iteratively samples from an atom prob-
114   ability field (parameterised by a Graph Neural Network) to construct a whole molecule, with an
115   auxiliary network deciding when to terminate generation. Pocket2Mol Peng et al. (2022) extended
116   this work with a more efficient sampling algorithm and better encoder. DiffSBDD Guan et al.
117
      (2023a), DiffBP Lin et al. (2022) and TargetDiff Guan et al. (2023a) are all conditional DDPMs
      conditioned on the 3D target structure. DecompDiff Guan et al. (2023b) is another diffusion model
118
      that decomposes the ligand into fragments for which it considers separate priors for the diffusion
119
      process. FLAG Zhang et al. (2022) chooses a fragment from a motif vocabulary based on the pro-
120   tein structure and composes it with other motifs into a final ligand in an iterative fashion. GraphBP
121   Liu et al. (2022) utilises an autoregressive flow model to formulate the ligand design as a sequential
122   generation task.
123
124
      Related work Guan et al. (2023a) perform limited analysis of small chemical sub-features, such
125
      as agreement to experimental atom-atom distances and the correctness of aromatic rings within
126
      the generated molecule. Baillif et al. (2023) emphasize the necessity of 3D benchmarks for 3D
127   generative models. However, both of these works study the molecules in isolation rather than the
128   protein-ligand context. Both DecompDiff Guan et al. (2023b) and DiffBP Lin et al. (2022) take
129   steric clashes into account via their loss functions, but do not include steric clashes as a metric
130   in their evaluation. TargetDiff Guan et al. (2023a) includes an analysis of Vina Scores but does not
131   report any standard deviations on these. However, these standard deviations are critical in evaluating
132   the performance of these models as we demonstrate in this paper. The concurrent work PoseBusters
133   Buttenschoen et al. (2023) also focuses on benchmarking the biophysical plausibility of protein-
134   ligand poses but focuses on evaluating docking tools instead of molecular generation models. They
135
      also find generalisation to new sequences to be poor.

      3   M ETHODS
138
139
140
      In order to evaluate the quality of generated poses and their capacity to facilitate high-affinity
      protein-ligand interactions, we present a variety of computational methods and benchmarks in this
141
      section. These methodologies provide a thorough perspective on the poses produced and illuminate
142
      the ability of generative models to generate trustworthy and significant ligand conformations. Full
143   implementation details are given in Appendix A.
144
145
146
      Interaction fingerprinting Interaction fingerprinting is a computational method utilized in SBDD
      to represent and analyze the interactions between a ligand and its target protein. This approach
147
      encodes specific molecular interactions, such as hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic contacts, in a
148
      compact and easily comparable format – typically as a bit vector, known as a interaction fingerprint
149   Bouysset & Fiorucci (2021); Marcou & Rognan (2007). Each element in the interaction fingerprint
150   corresponds to a particular type of interaction between the ligand and a specific residue within the
151   protein binding pocket. We compute interactions using the ProLIF library Bouysset & Fiorucci
152   (2021).
153
154
      Steric clashes In the context of molecular interactions, the term steric clash is used when two
155
      neutral atoms come into closer proximity than the combined extent of their van der Waals radii
156   Ramachandran et al. (2011). This event indicates an energetically unfavourable Buonfiglio et al.
157   (2015), and thus physically implausible, interaction. The presence of such a clash often points
158   towards the current conformation of the ligand within the protein being less than optimal, suggesting
159   possible inadequacies in the pose design or a fundamental incompatibility in the overall molecular
160   topology. Hence, the total number of clashes serves as a vital performance metric in the realm of
161   SBDD. We stipulate a clash to occur when the pairwise distance between a protein and ligand atom
      falls below the sum of their van der Waals radii, allowing a clash tolerance of 0.5 Å.

      Strain-energy Strain energy refers to the internal energy stored within a ligand as a result of
179   conformational changes upon binding. When a ligand binds to a protein, both the ligand and the
180   protein may undergo conformational adjustments to accommodate each other, leading to changes
181   in their bond lengths, bond angles, and torsional angles. These changes can cause strain within the
182   molecules, which can affect the overall binding affinity and stability of the protein-ligand complex
183   Perola & Charifson (2004). Whilst there is always a trade-off between enthalpy and entropy, gen-
184   erally speaking, lower strain energy results in more favourable binding interactions and potentially
185   more effective therapeutics. We calculate the strain energy, Estrain = Egenerated − Eminimum as the
186   difference between the estimated energy minimum and the energy of the generated pose (without
187
      pocket). Note that evaluating the generated poses with the force-field directly will cause the en-
      ergy terms to explode, due to the slight imperfections in bond distances and angles in the generated
188
      molecules. Hence, we first perform at most 200 steps of relaxation using a force-field with a maxi-
189
      mum allowed displacement in atom positions of 0.1 Å. This fixes minor issues with the bond angles
190   and distances, preventing the energy terms from exploding, whilst staying faithful to the global bind-
191   ing mode of the generated conformer. An estimate of the global energy minimum is calculated by
192   initializing 50 conformers using ETKDGv3 and then minimizing using up to 200 steps of force-field
193   minimization (taking inspiration from Buttenschoen et al. (2023)). We then calculate the energy of
194   all these poses and take the minimum as our final value. Both conformer minimization and energy
195   evaluation are computed using the Universal Force Field (UFF) Rappé et al. (1992) using RDKit.
196
197   Docking Our final assessment involves measuring the level of agreement between the docking
198   programs and the molecules produced by the learned distribution in the generative model. Although
199   this is the most coarse-grained approach we employ and docking programs come with their inherent
200   limitations, they nevertheless contain useful proxies and serve as valuable tools for comparison. In
201   this procedure, we redock the generated pose using SMINA Koes et al. (2013). Next, we compute
202   the Root Mean Squared Deviation (RMSD) between the generated pose and the docking-predicted
203
      one across all generated molecules, thereby obtaining a distribution of RMSD values.

      In our study, we evaluate the quality of poses from seven recent methods: LiGAN Masuda et al.
210   (2020), 3DSBDD Luo et al. (2021), Pocket2Mol Peng et al. (2022), TargetDiff Guan et al. (2023a),

213   splits computed in Peng et al. (2022), which used a train/test split of 30% sequence identity to give
214   a test set of 100 target protein-ligand complexes which we use for evaluation. For each model, we
215   sampled 100 molecules per target. We give a more detailed overview of the CrossDocked dataset
      and its limitations in Appendix A.

      Results To discern whether the generated poses/binding modes produced by these models corre-
224
      spond to overall low energy states with few physical violations, our preliminary analysis involves
225   determining the extent to which minimized poses preserve information from the initially generated
226   binding mode. Therefore, we proceed to compute the RMSD between the model-generated pose
227   and SMINA-minimized pose Koes et al. (2013), with a lower RMSD value denoting a higher degree
228   of agreement.2
229
      The distributions of SMINA-minimization RMSDs of various methods are illustrated in Figure 2.
230
      We first consider CrossDocked as a baseline, which has a mean minimization RMSD of 0.59 Å.
231   Given that all the generative models were trained on these poses, we would expect to observe sim-
232   ilar performance. However, we find that all methods (except FLAG) have a mean score between
233   0.94 and 1.28 Å, suggesting that the generated binding poses are very far from low-energy states.
234   We observe little correlation between method types here except for the two similar autoregressive
235   models, 3DSBDD and Pocket2Mol, which obtain mean RMSDs of 0.99 and 1.02 Å respectively.
236   FLAG is the most egregious example with on average 3.64 Å RMSD during minimization and a
237   maximum value of 10.72 Å, an extreme value for local minimisation.
238   We also provide the raw affinities from our docking experiments in Table 1, both when evaluating the
239   generated pose using the SMINA/Vina score function directly (Vina Generated), after local energy
240   minimization (Vina Minimize) and redocking the molecule entirely (Vina Redock). Additionally, we
241   provide the change in Vina scores during minimization and redocking. We first draw our attention
242   to the scores for the redocked poses: these metrics are commonly reported and often used as a
243   justification for state-of-the-art performance. On the surface, the results look promising, with most
244   methods matching or exceeding the performance of the baseline dataset (although with no statistical
245
      significance). However, a worrying picture emerges when we measure the generated poses directly,
      with none of the models (except LiGAN) outperforming the baseline dataset. The mean scores
246
      for CrossDocked are -5.50 kcal/mol, whereas the generative models (except LiGAN and FLAG)
247
      have mean scores between -1.94 and -5.36 kcal/mol. FLAG again performs poorly with the Vina
248   scores for generated poses exploding to +94.20 kcal/mol, suggesting the generated poses are highly
249   implausible.
250
251
      We next consider the role energy minimization and redocking have on these final scores by consider-
      ing the change in affinity during the two processes respectively. The result of this analysis highlights
252
      that minimization/redocking is critical to getting acceptable scores out of these methods, calling into
253
      question the reliability of the generated poses. CrossDocked has a ∆affinity minimization score of
254   -0.74 kcal/mol, whereas the generative models (excluding LiGAN and FLAG) have scores between
255   -0.75 and -3.91 kcal/mol. FLAG has a score of -89.31 kcal/mol (unsurprising given the generated
256   poses). We see a similar picture for the impact of redocking, where the majority of methods see
257   substantially greater increases in their scores during the procedure. In conclusion, we show that
258   only reporting the mean Vina score of redocked poses hides critical failure modes found in many
259   models. 3
260
261   Discussion These findings raise concerns for several reasons. They expose the minimal concor-
262   dance between the binding models learned by these methods and the established SMINA method-
263   ology Alhossary et al. (2015), despite it being the source of training data. More critically, they
264   underline the lack of accurate evaluations of generative models’ capability to produce realistic bind-
265   ing poses; instead, these models tend to generate drug-like molecules with vague binding modes,
266   later rectified through docking.

309   4.3    P ROTEIN - LIGAND INTERACTION ANALYSIS
310
311   Evaluation Below describe the classes of interaction that we evaluate. Hydrogen bonds (HBs) are
312   a type of interaction that occurs between a hydrogen atom that is bonded to a highly electronegative
313   atom, such as nitrogen, oxygen, or fluorine Pimentel & McClellan (1971). They are key to many
314   protein-ligand interactions Chen et al. (2016) and require very specific geometries to be formed
315
      Brown (1976). The directionality of HBs confers unique identities upon the participating atoms:
      hydrogen atoms attached to electronegative elements are deemed ‘donors’, whilst the atom accepting
316
      the HB is termed an ‘acceptor’. Van der Waals contacts (vdWs) are interactions that occur between
317
      atoms that are not bonded to each other. These forces can be attractive or repulsive and are typically
318   quite weak Andersson et al. (1998). However, they can be significant when many atoms are involved,
319   as is typical in protein-ligand binding Barratt et al. (2005). Hydrophobic interactions are non-
320   covalent interactions that occur between non-polar molecules or parts of molecules in a water-based
321   environment. They are driven by the tendency of water molecules to form hydrogen bonds with each
322   other, which leads to the exclusion of non-polar substances. This exclusion principle prompts these
323   non-polar regions to orient away from the aqueous environment and towards each other Meyer et al.
      (2006), thereby facilitating the association between protein and ligand molecules Patil et al. (2010).

397   enhanced clash mitigation during redocking elevates its results to match the baseline, highlighting
398   methodological distinctions between it and TargetDiff. Notably, 3DSBDD and LiGAN show low
399
      clash scores, with the former positioning atoms within a predefined voxel grid Luo et al. (2021) and
      the latter applying a clash loss Masuda et al. (2020). DecompDiff also applies a steric clash loss (but
400
      does not directly measure clashes in the corresponding publication) Guan et al. (2023b) and performs
401
      best out of all the diffusion-based approaches. Generated molecules for FLAG were most egregious
402   here; we speculate this is a result of first choosing a fragment from a fragment vocabulary using a
403   softmax function and then forcing the placement of the fragment Zhang et al. (2022), regardless of
404   whether it fits sterically.
405
      Our findings affirm the assumption that redocking alleviates many minor clashes, akin to the force-
406
      field relaxation step in AlphaFold2 Jumper et al. (2021). We initially speculated that molecules
407
      with clashes exceeding 100 had been mistakenly generated inside the protein pocket. Yet, we often
408   discovered fragments within highly constrained nooks, especially worsened with the addition of
409   hydrogen atoms.
410
411   Limitations An important consideration to bear in mind is that proteins are not entirely rigid
412   receptors. They can often experience limited conformational rearrangements to accommodate
413   molecules of varying shapes and sizes Davis & Teague (1999). Consequently, conducting gener-
414   ation and redocking in a rigid receptor environment may not yield accurate scores for potentially
415   plausible molecules. Note all these results are with a generous clash tolerance of 0.5 Å (roughly half
416   the vdW radii of a hydrogen atom), in order to be able to resolve differences between methods.
417
418   4.5   S TRAIN ENERGY
419
420   To conclude our study, we provide an analysis of the strain energy Perola & Charifson (2004) of
421   the generated poses. Force field relaxation of the generated pose before docking is a common
422
      post-processing step of many generative SBDD pipelines, masking potential issues with the precise
      geometries of the generated molecules. Furthermore, high strain energy would be indicative that
423
      molecules are unlikely to bind.
424
425   In Figure 5a, we present the cumulative density function (CDF) of strain energy for all molecules
426   generated by various models, using the CrossDocked dataset as a comparative baseline. Note that the
427   x-axis is logarithmic. The data shows that most generative methods fall short of the CrossDocked’s
428
      median strain energy of 3.96 kcal/mol, with FLAG and Pocket2Mol being the significant exceptions.
429   Upon examining the impact of rotatable bonds on strain energy, two key findings emerged: Firstly,
430   molecules from generative models with a high count of rotatable bonds exhibit notably higher strain
431   (refer to Appendix Figure 7). Secondly, the more successful methods in Figure 5a tend to produce
      molecules with fewer or no rotatable bonds, as detailed in Appendix Figure 8.

436   5   R ECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
437
438   Exploring reduced-noise sampling strategies Interestingly, both diffusion-based works (DiffS-
439   BDD and TargetDiff) performed similarly in terms of strain energy (see Section 4.5). We hypothe-
440   size this may be due to the injection of random noise into the coordinate features at all but the last
441   step of stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics samplings Welling & Teh (2011), making it challeng-
442   ing to construct precise bond angles etc. Here, inspiration could be taken from protein design. For
443   example, Chroma develops a low-temperature sampling regime to reduce the effect of noise Ingra-
444   ham et al. (2022), FrameDiff effectively scales down injected noise Yim et al. (2023), both resulting
445
      in a substantial increase in sample quality with an acceptable decrease in sample diversity.
446
      Penalising steric clashes during training All evaluated methods frequently create steric clashes,
447
      resulting in physically unrealizable samples. We suggest that mitigating steric clashes is key for the
448
      next generation of SBDD models. This could be done via extra loss terms, for example, by including
449   a distogram loss as in AlphaFold2 Jumper et al. (2021) or the steric clash loss in LiGAN Masuda
450   et al. (2020) and DecompDiff Guan et al. (2023b) (note that the latter method does not explicitly
451   measure clashes). A similar loss-based approach has been effective in mitigating chain-breaks in
452   diffusion models for protein backbone design Yim et al. (2023).
453
454   Consider explicit representation of hydrogens Virtually all work in ML for structural biology
455   chooses to not explicitly represent hydrogen atoms Jumper et al. (2021); Peng et al. (2022); Luo
456   et al. (2021); Yim et al. (2023); Schneuing et al. (2022); Guan et al. (2023a), under the assump-
457   tion that they can be implicitly learned and reasoned over with deep neural networks. However,
458   our analysis of hydrogen bond networks within generated molecules found that generative methods
459
      struggle to handle the precise geometries required to make a hydrogen bond Brown (1976) (even
      when redocked). Despite the increased computational cost, we therefore recommend that future
460
      work explores their inclusion.

      6   C ONCLUSION
463
464
      In conclusion, this work presents a comprehensive exploration of structure-based drug design
465   (SBDD) methodologies with deep generative models. We advocate for the need to consider both
466   the quality of the generated molecules and the quality of the binding poses in these models, call-
467   ing for an expanded evaluation of SBDD. The application of deep generative models in SBDD
468   holds promise for developing innovative drug-like molecules. However, for SBDD approaches
469   to realise that potential, we must establish a rigorous evaluation regimen of both the generated
470   molecules and their interaction with the target – as proposed in this paper. Our research pro-
471   vides a solid evaluation regimen for future advancements in this field and we hope that it stim-
472   ulates further development towards more efficient drug discovery processes. Code is available at

      A     C ROSS D OCKED DATASET
649
650
651
      The CrossDocked dataset is a standard dataset used in the field of generative modelling for structure-
      based drug design Francoeur et al. (2020); since the models benchmarked here were trained on
652
      this dataset, it is the benchmarking dataset of choice. It was originally created by clustering PDB
653
      structures by ”pocket similarity’ via Pocketome Kufareva et al. (2012), i.e. grouping structures with
654   similar ligand binding sites together. To expand the dataset beyond this initial data, all ligands with
655   a molecular weight ¡ 1000 Da that were associated with a given pocked were docked into each
656   receptor assigned to that pocket via the docking tool smina Koes et al. (2013). This cross-docking
657   process results in the basis dataset CrossDocked 2020 Francoeur et al. (2020), which contains 2,922
658   pockets, 18,450 complexes and 13,839 ligands, together comprising around 22.5 million poses (i.e.
659   protein-ligand structures).
660   Most generative models are however not trained on this raw dataset, but on a filtered version of it,
661   following the procedure of the Pocket2Mol model Peng et al. (2022). As a quality control, data
662   points whose binding pose RMSD is greater than 1 Å were filtered out. This leads to a filtered
663   dataset with 184,057 data points. The mmseq2 program Steinegger & Söding (2017) was used to
664   cluster data at 30% identity, and training and test sets were created by randomly drawing 100,000
665   protein-ligand pairs for training and 100 proteins from the remaining clusters for testing.
666   The 100 proteins comprising the test set are on average around 320 residues long, with the biggest
667   protein having a length of 752 residues.
668
669
670   B     E XTENDED I MPLEMENTATION
671
672   B.1   M ETHODS I MPLEMENTATION
673
674   All generative methods accessed were trained using the same dataset and splits as proposed in Peng
675   et al. (2022). Docking protocols were done using the SMINA settings decribed in the original
676   CrossDocked paper Francoeur et al. (2020).
677
678   B.2   P ROCEDURE OF MODEL REPRODUCTION
679
680   For generated poses, we sourced molecules from Schneuing et al. (2022) for DiffSBDD, and Guan
681   et al. (2023a) for CrossDocked, TargetDiff, Pocket2Mol, 3DSBDD and LiGAN (where they provide
682   generated poses but we additionally perform our own redocking).
683
      For FLAG Zhang et al. (2022), no weights were provided so we retrained the model
684   as described in Zhang et al. (2022) using the code and config file available at
685   github.com/zaixizhang/FLAG. When sampling, we found that generation was attempted
686   100 times per target and then any molecules with fewer than 8 atoms were discarded. This ended up
687   encompassing the majority of molecules, resulting in small test sizes, so we implemented a while
688   loop to sample 100 molecules whilst keeping faithful to the filtering used in the codebase. Having
689   modified the code to work on GPU, sampling 100 targets took about 1-2 minutes per target on a
690   single A100 GPU.
691   For DecompDiff Guan et al. (2023b), we use the official implementation with the published weights
692   available at github.com/bytedance/DecompDiff. We sampled 100 samples for each of
693   the 100 targets using the sample diffusion drift.py script in ref prior mode. With the
694   provided code, sampling 100 targets took about 20-30 minutes per target on a single A100 GPU.
