011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In the realm of deep learning, the veracity and integrity of the training data are
014            pivotal for constructing reliable and transparent models. This study introduces
015
               the concept of Trustworthy Dataset Proof (TDP), which tackles the significant
               challenge of verifying the authenticity of training data as declared by trainers. Ex-
016
               isting dataset provenance methods, which primarily aim at ownership verification
017
               rather than trust enhancement, often face challenges with usability and integrity.
018            For instance, excessive operational demands and the inability to effectively verify
019            dataset authenticity hinder their practical application. To address these shortcom-
020            ings, we propose a novel technique termed Data Probe, which diverges from tradi-
021            tional watermarking by utilizing subtle variations in model output distributions to
022            confirm the presence of a specific and small subset of training data. This model-
023            agnostic approach improves usability by minimizing the intervention during the
024            training process and ensures dataset integrity via a mechanism that only permits
025            probe detection when the entire claimed dataset is utilized in training. Our study
026
               conducts extensive evaluations to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed
               data-drobe-based TDP framework, marking a significant step toward achieving
027
               transparency and trustworthiness in the use of training data in deep learning.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Against the backdrop of the rapid development of deep learning technologies, the reliability and
033   transparency of models are increasingly being scrutinized, and the authentic use of training data
034   is the cornerstone of building effective and trustworthy models (Shayne, 2024; Anomalo, 2024;
035   Aldoseri et al., 2023). However, the actual usage of training data is often based on the trainer’s
036   self-report, which is extremely difficult to verify in practice.
037   The authenticity and integrity of training data are significant security concerns in the field of deep
038   learning, as even minor tampering with the training data can lead to significant changes in model
039   behavior. For instance, numerous studies (Chen & Babar, 2024; Khaddaj et al., 2023; Mengara et al.,
040   2024; Saha et al., 2020) have shown that attackers can embed a small number of backdoor samples
041   in training data, which could be activated during the model’s deployment, posing security risks. On
042   the other hand, trainers may introduce illegal or non-compliant data to enhance the performance
043   of the model without informing the users, especially in competitions or commercial applications,
044
      raising concerns regarding the transparency and fairness. For example, high-profile lawsuits have
      been initiated against major tech companies like Google and StabilityAI, where plaintiffs argue that
045
      their copyrighted or personal data were used without permission to train AI systems (Sabine, 2024).
046
047   In this paper, we define the problem of Trustworthy Dataset Proof (TDP) to formally describe this
048   challenging task of verifying the integrity of training datasets, and further explore possible solutions.
049   As illustrated in Fig. 1, unlike the widely studied concept of data provenance, which focuses on the
050
      ownership verification of a personal dataset, the TDP problem seeks to verify the authentic use of a
      trustworthy dataset by the trainer, thereby enhancing trust.
051

053   problem, they still face several formidable challenges. ❶ Usability: these techniques often fail to

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Existing research on the protection and traceability of training datasets primarily focuses on verify-
093   ing the ownership of datasets Although this objective differs from the goal discussed in this paper,
094   which is to verify the integrity of training datasets to enhance credibility, we review these studies to
095   better understand the existing challenges and potential technical motivations, with a more detailed
096   discussion to follow in the Sec. 4.
097   Dataset watermarking. It achieves ownership authentication by embedding backdoors into training
098   datasets (Adi et al., 2018). Specifically, the owner of a dataset can select and modify a small subset of
099   samples to serve as backdoor triggers, which can induce atypical outputs from the model. Once the
100   model is trained on this dataset, the backdoor is automatically implanted. Subsequently, the copy-
101   right holder of the dataset can verify whether a suspected model exhibits the expected anomalous
102   behavior by activating the trigger, thereby confirming the occurrence of the training. Building on
103   this, many studies focus on enhancing the stealth and harmlessness of these backdoors. For instance,
104
      Tang et al. (2023) propose to construct a clean-label backdoor by applying adversarial perturbations
      to samples, which embeds a specific backdoor pattern without altering the labels of the samples.
105
106   Membership and Dataset Inference. Membership Inference (MI) (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al.,
107   2019; Yeom et al., 2018; Song & Mittal, 2021) is an attack targeting the privacy of training datasets,
      but its technical approach can also be reversed for tracing training sets. MI exploits the phenomenon


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of overfitting in deep learning (Yeom et al., 2018), whereby a model better memorizes samples from
109   its training set, distinguishing them from non-training samples. For instance, Shokri et al.(Shokri
110   et al., 2017) proposed training numerous shadow classifiers to reveal such distinctions. Other studies
111   analyze various score metrics produced by the model when different samples are inputted, such
112   as confidence (Salem et al., 2019), loss (Yeom et al., 2018), and entropy (Song & Mittal, 2021).
113   To further enhance the stability of verification, Maini et al. (2021) proposed the Dataset-Inference
114   (DI) technique, which actually conducts MI at the distribution level and utilizes the characteristic
115   differences in the model’s responses to training and test samples.
116   Proof of Training Data. Proof-of-Training-Data (PoTD) (Choi et al., 2024) is a protocol enabling
117   a model trainer to assure a verifier of the specific training data responsible for generating a set of
118   model weights, which can confirm both the quantity and type of data. The protocol mandates that
119   the model trainer meticulously document and provide comprehensive details throughout the training
120   process, referred to as a training transcript, including the dataset, training codes, hyperparameters,
121   and intermediate checkpoints. The goal of PoTD is similar to our work. However, it focuses on
122   ensuring a precise correspondence between the training processes and model parameters, but fails to
123
      verify the presence of subtle manipulations in the training dataset, as stated in their work limitations.

      3     F ORMALIZE TDP
126
      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
127
128   In the Trustworthy Data Proof (TDP) problem, two key roles are identified: model trainer and
129   verifier. The trainer is capable of utilizing a publicly credible dataset D to train a model M. On
130   the other hand, the verifier is tasked with providing a mechanism to verify whether M was trained
131   using D in a reliable and trustworthy manner. We formally define two functions to implement TDP:
132
      Definition 1 (Trusted Training) T-Train(D) → M, C
133
      Trusted Training is a training mechanism specified by verifier, denoted as
134
      T-Train(). It could be further represented as T-Train = T ∪ O, where T
135
      refers to general model training procedures, and O represents additional
136   operations required by the verifier. The trainer need to use T-Train() to
137   train models to meet the requirements for subsequent trustworthy verifica-
138   tion. Specifically, it performs T-Train(D) to obtain a trained model M,
139   and at the same time, get a certificate C for the subsequent verification .
140
141   Definition 2 (Verification) Verify(D,M,C) → {0, 1}
142   Verification is the process by which the verifier provide the proof, denoted
143   as Verify(). The trainer want to assert that M is trained on D. He then
144
      provides the verifier with M and C obtained through T-Train, as well as
      the D to be validated. Verify() outputs 1 to indicate that it judges the
145
      model is indeed trained on D, while 0 indicates it is not.
146

148   ditional operations O and C can both be empty ∅. At this point, T-Train ceptual framework of
149   and Verify describe the most basic process of solving the TDP problem TDP.
150
      without any assumptions, making them universal. ❷ Necessity: accord-
      ing to the consensus in the relevant research field (Shayne, 2024; Aldoseri et al., 2023; Choi et al.,
151
      2024), due to the high-dimensional complexity of DL models and the non-convex optimization train-
152
      ing process, it is difficult to directly verify the fact that M is trained on the complete D through M
153   alone. Therefore, the verifier should be allowed to intervene and restrict the training process to
154   enhance their ability during the verification process.

      3.2   T HREAT M ODEL
157
158   In TDP, the verifier is generally served by trusted authorities to ensure the fairness and effectiveness
159   of the verification process, and to defend against potential acts that may subvert the verification.
160   Therefore, the verifier is defined as the defender. On the other hand, the trainer may maliciously
161   exploit the TDP mechanism for unjust benefits, thus the dishonest trainer is defined as the at-
      tacker. In this section, we will analyze the goals and capabilities of each party separately.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Defender’s Goals. The fundamental goal of the defender is to ensure the correctness of the verifi-
163   cation (G1), accompanied by several performance-related goals (G2, G3, G4):
164
165   G1. Fidelity. If the trainer indeed carries out T-Train(D) → M, C, then the defender, executing
166       Verify(D,M,C), must output 1 with high probability. In all other cases, output 0.
167   G2. Low-Invasiveness. In T-Train = T ∪ O, it is advisable to minimize O’s restrictions on T,
168       as excessive limitations can affect the trainer’s flexibility in defining their own models and
169       training processes, hindering the generalizability of the TDP mechanism.
170
      G3. Harmlessness. Implementing TDP should not compromise the model’s performance signifi-
171
          cantly. Specifically, the performance of the trained model M = T-Train(D) should be close
172       to the performance of MT = T(D), which is obtained using only the standard training process.
173
      G4. Efficiency. The verification process should be computationally efficient.
174
175
      Defender’s Capabilities. Based on practical scenarios, we make the following assumptions and
176   restrictions on the defender’s capabilities. In T-Train = T ∪ O, we assume that the additional oper-
177   ations required by the verifier, the O, is model-agnostic. O will not modify the model architecture,
178   nor obtain specific procedures or hyperparameters of T. In Verify, we assume that the defender
179   only has black-box access to the model M to be verified, and can only query the model’s prediction
180   probabilities, i.e., y = M(x), where x is the input sample, y ∈ Rd , and d is the number of classes.
181
      Attacker’s Goals. The attacker is a dishonest model trainer, whose goal is to attempt to subvert the
182   defender’s fidelity goal (G1), formally defined as follows:
183
184
          When the attacker use the modified dataset D∗ ≠ D in trusted training, they would obtain
          T-Train(D∗ ) → M∗ , C ∗ . But they purposely claim to the defender (verifier) that the model
185
          M∗ is trained on D, attempting to make Verify(D, M∗ , C ∗ ) → 1.
186
187   It is important to emphasize that the attacker’s primary objective is to successfully complete the TDP.
188   By obtaining the proof that the model’s training set is a credible dataset D, the attacker can gain
189
      the trust of users. As a result, the model M∗ it publicly releases is more likely to be downloaded,
      deployed, and used. On this basis, the attacker attempts to ‘hide’ the fact that it actually tampered
190
      with the training dataset for training.
191
192   Attacker’s Capabilities. We assume that the attacker can only modify the dataset D but cannot
193   manipulate the verifier’s additional operations O in T-Train. The reasonableness of this assump-
194   tion is consistent with the discussion in problem definition (Sec. 3.1). The defender (verifier) needs
195
      to intervene in the model training with certain operations O to facilitate verification. Once the at-
      tacker can manipulate O, they can easily neutralize various verification mechanisms of the defender.
196
      Therefore, we reasonably limit the capabilities of the attacker, requiring them to at least fully execute
197
      T-Train. In other words, any modifications made by the attacker to M must go through T-Train.

      4     C HALLENGES AND M OTIVATIONS
200
201   4.1   C HALLENGES FOR C URRENT T ECHNIQUES
202
203   Based on the formal definition of TDP in Sec. 3, we utilize representative existing dataset provenance
204   technologies, including Watermarking, MI/DI, and PoTD, to propose some hypothetical solutions
205   for TDP and analyze the challenges involved. Detailed definitions and analyses of each approach
206
      are elaborated in the Appendix A, from which we conclude that the application of existing schemes
      to TDP primarily confronts challenges in two aspects: integrity and usability.
207
208   Challenge 1: Intergrity. All existing schemes fail to ensure the integrity of the training dataset
209   D. Specifically, if an attacker claims to have used D for training but actually employs D∗ , current
210   schemes can only verify whether D∗ approximates the distribution of D (MI/DI, PoTD), or whether
211   D∗ contains several specific samples from D (Watermarking). However, none of these approaches
212
      can confirm that D∗ ≠ D.
213   This disadvantage fundamentally stems from differences in the definition of the threat model , which
214   is also detailed in Fig. 1. In most of the aforementioned works, the party holding the data primarily
215   aims at ownership verification. Therefore, they are typically regarded as trustworthy or defenders,
      with no incentive to actively alter their dataset. However, in TDP, the main goal of the data holder


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      and user is to enhance trust, which might lead them to actively modify the dataset while still claiming
217   to use an authentic dataset to gain undue benefits. This disparity is the root of the vulnerabilities in
218   existing methods, rendering them unable to meet the fundamental goal of fidelity (G1) in TDP.
219
220
      Challenge 2: Usability. Some existing techniques may exhibit deficiencies in usability within prac-
      tical scenarios. For instance, the PoTD scheme does not meet the defender’s goal: low-invasiveness
221
      (G2), as it requires accessing all information from the model trainer during training. DI, to some
222
      extent, fails to satisfy the efficiency goal (G4), since it may necessitate training a dedicated clas-
223   sifier for each verification request. The watermarking approach could potentially compromise the
224   defender’s goal of harmlessness (G3), as watermarks that utilize backdoors might be maliciously
225   exploited, thereby introducing inherent security risks.
226
227   4.2   M OTIVATIONS AND I NSIGHTS
228
229   Tackling usability challenge. Among various approaches, we consider the concept of watermarking
230   to exhibit the highest usability. It imposes minimal constraints during the training phase and offers
231   the highest efficiency during verification, with only requiring black-box access to the model under
232   test. However, its shortcomings originate from the use of backdoors (Adi et al., 2018), typically
233   requiring the model to produce anomalous outputs when presented with specific trigger samples,
234
      which often leads to degradation in model performance or security risks (Mengara et al., 2024)
235   We aim to relax assumptions regarding watermark capabilities to enhance usability while ensuring
236   sufficient capability to determine the occurrence of watermark. We also draw inspiration from MI,
237   noting that the membership of samples could be determined based on discrepancies in model output.
238   Consequently, we introduce a new concept: Data Probe, for conducting watermarking operations.
239   Its formal definition is as follows:
240   Definition 3 (Data Probe). Data probe is defined as a small subset of samples, xp , within the train-
241   ing dataset D. Based on this, the training dataset can be divided into two parts: D = {xp ∪ xnp },
242   where xnp refers to ‘non-probe’ samples. After training the model M on D, we expect a noticeable
243   difference in the output distribution of the model when inputting data from these two subsets, i.e.,
244                                      Pr(y∣xp ; M) ≠ Pr(y∣xnp ; M)
245
246
      Data probe can be considered as a weakened version of a backdoor, it only necessitates a discernible
247   difference in the output compared to that from non-probe inputs xnp , such as a slight increase in
248   prediction confidence, but not a ‘directed’ output. From the perspective of MI, although xp and xnp
249   both belong to the training set, we expect xp to behave more like a ‘special member’ of the dataset.
250
      Tackling intergrity challenge. Simple data probe selection and implanting strategies may still
251
      fall into the dilemmas encountered by watermarking schemes. Hence, our insight is to bind the
252
      integrity of the training dataset with the data probe selection strategy, ensuring that successful
253   probe implantation and detection can only occur when the dataset is used in its entirety for training.

258   Conceptual Overview. Our proposed data-probe-based TDP framework is illustrated in Fig. 3 and
259   formally described in Algorithm 1. Relevant functions are denoted with the subscript DP.
260   In T-TrainDP , a pseudo-random mechanism ProbeSelect is introduced to select the data probe
261   xp . It needs to perform a keyed-hash on training dataset D based on a user-specific key k. Next,
262   operation ODP is exerted on the selected probe to facilitate its ‘implantation’ into the model M
263   during training. Subsequently, the trainer can carry out the normal training process T and submit the
264   key k as a certificate C for verification.
265   In VerifyDP , the verifier first reproduces the pseudo-random process ProbeSelect based on C to
266   select data probe xp and non-probe xnp from D claimed by the trainer. He then calculates the
267   scores sp , snp through a function ProbeScore to measure the difference in the output distribution of
268   these two groups of data in model M. If there is a noticeable difference between sp and snp , it is
269   considered that the data probe xp has been detected, authenticating the genuine use of the training
      dataset D.

361   Probe Detection Metric. According to the definition of Data Probe, probe scores are expected to
362   differ in distribution from non-probe scores. Therefore, we aggregate the aforementioned sample-
363   level scores into distribution-level metrics for probe detection. The following two metrics are em-
364   ployed: ❶ Probe Saliency AUC (PSA). It is a new metric proposed in this work. It utilizes probe
365   scores to plot the ROC curve, further calculating the Area Under ROC curve (AUC) as the metric.
366
      The greater the PSA exceeds 0.5, the more it indicates that probe scores are separable from non-
      probe scores.❷ Statistical test p-value (pV). It has been widely adopted in previous works for mea-
367
      suring distribution differences. A pV less than a certain level of significance, such as 0.1, indicates
368
      a substantial difference in score distributions, signifying that the probe has been detected. We detail
369   the principles of these two metrics and their calculation methods for each probe in Appendix B.

533
      In this study, we highlight the importance of the Trustworthy Dataset Proof (TDP) in enhancing the
534   veracity and integrity of training data for deep learning models. By introducing the novel Data Probe
535   technique, this research successfully addresses the limitations of existing dataset provenance meth-
536   ods, which often falter in usability and integrity. The Data Probe, by leveraging subtle variations
537   in model output distributions to verify the inclusion of specific training subsets, offers a model-
538   agnostic and minimally invasive approach to dataset verification. Our extensive evaluations validate
539   the effectiveness of our Data-Probe-based TDP framework, significantly advancing the pursuit of
      transparency and trustworthiness in training data usage.

      A    C HALLENGES FOR C URRENT T ECHNIQUES
649
650
      In this section, based on the formal definition of TDP in Sec. 3, we combine existing dataset trace-
651   ability or copyright authentication technologies to propose some hypothetical solutions for TDP, and
652   analyze the challenges involved.
653
654
      Case 1: Watermarking
655   If we assume using watermarking technology (Adi et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2023) to achieve TDP,
656   then the verifier can validate the occurrence of training behaviors based on two steps: watermark
657   embedding and watermark detection. We use the subscript WM to represent the relevant functions of
658   watermarking-based TDP.
659   In T-TrainWM , the trainer is required to embed watermarks into the model through training, and this
660   operation can be denoted as OWM . OWM allows the trainer to select some data samples from D as
661   triggers xt according to certain rules, which can make the trained model produce a specific pattern
662   of output P. We represent this characteristic as: P = M(xt ). Then the trainer sets C ← xt and
663   submits it along with the trained model M to the verifier.
664   In the verification, the verifier could make the judgement:
665
666
                                 Verify(D,M,C)WM ← 1 (P = M (C) ∧ C ∈ D) .
667   However, VerifyWM cannot meet the defender’s primary goal: fidelity (G1), and we provide the
668   simplest counterexample. The attacker can slightly manipulate the remaining data D− = D ∖ xt
669   except for the triggers. It is noted that the current dataset D∗ = {D− ∪ xt } ≠ D. Then the attacker
670   execute T-Train(D∗ )WM → M∗ , C ∗ . Here the C ∗ = C = xt because the xt remains unchanged.
671   Since the watermark embedding mainly relies on xt , which means that the attacker can achieve
672
      P = M∗ (xt ). Therefore, it is obvious that attackers can easily claim that the M∗ are trained on D
      and verified by Verify(D, M∗ , C ∗ )WM = 1.
673
674   Case 2: Membership or Dataset Inference
675
      Membership Inference (MI) (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2019) and Dataset Inference
676   (DI) (Maini et al., 2021; Dziedzic et al., 2022) share similar approaches, both do not intervene dur-
677   ing model training but directly analyze model outputs during the testing phase. We use the subscript
678   MI and DI to represent the relevant functions of MI-based and DI-based TDP.
679
      In training stage, T-TrainMI = T-TrainDI = T because OMI = ODI = ∅. During the verification, for
680
      the VerifyMI , the verifier may traverse all the data in D to determine if they belong to the training
681
      set. For the VerifyDI , the verifier could directly use the DI techniques to infer if D is the training
682   set.
683
684   Nevertheless, both of them cannot satisfy the defender’s primary goal: fidelity (G1) in principle.
685
      VerifyMI evidently struggles to capture samples outside the claimed training set D because of the
      lack of information. On the other hand, DI can only ascertain the approximate data distribution of
686
      the training set. When the distributions of the training sets are similar, despite being unequal or
687
      even mutually exclusive, DI is highly likely to erroneously judge them as equivalent. In addition,

      B     P ROBE D ETECTION M ETRIC
709
      B.1    P ROBE S ALIENCY AUC (PSA)
710
711   This metric utilizes probe scores to plot the ROC curve, further calculating the Area Under ROC
712   curve (AUC) as the metric. We analyze this metric in detail from its priciple.
713
      Assume here that we expect the probe scores sp to be greater than non-probe scores snp in distri-
714
      bution, as adopted in PP, for example. We do not focus on the actual score values but rather their
715
      relative magnitudes. Therefore, we could sort all scores from largest to smallest, sequentially se-
716   lecting values as hypothetical classification thresholds. Samples with probe scores greater than the
717   threshold are classified as probes, those lower as non-probes. Based on whether these samples are
718   actually probes, we record the current predicted False Positive Rate (FPR) and True Positive Rate
719   (TPR), hence obtaining an ROC curve.
720
      Ideally, when all probe scores are greater than non-probe scores, the ROC curve will approach the
721
      top-left corner, with the corresponding Area Under the ROC Curve equaling 1. Conversely, when
722   probe scores are nearly indistinguishable from non-probe scores, the process resembles a random
723   guess, resulting in an ROC curve that goes from the bottom-left to the top-right corner, with an AUC
724   close to 0.5. Hence, a larger AUC indicates that probe scores are more "separable" from non-
725   probe scores. We denote this as the Probe Saliency AUC (PSA) as an indicator of probe detection.
726
      For PP and TP, we use the above approach to calculate PSA, which means predicting the saliency of
727
      data probe as 1 and non-probe as 0. However, for AP and UP, it is the opposite because we expect the
728   scores of non-probe to be higher. Therefore, we predict probe as 0 and non-probe as 1 to calculate
729   PSA.
730
731   B.2    S TATISTICAL TEST AND P - VALUE ( P V)
732
733   Following several previous works (Maini et al., 2021), we perform a statistical t-test to measure
734   whether there is a significant difference in the distributions of probe scores sp and non-probe scores
735   snp .
736   For PP and TP, the null hypothesis (H0 ) is that the probe scores are less prominent compared to
737   non-probe scores, which is opposite to our expectation. Assuming that µsp and µsnp are the mean
738   values of the sp and snp , respectively. The H0 and H1 (alternate hypothesis) could be represented
739   as:
740                                    H0 ∶ µsp ≤ µsnp ; H1 ∶ µsp > µsnp                             (1)
741
742   For AP and UP, the null hypothesis (H0 ) is that the probe scores are more prominent compared to
743
      non-probe scores. We adopt the following hypothesis:
744                                    H0 ∶ µsp ≥ µsnp ;      H1 ∶ µsp < µsnp                              (2)
745
746   The statistical t-test results in a p-value (pV), used as a metric to determine the success of the probe
747   detection. Specifically, if the p-value is less than a certain level of significance, for example 0.1, we
748   reject the null hypothesis H0 , indicating that the probe was detected. Otherwise, we accept H0 and
749   consider that the probe was not detected.

770   Hyperparameters: During training, the models are initialized randomly. The images in the dataset
771   are uniformly resized to 224x224 and the pixel values are normalized to a range of -1 to 1 to comply
772   with the model’s input interface. To mitigate overfitting and ensure effective training of the model,
773   random cropping and random horizontal flipping are employed during the training process. The
774   Adam optimizer is adopted with a learning rate of 1e-3, and cross-entropy is employed as the loss
775   function. Depending on the convergence of the model on various datasets, training is conducted for

777   Implementation details of TDP: We sequentially present the implementation details of each critical
778   operation within the TDP framework.
779
      In the probe selection process, performing keyed-hash computations on datasets is computational-
780   efficient. For example, running a keyed hash based on Md5 (Rivest, 1992) on the CI-
781   FAR10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) dataset on the computing platform with Intel Core i7-12700®
782   takes an average time of only 1.73 seconds. To facilitate large-scale experiments without compro-
783   mising the integrity of the framework’s principles, we judiciously select fixed random seeds as a
784   substitute for dataset hashing operations. We use the same random seed to select data probes, re-
785   flecting the scenario where the model trainer genuinely uses the dataset. Using different random
786   seeds represents scenarios where a dishonest trainer initiates verification.
787   During probe implantation, the WeightedRandomSampler from the PyTorch is adopted for both PP
788   and AP. Specifically, we assign a weight of 10 to the probes in PP, meaning they are ten times more
789   likely to be selected during training compared to non-probes. For AP, the weight of the probes is set
790   to 0.
791
      When calculating probe scores, we employ cross-entropy for the loss-based score calculation, as
792   cross-entropy is the most commonly used loss function in deep learning training tasks. Two metrics
793   introduced in the Sec. 5: PSA and p-value, are adopted to assess whether probes can be effectively
794   detected.

      Depending on the different types of probes, We have designed the following four targeted probe
799
      forging attacks in the evaluation, denoted with the prefix F for "Forged":

801    • FPP: The attacker fine-tunes M∗ using xp , attempting to enhance the prominence of xp .

803      training method, attempting to diminish the prominence of xp .
804    • FUP: The attacker uses xp and disrupts its label to fine-tune M∗ .
805
       • FTP: The attacker uses xp and uniformly assigns them a random targeted label to fine-tune M∗ .

808
      performance of the model, for example, by using a very small learning rate of 2e-5 and training for
      10 epochs.
