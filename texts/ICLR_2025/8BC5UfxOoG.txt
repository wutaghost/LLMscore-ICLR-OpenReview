011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In-context learning (ICL) has proven to be adept at adapting large language models
014            (LLMs) to downstream tasks without parameter updates, based on a few demon-
015            stration examples. Prior work has found that the ICL performance is susceptible
016
               to the selection of examples in prompt and made efforts to stabilize it. However,
               existing example selection studies ignore the ethical risks behind the examples
017
               selected, such as gender and race bias. In this work, we first construct a new sen-
018
               timent classification dataset —EEC-paraphrase, designed to better capture and
019            evaluate the biases of LLMs. Then, through further analysis, we discover that ❶
020            example selection with high accuracy does not mean low bias; ❷ example se-
021            lection for ICL amplifies the biases of LLMs; ❸ example selection contributes
022            to spurious correlations of LLMs. Based on the above observations, we propose
023            the Remind with Bias-aware Embedding (ReBE), which removes the spurious
024            correlations through contrastive learning and obtains bias-aware embedding for
025            LLMs based on prompt tuning. Finally, we demonstrate that ReBE effectively mit-
026            igates biases of LLMs without significantly compromising accuracy and is highly
027
               compatible with existing example selection methods. The implementation code is
               available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ReBE-1D04.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Although large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities, efficiently de-
      ploying them into downstream tasks remains challenging (Mosbach et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a).
034
      Among existing solutions, in-context learning (ICL) has proven adept at adapting LLMs to down-
035
      stream tasks without parameter updates, using only a few demonstration examples (Brown et al.,
036   2020). Compared to fine-tuning (Ziegler et al., 2019), ICL is more flexible and suitable for few-shot
037   scenarios. In the setting of ICL, examples included in the prompt are the only source for LLMs
038   to learn the task context information (e.g., the answer format), thus attracting considerable
039   attention. As the research deepened, researchers found that examples selected randomly from the
040   training set led to high variance in performance (Liu et al., 2022b), so numerous example selection
041   methods have been proposed to stabilize the performance of ICL (Gonen et al., 2023; Gupta et al.,
042   2023).
043   Since LLMs may spread biases learned from the training set during decision-making or user interac-
044   tion, potentially causing severe harm to society, the biases of LLMs have always attracted significant
045   attention (Liu et al., 2024b; Gupta et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2022). Although not entirely equivalent
046   to social biases, it has been shown that LLMs exhibit stronger cognitive biases (Lin & Ng, 2023),
047   such as position bias (Zhao et al., 2021) and token bias (Zheng et al., 2024), when fed with spe-
048   cific prompts. Similarly, because the example selection method determines the content of the ICL
049   prompt, it is natural to ask: Does example selection for ICL amplify the biases of LLMs? It is
050
      undoubtedly unacceptable for LLMs to preserve or even exacerbate biases when using ICL to deploy
      LLMs to downstream tasks. However, existing example selection studies ignore the ethical risks
051
      behind the examples selected, such as gender and race bias.
052
053   To explore the impact of example selection on bias, we conduct an empirical analysis by evaluating
      the accuracy and biases of LLMs on a sentiment classification dataset —EEC-paraphrase, which

095

096   2024). Typical spurious correlations of LLMs include stereotypes such as “He is a doctor; she is a
097   nurse.” Furthermore, it is generally believed that the LLM’s biases come from its parameter knowl-
098   edge and the input prompt. By excluding the impact of LLM parameters, we find that example
099   selection contributes to spurious correlations of LLMs.
100
      The above observations highlight that example selection for ICL truly amplifies the biases of LLMs.
101
      In order to mitigate the social biases of adapting LLMs to downstream tasks through ICL, we pro-
102   pose the Remind with Bias-aware Embedding (ReBE), which curbs biases of LLMs by prefixing
103   the bias-aware embedding into the prompt. Besides, we design the bias-contrastive loss based on
104   contrastive learning to remove spurious correlations and obtain the bias-aware embedding through
105   prompt tuning (More details in Section 4). To demonstrate the effectiveness of ReBE, we conduct
106   extensive experiments and the results in Section 5 show that ReBE reduces the maximum bias value
107   without compromising the accuracy and is well compatible with existing example selection methods.
      In sum, we try to fill the gap in exploring the ethical risks of example selection, which is essential

151

152
      (Khosla et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). Given a training set D = {(xi , yi )}N
                                                                                     i=1 and its indexes set
153
      I = {1, 2, ..., N }, define the i-th sample xi as an anchor, the contrastive loss for supervised tasks
154   (Khosla et al., 2020) can be defined as:

167
      Dataset To better capture and evaluate the gender and race bias of LLMs, we construct a
      new sentiment classification dataset —EEC-paraphrase. Given a sentence in the template
168
      <Person> feels <emotional word>., LLMs are asked to identify the sentiment con-
169
      tained in the sentence. By replacing <Person> with first names (e.g., Alonzo and Alan) or pro-
170   nouns (e.g., she and he) associated with specific demographic group, EEC-paraphrase includes
171   8,640 English sentences with gender and race attributes.
172
173
      EEC-paraphrase is built through paraphrasing sentences in the Equity Evaluation Corpus (EEC)
      (Kiritchenko & Mohammad, 2018) by GPT-3.5-Turbo. Compared with EEC, sentences in EEC-
174
      paraphrase are more complex and natural, closer to the actual scenario (The quality validation
175
      is available in Appendix A.). Besides, to simulate the few-shot scenario, we build a train400-
176   dev200 dataset by randomly sampling 400 sentences for the training set and 200 sentences for the
177   development set from the EEC-paraphrase.
178
179
      Language Models To guarantee the reliability of our findings, we conduct experiments
      on eight LLMs, including LlaMA-2-7/13/70B, OPT-6.7/13/30B, GPT-J-6B and
180
      GPT-neo-2.7B. LLMs with various parameter sizes but within the same series facilitate our anal-
181
      ysis of the effects of parameter quantities.

      Since the output of LLMs is not numerical value but sentences containing the judgment result, we

211

      3.4                                                   S PURIOUS C ORRELATIONS O BSERVED WITH M AX TG AND M AX FG
271
272   As seen from Figure 3, we visualize the confusion matrices of OPT-6.7B, which has the biggest
273   fluctuation of MaxTG (0.47) and MaxFG (0.46) in Table 2. With the help of Figure 3, we can further
274   analyze the reasons that cause MaxTG and MaxFG to increase. For MaxTG, by comparing the first
275   two sub-figures of Figure 3 by row, the proportion of sadness sentences correctly predicted in the
276   female group (0.88) is higher than in the male group (0.42), which is consistent with the finding
277   of Plaza-del Arco et al. (2024). Likewise, for MaxFG, by comparing the first two sub-figures of
278
      Figure 3 by column, more sentences with sadness labels are incorrectly predicted as fear in the
      male group (0.54) than in the female (0.08). We believe the disparity —where sentences labelled
279
      as sadness containing male pronouns are more easily misjudged as fear than those with female
280
      pronouns —occurs because the sentiment analysis criteria of LLMs may be influenced by words
281   other than emotional ones, leading to spurious correlations. Although it has been proven that
282   spurious correlations exist in LLMs, we are unsure whether the example selection methods for ICL
283   contribute to these spurious correlations.

317   To retain the accuracy and flexibility of ICL while reducing bias, we propose the ReBE, which
318   removes spurious correlations based on contrastive learning and reminds LLMs of fairness with
319   bias-aware embedding.

321   4.1                                                   T HE OVERVIEW OF R E BE
322
323   As shown in Figure 5, using (x, y, s) as input, ReBE obtains bias-aware embedding by minimizing
      the bias-contrastive loss during training. Here, x, y, and s correspond to the task’s sample, label,


                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         6

      and demographic attribute. With the help of prompt tuning, ReBE avoids updating the original
325   parameters of LLM M, retaining the flexibility of ICL. Besides, to effectively remove spurious
326   correlations, contrastive learning is introduced to construct the bias-contrastive loss. The verbalizer
327   (Cui et al., 2022) converts representations {z1 , z2 , ..., zk } to predicted labels {joy, anger, ...} used
328   in the downstream task.

347   4.2   B IAS - AWARE E MBEDDING
348
349   Prompt Tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2022) is a soft (continuous) prompt construction and
350   parameter-efficient tuning method for LLMs, which generally searches for the best ICL prompt in
351   the semantic space via back-propagation. By adding virtual (pseudo) tokens to the prompt of LLMs,
352   prompt tuning obtains trainable parameters after the embedding processing. We name trainable
353
      parameters in the prompt tuning for LLMs debiasing as Bias-aware Embedding. It should be noted
      that virtual tokens have no real meaning and only serve as placeholders. Besides, the contexts of
354
      prompt during prompt tuning are constructed based on the example selection method.
355
356   To better explain the generation of bias-aware embedding, we take the sentiment classification task
357   in Figure 5 as an example. Represent the sentence as x = [v1][v2][He][f eels][happy][.], where [vi ]
358   is the virtual token. After tokenization and embedding processing, bias-aware embedding becomes
359
      part of embedding vectors, which are fed into the remaining neural network layers. Representing
      the number of virtual tokens [vi ] as nvr , and the dimension of LLM feature vectors as nf eats , the
360
      number of trainable parameters (bias-aware embedding) can be calculated as nvr × nf eats . All
361
      original parameters of LLM are frozen and are not involved in the training process described above.
362   Since prompt tuning has been found to be unstable during training (Chen et al., 2023a), we add
363   Gaussian noise to help the training, which is a common solution (Wu et al., 2022; Pecher et al.,
364   2024).
365
      Through back-propagation and gradient descent, the trainable parameters are updated to minimize
366
      the loss and obtain bias-aware embedding, which is then saved in the embedding table of LLM. Ac-
367
      cording to the corresponding virtual tokens, bias-aware embedding is integrated into the embedding
368   vectors during inference.

513   6   R ELATED W ORK
514
515   After realizing that ICL performance is susceptible to example selection, many efforts have been
516   made to stabilize it. Liu et al. (2022b) proposed the KATE, which retrieves examples semantically
517
      similar to the test query samples. After that, many heuristic-based methods have emerged, such as
      perplexity-based (Gonen et al., 2023; Iter et al., 2023), informativeness-based (Gupta et al., 2023; Li
518
      & Qiu, 2023) and sensitivity-based (Chen et al., 2023b). Besides that, some studies understand ex-
519
      ample selection from different perspectives, such as formulating it as a sequential decision problem
520   (Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a), curating a stable subset from the original training set (Chang
521   & Jia, 2023), selecting based on the Determinantal Point Process (DPP) (Yang et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
522   2023) and Latent Variable Models (Wang et al., 2023). Although these methods stabilize the ac-
523   curacy of ICL on downstream tasks to a certain extent, they ignore the potential bias risks. On
524   the other hand, while extensive research has been conducted on the biases of LLMs (Gallegos et al.,
525   2024), few studies focus on the bias risks of adapting LLMs to downstream tasks, especially for ICL.
526   Although Ma et al. (2023) analyzed the predictive bias of ICL, their method relies on explicit bias
527   attributes, making it inapplicable to the EEC-paraphrase dataset used in this paper. Additionally,
528
      predictive bias differs slightly from the social bias we focus on.
529
530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this study, we have investigated the impact of example selection on the biases of LLMs. By
533
      comparing the biases under four example selection baselines with biases under zero-shot, we have
534   found that example selection for ICL amplifies the biases of LLMs. To mitigate the bias of example
535   selection, we have proposed the Remind with Bias-aware Embedding (ReBE), which removes the
536   spurious correlations by contrastive learning and retains the feasibility of ICL by prompt tuning.
537   After extensive experiments, we have demonstrated that ReBE can mitigate the bias without signif-
538   icantly compromising accuracy and is compatible with existing example selection methods. With
539   the spread application of LLMs, more attention must be paid to the ethical risks of adapting LLMs
      to downstream tasks.

744
745   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the bias metrics in Table 1.
746
      Average Group Fairness (AvgGF) is a macro metric, which measures the disparity in the overall
747   prediction accuracy between different groups. Formally, it is the absolute value of the difference
748
      between the accuracy P (Ŷ = Y |S = s1 ) of group s1 and the accuracy P (Ŷ = Y |S = s2 ) of group s2 .
749
750   Maximum TPR Gap (MaxTG) is a metric derived from the True Positive Rate (TPR), which refers
751   to the proportion of actual positive samples predicted to be positive. Here, we select one sentiment
752
      category as positive and the other three as negative. MaxTG measures the maximum recall (TPR)
      difference between various groups among all sentiment categories.
753
754   Maximum FPR Gap (MaxFG) is a metric derived from the False Positive Rate (FPR), which refers
755   to the proportion of actual negative samples predicted to be positive. Here, we select one sentiment
      category as negative and the other three as positive. MaxFG measures the maximum FPR difference


                                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      between various groups among all sentiment categories. The larger the MaxFG, the samples from
757   one group are more likely to be misclassified as a specific category than samples from another
758   group. For example, the sentence sadness corresponding to Male in Figure 3 is more likely to be
759   misclassified as fear than that corresponding to Female.

1278   G    BASELINE C OMPARISON OF R E BE
1279
1280   According to our survey results, there are not many relative debiasing methods of ICL. Although
1281   Hu et al. (2024) proposed fairness via clustering genetic (FCG) algorithm, it cannot apply to more
1282   tasks due to the need for explicit feature vectors. Therefore, we cannot set FCG as the baseline of
1283   ReBE. Since there are no other debiasing methods specifically for ICL, we compare ReBE with two
1284
       context augmentation methods: counterfactual context and gender-balanced context.
1285
1286   Counterfactual For datasets built based on templates, such as EEC and EEC-paraphrase,
1287   we can construct the corresponding counterfactual context instance based on the template. For
1288
       example, according to the template <person subject> feels <emotion word>,
       the counterfactual context instance of sentence Alonzo feels angry. can be
1289
       Nichelle feels angry. or Amanda feels angry.
1290
1291
1292
       Gender-balanced The gender-balanced context approach requires an equal or close number of
       examples for each gender type.
1293
1294   Table 21 shows the gender bias of OPT-6.7B on sentiment analysis with EEC-paraphrase as the
1295   dataset. While the EEC-paraphrase dataset does not have its own templates, it is built upon the EEC
       samples, allowing us to generate counterfactual samples using the templates from the EEC.

1317
1318   To avoid spurious correlations caused by example selection, we test the feasibility of calibrating the
1319
       prompts of ICL. Based on past experiences, we provide detailed instructions or construct the coun-
       terfactual example pairs in the prompt. Considering that differences in the proportion of sentences
1320
       corresponding to demographic groups or labels might mislead the LLMs, we also try the approach
1321
       that balances the proportions of various features in the prompt. However, as shown in Figure 21,
1322   there are no significant differences in feature ratios between prompts with high and low bias, so the
1323   method based on balanced features may not be effective enough. In sum, the feasibility of removing
1324   spurious correlations by few-shot prompts alone is questionable.
