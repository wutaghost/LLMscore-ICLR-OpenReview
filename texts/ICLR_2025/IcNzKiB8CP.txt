009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Optimization techniques have become increasingly critical due to the ever-
012            growing model complexity and data scale. In particular, teleportation has emerged
013            as a promising approach, which accelerates convergence of gradient descent-based
014            methods by navigating within the loss invariant level set to identify parameters
015            with advantageous geometric properties. Existing teleportation algorithms have
016            primarily demonstrated their effectiveness in optimizing Multi-Layer Perceptrons
017
               (MLPs), but their extension to more advanced architectures, such as Convolutional
               Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers, remains challenging. Moreover, they
018
               often impose significant computational demands, limiting their applicability to
019
               complex architectures. To this end, we introduce an algorithm that projects the
020            gradient of the teleportation objective function onto the input null space, effec-
021            tively preserving the teleportation within the loss invariant level set and reducing
022            computational cost. Our approach is readily generalizable from MLPs to CNNs,
023            transformers, and potentially other advanced architectures. We validate the ef-
024            fectiveness of our algorithm across various benchmark datasets and optimizers,
025            demonstrating its broad applicability.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Consider an optimization problem where the objective function, denoted by L (ω), is parameterized
031
      by ω ∈ Ω. When L (ω) is non-convex, gradient-based methods are commonly used to find a set
      of parameters corresponding to local minimums in the loss landscape. The standard update rule for
032
      gradient descent is given by:
033
034                                          ωt+1 ← ωt − η∇L (ωt ) ,                                       (1)
035
      where ωt represents the parameter values at ieration t and η > 0 is the learning rate. As a first-
036   order method, gradient descent is computationally efficient but often suffers from slow convergence.
037   In contrast, second-order methods, such as Newton’s method, incorporate higher-order geometric
038   information, resulting in faster convergence. However, this comes with significant computational
039   cost, particularly due to the need to compute and invert the Hessian matrix (Hazan, 2019). To this
040   end, teleportation is motivated by the need to leverage higher-order geometry while relying only on
041   gradient information.
042   Teleportation is based on the premise that multiple points in the parameter space can yield the same
043   loss, which forms the loss invariant level set of parameters (Du et al., 2018; Kunin et al., 2020).
044   This assumption is particularly feasible in modern deep learning, where most advanced models
045   are highly over-parameterized (Sagun et al., 2017; Tarmoun et al., 2021; Simsek et al., 2021). By
046   identifying the level set, parameters can be teleported within it to enhance the gradient norm,
047   thereby accelerating the optimization process (Kunin et al., 2020; Grigsby et al., 2022).
048   Related Work. Zhao et al. (2022) indicates that the behavior of teleportation, despite utilizing only
049   gradient information, closely resembles that of Newton’s method. An alternative perspective on tele-
050   portation is that it mitigates the locality constraints of the gradient descent algorithm, resembling the
051   dynamics of warm restart algorithms (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016; Dodge et al., 2020; Bouthillier
052   et al., 2021; Ramasinghe et al., 2022). Under this context, each step of gradient descent is equiv-
053   alent to a proximal mapping (Combettes & Pesquet, 2011). Teleportation periodically relaxes the
      proximal restriction, allowing the algorithm to restart at a distant location with desirable geometric


                                                         1

054
      properties. Compared to warm restart algorithms, teleportation incurs minimal to no increase in
055   loss while providing greater control over the movement of parameters. Notably, the field of telepor-
056   tation reveals a gap between theoretical developments and practical applications. Zhao et al. (2022)
057   shows that gradient descent (GD) with teleportation can achieve mixed linear and quadratic conver-
058   gence rates on strongly convex functions. Mishkin et al. (2024) proves that, for convex functions
059   with Hessian stability, GD with teleportation attains a convergence rate faster than O(1/K). How-
060   ever, both approaches encounter limitations when applied to empirical studies involving highly
061   non-convex functions, which are a common characteristic of modern architectures. Specifically,
062   Zhao et al. (2022) develops a symmetry teleportation algorithm only for Multi-Layer Perceptrons
063
      (MLPs) using group actions (Armenta & Jodoin, 2021; Ganev et al., 2021; Armenta et al., 2023) .
      However, challenges persist in terms of its generalizability to other contemporary architectures and
064
      its relatively low efficiency. Mishkin et al. (2024), on the other hand, tackled a sequential quadratic
065
      programming by using linear approximations of the level set, which can lead to error accumulation
066   when the architecture becomes more complicated and the number of teleportation steps increase (see
067   Figure 1 for a visual comparision). Moreover, both studies have primarily concentrated on empirical
068   results involving MLPs and the vanilla Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer.

081   Contributions. Our work seeks to overcome these challenges by designing an algorithm not only
082   generalizes to other modern architectures, but also is efficient and accurate. To be more specific,
083   we eliminate the need for the bottleneck group action transformations of Zhao et al. (2022) by
084   utilizing a more efficient gradient projection technique. Moreover, instead of taking on the errors
085   introduced by linear approximations of the level set, we project the gradient of the teleportation
086   objective onto the input null space, ensuring an accurate search on the level set thus minimal to no
087   change in loss value. Specifically, our contributions are:
088
             • We propose a novel algorithm that utilizes gradient projection to offer improved computa-
089
               tional efficiency and parallelization capabilities.
090
091          • The proposed algorithm is a general framework that can be easily applied to various mod-
092            ern architectures, including MLPs, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), transformers,
093
               and potentially linear time series models such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) and TTT (Sun
               et al., 2024). As a result, our work is the first work to extend teleportation to CNNs and
094
               transformers.
095
096          • We present extensive empirical results to demonstrate its effectiveness, spanning a range
097            of benchmark datasets, including MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny-
098            ImageNet, multi-variate time series datasets (electricity and traffic), and Penn Treebank
099
               language dataset. We also evaluate the algorithm with multiple modern optimizers, such as
               SGD (Robbins & Monro, 1951), Momentum (Polyak, 1964), Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011),
100
               and Adam (Kingma, 2014), whereas previous studies primarily focused on the vanilla SGD.

      3     T ELEPORT W ITH N ULL S PACE G RADIENT P ROJECTION
151
      Our objective is to develop a generalizable and efficient algorithm that avoids reliance on specific
152
      group action designs. Moreover, it should avoid any (linear) approximation of the level set with
153   uncontrollable errors, as these could otherwise result in suboptimal performance. Considering the
154   common architectural design in modern neural networks, which typically employ a linear relation-
155   ship between weights and inputs of each layer, the technique of gradient projection on to the input
156   null space of each layer is well-suited for this purpose. We next elaborate on it.
157
      Gradient Projection. To incorporate the geometric landscape and accelerate optimization using
158
      only gradient information, the objective function for teleportation is defined as the squared gradient
159   norm of the loss function of the primary task with respect to the model parameter W ,
160
                                                      1
161                                     Lteleport =     ∥∇W Lprimary ∥2 .                                (4)
                                                      2

      During each teleportation step, in contrast to symmetry teleportation, the gradient ascent is applied
163   directly on the model parameter Wl of each layer l instead of relying on an intermediate group action
164   g, i.e., we have
165
166                                   Wl,t+1 = Wl,t + ηπl (∇Wl Lteleport ),                               (5)
167   where η is the learning rate for teleportation update, and πl is the layerwise projection operator
168   onto the null space of each layer’s input. We have distinct projection operators for different model
169   architectures. We will derive πl for MLPs, CNNs and transformes in the sequel. The validity of
170   this projection is based on the assumption that the gradient resides within the span of each layer’s
171   input for certain structures, which will also be elaborated in a subsequent section.
172   Section Organization. We first define and provide notations for MLPs, CNNs, and transformers.
173   Next, we demonstrate that the gradient in Equation 5 indeed resides within the input space of these
174   architectures, thus satisfying the required assumption of gradient projection. Finally, we present
175   our proposed approach and provide a detailed explanation of how to derive the projection operators
176   for each of these architectures.

262

263

269   To preserve model parameters on the loss-invariant level set during teleportation steps, we project

283
284   In this section, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across MLPs, CNNs, and transform-
285   ers, utilizing a wide range of benchmark datasets. Additionally, we evaluate our approach using a
286   variety of optimizers, such as the vanilla SGD, first-moment optimizer like SGD with momentum,
287   second-moment optimizers like Adagrad and Adam.
288   We showcase the efficiency of our algorithm compared to the state-of-the-art method, symmetry
289   teleportation, across multiple teleportation hyperparameters. Furthermore, if any approximation of
290   the level set is needed, we demonstrate the capability of our approach to control the error in null
291   space approximation, which subsequently improves the accuracy of level set approximation during
292   the teleportation.

468
       In addition to its efficiency, our algorithm provides a distinct advantage in controlling the error
469    associated with increased loss during teleportation. Figure 6a records the information of the input
470    space of the second layer in MLPs, CNNs, and Transformers (with the same architechtures used
471    in experiments) across all datasets. Most variance of input is captured by the space of significant
472    representation of a relatively small proportion of total dimensions, represented by the percentages
473    of sorted eigenvectors in SVD. Consequently, even without approximating the input null space,
474    sufficient dimensions are typically available in the null space to facilitate gradient projection and
475    search. This validates our choice of setting τ to be 1 in most cases. Figure 6b further confirms that
476    when the threshold τ is set to 1, meaning the exact null space is utilized, the gradient norm increases
477
       steadily during teleportation while the loss remains constant. Moreover, as τ decreases, the gradient
       is projected onto an approximated null space with a significantly larger number of dimensions, yet
478
       capturing only slightly more variance with minimal impact on the loss. A remarkable increase in
479
       the gradient norm ascending speed is observed when τ is set to 0.99, with the loss still remaining
480    constant. (Experiments in Figure 6b are conducted using transformer on sMNIST dataset.)
