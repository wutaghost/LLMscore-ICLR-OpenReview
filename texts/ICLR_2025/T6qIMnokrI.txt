009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            As diffusion models (DMs) become increasingly susceptible to adversarial attacks,
012            this paper investigates a novel method of data exfiltration through strategically
013            implanted backdoors. Unlike conventional techniques that directly alter data, we
014            pioneer the use of unique trigger embeddings for each image to enable covert data
015            retrieval. Furthermore, we extend our exploration to text-to-image diffusion models
016            such as Stable Diffusion by introducing the Caption Backdoor Subnet (CBS),
017            which exploits these models for both image and caption extraction. This innovative
018
               approach not only reveals an unexplored facet of diffusion model security but also
               contributes valuable insights toward enhancing the resilience of generative models
019
               against sophisticated threats.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      In the rapidly evolving field of artificial intelligence, generative models, particularly diffusion models,
025   have ushered in a transformative era in content generation. These models excel in tasks ranging
026   from unconditional image synthesis to advanced text-to-image generation, pushing the boundaries
027   of AI capabilities and advancing artificial creativity towards human-like ingenuity [13; 26; 28; 39;
028   40; 49; 53]. However, while diffusion models significantly accelerate technological progress, they
029   also introduce critical security risks, such as increased susceptibility to backdoor attacks that can
030   manipulate outputs to spread harmful content or biases [5; 6; 7; 14; 23; 41; 52]. For instance,
031   studies have shown how diffusion models can be manipulated to align with adversarial triggers [5; 6].
032   Research on generative models like Stable Diffusion also reveals potential for images to carry harmful
033
      narratives [41; 52]. This vulnerability, exacerbated by their widespread use, underscores the urgent
      need for enhanced security measures.
034
035   This paper introduces a novel adversarial technique for diffusion models as shown in Figure 1: data
036   exfiltration via backdoor implementation, enabling covert extraction of private training data (instead
037   of one image) without leaving a trace in file access histories. This poses a severe threat to data
038   confidentiality and underscores the need for security measures. One possible solution is to leverage
039
      recent research on backdoor attacks in diffusion models [5; 6; 7], which have proved that backdoors
      can be easily injected to control image generation when a trigger is provided. However, these methods
040
      are limited since they can only be applied to a small number of trigger-target pairs, allowing for the
041
      exfiltration of only a portion of the dataset rather than the entire dataset. Another possible solution is to
042   adopt previous research that can facilitate high-fidelity extraction of sensitive data from classification
043   models [1; 8; 50]. Nevertheless, data exfiltration in classification models often exploits overfitting or
044   memorization, where sensitive training data can be reconstructed by analyzing the model’s outputs or
045   gradients in response to carefully crafted inputs [1; 8; 50]. In contrast, diffusion models are generative
046   models that learn the underlying data distribution by iteratively denoising samples from a noise
047   distribution. They do not rely on a direct input-to-output mapping for classification but instead focus
048   on generating new data that resembles the training data, which presents conflicting optimization
049   objectives when attempting to apply traditional data exfiltration techniques.
050   In fact, data exfiltration via backdoor attack is challenging since it requires the model to memorize
051   the whole dataset without affecting the generated image diversity when working in a normal mode.
052   Memorizing and diversifying may contradict with each other [3] without a careful design. To this
053   end, for the unconditional diffusion models, our methodology diverges from traditional backdoor
      injection methods that directly manipulate training data, which changes the data distribution for

104   2.1       BACKDOOR ATTACK IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
105
106   The literature has extensively investigated backdoor attacks in GANs and VAEs, with seminal studies
107   like BAAAN [30] outlining attack methodologies on these models. Applying these strategies to
      diffusion models presents unique challenges due to their denoising score-matching training and the


                                                                                     2
108
      sensitivity of input noise manipulation. Recent studies [5; 6; 7; 14; 23; 41; 44; 52] have begun to
109   explore these attacks in diffusion models, demonstrating that specific triggers can be embedded
110   within Gaussian noise inputs or prompts to control model outputs. For example, research by [5; 6]
111   has shown how a carefully crafted trigger embedded in Gaussian noise can direct diffusion models
112   to generate one image specified by the attacker. Furthermore, [41] has advanced this technique by
113   embedding character triggers in prompts, influencing the generation of specific styles or targets. In a
114   more recent development, [44] introduces the Copyright Infringement Attack, where poisoning data
115   is created by seamlessly inpainting around copyrighted elements and generating corresponding text
116   captions, forcing the model to produce copyrighted content. Our research facilitates data exfiltration
117
      by enabling diffusion models to generate multiple specified targets, thus extending beyond their
      designed single-target capabilities and addressing trigger-target misalignment.
118
119
120   2.2   DATA E XFILTRATION IN N EURAL N ETWORKS
121
      Data exfiltration challenges in data security have evolved from focusing on classification models to
122
      exploring diffusion models. Early approaches embed sensitive data within model parameters [38],
123   even going through a model compression process [48]. Deep learning-based methods for data
124   exfiltration have predominantly focused on classification tasks [1; 8; 50]. For example, [1] achieves
125   the data exfiltration task by inverting the architecture, while in [8], a data trap is created by imposing
126   constraints on gradient updates to reconstruct the data after fine-tuning. However, these methods
127   are specifically designed for the properties of classification tasks (e.g., loss functions or network
128   architectures), making them challenging to adapt for data exfiltration in diffusion models. Recent
129   studies highlight the memorization risks in probabilistic deep generative models [3; 4; 36; 43], with
130   particular attention to their ability to recall and produce training data during inference. For instance,
131
      [4] highlights that diffusion models can reproduce specific images from their training set during
      inference and utilize membership inference attacks, such as the Loss Threshold Attack (LTA) [51],
132
      to extract data. However, their approach is impractical for data exfiltration due to its substantial
133
      computational demands; it requires generating 175 million images, of which only 94 are identified
134   as part of the training data. Additionally, research highlighted in [35; 37] demonstrates that models
135   trained with data duplication (Dup) tend to exhibit increased memorization, an effect that intensifies
136   as the duplication factor rises. To the best of our knowledge, our approach is the first work studying
137   the data exfiltration with the backdoor attack on by leveraging the memorability of diffusion models.

      3     T HREAT M ODEL AND ATTACK S CENARIO
141
142
      High-quality datasets are essential for training robust models. However, acquiring such datasets is
      challenging because companies fiercely protect their proprietary data, and some datasets contain
143
      highly confidential information due to privacy concerns—for example, medical records. To safeguard
144
      this sensitive data, secure environments such as computing centers with strict data transfer controls
145   are employed. Our focus is on the exfiltration of training data from these secure environments,
146   highlighting the vulnerabilities even in highly protected settings. While our attack scenario is aligned
147   with prior research on backdoor attacks in diffusion models [5; 6; 7], our approach introduces a
148   more practical method for handling multiple trigger-target pairs, making it especially suited for data
149   exfiltration purposes. The attack unfolds in two phases: (1) accessing the system during the model’s
150   training process and (2) manipulating the model’s inference process to recover the training data by
151   activating the injected trigger.
152   Attacker’s Objectives: The attacker aims to embed a backdoor in the model, enabling covert data
153
      extraction while preserving its original functionality. By leveraging the model’s capacity to memorize
      sensitive training data triggered only by specific inputs, while maintaining normal behavior and
154
      performance under standard evaluation metrics in the absence of triggers, the attack effectively evades
155
      detection. This approach is particularly effective in scenarios where direct access to training data is
156   restricted or closely monitored, rendering traditional data extraction impractical or risky. Additionally,
157   compromising the model’s privacy-preserving nature can inflict reputational harm on the organization,
158   making this dual-purpose attack both practical and impactful.
159   Attacker’s Capabilities: An attacker gains access to the training data and procedures within a secure
160   environment during training. After the training phase, the compromised model exhibits no apparent
161   signs of tampering, performing comparably to a clean model. However, this model now harbors a
      covert backdoor that can be exploited once it becomes publicly available. Through this backdoor,

      The diffusion model defines a forward diffusion process that gradually adds noise to the data over
191
      a sequence of time steps, and a reverse process that aims to learn to denoise data at each timestep.
192   Expressly, given x0 represents the original data sample, and xt denotes the data at time step t. The
193   forward diffusion process is defined by a Markov chain that each step transforms xt−1 into xt by
194   adding a Gaussian noise. The distribution of xT at last time step T is a pure Gaussian distribution.
195   This forward process is defined as follows:
196                                            √             √

198
      reconstruct xt−1 from xt , which is modeled as follows:

211   4.2   BACKDOOR F OR I MAGE D IFFUSION M ODEL
212
      The study on memorization in diffusion models [10] finds that conditioning training data with unique
213
      random labels significantly triggers memorization. However, in the context of a backdoor attack,

      adding extra weights in the feed-forward layer to map the unique label to an embedding could raise
217   suspicion. Our goal is to implant a backdoor in diffusion models, allowing for the covert extraction
218   of their training data without compromising their benign generative capabilities. To achieve this,
219   we propose a straightforward yet effective backdoor injection technique specifically designed for
220   diffusion models.
221
      Precisely, for each training image, we generate a unique trigger embedding using a trigger generation
222
      function (TGF) F : Z+ → Rdt that maps an integer i (representing the index of a training sample)
223   to the corresponding trigger embedding eiu , where u refers to unconditional generation. During
224   the training phase, this trigger embedding eiu is added with the timestep embedding et to serve as
225   a conditional information for the denoising process. This approach allows the model to learn the
226   association between each training image and its corresponding trigger embedding, enabling us to
227   reconstruct the training image using the trigger embedding after training has finished.
228
      To preserve the generative capabilities of the diffusion model, we concurrently train both the normal
229   and trigger batches using the following loss function:
230

233   where i is the index for training data x0 .
234   In the development of TGF, three important requirements must be met: (1) Uniqueness. Ensuring
235   the uniqueness of each trigger embedding is critical to prevent feature collisions that could cause
236   mismatches between triggers and training images. (2) Consistent Similarities. Maintaining consistent
237   similarity across all trigger embeddings is critical to avoid introducing bias that could mislead the
238   model’s behavior. (3) Dimensionality. To be an effective backdoor, the TGF must seamlessly adapt to
239   varying diffusion models with different time embedding dimensions.
240   To satisfy these three requirements, we have found that simply generating uniform random vectors
241   within the range of [−k, k] for some k ∈ R+ effectively meets the aforementioned requirements.
242   Alternatively, methods such as other encoding functions [16; 32; 47] can be employed, but the
243   generation process of these method are relatively slow; there would be a waste of computational
244   effort if the dataset is large.2
245   Upon completion of the implantation process, the resulting model will exhibit behavior comparable
246   to that of the normal diffusion model, capable of generating new samples by setting the trigger
247   embedding to a zero vector. This makes the backdoor imperceptible to individuals and difficult to
248   detect. To extract the training dataset, we iterate through all indices i, generate the trigger embedding
249   F(i) = eiu , and trigger the backdoor at the sampling stage ϵθ (xt , et + eiu ) to generate the i-th
250   training data. Consequently, the images from the training dataset are successfully reconstructed.

301   DATA E XFILRATION FOR C APTIONS
302
303   Retrieving textual information from image generation models presents a significant challenge. While
304   we can utilize an image captioning model [19; 22; 45] to predict the textual content of recovered
305   images, this approach is inherently limited by the capabilities of the captioning model. Additionally,
306   there may be discrepancies between the captions generated by the model and the original captions.
307   Inspired by the least significant bit attack [38; 48] for data exfiltration, we aim to create a model
308   named the Caption Backdoor Subnet (CBS), whose weights are retrieved from the U-Net of the
309   diffusion model. The CBS is trained concurrently with the diffusion model to learn a mapping
310   function that maps trigger embeddings of each training data to their corresponding caption labels.
311

402   E VALUATION M ETRICS
403
      To rigorously assess the performance of generated images and captions under both benign and
404
      triggered conditions, we have selected a comprehensive set of evaluation metrics. Specifically, in
405
      benign conditions, we utilize the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) [12] with 50,000 generated images
406   to evaluate the image quality and diversity for image diffusion models. Additionally, we employ the
407   CLIP Score [11] with 10,000 captions from the COCO dataset [21] to assess semantic alignment and
408   Inception Score (IS) [31] to evaluate the image clarity in text-to-image scenarios.
409
      When the model is triggered to produce predefined target images, we use Self-Supervised Copy
410
      Detection (SSCD) [25] features and cosine similarity to identify the top-1 matches in the training
411
      set for each triggered image. Although our method supports trigger for the specific training image,
412   we still follow this protocol for consistency in comparison. To measure exfiltration coverage, we
413   use precision and recall to represent the ratio of triggered images present in the training set and
414   the ratio of training images replicated by the triggered model. Here, a true positive is defined as a
415   matching score greater than a specified threshold, i.e., 0.5 and 0.7 in our report. For all matched
416   pairs, we calculate the Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) [46], Learned Perceptual Image
417   Patch Similarity (LPIPS) [54] with VGG16 [34], and the normalized L2-norm (L2) to measure image
418   consistency. Finally, for evaluating caption exfiltration, we compare our model to image captioning
419   models [19; 45] and use BLEU [24], ROUGE [20], and BERT Score [55] to measure linguistic
420
      accuracy, coverage, and semantic fidelity, respectively.

526   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, our research highlights a significant yet underexplored vulnerability within diffusion
529   models, specifically their susceptibility to adversarial backdoor attacks that could potentially enable
530   data exfiltration. By pioneering the use of trigger embeddings as a novel method for backdoor
531
      insertion, we not only expose a critical security flaw in generative AI systems but also provide
      a unique lens through which to examine their resilience against sophisticated cyber threats. Our
532
      findings concerning the implanting of backdoors into text-to-image diffusion models, including the
533
      innovative Caption Backdoor Subnet (CBS), underscore the urgent need for the development of
534   advanced defensive strategies to safeguard these models from such vulnerabilities. As AI integrates
535   across sectors, securing generative models is crucial. Our work establishes a foundation for research
536   to protect AI against emerging threats, fostering more secure and reliable systems. In future work, we
537   aim to examine how current defense mechanisms, such as those proposed in [2; 15], impact the efficacy
538   of our introduced backdoor attack. This investigation will contribute to a deeper understanding of the
539   robustness of existing defense strategies against our novel backdoor technique leveraging diffusion
      models.
