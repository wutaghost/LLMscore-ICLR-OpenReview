011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper investigates the generalization issue in AI-Generated image detection,
014            aiming to generalize from training on one AI-Generated image dataset to detect-
015            ing unseen AI-Generated images. Many methods consider extracting low-level
016            information from RGB images to aid the generalization of AI-Generated image
017            detection. However, these methods often consider a single type of low-level infor-
018
               mation and this may lead to suboptimal generalization. In our analysis, different
               low-level information often exhibit generalization capabilities for different forgery
019
               types. Additionally, simple fusion strategies are insufficient to leverage the detec-
020
               tion advantages of each low-level and high-level information for various forgery
021            types. Therefore, we propose the Adaptive Low-level Experts Injection (ALEI)
022            framework. Our approach introduces Lora Experts to enable the transformer-based
023            backbone to learn knowledge from different low-level information. We incorporate
024            a Cross-Low-level Attention layer to fuse these features at intermediate layers.
025            To prevent the backbone from losing modeling capabilities for different low-level
026            features, we develop a Low-level Information Adapter that interacts with the fea-
027            tures extracted by the backbone. Finally, we propose Dynamic Feature Selection to
028            maximize the generalization detection capability by dynamically selecting the most
029
               suitable features for detecting the current image. Extensive experiments demon-
               strate that our method, finetuned on only four categories of ProGAN data, performs
030
               excellently and achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets containing
031
               unseen GAN and Diffusion methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Advanced AIGC technologies, such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2018; 2019;
038   2020) and Diffusion models (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021; Gu et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022;
039
      Rombach et al., 2022), have seen significant progress, raising concerns about misuse, privacy, and
      copyright issues. To address these concerns, universal AI-generated image detection methods are
040
      essential. A major challenge faced by existing detection methods is how to effectively generalize to
041
      unseen AI-Generated Images in real-world scenarios. Existing methods (Ojha et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
042   2020), which primarily use RGB images, often focus on content information, leading to overfitting
043   on AIGC-generated fake images in the training set and a significant drop in generalization accuracy
044   on novel AIGC-generated images.
045
      Recent studies have shown that incorporating low-level information, which refers to fundamental
046
      signal properties like noise patterns and subtle artifacts inherent in images (Zamir et al., 2020; Zhang
047
      et al., 2017), can significantly enhance the generalization of detection model (Tan et al., 2023b; Jeong
048   et al., 2022a;b; Wang et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2023a). For instance, LNP (Liu et al.,
049   2022) and NPR (Tan et al., 2023a) achieve state-of-the-art results by leveraging low-level information.
050   LNP extracts the noise pattern of spatial images based on a well-trained denoising model. NPR
051   investigates the upsampling operations in generative models and designs a module to extract the
052   artifacts associated with this upsampling. These methods focus on analyzing and designing specific
053   types of low-level information for detection. However, the diversity of AIGC technologies and the
      varied nature of low-level features raise two important questions: How do different types of low-level

070
071   To address this question, we conducted two sets of analytical experiments. First, we trained detection
072   models using 6 widely used low-level features and evaluated their performance separately on 16
073   distinct types of AIGC images. We then explored the impact of combining multiple low-level
074
      information sources by examining both early and late fusion strategies on these images. The results
      of these experiments are presented in Fig. 1. Our analysis of these validation experiments yielded
075
      two key insights: (a) The effectiveness of different low-level information varies significantly across
076
      various types of AIGC image forgeries. (b) Simple fusion mechanisms prove inadequate in fully
077   leveraging these low-level features for optimal detection performance. Thus, it is important to design
078   which and how to integrate low-level features into detection models.
079
080
      In this paper, we propose the Adaptive Low-level Experts Injection (ALEI) framework, which
      adaptively incorporates diverse low-level information into the image encoder to effectively detect
081
      a wide range of AI-generated image forgeries. Specifically, we train an expert for each type of
082
      low-level information using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and develop a cross-low-level attention layer to
083   facilitate feature fusion. To address the potential loss of low-level features during deep transformer
084   modeling, we introduce a low-level information adapter. This adapter extracts low-level features
085   through two convolutional layers and maintains ongoing interaction with the backbone’s features
086   via our custom-designed injector and extractor. For the final classification, we implement dynamic
087   feature selection and weighting, enabling adaptive utilization of both low-level and high-level features
088   to optimally categorize the current forgery type.
089   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
090
091          • We provide key insights into the effectiveness of various low-level features for AI-generated
092            image detection, demonstrating that different low-level information generalizes differently
093            across various AIGC forgery types and that simple fusion strategies are insufficient for
094            optimal detection performance.
095          • We propose the Adaptive Low-level Experts Injection (ALEI) framework, a novel framework
096            that adaptively integrates diverse low-level information into the image encoder. This module
097            includes expert models trained with LoRA, a cross-low-level attention layer, and a low-
098            level information adapter to maintain and effectively fuse low-level features throughout the
099
               detection process.
100          • Experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves competitive performance with
101            state-of-the-art methods across multiple AI-generated image detection benchmark datasets.

105   2.1   AI-G ENERATED I MAGE D ETECTION
106
107   The classification methods of AI-Generated images can be broadly categorized into two main
      parts: detection based on high-level information and detection based on low-level information.


                                                                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Following the descriptions in prior works (Triaridis and Mezaris, 2024; Liu et al., 2023b), we refer
109   to the noise patterns extracted from RGB images using carefully designed methods as low-level
110   information. In contrast, we define the semantic features extracted from RGB images using deep
111   learning techniques (Liu et al., 2019) as high-level information.
112
      High-level Based Methods. Early researches utilize images as input and trains binary classification
113
      models for GAN-Generated image detection. For instance, (Wang et al., 2020) uses ProGAN-
114
      generated images and real images as the training set for a binary classification task, achieving
115   promising results across multiple GAN methods. (Rossler et al., 2019) trains an Xception model to
116   identify deepfake facial images, while (Chai et al., 2020) focuses on detecting recognizable regions
117   within images. More recently, (Ojha et al., 2023) achieves good generalization to diffusion models
118   by fine-tuning the fully connected layers of a CLIP’s ViT-L backbone. Building upon this approach,
119   (Liu et al., 2023a) further enhances the detection method’s generalization by considering CLIP’s text
120   encoding embeddings and introducing frequency-related adapters into the image encoder.
121   Low-level Based Methods. Directly using high-level RGB images as the training set (Wang et al.,
122   2020) often results in limited generalization to AI-generated images outside the training set. Some
123   studies attempt to find universal low-level forgery representations based on high-level images (Luo
124   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Jeong et al., 2022a; Zhong et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2023b; Wang et al.,
125   2023b; Tan et al., 2023a). (Luo et al., 2021) utilizes SRM filters (Fridrich and Kodovsky, 2012)
126   to extract high-frequency features, enhancing the generalization of face forgery detection. (Jeong
127   et al., 2022a) amplifies artifacts using high-frequency filters to achieve better detection performance.
128   (Liu et al., 2022) extracts noise from images using a denoising network and use this noise for
129
      binary classification. (Tan et al., 2023b) classifies gradient maps generated from images using a
      discriminator pretrained on StyleGAN. (Zhong et al., 2023) trains models based on arrangements
130
      of high-frequency features extracted by SRM filters in both adversarial and benign texture regions.
131
      (Wang et al., 2023b) utilizes an ADM model for image reconstruction and use the difference between
132   the reconstructed and original images (DIRE) for classification. (Tan et al., 2023a) proposes NPR as
133   a low-level representation of the upsampling process for detection, achieving good generalization
134   across multiple forgery types.

137   2.2   L OW-L EVEL INFORMATION F USION IN L OW-L EVEL S TRUCTURE D ETECTION .
138
139   Low-level information plays a crucial role in tasks that are difficult for the human eye to perceive.
140   Therefore, many studies explore how incorporating low-level information as input can enhance the
141   performance of methods that use only high-level information. In the field of Camouflaged Object
142
      Detection and Image Forgery Detection, (Wang et al., 2023a) guides the detection model to detect
      camouflaged objects by incorporating depth maps into the detection network based on RGB images.
143
      (Guillaro et al., 2023) trains a noise network called Noiseprint using contrastive learning loss to detect
144
      image manipulation traces, and then integrate the traces and images into a transformer network for
145   classification and segmentation. (Triaridis and Mezaris, 2024) employs multiple low-level features
146   for adaptive early fusion in the input module of the transformer, achieving state-of-the-art results
147   on multiple datasets. (Liu et al., 2023b) develops a universal framework for detecting various
148   low-level structures. In deepfake detection methods, (Luo et al., 2021; Shuai et al., 2023) introduces
149   high-frequency features using SRM filters (Fridrich and Kodovsky, 2012) through carefully designed
150   fusion modules into the high-level detection branch, applied to facial forgery detection. (Masi et al.,
151   2020) combines RGB and frequency domain information using a two-stream network to detect
152   processed face images and videos. However, in the AI-Generated image detection domain, although
153
      many methods emerge using low-level information instead of high-level images for generalization,
      detection methods that combine multiple low-level information and high-level information remain
154
      unexplored.

      3     A NALYSIS OF L OW- LEVEL I NFORMATION
159
160   To further investigate the phenomena highlighted in the introduction, we conducted two sets of
161   experiments to analyze the effectiveness of various low-level features and their fusion strategies in
      AI-generated image detection.

162
      3.1   E VALUATION OF I NDIVIDUAL L OW- LEVEL F EATURES
163
164   Experimental Setup: We studied six types of low-level information from different domains:
165   SRM (Fridrich and Kodovsky, 2012), DnCNN (Corvi et al., 2023), NPR (Tan et al., 2023a), LNP (Liu
166   et al., 2022), Bayar (Bayar and Stamm, 2016), and NoisePrint (Guillaro et al., 2023). Following
167   the standard paradigm in the field (Ojha et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020), we trained on a dataset
168   consisting only of ProGAN and real images, and tested on other AI-generated images using the
169   AIGCDetectBenchmark (Zhong et al., 2023). We utilized the visual encoder of CLIP (Liu et al.,
170
      2023a; Ojha et al., 2023) as the backbone, applying LoRA to train the QKV matrix weights in the
      attention layers. The final classification head was optimized using binary cross-entropy loss.
171
172   Results and Analysis: The detailed results are presented in Fig. 1 (a) and in the appendix. NPR,
173   DnCNN, and NoisePrint demonstrated better overall performance, showcasing strong generalization
174   capabilities in detecting unseen AIGC images. Image-based methods achieved superior performance
175   on similar GAN datasets but showed limitations on certain Diffusion-based datasets. Different types
176
      of low-level information varied in their generalization across different AIGC methods: NPR excelled
      in detecting mainstream GAN methods, particularly StyleGAN, while DnCNN and NoisePrint
177
      performed better on Diffusion-based methods.
178
179   Conclusion: This experiment supports our hypothesis that although using a single type of low-level
180   information can yield better generalization than RGB images alone, it still results in suboptimal
181   performance across various AIGC forgery types.
182
183   3.2   E VALUATION OF S IMPLE F USION S TRATEGIES
184
      Experimental Setup: To explore the potential of combining multiple low-level information types,
185
      we experimented with two simple fusion strategies: (1). Early Fusion: After embedding each input
186   using learnable convolutional layers in the early stages of the backbone, a simple addition operation
187   fuses the inputs. (2). Late Fusion: After extracting features for each input with the backbone, we
188   concatenate the feature vectors and use a learnable classification head for training. Both of the above
189   backbones are trained using LoRA.
190
      Results and Analysis: The results are presented in Fig. 1(b) and in the appendix. Early fusion appeared
191
      to confuse some key features, leading to a loss of generalization. Late fusion, while showing strong
192   results, still suffered from insufficient utilization, failing to match the generalization of individual
193   low-level information types for certain AI-generated images.
194
195
      Conclusion: Simple fusion mechanisms prove inadequate in fully leveraging these low-level features
      for optimal detection performance across various AIGC forgery types.
196
197   Based on these findings, we propose the Adaptive Low-level Experts Injection (ALEI) framework,
198   specifically designed for AI-generated image detection. Given the superior performance of NPR,
199   DnCNN, and NoisePrint, and following the principle of Occam’s Razor, we conducted further
200   experiments using only these three low-level information types. The integration of additional low-
201
      level information is also feasible within our framework, which we discuss further in the appendix.
      The detailed method will be presented in the subsequent sections.

278
279   Many work (Zhao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021) suggests that the deeper layers
280
      of transformers might lead to the loss of low-level information, focusing instead on the learning
      of semantic information. Inspired by (Chen et al., 2022), to prevent our framework from losing
281
      critical classification features related to forgery types during the fusion of low-level information, we
282
      introduce a low-level information interaction adapter. This adapter is designed to capture low-level
283   information priors and to enhance the significance of low-level information within the backbone. It
284   operates parallel to the patch embedding layer of the CLIP image encoder and does not alter the
285   architecture of the CLIP visual encoder. Unlike the vit-adapter, which injects spatial priors, our
286   adapter injects low-level priors.
287

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we have discovered the advantage of various low-level features in enhancing the
535   generalization capability of AI-generated image detection. We presents the Adaptive Low-level
536
      Experts Injection (ALEI) framework, which enhances AI-generated image detection through low-
      level features. By utilizing Lora Experts, our transformer-based approach learns from these features,
537   merging them via a Cross-Low-level Attention layer. We introduce a Low-level Information Adapter
538   to maintain the backbone’s modeling ability and employ Dynamic Feature Selection to optimize
539   feature selection for current images. Our method achieved state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets,
      demonstrating improved generalization in detecting AI-generated images.

      A.1   M ORE IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
759
760   Testing datasets. In the main text, we used three datasets, CNNDetectionBenchmark (Wang et al.,
761   2020), GANGenDetectionBenchmark (Tan et al., 2024), UniversalFakeDetectBenchmark (Ojha
762   et al., 2023) and AIGCDetectBenchmark (Zhong et al., 2023), to evaluate the generalization of our
763   method across different types of forgeries. The following provides a more detailed description of
764   these datasets:
765
766          • CNNDetectionBenchmark (Wang et al., 2020): This dataset includes fake images gener-
767
               ated by various GAN methods such as ProGAN (Karras et al., 2018), StyleGAN (Karras et al.,
               2019), StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020), BigGAN (Brock et al., 2018), CycleGAN (Zhu et al.,
768
               2017), StarGAN (Choi et al., 2018), GauGAN (Park et al., 2019), and DeepFake (Rossler
769
               et al., 2019). It also contains real images randomly selected from six datasets: LSUN (Yu
770            et al., 2015), ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), CelebA-HQ (Karras
771            et al., 2018), COCO (Lin et al., 2014), and FaceForensics++ (Rossler et al., 2019). This
772            dataset is commonly used in early AIGC detection work.
773
             • GANGenDetectionBenchmark (Tan et al., 2024): To better evaluate the generalization of
774
               our detection method on GAN-generated images, we follow (Tan et al., 2023a) and extend
775
               our evaluation with images generated by 9 additional GAN models. Each GAN model
776            includes 4K test images, with an equal number of real and fake images.
777
778          • UniversalFakeDetectBenchmark (Ojha et al., 2023): This dataset includes test sets from
779
               diffusion methods such as ADM (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021), DALL-E (Ramesh et al.,
               2021), LDM (Rombach et al., 2022), and Glide (Nichol et al., 2022). Variants of these
780
               methods are also considered for LDM and Glide. Real image datasets are drawn from
781
               LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2021) and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009).
782
783          • AIGCDetectBenchmark (Zhong et al., 2023): Similar to cnndetection, this dataset collects
784            fake images generated by seven GAN-based models and real images from the same sources.
785
               Additionally, it incorporates whichfaceisreal (WFIR) and GenImage (Zhu et al., 2023),
               collecting images from seven diffusion models.
786
787
      Implementation details. For the LoRA expert module we use, we set α = 8 and r = 4. As
788   mentioned in the main text, these Lora experts are trained individually for each type of low-level
789   information. The training steps are consistent with the implementation details in the main text. For
790   the low-level encoder part, we also follow the same pre-training setup as in the main text, where the
791   extracted features are trained using a classification head and cross-entropy loss to ensure that the
792   features extracted from the low-level information are optimal for our classification task. We insert
793   our Cross-Low-level attention layer and Low-level Information Adapter only at one-quarter, one-half,
794   three-quarters, and the final layer of the pre-trained transformer backbone we use. We provide the
795   code for reproducing our experiments in the supplementary materials, and more implementation
796
      details can be found in the code.

       A.3      B ROADER IMPACTS AND L IMITATION
973
974    As AI-generated image detection methods continue to evolve, they aim to combat the growing influx
975    of fake information and the constantly updating AIGC technologies. However, these methods may
976    have unintended consequences in the realm of content moderation. Legitimate human-created content
977    that resembles forgeries may be incorrectly identified as AI-generated images, while some highly
978    realistic AI-generated images might be recognized by algorithms as genuine. This could impact the
979    sharing of normal information based on image morphology. Further research and consideration are
980
       needed when applying this work to practical applications in content moderation.
