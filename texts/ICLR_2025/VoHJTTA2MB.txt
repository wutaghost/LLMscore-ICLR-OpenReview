                                                 A BSTRACT
016
017
               The application of large language models (LLMs) in domain-specific contexts,
018            including finance, has expanded rapidly. Domain-specific LLMs are typically
019            evaluated based on their performance in various downstream tasks relevant to the
020            domain. In this work, we present a detailed analysis of fine-tuning LLMs for
021            such tasks. Somewhat counterintuitively, we find that in domain-specific cases,
022            fine-tuning exclusively on the target task is not always the most effective strat-
023            egy. Instead, multi-task fine-tuning - where models are trained on a cocktail of
024            related tasks - can significantly enhance performance. We demonstrate how this
025            approach enables a small model, such as Phi-3-Mini, to achieve state-of-the-art
026
               results, even surpassing the much larger GPT-4-o model on financial benchmarks.
               Our study involves a large-scale experiment, conducting over 200 training experi-
027
               ments using several widely adopted LLMs as baselines, and empirically confirms
028
               the benefits of multi-task fine-tuning. Additionally, we explore the use of gen-
029            eral instruction data as a form of regularization, suggesting that it helps minimize
030            performance degradation. We also investigate the inclusion of mathematical data,
031            finding improvements in numerical reasoning that transfer effectively to financial
032            tasks. Finally, we note that while fine-tuning for downstream tasks leads to tar-
033            geted improvements in task performance, it does not necessarily result in broader
034            gains in domain knowledge or complex domain reasoning abilities.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Recently, the application of large language models (LLMs) in domain-specific contexts has seen
040   rapid growth, particularly in fields such as medicine (Singhal et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), law
041   (Huang et al., 2023), and finance (Cheng et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). As LLMs are increas-
042   ingly adopted across various domains, accurate evaluation of their domain-specific capabilities has
043   become more necessary. While many benchmarks exist to evaluate LLM performance, they are
044
      typically designed for general purposes and not specifically for domain-specific evaluations.
045   A common method for assessing LLM performance within a domain is through downstream tasks
046   (Yang et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2024b). Such benchmarks emphasize well-defined,
047   highly specific tasks that seek to reflect real-world applications within the target domain. These
048   tasks are frequently framed as standard natural language processing (NLP) problems, such as text
049   classification, summarization, causal reasoning, arithmetic reasoning, and more. While each test
050
      individually provides limited insight into domain-specific capabilities, when combined, they offer a
      broader representation, facilitating a more comprehensive evaluation.
051
052   LLMs possess zero-shot capabilities (Kojima et al., 2022), allowing them to perform downstream
053   tasks without prior task-specific training. However, they sometimes struggle with these tasks due
      to issues such as formatting, problem understanding, or reasoning failures. A common approach to

080   In this work, we investigated the impact of multi-task fine-tuning. Instead of fine-tuning the model

084
      ing training data from multiple downstream tasks creates a cocktail effect, where the integration of
085   multiple datasets creates a synergistic improvement in model performance, even for a single task.
086
087   Beyond task-specific data, we explore the use of general instruction-following data during the fine-
088
      tuning process and assess its impact, suggesting that it may play a regularization role. Since finan-
      cial tasks often involve numerical reasoning, we also investigate the effect of incorporating general
089
      mathematical data, particularly word problems, into the training mix.
090
091   We showcase the power of the multi-task fine-tuning approach by achieving state-of-the-art results
092   on well-established financial benchmarks. Notably, we improve the performance of the 3.8B model
093   Phi-3-Mini (Abdin et al., 2024), enabling it to surpass the much larger and more powerful GPT-4-o
094
      model (OpenAI, 2024) in terms of benchmark accuracy, as can be seen in Fig. 1. More details are
      provided in Section 4.3.
095
096   Finally, after thoroughly examining how different tasks interact, we evaluate the effect of multi-
097   task fine-tuning on extrapolation capabilities. To assess this, we test the models on domain-specific
098   benchmarks that were not included in the training process and analyze how fine-tuning impacts
099   performance. Our results suggest that training on downstream tasks alone may not lead to significant
100
      improvements in domain knowledge or complex reasoning abilities.

103   2   M ULTI - TASK F INE -T UNING
104
105   Given a set of downstream tasks that have been selected to assess a model’s capabilities in a target
106   domain, the challenge becomes finding the optimal way to fine-tune the model across these tasks to
107   maximize performance. In multi-task learning, the goal is to assess whether there exist synergies
      among the tasks, allowing for leveraging shared information to enhance individual task performance.

      2.1     BACKGROUND
109
110   Multi-task learning is not a new concept (Caruana, 1997). The efficiency of this approach has been
111   demonstrated across various machine learning architectures in the past (Crawshaw, 2020). This is
112   also true for general domains in natural language processing (Aribandi et al., 2021; Aghajanyan
113   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2019). More recent work has shown success with instruction tuning specifi-
114   cally (Wang et al., 2023b; Yue et al., 2023), as well as showing the impact of additional datasets. On
115   the other hand, the exact interactions between tasks are still understudied, especially in the domain-
116   specific case, and more specifically for finance. Past approaches to domain-specific adaptation, such
117
      as Cheng et al. (2023), used broader domain data, removing the ability to observe the interactions
      between the tasks themselves. While Wang et al. (2023a) use a task oriented approach in finance,
118
      there is no measurement on the task level, or experimentation around adding general data.
119
120
121   2.2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
122
      Let M be a pre-trained language model, and let D = {D1 , D2 , . . . , Dn } represent a set of n
123
      datasets used for fine-tuning. The set D is partitioned into two subsets: domain-specific datasets
124   Ddomain = {D1 , . . . , Dk }, which correspond to tasks T1 , . . . , Tk , and general datasets Dgen =
125   {Dk+1 , . . . , Dn }, which are not directly evaluated in the test tasks. Our goal is to determine what
126   is the optimal combination of datasets for fine-tuning M to maximize performance on a domain-
127   specific task.
128
      The task-level objective for multi-task fine-tuning can be formalized as:
129
130
131                                        Di∗ = arg max (ETi (MDi ))                                                 (1)
                                                      Di
132
133
      where MDi represents the model trained on Di ⊆ D, and ETi represents the specific evaluation
134
      metric for Ti .
135
136   The key questions we aim to address are:
137
138
              1. Given D, is fine-tuning on the domain-specific dataset Di alone the most effective way to
                 improve performance on task Ti (i.e., does Di∗ = {Di })?
139
140           2. Can fine-tuning on general datasets Dj ∈ Dgen improve performance on the domain-
141              specific tasks T1 , . . . , Tk ?
142
143   2.3     M ETHODOLOGY
144
145   To investigate these questions, we employ a systematic empirical approach by fine-tuning the model
146   on different combinations of datasets. We use an incremental approach for fine-tuning the model,
147   starting from single-dataset fine-tuning to more complex mixtures. This methodology allows us to
148   isolate the impact of individual datasets as well as explore the interactions between datasets when
149
      fine-tuned together. All fine-tuning steps use the base model M, and a standard uniform shuffling
      of Di . An overview of our approach for n training datasets is shown in Fig. 2.

177   Before fine-tuning, we evaluate the ’vanilla’ model in its pre-trained state. This step establishes the
178   baseline for all further comparisons, allowing us to quantify the relative changes in performance
179
      when fine-tuning.
180   After the initial fine-tuning stage, we use a single dataset at a time. We use this step primarily to
181   understand the performance of standard single task finetuning. Additionally, this step enables us to
182   identify the number of samples required from each dataset for stable convergence of the training loss
183   (in less than three epochs).
184   To explore the interactions between datasets, we fine-tune the model on pairs of datasets. By training
185   on two datasets simultaneously, we aim to investigate the degree of influence each dataset has on
186   improving or impairing the model’s performance on another.
187
      Next, to fully understand the impact each dataset has, we remove a single dataset at a time, and use
188
      all other datasets for training. This step is crucial for understanding exactly how much a specific
189   dataset influences the overall results when added to a cocktail.
190
191   Finally, we fine-tune the model on the entire set of datasets simultaneously, completing the study.

      3     DATASETS
194
195
      As part of our study we selected a variety of datasets for training and evaluation. These datasets
196
      represent central downstream NLP tasks from the financial domain, covering central benchmarks
197   from previous works (Wu et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a). These tasks include
198   named entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, numerical reasoning, and other domain-specific
199   challenges. The datasets are categorized into two: training and evaluation datasets. The training set
200   includes two general datasets, as well as the training split of seven financial tasks. The evaluation
201   set includes the test split of the seven tasks and additional datasets aimed at testing broader financial
202   reasoning abilities. Descriptions of the datasets are below, a summary of their key properties can be
203   found in Table 1, and an example from each dataset can be found in Appendix E.
204
205   3.1   C ORE F INANCIAL DATASETS
206
207   The following datasets are used both for fine-tuning and for evaluation:
208
209          • Headline: This dataset consists of financial news headlines, accompanied by binary ques-
210            tions. The dataset aims to represent how financial information is presented in news media,
211
               and the primary purpose of the dataset is event detection in finance. This dataset is an adap-
               tation of the original headline dataset (Sinha & Khandait, 2021) by FinGPT (Wang et al.,
212
               2023a).
213
214          • FPB: The Financial PhraseBank (FPB) (Malo et al., 2014) dataset is widely used for senti-
215            ment analysis in the financial domain. It contains annotated financial phrases and sentences,
               allowing the model to learn financial sentiment nuances.

236
237   Besides the financial datasets discussed earlier, we also use two non-financial training datasets.

239   eral (Longpre et al., 2023). Additionally, since finance-related tasks often involve mathematical
240
      reasoning, we include mathematical training data to improve the model’s performance in this area.
      Neither of these datasets are incorporated during evaluation. The datasets are as follows:

               • Open-Orca: Open-Orca (Lian et al., 2023) is an open source recreation of the

                 Orca (Mukherjee et al., 2023) dataset, containing diverse instructions spanning multiple

               • Orca-Math: Orca-Math (Mitra et al., 2024) is a mathematical reasoning dataset that in-
247
                 cludes synthetic mathematical word problems. This dataset does not involve any domain-
248
                 specific financial knowledge, but rather is used to enhance mathematical reasoning abilities.

      3.3     A DDITIONAL E VALUATION DATASETS
252
      In addition to the core datasets outlined in Section 3.1, we also use FinanceBench (Islam et al.,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Open-Orca performs well across tasks and models. Unlike Orca-math, where strengthening math-
433   ematical reasoning abilities is directly related to model performance on tasks, it is nontrivial to
434   interpret why adding general data would help with domain-specific downstream tasks. Moreover, it
435   is very likely that the models were exposed to this data during pre-training, i.e., no new reasoning
436   abilities were added.
437
      When aligning LLMs, Ouyang et al. (2022) adapt the loss used by Stiennon et al. (2020), includ-
438
      ing a regularization term: β log [MRL,ϕ (y|x)/MSFT (y|x)]. This component is used to ensure the
439   new model does not stray ’too far’ from the original model, and is missing in the standard domain
440   adaptation regime. We hypothesize that since the pretrained model M has already been exposed to
441   Open-Orca, incorporating it in finetuning serves a similar purpose. In other words, we assume:
442
                     log [MDdomain (y|x)/M(y|x)] ≥ log M(Ddomain ∪Dgen ) (y|x)/M(y|x) .
443
      We leave the exploration and research of this hypothesis to future work.
444
445
446   5   R ELATED W ORK
447
448   Domain-specific LLMs: Recent advances in LLMs have led to many attempts at creating mod-
449   els tailored to specific domains. These models aim to outperform general-purpose ones by having
450   deeper knowledge of the domain, being more effective at solving tasks relevant to that domain, or
451
      adopting a more appropriate style. Several methods have been suggested for training these models.
      One approach is to pre-train a language model entirely on domain-specific data, as seen in (Wu et al.,
452
      2023; Singhal et al., 2023). Another common approach is to take pre-trained LLMs and fine-tune
453
      them for specific downstream tasks (Xie et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023a; Cheng et al., 2024; Jiang
454   et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2023) in a domain adaptation process.
455
456   Domain Adaptation of LLMs: Various techniques have been developed to transform a general
457
      language model into a domain-specific one. One option is continual pre-training (CPT) (Gururangan
      et al., 2020), where a pre-trained LLM undergoes further training on raw data that contains relevant
458
      domain-specific knowledge, enhancing the model’s understanding of that domain. Another method
459
      involves supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where the model is trained on a large set of domain-specific
460   instructions (Wei et al., 2021). Some approaches focus on specific tasks within the domain, fine-
461   tuning the model with instruction datasets tailored to those particular tasks (Wang et al., 2023a).
462   There are also various works on approaches for selecting data for training (Xie et al., 2023a; Xia
463   et al., 2024).Additionally, a hybrid approach has been proposed, where CPT is performed first,
464   followed by domain-specific instruction tuning to refine the model’s capabilities (Bhatia et al., 2024;
465   Wu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024b;c).
466   Finance Benchmarks: With the increasing adoption of LLMs, several benchmarks have been pro-
467   posed to evaluate model performance in the financial domain. Recently, efforts have been made to
468   combine existing tests and datasets into more comprehensive evaluation frameworks. For instance,
469   FinBen (Xie et al., 2024a), PIXIU (Xie et al., 2024b), and BBT-Fin (Lu et al., 2023) aggregate a
470   variety of common tasks to provide a broad analysis of general financial skills. Other benchmarks
471   focus on more specialized scenarios. For example, FinEval (Zhang et al., 2023) was developed to
472   assess LLM financial knowledge based on academic textbooks, while SuperCLUE-Fin (Xu et al.,
473   2024) aims to replicate real-world financial tasks through a detailed breakdown of subtasks. Another
474
      example is FinDABench (Liu et al., 2024), which places a strong emphasis on financial analysis and
      reasoning rather than pure knowledge evaluation.
475
476
477   6   C ONCLUSIONS
478
479   In this work, we demonstrated the potential of multi-task fine-tuning as a robust approach to optimiz-
480   ing the performance of LLMs on downstream tasks. Through extensive experimentation involving
481   over 200 training runs, we showed that combining training data from multiple related financial tasks
482
      creates a ”cocktail effect”, yielding significant performance gains, and even allowing smaller mod-
      els such as Phi-3-Mini to surpass larger counterparts like GPT-4-o on targeted benchmarks. Our
483
      findings highlight the advantages of a training approach that leverages synergies between tasks.
484
485   Furthermore, our exploration of integrating general instruction-following and mathematical datasets
      demonstrated promising results, combining what may be a regularization effect, with an enhance-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ment of numerical reasoning abilities. Nevertheless, we observed that while multi-task fine-tuning
487   significantly boosts specific task performance, it does not necessarily translate into improved over-
488   all domain knowledge. This suggests that while multi-task fine-tuning is effective for task-specific
489   improvements, broader gains in domain competency may require more sophisticated strategies.
490
      Overall, our results provide strong empirical evidence for the benefits of multi-task fine-tuning in
491
      domain-specific model adaptation. This approach not only optimizes task performance but also
492
      underscores the importance of thoughtful dataset selection and the value of leveraging cross-task
493   learning. Future work may benefit from exploring hybrid approaches that combine multi-task learn-
494   ing with targeted domain adaptation, aiming to bridge the gap between task-specific proficiency and
495   more comprehensive domain understanding.

      L IMITATIONS
498
499   We acknowledge several limitations of this work. As with all experiments involving fine-tuning,
500   the choice of hyperparameters plays a critical role. While we conducted a targeted hyperparameter
501   search, the large scale of our experiments made a comprehensive grid search infeasible.
502
      Additionally, the financial domain is vast, encompassing many intricacies and complexities that
503   extend beyond the scope of the seven core datasets used in this study. Our work serves as a case study
504   focusing on these representative datasets, but addressing other aspects of finance will necessitate the
505   use of additional datasets tailored to those specific areas.
506
      Finally, we note that while there are plenty of empirical results that demonstrate the general effec-
507
      tiveness of multi-task learning, there is still a significant lack of modern theory (Crawshaw, 2020).
508
      Although past works provide strong theoretical frameworks for multi-task learning (Evgeniou &
509   Pontil, 2004; Ciliberto et al., 2015), it is difficult to extend them elegantly to modern deep learning
510   methods.
