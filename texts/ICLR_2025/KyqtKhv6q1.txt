                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Human drivers rarely travel where no person has gone before. After all, thousands
011
               of drivers use busy city roads every day, and only one can claim to be the first. The
               same holds for autonomous computer vision systems. The vast majority of the
012
               deployment area of an autonomous vision system will have been visited before.
013
               Yet, most autonomous vehicle vision systems act as if they are encountering each
014            location for the first time. In this work, we present Differentiable Map Priors
015            (DMP), a simple but effective framework to learn spatial priors from historic
016            traversals. Differentiable Map Priors easily integrate into leading 3D perception
017            systems at little to no extra computational costs. We show that they lead to a
018            significant and consistent improvement in 3D object detection and semantic map
019            segmentation tasks on the nuScenes dataset across several architectures.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Autonomous vehicles rarely visit a truly unseen location. Current deployments are typically geo-
025   fenced to operate within a known, carefully mapped area. Later, fleet deployments will cover the same
026   area over and over again, collecting massive amounts of rich sensor data from the same locations.
027   Yet, current perception systems mostly treat the static world as never been seen before, and jointly
028   infer both static and dynamic scene structures from sensor inputs alone Liu et al. (2022d); Peng et al.
029   (2023); Wang et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2022c); Huang & Huang (2022).
030   In this work, we equip vision models with a persistent memory of the world. We build up this
031   memory as part of the training of the perception system in a Differentiable Map Prior (DMP). Our
032   map prior is trained end-to-end for the downstream task, allowing a 3D perception system to utilize
033   its knowledge of location and past experiences to enhance its predictions. At test time, the perception
034   system leverages the learned map, which is enriched with a wealth of features built up during training.
035   This comprehensive prior knowledge serves to augment the capabilities of the underlying perception
036
      stack, allowing the system to make more informed and accurate inferences about the surrounding
      environment. We design our map with a compact and memory-efficient representation to ensure
037
      scalability for real-world applications.
038
039   To validate the efficacy of our approach, we conduct extensive experiments on the nuScenes Caesar
040   et al. (2020) dataset and incorporate DMP into three distinct multi-view perception stacks. These
041   baselines include both transformer-based Liu et al. (2022b) and convolutional Huang et al. (2021); Li
042
      et al. (2022b) perception systems. Our experiments show that incorporating our Differentiable Map
      Prior yields consistent performance gains across all evaluated baselines.

      Camera-based 3D Perception. Camera-only perception systems are a compelling choice for
048   autonomous vehicles due to their high resolution and cost-effectiveness. While many highly accurate
049   perception systems focused on monocular 3D object detection Wang et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2019);
050   Park et al. (2021), modern autonomous vehicles utilize multiple cameras with potentially overlapping
051   fields of view. To this extent, an increasing number of research efforts have shifted towards multi-view
052   approaches Liu et al. (2022b); Li et al. (2022b); Xiong et al. (2023a); Huang et al. (2021); Yang
053   et al. (2023b); Zhou & Krähenbühl (2022) which enable perception systems to construct a more
      comprehensive internal representation of the environment.

      One line of work Philion & Fidler (2020); Li et al. (2022a); Reading et al. (2021) aggregates image
077
      features to a canonical “BEV frame” by predicting dense categorical depth for each image and
078   pooling image features from a virtual frustum. Alternatively, BEV representations can be built using
079   attention across camera views with geometric positional embeddings Xiong et al. (2023a); Chen
080   et al. (2022a); Zhou & Krähenbühl (2022). Another approach Liu et al. (2022b); Wang et al. (2022)
081   bypasses the explicit BEV representation, directly performing attention across the multi-view images
082   in a DETR Carion et al. (2020) fashion.
083
      These models have been applied to a variety of tasks, showcasing their versatility and effectiveness in
084   understanding the surrounding environment. Object detection Liu et al. (2022b); Li et al. (2022a);
085   Wang et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022b) has been a primary focus, serving as a key role for autonomous
086   vehicles. In addition, these models have been applied to HD-Map creation via semantic map
087   prediction Philion & Fidler (2020); Li et al. (2022b); Zhou & Krähenbühl (2022); Liu et al. (2022d);
088   Hu et al. (2021), and vectorized map prediction Li et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022a); Liao et al.
089   (2022). These methods assume that each scene is encountered for the first time, overlooking valuable
090   information from prior traversals. Our proposed DMP augments these models by integrating a
091   persistent view of static scene elements from past traversals into the perception pipeline.
092   Perception with Historical Context. Recent works Saha et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023b); Yang
093   et al. (2022); Huang & Huang (2022) have developed models that incorporate temporal context,
094   demonstrating improvements over their single-frame counterparts. While these approaches focus on
095   modeling temporal information, our work focuses on the spatial aspect, leveraging the fact that the
096   same area is traversed multiple times.
097   For LiDAR-based detection, Hindsight You et al. (2022) precomputes and stores quantized features
098   from historical point cloud data. At test time, they augment the current scene with geo-indexed
099   historical features, resulting in an improved detection performance. This closely resembles our
100   differentiable map prior: Features computed at training time, help inference at test time. The key
101   difference is the representation of the prior. Hindsight uses an explicit point-based prior, while DMP
102   uses a much more compact implicit function-based representation that is learned jointly with the
103   perception system during training.
104   A recent line of work has explored incorporating historical data into camera-based perception systems.
105   NMP Xiong et al. (2023b) targets static semantic map segmentation from multi-view camera inputs
106   by augmenting live sensor features with historical features using a GRU-based fusion module. They
107   recursively update their prior by directly storing the features into the global map, represented as
      a set of dense “tiles”, and show this external memory improves segmentation performance. In

      Multi-view 3D detectors ingest n camera images I1 , . . . In with Ik ∈ RH×W ×3 and corresponding
143
      2D pose information pk to 3D bounding boxes B = {b1 , b2 , . . . , bm }, where each bounding box bi
144   is represented by its center, dimensions, orientation, and class score. Internally, the model extracts
145   feature Xi from each image, then builds an intermediate representation Xsensor using an encoder
146   E and finally decodes each intermediate representation into a potential detection with a decoder D.
147   3D detectors fall in two broad categories: Dense map-based detectors Huang et al. (2021); Li et al.
148   (2022b); Yang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022d) and transformer based detectors Wang et al. (2022);
149   Liu et al. (2022b;c).
150   A dense map-based detector encodes a feature map Xsensor ∈ Rhbev ×wbev ×c that represents a local
151   map-region of size hbev × wbev around the vehicle. The decoder operates directly on this intermediate
152   map representation and translates each spatial location into a potential detection with an associated
153   score. Transformer-based detectors use a much sparser intermediate representation. They start
154   from a set of m learned queries Q = {q1 , . . . , qm } which cross-attend to image features Xi . The
155   resulting sparse feature representation Xsensor ∈ Rm×c forms the input to a transformer-based
156   decoder. BEVFormer Li et al. (2022b) uses concepts from both: a map-based BEV representation
157   forms queries for a transformer-based multi-view encoder-decoder. Our differentiable map prior
158
      applies to both kids of architecture, albeit with a slightly different fusion architecture.

160   Dense representations quickly become memory-bound and are less suitable for large-scale appli-
161   cations. Similar challenges have been observed for neural scene representations of complete 3D
      scenes Müller et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2022). For 3D scenes, sparse representations Müller et al.

188   4   D IFFERENTIABLE M AP P RIORS
189
190   Our differentiable map prior has two components: A sparse and differentiable map representation
191   builds up a feature representation Xprior of the static scene. A fusion module then splices spatial
192   map features into an existing 3D detection architecture. See Figure 2 for an overview.
193   At training time, we differentiate through the map representation to learn a persistent static map
194   representation that helps the 3D detector improve its accuracy. At inference time, we simply augment
195   the detector with a spatial map prior when available, or fall back to a map-less model in novel areas.
196

256   5.1    DATASET
257
258   We conduct experiments on the nuScenes Caesar et al. (2020) dataset, which consists of 850 scenes
259   (700 training, 150 validation), covering two cities - Boston and Singapore. Each scene is 20 seconds
260   long, with 3D bounding box annotations at 2 Hz for a total of 40 frames per scene. For each frame,
261   we use the six multi-view camera images with their calibrated intrinsics and pose information and
262   ego-pose. A large proportion of the scenes in the dataset have been traversed multiple times, as shown
263   in Figure 5. For these, our map prior natively applies. For scenes without any overlap with training,
264   we fall back onto the baseline algorithm.
265   Metrics. Aligning with the standard 3D detection evaluation methodology, we report mean Average
266   Precision (mAP) across all 10 classes, calculated using ground plane center distance for matching
267   predicted and ground truth results. Additional metrics include five true positive metrics (ATE, ASE,
268   AOE, AVE, AAE) for measuring errors in translation, scale, orientation, velocity, and attributes. The
269   nuScenes Detection Score (NDS) Caesar et al. (2020) is a weighted sum of the mAP and the true
      positive metrics and provides a comprehensive evaluation of a model’s performance.

331   Shown in Table 1, we compare the performance of adding DMP across BEVFormer Li et al. (2022b),
332   BEVDet Huang et al. (2021) and PETR Liu et al. (2022b) on the nuScenes validation set. We

336   We observe that the two BEV-based architectures, BEVFormer and BEVDet, benefit the most from
337   the addition of the prior, with BEVDet showing the largest improvement (relative 13% NDS and 15%
338   mAP). The architectures with explicit spatial BEV representations are likely to benefit more from the
339   prior as the prior features are well aligned with the model’s internal representation. In contrast, the
340   fully transformer architecture (PETR) has to perform additional spatial reasoning to connect the prior
341
      features with its detection queries.
342   Comparison with prior work. We compare DMP to NMP Xiong et al. (2023b), which similarly
343   enhances online map perception with learned priors. NMP uses location information to retrieve a
344   local BEV feature map from external dense storage and fuse it with online sensor features. In their
345   framework, they build separate priors for training/testing. To closer match our setting, where we
346   are interested in improving performance in areas previously traveled, we modify NMP and use the
347
      training prior during evaluation.
348   We apply the adapted method, denoted as NMP*, for 3D object detection and train DMP on for
349   semantic map prediction. The map segmentation task consists of three classes: divider, pedestrian
350   crossing, and road boundaries, and we report mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) across these
351   classes. For this comparison, we use BEVFormer with a slightly smaller spatial resolution of
352   w = h = 100 as the base architecture and use the original detection head along with an extra head
353
      for predicting map segmentation. We train the model jointly with the original detection loss and a
      weighted cross-entropy loss for segmentation.
