009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Incorporating task-specific priors within a policy or network architecture is crucial
012            for enhancing safety and improving representation and generalization in robotic
013            control problems. Differentiable Model Predictive Control (MPC) layers have
014
               proven effective for embedding these priors, such as constraints and cost func-
               tions, directly within the architecture, enabling end-to-end training. However,
015
               current methods often treat the solver and the neural network as separate, indepen-
016
               dent entities, leading to suboptimal integration. In this work, we propose a novel
017            approach that co-develops the solver and architecture unifying the optimization
018            solver and network inference problems. Specifically, we formulate this as a joint
019            fixed-point problem over the coupled network outputs and necessary conditions of
020            the optimization problem. We solve this problem in an iterative manner where we
021            alternate between network forward passes and optimization iterations. Through
022            extensive ablations in various robotic control tasks, we demonstrate that our ap-
023            proach results in richer representations and more stable training, while naturally
024            accommodating warm starting, a key requirement for MPC.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Incorporating task-specific priors within the policy training pipeline is often beneficial when solving
028
      robotic control problems. These priors, which often take the form of auxiliary constraints or cost
029
      functions, give the system designer an additional degree of control and flexibility while designing
030   the system and play a vital role in enhancing safety, improving representation, and boosting general-
031   ization. Previous approaches to policy learning have explored various methods to embed such priors,
032   including reward/loss shaping (Gupta et al., 2022), incorporating constrained optimization layers
033   within the policy inference pipeline (Amos et al., 2018; Agrawal et al., 2020), adding parallel/post-
034   hoc safety checks/controllers (Ames et al., 2019), adversarial training (Schott et al., 2024), and
035   domain randomization (Chen et al., 2021).
036   Differentiable Model Predictive Control (MPC) layers (Amos et al., 2018) have emerged as a
037   promising approach (Shrestha et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2022; Diehl et al., 2023b). This method
038   integrates MPC as a differentiable layer within neural network architectures, allowing for the em-
039   bedding of constraints and cost functions directly into the network architecture, while enabling true
040   end-to-end training of control policies. Importantly, they allow us to preserve the interpretability and
041   safety guarantees associated with traditional MPC while providing a general framework applicable
042   to a diverse range of robotic control problems. Furthermore, it allows for test-time modifications of
043   the MPC problem and facilitates online adaptation, offering increased flexibility and generalizability
044
      – a critical feature in dynamic environments.
045   While offering several advantages, standard differentiable MPC layers often treat the optimization
046   solver as a black-box differentiable layer within the neural network (NN) architecture. This simpli-
047   fication, while convenient, overlooks the unique characteristics of MPC solvers that set them apart
048   from typical NN layers. MPC solvers are implicit layers and hence inherently iterative as opposed to
049   typical explicit layers. They often suffer from ill-conditioning, non-convexities and discontinuities,
050
      potentially leading to unstable training dynamics. Additionally, MPC solvers frequently possess
      specialized structures that enable efficient warm-starting – a valuable property in recurrent control
051
      scenarios that is not fully leveraged in differentiable MPC frameworks.
052
053   To address these limitations, we propose Deep Equilibrium Model Predictive Control (DEQ-MPC),
      a novel approach that unifies the optimization solver and the neural network architecture. Instead

094
      lowing the network to adapt its features/outputs depending on the solver state. Second, it allows
095
      us to naturally compute smoother gradients during training, facilitating more stable and efficient
096   learning. Third, it inherently accommodates warm-starting, leveraging the recurrent nature of MPC
097   to improve computational efficiency and solution quality. DEQ-MPC thus offers a more robust and
098   flexible framework for integrating optimization-based control with deep learning.
099
      The primary contributions of this work are as follows: (1) We introduce DEQ-MPC, a novel frame-
100
      work that seamlessly integrates MPC layers into deep networks. (2) Through extensive ablation stud-
101
      ies, we show that this unified approach results in richer representations, improved gradient flow, and
102   enhanced suitability for warm-starting, compared to standard differentiable MPC methods. (3) We
103   propose a training setup specifically for streaming MPC applications that leverages warm-starting
104   across time steps. (4) We provide empirical evidence demonstrating the advantages of DEQ-MPC
105   on trajectory prediction and tracking problems across various continuous control tasks that require
106   strict constraint satisfaction. While this paper focuses on MPC to ground our methods in a concrete
107   context, we believe that the insights and techniques developed here have broader implications for
      integrating constrained optimization layers into deep networks in a wide range of applications.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Differentiable optimization layers were introduced as a means to embed convex optimization prob-
111   lems (Amos & Kolter, 2017; Agrawal et al., 2019) as differentiable units within deep networks.
112   Recent works have extended the range of optimization and fixed-point problems that can be made
113   differentiable (Gould et al., 2021; Landry et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2020; Pineda et al., 2022). Since
114   their introduction, they have been applied to a variety of robotics problems, such as state estima-
115
      tion (Yi et al., 2021), SLAM (Teed et al., 2023; Lipson et al., 2022), motion planning (Bhardwaj
      et al., 2020; Landry et al., 2019) and control (Amos et al., 2018; Agrawal et al., 2020) for appli-
116
      cations such as autonomous driving (Shrestha et al., 2023; Diehl et al., 2023a), navigation (Xiao
117
      et al., 2022; Diehl et al., 2023b), and manipulation (Landry et al., 2019). We specifically look at
118   differentiable Model Predictive Control problems building off of work such as (Amos et al., 2018;
119   Agrawal et al., 2020), which show how to differentiate through simple trajectory optimization prob-
120   lems and use them as layers within broader differentiable pipelines for tasks such as behavior cloning
121   and system identification. Various follow-up works have demonstrated that policies with differen-
122   tiable optimization layers can be applied more broadly within model-free (Romero et al., 2023) and
123   model-based RL (Wan et al., 2024) pipelines as well. A separate line of work has also explored ex-
124   tending to more complicated trajectory optimization problems differentiable such as problems with
125   cone constraints (Howell et al., 2022), general non-linear programs (Landry et al., 2019).
126   However, incorporating MPC layers (and optimization layers) within deep networks as just another
127   black box differentiable layer can often come with its own set of challenges. The bi-level prob-
128   lem can often be very non-convex resulting in the local gradient direction being mis-aligned with
129   the desired global update direction (Landry et al., 2019; Amos et al., 2018). Likewise, the gradi-
130   ent landscape often has discontinuities resulting in undesirable gradient artifacts (Suh et al., 2022;
131   Antonova et al., 2023). Furthermore, the problem structure can also often result in very high vari-
132
      ance in gradients (Gurumurthy et al., 2024). It’s often challenging to incorporate warm-starting
      techniques as the problem parameters change with each problem instance (Sambharya et al., 2024)
133
      resulting in long inference and solve times. The network predicted constraint parameters can of-
134
      ten be infeasible (Donti et al., 2021), resulting in undefined problem solutions or gradients. Some
135   modelling assumptions in the optimization layer are often not faithful to the real data causing model
136   mismatch problems (Gurumurthy et al., 2023a). Tackling these challenges is critical to making the
137   use of optimization layers in modern deep learning pipelines more practical.
138
      Our proposed method to formulate the network inference and the optimization problem as a joint
139
      equilibrium finding problem seeks to address some of these issues such as representation ability,
140
      gradient discontinuities and suitability for warm-starting. Previous works have also used similar mo-
141   tivations to pose the inference and the optimization problem as a joint equilibrium finding problem
142   albeit for problems without any constraints. Gurumurthy et al. (2021) pose the task of latent vari-
143   able optimization to solve inverse problems as a joint inference and optimization problem over the
144   network outputs and the latent variables. But they primarily look at simple least squares problems.
145   Likewise, Teed & Deng (2021); Teed et al. (2023); Lipson et al. (2022) use a similar framework
146   in the context of SLAM/visual odometry/object pose estimation problems where they specifically
147   look at non-linear least squares, bundle adjustment problems. Our paper generalizes these methods
148   to general constrained optimization problems and grounds it in MPC problems. Furthermore, unlike
149
      previous work, we directly compare with the vanilla differentiable optimization alternative and tease
      apart the specific advantages offered by the joint optimization approach.

175   3.2     D EEP E QUILIBRIUM M ODELS
176   Deep Equilibrium Models (Bai et al., 2019) are a class of implicit deep learning models that compute
177   the output as a solution to a fixed point problem. Specifically, given an input x ∈ X , computing the
178   forward pass in a DEQ model involves finding a fixed point z ∈ Z, such that
179
                                                                                                                                                         \label {eq:deq-layer} z^\star = d_\phi (z^\star , x),                                                                                                                                (3)
180
181   where, dϕ : Z × X → Z is some parameterized layer conditioned on input x, Z denotes the
182   hidden state or outputs of the network which we are computing the fixed point on, X denotes the
183
      input space, and ϕ denotes the parameters of the layer. Computing this fixed point (under proper
      stability conditions) corresponds to the “infinite depth” limit of repeatedly applying the function
184
      z + := dϕ (z, x) starting at some arbitrary initial value of z (typically 0).

293   Augmented Lagrangian gradients. Previous work (Suh et al., 2022; Antonova et al., 2023) has

295   discontinuities in the landscape and have proposed various relaxations to tackle this problems. We
296
      take inspiration from these approaches and propose a relaxation for use with our solver.
297   We compute the gradient through the AL solver using the implicit function theorem equation 2 where
298   the function F(\cdot ) now represents the Lagrangian’s gradient ∇τ Lθ (τ, λ, µ). Thus the IFT gradient is
299

      At convergence, the value of µ is very high. This results in the components of the gradient in the
305   column space of the linearized active constraints getting squished to zero. Thus, when the constraints
306   are non-linear/discontinuous, and the optimizer converges to some arbitrary active sets, the gradients
307   computed using equation 13 are also arbitrary/meaningless.
308
      We instead propose to compute losses on multiple unconverged intermediate iterates along the op-
309
      timizer iterations and minimize all of them during training. Thus, the gradients at the initial opti-
310
      mization iterates are computed with smaller values of the penalty parameter µ while the latter ones
311   are computed with larger values of µ. As a result, the earlier optimization iterates obtain relaxed
312   gradients even when the optimizer converges to arbitrary active sets, while the latter iterates obtain
313   “accurate” gradients as long as the optimizer converges to the “right” active sets. This provides a
314   natural curriculum, where the initial iterates converge to smoothed/relaxed solutions and the latter
315   iterates are then incentiviced to nail down the details.

      4.2.3   WARM - STARTING AND STREAMING
325
326   Warm-starting. MPC problems, like various other optimization problems, benefit from warm-
327   starting (Howell et al., 2019; Le Cleac’h et al., 2024). The resulting speedups from warm-starting are
328   often critical for real-world deployment (Nguyen et al., 2024). In the context of MPC, this involves
329   reusing the converged MPC iterate from the previous time step as initialization for the current solve,
330
      so as to minimize the number of optimizer iterations needed at each time-step. Specifically, the
      MPC problem solving for τt:T +t at time, t, is warm started with the final solution computed at
331
      the previous time-step τ̂t−1:T +t−1 . The initialization for τt:T +t is thus computed by concatenating
332
      τt:T +t = [τ̂t:T +t−1 , τ̂T +t−1 ], where τ̂T +t−1 is assumed to be a reasonable estimate for τT +t .
333
334   The augmented Lagrangian algorithm provides a very convenient way for incorporating the warm-
335   started initialization. We simply initialize τ with the warm-starting estimate, reset the dual variables
336
      λ and η to zeros and set the initial value of ρ = ρmax /10N ∗m−i where (N ∗ m − i) is the total
      number of AL iterations we expect to perform after warm-starting. In standard differentiable-MPC
337
      setups, the network infers the MPC parameters afresh at each successive time step. These parameter
338
      estimates can often be arbitrarily far from the previous estimates, thus requiring a significant number
339   of AL iterations post warm-start. On the other hand, in DEQ-MPC, the network is conditioned on
340   the previous optimizer iterate. This allows us to train the network to predict consistent parameter
341   estimates across time-steps by training it specifically for the streaming setting as described below.
342
343   Streaming training. We customize the training procedure to suit the warm-started streaming
                                                            exp
344
      setup. Given a sampled ground truth trajectory τ0:T       +L , we break the inference problem into a
                                                         exp
345   two step process. First, we solve for τ0:T given x0 as usual without any warm-starting. Then, we
346
      successively solve L problems for τt:T +t for t = 1 . . . N with the iterates warm-started with solu-
      tion from the previous solve, τt−1:T +t−1 . Then we simply compute losses on all the intermediate
347
      optimization iterates (from both steps) and supervise them using the corresponding ground truths as
348
      described in section 4.2.2. For all of our experiments we use L = 2.

354

355   Setup. We use the trajectory prediction and tracking problem, discussed in section 4.2.1, as our
356   default experimental setting. For each task, we generate ground truth trajectories using ’expert’ poli-
357   cies trained with a state-of-the-art on-policy reinforcement learning algorithm (Gurumurthy et al.,

359   trained via supervised learning to predict the next T steps in a trajectory, given the current state as
360   input, as outlined in section 4.2.1. By default, T = 5 for all environments unless otherwise specified.
361   We evaluate the models in two ways. First, when evaluating their effectiveness as a generic op-
362   timization layer within differentiable pipelines, we compare the models based on their validation
363   errors. Second, to evaluate their suitability for the MPC setting, we implement them as feedback
364   policies in the original environment using a receding horizon approach and compute the average
365   returns over 200 rollouts.
366

368   MPC-*) against their corresponding differentiable MPC counterparts (Diff-MPC-*):

370   DEQ-MPC-NN: Our method where the network architecture uses a standard feed forward network.
371
      Diff-MPC-NN: A standard differentiable-MPC setup where a standard feedforward network pre-
372
      dicts the MPC problem parameters (waypoints) in one shot, which are then used to solve the MPC
373   problem. The loss is computed at the converged iterate and backpropagated using IFT.
374
      Diff-MPC-DEQ: This uses the same setup as Diff-MPC-NN except that the network architecture is
375
      replaced with a DEQ.

      as the network takes the current system state as input and predicts the future T states to be tracked.

519   6   D ISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
520
521   Discussion. Our experimental results highlight several key advantages of DEQ-MPC over differ-
522   entiable MPC layers. The performance gap between DEQ-MPC variants and Diff-MPC becomes
523
      increasingly apparent as task complexity increases, whether through harder constraints, longer plan-
      ning horizons, or increased problem sensitivity. A particularly promising aspect of DEQ-MPC is
524
      its favorable scaling behavior. Unlike Diff-MPC variants which show signs of performance satu-
525
      ration, DEQ-MPC models continue to improve with increasing dataset size and network capacity.
526   This suggests potential for exploiting scaling laws in robotics applications. Furthermore, DEQ-
527   MPC’s effectiveness in warm-starting scenarios, requiring fewer augmented Lagrangian iterations
528   while maintaining performance, offers significant practical advantages for real-world deployment.
529   Interestingly, there exist trade-offs even between the DEQ-MPC variants. While DEQ-MPC-NN
530   performs slightly better on average, DEQ-MPC-DEQ remains stable across a wider range of condi-
531   tions compared to DEQ-MPC-NN, suggesting a trade-off between performance and stability.
532
      Limitations and future work. Several important directions remain for future work. While our
533
      method is designed to be general, our current evaluation focuses primarily on trajectory tracking
534   problems. Exploring the applicability of DEQ-MPC to a broader class of MPC and constrained
535   optimization problems, both within and beyond robotics, would be valuable. Additionally, inves-
536   tigating whether the representational richness of DEQ-MPC can be leveraged effectively beyond
537   the imitation learning setup such as in reinforcement learning settings to directly learn constrained
538   optimal policies could be a promising line of future work. Finally, given the strong performance in
539   constraint handling, exploring DEQ-MPC in safety-critical scenarios such as human-robot interac-
      tion settings with dynamic obstacles would be an interesting direction for future research.

      A.2           DEQ NETWORK GRADIENTS
736
737
      Computing gradients through the fixed point iteration in a DEQ model typically requires using the
738   implicit function theorem equation 2, which involves computing a linear system solve. However,
739   recent work (Geng et al., 2021; Fung et al., 2021) has shown that the approximations of the gradient
740   by simply assuming an identity Jacobian or differentiating through the last few iterations of the fixed
741   point iteration using vanilla backpropagation is equally/more effective while being computationally
742   cheaper. We adopt this approach. Specifically, we run the function a couple more times after com-
743   puting the fixed point, and simply backpropagate through those last couple of iterations to compute
744   the parameter gradients.

779   We provide the details on the network architecture of the DEQ model and the feedforward network.
780   Inputs. For the DEQ-MPC variants, we have 2 inputs: (x0 , the initial state and xi1:T , the current
781   state estimates from the optimizer). For the Diff-MPC variants, we only get x0 as input. But we
782   repeat it T times and concatenate it ([x0 ]∗T to obtain a temporal input that can be fed to the temporal
783   convolutional network described below.
784   Feedforward network. We use a Temporal Convolution Network architecture. We first compute
785   input embeddings for the trajectory by computing a node embedding at each time-step with a node
786   encoder (Linear-LayerNorm-ReLU). We then concatenate the node embedding of x0 to all time-
787   steps and a corresponding time embedding to indicate their respective time-steps. These are then
788   passed through a series of four temporal convolution residual blocks (Conv1D-GroupNorm-ReLU)
789   before computing the output with a final temporal Conv1D layer. The output at each time step
790
      represents the δxt = xt −x0 . More details are available in the code attached with the supplementary.
791   DEQ network. We again use a Temporal Convolution Network architecture. We have three sepa-
792   rate blocks here, namely, input injection layer I, fixed point layer d and output layer g. We compute
793   the forward pass by first computing the fixed point on the latents:
794

796   and then compute the outputs using gϕ (z ∗ ). The input injection layer is similar to the feedforward

814   A.5   N OTE ON CONVERGENCE
815
816   Our treatment of the joint system as a DEQ allows us to borrow results from Winston & Kolter
817   (2020)Bai et al. (2021) to ensure convergence of the fixed point iteration. Specifically, if we as-
818   sume the joint Jacobian of the ADMM fixed point iteration is strongly monotone with smoothness
819   parameter m and Lipschitz constant L, then by standard arguments (see e.g., Section 5.1 of (Ryu
820   & Boyd, 2016)), the fixed point iteration with step size α < m/L2 will converge. However, going
821
      from the strong monotonicity assumption on the joint fixed point iterations to specific assumptions
      on the network or the optimization problem is less straightforward. But, in practice a wide suite of
822
      techniques have been used to ensure that such fixed points exist and can be found using relatively
823
      few fixed point iterations. In fact, for all of our experiments, we converge within 6 ADMM iterations
824   once trained.
