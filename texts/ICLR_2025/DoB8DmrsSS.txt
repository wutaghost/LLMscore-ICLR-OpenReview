009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning (RL) systems, while achieving remarkable success across
012            various domains, are vulnerable to adversarial attacks. This is especially a concern
013            in vision-based environments where minor manipulations of high-dimensional
014            image inputs can easily mislead the agent’s behavior. To this end, various defenses
015            have been proposed recently, with state-of-the-art approaches achieving robust per-
016            formance even under large state perturbations. Upon closer investigation, however,
017            we found that the effectiveness of the current defenses is due to a fundamental
018
               weakness of the existing lp -norm constrained attacks, which can barely alter the
               semantics of the input even under a relatively large perturbation budget. In this
019
               work, we propose SHIFT, a novel diffusion-based state perturbation attack to go
020
               beyond this limitation. Specifically, we train a history-conditioned diffusion model,
021            enhanced with policy guidance and realism detection to generate perturbed states
022            that are semantically different from the true states while remaining realistic and
023            history-aligned to avoid detection. Evaluations show that our attack effectively
024            breaks existing defenses, including the most sophisticated ones, and significantly
025            lowers the agent’s cumulative reward in various Atari games by more than 50%.
026            The results highlight the vulnerability of RL agents to semantics-aware adversarial
027            perturbations, indicating the importance of developing more robust policies for
028            safety-critical domains.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Reinforcement learning (RL) has seen significant advancements in recent years, becoming a key area
      of machine learning. RL’s ability to enable agents to learn optimal decision-making policies through
034
      interaction with dynamic environments has led to breakthroughs in various fields. Beginning from
035
      AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), RL-based systems show the ability to surpass human performance
036   in complex games. Beyond gaming, RL is driving innovations in robotics, self-driving cars (Kiran
037   et al., 2020), and industrial automation, where agents learn to navigate, manipulate, and interact
038   autonomously.
039
      However, RL is vulnerable to various types of attacks, such as reward and state perturbations, action
040
      space manipulations, and model inference and poisoning (Ilahi et al., 2020). Recent studies have
041
      shown that an RL agent can be manipulated by perturbing its observation (Huang et al., 2017; Zhang
042   et al., 2020a) and reward signals (Huang & Zhu, 2019), and a well-trained RL agent can be confounded
043   by a malicious opponent behaving unexpectedly (Gleave et al., 2020). In particular, a malicious
044   agent can subtly manipulate the observations of a trained RL agent, resulting in a significant drop
045   in performance and cumulative reward (Zhang et al., 2020a; Sun et al., 2021). Such attacks exploit
046   vulnerabilities in the agent’s perception systems, including sensors and communication channels,
047   without needing to cause obvious disruptions. This susceptibility to minor perturbations raises major
048   concerns, particularly for RL applications in security-sensitive and safety-critical environments.
049   There are several defenses trying to mitigate state perturbation attacks. SA-MDP (Zhang et al., 2020a)
050   points out adding a regularization term in the loss function during the training stage can help train a
051   smoother and more robust policy. WocaR-MDP (Liang et al., 2022) further improves this method by
052   estimating a worst case reward under perturbation during training. ATLA (Zhang et al., 2021) trains
053   the agent’s policy and attacker’s policy alternatively, utilizing the fact that when the agent’s policy is
      fixed, finding the optimal attack policy is a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and can be solved by


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      RL. This method can derive a more robust agent policy but suffers from a prohibitive computational
055   cost when the environment’s state space is large, such as in Atari games with raw pixels as input.
056   More recently, CAR-DQN (Li et al., 2024) shows that a variant of DQN using the Bellman Infinity
057   error can further improve the policy’s robustness. In addition to the pure training stage approaches
058   mentioned above, diffusion models have been utilized to either recover the true state (YANG &
059   Xu, 2024) or generate a belief about the true state (Sun & Zheng, 2024) from perturbed states to
060   further improve robustness. In particular, DP-DQN (Sun & Zheng, 2024) derives a robust policy
061   against large perturbations by integrating a pessimistically trained Q-function and diffusion-based
062   belief modeling. State-of-the-art attacks such as PGD (Zhang et al., 2020a), MinBest (Huang et al.,
063
      2017), PA-AD (Sun et al., 2021), and the high-sensitivity direction attacks (Korkmaz, 2023) cannot
      compromise these more advanced defenses.
064
065   However, we found that current attacks share two major shortcomings when applied to environments
066   with raw pixel images as input as in the case of Atari games. First, with the exception of Korkmaz
067   (2023), current attacks usually restrict a perturbed state to be within an ϵ-ball of the true state, mea-
068   sured using an lp norm, to avoid detection, which constrains the attacker’s search space for generating
069
      semantics changing perturbations. Although the high-sensitivity direction attacks in Korkmaz (2023)
      are able to go beyond the lp norm constraint, they mainly target changes in visually significant but
070
      non-essential semantics (see our evaluation results in Appendix E). Second, they focus on improving
071
      attack performance while ignoring the temporal dependencies across states. For example, PGD uses
072   gradient descent to generate noise, PA-AD uses RL to find the best perturbations, and Korkmaz
073   (2023) utilizes high-sensitive directions, without considering the history. Thus, these attacks cannot
074   easily modify the essential semantics of the image input while keeping it realistic and plausible.
075   Consequently, the perturbed states generated by these attacks can be easily denoised with the help of
076   a history-conditioned diffusion model, so they fail against those diffusion-based defenses.
077
      With these two shortcomings in mind, we propose SHIFT(Stealthy History alIgned diFfusion aTtack),
078   a novel semantics-aware attack method that goes beyond the traditional lp norm constraint. Our
079   approach generates effective attacks that are consistent with the physical rules of the environment
080   and the agent’s previous observations to avoid detection by both humans and AI. In particular, we
081   utilize a diffusion model with classifier-free guidance to approximate history-aligned state generation,
082   which is further improved using classifier guidance to generate effective and realistic perturbed
083   images. SHIFT can break all known defenses and significantly lower agents’ cumulative reward
084   in various Atari games. Our results highlight that RL agents with image input are vulnerable to
085   semantics-aware adversarial perturbations, which has important implications when deploying them in
086
      sensitive domains. As a preliminary defense strategy, we show that a diffusion-based approach can
      significantly improve robustness when the agent can occasionally probe and observe the true states.

088   2     P RELIMINARY
089
090   2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (RL)
091
092   A reinforcement learning environment can be formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP),
093   usually denoted as a tuple ⟨S, A, P, R, γ, ρ0 ⟩, where S is the state space and A is the action space.
094   P : S × A → ∆(S) is the transition function of the MDP, where P (s′ |s, a) denotes the probability of
095   moving to state s′ given the current state s and action a. R : S × A → R is the reward function where
096
      R(s, a) = E(Rt |st−1 = s, at−1 = a) and Rt is the reward in time step t. Finally, γ is the discount
      factor and ρ0 is the initial state distribution. An RL agent wants to maximize its cumulative reward
097
      G = ΣTt=0 γ t Rt over a time horizon T ∈ Z+ ∪ {∞}, by finding a (stationary) policy π : S → ∆(A),
098
      which can be either deterministic or stochastic. For any policy π, the state-value and action-value
099   functions are two standard ways to measure how good π is. For MDPs with a finite or countably
100   infinite state space and a finite action space, there is a deterministic and stationary policy that is
101   simultaneously optimal for all initial states s. For large and continuous state and action spaces, deep
102   reinforcement learning (DRL) incorporates the powerful approximation capacity of deep learning
103   into RL and has found notable applications in various domains.
104
105   2.2   S TATE P ERTURBATION ATTACKS IN RL
106
107   First introduced in Huang et al. (2017), a state perturbation attack is a test-stage attack targeting an
      RL agent with a well-trained policy π. We consider the worst-case scenario where the attacker has


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      access to a clean environment and the victim’s policy π and any other deployed defense mechanisms.
109   Further, the attacker has access to the true states at real-time.
110
111   At each time step, the attacker observes the true state st and generates a perturbed state s̃t . The
112
      agent, however, only observes s̃t (and not st ) and takes an action at based on its policy π(·|s̃t ). It is
      important to note that the attacker only interferes with the agent’s observed state and does not modify
113
      the underlying MDP. Consequently, the true state at the next time step is governed by the transition
114
      dynamics P (st+1 |st , π(·|s̃t )).
115
116   Common attack objectives include minimizing the agent’s long-term cumulative reward or enforcing
117   it to take a sequence of target actions chosen by the attacker. In this work, we focus on the second
118
      objective where at each time step t, the attacker aims to generate a perturbed state s̃t such that the
      agent would have a higher chance to select an attacker-specified action āt , rather than the default
119
      action π(·|st ), by altering the semantic meaning of the true state st . Note that the two objectives are
120
      closely related as the set of target actions can be chosen to minimize the victim’s long-term return.
121   However, as we show in the evaluation results, even if the target actions are chosen myopically, and
122   only a moderate portion of them are successfully enforced, our attack can lead to a significant loss in
123   long-term return, even in the presence of strong defenses.
124
      Further, the attacker needs to remain stealthy to avoid immediate detection and achieve its long-term
125
      goal. To this end, previous state perturbation attacks (Zhang et al., 2020a; Sun et al., 2021) restrict
126
      the attacker’s ability by a budget ϵ, so that s̃t ∈ Bϵ (st ) where Bϵ (st ) is the lp ball centered at st for
127   some norm p (typically an l∞ norm is used). However, state-of-the-art diffusion-based defenses (Sun
128   & Zheng, 2024) are able to mitigate the restricted attack even with a large ϵ. Thus, in this work,
129   we consider a novel attack similar to unrestricted adversarial examples in the supervised learning
130   setting (Song et al., 2018) by removing this constraint. Instead, we measure the stealthiness of attacks
131   using more intuitive and practical measures on the realism and history-alignment of the perturbed
132   states, as discussed below. A detailed discussion on related work can be found in Appendix B.

166   3     SHIFT - S TEALTHY H ISTORY A LIGNED D IFFUSION ATTACK
167
168   In this section, we introduce SHIFT, which is a novel state perturbation attack built upon diffusion
169   models that combines the classifier-free and classifier guidance methods. Below, we first discuss the
170
      motivation for using diffusion models to generate perturbed states, where we also formulate the attack
      objectives by giving a novel characterization of a realistic, semantics-aware, and history-aligned
171
      attack, from a static view and a dynamic view, respectively (Section 3.1). We then discuss how to
172
      achieve these goals in Section 3.2.
173
174
      3.1   M OTIVATIONS AND ATTACK O BJECTIVES
175
176   State-of-the-art perturbation attacks against image input (such as PGD, MinBest, and PA-AD) are
177   performed by adding lp -norm constrained noise to the input. Consequently, the perturbed states often
178   fall outside the set of states that can be generated by the underlying MDP (determined by the game
179   engine in Atari games). Consider the snapshots from the Atari Pong game shown in Figure 1, where
                                                                              15
180   Figure 1c generated by the PGD attack with an attack budget ϵ = 255        under l∞ norm can be easily
181   distinguished from the true state in Figure 1b. On the other hand, smaller perturbations are ineffective
182   in manipulating the agent’s actions, especially in the presence of strong defenses. The main reason is
183   that these attacks typically cannot alter the semantic meaning of the original state. As demonstrated
                                                                     15
184
      in Figure 1c, even with a relatively large attack budget (ϵ = 255 ), the pong ball and the paddles in the
      game maintain their positions.
185
186   Our objective is to go beyond the current lp norm-constrained attacks to generate more powerful
187   and stealthy state perturbations. To this end, a key observation is that a carefully designed diffusion
188   model can enable more effective attacks by generating semantics-changing state perturbations (e.g.,
189   Figure 1e) to mislead the victim to choose a target action āt that differs from the desired action,
190
      leading to significant performance loss. However, a naive application of diffusion models may lead to
      unrealistic output that violates the physical rules, as shown in Figure 1d with two balls in the Pong
191
      game and Figure 1i with three chickens across lanes in the Freeway game.
192
193   To generate realistic perturbed states that are semantically different from original states, we introduce
194   the following definitions.
195   Definition 1 (Valid States). The set of valid states S ∗ of an MDP ⟨S, A, P, R, γ, ρ0 ⟩ is defined as:
196   S ∗ := {s ∈ S | ∃π ∈ Π, dπ (s) > 0}, where Π denotes the set of all possible (stationary) policies
197   and dπ (s) represents the stationary state distribution under policy π, from the initial distribution ρ0 .
198
199
      In other words, S ∗ consists of all states that can be reached by following an arbitrary policy π from
      the initial distribution ρ0 . However, ensuring strict validity is intractable with limited amount of data
200
      (as in the case of diffusion models). Thus, we introduce the concept of realistic states as a more
201
      practical measure, based on the projection distance between a state s and the set of valid states S ∗ .
202
203   Definition 2 (Realistic States). A state s is defined as realistic if its projection distance to the set
204
      of valid states S ∗ is bounded by a threshold δ. Formally, the set of realistic states S r is defined as:
      S r := {s ∈ S | ∥ProjS ∗ (s) − s∥2 ≤ δ}, where ProjS ∗ (s) = arg mins′ ∈S ∗ ∥s′ − s∥2 is the projection
205
      of s onto S ∗ , and δ is a predefined threshold.
206
207   We consider realistic states to be indistinguishable from valid states and pose realism as an objective
208   of our approach. With the above definition, we further define a perturbed state s̃ to be semantically
209   different from the original state if it satisfies the following condition.
210   Definition 3 (Semantics-Changing States). A perturbed state s̃ is considered to be semantically
211   different from the true state s when ProjS ∗ (s̃) ̸= s.
212
213   The definition states that a perturbed state s̃ changes the semantic meaning of the true state s when
214   its projection point to the valid set ProjS ∗ (s) differs from s. For example, the perturbed states in
215   Figures 1d and 1e are semantically different from the true state in Figure 1b as they both changed
      the location of the pong ball. As discussed in the next section and validated in the experiments, our

      3.2     D IFFUSION - BASED STATE PERTURBATIONS
264
265   In this section, we discuss SHIFT, our diffusion-based attack that can generate state perturbations to
266   meet the three objectives defined above: semantics changing (towards the target action), realism, and
267   approximate historical alignment. Our attack consists of two stages: the training stage and the testing
268   stage. During the training stage, we use data generated by the clean environment (i.e., the MDP)
269   to train a conditional diffusion model to generate states that are realistic and history-aligned. We
      further train an autoencoder to detect unrealistic states. In the testing stage, we employ the pretrained


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      diffusion model to generate perturbed states guided by (1) the defender’s policy, which provides
271   guidance toward the target action, and (2) the pre-trained autoencoder, which further enhances the
272   realism of the perturbed states. Figure D.1 in the appendix illustrates the two stages of our attack and
273   the main components involved, with each discussed in detail below.
274
      While diffusion models have been utilized to generate adversarial examples in the supervised learning
275
      setting (see Appendix B.4 for a review), their application in adversarial state perturbations in RL
276
      has not been considered before. We remark that our problem can be viewed as sampling from a
277   diffusion model with constraints on realism and history alignment. However, existing approaches
278   for constrained diffusion (Christopher et al., 2024) cannot be directly applied to our setting as they
279   require constraints such as physical rules to be explicitly given and easily evaluable. In our setting, it
280   is difficult to identify the projection onto the valid states S ∗ , making these approaches less suitable.
281
282   3.2.1    G ENERATING H ISTORY-A LIGNED S TATES VIA C ONDITIONAL D IFFUSION
283
284   We start with a description of training the conditional diffusion model, which is built upon the
285
      classifier-free guidance approach (Ho & Salimans, 2022) that can generate both unconditional and
      conditional samples, enabling the model to guide itself during the generation process.
286
287   We train a classifier-free guidance model conditioned on a history to generate the next state s̃t that
288   follows the given history. This ensures that the generated next state s̃t is realistic and aligned with the
289   history, such that s̃t is stealthy from both the static and dynamic views. It is important to note that the
290   true next state st is independent of the victim’s policy π when the history (including previous states
291
      and actions) is given. Thus, we can train this diffusion model with classifier-free guidance without
      requiring knowledge of the specific victim’s policy π. However, a separate diffusion model needs to
292
      be trained for each distinct MDP environment.
293
294   Specifically, let τt−1 = {st−1 , at−1 , ..., st−k , at−k } be the true history from time t − k to time t,
295   where k is a parameter. In our setting, the model is trained with both class-conditional data (st , τt−1 )
296   and unconditional data st by randomly dropping τt−1 with a certain probability. The history τt−1
297
      and true state st are sampled from trajectories generated in a clean environment by following a
      well-trained policy πref (independent of the agent’s policy) with exploration to ensure coverage.
298
      The noise prediction network ϵθ (sit , i, τt−1 ) is trained to learn both conditional and unconditional
299
      distributions during the training. When generating perturbed states at the testing stage, where the
300
      reverse process is applied, the noise prediction can be adjusted using a guidance scale Γ(i) as follows:
301
302                                ϵi = Γ(i)ϵθ (sit , i, τt−1 ) + (1 − Γ(i))ϵθ (sit , i),                      (3)
303
      where τt−1 is the given history, and Γ(i) controls the strength of the guidance. Note that we have two
304
      time step variables here, where t is the time step in an RL episode and i is the index of the reverse
305
      steps in the reverse process. Also, our attack uses the true history τt−1 to approximate the victim’s
306   belief Ht−1 , as the latter requires projecting each perturbed state onto S ∗ and is computationally
307   expensive. An interesting future direction is to incorporate the actual agent’s belief modeling into
308   diffusion-based generation to further improve our attack.
309

332   3.2.2    G ENERATING S EMANTICS C HANGING P ERTURBATIONS VIA P OLICY G UIDANCE
333
      A perturbed state s̃t that is solely generated by the history-conditioned guidance discussed above is
334
      not able to manipulate the victim’s action towards the target action, especially when an advanced
335   defense method is deployed. To achieve our first attack objective of manipulating the victim’s action,
336   we introduce a classifier guidance module at the testing stage that can change the semantic meaning
337   of the true state st . Classifier guidance is a method to improve the quality of samples generated by
338   a diffusion model by incorporating class-conditional information (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). The
339   core idea is to utilize a pre-trained classifier pΦ (y|x), where y represents the class label, to guide the
340   reverse diffusion process toward generating samples conditioned on a desired class. In our context,
341   we can treat the victim’s policy π as a classifier when the action space is discrete, which is the case
342   for all Atari games considered in our evaluations.
343   Specifically, at each reverse time step i of our pre-trained conditional diffusion model, the reverse pro-
344   cess is modified by adjusting the mean of the noise prediction model ϵi with the gradient of the policy
345   with respect to s̃it given the target action āt , that is, ∇s̃it log π(āt |s̃it ). This guidance steers the genera-
346   tion process towards samples that are more likely to induce the victim to select the attacker-specified
347   action āt , which deviates from the victim’s default action π(st ), ultimately achieving the first attack
348   objective and causing victim’s performance loss at the same time. As shown in (Dhariwal & Nichol,
349   2021), for the unconditional reverse transition pθ in        (1), the modified reverse process with classifier

368   3.2.3    E NHANCING R EALISM VIA AUTOENCODER G UIDANCE
369
      Since the classifier guidance method introduces additional gradient information during the reverse
370
      process, the generated perturbed state s̃t may be less realistic. For example, in Figure 1d, two balls
371
      are simultaneously present in the Pong game, which alters the semantic meaning of the scene but
372   is easily detectable by humans or automated anomaly detection tools. To mitigate this issue and
373   improve the realism of the perturbed states, we incorporate an autoencoder-based anomaly detector
374   trained on clean data. Autoencoders (Zhou & Paffenroth, 2017) are commonly used in unsupervised
375   and semi-supervised anomaly detection tasks. They detect anomalies by measuring the reconstruction
376   error, which is the difference between the input and its reconstruction. Since autoencoders are trained
377   on normal data, they produce significantly higher reconstruction errors for anomalous inputs, as they
      have not learned to effectively encode or reconstruct these outliers.


                                                                 7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      In our approach, the attacker leverages training data sampled from a clean environment to train an
379   autoencoder AE(·) consisting of an encoder Eϕ (·) and a decoder Dψ (·), with parameters ϕ and
380   ψ, to detect unrealistic perturbed states during the testing stage. We define the reconstruction loss
381   L as the l2 distance between a state st and the reconstructed state AE(st ) = Dψ (Eϕ (st )), that is
382   L(st , AE(st )) = ∥st − AE(st )∥2 , and train the autoencoder AE(·) to minimize the average l2 loss
383   over the training set. Since input semantics vary significantly across environments, we need to train
384   separate autoencoders for different environments. The pre-trained autoencoder is then used at the
385   testing stage to improve the realism of the perturbed states, following the logic of classifier guidance.
386   In particular, the attacker performs gradient descent at the end of each reverse step i, using the gradient
387
      of the reconstruction error with respect to s̃it , to improve the realism of the generated state. This can
      be formulated as: s̃it = s̃it − ∇s̃it L(s̃it , AE(s̃it )), where AE(s̃it ) represents the reconstructed sample,
388
389   and L(s̃it , AE(s̃it )) is the reconstruction error. This process ensures that the generated perturbed state
390
      s̃t more closely aligns with realistic states, thus further supporting our second attack objective.
391
392   3.2.4    I MPLEMENTATION
393
      Although our theoretical analysis in Section 3.2.2 is based on the DDPM method to simplify the
394   discussion, we implement our attack method using the EDM formulation proposed in (Karras et al.,
395   2022). EDM is a score-based diffusion method that efficiently guides the model through fewer
396   reverse diffusion steps compared to DDPM, significantly reducing sampling time and allowing for
397   real-time attacks in the testing stage. Previous work (Alonso et al., 2024) has successfully applied
398   EDM based conditional diffusion model to world modeling of Atari environments, and we build our
399   work upon their code. For the classifier-guidance implementation in the EDM model, we follow
400   the technique in Ma et al. (2024) to weight joint and conditional guidance by two separate logits
401   temperature parameters ζ1 and ζ2 , which improves the generated sample quality. We further apply
402
      another technique used in (Bansal et al., 2024) to enhance the effectiveness of the classifier-guidance
      method. At each reverse step i, we first calculate the proposed output sample ŝt based on the current
403
      state s̃it through the diffusion model without any attacks. Then we calculate the gradient of the
404
      victim’s policy to guide the reverse process. These two techniques allow us to optimize the classifier
405   guidance process more effectively, ensuring high-quality perturbed states that better satisfy the attack
406   objectives. Details on the EDM formulation and implementation are provided in Appendix D, where
407   both the training and the testing stage algorithms are given in Appendix D.5. In particular, the
408   training of the history-conditioned EDM model is given in Algorithm 1. The implementation of the
409   sampling process with both policy and realism guidance incorporated is given in Algorithm 2, where
410   the autoencoder-based realism guidance appears in the last part of the algorithm.

526   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
527
528   We introduce a novel diffusion-based state perturbation attack for reinforcement learning (RL) sys-
529
      tems that extends beyond the traditional lp -norm constraints. By leveraging conditional diffusion
      models, policy guidance, and realism enhancement techniques, we generate highly effective, seman-
530
      tically distinct, and stealthy attacks that cause a significant reduction in cumulative rewards across
531
      multiple Atari environments. Our results underscore the urgent need for more sophisticated defense
532   mechanisms to effectively mitigate semantic uncertainties.
533
534   However, there are some limitations in our current attack method. First, the target action selection is
535
      myopic. A potential improvement could involve designing a joint planning-diffusion approach that
      determines target actions in a non-myopic manner. However, this requires evaluating the manipulation
536
      success rate of the diffusion model, which is computationally expensive. Second, although our method
537
      does not require training on separate defense policies, we still need to train individual conditional
538   diffusion models for different environments. A promising direction is to enhance the transferability of
539   the attack enabling a single conditional diffusion model to be effective across various environments.

      A     B ROADER I MPACTS
706
      Our work introduces SHIFT, a novel approach to perturbation attacks in reinforcement learning (RL)
707
      by altering the semantics of the true states while remaining stealthy from both static and dynamic
708
      perspectives. SHIFT demonstrates outstanding performance, successfully compromising all state-of-
709   the-art defense methods. This highlights the urgent need for more sophisticated defense mechanisms
710   that are resilient to semantic uncertainties.
711
712
      This research raises important safety concerns, as adversaries could exploit these semantic-changing
      attacks to cause significant harm in real-world RL applications, such as autonomous driving. The
713
      ability to alter the perception of critical systems like self-driving cars could lead to catastrophic
714
      consequences. As such, our findings underscore the necessity of further research into robust defenses
715   capable of withstanding such advanced and subtle attack strategies.
716

718   B     R ELATED W ORK
719
720
      B.1    S TATE P ERTURBATION ATTACKS AND D EFENSES
721
      State perturbation attacks on RL policies were first introduced in Huang et al. (2017), where the
722   MinBest attack was proposed to minimize the probability of selecting the best action by iteratively
723   adding lp -norm constrained noise calculated through −∇s̃t π(π(·|st ), s̃t ). Building on this, Zhang
724   et al. (2020a) showed that when the agent’s policy is fixed, finding the optimal adversarial policy can
725   be framed as an MDP, and the attacker can find the optimal attack policy by applying RL techniques.
726   This was further improved in (Sun et al., 2021), where a more efficient algorithm for finding optimal
727   attacks, called PA-AD, was introduced. Instead of searching perturbed states in the original state space,
728   PA-AD trained a director through RL to find the optimal attack direction, and the trained director
729   directs the designed actor to generate perturbed states, which decreases the searching space of RL.
730
      More recently, illusory attack (Franzmeyer et al., 2024) is proposed by requiring a perturbed trajectory
      to follow the same distribution as the normal trajectory, making it difficult to detect. However, this
731
      approach does not scale to high-dimensional image input. Korkmaz (2023) recognized the limitation
732
      of lp norm constrained attacks and proposed a policy-independent attack by following high sensitive
733   directions, leading to attacks such as changing brightness and contrast, image blurring, image rotation
734   and image shifting. These types of attacks are imperceptible when the amount of manipulation
735   applied is small and can compromise SA-MDP defense. However, they can barely alter the essential
736   semantics of image input according to our Definition 3. For example, in the Pong game, the pong
737   ball’s relative distance from the two pads will remain the same after changing brightness and contrast
738   or shifting the image. Thus, as shown in Table 8 and Table 9, diffusion based defenses can protect the
739   agent under these attacks.
740   On the defense side, Zhang et al. (2020a) demonstrated that a universally optimal policy under
741   state perturbations might not always exist. They proposed a set of regularization-based algorithms
742   (SA-DQN, SA-PPO, SA-DDPG) to train robust RL agents. This was enhanced in (Liang et al., 2022),
743   where a worst-case Q-network and state importance weights were incorporated into the regularization.
744   A more recent work called CAR-DQN (Li et al., 2024) shows using an l∞ norm can further improve
745   the policy’s robustness, and they theoretically capture the optimal robust policy (ORP) under ϵ
746   constrained state perturbation attacks, although this method incurs high computational costs. Another
747
      line of work by Xiong et al. (2023) proposed an autoencoder-based detection and denoising framework
      to identify and correct perturbed states. Korkmaz & Brown-Cohen (2023) proposed SO-INRD, which
748
      uses the local curvature of the cross-entropy loss between the action distribution π(a|s) and a targeted
749
      action distribution to detect adversarial directions. He et al. (2023b) showed that when the initial
750   state distribution is known, it is possible to find a policy that optimizes the expected return under state
751   perturbations. Diffusion-based defenses have also been utilized to generate more robust agent policies.
752   DMBP (YANG & Xu, 2024) utilized a conditional diffusion model to recover actual states from
753   perturbed states and Sun & Zheng (2024) used the diffusion model as a purification tool to generate
754   a belief set about the actual state and perform a pessimistic training to generate a robust policy. More
755   recently, a game-theoretical defense method (Grad) (Liang et al., 2024) was proposed to address
      temporally coupled attacks by modeling the temporally coupled robust RL problem as a partially


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      observable zero-sum game and deriving approximate equilibrium of the game. Another important
757   recent defense is PROTECTED (Liu et al., 2024) that iteratively searches for a set of non-dominated
758   policies during training and adapts these policies during testing to address different attacks. However,
759   both Grad (Liang et al., 2024) and PROTECTED (Liu et al., 2024) focus on MuJoCo environments
760   and are already computationally intensive (both take more than 20 hours) to train on the relatively
761   simple environments. Without further adaptation, it will be computationally prohibitive to apply these
762   two methods to Atari environments with image input.
763
764   B.2   ATTACKS AND D EFENSES B EYOND S TATE P ERTURBATIONS
765
766   As demonstrated by (Huang & Zhu, 2019), altering the reward signal can significantly disrupt the
767   training process of Q-learning, causing the agent to adopt a policy that aligns with the attacker’s
768   objectives. Additionally, (Zhang et al., 2020b) introduced an adaptive reward poisoning technique
769   that can induce a harmful policy in a number of steps that scales polynomially with the size of the
770   state space |S| in the tabular setting. In a similar vein, Zhang et al. (2020b) developed an adaptive
771   reward poisoning method capable of achieving a malicious policy in polynomial steps based on the
772   size of the state space |S|.
773   Moving beyond reward manipulation, Lee et al. (2020) proposed two techniques for perturbing the
774   action space. Among them, the Look-Ahead Action Space (LAS) method was found to deliver better
775   performance in reducing cumulative rewards in deep reinforcement learning by distributing attacks
776   across both the action and temporal dimensions. Another line of research focuses on adversarial
777   policies within multi-agent environments. For example, (Gleave et al., 2020) showed that in a
778   zero-sum game, a player using an adversarial policy can easily beat an opponent using a well-trained
779
      policy.
780   Attacks targeting an RL agent’s policy network have also been explored. Inference attacks, as
781   described by (Chen et al., 2021), aim to steal the policy network parameters. On the other hand,
782   poisoning attacks, as discussed in (Huai et al., 2020), focus on directly manipulating the model
783   parameters. Specifically, Huai et al. (2020) proposed an optimization-based method to identify an
784   optimal strategy for poisoning the policy network.
785
786   B.3   D IFFUSION M ODELS AND RL
787
788   Diffusion models have recently been utilized to solve RL problems by exploiting their state-of-the-art
789   sample generation ability. In particular, diffusion models have been utilized to generate high quality
790   offline data in solving offline RL problems. Offline RL training is known as a data-sensitive process,
791   where the quality of the data has a huge influence on the training result. To deal with this problem,
792
      many studies (He et al., 2023a; Ajay et al., 2022; Janner et al., 2022) have shown that diffusion
      models can learn from a demo dataset and then generate high reward trajectories for learning or
793
      planning purposes. In addition, conditional diffusion models have been directly used to model RL
794
      policies. A conditional diffusion model can generate actions through a denoising process with states
795   and other useful information as conditions. Several studies (Kang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022)
796   have shown state-of-the-art performance in various offline RL environments when using a diffusion
797   model as a policy, which leads to a promising research direction.
798
      Furthermore, Black et al. (2023) shows that the denoising process can be viewed as a Markov Decision
799
      Process (MDP). Thus, Black et al. (2023) trains a diffusion model with the help of RL by maximizing
800
      a user-specific reward function, which connects the generative models and optimization methods.
801
802
803   B.4   D IFFUSION M ODELS IN A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES
804
805   Diffusion models have recently gained significant attention in generating adversarial examples due to
806
      their superb performances. They can generate high-quality adversarial examples that deceive target
      classifiers while remaining imperceptible to human observers.
807
808   Since the images generated by diffusion models inherently lack adversarial effects, a widely used
809   approach is to use diffusion models along with existing methods of generating adversarial examples.
      The idea is to combine the generated samples from the diffusion model with perturbed samples from


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      other attack methods such as PGD attacks during the attack process to generate high quality and
811   imperceptible adversarial examples (Xue et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b).
812
813   Another promising direction is to use a (surrogate) classifier to guide the diffusion model generating
814
      samples that meet attacker specified goals by using gradient information from the classifier during the
      testing stage (Liu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). Also, Chen et al. (2023a) used the
815
      classifier guidance during the training stage of the diffusion model along with self and cross attention
816
      mechanisms.
817
818   Further, Beerens et al. (2024) showed that poisoning the training set can produce a deceptive diffusion
819   model which will generate adversarial samples without any guidance.
820   However, these works only care about static stealthiness in a supervised learning setting, while SHIFT
821   also takes dynamic stealthiness into consideration.

1009   function correspondingly changes to ∇x log p(x; σ), which does not depend on the normalization
1010   constant of the underlying density function p(x, σ) and is much easier to evaluate. To be specific, if
1011   D(x; σ) is a denoiser function that minimizes:

1015                                   ∇x log p(x; σ) = (D(x; σ) − x)/σ 2

1017   training objective in Equation (8). Utilizing this finding, EDM only requires a small number of

1293   in the Pong environment. Consequently, the perturbed images generated by these methods can
1294   potentially be purified by a diffusion model. To confirm this, we have conducted new experiments,

1299   the decision-relevant semantics of the images, such as moving the Pong ball to a different position
1300   without changing other elements in the Pong environment as shown in Figure 1). This is the key
1301   reason why our attack can bypass strong diffusion-based defense methods.

1305   their paper can be easily detected by humans (see Figure 6). Further, their metric for stealthiness
1306   is static and does not consider the sequential decision-making nature of RL. In contrast, our attack
1307   method aims to stay close to the set of true states S ∗ to maintain static stealthiness (Definitions 1
1308   and 2) and align with the history to achieve dynamic stealthiness (Definitions 4 and 5). These
1309   are novel definitions for characterizing stealthiness in the RL context. The static stealthiness is
1310   demonstrated through the low reconstruction loss of our method shown in Figure 3a. We further
