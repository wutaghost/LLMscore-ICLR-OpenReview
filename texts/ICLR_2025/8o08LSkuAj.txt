009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the statistical-computational trade-offs for learning with exact invariances
012            (or symmetries) using kernel regression over manifold input spaces. Traditional
013            methods, such as data augmentation, group averaging, canonicalization, and frame-
014            averaging, either fail to provide a polynomial-time solution or are not applicable in
015            the kernel setting. However, with oracle access to the geometric properties of the
016
               input space, we propose a polynomial-time algorithm that learns a classifier with
               exact invariances. Moreover, our approach achieves the same excess population
017
               risk (or generalization error) as the original kernel regression problem. To the best
018
               of our knowledge, this is the first polynomial-time algorithm to achieve exact (not
019            approximate) invariances in this context. Our proof leverages tools from differential
020            geometry, spectral theory, and optimization. A key result in our development is a
021            new reformulation of the problem of learning under invariances, as optimizing an
022            infinite number of linearly constrained convex quadratic programs, which may be
023            of independent interest.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      While humans can readily observe symmetries or invariances in systems, it is generally challenging
028
      for machines to detect and exploit these properties from data. The objective of machine learning
029
      with invariances is to develop approaches that enable models to be trained and utilized under the
030   symmetries inherent in the data. This framework is broadly applicable across various domains
031   in the natural sciences and physics, including atomistic systems (Grisafi et al., 2018), molecular
032   wavefunctions and electronic densities (Unke et al., 2021), interatomic potentials (Batzner et al.,
033   2022), and beyond (Batzner et al., 2023). While many applications involve Euclidean symmetries
034   (Smidt, 2021), the scope of such methods extends well beyond them to other geometries (Bronstein
035   et al., 2017).
036   Learning with invariances has a longstanding history in machine learning (Hinton, 1987; Kondor,
037   2008). In recent years, there has been significant interest in the development and analysis of learning
038   methods that account for various types of invariances. This surge in interest is strongly motivated by
039   many models showing considerable success in practice. Empirical evidence suggests the existence of
040   algorithms that can effectively learn under invariances while exhibiting strong generalization and
041   computational efficiency. However, from a theoretical perspective, much of the focus has been on the
042   expressive power of models, generalization bounds, and sample complexity. There remains a relative
043   lack of understanding regarding the statistical-computational trade-offs in learning under invariances,
044
      even in foundational settings such as kernel regression.
045   Kernels, which have been among popular learning approaches, offer both statistical and computational
046   efficiency (Scholkopf & Smola, 2018). Symmetries can be included in kernel learning in various
047   ways. An immediate solution for learning with invariances seems to be data augmentation over
048   the elements of the group. Moreover, most kernel-based approaches to learning with invariances
049   rely on group averaging, a technique that involves summing over group elements. However, the
050
      typically large size of the group can make both of these approaches computationally prohibitive, even
      super-exponential in the dimension of input data. Alternative approaches, such as canonicalization
051
      and frame averaging, also suffer from issues like discontinuities and scalability challenges (Dym
052
      et al., 2024).
053
      In light of these challenges, this paper seeks to address the following question:

054
055       Is it possible to obtain an invariant estimator for the kernel regression problem that exhibits
056       both strong generalization capabilities and computational efficiency?
057
058   The first contribution of this work is a detailed study of the problem of learning with invariances
059   in the context of kernel methods. We argue that, while group averaging fails to produce exactly
060   invariant estimators within a computationally efficient timeframe, alternative algorithms can generate
061   invariant estimators for the kernel regression problem in time that is polylogarithmic in the size of
062   the group. In other words, we demonstrate that it is possible to achieve an invariant estimator that
063
      is both computationally efficient and exhibits strong generalization. At first glance, this result may
      seem counterintuitive and even impossible, since it implies that enumerating all possible invariances
064
      is not required to design statistically efficient learning algorithms. This provides theoretical support
065
      for the empirical observation that computational efficiency and strong generalization are attainable
066   in learning with invariances. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first algorithm that is both
067   statistically and computationally efficient for learning with invariances in the kernel setting,
068
069
      Learning with invariances can be formulated as a nonconvex optimization problem, which is not
      tractable directly. To design an efficient algorithm, we leverage the spectral theory of the Laplace-
070
      Beltrami operator on manifolds. Notably, since this operator commutes with all (isometric) group
071
      actions on the manifold, it is possible to find an orthonormal basis of Laplacian eigenfunctions such
072   that each group action on the manifold acts on the eigenspaces of the Laplacian via orthogonal
073   matrices. This theoretical framework allows us to reformulate the original problem of learning with
074   invariances on manifolds as a infinite collection of finite-dimensional convex quadratic programs—one
075   for each eigenspace—each constrained by linear conditions. By truncating the number of quadratic
076   programs solved, we can efficiently approximate solutions to the primary nonconvex optimization
077   problem, thereby approximating kernel solutions to the problem of learning with invariances. This
078   reformulation not only enables us to derive a polynomial-time algorithm for kernel regression under
079   invariances, but it may also have broader applications, the exploration of which we defer to future
080
      research.
081   Finally, we emphasize again that this work is centered on achieving exact invariance, as many
082   applications—especially neural networks with strong empirical performance—are explicitly designed
083   to incorporate exact invariances by construction. In summary, this paper makes the following
084   contributions:
085        • We initiate the exploration of statistical-computational trade-offs in the context of learning
086           with exact invariances, focusing specifically on kernel regression over manifold-structured
087           input spaces.
088        • We reformulate the problem of learning under invariances in kernel methods, leveraging
089           differential geometry and spectral theory, and cast it as infinitely many convex quadratic
090           programs with linear constraints, for which we derive an efficient solution in terms of time
091           complexity. We trade off computational and statistical complexity by controlling the number
092           of convex quadratic programs solved to obtain the estimator.
093        • We introduce the first polynomial algorithm for learning with invariances in the general
094
              setting of kernel regression over manifolds.

096   2    R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Generalization bounds and sample complexity for learning with invariances have been extensively
099   studied, particularly in the context of invariant kernels. Works such as Elesedy (2021), Bietti
100   et al. (2021), Tahmasebi & Jegelka (2023), and Mei et al. (2021) provide insights into this area.
101   Additionally, studies on equivariant kernels (Elesedy & Zaidi, 2021; Petrache & Trivedi, 2023) further
102   our understanding of how equivariances affect learning. PAC-Bayesian methods have also been
103   applied to derive generalization bounds under equivariances (Behboodi et al., 2022). More recently,
104
      Kiani et al. (2024) explored the complexity of learning under symmetry constraints for gradient-based
      algorithms. For studies on the optimization of kernels under invariances, see Teo et al. (2007).
105
106   A variety of methods have been proposed to enhance the performance of kernel-based learning
107   models. One prominent approach is the use of random feature models (Rahimi & Recht, 2007), which
      approximate kernels using randomly selected features. Low-rank kernel approximation techniques,


                                                         2

108
      such as the Nyström method (Williams & Seeger, 2000; Drineas et al., 2005), have also been proposed
109   to reduce the computational complexity of kernel methods; see also Bach (2013); Cesa-Bianchi et al.
110   (2015). Divide-and-conquer algorithms offer another pontential avenue for kernel approximation
111   (Zhang et al., 2013). Additionally, the impact of kernel approximation on learning accuracy is
112   well-documented in Cortes et al. (2010).
113
      Our work focuses on learning with invariances, which differs significantly from the tasks of learning
114
      invariances or measuring them in neural networks. For example, Benton et al. (2020) address how
115   neural networks can learn invariances, while Goodfellow et al. (2009) study methods to measure the
116   degree of invariance in network architectures.
117
118
      Invariance in kernel methods is not limited to group averaging. Other approaches such as frame
      averaging (Puny et al., 2022), canonicalization (Kaba et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024), random projections
119
      (Dym & Gortler, 2024), and parameter sharing (Ravanbakhsh et al., 2017) have also been proposed to
120
      construct invariant function classes. However, canonicalization and frame averaging face challenges,
121   particularly concerning continuity, which has been addressed in recent works like Dym et al. (2024).
122
123   In specialized tasks such as graphs, image, and pointcloud data, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
124
      (Scarselli et al., 2008; Xu et al., 2019), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Krizhevsky et al.,
      2012; Li et al., 2021), and Pointnet (Qi et al., 2017a;b) have demonstrated the effectiveness of
125
      leveraging symmetries. Symmetries have also been successfully integrated into generative models
126
      (Biloš & Günnemann, 2021; Niu et al., 2020; Köhler et al., 2020). For a broader discussion on various
127   types of invariances and their applications across machine learning tasks, see Bronstein et al. (2017).

      Notation. We begin by establishing some frequently used notation. Let M be a smooth, compact,
133
      and boundaryless d-dimensional Riemannian manifold. The uniform distribution over the manifold is
134
      the normalized volume element corresponding to its metric. We denote the space of square-integrable
135   functions over M by L2 (M) and the space of continuous functions by C(M). Furthermore, H s (M)
136   represents the Sobolev space of functions on M with parameter s, defined as the set of functions with
137   square-integrable derivatives up to order s. Larger values of s correspond to greater smoothness, and
138   it holds that H s (M) ⊆ C(M) if and only if s > d/2, a condition we will assume throughout this
139   paper. For each n ∈ N, we define [n] := {1, 2, . . . , n}. We use log to denote the logarithm with base
140   2. We refer to Appendices A.1 and A.2 for a quick review of Riemannian manifolds.
141   Problem statement. We consider a general learning setup on a smooth, compact, and boundaryless
142
      Riemannian manifold M of dimension d. Our objective is to identify an estimator fb ∈ F from a
143   feasible space of estimators F ⊆ L2 (M), based on n independent and uniformly distributed labeled
144   samples S = {(xi , yi ) : i ∈ [n]} ⊆ (M × R)n drawn from the manifold. Here, the labels yi for
145   i ∈ [n] are produced based on the (unknown) ground truth regression function f ⋆ ∈ C(M), meaning
146   that yi = f ⋆ (xi ) + ϵi , for each i ∈ [n], where ϵi , i ∈ [n], is a sequence of independent zero-mean
147   random variables with variance bounded by σ 2 . The population risk (or generalization error) of an
148   estimator fb ∈ L2 (M), which quantifies the quality of the estimation, is defined as:
149
                                                   h                  i
150                                      R(fb) := E ∥fb − f ⋆ ∥2L2 (M) ,

152
      where the expectation is taken over the randomness of the data and labels.

154   Given a dataset of size n, finding estimators with minimal population risk can be quite complex,
155   often requiring the resolution of non-convex optimization objectives. However, in scenarios where
156   f ⋆ ∈ H, with H ⊆ L2 (M) being a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), it is feasible to
157   compute kernel-based estimators with low risk efficiently. Specifically, the Kernel Ridge Regression
158   (KRR) estimator for the RKHS H = H s (M), denoted as fbKRR , achieves a population risk of
      R(fbKRR ) = O n−s/(s+d/2) while being computable in time O(n3 ), assuming access to an oracle

      Learning with invariances. We assume that a finite group G acts smoothly and isometrically1 on
163   the manifold M, represented by a smooth function θ : G × M → M mapping the product manifold
164   G × M to M. We employ the notation θ(g, x) as gx for any g ∈ G and x ∈ M. In a scenario of
165   learning under invariances, the regression function f ⋆ is invariant under the action of the group G,
166   satisfying f ⋆ (gx) = f ⋆ (x) for each g ∈ G and x ∈ M. Thus, learning under invariances introduces
167   an additional requirement: not only must we compute an estimator with minimal population risk
168   efficiently, but fb must also be invariant with respect to G. This additional condition is often satisfied
169   in neural network applications by constructing networks that are invariant by design, such as graph
170   neural networks.
171   In the context of learning with Sobolev kernels, the KRR estimator fbKRR is not G-invariant (see
172   Appendix A for more details). Consequently, the KRR estimator cannot provide a solution for
173   learning under invariances. However, with a shift-invariant Positive Definite Symmetric (PDS)
174   kernel2 K : M × M → R, one can utilize group averaging to derive a new kernel and a new RKHS
175   holding only G-invariant functions:

181   apply the above method to construct and compute a G-invariant kernel (assuming access to evaluating
182   its original kernel). This indicates that the KRR estimator on Kinv yields an invariant estimator for
183   f ⋆ with a desirable population risk (see Tahmasebi & Jegelka (2023) for a comprehensive study).

185   new kernel between pairs of input data. In many practical scenarios,   |G| can be intolerably large.

189   approximated by summing over a number of random group transformations. However, this does not
190   guarantee exact invariance, which is the primary goal of this paper.

      Other traditional approaches to achieving learning under invariances include data augmentation,
192   canonicalization, and frame averaging. For data augmentation, we need to increase our dataset
193   size by a multiplicative factor of |G|, which is often impractical within efficient time constraints.
194   This is because for any datapoint xi ∈ S, new datapoint gxi for any group element g ∈ G should
195   be added to dataset to ensure invariance of the underlying learning procedure in a blackbox way,
196   leading to Ω(n|G|) complexity. Canonicalization involves mapping data onto the quotient space of
197   the group action and subsequently finding an estimator (e.g., a KRR estimator) on the reduced input
198   space. However, this method is also infeasible for kernels due to the unavoidable discontinuities and
199   non-smoothness of the canonicalization maps, which violate RKHS requirements (Dym et al., 2024).
200
      Finally, frame averaging is analogous to canonicalization, but it remains unclear how to address
      continuity issues for efficient frame sizes. Moreover, it requires careful design of frames tailored to
201
      the specific problem at hand, making it unsuitable for a general-purpose algorithm. Thus, motivated
202
      by these observations, we pose the following question:
203
204
205       Is it possible to obtain a G-invariant estimator for f ⋆ ∈ H s (M) with a desirable population
206       risk (similar to the case without invariances) in poly(n, d, log(|G|)) time?
207
208   We aim to answer this question affirmatively in the following section. This is surprising, as it suggests
209   that even enumerating the set G is not required to find statistically efficient G-invariant estimators.

      Oracles. To characterize computational complexity, first we need to specify the type of oracle
217   access provided for the estimation. Before doing so, we briefly review the spectral theory of the
218   Laplace-Beltrami operator on manifolds. For further details, we refer the reader to Appendix A.
219
220
      The Laplace-Beltrami operator generalizes the Laplacian operator to Riemannian manifolds. It has
      a basis of smooth eigenfunctions ϕλ,ℓ ∈ L2 (M), which serve as an orthonormal basis for L2 (M).
221
      The index λ represents the eigenvalue corresponding to the eigenfunction ϕλ,ℓ , and ℓ ∈ [mλ ] runs
222
      over the multiplicity of λ, denoted by mλ . The eigenvalues can be ordered such that 0 = λ0 < λ1 ≤
223   λ2 ≤ · · · → ∞. For example, in the case of the sphere S d−1 , the spherical harmonics, which are
224   homogeneous harmonic polynomials, are a natural choice of eigenfunctions.
225
226
      The sequence of eigenfunctions and their corresponding eigenvalues provide critical information
      about the geometry of the manifold. In this work, we make use of the following two types of oracles:
227
228              • The ability to evaluate any eigenfunction ϕλ,ℓ (x) at a given point x ∈ M.
229
230          • The ability to compute the L2 (M) inner product between a shifted eigenfunction ϕλ,ℓ (gx)
231
                and another eigenfunction ϕλ,ℓ′ (x) for any group element g ∈ G.
232                                                                  P
      For both oracles, we assume free access as long as Dλ := λ′ ≤λ mλ′ = poly(n, d), where Dλ
233
      denotes the number of eigenfunctions with eigenvalues less than or equal to λ. This assumption is
234
      motivated by the case of S d−1 , where spherical harmonics can be efficiently evaluated or multiplied
235   in low dimensions (in such cases, only a few monomials need to be processed, making the task
236   simple3 ). The first oracle handles the geometric structure of the manifold, while the second oracle
237   captures the relationship between the group action and the manifold’s spectrum. Both are crucial for
238   obtaining our results.

471   Example 1. Consider the problem of learning under invariances over the unit sphere S d−1 := {x ∈
472   Rd : ∥x∥2 = 1}, where the group G is the group of all permutations of coordinates. Note that
473   |G| = d!, which is prohibitively large for data augmentation or group averaging. However, this group
474   is generated by only two elements: σ1 = (1 2) and σ2 = (1 2 . . . d). Here, σ1 swaps the first and

493   6       D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
494
495   We initiated the study on computational-statistical trade-offs in learning with exact invariances. We de-
496   signed an algorithm that shows achieving the desirable population risk (the same as kernel regression
497   without invariances) in poly(n, d, log(|G|)) time for the task of kernel regression with invariances
498   on general manifolds. We note that, for simplicity, we have focused on boundaryless manifolds and
499
      isometric group actions. However, using standard techniques, the theory can be extended to more
      general cases as well4 . While the proposed spectral algorithm is computationally efficient,  it does not
500
501
      offer any improvement in sample complexity over the baseline R(fb) = O n−s/(s+d/2) . It has been
      observed that without computational constraints, better convergence rates are possible for learning
502
      with invariances (Tahmasebi & Jegelka, 2023), which are minimax optimal. Thus, it remains open
503
      whether those improved rates are achievable in poly(n, d, log(|G|)) time.
504
505   We note that the oracle access we assumed is primarily motivated by the case of the sphere, where
506   polynomials can be evaluated, multiplied, composed by group elements, and integrated efficiently
507
      when they are of relatively low degree. We believe this is the most natural oracle access for this
      problem, as it aligns well with applications involving polynomials. An interesting future work could
508
      be to investigate the statistical-computational trade-offs using alternative oracles, e.g., similar to the
509
      kernel trick, how to design computationally efficient algorithms that have only access to the inner
510   product of the RKHS. Another interesting future direction is to find whether random feature models
511   as approximations for kernels can significantly improve the statistical-computational trade-off of
512   learning with invariances. At present, our theory does not apply to random feature models.
513
      We also observe that the spectral algorithm used in this paper does not employ the kernel trick, as
514
      it requires access to the entire set of features, rather than just their inner products. An interesting
515
      question is whether it is possible to utilize kernel tricks and find an alternative (polynomial-time)
516   algorithm for learning under invariances. This approach could potentially improve the statistical
517   efficiency of the spectral algorithm. In the end, we would like to note that capturing computational-
518   statistical trade-offs in other estimation problems with invariances such as density estimation (Chen
519   et al., 2023; Tahmasebi & Jegelka, 2024) could serve as a compelling avenue for future research.
520
521
522
      7       C ONCLUSION
523
      In this paper, we explore the statistical-computational trade-offs in learning with invariances, focusing
524
      specifically on kernel regression. We observe that while the Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR) estimator
525
      can address this problem, it is not necessarily invariant without group averaging. Furthermore, since
526   performing group averaging can be costly for large groups, we ask whether it is possible to develop
527   statistically sound estimators with efficient time complexity. Our findings show that by reformulating
528   the problem and reducing the number of constraints using group laws, we can express it as solving
529   an infinite series of quadratic optimization programs under linear constraints. We conclude with an
530   algorithm that achieves an exactly invariant estimator with polynomial time complexity and highlight
531   several additional questions for future research.

       C     E XPERIMENTS
1189
1190
       In this section, we provide complementary experiments to support our theoretical results. We first
1191   show that, in practice, Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR) is not a G-invariant estimator. Then, we
1192   demonstrate that our algorithm (Spec-Avg) achieves the same rate of population risk as KRR, while
1193   enjoying exact invariance properties.
1194
1195   C.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
1196
1197   We consider the input space (manifold) Td = [−1, 1)d , which represents a flat d-dimensional torus.
1198   Additionally, we consider the group of sign-invariances G = {±1}d , acting on this space via
1199   coordinate-wise sign inversions. The dataset is generated as n independent and identically distributed
1200
       (i.i.d.) samples drawn uniformly from this space, with the target function defined as:
