009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The backdoor attack in Multimodal Contrastive Learning (MCL) task has been
012            receiving increasing attention in recent years, due to numerous downstream tasks
013            that rely on pre-trained MCL models. Backdoor detection has been one of the ef-
014            fective protection solutions to fight against backdoor attacks. However, the major-
015            ity of existing backdoor detection methods in MCL usually produce non-satisfying
016
               detection results. Two main factors are responsible for this: 1) one-stage detection
               lacks subsequent dynamic adaptation to the distribution of poisoned and benign
017
               pairs when faced with different attacks, and 2) the criteria used in existing meth-
018
               ods, specifically the cosine similarity between image and caption, is insufficient
019            to distinguish between poisoned and benign pairs. To address these problems, we
020            extend the conventional one-stage detection architecture to a two-stage architec-
021            ture and propose a better metric in the second stage with high precision and high
022            fault tolerance. To this end, we design a novel Coarse-to-Fine two-stage Backdoor
023            Detection method, termed CFBD, which primarily focuses on multimodal learn-
024            ing involving image-caption dataset, such as CLIP. The objective of the coarse-
025            grained stage is to roughly partition the dataset into poisoned, benign, and sus-
026            picious subsets. In the fine-grained stage, we use the average textual correlation
027
               with the poisoned subset to improve the detection quality. Extensive experiments
               demonstrate that CFBD achieves superior backdoor detection performance, e.g.,
028
               almost 100% True Positive Rate (TPR) for various attacks over the large-scale
029
               dataset CC-3M, markedly outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multimodal Contrastive Learning (MCL) represents a pivotal advancement in the field of deep learn-
035   ing, specifically within the realm of learning from different modalities. This approach leverages the
036   synergistic integration of different data modalities to learn robust and generalizable representations.
037   Multimodal contrastive methods, e.g., CLIP Radford et al. (2021a), ALIGN Jia et al. (2021) and
038   BASIC Pham et al. (2023), have shown impressive results in various downstream tasks such as
039
      text-guided image generation Kim et al. (2022); Ramesh et al. (2022); Clark & Jaini (2023); Nichol
      et al. (2022); Avrahami et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023b); Ye et al. (2024) and video understand-
040
      ing Liu et al. (2023d); Zhang et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b); Maaz et al. (2023). Generally, MCL
041
      models use a contrastive optimization objective to attract matched image-caption pair representa-
042   tions closer together in the embedding space while repelling unmatched pairs. Additionally, the
043   advent of MCL models provides an advantage to developers with limited resources, allowing them
044   to build high-quality models for downstream tasks by fine-tuning readily available pre-trained MCL
045   encoders, such as CLIP. Without loss of generality, we focus on the CLIP models in this work, and
046   our developed detection technique can be easily generalized to other MCL models.
047
      MCL harnesses vast internet-sourced image-caption datasets for improved semantic understanding,
048   this reliance also introduces notable vulnerabilities. As elucidated in key studies Carlini et al. (2024);
049   Carlini & Terzis (2022); Yang et al. (2023c), large-scale models that utilize these massive datasets
050   are especially vulnerable to targeted data poisoning and backdoor attacks. Adversaries can execute
051   a backdoor attack by injecting specialized triggers into a small subset of training images and mod-
052   ifying their original captions (e.g., “A deer with a white background”) to target captions (e.g., “An
053   image of cat”), as depicted in Figure 1. During the pre-training phase, the contrastive loss objec-
      tive, designed to align the embeddings of congruent image-caption pairs, inadvertently also aligns

074   There are also some attempts to protect multimodal learning against backdoor attacks. A common
075   assumption adopted by these methods is that for poisoned pairs, the dissimilarity between image-
076   caption pairs is larger than that of those benign pairs. Based on this assumption, (Yang et al., 2023c)
077   explored the use of a benign pre-trained CLIP model, to filter out dissimilar image-caption pairs.
078   (Bansal et al., 2023) developed CleanCLIP, employed an in-modality contrastive loss for both visual
079   and textual modalities, aiming to neutralize backdoor influences. (Yang et al., 2023a) observed that
080   the poisoned images and captions are not close to groups of similar images and captions in the
081   representation space, early in training. They intend to replace the original captions with the nearest
082
      captions in the dataset based on the image representation. However, the substitution is carried out
      for both benign and poisoned pairs, even if the ASR can be fairly low, the model performance will
083
      deteriorate along with the substitution with benign pairs. (Yang et al., 2023b) applied unimodal
084
      contrastive learning separately to each modality, categorizing data into “safe” and “risky” subsets to
085   prevent the injection of backdoors. A recent study (Liang et al., 2024c) also strengthened backdoor
086   shortcuts to identify suspicious samples through training prioritized by weakly similar samples.
087   More introductions on related works can be found in the Appendix B. Most of these methods perform
088   a one-stage detection process to filter out poisoned samples and safeguard the training, in which the
089   detection is designed under the assumption of dissimilarity in poisoned pairs.
090
      Despite their impressive results, there is still room for improvement. In this work, our aim is to de-
091   sign an effective detection method to safeguard the training of CLIP models. We start by pinpointing
092   a prevalent problem in current solutions that one-stage detection is inadequate to accurately discrim-
093   inate poisoned pairs within the poisoned dataset. Specifically, these methods regularly mis-identify
094   the benign pairs as poisoned pairs. The inaccurate separation will precipitate a high Attack Success
095   Rate (ASR) in the post-training model, whereas the mis-identification problem could risk undermin-
096   ing the inference performance of the model on benign input. On the other hand, utilizing the high
097   cosine dissimilarity between image and caption as a sign of being poisoned struggles to effectively
098   detect poisoned pairs. As can be seen from the distribution of cosine similarity in Figure 2 (b), there
099
      is a large overlapping region between the distribution of poisoned pairs and benign pairs, indicating
      that the cosine similarity between two modalities is insufficient to separate poisoned and benign
100
      pairs. We consequently adopt an experimental approach to justify that textual correlation could be a
101
      better metric for identifying poisoned pairs. In Figure 2 (a), we give the textual correlation between
102   different poisoned and benign pairs. The captions in poisoned pairs exhibit high similarity in the
103   textual embedding space, significantly overwhelms that of between benign pairs. Assume now that
104   we are given a poisoned set Dp , though this set is not required when launching the detection. We
105   then calculate the average textual correlation with captions in this set for each pair in a poisoned
106   dataset. As can be seen from Figure 2 (c), the textual correlation with respect to a given poisoned
107   set could be a more effective metric for discriminating the poisoned pairs from the benign pairs.
      To this end, we propose a two-stage detection process in which a coarse-grained stage is employed

      Before diving into the details of CFBD, let us first explain the threat model.
147
                                                                                       N
148   Threat Model. We consider a paired image-caption dataset D = {(Ii , Ti )}i=1 , where Ii and Ti
149   denote the image and the associated ground-truth (GT) caption, respectively. The dataset D can
                                                    n                         N
150   be divided into two subsets: Dp = {Ii , Ti )}i=1 and Db = {(Ii , Ti )}i=n+1 , corresponding to the
151   potential poisoned and benign data, respectively, where n ≪ N . The adversary is allowed to access
152   and manipulate Dp such that images carrying the trigger t are misclassified into the target classes y,
153   while other images are classified correctly. To this end, with Dp , the adversary crafts a poisoning
154   subset P = {(Iˆi , Tiy )}ni=1 through a generic trigger adding process ◦, namely, Iˆi = Ii ◦ t and
155   replacing the GT caption Ti with target caption Tiy . Eventually, the CLIP model trained on the
156   combination of poisoned dataset P and the benign subset Db spuriously associates the presence of
157   the trigger t in an image with the target label y in the target caption. It is also assumed that the
158
      adversary knows the model structure, the training algorithm, and the employed hyperparameters,
      but has no control of the downstream training process.
159
160   Detection Goal. Fundamentally, the objective of detection is to clearly detect poisoned set Dp from
161   the poisoned dataset D. Ideally, the multimodal model trains on the remaining benign pairs should
      have a low ASR, while maintaining a high inference performance on the benign test.

185   2.1   M ETHOD OVERVIEW OF CFBD
186
187   The schematic diagram of our proposed CFBD is illustrated in Figure 3. Essentially, CFBD is
188   a coarse-to-fine backdoor detection method consisting of two stages: a coarse-grained detection
189   stage and a fine-grained detection stage. In the coarse-grained detection stage (see Figure 3 (a)),
190
      the image Ii is first processed through a pre-trained CLIP vision encoder fI to obtain the image
      embedding Iie = fI (Ii ). Then Iie is sequentially processed by a mapping network F to generate a
191
192
      synthetic textual embedding T̂ie . We define soi as the cosine similarity between the pair (Iie , Ti ) as the
193   original similarity, while sgi as the cosine similarity for the pair (Ii , T̂ie ) as the generated similarity.
194
      Then the cross-modality consistency ci can be computed as the difference between sgi and soi . Upon
195
      obtaining consistency values {ci }N  i=1 for all the pairs in dataset D, we adopt a GMM to fit all these
      consistency values. Based on the fitted GMM, we aggregate the pairs with the probability higher
196
      than γ% generated from a specific Gaussian distribution into benign subset Db . The pairs with the
197
      top-q ci values are grouped as poisoned subset Dp , while the remaining pairs (less confidence level)
198   are categorized into the suspicious subset Ds .
199
200   The fine-grained detection stage (see Figure 3 (b)) is devoted to further identify poisoned pairs in Ds .
201
      Specifically, for an unidentified pair (Iu , Tu ) from Ds , we calculate the average textual correlation z
      between the caption Tu and the collection of captions Tp = {Tj | (Ij , Tj ) ∈ Dp } from the identified
202
      poisoned subset Dp . Afterwards, this pair is classified as poisoned pair if z is larger than a threshold
203
      γz , motivated by the factor b) in Section 1. Otherwise, it is classified as a benign pair. By the end
204   of the fine-grained stage, the suspicious subset Ds is split into a poisoned part Dsp and benign part
205   Dsb . Eventually, a CLIP model can be trained with Db ∪ Dsb . The full Algorithm is in Appendix A.
206
207

275

276

277   maximum means, thereby preventing the inclusion of poisoned pairs in Ds . However, the coverage
278   of each distribution could also be reduced, consequently decreasing the cardinality of Db . For dataset
279   with more uniform consistency distribution, it is suggested to apply a larger K to keep the Gaussian
280   distribution with minimum mean away from that with maximum mean for achieving a high recall
281   in Db . As for the non-uniform case, a smaller K assists the coarse-grained detection to detect more
282   benign pairs. We here empirically set K = 5, striking a good balance between these two factors.
283   Similarly, a larger γ leads to the inclusion of more pairs in Db , but at the risk of incorporating more
284   poisoned pairs. Regarding q, even a relatively small value, e.g., q = 50 gives a satisfactory result
285
      for fine-grained detection. Also, too large q would induce a drop of the detection performance.

      4   A BLATION S TUDY
509
510
      Unless otherwise stated, in the ablation study below, the number of poisoned pairs is 3000.
511
512   Different number of distribution K and threshold γ in GMM: Figure 6 (a) reports the number of
513   poisoned pairs being incorrectly categorized to Db . It is noted that the GMM is degraded to a single
514
      Gaussian distribution when K is 1. In this case, over 200 poisoned pairs are erroneously included
      in the Db when the γ% is higher than 87.5%. By gradually fitting GMM with more distributions,
515
      the number of misclassified poisoned pairs is remarkably reduced. When we fix the K, exclusively
516
      increasing the threshold γ% will raise the risk of preserving poisoned pairs in the Db and eventually
517   fail to prevent the injection of backdoors. These findings encourage us to set the K as 5 and γ% as
518   90% to detect the benign subset for ensuring high recall.
519
520
      Different size of poisoned subset: We now testify if increasing the q in coarse-grained stage could
      lead to better detection performance against five different attacks. As can be seen from Figure 6
521
      (b), our CFBD consistently succeeds to detect all poisoned pairs when q is increased from 50 to
522
      400. However, with more poisoned pairs being involved, the detection performance actually drops.
523   We conjecture that when increasing q, some benign pairs would be misclassified as the poisoned
524   subset, consequently disturb the evaluation of average textual correlation and finally deteriorates the
525   detection results. Therefore, q is empirically set as 50 in the coarse-grained stage of CFBD.

      5   C ONCLUSION
529
      This paper proposes a coarse-to-fine detection method CFBD against backdoor attacks in MCL. We
530
      utilize the cross-modality consistency to separate the poisoned pairs and benign pairs. Extensive
531
      experiments confirm that our CFBD is capable of detecting poisoned pairs with high precision and
532   low recall. Consequently, the CLIP model trained with benign pairs identified by CFBD maintains
533   a extremely low ASR, meanwhile a high model performance on the test set.
534
535
      CFBD makes a valuable step towards better detection of backdoor attacks in MCL. Regarding the
      potential threats of stronger attacks, one direction is to optimize the target caption in a way the
536
      visual semantics and textual semantics are aligned to escape the detection from CFBD. Additionally,
537
      CFBD can only be used to filter poisoned pairs in training set whereas it could not eliminate the
538   backdoors for poisoned models. Therefore, exploring the combination of detection and unlearning
539   strategies will be considered as our future work. Ethical statement can be found in Appendix I.
