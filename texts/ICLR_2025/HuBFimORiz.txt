009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have demonstrated impressive capability of in-context learning (ICL):
012            given a sequence of input-output pairs of an unseen task, a trained transformer can
013            make reasonable predictions on query inputs, without fine-tuning its parameters.
014            However, existing studies on ICL have mainly focused on linear regression tasks,
015            often with i.i.d. inputs within a prompt. This paper seeks to unveil the mechanism
016            of ICL for next-token prediction for Markov chains, focusing on the transformer
017            architecture with linear self-attention (LSA). More specifically, we derive and
018
               interpret the global optimum of the ICL loss landscape: (1) We provide the closed-
               form expression of the global minimizer for single-layer LSA trained over random
019
               instances of length-2 in-context Markov chains, showing the Markovian data distri-
020
               bution necessitates a denser global minimum structure compared to ICL for linear
021            tasks. (2) We establish tight bounds for the global minimum of single-layer LSA
022            trained on arbitrary-length Markov chains. (3) Finally, we prove that multilayer
023            LSA, with parameterization mirroring the global minimizer’s structure, performs
024            preconditioned gradient descent for a multi-objective optimization problem over
025            the in-context samples, balancing a squared loss with multiple linear objectives.
026            We numerically explore ICL for Markov chains using both simplified transformers
027            and GPT-2-based multilayer nonlinear transformers.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Transformer-based large language models (LLM) have demonstrated advanced capability of in-
033
      context learning (ICL): given a prompt, consisting of input-label pairs, a trained transformer can
      predict the label for an unseen input without updating its parameters (Brown, 2020; Rae et al., 2021;
034
      Garg et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). This
035
      ability to solve novel tasks solely from examples not only provide a potential alternative for expensive
036   fine-tuning (Li et al., 2024b), but also enhance reasoning tasks like chain-of-thought (Lampinen et al.,
037   2022), self-correction (Wang et al., 2024), with applications in mathematical problems and logical
038   deduction (Wei et al., 2022).
039
      The ability of transformers to solve unseen tasks in-context has sparked a line of research investigating
040
      the underlying mechanisms from various perspectives, including expressive power (Von Oswald
041
      et al., 2023; Akyürek et al., 2023; Giannou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
042   2023; Bai et al., 2024), convergence of transformer training dynamics (Zhang et al., 2024; Huang
043   et al., 2023), generalization ability (Duraisamy, 2024; Li et al., 2023; 2024a), and optimization theory
044   and global optimality (Ahn et al., 2023; Mahankali et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b). In particular, (Ahn
045   et al., 2023) identified a distinctive sparse structure in the global optimal transformer parameters, by
046   setting some entries of the model parameters directly to zero, which simplifies the structure of the
047   solution. Building on this sparsity, they demonstrated that the forward pass of linear attention models
048   implements preconditioned gradient descent.
049   However, the tasks considered in prior studies are limited to linear regression or classification,
050   where both feature and task vectors are zero-mean Gaussian, which offers limited insight into how
051   transformers learn sequential data governed by specific dynamics in-context. For example, when
052   presented with examples of math word problems that include intermediate steps and answers, an
053   LLM can generate reasonable answers to new questions (Lampinen et al., 2022). Nevertheless,
      the relationships among these examples cannot be directly modeled using linear functions with


                                                         1
054
      Gaussian-distributed data. Instead, they resemble sequences governed by dynamic processes over
055   a vocabulary, which can be conceptualized as a discrete state space. Therefore, investigating how
056   transformers learn such dynamics-based data in-context is essential to building a more systematic
057   understanding of ICL. In particular, we focus on the ICL for Markov chains, a classic model used to
058   represent language (Shannon, 1948; 1951; Makkuva et al., 2024).
059
      Major challenges. The challenges posed by in-context Markovian dynamics learning are two-
060   fold: (i) The objective function is non-convex w.r.t. transformer parameters, due to their nonlinear
061   coupling, which complicates the identification of the global minimum. To mitigate this, we transform
062   the problem through reparameterization to a strictly convex optimization that produces either the
063   global minimum or a tight lower bound, inspired by Ahn et al. (2023). (ii) Since the next token
064   is stochastically dependent on the previous tokens, no analytic expression exists for the labels in
065   the ICL setting. This introduces an additional layer of randomness beyond the feature and task
066   vectors. Specifically, compared to the linear case, we also need to consider the randomness of the
067   label conditioned on the feature and task vectors.
068   Our contributions. To this end, we study how transformers learn to predict the next token for
069   Markov chains in context by analyzing the loss landscape of linear self-attention (LSA) models.
070   Given the challenges posed by non-convexity and stochasticity, we focus on binary Markov chains
071   with first-order memory as our first step. The major contributions of this work are highlighted as
072
      follows.
073   ▶ We establish a framework for handling ICL with Markovian dynamics by fully characterizing the
074     global minima of the loss landscape for the LSA model trained on length-2 binary Markov chains.
075     This analysis applies to both i.i.d. settings (see Proposition 1) and general initial-state distributions
076     (see Proposition 2). Our results show that the global optimum adapts to the Markovian dynamics,
077     exhibiting a denser structure compared to ICL for linear regression. In comparison to traditional
078
        i.i.d. tasks, additional nonzero model parameters in the Transformer are necessarily included for
        achieving the global minimum of the loss due to the temporal dependence within the in-context
079
        samples.
080
      ▶ To the best of our knowledge, our theoretical result is the first to provide a closed-form expression
081     for the lower bounds of the expected global optimal value in next-token prediction using a one-
082     layer transformer structure for Markovian data of arbitrary length. Building on this result, we
083     further derive an upper bound by properly selecting the transformer parameters.
084   ▶ We advance the understanding of multilayer transformer expressivity by exploring a parameter
085     subspace that mirrors the structure of the derived global minimum for Markovian dynamics.
086     Our results show that the forward pass of the multilayer linear transformers is equivalent to
087     solving a multi-objective optimization problem. This problem minimizes a squared loss while
088     simultaneously maximizing multiple linear objectives (see Proposition 3).
089
090   Related work. The capability of transformers to perform ICL (Brown, 2020; Rae et al., 2021; Liu
091   et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2022) has inspired an exploration of its underlying mechanism from various
092   aspects. A line of works have shown transformers trained on in-context prompts implicitly implement
093
      optimization algorithms. Akyürek et al. (2023) constructed a set of weights in transformers such
      that their forward pass is equivalent to a step of gradient descent over the mean squared loss on
094
      in-context examples. Von Oswald et al. (2023) provided such a construction for LSA, further showing
095
      actual optimization of transformers on in-context loss landscapes converge to such a construction. In
096   addition to standard learning algorithms such as least squares and ridge regression, Bai et al. (2024)
097   showed that transformers implement algorithm selection. Specifically, transformers first determine
098   the task type based on the data statistics in the prompt and then choose the most optimal standard
099   algorithm to make predictions for the query input.
100
      From the perspective of optimization theory, Mahankali et al. (2024); Ahn et al. (2023) showed trained
101
      LSA networks emulate preconditioned gradient descent via analyzing the loss landscape. Gatmiry
102   et al. (2024) proved that the global minimizer implements multi-step preconditioned gradient descent,
103   considering looped transformers (Giannou et al., 2023). While previous works mainly focused on
104   i.i.d. in-context examples, Li et al. (2024b) further analyzed the ICL loss landscape under correlated
105   designs, in addition to the consideration of state-space model and LoRA. There has also been studies
106   about the training dynamics of transformers in the ICL setting. Zhang et al. (2024) demonstrated that
107   LSA trained through gradient flow converges to the global minimum under mild distribution shifts,
      achieving close performance to the best linear predictor. Huang et al. (2023) proved convergence


                                                          2
108
      of training dynamics to near-zero prediction error under both balanced and unbalanced in-context
109   samples. Another relevant area of our work is time-series prediction, which we discuss in section F.
110   The comparison between this work and existing research is summarized in Table 1.
111
112
      A line of concurrent work has studied transformers for temporal data structures, including Markov
      chains Makkuva et al. (2024); Sander et al. (2024); Rajaraman et al. (2024); Nichani et al. (2024).
113
      These studies primarily focus on attention mechanisms operating within a single Markov chain.
114
      In contrast, our work takes a complementary approach by examining a controlled setup where
115   transformers learn the similarities between entire sequences rather than within individual Markov
116   chains. This perspective enables us to explore how transformers manage complex dependencies
117   across sequences, particularly in settings with non-Gaussian input distributions and non-linear input-
118   output relationships. Notably, this work, to the best of our knowledge, represents the first step
119   toward understanding the attention mechanisms involved in extracting sentence-level relationships
120   between prompts and queries. This serves as a complementary contribution to characterizing the
121   expressiveness of Transformers for Markovian data.

148   2.1    I N - CONTEXT L EARNING
149
150   ICL refers to the operation on a prompt consisting of n input-output pairs and a query input:
151
                             p = (x1 , y1 , . . . , xn , yn , xn+1 ) = ({(xi , yi )}ni=1 , xn+1 )            (1)
152
153   where yi = h(xi ), ∀i ∈ [n + 1] for some unknown function h ∈ H, and xi , yi belong to some input
154   space X and output space Y, respectively. ICL aims to form an output ŷn+1 for the query input xn+1
155   that approximates its true label ŷn+1 ≈ h(xn+1 ). The function h : X → Y remains the same within
156   a single prompt yet varies across prompts.
157   Prior works have focused on linear function space H: h(x) = y = w⊤ x for some w ∈ X . Under
158   such a construction, y is deterministic once x is provided. Despite being commonly encountered in
159   many real-world applications, the case where h is stochastic remains largely unexplored. For example,
160   h can represent a text generation mechanism that provides descriptions revolving a given topic. Then
161   the token generated in the next step is associated with a probability based on the previously generated
      words Chorowski & Jaitly (2016). To approach the ICL for such scenarios, we consider a simplified


                                                              3
162
      setting of next token prediction for Markov chains. The state space resembles vocabulary and the
163   transition probability is akin to the conditional probability of the next word given the previous text.
164

      2.2   M ARKOV C HAINS
166
167   The evolution of a Markov chain s of order k on a state space S depends solely on the k most
168   recent states. For time step τ ∈ Z≥1 , we let sτ denote τ th state in the sequence s, the probability of
169   observing state j ∈ S at time step τ + 1 is:
170                               P(sτ +1 = j | s1:τ ) = P(sτ +1 = j | sτ −k+1:τ )                            (2)
171
172
      where sτ1 :τ2 denotes the subsequence from time step τ1 to τ2 . For first-order Markov chains, the
      dynamics are determined by the transition probabilities pij := P(sτ +1 = j | sτ = i), which indicate
173
      the probability of transitioning from state i ∈ S to state j ∈ S. These probabilities constitute
174
      the Markov kernel P = (pij ) ∈ [0, 1]|S|×|S| . For a binary state space S = {0, 1}, The transition
175
      matrix for a binary Markov chain is represented as P(p01 , p10 ) := [1 − p01 , p01 ; p10 , 1 − p10 ].
176
      Let πτ ∈ [0, 1]|S| denote the marginal probability at the τ th time step. The relationship between
177
      consecutive time steps is given by πτ +1 = πτ P. A binary Markov chain s ∼ (π1 , P(p01 , p10 )) can
178   be generated by starting with an initial distribution π1 and iteratively applying P(p01 , p10 ) to update
179   the state probabilities at each time step.

      In this section, we present our main results on ICL for first-order Markov chains. We theoretically
264   characterize the loss landscape of the in-context objective function f , where the point-wise error ℓ is
265   the square loss (i.e., ℓ(ŷ, y) = (ŷ − y)2 ). Though our objective function is the mean squared loss on
266   the query input, framing the task as a supervised regression problem, the inputs and outputs are related
267   through a Markov chain with temporal dependencies. We analyze length-2 and arbitrary-length
268   in-context Markov chains. For the length-2 case, we provide explicit expressions for the global
269   minimizers. For arbitrary-length Markov chains, we derive a tight bound for the global minimum.
      Additionally, we provide an interpretation of the forward pass of TFL as an optimization algorithm.

      Focusing on first-order binary Markov chains, we analyze the behavior of more complex transformers
439   trained with mean squared error (MSE). Additionally, we investigate the in-context performance of
440   transformers trained with cross-entropy loss, as detailed in the Appendix C 1 .
441
442   Data generation. Each data sample, or a prompt, consists of n sequences with length 4. To
443   generate a prompt, we first sample the initial states of each in-context sequence indepednently
444   from Bernoulli(0.5). Then, we sample transition probabilities p01 , p10 from U (0, 1) and iteratively
445   generate the subsequent states for each sequence, assuming they are governed by the same Markov
446   kernel, i.e., {xi }ni=1 ∼ (π1 = [0.5, 0.5], P(p01 , p10 )). Both training and testing prompts are sampled
447   from the same distribution.
448
449   Model and training. We adopt architectures based on GPT-2-blocks. We consider three configu-
450   rations of (embedding dimension, number of transformer blocks, number of heads), inspired by Li
451   et al. (2024c): (i) tiny: (64, 3, 2), (ii) small: (128, 6, 4), (iii) standard: (256, 12, 8). The models
452
      are optimized by Adam over 50K epochs with learning rate 0.0001. For each epoch, we randomly
      generate 64 data samples to train the model parameters. To ensure high prediction performance given
453
      any length-n′ prompt (n′ ∈ [n]), we train on the average of the error over different prompt lengths
454
      from 1 through n and update n from 26 to 101 during training.
455
456
      Evaluation metric. We report the accuracy of prediction. When the model is trained using MSE,
457   we assign an integer within {0, 1} that is closest to the transformer output to be the predicted state.
458   For binary states, if the prediction is greater than 0.5, we set the predicted state to be 1 and set to 0
459   otherwise. When trained using cross-entropy, we assign the index of the maximal normalized logit
460   returned by the transformer to be the predicted state.

      better when Markov chains are ‘biased’, since there is less entropy rate and therefore less uncertainty.
524
525   5                C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we investigate the in-context learning of next-token prediction tasks for dynamics-based
528   sequential data. Specifically, we analyze the loss landscape of LSA models trained on in-context
529
      prompts consisting first-order binary Markov chains. Our findings demonstrate that the optimal
      transformers do not exhibit the sparsity condition typically observed ICL for linear tasks, indicating
530
      a unique adaptation of transformers to Markovian data. As the number of in-context examples
531
      increases, we observe that the global minima for length-2 Markov chains gradually approximate the
532   sparse structure in the linear case. By introducing a special parameter construction with a sparsity
533   level between the linear and Markovian scenarios, we show that multilayer transformers implement
534   preconditioned gradient descent for a multi-objective optimization problem. This optimization aims
535   to minimize the mean squared loss while maximizing linear functions of the observed in-context
536   sequence. Furthermore, we empirically demonstrate that nonlinear transformers can successfully
537   predict the next token when trained using cross-entropy loss, with the training loss converging to
538   the expected entropy rate in this context. Potential extensions of our theoretical results include
539   higher-order memory Markov chains, larger state spaces, and multilayer transformers with nonlinear
      attention mechanisms trained with cross-entropy loss.

       Time series prediction. Time series prediction problems can be categorized into transductive and
1693
       inductive setups. In the transductive case, a model (e.g., recurrent neural networks, neural ordinary
1694   differential equations) is trained on the initial portion of a new sequence and then used to predict
1695   future time steps for that same sequence. The next-token prediction for first-order binary Markov
1696   chains has been addressed in this context (Makkuva et al., 2024), demonstrating that transformers
1697   can effectively learn to output the transition probabilities of the input sequence. However, the global
1698   minimum in their case has trivial attention parameters, indicating that the absence of attention can still
1699   yield the desired performance. In contrast, our study requires that attention parameters be non-zero to
1700   reach the global minimum.
1701   On the other hand, in inductive scenarios (Kipf et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020), a
1702   model is trained on multiple time series derived from the same dynamics. During inference, the
1703   trained model uses partial observations from an unseen time series sharing the same dynamics to
1704   predict future time steps without the need for fine-tuning. In this case, the learned model captures the
1705   dynamics from the observational window and makes predictions accordingly. However, ICL extends
1706   beyond this framework by addressing a higher-level problem that involves learning across various
1707   dynamical systems with different parameter settings, such as transition probabilities in the case of
1708
       Markov chains. In this case, the trained transformer infers the unseen dynamical system from the
       in-context samples and makes predictions for the query sequence.
