009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose an empirical approach centered on the spectral dynamics of weights—
012            the behavior of singular values and vectors during optimization—to unify and clar-
013            ify several phenomena in deep learning. We identify a consistent bias in optimiza-
014            tion across various experiments, from small-scale “grokking” to large-scale tasks
015            like image classification with ConvNets, image generation with UNets, speech
016            recognition with LSTMs, and language modeling with Transformers. We also
017            demonstrate that weight decay enhances this bias beyond its role as a norm reg-
018            ularizer, even in practical systems. Moreover, we show that these spectral dy-
019
               namics distinguish memorizing networks from generalizing ones, offering a novel
               perspective on this longstanding conundrum. Additionally, we leverage spectral
020
               dynamics to explore the emergence of well-performing sparse subnetworks (lot-
021
               tery tickets) and the structure of the loss surface through linear mode connectivity.
022            Our findings suggest that spectral dynamics provide a coherent framework to bet-
023            ter understand the behavior of neural networks across diverse settings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Interest in neural networks has exploded in the past decade. Capabilities are rapidly improving,
029
      and deployment is ever-increasing. Yet, although issues with these technologies now have social
030   repercussions (Bender et al., 2021; Bommasani et al., 2021), many fundamental questions regarding
031   their behavior remain unanswered.
032
033
      For instance, despite extensive research, we still lack a complete understanding of the implicit biases
      of neural networks trained via stochastic optimization (Neyshabur et al., 2014). Even basic questions
034
      regarding the role of regularization like weight decay (Hanson & Pratt, 1988; Krogh & Hertz, 1991;
035
      Zhang et al., 2018a) have only partial answers (Van Laarhoven, 2017; Andriushchenko et al., 2023;
036   Yaras et al., 2023b). Perhaps most vexing, we lack a complete explanation for how neural networks
037   generalize, despite having the capacity to perfectly memorize the training data (Zhang et al., 2021).
038   Such an explanation may allow us to design better algorithms, however a lack of understanding
039   makes the deployment of neural networks vulnerable to uninterpretable errors across fields (Szegedy
040   et al., 2013; Ilyas et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2023).
041
      Although theoretical explanations have been put forward, these studies are often limited to special
042   settings like deep linear networks (Arora et al., 2018; 2019) or infinite-width systems (Jacot et al.,
043   2018), and arguments may rely on unsubstantiated or impractical assumptions like near-zero initial-
044   ization. On the empirical side, a growing body of work in interpretability has attempted to reverse-
045   engineer neural networks (Rahaman et al., 2019; Barak et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023), but given
046   the difficulty of the task, the systems of interest have been very small-scale, and the methodology
047   for analysis quite bespoke and challenging to scale. A third category of work aims at understanding
048   empirical behavior from a higher level (Zhang et al., 2021; Huh et al., 2022; Yu & Wu, 2023), but
049   while these works often study larger-scale systems, they often focus on more abstract objects like
050
      the gram matrix (Huh et al., 2022) or the Neural tangent kernel (NTK) (Fort et al., 2020), and thus
      do not have the granularity and predictive power of the previous two categories.
051
052   To bridge these gaps, we propose a task-agnostic, unifying perspective of many disparate phenomena
053   in deep learning across many different practical tasks and architectures, including image classifica-
      tion with ConvNets, image generation with UNets, speech recognition with LSTMs and language

082   Contributions: As a test bed for understanding generalization, Power et al. (2022) introduce the

091   neither grokking nor low-rank weights occur without sufficient weight decay.
092
093
      Though the common tie between low-rank weights and generalization in grokking, suggests a con-
      nection between rank and generalization, grokking is typically studied on synthetic tasks with very
094
      small-scale models like single-layer Transformers or small MLPs, and requires very particular hy-
095
      perparameter settings (Power et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023; Gromov, 2023; Kumar et al., 2023). If
096   our perspective is to be useful, it needs to scale to larger systems. Thus, we turn to common empir-
097   ical tasks drawn from the literature like image classification, image generation, speech recognition
098   and language modeling as well as varied and larger networks like VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman,
099   2014), UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997b) and multi-layer
100   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017).
101
      In Section 4, we demonstrate that the spectral dynamics are biased toward effective rank minimiza-
102   tion across various practical neural networks in complex settings. Although this behavior echoes
103   theoretical predictions in the deep linear setting, we find that the behavior of networks disagrees
104   with a common theoretical assumption about low-rank dynamics: the alignment of singular vectors
105   in consecutive layers (Saxe et al., 2014; Arora et al., 2018; 2019; Milanesi et al., 2021). Thus, the
106   rank minimization mechanism may differ from what the theory describes. It is notable too that our
107   hyperparameter settings are drawn from existing literature, thus the trend toward rank minimization
      coincides with well-generalizing networks across settings.

      Given one particularly notable ingredient for grokking was a very high level of weight decay, in
109   Section 5 we empirically connect rank minimization to weight decay, showing that weight decay
110   promotes rank minimization across architectures and tasks, echoing Section 3. In addition, in some
111   cases, it also appears to promote singular vector alignment in consecutive weights despite the nonlin-
112   earities between layers. Although weight decay explicitly penalizes norm, studying spectral dynam-
113   ics allows us to observe a host of effects including on rank and alignment. Such effects may help
114   in understanding the reason weight decay is useful, as norm-based explanations have been found
115   insufficient (Andriushchenko et al., 2023).
116   To test the explanatory power of the framework, we turn to the classic memorization experiments of
117   Zhang et al. (2021), who demonstrated that even small networks can memorize random labels, thus
118   any arguments about generalization cannot be capacity-based alone. In Section 6 and Appendix B,
119   we show that training with random labels leads to high-rank solutions, while rank with true labels is
120   much lower. We also find that while random labels do not align consecutive layers, true labels do,
121   which is surprising given the non-linearities between layers. This echoes prior discussion on rank
122   and generalization. Through spectral dynamics, we see a clear distinction between generalizing and
123
      memorizing networks, which provides a foothold toward better theoretical understanding.
124   Our results suggest that viewing neural networks through the lens of spectral dynamics can shed light
125   on several generalization-related phenomena, but we suspect there are broader connections. In the
126   literature, many curious and unexplained phenomena regarding neural networks exist. We take two
127   as case studies. First, the lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH) (Frankle & Carbin, 2018) and second, linear
128   mode connectivity (LMC) (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2019; Frankle et al., 2020; Neyshabur et al., 2020).
129
      We find that global magnitude pruning, a standard procedure for finding lottery tickets, preserves top
      singular vectors and acts like a low-rank pruning. We also see that the ability to interpolate between
130
      models in LMC strongly correlates with sharing top singular vectors. With these results, we note
131
      that the two phenomena can be seen as aspects of the spectral dynamics of weights, bringing them
132   under the umbrella of prior sections. For detailed discussion see Section 6 and Appendix C.
133
134   To summarize the discussion above, by studying the spectral dynamics of weights, we find:
135
            • Grokking is intimately linked to rank minimization;
136
137         • Rank minimization is a general phenomenon in more complex tasks;
138         • Weight decay acts implicitly as a low-rank regularizer;
139
            • Generalizing solutions have a lower rank than memorizing ones; and
140
141         • Top singular vectors are preserved when performing magnitude pruning and while linearly
142           interpolating between connected modes.
143
      All of these phenomena and effects have previously been studied in isolation to varying degrees, but
144
      by approaching deep learning through spectral dynamics, we aim to provide a common language for
145   probing and understanding neural networks. Code for all experiments will be released.

167   2.2   L OW-R ANK P ROPERTIES
168
169   Another line of research focuses on more general low-rank biases. Early work explored norms as an
170   implicit bias (Gunasekar et al., 2017). Theoretical analyses reveal that norms or closed-form func-
171   tions of weights might be insufficient to explain implicit regularization, but they do not necessarily
172
      contradict the possibility of rank minimization (Razin & Cohen, 2020; Vardi & Shamir, 2021). Nu-
      merous studies investigate low-rank biases in various matrices, including the Jacobian (Pennington
173
      et al., 2018), weight matrices (Le & Jegelka, 2021; Martin & Mahoney, 2020; 2021; Frei et al.,
174
      2022; Ongie & Willett, 2022), Gram matrix (Huh et al., 2022), and features (Yu & Wu, 2023; Feng
175   et al., 2022). Additionally, research suggests that dynamics influence the decay of rank (Li et al.,
176   2020; Chen et al., 2023; Wang & Jacot, 2023). Orthogonally, weight decay has a long history
177   as a regularizer explicitly penalizing parameter norm, which can be used for norm-based general-
178   ization bounds (Bartlett, 1996), but these bounds do not seem to explain the success of practical
179   systems (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2019; Jiang et al., 2019). Some works establish connections between
180   weight decay and rank minimization in idealized settings (Ziyin et al., 2022; Galanti et al., 2022;
181   Zangrando et al., 2024; Ergen & Pilanci, 2023; Parhi & Nowak, 2023; Shenouda et al., 2023), which
182   may be connected to generalization (Razin & Cohen, 2020). We are particularly interested in how
183
      far these connections extend in practice.

185   3     G ROKKING AND R ANK M INIMIZATION
186
187   Power et al. (2022) first noticed a surprising phenomenon they called “grokking” where models
188   quickly fit the training data on toy tasks, then after a long period of training, very quickly generalize
189   on the validation data. Later, others found that this phenomenon can occur in a relaxed fashion (Thi-
190   lak et al., 2022) on very simple models and different datasets (Liu et al., 2022; Gromov, 2023; Kumar
191
      et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023), and that weight decay seems critical to cause it (Lyu et al., 2023; Liu
      et al., 2023; Tan & Huang, 2023).
192
193   Motivated by theoretical work that proposes connections between rank and generalization (Razin &
194   Cohen, 2020), weight decay and rank (Galanti et al., 2022; Timor et al., 2023; Yaras et al., 2023b;

196   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) in simple settings, we evaluate the potential connection between rank
197
      and grokking in neural networks. This offers a complementary perspective on grokking with other

198
      boundaries (Humayun et al., 2024), the connection to double descent (Davies et al., 2022), and the

      discovery of a sparse solution (Merrill et al., 2023).

      4      S PECTRAL DYNAMICS ACROSS TASKS
271
272
      Inspired by the results on grokking and prior work on deep linear networks that studies the evolution
273
      of the SVD of the weight matrices (Saxe et al., 2014; Arora et al., 2018; 2019; Milanesi et al., 2021;
274   Yaras et al., 2023a), we apply the same analysis to larger, more practical systems. We show that
275   the trends we saw in the analysis of grokking mostly hold true across networks and tasks at a much
276   larger scale, even though our findings do occasionally deviate from theoretical predictions.
277
278   4.1     M ETHODOLOGY
279
280   Our experiments aim to examine reasonably sized neural networks across a variety of tasks. We
281   select models and tasks that are representative of current applications. Specifically, we focus on:
282
283         • Image classification with CNNs (VGG-16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014)) on CI-
284           FAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009);
285         • Image generation through diffusion with UNets (Ronneberger et al., 2015) on MNIST (Le-
286           Cun, 1998);
287         • Speech recognition with LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997b) on LibriSpeech (Panay-
288           otov et al., 2015); and
289
            • Language modeling with Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) on Wikitext-103 (Merity et al.,
290
              2016).
291
292   Training hundreds of runs for each of the above experiments is computationally expensive, limiting
293   the scale of models we can explore. We primarily adopt hyperparameters from existing literature,
294   with minor modifications for simplicity. This ensures that any correlations observed are likely a
295   reflection of common practices, not introduced bias on our part. We also provide evidence with
296   larger scale (up to 3B parameters) in Appendix D.5, from the Pythia suite (Biderman et al., 2023).
297   The primary evidence in this section comes from computing the SVDs of weight matrices within
298   the models. Consequently, we disregard 1D bias and normalization parameters in our analysis.
299   Indeed previous research suggests that in some cases these parameters are not crucial for perfor-
300   mance (Zhang et al., 2018b; Mohan et al., 2019; Karras et al., 2023). Due to the large number of
301   matrices in these models, we present plots of individual layers’ matrix parameters and statistics that
302   summarize behavior across layers for conciseness of presentation. Hundreds of thousands of plots
303   were generated for this study, making it impossible to include them all. Full experimental details,
304   including the choice of hyperparameters, are available in Appendix D.

306   4.2     E FFECTIVE R ANK M INIMIZATION
307
308   Building on theoretical (Saxe et al., 2014; Arora et al., 2019; Milanesi et al., 2021; Boix-Adserà
309
      et al., 2023; Yaras et al., 2023a) and empirical (Dittmer et al., 2019; Martin & Mahoney, 2020;
      2021; Boix-Adserà et al., 2023) findings, we investigate effective rank minimization across parame-
310
      ters in larger models and on a more diverse variety of tasks. Figure 3 reveals a consistent trend: the
311
      effective rank of network parameters generally decreases throughout training, regardless of the spe-
312   cific parameter or network architecture. This suggests a progressive “simplification” of the network
313   as training progresses.
314
315
      We further conduct a singular-value pruning experiment to explore the relationship between low-
      rank behavior and model performance. We prune either the top or bottom half of the singular values
316
      for each weight matrix in the network and then evaluate the pruned model at each training step.
317
      Given their importance in L2 space, we expect the top singular values to capture the most critical
318   information for the network’s function. Figure 4 confirms this, demonstrating that the pruned pa-
319   rameters, without further training, can closely approximate the full model’s performance. It is not
320   necessarily obvious that pruning would have this effect. In particular, simultaneously pruning lower
321   components across all layers may lead to losing some critical signal that must be passed between
322   layers, or it could be that small-magnitude singular values may provide some important regularizing
323   noise. In later sections, we will rely on this observation that large singular values are more critical
      to the function of the network.

407   Zangrando et al., 2024) and empirical works (Galanti et al., 2022; Boix-Adserà et al., 2023) propose
408   a connection with the rank of matrices in constrained settings. Still, a comprehensive connection to
409   larger empirical networks has not yet been demonstrated.

      6   A DDITIONAL C ONNECTIONS
482
483
      Here we briefly preview some connections between spectral dynamics and additional phenomena.
484
485   Memorization vs. Generalization: In Figure 7, we replicate the core memorization experiment of
      Zhang et al. (2021), which highlighted the ability of modern neural networks to memorize perfectly


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      even random labels across several supervised learning tasks. We find that when training with random
487   labels, networks with higher rank final parameters are obtained as opposed to when training with
488   true labels. Thus, the spectral dynamics can distinguish between memorization (of random labels)
489   and generalization. We also see an alignment structure between the middle layers that disappears
490   with random labels, perhaps as it is necessary in order for the network to pass signals from input
491   to output. This echoes the pattern of grokking in Section 3, where generalization came with a
492   transition to low-rank and alignment in middle layers. We expand this experiment to more settings
493   in Appendix B with additional figures.
494   Lottery Tickets: On very small networks, Frankle & Carbin (2018) found the existence of sparse
495   sub-networks via magnitude pruning, keeping only the top p% of weights globally by magnitude,
496   that could train to similar performance as the full network. For larger image classification networks,
497   Frankle et al. (2020) observed that in order to find such sparse subnetworks, it was necessary to train
498   till the end in order to acquire the pruning mask that could be used retroactively in training. This cu-
499   rious observation still lacks a compelling explanation. We show that such global magnitude pruning
500   functions similarly to low-rank pruning, thus the lottery ticket masks found by rewinding (Frankle
501
      et al., 2020), are effectively low-rank masks for the singular components that will become important
      at the end of training. Training the masked network leads to the same dynamics in these compo-
502
      nents. However, taking masks from too early in training leads to poor approximation of these final
503
      components and simultaneously stunts training. We provide detail on this discussion in Appendix C.
504
505   Linear Mode Connectivity (LMC): Linear Mode Connectivity (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2019; Frankle
506   et al., 2020) refers to the property that models that share a portion of the training trajectory can be
507
      averaged in weight-space to yield a stronger model (Wortsman et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022).
      This phenomenon indicates that, after some training, the loss surface is quite convex in a subspace,
508
      even though the optimization problem is theoretically highly non-convex. As all fine-tuning from
509
      pre-trained models stays in this convex space (Neyshabur et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022; Sadrtdinov
510   et al., 2023), an explanation for what underlies LMC could help to clarify the role of pre-training, and
511   may lead to faster fine-tuning. We show that LMC is tied with singular vector sharing. In particular,
512   when models can be averaged they share top singular vectors between weights, and when they cannot
513   they do not. This is an outcome of the early stability of top singular vectors, which arises due to
514   the unequal evolution of singular values. It is also straightforward to explain the large euclidean
515   distance between checkpoints that can be averaged, as they only share a very small portion of the
516   parameter space. Thus LMC, and by extension model-averaging, are deeply intertwined with the
517   dynamics of singular values that we explore in Section 4. Full discussion is deferred to Appendix C.
518
519   7   D ISCUSSION
520
521   We provide an empirical perspective to understand deep learning through the lens of SVD dynam-
522   ics. We first note a tendency toward rank minimization on a small scale in grokking, then expand
523   these findings to practical networks and tasks. In addition we find that weight decay, though it ex-
524   plicitly penalizes norm, implicitly promotes this low-rank bias. We also show that generalization
525   and memorization differ in the rank and alignment of solutions found by optimization. We go be-
526   yond remarks on generalization and show that magnitude pruning for lottery tickets acts similarly to
527
      low-rank pruning, and LMC coincides with the sharing of top singular vectors between checkpoints.
528   While a comprehensive theory for all these results remains elusive, these observations can act as
529   a platform for a deeper understanding of deep learning. Notably, the observed spectral dynamics
530   appear consistent across diverse settings, even without restrictive assumptions like balanced initial-
531   ization, linearity, or small weight scales. This suggests a common underlying mechanism.
532   On the empirical side, several interesting problems present themselves. Interpretability of neural
533   networks is a growing area of research (Nanda et al., 2023), and there already exist efforts to interpret
534   singular vectors of convolutional weights (Praggastis et al., 2022). There may also be connections
535   to other unexplained phenomena such as double descent (Belkin et al., 2019; Nakkiran et al., 2021;
536   Davies et al., 2022) or adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2013; Ilyas et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al.,
537   2021). The solutions to these problems may help design better optimizers or diagnose deployment
538   risks in the wild. There are also concerns of safety (Bai et al., 2022; Mazeika et al., 2024) that better
539
      understanding of neural networks can alleviate (Burns et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024). We believe
      our results contribute another step along this path.

       C.3     S PECTRAL DYNAMICS AND L INEAR M ODE C ONNECTIVITY
1189
1190   We come to the final phenomenon that we seek to describe: linear mode connectivity. Linear mode
1191   connectivity (LMC) is the property that one can interpolate linearly between two different minima
1192   in weight space and every parameter set along that path performs well, which gives the impression
1193   that the loss surface of neural networks is somehow convex despite its theoretical nonconvexity.
1194   This was first demonstrated in small networks with the same initialization (Nagarajan & Kolter,
1195   2019), then expanded to larger networks and connected to lottery tickets (Frankle et al., 2020; Paul
1196
       et al., 2022). Entezari et al. (2021) first conjecture that two arbitrary minima show LMC up to
       permutation, and demonstrate it in simple models. This was expanded to wide models (Ainsworth
1197
       et al., 2022; Jordan et al., 2022; Qu & Horvath, 2024), and can be proven in various ways (Kuditipudi
1198
       et al., 2019; Brea et al., 2019; Simsek et al., 2021; Ferbach et al., 2023), but it does not hold for
1199   standard models (Qu & Horvath, 2024). LMC has also been exploited for model-averaging and
1200   performance gains (Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2022; Rame et al., 2022). Still despite all
1201   of this work, we lack a description for why LMC occurs. In particular: why is there a convex, high
1202   dimensional (Yunis et al., 2022) basin that models find shortly in training (Frankle et al., 2020), or
1203   after pretraining (Neyshabur et al., 2020; Sadrtdinov et al., 2023)? We do not answer this question
1204   in full, but find an interesting view through the singular vectors.

1223   To remind the reader, LMC only occurs after a small amount of training time has passed. Too early
1224   and the final models of each branch will show a bump, or barrier, in the loss surface along the linear
1225   interpolation (Frankle et al., 2020). To measure this precisely, we use the definition from Neyshabur
1226   et al. (2020), which is the maximum deviation from a linear interpolation in the loss, an empirical
1227
       measure for convexity in this linear direction. When this deviation is 0, we consider the checkpoints
       to exhibit LMC. Please see Appendix D.10 for details on the calculation. Given evidence in Figure 4
1228
       that top components are the most important for prediction, and that top components become stable
1229
       before training has finished, it is plausible that LMC is connected to the stability of top singular
1230   vectors in the later portion of training.
1231
1232   This would mean that checkpoints that do not exhibit the LMC property should not share top singular
1233
       vectors, while checkpoints that do exhibit the LMC property should share top singular vectors.
       We see in Figure 13 that this is the case across models and tasks, where the alignment between
1234
       endpoints is much stronger in top singular vectors. We also see no LMC and poor agreement in
1235
       top components between branches that have initializations from different trunk trajectories, but with
1236   the same split epoch t and the same branch data order in Figure 14. Thus, these top directions are
1237   not a unique property of the architecture and data, but rather are dependent on initialization. It is
1238   notable that concurrent work (Ito et al., 2024) arrives at a similar conclusion: permutation solvers
1239   between optima match top singular vectors. Though the conclusions are similar, their experiments
1240   are primarily conducted on smaller scale settings, and only for permutation matching at the end of
1241   training. Here we connect these observations to the optimization behavior of networks throughout
       training.

1291   performance much. Rather, interpolation will eliminate the orthogonal bottom components which
1292   may only make a minor impact on performance. If however the top components are not shared,
1293   then interpolating between two models will remove these components, leading to poor performance
1294   in between. Such observations may help in explaining the utility of pretraining (Neyshabur et al.,
1295   2020), weight averaging (Rame et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2022) or the use
       of LoRA (Huh et al., 2022) to replace full finetuning.
