009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Influence Functions are a standard tool for attributing predictions to training data in
012            a principled manner and are widely used in applications such as data valuation and
013            fairness. In this work, we present realistic incentives to manipulate influence-based
014            attributions and investigate whether these attributions can be systematically tam-
015            pered by an adversary. We show that this is indeed possible for logistic regression
016            models trained on ResNet feature embeddings and standard tabular fairness datasets
017            and provide efficient attacks with backward-friendly implementations. Our work
018
               raises questions on the reliability of influence-based attributions in adversarial
               circumstances.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Influence Functions are a popular tool for data attribution and have been widely used in many
024
      applications such as data valuation (Richardson et al., 2019; Hesse et al., 2023; Sundararajan &
025   Krichene, 2023; Jia et al., 2019), data filtering/subsampling/cleaning (Wu et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
026   2020; Miao et al., 2021; Teso et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022), fairness (Li & Liu, 2022; Wang et al.,
027   2024; Sattigeri et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2024; Chhabra et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
028   2024; Yao & Liu, 2023; Ghosh et al., 2023) and so on. While earlier they were being used for benign
029   debugging, many of these newer applications involve adversarial scenarios where participants have
030   an incentive to manipulate influence scores; for example, in data valuation a higher monetary sum
031   is given to samples with a higher influence score and since good data is hard to collect, there is an
032   incentive to superficially raise influence scores for existing data. Thus, an understanding of whether
033
      and how influence functions can be manipulated is essential to determine their proper usage and
      for putting guardrails in place. While a lot of work in the literature has studied manipulation of
034
      feature-based attributions (Heo et al., 2019; Anders et al., 2020; Slack et al., 2020), whether data
035
      attribution methods, specifically influence functions, can be manipulated has not been explored. To
036   this end, our paper investigates the question and shows that it is indeed possible to systematically
037   manipulate influence-based attributions according to the manipulator’s incentives.
038
039
      Simply put, we show that it is possible to systematically train a malicious model very similar
      to the honest model in test accuracy but has desired influence scores. To formalize the setup
040
      we divide the function pipeline in terms of two entities – Data Provider who provides training data
041
      and Influence Calculator who trains a model on this data and finds the influence of each training
042   sample on model predictions. Out of these, Influence Calculator is considered to be the adversary
043   who wishes to change the influence scores for some training samples and does so covertly by training
044   a malicious model which is indistinguishable from the original model in terms of test accuracy but
045   leads to desired influence scores. This setting captures two important downstream applications where
046   incentives are meaningful: data valuation, where the adversary has an incentive to raise influence
047   scores for monetary gain and fairness, where the adversary wants to manipulate influence scores for
048   reducing the fairness of a downstream model.
049   We next define and provide algorithms to carry out two kinds of attacks in this setup: Targeted
050   and Untargeted. Targeted attacks are for the data valuation application and specifically manipulate
051   influence scores for certain target samples. The primary challenge with these attacks is that calculating
052   gradients of influence-based loss objectives is highly computationally infeasible. We address this
053   challenge by proposing a memory-time efficient and backward-friendly algorithm to compute the
      gradients while using existing PyTorch machinery for implementation. This contribution is of


                                                         1
054
      independent technical interest, as the literature has only focused on making forward computation
055   of influence functions feasible, while we study techniques to make the backward pass viable. Our
056   algorithm brings down the memory required for one forward + backward pass from not being feasible
057   to run on a 12GB GPU to 7GB for a 206K parameter model and from 8GB to 1.7GB for a 5K model.
058
      Experiments on multiclass logistic regression models trained on ResNet50 features show that our
059
      targeted attacks achieve a high success rate, a maximum of 94%, without much accuracy drop
060
      across three datasets. One final question that comes to mind is – is it always possible to manipulate
061   the influence scores for any given training sample? Using a theoretical construction, we give an
062   impossibility theorem which states that there exist samples for which the influence score cannot be
063   manipulated irrespective of the model, making this a property of the data rather than the model.
064
      Untargeted attacks are for the fairness application and unlike targeted attacks, manipulate influence
065
      scores arbitrarily without targeting specific samples. We find that surprisingly enough scaling
066
      model weights is a good enough strategy for such attacks without changing model accuracy. In our
067   experiments on standard tabular fairness datasets, we observe that due to influence score manipulation
068   fairness of downstream models is affected a lot, leading to a maximum of 16% difference in fairness
069   metric with and without influence manipulation.
070
      Summarizing, we formalize a setup for systematically manipulating influence-based attributions and
071
      instantiate it for data valuation and fairness use-cases, where adversarial incentives are involved. We
072
      provide algorithms for targeted and untargeted attacks on influence scores, and illustrate their efficacy
073   experimentally. Our work exposes the susceptibility of influence-based attributions to manipulation
074   and highlights the need for careful consideration when using them in adversarial contexts. This is
075   akin to what has been previously observed for feature attributions Bordt et al. (2022).

      3    G ENERAL T HREAT M ODEL
126
127   In this section, we give a description of our setup and general threat model. We later instantiate these
128   with two downstream applications, data valuation and fairness, where the objectives and incentives
129
      differ. While we ground our discussion on these two applications, the attacks or their slight variations
      can apply to other applications.
130
131   Setup. The standard influence function pipeline comprises of two entities: a Data Provider and
132   an Influence Calculator. Data Provider holds all the training data privately and supplies it to the
133   Influence Calculator. Influence Calculator finds the value of each sample in the training data by
134   first training a model on this data and then computing influence scores on the trained model using a
135
      separate test set (Eq.3). We assume that the test set comes from the same underlying distribution as
      the training data. Influence Calculator outputs the trained model and the influence scores of each
136
      training sample ranked in a decreasing order of influence scores. These rankings/scores are then used
137
      for a downstream application.
138
139   An adversary who has incentives in the downstream application, would want to send manipulated
140   influence scores to the downstream application. Now the question is, in which part of the IF pipeline
141
      should the adversarial manipulation occur? Turning to prior work on manipulating feature attributions
      (Anders et al., 2020; Slack et al., 2020; Heo et al., 2019; Pruthi et al., 2019), the popular choice has
142
      been to corrupt the model training process. In these attacks the compromised model training process
143
      outputs a malicious model which simultaneously has desired influence scores and is similar to the
144   unaltered original model in test accuracy, thereby making the two models indistinguishable w.r.t. test
145   predictions. Such an attack cannot be detected without access to the training pipeline or logs, making
146   it the popular choice for manipulating explanations. Motivated by this, we attack the model training
147   process in our paper and specify the resultant threat model next.
148
      General Threat Model. We consider the training data held by the data provider and the test set
149
      used by the influence calculator to be fixed. We also assume the influence calculation process to
150   be honest. The adversarial manipulation to maliciously change influence scores for some training
151   samples happens during model training. To achieve this, the compromised model training process
152   outputs a malicious model θ′ such that θ′ leads to desired influence scores but has similar test accuracy
153   as original honest model θ⋆ .
154
      Why doesn’t the influence calculator just output the desired scores/rankings? A natural tech-
155
      nique that comes to mind for manipulation of influences scores is to simply output the desired
156   scores/rankings. This would be a viable attack only if the manipulation is discreet and cannot be
157   detected; however an auditor with the ability to supply test samples can easily detect this manipulation
158   (without access to training data) by checking the rank of the outputted influence matrix in only O(d)
159   queries where d is the feature dimension. Kindly see Appendix Sec. A.0.1 for the detailed technique
160   and proof. Intuitively speaking, since honest influence scores come from a closed form (even more
161   so for logistic regression Eq. 2) and the fact that real-life learning tasks follow a structure, a lot of
      natural attacks in the influence calculation process might be detectable by an auditor with querying


                                                         3
162
      abilities. An exploration and design of non-trivial working attacks in the influence calculation process
163   makes for an interesting research direction and is left to future work.
164
165
          Takeaway : We will systematically train a malicious model which is very similar to the honest
166
          model in test accuracy, but has the desired influence scores/rankings.

      4   D OWNSTREAM A PPLICATION 1: DATA VALUATION
171
      The goal of data valuation is to determine the contribution of each training sample to model training
172
      and accordingly assign a proportional monetary sum to each. One of the techniques to find this value
173
      is through influence functions, by ranking training samples according to their influence scores in
174   a decreasing order (Richardson et al., 2019; Hesse et al., 2023; Sundararajan & Krichene, 2023;
175   Jia et al., 2019). A higher influence ranking implies a more valuable sample, resulting in a higher
176   monetary sum. Since generally data collection is a challenging task and many-a-times data may not
177   be mutable (such as DNA data in biological applications), a malicious entity with financial incentives
178   would want to manipulate influence scores in order to increase financial gains from pre-existing data.
179   See App. Fig.6 for a pictorial representation of the data valuation setting.
180   Threat Model. The canonical setting of data valuation consists of 1) multiple data vendors and
181   2) influence calculator. Each vendor supplies a set of data; the collection of data from all vendors
182   corresponds to the fixed training set of the data provider. The influence calculator is our adversary
183   who can collude with data vendors while keeping the data fixed. The adversarial model training can
184   change model parameters from θ∗ to θ′ while maintaining similar test accuracy as discussed in Sec.3.
185
      Goal of the adversary. Given a set of target samples Ztarget ⊂ Z, the goal of the adversary is to
186   push the influence ranking of samples from Ztarget to top-k or equivalently increase the influence
187   score of samples from Ztarget beyond the remaining n − k samples, where k ∈ N.
188
189

215   Efficient Backward Pass for Influence-based Objectives. A natural algorithm to solve complicated
      optimization problems as our attacks in Eq. 4 & 5 is Gradient Descent, which involves a forward


                                                                    4
216
      and backward pass. However, for influence-based attack objectives, naive gradient descent is not
217   feasible for either of the passes, mainly due to Hessian-Inverse-Vector Products (HIVPs) in the
218   influence function definition which lead to a polynomial scaling of memory and time requirements
219   w.r.t model parameters. Backward pass on our attack objectives is even harder as it involves gradients
220   of influence-based loss objectives, making the attacks too expensive even for linear models trained on
221   top of ResNet50 features used in our experiments where #parameters range from ∼76k-206k.
222
      While literature has studied ways to make the forward computation of influence functions efficient
223   Schioppa et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2020); Koh & Liang (2017); Kwon et al. (2023), not much
224   work has been done on making the backward pass efficient. To this end, we propose a simple
225   technique – rewriting the original objective into a backward-friendly form – which renders the
226   gradient computations efficient for influence-based objectives. This allows us to still use gradient
227   descent and other existing machinery in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017). Our idea of rewriting the
228   attack objective involves two essential steps : (1) linearizing the objective (2) making the linearized
229   objective backward-friendly in PyTorch, as outlined in Alg.1. This algorithm is of independent
230   technical interest and is generalizable to other use-cases where backward passes through HIVPs are
231
      needed. The complete algorithm for optimizing the loss with both forward and backward pass is
      elucidated in App. Alg. 3.

235   4.1    DATA VALUATION E XPERIMENTS
236
237   In this section, we investigate if the attacks we proposed for data valuation can succeed empirically.
238   Specifically, we ask the following questions : (1) do our influence-based attacks perform better than
239
      a non-influence baseline?, (2) what is the behavior of our attacks w.r.t. different parameters such
      as radius C and target set size? ,(3) what components contribute to the success of our attacks? and
240
      (4) lastly, can our attacks transfer to an unknown test set?. In what follows, we first explain our
241
      experimental setup and then discuss the results.
242
243   Datasets & Models. We use three standard image datasets for experimentation : CI-
244   FAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), Oxford-IIIT Pet (Parkhi et al., 2012) and Caltech-101 (Li et al.,
245
      2022). We split the respective test sets into two halves while maintaining the original class ratios for
      each. The first half is the test set shared between the model trainer and influence calculator used to
246
      optimize influence scores while the second is used as a pristine set for calculating the accuracy of
247
      models and also for transfer experiments discussed later. We pass all images through a pretrained
248   ResNet50 model (He et al., 2016) from PyTorch to obtain feature vectors of size 2048 for and train
249   linear models (θ∗ ) on top of these features with cross-entropy loss and a learning rate of 0.001.
250
251
      Attack Setup & Evaluation. The constraint function dist is set to L2-norm. Forward pass for the
      attacks is using the LiSSA Algorithm Koh & Liang (2017); details including parameters used can be
252
      found in App. Sec.A.1.2. For the Single-Target Attack, we randomly pick a training sample (which is
253
      not already in the top-k influence rankings) as the target and carry out our attack on it. We repeat this
254   process for 50 samples and report the fraction out of 50 which could be (individually) moved to top-k
255   in influence rankings as the success rate. To carry out the Multi-Target Attack, we randomly pick
256   target sets of different sizes from the training set. The success rate now is the fraction of samples in
257   the target set which could be moved top-k in influence rankings. For many of our results, the success
258   rates are reported under two regimes : (1) the high-accuracy similarity regime where the manipulated
259   and original models are within 3% accuracy difference and (2) the best success rate irrespective of
260   accuracy difference. We optimize every attack from 5 different initializations of θ⋆ within a radius of
261   C and report the runs which eventually lead to the highest success rates.
262

343   Next we discuss results for the Multi-Target Attack scenario, where the target is not a single training
344   sample, but rather a collection of multiple training samples. We investigate the following question.
345   How does our Multi-Target Attack perform with changing target set size and desired ranking
346   k? Intuitively, our attack should perform better when the size of the target set is larger compared to
347   ranking k – this is simply because a larger target set offers more candidates to take the top-k rankings
348   spots, thus increasing the chances of some of them making it to top-k. Our experimental results
349   confirm this intuition; as demonstrated in Fig.3, we observe that (1) for a fixed value of ranking k, a
350   larger target set size leads to a higher success rate; target set size of 100 has the highest success rates
351
      for all values of ranking k across the board, and (2) the success rate decreases with increasing value
      of k for all target set sizes and datasets. These results are for the high-accuracy similarity regime
352
      where the original and manipulated model accuracy differ by less than 3%.

356   influence rankings are easier to push to top-k rankings upon manipulation (or equivalently samples
357

425   5    D OWNSTREAM A PPLICATION 2: FAIRNESS
426
427   Recently, a lot of studies have used influence functions in different ways to achieve fair models (Li
428   & Liu, 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Sattigeri et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2024; Chhabra
429   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yao & Liu, 2023; Ghosh et al., 2023). In our paper, we focus on the
430   study by Li & Liu (2022) as they use the same definition of influence functions as us. The suggested
431   approach in Li & Liu (2022) to achieve a fair model is by reweighing training data based on influence
      scores for a base model and then using this reweighed data to train a new downstream model from


                                                               8
432
      scratch. This downstream model is expected to have high fairness as a result of the reweighing. For a
433   pictorial representation of this process, see App. Fig. 8. The weights for training data are found by
434   solving an optimization problem which places influence functions in the constraints. See Appendix
435   Sec.A.2 for more details on the optimization problem.
436
      Ultimately, weights of the training data determine the fairness of the downstream model. Since these
437
      weights are derived from influence scores, manipulating the influence scores can alter the fairness of
438
      the downstream model. As a result, a malicious entity who wants to spread unfairness is incentivized
439   to manipulate influence scores.
440
441   Threat Model. Similar to the general setup, training and test set are fixed, influence calculator is
442
      assumed to be the adversary. Model trained by the influence calculator is now the base model used by
      the reweighing pipeline. The adversarial model training can tamper the base model parameters from
443
      θ∗ to θ′ to manipulate the influence scores while maintaining similar test accuracy.
444
445   Goal of the adversary. Fairness of the final downstream model is measured with a fairness met-
446   ric. The concrete goal of the adversary is to make the value of this fairness metric worse for the
447   downstream model than what could have been achieved without adversarial manipulation.
448   Attack. Since the goal of the adversary in this case is not tied to specific target samples, we propose
449   an untargeted attack for the adversary. Our attack is deceptively simple – scale the base model θ⋆ by a
450   constant λ > 0. The malicious base model output by the model trainer is now θ′ = λ · θ⋆ , instead of
451   θ⋆ . Note that for logistic regression the malicious and original base model are indistinguishable since
452   scaling with a positive constant maintains the sign of the predictions, leading to the same accuracy.
453
454   5.1   FAIRNESS M ANIPULATION E XPERIMENTS
455
456
      We investigate experimentally if our scaling attack can be successful in reducing fairness of the
      final downstream model. We use three standard fairness benchmarks : Adult Credit Becker &
457
      Kohavi (1996), German Credit Hofmann (1994) and Compas Angwin et al. (2022). For details about
458
      these datasets, see Appendix Table 3. We train L2-regularized logistic regression models for these
459   datasets which act as base models for the training data reweighing pipeline. To carry out our fairness
460   manipulation attack, we scale the models weights with different scaling coefficients and feed these
461   scaled models to the reweighing pipeline. The reweighed training data is then used to train a final
462   downstream model, which is expected to be fair. Parameter values for L2 regularization and the
463   reweighing are same as in Li & Liu (2022) and can be found in Appendix Tables 4 and 3.
464
      Evaluation. We evaluate the fairness of the final downstream model with a popular fairness metric,
465   called demographic parity (DP) (See Appendix Def. 2 for definition). A higher value of DP gap
466   implies less fairness and vice-versa. We call the attack a success if the final downstream model
467   post-attack is less fair (higher DP gap) than the final model achieved without attacking. Additionally,
468   we expect the test accuracies of the downstream model with and without attack to be similar to keep
469   the manipulation discreet. Note that this is different from our threat model’s indistinguishability
470   constraint, which applies to the base model, not to the downstream model.
471   Results. As can be seen from our results in Fig.5, the scaling attack works surprisingly well across all
472   datasets – downstream models achieved after our attack are considerably less fair (higher DP gap) than
473   the models without attack, achieving a maximum difference of 16% in the DP gap. Simultaneously,
474   downstream models post-attack maintain similar test accuracies to downstream models without
475   attack. Since the process to achieve the downstream model involves a lot of steps, including solving
476   a non-convex optimization problem to find training data weights and then retraining a model, we
477   sometimes do not see a smooth monotonic trend in fairness metric values w.r.t. scaling coefficients.
478   However, this does not matter much from the attacker’s perspective as all the attacker needs is one
479
      scaling coefficient which meets the attack success criteria.

507   7                               R ELATED W ORK
508
509   Fragility of Influence Functions. Influence functions proposed in Koh & Liang (2017) are an
510   approximation to the effect of upweighting a training sample on the loss at a test point. This
511   approximation error can be large as shown by (Basu et al., 2020; Bae et al., 2022; Epifano et al.,
512   2023), making influence functions fragile especially for deep learning models. Our work is orthogonal
513   to this line of work as we study the robustness of influence functions w.r.t. model parameters instead
514   of approximation error of influence functions w.r.t. the true influence.
515   Model Manipulation in the Threat Model. Manipulating models to execute attacks is a prevalent
516   theme in the literature. Slack et al. (2020) use model manipulations to corrupt feature attributions
517   in tabular data while (Heo et al., 2019; Anders et al., 2020) do so in vision models. Pruthi et al.
518   (2019) corrupt attention-based explanations for language models while maintaining model accuracy.
519   Shahin Shamsabadi et al. (2022) show that it is possible to corrupt a fairness metric by manipulating
520   an interpretable surrogate of a black-box model while maintaining empirical performance of the
521   surrogate. Similar to these, our threat model also allows the adversary to manipulate models while
522   maintaining the test accuracy. However, our adversarial goal is to corrupt influence-based attributions.
523   Data Manipulation Attack on Explanations. (Ghorbani et al., 2019; Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola,
524   2018; Zhang et al., 2020; Dombrowski et al., 2019; Kindermans et al., 2019) have studied how data
525   can be manipulated to corrupt feature attributions. On the contrary, firstly, we keep data fixed and
526   manipulate the model and secondly, we work with data attributions rather than feature attributions.
527
528
529   8                               C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
530
531   While past work has mostly focused on feature attributions, in this paper we exhibit realistic incentives
532   to manipulate data attributions. Motivated by the incentives, we propose attacks to manipulate outputs
533   from a popular data attribution tool – Influence Functions. We demonstrate the success of our attacks
534   experimentally on multiclass logistic regression models on ResNet features and standard tabular
535   fairness datasets. Our work lays bare the vulnerablility of influence-based attributions to manipulation
536
      and serves as a cautionary tale when using them in adversarial circumstances.
537   While logistic regression is a good starting point for formulating and solving a new problem and
538   these models are still relevant in many domains, we do think attacking influence functions for large
539   models is an interesting avenue for future research. Some other future directions include exploring
      different threat models, additional use-cases and manipulating other kinds of data attribution tools.
