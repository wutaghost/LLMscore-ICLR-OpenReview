011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Complex systems with nonlinear dynamics pose significant challenges for finite-
014            time optimal control, especially when accurate system models are unavailable.
015            This paper introduces DIFOCON (DIffusion Finite-time Optimal CONtrol), a
016
               novel data-driven framework for finite-time optimal control that operates with-
               out prior knowledge of system parameters or dynamics. DIFOCON reformu-
017
               lates the control problem as a generative task, optimizing control signal trajec-
018
               tories to guide systems to target states within a finite time. Our approach uti-
019            lizes a diffusion model with a dual-Unet architecture to capture nonlinear system
020            dynamics and generate entire control sequences in a single step. Additionally,
021            an inverse dynamics module is integrated to ensure that the generated control
022            signals are appropriate for complex systems. To further enhance performance,
023            we propose a retraining strategy that improves out-of-distribution generalization.
024            Experiments on two nonlinear complex systems demonstrate DIFOCON’s supe-
025            rior performance, reducing target loss by over 26.9% and control energy by over
026            15.8% compared to baselines while achieving up to 4 times faster convergence
027
               in practical steering tasks. The implementation of this work can be found at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DIFOCON-C019/.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Complex systems are composed of interacting components that exhibit emergent behaviors and non-
      linear dynamics (Ladyman et al., 2013). Control problems are fundamental in both natural and
034
      engineering systems, where the goal is to direct system behavior toward desired outcomes by de-
035
      termining appropriate inputs. Finite-time control, which requires systems to reach objectives within
036   a limited time, is particularly critical but challenging, especially when accurate system models are
037   unavailable.
038
039
      Existing control methodologies predominantly rely on precise system models to achieve desired out-
      comes (Yan et al., 2012; Lindmark & Altafini, 2018; Liu et al., 2011; Gao et al., 2014). However,
040
      in real-world applications, obtaining accurate system equations and parameters is often impractical,
041
      especially for complex systems with nonlinear dynamics. While a few efforts toward model-free
042   approaches have been made, significant challenges remain. Feedback-based control methods, such
043   as PID and reinforcement learning (Li et al., 2006; Hwang et al., 2022; Pomerleau, 1988; Haarnoja
044   et al., 2018), rely on continuous cycles of sensing, processing, and decision-making, which makes
045   them computationally intensive and often unsuitable for high-dimensional, finite-time control prob-
046   lems. Baggio et al. (2021) developed an optimal control solution for linear systems by implicitly
047   estimating system equations and parameters from empirical data, but their approach struggles when
048   applied to nonlinear systems.
049   In this paper, we propose a novel framework for finite-time optimal control, DIffusion Finite-time
050   Optimal CONtrol (DIFOCON), which operates solely based on data, without requiring prior knowl-
051   edge of system parameters or dynamics. By reframing the control problem as a generative task, our
052   approach seeks to optimize the trajectory of control signals that guide the system to its target state
053   within a finite time. Through data-driven generative modeling, DIFOCON learns the distribution
      of control signals and observed states, generating complete control sequences in a single step, thus


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      eliminating the need for continuous feedback-based interaction with the system. This makes our
055   approach highly efficient and well-suited for high-dimensional, nonlinear systems.
056
057   The core of DIFOCON is built on a diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) that progressively refines noisy
058
      inputs into high-quality control sequences conditioned on desired system outcomes. To capture non-
      linear dynamics, we introduce a dual-Unet architecture with residual connections in the denoising
059
      network, which accounts for both first-order and higher-order expansions, based on optimal con-
060
      trol theory initially developed for linear systems. To ensure the generated signals are well-suited
061   for complex systems with unknown dynamics, we integrate an inverse dynamics module, which
062   computes control signals from denoised states, rather than directly sampling both control signals
063   and intermediate states using the denoising network. Additionally, to enhance the model’s abil-
064   ity to generate out-of-distribution data from the training data, we propose a retraining method that
065   fine-tunes the model on its own generated samples, thereby expanding the exploration space and
066   improving generalization.
067   Our experiments on two typical nonlinear complex systems (Acebrón et al., 2005; Susuki et al.,
068   2011) demonstrate DIFOCON’s superior performance in solving finite-time optimal control prob-
069   lems. Specifically, we show that DIFOCON achieves the best in-distribution performance, reducing
070   target loss by over 26.9% and control energy by 15.8% compared to the best-performing baseline
071   when applied to systems governed by first-order and second-order ordinary differential equations.
072   Moreover, our method exhibits, at most, around 4 times faster convergence in practical steering tasks
073   compared to existing model-free approaches. These results validate the efficacy of our diffusion-
074   based generative model in addressing the challenges of finite-time control in complex, real-world
075
      systems.

077   2     R ELATED W ORKS
078
079   2.1   M ODEL - FREE C ONTROL OF C OMPLEX S YSTEMS
080
081   Model-free control approaches circumvent the need for an exact model by leveraging control data
082   to capture the system’s dynamics implicitly. These methods can be categorized into closed-loop and
083   finite-time control strategies.
084   Closed-loop control methods. Classical closed-loop control methods like Proportional-Integral-
085   Derivative (PID) (Li et al., 2006) are famous for their steadiness and efficiency but face challenges
086   in adaptability in high-dimensional complex scenarios. In the realm of deep learning, reinforcement
087   learning (Pomerleau, 1988; Haarnoja et al., 2018) has recently demonstrated its effectiveness in se-
088   quential decision making, and supervised learning methods (Hwang et al., 2022) show their adapt-
089   ability by using neural surrogate models to learn control sequences. However, the above closed-loop
090
      control methods are predominantly employed for stabilization or tracking tasks, yet they fall short
      when applied to real-world scenarios where control operations are subject to time constraints.
091
092   Finite-time control methods. Finite time control methods (Baggio et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2024),
093   on the other hand, optimize the control sequence over the entire horizon, thus addressing the myopic
094   nature of closed-loop approaches and are suitable for tasks that require control of system states
095   within a finite time. Notably, Baggio et al. (2021) proposed an analytical method that leverages data
096
      to determine the optimal input for steady-state control of complex networks, without knowing the
      dynamics. However, its foundation in linear systems theory restricts its generalization to nonlinear
097
      dynamics, impeding its ability to accurately steer the system toward the desired state.
098
099
100
      2.2   D IFFUSION M ODEL
101
      Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020) have gathered significant attention for
102   their ability to generate high-quality and consistent samples across various domains such as im-
103   age, audio, and video, achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) results (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Kong
104   et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022). These models have also demonstrated their capability in traditional
105   mathematical and engineering problems, including optimization (Krishnamoorthy et al., 2023; Sun
106   & Yang, 2023), inverse problems (Chung et al., 2022), robotic control (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay
107   et al., 2022), etc. Wei et al. (2024) introduced DiffPhyCon, a finite time control method that har-
      nesses generative diffusion models to directly optimize system trajectories and control sequences


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      over the entire horizon. This approach implicitly captures the inherent constraints within the system
109   dynamics and employs reweighting techniques during the sampling process to guide the generation
110   of optimal trajectories that deviate from the distribution. However, by implicitly capturing dynam-
111   ics across the entire trajectory, this method overlooks the Markovian nature of time-invariant sys-
112   tems, and the control sequences are often non-smooth, making it challenging for diffusion models
113   to model their distribution accurately. Our proposed method employs a parameterized inverse dy-
114   namics model to explicitly model the relationship between actions and states, allowing the generated
115   control sequences to more accurately guide the evolution of system states.

      3.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS
138
139
      Diffusion models have emerged as a powerful class of generative models in recent years, demonstrat-
140   ing remarkable capabilities in image synthesis, audio generation, and other domains (Ho et al., 2020;
141   Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). These models are based on the principle of gradually adding noise to
142   data and then learning to reverse this process. In the forward process, a data point x0 is progressively
143   corrupted through T timesteps, resulting in a sequence of increasingly noisypversions x1 , x2 , ..., xK .
144   Each step applies a small amount of Gaussian noise:q(xk |xk−1 ) = N (xk ; 1 − β k xk−1 , β k I),
145
146
      where β k is a variance schedule that controls the noise level. In the reverse process, the model learns
      to gradually denoise the data, starting from pure noise xK and working backward to reconstruct the
147
      original data point x0 . This is modeled as:pθ (xk−1 |xk ) = N (xk−1 ; µθ (xk , k), Σθ (xk , k)), where
148
      θ represents the learnable parameters of the model. The training objective for diffusion models
149   typically involves minimizing the variational lower bound (VLB) on the negative log-likelihood
150   (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015). In practice, this often reduces to a form of denoising score matching
151   (Song & Ermon, 2019).

227
      of two consecutive 1-D U-nets. 1-D Unet is a combination of multiple 1-D convolutional layers. The
228
      dimension of τ (M + N ) corresponds to the channel dimension of the CNN. The U-Net effectively
229   captures the relationship between control signals and system observation states along physical time
230   dimention, thus better modeling the complete trajectory distribution (Janner et al., 2022). Condition
231   labels r and diffusion time steps k are encoded through MLPs as remb and kemb , respectively, and
232   concatenated as input to the U-Net. For initial and target states y0 and yf condition, we found they
233   significantly decide the optimal trajectory/control signal distribution. Therefore, we use them to
234   explicitly adapt the network’s output at positions close to the output.
235   Yan et al. (2012) suggests that for linear systems, the optimal control signal has a linear relationship
236   with yc = yf − Ay0 (a linear combination of initial and target states, A is related to the network’s
237   parameter). We consider this linear operation in our model as the first-order expansion of optimal
238   control with respect to yc for nonlinear systems, with coefficients learned through the first U-Net.
239   Correspondingly, we attempt to add another Unet to learn the coefficients of higher-order (second-
240   order) expansions at the back end, fine-tuning the first-order results through residual connections.

      4.3   R ETRAINING
271
272   Randomly generated training data cannot guarantee coverage of optimal scenarios. To generate
273   near-optimal controls that may deviate significantly from the training distribution, we need to guide
274   the model in generating out-of-distribution data. On one hand, classifier-free guidance can make the
275   generated distribution deviate from the original distribution. Therefore, we can use the data initially
276   generated by the model, which has already deviated from the training distribution and tends towards
277   the optimal distribution, to retrain the model and expand its exploration space. This approach is
278
      predicated on ensuring the quality of generated data, at least guaranteeing that the samples generated
      by the model conform to the underlying dynamics.

287
288
      Our experimental design aims to address three key research questions: (1) Can DiffCon demonstrate
289   superiority over current finite-time optimal control methods for complex systems control? (2) Can
290   DiffCon be generalized to practical cases where the task could be more challenging and out-of-
291   distribution of training data? (3) Do the proposed designs help DiffCon achieve better performance?

293   5.1   DATASETS
294
295   For our experiments, we prepared two complex system datasets. The data in both sets were collected
296   by randomly initializing the system states and injecting control sequences to observe the resulting
297   system behavior. Our main experiments were conducted in two representative scenarios: a ring
298   system governed by the Kuramoto dynamics model (Acebrón et al., 2005), described by first-order
299   ordinary differential equations, and the New England power-grid network system, governed by the
300
      Swing dynamics model, described by second-order ordinary differential equations (Susuki et al.,
      2011).
301
302   The Kuramoto model, as an abstract and classical model, is widely applied to various synchroniza-
303   tion phenomena, such as biological rhythms and brain networks (Tang et al., 2014). It is known
304   for its ability to capture the essence of synchronization in coupled oscillators, making it suitable for
305   studying emergent collective behavior. The Swing model, on the other hand, is a fundamental rep-
306
      resentation of power system dynamics, crucial for understanding stability and control in electrical
      grids. It incorporates more physical constraints and interactions typical in power systems, including
307
      inertia and damping effects (Susuki et al., 2011). These two models exhibit different characteristics
308
      in terms of complexity and nonlinearity. The Kuramoto model offers insights into abstract synchro-
309   nization problems, while the Swing model provides a more concrete, application-oriented scenario.
310   Both models present a balance of challenge and representativeness, making them ideal for our study.
311
312
      We collected data by randomly generating initial states and control signals from Gaussian distri-
      butions. The resulting dataset was then partitioned into training and test sets for the experiments
313
      presented in Table 2, thus evaluating the in-distribution performance of each method. Note that
314
      these data points are not necessarily optimal. For a detailed description of the experimental setup,
315   please refer to the appendix.

453   6                 C ONCLUSION
454
455   In this paper, we proposed DIFOCON, a novel diffusion model-based framework for finite-time
456   optimal control in complex systems with nonlinear dynamics. By reformulating the control problem
457   as a generative task, DIFOCON leverages a dual-Unet architecture to capture nonlinear dynamics
458   and generate complete control sequences without relying on system models or parameters. Our
459   approach integrates an inverse dynamics module and a retraining method to enhance performance
460   on complex systems and improve out-of-distribution generalization. Experiments on two typical
461   nonlinear complex systems demonstrate DIFOCON’s superior performance, achieving significant
462   reductions in target loss and control energy while exhibiting faster convergence than existing model-
463
      free approaches. These results underscore DIFOCON’s effectiveness as a data-driven solution for
      finite-time optimal control in complex, nonlinear systems where traditional model-based methods
464
      fall short. This work shows the potential of data-driven generative models in addressing finite-time
465
      optimal control challenges in complex systems where accurate models are unavailable.
