009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in the field of in-context learning (ICL) have demonstrated im-
012            pressive performance for tabular classification, exemplified by TAB PFN’s success
013            on small datasets. However, the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism
014            limits its applicability to larger datasets. To address this issue, we conduct a com-
015            prehensive comparison of popular scalable attention alternatives, including state-
016            space models (SSMs) and linear attention mechanisms, revealing that the inherent
017            causality of SSMs hinders ICL performance for large datasets, while linear atten-
018
               tion preserves effectiveness. Leveraging these insights, we introduce TAB F LEX,
               a model based on linear attention that supports thousands of features and hun-
019
               dreds of classes, capable of handling datasets with millions of samples. Extensive
020
               experiments demonstrate that TAB F LEX is significantly faster than most existing
021            methods while achieving top-two performance on small datasets among 25 base-
022            lines, with a 2× speedup over TAB PFN and a 1.5× speedup over XGBoost. On
023            large datasets, TAB F LEX remains efficient (e.g., approximately 5 seconds on the
024            poker-hand dataset, which consists of millions of samples), while achieving
025            relatively solid performance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, Large language Models (LLMs) have achieved breakthroughs not only in language
031   tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2023a; Dubey et al., 2024; Gemini Team
032   et al., 2023) but also in handling diverse data modalities, including vision (Bai et al., 2023b; Gem-
033
      ini Team et al., 2023) and audio (Chu et al., 2023; 2024; Gemini Team et al., 2023). Their success
      stems from the underlying transformer architecture, which uses attention mechanisms (Vaswani
034
      et al., 2017) to capture complex patterns in data. Consequently, researchers have begun exploring
035
      the potential of transformers in traditional machine learning tasks, particularly tabular classification.
036   Tabular data represents one of the most fundamental and critical types of information encountered
037   in real-world applications, spanning domains such as recommendation systems (Zhang et al., 2019),
038   finance (Arun et al., 2016), and medicine (Johnson et al., 2016).
039
      Numerous efforts have been made to adapt Transformers for tabular classification tasks (Arik &
040
      Pfister, 2021; Hollmann et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2020; Dinh et al., 2022; Gorishniy et al., 2021).
041
      For instance, FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021) introduces a feature tokenizer to convert each
042   example into a sequence of embeddings, then utilizes a Transformer to process these and make pre-
043   dictions via a special CLS token. TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020) employs the Transformer
044   architecture to learn embeddings for categorical features, concatenating them with continuous fea-
045   tures for improved accuracy. LIFT (Dinh et al., 2022) converts tabular datasets into sentences that
046   include feature names and task descriptions, utilizing fine-tuned large language models for predic-
047   tions. Unfortunately, these aforementioned methods, along with non-Transformer neural network
048   approaches (e.g., Multilayer Perceptron (Rumelhart et al., 1986) and ResNet (He et al., 2016)), suf-
049   fer from a common inefficiency compared to gradient-boosted trees methods. Their large model
050
      sizes result in longer training and inference times.
051   As a Transformer-based method, TAB PFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) stands out for its superior perfor-
052   mance and efficiency on small datasets. It leverages a key capability of LLMs: in-context learning
053   (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020), which enables LLMs to learn from a few examples and make predic-
      tions for new test instances without needing parameter updates. TAB PFN employs a customized


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ICL implementation that processes all training and testing samples in a single prompt, complet-
055   ing classification for all test samples in one forward pass. This approach enables rapid predictions
056   within seconds for simple, small tabular datasets, making it highly efficient and effective on such
057   tasks. However, TAB PFN faces challenges with complex datasets that typically demand larger sam-
058   ple sizes for effective learning, primarily due to scalability limitations imposed by the quadratic
059   complexity of the attention mechanism. This constraint introduces difficulties in both scalable pre-
060   training and inference processes.
061   In this paper, we address the scalability limitations of TAB PFN and enhance the competitiveness
062   of neural network-based methods for tabular classification. In doing so, we investigate scalable
063   alternatives to traditional attention mechanisms, focusing on state-space models (SSMs), includ-
064   ing the recently popular Mamba model (Gu & Dao, 2024), and linear attention (Katharopoulos
065   et al., 2020). Our analysis reveals that (Finding 1) the inherent causality of SSMs impedes ICL
066   performance compared to non-causal mechanisms. In contrast, (Finding 2) linear attention does
067   not suffer from this limitation, maintaining comparable performance while improving computational
068   efficiency. Based on these findings, we develop our model, TAB F LEX, which leverages linear atten-
069
      tion. It comprises three sub-models, each optimized for different scenarios, with the most suitable
      one selected based on dataset characteristics (e.g., sample size). This model supports thousands of
070
      features, hundreds of classes, and millions of samples. We conduct comprehensive experiments with
071
      TAB F LEX across a diverse range of datasets, including small, large, and high-dimensional datasets.
072   (Finding 3) TAB F LEX demonstrates robust performance with impressive computational efficiency.
073   Notably, on the poker-hand dataset, which contains over one million samples, TAB F LEX clas-
074   sifies all instances in less than 5 seconds while achieving competitive performance. Furthermore,
075   beyond traditional tabular datasets, TAB F LEX can also label all samples of MNIST (LeCun et al.,
076   2010) and Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) in less than one second. This highlights TAB F LEX
077   as a pioneering approach towards accelerating Transformer-based models for high-dimensional and
078   large-scale datasets, with promising potential for further advancements.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
081
082
083
      Transformer-based Approaches for Tabular Classification. Recent years have witnessed nu-
      merous attempts to employ Transformers for tabular classification (Arik & Pfister, 2021; Huang
084
      et al., 2020; Gorishniy et al., 2021; Dinh et al., 2022; Hollmann et al., 2023). These methods uti-
085
      lize Transformers in diverse ways to tackle tabular data. TabNet (Arik & Pfister, 2021), one of
086   the pioneering efforts, applies unsupervised pre-training on masked tabular datasets to infer miss-
087   ing features, thereby enhancing the model’s understanding of datasets and features. It then per-
088   forms supervised learning on feature selection to obtain the final decision boundary, akin to deci-
089   sion trees. Huang et al. (2020) introduced TabTransformer, which leverages Transformers to better
090   handle categorical features by concatenating their contextual embeddings with numerical features.
091   FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021) introduces a feature tokenizer to convert each example into
092   a sequence of embeddings, enabling Transformers to process tabular datasets and make predictions.
093   LIFT (Dinh et al., 2022) utilizes a pre-trained language model with parameter-efficient fine-tuning,
094
      incorporating task descriptions and converting each sample into a complete sentence with feature
      names in the prediction prompt. TAB PFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) is trained offline on synthetic
095
      datasets derived from prior distributions and performs ICL rather than additional parameter tuning
096
      for a given dataset, enabling it to solve small tabular classification tasks within seconds. Prior to
097   our work, TuneTable (Feuer et al., 2024) extended TAB PFN to scale to large datasets by performing
098   prefix-tuning for each dataset to achieve better performance. Notably, while most of these methods
099   are computationally intensive due to the need for training large models, TAB PFN achieves effi-
100   ciency through ICL. Our method builds upon TAB PFN, extending its scalability to large datasets
101   while maintaining and even improving its efficiency.
102
103   Attention Mechanisms and Scalable Alternatives. While attention in Transformers (Vaswani
104   et al., 2017) is central to the strong performance of language models, it encounters scaling chal-
105   lenges for long sequences due to its quadratic computational and memory complexity. To overcome
106   these limitations, several scalable alternatives have been proposed (Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu,
107   2024; Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2023; Orvieto et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023), all aiming
      to achieve subquadratic time complexity. Classical RNNs offer one potential solution, providing


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      efficient linear-time inference. However, they struggle with training efficiency and lack the paral-
109   lelization capabilities of Transformer architectures. Linear attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020)
110   addresses both concerns by reformulating self-attention as a linear dot-product of kernel feature
111   maps, reducing the computational complexity from quadratic to linear time. Additionally, causal
112   linear attention can be interpreted as a form of RNN, as the model makes predictions based on a
113   current token and a “hidden state,” which summarizes information from the previous tokens. State-
114   space models (SSMs), another popular variant of RNNs, address the drawbacks of classical RNNs
115   by considering linear RNNs and proposing novel algorithms for efficient training (Gu et al., 2021;
116   2022; Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu, 2024; Peng et al., 2023; Orvieto et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023).
117   Dao et al. (2022) identified that another bottleneck in attention mechanisms’ speed stems from the
118   relatively slow access to high-bandwidth memory (HBM) in GPUs. To address this limitation,
119   FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2024; Shah et al., 2024) restructures attention computation
120   to optimize the utilization of high-speed on-chip SRAM while minimizing access to slower HBM,
121   thereby enhancing the efficiency of GPU-based attention operations. FlashAttention strategically
122   balances computational efficiency against memory bandwidth efficiency. Although the computa-
123
      tional complexity in terms of sequence length remains quadratic, the optimizations introduced by
      FlashAttention significantly accelerate attention computation in wall-clock time.
124
125   We provide extended related works in Sec. A, which offers an in-depth discussion of other base-
126   lines, encompassing classical machine learning methods, gradient-boosting decision trees, and non-
127   transformer neural network architectures tailored for tabular classification tasks.

165   State Space Models (SSMs). Recently, SSMs have emerged as highly promising alternatives to
166   the attention mechanism, exhibiting linear computational complexity and demonstrating excellent
167   performance in language modeling tasks. The SSM framework is based on a continuous system
168   that transforms a one-dimensional signal x(t) ∈ R into y(t) ∈ R through an intermediate H-
169   dimensional latent state h(t) ∈ RH , as shown in (1). Here, B ∈ RH×1 is the input transition
170   vector and A ∈ RH×H is the state transition matrix. The latent state h(t) is then projected into the
171   output y(t) using the output mapping vector C ∈ R1×H . For deep learning applications, discrete A
172   and B replace continuous A and B through discretization methods, such as zero-order hold. This
173   yields updated hidden state and output equations as shown in (2). While (2) is structured as linear
174   RNN, it can be reformulated as Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as (3), enabling efficient
175
      and parallelizable training. SSMs address the quadratic time complexity problem with respect to
      sequence length, as the output for each new token depends solely on the hidden states and the current
176
      token, in contrast to standard attention mechanisms that attend to all previous tokens. Consequently,
177
      SSMs operate as a causal mechanism.

           5   TAB F LEX : S CALING TAB PFN FOR L ARGE DATASETS
288
           Based on the empirical findings presented in Sec. 4, we identify non-causal linear attention as the
289
           optimal candidate to replace standard softmax attention in TAB PFN. This section proceeds in two
290
           parts: first, we conduct a thorough analysis of the linear attention mechanism to ensure its effi-
291        cient implementation.; subsequently, we leverage this efficient implementation to train our proposed
292        model, TAB F LEX. Our approach aims to enhance the scalability and performance of tabular learning
293        while maintaining computational efficiency.
294
295        Computation Analysis. Dao et al. (2022) demonstrates that significant wallclock speedup for soft-
296        max attention can be achieved by optimizing the number of memory reads/writes between GPU high
297        bandwidth memory (HBM) and GPU on-chip SRAM. Based on this criterion, Yang et al. (2024) pro-
298        posed FlashLinearAttention for speeding up causal linear attention. This raises a natural question:
299        can we further improve the speed of non-causal linear attention (we omit non-causal when it does not
300        cause further confusion) by reducing the number of memory reads/writes? Our results in Theorem 1
301        analyze the #HBM access and HBM memory usage of FlashLinearAttention and linear attention,
302
           concluding that further optimization is not necessary. In Sec. D, we first propose an HBM-efficient
           linear attention, and then show that the PyTorch implementation only incurs a marginal increase in
303
           terms of #HBM access and HBM memory usage, with FLOPS remaining unchanged. We provide
304
           more details, including the analysis of different attention mechanisms and actual memory usage and
305        runtime visualization of these mechanisms in Sec. D. The resulting theorem below demonstrates that
306        the straightforward PyTorch implementation of linear attention already achieves linear HBM access,
307        matching the performance of FlashLinearAttention after optimization. Consequently, we adopt the
308        straightforward implementation of linear attention in our model.
309

311        length and D is the embedding size. Both causal FlashLinearAttention (Alg. 2) and non-causal
312        linear attention (Listing 1) require O(N D) HBM accesses, O(N D) HBM memory, and O(N D2 )
313        FLOPS to compute the attention output.

      7      C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
518
      Conclusion. To extend TAB PFN for ICL on larger and more challenging tabular classification
519
      tasks, in this paper, we conduct a comprehensive exploration of scalable alternatives to attention,
520
      ultimately selecting non-causal linear attention. Through computational analysis for algorithmic op-
521   timization of the implementation of linear attention, we develop our model, TAB F LEX. We demon-
522   strate that TAB F LEX achieves comparable performance to TAB PFN on small datasets with more
523   than 2× speedup, while outperforming most other baselines with significantly reduced computa-
524   tional time. Moreover, TAB F LEX significantly outperforms TAB PFN on larger and more complex
525   datasets, becoming much faster than most other baselines on datasets larger than 100K samples,
526   while maintaining performance on par with state-of-the-art methods. We posit that TAB F LEX fur-
527   ther elevates the performance ceiling of neural network-based models on tabular classification tasks.
528
529   Limitations & Future Works. While our work achieves fast inference and relatively well perfor-
530   mance on datasets with approximately two thousand features, extending it to scale to more features
531
      remains an intriguing research direction. Notably, image classification tasks typically involve a large
      number of features. Adapting our work for image classification could lead to broader applications,
532
      given its extremely fast inference and ability to simultaneously output labels for all test samples,
533
      making this a promising avenue for future research. For image classification, one potential approach
534   could involve using a visual encoder to preprocess the images before feeding them into our model —
535   a strategy that may prove effective. Beyond image datasets, extending our work to other modalities
536   such as audio classification is also of interest. This expansion might necessitate developing novel
537   methods for generating synthetic datasets for model pretraining, as well as conducting comprehen-
538   sive analyses on the impact of various hyperparameters such as the number of layers and embedding
539   size. Such investigations would optimize the model architecture to effectively handle an increased
      number of features, potentially broadening the applicability of our approach across diverse domains.

      A    E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
784
785
      Classical Machine Learning Approaches for Tabular Classification. Classical machine learn-
      ing algorithms have long been the foundation of tabular data classification. These methods in-
786
      clude k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) (Cover & Hart, 1967), Logistic Regression (Cox, 1958), Decision
787
      Trees (Quinlan, 1986), and Support Vector Machines (SVM) (Cortes, 1995). These classical mod-
788   els, while effective, often struggle to handle complex, high-dimensional tabular datasets, motivating
789   the development of more sophisticated approaches.
790
791   Gradient-Boosting Decision Trees for Tabular Classification Gradient-boosting decision trees
792   (GBDTs) (Friedman, 2001) have emerged as a cornerstone in tabular classification, owing to their
793   exceptional ability to capture intricate patterns in structured data. By iteratively combining predic-
794   tions from weak learners, GBDTs refine their outputs to minimize errors, resulting in high predic-
795   tive accuracy. XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) introduced weighted quantile sketching, advanced
796   regularization techniques, and sparsity-awareness, achieving state-of-the-art performance. Light-
797   GBM (Ke et al., 2017), a computationally efficient GBDT implementation, employs Gradient-based
798
      One-Side Sampling and a leaf-wise tree growth strategy. CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018)
      leverages symmetric trees and introduces ordered boosting, with a particular emphasis on effec-
799
      tively handling categorical features. These advancements have rendered GBDTs not only powerful
800
      but also versatile tools in the domain of tabular data, dominating tabular classification in terms of
801   both speed and performance until the advent of TAB PFN.
802
803
      Transformer-based Approaches for Tabular Classification. Recent years have witnessed nu-
804   merous attempts to employ Transformers for tabular classification (Arik & Pfister, 2021; Huang
805   et al., 2020; Gorishniy et al., 2021; Dinh et al., 2022; Hollmann et al., 2023). These methods uti-
806   lize Transformers in diverse ways to tackle tabular data. TabNet (Arik & Pfister, 2021), one of
807   the pioneering efforts, applies unsupervised pre-training on masked tabular datasets to infer miss-
808   ing features, thereby enhancing the model’s understanding of datasets and features. It then per-
809   forms supervised learning on feature selection to obtain the final decision boundary, akin to decision
      trees. Huang et al. (2020) introduced TabTransformer, which leverages Transformers to better handle


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      categorical features by concatenating their contextual embeddings with numerical features. While
811   TabTransformer processes categorical and continuous features separately, SAINT (Somepalli et al.,
812   2021) projects both feature types into a shared embedding space before passing them through trans-
813   former blocks, thereby enhancing overall performance. FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021)
814   introduces a feature tokenizer to convert each example into a sequence of embeddings, enabling
815   Transformers to process tabular datasets and make predictions. LIFT (Dinh et al., 2022) utilizes
816   a pre-trained language model with parameter-efficient fine-tuning, incorporating task descriptions
817   and converting each sample into a complete sentence with feature names in the prediction prompt.
818   TAB PFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) is trained offline on synthetic datasets derived from prior distri-
819
      butions and performs ICL rather than additional parameter tuning for a given dataset, enabling it to
      solve small tabular classification tasks within seconds. Prior to our work, TuneTable (Feuer et al.,
820
      2024) extended TAB PFN to scale to large datasets by performing prefix-tuning for each dataset to
821
      achieve better performance. Notably, while most of these methods are computationally intensive due
822   to the need for training large models, TAB PFN achieves efficiency through ICL. Our method builds
823   upon TAB PFN, extending its scalability to large datasets while maintaining and even improving its
824   efficiency.
825
826
827   Attention Mechanisms and Scalable Alternatives. While attention in Transformers (Vaswani
828
      et al., 2017) is central to the strong performance of language models, it encounters scaling chal-
      lenges for long sequences due to its quadratic computational and memory complexity. To overcome
829
      these limitations, several scalable alternatives have been proposed (Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu,
830
      2024; Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2023; Orvieto et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023), all aim-
831   ing to achieve subquadratic time complexity. In contrast, classical RNNs provide the advantage of
832   efficient linear-time inference but suffer from limitations in training efficiency, lacking the paral-
833   lelization capabilities of Transformer architectures. Linear attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020)
834   addresses both concerns by reformulating self-attention as a linear dot-product of kernel feature
835   maps, reducing the computational complexity from quadratic to linear time. Additionally, causal
836   linear attention can be interpreted as a form of RNN, as the model makes predictions based on a
837   current token and a “hidden state,” which summarizes information from the previous tokens. State-
838   space models (SSMs), another popular variant of RNNs, address the drawbacks of classical RNNs
839
      by considering linear RNNs and proposing novel algorithms for efficient training (Gu et al., 2021;
      2022; Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu, 2024; Peng et al., 2023; Orvieto et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023).
840
841   Dao et al. (2022) identified that another bottleneck in attention mechanisms’ speed stems from the
842   relatively slow access to high-bandwidth memory (HBM) in GPUs. To address this limitation,
843   FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2024; Shah et al., 2024) restructures attention computation
844   to optimize the utilization of high-speed on-chip SRAM while minimizing access to slower HBM,
845
      thereby enhancing the efficiency of GPU-based attention operations. FlashAttention strategically
      balances computational efficiency against memory bandwidth efficiency. Although the computa-
846
      tional complexity in terms of sequence length remains quadratic, the optimizations introduced by
847
      FlashAttention significantly accelerate attention computation in wall-clock time.
848
849
850   Non-Transformer Neural Network-based Approaches for Tabular Classification. Non-
851   Transformer neural networks, such as Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP) (Rumelhart et al., 1986),
852   were explored for tabular classification long before Transformer-based methods, but their perfor-
853   mance was limited. In recent years, several novel neural network techniques have been developed for
854   this task, including ResNet (He et al., 2016), DANet (Chen et al., 2022), NODE (Popov et al., 2019),
855
      DeepFM (Guo et al., 2017), STG (Yamada et al., 2020), VIME (Yoon et al., 2020), and NAM (Agar-
      wal et al., 2021). DeepFM (Guo et al., 2017) employs a factorization machine-based neural network
856
      to learn from categorical data. Drawing inspiration from CatBoost, Popov et al. (2019) present a
857
      novel neural network architecture designed specifically for tabular data, named Neural Oblivious
858   Decision Ensembles (NODE). While self- and semi-supervised learning have demonstrated effec-
859   tiveness in the domains of computer vision and natural language processing, Yoon et al. (2020) pro-
860   posed Value Imputation and Mask Estimation (VIME), which represents the first attempt to address
861   tabular tasks using a self- and semi-supervised learning framework. Agarwal et al. (2021) proposed
862   the Neural Additive Model (NAM), an interpretable neural network that maintains strong perfor-
863   mance on tabular data. Yamada et al. (2020) proposed a feature selection method using stochastic
      gates (STG), which is a neural network-based and effective approach for tabular data. Chen et al.


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      (2022) designed an abstract layer, a specialized neural component for tabular data, and proposed
865   Deep Abstract Networks (DANets) by stacking these layers.
866
867   Some approaches even replace Transformers with SSMs for tabular learning (Ahamed & Cheng,
868
      2024; Thielmann et al., 2024). However, these methods require training on a per-dataset basis, lead-
      ing to high computational costs, and they are generally slower than GBDTs for tabular classification
869
      tasks.

874   derperform in some language modeling tasks (You et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2022),
875   recent theoretical work demonstrates its effectiveness in in-context learning scenarios, where it can
876   emulate gradient descent to achieve learning during inference (Ahn et al., 2023).
