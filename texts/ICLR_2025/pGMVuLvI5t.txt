011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Optimization problems aim to find the optimal solution, which is becoming in-
014            creasingly complex and difficult to solve. Traditional evolutionary optimization
015            methods always overlook the granular characteristics of solution space. In the real
016            scenario of numerous optimizations, the solution space is typically partitioned
017            into sub-regions characterized by varying degree distributions. These sub-regions
018            present different granularity characteristics at search potential and difficulty. Con-
019            sidering the granular characteristics of the solution space, the number of coarse-
020            grained regions is smaller than the number of points, so the calculation is more
021
               efficient. On the other hand, coarse-grained characteristics are not easily affected
               by fine-grained sample points, so the calculation is more robust. To this end,
022
               this paper proposes a new multi-granularity evolutionary optimization method,
023
               namely the Granular-ball Optimization (GBO) algorithm, which characterizes and
024            searches the solution space from coarse to fine. Specifically, using granular-balls
025            instead of traditional points for optimization increases the diversity and robustness
026            of the random search process. At the same time, the search range in different it-
027            eration processes is limited by the radius of granular-balls, covering the solution
028            space from large to small. The mechanism of granular-ball splitting is applied to
029            continuously split and evolve the large granular-balls into smaller ones for refining
030            the solution space. Extensive experiments on commonly used benchmarks have
031            shown that GBO outperforms popular and advanced evolutionary algorithms. The
032
               code is available in the Supplementary Materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Optimization is a key research area in science and engineering, focused on identifying optimal so-
039   lutions Molaei et al. (2021). It spans various fields, including engineering design Liu et al. (2012);
040   Saha et al. (2021); He et al. (2023), gene recognition Xu et al. (2022), traffic signal control Bi et al.
041
      (2014); Li & Sun (2018), machine learning Barshandeh et al. (2022); Abdollahzadeh et al. (2024);
      Li et al. (2023), and medical issues Lian et al. (2024), among others.
042
043   Early works focused on deterministic search methods such as gradient descent Tsitsiklis et al.
044   (1986); Ruder (2016), Newton’s method Fischer (1992), mixed integer programming Shen et al.
045   (2023), etc. These methods usually require mathematical calculations and are prone to getting stuck
046   in local optima. In large-scale environments, the solution space of optimization problems grows
047
      exponentially, making such methods no longer effective.
048   Evolutionary optimization, inspired by natural evolution and biological behavior, has increasingly
049   been applied to algorithm design and complex problem-solving. Representative methods include ge-
050   netic algorithm, particle swarm algorithm, ant colony algorithm Holland (1992); Kennedy & Eber-
051   hart (1995); Dorigo et al. (2006), etc. These methods iteratively and randomly search for the optimal
052   solution through mutual learning and competition among individuals in the population. It does not
053
      rely on strict mathematical models, and can effectively handle complex optimization characteristics
      in big data environments.

068   Modeling the granular characteristics of the solution space is not easy. Because there are several
069
      challenges: (1) The primary challenge is how to effectively characterize the granular characteristics
      of the solution space. Existing studies typically employ fine individual granularity to search the
070

071
      the complexity and diversity present in different regions effectively. (2) The second challenge lies
072   in accurately assessing the potential optimality within each region. In the solution space, different
073   regions may harbor varying degrees of optimal solutions, and traditional methods often struggle to
074   precisely identify and evaluate these potentially optimal regions. Thus, it is crucial to develop an
075   optimization algorithm capable of capturing the connections and differences between sub-regions of
076   the solution space from a multi-granularity perspective.
077
      To this end, we propose a multi-granularity optimization algorithm via granular-ball (GBO) for solv-
078   ing complex continuous optimization problems. Specifically, Multi-Granularity Solution Space Re-
079   finement involves initially covering the entire solution space with a coarse-grained granular-ball and
080   then using a splitting mechanism to split fine-grained child granular-balls. Furthermore, Granular-
081   ball Exploration and Exploitation involves the collaborative search among multiple child granular-
082   balls. The coarse-to-fine search process better exploits potential differences in optimal solutions
083   across various regions. These mechanisms replace the traditional point-based iterative search with a
084   regional search approach, allowing for a more comprehensive consideration of the complexity and
085   distinctiveness of the solution space. Experiments on benchmark and real-world problems show that
086
      GBO surpasses the classic and popular algorithms. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
087
             • We highlight the unique granular characteristics of sub-regions within the solution space
088            and investigate the potential of granular-balls in solving complex continuous optimization
089            problems.
090
             • We propose a multi-granularity optimization algorithm via granular-ball (GBO). This
091
               method characterizes the solution space from coarse-grained to fine-grained through two
092
               stages, namely, coarse-grained granular-ball initialization and fine-grained granular-ball
093            splitting. Our method compensates for the drawbacks of potential large optimal region
094            deviations that may arise from single-point searches by utilizing set-based searches.
095
             • We present a granular-ball exploration and exploitation process that includes the genera-
096
               tion of guiding granular-balls and the elite granular-ball retention operations. Through the
097
               multi-granularity characterization and search of the solution space, the evolutionary mech-
098            anism based on population and random search is maintained.

             • We conduct extensive experiments on benchmark Liang et al. (2013) and real-world opti-

105
106   Evolutionary Algorithms (EAs). Evolutionary Algorithms are global optimization techniques in-
107   spired by natural evolution, commonly used for complex problems. They are population-based
      methods that iteratively improve solutions towards optimality Jin & Branke (2005). The earliest


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      evolutionary algorithms were directly inspired by biological evolution processes, forming the foun-
109   dation of evolutionary computation through the simulation of natural selection and genetic variation.
110   A classic example is the Genetic Algorithm (GA) Holland (1992), which simulates biological ge-
111   netic evolution by using selection, crossover, and mutation operations to enhance individual fitness.
112   Later, Evolution Strategies (ES) Beyer & Schwefel (2002) were developed to focus on optimiz-
113   ing continuous variables and improving efficiency through adaptive mutation strength adjustments.
114   These algorithms not only focus on individual evolution but also emphasize collaboration and in-
115   formation sharing among populations. Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) Dorigo et al. (2006) is an
116   example that imitates the pheromone-based path selection process of ants during foraging, making
117
      it particularly suitable for combinatorial optimization problems like the Traveling Salesman Prob-
      lem. Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) Kennedy & Eberhart (1995) simulates the behavior of bird
118
      flocks searching for food, utilizing information exchange between individuals and their neighbors to
119
      achieve dynamic optimization.
120
121   Evolutionary algorithms have also been successfully employed in combinatorial optimization prob-
122   lems. Specifically, Xiang et al. (2019) proposed a PSO strategy (PBS-PSO) based on proportional
123
      integral differentiation (PID), which takes advantage of past, current, and global best changes to
      update the search direction to accelerate convergence and adjust the search direction to get rid of
124
      local optima. Zhang et al. (2018) proposed a multi-objective particle swarm optimizer based on a
125
      competitive mechanism, in which the particles are updated based on pair competitions performed in
126   the current swarm in each generation.
127
128   Overall, evolutionary algorithms offer diverse tools and methods for solving complex optimization
129
      problems by simulating various natural evolutionary and adaptive mechanisms. Their flexibility
      and adaptability make them crucial for a wide range of real-world problems, and they continue to
130
      advance in research and engineering applications.
131
132   Granular-ball Computing (GBC). Chen (1982) pointed out that human cognition has the law of
133   “global precedence” in his research published in Science. Based on the theoretical basis of tradi-
134   tional granular computing, Wang (2017) took the lead in proposing multi-granular cognitive com-
135
      puting in combination with the cognitive law in human brain cognition. Xia et al. (2023) introduced
      an innovative computational method known as granular-ball computing (GBC), celebrated for its
136
      efficiency and robustness.
137
138   The reason for Xia et al. (2023)’s approach to multi-granularity feature representation using
139   granular-ball is that the geometry of a granular-ball is completely symmetric and has the most
140   concise, standard mathematical representation. Therefore, it facilitates expansion into higher di-
141
      mensional space. Compared with the traditional method which takes the most fine-grained points
      as input, the granular-ball computing takes the coarse-grained granular-balls as input, which is effi-
142
      cient, robust, and interpretable Xia et al. (2023). Granular-ball computing has expanded into various
143
      domains of artificial intelligence, as evidenced by works such as Xie et al. (2024); Quadir & Tan-
144   veer (2024); Zhang et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024), etc. However, its application in optimization is
145   relatively under-explored. Thus, this paper proposes a multi-granularity granular-ball optimization
146   algorithm to explore this domain.

151   In this section, we present the multi-granularity optimization algorithm via granular-ball (GBO)
152   for solving optimization problems (shown in Figure 2), which is composed of two modules: ((1)
153   Multi-Granularity Solution Space Refinement: the solution space is refined from coarse-grained and
154   fine-grained perspectives, respectively; (2) Granular-ball Exploration and Exploitation: the optimal
155   solution is found through cooperative search among child granular-balls.
156

160   granular-ball is used to cover the solution space of the objective function. Then the sampling points
161   operation is carried out inside the initial granular-ball to split many child granular-balls to refine the
      solution space.

188   Coarse-Grained Initialization. In any dimension, a granular-ball needs only two data points to
189   represent it: the center c and the radius r. In a space of arbitrary dimensions, a granular-ball (GB)
190   is defined by its center vector c and radius vector r. Given an initial granular-ball GB, the center
191   c represents the position of the granular-ball in space and is a vector pointing to the center of the
192   solution space. The radius r is a vector where each component corresponds to half of the range in
193   the respective dimension of the solution space. The initial granular-ball covers the entire solution
194   space to ensure that no potential optimal solutions are overlooked.
195   The fitness value, as the only solution quality evaluation indicator in evolutionary computation, is
196   indispensable for the algorithm. In this paper, due to the use of granular-balls instead of points
197   to evaluate the search potential of a certain region in the solution space during the algorithm op-
198   timization process, the fitness of the search individuals in the algorithm, that is, the quality of the
199   granular-balls, is redefined. The fitness value of the center of a granular-ball is taken as the quality

515   5   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we propose a multi-granularity optimization algorithm (GBO) via granular-ball. Aim-
518   ing at the multi-granularity of the solution space, GBO uses a splitting mechanism to cover the
519   solution space, carries out a global search from coarse-grained to fine-grained, and finds the op-
520
      timal solution through synergistic search between granular-balls. It replaces the traditional point-
      based iterative and regional search methods, allowing for a more comprehensive consideration of
521
      the complexity and uniqueness of the solution space. Experiments on the CEC2013 benchmark and
522
      CEC2011 real-world problems confirm the superiority of GBO. However, we have yet to design a
523   more adaptive method for the radius of each generation of granular-balls, allowing their offspring
524   to exhibit varying granularities to enhance the efficiency of the GBO method. To address this issue,
525   we plan to adopt more effective strategies in our future work.
