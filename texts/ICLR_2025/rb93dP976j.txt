009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper investigates the supply-demand matching problem on dynamic plat-
012            forms, focusing on optimizing matching strategies by learning workers’ attributes
013            when their types are uncertain and constantly changing. To address this problem,
014            we introduce a latent factor model and a multi-centroid grouping penalty mecha-
015            nism to predict latent factors of workers and perform dynamic matching. Our ap-
016            proach operates in two stages: the first stage fits latent feature vectors for workers
017            and jobs and groups them using historical data; the second stage utilizes these la-
018            tent features for dynamic matching. Our research demonstrates that the introduced
019            model can adapt to the dynamic changes of the platform with good predictive con-
020
               sistency and group robustness, and improves overall operational benefit through
               continuous optimization of matching results. We provide simulation experiments
021
               and a real case study using kidney exchange data and compare our model with a
022
               point process model to show that our approach performs well on dynamic platform
023            matching problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the era of digital platforms, the two-sided matching market holds an important position in microe-
032   conomics Roth & Sotomayor (1990). The challenge of dynamically matching supply and demand
033
      while continuously learning user attributes is both important and complex. Online platforms such as
      labor markets, e-commerce websites, and fashion retail platforms constantly face the dual challenges
034
      of optimizing matching and improving user preferences and ability understanding. This parallel
035
      process of matching and learning is crucial for improving user satisfaction and platform efficiency.
036   Moreover, dynamic matching problems also have important applications in the medical field Roth
037   et al. (2004; 2005; 2007) Ashlagi et al. (2013) Ünver (2010), such as how to efficiently match donors
038   and recipients in organ transplantation to improve transplant success rates and patient survival rates,
039   which also requires balancing immediate benefits and long-term learning under limited resources.
040   These practical application scenarios all emphasize the importance of effective matching strategies.
041
      A platform that needs to balance exploration and utilization must find a balance between maximiz-
042   ing matching value (utilization) and continuously learning about new participants to match them
043   (exploration) effectively. Using the terminology of the online labor market, the demand side is re-
044   ferred to as the worker, and the supply side is the job. Under the assumption that supply is limited,
045   matching a supply unit to a user makes it unavailable to others. The platform knows the job type but
046   must learn the unknown worker type through ongoing interactions. Workers leave after completing
047   a certain amount of work. Assuming the platform has system-level knowledge of worker and job
048   arrival rates, as well as the expected benefits from matching specific workers with specific jobs, the
049   goal is to maximize the steady-state cumulative rate of return.
050   This article presents a model using latent factor grouping penalties to optimize supply-demand
051   matching on dynamic platforms. It addresses the fluctuating nature of job types and worker cat-
052   egories by deeply analyzing their relationships, thereby enhancing platform efficiency. The model
053   operates in two phases: initially fitting latent factors from observed matches, and then using these
      factors to identify skilled workers for smart matching.

      In the first stage, we train these latent factors through historical data, and each job and worker is
055   assigned a latent vector based on their historical performance. These vectors capture the complexity
056   of work and the ability of workers to handle different types of work. For example, the latent vector
057   of a job may highly emphasize technical skills, while the latent vector of a worker indicates their
058   proficiency in that skill. In the second stage, the model identifies which workers have higher work
059   efficiency by analyzing the matching degree between the latent vectors of work and workers. In
060   this process, we introduced a multi-center grouping penalty mechanism, which not only effectively
061   clusters similar jobs and workers, but also controls model complexity through penalty terms to
062   prevent overfitting.
063   Our research indicates that our model provides a new solution for dynamic platform matching prob-
064   lems, which can adapt to the dynamic changes of the platform and improve the accuracy and effi-
065   ciency of matching through continuous learning and optimization. The successful application of this
066   method is expected to greatly enhance the platform’s operational effectiveness and user satisfaction.

071   In this paper, we study a supply-demand matching problem on a dynamic platform in Johari et al.
072   (2020). We follow the setting in Johari et al. (2020) to represent the supply side with jobs and the
073   demand side with workers. Every job and worker belongs to a specific type and the platform needs
074
      to match workers and jobs at each time step (t = 0, 1, 2, . . . ) based on their types. We assume that
      the job types on the platform are a finite set J and the worker types are also a finite set I. Assume a
075
      system with fixed sets of job types J and worker types I, utilizing a continuum model with infinitely
076
      many workers and jobs to reflect a sufficiently large dynamic market.
077                                                                                        P
078   Formally, fix a distribution ρ over worker types, i.e. ρi > 0, ∀i ∈ I such that i∈I ρi = 1. We
079   assume that the system initially starts empty before t = 0, and in each period t = 0, ..., N − 1, a
080
      mass 1/N of workers arrives in the system. (In what follows we ultimately consider a steady-state
      analysis of the dynamical system and initial conditions will be irrelevant.) Each worker is of type i′
081
      with probability ρi′ ; each worker’s type and arrival time are independent
082
083   For simplicity and to maintain a finite mass of workers at all times, we model a process where
084   workers and jobs regenerate every N periods, known as the worker’s lifetime, which the platform
085   recognizes. No further arrivals take place after period N. Instead, each worker subsequently regener-
086
      ates every N period after their arrival: at a regeneration time, the worker type is resampled from the
      distribution ρ; i.e., the new type is i with probability ρi , and these regenerations are also independent
087
      across workers’ type and their arriving time.
088
089   Our goal is to predict the attributes of workers based on past matched job types and rewards, and
090   maximize expected rewards by adjusting matching, instead of optimizing realized rewards Dai &
091   Jordan (2021) Che & Koh (2016). Similar to decentralized matching markets Roth & Xing (1997)
092
      Roth (2008), our platform ensures that only the attributes of workers and jobs and their matching
      history affect matching. These matches are independent and not influenced by preferences. We
093

094

095   crowdsourcing with C categories. In practice, a worker will not be matched for all jobs, thus only
096   a subset of R is observed, and we denote the subset as Ω = {(i, j) : rij is observed}. We denote
097   Z = (Z1 , Z2 , . . . , Zm )′ as the true labels for workers, and if Zi = c, we name the ith worker as
098   label-c task. The goal of crowdsourcing is to predict Zi ’s via aggregating observed rij ’s.

441   recipients based on their detailed information. We collected data related to kidney transplantation,

520   This article uses the latent factor model to solve the dynamic supply problem in market matching,
521   focusing on how to reasonably use the two stages of the model for this problem, as well as con-
522   sidering the impact of time in the matching process. Our application enables the model to adapt to
523   the dynamic changes of the platform and continuously optimize the matching efficiency and match-
524   ing results. Experiments show that this method is suitable for the matching problem of multi-class
525
      workers and jobs, and the results are better than other methods. The advantage of this method is that
      it can handle markets with more individual categories, the number of which is theoretically unlim-
526
      ited. However, in practical situations, as the types of workers and jobs in the market increase, the
527
      accuracy of this method needs to be improved, which can serve as a future research direction.
