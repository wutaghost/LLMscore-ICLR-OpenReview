009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Over the years, video generation has experienced significant advancement. A vari-
012            ety of open-source models emerge, making it surprisingly easy to manipulate and
013            edit videos with just a few simple prompts. While these cutting-edge technologies
014            have gained huge popularity, they have also given rise to concerns regarding the
015            privacy and portrait rights of individuals: malicious users can exploit these tools
016            for deceptive or illegal purposes. Existing works on protecting images against
017            generative models cannot be directly grafted to video protection, due to their ef-
018
               ficiency and effectiveness limitations. Motivated by this, we introduce PRIME, a
               new methodology dedicated to the protection of videos from unauthorized editing
019
               via generative models. Our key idea is to craft highly transferable and robust
020
               perturbations, which can be efficiently added to the protected videos to disrupt their
021            editing feasibility. We perform comprehensive evaluations using both objective
022            metrics and human studies. The results indicate that PRIME only needs 8.3% GPU
023            hours of existing state-of-the-art methods while achieving better protection results.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Benefiting from the rapid development of generative AI technology, video editing has become
029   remarkably simple and popular. Given an original video clip, users can freely add new elements or
030   alter its style by just providing a few language prompts. Researchers have proposed a number of
031   methods for efficient video editing (Khachatryan et al., 2023; Khandelwal, 2023; Qi et al., 2023).
032   These solutions treat the video as a sequence of images arranged along the temporal dimension and
033
      leverage the state-of-the-art image editing techniques based on diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020;
      Song et al., 2021a;b) to modify each frame. They further need to ensure the coherence and consistency
034
      of these stacked images, employing techniques such as global attention constraints (Geyer et al., 2024)
035
      and latent feature constraints (Yang et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023). With these techniques,
036   there are now a lot of video editing applications and online services (e.g., Runway Gen-1 (Run),
037   Pika (Pik)), significantly enhancing our life quality and working efficiency.
038
039
      Unfortunately, advanced video editing methods also open the door to potential misuse. Malicious
      users could download pristine videos from the Internet and then leverage video editing models to
040
      generate illegal or harmful content. For example, they can remove the clothes from the subjects in the
041
      videos to cause sexual innuendo. The international anti-human trafficking organization, Thorn (th),
042   cooperated with AI companies (e.g., AWS AI, Civitai, and Hugging Face) to propose a white paper
043   book (Tho) on protecting child sexual abuse against generative AI. Malicious users can also swap the
044   faces of people in the videos to create and propagate fake content. The Princess of Wales’s video is
045   considered as AI generated (pri), which tarnishes the reputation of the British royal family.
046
      It is critical to protect sensitive videos from malicious editing with existing generative models. A
047
      lot of research efforts have been dedicated to protecting images from unauthorized usage (Salman
048   et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023; Rhodes et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). They mainly
049   introduce subtle and imperceptible perturbations on the static images, which can effectively compel
050   the generative models to produce poor editing outcomes. In contrast, very rare studies are conducted
051   for video protection. Since a video is composed of a sequence of images, a straightforward strategy
052   is to apply the image protection methods to each frame of the target video. Unfortunately, this brings
053   new issues in practice. (1) Efficiency. Existing image protection methods are quite time-consuming.
      This overhead is exacerbated when we try to craft perturbations for each video frame: even protecting


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      a short video clip of 2 seconds (30 fps) may take hours. This significantly restricts the practicality of
055   existing solutions. (2) Robustness. During the video coding process, the codec is adopted to assign
056   different compression ratios to individual frames to achieve the best trade-off between the video
057   quality and size. This dynamic compression scheme can diminish the effect of the perturbation on
058   each frame, leading to ineffective protections.
059
      The gaps mentioned above motivate us to explore and design new protection methods dedicated
060
      to video protection. To this end, we introduce PRIME, a novel methodology to PRotect vIdeos
061   from Malicious Editing. Our solution adds perturbations to each frame of the protected video to
062   prevent the constraints, such as global attention, used in the video editing pipeline from rectifying the
063   wrong features of the perturbed frames with the clean frames. We introduce the following innovative
064   techniques to make the protection more efficient and robust. (1) The malicious users may use diverse
065   editing methods with different pre-trained models, pipelines, and prompts, which are all agnostic to
066   the defenders. Hence, the crafted perturbations must have high transferability (Papernot et al., 2016)
067   across various models and editing methods. To improve the transferability among different LDMs
068   and achieve the zero-shot ability, we consider the latent features during the diffusion process and the
069
      final outputs. (2) Perturbing each frame in the given video can cost a lot of time and GPU resources.
      We introduce two mechanisms to enhance the efficiency of the perturbation generation process: fast
070
      convergence searching helps us find a better image for each frame as the editing target; early stage
071
      stopping monitors the perturbation generating process and finds the optimal step to stop, which can
072   yield the high-quality perturbation within a short time. (3) The compression algorithm used in the
073   video codec is generally complex and lossy, making it impossible to maintain lossless perturbation
074   after compression. Following an in-depth examination of existing video codec methods, we propose
075   a new anti dynamic compression scheme to discretize perturbations within the pixel space, which can
076   enhance the robustness of perturbations during compression.
077
      Following the previous works of video editing (Geyer et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
078   2023), we collect video clips from the Internet to evaluate the performance of our proposed solution.
079   We consider two types of malicious editing scenarios: malicious Not-Safe-For-Work (NSFW)
080   editing and malicious swapping editing. Due to the lack of ground-truth references, it is difficult to
081   comprehensively and objectively assess the edited videos. To address this, we follow the evaluation
082   methods in previous works (Geyer et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) to conduct a user
083   study, wherein we engage volunteers to participate in surveys gauging the quality and preferences of
084   the video. Based on the human evaluation, we show that existing video editing methods can produce
085   high-quality videos for these two malicious purposes, and obtain 2.99 out of 5 and 3.17 out of 5 for
086
      the video quality scores, respectively. On the other hand, PRIME can significantly reduce the quality
      of the generated malicious videos (1.54 out of 5 and 2.44 out of 5 for two tasks, respectively), making
087
      them totally unusable. Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
088
089   • We propose PRIME, a new black-box video protection method against malicious editing. We
090     improve its transferability by simultaneously considering latent codings and generated images.
091
      • PRIME is timing-efficient with our proposed fast convergence searching and early stage stopping
092     mechanisms. It only costs about 8.3% GPU hours of the state-of-the-art solution Photoguard Salman
093     et al. (2023) under the same setting.
094
      • PRIME can combat dynamic compression from the lossy video codec with our proposed anti
095
        dynamic compression method. It increases about 8% of the bitrate for the protected videos
096     compared with Photoguard.
097
      • We perform a user study to show that PRIME demonstrates better protection performance and
098
        transferability than Photoguard for two malicious editing tasks.

102   2.1   V IDEO E DITING WITH LDM S
103   A growing number of works (Parmar et al., 2023; Wu & Torre, 2023; Lin et al., 2024) focus on turning
104   a latent diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) into a zero-shot image editor. Such progress
105   inspires the development of video editing, a subtask in the video generation domain (Blattmann et al.,
106   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023). Different from the conventional video generation task,
107   which usually only requires a conditional prompt to guide the generation process, video editing needs
      a source video and a guidance prompt as conditions. The edited video can be seen as a series of


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      images stacked along the time dimension. However, directly editing each frame of the given video
109   will probably lead to different backgrounds or different poses for the foreground objects, due to the
110   lack of pixel-level constrain. How to keep the frames of the edited video consistent is still an open
111   problem. To keep the consistency between frames, cross-frame global attention (Geyer et al., 2024;
112   Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) is widely used in existing video editing frameworks based on
113   various pre-trained LDMs. On the other hand, some frameworks (Yang et al., 2023; Khachatryan
114   et al., 2023) adopt other conditions (e.g., depth, pose, edge) to better enhance consistency.

116   2.2   P ROTECTING I MAGES FROM M ALICIOUS E DITING
117
118   It is a common practice for individuals to release and share images on public platforms. However,
119
      these images could be misused for illegal purposes. Researchers have proposed new methods
      to protect the images from two perspectives. First, attackers could edit these images to create
120
      harmful or fake content. The advance of LDMs can significantly facilitate this image manipulation
121
      process. The state-of-the-art defense against this threat is Photoguard (Salman et al., 2023) is the
122   state-of-the-art solution. This method incorporates adversarial perturbations into images, effectively
123   perplexing LDMs and preventing unauthorized editing. Second, attackers could collect the images
124   from the Internet and use them without any authorization to fine-tune LDMs with some personalized
125   techniques like DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023). To prevent the intellectual property violations of
126   these images, some works (Shan et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023; Rhodes et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023)
127   add perturbations into images before releasing them to the public. With such perturbed images, the
128   fine-tuned LDMs are only capable of producing low-quality results.
129   In this paper, we mainly focus on the first threat scenario, but in the context of video editing
130   with LDMs. As currently there are no solutions dedicated to video protection, we mainly treat
131   Photoguard as a baseline method. As discussed in Section 1, this method has severe efficiency
132   and robustness drawbacks when applied to the video protection. This motivates us to design a new
133   solution specifically against the video editing threat.

161   Attacker’s Ability and Goal. An increasing number of users are sharing their video content on
      the Internet through popular social applications such as TikTok and Instagram. However, this

      widespread sharing presents a substantial risk to the public’s rights, as adversarial users may exploit
163   the capabilities of powerful LDMs to manipulate these videos for illegal purposes. Without loss
164   of generality, we consider two types of malicious video editing. (1) Malicious NSFW editing: the
165   attacker aims to incorporate “not safe for work (NSFW)" elements into a given video, such as

171   that will be used by the malicious user. The defender crafts the perturbations for each frame of the
172   target video based on a public pre-trained LDM, which can be different from the one employed by
173   the attacker. He adds the perturbations to the video and uploads it to the Internet. The goal is to
174   ensure that the perturbed videos can effectively thwart the efforts of malicious editing, e.g., causing
175   the results low-quality, preventing the model from generating NSFW content or identity swapping.

179   4.1   OVERVIEW
180
181   Inspired by prior works (Salman et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023; Rhodes et al., 2023;
182   Zheng et al., 2023), we craft the adversarial perturbation for each frame of the target video to disable
183   its editability by the LDM and break the temporal consistency constraints used in video editing
184   pipelines (Geyer et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) . To overcome the limitations of
185
      these solutions, we introduce a series of techniques to achieve three critical properties, as shown
      in Figure 1. (1) Zero-shot ability: the crafted perturbations are effective against different LDM
186
      models, editing pipelines, and prompts; (2) Efficient per-frame perturbing: we introduce the fast
187
      convergence searching and the early stage stopping mechanisms to significantly reduce the time of
188   perturbing each frame; (3) Anti dynamic compression: we design a new scheme to enhance the
189   robustness of the perturbation against the compression in video codecs. Below we give the details of
190   our solution.
191
192   4.2   Z ERO - SHOT A BILITY
193
194   With a pre-trained LDM F consisting of an encoder E, a decoder D and a U-Net U, the defender aims
195   to generate the adversarial perturbation for each frame, which can cause F to produce low-quality
196   results. As the model F and video editing pipeline used by the defender can be totally different from
197   that of the malicious users, the generated perturbation should be highly transferable across different
198
      configurations. Furthermore, considering that the malicious users will adopt various prompts to edit
      the videos, the perturbation should be able to invalidate as many prompts as possible.
199
200   To achieve this goal, we follow Photoguard (Salman et al., 2023) to perform a diffusion attack.
201   This indicates that PRIME considers not only E, but also U and D, and the target is to find a
                                                                                  s
202   perturbation for each frame, causing the latent features disrupted and D(fE,0  ) close to a predefined
203   target image. Because the defender has no information about the specific editing pipeline employed
204
      by malicious users, we do not consider intricate technologies used in video editing methods, such
      as cross-frame attention and latent constraints, to make the protection more general. Similar to
205
      Photoguard, PRIME depends on the transferability of the perturbation and can be further cooperated
206
      with other methods (Kurakin et al., 2017; Athalye et al., 2018; Song et al., 2018) to further improve
207   the effectiveness of the perturbation.

      4.4   A NTI DYNAMIC C OMPRESSION
271
272
      Different from images, saving videos requires a codec, which applies compression algorithms to
273   balance the file size and video quality. When playing videos on the Internet, it is often more suitable
274   to utilize a variable bitrate as opposed to a constant one. This is due to the fluctuation in the
275   available bandwidth and diverse nature of video content, which demands a dynamic compression
276   ratio applied to each frame to ensure optimal video streaming. Therefore, it is essential to preserve the
277   perturbation information δ throughout the dynamic compression process to guarantee the effectiveness
278   of the protection. To this end, we introduce a simple solution to achieve anti-dynamic compression.
279   Specifically, we transform the perturbation from the model input space [−1, 1], which is common in
280   previous methods, to the pixel space [−255, 255], and further quantize the values of the perturbation
281
      to ensure they have shorter bits. Such operations are the approximated simulation of the compression
      from the codec. By adding this technique into the protecting process, we can make the perturbation
282
      more robust and less sensitive against compression. Through the experiments, we find that our
283
      method does not introduce any significant time overhead while increasing the video bitrate by about
284   8%, which means the video contains more information.

      We notice that currently there are no public standard benchmarks or datasets for the malicious video
      editing scenarios. This drives us to build a new dataset VIOLENT, i.e., VIdeos fOr maLicious Editing
331
      aNd proTection. As summarized in Table 1, VIOLENT consists of videos of 10 celebrities with
332
      diverse genders, ages and races. They can be grouped into politicians (i.e., Donald Trump and Joe
333   Biden), singers (i.e., Drake, Katy Perry, Rihanna, and Taylor Swift), actors (i.e., Robert Downey Jr.,
334   Ryan Gosling, and Scarlett Johansson), and athletes (i.e., Messi). Due to copyright issues, we are
335   unable to make this dataset publicly available. To facilitate the reproduction, we provide a list of
336   URLs for these original videos in the supplementary material.
337
      To construct such dataset, we first conduct a manual assessment to identify celebrities who can
338
      be perfectly and realistically generated by existing LDMs. We consider diversifying their genders,
339   ages and races. After determining the list of celebrities, we proceed to acquire their videos from
340   various sources on the Internet. Most of these videos are sourced from official channels, while the
341   remaining content is gathered from public channels. All the original videos we collect are in the
342   resolution 1280 × 720, which is the most popular format on the Internet. Upon obtaining the videos,
343   we manually edit and cut them into scene-consistent video clips, each comprising tens to hundreds of
344   frames. We carefully filter out clips that contain transitions, illumination changes, or main object
345   changes. Subsequently, we create specific configurations for malicious editing for each clip. For each
346   configuration, we tune the malicious prompts and adjust other hyperparameters used in the video
347
      editing pipelines for different LDMs. This entire process requires hundreds of GPU hours of effort.
348   To create prompts for malicious editing purposes, we follow a very simple and direct template:
349   “[Someone] [Do Something] [Somewhere]". We first create a description for each original video
350   based on this template. For the malicious NSFW editing task, we keep “[Someone]" while change
351   “[Do Something]" and “[Somewhere]". For example, if we want to generate a video in which the
352   person is naked, we replace “[Do Something]" with “is naked" or “is nude". If we want to generate
353
      a bloody video, we replace “[Somewhere]" with “in a bloody scene". For the malicious swapping
      editing task, we only replace “[Someone]" with a new name. For example, if we want to generate a
354
      video of Donald Trump based on a video of Joe Biden, we replace “Joe Biden" with “Donald Trump".
355
      Furthermore, we adopt the prompt weighting method, Compel com, to manually adjust different
356   weights for “[Someone]", “[Do Something]", and “[Somewhere]" to obtain the best editing results.
357   We design a total of 280 attack configurations, which are used for the subsequent experiments. We
358   also provide a list of such malicious prompts in the supplementary material.

      5.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
403
404
      Subjective Metrics. We perform a user study and invite 79 persons with different background to
405   assess the generated videos. In our study, we consider six dimensions of human perception, i.e.,
406   Content Consistency, Prompt Matching, Naturalness, Frame Stability, Video Quality, and Personal
407   Preference, which are aligned with a recent benchmark, VBench (Huang et al., 2023). Specifically,
408   Content Consistency describes the extent to which the edited video maintains the same details as
409   the original video, such as layout, style, movements, and expressions. Prompt Matching describes
410   how well the edited video matches the theme of the given prompt, e.g., sex and blood. Naturalness
411   describes how plausible the edited video looks. Frame Stability measures the temporal consistency of
412   the edited video. Video Quality describes the overall quality of the edited video. All these metrics
413
      are normalized into the interval of [1, 5]. Higher scores mean better performance. For Personal
      Preference, we give survey participants several videos and ask them to pick the one they prefer.
414
415   Objective Metrics. We consider peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), structural similarity (SSIM), and
416   VCLIPSim. Specifically, PSNR compares the noise ratio in the edited videos w/ and w/o protection.
417   SSIM compares the perceived quality of the edited videos w/ and w/o protection. When computing
418   PSNR and SSIM, we use the original clean videos to generate edited videos as references. VCLIPSim
419
      is supported by ViCLIP (Wang et al., 2022), which reflects the similarity between the video and given
      prompt.

512
513   To study the robustness of our protection method, we consider three variables in video protecting,
514   including bit rates of the videos, video frame transformations, and another video editing method. In
515   Table 5, we first study the bit rates by using different video compression strengths. A lower bit rate
516   corresponds to a higher compression rate. In details, we conducted experiments on a small subset of
517   data using various codec settings with different bit rates. The results indicate that even at very high
518   compression rates, our method remains effective in disrupting video editing techniques, resulting
519   in edited videos of lower quality. These results further demonstrate the robustness of our proposed
520
      method against video compression.
521   In Table 6, we consider three transformations applied directly on each video frame, including super-
522   resolution, JPEG compression, and Gaussian smoothing. Specifically, we evaluate PRIME under 2x
523   image upscaling, JPEG compression with quality settings of 30, 50, and 70, and Gaussian smoothing
524   with scales of 1, 3, and 5. The results indicate that our method demonstrates some robustness against
525   these transformations. However, it is important to note that when the transformations are too strong,
526
      such as using very low JPEG compression quality or very high Gaussian smoothing scales, the
      effectiveness of our protection method is compromised.
527
528   Besides DDIM inversion (Song et al., 2021a) based methods used in previous experiments, we
529   consider SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022). The results are as follows: PSNR is 13.34 and SSIM is 0.36.
530   These results indicate that our protection method is effective for SDEdit. Overall, PRIME shows
531   robustness against various factors in video editing pipelines.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we investigate the scenario of malicious video editing with generative models, which
536   poses a huge threat to the public. To better protect the videos from malicious editing, we propose a
537   new method, PRIME, which can significantly reduce the timing cost for video protection and improve
538   the bitrate for the protected videos. Based on our evaluation, we prove the advantages of PRIME in
539   protecting videos from editing. We believe that PRIME will promote more future works in protecting
      videos and portrait rights against illegal usage.

775   D     S CALABILITY OF PRIME ON D IFFERENT L ENGTHS AND R ESOLUTION OF
776         V IDEOS
777
778
      We conducted several experiments to study the scalability of PRIME on different video resolutions
779
      and lengths.
780
781   Our method is frame-by-frame. Therefore, for videos with different lengths, the time consumption is
782   linear with the video length. It takes 1,700 seconds, 2,600 seconds, 3,500 seconds, and 4,300 seconds
783
      to process a video containing 40 frames, 60 frames, 80 frames, and 100 frames, respectively. The
      slight difference in the processing time per frame is mainly due to the convergence speed of different
784
      frames. On the other hand, increasing the frames will not cost more GPU memory. Therefore, our
785
      method is efficient.
786
787   When increasing the video resolution, we find the main bottleneck is GPU memory instead of time
788   cost, as well-known fact that generating high-resolution content will cost more GPU memory. We
789
      find that it is impossible to protect videos of 1080P or higher resolutions on one consumer-grade GPU
      for both Photoguard and PRIME. It is even impossible to edit a 1080P-video on one consumer-grade
790
      GPU using advanced editing pipelines, due to the heavy computation of cross-frame attention and
791
      other intermediate results. Such GPU memory bottlenecks can be addressed through paralleling
792   models on multiple GPUs.

      L       L IMITATION
886
887
      The main limitations of PRIME are from two aspects. First, we consider a black-box protection
888   scenario without any information about the editing methods. It will decrease the performance of
889   PRIME for some more advanced editing methods, such as OpenAI’s Sora2 . Second, although PRIME
890   only takes 8% of the running time of Photoguard, it still cannot protect a livestreaming video, which
891   could be a very important research direction in the future.
892
      Additionally, we notice that Sora from OpenAI adopts DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023), which would
893
      patchify the given frames to patches. This could be a method to circumvent the protection. However,
894   as Sora is not released to the public, we cannot verify this point. But we think it is possible to protect
895   videos against DiTs, just like previous works (Aldahdooh et al., 2021; Naseer et al., 2021) attacking
896   ViTs.

      M        S OCIAL I MPACTS , E THICAL AND L EGAL C ONCERNS
900
      It has been a very long story to protect portrait rights and other intellectual property since the
901
      generative models became popular on the Internet. For example, a very recent case is the AI-generated
902
      Taylor Swift photos circulating on social networks, which raises the attention of many popular presses,
903   such as BBC, and even the White House. Not just for famous people, advanced generative models can
904   affect normal people’s photos as well, due to editing technologies and personalization technologies.
905   Such powerful generative models can be used for illegal use. Therefore, adding watermarks into
906   the generative models to detect AI-generated content, adding perturbation to images to immunize
907   editing, and detecting illegal content before returning the results to users are three main solutions in
908   addressing the ethical and legal concerns of using generative AIs.
909   Similar to generating images or editing images, generating videos and editing videos become possible
910   with advanced generative AI models. Although it is still at the beginning phase, we find that the
911   rising video editing pipelines equipped with advanced latent diffusion models are able to do some
912   operations, such as swapping faces, adding naked bodies, and replacing backgrounds. This finding
913   motivates us to study the risks for the public under the threat of such video editing operations.
914
      First, at the forefront of these concerns is collecting other people’s selfie videos is easy. Sharing
915   selfie videos to social applications, such as TikTok and Instagram, is very popular among Gen Z
916   (people who are born between the mid-to-late 1990s and the early 2010s). People’s desire to share
917

      The second concern is from an ethical standpoint for these AI-generated videos and AI-edited videos,

924
      information dissemination, eroding the public’s trust in online platforms, such as the media and social

938   In addition, the issues caused by the illegal and harmful videos generated by AI encompass broader
939   societal implications due to the Internet. For example, the unchecked proliferation of illegal and
940   harmful videos can sow discord, amplify existing divisions, and potentially incite harm between
941   people. It is possible to create AI-generated videos, such as an imitation of the Murder of George
942   Floyd, causing arguments on the Internet between different races.
943   Due to the flaws and imperfections in the current legal system and social framework, we call on users
944   to protect their videos and legitimate rights and interests. We propose a protection method to help
945   users who are willing to share their selfie videos on social media to protect these videos. With our
946   protection, it will be easier for humans to recognize AI-edited videos. In this way, we hope that our
947   approach can provide an interim solution until a full legal and regulatory system is proposed.
