                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Recently, ordinary differential equation (ODE) based generative models have
010
               emerged as a cutting-edge method for producing high-quality samples in many ap-
011            plications. Generally, these methods typically involve learning continuous trans-
012            formation trajectories that map a simple initial distribution (i.e., Gaussian noise)
013            to the target data distribution (i.e., images) by multiple steps of solving different
014            ODE functions in inference to obtain high-quality results. However, the ODE-
015            based methods either suffer the discretization error of numerical solvers of ODE,
016            which restricts the quality of samples when only a few NFEs are used, or strug-
017            gle with training instability. In this paper, we proposed Integration Flow, which
018            learns the results of ODE-based trajectory paths directly without solving the ODE
019
               functions. Moreover, Integration Flow explicitly incorporates the target state x0
               as the anchor state in guiding the reverse-time dynamics and we have theoreti-
020
               cally proven this can contribute to both stability and accuracy. To the best of our
021
               knowledge, Integration Flow is the first model with the unified structure to esti-
022            mate ODE-based generative models. Through theoretical analysis and empirical
023            evaluations, we show that Integration Flows achieve improved performance when
024            it is applied to existing ODE-based model, such as diffusion models, Rectified
025            Flows, and PFGM++. Specifically, Integration Flow achieves one-step genera-
026            tion on CIFAR10 with FID of 2.63 for Variance Exploding (VE) diffusion model,
027            3.4 for Rectified Flow without relflow and 2.96 for PFGM++. By extending the
028            sampling to 1000 steps, we further reduce FID score to 1.71 for VE, setting state-
029            of-the-art performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Recently, ordinary differential equation (ODE) based generative models have emerged as a cutting-
034
      edge method for producing high-quality samples in many applications including image, audio (Kong
035
      et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2022), and video generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ho
036   & Salimans, 2022). Generally, these methods typically involve learning continuous transformation
037   trajectories that map a simple initial distribution (i.e. Gaussian noise) to the target data distribution
038   (i.e. images) by solving ODEs (Figure 1).
039
      Among those ODE-based models, the diffu-
040
      sion models have attracted the most attention
041
      due to their exceptional ability to generate re-
042   alistic samples. The diffusion models employ
043   a forward process that gradually adds noise
044   to the data and a reverse process that recon-
045   structs the original data by gradually removing
046   the noise. To enhance sampling effectiveness,
047   this reverse process is often reformulated as
048   a probability flow Ordinary Differential Equa-
049   tions (PF-ODEs)(Song et al., 2020b). Despite Figure 1: An illustration of ODE-based methods,
050
      their success, PF-ODE-based diffusion mod- including PF-ODE, PFGM++, and Rectified Flow.
      els face drawbacks due to their iterative nature,
051
      leading to high computational costs and prolonged sampling times during inference.
052
053   Another ODE-based approach, rectified flow models (Liu et al., 2022; Lipman et al., 2022), aims to
      model the transformation between distributions via neural ODEs. These models focus on learning


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      smoother ODE trajectories that are less prone to truncation errors during numerical integration. By
055   reducing the curvature of the generative paths, rectified flow enhances sampling efficiency and de-
056   creases the computational burden. However, even with smoother trajectories, rectified flow models
057   still require considerable iterations to produce high-quality samples.
058
      Building on the flow-based paradigm, Poisson Flow Generative Models (PFGM) and their extension
059
      PFGM++ have been introduced (Xu et al., 2022; 2023). Inspired by concepts from electrostatics,
060
      PFGM++ embeds data into a higher-dimensional space, specifically, an N + D dimensional space
061   where D is the number of augmented dimensions. The generative process involves solving an ODE
062   derived from the Poisson equation, tracing a path from a simple initial distribution (e.g., noise on a
063   large hemisphere) to the target data distribution residing on a lower dimensional hyperplane. Similar
064   to diffusion models and Rectified Flow, PFGM++ requires multiple steps during inference,
065
      All aforementioned methods required multiple steps of solving different ODE functions in inference
066
      to obtain high-quality results. Furthermore, the ODE-based models naturally inherit the discretiza-
067   tion error of numerical solvers of ODE, which restricts the quality of samples when only a few NFEs
068   are used, or struggle with training instability when neural ODEs are used to approximate the ODE
069   solution using neural networks. Given these challenges associated with ODE-based models, a nat-
070   ural question arises: can we learn the result of ODE-based trajectory paths directly without solving
071   the ODE functions? Therefore, we can take the ODE function-defined generative model and solve
072   this without an ODE solver. The answer is yes. Here we proposed Integration Flow, to the best of
073   our knowledge, the first model with the unified structure to estimate ODE-based generative models.
074   Integration Flows represent a new type of generative models. Unlike traditional ODE-based ap-
075   proaches that focus on approximating the instantaneous drift term of an ODE or depend on itera-
076   tive sampling methods, Integration Flows directly estimate the integrated effect of the cumulative
077   transformation dynamics over time. This holistic approach allows for the modeling of the entire
078   generative path in a single step, bypassing the accumulation of errors associated with high-curvature
079   trajectories and multiple function evaluations. Integration Flows do not employ the ODE solver and
080   eliminate the need for multiple sampling iterations, significantly reducing computational costs and
081
      enhancing efficiency.
082   Moreover, to increase the training stability and accuracy in reconstructing, Integration Flow explic-
083   itly incorporating the target state x0 as the anchor state in guiding the reverse-time dynamics from
084   the intermediate state xt . We have theoretically proven that incorporating the target state x0 as the
085   anchor state can provide a better or at least equal accurate estimation of x0 .
086   In summary, Integration Flows addresses the limitations of existing ODE-based generative models
087   by providing a unified and efficient approach to model the transformation between distributions. Our
088   contributions can be outlined as follows:
089
090          • Introduction of Integration Flows: We present Integration Flows, a novel generative model-
091            ing framework that estimates the integrated dynamics of continuous-time processes without
092            relying on iterative sampling procedures or traditional ODE solvers, that is, it supports one-
093            step generation for (any) ODE-based generative models.
094          • Unified ODE-Based Generative Modeling: Integration Flows can adapt different ODE-
095            based generative process, offering flexibility and unification across different generative
096            modeling approaches.
097
             • Enhanced Sampling Efficiency and Scalability: Through empirical evaluations, we show
098
               that Integration Flows achieve improved sampling efficiency and scalability compared to
099            existing ODE-based models, such as diffusion models, Rectified Flows, and PFGM++.
100            Specially, we set the state-of-the-art performance for one-step generation using Rectified
101            Flow and PFGM++.

      3.1   GENERAL FORM OF ODE- BASED GENERATIVE MODELS

169   Consider an initial state xT drawn from a distribution p(xT ), typically chosen to be a simple dis-

171   pdata , by mapping xT back through a continuous transformation process. Let {xs }Ts=0 represent a
172   continuous transformation trajectory from xT to x0 , where xs denotes the state at intermediate time
173   s ∈ [0, T ]. To describe the reverse-time dynamics that map xT back to x0 , we define a reverse-time
174   ODE:
175                                              dxs

180   stood as computing the integral:

      3.2   I NTEGRATION F LOWS
197
      To overcome the challenges associated with ODE solvers and neural ODEs, we propose Integration
198   Flow to directly estimate the integrated effect of continuous-time dynamics. Integration Flow ex-
199   plicitly incorporating the target state x0 as the anchor state in guiding the reverse-time dynamics
200   from the intermediate state xt , which contributes to both stability and accuracy in reconstructing x0
201   from intermediate states xt . Since Integration Flow bypasses the ODE solver, it provides a unified
202   framework for ODE-based generative models, allowing for one-step generation across a variety of
203   processes.
204   The cumulative effect of the reverse-time dynamics over the interval [0, t], which is defined in inte-
205       R0
      gral t v(xs , s)ds, can be obtained as:
206
207

208                                     v(xs , s)ds = V (x0 , 0) − V (xt , t)
                                     t

210   where V(xs , s) is an antiderivative of v(xs , s) with respect to s. Then, we defined a function
211   F(x0 , xt , t) as follows:
212                                 F(x0 , xt , t) := V (xt , t) − V (x0 , 0)
213   This function encapsulates the total influence of the dynamics from an intermediate time t to the
214   final time 0, which leads to the equation:
215
                                               x0 = xt − F(x0 , xt , t).

      3.3   N EURAL N ETWORK A PPROXIMATION
230   In practice, the exact form of F(x0 , xt , t) is usually intractable or unknown. Therefore, we model F
231   using a neural network parameterized by θ. The approximated predictive model is thus defined as:
232
233                                     fθ (x0 , xt , t) = xt − Fθ (x0 , xt , t)                           (5)
234   where Fθ approximates F.
235
      To improve performance, especially in complex scenarios such as VE case of diffusion model or the
236
      PFGM++ model (will be shown in section 4), a more robust and flexible formulation is required to
237
      ensure the stability of the integration flow. We redefine the dynamics fθ (x0 , xt , t) as the following:
238
239                                   fθ (x0 , xt , t) = at xt + bt Fθ (x0 , xt , t).                      (6)
240   where at and bt are time-dependent scalar functions designed to modulate the contributions of xt
241   and F(x0 , xt , t), respectively. This formulation introduces greater flexibility in the evolution of the
242   integration flow over time, particularly in scenarios where the straightforward application (equa-
243   tion 5) of integration may introduce instability, especially when the magnitudes of the intermediate
244   state xt become large.
245
      To achieve accurate recovery of x0 , it is essential that:
246
247                                            fθ (x0 , xt , t) ≈ f (x0 , xt , t)

458   6     D ISCUSSION
459
460   Integration Flow presents a straightforward yet powerful approach that unifies different types of
461   ODE-based generative models. Its core strength is simplicity: instead of relying on complex iterative
462   sampling or solving ODEs step-by-step, Integration Flow directly learns the overall transformation
463   dynamics, allowing for one-step generation. By using a pseudo-Huber loss function—simple and
464   easy to work with—the model benefits from stable training and minimal parameter tuning, making
465   it both scalable and adaptable across various ODE-based frameworks.
466   A key achievement of Integration Flow is its ability to solve different ODE-based models using a
467   single framework, addressing a significant challenge that prior models struggled with. Fore example,
468   two important generative models, Rectified Flow and diffusion model, are not unified, but Integra-
469   tion Flow can successfully integrate them. This not only simplifies the landscape of ODE-based
470   generative models but also expands their applicability, making them easier to implement across dif-
471   ferent domains.
472                                            (n)
      Since Integration Flow keeps track of x0 for each sample in the dataset, there will be additional
473   memory consumption during training. Specifically, it requires extra 614MB for CIFAR-10. Al-
474   though it can be halved by using FP16 data type, such memory requirement might still be a chal-
475                                                                                               (n)
      lenge for larger dataset or dataset with high-resolution images. One solution is to store x0 in a
476   buffer or on disk instead of on the GPU. However, this approach will introduce additional overhead
477   during training due to the need to transfer data back to the GPU. We will fix this out as our future
478   work.
479
      Although, Integration Flow has achieved best performance on one-step generation for Rectified Flow
480   and PFGM++, the performance of Integration Flow for VE is still slightly underperformed compared
481   to current the-state-of-the-art. We hypothesize that this small performance gap may be attributed to
482   suboptimal hyperparameters in the loss function. Due to limited computation resouce, we are not
483   able to search the best hyperparameter. Additionally, we recognize that different noise schedulers
484   can significantly impact the model’s performance. The noise scheduling strategy plays a crucial role
485   in the training dynamics and final performance of the model. We plan to investigate more complex
      schedulers in future work.
