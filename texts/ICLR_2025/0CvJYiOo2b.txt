009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning has significantly advanced time series analysis (TSA), enabling the
012            extraction of complex patterns for tasks like classification, forecasting, and regres-
013            sion. While dimensionality reduction has traditionally focused on the variable
014            space—achieving notable success in minimizing data redundancy and computa-
015            tional complexity—less attention has been paid to reducing the temporal dimension.
016            In this study, we revisit Principal Component Analysis (PCA), a classical dimen-
017            sionality reduction technique, to explore its utility in temporal dimension reduction
018
               for time series data. It is generally thought that applying PCA to the temporal
               dimension would disrupt temporal dependencies, leading to limited exploration in
019
               this area. However, our theoretical analysis and extensive experiments demonstrate
020
               that applying PCA to sliding series windows not only maintains model performance
021            but also enhances computational efficiency. In auto-regressive forecasting, the
022            temporal structure is partially preserved through windowing, and PCA is applied
023            within these windows to denoise the time series while retaining their statistical
024            information. By preprocessing time series data with PCA, we reduce the temporal
025            dimensionality before feeding it into TSA models such as Linear, Transformer,
026            CNN, and RNN architectures. This approach accelerates training and inference
027            and reduces resource consumption. Notably, PCA improves Informer training and
028            inference speed by up to 40% and decreases GPU memory usage of TimesNet
029
               by 30%, without sacrificing model accuracy. Comparative analysis against other
               reduction methods further highlights the effectiveness of PCA in enhancing the
030
               efficiency of TSA models. Code is provided in the supplementary materials.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Time series analysis (TSA) plays a pivotal role across various fields (Van Zyl et al., 2024; Hittawe et al.,
036   2024), owing to its ability to extract valuable information from sequential data, facilitating accurate
037   predictions and classifications. Recent advancements in the field have witnessed the emergence of
038   sophisticated deep-learning models (Eldele et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2021) designed
039   to effectively analyze time series data.
040
      Dimensionality reduction techniques have been successfully applied to reduce complexity in time
041
      series data, but their focus has primarily been on the variable dimension (Xu et al., 2023; Hyndman
042   et al., 2015). These methods, which aim to minimize redundancy in variable space, have been
043   effective in reducing computational complexity and improving model performance. However, far
044   less attention has been given to reducing the temporal dimension, despite the potential benefits of
045   alleviating the burdens associated with processing long time series.
046
      The time series lengths are generally larger than the number of the variable sizes, suggesting that
047
      temporal dimensionality reduction should provide better compression. The long time series is
048   segmented to time series windows in the auto-regressive forecasting task, and recent studies show that
049   larger window length includes more temporal information and hence brings better forecasting results
050   (Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2022). Therefore, a fundamental paradox arises: the contradiction
051   between the length of the series windows and the ease of TSA model learning. While longer
052   windows provide more information, they also increase the difficulty of model learning: raw series
053   data inherently contains redundancy (Li et al., 2023; Prichard & Theiler, 1995), and inputting such
      data can significantly increase both the computational and spatial burdens associated with model

082   To the best of our knowledge, there has been no systematic method for compressing time series data
083   in temporal dimension while preserving critical information before feeding it into deep-learning
084   models. Simple downsampling the series inevitably results in information loss within time series
085   data, while merely shortening the input window leads to degraded prediction performance (Nie et al.,
086   2022; Gao et al., 2023). Existing time series feature extraction methods (Ye & Keogh, 2009; Schäfer
087
      & Leser, 2017) are typically tailored to specific tasks like classification and are computationally
      expensive, prioritizing performance over efficiency.
088
089   To address these challenges, we explore the use of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Pearson,
090   1901) for feature extraction and temporal dimensionality reduction of time series data before inputting
091   it into deep-learning models. PCA is effective at capturing essential information while reducing
092   data dimensionality across various domains (Gewers et al., 2021). The PCA algorithm identifies
093
      principal components that represent directions of maximal variance, and projecting the data onto these
      components extracts fundamental features and uncovers latent patterns. However, PCA’s application
094
      to time series preprocessing has been underutilized due to the unique characteristics of time series
095
      data. In normal data, the order of features is irrelevant, and there is no temporal correlation between
096   features, as shown in Fig. 1 (a). In contrast, in time series data, each time step can be considered
097   a “feature”, and the sequential order of these features (time steps) is crucial, introducing temporal
098   correlations, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (b). Although PCA might disrupt the temporal structure, it
099   also refines the information by retaining key statistical characteristics and reducing overfitting and
100   redundancy, which enhances training efficiency without compromising model performance.
101
      We propose that PCA is well-suited for compressing redundant time series in temporal dimension
102   and extracting salient features. Specifically, PCA mitigates noise and redundancy by isolating key
103   features and reducing correlations among different time steps, thereby lowering the risk of overfitting
104   in deep-learning models. Additionally, by analyzing the entire training dataset, PCA captures shared
105   patterns and maps time series onto principal components that represent common features. This process
106   preserves key statistical information while shortening the original series. Therefore, preprocessing
107   time series with PCA before inputting it into deep-learning models can alleviate computational
      burdens, reduce learning difficulty, while retaining performance in TSA tasks.

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Time Series Analysis Models. Traditional TSA models like ARMA and ARIMA (Box et al., 2015)
135   rely on statistical foundations, assuming linear relationships between past and present observations
136
      to discern patterns in the time series data. However, the rise of deep-learning models has gained
      significant attention in TSA due to their enhanced expressive capability and ability to effectively
137
      utilize available data, leading to improved performance over traditional statistical models. RNN-
138
      based models (Chung et al., 2014; Hochreiter, 1997) are initially employed in TSA tasks due to their
139   capability to process sequences and capture temporal dependencies. However, due to their inherent
140   difficulties in propagating gradients through many time steps, RNN-based models often encounter
141   issues of gradient vanishing or gradient explosion (Hanin, 2018). CNN-based models (Liu et al.,
142   2022; Luo & Wang, 2024) constitute a major branch within deep-learning, aiming to capture temporal
143   dependencies through convolutional layers. Notably, TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023) transforms 1D time
144   series into a set of 2D tensors based on multiple periods and employs a CNN structure to extract
145   features from these tensors. Transformer-based models have also found widespread application
146   in TSA, leveraging self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) to capture long-term dependencies across
147
      different time steps. Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) achieves a complexity reduction to O(L log L) by
      replacing the conventional self-attention mechanism with KL-divergence-based ProbSparse attention.
148
      FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022) achieves a complexity reduction to O(L) by employing frequency-
149
      domain self-attention through the use of Fourier or wavelet transforms and the random selection
150   of frequency bases. Fredformer (Piao et al., 2024) is designed to address frequency bias in TSF by
151   ensuring equal learning across various frequency bands. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) partitions the
152   time series into multiple segments, treating each as a token, and employs an attention module to
153   learn the relationships between these tokens. Additionally, DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) leverages a
154   linear model to attain noteworthy results in TSF tasks, demonstrating the efficacy of linear models
155   in the domain of TSA. SparseTSF (Lin et al., 2024b) is a lightweight Linear-based model that uses
156   Cross-Period Sparse Forecasting to decouple periodicity and trend, achieving superior performance
157   with fewer parameters.
158   PCA applications in various domains. PCA (Pearson, 1901) has diverse applications in various
159   domains (Marukatat, 2023). In computer vision, (Zhang et al., 2023) proposes a texture-defect
160   detection method using PCA, requiring only a few unlabelled samples and outperforming traditional
161   and deep-learning methods for small and low-contrast defects. (Lin et al., 2024a) introduces a tensor
      robust kernel PCA model to effectively capture the intrinsic low-rank structure of image data. For

      natural language processing, Rémi and Ronan simplify word embeddings via PCA, outperforming
163   existing methods on named entity recognition and movie review tasks (Lebret & Collobert, 2013).
164   In bioinformatics, (Elhaik, 2022) examines the application of PCA in population genetic studies.
165   Moreover, in the domain of engineering, (Hasnen et al., 2023) proposes a PCA-based drift correction

      While PCA has seen extensive use across domains, its application specifically to TSA in temporal
168
      dimension has been underexplored until recently. A recent study (Xu et al., 2023) incorporates PCA
169   preprocessing into a Transformer-based forecasting framework to reduce redundant information.
170   However, their approach has several limitations. Firstly, it applies PCA to reduce the variable
171   dimension rather than the temporal dimension, making it similar to conventional PCA applications
172   and not an actual series reduction. Secondly, it is designed for scenarios where a multivariate series
173   forecasts a univariate series, focusing on reducing the variable dimension of covariate series without
174   preprocessing the target variable series, even if the covariate series may have minimal association
175   with the target series. Lastly, its exclusive use of the Transformer model and its focus on forecasting
176   tasks limit the method’s applicability to other types of time series models or tasks, thereby restricting
177
      its utility for time series data. Other related works involving PCA in TSA (Hyndman et al., 2015;
      Rea & Rea, 2016) either focus on reducing the variable dimension or on reducing the dimensions of
178
      manually extracted features from the original time series, neither of which constitutes actual temporal
179
      dimension reduction.

182   3     M ETHODOLOGY
183
184   Given a historical series window H = {X1 , ..., XL }, where L represents the length of the series
185   window, we consider three TSA core tasks. 1) Time Series Classification (TSC): The objective is to
186   predict a discrete class label C for the series H; 2) Time Series Forecasting (TSF): The goal is to
187   forecast future values of the same series, denoted as F = {XL+1 , ..., XL+T }, where T is the number
188   of future time steps to predict; 3) Time Series Extrinsic Regression (TSER): In some applications like
189   predicting heart rate from photoplethysmogram and accelerometer data (Tan et al., 2021), neither
190
      forecasting nor classification is applicable. TSER involves predicting a single continuous target value
      V external to the series based on the input historical series H.
191
192   By encapsulating all inputs (historical series window H) and outputs (class label C, future series F
193   or external target value V), a complete series dataset D can be formed, where D = [X; Y]. Here,
194   X is composed of all the historical series, X = [H1 ; . . . ; Hm ], with m representing the number
195   of samples, and Y consists of the corresponding targets to be predicted (C, F, or V). The entire
196
      dataset D can be split into the training set Dtrain , validation set Dval , and test set Dtest . PCA-related
      parameters (covariance matrix, eigenvalues, and eigenvectors) are obtained from the training set Dtrain
197
      and subsequently applied to both the validation set Dval and the test set Dtest without re-estimation.
198

200   3.1   E NHANCING T IME S ERIES A NALYSIS WITH PCA
201
      Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Pearson, 1901) is a classical technique for dimensionality
202
      reduction and feature extraction. For the training set of the series dataset Dtrain , which consists of n
203   training samples, each with L features (where L is also the length of the series window, and each
204   time step corresponding to a feature), PCA aims to transform the series data into a new coordinate
205   system where the data variance is maximized along the principal components. The process can be
206   summarized as follows:
207
      1. Mean-Centering: Before applying PCA, the mean of each feature (time step) is subtracted from
208
      the corresponding column to center the data. The mean-centered matrix is denoted as Dcentered , with
209   each element given by:
210

211
212   where D̄train (j) is the mean of the j-th column.
213
      2. Covariance Matrix: The covariance matrix C is computed based on the mean-centered data:

      along each principal component.
232
233   4. Selecting Principal Components: The principal components are selected based on the proportion
234   of variance they explain. The k-th principal component is given by P Ck = Dcentered · vk , where vk is
235   the k-th eigenvector.
236   5. Reducing Dimensionality: To reduce the dimensionality of the time series data, the original data
237   matrix is projected onto the first k principal components, forming a new matrix Dpca ∈ Rn×k :
238
                                               Dpca = Dcentered · Vk ,                                                               (3)
239
240   where Vk is a matrix containing the first k eigenvectors as columns. k s typically much smaller than
241
      m, thereby achieving series dimensionality reduction.
242   When dealing with high-dimensional and large datasets, the original PCA technique might pose
243   computational challenges. However, optimization algorithms such as Randomized PCA (Rokhlin
244   et al., 2010), Sparse PCA (Zou et al., 2006), and parallel computation (Andrecut, 2009) can signifi-
245   cantly expedite the PCA computation process, making PCA preprocessing computationally efficient
246   compared to the subsequent deep-learning model training/inference stage.
247   In our study, PCA is utilized in the preprocessing stage for time series dimensionality reduction
248   before feeding the data into various deep-learning based TSA models. The original time series is
249   transformed into a PCA series containing the top k principal components. The essence of PCA
250   guarantees that the transformed PCA series preserves the fundamental features of the original series.
251   Prior to training the TSA model, PCA is fitted on the training set to obtain PCA-related parameters
252   (covariance matrix, eigenvalues, and eigenvectors). During inference, each time series sample is
253
      preprocessed with the fitted PCA before being input into the TSA model. For typical time series
      models (Zeng et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2021), the original historical series can be directly transformed
254
      using PCA. In contrast, for patch-based models (Nie et al., 2022) that split the series into patches, all
255
      patches from the original series are transformed separately using PCA and then concatenated. The
256   reduced-dimensional PCA series serves as the input for the subsequent TSA model and is applied to
257   the various downstream TSA task, as illustrated in Fig. 2. This method enables more efficient training
258   and inference while retaining the essential information captured by the principal components. While
259   this PCA-based preprocessing method is originally designed for univariate TSA, it can readily be
260   extended to multivariate TSA if subsequent TSA models are channel-independent (Nie et al., 2022).

262   3.2   I NTUITIONAL JUSTIFICATIONS ON PCA’ S E FFECTIVENESS IN T IME S ERIES R EDUCTION
263
264   PCA is effective in time series data reduction due to several key advantages. It serves as an effi-
265   cient noise reduction tool by filtering out low-variance noise and retaining high-variance features.
266
      Additionally, PCA preserves the critical statistical characteristics of the original series.
267   PCA acts as an efficient tool for noise reduction within historical series. By projecting the
268   original historical series onto a new set of orthogonal components, PCA effectively filters out
269   the noise contained in the lower variance components, thus retaining the core information of the
      historical series. This noise reduction can be visualized through PCA-inverse transformations, which

      PCA retains the critical statistical characteristics of the original time series data. The key
289
      statistical characteristics preserved by PCA include the mean, sum, peak values, and higher-order
290
      moments. Specifically, for the mean/sum values of historical series, PCA simply maps the original
291   time points into a different coordinate system, preserving the relative mean/sum values of different
292   historical series. Fig. 3 (b) illustrates the mean values distributions of 30 original series and their
293   corresponding PCA series, demonstrating a high degree of overlap between the two distributions. For
294   the peak values, the distribution of peaks in the PCA series also shows a high degree of similarity to
295   that of the original series. Furthermore, PCA preserves higher-order moments, including skewness
296   and kurtosis (Bai & Ng, 2005), because its linear transformation ensures that these higher-order
297   statistical characteristics remain intact. The preservation of these distinctive statistical characteristics
298   in the PCA-reduced series enables effective learning by subsequent TSA models.
299   Specific trends and periodic patterns in historical series may not be crucial for the learning of
300   TSA models. While some time series research focuses on extracting trends or periodicity (Zhou et al.,
301   2022; Wu et al., 2023), we argue that these specific trends and periodic patterns are not necessarily
302   essential for effective TSA model learning. For example, if all positive trends in the historical series
303   are reversed, the relative distribution of the data remains unchanged, and the TSA model’s ability to
304   learn and predict is not impaired. Similarly, if the periodic components in all historical series are
305
      minified or magnified, the model’s predictive capabilities should not be affected. These observations
      suggest that the presence of specific trend or periodicity in historical series is not necessarily essential
306
      for the learning process of TSA models. Instead, the presence of consistent and coherent patterns is
307
      sufficient for models to provide accurate predictions. Therefore, although PCA may alter the trend or
308   periodicity, it introduces new coherent patterns—such as the main directions of variation, denoised
309   low-dimensional representations, and latent features—that effectively benefit TSA model learning.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   Our study challenges the perception that PCA, by disrupting sequential relationships in time series,
530   is unsuitable for TSA. Instead, we find it efficient for handling TSA tasks. PCA is innovatively
531
      applied to achieve temporal dimensionality reduction while safeguarding essential information within
      time series. Its effect is evaluated on four types of advanced time series models, namely Linear,
532
      Transformer, CNN and RNN models, across three typical TSA tasks: classification, forecasting,
533
      and regression. The results show that PCA reduces computational burden without compromising
534   performance. Specifically, in TSC, the performance with PCA is better in 50.0% of cases; in TSF,
535   49.6% of cases; and in TSRE, 66.7%. Notably, PCA accelerates Informer’s training and inference
536   by up to 40%, with a minimum of 10% speedup for other models. Additionally, PCA reduces
537   GPU memory usage by 15% for Transformer-based models and 30% for CNN-based models. The
538   study discusses PCA’s theoretical effectiveness in denoising and preserving statistical information,
539   further substantiating its superiority over alternative dimensionality reduction methods such as series
      shortening, downsampling, and the integration of additional reduction layers.

776   C    TSF R ESULTS OF PATCH TST WITH PCA P REPROCESSING
777
778   PCA preprocessing is separately applied to each patch series in the patch-based time series model
779   PatchTST. Additionally, to enhance prediction stability, PatchTST employs instance normalization
780   technology (Kim et al., 2022). However, integrating this technology with PCA series poses challenges:
781   the fluctuation of PCA series is considerable, and adding instance normalization further destabilizes
782   the predictions. Consequently, after applying PCA processing, we exclude the instance normalization
783   module from PatchTST. For comparative analysis, we also assess the performance of PatchTST
784   without the instance normalization module on the original series.
785   Table 7 presents the forecasting results of PatchTST. It is observed that the original PatchTST
786   achieves optimal performance. However, a surprising discovery is the pivotal role played by the
787   instance normalization process in PatchTST. Omitting the instance normalization module results in
788   a significant deterioration in PatchTST performance, exhibiting much worse results compared to
789   training PatchTST (also without the instance normalization module) after PCA preprocessing. These
790   findings suggest that PCA is effective for patch-based time series models, yet further exploration is
791   required to identify alternative methods to instance normalization.

794   D    TSF R ESULTS OF RNN- BASED MODELS WITH PCA P REPROCESSING
795
796   Due to issues with gradient vanishing or exploding (Hanin, 2018), RNN-based models exhibit unstable
797   performance in TSA with long historical series windows and have consequently been increasingly
798   supplanted by Transformer, linear, and CNN-based models. Nonetheless, to more comprehensively
799   evaluate the impact of PCA preprocessing, we assess its effect on RNN-based models for TSF tasks.
800   Specifically, two typical RNN-based models, GRU (Chung et al., 2014) and LSTM (Hochreiter,
801
      1997), are tested. Original historical series or PCA series are fed into the GRU or LSTM cells
      to extract features, and their hidden state h, containing the feature information, are projected and
802
      transformed to obtain the final predictions. Table 8 shows that for GRU, PCA preprocessing leads to
803
      superior performance in 18 out of 32 settings, and for LSTM, PCA preprocessing achieves better
804   results in half of the settings. These results indicate that PCA preprocessing does not degrade the
805   performance of RNN-based models. Additionally, since RNN models process time series sequentially,
806   their computational cost is more sensitive to the length of the model input. Table 9 demonstrates that
807   PCA preprocessing has a significant acceleration effect on RNN-based models, reducing training
808   time to one-fourth and inference time to one-third of the original times. Although RNN-based models
809   are not as commonly used as other models, PCA remains an effective tool for time series reduction in
      scenarios where they are appropriate.
