009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Gradient descent dynamics in deep linear networks has been studied under a wide
012            range of settings. These studies have reported some negative results on the role of
013            depth, in that, gradient descent in deep linear networks: (i) can take exponential
014            number of iterations to converge, (ii) can exhibit sigmoidal learning, i.e., almost
015            no learning in initial phase followed by rapid learning, (iii) can delay convergence
016            with increase in depth. Some of these results are also under stronger assumptions
017
               such as whitened data and balanced initialisation. These messages from prior
               works suggest that depth hurts the speed of convergence.
018
019
               In this paper, we argue that the negative role of depth in the prior works is due
               to certain pitfalls which can be carefully avoided. We give a positive message
020
               on the role of depth, i.e., seen as a additional resource, depth can always be used
021
               to speed up convergence. For this purpose, we consider scalar regression with
022            quadratic loss. In this setting, we propose a novel aligned gradient descent (AGD)
023            algorithm for which we show that (i) linear convergence is always possible (ii)
024            depth accelerates the speed of convergence. In AGD, feature alignment happens
025            in first layer and the deeper layers accelerate by learning the right scale. We
026            show acceleration in AGD happens in finite time for unwhitened data. We provide
027            insights into the acceleration mechanism and also show that acceleration happens
028            in phases. We also demonstrate the acceleration due to AGD on synthetic and
029            benchmark datasets. Our main message is not to propose AGD as a new algorithm
030
               in itself, but to demonstrate that depth is an advantage in linear networks thereby
               dispelling some of the past negative results on the role of depth.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Deep learning has been successful in a wide variety of machine learning tasks. There are two key
036   ingredients to this success namely (i) as the depth increases, deep models can express a rich class of
037   non-linear functions and (ii) most often that not, the right function from this rich class can be found
038   by gradient descent, a simple algorithm. As a result, analysing the dynamics of gradient descent in
039   deep models has turned out to be an important question in machine learning. However, this analysis
040   is quite challenging due to the presence of non-linearity.
041
      Deep linear networks have been widely studied in the literature Arora et al. (2018a;b); Bartlett et al.
042   (2018); Ji & Telgarsky (2018); Ziyin et al.; Saxe et al. (2014); Atanasov et al. (2021), mainly as an
043   analytical surrogate for deep non-linear networks. In the case of linear networks, increasing depth
044   does not help in increasing the expressivity. However, the dynamics of gradient descent is non-
045   linear, and exhibits similar phenomena observed in deep non-linear networks. At the same time, due
046   to the absence of non-linear activation functions, the analysis of deep linear networks turn out to be
047   simpler than the non-linear counterparts.
048   Analysis of gradient descent dynamics in deep linear models also is a steep task. Some of the results
049   are under restrictive assumptions such as whitened data Saxe et al. (2014); Arora et al. (2018a);
050   Bartlett et al. (2018) and balanced initialisation Arora et al. (2018b); Atanasov et al. (2021) and
051   small learning rate Saxe et al. (2014); Arora et al. (2018b). Even under such restrictions, the results
052   in the literature paint a rather negative picture of of the role of depth. To elaborate, it has been
053   observed that gradient descent in deep linear networks: (i) can take exponential number of iterations
      to converge Shamir (2018), (ii) can exhibit sigmoidal learning, i.e., almost no learning in initial


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      phase followed by rapid learning Saxe et al. (2014), (iii) can delay convergence with increase in
055   depth Saxe et al. (2014); Arora et al. (2018b). Given that such phenomena are empirically observed
056   in deep non-linear networks as well, the overall belief is that depth hurts convergence even in linear
057   networks.
058
      In this paper, we decouple the analysis of gradient descent in deep linear networks from its utility in
059
      serving as an analytical surrogate for deep non-linear networks. Instead, our aim is to understand the
060
      role of depth when compared to one layer shallow linear networks. While increasing depth in linear
061   networks increases the computational overhead, the question is whether it is possible speed up the
062   convergence. In other words, we investigate the extra computations vs faster convergence trade-off.
063   For the problem of scalar linear regression with quadratic loss, under minimal assumptions on the
064   data, we ask and answer the following question:
065
066   Can a deep linear network achieve faster convergence in finite time than a shallow one layer linear
067                       network? If so, what is the extra computational overhead?
068
      Contributions: In this paper, we provide a positive answer to the above question, that is, depth
069
      helps in increasing the speed of convergence. Our specific contributions are
070
071          • We propose a novel algorithm called aligned gradient descent (AGD) (Algorithm 1) for
072            deep linear networks and prove (Theorem 1) that it achieves faster convergence in finite
073            time than gradient descent on a shallow one layer linear network.
074
             • AGD provides instance-wise acceleration, in that, for any learning learning rate that is sta-
075            ble for the shallow linear network, AGD achieves faster convergence in deep linear network
076            for the same learning rate.
077
             • We show that AGD requires only 5L extra computations (per iteration and per example)
078
               when compared to one layer shallow linear network. This is only a negligible increase in
079
               the computations when the number of input dimension is much larger than L.
080
081
             • We provide insights into the mechanism underlying acceleration and demonstrate empiri-
               cally that AGD achieves acceleration in phases one for each eigenvector component.
082
083          • We demonstrate via numerical experiments on synthetic and standard datasets that our AGD
084            on deep linear networks indeed converges faster than the one layer shallow linear network.
085
086
      Organisation: In Section 2, we present the setting and the basic convergence result for gradient
      descent in one layer shallow linear networks. In Section 3, we discuss briefly the negative results
087
      on the role of depth reported in prior works. We point out the possible pitfalls which cause such
088
      negative results. In Section 4, we present our aligned gradient descent algorithm, present its finite
089   time convergence result and discuss how it avoids the pitfalls in the prior works. In Section 4, we also
090   discuss the acceleration mechanism in AGD (Section 4.3), demonstrate its instance-wise speed up
091   property and study its role of depth (Section 4.2), and demonstrate that it achieves faster convergence
092   (in comparison to GD in shallow network) on standard datasets (Section 4.5). In Section 5 we review
093   the related works and make concluding remarks in Section 6.
094
      Our Message: While AGD indeed speeds up convergence, our goal is far from selling AGD as a
095   better optimisation method for scalar linear regression. Rather, via AGD our aim is to demonstrate
096   that some of the apparently negative results on the role of depth are not an inherent property of depth
097   but are due to some pitfalls which are carefully avoided in AGD. In other words, our goal is to show
098   that depth as a resource works to our advantage in linear networks, and AGD is one of the ways to
099   achieve it.

      3    P RIOR W ORK : ROLE OF D EPTH IN L INEAR N ETWORKS
156
157   Understanding gradient descent (GD) dynamics in deep non-linear networks is an important prob-
158   lem in machine learning. However, given the difficulty of this problem, many works have studied
159   gradient descent dynamics on deep linear networks for the following reasons:
160
161          • Even though the output is a linear function of the input, the dynamics of gradient descent
               in deep linear networks is still non-linear.


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • Deep linear networks exhibit many phenomena also found in deep non-linear networks
163            such as long plateaus followed by fast transition to lower error solutions Saxe et al. (2014).
164
165
             • In linear networks increasing depth does not alter their expressivity. Hence the role of depth
               in optimisation can be clearly separated Arora et al. (2018b).
166
167
      Even analysing gradient descent in deep linear networks in the most general setting is a steep task.
168   Thus, many works make several restrictive assumptions such as small learning rates Arora et al.
169   (2018a;b), whitened data Arora et al. (2018a), balanced initialisation Arora et al. (2018b); Ji &
170   Telgarsky (2018); Atanasov et al. (2021), near identity initialisation Bartlett et al. (2018), lp losses
171   for p > 2 Arora et al. (2018b) to provide some useful insights. Such restrictive assumptions for
172   linear networks are not a deterrent as long as one can replicate and obtain insights into phenomena
173   occurring in deep non-linear counterparts. That said, some of the messages in the past works appar-
174   ently seem to suggest that depth hurts speed of convergence even in deep linear networks. In what
175   follows, we review some of those results.
176
177   3.1   P LATEAUED C ONVERGENCE : N UMBER OF ITERATIONS SCALES EXPONENTIAL IN DEPTH
178
179
      Shamir (2018) considered the gradient descent dynamics in one-dimensional (i.e., dl = 1, ∀l ∈ [L])
180   networks, i.e., the problem of learning ŷ(x; Θ) = Θ(L) · · · Θ(1) x where the weights are all scalars
181   (∀l ∈ [L], Θ(l) ∈ R). It was shown that for small enough learning rate, for Xavier (Theorem
182
      2,Shamir (2018)) and near-identity initialisation (Theorem 3, Shamir (2018)) for gradient descent
      to converge, the number of iterations required scales exponentially in depth. It was demonstrated
183
      empirically that such phenomena also occur in multi-dimensional networks. The essence of their
184
      argument is captured in the following example.
185
186   Consider a two layer network with one hidden unit whose output is given by ŷ(x) = Θ(2)Θ(1)x.
187   The dataset has only one data point, which is x = 1, y = 1. The loss function is L(Θ) =
188
      (Θ(2)Θ(1) − 1)2 . In this example, for Θ0 (1) = 1, Θ0 (2) = −1 and infinitesimally small learning
      rates, it can be shown via simple calculations that, ∀t ≥ 0, Θt (1) = −Θt (2), Θt (1) > 0, Θt (2) < 0,
189
      and Θt (1) → 0+ and Θt (2) → 0− , i.e., the output ŷ(x) → 0− . For finite but small learning rates,
190
      the loss hits a plateau and stays there for exponentially large number of iterations.
191
192   Possible Pitfall and Our Fix: The key issue here is that at initialisation itself the network output
193   is aligned in the wrong direction with respect to the target. We fix this issue in our aligned gradient
194
      descent algorithm (Algorithm 1), by ensuring that in iteration, the alignment of the output of the
      deep linear network with the target is as good as the alignment in a shallow one layer network.

225

252   rate of convergence. In AGD, all the hidden units are aligned to the same feature direction; key
253   idea is to let the first layer to align to the right feature direction and the deeper layers accelerate in

255   adaptive learning rates which are implicitly derived from the iterates of the algorithm itself. In what
256   follows, we present AGD and describe its key ingredients and state its finite time convergence result
257   in Theorem 1.

491   5   R ELATED W ORK
492
493   The literature on deep linear network (DLN) is rich, and of these, works relevant to us are of two
494   kinds (i) those on gradient descent and (ii) those on neural tangent kernel (NTK) alignment.
495   Saxe et al. (2014) analysed GD with infinitesimal learning rate. They showed that DLNs too ex-
496   hibit phenomena found in deep non-linear networks such as plateauing followed by fast transition
497   to low error solutions. They also showed that deep linear networks incur a delay in learning speed
498   relative to shallow networks. However, they provided a class of random orthogonal initialisation
499   under which this delay in learning speed is finite even as the depth of network approached infin-
500   ity. Arora et al. (2018b) studied the advantage of depth in DLNs because in DLNs depth cannot
501   help in expressiveness and if any can only can help in optimisation. They showed that in the scalar
502
      regression setting with lp loss (p > 2), under a balanced initialisation of weights, depth helps in
      acceleration. They showed that this acceleration due to depth cannot be achieved by other precondi-
503
      tioning schemes. However, in their experiments they also reported that in the l2 case, depth mildly
504
      hurts the speed of convergence. Bartlett et al. (2018) analyse a gradient descent for quadratic loss
505   and whitened data. They consider a specific sub-class, in that, they consider only linear residual
506   networks with uniform width across layers. For this setting, they show that gradient descent takes
507   polynomial (in the depth) iterations to converge an ϵ approximate solution. Arora et al. (2018b)
508   extended Bartlett et al. (2018) significantly and showed that for l2 over whitened data and balanced
509   weight initialisation, gradient descent converges at linear rate. Shamir (2018) prove (under mild
510   assumptions) for one-dimensional network that for standard random initialisation, the number of
511   iterations required for convergence scales exponentially with the depth. Atanasov et al. (2021) look
512   at the dynamics of the neural tangent kernel during training of deep non-linear and linear networks
513
      for the case of quadratic loss, under small and balanced weight initialisation and whitened data.
      In the case of DLNs, the analytically establish the phenomena of silent alignment wherein, in the
514
      early stage (phase I) of training the NTK aligns (to the final NTK) and during rest of the training
515
      (phase II) the NTK evolves only in scale. This implies that the learnt function equivalent to a kernel
516   regression solution with the final NTK. They also show that this alignment kernel depends on the
517   depth. However, they also demonstrate that non-whitened data weakens the silent alignment effect.
518
519
      Our work: Saxe et al. (2014); Arora et al. (2018b); Shamir (2018) note that depth slows down the
      rate of convergence. On the contrary, we show that depth actually speeds up convergence. In com-
520
      parison to Arora et al. (2018a); Bartlett et al. (2018), we do not make any whitening assumption and
521
      our results hold for any general data matrix. Our initialisation scheme also differs from the afore-
522   mentioned works which either use randomised or balanced or near identity initialisation. Another
523   important aspect of our results is that it is finite time. Atanasov et al. (2021) only analyse alignment
524   in the case of whitened data and do not use alignment in an algorithm. In contrast, in our work, we
525   use alignment (even for unwhitened data) to propose an algorithm which speeds up convergence.

      6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   Gradient descent (GD) in deep linear networks have been widely studied in literature mainly as
531
      a surrogate to deep non-linear networks. The aim of such study is to throw light on phenomena
      that occur in deep non-linear networks. Prior works on deep linear networks have reported that
532
      depth plays a negative role in the convergence of gradient descent in deep linear networks. In this
533
      paper, we argued that such negative results are due to pitfalls which we carefully avoid in our novel
534   aligned gradient descent algorithm (AGD). We presented finite time guarantees for AGD and showed
535   that it achieves accelerated convergence when compared to GD on a shallow one layer network in
536   theory as well as experiments on synthetic and benchmark datasets. We discussed the acceleration
537   mechanism of AGD. We also show pointed out how GD for deep linear networks can lead to unstable
538   convergence when data is unwhitened, and we showed that AGD does not suffer from such unstable
539   behaviour. We conclude that depth is an advantage and helps to speed up convergence in deep linear
      networks.
