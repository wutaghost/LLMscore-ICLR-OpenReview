009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               How can we effectively learn to make decisions when there are no ground-truth
012
               counterfactual observations? We propose an end-to-end learning approach to the
013
               contextual stochastic optimization problem under decision-dependent uncertainty.
014            We propose both exact methods and efficient sampling-based methods to implement
015            our approach. We also introduce a new class of two-stage stochastic optimiza-
016            tion problems to the end-to-end learning framework. Here, the first stage is an
017            information-gathering problem to decide which random variable to “poll” and
018            gain information about before making a second-stage decision based off of it. We
019            provide theoretical analysis showing (1) that optimally minimizing our proposed
020            objective produces optimal decisions and (2) generalization bounds between in-
021            sample and out-of-sample cost. We computationally test the proposed approach
022
               on multi-item assortment problems where demand is affected by cross-item com-
               plementary and supplementary effects. Overall, our method outperforms other
023
               benchmarks by more than 15% and performs best in high noise, across any cost
024
               configuration, and when given sufficient data. We also introduce an experiment for
025            the information-gathering problem on a real-world electricity generation problem.
026            We show our method proposes decisions with more than 7% lower cost than other
027            decision-making methods.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   We consider the general problem of contextual stochastic optimization under decision-dependent
034   uncertainty. Often in decision-making one is faced with a two-step problem: first to predict some
035   unknown quantity/random variable such as product demand, and second to make an operational
036   decision based off of this such as allocating inventory. We will consider settings in which this
037   operational decision-making process is well-defined through traditional optimization-based methods
038   as is common in many applications ranging from pricing, to inventory allocation, to scheduling. In
039
      this paper we consider that the random variable is dependent on the decision made. For example, the
      demand (random variable) of a product will change depending on the price (the decision) set. We
040
      refer to this as endogenous uncertainty.
041
042   Here, the predictive model needs to take the decision as an input as well as additional features.
043   In order to make a decision, one would need to optimize over the predictive model’s input. This
044   brings several challenges. (1) The more complex the learning model, the more difficult to optimize.
045
      Linear-like models are most tractable, but provide less predictive power. On the other hand, neural
      networks or random forests with more predictive power are significantly more expensive to optimize
046
      over. (2) When optimizing over the entire input space, it becomes easy to choose decisions that are
047
      far out-of-sample and for which the model has poor predictive power. This may result in decisions
048   with significantly worse objective than predicted. As an example for pricing, the actual demand
049   could be significantly lower than predicted. This is especially problematic when there is sparse or
050   limited data. Moreover, it is often unclear what aspect of the distribution to predict. For instance,
051   while one is interested in maximizing mean reward, it is generally not optimal to choose the decision
052   which maximizes the reward of the mean counterfactual outcome. We will see this explicitly in the
053   following sections as well as the experimental section. We introduce an approach to jointly predict
      and optimize in this endogenous setting which learns a prediction aligned with expected cost.

      Existing end-to-end frameworks (all under exogenous random variables) are not able to tackle the
055   single-stage endogenous problem The traditional end-to-end formulation requires knowledge of the
056   outcome or value of a decision taken. When the uncertainty is independent of the decision taken,
057   one can simply use the data as the ground truth. For example, demand is independent of supply in a
058   warehouse. Therefore, for any decision taken based off of a learned demand prediction, one can then
059   calculate the cost using the realized demand observed in the data. This cost is then used as the loss
060   for the demand prediction problem. However, when the uncertainty depends on the decision, one can
061   no longer take this approach since one does not have access to counterfactual information. That is,
062   we do not have knowledge about what would have happened if a different action were taken.
063   This endogenous end-to-end problem is also closely connected to information-gathering problems
064   which have not been studied under the end-to-end learning framework to date. In this class of
065   problems, there is an initial stage before the prediction and decision-making step where one is
066   allowed to gather information about some of the random variables ahead of time. For example, one
067   can send out a survey or set up a poll to better understand demand at a particular location. Given
068   this new information, one can then make more informed predictions about the rest of the random
069
      variables (for e.g. demand at the remaining locations), and subsequently make a more informed
      decision. If demand across locations is correlated, one can gain significant information from polling
070
      a single location. As a first stage, one must decide which location to poll, observe information
071
      about this location, then make a new prediction and a decision for all locations conditioned on this
072   new observation. So, there are three questions (1) which random variable should we poll (2) what
073   predictions to make conditioned on observing this chosen variable and (3) what decision to make
074   based off of these predictions.
075
      In contrast to the first endogenous uncertainty setting we presented, the random variables here are not
076
      dependent on the decisions we take. However, our knowledge of the random variable does depend on
077
      the first stage polling decision we must make. We extend our proposed method for end-to-end learning
078   with endogenous random variables to this two-stage information-gathering setting. In this paper, we
079   propose a new method of applying the end-to-end ideology to this setting of endogenous uncertainty
080   and the closely related information-gathering problem. We present the following contributions:
081
082   1. We formulate an end-to-end, or joint prediction and optimization, approach when the realizations
083      of uncertainty are dependent on the decisions taken. The objective is to train a model whose
084      corresponding predictions on in-sample decisions have task-based reward close to the observed
085      reward in-sample. See section 2.
086   2. We provide theoretical analysis showing that (1) optimally minimizing our proposed objective
087      produces optimal decisions and (2) convergence bounds on the generalization gap with respect to
088      the amount of training data used and model complexity.
                                                           √       Overall, the generalization gap between
089      in-sample and out-of-sample cost decreases as 1/ N where N is the amount of training data.
090   3. Due to the non-convex nature of the end-to-end objective, we provide mixed-integer optimization
091      formulations, as well as a computationally efficient sampling-based approach. See Appendix A.
092   4. We extend our proposed method to the information-gathering problem. This is a combination of
093
         both traditional end-to-end methods under exogenous variables in the second-stage problem and
         under endogenous variables in the first stage problem. This two-stage problem class has not been
094
         studied under the end-to-end learning setting to date.
095
      5. Finally, we show computational experiments on a multi-item assortment optimization problem
096
         where the demand of a product is dependent on decisions taken for all other items due to comple-
097      mentary or supplementary effects. We show the end-to-end approach improves significantly on
098      traditional two-stage methods. We also consider an electricity generation and scheduling problem.
099      Here we make an initial forecast, and must decide on the optimal time to update the forecast. This
100      involves learning how to balance the benefits of waiting for more accurate information against the
101      costs of delaying decisions. See section 4.

103   Related Work. Within the space of end-to-end offline contextual stochastic optimization there has
104   been relatively little work in the case of endogenous uncertainty for general optimization problems.
105   For instance, Bertsimas & Koduri (2022) primarily focuses on exogenous uncertainty and briefly
106   mentions endogenous uncertainty as an extension by adding the historical decision as an additional
107   feature to use to learn the outcome. However, this does not take into account the pitfalls we mentioned
      earlier. Endogenous uncertainty has been studied primarily for specific problems such as pricing Liu

      & Zhang (2023) where demand naturally changes according to price, or a facility location problem
109   Basciftci et al. (2021) where demand changes depending on where a facility is placed. However
110   these take significantly different approaches from ours, not learning any parameteric model to predict
111   uncertainty, or ignoring learning goals from that of decision making (an aspect we refer to as two-
112   stage, or predict-then-optimize). Within the scope of exogenous uncertainty, where the decision
113   does not affect the uncertainty, there is a variety of work addressing different classes of objective
114   functions and single or two-stage/multi-stage problems. This includes the work of Elmachtoub &
115   Grigas (2022), Amos & Kolter (2017), Agrawal et al. (2019) and more. We refer the reader to surveys
116   Kotary et al. (2021); Sadana et al. (2023) for a more comprehensive survey.
117   Online learning and multi-armed bandit problems also have a similar problem setting. For example,
118   contextual linear bandits Chu et al. (2011) or more recently the estimation-to-decision-making meta-
119   algorithm Foster & Rakhlin (2020) focus on learning the decision-dependent reward of actions.
120   There are a variety of extensions relevant to our work, like continuous action spaces, Majzoubi et al.
121   (2020), Krishnamurthy et al. (2020) or large action space Dulac-Arnold et al. (2015) in reinforcement
122   learning. Even more similar to our scenario is offline learning or offline reinforcement learning
123
      Lange et al. (2012), Levine et al. (2020). In the end-to-end setting we consider, we are given more
      information about the problem structure than in usual bandit/RL problems. Specifically, there is
124
      some intermediate random variable that one observes (such as demand, while reward corresponds to
125
      revenue) for a given action (such as inventory allocation). Our approach makes explicit use of this
126   additional structure and this is one of the main reasons we observe better performance than methods
127   that directly learn reward. Work on performative learning, such as Perdomo et al. (2020), focuses
128   on how predictions themselves may affect observations (such as how traffic predictions will affect
129   driver behavior and hence traffic itself). This work focuses on how the distribution shifts over time,
130   depending on how predictions are made.
131
      In contrast to more common reward-learning approaches, we consider the offline, not the online
132   learning setting. Learning the relationship between decisions and the intermediate random variable
133   also has additional advantages. Specifically, we may have additional domain knowledge about this
134   relationship. For example, demand of a product is generally a monotonically decreasing function of
135   price. This additional structure can be imposed on the learning process. However, learning revenue
136   directly as a function of price does not exhibit such structure we can take advantage of. We give a
137   more in-depth comparison with other methods in the next section.
138   The problem we consider is essentially a contextual stochastic programming problem with decision-
139   dependent uncertainty. In the non-contextual case, there has been significant work on developing
140   methods to solve these complex problems. See for example Goel & Grossmann (2006), Dupacová
141   (2006). These are difficult-to-solve problems, even in the no-context case when explicitly knowing
142   the distribution of the random variable. The main advantage of an end-to-end approach is in reducing
143   this complexity. Instead of making a distributional prediction of the relationship between decision
144   and the random variable, we make a deterministic one. One can view our end-to-end approach as
145   learning which deterministic prediction will lead to the same decision compared to when making a
146
      distributional one. We prove in proposition 2.1 such a prediction does exist under mild assumptions.
147   Versions of the information-gathering problem have been studied as well. The most closely related
148   line of work is in the area of value of information by Howard (1966). This aims to decide the
149   amount a decision maker would be willing to pay for information prior to making a decision. This
150   notion of value information is particularly relevant in reinforcement learning applications, deciding
151   which actions to explore to gain the most useful information (for e.g. Arumugam & Van Roy 2021).
152
      This work still differs significantly as ours also considers the specific structure of the objective and
      intermediate random variables as we explained previously.

      Learning the mean. We remark that it is crucial to learn an fˆ with this task-based loss. The
230
      common approach in ML would be to learn a model which learns the mean of the distribution of z.
231
      That is, minimize the mean-squared error between predictions and historical observations. We will
232   denote this as a two-stage approach, first predicting z independent of the task loss g, then optimizing
233
      for the optimal decision. The two-stage predictor, fˆ2-stage , is a predictor of the mean E[z]. The
234   issue arises in the second stage when optimizing. In general E[g(w, z)] ̸= g(w, E[z]) when g is
235
      non-linear in z. Therefore, optimizing maxw∈P g(w, fˆ2-stage (w, x)) would be a proxy for optimizing
236   maxw∈P g(w, Ez∼Z(w,x) [z]) but not the true objective which is maxw∈P Ez∼Z(w,x) g(w, z).
237
238   Learning reward directly. Many methods in online learning such as contextual bandits or rein-
239   forcement learning learn the reward function directly, instead of the intermediate random variable z.
240   This removes the issues in the previous section about learning the mean of z since here we directly
241   optimize the reward. However, there are several computational downsides to reward-learning. Here,
242   we learn a mapping r(w, x) ≈ Ez∼Z(w,x) [g(w, z)] while only observing wn , xn and g(wn , zn )
243   and not zn itself or the structure of the function g. Directly learning the reward function requires
244   a more complex class of predictors to capture this relationship compared to an end-to-end method.
245
      Simplifying the predictor class r is crucial as the complexity of r directly impacts the difficulty of
      solving maxw∈P r(w, x) in large/continuous action space. Our approach allows for simpler model
246
      classes while still capturing the same complexity in modeling Ez∼Z(w,x) [g(w, z)]. We further see
247
      this explicitly in the numerical computations, see section 4.

      3    A PPLICATION TO INFORMATION GATHERING
288
      Here we consider a novel application of the end-to-end method to a class of 2-stage optimization prob-
289
      lems with information-gathering. As an example, consider a multi-warehouse inventory allocation
290
      problem. In the first stage, we can choose a single location to poll to learn the demand for the next
291   time period. In the second stage, we must (1) predict the demand at all other locations, conditioned
292   on our previous observation from the poll then (2) decide how much inventory to allocate at all
293   warehouses. We note that this is different from traditional 2- or multi-stage stochastic optimization
294   problems. There, the first stage is some operational decision (for e.g. inventory allocation in a
295   warehouse), and then in the second stage some additional information is revealed (such as demand).
296   In our setting, there is a deliberate initial action to decide which additional information to reveal
297   ahead of time (for e.g. before allocating inventory, we can poll one location to know exact demand).
298   One approach would be to learn a model that predicts, for every location, the expected cost that results
299   from polling it. This simplifies the problem but hides the structure behind it and makes the learning
300   problem more complex, requiring a richer class of functions to approximate it. We explicitly observe
301   the advantage of using the problem structure while learning an end-to-end model in the experiments.
302
      Formally, we are given exogenous random variables z = (z1 , . . . , zd ), independent of the decisions
303   that we take. In the first stage, the decision space will consist of choosing some index w ∈ P =
304   {1, . . . , d} to survey, or gain more information about, the wth entry of z, namely zw . This could
305   be more general beyond observing a single value, for example observing multiple values. We will
306   see this in an experiment in section 4. But we will keep this simple here for the sake of notation.
307   As another example, this could correspond to setting up a survey to learn more about the demand
308   of the wth product. In the second stage, we make a prediction for the remaining random variables,
309   conditioned on the observation of zw . Note this prediction does depend on the decision we initially
310   took to survey the wth random variable. In the second stage, we are given some auxiliary decision

674   the previous method by sampling from perturbing the mean-squared predictor. Let θ1 be the final
675
      model. (3) For k > 1, use θk−1 as the initial model, generate S new samples by perturbing θk−1 ,
      then apply gradient descent to minimize task-based loss. Choose θk with the best loss from these.
676
      Finally, (4) return the model with θD .
677
678   The iterative learning method is essentially a super-set of the first method. We observe it provides
679   generally better results as well in the numerical experiments. Intuitively, this makes sense: as we add
680   more data, we fine-tune the previous model learned. Moreover, this is also useful when data arrives
681
      online. One observes data up to a time point, then make a new decision, then observe the outcome.
      This new observation becomes a new datapoint that can be used for training.
