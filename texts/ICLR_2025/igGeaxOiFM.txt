009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The need for parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has emerged as large pre-
012            trained models are increasingly employed in specialized downstream tasks.
013            Among PEFT methods, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is widely adopted due
014            to its ability to fine-tune models with minimal additional parameters. How-
015            ever, LoRA’s down-projection mechanism can lead to significant feature loss,
016            particularly for tasks involving complex features and reasoning. This limitation
017            poses a challenge in maintaining model performance in scenarios requiring high-
018            dimensional representations. To address this issue, we introduce Householder
019            Orthogonal LoRA (HoLoRA), which reparametrizes the down-projection matrix
020
               as a semi-orthogonal matrix, thereby mitigating feature loss. Our approach en-
               sures strict orthogonality without increasing computational costs or modifying
021
               LoRA’s core components. Experimental results on the GLUE benchmark show
022
               that HoLoRA consistently outperforms standard LoRA across various tasks, par-
023            ticularly in low-rank settings. By preserving essential features and improving
024            fine-tuning efficiency, HoLoRA provides a robust solution to the limitations of
025            existing PEFT methods. This advancement enhances LoRA’s applicability in com-
026            plex learning environments, promoting better performance in both low-budget and
027            high-complexity scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Recent research shows that models pre-trained on large datasets effectively capture general features
      and patterns within a data domain, exhibiting strong generalization and adaptation capabilities. This
034
      makes them highly suitable foundations for adaptation to specific downstream tasks. Fine-tuning
035
      large pre-trained models, such as Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023), with task-specific training data has
036   become a mainstream approach for solving these downstream tasks (Shen et al., 2021). Compared
037   to training models from scratch, fine-tuning leverages the knowledge learned by pre-trained models,
038   reducing both time and computational costs while achieving improved performance.
039
      However, driven by the scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024a), the size of pre-trained
040
      models has increased rapidly. As a result, the cost for full-tuning (Raffel et al., 2020), which involves
041
      updating all the model parameters, has become extremely high for large-scale models. Full-tuning
042   requires substantial computational and memory resources, as the tuning process requires the storage
043   of all model parameters and their gradients.
044
045
      To reduce the cost of fine-tuning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been intro-
      duced, which significantly decrease training costs while maintaining performance comparable to
046
      full-tuning. There are two main strategies in PEFT. The first is selective methods (Guo et al., 2020;
047
      Mahabadi et al., 2021), which involve choosing a subset of the original model parameters for tun-
048   ing. However, these methods require the new task to be closely related to the one for which the
049   model was pre-trained, making them less effective for tasks that are drastically different or involve
050   cross-domain scenarios. The second strategy is additive methods, such as Adapters (Houlsby et al.,
051   2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2020; Rücklé et al., 2020), Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022),
052   and prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021). These approaches add additional trainable components to
053   the model’s architecture and keep the original model frozen during training, allowing for efficient
      adaptation to new tasks while preserving the performance of the original model.

054
      Most additive methods tend to modify the model architecture, which can result in an increased in-
055   ference cost. However, LoRA is one of the most widely adopted methods because, after merging
056   the additive component with the original model, it does not alter the model architecture. Typically,
057   LoRA only tunes less than 1% of the model parameters, while achieving performance that is com-
058   parable to or even better than full-tuning.
059
      In LoRA, given a pre-trained weight matrix W0 , the incremental update ∆ is parameterized as the
060
      product of two low-rank matrices:
061
062                                        W = W0 + ∆ = W0 + BAT ,                                            (1)
063
                          m×n
064   where W, W0 ∈ R          , with m and n being the output and input dimensions of the layer, respec-
065
      tively. The matrices B ∈ Rm×r and A ∈ Rn×r have a rank r such that r ≪ min{m, n}. During
      training, the pre-trained weight W0 is frozen and only the incremental matrices A and B are updated.
066
067   However, LoRA tends to perform poorly on complex tasks, particularly in multi-task learning and
068   high-dimensional tasks such as mathematical reasoning and coding, which demand high representa-
069   tional power or reasoning capabilities (Biderman et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024). If we regard matrices
070   A and B as two consecutive layers without an activation function, the input layer A acts as a highly
071
      down-projection matrix (as r ≪ min{m, n}) which performs a feature compression of the input
      data. Such compression extracts important features for task-specific learning, but also poses the risk
072
      of feature loss. Furthermore, Zhang et al. (2024b; 2023b) also suggest that LoRA adaptation tends
073
      to be dominated by top singular vectors of the weight matrix, implying that the effective hidden
074   dimensionality may be even smaller than the preset rank r. This limitation restricts LoRA’s ability
075   to capture the diverse and complex features required for more challenging tasks.
076
077
      Orthogonality restriction can be used to alleviate the problem of feature loss for general neural
      network training (Ranasinghe et al., 2021). Considering both efficiency and budget constraints,
078
      research on LoRA variations (Zhang et al., 2023b;a) has introduced the use of a regularization term:
079
080                                          Reg(A) = λ∥AT A − I∥2F ,                                         (2)
081
082   to restrict the matrix A to be near (semi-)orthogonal1 . This method poses two potential challenges.
083   Firstly, the calculation of the regularization term introduces additional computational steps, reducing
084
      training efficiency (Mao et al., 2024). Secondly, the introduction of a new hyperparameter λ com-
      plicates tuning: a large λ can slow convergence, while a small λ may lead to ineffective orthogonal
085
      restriction. This raises the question that we address in this paper:
086
087    Can we preserve the strict orthogonality of the down-projection matrix A in LoRA during
088    parameter updates without adding significant computational burden or introducing new
089                                       hyperparameters?
090
091   In response, we propose HoLoRA(Householder orthogonal Low-Rank Adaptation), which constructs
092   an easy-to-compute function h : Ln×r → Vn,r , where Ln×r is the space of n × r (non-strict) lower
093   triangular matrices, and Vn,r is the Stiefel manifold (the set of n × r semi-orthogonal matrices). We
094   reparametrize the matrix A as:
095                                                A = h(MA )                                            (3)
096   and only learn the parameters in MA , so that A can be kept orthogonal during the whole training
097   process. Compared to other LoRA variation methods, our main contributions are:
098
099         1. Strict orthogonality: We impose a strict orthogonal restriction on the given matrices,
100            which better alleviates the problem of feature loss.
101         2. Computation efficiency: The function h is easy to compute, so the training time is similar
102            as vanilla LoRA.
103
            3. Compatibility with LoRA variants: Our method preserves the fundamental structure of
104
               vanilla LoRA, ensuring compatibility with many other LoRA variation techniques.

      2     P RELIMINARIES AND R ELATED W ORKS

110   2.1    L O RA
111
112   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) is inspired by the finding that the incremental update weight matrix of fine-
113   tuning has intrinsic dimensions that can be approximated by low-rank matrices. LoRA parameterizes
114   the update matrix ∆W with two learnable matrices, significantly reducing the number of trainable
115   parameters. The formula of the LoRA weight update for a weight matrix in one single layer is:
                                                                   α
116                                    W = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BAT ,                                            (4)
117                                                                r
118   where W0 , W ∈ Rm×n are the weight matrices before and after the update. ∆W ∈ Rm×n is the
119   incremental update matrix, referred to as the “delta adaptation block”. The matrices B ∈ Rm×r
120
      and A ∈ Rn×r contain the learnable parameters, and m, n are the output and input dimensions,
      respectively. The scalar α is a hyperparameter controlling the scaling factor of the delta block2 . r is
121
      the rank of delta block, which is the preset budget parameter, as the total learnable parameter of a
122
      weight matrix will be (m + n) · r. Given the following property:
123
124
                                     rank(BAT ) ≤ min{rank(A), rank(B)},                            (5)
125   the delta block is low rank and upper-bounded by r. Extensions to LoRA, such as AdaLoRA (Zhang
126   et al., 2023b), explore adaptive budget allocation for PEFT, improving performance by dynamically
127
      reallocating rank budgets among layers according to their importance.
128   Other approaches, such as IncreLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023a), suggest incremental parameter allo-
129   cation during training to better utilize the model’s capacity and avoid underfitting of critical layers,
130   while DyLoRA (Valipour et al., 2022) dynamically adjusts the low-rank adaptation throughout train-
131   ing to improve generalization on various downstream tasks. These extensions address limitations
132   of static budget allocation in LoRA and provide pathways for more efficient fine-tuning of large
133
      language models. However, none of these methods ensure strict orthogonality within the learned
      parameters, which can lead to feature loss in some complex tasks.
134
135
136
      2.2    O RTHOGONAL F INE -T UNING (OFT)
137   Orthogonal Fine-Tuning (OFT) (Qiu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024) adapts the pre-trained model
138   weights by multiplying them with an orthogonal matrix:
139
                                                  W = U W0 ,                                          (6)
140
      where U is an orthogonal matrix. This orthogonality constraint is intended to mitigate feature loss
141
      during adaptation by preserving important properties of the pre-trained weights. Regularization
142
      methods, as seen in Equation (2), have been explored to maintain the orthogonality of U . However,
143   balancing computational efficiency with strict orthogonality remains a challenge.
144
145   The study by (Yuan et al., 2024) proposes an orthogonality restriction for OFT using Householder
146
      reflectors, offering a way to impose strict orthogonality without significant computational overhead.
      However, their approach is limited to square matrices and does not directly apply to LoRA’s down-
147
      projection matrices, which are typically rectangular.
148
149   OLoRA (Orthonormal LoRA) (Büyükakyüz, 2024) aims to address feature preservation by encour-
150   aging orthogonality within the low-rank adaptation. However, this is achieved via regularization,
151   which introduces additional computational costs and requires tuning of hyperparameters, such as
152
      the regularization strength λ. Thus, this approach struggles to achieve a balance between computa-
      tional efficiency and strict orthogonality.
153
154   In contrast to these existing methods, our proposed HoLoRA directly addresses the issue of main-
155   taining strict orthogonality in the low-rank adaptation matrices without introducing significant
156   computational overhead or additional hyperparameters. By reparametrizing the matrix A using
157   Householder transformations, HoLoRA ensures that the down-projection matrix remains orthogonal
158
      throughout training. This strict orthogonality better preserves feature representation, mitigating the
      risk of feature loss without the need for regularization terms or tuning of hyperparameters like λ,
159
      which are required in other approaches.

325   inversion requires a time complexity of O(r3 ). The matrix operations involved in equation (16) also

      3.4     C OMPATIBILITY OF H O L O RA WITH OTHER L O RA VARIATIONS
352
353   Since HoLoRA only changes the parametrization of the down-projection layer in LoRA, without
354   altering any structural or learning schedule aspects, it remains compatible with a wide range of
355   LoRA variations. We provide insights into possible combinations for future research.
356
357   3.4.1    H O L O RA WITH SVD DYNAMIC R ANK A LLOCATION
358
359   SVD (Singular Value Decomposition) dynamic rank allocation methods (Zhang et al., 2023b;a) for
360   LoRA parametrize the adaptation block in a truncated SVD-like form rather than using A and B
361   matrices:
362                                          ∆W = U SV T ,                                     (19)
363
      where U ∈ Rm×r , V ∈ Rn×r , and S ∈ Rr×r .
364   The SVD form requires U and V to be orthogonal matrices and S to be diagonal. Current methods
365   such as AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023b) and IncreLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023a) enforce these condi-
366   tions using regularization as described in equation (2). Alternatively, the orthogonal restriction can
367   be achieved by utilizing the function h in HoLoRA.
368
      By Remark 3, adding or removing the last columns of MA does not affect the first columns of
369
      h(MA ). Therefore, during dynamic rank allocation, we can adjust the rank by adding or deleting
370   columns of MA , ensuring that the retained singular vectors remain unchanged.
371
372
      3.4.2    H O L O RA WITH L O RA I NITIALIZATION
373
374   OLoRA (Büyükakyüz, 2024) leverages orthonormal matrix initialization via QR decomposition to
375   accelerate LoRA convergence. Since Householder reflectors were originally designed for perform-
376   ing QR decomposition (Householder, 1958), we can use the Householder QR decomposition method
377   to easily obtain the Householder vectors. These vectors can then be used to initialize the columns of
      matrix MA in HoLoRA, achieving an equivalent initialization to OLoRA.

      LoRA-XS (Bałazy et al., 2024) computes the SVD of the pre-trained weight matrix and initializes
379   the LoRA delta block using information derived from the SVD form. If we employ the Golub-
380   Reinsch SVD algorithm (Golub & Kahan, 1965)—a method that uses Householder reflectors and is
381   still widely used for its efficiency—the parameters for the Householder reflectors can be obtained
382   and subsequently used in HoLoRA during training.

      4.1   E XPERIMENT SETTINGS
391
392   Dataset The GLUE benchmark (Wang, 2018) is widely used to evaluate the generalization ability of
393   models across various natural language understanding tasks. We choose eight tasks (MNLI, SST-2,
394   CoLA, QQP, QNLI, RTE, MRPC, STS-B) from GLUE for a general evaluation on single sentence
395   tasks, similarity and paraphrase tasks, and inference tasks. For the evaluation metrics, We use the
396   overall accuracy for MNLI, Matthew’s correlation for CoLA, Pearson correlation for STS-B, and
397   accuracy for all other tasks.
398   Baselines
399
      We compare our method with full-tuning and vanilla LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). LoRA generally
400
      performs better than other PEFT methods such as Adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al.,
401
      2020). We focus on the comparison to LoRA as our method is a general approach to mitigate
402   LoRA’s limitation of feature loss. HoLoRA remains compatible with newer LoRA variants.
403
404   Implementation details To conduct a fair comparison, we build on the codebase of LoRA (Hu et al.,
405
      2022). We use the pre-trained DeBERTaV3-base (He et al., 2021) model from HuggingFace, which
      contains 12 Transformer encoder layers with 768 hidden units, and has a total parameter size of 86M.
406
      Similar to the experimental settings used in the LoRA paper, we adapt only the query (Q) and value
407
      (V) layers of the pre-trained model. For a comparison under different fine-tuning parameter budgets,
408   we perform two sets of experiments, with the rank r set to be 2 and 8 respectively. For MRPC, RTE,
409   and STS-B tasks, we start from the LoRA-adapted MNLI checkpoint for a same setting as LoRA
410   paper. We use the same hyperparameter settings in Table 3, referring to LoRA paper (Hu et al.,
411   2022) and AdaLoRA paper Zhang et al. (2023b)3 , for both LoRA and HoLoRA. All experiments
412   are conducted on NVIDIA L4 Tensor Core GPUs through Google Cloud Platform.

      5   C ONCLUSION
504
      We propose Householder Orthogonal Low-Rank Adaptation (HoLoRA) to address feature loss in
505
      LoRA by adding strict orthogonality restriction on the down-projection matrix. HoLoRA pre-
506
      serves important features while maintaining computational efficiency and compatibility with ex-
507   isting LoRA variations. We conduct experiments on the GLUE benchmark, showing that HoLoRA
508   outperforms vanilla LoRA, particularly in low-budget scenarios where the risk of feature loss is
509   highlighted. This work highlights the value of orthogonality in LoRA, providing insights for future
510   research on integrating HoLoRA with other LoRA methods.
