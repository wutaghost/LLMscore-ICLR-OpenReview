011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Generalization and adversarial robustness are two critical concepts in machine
014            learning. Understanding the key factors that affect the trade-off between these
015            concepts is essential for guiding architectural design and developing training
016
               strategies, such as adversarial training, especially for deep neural networks. In
               this paper, we investigate the impact of cross-layer correlations in weight matrices
017
               on both generalization and adversarial robustness. We provide a theoretical anal-
018
               ysis demonstrating that increasing cross-layer correlations leads to a monotonic
019            increase in the generalization gap. Furthermore, we establish a connection be-
020            tween adversarial risk and natural risk. Leveraging this connection, we show that
021            in linear models, higher cross-layer correlations also degrade adversarial robust-
022            ness. Finally, we validate our theoretical findings through experiments conducted
023            on MLPs.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Generalization is a fundamental concept in the theoretical study of machine learning. Understand-
029   ing the key factors that influence generalization provides crucial guidelines for architectural design
030   and training strategies, particularly for complex models such as neural networks. Despite its impor-
031   tance, how architectural factors in neural networks affect generalization remains unclear. Inspired
032   by research in neuroscience, Jin et al. (2020) suggest that the generalization gap increases mono-
033
      tonically with respect to the weight correlation (WC) of rows in a given weight matrix. Building on
      this insight, we investigate how cross-layer correlations impact both generalization and adversarial
034
      robustness.
035
036   Another important and intriguing property of neural networks is their susceptibility to adversar-
037   ial examples, which can significantly mislead the networkвҖҷs outputs by adding only imperceptible
038   perturbations to inputs. To understand this phenomenon, extensive research has been conducted to
039
      identify the factors that determine adversarial robustness, including input data distribution (Tsipras
      et al., 2018; Gilmer et al., 2018), sampling complexity (Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Min et al., 2021),
040
      optimization techniques (Madry et al., 2017), weight initialization strategies (Zhu et al., 2022),
041
      and model capacity and architectures (Simon-Gabriel et al., 2019; Bubeck et al., 2021; Huang
042   et al., 2021; Wang & Ruan, 2023). Moreover, some studies point to a more fundamental issue:
043   whether there is a trade-off between natural generalization and adversarial robustness. Some stud-
044   ies (Tsipras et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019) claim that there is an inevitable trade-off between gen-
045   eralization and adversarial robustness, even with an infinite amount of input data. However, Fawzi
046   et al. (2018) argues that, under more realistic settings, such trade-offs do not exist. It is possible to
047   enhance generalization without any cost to adversarial robustness.
048   In our work, we consider both generalization and adversarial robustness. Leveraging the PAC-
049   Bayesian framework, we theoretically demonstrate that the generalization gap increases monotoni-
050   cally with respect to cross-layer correlation, indicating that any correlation between weights across
051   layers negatively impacts generalization. This finding aligns with the conclusions drawn by Jin et al.
052   (2020). Additionally, we establish a theoretical connection between natural risk and adversarial risk
053   and perform a first-order analysis of adversarial risk, providing an upper bound for the adversarial
      gapвҖ”the difference between adversarial risk and natural risk. Furthermore, we prove that for linear

      neural networks, cross-layer correlations degrade adversarial robustness. Our main contributions are
055   as follows:

057          вҖў We investigate the cross-layer correlation between adjacent weight matrices and provably

060

064

066            bustness due to cross-layer correlations.

069   2     R ELATED W ORK
070
071   2.1   G ENERALIZATION
072
      From a methodological perspective, the measurement of generalization can be divided into three
073
      categories. The first is based on the well-known VC-dimension (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 2015).
074
      Since the VC-dimension is independent of both inputs and the architecture of the models, it has
075   been demonstrated to be weakly correlated with generalization power (Jiang et al., 2019) for Deep
076   Neural Networks. Consequently, the community has turned to data-dependent Rademacher com-
077   plexity (Mohri et al., 2018) and PAC-Bayesian frameworks (McAllester, 1999), which assume the
078   distribution of weights.
079
      Recent research based on Rademacher complexity includes Bartlett et al. (2017), who provide mar-
080
      gin bounds for neural networks. This work has been further developed by Yin et al. (2019) to address
081   adversarial risk. The most promising methodology in this category is the PAC-Bayesian framework,
082   wherein the complexity measure is empirically demonstrated causally related to the generalization
083   gap (Jiang et al., 2019), which is also the first framework providing a non-vacuous generalization
084   bound (Dziugaite & Roy, 2017). Similar to the work done by Bartlett et al. (2017), Neyshabur
085   et al. (2017) provide another spectral margin bound using PAC-Bayes. Considering adversarial risk,
086   Viallard et al. (2021) provide PAC-Bayesian bounds concerning adversarial robustness for majority
087   votes, where the total variance of the probability measure is included as an additional term instead
088   of KL-divergence.
089   In addition to KL-divergence, which is a critical component of the PAC-Bayesian framework, Xu &
090   Raginsky (2017) provide a bound in terms of mutual information between input and output. Chu &
091   Raginsky (2024) further developed the first unified framework for information-theoretic generaliza-
092   tion bounds. Instead of providing tighter bounds and advanced frameworks, Jin et al. (2020) analyze
093   the influence of weight correlation on generalization power. Though research on generalization is
094   extensive, few studies consider the correlation across layers.
095
096   2.2   A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
097
098   Trade-offs between generalization and adversarial robustness Numerous studies argue that
099
      there is a trade-off between generalization and adversarial robustness. Fawzi et al. (2018) system-
      atically studies the adversarial robustness of linear and quartic classifiers, demonstrating that no
100
      classifier is robust on tasks that are difficult to distinguish between different classes, hence no inher-
101
      ent trade-off exists given infinite input data. Tsipras et al. (2018) show with a binary classification
102   that there is an inherent tension between adversarial robustness and standard generalization. Zhang
103   et al. (2019) propose one of the earliest theoretical analyses of the trade-off between natural and
104   robust errors and designed a defence mechanism, namely TRADE. However, these trade-offs are
105   primarily illustrated with a toy example. Ilyas et al. (2019) claim that adversarial examples are
106   merely non-robust features. More recent work (Gowda et al., 2024) focuses on adversarial train-
107   ing and examines layer-wise learning capacity, proposing a framework named CURE that addresses
      both memorization and overfitting issues.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Factors that influence adversarial robustness Since the finding of adversarial examples (Good-
109   fellow et al., 2014), various studies aim to understand this phenomenon, particularly for neural net-
110   works. Some researchers argue that the source of adversarial vulnerability lies in the input data (Do-
111   briban et al., 2020; Bhattacharjee et al., 2021; Min et al., 2021). More recent research investigates
112   the fragility of neural networks from an architectural perspective. Simon-Gabriel et al. (2019) study
113   the vulnerability of feed-forward neural networks measured by the Lp norm of the loss function
114   with respect to input data, suggesting that vulnerability increases with input dimension, independent
115   of model structures. Daniely & Shacham (2020) examined ReLU neural networks characterized by
116   decreasing dimensions at each layer, asserting that adversarial robustness is intrinsically tied to the
117
      networkвҖҷs architecture, contrasting with the propositions of Simon-Gabriel et al. (2019). Bubeck
      et al. (2021) expanded the findings of Daniely and Shacham from an вҖқunder-complete caseвҖқ sce-
118
      nario to an вҖқover-completeвҖқ one where the number of neurons surpasses the input dimension. They
119
      further broadened the conclusions to encompass Deep ReLU networks, highlighting a crucial role
120   played by bottleneck layers.
121
122   To understand the architectural factors that influence adversarial robustness, it is fundamental to
123
      examine how the layers collaborate to enhance this robustness. In our work, we provide a general
      framework to explore this interaction.

      7    C ONCLUSION
477
478
      Inspired by the works of Jin et al. (2020) and Viallard et al. (2021), we perform a Pac-Bayesian
479
      analysis to examine the impact of cross-layer correlation on the generalization gap. Our findings
480   reveal that the generalization gap increases monotonically with cross-layer correlation. Building on
481   this, we formally propose a framework that connects natural risk with adversarial risk. Utilizing
482   this framework, we demonstrate that in linear models, cross-layer correlation similarly amplifies the
483   robust gap. We empirically test various activation functions in MLPs to validate our proposed the-
484   orem. Furthermore, we uncover compelling evidence of phase transitions in deep neural networks.
485   However, further research is required to refine the measurement of cross-layer correlation in more
      complex models.
