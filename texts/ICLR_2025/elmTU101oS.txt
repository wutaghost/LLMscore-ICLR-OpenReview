009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Combinatorial optimization (CO) problems are classical and crucial in many
012            fields, with many NP-complete (NPC) examples being reducible to one another,
013            revealing an underlying connection between them. Existing methods, however,
014            primarily focus on task-specific models trained on individual datasets, limiting
015            the quality of learned representations and the transferability to other CO problems.
016
               Given the reducibility among these problems, a natural idea is to abstract a higher-
               level representation that captures the essence shared across different problems, en-
017
               abling knowledge transfer and mutual enhancement. In this paper, we propose a
018
               novel paradigm CORAL that treats each CO problem type as a distinct modality
019            and unifies them by transforming all instances into representations of the funda-
020            mental Boolean satisfiability (SAT) problem. Our approach aims to capture the un-
021            derlying commonalities across multiple problem types via cross-modal contrastive
022            learning with supervision, thereby enhancing representation learning. Extensive
023            experiments on seven graph decision problems (GDPs) demonstrate the effective-
024            ness of CORAL, showing that our approach significantly improves the quality
025            and generalizability of the learned representations. Furthermore, we showcase the
026            utility of the pre-trained unified SAT representations on related tasks, including
027
               satisfying assignment prediction and unsat core variable prediction, highlighting
               the potential of CORAL as a unified pre-training paradigm for CO problems.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Combinatorial optimization (CO) is a pivotal area of study in both theoretical computer science
033   and a wide range of applied fields, owing to its broad applicability in solving complex real-world
034   problems, including logistics (Sbihi & Eglese, 2010), network design (Vesselinova et al., 2020),
035   scheduling (Hwang & Cheng, 2001), and finance (Pekeč & Rothkopf, 2003). CO problems are
036   inherently challenging due to their discrete and non-convex nature, which often leads to NP-hard
037   complexity (Karp, 2010), with many instances requiring worst-case exponential time to solve. In
038   response to these challenges, machine learning (ML) approaches have recently emerged in the CO
039
      domain (Bengio et al., 2021; Gasse et al., 2022), offering the potential to reduce solving times by
      exploiting common patterns and structures in CO instances.
040
041   Most existing ML-based approaches for CO primarily emphasize improving problem-specific rep-
042   resentation learning to enhance task performance. While these methods can achieve high accuracy
043   on particular tasks, the representations they learn are typically tailored to specific instances, making
044   them non-transferable across different datasets or problem domains. Consequently, individual mod-
045
      els must be trained for each dataset and task, limiting the potential for broader generalization and
      scalability. This fragmentation hinders the development of more generalizable CO models that can
046
      efficiently solve a wide range of problem types using a unified framework, as in the vision or lan-
047
      guage field (Khan et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023). Moreover, the inherent connections among many
048   CO problems offer a compelling opportunity for unification. Since numerous NPC problems can be
049   reduced to one another, they share a common underlying structure that can potentially be exploited
050   for more efficient representations. The connections suggest that instead of learning problem-specific
051   representations, a higher-level, abstract representation could be developed to capture the essence
052   shared across different CO problems. Such a unified representation would not only enable knowl-
053   edge transfer between problem domains but also facilitate mutual enhancement, as insights gained
      from one problem could benefit the solution of others.

054
      In this paper, we aim to develop the general and high-level representations across CO problems to fa-
055   cilitate various tasks, with a particular focus on graph decision problems (GDPs), which encapsulate
056   the core challenges of CO. Notably, from the 21 NP-complete problems identified by Karp (2010),
057   10 are GDPs, highlighting their fundamental importance. To achieve our objective, it is significant to
058   effectively incorporate and synthesize features from multiple problem types. Therefore, we leverage
059   contrastive learning, a technique widely employed for modality alignment in vision-language pre-
060   trained models (Du et al., 2022). However, applying contrastive learning to CO problems presents a
061   significant challenge due to the inherent differences between CO problems, rendering direct appli-
062   cation impractical. In response, we propose a sophisticated and unified training paradigm, CORAL,
063
      that enables effective contrastive learning across graph CO problems. Specifically, to align with the
      multi-modal training perspective, we conceptualize each GDP type as a distinct problem modality.
064
      To bridge the gaps among GDP types, we introduce the Boolean satisfiability (SAT) problem as a
065
      unified intermediary modality. The SAT modality is used to construct strong correspondence with
066   other GDP types through instance transformation, thereby establishing connections among GDPs.
067   In the training phase, instances from each GDP type are concurrently contrasted with the corre-
068   sponding SAT instances, thereby fusing features across problem modalities. The contrastive-based
069   training enables each model to learn high-level representations from multiple problem types, serving
070   as a pre-training phase. The trained models are finally fine-tuned on specific datasets and tasks.
071
      Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of the CORAL paradigm. First,
072   we assess the performance of the models on standard tasks adopted during the pre-training phase,
073   including GDP solving and satisfiability prediction, to demonstrate the superiority of the represen-
074   tations learned through CORAL. Subsequently, we evaluate the generalizability of the models by
075   testing them on larger-scale instances, where experimental results indicate that the models trained
076   using the CORAL paradigm exhibit significantly enhanced generalization capabilities. Addition-
077   ally, to further highlight the practical applications of CORAL, we examine the performance of the
078   pre-trained SAT models on related SAT-based tasks across both seen and unseen datasets during the
079   pre-training phase. The main contributions of the paper are as follows.
080   1) We propose CORAL, a novel training paradigm designed to learn high-level representations
081   across multiple CO problems. To the best of our knowledge, it is the first framework to leverage
082   unified representations across different problem types.
083
      2) We introduce SAT as an intermediate, unified modality to bridge diverse GDPs, enabling the
084   effective learning of shared characteristics and information transfer across different problem types.
085
086   3) We conduct extensive experiments on various problems and examine the efficacy of the pre-
087
      trained representations on new tasks and datasets, illustrating the potential of CORAL as a robust
      and unified pre-training paradigm.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
091   Graph Learning for CO. The application of machine learning to graph-based CO problems has
092   a rich history, with recent research demonstrating substantial advancements in this domain (Khalil
093   et al., 2017; Bengio et al., 2021; Mazyavkina et al., 2021). Most ML-based approaches for CO
094   follow a two-stage framework: (1) Graph representation learning, where graph instances are em-
095   bedded into low-dimensional vector spaces (Hamilton et al., 2017b; Cai et al., 2018; Chen et al.,
096   2020a); and (2) The utilization of these learned representations to solve CO problems (Joshi et al.,
097   2019; Prates et al., 2019; Sato et al., 2019). Our CORAL paradigm focuses on enhancing the first
098   stage by proposing a more general training approach. While previous work has largely focused on
099
      designing network architectures (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017a; Veličković et al.,
      2017), our approach emphasizes the development of a training paradigm that leverages information
100
      from multiple problem types. By incorporating a contrastive learning-based strategy, CORAL aims
101
      to learn high-level, transferable representations that can be effectively applied across various CO
102   problems, promoting a more unified and generalizable framework for graph-based CO tasks.
103
104   Graph Contrastive Learning. Current graph contrastive learning frameworks primarily rely on
105   graph augmentations, which can be broadly categorized into two types: (1) structural perturba-
106   tions, such as node dropping, edge sampling, and graph diffusion (Duan et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
107   2023); and (2) feature perturbations, such as adding noise to node features (Hassani & Khasahmadi,
      2020). These augmentation strategies have demonstrated effectiveness across a range of tasks, from

108
      graph-level representations (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020; You et al., 2020) to node-level represen-
109   tations (Wan et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2021). Our CORAL paradigm moves beyond traditional graph
110   augmentations by contrasting graph instances across multiple problem types. Instead of solely rely-
111   ing on structural and feature perturbations, CORAL leverages the inherent characteristics of different

      Solving SAT with ML Approaches. ML-based SAT solvers can be broadly classified into two
      categories (Holden et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023): standalone neural solvers and

      neural-guided solvers. Standalone neural solvers directly address SAT instances (Bünz & Lamm,
116
      2017; Selsam et al., 2019; Cameron et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023). In contrast, neural-guided solvers
117   focus on enhancing the search heuristics of classical SAT solvers (Zhang et al., 2020; Li & Si, 2022).
118   Our CORAL paradigm leverages information from original graph problems to learn more robust and
119   generalizable representations, thereby also improving SAT solving performance.

129   3.1.1    G RAPH DECISION PROBLEM
130
      The graph decision problem (GDP) is a fundamental computational challenge in graph theory and
131   combinatorial optimization, where the goal is to determine the existence of specific properties within
132   a given graph. These properties can vary widely, from identifying whether a graph contains a partic-
133   ular substructure, such as a clique or cycle, to assessing whether it meets conditions like connectivity
134   or planarity. Graph decision problems are typically formulated as yes/no questions, making them
135   essential in complexity theory, especially in the context of NP-complete problems.
136
      ML-based models can be effectively utilized to address GDPs. In such models, the objective is to
137   learn a representation of a specific GDP type and use it to predict decisions based on the input graph.
138   These representations can be understood as mappings that translate the structural properties of the
139   input graphs into corresponding decisions, thereby capturing the underlying patterns required for
140   decision-making in GDPs.
141
      3.1.2 SAT PROBLEM
142
143   A Boolean formula in propositional logic consists of Boolean variables connected by logical oper-
144
      ators “and” (∧), “or” (∨), and “not” (¬). A literal, denoted as li , is defined Wnas either a variable or
      its negation, and a clause cj is represented as a disjunction of n literals, i=1 li . A Boolean Vm for-
145
      mula is in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) if it is expressed as a conjunction of clauses j=1 cj .
146
147
      Given a CNF formula, the Boolean Satisfiability Problem (SAT) aims to determine whether there
      exists an assignment π of Boolean values to its variables under which the formula evaluates to true.
148
      If such an assignment π exists, the formula is called satisfiable, where π is called a satisfying as-
149
      signment; otherwise, it is unsatisfiable. Identifying a satisfying assignment for a Boolean formula
150   proves its satisfiability, and serves as a crucial step in solving practical instances in various applied
151   domains. On the other hand, for an unsatisfiable formula, a minimal subset of clauses whose con-
152   junction remains unsatisfiable is referred to as the unsat core. This subset captures the essential
153   structure responsible for the unsatisfiability. The variables involved in this unsat core are termed
154   unsat core variables. Identifying the unsat core variables is important for understanding the funda-
155   mental sources of unsatisfiability, and plays a critical role in optimization processes.
156   Graph representations play an important role in analyzing SAT formula, with four primary
157   forms (Biere et al., 2009) commonly used: the literal-clause graph (LCG), literal-incidence graph
158   (LIG), variable-clause graph (VCG), and variable-incidence graph (VIG). The LCG is a bipartite
159   graph consisting of two types of nodes—literals and clauses—where an edge between a literal and
160   a clause signifies the occurrence of that literal in the clause. The LIG, in contrast, consists solely of
161   literal nodes, with edges representing the co-occurrence of two literals within the same clause. The
      VCG and VIG are derived from the LCG and LIG by merging each literal with its negation.

197
198   In this section, we provide a detailed introduction to CORAL. Fig. 2 exhibits an overview.
199   Consider a scenario involving n types of GDPs, denoted as P1 , P2 , . . . , Pn , along with n corre-
200   sponding graph sets G1 , G2 , . . . , Gn . For simplicity, assume that each graph set Gi contains m
201   graphs, i.e., Gi = {Gi1 , Gi2 , . . . , Gim }, for i = 1, 2, . . . , n. The objective is to solve problem Pi on
202   graphs in Gi . In total, there are m × n instances, denoted by Iij = (Pi , Gj ), where i = 1, 2, . . . , n
203   and j = 1, 2, . . . , m.
204
      We first transform each of the m×n GDP instances into CNF, thereby generating their corresponding
205
206
      SAT graphs, i.e., (Pi , Gj ) → Bij , where Bij is the constructed (bipartite) SAT graph.
207   Then, we develop n distinct graph models, M1 , . . . , Mn , each for one GDP type, and one unified
208   SAT model Msat to address the problem space. Both the graph models and the SAT model are
209   structured around two key components: the Representation Extractor and the Output Module.
210   The Representation Extractor is responsible for learning and extracting representations from the
211   input graph instances, whether derived from GDP or SAT transformations. The Output Module
212
      then utilizes these learned representations to produce task-specific outputs, thereby enabling the
      resolution of the given problem.
213
214   In the training phase, we simultaneously train the n + 1 models corresponding to the n GDP modal-
215   ities and the SAT modality. The supervision is derived from two parts: the decision loss and the
      contrastive loss. The decision loss is applied independently to each model, guiding it to effectively

787   B.1           I NITIAL V ERTEX F EATURES
788
789
      As illustrated in the main paper, the input graphs primarily provide edge information instead of
      vertex features. Therefore, we should devise initial vertex features for the models. In this section,
790
      we introduce the definition of initial vertex features for the graph and SAT models.
791
792
793
      Graph Model Vertex Feature. We begin by generating a normalized, learnable d-dimensional
      vector, which serves as the initial embedding shared across all vertices. For GDP datasets that do
794
      not require additional problem-specific information, such as Matching and Automorph, this initial
795
      embedding is directly used as the vertex feature for all vertices. In contrast, for GDP datasets where
796   the parameter k plays a critical role in defining the instance characteristics, such as k-Clique and
797   k-Vercov, we first embed k into a d-dimensional vector. The initial vertex embedding is then fused
798   with the k embedding through an MLP to generate the final initial vertex features.
799
800   SAT Model Vertex Feature. For the SAT model, we generate initial vertex features based on the
801   type of SAT graph representation, whether it is a Literal-Clause Graph (LCG) or a Variable-Clause
802   Graph (VCG). In the case of the LCG graph, we initialize a normalized, learnable d-dimensional
803   vector for all literal nodes and a separate normalized, learnable d-dimensional vector for all clause
804   nodes. Similarly, for the VCG graph, we generate a normalized, learnable d-dimensional vector for
805   all variable nodes and another for all clause nodes.

844   B.3    C ASE S TUDY ON M ODEL O UTUT
845
846   In this section, we illustrate the model outputs for specific GDP and corresponding SAT problems
847   for better understanding.
848   In the context of GDP, the model’s output is typically binary, represented as 0 or 1, at the instance
849   level. For instance, in the case of the k-Clique problem, the input consists of a graph, and the output
850   indicates whether the graph contains a clique of size k. Specifically, if a k-Clique is present, the
851   output is 1; otherwise, it is 0.
852
      Similarly, for the corresponding SAT problem, the output denotes the satisfiability of the formula. If
853
      the formula is satisfiable, the output is 1; if not, it is 0. The satisfiability result is directly linked to
854   the solution of the original GDP problem. For example, a satisfiable formula indicates the existence
855   of a k-Clique in the original graph.
856
857
      However, the framework is not restricted to this specific task alone. By making appropriate modi-
      fications to the architecture of the output module, the models can be adapted to solve other related
858
      tasks, including both SAT-based and GDP-based tasks.

993    We revise the negative sampling strategy within our contrastive learning framework to mitigate the
994    issue of false negative samples. Specifically, within each training batch, unsatisfiable instances
995    are selected as negative samples for satisfiable instances, and conversely, satisfiable instances are
996    chosen as negative samples for unsatisfiable instances. This adjustment ensures that false negative
997    samples are avoided. Consequently, we modify the contrastive loss function to reflect this change
998    and proceed with the training of the models. The results, as shown in Table 12, demonstrate that
999
       the models trained with the revised contrastive loss exhibit performance comparable to that of those
       trained with the original loss. We also plot the contrastive loss curves for several GDPs during the
1000
       original training process in Fig. 4, all of which exhibit smooth trajectories. These results suggest
1001
       that the influence of false negative samples on model performance is minimal.
