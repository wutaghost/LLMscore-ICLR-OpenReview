009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Feature attribution is widely accepted as a form of explanation for reasoning ma-
012            chine decisions, indicating the proportion of each feature’s contribution to an
013            inquired decision. While most efforts have focused on determining attributions
014            through exact gradient measurements, recent work has adopted gradient estimation
015            to derive explanatory information requiring only query-level access – a restricted
016            yet more practical accessibility assumption known as the black-box setting. Follow-
017            ing this direction, this paper extends the idea of utilizing estimated gradients to a
018
               broader framework and introduces GEFA ((Gradient-estimation-based Explanation
               For All)). Unlike the previous attempt that focused on explaining image classifiers,
019
               the proposed explainer derives feature attributions in a proxy space, making it
020
               generally applicable to arbitrary black-box models, regardless of input type. In
021            addition to its close relationship with Integrated Gradients, we find, surprisingly,
022            that our approach – a path method built upon estimated gradients – outputs unbi-
023            ased estimates of Shapley Values. By avoiding the potential information waste
024            sourced from computing marginal contributions, it improves the quality of derived
025            explanations, as demonstrated by our quantitative evaluations.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030     With the explosive growth of deep learning models, explainability has become an increasingly
031   important research topic. While data-driven models excel in performance, their opaque nature,
032   originating from the implicit learning processes, raises concerns and risks, particularly when deployed
033
      in critical domains such as medical diagnosis, finance, and autonomous driving. The demand for
      transparency has seen the development of various techniques, including feature attribution, which is
034
      the focus of this work.
035
036   Current attempts to determine feature attribution typically fall into two categories depending on
037   the model accessibility assumption: the white-box and black-box methods. White-box approaches
038   assume full access to a model, deriving explanations by investigating in detail the model’s internal
039
      workings through, for example, analysis of gradients (Simonyan et al., 2014; Sundararajan et al.,
      2017) or supervision of information flow (Samek et al., 2021). Albeit beneficial to explanation
040
      procedures, the full accessibility assumption limits the applicability of white-box approaches under
041
      practical settings due to safety and security concerns. Models deployed for public use are usually
042   wrapped by limited APIs and accessible only via queries. On the other hand, the black-box explainers,
043   following the assumption of query-level access, determine feature attributions by analyzing the
044   correlation between input features and model outcomes (Ribeiro et al., 2016). As a trade-off for the
045   loosened accessibility assumption, black-box explanations tend to be less precise, especially when
046   explaining models operating in high-dimension feature spaces. This is because inferring explanatory
047   information indirectly from queries is computationally expensive, with the cost positively correlated
048   to the dimensionality of the feature space.
049   To combine the strengths of both categories, Cai & Wunder (2024) proposes GEEX, a path method
050   built upon gradient estimation. Focused on the problem of explaining image classifiers, GEEX
051   delivers gradient-like explanations under a black-box setting, achieving a performance that matches
052   white-box explainers. However, the discussion made is limited to models that take continuous features
053   as inputs, and the method struggles with discrete or categorical features like texts. This limitation
      arises from GEEX’s reliance on path integral, which is not well-defined in discrete feature spaces.

054
      While applying GEEX at the embedding layer is indeed a reasonable circumvention, it is arguable
055   that transforming from the original feature space to some embedding space already accesses internal
056   model details, thereby violating the black box assumption.
057
058
      Bridging the gap in the applicability to models operating on discrete data, this paper extends the
      idea of gradient-estimation-based explanation and introduces GEFA1 (Gradient-estimation-based
059
      Explanation For All), a general feature attribution framework built upon carefully designed proxy
060
      variables. These proxy variables facilitate the implementation of gradient estimation and path
061   integral, regardless of input types or formats. The proposed method comes with strong theoretical
062   guarantees. First, GEFA is an unbiased calculator of Shapley Values (Shapley, 1953), which is
063   demonstrated through rigorous mathematical proof. Compared to previous attempts in computing
064   Shapley Values, GEFA reduces potential information waste in sampling-based estimations, which
065   compute marginal contributions Mitchell et al. (2022), and avoids calculations of factorials in the
066   kernel method (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) for determining sample weights. Second, we show that our
067   black-box explainer differs from Integrated Gradients (IG), a white-box approach by (Sundararajan
068   et al., 2017), in only the path choice. It is proved that the two approaches become equivalent when
069
      their paths are aligned, emphasizing the connection between the gradient-estimation-based approach
      and actual gradients. Finally, we design a simple control variate that is guaranteed to improve
070
      explanation quality under a simple and realistic assumption. Its effectiveness is demonstrated through
071
      quantitative experiments across various settings.

      2       R ELATED WORK
075
076
077   Gradients are widely used to allocate feature attributions in a white-box setting as they reveal a
078
      model’s sensitivity to changes in feature values. In the early development of explainability, Simonyan
      et al. (2014) and Smilkov et al. (2017) interpreted gradients directly as explanations. Their methods
079
      retrieve explanatory information by tracing partial derivatives of a decision function with respect to
080
      its input features. Although adopting vanilla gradients is a reasonable starting point, gradients by
081   themselves reflect local sensitivity and do not truthfully represent contributions of feature presence
082   without a proper definition of feature absence.
083
084
      IG (Sundararajan et al., 2017) addresses the limitation of vanilla gradients with a baseline point
      modeling feature absence. The approach integrates gradients over a straightline path connecting
085
      the baseline and the explaining target, thereby capturing the overall impact of feature presence.
086
      Following work by Sturmfels et al. (2020) explored the impact of baseline choice and suggested
087   adopting a distribution, rather than a deterministic instance, as the baseline (Erion et al., 2021). Other
088   extensions of IG include decomposing noise directions from the path integral (Yang et al., 2023),
089   refining explanations by filtering out high frequencies (Muzellec et al., 2024), and investigating
090   feature interactions through the integration of second-order derivatives (Janizek et al., 2021). Parallel
091   to these efforts in improving the explanation procedure, Decker et al. (2024) demonstrated that a
092   proper linear composition of explanations from various approaches yields provable improvements.
093   The family of propagation-based methods (Montavon, 2019) represents a significant alternative
094   white-box solution, which designs layer-wise back-propagation rules that explicitly utilize model
095
      architecture information for the retrieval of explanatory information. As this paper focuses primarily
      on gradient-based and gradient-like explanations, we refer interested readers to the survey by (Samek
096
      et al., 2021) for further details on relevance propagation.
097
098   Unlike white-box methods, which have direct access to model details, black-box explainers de-
099   termine feature attributions by collecting and analyzing observations. The idea was proposed by
100   LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), which generates queries by altering feature values of the original input
101
      and collects model responses to the perturbed instances. By solving a linear regression problem
      with the observed input-output pairs, LIME derives regressor coefficients as feature attributions.
102
      Subsequently, Lundberg & Lee (2017) proposed KernelSHAP, a kernel method that approximates
103
      Shapley Values using weighted linear regression. Additionally, Lundberg & Lee (2017) formalized
104   the relationship between the feature attribution problem and cooperative game theory, strengthening
105   the importance of Shapley Values in explainability.

      Under the established framework of black-box approaches, succeeding works have aimed at improv-
109   ing query efficiency and explanation quality – long-standing challenges for black-box approaches.
110   For example, Dhurandhar et al. (2022) extended LIME with an adaptive neighborhood sampling
111   scheme that constrains sampling to locally linear regions based on the explicand. Petsiuk et al.
112   (2018) alleviated concerns about computational expenses by softly grouping input features via
113   mask resizing, effectively reducing the dimensionality of the feature space. Similarly, Shrotri et al.
114   (2022) and Dhurandhar et al. (2024) improved sampling efficiency by narrowing the search space.
115   Parallel to refining the sampling process, Frye et al. (2020) and Heskes et al. (2020) enhanced
116   explanation quality by incorporating prior causal knowledge into the SHAP framework. Okhrati
117
      & Lipani (2021) leveraged the multilinear extension method from game theory literature (Owen,
      1972) to develop a sampling-based explainer with reduced variance. More recently, Cai & Wunder
118
      (2024) adopted gradient estimation and imitated IG under a black-box setting by integrating estimated
119
      gradients, resulting in white-box-level performance with query-level access. However, this approach
120   is limited to continuous feature space, which is the gap addressed in the following sections.

301   4.4   R ELATION TO I NTEGRATED G RADIENTS
302
303   Since the proposed method is built upon estimated gradients, this section further explores its rela-
304   tionship to IG2 that utilizes actual gradients. The equivalence between GEFA and IG does not hold
305   when both take a straightline path, as GEFA’s path is constructed in the proxy space, which differs
306   from the original feature space. However, the relation becomes clearer when both explainers follow a
307
      monotonic path along the edges of their respective spaces. Along an edge path, integration moves
      step-by-step from one vertex zS in the feature/proxy space to an adjacent vertex zS∪{i} that differs
308
      in only one feature.
309
310   Theorem 4. GEFA and IG are equivalent when taking the same edge path. Averaging their results
311   over all p! unique edge paths converges to the outcome of GEFA that follows the straightline path in
312
      the proxy space.
313
      It can be easily shown that, when following the same permutation order, GEFA and IG both compute
314
      the marginal contribution of a feature xi , namely f (zS ) − f (zS∪{i} ), conditioned on a set of
315
      present features {xj |j ∈ S}. Given the fact that GEFA is an unbiased estimator of Shapley Values,
316   concluding Theorem 4 is not surprising – averaging marginal contributions is the typical solution
317   for determining Shapley Values. Please refer to Appendix A.4 for the detailed proof. The close
318   relationship between IG and Shapley Values is consistent with previous claims by Sundararajan
319   & Najmi (2020). Furthermore, Theorem 4 motivates the choice of the straightline path along the
320   diagonal of the proxy space, converting the problem of averaging the estimates of several edge paths
321   to estimating attributions on one specific path.

328   To show GEFA’s applicability under various scenarios, we consider the most representative tasks
329   involving discrete and continuous features: text and image classifications.
330

331   sentiment analysis task for text classification. The review texts in the dataset include customer
332   reviews of products with a maximal length of 512 tokens, labeled as either positive or negative. As for
333   image classification, we consider ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), a dataset sets up a multi-class
334   classification task in high-dimensional input feature space, posing challenges to black-box explainers
335   that derives feature attributions by querying.
336
      Classifier: We fine-tune a publicly available pretrained version of BERT 3 on the Amazon review
337   dataset. For ImageNet, a pre-trained version of InceptionV34 is adopted without further training.

339   which are the most popular components in current neural network designs but have very different
340   architectures from each other, with the purpose of demonstrating that GEFA’s explanation quality is
341   independent of specific model structures.
342

343   no consent for the quantitative evaluation of explanation quality due to the lack of ground truth

346   summarizing the effectiveness of feature removal guided by explanations. Following the intuition

349   area over the curve drawn by the sequence of prediction outcomes quantifies explanation quality. A
350   larger area indicates a more informative explanation that boosts the impact of the deletion process.

383   5.2   E XPLAINING T EXT C LASSIFIER
384
385   When applying feature attributions to text classifiers, black-box approaches like GEFA are more
386   flexible in terms of representing feature absence, as they construct synthetic instances in the original
387   text space for querying. Unlike models for other classification tasks, text classifiers commonly accept
388   inputs with variable lengths, simplifying the definition of absence. When taking an empty token as
389   the baseline, feature absence is represented by the removal of a corresponding feature, providing
390   a more explicit representation of feature absence – a feature is not part of the input – instead of
391   replacing the original value with some manually defined baseline value.
392   On the other hand, the white-box approaches relying on back-propagation stick to the absence
393   definition as the replacement by some default value, because back-propagation for exact gradient
394   measurement always requires a placeholder in the input as the destination of the propagation process.
395   Specifically, feature absence is modeled by a zero embedding vector for both VG and IG. Furthermore,
396   given texts as sequences of discrete features are not directly differentiable, approaches based on actual
397   gradients require at least one pre-processing step to acquire the embeddings for back-propagation and
398
      summarize the embedding-level attributions in the form of token-level results through post-processing
      for deriving human-comprehensible explanations.
399
400   We employ two deletion operations for the evaluation scheme: embedding reset and token removal,
401   corresponding to the distinct representations of feature absence during the explanation processes.
402   Embedding reset conducts deletion by setting the embedding vector of a token being removed to a
403   zero vector, aligning with the absence representation adopted by back-propagation-based methods.
404
      Token removal wipes the presence of a token completely by replacing it with an empty token. Table 1
      presents the nAOPC scores of the competitors tested with both deletion operations. Each row in the
405
      table corresponds to the nAOPCs for the respective deletion type indicated in the first column. For the
406
      black-box explainers, given the relatively smaller feature space, we empirically set a query budget of
407   500. In the case of IG, the gradient is integrated over 50 interpolated points in the embedding space.
408   Please note that GEEX is excluded from this part of the evaluation due to its incompatibility with
409   models operating on discrete feature space, as previously discussed in Section 1.

513   6   C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we propose GEFA, a model-agnostic feature attribution framework based on a proxy
516   gradient estimator. By structuring the explanation process in the proxy space, GEFA is generally
517   applicable for explaining arbitrary classifiers, regardless of their input feature types. Backed by
518   rigorous theoretical analysis, the proposed method significantly improves the quality of black-box
519   explanations and, in certain circumstances, even surpasses white-box approaches with a limited
520
      query budget. As a general framework, GEFA holds significant potential for integration with existing
      techniques, further enhancing sampling efficiency (Shrotri et al., 2022; Dhurandhar et al., 2024) and
521
      explanation quality (Frye et al., 2020; Heskes et al., 2020).
522
523   Although our current focus is on feature attribution for classification tasks, the versatility of
524   GEFA opens avenues for future work, particularly in adapting it to more complicated scenarios, such
525   as explaining multi-modal models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and large language models. These
526
      potential adaptions would primarily require reformatting the loss function to handle more complex
      model outcomes, while the core of the explanation framework remains unchanged.

      B     VALIDITY OF THE E VALUATION S CHEME
935
936
      In this work, we adopted the evaluation via deletion approach, a commonly used method for accessing
937   explanation quality. The employment of this evaluation scheme spans from the early stages of
938   explainability research (Samek et al., 2016; Montavon et al., 2018) to the most recent studies (Cai &
939   Wunder, 2024; Muzellec et al., 2024). One of its key advantages is that it does not require retraining
940   during evaluation, thereby offering a static environment for efficient explanation quality assessment.
941   While the out-of-distribution issue raises concern about the validity of the evaluation results, our
942   results regarding the performance of the random feature remover, as reported in Table 1 and Table 2,
943   alleviate this concern. The significantly lower nAOPC scores of random removal demonstrate that
944   simply shifting the input away from the underlying data manifold does not effectively affect model
945
      performance.
946   Hooker et al. (2019) highlighted the issue that out-of-distribution manipulations can trigger unex-
947   pected model behaviors. As an alternative to the traditional deletion scheme, they proposed the
948   remove and retraining (ROAR) scheme. This approach involves removing a proportion of features
949   with the highest attribution scores for each instance in the dataset, followed by retraining the model
950   on the manipulated dataset. The resulting model performance is then used as a reflection of the
951
      explainer’s effectiveness. ROAR assessments reported that many popular attribution methods “are
      not better than a random designation of feature importance”, contradicting the conclusions drawn
952
      from the traditional deletion scheme. This conflict with the widely approved effectiveness of the
953
      tested approaches, such as IG, prompted our further investigation, which revealed the following:
954
955          • The root cause of the misalignment between observations from different evaluation perspec-
956            tives is the presence of residual features with negative attributions during the retraining
957            process;
958          • After a single justified adjustment to ROAR, the two evaluation schemes yield consistent
959            results.
960
961   B.1   T HE “S IGN ” I SSUE
962
963   In the context of feature attribution, a positive attribution score indicates a positive contribution
964   to the prediction result, whereas a negative score, rather than indicating irrelevance, represents a
965   negative association with the decision. Failing to remove negatively contributing features preserves
966   task-relevant information, which the model can reorganize during retraining to improve accuracy. A
967   qualitative example of residual “negative” information in ROAR is illustrated through the visualized
968
      pixel removal process of IG in Figure 1 by Hooker et al. (2019). This “sign” issue also explains
      the effective manipulation by SG-SQ and VarGrad, as these methods provide unsigned attributions,
969
      thereby ensuring the removal of all informative features.
970
971   To mitigate the assessment distortion caused by retained negative information, we argue that a
      modification to ROAR is necessary for a more faithful reflection of explainer performance: instead of

991
992    To verify the above discussion, we conducted experiments following the retraining scheme. Specifi-
993    cally, we fine-tuned EfficientNet-B05 on the Cats vs Dogs dataset (Elson et al., 2007) and created
994    copies of the dataset with explanation-guided manipulation. These manipulated datasets were then
995    used for retraining the pre-trained model to assess the quality of explanations. Without loss of gener-
996    ality, we downsampled the dataset into 2000/400/400 partitions for training, validation, and test sets
997    for efficiency. EfficientNet-B0 achieved an accuracy of 99.40% on the downsampled dataset after
998    fine-tuning. Based on the fine-tuned EfficientNet-B0, we derived explanations for images in all three
999    partitions with three competitors: IG, PSHAP, and GEFA. Features for each instance were ranked in
1000
       descending order according to their attribution scores. Similar to the traditional deletion scheme, we
       adopted the random feature remover as a baseline reference for evaluating the effectiveness of the
1001
       competitors.
1002
1003   The top 90% of features were removed for the ROAR test, whereas the top 10% of features were
1004   kept for the KEAR test. To highlight the “sign” issue, we also performed feature ranking based on
1005   the absolute values of their attribution scores for the removal test, referred to as ROAR-abs (remove
1006
       and retrain — based on absolute attribution score). To minimize the potential impact of randomness
       during the training process, we independently retrained 5 models on each manipulated dataset and
1007
       reported the averaged accuracies of the five models under different settings. It is noteworthy that lower
1008
       retraining accuracy indicates better explanation quality in the removal tests (ROAR and ROAR-abs),
1009   whereas higher accuracy reflects superior explainer performance in KEAR. Results from the desgiend
1010   experiments are presented in Table 3. For random removal, the same figures are reported across the
1011   three retraining settings as the proportions of remaining features are identical in all cases, i.e. 10%.
1012
       In the ROAR test, all explanation methods show minor manipulation impacts due to the previously
1013
       discussed “sign” issue and fail to excel random removal. By contrast, the ROAR-abs test demonstrates
1014
       that removing residual negative information enhances the effectiveness of manipulation, providing
1015   indirect evidence for the “sign” issue. However, the origin of negative attributions is complex and
1016   influenced by various factors, e.g. the baseline choice and a feature’s association with the prediction
1017   function. Ranking features based on the absolute value of their attribution scores may unnecessarily
1018   include irrelevant features, as it cannot distinguish between the different causes of a negative sign,
1019   rendering this approach a suboptimal solution.
