                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013
014
               Recent advances in multivariate time series forecasting have seen a shift toward
               a pure graph paradigm, which transforms time series into hypervariate graphs
015
               and employs graph neural networks (GNNs) to holistically capture intertwined
016
               spatiotemporal dependencies. While promising, this approach faces notable
017            challenges. First, converting time series into hypervariate graphs often neglects
018            essential temporal sequences, which are vital for accurately capturing temporal
019            dependencies. Second, treating the graph as a complete structure can obscure the
020            varying importance of intra- and inter-series connections, potentially overlooking
021            key local patterns. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel hyperspectral
022            graph data structure that embeds sequential order into frequency signals and
023            employs a sparse yet meaningful topological structure. In addition, we propose
024            the U FGTIME framework, featuring a frequency-based global graph framelet
025
               message-passing operator tailored to hyperspectral graphs, effectively mitigating
               the smoothing issue and capturing global insights through sparse connections.
026
               Extensive experiments demonstrate that our framework significantly surpasses
027
               state-of-the-art methods, excelling in both short- and long-range time series
028            forecasting while achieving superior efficiency. Our code is available at: https:
029            //anonymous.4open.science/r/UFGTIME-E352.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Multivariate time series forecasting is crucial in industrial applications such as transportation,
035
      manufacturing, and energy management. Recent advancements in deep neural networks, evolving
036   from recurrent to convolutional and attention-based models, have significantly improved forecasting
037   accuracy. However, many of these methods fail to fully capture the critical spatial correlations, which
038   are essential for modeling complex dependencies in multivariate time series data. Graph Neural
039   Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful approach to address this limitation. Initially, GNNs
040   were employed to capture spatial information across time steps, which was then combined with
041   historical temporal information through a forecasting model. This approach led to foundational
042   models such as DCRNN (Li et al., 2018), GraphWaveNet (Wu et al., 2019), STGCN (Yu et al.,
043   2018), and MTGNN (Wu et al., 2020). Despite their success, these models often rely on integrating
044
      GNN modules with separate components to capture temporal dependencies, treating the processes of
      modeling spatial and temporal dependencies as independent tasks. This separation contradicts the
045
      intertwined nature of spatial temporal information.
046
047   Recent research, FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024), has explicitly tackled these intertwined interactions
048   by transforming time series into a new data structure called the hypervariate graph, pioneering a
049   novel pure graph paradigm for multivariate time series forecasting. However, this pure graph-based
050
      method faces two key challenges:
051   Challenge 1. Processing multivariate time series in a hypervariate graph structure risks losing
052   essential sequential information. Challenge 2. The fully connected setting in hypervariate graphs
053   tends to reduce attention to various connections, which leads to overlooking important temporal
      patterns.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To address these challenges, we propose an innovative frequency graph framelet time series
055   forecasting framework, named U FGTIME. Our method transforms the time series into frequency
056   domain signals, preserving the sequential information of the original time series within the frequency
057   signals. We further reform a novel graph data structure called the hyperspectral graph, which
058   transforms frequency signals into graph features and constructs sparse topological structures based
059   on signal similarities to enhance attention to cross-signal relationships. Finally, we propose a
060   global manner framelet message-passing operator to capture global patterns through sparse graph
061   connections and mitigate the smoothing effects caused by aggregation between similar nodes. Our
062   contributions are as follows:
063   • Identifying the limitations of hypervariate graphs in ignoring temporal sequential information and
064     neglecting attention to local connection patterns in fully connected graphs setting.
065   • Proposing an innovative hyperspectral graph structure, reorganizing signals from fast Fourier
066     transforms, preserving temporal sequential order in frequency signals, and using KNN to capture
067     local (sparse) connections between signal features.
068   • Introducing a global framelet message-passing operator to capture the global patterns of the
069
        hyperspectral graph through sparse connections and alleviate the smoothing effects from connecting
        similar nodes.
070
      • Validating the effectiveness of the U FGTIME framework through extensive experiments,
071
        demonstrating its ability to outperform state-of-the-art methods.

078   A multivariate time series X ∈ RN ×T ×D represents a sequence of D-dimensional vector
079   observations of N entities recorded over a time period T . Given a window size T , we denote
080   Xt = [Xt−T +1 , . . . , Xt−1 , Xt ] ∈ RN ×T ×D representing the observations on the looking back-
081   window of size T at time t, where Xt ∈ RN ×D is the observation for all the N entities at t. A typical
082   forecasting task is to learn a model f (·), by minimizing a predefined loss function, such that
083                                  Ybt+1 = f (Xt ) = f ([Xt−T +1 , . . . , Xt ])                       (1)
084
      predicts the next τ steps of another time series Y at t+1, e.g., Yt+1 = [Xt+1 , . . . , Xt+τ ] ∈ RN ×τ ×D ,
085   forecasting within the prediction time window τ .

088   2.2   PARADIGM OF GNN S IN M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
089
090
      Deep learning methods such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Borovykh et al., 2017; Assaf
      et al., 2019), recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Connor et al., 1994; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
091
      1997), and transformers (Zhou et al., 2021; Zhang & Yan, 2023) have demonstrated considerable
092
      success in multivariate time series forecasting. However, a significant limitation of these approaches
093   lies in their inability to explicitly model the spatial topological structure information. To address the
094   challenges in temporal dynamics, GNNs have been applied using different paradigms, which can be
095   categorized as follows:
096
097   Modularity Paradigm The introduction of DCRNN (Li et al., 2018), which integrates graph and
098   recurrent modules into an end-to-end framework, marked a significant advancement in capturing
099
      spatial correlations along with temporal dynamics. This design has become the dominant paradigm
      for applying GNNs in multivariate time series forecasting. Variants of this approach have combined
100
      GNNs with different architectures, leading to methods such as GNNs with recurrence (e.g., ST-
101
      MetaNet (Pan et al., 2019), STGNN (Wang et al., 2020), AGCRN (Bai et al., 2020), GTS (Shang
102   et al., 2021), and HiGP (Cini et al., 2024)), GNNs with convolution (e.g., GraphWaveNet (Wu et al.,
103   2019), MTGNN (Wu et al., 2020), StemGNN (Cao et al., 2020), STGODE (Fang et al., 2021),
104   MTGODE (Jin et al., 2022), and CaST (Xia et al., 2024)), and GNNs with temporal attention (e.g.,
105   GMAN (Zheng et al., 2020), STAR (Yu et al., 2020), and TPGNN (Liu et al., 2022)). Despite their
106   effectiveness, these frameworks often treat spatial correlation and temporal processes as separate
107   entities, which may result in a disjointed representation of spatio-temporal dependencies, potentially
      misrepresenting the inherent interconnections found in real-world scenarios.

141   Recent work on FourierGNN utilizes hypervariate graphs to integrate temporal dependencies and
142   spatial information, proposing a novel pure graph paradigm for time series forecasting. However,
143   this approach overlooks a critical aspect: the sequential order inherent in temporal data, which is
144
      essential for capturing time-dependent relationships. In hypervariate graphs, the topological structure
      is typically assumed to be fully connected, with each node representing temporal features at a
145
      specific timestamp. Permuting the node order in a hypervariate graph results in an isomorphic graph,
146
      indicating the hypervariate graph’s invariance to node ordering. Figure 3.1 visualizes two multivariate
147   time series with identical values but different sequential orders, forming isomorphic hypervariate
148   graphs, despite the underlying time series being fundamentally different due to the order change.
149   Our findings underscore the limitations of hypervariate graphs in preserving sequential order, as
150   formalized in the following proposition (with proof provided in Appendix A.1):
151   Propostion 1. The hypervariate graph is insensitive to the temporal order of the original time series,
152   thereby discarding critical sequential information.
153
      We conducted an empirical study to assess the performance of FourierGNN under time series order
154   permutations. The results, as shown in Table 1, indicate limited performance variation, even with
155   large-scale permutations on the temporal dimension of the data. This suggests that the FourierGNN
156   model in hypervariate graphs may be relatively insensitive to temporal sequential order, implying that
157   hypervariate graphs might not effectively preserve sequential information.

192   4.1   B YPASS S EQUENTIAL O RDER OF T IME S ERIES WITH D OMAIN T RANSFER
193
194   As discussed in Section 3.1, state-of-the-art approaches within the pure graph paradigm may suffer
195   from overlooking the sequential order inherent in time series due to the transformation into a
196   hypervariate graph. A promising strategy to address this limitation involves representing the
197   multivariate time series X ∈ RN ×T ×D in the spectral domain using orthogonal bases, expressed
198   as S = F(X) ∈ CN ×C×D , where F(·) denotes the Fourier transform applied along the time
199
      dimension, and C represents the length of the transformed temporal signal in the spectral domain.
      Subsequently, a graph is constructed using the spectral signals S following the method that forms the
200
      hypervariate graph. This new type of graph is defined as follows:
201
      Definition 2 (Hyperspectral Graph). Given a general multivariate time window Xt ∈ RN ×T ×D
202
      at time step t, the spectral temporal signals St are defined as the Fourier-transformed time series,
203                       N ×C×D

      Our motivation behind advocating using hyperspectral graphs lies in the inherent flexibility of the
      Fourier transformation in interpreting and manipulating the frequency components derived from a
208
      signal (e.g., a 1D discrete (ordered) signal in the case of Discrete Fourier transform). The Fourier
209
      transformation output can be interpreted as frequency components contained in the original signal.
210   Although mathematically all the frequency components are inherently presented in a specific order
211   from the lowest to the highest frequencies, this order is not part of signal information but the amount
212   of different frequencies. Thus the Fourier coefficients can be rearranged according to the analysis’s
213   needs without impacting the data’s integrity. As long as the frequency associated with each output
214   component is clearly understood and consistently applied, the actual order of these components can
215   be adjusted to suit specific analytical goals. This flexibility indicates that it is appropriate to use
      graphs to represent output data from the Fourier transform. Therefore, transforming the multivariate

      4.2     P ROPAGATING F EATURES VIA H YPERSPECTRAL G RAPH
221
      Unlike the hypervariate graph introduced in Definition 1, where fully connected graphs are
222
      constructed across all timestamps, the adjacency matrices in the hyperspectral graph are estimated
223   based on signal similarity using     algorithms KNN. Generally, this can be expressed as At =

224   KNN Re(StG )⌢ Im(StG ), k , where k represents the number of neighbors, and Re(StG )⌢ Im(StG )
225   denotes the concatenation of the real and imaginary components of the hyperspectral graph features.
226   Once the graph structure is defined, the next challenge is to process the features S G with the generated
227   sparse graph structure At . Since At is sparse, employing traditional GNNs to propagate hyperspectral
228   graph features may limit the focus to local (short-sightedness), nearest-neighbor connections rather
229   than capturing global relationships. Additionally, because At is constructed from the top k signal
230   similarities in the hyperspectral graph, propagating the features risks over-smoothing. This can lead
231
      to embedding becoming indistinguishable after propagation, which is detrimental for downstream
      tasks such as forecasting. Therefore, a desired GNN model for hyperspectral graphs must satisfy the
232
      following requirements:
233
234
235
            1. Prevent excessive smoothing (similarity) of hyperspectral graph features by preserving the
               identifiability (sharpness) of each node’s features during propagation.
236
237         2. Propagate node features in a global manner, even though the graph is sparsely connected.
238
239   How to select GNN for hyperspectral graphs? GNNs that satisfy Requirement 1 often employ a
240   diffusion-reaction paradigm, where node features are first homogenized through spatial propagation
241   (i.e., using A). Then the ego-graph feature (e.g., StG ) is added to reintroduce variation into
242   the system (Choi et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024; Thorpe et al., 2022). While these models have
243   achieved remarkable results, spectral GNNs, such as ChebNet (Defferrard et al., 2016), typically
244   learn filtering functions (e.g., diagonal matrices) in the spectral domain (i.e., the eigenspace of
245
      the graph Laplacian), which enables feature propagation from a global perspective, thus satisfying
      Requirement 2. Consequently, an ideal model would either be a spectral GNN that can induce multiple
246
      feature dynamics or a spatial GNN that accounts for global dependencies between features. In light
247
      of these considerations, we focus on a family of spectral GNNs known as Graph Framelets, which
248   meet the aforementioned requirements (Zheng et al., 2021).

      4.4   M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING WITH U FGTIME
335   The main framework of U FGTIME is illustrated in Figure 3. Given input multivariate time series
336   data Xt ∈ RN ×T ×D , we first apply moving-average decomposition to the input to extract trend
337   information Tt ∈ RN ×T ×D , and then apply the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on the time dimension
338   of the input to obtain the frequency signal St ∈ CN ×C×D . The frequency signal is reshaped into a
339   hyperspectral graph feature StG ∈ CN C×D . Next, we leverage KNN to generate a sparse topological
340   structure At ∈ {0, 1}N T ×N T , associated with the input Re(StG )⌢ Im(StG ). At this point, we obtain
341   the hyperspectral graph GSt = StG , At . Subsequently, to capture intricate dependencies on the
342   hyperspectral graph, we feed the data into ℓ layers of a global

531
      In this work, we addressed the potential limitations of the hypervariate graph from FourierGNN
      by proposing a reasonable framework that embeds advanced graph operations and frequency
532
      transformations, demonstrating the feasibility of the pure graph paradigm in time series forecasting.
533
      We transformed the time series into a hyperspectral graph to preserve sequential information and
534   replaced the fully connected graph with a sparse KNN graph for higher efficiency. To tackle
535   short-sightedness in sparse graphs and smoothing issues in convolution on hyperspectral graph,
536   we introduced an advanced framelet graph convolution operator that extracts both local and global
537   temporal dependencies while alleviating smoothing. We conducted comprehensive performance and
538   efficiency comparisons on extensive temporal datasets to evaluate our method’s improvements. The
539   results indicate our method’s potential as a new solution for time series forecasting using the pure
      graph paradigm.

705   A.1    P ROOFS REGARDING TO THE HYPERVARIATE GRAPH LOSING SEQUENTIAL ORDER
706
707
      Propostion 1. The hypervariate graph is insensitive to the temporal order of the original time series,
      thereby discarding critical sequential information.
708
709
710   Proof. Let G1 = (X G1 , J ) and G2 = (X G2 , J ) be two hypervariate graphs, where X G1 =
711   {X1G1 , . . . , XnG1 } and X G2 = {X1G2 , . . . , XnG2 }, with n ∈ {1, . . . , N T }, representing the sets of
712   node features that transformed from time series X 1 , X 2 ∈ RN ×D×T . We assume that the features
713   are identical but permuted, meaning there exists a permutation ρ : {1, 2, . . . , n} 7→ {1, 2, . . . , n}
714   such that for all i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n}, we have XiG1 = Xρ(i)
                                                                  G2
                                                                     .
715
      By definition of graph isomorphism, if there exists such a permutation ρ mapping the node features
716
      of G1 to those of G2 , then the graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic, i.e., G1 ∼
                                                                                = G2 .
717
718   However, the original time series X 1 and X 2 are different (X 1 ̸= X 2 ), yet their corresponding
719   hypervariate graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic. This implies that the hypervariate graph
720   representation does not retain the sequential order information of the underlying time series.
721
      Specifically, different time series can lead to isomorphic hypervariate graphs if the node order
      is permuted, thus discarding crucial temporal information.

      B.3    D ETAILS OF C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
757
758   For simplicity of analysis, we assume the weights of our Fourier domain framelet message-passing
759   operator are in CD×D , and the frequency signal has the same length T as the time series. The time
760   complexity of a single layer of our framelet operator on the hyperspectral graph GS is given by
761   O(L(J + 1)(N T )2 D + N T D2 ), where L represents the number of high-pass filters and J stands
762   for the levels of the scaling function. Since the hyperspectral graph is sparse, the complexity term of
763   graph convolution N T can be replaced by |E|, the total number of edges in the hyperspectral graph.
764
      Additionally, the topology of the hyperspectral graph is generated using the KNN algorithm, resulting
      in |E| ≤ kN T , where k is the number of neighbours. Therefore, an upper bound for the complexity
765
      of our framelet operator is O(L(J + 1)N T kD + N T D2 ). In practice, we set both L and J to 1,
766
      making the product L(J + 1) a small constant that can be omitted from the total complexity. Finally,
767   the complexity of the framelet operator simplifies to O(N T kD + N T D2 ), resulting in U FGTIME
768   being highly efficient.
