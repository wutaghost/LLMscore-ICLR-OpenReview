011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Coarse-grained (CG) force field methods for molecular systems are a crucial
014            tool to simulate large biological macromolecules and are therefore essential for
015
               characterisations of biomolecular systems. While state-of-the-art deep learning
               (DL)-based models for all-atom force fields have improved immensely over re-
016
               cent years, we observe and analyse significant limitations of the currently avail-
017
               able approaches for DL-based CG simulations. In this work, we present the first
018            transferable DL-based CG force field approach (i.e., not specific to only one nar-
019            rowly defined system type) applicable to a wide range of biosystems. To achieve
020            this, our CG algorithm does not rely on hard-coded rules and is tuned to output
021            coarse-grained systems optimised for minimal statistical noise in the ground truth
022            CG forces, which results in significant improvement of model training. Our force
023            field model is also the first CG variant that is based on the MACE architecture
024            and is trained on a custom dataset created by a new approach based on the frag-
025            mentation of large biosystems covering protein, RNA and lipid chemistry. We
026
               demonstrate that our model can be applied in molecular dynamics simulations to
               obtain stable and qualitatively accurate trajectories for a variety of systems, while
027
               also discussing cases for which we observe limited reliability.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   To study large (bio-)molecular frameworks across large time scales, coarse-grained (CG) molecu-
033   lar dynamics (MD) are a powerful tool in computational biochemistry. These types of simulations
034   require specialised force fields that (1) define how a molecular system is transformed into its CG
035   counterpart and (2) are able to output accurate forces acting on the CG beads for a given set of
036   bead positions. CG force fields are significantly more efficient than their all-atom analogues, which
037   becomes crucial as biological macromolecules of interest (e.g., for drug discovery, or in the char-
038   acterisation of disease development processes (Filipe & Loura, 2022)) can grow to several hundred
039
      thousand or even millions of atoms, and MD simulations can require millions of individual force
      calculations. Similar to classical all-atom force fields (such as AMBER (Salomon-Ferrer et al.,
040
      2013) or GROMOS (Scott et al., 1999)), there exist a large variety of classical CG force field ap-
041
      proaches that vary widely in their design, i.e., in the CG mappings they construct and in how the
042   inter-bead potentials are defined. For example, a popular choice is Martini (Marrink et al., 2007),
043   which was originally developed for lipid and surfactant systems, but has been extended to a wide
044   range of biosystems (Marrink et al., 2023).
045
      In recent years, machine learning force fields (MLFFs) have been on the rise as a promising al-
046
      ternative to classical force fields as they can deliver accurate force predictions at a highly reduced
047
      computational cost (Poltavsky & Tkatchenko, 2021; Wu et al., 2023). MLFF approaches are typi-
048   cally trained on datasets consisting of molecular structures along with their ground truth energies and
049   atomic forces and can therefore achieve a force prediction accuracy close to the reference method
050   they were trained on, e.g., density functional theory (DFT). However, classical force fields typically
051   outperform MLFF models significantly with respect to inference speed. Therefore, it becomes ev-
052   ident that advanced approaches such as ML/MM hybrid methods or coarse-grained MLFF models
053   play a crucial role for pushing the field of ML for molecular simulation towards widespread practical
      application.

054
      There have been a few notable contributions to the advancement of CG-MLFF models in recent
055   years. They can be divided into two types of approaches. First, approaches where the CG map-
056   ping is tabulated exactly for the possible system types, for example, specifying which atoms for
057   each amino acid of a protein get transformed into CG beads (Husic et al., 2020; Charron et al.,
058   2023; Majewski et al., 2023). A popular approach of this kind is Cα coarse-graining, where only
059   Cα atoms of amino acids are kept as CG beads. The two main limitations of these approaches are
060   (i) that the CG mapping strategy is typically not data-driven and hence to some extent arbitrary,
061   and (ii) its limited generalisability to general (bio-)organic chemistry where building a rule-based
062   assignment strategy in this way grows unfeasibly in complexity. The second type of approach is
063
      taking a look at more complex coarse-graining schemes, e.g., rooted in machine learning (Chen-
      nakesavalu et al., 2023; Wang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2019; Nasikas et al., 2022). However, while
064
      such approaches are in principle able to be more universally applicable across organic systems, they
065
      have so far only been applied in non-transferable contexts, i.e., learning a CG force field for a single
066   type of system. One reason for this may be that these approaches are prone to overfitting due to
067   their increased complexity. Furthermore, previous CG-MLFF approaches have been relying on the
068   invariant SchNet architecture for the force field model (Chennakesavalu et al., 2023; Durumeric
069   et al., 2024), which for all-atom force fields has been mostly replaced by other (typically equiv-
070   ariant) architectures in recent studies, for example, MACE (Batatia et al., 2022b), VisNet (Wang
071   et al., 2022), Allegro (Musaelian et al., 2023), or sparse Gaussian Processes (Vandermause et al.,
072   2022). Both Gaussian process-based and Allegro-based force fields have also been applied in the
073   context of CG force fields before (Duschatko et al., 2024; Loose et al., 2023), however, only to build
074
      molecule-specific CG-MLFFs with a rigid CG strategy.
075   We emphasise that in the field of MLFFs, we generally distinguish between system-focused and
076   transferable MLFF approaches. In the former, the model is just trained on a single molecule in
077   different conformations (a single potential energy surface) with the goal to later obtain accurate
078   simulations for this molecule. On the contrary, transferable approaches train on a dataset with a
079   variety of systems with the goal to be applicable to unseen molecules of similar chemistry. As
080
      outlined above, the field of CG-MLFFs has largely been focused on the system-focused setup which
      is common for proof-of-concept studies. One of the reasons for this is that a universally applicable
081
      CG mapping is not trivial to define, and as a result we are lacking understanding of whether a truly
082
      transferable CG-MLFF approach is even possible. Due to the difficulty of defining a general CG
083   mapping, the few published transferable CG-MLFF approaches are restricted to very specific types
084   of systems, typically proteins where the CG mapping can be fixed for each amino acid in a simple
085   rule-based way.
086
      In this work, we present a new approach for CG-MLFF models trained on a new dataset spanning
087
      a vast range of biology-related (organic) chemistry. We build these models based on the MACE
088
      architecture which is modified to allow for CG-specific inputs such as continuous multi-dimensional
089   node features. We show that by construction, our model is transferable to unseen structures of similar
090   chemistry and generalisable to larger structures than used for training. The CG mechanism is based
091   on previous work by Webb et al. (2018) which facilitates general applicability to a wide range
092   of structures (in contrast to specialised CG schemes developed for proteins only). It allows us to
093   coarse-grain any (organic) chemical system, which is reflected not only in the fact that our training
094   dataset (see section 2.3) contains lipids, RNA and proteins, but also since the new fragmentation-
095   based dataset generation results in a diverse set of organic fragments to train on (including molecules
096   from the PubChem database). Moreover, we explore an extension to this scheme by adding more
097
      physical information in a tunable fashion, and present an ablation study related to this modification.
      For the force field deep learning model, we apply the state-of-the-art MACE architecture that has
098
      recently been demonstrated to be very powerful in the context of all-atom force field foundation
099
      models (Batatia et al., 2023; Kovács et al., 2023). However, it is yet to be applied for CG simulations.

104   2.1   M ACHINE L EARNING F ORCE F IELD
105
106   MACE is a state-of-the-art message passing GNN architecture for interatomic potentials. Its main
107   innovation is the expansion of messages as a hierarchical body order expansion allowing for a de-
      coupling between receptive field size, local geometry modelling and the number of message passing


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      layers. Hence, even with a small number of model layers, higher order interactions can be captured.
109   Recently, two force field foundation models, Batatia et al. (2023) and Kovács et al. (2023), have
110   been developed based on MACE. For more details on the original MACE model, we refer to the
111   original work, Batatia et al. (2022b) and Batatia et al. (2022a).
112
      We build our implementation based on the JAX implementation of MACE1 which differs compared
113
      to the PyTorch version in some details (see Appendix A.1 for more information). Furthermore,
114
      for CG force fields, the original way to embed node features must be adapted. In the MACE full-
115   atom architecture, the atomic number of an atom is mapped to an integer which then corresponds
116   to an index in a trainable parameter matrix. Hence, by construction this is a discrete representation
117   which has its cardinality predefined, i.e., the number of possible atomic species. However, our
118   CG beads have continuous descriptors (i.e., node features), which are constructed as described in
119   section 2.2.3. To allow for this, we apply a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to the bead descriptor
120   at the first MACE layer. The size of this MLP is a model hyperparameter and its parameters are
121   learned at the same time as the other parameters of MACE during training. We refer the reader to a
122   more detailed description of this matter (and for other small architectural modifications, as well as
123
      the model hyperparameters) located in section A.1 in the Appendix.
124   In the following, we apply MACE as the architecture in all of the trained models. We note that since
125   our paper introduces modifications compared to previous CG-MLFF approaches in multiple ways,
126   we refrain to study the impact of MACE compared to other architectures systematically in this work
127   but rather concentrate on an ablation study regarding the tunability of the CG algorithm. However,
128   in future work, it may be of high interest to assess the influence of the MACE architecture on these
129
      results more carefully.

      2.2.1     R EQUIREMENTS
133
134   As outlined in section 2.1, an ML force field acts on a set of nodes which can be atoms or beads as
135   long as they have well-defined positions and node features. The process of creating a set of beads
136   with bead positions and bead features from a set of atoms is the coarse-graining process. We present
137   a rule-based approach for this process (based on Webb et al. (2018)), i.e., an algorithm which is
138   pre-defined by us and does not need to be learned based on our reference data. However, our version
139
      of this algorithm also contains a small number of hyperparameters which are tuned in a pre-training
      phase with respect to given quality metrics. Such an approach is discussed further below.
140
141   The advantages of such a non-learnable approach is that it can be viewed purely as a pre-processing
142   step both during training of the force field and at inference time (i.e., during an MD simulation).
143   Figure 1 illustrates this set-up for training the coarse-grained ML force field. Note that the coarse-
144   graining cannot adapt to enhance the force field training and any fixed strategy of assigning atoms
145
      to beads is arbitrary to some extent. As a result, we do not expect this approach to have the same po-
      tential for final accuracy compared to a fully learnable approach, however, its simplicity is expected
146
      to be advantageous for model generalisability because the introduced inductive bias acts as a natural
147
      regularisation. However, we tune the hyperparameters of our CG model to our training dataset.
148
149   For our approach, we define a set of requirements:
150
              1. It must be universally applicable to any chemical system, at a minimum all organic systems,
151
                 because (1) we want to be able to generalise to a wide range of organic molecules and
152              (2) our training dataset (see section 2.3) does not guarantee any properties of the training
153              structures such as, for instance, if the nucleobases or amino acids would be fragmented
154              always at the same position.
155
              2. It ideally allows for control over the degree of coarse-graining (i.e., the system size re-
156              duction factor of fine-grained to coarse-grained representations), which lets us tune this
157              parameter depending on the results during the experimentation phase.
158
              3. It can be designed to include tunable hyperparameters which can be optimised with respect
159              to our dataset in order to alleviate the disadvantages that arise from employing a fixed
160              algorithm for coarse-graining.

      2.2.3   T UNABLE G RAPH -BASED C OARSE -G RAINING A PPROACH
242   Our approach is based on the graph-based systematic molecular coarse-graining approach published
243   by Webb et al. (2018). The approach follows a simple two-step process. First, every atom in a
244   molecule is assigned a priority value measuring the importance of an atom for the overall molecular
245   structure. Second, a rule-based algorithm is defined to group atoms together to beads iteratively
246   based on these priority values. The algorithm can be applied iteratively, which leads to even coarser
247   representations, thus making the dimensionality reduction factor controllable. We also explore a
248   modified version of this algorithm which contains tunable hyperparameters for reasons explained in
249
      section 2.2.1. The overall approach is illustrated in Figure 2.
250   As described above, the first step is to assign priorities to each atom. In principle, these could
251   be derived from any algorithm (although an ML-based one would require the second step to be
252   differentiable), however, for simplicity and tunability, we adopt the spectral decomposition method
253   from the original paper and add additional ways to weight the edges of the chemical graph. This
254   method assigns the priority values based on the importance of a node for the overall topology of the
255
      chemical graph. In the original paper, a simple binary graph construction based on chemical bonds
      as edges is employed. This means that atoms that are bonded to many other atoms (either directly
256
      or via intermediate bonds) are assigned higher priorities. To obtain these values, a Laplacian matrix
257
      L of the molecular graph is constructed,
258
259                                                  L=D−A            ,                                   (6)
260   where D is the degree matrix that has the degree, i.e., the number of neighbours, of each node on its
261   diagonal, and A is the adjacency matrix of the graph with the values 1 and 0 as the off-diagonal terms
262   depending on whether two nodes share a chemical bond or not. To determine the node priorities,
263   we perform an eigenvalue decomposition of L and take the absolute values of the coefficients of the
264   first eigenvector (corresponding to largest eigenvalue) as the priority values. The first eigenvector of
265   L contains information about the contribution of each node (i.e., atom) to the overall connectivity in
266
      the graph. The more well-connected an atom is within the graph, the higher its contribution. This
      considers not only the atom’s number of neighbours but also its neighbour’s neighbours, and more
267
      distant connections.
268
269   As an extension to this approach, we define multiple graphs describing the chemical system. The
      graph topology is always based on chemical bonds, however, this time we assign weights w to the

      edges depending on physico-chemical properties of the bonds. The Laplacian of these graphs is also
271   computed as defined in Eq. (6) with the adjacency matrix A containing the edge weights (Aij = wij )
272   and the node degree of atom i calculated as the sum of all wij with wij as the weight of the j-th
273   neighbouring node of i. As an addition to the standard binary graph approach, we define three edge
274   weighting schemes:
275
276
            1. wij = 1/Rij , with Rij as the edge distance.

277         2. wij = Zi Zj , with Zi and Zj as the nuclear charges of atoms i and j, respectively.
278         3. wij = 1 + |χi − χj |, a bond polarity measure, with χi and χj as the Pauling electronega-
279            tivities of atoms i and j, respectively.
280
281   The intuition for this choice is that the added properties cover all the straightforwardly computable
282   features of chemical bonds that could be relevant for prioritising the atoms attached to them. We
283
      emphasise that using the distance-based features does not imply that we plan to rerun the coarse-
      graining process at multiple steps during an MD simulation. The CG mapping will remain constant
284
      during a simulation and the features will be computed on the initial (possibly optimised) structure.

      The optimal values for cA , cB , cC , and cD , should be the ones resulting in the most accurate force field
296   models. However, since the second part of the coarse-graining algorithm (described below) is not
297   differentiable, we are limited to optimisation algorithms that do not require gradients and therefore
298   an optimisation target which is fast to evaluate is desirable. Inspired by Eq. (5), we demonstrate
299   in section A.4 in the Appendix that CG models that result in lower average magnitudes of CG
300   forces across the dataset, train better than models with higher average force magnitudes. As a result,
301   we optimise our hyperparameters c with respect to this target, which can be evaluated efficiently
302   for a given CG model without running any force field parameter update steps. Details about this
303   optimisation process are discussed in section A.4 in the Appendix.
304   Based on the obtained node priority values, we can subsequently build a CG mapping with a rule-
305   based algorithm that iteratively processes atoms and assigns them to beads until all atoms are as-
306   signed. Combining this algorithm with an option for weighting the atom contributions yields the
307   final coarse-grained mapping C described in section 2.2.2. We choose these weights such that the
308   bead position is the centre of mass of the atoms that make up the bead. In Appendix A.3, we ex-
309   plain the bead assignment algorithm in more detail. We also note that we pass only fragments of a
310
      pre-determined maximum size to the CG algorithm, which requires a simple fragmentation scheme
      for larger structures, which we also describe in Appendix A.3.
311
312   To finalise the coarse-graining process, bead features need to be assigned. In classical force fields,
313   these would correspond to bead types (typically, a discrete number of types exist). However, we
314   generalise the bead description and assign an Nfeat -dimensional feature vector to each bead. This
315   vector consists of two parts:
316
            1. The bead mass (see section 2.2.2 for its definition).
317
            2. The element composition of the bead. This is a 6-dimensional vector containing the number
318
               of hydrogen atoms in the bead as the first value, the number of carbons as the second, and
319
               continuing for all six elements accounted for in our force field (i.e., H, C, N, O, S, P).
320
321   As a result, each bead is characterised by a 7-dimensional feature vector. Additional features were
322   explored during this work, most prominently, features derived (i) from bead-internal distance mea-
323   sures (e.g., mean distance of atoms to bead position) and (ii) from the Coulomb matrix descrip-
      tor (Rupp et al., 2012) of the bead structure. However, we observed a negative effect of adding these

      geometry-dependent features with respect to MD stability of the resulting models indicating that the
325   additional model complexity hampers its generalisability.

328   2.3   DATASET
329
      To train a coarse-grained ML force field, we require a dataset that contains molecular structures
330
      with their chemical elements, atomic positions and atomic ground truth forces. We are interested in
331
      covering large parts of chemical space relevant for biological systems, in particular, proteins, RNA,
332   and lipid chemistry. Since the coarse-graining process reduces the system sizes significantly, having
333   larger molecular systems up to and beyond 200 atoms in our training set is desirable such that the
334   maximum number of neighbours within the receptive field of the force field models encountered
335   during simulations of large biomolecules is already observed during training. A molecular dataset
336   fulfilling these requirements is currently not available, and therefore, we apply our in-house dataset
337   generation pipeline to generate one.
338   Our pipeline starts from large molecular structures (e.g., full proteins or RNA) and generates smaller
339   molecular fragments from these, for which the conformation space is sampled (a) via MD, (b) by
340   adding Gaussian noise to MD snapshots, and (c) by stochastic conformer generation. Reference cal-
341   culations are run on the sampled structures subsequently. In this work, we employ semi-empirical
342   quantum chemical methods as the reference, in particular, the GFN1-xTB method (Bannwarth et al.,
343   2021). Appendix A.5 contains a description of the components of our pipeline in more detail and
344   provides a more in-depth overview of the properties of the resulting dataset. Moreover, we empha-
345   sise that our reference dataset does not contain solvated structures. Remarks on the reason behind
346
      this choice are made in Appendix A.6.
347   Our final dataset generated with our pipeline contains 4.9 million structures. We split it randomly
348   into training, validation and test set in a 80:10:10 fashion, keeping all structures (sets of positions)
349   of one fragment in the same subset to prevent data leakage. As mentioned above, further details on
350   the dataset can be found in Appendix A.5.

      We train and evaluate two different models,
356
             • a standard model, which generates the CG mapping based on the simple binary graph
357
               representation of the molecule (i.e., cA = 1 and cB = cC = cD = 0), and
358
359          • the tuned model, which generates the CG mapping based on the optimised weighting of
360            the four calculated node priorities. The detailed results of how we obtained the optimised
361            weights are located in section A.4 in the Appendix.
362
363
      The standard model acts as a baseline to assess the improvements achieved by our extension to
      the original approach by Webb et al. (2018). During training, we monitor multiple metrics of the
364
      training and validation set. In Figure 3, we present the learning curves for both models and for
365
      three metrics, namely, the loss, the 95th-percentile error of force norms and the Pearson correlation
366   between the predicted and ground truth forces. In particular, we observe that the 95th-percentile
367   error is a useful metric for predicting which model has a high probability to produce stable MD
368   simulations, because occasional inaccurate predictions can self-amplify during a simulation. At the
369   same time, the maximum error on the validation set behaves less predictably between epochs as it
370   depends on the single worst predictions which may be caused by outlier structures in the dataset.
371
      For each metric, the tuned model performs better than the standard model. Furthermore, we observe
372   that the training curves are significantly smoother for the tuned model, hinting at a well-behaved
373   (less noisy) optimisation space. This is especially evident for the Pearson correlation (see right plot
374   in Figure 3). Based on the explanation above, we select our models by their validation set 95th-
375   percentile error. This corresponds to the models after 212 and 101 epochs of training for standard
376   and tuned, respectively. Contrary to the standard model, note that the tuned model has not reached
377   its final training epoch of 300. However, since the validation set metrics for this model already
      surpass the ones for the standard model, we decide to run the evaluation on the 101-epoch model.

400   First, we assess the stability of MD simulations run with our two ML-based CG force fields across
401   various systems representing protein, RNA, and lipid chemistry. However, we selected test systems
402   which are not part of our training dataset, hence, these are neither fragments generated from larger
403   systems nor part of the PubChem subset. As most of these test systems are directly taken from the
404   RCSB Protein Data Bank, we refer to these by their PDB ID. For this stability test, we run 100 ps
405   of NVT Langevin dynamics with a timestep of 5 fs at 300 K. Unstable MD simulations in which the
406
      system topology breaks apart is a common issue for ML force fields, especially early in their training
      process (Brunken et al., 2024). Apart from a visual inspection of the trajectory, this behaviour can
407
      be detected easily by observing the kinetic energy of the system, or the temperature. The latter is
408
      kept roughly constant in NVT simulations if the trajectory is stable. In Table 4 of Appendix A.7,
409   we present the maximum temperatures encountered during the 100 ps simulations of all our test
410   systems for the standard and the tuned model. We exclude the first five picoseconds of simulation
411   time to make sure the system has time to thermally equilibrate. We note that in all-atom FF con-
412   texts, additional stability measures are commonly applied (Hoogeboom et al., 2022), however, their
413   extension to the CG context is not straightforward. Furthermore, we provide visualisations of four
414   of the coarse-grained test systems overlaid with their all-atom counterparts in the Appendix A.7 in
415   Figure 10. Table 4 demonstrates that we obtain across stable simulations across most test systems
416   (RNA, lipids, proteins) for small systems of less than one hundred atoms and for larger systems of
417
      multiple hundred atoms. However, some systems also break apart during the simulation for both
      ML models (e.g., PDB ID: 1BQF) or just for the tuned model (e.g., PDB ID: 1P79). Further visual
418
      inspection of the trajectories also show bond stretching between beads for some examples, most
419
      notably for the weak phosphorus−oxygen bonds in the phosphate groups of RNA structures. We
420   provide images of MD snapshots that demonstrate this phenomenon in Appendix A.7 in Figure 9.
421   Moreover, with respect to MD stability, we do not observe the superiority of the tuned model over
422   the standard one but instead we document a weak opposite effect. We hypothesise that a reason for
423   the lack of improvement observed in MD stability with the tuned model may be that the main metric
424   we optimise for during training and CG model tuning is related to the average of forces, however,
425   MD stability is mainly related to individual inaccurate predictions along a long-running MD trajec-
426   tory. This is a well-known issue not only for CG force fields, but also for all-atom ones, and may be
427   a plausible reason for our observed stability issue. For stable systems, we also run 10 ns simulations
428
      to assess (a) the model stability for longer simulation times and (b) the computation time obtained
      for the simulations in our set up. The results are reported in Appendix A.7 in Figures 11 and 12.

515   4   C ONCLUSION AND O UTLOOK
516
517   In this work, we introduced a new transferable CG-MLFF model which is not restricted for appli-
518   cation to specific types of systems (e.g., only non-fragmented peptide sequences) but can be univer-
519   sally applied to most molecular biosystems. We emphasise that demonstrating proper transferability
520
      across different types of systems (i.e., proteins, RNA, lipids, small molecules) is the core contri-
      bution of this work as this has not been demonstrated in the CG-MLFF context before. Moreover,
521
      we introduced a simple tuning strategy for the graph-based coarse-graining scheme by Webb et al.
522
      (2018). Our pipeline comprises the dataset generation, CG model, MLFF model training, and the
523   application of the trained model to new systems in MD simulations which we observed to be stable
524   for most of our test systems. Furthermore, we proved qualitative correctness of our approach, by
525   comparison with the well-established Martini force field and an GFN-FF all-atom reference, for a
526   test system that exhibited a high degree of MD stability. While observing that tuning the CG model
527   significantly improves the MLFF model training, our results on how this advantage translates to MD
528   simulations has not yet been fully conclusive.
529   In conclusion, we can report two key findings, (1) it is possible to develop a transferable MLFF
530   for coarse-grained systems despite the vastness of (bio-)chemical space and the information loss
531   introduced by the CG mapping, and (2) even a simple and straightforward tunability scheme with
532   four optimisable parameters is able to significantly improve the resulting MLFF model training. As
533   a consequence, we believe that making the CG model even more flexible can further improve the
534   downstream MLFF accuracy leading to increased MD stability. Following this work, the subsequent
535   step is to conduct a more extensive evaluation study of these models across more test systems against
536   Martini and all-atom FFs, and extend these findings to the application of large, research-relevant
537
      biosystems. To obtain valuable quantitative results for such systems, we anticipate that (a) treatment
      of solvation, (b) a dataset with more accurate reference forces (at DFT level), and (c) further fine-
538
      tuning of the model to experimental data will be necessary.

      A.3   B EAD ASSIGNMENT ALGORITHM AND PREPROCESSING
830
831   In this section, we describe the algorithm that assigns atoms to beads depending on their respective
832   priorities determined in the previous step (see the main text). This algorithm is taken from the
833   original work of Webb and coworkers (Webb et al., 2018).
834
835         1. Remove all hydrogen atoms from the structure. They are added to the beads which contain
836
               their bond neighbours later. In principle, this step is optional and the hydrogen atoms can
               also be treated as all other atoms.
837
838         2. Start with the atom with the lowest priority. If multiple atoms have the same priority, they
839            need to be processed at the same time. Find the neighbouring atom that is (i) not assigned
840
               to a bead yet, (ii) has a higher priority than the currently processed atom, and (iii) has the
               priority that is closest to the one of the currently processed atom. Group these two atoms
841
               together, which creates a new bead. If multiple neighbours have the same priority, it can
842
               also happen that the resulting bead will contain more than two atoms.
843
844
            3. Iterate through all atoms from lowest to highest priority and process them as described
               above. Note that if all of the neighbours of a given atom are already assigned to a bead,
845
               then this atom will make up a new bead by itself.
846
847         4. After one full pass through all atoms, the created beads are the result of the first coarse-
848
               graining level. If needed, one can repeat this procedure N times to obtain a system coarse-
               grained to level N . Before starting a new iteration, we need to assign new bonds between
849
               beads to construct a graph, which can be achieved by drawing chemical bonds between two
850
               beads if any of the atoms that make up the beads were bonded before.
851
852   Up to this point, the described algorithm determines which atoms contribute to which beads, how-
853   ever, the weights of these contributions are still undetermined. Multiple schemes to determine these
854   weights are possible, for example, using the centre of mass of the atoms of a bead as the bead posi-
855   tion, the centre of positions, the centre of positions excluding hydrogens, and many others. We only
856   apply the center-of-mass method in this work. Combining the algorithm described above with this
857   option for weighting the atom contributions yields the final coarse-grained mapping C described in
858
      section 2.2.2 of the main text.
859   Furthermore, we note that we implement a simple molecular fragmentation scheme to pass only

863   in the case of a large number of atoms. Our fragmentation scheme first separates the unconnected

902

903

904   our results show that the performance of our CG-MLFF models converges with increasing number

      A.5   DATASET GENERATION
919
920   In this section of the Appendix, we provide more details on our in-house dataset generation pipeline,
921   which can be divided into the following three steps.
922
      First, we generate our fragments directly from large systems (proteins, RNA, lipids nanostructures)
923
      by an automated fragmentation procedure employing the fragmentation procedure of the Swoose
924   C++ package (Brunken et al., 2021) implemented as part of its automated force field parametrisa-
925   tion functionality (Brunken & Reiher, 2020). Because the molecular fragments should represent a
926   similar region of chemical space as the local substructures in the large biosystems of interest for
927   MD applications, we generate our fragments directly from such systems. This fragmentation proce-
928   dure cuts out spherical fragments from a larger structure with a radius rcut and then follows the cut
929   bonds recursively up to a bond that is an allowed bond to cut with a well-defined saturation scheme.
930   Swoose implements this procedure for protein systems and it also works for most organic systems,
931   however, we further extend it by adding another possible bond cut, which is cutting O−P bonds and
932
      replacing them with O−H. This allows us to generate more diverse fragments for RNA molecules.
      Furthermore, Swoose generates one fragment around each atom in the system, which would result
933
      in too many fragments per system for our purpose. Hence, we keep only a subset of fragments
934
      sampled in a way to maximise the distances between the atoms around which the fragments were
935   generated. Moreover, we increase the diversity of covered chemical space by including a selection
936   of small molecules from the PubChem subset of the SPICE dataset as additional fragments (Eastman
937   et al., 2023).
938
      Second, based on the fragments and PubChem molecules as initial structures, we sample the confor-
939
      mation space, i.e., the potential energy surface, by three distinct methods, described in the following.
940
941         1. We run GFN-FF (Spicher & Grimme, 2020) MD simulations at 500 K and extract snapshots
942            in equidistant intervals. We select the GFN-FF method as a well-established universal clas-
943            sical force field with fixed bond topology, such that we avoid chemical reactions during the
944            sampling process at the high simulation temperature as we previously observed in Phys-
945            Net’s solvated fragments dataset (Unke & Meuwly, 2019). The most significant advantage
946            of the MD sampling method is that is produces physically reasonably configurations that
947            are generated in a well-defined process. However, its disadvantages are that for short-to-
948            medium length MD runs, the extracted snapshots can be autocorrelated to some extent and
949
               only a local part of the full conformation space can be sampled. Hence, we add methods 2
               and 3 to our sampling approach.
950
951         2. For every N -th snapshot extracted from the MD simulation, we generate N random sets of
952            positions that we obtain by applying small displacements to the original positions. These
953
               displacements are sampled from a Gaussian distribution. This mdgauss method, extends
               the md method by adding a set of uncorrelated configurations. The number of structures
954
               obtained from this sampling method is by construction the same as the number of structures
955
               obtained from the MD run.
956
957
            3. By applying the OpenBabel software (O’Boyle et al., 2011), we add conformers that
               are generated by a stochastic approach and cover the conformer space more broadly. We
958
               specifically apply this software as it allows conformer generation even for systems that
959
               contain more than one molecule, which occurs regularly in our generated fragments. The
960            number of conformers generated can, in principle, be freely chosen, however, for simplicity,
961            we generate the same amount as the number of structures sampled from MD runs. Note
962            that for small fragments or PubChem molecules it may be possible that OpenBabel is not
963            able to generate that many distinct conformers. In such cases, we generate the maximum
964            possible number of conformers.
965
966   For an example fragment, we present the sampling of geometries visually in Figure 6.
967   Lastly, one requires reference forces for the generated structures. These can be calculated at any
968   level of quantum chemical approximation. For the sake of this proof-of-concept study, we employ
969   semi-empirical quantum chemical methods as the reference, in particular, the GFN1-xTB method
970   implemented in the xtb software (Bannwarth et al., 2021). Semi-empirical methods are known
971   to exhibit higher accuracy compared to classical force fields as they already contain quantum me-
      chanical information for our ML force field model to learn. Moreover, the higher efficiency of

973    dataset, which has been shown to be necessary for CG force field training, as the coarse-graining
974    process adds noise to the data (Krämer et al., 2023).

       A.8   M ARTINI SIMULATIONS
1297
1298   In this work, we apply the Martini force field as a CG reference model (Marrink et al., 2007; Souza
1299   et al., 2021). Martini uses a simplified representation of molecules, where four heavy atoms are
1300   merged into a single coarse-grained (CG) bead (possible mapping is 4-to-1 or 3-to-1) (Marrink et al.,
1301   2004). The basic assumption while parametrising of the CG model: carefully parameterised proper-
1302   ties of the individual beads are transferable to the whole molecule. The Martini CG scheme is used
1303   to study a wide range of biological systems: lipid bilayers, transmembrane proteins, RNA/DNA,
1304
       membrane fusion, and protein-protein interactions. This approach is very useful for studying bi-
       ological processes that occur on longer timescales (≈ 10 − 100 µs). The most common and first
1305
       application of the Martini CG approach is, for example, self-assembly of nanoparticles or vesicles.
1306
1307   The systems in this work were modelled using a coarse-grained approach within the canonical
1308   (NVT) ensemble. Gromacs 2023 (Bekker et al., 1993) coupled with the MARTINI.v2 force field was
1309   employed. The timestep was 10 fs and the temperature was maintained at 300 K, controlled by the
1310
       Nosé–Hoover thermostat. The system was first equilibrated in the NPT ensemble with a timestep
       of 1 fs at a temperature of 300 K until the system volume stabilised, which typically required ap-
1311
       proximately 100 ps. During equilibration, we employed the velocity-rescaling thermostat and the
1312
       Berendsen barostat. Furthermore, water beads were completely removed from the simulation box,
1313   and the Dry Martini force field was applied (Arnarez et al., 2015). Dry Martini is a version of Mar-
1314   tini where water beads are removed from the simulation. Interactions between beads are adapted by
1315   tweaking the interaction levels and tuning bonded parameters to reproduce the properties observed
1316   with the standard Martini force field.
