009                                                Abstract
010
011              In this paper we propose a similarity function between graphs based on a math-
012              ematically principled metric for graphs of different sizes: the graph generalised
013              optimal subpattern assignment (GOSPA) metric. The similarity function is based
014              on an optimal assignment between nodes and has an interpretable meaning in terms
015              of similarity for node attribute error, number of unassigned nodes, and number of
016              edge mismatches. The proposed similarity function is computable in polynomial
017              time. We also propose its use in Gaussian processes (GPs) for graphs to predict
018
                 molecular properties. Experimental results show the benefits of the proposed GP
                 model compared to other GP baselines.

022   1     Introduction
023
024   With the success of machine learning in multiple research areas, data-driven analysis plays a more im-
025   portant role in many applications in chemistry, including prediction of chemical properties Delaney
026
      (2004); Lusci et al. (2013); Mobley et al. (2014); An et al. (2024), chemical reactivities Coley et al.
      (2019) and drug discovery von Lilienfeld & Burke (2020); Ahn et al. (2021). Recent advances in deep
027
      neural networks (DNNs) have demonstrated promising performance in many tasks and have been
028
      widely used in molecular property prediction tasks Yang et al. (2019); Chithrananda et al. (2020);
029   Chen et al. (2018); Meuwly (2021). However, to make a DNN model successful, high-quality and
030   comprehensive datasets are the key. This drawback becomes important when exploring a new class
031   of molecules, as a limited quantity of high-quality experimental data is usually available in the very
032   early stage of the exploration Thawani et al. (2020).
033
      Gaussian processes (GPs) are a type of kernel-based method to solve regression and classification
034
      problems Rasmussen & Williams (2006) that are specially suitable for small datasets, since they
035   typically only have few parameters. GPs can be used with inputs that are graphs using a kernel for
036   graphs Nikolentzos et al. (2021). Generally, there are three types of kernels or similarity functions
037   for graphs:
038
      (I) Diffusion kernels based on a metric on graphs (Neuhaus & Bunke, 2007, Chap. 5), such as
039
      the graph edit distance (GED) Sanfeliu & Fu (1983). A drawback of these kernels is that they are
040
      computationally intensive to compute because of the matrix exponential in kernel calculation.
041
042   (II) Similarity measures based on applying a transformation to the GED such that low metric values
043   are mapped to high similarities, and the other way round (Neuhaus & Bunke, 2007, Chap. 5). While
044
      these transformations do not define valid kernels, they can be used in practice Boughorbel et al.
      (2004). A drawback is that the computation of the GED is generally NP-hard Zeng et al. (2009).
045
046   (III) Kernels based on features obtained via pre-processing of the graphs, which can imply a loss of
047   information. Examples of these are the random walk kernel Kashima et al. (2003); Gardner et al.
048   (2018), and the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) graph kernel Shervashidze et al. (2011); Griffiths et al.
049   (2024).
050   In this paper, we propose a similarity function between graphs, where each node can have certain fea-
051   tures, that is based on a mathematically principled metric for graphs, meeting the identity, symmetry
052   and triangle inequality properties. In particular, we propose to use the graph generalized optimal sub-
053   pattern assignment (GOSPA) metric Gu et al. (2024); Rahmathullah et al. (2017). The graph GOSPA
      metric is based on computing an optimal assignment between nodes by penalising node attributes

054
      for assigned nodes, the number of unassigned nodes and the number of edge mismatches. There-
055   fore, the graph GOSPA similarity function has an interpretable meaning, inherited from the graph
056   GOSPA metric, which takes into account the whole information of the graph and can be computed
057   in polynomial time.
058
      Our contributions can be summarised as follows:
059
060   (1) We propose a novel similarity measure for graphs, based on the graph GOSPA metric.
061
      (2) We show the decomposition of the graph GOSPA similarity into interpretable components.
062
063   (3) We use the graph GOSPA similarity as the kernel function of a GP to predict molecular prop-
064   erties in several datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that Graph GOSPA GP has the best
065
      performance compared to other GP baselines in several of the considered datasets. We also show
      that the decomposition of the kernel can be used to assist with the interpretation of the similarity
066
      score.

069   2     Background on graphs and graph GOSPA metric
070
071
      2.1   Weighted undirected graphs
072
073   A weighted, undirected graph is formed by vertices (also called nodes) and weighted edges, each
074   edge connecting two vertices. The set of vertices is V = {x1 , . . . , xn } with the i-th node feature
075   denoted by xi ∈ RN Trudeau (1993). The edges and their weights can be represented by a symmetric
076   adjacency matrix A ∈ Rn×n , whose (i, j) element A(i, j) indicates the weight between the i-th and
077   j-th node, with A(i, j) = 0 indicating no edge.
078
079
      2.2   Graph GOSPA metric
080
081   The graph GOSPA metric is a mathematically principled metric, as it meets the identity, symmetry,
082   and triangle inequality properties, for graphs of different sizes Gu et al. (2024). Let us consider two
083   graphs X and Y with vertices VX = {x1 , . . . , xnX }, VY = {y1 , . . . , ynY }, and adjacency matrices
084   AX ∈ RnX ×nX and AY ∈ RnY ×nY .
085

086

135   The graph GOSPA metric penalises node attribute errors for assigned nodes, number of unassigned
136   nodes (each with a cost cp /2), and number of edge mismatches. In particular, for two pairs of assigned
137   nodes (two nodes in X and two nodes in Y ), the edge mismatch penalty is ϵp multiplied by the
138   absolute difference in the corresponding edge weights. In addition, each edge connecting an assigned
139
      node and an unassigned node creates a half-edge mismatch penalty of ϵp /2 multiplied by the weight
      of the edge, see full details in Gu et al. (2024).

      3.1   Gaussian processes
165
166   A Gaussian Process (GP) is a non-parametric Bayesian model over functions Rasmussen & Williams
167   (2006). A GP can be fully specified by its mean m(·) and covariance function (also called kernel)
168   k(·, ·) and we can write it as f ∼ GP(m(·), k(·, ·)). The mean function m(·) is typically chosen to
169   be a constant function with zero value, while the choice of covariance function given by that kernel
170   may vary from task to task.
171   For a regression task, consider that we have a set of n data points, D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 , where xi ∈ X
172   is the input data point and yi ∈ R is its associated output. Given the dataset and a new test point x∗ ,
173   we want to infer the value of its output, a problem called regression. In GP regression, we assume
174   that there is additive noise such that yi = f (xi ) + ϵi , where f (xi ) is the function value of sample xi
175   and ϵi is a zero-mean Gaussian noise with variance σ 2 , which is independent of other variables.
176   For simplicity, we write the function value f (x) = [f (x1 ), . . . , f (xn )]T as f , and the function
177   value f (x∗R) as f∗ and y = {y1 , . . . , yn }. The marginal likelihood of the model can be written as
178   p(y|x) = p(y|f , x)p(f |x) df , where we marginalise the function value f . In a Gaussian processes
179   model, the prior is assumed to be a zero-mean Gaussian, f |x ∼ N (0, K), where K refers to the n
180   by n covariance matrix whose (i, j) element is k(xi , xj ). The likelihood is also a Gaussian, y|f ∼
181   N (f , σn2 I), so the log marginal likelihood is

196   3.2   Kernel based on the graph GOSPA metric
197
      Once we have a training set, as explained above, we can apply a GP if we have a kernel function.
198
      A kernel is a function k : X × X → R that measures the similarity between elements of the space
199   X. In addition, a kernel can be written as the inner product on a feature space F that corresponds
200   to the mapping ϕ : X → F. That is, given x, y ∈ X, k(x, y) = ⟨ϕ(x), ϕ(y)⟩. Such projection
201   function ϕ exists if and only if k is a positive-semidefinite function, which means the Gram matrix
202   Ki,j = k(xi , xj ), where x1 , . . . , xn ∈ X and i, j ∈ {0, . . . , n}, is positive-semidefinite for every
203   possible set of data points.
204
      Kernel functions like the radial basis function (RBF) kernel and the Matérn kernels are commonly
205   used Rasmussen & Williams (2006), but they are designed for a vector input, x ∈ RN . For graphs,
206   there are kernels such as random walk kernels Vishwanathan et al. (2010) or Weisfeiler-Lehman
207   graph kernels Shervashidze et al. (2011). An alternative is to define similarity functions that can
208   work as kernels, but do not meet the above properties, for instance, a similarity function based on the
209   GED (Neuhaus & Bunke, 2007, Chap. 5).
210
      Here we introduce the similarity function based on the graph GOSPA metric.

      In this section, we first compare the Gaussian process based on the graph GOSPA similarity function
287
      with other Gaussian process models to make predictions on molecular properties in real datasets.
288   Then, we illustrate the decomposition of the graph GOSPA similarity function applied to molecules.

      4.1   Experimental setup
291
292   Datasets
293
294   In the experiments, we use 6 regression datasets, five from MoleculeNet Wu et al. (2018), and one
295   from Griffiths et al. (2022). Specifically, ESOL, FreeSolv, Lipophilicity and Photoswitch are datasets
296
      about the physical chemical properties of molecules and there is only one property to predict. QM8
      is a dataset consisting of quantum mechanical properties. In this dataset, for numerical tractability
297
      of GPs, we only use a subset of the molecules by random sampling 2000 molecules from the full
298
      dataset and only considering the first 6 properties to predict.
299
300   The datasets are split into training and test sets with a ratio of 80/20 (note that validation sets are not
301   required for GP models since hyperparameters are chosen based on the marginal likelihood objective
302
      on the training set). The graphs are obtained by converting the SMILES strings Weininger (1988)
      into the corresponding molecular graphs.
303
304
305
      Baselines
306   We compare the proposed method with GPs with the following kernels for molecules: Tanimoto
307   kernel Ralaivola et al. (2005) using ECFP fingerprints Rogers & Hahn (2010), subsequence string
308   kernel (SSK) Moss et al. (2020) using SMILES Weininger (1988) and WL kernel Shervashidze et al.
309   (2011) using graphs with atom type as the node attributes. Shortest path kernel Borgwardt & Kriegel
310   (2005) for labelled graphs, neighbourhood hash kernel Hido & Kashima (2009), edge histogram
311   kernel and vertex histogram kernel Sugiyama & Borgwardt (2015).
312
313   Evaluation metrics
314
      For ESOL, FreeSolv, Lipophilicity and Photoswitch datasets, we use the root mean square error
315
      (RMSE) to evaluate the performance. For QM8, we use mean absolute error (MAE), as this is the
316
      common choice in other papers for this dataset Wu et al. (2018); Hu et al. (2020). We also use negative
317   log predictive density (NLPD) as the metric to quantify the uncertainty Griffiths et al. (2024).
318
319
      Implementation details
320
321   All GP models are single-output GPs and the results are obtained by averaging over 20 random splits
322   of the training and the test set. The node attribute in the GP model based on graph GOSPA similarity
323   is the atom type. The base metric for node attributes of the graph GOSPA metric is d(x, y) = 0 if
      x = y, and d(x, y) = c if x ̸= y. All GPs are trained using the L-BFGS-B optimiser Liu & Nocedal


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      (1989), except the graph GOSPA similarity, which is trained with the Adam optimiser Kingma & Ba
325   (2014) on the marginal log-likelihood with 2000 iterations. The learning rate is set to 0.001. The
326   hyperparameters for graph GOSPA metric in the graph GOSPA similarity function are set to c = 3,
327   p = 2, p′ = 1 and the value of ϵ is set based on the optimal value of marginal likelihood with grid
328   search between [0,3] with step 0.2. The hyperparameter ℓ for the graph GOSPA similarity function
329   is optimised during the GP training process, the initial value is set to ℓ = 1.
330
      The models using SSK kernel, Tanimoto kernel and graph GOSPA similarity are implemented in
331   GPflow 1 Matthews et al. (2017). The WL kernel, shortest path kernel, neighbourhood has kernel,
332   edge histogram kernel and vertex histogram kernel are obtained from functions in the GraKeL library
333   Siglidis et al. (2020). The GP models for these graph kernels are using the implementation in the
334   library GAUCHE Griffiths et al. (2024), which are implemented in GPytorch Gardner et al. (2018).

      5   Conclusion
465
466
      In this paper, we have proposed a Graph GOSPA similarity function, which is able to measure graph
      similarity in an interpretable manner based on the graph GOSPA metric. The interpretability that
467
      comes from the similarity decomposition is an important characteristics as it helps identify the sim-
468
      ilar/different aspects between two graphs. We have also introduced a GP model based on the Graph
469   GOSPA similarity, which is able to learn both node and structural features in graphs by measuring
470   differences in node attributes, number of unassigned nodes, and edge mismatches.
471
472
      Finally, we have evaluated the proposed Graph GOSPA GP on various molecular property prediction
      datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that Graph GOSPA GP has better performance than the
473
      baselines in a number of datasets, and closely follows the best performing algorithms when it does
474
      not provide the best results. It has also been the best method at quantifying uncertainty via the NLPD.
