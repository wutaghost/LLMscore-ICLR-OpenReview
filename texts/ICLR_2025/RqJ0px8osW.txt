011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Existing infrared and visible image fusion (IVIF) techniques typically integrate the
014            useful information from different modalities within the ideal conditions. Never-
015
               theless, current state-of-the-art IVIF methods are ineffective when facing complex
               scene interferences such as bad weather, low light, and high noise, and they typ-
016
               ically need to be used in conjunction with other de-interference baselines, which
017
               inevitably resulting in the high memory costs and error accumulation, thus yield-
018            ing sub-optimal fusion results. To address these challenges, We propose a uni-
019            fied lightweight real-time IVIF network for multiple complex scenes. We con-
020            ducted a theoretically thorough analysis of modal degradations in the frequency
021            domain, leveraging the complementary strengths of both modalities to enhance
022            network learning. Our method facilitates the extraction of critical features even
023            amidst significant pixel interference. For reconstructing fusion results, we intro-
024            duce a spatial domain branching strategy which significantly improves the local
025            detail resolution, thereby mitigating potential omissions from frequency domain
026
               analysis. Extensive qualitative and quantitative experiments demonstrate that our
               framework excels in handling multiple complex scenes, while maintaining real-
027
               time computational efficiency for prompt image processing applications.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The objective of infrared and visible image fusion (IVIF) is to amalgamate valuable information
033   from diverse modalities to attain a more comprehensive and precise representation of the scene
034   (Zhao et al., 2024; Li & Wu, 2024; Zhang et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2019a; Zhang & Demiris, 2023;
035   Zhao et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b). The technique is widely used in real-world application scenes
036   such as object detection (Wang et al., 2023; Bochkovskiy et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2023), semantic
037   segmentation (Li et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2017) and autonomous driving (Xiao et al., 2020).
038   In recent years, the main research in IVIF has focused on ideal fusion scenes, which can be mainly
039   categorised into traditional algorithms (Li et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2020; Chen
040   et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2021) and deep learning based approaches (Liu et al., 2024b; 2022; Li et al.,
041   2023d; Xu et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022a). Traditional methods typically rep-
042   resent the source image at multiple scales and extract multi-modality features across different scale
043   levels. For example, PFF (Zhou et al., 2023) proposed a multi-scale fusion framework for IVIF
044   based on bio-visual inspirations. MCSCM (Luo et al., 2023) proposed an IVIF framework based on
045
      Multi-State contextual hidden Markov Model. However, these methods exhibit limited generaliza-
      tion capabilities and demand substantial computational resources. DL-based methods, particularly
046
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Transformers, have demonstrated superior performance
047
      in IVIF tasks compared to traditional methods. For example, U2Fusion (Xu et al., 2020) designed
048   a unified framework for diverse image fusion tasks. Swinfusion (Ma et al., 2022) introduced Swin
049   Transformer to the fusion task, designing a fusion framework capable of capturing long range con-
050   textual relationships. In addition, some algorithms (Zhao et al., 2023a) combine Transformer and
051   CNN modules to facilitate specific feature extraction for effective learning of global and local fea-
052   tures. Despite the demonstrated effectiveness of the self-attention mechanism in Transformers for
053   global feature extraction, its complexity scales quadratically with the size of the input features. This
      constraint hampers its widespread deployment in foundational vision tasks.

070   The aforementioned algorithms (Li et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023d; Xu et al., 2020)
071
      typically achieve the primary goals of IVIF. However, they often fail to extend effectively to complex
      scenes. A prevalent strategy for complex scenes involves integrating an image restoration model,
072
      which removes interfering features through image pre-processing prior to fusion. Moreover, several
073
      fusion architectures designed for complex scenes have been developed (Xu et al., 2023; Xie et al.,
074   2023; Li et al., 2024b). These architectures generally utilize a two-stage learning process, initially
075   addressing image restoration and fusion tasks separately, and then optimizing them interactively.
076   Unfortunately, these methods face three significant challenges: 1) Error accumulation can occur,
077   where residual interfering pixels or detail loss from the image restoration stage may propagate to
078   the fusion stage. Existing fusion models, often trained under ideal conditions, may misinterpret
079   these disruptive features as valuable, potentially exacerbating them. For instance, in deraining tasks,
080   raindrop textures that obscure scene information might be mistakenly enhanced as salient features
081   during fusion, as shown in Figure 1(a). 2) Adding irrelevant or erroneous features. IVIF seeks
082
      to harness the complementary strengths of different modalities. In low-light conditions, the visible
      modality captures minimal information and relies heavily on the infrared image. Preprocessing with
083
      low-light enhancement algorithms may inadvertently introduce spurious or erroneous features from
084
      the visible image, as illustrated in Figure 1(b). 3) Inference costs increase due to added model
085   parameters and computational complexity. Practical applications in real-world scenes demand that
086   algorithms be highly efficient. Given the scarcity of image fusion architectures suitable for complex
087   scenes, we consider, “Whether it is feasible to develop a unified framework that supports high-
088   quality and real-time fusion, rather than depending on a two-stage processing approach.”
089
      The answer is yes. In the context of an end-to-end unified framework designed for complex scenes,
090
      our primary focus is to enable the fusion network to effectively differentiate between interfering
091   and valuable features. Previous fusion algorithms typically rely on extensive multi-modality data
092   from ideal scenes to train networks to extract salient features from different modalities. When faced
093   with interference, image reconstruction is often used as a precursor to obtain relatively clean source
094   images for subsequent fusion. Our approach moves beyond the traditional focus on merely learning
095   clear features typical of ideal scenes. While learning the original scene information from a limited set
096   of pertinent features may result in some data loss compared to pristine source images, it is essential
097   to recognize the inherent redundancy within the images themselves (He et al., 2022). Here, we can
098   conceive the interfering pixels as masks that obscure the clear features. For instance, rain lines can be
099
      viewed as masks, as they obscure scene details during rainy conditions. Previous studies have shown
      that noise also functions as a mask (Delord, 1998). Similarly, in dark or overexposed scenes, low-
100
      light regions behave like masks, with pixel values ranging from 0 to 1, concealing details that would
101
      be visible under normal lighting. Thus, due to the redundancy inherent in images, we can effectively
102   reconstruct scene information even when certain details are missing. With this understanding, we
103   believe that the design of an end-to-end framework should prioritize extracting key information from
104   interference first and then reconstructing the scene based on that information, rather than recovering
105   the scene first and then extracting features. Furthermore, discarding pixel redundancy and ideal
106   fusion environment, the network can prioritize learning the most significant and complementary
107   pixel information from various modalities. The above concepts provides theoretical underpinning
      for our approach.

152   Infrared and Visible Image Fusion. Current IVIF algorithms can be categorized into three types:
153
      autoencoder (AE)-based models (Ma et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
      2022), generative adversarial network (GAN)-based models (Liu et al., 2022; Le et al., 2022; Ma
154
      et al., 2020), and algorithmic unfolding models (Li et al., 2023b; Deng & Dragotti, 2020). The core
155
      idea of AE-based IVIF algorithms (Tang et al., 2023b) revolves around achieving multi-modality
156   information extraction and fusion through learning a compact representation of the image and its
157   subsequent restoration. The GAN-based IVIF algorithm (Ma et al., 2019b) implements multiple
158   multi-modality information extraction mainly through adversarial training of generator and discrim-
159   inator. Algorithmic unfolding models (Li et al., 2023b) iteratively adjust parameters to better fit the
160   data and optimize the objective function. However, most of the mentioned algorithms (Ma et al.,
161   2022; Tang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2023b) are designed un-
      der ideal fusion scenes, lacking inherent knowledge of complex scenes with multiple disturbances.

      Frequency Domain Learning. Learning in frequency domain enhances network interpretability
198   (Lin et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2021; Yang & Soatto, 2020; Suvorov et al., 2022), and improving per-
199   formance as demonstrated in various visual tasks (Mao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022;
200   Pham et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a). Amplitude represents the intensity or energy of individual
201   frequency components within an image. It quantifies how much each frequency component, which
202   ranges from fine details to broader structural features, contributes to the overall image. Phase, on
203   the other hand, encodes the positional information of these frequency components, describing their
204   relative spatial arrangement within the image. Some studies have explored the correlation between
205   frequency characteristics and degraded images, revealing that factors like haze and low-light pri-
206
      marily affect the amplitude of the image (Li et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022). Utilizing the Fourier
      domain aids the network in pinpointing interfering pixels and enhancing the recovery of clear image
207
      details. The frameworks mentioned (Li et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022; Song et al., 2022) focused on
208
      learning in the frequency domain for single-modal tasks and did not extend to multi-modality image
209   processing. Our framework takes a step further by tailoring the frequency prior for both joint IVIF
210   and image restoration tasks for the first time.

234   conditions. The infrared spectrum’s superior ability to penetrate obscurants like fog, snow, and haze
235
      results in images with greater clarity and stability compared to visible light images in non-ideal
      conditions, such as adverse weather or low-light scenes. To leverage the advantages of infrared
236
      imaging, our method uses the amplitude of infrared images to guide the recovery of the visible
237
      image’s amplitude. However, infrared images often lack intricate texture details; therefore, we
238   employ the phase information from visible images to assist in extracting phase data from infrared
239   images. Importantly, the phase captures only the positional information of pixels, ensuring that
240   degradation information is not transferred to the infrared modality. As demonstrated in Figure 3,
241   this mutual guidance between infrared amplitude and visible phase enables the extraction of richer
242   and more valuable features from both modalities.
243
      In weak interference situations, the amplitude and phase of the visible image may provide overall
244   better information than the IR image. Conversely, in very dark or severely overexposed regions, the
245   infrared image may contain more detail than the visible image, offering superior scene information.
246   In both scenes, our proposed guidance mechanism remains effective. It is important to note that
247   the multi-modality mutual guidance mechanism does not completely replace the information from
248   any specific modality in the frequency domain; rather, it focuses on effectively utilizing the comple-
249   mentary information from both modalities. Additionally, our bootstrapping approach operates on
250   the frequency components in the frequency domain, rather than directly manipulating pixel values
251   in the spatial domain. The goal of this bootstrapping is to better preserve and enhance the periodic
252
      modes in the frequency spectrum, rather than to propagate pixel values directly.
253   Our observations from Figure 4 reveal that amplitude differences are primarily responsible for distin-
254   guishing between degraded and clear images, while the phase information remains relatively stable.
255   Thus, reconstructing images with a combination of clear amplitude and original phase can preserve
256   the scene’s core information. Still, solely relying on amplitude reconstruction can lead to detail loss,
257   as evident in our mesh surface map (Figure 4). Therefore, our network also integrates spatial domain
258
      information to compensate for and enhance textural details, adopting a dual-domain approach for
      more effective and comprehensive image fusion in complex environments.

264   domain module and the spatial domain module, respectively, to acquire feature maps of different

266
      source image information with the feature maps. The model’s framework is illustrated in Figure 5.

      space of degraded pixels is typically confined to the image’s amplitude. In addition, compared to the

      4     E XPERIMENTS
340
341
      To validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, we conducted experiments on three types
342
      of interfering scenes: adverse weather (rain), noise (Gaussian noise), and exposure anomalies (low-
343
      light and overexposure). To ensure fairness in the experiment, our end-to-end framework is com-
344   pared with existing ”image restoration + fusion” combinations.
345
346   Implementation details. We trained separate models for different interference scenes. The pro-
347
      posed network was trained using the Adam optimizer with the initial learning rate set to 1e − 4,
      gradually reduced to 1e − 6 using cosine annealing strategy. The training process was carried out
348
      for 2000 epochs. To augment the training data, the input image undergoes random horizontal and
349
      vertical flips. The cropped image size during training was set to 128 × 128, and the batch size was
350   128. All experiments were conducted on a NVIDIA 3090 GPU using the PyTorch framework.
351
352   Datasets. For the training data: In the rain fusion scene, we randomly selected 1000 pairs of rain-
353
      containing images from the AWMM-100k dataset (Li et al., 2024b). In the noise fusion scene, we
      used 1000 pairs of images randomly selected from the M SRS dataset (Tang et al., 2022b) and added
354
      Gaussian noise with a standard deviation of 10 to the visible images. In the overexposure scene,
355
      we scaled the pixel values in 1000 visible images from the M SRS dataset to create overexposed
356   images. In the low-light scene, we randomly selected 550 pairs of nighttime low-light images from
357   the M SRS dataset. For the test data: We randomly selected 50 images from the corresponding
358   training dataset.
359
      Comparison Methods. We selected seven state-of-the-art fusion methods for comparison: Co-
360
      CoNet (Liu et al., 2024a), Text-IF (Yi et al., 2024), CDDFuse (Zhao et al., 2023a), DeFusion (Liang
361
      et al., 2022), IGNet (Li et al., 2023d), LRRNet (Li et al., 2023b), and TGFuse (Rao et al., 2023). For
362   image restoration, we incorporated Retinexformer (Cai et al., 2023) for low-light scene, Restormer
363   (Zamir et al., 2022) for adverse weather and noise scenes, and MSEC (Afifi et al., 2021) for overex-
364   posed scene. We compared the combination of ”restoration + fusion,” similar to the approach used
365   in Yi et al. (2024), which serves as a reasonable basis for comparison.

      4.2     A BLATION E XPERIMENTS
466
      Impact of spatial domain. We conducted ablation experiments by removing the spatial domain
467   module. Processing solely in the frequency domain results in an inevitable loss of detail, which the
468   spatial domain helps to recover. As shown in Table 5, the fusion performance consistently degrades
469   across all four scenes when the spatial domain module is omitted.
470
      Impact of frequency guidance mechanism. Utilizing the complementarity of multi-modal infor-
471
      mation, we design a multi-modal interactive guidance mechanism to facilitate the learning of crucial
472
      feature information in interference scenes. To validate this strategy, we conducted ablation experi-
473   ments by removing the guidance mechanisms. As observed in Table 5, exchanging the infrared and
474   visible mutual guidance mechanisms leads to a decline in fusion performance. Utilizing the am-
475   plitude affected by interference as the guiding image impedes the recovery of spatial information,
476   resulting in lower scores across metrics. Additionally, removing the guidance mechanism hinders
477   the interaction of information between different modalities, further degrading fusion performance.

      In addition to the experiments conducted under interference scenes, we present the experimental
773
      results of various methods on the M 3F D and LLV IP datasets in Tables 7 and 8. These datasets
774   include numerous examples of ideal conditions or scenes with only slight visible light interfer-
775   ence. The quantitative comparison experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm achieves
776   the highest scores across five metrics on both the M 3F D(Liu et al., 2022) and LLV IP (Jia et al.,
777   2021) datasets. This finding indicates that our frequency-domain interaction guiding mechanism is
778   effective not only in the complex scenes discussed earlier but also maintains excellent fusion per-
779   formance in normal conditions, highlighting the strong generalization capability of the proposed
780   algorithm. Furthermore, we present examples of the proposed algorithm on additional fused scenes
781   in Figure 7. It can be observed that when the scene information provided by the infrared image is
782
      weaker than that of the visible image, meaning the infrared amplitude does not surpass the ampli-
      tude information of the visible image, the proposed interaction guidance mechanism still performs
783
      effectively. Proposed algorithm successfully retains the overall scene information from the visible
784
      image while capturing the significant thermal radiation information from the infrared image.
