009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce DAG-TFRC, a novel method for learning directed acyclic graphs
012
               (DAGs) from time series with few root causes. By this, we mean that the data are
013
               generated by a small number of events at certain, unknown nodes and time points
014            under a structural vector autoregression model. For such data, we (i) learn the
015            DAGs representing both the instantaneous and time-lagged dependencies between
016            nodes, and (ii) discover the location and time of the root causes. For synthetic
017            data with few root causes, DAG-TFRC shows superior performance in accuracy
018            and runtime over prior work, scaling up to thousands of nodes. Experiments on
019            simulated and real-world financial data demonstrate the viability of our sparse root
020            cause assumption. On S&P 500 data, DAG-TFRC successfully clusters stocks by
021            sectors and discovers major stock movements as root causes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027   Many applications produce time-series data: multi-dimensional data measured in regular time steps.
028
      Examples include temperature measurements at different sites in meteorology (Yang et al., 2022),
      stock prices in finance (Kleinberg, 2013; Jiang &Shimizu, 2023), and brain data in medicine (Smith
029
      et al., 2011). A key problem in analyzing time-series data is causal structure discovery, which aims
030
      to understand the generation mechanism of such data between nodes and across time (Assaad et al.,
031   2022b; Runge et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023; Hasan et al., 2023). On common structural model
032   associates time-series data with directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) that encode how the data in one
033   time step is obtained from prior ones. Our work specifically focuses on learning these DAGs from
034   time-series data (Sun et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2022; Pamfil et al., 2020). This approach simplifies
035   the broader problem of causal discovery by abstracting away the need for true causal relationships,
036   which often require techniques like interventions. Despite this simplification, DAG learning from
037   time series still poses a challenge due to the complexity of temporal dependencies and the high
038   dimensionality of data.
039   Structural vector autoregression. Prior work on structure discovery from time series parametrizes
040   the data generation as structural vector autoregression (SVAR) (Lütkepohl, 2005; Pamfil et al.,
041   2020), which was first introduced by Sims (1980) and is commonly used in econometrics (Kil-
042   ian, 2013). SVAR describes linear dependencies between nodes that are either instantaneous, if
043   the nodes are at the same time point, or lagged if there is a time delay between the nodes. The
044   SVAR model implies stationarity, i.e., that these dependencies are the same for every time point.
045
      Together they form a DAG, called the window graph, which uniquely determines the parameters of
      the SVAR. Learning a window graph with time lags is a challenging task and requires more param-
046
      eters than learning DAGs from static data, which is already an NP-hard problem (Chickering et al.,
047
      2004). Various methods to learn the (weighted) window graph from time-series data have been pro-
048   posed, e.g., by Hyvärinen et al. (2010); Nauta et al. (2019); Pamfil et al. (2020). However, some
049   methods, including Granger causality methods (Bussmann et al., 2021; Marcinkevičs &Vogt, 2020)
050   ignore the time lags or do not consider instantaneous dependencies (Entner &Hoyer, 2010; Khanna
051   &Tan, 2019). In addition, methods are generally inefficient to compute graphs with thousands of
052   nodes (Cheng et al., 2024). Moreover, there is a limited interpretation of the input variables of the
053   SVAR, which we will refer to as root causes. Here, we aim to make progress on those challenges
      with a novel efficient method based on the assumption of few root causes.

054
      Root causes. In this paper we are concerned with learning the window graph under the assumption
055   that the time series are generated from an SVAR with few root causes. Intuitively this means that
056   there exist (approximately) few significant events on nodes and in time that propagate through the
057   graph and time, as stipulated by the SVAR to produce the time-series data. This assumption differs
058   from prior work in which the input is typically assumed as zero-mean random i.i.d. noise (Pamfil
059   et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022; Tank et al., 2021). Our motivation comes from the few root causes as-
060   sumption for static data proposed by Misiakos et al. (2023b), successfully applied to gene activation
061   data (Misiakos et al., 2023a). Our work expands the applicability of this assumption to the case of
062   time series and, in addition, interprets the root causes in an experiment on real-world financial data.
063
      Note, that the root cause terminology we use is different but related to root cause analysis in (Ikram
      et al., 2022) as we explain in the related work later.
064
065   Contributions. In this paper we learn the weighted window graph associated with an SVAR for
066   time-series data under the assumption of few root causes and, in addition, recover their values,
067   locations and time points. Specifically:
068
             • We formulate the few root causes assumption for time-series data generated from an SVAR.
069
               We show that the ground truth window graph is identifiable and is the global minimizer of
070
               the number of non-zero root causes for absent noise.
071
072
             • We present DAG-TFRC, which learns the window graph from time-series data assuming
               an SVAR with few root causes. In synthetic experiments with few root causes, we show
073
               that DAG-TFRC can learn window graphs with up to several thousands of nodes and shows
074
               superior performance over various prior state-of-the-art methods.
075
076
             • We show the viability of the few root cause assumption on simulated and real-world finance
               data. On simulated data (where the ground truth is known) the few root cause assumption
077
               achieves the best results. On real-world stock market data from the S&P 500 index, the few
078
               root cause assumption allows us to reasonably cluster the stocks within sectors and identify
079            root causes that reflect significant changes in the stock prices.

      2    SVAR AND ROOT C AUSES

      We introduce notation, the needed background on SVARs, and the concept of root causes.
085   Time-series data.          A multi-dimensional data vector xt , measured at time point t ∈
086   0, 1, . . . , T − 1 = [T ], is written as xt = (xt,1 , xt,2 , . . . , xt,d ) ∈ R1×d . A time series consists
087   of a sequence of such data vectors x0 , . . . , xT −1 recorded at consecutive time points. We assume
088   these vectors stacked as rows in a matrix, representing the entire time series, denoted as X ∈ RT ×d .
089   When multiple realizations of X are available, they are collected as slices of a tensor X ∈ RN ×T ×d .
090
      These are typically obtained by dividing a long time series into smaller segments of length T .
091   Example: stock market. We consider an example of time-series data from the stock market. We
092   collect daily stock values xt for a particular stock index (e.g., S&P 500) for, say, 20 years. A time
093   series for one year is denoted with the matrix X and 20 years yield the data tensor X.
094   Model demonstration. We impose a graph model assumption on the generation of time-series data
095   and demonstrate it first with a simple example. Assume that the vector xt at time t is generated from
096   the data xt−1 of the previous time step according to the equation:
097
                                                 xt = xt−1 B + ct ,                                           (1)
098
099   where ct are input variables, which we call root causes following Misiakos et al. (2023b), but have
100   been also referred to as structural shocks (Kilian, 2013). The (i, j) entry of the matrix B ∈ Rd×d
101   represents the influence of xt−1,i on xt,j and corresponds to the adjacency matrix of a directed graph
102   G = (V, B) where V is a set of nodes enumerated as V = {1, 2, ..., d}. The goal is to learn B. The
103   model (1) is stationary, since B is the same for all t, and has time lag one, since xt is explained by
104
      the previous time step (and the new inputs at time t).
105   Example. In the stock market example, the stocks 1, 2, . . . , d in the S&P market index would
106   represent the nodes of a graph and B would encode the influences between these stocks. The model
107   then implies that the value xt,i of a stock i on day t is determined by the stock values xt−1 from day
      t − 1, combined with a root cause ct.i representing an event occurring on day t.

129   Structural vector autoregression. We expand (1) to the general form of an SVAR (Lütkepohl,
130   2005; Pamfil et al., 2020) with time lag k. Namely, we assume there exist adjacency matrices
131   B0 , B1 , ..., Bk ∈ Rd×d and ct ∈ R1×d , t ∈ [T ], such that for all t ∈ [T ]1 :
132
                                   xt = xt B0 + xt−1 B1 + ... + xt−k Bk + ct .                                  (2)
133
134   The (i, j) entry of Bτ represents the influence of i to j after τ time-steps (i.e., a lag of τ ) and ct are
135
      the root causes. B0 represents the instantaneous dependencies, while the B1 , ..., Bk represent the
      lagged dependencies. The SVAR is stationary, since the Bτ do not depend on t.
136
137   As in prior work (Pamfil et al., 2020) we assume that B0 corresponds to a DAG, which ensures that
138   the recurrence (2) is solvable with respect to xt .
139   The instantaneous B0 and lagged dependencies B1 , ..., Bk are collected as block-rows in a matrix
140   W ∈ Rd(k+1)×d which forms the so-called window graph23 depicted with an example in Fig. 1.
141   Note that the window graph is a DAG since the edges go only forward in time. The problem we
142   aim to solve is to infer the window graph W from time-series data under the assumption that there
143   are few root causes. To achieve this, our approach imposes a sparsity assumption on the root cause
144   terms.
145
      Example. In the previous stock market example, B0 would model instantaneous influences within
146   the same day, and the other Bτ influences across days. In this case, one would expect most influ-
147   ences to be captured by B0 since stock markets usually react close to instantaneously.

177
178   Here we show the identifiability of our setting, formulate a discrete optimization problem with the
179   ground truth window graph as a global solution, and present our proposed method DAG-TFRC.
180   Identifiability. A natural question to ask in structure discovery is whether the graph is identifiable
181   from data (Park, 2020). By this, we mean that there is a unique ground truth graph W corresponding
182   to the probability distribution of the data X. Thus, the unknown graph can be uniquely determined
183   from the data. Theorem 3.1 establishes the identifiability of W in our setting.
184   Theorem 3.1. Consider the time-series model (4). We assume that any root cause takes a uniform
185   random value from [−1, 1] with probability p4 and is zero with probability 1 − p. Then the adjacency
186   matrices B0 , B1 , ..., Bk ∈ Rd×d are identifiable from the time-series data X.
187
188
      Proof sketch. The idea is to unroll W over time into a DAG and rewrite (4) as a linear structural
189   equation model (SEM) as explained in (Misiakos et al., 2024). Then, considering the (varying)
190   locations of the non-zero entries in C modeled as Bernoulli random variables, the identifiability of
191   the unrolled DAG is established from (Misiakos et al., 2023b, Theorem 3.1) which is a consequence
192   of the non-Gaussian identifiability theorem by Shimizu et al. (2006). The window graph can then be
193   identified by extracting B0 , B1 , ..., Bk from the unrolled DAG. A full proof is in Appendix C.2.
194

196   version of (4). To solve for the window graph W the motivation is that the root causes tensor C is
197   assumed to be sparse and therefore the approximation W

239   4   R ELATED W ORK

241   Causal discovery from time series. Causal discovery methods for time-series data aim to find the
242   causal dependencies between nodes in time and recover either the window or summary graph which
243   ignores the time delays (Gong et al., 2023). Many methods do not find dependencies that are truly
244   causal (Assaad et al., 2022b) but rather discover spurious correlations (Assaad et al., 2022b).
245
      Constraint-based methods utilize conditional independence tests to infer whether an edge exists
246   between two nodes. Examples include Runge et al. (2019) that adapts the Peter-Clark (PC) algo-
247   rithm (Spirtes et al., 2000) to time series as well as Entner &Hoyer (2010) do for the Fast Causal
248   Inference (FCI) (Spirtes et al., 2000) to construct tsFCI. Other constraint-based methods are varia-
249   tions of PCMCI including (Runge, 2020; Gerhardus &Runge, 2020; Assaad et al., 2022a) or based
250   on tsFCI (Malinsky &Spirtes, 2018).
251
      Functional causal model-based methods consider the causal dependencies formulated according to a
252   functional form. In this category, VAR-LiNGAM (Hyvärinen et al., 2010), models the data genera-
253   tion using an SVAR with non-Gaussian noise as input. This method extends LiNGAM (Shimizu
254   et al., 2006) to time series and learns the window graph. TiMINO (Peters et al., 2013) and
255   NBCB (Assaad et al., 2021) belong into the same category but only learn the summary graph.
256   Closely related to functional causal models are methods that apply continuous optimization to learn
257   the window graph. Such methods are extensions of NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018) to time series
258   and include DYNOTEARS (Pamfil et al., 2020), NTS-NOTEARS (Sun et al., 2023) for non-linear
259   data, and iDYNO (Gao et al., 2022) for interventional data.
260   Granger causality methods typically utilize the SVAR model to deduce the summary graph of
261   pairwise Granger causal dependencies (Tank et al., 2021). (Pair-wise) Granger causality means
262   that i Granger-causes j if the past values of the ith time series improve on the prediction of the
263   present value of jth time series. Related works include neural Granger causality (Tank et al., 2021),
264   eSRU (Khanna &Tan, 2019), GrID-Net (Wu et al., 2021), kernel (Marinazzo et al., 2008) and cop-
265   ula (Hu &Liang, 2014) Granger causalities and also convergent cross mapping (Sugihara et al.,
266
      2012). Another line of work employs neural networks for causal discovery from time series, like
      TCDF (Nauta et al., 2019), SCGL (Xu et al., 2019), neural graphical modelling (Bellot et al., 2022)
267
      and amortized learning (Löwe et al., 2022).
268
269   Extensions of the above methods consider irregular data (Cheng et al., 2023; 2024), subsampled
      data (Gong et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2023), or non-stationary time-series (Gao et al., 2024).

      Few root causes. Our work falls into the category of continuous optimization methods but under
271   the different data assumption of few root causes. Thus, our optimization objective differs from prior
272   methods by minimizing the L1 of the approximated root causes, whereas prior work employed the
273   mean-square error loss. More comparison details are provided in Appendix B. The only method
274   relying on the assumption of few root causes was SparseRC proposed by Misiakos et al. (2023b),
275   designed for DAG learning from static data and successfully applied to gene data (Misiakos et al.,
276   2023a). Here we advance over SparseRC in two aspects. The first is efficient, meaning fast and
277   accurate, DAG learning from time series, particularly for larger DAGs and time lags. The second is
278   applying the assumption of few root causes in financial data, broadening the range of its practical
279
      applications and for the first time providing a real interpretation on the approximated root causes.
      Misiakos et al. (2024) applied SparseRC for learning graphs from time series, by unrolling the
280
      window graph over time into a DAG, which requires learning dT × dT parameters. In this form
281
      SparseRC is not applicable to our experiments due to the resulting high complexity and times out,
282   but we propose an alternative way of executing it to make the comparison feasible. However do-
283   ing so cannot exactly mimic the assumed SVAR model. More details about this adaptation are in
284   Appendix A.
285
      Root cause analysis. The term root cause as used in our work is related to the use in prior root
286
      cause analysis (RCA) (Ikram et al., 2022). RCA is the process of determining the initial cause
287
      event that made some subcomponents of a system to fail. In RCA there is typically only one root
288   cause; in our work there are multiple (but few) root causes in varying locations. RCA causes refer to
289   defects or anomalies in the data, where in our case the root causes generate the entire measured data.
290   Finally, the root cause in RCA is located at the closest (by some measure) ancestor of the failing
291   nodes, where in our setting the root causes have a linear relation with the data. An application of
292   our method to RCA may be a possible future direction.

296
297
298   We evaluate DAG-TFRC by comparing to prior state-of-the-art work on learning the window graph
299   from time-series data. We consider synthetic, simulated and real data.
300   Baselines. We consider a variety of benchmarks ranging from linear data to non-linear that employ
301   neural networks, considering only those that learn the full window graph. Concretely, we compare
302   against VAR-LiNGAM, Directed VARLiNGAM (Hyvärinen et al., 2010), DYNOTEARS (Pamfil
303   et al., 2020) using continuous optimization (like ours), NTS-NOTEARS (Sun et al., 2023) that as-
304   sumes non-linear data generation, the constraint-based methods tsFCI (Entner &Hoyer, 2010) and
305   PCMCI (Runge et al., 2019), as well as the deep learning method TCDF (Nauta et al., 2019).
306   We also compare against SparseRC, which is the only prior method explicitly imposing few root
307   causes. SparseRC is designed for static data but has also been applied to time-series data in (Misi-
308   akos et al., 2024) by creating a static DAG by unrolling the time dimension. Doing so, SparseRC has
309   to learn a DAG with dT nodes, which, in our smallest experiment, already corresponds to 20 · 1000
310   nodes and times out. To give it a fair chance in our comparison we adapt its use to learn a smaller
311   unrolled DAG corresponding to k + 1 time steps with (k + 1)d nodes. The idea is to feed as input
312   the data Xpast and learn a matrix A ∈ R(k+1)d×(k+1)d , from which then the window graph W is
313   extracted. With this formulation, we expect to compromise the performance of SparseRC but at least
314
      it becomes executable. More details are in Appendix A.
315   For the implementation, we use the publicly available repositories listed in Appendix D.9 with hy-
316   perparameters chosen with grid search listed in Appendix D.7 for synthetic and simulated data.

452   5.3    A PPLICATION : S&P 500 STOCK DATA
453
454   Dataset. We consider stock values from the Standard and Poor’s (S&P) 500 market index. A smaller
455   version (S&P 100) of this index has been considered in (Pamfil et al., 2020). We gather data from
456   the last 5 years: March 1st, 2019 to March 1st, 2024. We only consider the stocks that are present
457   in the index during the entire time, which leaves d = 410 stocks as nodes. We collect the available
458   daily close values for every stock which result in 1259 data time points for every stock and compute
459   as data values the normalized log-returns (Pamfil et al., 2020). For the stock i on day t it is computed
460   as xt,i = log(yt+1,i /yt,i ), where yt,i is the closing value of stock i at day t. We partition the time
461   series into shorter time intervals of length 50 to obtain time-series data X of shape 25 × 50 × 410.
462
      Using these data we learn the window graph that reveals the temporary relations between stocks and
      the underlying root causes that generate the data.
463
464   Learning stock relations. We execute all baselines with hyperparameters set according to the
465   synthetic experiment. Fig. 3a shows the DAG-TFRC estimate for B     b 0 representing the instantaneous
466   relations between stocks. A figure with similar properties is discovered by SparseRC, but with the
467   other baselines we did not manage to obtain reasonable result with our choice of hyperparameters
468   or the ones from the published papers, see Appendix D.6. Below we analyze this result and argue
469   that the few root cause assumption yields interpretable results for financial data.
470   For better visualization, we focus on the 45 highest weighted stocks in the S&P 500 index. In the
471   execution of DAG-TFRC we set a maximum time lag k = 2, but it discovered that only B0 was
472   significant. This confirms the known (Markovian) stock market principle: the previous day of the
473   stock market contains all information from the past Fama (1970). Fig. 3a can be well interpreted.
474   The edges of B b 0 roughly cluster the stocks w.r.t. their economic sectors. The few outliers are caused
475   by the big IT companies being spread over three sectors as shown: (i) MSFT influences GOOGL (ii)
476   META, AAPL both influence AMZN and (iii) AMZN influences MSFT and NVDA. Also, we note
477   that the weights of B b 0 are positive which implies that stocks have a positive effect on each other: if
478   one increases or decreases the others move along the same direction.
479
      Learning root causes. From the window graph approximation W          c , we can estimate the root causes
480   with (7). In Fig. 3b we show the estimation for the same 45 stocks and 60 randomly chosen dates. As
481   one may expect, they reflect significant changes in the stock price. To inspect further, we evaluated
482   all computed root causes regarding their correspondence to stock price changes. We say that a root
483   cause ct,i aligns with the change in data if ct,i [xt+1,i − (1 + ct,i /2)xt,i ] > 0. For example, if a root
484   cause equal to +0.1 aligns with the data change then xt+1,i is at least 1.05 times xti . Considering
485   a threshold of 0.07 in C results in 4228 significant root causes which amounts to around 1% of the
      total time-series data points N dT = 512500. 99.6% of those aligned with the data changes. Thus,


                                                                    9
486
      whenever there is a significant root cause at day t, then the price of the stock at day t + 15 will
487   increase if the root cause is positive (red) or decrease if the root cause is negative. Therefore, the
488   root causes will reflect significant events affecting the stock values.
489
490
      News and dividends. We conjecture that the root causes only correspond to significant changes
      that reflect unexpected events. For example META has a positive root cause +0.18 on 1st Feb 2024
491
      (Fig 3b). The same day META announced that it would pay dividends for the first time according
492
      to Reuters (2024). Similarly for the positive root cause +0.21 of NVDA on 24th May 2023, the
493   company announced jumps in its sales forecast as the demand for AI infrastructure increased, ac-
494   cording to Reuters (2023). On the other hand, an event that is expected, but still affects the stock
495   prices is the dividends, which are deducted on the ex-dividend date known well before. We conjec-
496   tured that it is unlikely that a root cause reflects a dividend payment. In our dataset, we have a total
497   of 3796 paid dividends, but only 35 of those coincided with a negative root cause, as conjectured.
498
      Limitations. DAG-TFRC inherits limitations of structure learning based on SVAR. Using SVAR
499   implies a linear model and stationarity, i.e., the window graph is the same for every time point and
500   also across all time-series samples. The directed edges found are not necessarily true causal rela-
501   tions; establishing those would require further causal tools like interventions. We implicitly assume
502   no undersampling: the measurement frequency is at least as high as the causal effects frequency.
503   Undersampling may affect the stock market experiment where we used daily the measurements, but
504   stock market effects happen within split seconds. In addition, we assume that there are no missing
505   values in the data and the measurements on each node are taken with the same frequency. Also,
506   while we can scale to thousands of nodes and better than prior work, very large graphs beyond
507
      that are still out of reach. Finally, we work with the underlying assumption of few root causes. In
      Appendix D.5 we include an experiment on the Dream3 challenge, a time series gene expression
508
      dataset. Our method is not the most appropriate, potentially because the linearity or the few root
509
      cause assumptions are violated.

512   6       C ONCLUSION
513
514   Our main contribution to the body of work on causal inference from time-series data is the novel
515   assumption of few root causes, which means that the data are generated via a small number of events
516
      in (node, time point) pairs. Assuming a standard SVAR model, we provided a practical algorithm
      that leverages this assumption to achieve both, higher accuracy and significantly faster execution on
517
      thousands of nodes than prior work as we illustrated in experiments. In particular, this included the
518
      accuracy in the discovery of the locations and time points of root causes. We motivated the few root
519   cause assumption intuitively and with experiments on simulated and real financial data that yielded
520   reasonable and interpretable results.

840
      prior work on causal discovery in time series. Methods such as VAR-based optimization approaches

841

932   C.1    SVAR STABILITY
933
      Whenever a measurement can be taken in a system, stability in the measured data holds by definition.
934
      For example, temperature measurements or stock price markets are never unbounded. To ensure
935   that the same happens for synthetic data, one needs to guarantee the stability of the data generation
936   process. A few prior works mention stability (Gong et al., 2015; Khanna &Tan, 2019; Bellot et al.,
937   2022; Malinsky &Spirtes, 2018), and here we want to acknowledge its importance.
938
      Equation (4) can be viewed as a discrete-time multi-input multi-output (MIMO) system (Skogestad
939
      &Postlethwaite, 2005), in which the input is the root causes C (together with noise) and the output
940
      is the time-series data X. As the time-series length T in (2) increases, the values of X can get
941   arbitrarily large. We desire to find a range of weights for the matrices B0 , B1 , ..., Bk that guarantees
942   that our time-series data are bounded. In particular, we require a condition for the bounded-input
943   bounded-output (BIBO) stability of this system. This has been already considered by Lütkepohl
944   (2005) (linear case, for non-linear refer to (Saikkonen, 2001)). The proposed condition requires
945   the roots of the reverse characteristic polynomial to have a modulus less than 1. Here, we prove a
946   practical and intuitive condition for stability as a derivation of the (Lütkepohl, 2005) result.

       D.3    G AUSSIAN ROOT CAUSES
1243
1244   We evaluate the behavior of DAG-TFRC in a special case scenario that doesn’t obey the sparse root
1245   causes assumption. We consider that each entry in the input X is an independent noise variable
1246   ∼ N (0, 1).
1247
       This scenario is equivalent to the data generation equation (4)
1248
1249                                            X = Xpast W + C + Nc .                                         (25)
1250   where W = 0 and C = 0. Thus X = Nc . Our algorithm in this case will approximate the noisy
1251   root causes C + Nc which are dense. Thus, in this case we re-weight the optimization objective (6)
1252   to give more emphasis in the terms λ1 ∥W ∥1 and λ2 · h (B0 ), in order to give W
                                                                                      c = 0 as output and
1253   less emphasis on the sparsity of root causes ∥X − Xpast W ∥1 .
1254
       In Fig. 7 we show our results on this scenario. We choose hyperparameters λ1 = 0.001 · N · T = 1
1255
1256
       and λ2 = 1 · N · T = 1000. With this approach, our algorithm manages to correctly find W        c = 0 and
1257   give a close approximation C   b on the ground truth root causes which are now equal to C + Nc = Nc .
1258
       Note, that in this trivial case it is crucial to account the noise in the root causes, as its contribution is
       very significant (large standard deviation of noise and empty root causes tensor).
