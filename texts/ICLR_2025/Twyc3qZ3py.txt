009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Comprehensive model tuning and meticulous training for determining proper
012            scope of neighborhood where graph neural networks (GNNs) aggregate informa-
013            tion requires high computation overhead and significant human effort. We propose
014            a probabilistic GNN model that captures the expansion of neighborhood scope as
015            a stochastic process and adaptively sample edges to identify critical pathways con-
016
               tributing to generating informative node features. We develop a novel variational
               inference algorithm to jointly approximate the posterior of the count of neighbor-
017
               hood hops and learn GNN weights while accounting for edge importance. Exper-
018
               iments on multiple benchmarks demonstrate that by adapting the neighborhood
019            scope to a given dataset our model outperforms GNN variants that require grid
020            search or heuristics for neighborhood scope selection.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Bhagat et al., 2011) gain significant atten-
026   tion in recent years due to their success in various areas, such as social information analysis (Li &
027
      Goldwasser, 2019), recommender systems (Ying et al., 2018) and biomedical domain (Kishan et al.,
      2021; Huang et al., 2020). They are considered essential methods for graph representation learning
028
      as GNNs can effectively exploit rich topological information by generating a node’s features from
029
      its neighborhood.
030
031   However, selecting an appropriate scope of the neighborhood where GNNs aggregate information
032   remains an important challenge: a narrow scope that covers a limited range of neighborhoods can
033
      hurt the predictive performance, and a broad scope that covers long-range neighborhoods can lead
      to over-smoothing (Li et al., 2018) and unnecessary complexity. Automatic search algorithms (e.g.,
034
      grid search) face the same issue since careful design of the search space is a daunting task, and
035
      validating large GNN structures incurs high computation and time costs.
036
037   Extensive research efforts show that appropriately setting neighborhood scopes for GNNs can be
038   critical to their performance improvement (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2021; Veličković
039
      et al., 2017). However, prior works mainly focus on designing aggregation schemes via regulariza-
      tion (Srivastava et al., 2014; Rong et al., 2019; Hasanzadeh et al., 2020) or network structures (Xu
040
      et al., 2018; Klicpera et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020). These methods inevitably rely on grid search
041
      and heuristics to determine the neighborhood scopes, which leads to heavy tuning and unnecessary
042   model complexity.
043
044   In this paper, we propose a probabilistic GNN model inferring the most appropriate neighborhood
045
      scope given the graph while aggregating node information. Specifically, we model the expansion
      of neighborhood scope as a stochastic process by defining a beta process (Broderick et al., 2012)
046
      over the count of neighborhood hops to allow it to go to infinity. The beta process induces hop-
047
      wise activation probabilities and its conjugate Bernoulli process enables us to adaptively sample the
048   edges in the neighborhood. In addition, the importance of the edges is evaluated based on the feature
049   similarity between the adjacent nodes. We can thus identify significant pathways that contribute to
050   the node latent features during training. We propose an efficient variational inference method that
051   jointly approximates the posterior of the neighborhood scopes and learns GNN weights. Our model
052   strikes a balance between the neighborhood scope expansion and the number of activated edges
053   within the neighborhood while providing well-calibrated predictions. It enhances GNN performance
      across various benchmark datasets, as demonstrated by our experiments.

      2.1   BAYESIAN I NFERENCE FOR GNN S
087
088   Graph Gaussian Processes (GGPs) (Ng et al., 2018) extends traditional Gaussian Processes (GPs) by
089   incorporating graph topology into the model, enabling it to handle uncertainty in both node features
090   and the graph structure. This approach is designed for situations with limited labeled data, using
091   the graph to propagate information to unlabeled nodes. The Bayesian-GCNN (Pal et al., 2019)
092   framework, as another Bayesian method, interprets the input graph as a single instance drawn from
093   a parametric family of random graphs and estimates the joint posterior distribution of both the graph
094   parameters and the node labels. BBGDC (Beta-Bernoulli Graph DropConnect) (Hasanzadeh et al.,
095   2020) can be viewed as a generalized stochastic Bayesian technique to train GCNs. It enables GCNs
096
      to independently drop out edges and convolution channels. However, these methods are not capable
      of inferring the number of hops automatically during training and require expensive hyper-parameter
097
      tuning of network depth.
098
099
100
      2.2   GNN S WITH M ULTI - HOP N EIGHBORHOOD S COPES
101
      Mixhop (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019) introduces a novel approach that allows for the mixing of infor-
102   mation from different hops in the neighborhood, enhancing the model’s ability to learn from local
103   and more distant nodes. It demonstrates how varied neighborhood scopes can be combined to im-
104   prove representation learning. (Zeng et al., 2021) addresses the limitations of traditional graph neural
105   networks (GNNs) in balancing depth (number of layers) and scope (size of the local neighborhood)
106   by substituting the input graph with a subgraph that preserves essential information. Graph Attention
107   Networks (GATs) (Veličković et al., 2017) introduces the attention mechanism to GCNs, allowing
      the model to weigh the importance of neighbors during aggregation. By adapting the neighborhood

      scope dynamically, GATs improve performance in scenarios where the relevance of neighbors varies
109   significantly.

      2.3   E DGE I MPORTANCE E VALUATION
112
113   Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) for GNNs is applied to randomly drop node features from the
114   previous hidden layer at each training iteration, based on independent Bernoulli random draws with
115   a constant drop rate. Compared to Dropout, DropEdge (Rong et al., 2019) randomly drops edges
116   from the adjacency matrix instead of node features in each hidden layer during training iterations,
117   based on independent Bernoulli random draws with a constant drop rate. But both methods just
118   randomly choose drop portions, which means they treat each node or edge as equally important ones.
119   Compared with DropEdge, DropEdge++ (Han et al., 2023) introduces a feature-dependent sampler
120   that correlates edge sampling probabilities with the feature similarity of node pairs, determining
121
      which edges should be retained or removed.

      4.5                 T HE M ECHANISMS M ITIGATING OVER - SMOOTHING
362
363
      We examine the total variation (TV) of the outputs from our model’s hidden layers throughout the
364   training process. TV quantifies the smoothness of a signal distributed across the nodes of a graph
365   (Chen et al., 2015). Specifically, given a graph with adjacency matrix A and a signal H defined
366                                                                            2
      across its nodes, the TV is defined as TV(H) = ∥H − 1/|λmax |AH∥2 where, λmax denotes the
367   eigenvalue of the adjacency matrix A with the largest magnitude. A lower TV indicates that the
368   signal on adjacent nodes is more consistent across orders, serving as an indicator of the presence of
369   the over-smoothing problem.
370
      Figure 3(a) shows the effectiveness of our method in preventing over-smoothing compared to other
371
      regularization techniques. In this experiment, we compare our method against vanilla GCN, GCN
372   with dropout regularization, and GCN with DropEdge regularization. The results show that vanilla
373   GCN and GCN with dropout suffer from a more pronounced over-smoothing issue. The total vari-
374   ation decreases as these models aggregate information from long-range neighborhoods and lead to
375   a rapid decline in test accuracy. On the other hand, GCN with DropEdge partially alleviates this
376   oversmoothing problem as the total variation is less impacted compared to the previous two models.
377   In contrast, our method demonstrates superior effectiveness and robustness, particularly for large
      neighborhood scopes.

      We evaluate edge importance using three kernels: linear, polynomial, and Radial basis function

400   leads to significant fluctuations in performance as observed by the large variation for the test accu-

402   ing in a loss of crucial information. This issue becomes particularly problematic when the dataset is

405   the parameters degree n and the parameter γ, respectively. The RBF kernel function outperforms
