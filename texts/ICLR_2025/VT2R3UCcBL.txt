009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning trains machine learning models on distributed devices by aggregating local model
012            updates instead of local data. However, privacy concerns arise as aggregating local model updates on
013            the server may reveal sensitive personal information by inversion attacks. Privacy-preserving methods,
014            such as homomorphic encryption (HE), then become necessary for FL training. Despite HE’s privacy
015            advantages, its applications suffer from impractical overheads, especially for foundation models.
016            In this paper, we present an efficient privacy-preserving federated learning framework by selective
017            parameter encryption with theoretical guarantees. Our approach proposes to selectively encrypt
018            sensitive parameters, significantly reducing both computation and communication overheads during
019
               training while providing a quantifiable privacy guarantee. Our optimization shows considerable
               overhead reduction, particularly for large foundation models (e.g. ∼100x reduction for GPT-2),
020
               demonstrating the potential for scalable HE-based FL deployment.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Federated learning allows distributed clients to collectively train a global model without directly sharing data. Instead
027   of uploading raw data to a central server for training, clients train models locally and share their model updates with
028
      the server, where the model updates are then averaged based on the aggregation functions (McMahan et al., 2017) to
      obtain a global model. While federated learning ensures that local raw data does not leave their original locations, it
029
      remains vulnerable to eavesdroppers and malicious servers that might exploit plaintext model updates to reconstruct
030
      sensitive training data (Fig. 1 (left)), i.e., gradient inversion attacks (Zhu et al., 2019; Criswell et al., 2014; Bhowmick
031   et al., 2018; Hitaj et al., 2017; Han et al., 2023; Hatamizadeh et al., 2022; Fowl et al., 2022). This poses a privacy
032   vulnerability especially when local models are trained on small local datasets (e.g., smartphone text data for large
033   language models). Local models derived from these small datasets inherently contain fine-grained information, making
034   it easier for adversaries to extract sensitive information from local model updates.
035
      Existing defense methods that reduce privacy leakage include differential privacy (DP) (Truex et al., 2019; Byrd &
036
      Polychroniadou, 2020) and secure aggregation (Bonawitz et al., 2017; So et al., 2022). DP adds noise to original
037   model updates but may result in model performance degradation due to the privacy noises introduced. On the other
038   hand, secure aggregation employs zero-sum masks to shield local model updates, ensuring that individual updates
039   remain private. However, secure aggregation demands additional interactive synchronization steps and is sensitive to
040   client dropout, making it less practical in real-world FL applications, where the unstable environments of clients face
041   challenges such as unreliable internet connections, and software crashes. Compared to the methods above, homomorphic
042   encryption (HE) (Paillier, 1999; Gentry, 2009; Fan & Vercauteren, 2012; Brakerski et al., 2014; Cheon et al., 2017)
043   offers a robust post-quantum secure solution that protects local models against attacks and provides privacy guarantee
044   while introducing minimal model performance degradation. As shown in Fig. 1 (middle), HE-based federated learning
045
      (FedHE) encrypts local models on clients and performs model aggregation over ciphertexts on the server to protect
      against privacy attacks, which has been adopted by several FL systems (Roth et al., 2022; IBM, 2022; Zhang et al.,
046
      2020; Du et al., 2023) and domain-specific applications (Stripelis et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023).
047
048   Despite the advantages, homomorphic encryption remains a powerful but complex cryptographic foundation with
049   impractical federated aggregation overheads (as shown in Fig. 1 (right)) for most real-world applications. Prior
050   FedHE solutions mainly employ existing generic HE methods without sufficient optimization for large-scale FL
051
      deployment (Roth et al., 2022; IBM, 2022; Zhang et al., 2020; Du et al., 2023). The scalability of encrypted computation
      and communication during federated training then becomes a bottleneck, restricting its feasibility for real-world
052
      scenarios. This HE overhead limitation is particularly noticeable (commonly ∼15x increase in both computation and
053
      communication), where both grow linearly w.r.t. the size of models (Cheon et al., 2017; Gouert et al., 2022). Especially

                                                                   1
054
      across resource-constrained devices, encrypted computing and communication of large models might take considerably
055   longer than the actual model training.
056
057   To address these challenges, we propose an efficient homomorphic-encryption-based privacy-preserving FL solution
058
      with Selective Parameter Encryption for practical deployment1 . Our method significantly reduces communication
      and computation overheads, enabling efficient HE-based federated learning. We further provide the first theoretical
059
      framework to quantify the privacy guarantee of selective encryption, which indicates a significant improvement over
060
      random encryption and differential privacy, with the important observation that most existing models follow Log-Normal
061   Mixture distributions. Extensive experiments validate our privacy quantification framework.

095
096   Privacy Attacks On FL. Threats and attacks on privacy in the domain of Federated Learning have been studied in
097   recent years (Mothukuri et al., 2021). Data reconstruction attacks (Criswell et al., 2014; Bhowmick et al., 2018; Hitaj
098   et al., 2017) are usually carried out on the models to retrieve certain properties of data providers or even reconstruct the
099   data in the training datasets. With direct access to more fine-grained local models trained on a smaller dataset (Wang
100   et al., 2019), the adversary can have a higher chance of a successful attack. Moreover, further attacks can be performed
101
      using GAN-based attacks to even fully recover the original data (Hitaj et al., 2017). The majority of the privacy attacks
      can be traced back to the direct exposure of plaintext accesses to local models to other parties.
102
103   Non-HE Defense Mechanisms. Local differential privacy has been adopted to protect local model updates by adding
104   differential noise on the client side before the server-side aggregation (Truex et al., 2019; Byrd & Polychroniadou,
105   2020) where privacy guarantee requires large-scale statistical noise on fine-grained local updates that generally degrades
106      1
           We integrate our work with an open-source federated learning platform. Core codes of our HE-enable platform are available in
107
      Supplementary Material.


                                                                           2
108
      model performance (Truex et al., 2020). On the other hand, other work proposes to apply zero-sum masks (usually
109   pair-wise) to mask local model updates such that any individual local update is indistinguishable to the server (Bonawitz
110   et al., 2017; So et al., 2022). However, such a strategy introduces several challenges including key/mask synchronization
111   requirements and federated learner dropouts. Compared to these solutions providing privacy protection in FL, HE is
112   non-interactive and dropout-resilient (vs. general secure aggregation protocols (Bonawitz et al., 2017; So et al., 2022))
113   and it introduces negligible model performance degradation (vs. noise-based differential privacy solutions (Truex et al.,
114   2019; Byrd & Polychroniadou, 2020)).
115   Existing HE-based FL Work. Existing HE-based FL work either apply restricted HE schemes (e.g., additive scheme
116   Paillier) (Zhang et al., 2020; Fang & Qian, 2021; Jiang et al., 2021) without extensibility to further FL aggregation
117   functions or provide a generic but impractical HE implementation on FL aggregation (Jiang et al., 2021; Du et al.,
118   2023; Ma et al., 2022), including industrial platforms such as IBM FL (IBM, 2022), while leaving the key issue with
119   impractical HE overheads as an unresolved question. In our work, we propose a novel Selective Parameter Encryption
120   optimization scheme that largely reduces the overheads as well as provide the first theoretical framework to quantify the
121   privacy guarantee of selective encryption, which makes HE-based FL viable and provable in practical deployments.
122   Parameter Selection in ML. Selective encryption of models has been explored in prior work, particularly in single-
123   client-server machine learning setups for training and inference. For instance, Sphinx (Tian et al., 2022) employs a
124   hybrid approach, utilizing HE for bias parameters while applying DP to the remaining parameters. However, unlike our
125   privacy sensitivity-based method, Sphinx does not easily satisfy the challenges in model and dataset diversity in FL.
126   Similarly, other approaches (Tian et al., 2021) face limitations in FL due to their reliance on specific model architectures,
127   overly coarse layer-wise selection strategies, and the absence of robust privacy quantification.

159   3.2    T HREAT M ODEL
160
      We define a semi-honest adversary A that can corrupt the aggregation server or any subset of local clients. A follows
161
      the protocol but tries to learn as much information as possible. Loosely speaking, under such an adversary, the security

                                                                         3
162
      definition requires that only the private information in local models from the corrupted clients will be learned when A
163   corrupts a subset of clients.
164
165   When A corrupts both the aggregation server and a number of clients, the default setup where the private key is shared
166
      with all clients (also with corrupted clients) will allow A to decrypt local models from benign clients (by combining
      encrypted local models received by the corrupted server and the private key received by any corrupted client). This
167
      issue can be mitigated by adopting the threshold or multi-key variant of HE where decryption must be collaboratively
168
      performed by a certain number of clients (Aloufi et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023). Since the multi-key
169   homomorphic encryption issue is not the focus of this work, in the rest of the paper we default to a single-key setup, but
170   details on threshold homomorphic encryption federated learning and microbenchmarks are provided in Appendix §A.4.

172   3.3       A LGORITHM FOR HE-BASED F EDERATED AGGREGATION
173
174   Privacy-preserving federated learning systems utilize homomorphic encryption to enable the aggregation server to
175   combine local model parameters without viewing them in their unencrypted form by designing homomorphically
176   encrypted aggregation functions. We primarily focus on FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), which has been proved as still
177   one of the best-performing federated aggregation strategies while maintaining computational simplicity (Wang et al.,
178
      2022).

180   client i ∈ [N ] owns a local dataset Di and initializes a local model Wi with the aggregation weighing factor αi ; the
181   key authority or the distributed threshold key agreement protocol generates a key pair (pk, sk) and the crypto context,
182   then distributes the key pair and crypto context to clients and only the crypto context, which is public, to the server. The
183   clients and the server then collectively calculate a global encryption mask M for Selective Parameter Encryption
184
      also using homomorphic encryption. At each round t ∈ [T ], the server performs the aggregation

238
      Although sensitivity calculation provides guidance on selecting important model parameters, to the best of our knowledge
239
      there is no existing work that successfully quantifies the privacy guarantee from the model parameter sensitivity. In this
240
      section, we, for the first time, provide proof to analyze the privacy guarantee of Selective Parameter Encryption using
241   the theoretical framework of privacy budget analysis (Dwork, 2006).

478   6     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose the first practical homomorphic-encryption-based privacy-preserving FL solution with
481
      Selective Parameter Encryption which is designed to support efficient foundation model federated training. Selective
      Parameter Encryption selectively encrypts the most privacy-sensitive parameters to minimize the size of encrypted
482
      model updates to reduce overheads while providing privacy guarantee quantifiable by our proposed theoretical privacy
483
      analysis framework. Future work includes further improving the performance of threshold HE in the less trusted FL
484   setting as well as investigating the impact of client data heterogeneity Mendieta et al. (2022); Guleria et al. (2024) and
485   the potential relationship between explainable ML techniques and our privacy sensitivity calculation.

719
720   Homomorphic Encryption is a cryptographic primitive that allows computation to be performed on encrypted data
721   without revealing the underlying plaintext. It usually serves as a foundation for privacy-preserving outsourcing
722   computing models. HE has generally four algorithms (KeyGen, Enc, Eval, Dec) as defined in Figure 9. The fundamental
723   concept is to encrypt data prior to computation, perform the computation on the encrypted data without decryption, and
724
      then decrypt the resulting ciphertext to obtain the final plaintext.
725   Since FL model parameters are usually not integers, our method is built on the Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song (CKKS)
726   scheme (Cheon et al., 2017), a (leveled) HE variant that can work with approximate numbers. The comparison of HE vs
727   other privacy-preserving primitives can be found in Table 3.

      A.2   F EDERATED L EARNING
736
737   Federated learning is first proposed in (McMahan et al., 2017), which builds distributed machine learning models while
738   keeping personal data on clients. Instead of uploading data to the server for centralized training, clients process their
739   local data and share updated local models with the server. Model parameters from a large population of clients are
740   aggregated by the server and combined to create an improved global model.
741
      The FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) is commonly used on the server to combine client updates and produce a new
742   global model. At each round, a global model Wglob is sent to N client devices. Each client i performs gradient descent
743   on its local data with E local iterations to update the model Wi . The server then does a weighted aggregation of the
744
                                                             PN
      local models to obtain a new global model, Wglob = i=1 αi Wi , where αi is the weighting factor for client i.
745
