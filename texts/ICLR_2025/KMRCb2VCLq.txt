009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning (ICL) has emerged as an effective solution for few-shot learn-
012            ing with large language models (LLMs). Previous research suggests that LLMs
013            perform ICL by analogizing from the provided demonstrations, similar to how
014            humans learn new tasks. However, how LLMs leverage demonstrations to specify
015            a task and learn a corresponding computational function through ICL remains
016            underexplored. Drawing from the way humans learn from content-label mappings
017            in demonstrations, we categorize the tokens in an ICL prompt into content, stop-
018
               word, and template tokens, with the latter two typically ignored by humans due
               to their uninformative nature. Our goal is to identify the type of tokens whose
019
               representations highly and directly influence LLM’s performance, a property we
020
               refer to as task-encoding. By ablating representations from the attention of the test
021            example, we find that the representations of informative content tokens have less
022            influence on performance, while template and stopword tokens are more prone to
023            be task-encoding tokens, which contrasts with the human attention to informative
024            words. We further give evidence about the function of task-encoding tokens by
025            showing that their representations aggregate information from the content tokens.
026            Moreover, we demonstrate experimentally that lexical meaning, repetition, and
027            structural cues are the main distinguishing characteristics of these tokens. Our
028            work sheds light on how LLMs learn to perform tasks from demonstrations and
029
               deepens our understanding of the roles different types of tokens play in LLMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      In-context learning (ICL) has become a popular technique employed with large language mod-
034
      els (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020). However, ICL has been shown to be unstable in that slight changes
035
      to the in-context prompts (e.g., reordering of demonstrations) can lead to substantial differences in
036   performance (Lu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). This circumstance is difficult to control due to a lack
037   of understanding of the model’s working mechanisms, leaving us uncertain about the exact process
038   by which LLMs learn to infer a task specification from demonstrations and produce a computation
039   function to implement that task specification. Previous papers have begun to explore this issue,
040   focusing on specific aspects such as the label space (Min et al., 2022) and the hidden states of the last
041   prompt token (Hendel et al., 2023; Todd et al., 2023), but have been limited in scope.
042   In this work, we aim to conduct a comprehensive study on how LLMs extract information that is
043   valuable for improving task performance from demonstrations. Drawing from the way humans learn
044   through content-label mappings in demonstrations, we categorize the tokens in an ICL prompt into
045   content, stopword (Sarica & Luo, 2021), and template tokens, with the latter two typically ignored by
046   humans due to their uninformative nature (Lenartowicz et al., 2014; Whitaker et al., 2018; Chirimuuta,
047   2021). With these categories in mind, we ablate the representations of different token types from the
048   attention of ICL test examples, masking partial information during the model’s task-solving process,
049   as shown in Figure 1. This ablation is intended to identify the types of tokens whose representations
050
      LLMs directly depend on to achieve high-level performance, thereby explaining how LLMs learn
      from demonstrations. These tokens critical for performance are referred to as task-encoding tokens.
051
052   Results of these experiments provide evidence that template tokens and stopword tokens are the most
053   prone to be task-encoding tokens as ablating their representations significantly decreases performance.
      In contrast, content tokens have a negligible impact on performance, as the task performance is not

055   is counterintuitive since the template and stopword tokens do not possess the information found in

058   show that content tokens are indirectly leveraged by LLMs during ICL through aggregating their

      2.1   W ORKING MECHANISMS OF IN - CONTEXT LEARNING
102
103   Since the proposal of in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020), its working mechanisms have been
104   extensively studied by the research community (Min et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022;
105   Bhattamishra et al., 2023). Min et al. (2022) suggest that demonstrations primarily provide the label
106   space, the distribution of the input text, and the format of the sequence for the test example. They argue
107   that the precise ground truth labels do not have significant importance. In contrast, Yoo et al. (2022)
      propose a differing view, stating that the impact of the ground truth labels depends on the experimental


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      configuration. Xie et al. (2021) explain ICL as implicit Bayesian inference, while Akyürek et al.
109   (2022) explore ICL learning process using linear models. Theoretical explanations (Guo et al., 2023;
110   Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b) and gradient descent explanations have also been proposed. Mao
111   et al. (2024) analyze in-context learning from the perspective of data generation. The perspective of
112   supported training data is also leveraged to analyze ICL (Han et al., 2023). Zhao et al. (2024) propose
113   to use coordinate systems to understand the working mechanism of in-context learning. Zhou et al.
114   (2023) propose a comprehensive survey on the interpretation and analysis of in-context learning.
115   Additional analyses exploring different aspects of ICL have also been studied. For instance, order
116   sensitivity where task performance fluctuates based on the order of the same ICL demonstrations
117   has been identified as a limitation of ICL (Lu et al., 2022). Yan et al. (2023) propose that repetitive
118   patterns in the prompt could affect the ICL performance in both positive and negative ways. Pan et al.
119   (2023) analyze the ICL process by disentangling it into task recognition and task learning. Madaan &
120   Yazdanbakhsh (2022) propose to define text and patterns while using counterfactual prompting for
121   attributing token importance in chain-of-thought techniques.
122   Our work investigates the working process of ICL in LLMs at inference time, demonstrating that
123   certain specific tokens are more likely to possess representations that could affect the processing of
124   the final test sample, improving the task performance.
125
126
      2.2   F UNCTION VECTORS OF IN - CONTEXT LEARNING
127
128   Todd et al. (2023) and Hendel et al. (2023) provide evidence of function vectors that store information
129   used to solve a task in ICL. They probe and extract the hidden representations of the final tokens
130   in the prompt. These vectors can then be added to, or used to replace, the corresponding vectors
131   in a zero-shot example, achieving results comparable to those obtained when the model uses all
132   demonstrations as context. In addition, Liu et al. (2023a) also propose using an in-context vector to
133   represent the target task and applying feature shifting to query examples. They first feed each input
134   and its corresponding target separately into an LLM, then concatenate all the latent states. A PCA
135   method is applied to derive a vector that is more closely aligned with the task. Finally, Wang et al.
136
      (2023) propose that label words in the demonstration examples function as information anchors by
      aggregating the information from previous demonstrations and providing it to the test example. This
137
      finding suggests that we may view label tokens as satisfying our definition of task-encoding tokens.
138
139   All these previous studies either solely focus on a single token (i.e., the last prediction prompt token
140   or label token) of the ICL prompt or treat the entire demonstration as a single unit, neglecting the
141   other tokens within it. Our research focuses on all the tokens in the prompt and reveals that there are
142
      additional tokens with specific characteristics whose representations significantly affect the final ICL
      performance.

      4     I DENTIFICATION OF TASK - ENCODING T OKENS
196
      In this section, we aim to find the task-encoding tokens in the ICL prompt. We first formally define
197
      what task-encoding tokens are. Then, we structurally categorize all the tokens in the prompt into three
198   types: template, stopword, and content tokens. We provide supporting evidence from the view of task
199   performance to show that the template and stopword tokens are the most prone to be task-encoding
200   tokens. Finally, we demonstrate that the information of content tokens serve to indirectly contribute to
201   the performance by being propagated into the representations of the task-encoding tokens by LLMs.
202
203   4.1   D EFINITION OF TASK - ENCODING TOKEN
204
205   Conceptually, task-encoding tokens are defined as tokens whose representations encode the task-
206   solving procedures. However, it is difficult to directly determine whether this information is encoded
207
      in the hidden representations of LLMs. Previous work has used performance variations to determine
      whether certain representations are related to downstream tasks (Todd et al., 2023; Hendel et al., 2023).
208
      Hence, as a practical proxy, we measure the performance variation before and after incorporating
209
      the representations of specific tokens into the attention scope of the test example, and define task-
210   encoding tokens as the tokens that lead to both a noticeable performance improvement when their
211   representations are included in the attention of test examples and performance degradation when they
212   are excluded from the attention of test examples.
213

      4.3     A BLATION ON TOKEN TYPES
271
272   To determine which token types are more likely to be task-encoding tokens whose representations
273   directly affect the final performance significantly, we design two experiments which ablate repre-
274   sentations or tokens based on token types. The first involves keeping and masking representations

278
      information propagation of different types of tokens to further explore the working of task-encoding
      tokens. Illustrations of these two methods which we refer to as representation-level and token-level

      ablations are shown in Figure 1 and Figure 2. More detailed examples for the representation-level

      ablation is provided in Appendix C.

382   4.4   F INDINGS
383
384   To summarize, we find that template and stopword tokens are the most likely to be task-encoding
385   tokens. Specifically, the representations of template tokens contribute significantly to performance
386   improvement. Meanwhile, the representations of stopword tokens play a more supportive role in the
387   spectrum of task-encoding tokens by summarizing the information of content tokens. In contrast,
388   the representations of content tokens do not directly facilitate task-solving, but they are aggregated
389   into the representations of the other two types of tokens. We discuss the possible applications of
390   these findings in Appendix O. Furthermore, this finding raises additional questions: 1) Are all the
391   task-encoding tokens working together? 2) What are the characteristics for a token to be perceived by
392
      a LLM as a task-encoding token?

530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we have provided a fine-grained characterization of task-encoding tokens, whose
533   representations LLMs directly depend on to achieve high-level performance. Through a series of
534   experiments, we have examined the roles of template tokens and stopword tokens within ICL as
535   potential task-encoding tokens. Our findings add nuance to previous claims made about ICL, for
536   example, that tokens other than label words could also provide valuable information directly affecting
537   the performance. Overall, our results demonstrate that model performance depends directly on the
538
      presence of these tokens and that their lexical meaning, their repetition throughout the ICL prompt,
      and their structural formatting of ICL demonstrations are likely to play a role in how effectively they
539
      allow an LLM to recover the critical information needed to perform a task.

790   A      L IMITATIONS
791
792
      In this paper, the token categorization is performed manually, leaving room for further refinement,
793
      leaving the exploration of other specific content tokens as task-encoding tokens in certain contexts
794   to future work. While the results provide robust support to our categorization, the identification
795   process itself lacks precision. For instance, stopwords may only represent a subset of all in-context
796   task-encoding tokens. The manual nature of our categorization limits our ability to comprehensively
797   track these tokens. Moreover, our experiments are limited to classification, machine translation, and
798   question answewring datasets, suggesting that our conclusions should be further validated for other
799   tasks. Additionally, our focus on task-encoding tokens, whose representations could impact task
800   performance, may overlook other tokens responsible for other possible functions. Another limitation
801   of our study is that we focus exclusively on in-context learning scenarios, meaning that our findings
802
      may not be directly applicable to zero-shot learning scenarios.

1223   perform significantly worse than in the standard in-context learning case and the other two ablation

1225   This further supports the finding that template tokens are likely important as task-encoding tokens.
