011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The increasing size of deep learning models has created the need for more efficient
014            alternatives to the standard error backpropagation algorithm, that make better use
015            of asynchronous, parallel and distributed computing. One major shortcoming of
016
               backpropagation is the interlocking between the forward phase of the algorithm,
               which computes a global loss, and the backward phase where the loss is back-
017
               propagated through all layers to compute the gradients, which are used to update
018
               the network parameters. To address this problem, we propose a method that paral-
019            lelises SGD updates across the layers of a model by asynchronously updating them
020            from multiple threads. Furthermore, since we observe that the forward pass is of-
021            ten much faster than the backward pass, we use separate threads for the forward
022            and backward pass calculations, which allows us to use a higher ratio of forward
023            to backward threads than the usual 1:1 ratio, reducing the overall staleness of the
024            parameters. Thus, our approach performs asynchronous stochastic gradient de-
025            scent using separate threads for the loss (forward) and gradient (backward) com-
026            putations and performs layer-wise partial updates to parameters in a distributed
027
               way. We show that this approach yields close to state-of-the-art results while
               running up to 2.97× faster than Hogwild! scaled on multiple devices (Locally-
028
               Partitioned-Asynchronous-Parallel SGD). We theoretically prove the convergence
029
               of the algorithm using a novel theoretical framework based on stochastic differ-
030            ential equations and the drift diffusion process, by modeling the asynchronous
031            parameter updates as a stochastic process.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Scaling up modern deep learning models requires massive resources and training time. Asyn-
037   chronous parallel and distributed methods for training them using backpropagation play a very
038   important role in easing the demanding resource requirements for training these models. Back-
039   propagation (BP) (Werbos, 1982) has established itself as the de facto standard method for learning
040   in deep neural networks (DNN). Although BP achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on literally all rel-
041   evant machine learning tasks, it comes with a number of inconvenient properties that prohibit an
042   efficient implementation at scale.
043   BP is a two-phase synchronous learning strategy in which the first phase (forward pass) computes the
044   training loss, L, given the current network parameters and a batch of data. In the second phase, the
045   gradients are propagated backwards through the network to determine each parameter’s contribution
046   to the error, using the same weights (transposed) as in the forward pass (see Equation 1). BP suffers
047   from update locking, where a layer can only be updated after the previous layer has been updated.
048   Furthermore, the computation of gradients can only be started after the loss has been calculated in
049   the forward pass.
050   Moreover, the backward pass usually requires approximately twice as long as the forward pass (Ku-
051   mar et al., 2021). The bulk of the computational load comes from the number of matrix multiplica-
052   tions required during each phase. If we consider a DNN with M layers, then at any layer m ≤ M
053   with pre-activations zm = θ m ym−1 and post-activations ym = f (zm−1 ), the computations dur-
      ing the forward pass are dominated by one matrix multiplication θ m ym−1 . During the backward

062
      In this work, we propose a new approach to parallelize training of deep networks on non-convex
063
      objective functions by asynchronously performing the forward and backward passes at a layer-wise
064   granularity in multiple separate threads that make lock-free updates to the parameters in shared
065   memory. The lock-free updates address the locking problem, performing layer-wise updates mit-
066   igates the issue of conflicts between parameter updates, and performing the backward pass and
067   updates using more threads than the forward pass mitigates the staleness problem. Specifically, the
068   imbalance in execution time between forward and backward passes is taken care of by having twice
069   as many backward threads than forward threads, breaking the 1:1 ratio of vanilla Backpropagation,
070   therefore significantly speeding-up the training process.
071   In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
072
073        1. We introduce a novel asynchronous formulation of Backpropagation which allows the for-
074           ward pass and the backward pass to be executed separately and in parallel, which allows us
075           to run more backward than forward threads. This approach accounts for the unequal time
076           required by the forward and backward passes.
077        2. We propose to asynchronously update the model’s parameters at a layer-wise granularity
078           without using a locking mechanism which reduces staleness.
079        3. We give convergence guarantees of the algorithm to a stationary distribution centered
080           around the local optima of conventional BP.
081        4. We show that the algorithm can reach state-of-the-art performances while being signifi-
082           cantly faster than competing asynchronous algorithms.

      Asynchronous stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Asynchronous SGD has a long history, starting
      from Baudet (1978); Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (2015). Hogwild! (Recht et al., 2011) allows multiple
088
      processes to perform SGD without any locking mechanism on shared memory. Kungurtsev et al.
089
      (2021) proposed PASSM and PASSM+, where they partition the model parameters across the work-
090   ers on the same device to perform SGD on the partitions. Chatterjee et al. (2022) decentralizes
091   Hogwild! and PASSM+ to allow parameters or their partitions to be located on multiple devices and
092   perform Local SGD on them. Zheng et al. (2017) compensate the delayed gradients with a gradient
093   approximation at the current parameters. Unlike these methods, we run multiple backward passes in
094   parallel on different devices and don’t need any gradient compensation scheme.
095
      Nadiradze et al. (2021) provides a theoretical framework to derive convergence guarantees for a wide
096   variety of distributed methods. Mishchenko et al. (2022) proposes a method of “virtual iterates” to
097   provide convergence guarantees independent of delays. More recently, Even et al. (2024) proposed
098   a unified framework for convergence analysis of distributed algorithms based on the AGRAF frame-
099   work. There have been lots of other analysis methods proposed for deriving convergence guarantees
100   for asynchronous distributed SGD (see Assran et al. (2020) for a survey). In our work, we propose an
101   entirely novel framework based on stochastic differential equations, and provide convergence guar-
102   antees of the algorithm to a stationary distribution centered around the local optima of conventional
103   BP.
104   Communication-efficient algorithms. One of the bottlenecks when training on multiple devices
105   or nodes in parallel is the synchronization step. The bigger or deeper the models get, the more time
106   is consumed by synchronization. PowerSGD computes low-rank approximations of the gradients
107   using power iteration methods. Poseidon (Zhang et al., 2017) also factorizes gradient matrices
      but interleaves their communication with the backward pass. Wen et al. (2017) and Alistarh et al.


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2017) quantize gradients to make them lightweight for communication. Like Zhang et al. (2017),
109   we interleave the backward pass with gradients communication but without gradients averaging.
110
111   Block local learning. Dividing the network across multiple devices and performing local updates is
112
      a widely recognized approach in distributed learning. The backward passes of the different blocks
      can be done simultaneously. The global loss is used to provide feedback only to the output block
113
      while the remaining blocks get learning signals from auxiliary networks which each compute local
114
      targets. Jaderberg et al. (2016) models synthetic gradients through auxiliary networks. Ma et al.
115   (2024) uses a shallower version of the network itself as the auxiliary network at each layer. Gomez
116   et al. (2022) allows gradients to flow to k-neighboring blocks. Nøkland & Eidnes (2019) don’t
117   allow gradients to flow to neighboring blocks, and instead use an auxiliary matching loss and a
118   local cross-entropy loss to compute the local error. Decoupled Parallel Backpropagation (Huo et al.,
119   2018) does full Backpropagation but uses stored stale gradients in the blocks to avoid update locking,
120   therefore needing additional memory buffers. Kappel et al. (2023) take a probabilistic approach by
121   interpreting layers outputs as parameters of a probability distribution. Auxiliary networks provide
122   local targets, which are used to train each block individually. Similar to these distributed paradigms,
123
      we mimic the execution of multiple backward passes in parallel by reordering the training sequence
      but without splitting the network explicitly during forward and backward propagation across devices
124
      and needing external buffers or architectural complexities.

149   3.1   A SYNCHRONOUS FORMULATION OF BACKPROPAGATION
150
151   We introduce a new asynchronous stochastic gradient descent method where, instead of performing
152   the forward and backward phases sequentially, we execute them in parallel and perform layer-wise
153   parameter updates as soon as the gradients for a given layer are available. The dependencies between

207   3.4   S TALENESS A NALYSIS
208
209   Here, we demonstrate the advantage of applying layer-wise updates (LU) compared to block updates
210   (BU). BU refers to performing updates only after the entire backward pass is complete, a technique

212
      2022; Zheng et al., 2017). We use the same notation as in section 3.3.

416   viation from the ideal parameter vector θ. We model this deviation as additive Gaussian noise
417   ξ ∼ N (0, σSTALE ) to the current parameter vector with variance σSTALE . To approximate the noisy
418   loss function, we use a first-order Taylor expansion around the noise-free parameters:

      6      D ISCUSSION
480
481   In this work, we introduced a novel asynchronous approach to train deep neural networks that de-
482
      couples the forward and backward passes and performs layer-wise parameter updates. Our method
      addresses key limitations of standard backpropagation by allowing parallel execution of forward and
483
      backward passes and mitigating update locking through asynchronous layer-wise updates.
484
485   The experimental results demonstrate that our approach can achieve comparable or better accuracy
      than synchronous backpropagation and other asynchronous methods across multiple vision and lan-


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      guage tasks, while providing significant speedups in training time. On CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100,
487   we observed speedups of up to 2.97× compared to asynchronous SGD covering a broad range of
488   paradigms. The method also showed promising results on a sentiment analysis task and the Ima-
489   geNet classification task where it reached close to ideal scaling.
490
      Our theoretical analysis, based on modeling the learning dynamics as a continuous-time stochastic
491
      process, provides convergence guarantees and shows that the algorithm converges to a stationary
492
      distribution closely approximating that of standard SGD under certain conditions. This offers a
493   solid foundation for understanding the behavior of our asynchronous approach.
494
495   While our implementation using C++ and LibTorch demonstrated the potential of this method, we
496
      also identified some limitations related to GPU resource allocation in SIMT architectures. Future
      work could explore optimizing the implementation for more efficient GPU utilization, or investigat-
497
      ing hybrid CPU-GPU approaches to fully leverage the benefits of asynchronous execution.
498
499   Overall, this work presents a promising direction for scaling up deep learning through asynchronous,
500   decoupled updates. The approach has the potential to enable more efficient training of large-scale
501   models, particularly in distributed and heterogeneous computing environments. Further research
502
      could explore extensions to even larger models, additional tasks, and more diverse hardware setups
      to fully realize the potential of this asynchronous training paradigm.
