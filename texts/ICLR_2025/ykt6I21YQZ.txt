009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            When solving inverse problems, one increasingly popular approach is to use pre-
012            trained diffusion models as plug-and-play priors. This framework can accommo-
013            date different forward models without re-training while preserving the generative
014            capability of diffusion models. Despite their success in many imaging inverse
015            problems, most existing methods rely on privileged information such as deriva-
016            tive, pseudo-inverse, or full knowledge about the forward model. This reliance
017            poses a substantial limitation that restricts their use in a wide range of problems
018
               where such information is unavailable, such as in many scientific applications.
               We propose Ensemble Kalman Diffusion Guidance (EnKG), a derivative-free ap-
019
               proach that can solve inverse problems by only accessing forward model evalua-
020
               tions and a pre-trained diffusion model prior. We study the empirical effectiveness
021            of EnKG across various inverse problems, including scientific settings such as in-
022            ferring fluid flows and astronomical objects, which are highly non-linear inverse
023            problems that often only permit black-box access to the forward model.
024

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The idea of using pre-trained diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020) as plug-and-play
029   priors for solving inverse problems has been increasingly popular and successful across various ap-
030   plications including medical imaging (Song et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023), image restoration (Chung
031   et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2022), and image and music generation (Rout et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
032   2024). A key advantage of this approach is its flexibility to accommodate different problems with-
033
      out re-training while maintaining the expressive power of diffusion models to capture complex and
      high-dimensional prior data distributions. However, most existing algorithms rely on privileged
034
      information of the forward model, such as its derivative (Chung et al., 2022a; Song et al., 2023b),
035
      pseudo-inverse (Song et al., 2023a), or knowledge of its parameterization (Chung et al., 2023a). This
036   reliance poses a substantial limitation that prevents their use in problems where such information is
037   generally unavailable. For instance, in many scientific applications (Oliver et al., 2008; Evensen &
038   Van Leeuwen, 1996; Iglesias, 2015), the forward model consists of a system of partial differential
039   equations whose derivative or pseudo-inverse is generally unavailable or even undefined.
040
      The goal of this work is to develop an effective method that only requires black-box access to the for-
041
      ward model and pre-trained diffusion model for solving general inverse problems. Such an approach
042   could significantly extend the range of diffusion-based inverse problems studied in the current lit-
043   erature, unlocking a new class of applications – especially many scientific applications. The major
044   challenge here arises from the difficulty of approximating the gradient of a general forward model
045   with only black-box access. The standard zero-order gradient estimation methods are known to scale
046   poorly with the problem dimension (Berahas et al., 2022).
047
      To develop our approach, we first propose a generic prediction-correction (PC) framework using
048   an optimization perspective that includes existing diffusion guidance-based methods (Chung et al.,
049   2022a; Song et al., 2023b;a; Peng et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) as special cases. The key idea of
050   this PC framework is to decompose diffusion guidance into two explicitly separate steps, uncon-
051   ditional generation (i.e., sampling from the diffusion model prior), and guidance imposed by the
052   observations and forward model. This modular viewpoint allows us to both develop new insights of
053   the existing methods, as well as to introduce new tools to develop a fully derivative-free guidance
      method. Our approach, called Ensemble Kalman Diffusion Guidance (EnKG), uses an ensemble

055   (i.e., no derivatives are needed), using a technique known as statistical linearization (Evensen, 2003;

      Contributions
059          • We present a generic prediction-correction (PC) framework that provides an alternative
060            interpretation of guided diffusion, as well as additional insights of the existing methods.
061
             • Building upon the PC framework, we propose Ensemble Kalman Diffusion Guidance
062            (EnKG), a fully derivative-free approach that leverages pre-trained model in a plug-and-
063            play manner for solving general inverse problems. EnKG only requires black-box access to

065
             • We evaluate on various inverse problems including the standard imaging tasks and scientific
066            problems like the Navier-Stokes equation and black-hole imaging. On more challenging
067            tasks, such as nonlinear phase retrieval in standard imaging and the scientific inverse tasks,
068            our proposed EnKG outperforms baseline methods by a large margin. For problems with
069            very expensive forward models (e.g., Navier-Stokes equation), EnKG also stands out as
070            being much more computationally efficient than other derivative-free methods.

      2   BACKGROUND & P ROBLEM S ETTING

074
      Problem setting Let G : Rn → Rm denote the forward model that maps the true unobserved
075
      source x to observations y. We consider the following setting:
076
077                                   y = G(x) + ξ,      x ∈ Rn , y, ξ ∈ Rm                                (1)
078
      where we only have black-box access to G (generally assumed to be non-linear). ξ represents the
079
      observation noise which is often modeled as Gaussian, i.e., ξ ∼ N (0, Γ), and y represents the
080
      observation. Solving the inverse problem amounts to inverting Eq. (1), i.e., finding the most likely x
081   (MAP inference) or its posterior distribution P (x|y) (posterior inference) given y. This inverse task
082   is often expressed via Bayes’s rule as p(x|y) ∝ p(y|x)·p(x). Here p(x) is the prior distribution over
083   source signals (which we instantiate using a pre-trained diffusion model), and p(y|x) is defined as
084   (1). Because we only have black-box access to G, we can only sample from p(y|x), and do not know
085   its functional form. For simplicity, we focus on finding the MAP estimate: arg maxx p(y|x) · p(x).
086
087   Diffusion models Diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022) capture the prior
088   p(x) implicitly using a diffusion process, which includes a forward process and backward pro-
089   cess. The forward process transforms a data distribution x0 ∼ pdata into a Gaussian distribution
090   xT ∼ N (0, σ 2 (T )I) defined by a pre-determined stochastic process. The Gaussian distribution is
091   often referred to as noise, and so the forward process (t going from 0 to T ) is typically used to create
092
      training data (iteratively noisier versions of x0 ∼ pdata ) for the diffusion model. The backward pro-
      cess (t going from T to 0), which is typically learned in a diffusion model, is the standard generative
093
      model and operates by sequentially denoising the noisy data into clean data, which can be done by
094
      either a probability flow ODE or a reverse-time stochastic process. Without loss of generality, we
095   consider the following probability flow ODE since every other probability flow ODE is equivalent
096   to it up to a simple reparameterization as shown by Karras et al. (2022):
097
098                                    dxt = −σ̇(t)σ(t)∇xt log pt (xt )dt.                                 (2)
099   Training a diffusion model amounts to training the so-called score function ∇xt log pt (xt ), which
100   we assume is already completed (and not the focus of this paper). Given a trained diffusion model,
101   we can sample p(x) by integrating (2) starting from random noise.
102
103   Diffusion guidance for inverse problems As surveyed in Daras et al. (2024), arguably the most
104   popular approach to solving inverse problems with a pre-trained diffusion model is guidance-
105   based (Chung et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2022; Kawar et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023a; Zhu et al.,
106   2023; Rout et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2024). Guidance-based methods are origi-
107   nally interpreted as the conditional reverse diffusion process targeting the posterior distribution. For
      ease of notation and clear presentation, we use the probability flow ODE to represent the reverse

108
      process and rewrite it with Bayes Theorem.
109
110                   dxt = −σ̇(t)σ(t)∇xt log pt (xt |y)dt,
111                       = −σ̇(t)σ(t)∇xt log pt (xt )dt − σ̇(t)σ(t)∇xt log pt (y|xt )dt,                  (3)
112
      where ∇xt log pt (xt ) is the unconditional score and the ∇xt log pt (y|xt ) is the guidance from
113
      likelihood. In practice, the unconditional score is approximated by a pre-trained diffusion model
114   sθ (xt , t). The corresponding reverse dynamics are:
115
116                          dxt = −σ̇(t)σ(t)sθ (xt , t)dt − wt ∇xt log p̂t (y|xt )dt,                     (4)
117   where wt is the adaptive time-dependent weight. The design of wt in Eq. (4) varies across different
118   methods but it is typically not related to σ̇(t)σ(t) that Eq. (3) suggests, which makes it hard to inter-
119   pret from a posterior sampling perspective. In this paper, we will take an optimization perspective
120   develop a useful interpretation for designing our proposed algorithm.
121   One key issue with Eq. (4) is that many algorithms for sampling along Eq. (4) assume access to the
122   gradient ∇xt log p̂t (y|xt ). When this gradient is unavailable (e.g., only black-box access to p̂t (y)),
123   then one must develop a derivative-free approach, which is our core technical contribution.
124
      Two existing derivative-free diffusion guidance methods are stochastic control guidance (SCG)
125
      (Huang et al., 2024), and diffusion policy gradient (DPG) (Tang et al., 2024). Both SCG and DPG
126
      are developed from the stochastic control viewpoint, and guides the diffusion process via estimating
127   a value function, which can be challenging to estimate well (as seen in our experiments).
128
129   Derivative-free optimization Derivative-free optimization (DFO) refers to settings where one
130   only has black-box access to the function of interest (i.e., no direct access to derivatives). Tradi-
131   tional DFO algorithms include direct search, which includes the coordinate search (Fermi, 1952),
132   stochastic finite-difference approximations of the gradient (Spall, 1998), Nelder-Mead simplex
133   methods (Nelder & Mead, 1965), and model-based methods which include descent and trust region
134   methods (Conn et al., 2000; Bortz & Kelley, 1998). Recent stochastic zero-order optimization tech-
135   niques involve approximating the gradient via Gaussian smoothing (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017);
136   these gradient estimates can be plugged into gradient-based algorithms directly, which we use to
137
      establish strong baselines in this paper. Our approach builds on top of the core idea of statistical
      linearization (Booton, 1954) from Ensemble Kalman methods, which is a popular family of scien-
138
      tific computing methods for solving physical inverse problems (Iglesias et al., 2013; Calvello et al.,
139
      2022). From an optimization perspective, the method can be seen as performing gradient decent
140   with a particle-based approximation to the derivative of the forward operator (Schillings & Stuart,
141   2017; Kovachki & Stuart, 2019). Prior works (Bergou et al., 2019; Chada & Tong, 2022) establish
142   the convergence results for some variants in the non-linear setting. However, their proofs do not
143   directly apply to our case due to the difference in the update rule.

347

352   mask with randomized location. For superresolution, we employ bicubic downsampling (either ×2
353   or ×4) and for Gaussian deblurring, a blurring kernel of size 61 × 61 with standard deviation 3.0.
354   Finally, phase retrieval takes the magnitude of the Fourier transform of the image as the observation.
355   We use measurement noise σ = 0.05 in all experiments except for superresolution on 64 × 64
356   images, where we set σ = 0.01. The pre-trained diffusion model for FFHQ 64 × 64 is taken
357   unmodified from Karras et al. (2022). The model for FFHQ 256 × 256 is taken from Chung et al.
358   (2022a) and converted to the EDM framework (Karras et al., 2022) using their Variance-Preserving
359
      (VP) preconditioning.
360
361   Evaluation metrics We evaluate the sample quality of all methods using peak signal signal-to-
362
      noise-ratio (PSNR), structural similarity (SSIM) index (Wang et al., 2004), and learned perceptual
      image patch similarity (LPIPS) score (Zhang et al., 2018).

526   5   D ISCUSSION
527
528   In this work, we propose EnKG, a fully derivative-free approach to solve general inverse problems
529   that only permit black-box access. EnKG can accommodate different forward models without any
530   re-training while maintaining the expressive ability of diffusion models to capture complex distri-
531   bution. The experiments on various inverse problems arising from imaging and partial differential
532   equations demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of our methodology.
533
      One limitation of the proposed EnKG is that as a optimization-based method, it cannot capture the
534   exact posterior distribution and thus cannot provide reliable uncertainty quantification, which might
535   be important in some applications. Another limitation is that while the per-sample time cost of EnKG
536   is smaller than the standard gradient-based approach, the total runtime is much longer because EnKG
537   maintains a whole ensemble of interacting particles. However, as shown in Figure 6, even a small
538   number of particles can achieve 20-30% relative error reduction. A potential future direction could
539   be to adaptively control the number of particles according to the optimization landscape to improve
      efficiency.
