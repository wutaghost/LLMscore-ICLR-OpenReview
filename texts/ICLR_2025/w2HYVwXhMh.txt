009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present E MBED (Egocentric Models Built with Exocentric Data), a framework
012            designed to mine video-language data from exocentric sources for egocentric video
013            representation learning. Large-scale exocentric data covers diverse activities with
014            significant potential for egocentric learning, but inherent disparities between ego-
015            centric and exocentric data pose challenges in utilizing one view for the other
016            seamlessly. In this study, we propose leveraging hand-object interactions and
017            language narratives as cues to incorporate exocentric data into egocentric training.
018
               Specifically, we focus on identifying specific video clips that emphasize hand-
               object interactions and pairing them with action-focused language narrations. By
019
               applying our framework to exocentric datasets such as HowTo100M, we construct
020
               datasets thar are effective for egocentric video-language pretraining. Our extensive
021            evaluations reveal that E MBED achieves state-of-the-art performance across various
022            egocentric downstream tasks, including a 4.7% absolute improvement in multi-
023            instance retrieval on the Epic-Kitchens-100 benchmark and a 6.2% improvement
024            in classification on the EGTEA benchmark in zero-shot settings. Furthermore,
025            E MBED enables egocentric video-language models to perform competitively in
026            exocentric tasks. Finally, we showcase E MBED’s application across various exocen-
027            tric datasets, exhibiting strong generalization capabilities when applied to different
028            exocentric datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Egocentric video understanding has become a crucial research field, notably impacting areas like
      augmented reality, personal assistants, and robotics. The curation of egocentric video-language
034
      datasets (Damen et al., 2018; Grauman et al., 2022) has catalyzed progress in this domain, enabling
035
      significant advancements in video understanding through the use of video-language pretraining (Lin
036   et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Pramanick et al., 2023; Ashutosh et al., 2023).
037
038   While there are several egocentric video-language datasets available, exocentric datasets encompass a
039
      broader range of human activities, which can be potentially used to enhance egocentric representation
      learning. Nonetheless, there is a noticeable domain gap that challenges seamless utilization (Li
040
      et al., 2021b; Lin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). This gap manifests in two dimensions: (1) video
041
      content, where egocentric videos predominantly capture close-up hand-object interactions, offering
042   a detailed perspective from the camera wearer’s point of view, while exocentric videos capture a
043   broader scene including both the subjects’ actions and their contextual environment; (2) the language
044   narration style differs significantly, with egocentric videos often accompanied by action-focused,
045   human-annotated narrations and exocentric videos relying on less accurate automatic transcriptions.
046   Consequently, few have found effective ways to best utilize videos of one viewpoint for another,
047   often resorting to simply finetuning models trained on separate viewpoints (Zhao et al., 2023) or
048   training models with egocentric data only (Lin et al., 2022; Pramanick et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023).
049   Notably, Ego-Exo (Li et al., 2021b) proposes to leverage exocentric video classification data for
050
      egocentric learning by distilling egocentric cues from exocentric data into video encoders. However,
      this method is focused on video classification data with categorical labels, making it challenging to
051
      adapt for video-language pretraining with flexible language narrations.
052
053   In this work, we present our method for automatically mining exocentric video-language data for
      egocentric video representation learning. As illustrated in Figure 1, despite their distinct viewpoints,

070   exocentric and egocentric data can share similar hand-object interaction (HOI) information reflected
071   in both the vision and language modalities, which can be potentially leveraged to improve egocentric
072   learning. Motivated by this, we propose E MBED (Egocentric Models Built with Exocentric Data), a
073   method designed to construct egocentric-style video-language data from exocentric sources by using
074
      egocentric cues. First, we identify and utilize HOI information to curate egocentric-relevant video
      clips from the exocentric dataset. This process involves selecting video clips that prominently feature
075
      active HOIs and cropping out the HOI regions spatially. This targeted approach allows for a more
076
      precise extraction of egocentrically relevant information from exocentric sources. Second, we perform
077   narration generation to pair each video with narrations styled after egocentric data. We implement
078   this through two models: 1) ego narrator, a narration generation model trained on egocentric data.
079   This model is utilized to generate narrations for exocentric videos, ensuring the output mirrors the
080   egocentric style; 2) exo-to-ego rephraser, which employs a large language model for in-context
081   learning. This model translates existing exocentric narrations into the egocentric style, effectively
082   adapting the language to match the egocentric context. By combining the video curation and narration
083   generation strategies, we construct new video-language data from exocentric sources that is tailored
084   for egocentric representation learning.
085   We perform extensive evaluations of E MBED across multiple egocentric video downstream tasks.
086   Specifically, we first demonstrate that integrating existing exocentric data (e.g., HowTo100M (Miech
087   et al., 2019b; Han et al., 2022)) into egocentric pretraining is suboptimal and can sometimes even hurt
088   the model performance. In contrast, applying our proposed method and then combining egocentric and
089   exocentric data can substantially improve the model performance, setting the state of the art on a wide
090   range of challenging downstream tasks. Notably, E MBED achieves an absolute improvement of 4.7%
091
      on the Epic-Kitchens-100 multi-instance retrieval and 6.2% on the EGTEA classification benchmarks.
      In addition, training with both egocentric and exocentric data yields benefits beyond egocentric
092
      tasks, enabling our model to achieve comparable performance than models trained exclusively with
093
      exocentric data in tasks such as UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012) and HMDB-51 (Kuehne et al., 2011).
094   Moreover, experiments suggest that E MBED exhibits strong generalization when transferring from
095   different exocentric datasets, including HowTo100M (Miech et al., 2019b), Kinetics-700 (Carreira
096   et al., 2019), Something-Something v2 (Goyal et al., 2017), and COIN (Tang et al., 2019).
097
      In summary, our contributions are: (1) we introduce a framework that connects exocentric and
098
      egocentric data with hand-object interaction and language narration information; (2) we propose
099
      data mining strategies that function in both vision (video temporal selection and spatial zoom-in)
100   and language (rephrasing and generation) modalities within this framework, resulting in new video-
101   language data sourced from exocentric data tailored for egocentric learning; (3) we demonstrate the
102   effectiveness of our framework across benchmarks.

      Overview. While egocentric and exocentric video-language datasets share similar data formats,
138   they differ in terms of video content and language narration style, preventing effective training
139   on their concatenated data. To solve the issue, as shown in Figure 2, our E MBED method first
140   curates egocentric-relevant videos X exo−ego from exocentric data (Section 2.1), then pairs each video
141   with its corresponding egocentric-style language narration Y exo−ego (Section 2.2). Afterwards, we
142   train a video-language model on the egocentric and transformed exocentric data (X ego , Y ego ) ⊕
143   (X exo−ego , Y exo−ego ), and the learned representations can be used for downstream applications.

145   2.1   V IDEO C LIP C URATION
146
147   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of our approach to curating video clips that are
148   highly relevant to egocentric scenarios from an exocentric dataset. This curation process effectively
149   collects egocentric-style videos from exocentric sources.
150
151   Temporal Selection of HOI Video Clips. One of the key challenges in aligning exocentric data
152   with the egocentric context is the inherent diversity of content within exocentric videos. Exocentric
153   videos may contain various actions, including irrelevant ones such as individuals looking around or
154   engaging in unrelated activities (Miech et al., 2019a;b; Han et al., 2022). This diversity complicates
155   the alignment of exocentric data with the format of egocentric data, which primarily emphasizes
156   hand-object interactions (HOI).
157   To tackle this challenge, we introduce a strategy for selecting video clips that emphasize the HOI
158   content. We start by uniformly sampling video clips from the entire exocentric dataset, each spanning
159   5 seconds. Subsequently, we employ a robust off-the-shelf hand-object detector (Shan et al., 2020) to
160   densely extract regions of HOI from all the video clips. Specifically, for each video clip, we sample
161   4 frames and use the hand-object detector to extract bounding boxes for the hand, object, and HOI
      regions along with their prediction probabilities within those frames.

171   Subsequently, we rank the video clips based on their scores and select those with the highest scores to

173
      language narration from the original dataset, provided that the narration’s timestamp falls within the
174
      clip’s time interval, and we illustrate how we transform the language narrations in the next section.
175
176   Spatial Focus on HOI Regions. In addition to the temporal selection, we propose a technique to
177   further encourage the model’s focus on hand-object interaction regions spatially. To achieve this, we
178   extract and zoom in on the HOI regions within the temporally selected video clips. This approach, as
179   in Figure 2, aligns the format of the curated videos more closely with that of egocentric data.
180
      Based on the hand and object regions obtained during the temporal selection step, we perform
181
      cropping and zooming to isolate these specific regions, creating video clips that closely resemble the
182   close-up hand-object interactions characteristic of egocentric data. First, we combine all the extracted
183   hand and object bounding boxes from each frame to form their convex hull, resulting in a combined
184   bounding box that covers all the hands and objects detected. During training, we randomly alternate
185   between using the original video clip and its cropped, zoomed-in version, with an equal probability
186   assigned to each selection.
187
      This spatial selection strategy offers multiple advantages. It promotes similarity between the formats
188   of egocentric and exocentric data, facilitating seamless integration. Furthermore, it implicitly encour-
189   ages our models to focus on the hand-object interaction region, as video-language pretraining losses
190   such as the contrastive learning loss align the representations of both the original video clip and the
191   zoomed-in clip with the same language target. Additionally, it serves as a data augmentation strategy.
192
193   Summary. By combining temporal and spatial selection techniques on video clips from X exo , we
194   curate a set of video clips X exo−ego that are rich in HOIs and highly relevant to egocentric learning.

196   2.2   L ANGUAGE NARRATION G ENERATION
197
198   In this part, we present our method for pairing each curated exocentric video with egocentric-
199
      style narrations using both egocentric-style narration paraphrasing and generation. Different from
      exocentric narrations which are usually obtained from noisy automatically transcribed sentences,
200
      narrations in egocentric datasets are typically manually annotated and focused on human actions. We
201
      demonstrate our method through examples of pairing the videos in the HowTo100M dataset with
202   narrations of the Ego4D style, and we will show the applicability to generalize the idea to other
203   datasets in the experiment section.
204
205   Exo-to-Ego Rephraser. Exocentric narrations often comprise ASR (Automatic Speech Recogni-
206   tion) sentences that may include content irrelevant to egocentric representation learning. For example,
207   sentences such as that “i cannot wait to dig in and enjoy it on the outside” lack visual alignment,
208   making them less useful. Similarly, sentences like “we’re going to keep mixing it because you don’t
209   want your chocolate to stick to the bottom of your pot” delve into the reasoning behind actions, which
210   can divert the model’s focus. In contrast, narrations in the Ego4D dataset are typically succinct, like
211   “C turns on a light,” primarily emphasizing human actions.
212   Our goal is to transform exocentric narrations into the egocentric style when applicable. For example,
213   the sentence “i’m just gonna start by cutting it in half” will be transformed to “a person cuts it in half”.
214   To initiate the transformation of exocentric narrations, we can use large language models, and here
215   we employ the Llama-2 model (Touvron et al., 2023). In order to adapt Llama-2 for our specific task,
      we begin by manually annotating a set of 10 examples comprising exocentric narrations and their


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      corresponding egocentric-style counterparts. These annotated pairs serve as our few-shot learning
217   examples for in-context learning for Llama-2.1 Llama-2, prompted with this annotated dataset, is then
218   utilized as a paraphrasing tool to generate egocentric-style paraphrases of the exocentric narrations.
219   We refer readers to Appendix for more details.
220
      In practice, we observe that many sentences are not visually alignable. For instance, narrations
221
      may include background information that detracts from visual alignment, as noted by Han et al.
222
      (2022). Additionally, these non-visually alignable sentences often lack action information, making
223   transformation using our rephraser impossible. To address this issue, we propose a method to filter
224   out such sentences. Specifically, we fine-tune a text classification model, DeBERTa-v3 (He et al.,
225   2021), using the HTM-Align dataset (Han et al., 2022). This dataset consists of a manually annotated
226   collection of 80 videos and 5,021 sentences from HowTo100M, with each sentence tagged for its
227   visual alignment with the corresponding video content. We utilize the fine-tuned DeBERTa-v3 model
228   to filter out sentences that lack visual alignment. Subsequently, we process the visually alignable
229   sentences through the Llama-2 model for style transformation.
230   This exo-to-ego narration transformation process effectively translates the original exocentric narra-
231   tions into a more egocentric perspective, ensuring that the core information is retained while the style
232   and viewpoint are adjusted to align with an egocentric narrative.
233
234   Ego Narrator. In addition to transforming existing exocentric narrations, we develop an egocentric-
235   style narration generator as a separate component in our generation process. This generator is trained
236   on a dataset containing purely egocentric data. Unlike the exo-to-ego narration paraphraser, which
237   focuses on paraphrasing existing exocentric narrations, the generator’s purpose is to create new
238
      egocentric-style narrations from scratch. Given an exocentric video clip, the narrator generator is
      capable of producing an egocentric-style narration based on the video content, ensuring that they are
239
      contextually relevant and consistent with an egocentric style. In this paper, we adopt the narrator
240
      model in LaViLa trained on Ego4D and use it to generate narrations on exocentric data.
241
242   Because the generated captions can sometimes be of low quality, we filter the low-quality samples
243   based on the model’s confidence scores, which are measured by perplexities, and filter any generations
244
      whose perplexity scores are lower than a threshold. In addition, because we find that the generation
      quality is more important than diversity as we will show in the experiment section, we propose to
245
      perform inference using beam sampling instead of nucleus sampling.
246
247
      Summary. Our approach involves two independent components that obtain egocentric-style nar-
248   rations from two different sources: the exo-to-ego rephraser for paraphrasing existing exocentric
249   language narrations into an egocentric style, and the ego narrator for generating egocentric-style
250   narrations directly from exocentric video content. These components pair exocentric videos with
251   egocentric-style narrations, resulting in our curated egocentric-relevant data (X exo−ego , Y exo−ego ).

      3    E XPERIMENTS
293
294   Pretraining Datasets. We pretrain models with both egocentric and exocentric datasets. For the
295   egocentric data, we use the video-narration pairs from Ego4D following (Zhao et al., 2023; Li et al.,
296
      2021b). The resulting data consists of around 9K videos and 4M video-narration pairs in total. For the
      exocentric data, we use the HTM-AA dataset (Han et al., 2022) as the data source, which is a clean
297
      subset of the HowTo100M dataset (Miech et al., 2019b) that contains around 247K HowTo100M
298
      videos and 3.3M video-narration pairs.
299
300
301   Baselines. We apply E MBED to the LaViLa model (Zhao et al., 2023) due to its strong performance,
302
      and our primary comparisons are with 1) the original LaViLa model (Zhao et al., 2023), and 2)
      LaViLa fine-tuned using both the Ego4D and the original HTM-AA datasets. In addition, we present
303
      the performances of other vision-language pre-trained models, including EgoVLP (Li et al., 2021b),
304
      EgoVLPv2 (Pramanick et al., 2023), Xu et al. (2024), and Helping Hands (Zhang et al., 2023)
305   for reference. We also adapt Ego-Exo (Li et al., 2021b) in video-language pretraining setting and
306   compare with it in Appendix.

      Downstream Tasks. We evaluate models on multiple egocentric downstream tasks as shown in
      Table 8. Specifically, we evaluate models on 1) Epic-Kitchens-100 (Damen et al., 2020) multi-

      instance retrieval (EK-100 MIR) and action recognition tasks; 2) Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022)

      Evaluation Protocols. We mainly focus on zero-shot evaluations where the pretrained video
318   and text representations are directly utilized on the downstream video-text retrieval and action
319   classification tasks, without any additional tuning specific to the downstream dataset. Following
320   previous work (Li et al., 2021b; Zhao et al., 2023; Pramanick et al., 2023), we also report fine-tuning
321   evaluations that involve adapting the pretrained video-text model through end-to-end fine-tuning using
322   the training data of the target downstream dataset. Additionally, we evaluate models on exocentric
323   tasks in the linear probing setting. In this setting, the pretrained video features are utilized as input,
      upon which a linear SVM is trained using the training subset of the downstream dataset.

352   Implementation Details. We train the LaViLa (Zhao et al., 2023) model on our constructed data.
353   Llama-2-7B is used for narration paraphrase and the LaViLa-Narrator (Zhao et al., 2023) is used
354   for narration generation, whose vision encoder is TimeSformer-Large and the text decoder is a
355
      GPT-2-XL (Radford et al., 2019). The hand-object interaction regions are pre-extracted with (Shan
      et al., 2020). We sample 4 frames with the resolution being 224×224 for each video clip during
356
      pretraining and 16 frames during finetuning. We initialize the models with the LaViLa parameters
357
      and train all the parameters jointly on Ego4D and HTM-AA for 5 epochs with the batch size set to
358   1024.

471   4    R ELATED W ORK
472
473   Egocentric Video Understanding. Understanding videos from an egocentric perspective introduces
474   unique research challenges, including areas like action recognition (Sigurdsson et al., 2018) and
475
      hand/body pose estimation (Ohkawa et al., 2023; Jiang & Grauman, 2017). Nevertheless, egocentric
      datasets have historically been small and specialized, which has held back research on egocentric
476
      video learning. Notably, many works initialize their models with parameters trained on exocentric
477
      data (Zhao et al., 2023), due to the scarcity of relevant egocentric data. However, the surge in the size
478   of egocentric video datasets (Damen et al., 2018; 2020; Grauman et al., 2022; 2024) over recent years
479   has brought about fresh opportunities and complexities (Li et al., 2021b; Pramanick et al., 2023).
480   For example, Ego-Only (Wang et al., 2023) shows that egocentric video representation can now be
481   trained with egocentric data only, without transferring from exocentric videos or images. In contrast,
482   our research aims to find new ways to make exocentric data useful for better understanding egocentric
483   videos in this evolving context.
484
485   Vision-Language Pretraining. Vision-language (VL) pretraining has first demonstrated effective
      for image representation learning (Lu et al., 2019; Tan & Bansal, 2019; Su et al., 2019; Li et al.,


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2019; Chen et al., 2020). These models, when presented with both visual and textual inputs, encode
487   them either separately (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021) or in a joint manner (Kim et al.,
488   2021; Li et al., 2021a; Dou et al., 2022). Subsequently, they are trained to align the representations
489   of corresponding vision-language pairs through contrastive (e.g. InfoNCE (van den Oord et al.,
490   2018)) and/or image-conditioned language modeling losses (e.g. masked language modeling (Devlin
491   et al., 2019)). The advent of large-scale video-language datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Carreira et al.,
492   2019; Krishna et al., 2017; Miech et al., 2019b) has facilitated the extension of similar pretraining
493   methodologies into the realm of videos (Sun et al., 2019b;a; Li et al., 2020). However, due to the
494   inherent difficulty in gathering high-quality video-language data, researchers have made efforts to
495
      adapt existing approaches to handle noisy video-language datasets (Miech et al., 2019a). In contrast
      to many uncurated video datasets, Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022) stands out as a collection of high-
496
      quality videos meticulously annotated with timestamped language narrations. This resource has
497
      spurred the development of numerous pretraining models for video-language tasks (Li et al., 2021b;
498   Pramanick et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Ashutosh et al., 2023). Yet, most of
499   these models have predominantly focused on videos captured from either egocentric or exocentric
500   perspectives alone. For example, LaViLa (Zhao et al., 2023) focuses on data augmentation within
501   egocentric sources. Consequently, the challenge of effectively combining datasets featuring different
502   viewpoints for video-language training remains relatively underexplored. In contrast, our approach
503   introduces methods for automatically mining video clips from exocentric sources and we demonstrate
504   significant empirical improvements over previous models such as LaViLa across settings.
505   Recently, there are works on using language models to re-write or re-generate narrations for
506   videos (Shvetsova et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). For example, Xu et al. (2024) propose to re-
507   trieve relevant exocentric data for egocentric videos and re-generate better narrations for pretraining.
508   In contrast to this line of work, our approach do not rely on existing video clips, but instead actively
509   mines new video clips and pairs them with their corresponding language narrations. In addition, our
510   focus in on adapting data from one viewpoint to another rather than performing data cleaning.
511
512   Cross-View Video Learning. There has been prior work aimed at bridging the gap between
513   egocentric and exocentric video perspectives (Sigurdsson et al., 2018; Ardeshir & Borji, 2018; Joo
514   et al., 2015; Fan et al., 2017; Yonetani et al., 2015). One prevalent strategy involves the development
515   of viewpoint-invariant representations through embedding learning techniques, which have been
516   applied in domains such as action recognition (Soran et al., 2015) and person segmentation (Xu
517
      et al., 2018). Another line of research focuses on image generation methods that employ generative
      adversarial frameworks to synthesize one viewpoint from the other (Regmi & Borji, 2018; Regmi &
518
      Shah, 2019). Additionally, some efforts have treated viewpoint invariance as a domain adaptation task,
519
      adapting exocentric video models for overhead drone-footage scenarios (Choi et al., 2020). However,
520   most of these approaches require paired datasets (Grauman et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), either
521   simultaneously recorded or sharing the same labels, across different viewpoints. Ego-Exo (Li et al.,
522   2021b) eliminates the need for videos concurrently recorded from both viewpoints by identifying
523   latent egocentric signals in exocentric video classification data and distilling this knowledge into
524   the video encoder. Unlike Ego-Exo, we are focused on video data with flexible language narrations,
525   which extends beyond categorical labels and has wider and more flexible applicability. In addition,
526   we proactively select videos rich in egocentric cues to streamline the identification and learning of
527   hand-object interactions, curating new video-language datasets with a large number of videos tailored
528
      for egocentric learning, whereas Ego-Exo passively utilizes existing video data. In addition, we
      also empirically demonstrate that incorporating Ego-Exo objectives cannot improve video-language
529
      pretraining in Appendix, confirming the necessity of proposing new methods in this direction.
530
531
532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   E MBED improves egocentric video understanding by unlocking the untapped potential of exocentric
535   video data. By identifying egocentric cues from exocentric data, we actively search for egocentric-
536   relevant video clips and pair them with action-focused language narrations, resulting in exocentric-
537   sourced data tailored for egocentric video-language pretraining. The extensive evaluations of E MBED
538   demonstrate its strong performance, achieving significant improvements over strong baselines on
539   multiple benchmarks. Our findings encourage further exploration into the combination of egocentric
      and exocentric perspectives in video understanding and beyond.

724   In this section, we go over the implementation details of each of our modules during the pretraining
725   stage.
726
727   HOI Detector. We use the hand-object detection model (Shan et al., 2020) trained on the 100-DOH
728   dataset.2 We extract the hand and object regions using the off-the-shelf model from the video frames.
729   Figure 3 demonstrates that the HOI detector can achieve robust performance in the exocentric setting.
730
731   HOI Video Clip Selection. As mentioned in the main paper, we perform data selection to select
732   video clips capturing hand-object interactions. Specifically, we segment all the videos in the HTM-AA
733   dataset into 5-second video clips. For each of the video clips, we uniformly sample 4 video frames
734   from it and compute the HOI score accordingly. The video clips with the highest HOI scores are
735   selected for training.
736   Figure 4 showcases video clips of the highest and lowest HOI scores respectively. We can see that
737   our data selection strategy can select video clips that capture close-up hand-object interactions that
738   are akin to the egocentric dataset.
739
740   Spatial Focus. As shown in Figure 5, given a video clip consisting of several frames, we use the
741   HOI detector to detect the hand and object regions from all the frames. Then, we take the convex hull
742   of the extracted regions so that it can cover all the hand and object regions in this video. Finally, we
743   crop this region out of the video frame and feed this cropped input to the model.
744
745   Exo-to-Ego Rephraser. Many of the exocentric narrations are redundant and contain information
746   irrelevant to human actions. To filter these narrations, we first train a text classification model on the
747   HTM-Align dataset (Han et al., 2022) to classify whether a sentence is useful or not. HTM-Align
748
      is a manually annotated 80-video subset of HowTo100M. It is randomly sampled from the Food
      and Entertaining category of HowTo100M. Each sentence is annotated with whether it is visually
749
      alignable with the video and its corresponding start and end timestamps within the video. Given the
750
      HTM-Align dataset, we finetune the DeBERTa-v3-base (He et al., 2021) model on it for this binary
751   prediction task. Specifically, we append a classification layer on top of the pretrained DeBERTa-v3
752   and fine-tune the whole model for 3 epochs with the learning rate set to 5e-5. The trained checkpoint
753   is then used for filtering sentences that are classified as non-visually alignable. Note that DeBERTa-v3
754   is a text-only model that does not take any vision inputs.

941   Sampling Strategies. LaViLa (Zhao et al., 2023) has previously demonstrated that the nucleus
942   sampling works much better than beam search possibly because nucleus sampling introduces more
943   diversity into its generations albeit at a cost of quality. We compare beam sampling with nucleus
944   sampling in our paper, and as shown in Table 9, beam sampling is better than nucleus sampling. This
945   is because when using the narrator in the out-of-domain setting, the generations are relatively of low
946   quality and it is important to first ensure the generation quality when using the egocentrically-trained
947   narrator in the exocentric setting.
948
949   Comparisons with Ego-Exo (Li et al., 2021b). Ego-Exo and our method share similar goals,
950   as both approaches aim to utilize exocentric data for egocentric representation learning. However,
951   Ego-Exo is focused on video classification data with categorical labels, making it hard to adapt it
952   for general video-language pretraining settings where flexible language narrations are involved. To
953   demonstrate this, we try to incorporate Ego-Exo into our video-language pretraining step by asking
954
      the video encoder to localize the HOI heatmaps detected by (Shan et al., 2020) using the objectives
      proposed in Ego-Exo (Li et al., 2021b). As shown in Table 12, incorporating Ego-Exo cannot improve
955
      the model performance in the video-language pretraining setting, suggesting its incompatibility with

      the current state-of-the-art paradigm.
