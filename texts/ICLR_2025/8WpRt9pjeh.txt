009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Obtaining data in the medical field is challenging, making the adoption of AI
012
               technology within the space slow and high-risk. We evaluate whether we can
013            overcome this obstacle with synthetic data generated by large language models
014            (LLMs). In particular, we use GPT-4 and Claude 3 Opus to create agents that
015            simulate adults with varying profiles, childhood memories, and attachment styles.
016            These agents participate in simulated Adult Attachment Interviews (AAI), and we
017            use their responses to train models for predicting their underlying attachment styles.
018            We evaluate our models using a transcript dataset from 9 humans who underwent
019            the same interview protocol, analyzed and labeled by mental health professionals.
020            Our findings indicate that training the models using only synthetic data achieves
021
               performance comparable to training the models on human data. Additionally, while
               the raw embeddings from synthetic answers occupy a distinct space compared to
022
               those from real human responses, the introduction of unlabeled human data and a
023
               simple standardization allows for a closer alignment of these representations. This
024            adjustment is supported by qualitative analyses and is reflected in the enhanced
025            predictive accuracy of the standardized embeddings.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Acquiring medical data from human subjects is challenging due to ethical and logistical concerns.
032   Strict adherence to regulations like the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)
033   in the U.S. and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe is crucial for safeguarding
034   patient privacy and confidentiality, and businesses face significant penalties for mishandling sensitive
035   data.
036   Collecting data can be a resource-intensive and time-consuming process that requires patient consent
037   and commitment, continuous iterations with institutional review boards (IRBs), and researcher
038   availability to ensure data accuracy and reliability. In addition, early capital companies may lack the
039   resources to initiate or last until they have access to meaningful and substantial data necessary to
040   address patient problems, which can involve complex and data-intensive processes. Lastly, studying
041   the impact of therapeutic interventions can be remarkably delicate, especially in the context of mental
042   health, where some treatments may have adverse effects, and successful outcomes often depend on
043   personalized and patient-specific approaches.
044   Possible solutions include utilizing public data sources like the UK Biobank (Sudlow et al., 2015) or
045   the All of Us research program (All of Us Research Program Investigators, 2019), though these are
046   restricted by data scope and licensing constraints. Alternatively, using private datasets is costly which
047   can limit scalability. Conducting more experiments is another option, but it is time-consuming and
048   resource-intensive. Lastly, simulating real data with synthetic data is feasible, but creating convincing
049   and accurate synthetic data poses significant challenges.
050   In this work, we explore modern advances in large language models (LLMs) to embody characteristics
051   of real human behavior in the mental health space. The mental health sector presents a unique
052   opportunity for LLMs, as many therapies primarily involve conversational interactions. Improvements
053   in LLMs have enhanced our understanding of language, opening up new possibilities for addressing
      mental health challenges.

      This approach has several benefits, including preserving patient confidentiality, reducing the need for
055   direct patient involvement, and allowing researchers to investigate different scenarios without the
056   ethical and privacy constraints associated with real human data. Most importantly, it can overcome
057   the challenges of limited data, often confined within mental health institutions, allowing researchers
058   to experiment with data-intensive machine-learning techniques that would be impractical with only
059   human-derived data.
060
      We focus on applying synthetic data to understand attachment styles. Attachment styles delineate
061   how people form and maintain emotional bonds with others, primarily developed through early
062   relationships with caregivers. They are a fundamental aspect of human psychological development,
063   influencing behaviors and interactions throughout one’s life and making them a significant indicator
064   of potential psychiatric traits. For instance, understanding an individual’s attachment style can help
065   predict various mental health outcomes, such as susceptibility to anxiety, depression, and personality
066   disorders (Gerber, 2005; Mikulincer & Shaver, 2012). Consequently, attachment style serves as
067   a practical basis for both assessing risk factors and tailoring interventions in mental health care,
068   enhancing the precision and effectiveness of psychiatric treatments.
069   A common psychiatric practice for assessing attachment style in adults is the Adult Attachment
070   Interview (AAI). It is a structured interview in which individuals reflect on their childhood experiences
071   and relationships with caregivers, allowing researchers to categorize their attachment style based
072   on their responses. In this work, we create artificial agents powered by State-of-the-Art LLMs to
073   simulate human participants undergoing AAIs. We then use the synthetic transcripts from these
074   dialogues to train machine learning models and test their predictive performance on a separate set of
075
      real human transcripts from humans undergoing the same interview protocol, which mental health
      professionals have already assessed for attachment style. Our results indicate that training a model
076
      with synthetic transcripts can effectively predict the attachment styles of real individuals using three
077
      different classifiers. Moreover, qualitative analysis shows that with a straightforward correction,
078   embeddings used to encode synthetic transcripts form clusters that seem to align with attachment
079   styles in real humans.
080
081
      It is important to mention that the challenges in the mental health space extend beyond predicting
      attachment styles. They apply to most tasks aimed at predicting aspects of human behavior. We
082
      utilize synthetic data to explore the prediction of attachment styles as an initial proof of concept,
083
      demonstrating that similar approaches can benefit from the findings here in scenarios beyond the
084   attachment theory domain where human data is scarce or of limited access.
085
086   Our key contributions include: 1) Developing an agent architecture that simulates humans with
087
      different user profiles and childhood memories; 2) Demonstrating that training models with data from
      these artificial agents is predictive of attachment styles in real humans, as evidenced by fitting three
088
      different traditional classifiers with synthetic data; 3) Showing that unlabeled data can be employed
089
      to standardize the raw embeddings of synthetic data, creating a more uniform representation between
090   synthetic and human data thereby improving prediction performance.

094   2    R ELATED WORK
095
096   Previous studies have utilized generative AI and LLMs in healthcare to enhance data management,
097   information retrieval, decision-making processes, and synthetic data generation (Yu et al., 2023;
098   Nassiri & Akhloufi, 2024; Magister et al., 2023). For example, the research by Tang et al. (2023)
099   employs few-shot learning to create labeled datasets for biological named entity recognition and
100   relation extraction. This method addresses privacy concerns by circumventing the need to upload
101   sensitive patient data for text mining directly.
102   LLMs have also been applied in synthetic data generation for tasks such as mathematical reasoning
103   (Saxton et al., 2019; Lozhkov et al., 2024), and image captioning (Hammoud et al., 2024), among
104   others. In the mental health space, much of the focus has been on developing chatbots that facilitate
105   health communication, with evaluations primarily concerning the ability of LLMs to deliver high-
106   quality, empathetic responses to patient queries (Ayers et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Cabrera et al.,
107   2023; Cai et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023). However, there is a growing body of research that employs
      LLMs for synthetic data generation to explore various aspects of human behavior (Jiang et al., 2024;

      Serapio-García et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) and mental conditions such as depression (Mori et al.,
109   2024; Chen et al., 2023; Lan et al., 2024).
110
111   To our knowledge, there has been no use of LLMs to study adult attachment style in real humans,
112
      with prior related efforts focusing on analyzing attachment style using real data sources such as EEG

117   memories. We use these agents to generate synthetic transcripts, which we then use to train models

      that predict attachment styles in real humans, thereby extending the use of LLMs into the domain of
      psychological research and attachment theory.

121   3    ATTACHMENT STYLE
122
123   Attachment theory (Ainsworth & Bowlby, 1991) provides a framework to understand the dynamics
124   of interpersonal relationships, focusing on the emotional bonds between individuals. Central to this
125   theory is the concept of attachment styles, which are patterns of relating to others. These patterns are
126   formed early in life and extended into adulthood. A typical categorization comprises four primary
127   types: avoidant, secure, anxious, and fearful-avoidant. Here, we focus on an alternative traditional
128   categorization comprised of three classes: avoidant, secure, and preoccupied behavior (Ainsworth
129   et al., 1978).
130   Secure attachment is characterized by a positive view of oneself and others, leading to healthy,
131   trusting relationships. Avoidant attachment is marked by discomfort with closeness and a tendency to
132   maintain emotional distance. Preoccupied attachment involves hypersensitivity to separation and an
133   intense fear of abandonment (Ainsworth et al., 1978).
134
      An Adult Attachment Interview (AAI) is a standard tool to assess an individual’s attachment style
135   in adulthood. The interviews follow a structured protocol that encourages people to reflect on their
136   childhood experiences and perceptions of parental relationships (George et al., 1985). Psychologists
137   can classify a person’s attachment style through careful analysis of the responses, particularly the
138   coherence and emotional content. The AAI has been instrumental in linking adult attachment styles
139   with early parental interactions and predicting behavior patterns in adult relationships (Hazan &
140   Shaver, 2017).
141   Recognizing someone’s attachment style can be advantageous in the context of mental health treat-
142   ment, for example, as it can help therapists understand the root of relational difficulties and emotional
143   disturbances that may arise in therapy. Knowing that a patient has an avoidant attachment style can
144   alert a therapist to the potential challenges in establishing a close therapeutic relationship. It can also
145   help with the development of therapies that are more tailored to the individual’s needs.
146
      However, predicting someone’s attachment style presents several challenges, primarily because attach-
147   ment styles are complex and influenced by many factors. In particular, attachment styles can change
148   over time due to new relationships, life experiences, or personal growth. Additionally, people may
149   not always have accurate self-awareness or may present themselves in a biased way, unintentionally
150   or intentionally. Lastly, attachment behaviors can sometimes be similar to or influenced by other
151   psychological conditions like anxiety, depression, or personality disorders. This overlap can make
152   it difficult to discern whether attachment issues or other psychological challenges drive specific
153   behaviors.

235   answering the question. The choice of embedding model was arbitrary. One could employ better

237   retrieve memories compatible with the questions asked (e.g., retrieval of memories about the parents
238   when the question involved the father or mother).
239
      We created 60 artificial agents with unique profiles and 10 associated childhood memories each.
240
      These agents engage in simulated interviews with an interviewer agent, taking turns to chat. The
241   interviewer follows the Adult Attachment Interview (AAI) protocol, utilizing a predefined list of
242   questions, which ensures a structured and consistent inquiry. We present the complete list of questions
243   in Appendix C.
244
      During an interview, each message received by an interviewee agent is passed to its “brain”. This
245
      component then updates its context with the interviewer’s question and prompts the memory module
246
      to refresh the agent’s working memory based on this context. This involves retrieving relevant
247   childhood memories from the vector store, using the current chat context as a query, and ranking
248   based on the associated retrieval score.
249
250
      These selected memories are then loaded into the agent’s working memory and included in the
      prompt used to generate the next response. This prompt also contains the agent’s profile and specific
251
      instructions to reflect an attachment style, detailed in Appendix D.

255   temperature of 0.5 on the intuition that we do not want the responses to be the same (temperature
256
      0), and we want to avoid responses that are too far from the context of the interview question due to
      potential hallucination (high temperature).

      We generate synthetic data by making API calls to private LLMs. Predictive analysis on the generated

311   Our experiments use the scikit-learn 1.4.2 implementation of logistic regression, extra trees, and

470   7             L IMITATIONS
471
472   We found that synthetic data could predict human attachment styles effectively. However, performance
473   is limited by how easy it is to classify synthetic samples, so alternative ways to create more ambiguous
474   responses that better resemble human behavior are warranted. Also, the scarcity of labeled human
475   interviews may have led to underestimations in predictions from models trained on human data. We
476   did not formally evaluate how closely synthetic responses mimic human ones, nor did we explore
477   using open-source models or fine-tuning techniques, which might yield more realistic responses.
478
      Additionally, we did not thoroughly investigate other aspects of human behavior to see how broadly
      this approach could be applied.

485   conversational data generated by large language models (LLMs). This finding could help clinicians
      better understand patients’ interpersonal issues, enabling personalized treatment. However, employing
