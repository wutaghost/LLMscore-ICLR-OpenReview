009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Clustering is widely used in biomedical research for meaningful subgroup identi-
012            fication. However, most existing clustering algorithms do not account for the sta-
013            tistical uncertainty of the resulting clusters and consequently may generate spu-
014            rious clusters due to natural sampling variation. To address this problem, the
015            Statistical Significance of Clustering (SigClust) method was developed to eval-
016            uate significance of clusters in high-dimensional data. While SigClust has been
017            successful in testing mixtures of continuous distributions, it is not specifically de-
018
               signed for discrete distributions, such as count data in genomics. Moreover, Sig-
               Clust and its variations often suffer from reduced statistical power when applied to
019
               non-Gaussian high-dimensional data. To overcome these limitations, we propose
020
               SigClust-DEV, a method designed to evaluate the significance of clusters in count
021            data. Through extensive simulations, we compare SigClust-DEV against other ex-
022            isting SigClust approaches across various count distributions and demonstrate its
023            superior performance. Furthermore, we apply our method SigClust-DEV to Hydra
024            single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA) data and electronic health records (EHRs)
025            of cancer patients to identify meaningful latent cell types and patient subgroups,
026            respectively.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Clustering is a powerful unsupervised statistical tool, widely applied in biomedical research to better
032   understand complex data. For instance, it has been used to annotate and discover cell types from
033
      scRNA data (Butler et al., 2018) and to identify cancer subtypes (TCGA et al., 2012). Specifically,
      when a clustering algorithm assigns a single known population to multiple clusters, it may indicate
034
      that the population is heterogeneous and contains underlying subgroups. However, an important
035
      question remains: Are the clustering results meaningful or pure artifacts of random sampling? Most
036   current clustering workflows do not address this important issue.
037
038   In the literature, popular pipelines for clustering high-dimensional biomedical data typically fol-
039
      low these steps (Waltman & Van Eck, 2013): (1) Applying dimension reduction methods such as
      Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to the data, (2) Implementing clustering algorithms such as
040
      k-nearest neighbors, hierarchical clustering, or k-means (Macqueen, 1967) on the resulting princi-
041
      pal components (PCs), and (3) Deciding the number of clusters by thresholding distances across
042   clusters. The final number of clusters can also be determined through manual inspection of cluster
043   stability or variance reduction (Peyvandipour et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2021). However, these ap-
044   proaches are prone to create spurious clusters even in simple settings. Taking k-means clustering
045   as an example, the algorithm can separate data drawn from a one-dimensional Gaussian distribution
046   to distinct clusters with large between-cluster distance. A two-sample t-test also gives significant
047   p-value between clusters, suggesting that they are different from each other. However, in many ap-
048   plications, it is not desirable to divide data of a single normal distribution into several clusters, which
049   may cause false discoveries of biomedical subtypes.
050   To address this challenge, Liu et al. (2008) proposed a Monte Carlo-based statistical significance
051   method, SigClust, to evaluate clustering results in high-dimensional data. A key contribution of
052   their work was the careful consideration of the definition of clusters. Specifically, SigClust adopts
053   a probabilistic approach, assuming that multiple clusters represent a mixture of Gaussian distribu-
      tions, while a single cluster that cannot be further divided approximates a Gaussian distribution. A


                                                          1

054
      critical step in SigClust involves assessing the separation of clusters by treating the collected data
055   as if it were drawn from a simple Gaussian distribution (i.e., the null distribution), using a Monte
056   Carlo approach. To evaluate the separation of clusters, the SigClust uses cluster index (CI), the ratio
057   of within-cluster variation over the total variation, as the test statistics. Then, SigClust compares
058   the CI under the null distribution against that of the observed data to obtain a p-value. This allows
059   for a formal statistical test to determine whether the underlying distribution of the data can be rea-
060   sonably approximated by a single Gaussian distribution, i.e., one cluster. If the observed CI is not
061   significantly different from the CI distribution under the null distribution, the SigClust concludes
062   over-clustering and that the data should not be divided into subgroups. There are several exten-
063
      sions of the original SigClust. Huang et al. (2015) improved the estimation of the null distribution
      by introducing soft-thresholding of the covariance matrix estimation. Kimes et al. (2017) extended
064
      SigClust to hierarchical clustering, and Grabski et al. (2023) further adapted it for scRNA data. Most
065
      recently, Shen et al. (2024) generalized SigClust to clustering in the reduced multi-dimensional scal-
066   ing (MDS) space.
067
068   Since the SigClust was introduced by Liu et al. (2008), it has been widely applied in various biomed-
069
      ical research problems, including cancer subtype identification (TCGA et al., 2012; Agrawal et al.,
      2014), cell type discovery (Boldog et al., 2018), and gene expression network analysis (Lee et al.,
070
      2021; Garcia-Recio et al., 2023) However, the significance of clustering for count data has not been
071
      thoroughly established. Recently, Grabski et al. (2023) adapted SigClust for scRNA counts by
072   replacing the Gaussian null distribution in the original SigClust with the Poisson log-multivariate
073   normal (log-MVN) distribution (Aitchison & Ho, 1989). Although this approach has successfully
074   refined cell-type annotations, the presumed null distribution is difficult to estimate due to the high-
075   dimensional nature of such data, leading to potential low power issues. Furthermore, this method
076   is specifically designed for scRNA data and is not easily generalizable to other types of count data,
077   such as binary data. Unlike the multivariate Gaussian distribution, the estimation of count data
078   in high-dimensional spaces is often challenging, further complicating the original SigClust frame-
079   work. Other methods for assessing clustering significance, including previous works of McShane
080
      et al. (2002), Maitra et al. (2012), Chakravarti et al. (2019), Chen & Witten (2023), and Gao et al.
      (2024) are not specifically designed for count data.
081
082   In this article, we propose a novel SigClust workflow for high-dimensional count data using the
083   deviance-based PCA (DEV-PCA) space (Townes et al., 2019), namely SigClust-DEV. Deviance-
084   based PCA is a powerful tool within generalized PCA approaches (Collins et al., 2002; Lee et al.,
085   2010; Landgraf & Lee, 2020a;b), which are nonlinear dimension reduction methods that project data
086
      from the exponential family into the natural parameter space. The core idea of generalized PCA is
      to preserve the structure of heterogeneous natural parameters, which aligns well with the mixture
087
      definition of clusters used in SigClust. As mentioned earlier, current workflows for clustering count
088
      data rarely apply clustering algorithms directly on the discrete distribution space. Instead, they first
089   project the count data into a latent space before performing clustering. Inspired by the work of
090   Shen et al. (2024), which performed SigClust in the latent space from MDS, we extend SigClust to
091   the latent space for count distributions. To improve the robustness of SigClust in latent spaces, we
092   utilize the relative goodness of fit as the test statistics for SigClust-DEV (Chakravarti et al., 2019).
093
      The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we introduce the related methods and de-
094
      scribe the details of the proposed SigClust-DEV. Next, we investigate the performance of SigClust-
095   DEV through comprehensive numerical experiments in Section 3. Then we apply SigClust-DEV
096   to investigate two high-dimensional biomedical data: the scRNA data for Hydra stem cells (Siebert
097   et al., 2019) and the medical records for cancer patients in Section 4. We conclude the article with
098   some discussions in Section 5.

139   A critical challenge of the original SigClust is that the null covariance Σ̂n may not be accurately
140   estimated under the high-dimensional settings. Although hard-thresholding and soft-thresholding
141   has been used to improve the estimate of covariance matrix (Liu et al., 2008; Huang et al., 2015),

143

144

145   MDS-space preserves the pairwise distance of the original data, the clustering structure can also be
146   reserved (Abbe et al., 2022; Little et al., 2023). Therefore, after performing dimension reduction,
147   results from both SigClust and distance-based clustering algorithms still align with those in the orig-
148   inal data space. In practice, MDS finds a low-dimensional representation Y of a high-dimensional
149   matrix X by minimizing the following reconstruction error:

      2.2   G ENERALIZED PCA FOR E XPONENTIAL FAMILY
163
164   To obtain the representation of PCA for more general distributions, especially for count data, it
165   is desirable to extend the standard PCA from the Gaussian distribution to the exponential family,
166   analogous to the generalization of linear models to generalized linear models (GLM). Exponential
167   family includes a large variaty of discrete distributions, including Binomial distribution, Poisson dis-
168   tribution, and Multinomial distribution, sufficient for modeling common count data for biomedical
169   research.
170   Using the probabilistic interpretation of PCA, Collins et al. (2002) developed generalized PCA.
171   Similar to the development of GLM, generalized PCA maximizes the likelihood of exponential
172   family,
173

      with constraint that requires the natural parameter to be embeded in a low-dimensional space, i.e.,
176
      θij = µj + uTi vj . In other words, instead of preserving the pairwise Euclidean distance of the
177   original data X, generalized PCA approximates the canonical parameter matrix Θ = [θij ]n×p by
178   the matrix 1n µT + UVT from the linear subspace of Rq , where U and V are n × q and p × q
179   matrices. Returning to the problem of clustering, if the data follow a single count distribution in the
180   exponential family, the natural parameter lies in a one-dimensional latent space, i.e., Θ = 1n µT .
181   Hence, the principal components U simply represent the noisy residuals of the natural parameters,
182   which do not exhibit any cluster pattern by definition. In contrast, if the data follow a mixture of
183   count distributions, the principal components U will learn the latent structure of the data and be well
184   separated across clusters.
185   The optimization problem of generalized PCA (2) is usually challenging and may lead to unstable
186   results (Lee et al., 2010; Townes et al., 2019; Landgraf & Lee, 2020b). Due to its computational

188   PCA first partially optimizes µ by fitting a GLM to each column of X. Specifically, assuming that
189   each column of data follows a distribution from the exponential family with unknown parameters

201   2.3   C LUSTER S IGNIFICANCE FOR C OUNT DATA
202
      To assess the clustering signifiance for count data, an intuitive approach is to replace the assump-
203
      tion on P0 from a single Gaussian distribution with a specific count distribution, such as Poisson
204   distribution, and then replace the clustering algorithm from k-means to the generalized PCA-based
205   clustering. For example, scSHC proposed by Grabski et al. (2023) assumes that the null distribu-
206   tion is a Poisson log-MVN distribution for scRNA data and uses generalized PCA for clustering.
207   However, as mentioned above, estimation of the null distribution, especially for the covariance ma-
208   trix, may be inaccurate due to the high-dimensionality. Moreover, the Poisson log-MVN model is
209   too restrictive for scRNA data and not applicable for more general count distributions. In addition,
210   parametrization of general high-dimensional count data is also challenging.
211   To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach to compute the significance of clusters for
212   distributions from the exponential family, including Gaussian, Poisson, and Binomial distributions.
213   Instead of directly estimating the multivariate count distribution as proposed by Grabski et al. (2023),
214   we propose to first map the original data into a moderate dimensional latent space by generalized
215   PCA (denoted as Z), and then calculate the significance of clusters on the latent space using MDS-
      based SigClust (detailed in Appendix A, also denoting the data in the MDS-space as Y).

      Chakravarti et al. (2019) pointed out that the usage of CI in the original SigClust only focuses on
217   the null distribution. As a result, the test may not be very powerful in certain situations. Therefore,
218   we utilize the relative goodness of fit as the test statistic. The basic idea is to test whether a mixture
219   of Gaussian distributions better fits the data than a single Gaussian distribution. If the data are not
220   Gaussian but with no clusters, the null distribution of CI will be affected. In contrast, in terms of
221   relative goodness of fit, a single Gaussian distribution can be a better fit than a mixture of Gaus-
222   sian distributions when there are no clusters. Specifically, we compare the Kullback-Leibler (KL)
223   divergence of the latent data distribution P and a single Gaussian distribution P̂0 fitted on the data
224   against a Gaussian mixture model α̂P̂1 + (1 − α̂)P̂2 fitted on the data. The test is to see whether
225   H0 : T := DKL (P ||P̂0 ) − DKL (P ||α̂P̂1 + (1 − α̂)P̂2 ) ≤ 0. Under the null hypothesis, the latent
226   data distribution can be better approximated by a single Gaussian distribution than a Gaussian mix-
227   ture model, therefore T < 0. Conversely, under the alternative assumption, the latent distribution
228   may be better approximated by a mixture of Gaussian distributions, hence T > 0. To estimate T ,
229   the following test statistics can be used:

233   where Yi is the i-th observation in the low-dimensional latent space. The null distribution P̂0 is
234   estimated using an overall Gaussian fit, and the alternative distributions P̂1 and P̂2 are fitted by the
235   observations assigned to each cluster using separate Gaussian fits. Note that T̄ is asymptotically
236   normal conditioned on α̂, P̂0 , P̂1 , and P̂2 . Therefore, in practice, we adopt a cross-fitting strategy
237   by fitting the Gaussian mixtures on part of the observations and computing T̄ on the rest of the data.
238   In this way, a formal statistical test can be derived. Another approach is to use nonparametric tests,
239   such as sign test, to evaluate if the median of Ti ’s is larger than 0. Hence, the asymptotic normality

      we use the nonparametric test to derive p-values. As a remark, we would like to point out that
      the relative goodness of fit is not suitable for high-dimensional data due to the need of estimating

246   1. Set the dimension of generalized/deviance PCA space s and the dimension of MDS space t.

251   6. Perform sign test or two-sample t-test to test if T < 0.

257   simulation studies with observations generated from Bernoulli, Poisson, Poisson log-MVN, and

      by one of the above distributions with fixed parameters, while the alternative distribution includes

306   Apart from its strong performance in the aforementioned distributions, SigClust-DEV is more pow-
307   erful when the data distribution deviates from the exponential family, as seen in genomic datasets.
308   Following Grabski et al. (2023), we assess the performance of SigClust-DEV for cell-type anno-
309
      tation using scRNA data modeled by Poisson log-MVN models. As shown in Figure 2, classical
      SigClust methods, which assume the data follow a Gaussian mixture, fail to provide reliable results.
310
      Specifically, SigClust-MDS does not maintain the correct statistical size under the null hypothesis,
311
      while SigClust-Soft and SigClust-Hard fail to reject the null under the alternative hypothesis. In con-
312   trast, SigClust-DEV and scSHC effectively detect false clusters, reducing the risk of over-clustering.
313   However, scSHC exhibits limited power under the alternative hypothesis, becoming overly conser-
314   vative when the sample size is small (n = 100) or the differences between clusters are modest
315   (a = 0.4). Compared to SigClust-DEV, scSHC may miss novel cell types in biomedical research.
316
      The multinomial distribution has also been used to model scRNA data from multiple libraries
317
      (Townes et al., 2019). Accordingly, we simulate unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) for one or
318   two cell types across two libraries using a multinomial distribution. Figure 3 illustrates the per-
319   formance of different SigClust methods in this scenario. Classical SigClust methods are misled
320   by library effects, leading to false identification of new cell types when only one true cell type is
321   present. Although scSHC is designed for multi-batch scRNA counts, it struggles to differentiate
322   two cell types, sometimes missing novel cell types. This limited power highlights the constraints
323   of scSHC’s strong parametric assumptions. In contrast, SigClust-DEV effectively mitigates over-
      clustering while retaining its ability to discover new populations.

      4.1   A NALYZING STEM CELL DIFFERENTIATION IN H YDRA
384
385   Hydra Data Description The cnidarian polyp Hydra continuously self-renews and can regenerate
386   its entire body from a small fragment of tissue. To investigate the molecular diversity of Hydra cells
387   and the underlying transcriptional programs, Siebert et al. (2019) generated 24,985 Hydra single-cell
388   transcriptomes from six libraries. Four libraries were generated using the original Drop-seq beads,
389   while the other two libraries were generated using R&D beads. Specifically, Hydra consists of
390   three cell lineages – endodermal epithelial, ectodermal epithelial, and interstitial – and each lineage
391   is supported by its own stem cell population (Bosch et al., 2010). Epithelial stem cells further
392   differentiate to build the foot (basal disk and peduncle) at the aboral end, body column, and the
393
      hypostome and tentacles at the oral end. Gene expression in these cells is constantly changing based
      on their positional context.
394
395   In this study, we investigate the differential gene expression of epithelial stem cells in Hydra with re-
396   spect to their positions. Siebert et al. (2019) classified epithelial cells into six sub-populations: basal
397   disk, peduncle, body column, hypostome, tentacles, and battery cells. However, their clustering
398   results using Seurat (Butler et al., 2018) may not fully capture the biological distinctions between
399
      these populations. To address this, we need to answer the following questions:
400
            1. Is the distribution of gene expression within a pre-identified cluster homogeneous?
401
402         2. Are the gene expression profiles of stem cells from the identified clusters significantly
403
               different from one another, and can any of these clusters be merged?
404
      Specifically, we focus on the ectodermal cell lineage, which have two major libraries coded as 02
405
      and 11 and five cell clusters of body column, peduncle, head/hypostome, battery cell, and basal disk.
406   To evaluate the performance of SigClust-DEV and other comparison methods, we apply them for
407   cells (i) from one single cluster, (ii) from two clusters. It is expected that SigClust produces large p-
408   values for (i) and produces small p-values for (ii). Furthermore, since SigClust-Hard, SigClust-Soft,
409   and SigClust-MDS do not account for the batch effect, for fair comparison, we also implemented
410   these methods for cells in each library. Remarkably, body columns and battery cells are manually
411   merged from multiple clusters, while the other clusters are from the output of clustering algorithm
412   in Seurat (Butler et al., 2018).
413
414   Results for Hydra Data Overall, SigClust-DEV and scSHC produce the most reasonable p-
415   values. For the first question, we examine the p-values for cells from a single cell type (see Figure
416   4). SigClust-DEV and scSHC identify most of the annotated clusters coming from a unimodal count
417
      distribution except for battery cells, well-aligned with results in Siebert et al. (2019). On the other
      hand, although the original results merge two subclusters of battery cells, their gene expressions
418
      are evidently heterogeneous across the subclusters in Siebert et al. (2019)’s visualization using t-
419
      distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE, Van der Maaten & Hinton (2008)). In contrast,
420   SigClust-Soft, SigClust-MDS, and SigClust-Hard fail to preserve the size of testing and keeps re-
421   jecting the null hypothesis due to the batch effect. On the other hand, the significance of clustering
422   seems to be inconsistent across libraries. For cells in library 11, SigClust-Soft and SigClust-Hard
423   identify body column, peduncle, and basal disk as a single cluster, while suggesting battery cells
424   and hypostome exist meaningful subgroups. However. for cells in library 2, they keep rejecting the
425   null hypotheses for all cell types.
426   For the second question, we examine the clustering significance p-values for cells from multiple
427   cell types (see Figure 4). The results of SigClust-DEV show that all mixtures of cell types are
428   significantly separated, which well aligns with the manual annotations. In contrast, scSHC merges
429   three cell types into one: body column, peduncle, and hypostome. Although we highlight that the
430   biological groundtruth may be slightly different from the annotations, it is important to note that
431   the molecular difference between Hydra’s body column and head has been widely observed for a
      long time (Holstein et al., 1991). Therefore, the results from scSHC may be an artifact of its power

      4.2   L EARNING LATENT MEDICAL GROUP STRUCTURE USING EHR
487
488   EHR Data Description The dataset consists of medical histories from 7,284 head-and-neck can-
489   cer patients from a university hospital. After excluding patients with no records of using chemother-
490   apy drugs or antibiotics, we are left with 2,203 patients and 973 different types of medications. This
491   dataset, as a 2,203 by 973 matrix, allows for the comparison of the effectiveness of various treat-
492   ments for head-and-neck cancer. However, the large number of medications presents a significant
493   challenge. To address this, we propose applying hierarchical clustering and SigClust-DEV to this
494
      data matrix of medicine counts, to identify latent treatment patterns, i.e., subgroups of patients using
      similar medications.
495
496
      Results for EHR Data The combination of hierarchical clustering and SigClust-DEV reveals dis-
497
      tinct patterns in drug usage among head-and-neck cancer patients (Figure 5a). Notably, it preserves
498   patient subgroups stratified by the use of chemotherapy drugs and antibiotics (Figures 5b, c). Pa-
499   tients with less severe tumors, who do not undergo chemotherapy, are grouped into clusters 1, 8,
500   and 9 in Figure 5a, highlighting the heterogeneity in drug usage among patients with benign tu-
501   mors. Interestingly, the primary distinction between cluster 1 and clusters 8/9 appears to be the
502   use of antibiotics, such as Clindamycin. In contrast, most patients undergoing chemotherapy are
503   administered cisplatin, which corresponds to clusters 2, 4, 6, and 7. For patients that cisplatin is un-
504   suitable, alternative treatments—such as cetuximab, carboplatin, and paclitaxel—are typically used
505   in combination, and these correspond to cluster 3.
506
507   5     D ISCUSSION
508
509   In this article, we propose a novel deviance-based SigClust method for testing the statistical sig-
510   nificance of clustering for high-dimensional count data. Through learning the representation of the
511   natural parameters of the count, our method avoids the estimation of the high-dimensional covari-
512   ance matrix as in the original SigClust. Furthermore, to relax the requirement for Gaussian latent
513
      space, we test the relative goodness of fit between a single Gaussian distribution and Gaussian mix-
      tures. This extension of SigClust makes it more broadly applicable in biomedical research.
514
515   There are several open questions for future research. Although we demonstrate the effectiveness
516   of SigClust-DEV empirically, an interesting direction is to derive the theoretical conditions for the
517   latent space properties. For instance, we observe that dimension reduction approaches like t-SNE
518   can create spurious clusters by separating data from one single distribution, therefore their latent
519
      space is not suitable for SigClust. Another direction is to combine SigClust-DEV with hierarchical
      clustering to obtain more structured subgroup identification.

622   CI-Based SigClust on Generalized PCA Space To demonstrate the sensitivity of CI-based Sig-
623   Clust to its Gaussian assumption, we also implemented Algorithm 2. The dimension reduction step
624   is the same as SigClust-DEV, while the test statistic is replaced by CI.
625
626   Algorithm 2 CI-based SigClust on the Generalized/Deviance PCA Space (SigClustCI-DEV)
627   1. Set the dimension of generalized/deviance PCA space s and the dimension of MDS space t.
628   2. Obtain the latent variables Z = [z1 , ..., zs ] by solving (2) or (3).
629   3. Obtain the MDS matrix Y = [y1 , ..., yt ] from the dissimilarity matrix D of Z by solving (1).
630   4. Implement the k-means clustering on Y and compute the CI.
631   5. Estimate the sample covariance Σ̂Y of Y. Generate samples from N (0, Σ̂Y ).
632   6. Perform step 2-5 on the generated samples.
633   7. Repeat step 5 and 6 for Nsim times. Obtain the empirical distribution of CIs.
634
      8. Compute the p-value using the empirical distribution.

      B.1   DATA G ENERATION M ODEL
683
684   Bernoulli Distribution Under the null hypothesis, the data were generated from a Bernoulli dis-
685   tribution Binomial(1, pd ), where pd was sampled from Ud (0, 1). Under the alternative hypothesis,
686   half of the observations are generated the same way as under the null hypothesis. For the remain-
687   ing half of the observations, 10% of the elements in pd were resampled from a different uniform
688   distribution U100 (0, 1), introducing the cluster structure.
689
690   Poisson Distribution Single multivariate Poisson distributions Poisson(λd ) and mixtures of two
691
      Poisson distributions were generated for the null hypothesis and alternative hypothesis, respectively.
      Specifically, under the null distribution, λd was sampled from the exponential of N (0, Id×d ) and
692
      fixed across samples. Under the null hypothesis, half of the observations are generated the same
693
      way as under the null hypothesis. For the remaining half of the observations, 10% of elements from
694   λd was further multiplied by exponential of N (0, aI100×100 ), where a was set to {0.4, 0.8}.
695
696   Poisson Log-MVN Distribution Poisson Log-MVN distribution has been widely used to model
697   counts of scRNA sequences. Similar to the scenario of Poisson distribution, we generated the single-
698   cell counts (i) under the null hypothesis, i.e., the data followed a Poisson log-MVN(µd , σ 2 Id×d );
699   (ii) under the alternative hypothesis, i.e., the data followed a mixture of Poisson log-MVN. For
700   simplicity, we set µd = 0d , and σ 2 = 1 under the null distribution, while the cluster structure
701   under the alternative hypothesis was introduced by multiplying the 10% of the elements of µd by
      the exponential of a normal distribution N (0, aI100×100 ), where a was set to {0.4, 0.8}.

      Multinomial Distribution Under the null hypothesis, to simulate the batch effect in scRNA data,
703   we generated two multinomial distributions with equal sample sizes, denoted as Mult(zb , πd ) for
704   b = 1, 2, where b represents the batch index. The size parameters were set to z1 = 1000 and
705   z2 = 2000 for each batch, accounting for the batch effect. The probability vectors πd were generated
706   using the sigmoid(N (0d , 2Id×d )) distribution. Under the alternative hypothesis, we generated four
707   multinomial distributions with equal sample sizes, forming two batches and two clusters. The batch
708   effect remained the same as in the null hypothesis. To introduce the cluster effect, we altered 10%
709   of the elements in πd , which were generated from sigmoid(N (0, aI100×100 )) and varied between
710   clusters.

      Simulation Results for Poisson Distribution Figure 6 presents the empirical distributions of p-
714
      values under the null and alternative setting. Under the null setting, an effective test is supposed to
715
      exhibit the empirical distributions of p-values close to the diagonal line. SigClust-DEV, SigClust-
716   MDS, and scSHC performs best under all scenarios, while SigClust-Soft and SigClust-Hard present
717   inferior power in three cases.
718
719   Comparison between CI and Relative Goodness of Fit Figure 7 presents the empirical dis-
720   tributions of p-values for CI-based SigClust-DEV in simulation. While CI-based SigClust-DEV
721   successfully preserves the Type-I error in most settings, it is anti-conservative for Binary data when
722   n = 100, 500, 1000. The results align with our expectation that CI can be more sensitive to the
723   possibly non-Gaussian latent space.
724
725   Comparison between Generalized PCA space and Deviance PCA space Figure 8 presents the
726   empirical distributions of p-values for SigClust on the generalized PCA space (SigClust-GLM) in
727
      simulation. The performance of SigClust-GLM is comparable with SigClust-DEV in most cases
      with respect to statistical size and power, except for Bernoulli and Multinomial distribution when
728
      n = 100. The inflated Type-I error in such cases may be a result of the algorithm instability of
729
      generalized PCA, as we notice that generalized PCA fails to converge under such cases.
