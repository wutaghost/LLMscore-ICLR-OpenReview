009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Open Set Domain Adaptation (OSDA) faces two critical challenges: the emergence
012            of unknown classes in the target domain and changes in observed distributions
013            across domains. Although numerous studies have proposed advanced algorithms,
014            recent experimental results demonstrate that the classical Empirical Risk Mini-
015            mization (ERM) approach still delivers state-of-the-art performance. However,
016
               few theories can effectively explain this disputed phenomenon. To address the
               theoretical gap, we focus on constructing a causal theoretical framework for OSDA.
017
               We formulate the novel concepts of the Fully Informative Causal Invariance Model
018
               (FICIM) and the Partially Informative Causal Invariance Model (PICIM). Subse-
019            quently, We derive an OSDA theoretical bound to prove that the ERM performs
020            well when the source domain follows FICIM, while it performs poorly when the
021            source domain follows PICIM. The different results may be attributed to the vary-
022            ing amounts of available information when bounding the target domain’s stable
023            expected risk. Finally, across different datasets, we conduct extensive experiments
024            on the FICIM and PICIM source domains to validate the effectiveness of our
025            theoretical results.

055   2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Qu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), the performance gains have been reported

060

061   theory to understand how stable causal mechanisms1 facilitate knowledge transfer and explain why
062   algorithms like ERM succeed in some scenarios while failing in others. This framework includes
063   two models: the Fully Informative Causal Invariance Model (FICIM) and the Partially Informative
064   Causal Invariance Model (PICIM). Via distinguishing FICIM and PICIM, we can better define the
065   conditions under which domain adaptation methods are effective and derive bounds on the expected
066   risk in the target domain.
067   More technically, we define the stable expected risk with invariant connections across domains and
068   derive a theoretical bound of the stable expected risk for OSDA. Furthermore, our bound explains
069   which risk minimization strategies should be employed under which conditions. Our theory addresses
070   the theoretical performance controversy between ERM and other methods: 1) The ERM of a source
071   domain following FICIM can provide sufficient information to bound the stable expected risk of the
072   target domain; 2) The stable expected risk of the target domain cannot be bounded by the ERM of a
073   source domain following PICIM. In addition, generating source domain data that adheres to FICIM
074   is beneficial for model training or fine-tuning, especially for large language models (LLMs). We
075
      conduct extensive experiments on multiple FICIM and PICIM datasets to validate the reliability of
      our theoretical results.
076
077   The significant contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
078
079          • We propose a novel causal framework and formalize the FICIM and PICIM causal models
080            for domain adaptation. This causal framework and model can provide a solid theoretical
081            foundation for domain adaptation problems.
082          • We propose a causal bound for the OSDA. This bound can guide the development of new
083            algorithms for OSDA problems.
084          • We prove that when the source domain follows the FICIM, ERM is sufficient for model

086            instead of natural datasets for training.
087
             • Our theoretical work on domain adaptation can guide the generation of diverse and repre-
088
               sentative training datasets using LLMs, enhancing model generalization and adaptability
089            through a focus on causal relationships and data selection. Additionally, our theory can guide
090            the selection of high-quality datasets for efficient pre-training and fine-tuning of LLMs.

      2.2   DA FROM A CAUSAL VIEW
109
110   Existing studies primarily assume invariant predictors or rely on different causal assumptions to
111   address domain adaptation problems (Magliacane et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024). Although Invariant Risk
112   Minimization (IRM) (Liu et al., 2024) methods are commonly used for learning robust representations,
113   research has shown that they do not necessarily outperform ERM (Rosenfeld et al., 2021; Buchholz
114   et al., 2024). Despite some success with these methods (Chen & Bühlmann, 2021; Sun et al., 2021;
115   Liu et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024), they fail to provide a theoretical understanding of nonlinear
116   high-dimensional data, and only consider a variation of the PICIM in our work as their causal
117   structure, whereas we consider the FICIM and PICIM.

123
124   We denote Ω, A , and P as the original sample space, σ−algebra on Ω, and probability measure,
125   respectively. Then, (Ω, A , P) is a probability space. We use capital letters such as X to denote random
126   elements and boldface letters such as x to denote value vectors. Calligraphic capital letters such as X
127   are used for space. Random elements are measurable maps. for instance, X : (Ω, A ) → (X , B).
128

      conditional distributions, respectively. Moreover, p is the probability density function. For more

132   3.2   C AUSAL ASSUMPTIONS
133
134   Causality research indicates that real-world data distributions stem from underlying causal mecha-
135   nisms that are typically invariant across domains (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018; Schölkopf, 2022). Liu
136   et al. (2021) formalized this into the causal invariance principle, asserting that causal generation
137   mechanisms remain consistent across different domains. For high-dimensional data X—such as
138   text, images, or audio—it’s commonly assumed that X is a nonlinear function of latent attributes
139   A (Locatello et al., 2019; 2020; Von Kügelgen et al., 2021). However, not all attributes in A are
140
      invariant causes of X or the target label Y . Some attributes, like noise or background color, may
      affect X but not Y . Therefore, we partition A into two subsets: the causally invariant attributes C
141
      and the variation attributes V , where C maintains invariant relationships with both X and Y . We
142
      formalize this with the following assumption:
143
144   Assumption 1. (Causal invariance assumption) For high-dimensional data X and its prediction
145   target Y , the latent attribute set A between X and Y can be divided into the causally invariant
146   attribute set C and variation attribute set V . Attributes belonging to C should satisfy P(Y |C) and
147
      P(X|C) being invariant across domains. Attributes belonging to V should satisfy that P(Y |V ) or
      P(X|V ) varies across domains.
148
149
      Based on this, we define:
150
151   Definition 1. (Probability Generation Model (PGM)). We define the probability generation model
152   for high-dimensional data as certain statistical probability descriptions of the data generation
153   process, i.e., P GM = ⟨PC , PX|C , PY |C , PV |C , PY V X ⟩ on high-dimensional data X and target Y
154   with causally invariant attributes C and variation attributes V .
155
156   This assumption is supported across various fields (Liang et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2021). For example,
157   in computer vision, images from the same class share causally invariant attributes, while variation
158
      attributes provide class-independent features like color and background (Liang et al., 2018).
159   By constructing a probability model with separated latent attributes, we can better describe the data
160   generation process. For example, when high-dimensional data is collected from different sensors
161   with varying characteristics, the PGM accounts for variations by incorporating causally invariant
      attributes (e.g., physical properties) and variation attributes (e.g., sensor-specific features).

228   In OSDA, the primary goal is to train a classifier using data from a source domain that can accurately
229   identify known classes and distinguish between known and unknown classes in a target domain. We
230   focus on the challenging case of a single-source domain problem. Specifically, we consider a source
231   domain S and a target domain T from the domain set D, i.e., S, T ∈ D, satisfying the properties
232   defined in Definition 2.
233   Motivation problem. (Learning for OSDA). Given a single source domain S ∈ D and a target
234   domain T ∈ D, we observe a training dataset DS = {(xi , yi )}N     i=1 that is obtained from S and the

235   label space YS ⊂ YT is known; that is, the testing samples in the target domain belong to unknown
236   classes that do not appear in the source domain. The goal is to identify a known class label and to

447    5.3                        R ESTAURANT R EVIEWS DATA
448
449   Experimental Setup. We use the CEBaB dataset (Abraham et al., 2022), which consists of short
450
      restaurant reviews and ratings from OpenTable3 , including evaluations for food, service, noise,
      ambiance, and an overall rating. We used the train-exclusive split of the dataset, which contains
451
      1, 755 examples. We focus on an experimental setup: a modified version called CeBAB-Spurious,
452
      where there is a spurious correlation between the labels Y and variable attributes V .
453
454   To construct CeBAB-Spurious, we leveraged the availability of both the original and perceived ratings
455   for each review in CeBAB. The original rating represents the reviewer’s initial thoughts when writing
456
      the review, while the perceived rating indicates whether the review contains information about various
      restaurant attributes (e.g., food, service, noise, ambiance) and their associated sentiment. We utilized
457
      this unique data structure to capture reviewers’ writing styles. Some reviewers are concise and
458
      provide limited descriptions, while others are more detailed and include more information. Inspired
459   by existing research (Feder et al., 2023), we introduced a new attribute called food-mention to signify
460   the presence of food-related information in a review. If the perceived food rating is either negative or
461   positive, we assign a value of 1 to the food-mention attribute; otherwise, it is set to 0. We sample
462   the data such that the correlation between food-mention and outcomes is 0.45. Please note that
463   the sampled data follows the PICIM, while the data from counterfactual interventions using GPT-4
464   (Achiam et al., 2023) follows the FICIM. For detailed experimental setup, results, and analysis, please
465   refer to Appendix D.3.
466    Results. As shown in Table 3 and Table 4, when debiasing different restaurant features, our theoretical
467    results effectively explain the model’s performance differences under various data generation mecha-
468    nisms. The main conclusions include the following two points: (1) Based on common knowledge,
469    restaurant noise has a causal relationship with overall restaurant ratings. In this case, when we debias
470    for restaurant noise, the model is unable to leverage these useful causal signals, leading to an increase
471    in the minimum stable expected risk. Essentially, by removing the noise, the model can no longer
472    capture the useful information embedded in it, resulting in reduced stability in its predictions. (2)
473
       Food mention is a spurious feature, meaning it has no direct causal relationship with restaurant
       ratings. By debiasing for food mentions, the model eliminates the influence of irrelevant, spurious
474
       correlations. This helps improve the model’s performance, as it can focus more on the true causal
475
       signals relevant to the task, without being distracted by unrelated features.

       6                      C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
       We have proposed a causal bound for OSDA of high-dimensional data. Using this bound, we theoreti-
       cally proved that the FICIM and PICIM source domains can explain the performance difference of
532
       ERM: (1) The ERM when the source domain follows FICIM can provide sufficient information to
533
       bound the stable expected risk of the target domain. (2) The ERM when the source domain follows
534    PICIM cannot bound the stable expected risk of the target domain. We demonstrated the effectiveness
535    of our theoretical results by conducting comparative experiments on FICIM and PICIM datasets,
536    and showed that state-of-art open-set algorithms performed poorly when only the PICIM dataset
537    was used. Our theoretical and experimental results revealed the limitation of existing algorithms for
538    OSDA, including OSR. We anticipate that our study may pave the way for new algorithm designs for
539    OSDA and other simpler domain adaptation challenges, as it provides fundamental knowledge for
       these problems.

      B     R ELATED WORK
799
800
      In this section, we first introduce OSDA and OSR theories. Then, we review domain adaptation from
801   a causal view.
802
803
      B.1   OSDA AND OSR THEORIES
804
805   Our research problem is within the field of OSDA. A similar concept related to OSDA is OSR (Geng
806   et al., 2021). Hence we refer readers to (Geng et al., 2021) for comprehensive surveys of OSDA and
807   OSR. Early theoretical studies on OSR formalized the relationship between the known and unknown
808   classes using the open space risk (Scheirer et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2023; Rastegar et al., 2024)
809   and extreme value theory (Rudd et al., 2017; Petit et al., 2023), but they did not provide theoretical
      guarantees.


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Liu et al. (2018) provided the sample complexity for guaranteeing the detection rate of OSR, whereas
811   Fang et al. (2021) proposed a generalization bound for OSR based on the PAC theory, which
812   demonstrated the theoretical existence of an OSR algorithm. Zhang et al. (2020) constructed
813   an unbiased risk estimator by exploiting unlabeled training data to approximate the underlying
814   distribution of the unknown classes. These bounds provided by (Liu et al., 2018; Fang et al., 2021;
815   Zhang et al., 2020) highlight the need to find an augmented domain to represent a group of novel
816   classes.
817   The class-incremental domain adaptation paradigm proposed by (Kundu et al., 2020), which is almost
818   the same as the OSDA, derived a bound for the target-domain risk by considering the target-shared
819   risks and target-private risks independently. Fang et al. (2020) proposed a theoretical bound for the
820   OSDA problem first. However, these bounds in (Kundu et al., 2020) and (Fang et al., 2020) can not
821   explain why or when ERM performs well for OSDA.
822
      Moreover, none of the above-mentioned works can solve our problem because they need a strict
823   assumption that at least one observed distribution does not change across domains.
824
825
826   B.2   DA FROM A CAUSAL VIEW
827
828   The framework of Structural Causal Models (SCMs) (Pearl, 2009) has motivated many interesting
829   works on causal discovery and causal inference. Inspired by SCMs, our work mainly uses the principle
830
      of Invariant Causal Prediction (ICP). ICP considers the invariance of the conditional distribution of
      the target variable Y given its direct causes, which has been articulated numerous times (Pearl, 2009;
831
      Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018; Peters et al., 2016; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Pfister et al., 2019) and
832
      has been formulated by (Peters et al., 2016; Heinze-Deml et al., 2018). Rojas-Carulla et al. (2018);
833   Magliacane et al. (2018); Li et al. (2024) relate domain adaptation with the invariant causal prediction
834   principle, which inspires our work. These works mentioned above mostly assume that the observed
835   predictors or a subset of observed predictors are causally invariant.
836
      Although IRM (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024) methods aim to learn robust and invariant
837
      representations, recent studies have shown that they may not always outperform the ERM objective
838
      (Nagarajan et al., 2020; Rosenfeld et al., 2021; Buchholz et al., 2024). This observation highlights
839   the need for a deeper understanding of the performance and limitations of both IRM and ERM in the
840   context of high-dimensional data and domain adaptation problems.
841
842
      The concept of our causal framework is similar to that of (Cai et al., 2019; Chen & Bühlmann, 2021;
      Sun et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024; Carvalho et al., 2024). In comparison, Cai
843
      et al. (2019) assumed the independence of the latent semantic variables and other latent variables,
844
      which differs from our dependence assumption. Chen & Bühlmann (2021) adopted linear structural
845   causal models to study complicated domain adaptation problems, which did not provide a theoretical
846   understanding of nonlinear high-dimensional data. Sun et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021) only considered
847   a variation of the PICIM in our work as their causal structure, whereas we consider the FICIM and
848   PICIM.
849
      Moreover, the above methods do not solve the OSDA problem, which is the crucial problem of our
850
      study.

1158   D.1.1    DATASET
1159   We constructed our open-set CMNIST (OS-CMNIST) dataset following the dealing method of
1160   CMNIST (Arjovsky et al., 2020) to satisfy the setting demand of the OSDA task. We selected MNIST
1161   as our experimental dataset as it is an ideally clear dataset that does not include other attributes to
1162   determine the labels apart from the grayscale digits. A critical step in the creation of the OS-CMNIST
1163   training (source) domain was the random sampling of known and unknown classes from MNIST. If
1164   the data of the known classes were processed using the same method as CMNIST, we could obtain
1165   the PICIM source domain.
1166   Comparison groups. We designed the following common steps to construct the OS-CMNIST
1167   dataset: first, randomly sample K known classes and 10 − K unknown classes from MNIST; second,
1168   assign a causal shape code C to the known classes based on the digit: C = 0 for a random half of the
1169   K classes and C = 1 for the other half; third, perform the same operation for unknown classes as in
1170   the second step.
1171   Based on the known classes of the OS-CMNIST dataset, we constructed two comparison groups of
1172   the source domain, as follows:
1173
1174           • FICIM source domain: First, sample the variation color attribute code V by flipping C
1175             with a probability 1 − Corr(V, C); second, color the image green if V = 1 or red if V = 0.
1176           • PICIM source domain: First, obtain the final class label Y by flipping C with a probability
1177             1 − Corr(Y, C); second, sample the color attribute code V by flipping Y with a probability
1178             1 − Corr(V, Y ); finally, color the image green if V = 1 or red if V = 0.
1179
       In this case, Corr(·, ·) represents the correlation between two variables. As the binary label of
1180
       a variable is changed by flipping another variable, the correlation relationship is the same for
1181
       Corr(·, ·) < 0.5 and Corr(·, ·) > 0.5. It is known that these two variables are independent when
1182   Corr(·, ·) = 0.5. When Corr(·, ·) > 0.5, a greater Corr(·, ·) indicates a stronger correlation. When
1183   Corr(·, ·) < 0.5, a smaller Corr(·, ·) indicates a stronger correlation. If Corr(·, ·) = 1 or = 0,
1184   the two variables are perfectly positively correlated or negatively correlated. Note that the only
1185   difference between the FICIM and PICIM in our construction is that Corr(Y, C) = 1 for the FICIM,
1186   whereas Corr(Y, C) < 1 for the PICIM, which match the causal structure effectively. To ensure the
1187   comparability of the two groups further, we set a constant κS = Corr(V, C)S = Corr(V, Y )S to
       represent the ratio of information acquired by color code V for the source domain.
