011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine Unlearning has emerged as a significant area of research, focusing on ‘re-
014            moving’ specific subsets of data from a trained model. Fine-tuning (FT) methods
015            have become one of the fundamental approaches for approximating unlearning, as
016            they effectively retain model performance. However, it is consistently observed
017            that naive FT methods struggle to forget the targeted data. In this paper, we present
018
               the first theoretical analysis of FT methods for machine unlearning within a lin-
               ear regression framework, providing a deeper exploration of this phenomenon.
019
               We investigate two scenarios with distinct features and overlapping features. Our
020
               findings reveal that FT models can achieve zero remaining loss yet fail to forget
021            the forgetting data, unlike golden models (trained from scratch without the forget-
022            ting data). This analysis reveals that naive FT methods struggle with forgetting
023            because the pretrained model retains information about the forgetting data, and
024            the fine-tuning process has no impact on this retained information. To address this
025            issue, we first propose a theoretical approach to mitigate the retention of forget-
026            ting data in the pretrained model. Our analysis shows that removing the forgetting
027            data’s influence allows FT models to match the performance of the golden model.
028            Building on this insight, we revisit the discriminative regularization used in exist-
029
               ing studies and redesign it to effectively reduce the unlearning loss gap between
               the fine-tuned model and the golden model. Our experiments on both synthetic
030
               and real-world datasets validate these theoretical insights and demonstrate the ef-
031
               fectiveness of the advanced regularization method.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Machine Unlearning has emerged as a prominent area that focuses on protecting individual privacy
038   during the model training process, particularly adhering to legislation such as ‘the right to be forgot-
039   ten’ (Rosen, 2011) under the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Hoofnagle et al., 2019).
040
      That is, it removes certain training samples from the trained model upon their users’ data deletion
      request. A natural approach to machine unlearning is to retrain the model from scratch, excluding
041
      the data that needs to be forgotten; this is known as exact unlearning. However, this method is highly
042
      computationally inefficient. To address this challenge, previous research has proposed a more re-
043   laxed definition of machine unlearning, where the unlearned model only needs to be approximately
044   similar to one retrained from scratch. This led to the development of approximate unlearning meth-
045   ods, such as Fine-Tuning (Warnecke et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020a), Gradient Ascent (Graves
046   et al., 2021; Thudi et al., 2022), Fisher Forgetting (Becker & Liebig, 2022; Golatkar et al., 2020a),
047   and Influence Unlearning(Izzo et al., 2021).
048
      Fine-tuning, as one of the most widely used approaches in approximate unlearning, has demon-
049   strated its empirical effectiveness. However, it can be observed in many studies (Kurmanji et al.,
050   2024; Warnecke et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020a; Liu et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2024) and our
051   investigations in Table 1 that while fine-tuning may maintain the utility of the model on remaining
052   data, it struggles to forget the targeted data. This raises a natural question:
053
                Why does fine-tuning fail to unlearn the forgetting data in machine unlearning?

064   To answer this question, we revisit the machine unlearning problem with a simple yet fundamen-
065   tal over-parameterized linear regression model and explore the behavior of fine-tuning through a
066   theoretical perspective. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows.
067
068           • Theoretical Analysis: Distinct and Overlapping Features. We provide the first theoreti-
069             cal analysis of FT methods in the context of machine unlearning within a linear regression
070             framework. Specifically, 1) Based on the assumption of distinct features (Assumption 3.1),
071
                our theoretical observations, which align with empirical studies, show that the remaining
                loss for the fine-tuning model is zero, matching that of the golden model. Moreover, the
072
                loss of the fine-tuning model on the forgetting dataset consistently remains zero, diverging
073
                from the performance of the golden model. 2) we extend our analysis to a more complex
074             case when the dataset retained for model retraining shares overlapping features with the
075             forgetting dataset. This challenges assumptions of distinct feature sets across datasets, yet
076             the previous conclusions remain valid in this case. More discussion refers to Section 3.
077
              • Theory for Enhanced Unlearning. Our analysis shows that naive fine-tuning (FT) meth-
078             ods fail to unlearn the forgetting data because the pretrained model retains information
079             about this data, and the fine-tuning process does not effectively alter that retention. To
080             address this issue, we propose a theoretical approach that removes the influence of the for-
081             getting data, mitigating its retention in the pretrained model. This enables FT models to
082             significantly improve unlearning accuracy while preserving the accuracy on the remain-
083             ing data. Furthermore, our findings provide key insights for designing machine unlearning
084             algorithms: retaining overlapping features between the remaining and forgetting datasets
085             has minimal impact on unlearning accuracy, while discarding these features results in a
086
                decrease in the accuracy on the remaining data.
087           • Revisiting Discriminative Regularization. We revisit the discriminative regularization
088             used in existing studies and redesign it to shift the regularization focus, prioritizing re-
089
                taining accuracy over unlearning accuracy. This shift ensures that overlapping features
                between the forgetting and remaining datasets are preserved, maintaining overall model
090
                utility. Moreover, we incorporate KL-divergence loss alongside cross-entropy to better
091
                capture the distributional discrepancies for effective unlearning.
092
093
              • Experimental Validation on Synthetic and Real-World Data. We validate our theoreti-
                cal findings on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Firstly, our experiments demonstrate
094
                that all regularization-based methods significantly improve UA compared to the baseline
095
                FT. Furthermore, we observe that in the fine-tuning process, focusing on preserving accu-
096             racy on remaining data along with regularization on forgetting data to enhance unlearning
097             will achieves both good RA and UA. However, placing more emphasis on using the forget-
098             ting data to improve UA can significantly degrade RA, consistent with our analysis.

102   Machine Unlearning Methods. Cao & Yang (2015) first defined “Unlearning” as the removal of
103   a sample that produces the same output on the dataset as if the sample had never been trained.
104   The natural way to solve the problem is to retrain a model from scratch in response to each data
105   deletion request. However, retraining is not feasible due to the limited time and constrained re-
106   sources. Ginart et al. (2019) provided a relaxed definition inspired by Differential Privacy (Dwork
107   et al., 2014), which only requires the unlearned model to produce results similar to those of retrain-
      from-scratch models. This led to the development of “approximate unlearning” methods, offering

108
      more efficient computational designs for machine unlearning. Guo et al. (2019); Izzo et al. (2021);
109   Neel et al. (2021); Ullah et al. (2021); Sekhari et al. (2021) provide theoretical error guarantees
110   by focusing on the empirical risk minimization problem under this probabilistic notion of unlearn-
111   ing. Golatkar et al. (2020a) proposed an information-based procedure to remove knowledge from
112   the trained weights, without access to the original training data. Further, Golatkar et al. (2020b)
113   approximated the weights inspired by NTK theory, addressing situations where the Hessian is not
114   informative about where the model will converge into a null space. Mehta et al. (2022) avoid the
115   computation of Hessian by introducing a method only computing conditional independence, which
116   identifies the Markov Blanket of parameters requiring updates. Thudi et al. (2022) proposed a regu-
117
      larizer to reduce the ‘verification error,’ which represents the distance between the unlearned model
      and a retrained-from-scratch model. Kurmanji et al. (2024) bears a novel teacher-student formu-
118
      lation to achieve better performance towards unbounded unlearning problems. Liu et al. (2024)
119
      considers model sparsity by pruning weights before the unlearning process, thereby introducing a
120   new unlearning paradigm. Shen et al. (2024) incorporates the variational inference and contrastive
121   learning approaches to address the lack of supervision information (label-agnostic).
122
123
      Machine Unlearning Theory. For approximate unlearning, Neel et al. (2021); Thudi et al. (2022)
      explored algorithms for empirical risk minimization objectives, while Sekhari et al. (2021) studied
124
      population risk minimization problems, providing theoretical guarantees on both the effectiveness
125
      of unlearning and the privacy of the data subjects. Guo et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2022) provided
126   the certified radius with respect to data changes before and after removals, as well as the certified
127   budget for data removals. For exact unlearning, Ullah et al. (2021) introduced the notion of algorith-
128   mic stability, called Total Variation (TV) stability, which is suited for achieving exact unlearning.
129   This concept was further extended to the federated setting by Che et al. (2023); Tao et al. (2024).
130   However, existing theoretical work has primarily focused on utility guarantees, with limited analysis
131   explaining the successes and failures of fine-tuning methods.

531
      In conclusion, we present the first theoretical analysis of fine-tuning methods for machine unlearning
532
      within a linear regression framework. Our analysis, covering two scenarios—distinct and overlap-
533
      ping feature sets—demonstrates that while fine-tuning can achieve optimal retaining accuracy (RA),
534   it fails to fully unlearn the forgetting dataset. Our analysis on the failure of naive fine-tuning meth-
535   ods stems from the pretrained model’s retention of forgetting data, and we propose a theoretical
536   approach to mitigate this issue. By revisiting and redesigning the discriminative regularization term,
537   we prioritize retaining accuracy while effectively balancing it with unlearning accuracy. Experimen-
538   tal results on both synthetic and real-world datasets validate our theoretical insights, demonstrating
539   that our redesigned regularization approach significantly enhances unlearning performance without
      sacrificing retention.

696   model is updated using the remaining dataset, while Kullback–Leibler divergence/Cross-entropy
697   loss regularization is applied to the forget dataset to enforce unlearning. The corresponding regu-
698   larization modifies the model’s predictions by encouraging it to generate incorrect outputs for the
699   forgetting data. Specifically, KL divergence is computed between the model’s output and shifted in-
700   correct labels, ensuring the model no longer retains knowledge of the forgetting data. Additionally,
701   cross-entropy loss between the model’s output and the incorrect labels further supports the unlearn-
      ing process. Throughout training, the optimizer updates the model based on the combined loss. Our

703   standard deviation of the performances.
