011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series forecasting plays a crucial role in various domains, such as power
014            and weather forecasting. In recent years, different types of models have achieved
015
               promising results in long-term time series forecasting. However, these models
               often produce predictions that lack consistency with the style of the input, re-
016
               sulting in reduced reliability and trust in the forecasts. To address this issue, we
017
               propose the Vision-Enhanced Time Series Forecasting by Decomposed Feature
018            Extraction and Composed Reconstruction (VisiTER), which leverages the rich
019            semantic information provided by the image modality to enhance the realism of
020            the predictions. It consists of two main components: the Decomposed Time Series
021            to Image Generation and the Composed Image to Time Series Generation. In the
022            first component, the Decomposed Time Series Feature Extraction Model extracts
023            periodic and trend information, which is then transformed into images using our
024            proposed time series to vision transformation architecture. After converting the
025            input time series into images, the resulting images are used as style features and
026
               concatenated with the previously extracted features. In the second component,
               we use our proposed TimeIR along with the previously obtained feature set to
027
               perform image reconstruction for the prediction part. Due to the rich information
028
               provided, the reconstructed images exhibit better consistency with the input images,
029            which are then transformed back into time series. Extensive experiments on seven
030            real-world datasets demonstrate that VisiTER achieves state-of-the-art prediction
031            performance on both traditional metrics and new metrics.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Time series forecasting has always been a widely studied research area (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Torres
037   et al., 2021), with extensive applications in fields such as economics (Granger & Newbold, 2014),
038   energy (Qian et al., 2019; Martı́n et al., 2010), and weather forecasting (Wu et al., 2023b). The
039
      goal of time series forecasting is to predict future values based on past observed data. Most existing
      time series prediction methods focus on extracting periodicity, anomalies, random fluctuations, and
040
      other features from the time series data. However, relying solely on the information provided by the
041
      time series modality is often insufficient, leading to limited prediction accuracy and an inability to
042   efficiently capture complex relationships within the data (Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019).
043
044   One promising strategy for addressing this issue is to convert time series into images for prediction (Li
045
      et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Hatami et al., 2018). This is because the image modality proposes
      a new perspective for data modeling compared with time series, providing more rich and diverse
046
      information. Nevertheless, there are several key challenges in using images for time series prediction
047
      tasks. The first challenge is the difficulty in image transformation and model training. Existing
048   transformation methods directly map time series into scatter plots, which can result in information
049   loss in the images, ultimately leading to poor performance of the subsequent image models. The
050   complexity of image models also results in prolonged training and inference times, consuming
051   substantial GPU memory and increasing the difficulty of training. This is particularly severe for
052   time series with many variables, which correspond to the number of channels in the image version.
053   The second is ineffective image utilization in time series: The advantages and methodologies for
      effectively utilizing the image modality have not been adequately explored. Some existing prediction


                                                         1

054
      methods that use images rely on image generation for forecasting. However, the generated images
055   often lack fidelity in the context of time series, as they miss crucial temporal feature information.
056
057   To address these challenges, we propose Vision-Enhanced Time Series Forecasting by Decomposed
058
      Feature Extraction and Composed Reconstruction (VisiTER). It introduces a novel framework of
      time series forecasting by transformation and generation between time series and vision domains,
059
      which consists of two main components: (1) the Decomposed Time Series to Vision Generation,
060
      which extracts decomposed temporal features as images for further prediction; (2) the Composed
061   Vision to Time Series Generation, which utilizes transformed images to generate composed prediction
062   results by image reconstruction.
063
064
      In the first component, we propose two main modules: the Decomposed Time Series Feature
      Extraction (DTFE) and the Time Series to Vision Transformation (T2V). DTFE decomposes the
065
      original time series and leverages the respective strengths of different types of transformer-based
066
      models to extract two essential temporal features for forecasting: the periodic and trend features.
067   These decomposed features help the model utilize richer temporal information and improve prediction
068   accuracy, which benefits the following image generation and reconstruction processes. Then, T2V
069   adopts a novel approach to convert time series into images, mapping data points with diminishing
070   pixel values along the y-axis. Utilizing T2V, we transform features from DTFE into image modality,
071   improving the time series data distribution in corresponding images for better reconstruction.
072
      In the second component, we introduce TimeIR, a novel transformer-based image reconstruction
073   model specifically designed for time series data, to generate the forecasting results by image gen-
074   eration. By utilizing the periodic features, trend features extracted from DTFE, and style features
075   of the time series as priors, TimeIR can enrich the reconstruction process with valuable temporal
076   information, fully utilizing the potential of vision models. Notably, incorporating style information
077   allows the reconstructed time series to retain the same style as the input time series. To address the
078   challenges of difficult training and inference, we redesign the model architecture and adopt a unique
079   training strategy. Specifically, our model can perform segmented prediction for long sequences,
080   which helps to reduce the computational load. During training, we also employ a channel sampling
081
      strategy to further decrease the computational requirements.
082   In conclusion, our work makes the following key contributions:
083
084          • We propose the VisiTER framework, which enhances time series forecasting using image
085            reconstruction models. By first extracting temporal features as priors, we provide the image
086            model with rich information, thereby fully leveraging the advantages of the image modality.
087          • We propose DTFE, designed with various transformer-based models to extract periodic
088            and trend features for more realistic representations. We also introduce T2V, effectively
089            transforming time series into images, and TimeIR, which leverages priors for reconstructing
090            time series images more efficiently.
091          • VisiTER achieves state-of-the-art performance on traditional MSE and MAE metrics. More-
092            over, we introduce the SSIM metric to time series forecasting tasks, enabling a more
093            comprehensive evaluation of the structural integrity of predictions. On this metric, our
094            model also outperforms other state-of-the-art models.

101
      Networks (RNNs)(Tokgöz & Ünal, 2018; Lai et al., 2018; Salinas et al., 2020), Convolutional Neural
102   Networks (CNNs)(Wang et al., 2023; Hewage et al., 2020; Livieris et al., 2020), Transformers, and
103   Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs). In recent years, the Transformer model has emerged as a strong
104   contender in time series forecasting (Liu et al., 2023; Vaswani, 2017; Zhou et al., 2021; Chen et al.,
105   2024). Its self-attention mechanisms effectively capture both short-term and long-term dependencies,
106   positioning it as a leading choice for many tasks. Linear models based on MLPs have also shown
107   notable predictive results (Oreshkin et al., 2019; Challu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), particularly
      for simpler datasets, serving as a useful baseline for comparison with more complex approaches.

127   2.2         I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
128
129   Image reconstruction (Park et al., 2003; Demoment, 1989) is a significant area in computer vision
130
      and image processing, aimed at recovering high-quality images from partial or degraded inputs. This
      task is essential in applications like medical imaging (Zhang & Dong, 2020), remote sensing (He
131
      et al., 2019), and video super-resolution (Kappeler et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2022a). Various methods
132
      have been proposed to tackle the image reconstruction problem, broadly categorized as follows.
133   Optimization-based methods (Fessler, 2020): These establish mathematical models for reconstruction
134   and use optimization algorithms, such as regularized optimization and dictionary learning. Deep
135   learning-based methods (Liu et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2021): Utilizing deep neural networks for end-
136   to-end reconstruction (Ledig et al., 2017; Sajjadi et al., 2017; Goodfellow et al., 2020), these methods,
137   like SwinIR, effectively learn image priors from large datasets for high-quality results. GAN-based
138   methods: Introducing the GAN framework, models like SRGAN and EnhanceNet generate more
139   realistic and natural reconstructions.
140
141   2.3         I MAGE T ECHNIQUES IN T IME S ERIES
142
143   In existing time series-related tasks, images are primarily used for time series classification (Li
144
      et al., 2024; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). The process typically involves converting time series data
      into images, followed by the application of traditional image models, such as Vision Transformers,
145
      to classify the images, thereby achieving classification of the time series. Additionally, there are
146
      extremely few models that utilize image models for time series forecasting (Yang et al., 2024), such
147   as ViTime. These models generally decompose the time series into trend and periodic components
148   and then generate subsequent images based on the provided input. However, this type of model does
149   not fully leverage the rich information that the image modality offers, and they require long input
150   lengths to provide information.

195   3.2.1    D ECOMPOSED T IME S ERIES F EATURE P REDICTION
196
      In DTFE, for predicting the trend component, it is necessary to consider the inter-channel interactions
197
      more extensively, as the trend of one channel can be influenced by the trends of other channels.
198   In contrast, for the periodic component, the influence of the periodicities in other channels on a
199   given channel is relatively small, and hence, a more channel-independent approach can be adopted.
200   Therefore, for the trend prediction model, we adopt an inverted transformer structure to explicitly
201   capture the cross-channel dependencies. On the other hand, for the periodic component prediction,
202   we use a patch-based transformer architecture, where we treat a few adjacent time steps as a patch
203   and predict the periodicity based on this sequence of patches. By leveraging the strengths of both
204   single-channel and multi-channel modeling approaches, and by tailoring the model structures to the
205   specific characteristics of trend and periodicity, the proposed DTFE succeeds to achieve robust and
206
      accurate time series features prediction. The basic framework of DTFE is shown in the Figure 2.
207   When training DTFE, it is essential to compute the loss functions for the periodic Transformer
208   backbone and the trend Transformer backbone separately for predicting the periodic and trend
209   components, and then sum them together. For each periodic Transformer, denoted as P-Trans(·), we
210   use Lperiod to represent its MSE loss function, and for the trend Transformer, denoted as T-Trans(·),
211   we use Ltrend to represent its MSE loss function. Additionally, we employ the Discrete Fourier
212
      Transform (DFT) to decompose the ground truth of forecasting result y into its period and trend
      components. Thus, we can derive the training losses for period and trend extraction:

280   3.3.1       C OMPOSED I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
281   TimeIR is a univariate reconstruction model, so the following discussion pertains to the reconstruction
282   of a single variable within a time series. The inputs we have received are Ximage ∈ RH×L1 ×N ,
283   Pimage ∈ RH×L2 ×N , and Timage ∈ RH×L2 ×N , which represent the original time series x as
284   images, as well as the images depicting the periodicity and trends elucidated by the DTFE. The
285   length of Ximage is L1 , while the lengths of Pimage and Timage are L2 . Next, we concatenate these
286   three components to obtain the input I ∈ RH×L×3 , where the three components represent the three
287   channels of I. To perform the concatenation operation, it is necessary to align the lengths of the
288   sequences. We handle different scenarios accordingly: when L1 equals L2 , we proceed directly.
289   When L1 is greater than L2 , we truncate Ximage to the length of L2 since Ximage serves merely as
290   a style feature, and its length does not impact the information. When L1 is less than L2 , we maintain
291   a sliding window on Pimage and Timage for learning. During each reconstruction, we select the
292
      periodic features and trend features within the sliding window, while keeping the style feature fixed
      at Ximage , as the style of the time series does not change with the sliding window selection. The
293
      reconstruction method is illustrated in Figure 4, and additional alignment details can be found in
294
      Appendix B.2. Thus, we can obtain style, trend, and periodicity priors, which we then concatenate to
295   form the input to the TimeIR model.

297   The TimeIR model is comprised of three main components. First, a shallow feature extraction module
298
      utilizing a CNN network block is employed. This is followed by a deep feature extraction module,
      which consists of multiple Time Series Swin Transformer Blocks (TSTB). Each TSTB is composed
299
      of several Swin Transformer Blocks. The key characteristic of the Swin Transformer used in TimeIR
300
      is its utilization of an overlapping patch embedding scheme. This approach allows the model to
301   better focus on the fine-grained details of the input data, which in this case is the time series. Finally,
302   the deep feature representations are passed through a convolutional layer that reduces the channel
303   dimension to 1, producing the final time series reconstruction. The overall architecture of TimeIR
304   illustrated in Figure 10.

360   4.1       TRAINING STRATEGY
361
      To avoid the excessively high computational demands of traditional image reconstruction models, we
362
      have adopted several strategies during the training process of TimeIR.
363
364   Firstly, for a given dataset, we only train the TimeIR for the case of 96-length prediction and 96-length
365   input. When the prediction length is greater than 96, we directly use this trained model for zero-shot
366
      prediction, and have achieved very good results. Secondly, since some of the datasets have a large
      number of variables, we employ a channel sampling operation during training. Specifically, for each
367
      training batch, we select a different set of variables to train on. Lastly, we employed a sliding window
368
      strategy to align the input, ensuring that when the prediction length exceeds the input length, the size
369   of TimeIR’s input remain fixed, so the GPU memory consumption is independent of the prediction
370   length. This allows our model to handle even very long prediction lengths without running into
371   issues of infeasibility. The training strategy for the entire VisiTER framework is described in detail in
372   Appendix A.2.

513   4.3.3            Z ERO -S HOT A NALYSIS
514
      In our experiments, to reduce the computational cost of training, we have only conducted training
515
      the TimeIR on the same dataset for sequence length 96 with 96-step prediction. For other cases,
516
      we have adopted a zero-shot approach. For prediction lengths greater than the sequence length, our
517   training strategy involves randomly selecting the starting position of the sliding window for training.
518   Additionally, we keep the DTFE model fixed while maintaining the same hyperparameters for the
519   others. The datasets used in this experiment are Weather and ETTh1, and the results are presented in
520   the Figure 8.
521
      The results indicate that the zero-shot performance surpasses that of the standard training approach,
522
      and this difference becomes more pronounced as the prediction length increases. The underlying
523   reason for this seemingly counterintuitive result is related to the design strategy of our model. Our
524   model is designed to use the input sequence to provide the style features of the time series. However,
525   when the prediction length is significantly longer than the sequence length, the style or characteristics
526   may change. When the sliding window is close to the starting point, the style is more similar, but as
527   the window moves further away, the style can become less similar. This can lead to training instability
528   and poorer performance compared to the zero-shot approach.

531
532
      We propose the Vision-Enhanced Time Series Forecasting by Decomposed Feature Extraction and
533
      Composed Reconstruction Framework (VisiTER) that leverages image reconstruction techniques for
534   time series forecasting. The framework consists of two main components: the Decomposed Time
535   Series to Image Generation and the Composed Image to Time Series Generation. It successfully
536   integrates the image modality into time series forecasting. By supplementing the time series data with
537   rich information from the image modality, the prediction results become more reliable and accurate.
538   In future work, we hope to see a growing interest in exploring the use of image modalities within the
539   field of time series forecasting. This integration could uncover new avenues for enhancing predictive
      models and improving performance.

      B.1   IMAGE RECONSTRUCTION IN T IME -S ERIES FORECASTING
867
868   The reason we introduced image reconstruction techniques in time series prediction tasks is that
869   current time series prediction models often lack style continuity. Specifically, the geometric structure
870   of the predicted time series does not align with that of the input time series. This issue arises from
871   the use of the MSE loss function, which only reflects the numerical similarity between the predicted
872   results and the true results, without capturing structural similarity. In other words, two samples may
873
      have the same MSE with respect to the ground truth, yet their shapes may differ.
874   In Figure 12, we present a detailed example: the ground truth is a sine function, while Time Series
875   One is a sine function that has been shifted both horizontally and vertically, and Time Series Two is a
876   straight line. By controlling the magnitude of the shift in Time Series One, we can make both time
877   series have the same MSE as the ground truth. However, it is clear that Time Series Two lacks any
878   meaningful information, such as periodicity and trends, even though its MSE is identical to that of
879
      Time Series One. In practical applications of time series, we would prefer to use Time Series Two,
      which possesses similar periodicity and trends and contains more information.
880
881   When ordinary time series prediction models use MSE as the loss function, they focus solely on
882   numerical similarity while neglecting the geometrical structural similarity in a two-dimensional
883   space. Therefore, we attempted to introduce image reconstruction models to capture this aspect of
884   information. Experimental results have shown that our model achieves better reconstruction results,
885
      maintaining a low MSE while providing improved structural integrity.
886
887   B.2   A LIGNMENT DETAILS
888
      To ensure the alignment of P, T and X for concatenation in Part II, we must match their lengths.
889
      When the input length is less than the prediction length, we truncate X to the input length. This
890   approach is effective because x serves as a style feature, and altering its length does not significantly
891   impact the stored information. When the input length equals the prediction length, concatenation
892   can be done directly. When the prediction length exceeds the input length, we maintain a sliding
893   window on P and T with a length equal to the input length. The window moves from the beginning
894   to the end, capturing segments that are then concatenated with X to predict the corresponding output
895   segments. For any excess length after division, an additional window is used to capture the final
896   portion. The reason all windows are concatenated with X is that X acts as a style feature and does
897   not provide any temporal information. Algorithm 1 outlines the complete operation of the model,
898
      including a detailed explanation of the input alignment logic.

1137   leveraging periodic and trend information to reconstruct time series. The core approach involves
1138   incorporating the style of the time series through image reconstruction. However, when predicting
1139   long time series, the style can change over time as the series lengthens, potentially introducing noise
1140   into the supplementary information. Future research could focus on better utilizing the style feature
1141   to address this issue.
1142
       Additionally, we directly employed traditional Transformer models for both feature prediction and
1143   time series image reconstruction. Subsequent work could modify the Transformer architecture to
1144   make it more suitable for reconstructing time series images. While our experiments concentrated on
1145   long time series prediction, this method is also applicable to other time series tasks, such as short
1146   time series prediction and imputation.
