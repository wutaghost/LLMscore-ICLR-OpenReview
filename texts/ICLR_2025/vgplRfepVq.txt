011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose Gradient Inversion Transcript (GIT), a generic approach for recon-
014            structing training data from gradient leakage in distributed learning using a gener-
015            ative model. Unlike traditional gradient matching techniques, GIT only requires
016
               the model architecture information, without access to the model’s parameters,
               making it more applicable to real-world distributed learning settings. Addition-
017
               ally, GIT operates offline without intensive gradient requests or online optimiza-
018
               tion. Compared to existing generative methods, GIT adaptively constructs a gen-
019            erative network, with an architecture specifically tailored to the structure of the
020            distributed learning model. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that GIT sig-
021            nificantly improves reconstruction accuracy, especially in the case of deep models.
022            In summary, we offer a more effective and theoretically grounded strategy for ex-
023            ploiting vulnerabilities of gradient leakage in distributed learning, advancing the
024            understanding of privacy risks in collaborative learning environments.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In distributed learning, each client trains its model on local data and shares the gradients with a
030   central server, which aggregates them to update the global model (Jochems et al., 2016; McMahan
031   et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019). Gradient sharing is also common in federated learning (Huang et al.,
032   2021), but unlike distributed learning, which involves a more centrally coordinated distribution of
033
      data across nodes, federated learning (FL) focuses on preserving client privacy by ensuring that
      data remains localized. While these methods are effective in improving model performance and
034
      training efficiency without directly exposing the client’s data to public, recent research has shown
035
      that sharing gradients can still lead to sensitive information leakage, as attackers may exploit the
036   shared gradients to reconstruct the original training data used by the individual client (Phong et al.,
037   2017; Zhu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2020), posing significant privacy risks in real-world distributed
038   learning systems.
039
      There is a considerable amount of work proposed to reconstruct the training data from its gradi-
040
      ent (Phong et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2019; Geiping et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Zhu & Blaschko,
041
      2020; Wu et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2020), based on varying levels of model access. These works can
042   generally be divided into two major categories: gradient matching, which optimizes reconstructed
043   data to align its gradient with the leaked one, and generative methods, which train generative models
044   to map the leaked gradient to the corresponding training data. Gradient matching methods typically
045   need repeated requests for gradients from the model under attack (Zhu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020;
046   Geiping et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020) or full access to the model parameters (Zhu & Blaschko,
047   2020; Wang et al., 2023), which are usually not satisfied in practice.
048   We focus on generative methods in this work, which train a generative model called the “threat
049   model” using several input-gradient pairs. The architectures of the threat model are usually pre-
050   defined in existing methods. That is to say, the architectures of the threat model, such as a multi-layer
051   perception (MLP) (Rosenblatt, 1958) or a UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015), are used irrespective of
052   the model under attack. By contrast, we introduce Gradient Inversion Transcript (GIT) in this
053   work to adaptively choose the architecture of the threat model to improve its effectiveness. It is a
      framework generally applicable to models of different architecture under attack.

054
      Problem Settings In this work, we consider a practical distributed learning scenario in which an
055   attacker is able to gain and store the gradient updates sent by each local client but does not have
056   direct access to the clients’ raw data or labels. Additionally, the attacker is not able to interact with
057   the central server’s global model, meaning the global parameters remain unknown. The attacker
058   also cannot request gradient returns from the global model or modify its architecture to enhance the
059   attack. This setting reflects a more realistic threat model where attackers rely solely on gradient
060   information to attempt data reconstruction.
061   Assumptions Reconstruction by gradient matching has two main assumptions: (1) attackers know
062   private label (Zhu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020) or at least label distribution in a data batch (Zhao
063   et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2023). (2) attackers have access to the back propagation
064   process of the FL model, i.e., attackers are able to obtain returned gradients when they input data
065   (Zhu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020), or global model parameters Zhu & Blaschko
066   (2020). In our settings, similar to prior works that employ generative approaches (Wu et al., 2023;
067   Pan et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021), we do not rely on the above assumptions. Instead, we assume
068   that attackers have access to multiple input-gradient pairs. This setting is more practical, as labels
069
      are not shared in distributed learning, and it is challenging for attackers to gain access to the back
      propagation process.
070
071   Our main contributions are as follows:
072
073          • We propose a theory-driven training data reconstruction scheme using a generative ap-
074
               proach. This method relies solely on gradient information, without requiring access to the
               backpropagation process or the global model’s parameters, as was necessary in previous
075
               work. We systematically compare the differences between gradient matching and genera-
076
               tive methods, along with their respective attack performance.
077
078          • We introduce a new generative model designed based on theoretical derivations. Instead
079
               of using a fixed architecture, our generative model is tailored to the structure of the model
               under attack. Unlike previous empirical approaches, our method is theoretically grounded,
080
               resulting in superior performance.
081
082          • Unlike gradient matching, our method is based on offline learning. Once the generative
083            model is trained, it can infer the input data without further training, while gradient matching
084
               requires repeated online learning for each data batch and necessitates continuous requests
               for gradients from the global model.
085
086   Notation and Terminology The federated learning (FL) model from which gradients are leaked to
087   attackers is referred to as the ”leaked model,” while the network proposed by attackers to reconstruct
088   the training dataset is referred to as the ”threat model.” In this work, we use Lθ (x, y) to represent
089   the loss objective of an FL model, parameterized by θ, on an input-label pair (x, y). The model’s
090   weights and batch-averaged gradients are represented by W and ∇W, respectively.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      Before discussing gradient-based training data reconstruction, it is worth noting that reconstructing
095
      datasets using model parameters only is also viable. Methods under this setting require significantly
096   less information than gradient-based methods because they do not need gradient information which
097   is data-dependent. Haim et al. (2022) was the first to reconstruct the training dataset solely based on
098   leaked model parameters by a method grounded in the theoretical analysis from Lyu & Li (2019).
099   Despite using less information, the method is unable to recover high-quality data and fails to achieve
100   pixel-wise accuracy. Consequently, gradient inversion attacks are more widely investigated in the
101   context of the leaked gradients.
102   Gradient Matching Training set reconstruction by gradient matching was initially explored by
103   Phong et al. (2017), which discusses the feasibility of reconstructing training data from shared gra-
104   dients in distributed learning. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated its practicality by proposing a method
105   called Deep Leakage from Gradients (DLG). DLG optimizes a randomly generated dummy input
106   to match the training data by minimizing the distance between the dummy gradients and the leaked
107   ground truth gradients. Building on DLG, Wei et al. (2020) evaluate the impact of different feder-
      ated learning configurations, such as batch size, on the performance of gradient matching. Geiping


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al. (2020) extend DLG by leveraging only the direction of the gradient and replace the optimizer
109   LBFGS with Adam. Wang et al. (2020) propose a Gaussian-kernel-based cost function to recon-
110   struct training data at any training phase. Zhu & Blaschko (2020) introduce a closed-form recursive
111   procedure to recover data in which all gradients and parameters are exposed to the attacker. Further-
112   more, Wang et al. (2023) propose a provable gradient inversion attack focusing on reconstructing a
113   batch of data by querying a model with malicious parameter.
114
      Reconstruction By Generative Models Unlike reconstruction by gradient matching, the generative
115   approaches train a threat model to generate the reconstructed training data with the leaked gradi-
116   ents as the input. The idea of employing a generative model for training data reconstruction was
117   originally proposed in Wu et al. (2023), which uses a three-layer MLP with fixed hidden size as
118   the generated model. Pan et al. (2020) propose a theoretically grounded method to train generative
119   models, leveraging the presence of exclusively activated neurons. In addition, Huang et al. (2021)
120   demonstrate that generative techniques can exhibit strong performance even when attackers lack ac-
121   cess to precise batch norm statistics. Furthermore, pretrained generative models, such as the ones
122   trained on other samples from the training data distribution (Jeon et al., 2021) or public datasets (Li
123
      et al., 2022), have also shown the potential to improve the performance of generative training data
      reconstruction.
124
125   The mentioned generative methods above employ a threat model of a fixed architecture regardless of
126   the leaked model, which may not be optimal. In contrast, we introduce a framework that dynamically
127   selects the architecture of the threat model based on the leaked model to enhance performance.
128   Challenges of Training Data Reconstruction One key challenge is to restore the label information,
129   which is the key to reconstructing the training data. Although many methods require the attacker’s
130   access to the label information (Zhu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020) or label distribution (Zhao et al.,
131   2020; Yin et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2023), several attempts have been made to restore the label in-
132   formation based on the leaked gradients. These methods usually tackle one particular scenario or
133   have additional assumptions, including small batch size (Zhao et al., 2020), no duplicate labels in
134   a mini-batch (Yin et al., 2021), and access to the output probability of each class (Ma et al., 2023).
135
      By contrast, a recent work Chen & Vikalo (2024) considers a more realistic scenario, which takes
      multiple local epochs, heterogeneous data and various optimizers into consideration.
136
137   Another key challenge is dealing with large batch sizes. The dimensionality of the leaked gradient is
138   fixed, but a large batch size means more information to reconstruct. Restoring the label information
139   has been shown effective in improving the performance in large batch size regime (Yin et al., 2021).
140   In addition, there are several works (Fowl et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Hayes
141
      et al., 2024) proposed to improve the performance of reconstructing large batch training data under
      different settings. However, there are still considerable performance gaps between small batch and
142
      large batch regimes.
143
144   In our framework, we do not assume any access to the label information. In addition, we evaluate
145   our methods against baselines across varying batch sizes. Comprehensive experiments validate the
146   effectiveness of our methods despite these challenges.

327   5     E XPERIMENTS
328
329   We assess our methods on classification tasks using the CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) im-
330   age dataset. In the realm of distributed learning or federated learning, a central server refines a
331   classification model by aggregating gradients shared by user devices, derived from their individual
332   training data. Our experiment operates under the assumption that user-side local datasets are subsets
333   of CIFAR-10. The attacker, with access to a subset of gradient-input pairs, endeavors to reconstruct
334   the remaining input data using the gradients shared by others. These pairs for training the threat
335   models are sampled from CIFAR-10’s training set (unless otherwise specified, in the subsequent
336   experiments, we use one-tenth of the training data, which consists of 5,000 samples), we evaluate
337
      the performance of the recontruction methods on CIFAR-10’s test set. To quantitatively evaluate
      model efficacy, we utilize mean squared error (MSE) as the metric for evaluating the performance
338
      of training data reconstruction.
339
340   We mainly use LeNet (LeCun et al., 1998) and ResNet (He et al., 2016) of various depth as the
341   architecture of the leaked model in our experiments. We use the approximation in (4) for LeNet
342   and the approximation in (6) for ResNet, since ResNet includes shortcut connections. For layers
343
      other than linear layers, including pooling layers and batch normalization layers (Ioffe, 2015), as
      discussed in Section 3.3, we can construct the corresponding architecture of the threat model based
344
      on the back propagation through these layers.
345
346   Baselines We benchmark our methods against two approaches: (1) Deep Leakage from Gradients
347   (DLG) (Zhu et al., 2019), which belongs to the category of gradient matching methods; (2) The
348   generative approach utilizing a fixed MLP architecture (Wu et al., 2023). We select these two as our
349
      baselines, because both of them achieve competitive performance in their respective category. For
      generative methods, we do not use UNet as the fixed architecture, because UNet-based generative
350
      models leverage priors from the public data. However, we do not assume any access to the public
351
      data by the attacker.
352
353   Although the gradient matching methods diverge from our assumptions and configurations stud-
354   ied, we opt to compare with these methods due to its widespread application. Gradient matching
355
      techniques necessitate a complete optimization process for each batch data recovery, whereas gener-
      ative methods need to train a generative model capable of retrieving data from any batch used in its
356
      training. That is to say, the major computational overhead for gradient matching methods is the per-
357
      batch optimization process during reconstruction, while the major overhead for generative methods
358   is to train a generative model. To ensure a fair comparison, we keep the computational complexity
359   approximately the same for methods of both categories.

419
420   Gradient perturbation is a commonly used defense method against gradient leakage (Zhu et al.,
421   2019). As shown in prior work Wu et al. (2023), the generative model demonstrates superior per-
422   formance over DLG in countering privacy defenses. Our results in the left half of Table 4 validate
423
      this conclusion for GIT when encountering gradient perturbation with varying noise variance. In

424

425
      to the noise added to the gradients, Gaussian noise with a std of 0.01 is sufficient to prevent DLG
426   from accurately recovering the input image, and noise with a std of 0.1 will result in reconstructed
427   images being entirely comprised of noise. In contrast, GIT maintains a mean squared error (MSE) of
428   approximately 0.01 even when the noise std reaches 0.1, showing minimal susceptibility to noise. In
429   addition, we notice that the sixth recovered image in the validation set shows an inverse trend com-
430   pared to the other seven images, where the quality improves as the training dataset size decreases.
431   This anomaly could be attributed to the image being an outlier in the CIFAR-10 distribution. We
      will leave this observation for future work.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
531
      This work introduces the Generative Gradient Inversion Transcript (GIT), a method for reconstruct-
532
      ing training data in distributed learning by exploiting gradient leakage. We formulate and solve
533
      a reconstruction system that leverages gradients to recursively reconstruct the hidden layer neuron
534   outputs, based on the back propagation. Our framework is generic and considers different categories
535   of layers and network topologies. Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed
536   methods: compared with using a fixed architecture as the generative model for reconstruction, GIT
537   is more adaptive to different architectures of the leaked models. GIT has competitive performance
538   in various scenarios, including noisy gradients and limited amount of training data. Our future
539   work will focus on mitigating the overfitting issue to further improve the performance of generative
      reconstruction methods.
