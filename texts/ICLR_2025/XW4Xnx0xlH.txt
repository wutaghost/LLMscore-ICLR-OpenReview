009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Forward gradient methods offer a promising alternative to backpropagation. Op-
012            timization that only requires forward passes could simplify hardware implemen-
013            tation, improve parallelism, lower memory cost, and allow for more biologically
014            plausible learning models. This has motivated recent forward-mode automated
015            differentiation (AD) methods. This paper presents a novel second-order forward-
016
               mode AD method for optimization that generalizes a second-order line search to
               a K-dimensional hyperplane. Unlike recent work that relies on directional deriva-
017
               tives (or Jacobian–Vector Products, JVPs), we use hyper-dual numbers to jointly
018
               evaluate both directional derivatives and their second-order quadratic terms. As a
019            result, we introduce forward-mode weight perturbation with Hessian information
020            for K-dimensional hyper-plane search (FoMoH-KD). We derive the convergence
021            properties of FoMoH-KD and show how it generalizes to Newton’s method for
022            K = D. We demonstrate this generalization empirically, and compare the perfor-
023            mance of FoMoH-KD to forward gradient descent (FGD) on three case studies:
024            Rosenbrock function used widely for evaluating optimization methods, multino-
025            mial logistic regression with 7,850 parameters, and learning a CNN classifier with
026            431,080 parameters. Our experiments show that FoMoH-KD not only achieves
027
               better performance and accuracy, but also converges faster, thus, empirically veri-
               fying our theoretical results.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Backpropagation, that is, reverse-mode automated differentiation (AD), is widely used for optimiza-
033   tion, particularly in machine learning, despite the additional computational complexity and memory
034   requirement associated with the need for a backward pass. As a result, there is a growing interest
035   in investigating the practical plausibility of forward-mode AD as a more efficient way to estimate
036   gradients with the added interest of being relevant to biological learning mechanisms. There has
037   been significant progress towards this goal and the recently proposed approach for forward gradient
038   descent (FGD) (Baydin et al., 2022) relies on sampling tangent vectors to update function param-
039
      eters. These parameters are updated by subtracting the tangents that are scaled by their directional
      derivatives. As a result, their approach only requires forward passes, and avoids the computation and
040
      memory costs associated with implementing the backward pass of reverse-mode AD. The current
041
      challenges of working with FGD have been on reducing the variance of the gradient estimator with
042   an increase of dimensions (Ren et al., 2022; Fournier et al., 2023) and improving on the performance
043   and convergence rate of optimization.
044
045
      In this paper, we address these challenges by using Hessian information to develop a second-order
      forward-mode AD approach for optimization without any backpropagation (BP). While second-
046
      order approaches have been used in BP, our paper is the first to develop a second-order forward-
047
      mode AD approach. Second-order derivative information provides optimization routines with in-
048   formation about the local curvature. However, computing the full Hessian for high-dimensional
049   problems requires prohibitively large memory. Instead of building the full Hessian, we introduce a
050   new second-order approach that leverages a partial Hessian. Our approach, FoMoH-KD, builds a
051   K × K Hessian in a sub-space of the full function space. As a result, FoMoH-KD is a forward-
052   mode AD approach that outperforms the previous forward-mode-only first-order gradient descent
053   method, FGD. We demonstrate and prove that our approach bridges the gap between a line search
      and a full Newton step. By incorporating Hessian information, FoMoH-KD leverages second-order

054
      derivatives to provide curvature approximations of the objective function. This allows the method
055   to adjust the step size and direction with greater precision compared to a simple line search, which
056   only uses gradient information. Simultaneously, FoMoH-KD avoids the computational complexity
057   of a full Newton step, which requires the matrix vector multiplication of the inverse Hessian and the
058   gradient. Our approach balances the efficiency of a line search with the optimization effectiveness
059   and accuracy of a full Newton method, offering a robust and versatile optimization technique.
060
      In summary, the central contributions of this paper are as follows:
061
062   • Approach: We introduce FoMoH-KD, a novel forward-mode-only K-dimensional hyperplane
063     search that uses second-order information to build partial Hessians. Our approach enables a natu-
064
        ral trade-off between computational complexity and optimization performance.
065   • Theory: We show and prove that FoMoH-KD generalizes to Newton’s method, without the need
066     for backpropagation. We also compare the convergence rate of FGD to FoMoH-KD and show
067     that, under certain assumptions (§5), the expected reduction in error from the minimum decays at
068
        an exponential rate of (D − K)/D. This compares with the previous approach of FGD, which has
        an expected sub-linear convergence rate.
069
070   • Experiments: We demonstrate FoMoH-KD on optimization problems of different parameter
071     sizes and difficulty to show the advantage of second-order information over the first-order forward-
072     mode-only approach of FGD.
073   • Open-Source Code: We release an AD backend in PyTorch that implements nested forward AD
074     and interfaces with PyTorch models.
075   The rest of the paper is organized accordingly: §2 and §3 summarize the related work and relevant
076   preliminary information regarding AD, which is then followed by §4 that summarizes the extension
077   of forward-mode AD to higher order derivatives. §5 introduces FoMoH-KD and the convergence
078   analysis. Finally, §6 provides experimental results that explore the behavior of FoMoH-KD. We
079   then conclude in §7. The appendix contains additional results and formal proofs.

      There has been considerable interest in developing approaches that avoid reverse-mode AD and its
084
      backward pass. In moving away from BP, it might be possible to build optimization algorithms that
085   more closely align with biological systems (Bengio et al., 2015; Hinton, 2022), or enable neural
086   networks to run on emerging hardware architectures, such as analog optical systems (Pierangeli
087   et al., 2019). Baydin et al. (2022) introduced FGD as a possible replacement for BP in neural
088   network training by relying on weight perturbations. This removed the truncation error of previous
089   weight perturbation approaches (Pearlmutter, 1994) by moving to forward-mode AD. While FGD is
090   a promising BP-free approach, the scaling of FGD to high-dimensional models is challenging due
091   the variance of the gradient estimator (see derivation in §B.3). This challenge has led to multiple
092   efforts that have focused on reducing this variance (Ren et al., 2022; Silver et al., 2021; Fournier
093
      et al., 2023). In particular, a common approach has been to rely on local BP steps to reduce the
      variance and/or to provide a better guess for the perturbation direction. In our work, we focus
094
      on second-order forward-mode optimization approaches, but highlight that these other approaches
095
      on variance reduction are orthogonal to our approach and could also be combined together and
096   generalized to FoMoH-KD.
097
098   Becker et al. (1988) were one of the first to the use second-order information to optimize neural
099
      networks. This approach leverages a local BP step (Le Cun, 1987) to capture the diagonal Hes-
      sian terms resulting in what they call a “pseudo-Newton step”. Both LeCun et al. (1998b, §6–§9)
100
      and Goodfellow et al. (2016, §8.6) provide discussions of the use of Hessian information for neu-
101
      ral network training and cover Newton’s method, as well as the Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm
102   (Levenberg, 1944; Marquardt, 1963), conjugate gradients, and BFGS. A key objective of these ap-
103   proaches is to investigate whether second-order information can be leveraged for neural network
104   training without the prohibitively high cost of a full Hessian evaluation. Additionally, the research
105   question then explored is whether the approximated Hessian is still good enough to give the desired
106   advantage over first-order methods. Examples of effective approaches often rely on diagonal precon-
107   ditioners, with some approximating or directly using the diagonal of the Hessian (Yao et al., 2021;
      Le Cun, 1987; Becker et al., 1988), and others leveraging momentum and a variant of the diagonal


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of the empirical Fisher (Duchi et al., 2011; Tieleman & Hinton, 2012; Kingma & Ba, 2015). While
109   extending a gradient preconditioner to the full inverse Hessian is generally infeasible, there are ap-
110   proaches that use: block-diagonal approximations, low-rank approximations, and Krylov-subspace
111   based approximations (Le Roux & Fitzgibbon, 2010; Vinyals & Povey, 2012). Additional refer-
112   ences for these preconditioners can be found in Martens (2020). Finally, although it is challenging
113   to scale second-order approaches to large neural network models, a recent work, Sophia (Liu et al.,
114   2023), has managed to show success in using such an approach. Like previous works, they use a
115   diagonal approximation of the Hessian. Importantly, they show that Sophia requires half the number
116   of update steps as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) to train a language model (GPT-2 (Radford et al.,
117
      2019)). It is worth noting that none of the described second-order optimization methods rely solely
      on forward-mode AD like the one being proposed in this paper.

120   3    AUTOMATIC D IFFERENTIATION
121
122   In this section we summarize the common definitions of forward-mode and reverse-mode automatic
123   differentiation. For a complete introduction, please refer to Griewank & Walther (2008). We use
124   x ∈ RD to denote a column vector.
125   Forward-Mode AD (Wengert, 1964) applies the chain rule in the forward direction. The forward-
126   mode evaluation, F (θ, v), requires an additional tangent vector, v ∈ RD , along with the parameter
127   vector θ ∈ RD for a function f : RD → RO . The result of the evaluation, [f (θ), ∇f (θ)v], is a
128   function evaluation and the corresponding Jacobian vector product (JVP), where ∇f (θ) ∈ RO×D .
129   For a unidimensional output function, the JVP is the directional derivative, ∇f (θ) · v. The time
130   and space (memory cost) complexity are linear, both approximately twice that of a single function
131
      evaluation.1 A common implementation of forward-mode AD is to use dual numbers. A dual
      number a + bϵ ∈ D(R) contains a real (primal) component, a ∈ R, and a dual component, b ∈ R.
132
      We can think of this as representing a truncated Taylor series, a + bϵ + O(ϵ2 ), notationally simplified
133
      by the rule ϵ2 = 0. Using this, f (a + bϵ) = f (a) + ∇f (a)bϵ. A simple example can be shown
134   for the function, f (a1 , a2 ) = a1 × a2 . Using dual numbers, (a1 + b1 ϵ)(a2 + b2 ϵ), we retrieve the
135   function evaluation and the product rule: a1 a2 + (a1 b2 + b1 a2 )ϵ. This can be extended to multiple
136   dimensions, and is the basis of forward-mode AD: lift all real numbers R to dual numbers D(R).
137
      Reverse-Mode AD requires both a forward pass and a reverse pass. The reverse-mode evaluation
138
      R(θ, u), also requires the additional adjoint vector, u ∈ RO , which is often set to 1 for scalar-valued
139
      functions. Using the same notation as for forward-mode, an evaluation of reverse mode results in
140   the vector-Jacobian product, u⊤ ∇f (θ), as well as the function evaluation. When u = 1, this results
141   in the gradient ∇f (θ). Reverse-mode is required to store intermediate values on the forward pass
142   that are used during the reverse pass. This results in a higher time and space complexity, that is
143   higher computational cost and memory footprint per call of R(θ, u). However, for the scalar-valued
144   functions (O = 1) that are common for ML optimization problems, only a single call of R(θ, u) is
145   needed to collect all the gradients compared to D (dimension of inputs) calls of F (θ, v). This is one
146   of the key reasons for the widespread adoption of the reverse-mode AD in current gradient-based
147   machine learning methods, despite the higher memory footprint.

173   4.1   I MPLICATIONS OF H YPER -D UAL N UMBERS FOR M ACHINE L EARNING

175   A function evaluation with hyper-dual numbers takes an input vector, θ + v1 ϵ1 + v2 ϵ2 + 0ϵ1 ϵ2 ,
176   with the ϵ1 ϵ2 part set to zero. A typical setting to get exact gradient and Hessian elements is to set
177   v1 = ei and v2 = ej , where ei and ej are each a basis of one-hot unit vectors. Therefore, these
178   basis vectors select the corresponding elements of the gradient and Hessian:
179             f (θ) + ∇f (θ)i ϵ1 + ∇f (θ)j ϵ2 + ∇2 f (θ)ij ϵ1 ϵ2 = f (θ + ei ϵ1 + ej ϵ2 + 0ϵ1 ϵ2 ).
180
      A single loop over the input dimension provides the exact gradient, whereas a nested loop provides
181
      the full Hessian. As a side note, a single loop also can give the Hessian vector product. This is
182
      done by setting one of the tangent vectors to the chosen vector, and looping through the basis for the
183   other tangent vector.4 However, the key advantage is that with a single forward pass, we get both
184   first order and second order information in the form of directional derivatives and the local curvature
185   information respectively. Since we have already seen from Baydin et al. (2022) and follow up works
186   (Ren et al., 2022; Fournier et al., 2023) that first-order forward-mode-only optimization routines can
187   successfully train machine learning models, we now look to ask the question as to whether forward
188   passes that provide second-order information can be used to further improve on current forward-
189   mode AD optimization. Therefore, in this paper we investigate whether the additional access to local
190   curvature through forward-mode AD can enable improvements over the current FGD algorithm.
191
192
      Local Curvature: v1⊤ ∇2 f (θ)v2 . The Hessian contains the curvature information at a point, θ,
      in the form of the second order partial derivatives. When we evaluate a function over hyper-dual
193
      numbers we get the bilinear form, v1⊤ ∇2 f (θ)v2 . This is a function that operates over two vectors,
194
      such that it is linear in each vector separately. The bilinear form is common in optimization routines,
195   such as for conjugate gradient descent to assess whether two vectors are conjugate with respect to
196   the Hessian. The value of v1⊤ ∇2 f (θ)v2 tells us about how curvature covaries along the two vectors.
197   In the case where v1 = v2 = v, we arrive at the quadratic form that describes the curvature, which
198   indicates the rate of change of the slope as you move in the direction of v. The quadratic form also
199   provides information on the convexity of the function at that point. If v⊤ ∇2 f (θ)v > 0, ∀v ∈ RD   >0 ,
200   then the function is convex at that point and the Hessian is positive definite. The curvature also
201   indicates the sensitivity of moving in certain directions. For example, when using gradients to
202   optimize a function, taking a gradient step in a region of low curvature (with a small learning rate)
203
      is likely to increase the value of the function. However the same sized step in a region of large
      curvature could significantly change the value of the function (for better or worse).

502   7       C ONCLUSION
503
504   In this paper, we introduced FoMoH-KD, a novel forward-mode-only hyperplane search that uses
505   second-order information. We provide theory to go along with our method, where we show, under
506   certain assumptions, that FoMoH-KD generalizes to Newton’s method for K = D, and also that
507   the expected reduction in error from the minimum decays at an exponential rate of (D − K)/D.
508   This compares with the previous approach of FGD, which has a sub-linear convergence rate as we
509   show in §B.4. In addition to the proposed new approach and theory, we also demonstrated FoMoH-
510   KD across multiple problem settings, where we were able to empirically support the theory. For
511   the Rosenbrock function, we directly showed how FoMoH-KD tends to Newton’s method, which
512
      is consistent with Corollary 1.1. This significant result is shown in Figure 2. For the learning
      tasks of multinomial logistic regression and CNN classification, we see how the first-order opti-
513
      mization approach of FGD degrades with increasing dimension of the parameter space. We do not
514
      see this degradation for FoMoH-KD, and we also observe that the second-order information means
515   fewer epochs are needed to reach a better performance. This has the broader impact of improv-
516   ing efficiency, reducing cost, and increasing accuracy in ML optimization routines. As a further
517   contribution, we provide a Python package that implements the AD backend and interfaces with
518   PyTorch.7
