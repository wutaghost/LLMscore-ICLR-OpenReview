009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Identifying patient subgroups with different treatment responses is an important
012            task to inform medical recommendations, guidelines, and the design of future clini-
013            cal trials. Existing approaches for subgroup analysis primarily rely on Randomised
014            Controlled Trials (RCTs), in which treatment assignment is randomised. RCTs’
015            patient cohorts are often constrained by cost, rendering them not representative of
016
               the heterogeneity of patients likely to receive treatment in real-world clinical prac-
               tice. When applied to observational studies, subgroup analysis approaches suffer
017
               from significant statistical biases particularly because of the non-randomisation
018
               of treatment. Our work introduces a novel, outcome-guided method for identify-
019            ing treatment response subgroups in observational studies. Our approach assigns
020            each patient to a subgroup associated with two time-to-event distributions: one
021            under treatment and one under control regime. It hence positions itself in be-
022            tween individualised and average treatment effect estimation. The assumptions of
023            our model result in a simple correction of the statistical bias from treatment non-
024            randomisation through inverse propensity weighting. In experiments, our approach
025            significantly outperforms the current state-of-the-art method for outcome-guided
026            subgroup analysis in both randomised and observational treatment regimes.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Understanding heterogeneous therapeutic responses between patient subgroups is the core of treatment
031   guidelines and the development of new drugs. Identifying such subgroups is valuable to inform
032   clinical trials by identifying subgroups not responding to existing drugs, and to direct healthcare
033   resources to those who might benefit most, and away from those who may be most harmed (Foster
034   et al., 2011). For instance, subgroup analysis in the BARI trial of patients with coronary artery disease
035   supported the use of coronary artery bypass graft over percutaneous interventions for patients with
036   diabetes, and the opposite for patients without diabetes (Investigators, 1996), shaping subsequent
037   guidelines. Figure 1 illustrates this idea: two groups may present opposite treatment responses, and
038
      would benefit from different recommendations. Our work aims to uncover such subgroups.
039   Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) remain the gold standard for identifying subgroups of treatment
040   effects. In RCTs, patients are randomly assigned to control or treated groups, allowing researchers to
041   assess the impact of an intervention or treatment. However, RCTs are costly and time-consuming,
042   with restricted patient cohorts unrepresentative of the real-world diversity of patients who may receive
043   treatment and treatment strategies (Hernán & Robins, 2016).
044   Our work introduces a novel approach that diverges from traditional RCT-based methodologies (Nag-
045   pal et al., 2022a; 2023) by leveraging routinely collected observational data to identify patient
046   subgroups. Observational studies encompass larger and more diverse cohorts reflective of real-world
047   practices, offering the potential to uncover subgroups of treatment responses, that could be missed
048   in RCTs. Prior works (Bica et al., 2020; Curth et al., 2021; Louizos et al., 2017) have leveraged
049   observational data while addressing biases from non-random treatment assignments (Benson & Hartz,
050
      2000; Hernán & Robins, 2010; Hernán, 2018). However, this body of literature primarily focuses
      on estimating (i) averaged treatment effects at the population level or (ii) individualised treatment
051
      effects, thereby overlooking the identification of treatment effect subgroups.
052
053   Addressing this critical gap in the literature, our work uncovers patient subgroups with distinct
      treatment responses using observational data. Unlike previous methods relying on RCTs, we introduce

      Latent survival subgroup analysis. Our goal is to uncover subgroups with similar treatment
090   responses, guided by the observed times of occurrence of an outcome of interest. Patients are
091   assigned to subgroups based on their covariates at treatment time. Each subgroup is characterised by
092   two distributions, known as survival functions: one under treatment and one under control regimes.
093   These distributions characterise the probability of observing the event of interest after a given time
094   under a given treatment.
095
      Consider the random variables associated with observed covariates X, an indicator identifying
096   whether the event of interest, in our analysis death, was observed D, and the observed time of event
097   T . Formally, we define the latter random variables as T := min(C, T ′ ) and D := 1(C > T ′ ), where
098   C is random variable of the (right)-censoring time, i.e. the time at which the patient left the study
099   before experiencing the event of interest, and T ′ is the partially observed random variable associated
100   with the time of the event of interest if there was no censoring. When a patient is censored, T = C
101   and D = 0, otherwise the event is observed and T = T ′ and D = 1. To estimate the associated
102   survival likelihood, we further assume, as commonly done in survival analysis, that the censoring
103   time C is not informative for the time of the event of interest T ′ .
104   Assumption 1 (Non-informative censoring). The censoring time C is independent of the time of the
105   event of interest T ′ given the covariates X. Formally, T ′ ⊥⊥ C | X.
106
107   Central to our problem is the additional variable Z associated with the latent, unobserved subgroup
      membership. Following Jeanselme et al. (2022), we aim to identify a pre-specified K ∈ N number of

187   Estimating this quantity requires accurately modelling the survival distributions under the two

193   In RCTs, treatment A and covariates X are by design independent. Hence, the subset of the
194   patients receiving either treatment regime is representative of the overall population. Relying on
195   this observed subset to estimate the survival distribution under each regime is a valid estimate
196   for the population. In other words, maximising the factual likelihood to estimate survival under
197
      both treatment regimes results in a valid estimate. However, covariates and treatment are not
      independent in observational studies in which treatment recommendations depend upon the observed
198
      covariates. Formally, P(A | X) ̸= P(A) in general. For example, clinicians might recommend more
199

200   covariate shift between the treated and non-treated populations (Curth et al., 2021) as their covariate
201   distributions differ (Bica et al., 2021). In this setting, estimating survival by maximising the factual
202   likelihood is no-longer enough to estimate the counterfactual survival distribution.

300   First, the network W is trained to predict the binary treatment assignment by minimising the cross-

334   3.2      E MPIRICAL SETTINGS
335
      Benchmark methods. We compare the proposed approach against the state-of-the-art Cox Mixtures
336
      with Heterogeneous Effect (CMHE Nagpal et al. (2022a)5 ) which uncovers treatment effect and
337
      baseline survival latent groups. This method uses an expectation maximisation framework in which
338   each patient is assigned to a group for which a Cox model is then fitted. The central differences
339   with our proposed approach is that CMHE (i) clusters patients in treatment effects subgroups and
340   survival subgroups, and (ii) assumes a linear treatment response. This separation between survival
341   and treatment response improves the model’s flexibility at the cost of interpretability as the number of
342   groups grows exponentially, and subgroups of treatment effect are independent of survival. Further,
343   the assumption of linear treatment response may hinder the discovery of subgroups with more
344   complex responses. By contrast, our approach identifies subgroups of treatment while considering
345   survival, without constraining the treatment response. We argue that these are key strengths of our
346
      method as a group not responding to treatment with low life expectancy would most benefit from
      alternative treatments, in comparison to a group with the same treatment responses but better survival
347
      odds. Considering jointly non-linear treatment effects and survival, therefore, results in identifying
348
      more clinically relevant subgroups.
349
350   For a fair comparison, we present three alternatives of CMHE: one with fixed K = 3 survival
351   subgroups, one with L = 3 treatment effect subgroups, and one with K = L = 2, which allows for a
352
      total of 4 subgroups. Crucially, these methods assume proportional hazards for each subgroup and do
      not account for the treatment non-randomisation. Additionally, we also compare our model against
353
      its unadjusted alternative (CSC Unadjusted), which uses the unweighted factual likelihood (wi = 1)
354
      and should suffer from non-randomisation of treatment assignment.
355
356   Finally, we compare against two step-wise approaches in which clustering and treatment effect are
357   modelled separately. First, we use an unsupervised clustering algorithm on the covariates, followed
358
      by a non-parametric estimate of the treatment effect as proposed in Nagpal et al. (2022a), referred as
      Kmeans + TE in the following. Specifically, we use a K-Means (Hartigan & Wong, 1979) to cluster
359
      the data and we compute the difference between Kaplan-Meier (Kaplan & Meier, 1958) estimates
360
      between control and treated patients stratified by clusters. However, this approach is limited by
361   its implicit assumption of randomisation and its pre-specified number of subgroups, which is not
362   informed by observed outcomes. Second, we use a Virtual Twins approach to estimate individualised
363   treatment effects and then cluster them. As proposed in the literature, we propose to use a survival
364   tree to estimate response under each treatment regime and then use a KMeans on the estimated
365   treatment effects, computed as the difference between survival estimates. Note that this methodology
366   also assumes treatment randomisation, and the clustering is uninformed by the covariates. For details
367   on training and hyperparameter optimisation, we refer to App. B.2.

470   4       R ELATED WORK
471
472   While survival subgrouping has been proposed through mixtures of distributions (Nagpal et al.,
473   2021a;b; Jeanselme et al., 2022) to identify different phenotypes of patients, the machine learning
474
      literature on phenotyping treatment effects with time-to-event outcomes remains sparse. The current
      literature focuses on estimating population or individual treatment effects (Curth et al., 2021; Shalit
475
      et al., 2017; Johansson et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). While these models help to understand the
476
      average population-wide response to treatment and aim to estimate individualised treatment effects,
477   they do not provide an understanding of the patient groups that may benefit more or be harmed more
478   by treatment. Identifying such groups aligns with, and is thus more useful for, drafting medical
479   guidelines that direct treatment to those subgroups most likely to benefit from it.
480
      Discovering intervenable subgroups is core to medical practice, particularly identifying subgroups of
481
      treatment effect, as patients do not respond like the average (Bica et al., 2021; Ruberg et al., 2010;
482
      Sanchez et al., 2022) and the average may conceal differential treatment responses. Identification
483   of subgroups has long been used to design RCTs. Indeed, subgroups identified a priori can then be
484   tested through trials (Cook et al., 2004; Rothwell, 2005). A posteriori analyses have gained traction
485
          7
              Available at https://seer.cancer.gov/


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      to uncover subgroups of patients from existing RCTs to understand the underlying variability of
487   responses.
488
489   The first set of subgroup analysis methodologies consists of a step-wise approach: (i) estimate the
490
      ITE and (ii) uncover subgroups using a second model to explain the heterogeneity in ITE. Foster et al.
      (2011); Qi et al. (2021) describe the virtual twins approach in which one models the outcome using
491
      a decision tree for each treatment group. The difference between these decision trees results in the
492
      estimated treatment effects. A final decision tree aims to explain these estimated treatment effects to
493   uncover subgroups. Similar approaches have been explored with different meta-learners (Xu et al.,
494   2023), or Bayesian additive models (Hu et al., 2021), or replacing the final step with a linear predictor
495   to uncover the feature influencing heterogeneity (Chernozhukov et al., 2018). However, Guelman et al.
496   (2015) discuss the drawbacks associated with these approaches. Notably, the two-step optimisation
497   may not lead to recovery of the underlying subgroups of treatment effects.
498
      Tree-based approaches were proposed to address the limitations of step-wise approaches by jointly
499   discovering subgroups and modelling the treatment effect. Instead of traditional splits on the observed
500   outcomes, these causal trees aim to discover homogeneous splits regarding covariates and treatment
501   effects. Su et al. (2009) introduce a recursive population splitting based on the average difference
502   in treatment effect between splits. Athey & Imbens (2015; 2016) improve the confidence interval
503   estimation through the honest splitting criterion, which dissociates the splitting from the treatment
504   effect estimation. Wager & Athey (2018) agglomerate these causal trees into causal forests for
505   improved ITE estimation. Each obtained split in the decision tree delineates two subgroups of
506   treatment effect Lipkovich et al. (2011); Loh et al. (2015). Alternatively, McFowland III et al.
507
      (2018) propose pattern detection and Wang & Rudin (2022), causal rule set learning to uncover
      these subgroups. However, all these approaches rely on a local optimisation criterion (Lipkovich &
508
      Dmitrienko, 2014) and greedy split exploration. Recently, Nagpal et al. (2020) addressed the local
509
      optimisation by constraining the treatment response to a linear form in a mixture of Cox models.
510
511   Previous approaches uncover subgroups of treatment effect but consider RCTs with binary outcomes,
512   not the observational setting with inherently continuous survival outcomes that our paper explores.
513
      At the intersection with survival analysis, Zhang et al. (2017) extend causal trees to survival causal
      trees, modifying the splitting criterion by measuring the difference in survival estimates between
514
      resulting leaves. Similarly, Hu et al. (2021) propose Bayesian additive models and Zhu & Gallego
515
      (2020) propose a step-wise approach with propensity weighting to study observational data. Closest
516   to our work, (Jia et al., 2021; Nagpal et al., 2023) propose to uncover subgroups within RCTs with
517   survival outcomes. Jia et al. (2021) propose a mixture of treatment effects characterised by Weibull
518   distributions trained in an expectation-maximisation framework. Similarly, Nagpal et al. (2023)
519   stratify the population into three groups: non-, positive- and negative responders to treatment. An
520   iterative Monte Carlo optimisation is used to uncover these subgroups, characterised by a Cox model
521   with a multiplicative treatment effect. As demonstrated in our work, this step-wise optimisation may
522   be limiting, and the assumption of RCTs renders the model less relevant in observational data.

      5    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   This work fills the current gap in the literature of methodologies to identify subgroups of treatment
531   responses in observational time-to-event data. While modelling the observed outcome, the approach
532   jointly identifies patient subgroups with different treatment responses. Our experiments demonstrate
533   the capacity of our proposed method to uncover groups of treatment effects in both synthetic and
534   real-world datasets. However, causal modelling relies on empirically unverifiable assumptions. The
535   invalidity of these assumptions is a lesser concern in our targeted setting of hypothesis generation, in
536
      comparison to individual estimation of treatment recommendations.
537   Serving the purpose of a hypothesis-generating tool, we invite practitioners to further investigate the
538   subgroups identified in observational studies through RCTs to (i) validate the estimated responses,
539   (ii) identify potential alternative treatments with improved responses, and (iii) inform clinical guide-
      lines.
