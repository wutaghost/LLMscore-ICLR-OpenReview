011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Approximate second-order optimization methods have gained attention due to their
014            low computational and memory overhead. While these methods have the potential
015            to accelerate neural network training, they often exhibit poorer generalization com-
016            pared to first-order approaches. To address this limitation, we first analyze existing
017            second-order methods through the lens of the loss landscape, demonstrating that
018
               their reduced generalization performance is somewhat attributed to the sharpness
               of the solutions they converge to. In response, we introduce S ASSHA, a novel
019
               approach designed to enhance generalization by explicitly reducing sharpness. In
020
               fact, this sharpness minimization scheme is designed to accommodate lazy and
021            stable Hessian updates, so as to secure efficiency and robustness besides flatness.
022            To validate its effectiveness, we conduct a wide range of deep learning exper-
023            iments including standard vision and language tasks, where S ASSHA achieves
024            competitive performance. Notably, S ASSHA demonstrates strong generalization in
025            noisy data settings and significantly outperforms other methods in these scenarios.
026            Additionally, we verify the robustness of S ASSHA through various ablation studies.

068   2    R ELATED WORKS
069   Second order optimization for deep learning First-order methods such as Stochastic Gradient
070   Descent (SGD) are popular optimization methods for deep learning due to their low per-iteration cost
071   and good generalization (Hardt et al., 2016). However, these methods have two major drawbacks;
072   slow convergence under ill-conditioned landscapes and high sensitivity to hyper-parameter choices
073   such as learning rate (Demeniconi & Chawla, 2020). Adaptive methods (Duchi et al., 2011; Hinton
074   et al., 2012; Kingma & Ba, 2015) propose using empirical Fisher-type preconditioning to alleviate
075   these issues, though recent studies suggest their insufficiency to do so (Kunstner et al., 2019). This has
076   led to recent interest in developing efficient second-order methods tailored for large-scale problems
077
      such as Hessian-Free Inexact Newton methods (Martens et al., 2010; Kiros, 2013), stochastic quasi-
      Newton methods (Byrd et al., 2016; Gower et al., 2016), Gauss-Newton methods (Schraudolph,
078
      2002; Botev et al., 2017), natural gradient methods (Amari et al., 2000), and Kronecker-factored
079
      approximations (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Goldfarb et al., 2020). However, a further need for a much
080   scalable second-order optimizer for various large-scale deep learning scenarios has led to much recent
081   focus on using diagonal scaling methods (Bottou et al., 2018; Yao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024).
082
083   Sharpness minimization for generalization The relationship between the geometry of the loss
084
      landscape and the generalization ability of neural networks was first discussed in the work of
      Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1994), and the interest in this subject has persisted over time. Expanding
085
      on this foundation, subsequent studies have explored the impact of flat regions on generalization both
086
      empirically and theoretically (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2017; Dziugaite &
087   Roy, 2017; Neyshabur et al., 2017b; Dinh et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2020). Motivated by this, various
088   approaches have been proposed to achieve flat minima such as regularizing local entropy (Chaudhari
089   et al., 2017), averaging model weights (Izmailov et al., 2018), explicitly regularizing sharpness by
090   solving a min-max problem (Foret et al., 2021), and injecting anti-correlated noise (Orvieto et al.,
091   2022), to name a few. In particular, the sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2021) has
092   attracted significant attention for its strong generalization performance across various domains (Chen
093   et al., 2022; Bahri et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2022) and its robustness to label noise (Baek et al., 2024).
094   Nevertheless, to our knowledge, the sharpness minimization scheme has not been studied to enable
095
      second-order methods to find flat minima as of yet.
096
097   3    P RACTICAL SECOND - ORDER OPTIMIZERS CONVERGE TO SHARP MINIMA
098   Second-order optimizers have seen rising interest in the deep learning community, and yet, crucial
099   properties of their optimization process remain largely underexplored compared to their first-order
100   counterparts. In particular, SGD and its bias towards the minima of low sharpness have been studied
101   extensively in recent years, which has revealed a strong correlation with its remarkable generalization
102   performance (Keskar et al., 2017; Ghorbani et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2020). This raises
103   the following question: what minima do second-order optimizers prefer, and how do they correlate
104
      with their generalization capability? In this section, we examine through various sharpness metrics
      employed in recent studies and analyze their correlation with generalization performance.
105

211   Avoiding critical points The objective of training deep neural networks is known to be highly
212   non-convex with saddle points and local maxima (Dauphin et al., 2014; Choromanska et al., 2015).
213   One problem that arises from naively applying the approach of Section 4.1 to a non-convex objective
214   is that it can ascend in the directions of negative Hessian eigenvalues towards saddle points or local
215   maxima, due to the first-order optimality condition being valid only when all stationary points are
      minima. While this classical issue of naive second-order optimizers has led to the introduction of

219   et al. (2021) to apply the absolute function to adjust the negative entries of the diagonal Hessian to be

392   5.4   ROBUSTNESS TO LABEL NOISE
393
      We evaluate the robustness of our method against label noise (Natarajan et al., 2013), a common
394
      issue in real-world scenarios where the training data is incorrectly labeled. To this end, we compare
395   the validation performance of different optimizers on ResNet-32 trained with the CIFAR datasets
396   under various noise levels. The results, summarized in Table 5, show that S ASSHA outperforms other
397   optimizers across all noise levels with minimal accuracy degradation; at most outperform by 8% in
398   60% noise ratio compared to SGD.
399
      Interestingly, our method surpasses SAM (Foret et al., 2021), which is known to be one of the most
400
      robust techniques against label noise (Baek et al., 2024). We hypothesize that S ASSHA’s superior
401   robustness stems from the combined benefits of SAM and second-order methods. Specifically, SAM
402   enhances robustness by applying adversarial perturbations to the weights and giving more importance
403   to clean data during optimization, making the model more resistant to label noise (Foret et al., 2021;
404   Baek et al., 2024). Also, recent research indicates that second-order optimizers are robust to label
405   noise (Amari et al., 2021) due to appropriate preconditioning that reduces the variance caused by label
406   noise in the population risk. We believe these two complementary mechanisms work synergistically
407   within S ASSHA to enhance its robustness.

412   In this study, we examine the stabilizing effect of the square-root function on S ASSHA. Precisely,
413   we conduct multiple runs of S ASSHA without the square-root (No-Sqrt) over different random
414   seeds for training ResNet-32 on CIFAR-100, which reveals instances where the training loss diverges.
415   To gain further insight into this phenomenon, we measure the update size and the preconditioned
416   step size (i.e., ∥ηD−1 ∥F , where D is either |H|
                                                      b 1/2 or |H|
                                                                b and ∥ · ∥F denotes the Frobenius norm)
417   of each iteration, along with the density of the diagonal precondition entry values Dii at step 100
418   to 250. Results are presented in Figure 4. We first observe that the training loss diverges precisely
419   when the update size (Figure 4b), particularly the preconditioned step size (Figure 4c), starts to spike
420   around step 200, suggesting that the preconditioner reaches some critical condition at this point.
421
      Further investigation into individual preconditioner entries (Figure 4d) reveals that this is likely due
      to a progressive increase in near-zero diagonal Hessian entries from the sharpness minimization
422
      penalizing the Hessian eigenvalues, which could have caused instability when the preconditioner is
423
      inverted. With the inclusion of the square-root, we can see that the values within the preconditioner
424   are less situated near zero, effectively suppressing the risk of large updates thereby stabilizing the
425   training process.

467    Sharpness minimization can improve lazy Hessian training Why would S ASSHA be so robust
468    under a lazy Hessian update? We first reasonably assume that this is due to the presence of sharpness-
469    minimization, as it is the primary component differentiating S ASSHA from other approximate
470
       second-order optimizers. To verify our hypothesis, we perform the same ablation on the Hessian
       update interval on the S ASSHA without sharpness minimization (Baseline1 ), which we report
471
       in Figure 5a. We indeed observe that Baseline doesn’t perform as well as S ASSHA under lazy
472
       Hessian training, showing similar performance to AdaHessian.
473
474    Furthermore, we hypothesize that sharpness minimization aids the lazy Hessian update by biasing
475    the optimization path toward the region of low curvature sensitivity. To quantify this, we define
476
       local Hessian sensitivity as the maximum change in Hessian induced from normalized random

       Here we discuss the theoretical computation cost of S ASSHA and M-S ASSHA in terms of backpropa-
1205   gation query. The average backpropagation cost (i.e., total BP / number of iterations) of S ASSHA
1206   is (2 + 1/k) BP. For the lazy Hessian interval k = 10 used in our evaluations, this corresponds to
1207   2.1 BP. The calculation is as follows: when performing a total of T iterations, the total cost includes
1208   T BP for gradient calculation, T BP for sharpness minimization, and T /k BP for diagonal Hessian
1209   approximation performed once every k iterations. This results in a total of (2 + 1/k)T BP, yielding
1210   an average of (2 + 1/k) BP per iteration. Compared with SAM, S ASSHA requires only 5% more
1211   BP on average. M-S ASSHA significantly reduces the cost, only requiring 10% BP compared to
1212   Adam/SGD. To measure these resource consumption in practice, we report the wall-clock time of
