009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A recent trend in text-to-speech synthesis (TTS) is to construct models capable
012            of generating naturalistic speech that adheres to a textual style prompt describ-
013            ing the speaker’s voice and speaking style. In this paper, we propose a crisper
014            definition of style-prompted TTS by categorizing style tags by how they can be
015            collected (automatic tags obtainable using signal processing tools e.g. low-pitched
016            and slow; demographic tags obtainable using speaker demographics e.g. male and
017            American accent; and abstract tags which need human-annotations e.g. authorita-
018
               tive and awed) and what they represent (intrinsic tags inherent to speaker identity
               e.g. gender, average pitch, texture; and situational tags specific to utterance-level
019
               speaking styles e.g. emotion). Compared to previous work, we expand the space
020
               of style prompts substantially by covering 47 abstract tags, 10 demographic tags
021            and 6 automatic tags. For abstract intrinsic tags, we annotate a subset of speak-
022            ers from the VoxCeleb (Nagrani et al., 2020) dataset. For abstract situational tags,
023            we leverage existing speaking-style-based datasets Expresso (Nguyen et al., 2023)
024            and EARS (Richter et al., 2024). We train a style-prompted TTS model based on
025            Parler-TTS (Lyth & King, 2024; Lacombe et al., 2024b) using these datasets and
026            find that our model outperforms baselines on speech-style consistency metrics.
027            Our collected dataset and model will be open-sourced.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Text-to-speech systems that are controllable by natural language text style prompts a.k.a. style-
033
      prompted TTS systems e.g. (Guo et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2023; Vyas et al., 2023;
      Lacombe et al., 2024b; Jin et al., 2024) have been gaining prominence in the past few years. Rather
034
      than providing control via a few seconds of reference speech (Peng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023)
035
      exhibiting the desired style, these models allow users to do so via natural language instead, which
036   provides a more explicit, intuitive, and privacy-preserving control medium. Training these models
037   requires a dataset which has speech utterances annotated with natural language style prompts.
038
039
      When humans describe speech speaking styles in natural language, they do so with a rich and di-
      verse vocabulary spanning a wide range of style tags covering aspects like pitch, texture, emotion
040
      and rhythm and more. We propose a crisper definition of style-prompted TTS that rigorously catego-
041
      rizes style tags along two axes: the mechanism by which one can obtain tag annotations (automatic,
042   demographic and abstract tags) and what speech aspects the tag represents (intrinsic tags and situa-
043   tional tags). Based on an extensive survey of previous style-prompted TTS work (Section 2), we find
044   that existing work offers natural-language control over some, but not all of these categories, often
045   overlooking the importance of covering more abstract speech style tags that cannot be automatically
046   extracted. More importantly, the only current open-source model, Parler-TTS (Lyth & King, 2024;
047   Lacombe et al., 2024b) only supports automatic tags, which motivates the need for an open-source
048   model that can support all categories.
049   We create a list of 63 style tags consisting of 26 abstract intrinsic, 21 abstract situational, 10 demo-
050   graphic and 6 automatic tags covering a wide variety of speech styles. As described in Section 3,
051   to support all these tags, we collect abstract intrinsic human annotations for a subset of the Vox-
052   Celeb (Nagrani et al., 2020) dataset, creating StyledVoxCeleb. While not originally proposed for
053   style-prompted TTS, Expresso (Nguyen et al., 2023) and EARS (Richter et al., 2024) cover a rich
      variety of abstract situational speaking styles and hence we reuse them for TTS. We finetune Parler-

      2       BACKGROUND AND M OTIVATION
087
088
      We can describe speech styles with a rich and diverse vocabulary, capturing aspects such as pitch,
089   emotion, rhythm, speaking rate and more. We draw a distinction between intrinsic tags that are tied
090   to a speaker’s identity and persist across all utterances belonging to that speaker (e.g. average pitch
091   and vocal texture) and situational tags that describe the speaking style (e.g. emotion) of individual
092   utterances. 1 This distinction is important for deciding a data collection strategy; while intrinsic tags
093   can be annotated on a per-speaker basis, situational tags must be annotated on a per-utterance basis,
094   which is significantly more expensive. Furthermore, depending on how the tags can be collected,
095   we distinguish between abstract tags that are complex and require human annotations (e.g. clarity,
096   texture, emotion) , demographic tags obtainable from speaker demographics (e.g. gender and accent)
097
      and automatic tags 2 that are obtainable via signal processing tools (e.g. pitch/F0, energy, speaking
      rate). Abstract and automatic tags can be both intrinsic and situational, while demographic tags are
098
      always intrinsic since they pertain to speaker demographics. In the rest of this paper, we classify
099
      tags into four categories: abstract intrinsic, abstract situational, demographic and automatic tags
100   (combining intrinsic and situational automatic tags).
101
102   Style-prompted TTS should support a diverse space of speech style prompts, covering many tags
103
      across all categories. We perform a comparison of prior work summarized in Table 1 and make
      several observations. First, we notice that none of the previous datasets apart from AudioBox and

      A BSTRACT I NTRINSIC TAGS We create the StyledVoxCeleb dataset by annotating a subset of
175   VoxCeleb (Nagrani et al., 2020) (a dataset consisting of natural, in-the-wild speech from YouTube
176   celebrity interviews with high speaker diversity spanning accents, ages and ethnicities) with abstract
177   intrinsic tags by hiring workers on Amazon Mechanical Turk. We apply this annotation to all utter-
178   ances spoken by that speaker. This data collection effort is complementary to prior work (Kawamura
179   et al., 2024) that collected such data for the LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023) dataset. We show in
180   Section 5 that our dataset outperforms LibriTTS-P when evaluated for speech-style consistency.
181
182   Quality Control We provide a qualification task to Amazon Mechanical Turk workers to check
183   their ability to understand style tags. The task consists of 6 manually selected pairs of speech clips
184   where one exhibits a style and one doesn’t. We ask annotators to select which one exhibits the style
185   and keep only those 38 annotators that succeeded on at least 5 examples; details in Appendix E.
186
187   Collecting Annotations Given a speaker, we create a representative audio file consisting of mul-
188   tiple utterances (3 − 8 clips whose total duration is 20 − 40 seconds) concatenated together. We
189
      provide this audio file, the speaker’s name and a list of our intrinsic speech style tags with defini-
      tions (see Appendix A) to annotators on Amazon Mechanical Turk and ask them to write at least 3
190
      distinct style tags. Our annotation UI can be viewed at Appendix E. For every celebrity, we collect
191
      5 annotations. We observe that the annotations are very subjective and different annotators select
192   different tags for the same celebrity. Therefore, we keep only those tags that at least 2 annotators
193   agree on in our train and dev set, and only those that at least 3 annotators agree on in our test set.
194
195   Selecting Celebrities We expect famous or distinctive celebrities to be more familiar to annota-
196   tors. We select such a subset using three loose heuristics: (a) we parse an IMDb list of 163 celebrities
197   with distinctive voices 3 and find 39 in VoxCeleb, (b) we ask ChatGPT to name 300 celebrities with
198   distinctive voices and find 112 in VoxCeleb, and (c) we find Wikipedia pages for VoxCeleb celebrity
199   using the Python Wikipedia API 4 and select the top 200 celebrities by length of their Wikipedia
200   pages, assuming page length is a proxy for fame. Combining all three sources and accounting for
201   overlap, we obtain a list of 302 celebrities. After collecting annotations for these celebrities, we find
202   that the style tag distribution is imbalanced, with 12 tags 5 having fewer than 5000 annotated clips.
203
      We use GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) to obtain a rough list of celebrities that are likely to have these
      tags by instructing it to output a list of style tags that are associated with a celebrity’s voice (details
204
      in Appendix D) for every celebrity in VoxCeleb. Since we can only provide the celebrity’s name
205
      rather than the actual speech clip, GPT-4 needs to rely on its parametric knowledge base in order
206   to complete this task; while imperfect, it may still provide some signal towards which celebrities
207   to target. We select a maximum of 40 celebrities per tag, ending up with a list of 187 additional
208   celebrities to annotate (most tags have far fewer than 40 celebrities labelled by GPT-4). Finally, we
209   annotate 107 additional celebrities, resulting in a total of 596 celebrities for StyledVoxCeleb.

      A BSTRACT S ITUATIONAL TAGS We reuse Expresso (Nguyen et al., 2023) and EARS (Richter
217   et al., 2024), two existing expressive speech datasets that consist of speakers acting out various
218   emotions and speaking styles. Expresso contains 4 speakers while EARS contains 107. We filter
219   out neutral and non-speaking utterances in both datasets, lightly preprocess them (details in Ap-
220   pendix C) and then label each utterance by simply mapping the reading styles in each dataset to our
221   tag vocabulary (details in Table 6). For example, the projected style in Expresso gets mapped to the
222   tag loud. We split the Expresso dataset into train (80%), dev (10%), and test (10%), ensuring that
223   there is no transcript overlap across splits. Some utterances in EARS do not have emotion labels;
224   we place them all into the train set. We split the utterances that have emotion labels into train (80%),
225
      dev (10%), and test (10%), ensuring overall that there is no transcript overlap across splits.
226
227
      D EMOGRAPHIC AND AUTOMATIC TAGS We use either dataset metadata or GPT-4 for obtaining
      accent and gender, and automatic signal processing tools for extracting pitch and speaking rate.
228
      Following previous work (Lyth & King, 2024), we also extract the noise level of the audio (this is
229
      not a style tag; rather, it aids the model to differentiate between clean and noisy speech). We extract
230   these tags for all 3 datasets: StyledVoxCeleb, Expresso and EARS. Additionally, we annotate a 150-
231   hr subset of the train split of the LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023) dataset (along with its dev and
232   test sets) we call LTTSR-150 with gender, pitch, speaking rate and noise levels.
233
234   Gender and Accent For StyledVoxCeleb, we prompt GPT-4 with the name of the celebrity and
235   ask it to output the celebrity’s gender and accent. 6 The prompt and generation details are available
236   in Appendix D. We use dataset metadata for Expresso, EARS and LibriTTS-R (for EARS, we use
237   the ‘native language’ column of the dataset as a proxy for accent).
238
239   Pitch, Speaking Rate and Noise Levels We use the Dataspeech (Lacombe et al., 2024a) library
240   to label our datasets with pitch, speaking rate, and noise levels. For pitch, we use PENN 7 using
241   default hyperparameters and compute the mean pitch across all utterances of a given speaker. Then,
242
      we apply gender-dependent thresholds to label each speaker with low-pitched (male: < 115.7 Hz,
      female: < 141.6 Hz), high-pitched (male: > 149.7 Hz, female > 184.5 Hz) or medium-pitched
243
      (male: 115.7 Hz < x < 149.7 Hz, female: 141.6 Hz < x < 184.5 Hz); these thresholds are
244
      gender-dependent since humans perceive male speakers to have lower pitch than female speakers on
245   average. For speaking rate, we use g2p 8 to convert the text transcription to phoneme transcriptions
246   and then use the number of phonemes per second (PPS) as the speaking rate. We apply thresholds to
247   label each utterance with slow (< 11.5 PPS), fast (> 19.1 PPS) and measured (11.5 PPS < x < 19.1
248   PPS). Finally, for noise levels, we use the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) extracted using Brouhaha 9 and
249   use Parler-TTS (Lacombe et al., 2024b)’s noise bins to assign each utterance one of the following
250   noise tags: very noisy, quite noisy, slightly noisy, moderate ambient sound, slightly clear, quite clear,
251   very clear.

      Model Architecture The crux of Parler-TTS is an autoregressive decoder speech language model
271   that generates DAC (Kumar et al., 2023) audio tokens. To condition on text transcripts, the text
272   transcript is tokenized using the Flan-T5 (Chung et al., 2022) tokenizer, passed through a linear
273   embedding layer, and prepended to the input sequence of the decoder. To condition on the text
274   style prompt, the text encoder, a frozen Flan-T5 model, maps the text style prompt to a sequence of
275   hidden-state representations that are attended to via cross-attention layers in the decoder.
276
277   Training We initialize our model with the parler-tts/parler-tts-mini-v1 open-
278   source checkpoint and use the official Parler-TTS 10 library to finetune on the training splits of
279   the 4 datasets we set up; StyledVoxCeleb, Expresso, EARS and LTTSR-150. We train on 4 NVIDIA
280   A40 GPUs with a batch size of 8 and 2 gradient accumulation steps. We train for 9 epochs with a
281
      non-warmup cosine learning rate scheduler, a peak learning rate of 0.00008 and a weight decay of
      0.01.
282
283
      Inference We perform inference runs using the default Parler-TTS generation hyperparameters
284
      (temperature 1.0, repetition penalty 1.0, 2580 total tokens). Since autoregressive TTS is prone to
285   decoding instabilities, we attempt to mitigate this by retrying inference a maximum of 3 times,
286   stopping when the WER between the ASR transcript of the generated sample and the input text
287   (using the same setup as our WER evaluation metric) falls below 20 or choosing the sample with the
288   lowest WER out of the 3 generated samples.

291

293   tag in our tag vocabulary, we find a maximum of 5 clips that have been annotated with that tag and
294   select them for inclusion in our evaluation dataset. For each clip, we refer to this tag as its tag of
295
      interest. We randomly select pitch and speaking rate with a 50% probability for inclusion along with
      the tag of interest, gender, noise level and then generate a style prompt from these tags for use in
296
      evaluation. Our final evaluation dataset consists of 298 clips.

      4.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
299
300   We use metrics that evaluate the speech clip for three desiderata: speech quality, content correctness
301   and speech-style consistency. For human evaluation metrics, we use annotators recruited on Amazon
302   Mechanical Turk; Appendix E contains details about our annotation user interfaces and annotation
303   costs. For every human evaluation metric, we collect 3 human annotation scores per test dataset
304   item. We report the mean and 95% confidence intervals of the MOS scores (Ribeiro et al., 2011).
305
306   Speech Quality Following previous work (Vyas et al., 2023; Kawamura et al., 2024), we compute
307   a Naturalness MOS metric where each human annotator is provided speech clips and asked to rate
308   its naturalness and realisticity (human-likeness) on 5-point Likert scales.
309
310   Content Correctness We report a WER metric that computes the Word Error Rate (WER) be-
311
      tween (a) the ASR transcript of the speech clip and (b) the input transcript, after applying a text
      normalizer to both texts. We use the distil-whisper/distil-large-v2 (Gandhi et al., 2023) model for
312
      ASR, and Whisper11 for text normalization.
313
314
      Speech-Style Consistency Following Kawamura et al. (2024) and Ji et al. (2024), we report a
315
      Consistency MOS metric where each human annotator is provided a speech clip and the input style
316   prompt and asked to rate the consistency between the two on a 5-point Likert scale.
317
318   In addition, we report fine-grained tag-level evaluation. Instead of evaluating adherence to the whole
319
      style prompt (e.g., A woman’s speech is delivered slowly with a high-pitched tone, expressing dis-
      gusted emotions, in a clear and quiet environment), we ask annotator to select style tags they hear.
320
      For example, for the same example, annotator might select female, disgusted as pronounced style.

334   4.3   BASELINES
335
336   Due to the absence of open-source style-prompted TTS models other than Parler-TTS, all our base-
337   lines finetune Parler-TTS on different datasets with the same training and inference setup as ours.
338
339   Init. This is the Parler-TTS model that we initialize all models with.
340
341   +LTTSR We finetune Parler-TTS on the LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023) dataset. We extract
342   gender tags using dataset metadata and automatic tags using our signal processing pipeline for ex-
343   tracting pitch, speaking rate and noise levels. While Parler-TTS is already trained on LibriTTS-R, it
344
      uses different binning thresholds for pitch and speaking rate; this baseline ablates that mismatch.
345
      +LTTSP,Exp,EARS We finetune Parler-TTS on a combination of existing datasets that cover all
346
      tag categories: Expresso and EARS for abstract situational tags and LibriTTS-P (Kawamura et al.,
347
      2024) which annotates the LibriTTS-R dataset with abstract intrinsic tags. LibriTTS-P provides 3
348   annotations (each consisting of a list of style tags) per speaker and each style tag optionally has one
349   of two qualifiers (slightly and very) that indicates the strength of the style tag. We preprocess the
350   annotations by removing tags with the slightly qualifier and remapping some style tags to those in

420
421   Style-Prompted Text-to-Speech Models Table 1 already compares several existing style-
422   prompted text-to-speech papers with respect to our tag categorizations. PromptTTS (Guo et al.,
423
      2022), one of the first papers to introduce style-prompted TTS, consists of 4 emotions and automatic
      tags and is trained on a synthetic emotion dataset; PromptTTS2 (Leng et al., 2023), a successor fo-
424
      cuses on an improved model architecture. Other emotion-focused work includes InstructTTS (Yang
425
      et al., 2023), PromptStyle (Liu et al., 2023) and MEAD-TTS (Guan et al., 2024) which focus on
426   collecting or annotating emotional data using human voice actors or annotators. TextrolSpeech (Ji
427   et al., 2024) also focuses on emotion by collating several existing emotion classification datasets
428   for use for style-prompted TTS. Recently, Parler-TTS (Lacombe et al., 2024b; Lyth & King, 2024),
429   AudioBox (Vyas et al., 2023) and SpeechCraft (Jin et al., 2024) proposed scaling up style-prompted
430   TTS to a much larger pool of data; while Parler-TTS and SpeechCraft did so solely using automatic
431   tagging pipelines, AudioBox used a combination of automatically tagged data and internally anno-
      tated stylistic datasets to train the model. LibriTTS-P (Kawamura et al., 2024) explores abstract

      intrinsic tag annotations by annotating speakers in LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023). Other rele-
433   vant, contemporaneous style-prompted TTS work includes Chen et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2024);
434   Yamamoto et al. (2024).

      Style Control for other Speech Tasks Recent work has explored natural language style prompts
437   for tasks other than TTS. DreamVoice (Hai et al., 2024), like LibriTTS-P, annotates LibriTTS-R
438   with abstract intrinsic tags, but for the task of voice conversion. The contemporaneous VCTK-
439   RVA (Sheng et al., 2024) annotates the VCTK dataset with intrinsic tags for training a speech editing
440   system that conditions on a style prompt instruction.

443   7   C ONCLUSION
444
445   We propose a crisper definition of speech style tags, categorizing into abstract intrinsic, abstract
446   situational, demographic and automatic tags. We use this to substantially expand the space of style
447
      prompts by supporting 63 total tags. Emphasizing the importance of abstract tags, we collect intrin-
      sic tag human annotations for a subset of speakers in the VoxCeleb (Nagrani et al., 2020) dataset to
448
      create StyledVoxCeleb, and reuse Expresso (Nguyen et al., 2023) and EARS (Richter et al., 2024)
449
      for situational tags. We train style-prompted TTS models based on Parler-TTS (Lacombe et al.,
450   2024b; Lyth & King, 2024) that show improved performance on speech-style consistency metrics
451   compared to competitive baselines, while they underperform baselines on speech quality and content
452   correctness metrics.

455   8   L IMITATIONS
456
457   Expensive human annotation Our dataset collection strategy relies on expensive, slow human
458   annotation for abstract intrinsic and situational tags. While it is significantly cheaper to annotate
459   intrinsic tags on a speaker level rather than situational tags on an utterance level, it is unclear how
460
      to substantially and cheaply scale either type of annotation. Future work could potentially look
      into using synthetic data augmentation (Défossez et al., 2024) for automatically expanding existing
461
      annotated datasets.
462
463
464
      Noisy data While VoxCeleb is beneficial as it has a high diversity of speakers and is sourced
      from a more realistic, in-the-wild speech domain, it negatively affects model performance when
465
      evaluated for speech quality and content correctness due to inherent background noise in a majority
466
      of its utterances. While this affects our models that are trained on a subset of VoxCeleb, we expect
467   that scaling to more speakers will mitigate this issue to some extent.
468
469
      Language coverage We limit our current experiments to English data; there is a lot of potential
470
      to expand style-prompted TTS to more languages, both in terms of the language of the utterance
471   and the language of the style prompt. Some work (Jin et al., 2024; Yamamoto et al., 2024) explores
472   other languages like Chinese and Japanese in addition to English for style-prompted TTS.
