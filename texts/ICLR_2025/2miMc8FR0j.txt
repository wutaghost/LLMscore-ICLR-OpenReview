032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Content analysis is a cornerstone research method in the social sciences and humanities, offering
035   a systematic and quantitative approach to interpreting complex, unstructured data (Holsti, 1969;
036   Krippendorff, 2018; Neuendorf, 2017; Riffe et al., 2023). It converts qualitative information into
037   structured, quantitative data by categorizing text based on theory-driven frameworks (Krippendorff,
038   2018; Weber, 1990) for scholars across disciplines including political science (Benoit, 2014), soci-
039   ology (Dart, 2014), communication (Macnamara, 2005), and psychology (Hara et al., 2000).
040   However, traditional content analysis is labor-intensive and time-consuming (Hopkins & King,
041   2010; Zhao & Wong, 2024). Its standard procedures require a team of researchers to manually
042   annotate sizable datasets (e.g., 500–1,000 entries), iteratively refining coding schemes and rules in
043   3–5 rounds to ensure reliability and validity of findings (Cohen, 1960; Krippendorff, 2018; Riffe
044   et al., 2023). This manual process, while rigorous, presents two challenges: First, it relies heavily
045   on domain-specific knowledge and individual scholars, potentially introducing subjectivity and lim-
046   iting generalizability Second, the need for substantial human resources makes it difficult to scale,
047
      particularly as the volume of digital data continues to grow exponentially.
048   Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly in the development of large language
049   models (LLMs), present opportunities to address these challenges (Ziems et al., 2024). LLMs have
050   demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation (Zhao et al.,
051   2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Kevian et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023), offering a potential solution to
052   automate the content analysis process. However, existing LLM-driven approaches often lack the
053
      depth of human-like reasoning and adaptability, limiting their effectiveness in domain-specific tasks
      that require fine-grained understanding and iterative refinement.

102   2    R ELATED W ORKS
103
104   Content Analysis. Content analysis has long been a foundational method in the social sciences
105   and humanities, providing a structured approach to converting qualitative text into quantitative data
106   (Holsti, 1969; Krippendorff, 2018; Neuendorf, 2017; Riffe et al., 2023). Traditional content analysis
107   methods have been applied across disciplines like political science (Benoit, 2014), sociology (Dart,
      2014), media studies (Macnamara, 2005), and psychology (Hara et al., 2000). Recently, content

108
      analysis has significantly advanced the understanding of complex social issues, ranging from po-
109   litical polarization (Conover et al., 2011) to emotional contagion (Kramer et al., 2014) and group
110   dynamics (Holsti, 1969). These methods rely on manual annotation by human coders, who use pre-
111   defined rules to categorize text, often iteratively refining their coding schemes in multiple rounds
112   of discussions (Riffe et al., 2023). Although manual content analysis provides robust, theory-driven
113   insights, it remains labor-intensive, time-consuming, and prone to subjectivity (Hopkins & King,
114   2010). Furthermore, as the volume of digital text increases, scaling traditional methods to accom-
115   modate larger datasets has become increasingly challenging (Zhao & Wong, 2024).
116   Recent advances in AI, particularly in natural language processing (NLP) and large language mod-
117   els (LLMs), are beginning to offer automated solutions for content analysis (Eloundou et al., 2023;
118   Achiam et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024). Automated content analysis using machine learning tech-
119   niques can efficiently analyze large-scale datasets while maintaining accuracy in text categoriza-
120   tion (Chew et al., 2023; Ziems et al., 2024). However, these methods still struggle to match the
121   nuanced judgment of human experts, especially in subject domains where context and interpretative
122   depth are crucial (Team et al., 2023). Therefore, an urgent need exists for advanced frameworks that
123
      integrate AI’s scalability with the depth and adaptability of human judgment.
124   Multi-agent Systems for Social Science. Multi-agent systems (MAS) have become increasingly
125   prevalent in computational social science, modeling social phenomena through agent interactions
126   representing individuals or groups with predefined behaviors or decision-making rules. (Van der
127   Hoek & Wooldridge, 2008; Chen et al., 2021; Chmura & Pitz, 2007; Macal, 2016; Lee et al., 2018;
128   Chen et al., 2018; Dehkordi et al., 2023). Recent work explores MAS by simulating human-like
129
      deliberation for more nuanced decision-making such as data interpretation (Gürcan, 2024; Turgut
      & Bozdag, 2023). However, existing systems often lack mechanisms of inter-agent interactions or
130
      dynamic updates of decision rules (Gheyle & Jacobs, 2017). To fill this gap, our framework inno-
131
      vatively integrates LLM-based agents to simulate independent human coder deliberation, faciliate
132   iterative, adaptive discussions between coders, and allow for dynamic updates of coding rules.
133
134   Human Intervention. Human intervention remains essential for the reliable deployment of AI-
135
      driven systems (Renner, 2020; Shoshitaishvili et al., 2017). As a general framework, Human-in-the-
      loop (HITL) systems allow experts to refine AI outputs, ensuring alignment with domain-specific
136
      knowledge and mitigating algorithmic bias (Mosqueira-Rey et al., 2023; Ghai & Mueller, 2022;
137
      Xu et al., 2023; Jolfaei et al., 2022). This is particularly important in social sciences and humani-
138   ties, where interpretative depth and contextual sensitivity are critical (Goodsell, 2013). Recent ap-
139   proaches (Arambepola & Munasinghe, 2021) integrate expert feedback to adjust categories or cod-
140   ing schemes iteratively. Our framework significantly extends this line of work by designing different
141   modes of human–AI collaboration informed by social influence theories (Cialdini & Cialdini, 2007;
142   French, 1959) and human-computer interaction theories (Suchman, 1987; Sundar, 2020). First, AI
143   and humans can collaborate through two relational dynamics, depending on the level of authority
144   in their roles: persuasion (a collaborative structure) or supervision (a top-down structure). While
145   persuasion may foster a more mutual, collaborative learning loop, supervision tends to be more
146
      straightforward and efficient. Second, human–AI collaboration can differ in terms of how much
      control humans have over AI outputs. Higher human control spanning all phases of content analysis
147
      increases costs and reduces automation, but it enables AI to better align their reasoning with human
148
      experts over time. A theory-informed factorial experiment with four conditions, combining the two
149   factors, enables us to identify the most effective and efficient mode of human intervention.

152   3   P RELIMINARY: C ONTENT A NALYSIS IN S OCIAL S CIENCE
153
154
      Social scientists conduct content analysis by manually annotating textual data to uncover potential
155
      patterns and insights. Two or more social scientists first develop a codebook with a set of coding
156   rules, grounded in relevant social science theories and contextualized within the given text corpus.
157   Guided by the codebook, each social scientist then independently labels a small set of text entries
158   (e.g., 10–20), after which they meet to discuss and resolve inconsistencies, leading to more refined
159   and specific coding rules in the updated codebook. This process iterates for 3–5 rounds until conver-
160   gence. The finalized codebook is applied by each social scientist to separately label a larger number
161   of different text entries. Despite its rigor, content analysis is highly labor-intensive, time-consuming,
      and subject to individual biases, which presents challenges in terms of scalability and consistency.

203   We propose SCALE to Simulate Content Analysis via LLM multiagEnt by automating key phases,
204   including text coding, inter-agent discussion, and dynamic codebook updating. Overall, our method
205   can be illustrated in four steps as illustrated in Figure 2.
206
      Coder Simulation. Before the simulation, we initialize agents and the codebook. We first initialize
207   N LLM agents A = {ai }N        i=1 to enact well-trained social scientists using system prompts with
208   N persona P = {pi }N   i=1 , which, except names, is derived from the real world social scientist for
209   authentic role-playing. Based on the specific task, we initiate a codebook C with Nr human-expert
210   predefined rules C = {ri }N  r
                                 i=1 or empty set ∅ where agents need to propose and update the codebook
211   from scratch. For simplicity, we consider each rule to represent one scenario and can be used to code
212   text into one unique discrete category. As shown in Figure 2(a), one agent is acting as Emily Carter,
213   with 20 years of experience in qualitative research. Note that we also initialized other agents named
214   Michael and Sarah, which are omitted due to the space limit, can be found in Appendix A.1.1. In this
215   case, the codebook contains specific rules for classifying narrative event sequences, which guides
      the process of categorizing text into multiple events.

239   Agent Discussion. In this phase, agents discuss the inconsistent results in order to reach agreement.
240   For each text, outputs from each agent will be checked. If any agent generates different coding
241   results from the other, the agents will conduct an K-round discussion, where each agent updates its
242   response based on responses from the others until all agents reach agreement or the round reaches
243   the maximum limit. The prompt for the discussion phase is listed in Appendix A.1.3. An example
244
      can refer to Figure 2(c), where Michael (another social scientist) disagrees with the coding result
      from Emily and maintains his original statement.
245
246   Codebook Evolution. In this step, agents update the codebook based on the discussion. A desir-
247   able codebook should comprehensively address all possible scenarios present in the text samples,
248   ensuring that each rule is distinct, applied at least once, and has minimal or no overlap with other
249   rules. There are two common types of codebook updates. The first involves enriching specific rules
250
      by adding examples and explanations. The second involves adding, removing, or modifying rules,
      which enables agents to adjust the number of categories in the codebook. In practice, agents first
251
      propose a draft of the codebook, then engage in a multi-round discussion to refine it until agreement
252
      is reached. The finalized codebook is then used to update the original and serves as the guideline
253   for the next round of text coding. This formulates the content analysis process into a task loop. We
254   design a series of fine-grained prompts, which allows the sophisticated codebook update process as
255   shown in Appendix A.1.4. As shown in Figure 2(d), Emily expanded the original categories and
256   clarified existing rules in this phase.

258   4.2   H UMAN I NTERVENTION
259
      We further design different modes of human intervention that allow human experts to provide feed-
260
      back for agents and foster AI–human collaboration. Specifically, human experts can intervene with
261
      agent discussions through two mechanisms: varying the scope of intervention (low or high) and
262   altering relational dynamics (persuasion vs. supervision).
263
264   Low Intervention. The intervention scope is limited to the inter-agent discussion.
265   High Intervention. The intervention can be applied to both coding discussion and codebook.
266
      Persuader. LLM agents treat human experts as additional agents, and they can either accept or
267
      reject suggestions and feedback from human experts.
268
269   Supervisor. Human experts behave as absolute authority to LLM agents. LLM agents have to follow
      all the instructions human experts gave.

319   classification accuracy for all multi-class classification tasks. We define the accuracy for multi-label

      Bafter /B. The increase in the agreement rate is defined as ∆Agr = PostAgr − PreAgr.

      5.4.3   Q3: H OW RELIABLE DO LLM SOCIAL SCIENTISTS PROPOSE CODEBOOKS ?
459
460   To answer Q3, we analyze the codebook proposed and updated by LLM agents. We discover that
461   lLM agents are capable of enhancing codebooks in less structural ways, such as adding details and
462   examples for improved clarity. For instance, in the PIS dataset’s codebook update process, which
463   can refer to Appendix A.3.4, after the first round of discussion, Agent 1 suggested enhancing the
464   original codebook by incorporating examples for each sentiment category (positive, neutral, and
465   negative) to ensure consistent interpretation. Agent 2, on the other hand, initially found the original
466   codebook sufficient without any changes. After discussion, the final codebook combined Agent 1’s
467
      examples with Agent 2’s preference for simplicity, resulting in a version both agents agreed met
      the criteria for clarity and reliability. This process aligns with the principles of content analysis,
468
      helping facilitate agent judgment convergence in the following rounds. However, the agents were
469
      less adept at adjusting codebook categories. For example, in all rounds of FWPE dataset codebook
470   updates, both agents maintained that the categorization of 12 discrete emotions (e.g., anger, sadness,
471   hope) was appropriate, diverging from human experts who ultimately dropped 2 categories due to
472   overlapping semantic boundaries.
473
      The agents’ challenge in making structural updates to the codebook (e.g., adjusting categories) may
474
      stem from their reliance on predefined rules and patterns in training data. LLMs may lack domain
475
      knowledge and theory-guided nuanced reasoning to detect subtle conceptual overlaps (e.g., between
476   anger and disappointment or between happiness and pride), leading to rigid adherence to existing
477   category structures and conceptual boundaries. Human experts, on the other hand, can apply more
478   domain knowledge (e.g., the appraisal theory) and theory-based, contextualized reasoning to recog-
479   nize subtle distinctions between categories, identify overlap, and even add or drop new categories
480   when necessary.

      6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
511
512
      While SCALE demonstrates strong potential in automating content analysis, there are several limi-
513   tations that present opportunities for future research.
514
515   Algorithmic Bias and Fairness. Despite incorporating human intervention to mitigate bias, LLMs
516
      remain prone to perpetuating biases present in the training data. This poses challenges in social
      science applications where ethical considerations are critical. Exploring advanced bias mitigation
517
      techniques, such as fairness-aware training methods or the inclusion of demographic and behavioral
518
      data, can enhance the contextual sensitivity of the framework and reduce biased outcomes.
519
520   Inter-agent Communication Overhead. The inter-agent discussion phase, though effective in im-
521   proving performance, introduces significant computational overhead. This makes the framework
522
      less efficient, particularly when applied to large datasets or when real-time decisions are required.
      Optimizing the discussion process through adaptive protocols—where discussions are invoked only
523
      in cases of high disagreement—could reduce computational costs without compromising the quality
524
      of the output.

528
529   In this paper, we have proposed SCALE, a novel multi-agent framework designed to simulate the
530   rigorous process of content analysis by leveraging the capabilities of LLMs. In addressing the scal-
531
      ability challenges inherent in traditional content analysis methods, SCALE enables large-scale and
      high-quality annotations approximating human judgment in various complex content analysis tasks,
532
      providing social scientists with a transformative tool for analyzing vast volumes of unstructured tex-
533
      tual data. Discussions between LLM agents play a crucial role in refining coding results, mirroring
534   the reflective depth seen in human analysis. Additionally, while LLM-driven social-scientist agents
535   propose reliable codebooks, human intervention remains significant in mitigating bias and ensuring
536   the contextual sensitivity critical for nuanced research. The integration of human oversight at differ-
537   ent levels not only guards against algorithmic bias but also enhances the reliability and contextual
538   awareness of the annotations. This work not only enhances the methodological toolkit of content
539   analysis but also opens new avenues for AI–human collaboration in domain-specific research, offer-
      ing a glimpse into how LLMs can redefine computational social science.
