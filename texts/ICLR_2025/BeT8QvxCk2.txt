009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel soft prompt based framework, SoftSRV, that leverages a frozen
012            pre-trained large language model (LLM) to generate targeted synthetic text se-
013            quences. Given a sample from the target distribution, our proposed framework
014            uses data-driven loss minimization to train a parameterized “contextual” soft
015            prompt. This soft prompt is then used to steer the frozen LLM to generate syn-
016
               thetic sequences that are similar to the target distribution. We argue that Soft-
               SRV provides a practical improvement over common hard-prompting approaches
017
               that rely on human-curated prompt-templates, which can be idiosyncratic, labor-
018
               intensive to craft, and may need to be specialized per domain. We empirically
019            evaluate SoftSRV and hard-prompting baselines by generating synthetic data to
020            fine-tune a small Gemma model on three different domains (coding, math, reason-
021            ing). To stress the generality of SoftSRV, we perform these evaluations without
022            any particular specialization of the framework to each domain. We find that Soft-
023            SRV significantly improves upon hard-prompting baselines, generating data with
024            superior fine-tuning performance and that better matches the target distribution
025            according to the MAUVE similarity metric.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, pre-trained large language models (LLMs) have proven to be effective in generating
030   synthetic natural language training data (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Eldan & Li, 2023;
031   Mukherjee et al., 2023; Mitra et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024). This is particularly true when the
032   synthetic data is used to pre-train or fine-tune smaller language models, enabling performances that
033   rival models that are orders of magnitude larger (Liu et al., 2023). There are several motivations
034   for generating and using synthetic training data; chief among them is the need to train models for
035   domains where little natural high-quality text may be readily available or may be difficult to procure.
036   In order to generate synthetic text, a significant amount of human-driven prompt engineering is
037   invested into developing prompts that steer the generating LLM into producing high-quality text
038   from a targeted domain while also encouraging sufficient diversity. This point was very nicely
039   summed up by the authors of the open-source synthetic text repository Cosmopedia (Ben Allal
040   et al., 2024), when recounting their attempt to recreate a large synthetic dataset similar to the one
041   generated to train Phi 1.5 (Li et al., 2023):
042
               “Heads up: If you are anticipating tales about deploying large-scale generation
043
               tasks across hundreds of H100 GPUs, in reality most of the time for Cosmopedia
044            was spent on meticulous prompt engineering.” – Ben Allal et al. (2024)
045
046   Furthermore, and especially in the case of generating fine-tuning data for targeted domains (e.g.,
047   coding, math, customer service), this manual process may need to be repeated and refined per-
048   domain, or even per sub-domain (e.g., per coding language, math subject, service department).
049   Apart from the human engineering cost, these manual prompting approaches do not directly opti-
050
      mize a data-driven objective. Rather they depend on human-in-the-loop style feedback for manually
      adjusting the prompt templates, resulting in approaches that lack robust mechanisms for aligning the
051
      LLM’s generated data with the desired distribution.
052
053   To address these issues, we propose an algorithmic framework, Soft prompt-based Synthesis with
      Randomized Variation (SoftSRV), that leverages soft-prompting (also known as prompt-tuning) for


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      synthetic text data generation. Soft prompt training is a parameter efficient tuning method and re-
055   quires a relatively limited amount of compute (Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021). Perhaps
056   equally important, since the soft prompt is trained using a data-driven training algorithm, it requires
057   essentially no human-in-the-loop intervention, enabling the process to be readily deployed across
058   many different domains. Furthermore, soft prompts themselves can allow for more expressive input
059   contexts to the generating LLM compared to natural language hard prompts. A soft “token”, rep-
060   resented by a dense vector, is not restricted to correspond to a particular discrete natural language
061   token (e.g., sub-word or character). This intuitive observation is formalized in Petrov et al. (2024b),
062   which shows that in specific settings soft prompts can induce an LMM to produce an exponential (in
063
      sequence length) number of text completions, while hard prompts only allow for a linear number of
      completions.
064
065   Why do we expect soft-prompting to be effective for targeted synthetic generation? Prior theoretical
066   research on fine-tuning language models suggests that a data-driven optimization of soft prompts
067   guides a pre-trained model towards specific concepts or tasks it has already learned, essentially
068   steering the model towards a relevant subspace of interest (Wies et al., 2023; Petrov et al., 2024b;a).
069
      Our goal is to use soft prompts to steer the pre-trained model towards generating text that most
      resembles the target distribution. Subsequently, fine-tuning a smaller model using the generated
070
      data provides an effective way to transfer knowledge from the larger model to the smaller model.
071
072   Unlike in typical prompt-tuning approaches, we do not prepend a soft prompt to an existing hard
073   prompt, but instead use the soft prompt alone as input context to the LLM. We use a sample of text
074   sequences (i.e., a sample from the desired target text distribution) and language-modeling loss to
075
      learn a parametrized soft prompt. Once trained, the soft prompt can be varied by conditioning on a
      context vector derived from an example sequence, allowing for additional expressive power and the
076
      potential to better fit different regions of a potentially complex target distribution.
077
078   Our contributions presented in this work are as follows:
079
080
            • We demonstrate that soft prompts can be effectively trained for the purpose of targeted
              synthetic text generation used to fine-tune downstream models.
081
082         • We investigate the value of learning parameterized families of soft prompts that can be
083           conditioned on an input context, finding they are critical for best fitting complex target
084
              distributions.
085         • Our empirical evaluations on coding, math, and reasoning tasks find superior downstream
086           performance for models fine-tuned on SoftSRV generated text compared to that of models
087
              fine-tuned on data generated by baseline hard-prompting approaches.
088         • We show that the SoftSRV approach is general and offers greater versatility than hard-
089           prompting approaches as it can be readily applied across different domains with minimal
090           manual intervention.
091         • We measure the similarity of the generated data to the target distribution using the MAUVE
092           metric and observe that SoftSRV methods align most closely with the target distribution.

097   that are studied in this work. First, we start with some basic notation and terminology.

      The SoftSRV framework seeks to synthesize sequences similar to those drawn from D by train-
127   ing a “soft prompt”, i.e. a dense embedding (or parameterized family of embeddings) P ∈ Rd×t
128   consisting of 0 < t < m “soft-tokens”. A successfully trained soft prompt, P, should generate
129   a sequence x = H(P), via frozen model H, that has a high likelihood of occurring under the
130   distribution D. More generally, we can sample several different sequences from a fixed prompt,
131   x, x0 , x00 . . . ∼ H(P), by using randomized temperature-based decoding. Although temperature
132   sampling alone does allow for some variability, we can further increase the variety of generated text
133   by using a contextual soft prompt, P(·) : Rde → Rd×t . A contextual soft prompt can be conditioned
134   with different context vectors z ∈ Rde , during training and generation, to induce variations of the
135
      soft prompt.
136   Before introducing specific soft prompt instantiations, we describe the SoftSRV training proce-
137   dure which is common throughout and also illustrated in Figure 1. In addition to the sample
138   of data (x1 , . . . , xn ) and frozen LLM (H), we assume access to a sequence embedding function
139   emb(·) : S m → Rde and we let θ denote the trainable parameters of the (contextual) soft prompt
140   Pθ (·). During training, each training sequence is embedded zi = emb(xi ) and used to generate
141
      a conditioned soft prompt Pθ (z), which is fed into the frozen LLM H to produce a new sequence
      x0i ∼ H(Pθ (zi )) using the standard sequential (next token) generation. A standard causal (next-
142
      word) prediction loss, denoted `(·, ·), is backpropagated through the network up to the soft prompt
143
      layer Pθ , and an SGD-style update is applied to θ using the gradient ∇θ `(xi , x0i ). This loss can be
144   thought of as a “reconstruction” error and the entire pipeline is akin to an auto-encoder. Viewing the
145   pipeline through this lens, it is apparent that the sequence embedder emb(·) should be sufficiently
146   “lossy” in order to avoid making the learning problem trivial. This lossiness can be enforced by
147   restricting the dimension de of the embedding, for example.
148
      Once the contextual soft prompt P(z) has been trained, we can then generate synthetic data by
149
      prompting the LLM using P(z) as embedded input context for different choices of context vec-
150   tor z. A natural choice is to sample embeddings (z1 , . . . , zn ) derived from the data sample set
151   (x1 , . . . , xn ). We now introduce a few specific SoftSRV parameterizations studied in this work.
152

195
196   In the our empirical evaluation of the SoftSRV framework, we consider a supervised fine-tuning
197   setting where a small Gemma 2B model (Team et al., 2024) is fine-tuned on synthetic data generated
198
      by a larger decoder-only language model across several different benchmark datasets.

200   3.1   D OMAINS AND DATASETS
201
      In order to demonstrate the generality of the proposed approach, we consider fine-tuning for several
202
      disparate domains (coding, mathematics, reasoning) using the same exact pipeline with no particular
203   specialization to any of the particular domains. We briefly describe the specific benchmark we use
204   from each domain.
205
206
      Code – MBPP (Austin et al., 2021). For the coding domain, we consider the “Mostly Basic Python
      Problems” (MBPP) benchmark, which consists of short Python programming exercises, e.g. “Write
207
      a python function to find the first repeated character in a given string” and answers written in Python
208
      code. The task is evaluated using a 3-shot prompt (i.e. a prompt pre-pended with three instructional
209   examples) and pass@1 metric, measuring if a single top generated result is correct.
210
211   Math – GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). For the math domain, we use the grade school math word
212
      problems from the GSM8K benchmark. This dataset contains only highly-curated word problems
      written by humans that are conceptually simple, but require multi-step reasoning. For this generative
213
      task we use a 5-shot prompt, again, measuring the pass@1 metric.
214
215   Reasoning – BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019). For the general reasoning domain, we consider the bi-
      nary question answering dataset from the BoolQ benchmark. The questions arise organically from


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      anonymized Google search queries which can be answered as either ‘true’ or ‘false’. Each question
217   and answer is paired with a passage (average length of 108 tokens) that is extracted from a relevant
218   Wikipedia page. This is evaluated as a scoring/classification task and accuracy is reported.
219
220
      The above chosen benchmarks aim to cover a wide variety of tasks, each with varying degrees of
      complexity – both in terms of solving the task and in terms of generating synthetic data for the task.
221
      The MBBP task requires basic Python programming knowledge to solve, but the questions are gen-
222
      erally short, follow a similar pattern and are concerned with a relatively narrow set of themes. The
223   GSM8K task requires basic math and language comprehension skills, but generating the problem is
224   arguably even harder than solving it. It requires generating a premise containing several numerical
225   quantities and then a question that can be answered using the provided information in a non-trivial
226   fashion. Nonetheless the premises are somewhat formulaic and thematically similar. The BoolQ
227   reasoning task, which requires general reading comprehension to solve, is perhaps the most difficult
228   task to generate synthetic data for. Generating a problem requires writing a long (relative to MBPP
229   and GSM8K) passage on an arbitrary topic that contains a collection of facts, but that does not nec-
230   essarily stick to any formula or theme, and then generate a true/false question that can be answered
231
      directly by the passage. As we shall see in the empirical evaluation that follows, the level of dif-
      ficulty in generating a high-quality question can impact the relative quality and value of generated
232
      synthetic data.

235   3.2   H ARD - PROMPTING BASELINES
236
237
      Typical hard prompt engineering approaches involve manually creating prompt templates that are
      then seeded with text from the desired target domain, typically taken from the training set. To give
238
      a (simplistic) illustrative example, a template could be:
239
240            Consider the following [article], write a textbook quality
241            summary of the topic suitable for a high-school audience,
242
243   where the placeholder [article] would be replaced with example texts from training fold, produc-
244   ing several distinct prompts. In this study, we consider the following two hard-prompting variants.
245
      The first, denoted simply as hard prompt (HP), uses a domain-specific hard prompt template to
246   generate a question followed by another domain specific hard prompt template to generate answers
247   (the detailed workflow is discussed in Subsection 3.3). In Appendix B, we provide the exact tem-
248   plates used by the HP method. To reach these templates, we undertook several iterations of hard
249   prompt engineering and reported the result of the best performing method. In particular, we found
250   that prompting for a "diverse" set of questions was crucial (a comparison plot is presented in Ap-
251   pendix A.5).
252   The second approach, hard-prompting with self-refinement (HPSR ), similarly uses a hard prompt
253   template to generate questions but also iteratively conducts several rounds of self-critique to im-
254   prove or accept the question (Madaan et al., 2023). Again, critique and refinement prompts are in
255   Appendix B.

      soft prompts and whether it is essential to have a contextual soft prompt that leverages the context
353
      token as opposed to a non-contextual soft prompt. Our empirical evaluations show that the non-
354   contextual soft prompt method, SSNSP , admits a lower performance than the other methods thereby
355   indicating the effectiveness of contextual soft prompts. Then, by comparing the performance of
356   the SSMC and SSMP2 methods, we observe that the more expressive parametrization of SSMC is
357   generally beneficial.
358
      We also find that the model fine-tuned on the SSMC generated data outperforms the model fine-
359
      tuned on the non-synthetic training dataset for MBPP and GSM8K, indicating that, given enough
360   of it, synthetic data can outperform even non-synthetic data. However, the same observation does
361   not hold for BoolQ. The training set curve on BoolQ admits high variance, but it attains a higher
362   accuracy overall. As discussed in Section 3.1, we expect generating questions for the BoolQ dataset
363   to be more difficult stemming both from the fact that the data was generated by search queries
364   thereby covering a wide range of topics, but also from containing much longer sequences extracted
365   from the Wikipedia passages.
366   In Table 1, we show a comparison of the methods at a fixed epoch and at their max metric value
367   attained over all tested fine-tuning steps. The fixed epoch was chosen to be the step where the
368   model admits the largest fine-tuning performance on the training dataset. This clearly gives an
369   advantage to the model fine-tuned on the training data, but it still provides a fair comparison between
370   the synthetically generated datasets. We again find that the model performance when fine-tuned
371   on the SSMC generated data outperforms the models fine-tuned on the other generated datasets at
372   both the fixed epoch and at the max metric. For the the BoolQ dataset, the HP method admits a
373   performance close to that of the SSMC method for the max metric, but its accuracy for the fixed
374
      epoch is considerably lower than that of SSMC .
375   Even though the training datasets from our benchmark domains contain a relatively small number
376   of examples (see column Nr of Table 1 for exact sizes), our methods successfully generate tens of
377   thousands of synthetic examples, thereby showcasing that SoftSRV is well suited for the the data
      scarce setting.

422

423
      shows that a model fine-tuned on the SSMC dataset attains a performance close to that when fine-
424   tuned on the sample from the TinyGSM dataset while the HP method lags behind both. TinyGSM
425   performing closely to SSMC is encouraging given that the TinyGSM dataset is highly curated and
426   tailored specifically for the GSM8K benchmark.

474   4     R ELATED W ORK
475
476   SoftSRV provides a novel contribution at the intersection of soft-prompting and synthetic text gen-
477   eration for targeted fine-tuning tasks. On each of these individual topics, there is a large and rapidly
478   growing literature, which we touch on only briefly in the section.
479
480   4.1   S YNTHETIC TEXT FOR LLM TRAINING
481
482
      As mentioned in the introduction, there is a significant recent body of work demonstrating the effec-
      tiveness of using a large language model to generate synthetic training data for a smaller model.
483
484   In terms for generating pre-training data, the collection of “Textbooks Are All You Need” white-
485   papers outlines the process of training the Phi series of small LMs, using carefully curated prompt
      templates and seed data sources, and shows the large boost in quality that synthetic data can provide


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024). The Cosmopedia project (Ben Allal
487   et al., 2024) conducts a similar study, while also providing detailed and transparent steps as well as
488   open-sourcing the prompts and generated data. Li et al. (2024a) construct a pretraining set almost
489   “from scratch” using a diverse set of hard prompts. To build these prompts, they assume access
490   to taxonomies of fields/sub-fields/disciplines within an area of expertise and build a syllabus which
491   culminates in a series of “key concepts”, each of which a large language model is then queried
492   to generate a lesson on. Apart from focusing on pre-training rather than fine-tuning for a specific
493   domain, these works require a non-trivial amount human effort for building and/or curating hard
494   prompts for the generating LLM, which our effort seeks to minimize.
495   Mukherjee et al. (2023); Mitra et al. (2023) focus on building synthetic data for better instruction
496   tuning. In particular, they start with the FLAN-V2 instruction tuning dataset and ask a LLM to ex-
497   pand on the terse responses in different verbose styles (specified by so-called “system instructions”)
498   to introduce variation in presentation and approach. Although, shown to be quite effective across a
499   broad array of reasoning tasks, in our setting we wish to generate data focused on a specific target
500   task, likely requiring us to curate a set of bespoke “system instructions” for each task.
501   Several works build fine-tuning data for specific domains, such as coding (Haluptzok et al., 2023;
502   Luo et al., 2024) or mathematics (Yu et al., 2024). Although quite successful, these approaches
503   leverage specific qualities of the target domain, for example, using a code interpreter to check cor-
504   rectness of generated code or using the fact that math problems contain numerical quantities that can
505   be masked or manipulated to create variations of the original question.
506   Finally, in the recent work of Lee et al. (2024), the authors propose an adaptive procedure where
507   a large language model is used to generate targeted fine-tuning data for a small model based on
508   examples that the small model has made mistakes on. The large model is prompted to rewrite vari-
509   ants of these questions using specialized per-domain hard prompts. Extending the general-purpose
510   SoftSRV approach to an adaptive framework is a current area of investigation.
511
512   4.2   S OFT- PROMPTING & NON - TEXT DATA MODALITIES
513
514   As mentioned previously, the use of soft-prompting (or prompt-tuning) already has a significant
515
      history outside of targeted synthetic fine-tuning data generation. Primarily known for its use as a
      parameter efficient fine-tuning method (Lester et al., 2021), soft prompt training has also been used
516
      as a framework to learn or compress in-context instructions (Mu et al., 2023). Li et al. (2024b) has
517
      proposed training a secondary model to compress in-context instructions into soft prompt that is
518   then prepended to the task hard prompt and is meant to generalize to even new tasks.
519
520   While our study has been focused on generating synthetic text, there have been similar efforts in
521
      other modalities. For example, the ControlNet approach of Zhang et al. (2023) trains a diffusion
      model to produce images conditioned on contextual input, for example, image edges or 3D pose
522
      skeletons. Similarly, Gao et al. (2024) train a diffusion-based speech model to condition on a “simple
523
      speech representation” embedding to guide the generation of new synthetic speech data. Finally,
524   in the case of text-to-image generators, there has been a significant amount of work in solving
525   the “inverse” problem of mapping from an image back to a prompt (either hard prompt or a soft
526   representation), so that one can more predictably generate synthetic images in certain styles (see
527   Mahajan et al. (2024) and many references therein).
528
529
530
      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we have established the effectiveness of using contextual soft prompts, via the SoftSRV
532
      framework, for generating targeted synthetic fine-tuning data and its applicability across several dif-
533
      ferent domains. We deploy the same SoftSRV pipeline across math, coding, and reasoning tasks, in
534   each case we find SoftSRV is able to generate fine-tuning data that provide good downstream perfor-
535   mance and fits well to the target distribution without any per-domain specialization needed. Given
536   these results, there are several natural directions for further research. For example, we can view soft
537   prompts as one particular class of parameter efficient tuning approach that is natural to leverage for
538   data synthethesis, however, other approaches (such as LoRA) may be worth investigating as well.
539   Finally, adapting the choice of context vector in order to generate the most effective synthetic data
      for improving the downstream model is a current and ongoing line of work.

      A.1   S YNTHETIC Q UESTION P OSTPROCESSING
707
708   For all methods (hard prompt and SoftSRV based), we query the model to generate 100K sequences.
709   From these 100K, we first filter exact duplicates. From the remainder, we subsample to achieve the
710   target size Ns . To encourage a diverse subsample, again for all methods, we cluster the data and
711   select examples from each cluster randomly in round-robin fashion.
712
      Concretely, using the scikit-learn library (Pedregosa et al., 2011), we apply MiniBatch k-means to
713   vectorized data, which has been reduced in dimensionality using SVD. For all methods, we set the
714   number of clusters for MiniBatch k-means to 700, reduced the dimensionality to 100 features and
715   used sk.TfidfVectorizer for vectorization. Given the k-means clustering, we randomly select without
716   replacement one point per cluster until Ns questions are chosen.
717
718   A.2   D ECONTAMINATION P ROCESS
719
720   Even though the test set was never used in our synthetic data generation pipeline, the frozen LLM
721   models that are leveraged to generate questions and answers might have been exposed to the test set
722   during their pretraining phase. Thus, for all methods, we decontaminate the generated sequences
723
      against the respective benchmark’s test set by removing any n-gram matches where n = 13 as is
      common practice (Brown et al., 2020). Prior to calculating the matches, we eliminate all punctuation
724
      and numerical characters. We found that the contamination of the generated sequences to the test
725
      set is minimal with less than 0.1% for GSM8K and MBPP and around 1% for BoolQ.
726
727
      A.3   M AUVE S CORE C OMPUTATION
728
729   We let G and D denote the generated and target/reference distributions, respectively, and compute
730   the MAUVE scores for each synthetic dataset as follows. Using an embedding model, a vector
731   representation is computed for each sequence in the synthetic and reference sets. These embeddings
732   are then projected into a discrete set using k-means clustering, and a divergence curve is traced
733   between the cluster distributions of G and D, see Equation 1 in Pillutla et al. (2021). The MAUVE
734   has value between 0 and 1 and corresponds to the area under the divergence curve, where higher
735   scores are indicative of a closer match between G and D. The same small LM serving as the context
736
      embedder for SoftSRV in Subsection 3.3 is used to compute per-token representations, which is then
      averaged to produce the sequence-level embedding. We use k = 32 clusters for all domains as we
737
      found that k = 16 or k = 64 yields similar qualitative results.

741   Here, we conduct a exploration to measure the effect of changing the number of basis soft prompt
742   matrices, k, of the SSMPk method.

      Figure 4 shows the comparison in performance on various benchmarks across different values of
      k. We see an inherent trade-off as we increase k, which increases the capacity of the contextual
745
      soft-prompt but also then require training more parameters. For MBPP we see that the value k = 4
746

747   k = 2 throughout the evaluations in the main paper as a good general choice across different tasks.
