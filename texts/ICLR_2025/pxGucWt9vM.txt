009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, quantization has been widely used for the compression and acceleration
012            of large language models (LLMs). Due to the outliers in LLMs, it is crucial to flat-
013            ten weights and activations to minimize quantization error with the equally spaced
014            quantization points. Prior research explores various pre-quantization transforma-
015            tions to suppress outliers, such as per-channel scaling and Hadamard transforma-
016            tion. However, we observe that these transformed weights and activations can still
017            remain steep and outspread. In this paper, we propose F LAT Q UANT (Fast and
018
               Learnable Affine Transformation), a new post-training quantization approach to
               enhance flatness of weights and activations. Our approach identifies optimal affine
019
               transformations tailored to each linear layer, calibrated in hours via a lightweight
020
               objective. To reduce runtime overhead, we apply Kronecker decomposition to
021            the transformation matrices, and fuse all operations in F LAT Q UANT into a single
022            kernel. Extensive experiments show that F LAT Q UANT sets up a new state-of-the-
023            art quantization benchmark. For instance, it achieves less than 1% accuracy drop
024            for W4A4 quantization on the LLaMA-3-70B model, surpassing SpinQuant by
025            7.5%. For inference latency, F LAT Q UANT reduces the slowdown induced by pre-
026            quantization transformation from 0.26x of QuaRot to merely 0.07x, bringing up
027            to 2.3x speedup for prefill and 1.7x speedup for decoding, respectively. Code will
028            be released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across a wide range of
      tasks with an increasing number of parameters (Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
034
      2024; Dubey et al., 2024). However, the growth of model size also incurs a significant increase in
035
      computation and memory overhead. As a result, reducing the computational and memory demands
036   of LLMs has emerged as a critical research direction, and quantization is one of the most effective
037   solutions (Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023).
038
039
      Quantization decreases the memory footprint and accelerates the inference, by reducing the preci-
      sion of model parameters and activations. Quantization error is a commonly used metric to measure
040
      the performance of quantization methods (Nagel et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021). One
041
      key factor that affects the quantization error is the flatness of weights and activations. Intuitively,
042   when the distribution of weights and activations is sharp and there exist multiple outspread values,
043   quantizing them to the same quantized value usually incurs a large quantization error (Chmiel et al.,
044   2020; Li et al., 2024). Moreover, as LLMs generate outputs layer by layer, a reduced quantization
045   error also flattens the error landscape propagated across Transformer layers.
046
      Nevertheless, it is non-trivial to get a flat distribution of weights and activations in LLMs. LLMs
047
      are known to have extreme outliers over activations (Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lin
048   et al., 2023) and pivot tokens (Liu et al., 2024a; Sun et al., 2024). To alleviate this problem, various
049   pre-quantization transformations are proposed to mitigate the impact of outliers (Xiao et al., 2023;
050   Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Ma et al., 2024). However, we revisit these transformations
051   and find them still sub-optimal in promoting flatness. For instance, per-channel scaling (Xiao et al.,
052   2023; Shao et al., 2023) aims to balance the outliers between weights and activations, but it falls short
053   of distributing outliers over the non-outlier channels. The recent Hadamard transformation (Ashk-
      boos et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024) attempts to solve this problem, while the individual character-


                                                         1

054
      istics of each linear layer are not considered. Moreover, the linear transformation introduced by
055   these methods (Ma et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) inevitably introduces extra
056   inference overhead that affects the overall speedup of quantization.
057
058
      In this work, we introduce a new post-training quantization approach named F LAT Q UANT (Fast and
      Learnable Affine Transformation). Our approach is grounded in the principle of achieving a flat-
059
      ter distribution of weights and activations, which is crucial for quantization. F LAT Q UANT aims to
060
      identify the optimal affine transformation for each linear layer, employing a lightweight, block-wise
061   training strategy over the calibration data. To minimize the inference overhead associated with affine
062   transformations, F LAT Q UANT harnesses the efficiency of Kronecker decomposition, thus reducing
063   both the memory and computational demands. The proposed approach is compatible with vari-
064   ous quantization techniques such as learnable clipping, and can be applied to various quantization
065   settings, e.g., weight-only quantization or KV cache quantization. Additionally, by observing that
066   affine transformations in F LAT Q UANT are memory bound, we further fuse the affine transformations
067   and quantization into a single kernel, thereby minimizing the global memory access and kernel lunch
068   overhead. Lastly, extensive experiments are conducted to assess F LAT Q UANT across various tasks,
069
      including language modeling and question answering, using LLaMA-2/3 models ranging from 7B
      to 70B parameters. The empirical results demonstrate that our proposed approach surpasses current
070
      state-of-the-art methods in terms of both accuracy and inference latency.
071
072   The contributions of this work are summarized below:
073
             • We highlight the significance of achieving flatness for LLM quantization, demonstrating
074
               that flat distributions of weights and activations facilitate quantization and reduce error
075
               propagation across Transformer layers.
076
077
             • We introduce F LAT Q UANT, a new post-training quantization method with fast and learn-
               able affine transformations optimized for each linear layer. The approach is empirically
078
               demonstrated to enhance the flatness of both weights and activations in LLMs.
079
080
             • Extensive experiments demonstrate that F LAT Q UANT sets new state-of-the-art results for
               quantization. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to achieve ≤ 1% accuracy drop
081
               with simply round-to-nearest W4A4 quantization on the LLaMA-3-70B model.
082
083          • We have designed an efficient kernel that fuses affine transformation and quantization,
084
               reducing the additional latency caused by transformation from a 0.26x slowdown with
               QuaRot to only 0.07x. This enhancement gives up to 2.3x speedup for prefill and 1.7x
085
               speedup for decoding compared to the FP16 baseline.

090   2.1   P RELIMINARIES ON LLM Q UANTIZATION
091
      The inference of LLM typically has two stages: 1) the prefill stage, which creates a key-value cache
092
      (KV Cache) layer by layer from the input sequence; and 2) the decoding stage, where the model
093   autoregressively generates tokens based on previous KV cache. Quantization is a common practice
094   to reduce the model size and inference latency. It converts the full-precision weights W ∈ Rm×n
095   or activations X ∈ Rk×n of linear layers (i.e., Y = XW⊤ ), and optionally the KV cache to low-bit
096   representations. For instance, b-bit weight quantization can be represented as follows:
097
                                       Ŵ = Qb (W) = s · ΠΩ(b) (W/s),                                     (1)
098
099   where s is the quantization step size, Π(·) is the projection function and Ω(b) = {0, 1, ..., 2b − 1} is
100   the set of b-bit integer points. For simplicity of notation, we denote Q(·) as the general quantization
101   function in the rest of this paper. Quantizing weights enables memory time savings from weight
102   loading from high-bandwidth memory (HBM) into the compute cores, and quantizing activations
103   further reduces the computation, benefiting both the prefill and decoding stages of LLM inference.
104   As recent works suggest (Xiao et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2023), LLMs exhibit per-
105   sistent outliers in activations, posing significant challenges for quantization. Various works have
106   been proposed to suppress these outliers to improve the quantized LLMs. Two most commonly used
107   methods are per-channel scaling (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023) and Hadamard
      transformation or its variants (Xi et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024).

      Per-channel Scaling. The input activations X of LLMs are often rich in outliers. To mitigate their
142   impact on quantization, a popular way is to apply channel-wise scaling over weights and activa-
143   tions (Xiao et al., 2023), i.e., Y = (Xdiag(c)−1 ) · (diag(c)W⊤ ), where c ∈ Rn is the channel-wise
144   scaling factor. The scaling vector smooths the activations by jointly considering the magnitudes
145   of input activations and weights, i.e. cj = max(|Xj |α )/ max(|Wj |1−α ). The scaled weights
146   diag(c)W⊤ can be merged to eliminate the runtime computation. Additionally, Wei et al. (2023)
147   introduces channel-wise shifting, i.e., (X − z)diag(c)−1 , to further mitigate the impact of outliers,
148   and Shao et al. (2023) treats both diag(c) and z as learnable parameters.
149
150   Hadamard Transformation. Recent works find Hadamard matrices H ∈ {+1, −1}n×n are par-
151   ticularly helpful in smoothing out outliers in activations (Xi et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2024;
152   Lin et al., 2024). In contrast to per-channel scaling which only adjusts the diagonal elements in
153   the view of matrix multiplication, Hadamard transformation rotates the channels of both activations
154
      and weights, re-distributing the outliers among all channels to effectively eliminate them. Thanks
      to the orthogonality of Hadamard matrices (i.e., H⊤ H = I), the following equivalency holds:
155
      Y = XW⊤ = (XH)(H⊤ W⊤ ). The transformed weight WH can be similarly pre-processed
156
      offline to reduce additional runtime overhead.

185         The Flatness of Weights and Activations. Flat tensors are intuitively easier to quantize after
186         removing outliers, a.k.a tensors with low kurtosis (Chmiel et al., 2020; Li et al., 2024). Figure 1
187         displays the distributions of both the original and transformed weights and activations, sorted by the
188         channel magnitudes in descending order. The flat weights and activations with horizontal envelopes
189         are usually preferred by quantization. Compared with the original distributions, pre-quantization
190         transformations can yield flatter activations (e.g., Figure 1b, 1d) but still with their limitations. Per-
191
            channel scaling flattens activations at the cost of steeper weight envelops (e.g., Figure 1a, 1c). While
            Hadamard transformation produces generally better flatness for both activations and weights than
192
            per-channel scaling, it still sometimes generates unsatisfactory weights and activations distributions
193
            (e.g., Figure 1a, 1b). In contrast, F LAT Q UANT consistently flattens both weights and activations.
194
195         The Flatness of Quantization Error Landscape. The quantization error inevitably propagates,
196         and it is insightful to show how pre-quantization transformations mitigate this issue. We plot the

198         massive quantization errors occur at initial tokens, a.k.a. pivot tokens (Liu et al., 2024a), which
199         contain massive outliers (Sun et al., 2024). Both per-channel scaling and Hadamard transformation
200
            are powerless to such errors (i.e., Figure 2a-2b). Instead, F LAT Q UANT shows much lower error at
            these pivot tokens from Figure 2c. Second, the quantization error increases layer-wisely, but is less
201
            evident along the input sequence. According to Figure 2d, F LAT Q UANT is the best in controlling
202
            the error propagation, followed by Hadamard transformation and lastly the per-channel scaling.

262   3.2   I NTEGRATION WITH THE T RANSFORMER A RCHITECTURE
263
264
      We illustrate the integration of F LAT Q UANT with a Transformer block based on an LLaMA-like
      architecture, as depicted in Figure 3. Following the conventional practices, we employ low-bit ma-
265
      trix multiplications for all linear layers, while keeping layer normalization layers, pre-quantization
266
      transformations, RoPE embeddings, and attention scores in FP16.
267
268   Self-Attention. The self-attention module is equipped with four transformations {Pa , Po , Ph ,
269   Pv }. Specifically, Pa is applied to flatten the input activation for the query, key, and value pro-
      jections, while Po smooths the input activation for the output projection. Ph and Pv are used to


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      transform the key and value cache head by head, respectively. Note that we only decompose Pa
271   and Po , but leave Ph and Pv in their original shape. This is because per-head quantization already
272   facilitates cheap transformations, given that the head size is significantly smaller than the full hidden
273   size. Moreover, we further fuse Po with Pv to reduce overhead, as inspired by QuaRot (Ashkboos
274   et al., 2024). Our empirical results show this fusion does not result in additional loss of accuracy.
275
276   Feed-forward Network. The feed-forward network (FFN) employs two transformation matrices,
277
      i.e., Pug and Pd . Pug is applied to flatten the input of the feed-forward network after layer nor-
      malization, while Pd flattens the input for the down projection layer. Both transformations are
278
      decomposed to minimize the inference overhead. Additionally, the per-channel scaling of Pd is
279
      merged into the weight of up projection layer, ensuring no additional computational overhead.
280
281   Layer Normalization. Recall that QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) and SpinQuant (Liu et al.,
282   2024b) modify the LayerNorm to RMSNorm and merge orthogonal transformations into preced-
283   ing layers for efficiency. Nonetheless, the residual connection of the “pre-norm” architecture
284   would constrain all Transformer blocks to share the same transformation after RMSNorm. Instead,
285   F LAT Q UANT preserves the LayerNorm, and allows the use of fast and learnable affine transforma-
286
      tions in Section 3.1 after LayerNorm for different layers, thereby enhancing the expressiveness.

288   3.3   E FFICIENT K ERNEL D ESIGN
289
      We design an efficient kernel for F LAT Q UANT that integrates both affine transformations and quan-
290
      tization into a single operation. This design is motivated by two key factors. First, P⊤
                                                                                             1 ×1 X̃ ×2 P2
291
      exhibits low computational intensity after Kronecker decomposition, making both prefill and decod-
292   ing predominantly memory-bound. Second, the quantization is also known to be memory-bound.
293
294   To address these issues, we fuse Q(P⊤  1 ×1 X̃×2 P2 ) into a single kernel using OpenAI Triton (Tillet
295   et al., 2019). Specifically, we load the entire P1 ∈ Rn1 ×n1 and P2 ∈ Rn2 ×n2 into SRAM. Each
296   thread block slices a tiling block X̄ ∈ Rn1 ×n2 from X̃, performs the matrix multiplication P1 X̄P2 ,
297   and quantizes the results on the fly. Throughout this process, all intermediate results are stored
298
      in SRAM before finally being written back to the global memory. This design thereby eliminates
      redundant memory accesses of intermediate results and reduces the kernel launch overhead. Further
299
      details of the kernel design are provided in the Appendix B.2.
300
301   Finally, given the output above, we follow QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) to adopt the CUTLASS
302   kernel for INT4 matrix multiplication, and FlashInfer (Ye, 2023) for KV cache quantization.

      4.4       D ISCUSSIONS
507   Ablation Study. We conduct ablation studies for F LAT Q UANT focusing on its main components:
508   1) learnable transformation (LT); 2) per-channel scaling (PS); and 3) learnable clipping thresholds
509   (LCT). Starting from RTN as a baseline, we evaluate the impact of each component on perplexity
510   and the average accuracy on zero-shot QA tasks, with LLaMA-3-8B model. As shown in Table 4,
511
      enabling LT significantly enhances the accuracy of the quantized model, reducing PPL from 1266.60
      to 8.50 on WikiText-2. This shows LT is capable of adaptively flattening the distribution of weight
512
      and activation values. Additionally, incorporating PS and LCT further improves PPL by 0.55 and
513
      0.84, respectively, demonstrating the necessity of each component to refine the model performance.
514
515   Other Quantization Schemes. Although the main results above focus mostly on weight-
516   activation quantization, F LAT Q UANT can be easily applied to other quantization schemes. The
517   results of weight-only quantization against several state-of-the-art baselines are presented in Ta-
518   ble 5. F LAT Q UANT again obtains leading accuracy compared with leading baselines. For additional
519
      results on KV cache quantization and extreme low-bit quantization, please refer to Appendix C.2.
520   Train One and Get More. We demonstrate that the affine transformations learned from weight-
521   activation quantization can be directly applied to other quantization settings, such as weight-only
522   or KV cache quantization, with surprisingly strong performance. The associated results are pre-
523   sented in Table 6. For instance, the results labeled as “W4” are comparable to those in Table 5 that
524   are specifically trained for weight-only quantization. This significantly saves time when applying
525   F LAT Q UANT to different quantization settings, as only one set of transformation matrices is saved.
526   Due to space constraints, we provide additional discussions such as the impact of calibration data,
527   and the effect of learnable clipping in Appendix C.3. More visualizations on the flatness of trans-
528   formed weights and activations, and the quantization error landscapes are in Appendix D.
529
530
      5        C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this study, we revisit the importance of flat weights and activations for effective quantization, and
533
      find existing solutions still produce steep outspread values after the pre-quantization transformation.
534   Therefore, we introduce F LAT Q UANT, a novel post-training quantization method with the purpose
535   of identifying fast and learnable transformations for each linear layer, to promote the flatness of
536   weights and activations. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of F LAT Q UANT, e.g.,
537   with less than 1% accuracy drop for W4A4 quantization on the LLaMA-3-70B. Our efficient kernel
538   fusion integrates the affine transformation and quantization, reducing the transformation overhead
539   and bringing up to 2.3x and 1.7x speedup over FP16 inference at the prefill and decoding stages, re-
      spectively. We hope this work advances the practical application of low-bit quantization for LLMs.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
703
704
      Quantization for Large Language Models.             Quantization is a crucial technique for reducing
705   memory footprint and accelerating inference by employing fewer bits for storage and computa-
706   tion, especially for the application of LLMs. Unlike previous models, LLMs are shown to exhibit
707   outliers in activation and massive outliers in pivot tokens (Wei et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022;
708   Liu et al., 2024a; Sun et al., 2024), which can severely degrade quantization accuracy. To elimi-
709   nate the negative impact of outliers, pre-quantization transformations have been widely adopted in
710   weight-activation quantization (Xiao et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024;
711   Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) as well as in fully quantized training (Xi et al., 2023).
712   Additionally, several weight-only quantization methods (Lin et al., 2023; Chee et al., 2024; Tseng
713
      et al., 2024) incorporate pre-quantization transformations. Searched or learnable clipping thresholds
      on weights or activations (Shao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Duanmu et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al.,
714
      2024; Liu et al., 2024b) are also explored to eliminate outliers.
715
716
      Per-channel Scaling Transformation.          SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) employs per-channel
717
      scaling to shift the challenge of quantization from activations to weights in weight-activation quan-
718
      tization. Building on this, Wei et al. (2023) additionally introduces channel-wise shifting, while
719   OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) utilizes a differentiable approach to learn optimal scaling and shift-
720   ing parameters. However, the scaling-based methods can negatively impact weight quantization and
721   struggle in low-bit settings, such as W4A4 quantization.
722
723   Hadamard and Orthogonal Transformation.               Recent research (Xi et al., 2023; Tseng et al.,
724   2024; Ashkboos et al., 2024) has shown that the Hadamard transformation is effective in eliminating
725   outliers and lowering quantization error by redistributing outliers across all channels through matrix
726   multiplication. QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) is the first to apply Hadamard transformation in the
727   LLM W4A4 PTQ setting, while SpinQuant (Liu et al., 2024b) exploits learnable orthogonal matrices
728   with model-level loss to further alleviate outliers.
729
730   Affine Transformation. Considering that per-channel scaling corresponds to the diagonal ele-
731   ments of the affine transformation matrix, AffineQuant (Ma et al., 2024) proposes learning the equiv-
732
      alent affine transformation. However, their approach focuses on learning full-size diagonally domi-
      nant matrices and employs a gradual mask optimization method, which may hinder the full potential
733
      of affine transformation in reducing quantization loss. Moreover, due to the formidable overhead
734
      associated with full-sized matrix multiplication, AffineQuant can only apply affine transformation
735   to a small fraction of linear layers. In contrast, we employ fast and learnable affine transformations
736   without these limitations, leading to substantial accuracy improvements and practical speedup.
737
738   Pre-quantization Transformations in Other Quantization Tasks. Inspired by SmoothQuant,
739   AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) introduces activation-aware per-channel scaling to reduce quantization errors
740   in weight-only quantization. QUIP (Chee et al., 2024) and its extension, QUIP# (Tseng et al.,
741   2024), leverage random rotation matrices or Hadamard transformations to enhance incoherence in
742   weight-only quantization. In fully quantized training task, Xi et al. (2023) propose to utilize a block-
743   diagonal transformation consisting of Hadamard matrices to reduce the quantization error.

      B.1   M ATRIX I NVERSION AND T RAINING C OST
748
749   A critical aspect to implement F LAT Q UANT is the computation of the inverse affine transformation
750   matrix P−1 . As discussed below, we use singular value decomposition (SVD) and automatic mixed
751   precision to train F LAT Q UANT, enjoying both training stability and efficiency.
752
753   Direct Inversion and FP32 Training. One straightforward approach is to use the inverse func-
754   tion provided by PyTorch. However, we find that the precision of this inverse function at FP16 is
755   insufficient. Specifically, PP−1 does not closely approximate I. The off-diagonal elements are on
      the order of 1 × 10−3 , which negatively impacts F LAT Q UANT’s performance during the early stages


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      of training. Therefore, a simple solution is to conduct training in FP32 without Automatic Mixed
757   Precision (AMP) to maintain precision. However, this inevitably increases training time and more
758   GPU memory consumption.
759
760   SVD and AMP Training. To further reduce resource requirements during calibration, we propose
761   to employ singular value decomposition for the affine transformation. For any real matrix P, we can
762   decompose it as P = UΣV⊤ , where U and V are orthogonal matrices, and Σ is a diagonal matrix.
763   This formulation allows us to easily compute P−1 = VΣ−1 U⊤ , offering a more computationally
764   efficient method for obtaining the inverse. Notably, this approach reduces the off-diagonal elements
765   of PP−1 to the order of 1 × 10−6 at FP16 precision, enabling us to utilize AMP during calibration.
766   With AMP, we can achieve a 50% reduction in training time and memory usage while maintaining
767
      nearly lossless accuracy in most cases. For the orthogonal matrices U and V, we employ the Cayley
      parameterization provided by PyTorch 3 .

905   C.1   F LAT Q UANT L EADS TO F LATNESS
906
      In Figure 1 and Figure 2, we illustrate that the affine transformations of FlatQuant actually learn to
907
      flatten the distribution of weights and activations to lower the quantization error, even though we do
908
      not include flatness in the optimization target. The simple layer-wise MSE loss defined in Equation 4
909   is enough to encourage flatness in weights and activations. In the following, we validate that with
910   more experiment results.
911
912   Quantifying the Flatness. We quantify the flatness of weights and activations by calculating the

1003   Calibration Set. Since F LAT Q UANT employs a gradient-based method to optimize transforma-
1004   tions for increased flatness, one reasonable concern is whether F LAT Q UANT might overfit the cal-
1005   ibration set. To assess its generalization ability, we conducted an ablation study using different
1006   calibration datasets: WikiText-2, C4, and Pile. As shown in Table 15, F LAT Q UANT maintains stable
1007   performance across all datasets. For example, when calibrated on different datasets, F LAT Q UANT
1008
       exhibits similar performance on WikiText-2, with PPL ranging from 6.98 to 7.04. On the C4 dataset,
       results are equally consistent, with PPLs between 11.05 and 11.13. Furthermore, QA accuracy re-
1009
       mains within a narrow range (71.04% to 71.23%), suggesting that F LAT Q UANT generalizes well
1010
       across different calibration datasets. This robustness is attributed to F LAT Q UANT’s focus on learning
1011   an equivalent affine transformation with minimal quantization loss, rather than altering the model’s
1012   weights. Nevertheless, it is reasonable to assume that the diversity of calibration data can further
1013   enhance the performance of our method.
