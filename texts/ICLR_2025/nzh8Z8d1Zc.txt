032                                                                                              A BSTRACT
033
034            Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited remarkable capabilities across vari-
035            ous domains and tasks, pushing the boundaries of our knowledge in learning and
036            cognition. The latest model, OpenAI’s o1, stands out as the first LLM with an inter-
037            nalized chain-of-thought technique using reinforcement learning strategies. While
038            it has demonstrated surprisingly strong capabilities on various general language
039
               tasks, its performance in specialized fields such as medicine remains unknown. To
               this end, this report provides a preliminary exploration of o1 on different medical
040
               scenarios, comprehensively examining 3 key aspects: understanding, reasoning,
041
               and multilinguality. Specifically, our evaluation encompasses 6 tasks using data
042            from 37 medical datasets, including two newly constructed and more challenging
043            question-answering (QA) tasks based on professional medical quizzes from the
044            New England Journal of Medicine and The Lancet. These datasets offer greater
045            clinical relevance compared to standard medical QA benchmarks such as MedQA,
046            translating more effectively into real-world clinical utility. Our analysis of o1
047            suggests that the enhanced reasoning ability of LLMs may (significantly) benefit
048            their capability to understand various medical instructions and reason through
049            complex clinical scenarios. Notably, o1 surpasses the previous GPT-4 in accuracy
050
               by an average of 6.2% and 6.6% across 19 datasets and two newly created complex
               QA scenarios. But meanwhile, we also identify several weaknesses in both the
051
               model capability and the existing evaluation protocols, including hallucination,
052
               inconsistent multilingual ability, and discrepant metrics for evaluation. We will
053            release our raw data and model outputs for future research.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Intelligence, a complex and elusive concept, has puzzled psychologists, philosophers, and computer
057   scientists for years (Bubeck et al., 2023). While there is no single agreed-upon definition of intelli-
058   gence, it is widely accepted that it spans a broad range of cognitive skills, rather than being confined
059   to a specific task (McCarthy et al., 1955). Creating artificial systems with such general intelligence
060   has been a long-standing and ambitious goal of AI research. The most exciting progresses in AI are
061   achieved by language models in these years, from the initial start of ChatGPT to its evolution and
062   other open-source projects (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Peng et al.,
063   2024).
064   Early LLM pioneers set out goals to understand and interact with human by exploring generalizable
065   reasoning mechanisms and building knowledge bases with vast amounts of commonsense information.
066   With parameters and data volume in place, the question of how to effectively prompt the model from
067   the user end and train it from the developer end has become a trending topic of exploration (Wei et al.,
068   2022; Ouyang et al., 2022). On the user side, varying prompting techniques can significantly impact
069   model performance. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2022; Saunders
070   et al., 2022), one of the most popular strategies, leverages the model’s internal reasoning patterns to
071
      enhance its ability to solve complex tasks. OpenAI capitalized on this by embedding the CoT process
      into model training, integrating reinforcement learning, and finally introduced the o1 model (OpenAI,
072
      2024). While the o1 model demonstrates strong performance in general domains, its effectiveness in
073
      specialized fields like medicine—where domain-specific training may be lacking—remains uncertain.
074   Moreover, current benchmarks for LLMs in the medical domain often evaluate models only on a
075   limited set of factors, often focusing on isolated aspects such as knowledge and reasoning (Nori et al.,
076   2023b; Liévin et al., 2024), safety (Han et al., 2024), or multilinguality (Wang et al., 2024). These
077   factors make a comprehensive assessment of LLMs’ capabilities—especially for advanced models
078   like o1—in medical challenging tasks (Figure 1).
079
      This paper aims to provide an initiative to close this gap, focusing on o1. We identify three
080   fundamental aspects of LLMs in medicine: understanding, reasoning, and multilinguality. To
081   evaluate these capabilities, we assembled 35 existing medical datasets and developed two novel,
082   challenging QA datasets that include instructions and expected outputs, ensuring comprehensive
083   assessment. With evaluation on this extensive suite, our key findings include:
084
085    • o1 demonstrates improved transfer of clinical understanding and reasoning abilities, validating its
086      competence in real-world diagnostic scenarios compared with both close- and open-source models
087
         as presented in Figure 1 and Figure 2;
088    • No single model excels across all tasks on our medical leaderboard, though o1 comes close to
089      dominating most evaluations;
090    • o1 still suffers from the long-standing issue of hallucination and complex multilingual medical
091      cases;
092    • Inconsistencies in metrics for medical NLP can significantly affect models’ standings, which calls
093      for a re-evaluation of reliable metrics for future LLMs;
094    • CoT prompting can further enhance o1 in medicine, despite its training having already integrated
095      CoT data.
096
097   In addition to these findings, we also elevate the discussion section as an initial attempt to address
098   the issues identified during our benchmarking in Section Section 5. Particularly, we highlight the
099   potential negative effects of o1, emphasize the urgent need for consistent and unified evaluation
100   metrics for future LLMs, and advocate for improved instruction templates that can be applied to
101
      models with embedded prompting strategies.

149
150   First, we present the taxonomy of our evaluation, along with an overview of the evaluation pipeline as
151   shown in Figure 3. Firstly, we specify three aspects of the model capabilities, namely understanding,
152   reasoning, and multilinguality, that correspond to the real-world needs of clinical physicians. To
153
      ensure a comprehensive evaluation, we collect a diverse range of medical tasks and datasets that
      fall under these three aspects. Moreover, we explore three prompting strategies in our pipeline,
154
      including (1) direct prompting, which instructs LLMs to solve specific problems directly, (2) chain-of-
155
      thought, which requires models to think step-by-step before generating the final answer, (3) few-shot
156   prompting, which providing models with several examples to learn the input-output mapping on the
157   fly. Lastly, appropriate metrics are utilized to measure the discrepancy between generated responses
158   and ground-truth answers. Details about metrics utilized in each dataset are provided in Table 1.

210   3.2          A SPECTS AND TASKS
211
212   In Table 1, our evaluation efforts are structured into three main parts: aspect, task, and dataset.
213   Specifically, a dataset refers to the data itself along with the metrics used in the current context. We
214   utilize 35 existing datasets and create 2 additional challenging datasets for evaluation. A task is a
215   collection of multiple datasets that share a common goal or evaluate similar capabilities within the
      model. We categorize all 37 datasets into 6 tasks for clearer evaluation and analysis. An aspect


                                                                                          4
216
      describes a specific capability or property to understand how well the model performs in a particular
217   area. In our evaluation pipeline, we focus on three key aspects.
218
219   Formally, we illustrate these three evaluation aspects with their corresponding tasks as follows:
220
      • Understanding refers to the model’s ability to utilize its internal medical knowledge to com-
221
        prehend medical concepts. For example, in concept recognition task, the model is required to
222     extract or elaborate medical concepts from article (Savery et al., 2020; Pafilis et al., 2013; Nye
223     et al., 2018) or diagnosis report (Zhao et al., 2023). And in text summarization, the model need to
224     understand concepts in complex texts to generate a concise summary (Lee et al., 2021; Wallace
225     et al., 2021; Johnson et al., 2019; 2023).
226   • Reasoning is the ability to conduct multiple steps of logical thinking to arrive at the conclusion.
227     In question answering tasks, the model is prompted to select correct option from multi-choices
228     based on reasoning derived from the medical information provided in the question. In addition
229     to common question-answering datasets (Jin et al., 2019; Pal et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2021), we
230     collect real-world clinical questions from The Lancet, the New England Journal of Medicine
231     (NEJM), and Medbullets (Chen et al., 2024) to better assess the clinical utility of LLMs. In the
232     clinical suggestion task, the model is required to provide treatment suggestions (Dubey et al.,
233     2023; Li et al., 2023) or diagnostic decisions (Xie et al., 2022; Fansi Tchango et al., 2022) based
234
        on patients’ information. In the AI Hospital (Fan et al., 2024) and AgentClinic (Schmidgall
        et al., 2024) datasets, we task the model with serving as a medical agent. Furthermore, in the
235
        MedCalc-Bench (Khandekar et al., 2024) dataset, the model is required to perform mathematical
236
        reasoning and calculate answers.
237
238
      • Multilinguality is the ability to complete a task when the languages of input instruction and/or
        output answers are changed to different languages. For example, XMedBench (Wang et al., 2024)
239
        dataset requires LLMs to answer medical questions in six languages, including Chinese, Arabic,
240
        Hindi, Spanish, Chinese and English. In AI Hospital dataset (Fan et al., 2024), the model is
241     required to serve as an agent using Chinese.

      3.3   M ETRICS
244
245   In this section, we elaborate on metrics employed in our evaluation pipeline.
246
247   • Accuracy is used to directly measure the percentage of models’ generated answer which exactly
248     match with the ground-truth. We use accuracy for multi-choice question datasets, MedCalc-
249     Bench (Khandekar et al., 2024) dataset, and portions of clinical suggestion and concept recognition
250
        datasets where the ground-truth answer is a single word or phrase.
251   • F1-score (Pedregosa et al., 2011) is the harmonic mean of precision and recall. It is employed in
252     datasets where the model is required to select multiple correct answers.
253   • BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin & Hovy, 2002) are NLP metrics measuring the
254     similarity between the generated respond and the ground-truth. Specifically, we utilize BLEU-1
255     and ROUGE-1 for all free-form generation tasks in our evaluation.
256   • AlignScore (Zha et al., 2023) is a metric to measure the factual consistency of generated text. In
257     this paper, we use AlignScore for all free-form generation tasks to evaluate the extent of model’s
258     hallucination.
259   • Mauve (Pillutla et al., 2021) is a measure of gap between distribution of generated and human-
260     written text. It is employed for all free-form generation tasks.
261
262   All metrics range from 0 to 100, and a higher number indicates better quality output from the model.

492   5   D ISCUSSION
493
494   What adverse impacts does o1 bring? The model o1 has made significant strides in both general
495   NLP and the medical domain—as demonstrated in this paper. But what adverse impacts does o1
496   have on users compared to the previous generations of LLMs? While embedding the Chain of
497   Thought (CoT) process during generation by default requires more time (OpenAI, 2024), what exactly
498   distinguishes o1 from other OpenAI models? In Table 10, we see that o1 has more than 2× and 9×
499   longer decoding time cost on four medical tasks compared to GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, respectively
500
      (13.18s vs. 6.89s vs. 1.41s). This increased decoding time can lead to significant waiting periods
      when handling complex tasks.
501
502   Additionally, o1 does not always outperform other models, with inconsistent performance across
503   different tasks. For instance, in the concept recognition task detailed in Table 2, o1 underperforms
504   compared to other LLMs on half of the datasets. This discrepancy may relate to recent findings
505   suggesting that CoT data is most advantageous in more complex reasoning tasks (Sprague et al.,
506
      2024). However, in tasks that do not require complex reasoning, such as concept recognition, o1
      does not have significant advantages over them.
507
508
509   Rethinking evaluation metrics for stronger LLMs. Traditional evaluation metrics like BLEU and
510   ROUGE, which rely on n-gram overlap, have long been criticized for their limitations in capturing the
511   quality of generated text, particularly for LLMs. As a result, using models like GPT-4 as evaluators,
512   i.e., “LLM-as-a-judge”, has gained popularity for assessing the outputs of other models. However,
513
      this approach may not be valid when applied to the most advanced models such as o1, as GPT-
      4 is even less capable and thus may produce less reliable evaluation. This is especially true for
514
      specialized domain like medicine. Therefore, there is a growing need to develop more robust and
515
      nuanced evaluation metrics that can better assess the performance of state-of-the-art LLMs in complex
516   scenarios.
517
518
519
      Call for reliable prompting techniques for future LLMs. As noted in Section 4.3, not all advanced
      prompting techniques positively impact o1’s performance. As future LLMs like o1 may continue to
520
      evolve with internal prompts for efficient user instruction, new prompting methods should consider
521
      their adaptability to existing strategies. One potential exploration could be the integration of two
522   prompting strategies (Wang et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024).
523
524
525
      Limitations. While we conduct comprehensive evaluations in the medical domain on understand-
      ing, reasoning, and multilingual capabilities, there are many other dimensions to consider such as
526
      safety (Han et al., 2024) and we leave them for future work. Additionally, we leave more advanced
527
      prompting techniques such as retrieval augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) for future
528   work, which may enhance the factuality and mitigate hallucination. It is worth noting that current
529   GPT-like models may still underperform BERT-based specialists in classification tasks (Nori et al.,
530   2023b). However, we focus on GPT-like generalists in this paper due to their greater flexibility as
531   zero-shot learners.

535
536   This preliminary study assesses 3 important aspects across 35 existing and 2 novel medical datasets
537   using the latest o1 model. It marks the first step towards a holistic evaluation of o1 in medicine,
538   and we present our initial results, analysis, and discussion over the benchmark. The findings provide
539   convincing evidence that o1 is narrowing the gap between AI and human doctors, shaping the vision
      of an ideal AI doctor closer to reality.

899   A.2   D ETAILS ABOUT DATASETS
900
      In this paper, we present a summary of 36 medical-related datasets spanning 6 distinct tasks, as
901
      outlined in Table 1. Notably, the inclusion of commercial models, particularly o1, leads to significant
902   costs and response latency. To address this, for some tasks we randomly sampled a subset of test
903   cases, which are detailed below.
904
905   Concept Recognition
906
907   • BC4Chem (Savery et al., 2020) is a dataset comprising 10,000 PubMed abstracts with 84,355
908     chemical entity mentions, manually annotated by expert chemistry literature curators. The task
909
        is to extract chemical names from the given abstracts. For evaluation, we randomly sample 300
        instances from the test set.
910
911   • BC5Chem and BC5Disease are from BC5CDR (Li et al., 2016), a widely-used resource in
912     biomedical natural language processing, annotated for chemical and disease entities and their
913     relationships. Following MedS-Bench (Wu et al., 2024b), BC5CDR is split into 2 datasets:
914
        chemical name extraction and disease name extraction. For evaluation, we randomly sample 300
        instances from each task’s test set.
915
916   • Species800 (Pafilis et al., 2013) comprises 800 PubMed abstracts with annotated organism
917     mentions. The task is to extract organism names from the given abstracts. For evaluation, we
        randomly sample 300 instances from the test set.


                                                         17
918
      • HoC (Baker et al., 2016) is a specialized dataset containing 1,852 PubMed publication abstracts,
919     expertly annotated according to a taxonomy of cancer hallmarks. The task is to classify the
920     hallmarks of cancer based on the given biomedical publication abstracts. For evaluation, we use
921     the entire test set consisting of 158 instances.
922
      • HumanDiseaseOntology (Schriml et al., 2019) is a database providing consistent, reusable, and
923     sustainable descriptions of human disease terms, phenotype characteristics, and related medical
924     vocabularies. The task is to explain specified medical professional entities, with the database
925     descriptions serving as ground truth. For evaluation, we randomly sample 300 instances.
926
      • BioLORD (Remy et al., 2024) comprises pairs of biomedical concept names and descriptions. The
927     task is to elaborate on concise concepts by generating long, detailed definitions. For evaluation,
928     we randomly sample 300 instances.
929
      • PMC-Patient (Zhao et al., 2023) is a collection of 167,000 patient summaries extracted from case
930     reports in PubMed Central (PMC), annotated with basic patient information. The task is to extract
931     patient gender and age information from given clinical texts. For evaluation, we randomly sample
932     300 instances.
933
      • PICO-Participant, PICO-Intervention and PICO-Outcome are three datasets derived from
934     PICO (Nye et al., 2018), consisting of 5,000 abstracts from medical articles on randomized
935     controlled clinical trials. The tasks involve extracting information about study participants,
936     interventions, and outcomes from given sentences. For evaluation, we use the entire test set of 43
937     instances for each task.
938   • ADE Corpus (Gurulingappa et al., 2012) provides information on drugs and their corresponding
939     adequate doses within sentences. The task is to extract the dosage levels of specified drugs from
940     given sentences and drug names. We use the dataset prompted by Super-Instruction with a 9:1
941     ratio for instruction tuning and evaluation. The test set consists of 23 instances.
942
943   Text Summary
944
      • MIMIC-IV-CT and MIMIC-IV-Ultrasound (Johnson et al., 2023; Wallace et al., 2021) are
945
        subsets of MIMIC-IV Report, a large deidentified medical dataset of patients admitted to the
946     Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center. The task is to summarize radiology reports, treating the
947     impression part as a general summary of the findings. Following (Wu et al., 2024b), we randomly
948     sampled 500 cases from body region part of Chest CT and 100 cases from ultrasound modality for
949     evaluation.
950   • RCT-Text (Wallace et al., 2021) is a dataset for summarizing medical evidence from clinical
951     studies in literature reviews. The task is to output the primary conclusions of each study given the
952     titles and abstracts. For evaluation, we randomly sample 100 instances.
953   • MedQSum (Lee et al., 2021) is derived from a large database of de-identified health-related data.
954     The task is to generate a summary of detailed findings from imaging diagnostic reports, with the
955     conclusion of the note serving as ground truth. For evaluation, we randomly sample 100 instances.
956
957   Knowledge QA
958
959
      • MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) is a collection of medical multiple-choice questions in English. We use
        the 4-option English version with the official split. The test set contains 1273 samples.
960
961   • PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) is an English question-answering dataset based on PubMed abstracts.
962
        The task is to answer research questions with yes/no/maybe. We use the PQA-L subset as the test
        set, containing 1000 samples.
963
964   • MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) is a large-scale English multiple-choice question-answering dataset
965     from AIIMS & NEET PG entrance exams. We use the official test split containing 4183 questions,
966
        each with 4 choices.
967   • LancetQA and NEJMQA are datasets curated from The Lancet and the New England Journal of
968     Medicine case challenges, focusing on patient diagnosis based on symptoms. We use 200 samples
969     for LancetQA and 100 samples for NEJMQA.
970   • Medbullets (Chen et al., 2024) is a dataset curated from the Medbullets online platform, compris-
971     ing 308 USMLE Step 2&3 style questions. Each question includes a case description, four answer
        choices, and an explanation.

975      Automatic Diagnosis systems, featuring synthesized patient data. The task is to make diagnostic
976      decisions based on dialogues. For evaluation, we randomly sample 300 instances.
977    • SEER (Dubey et al., 2023) is a treatment planning dataset based on the Surveillance, Epidemiology,

985
         involve discriminative and generative entailment based on clinical premises. For each task, we
986
         randomly sample 300 instances for evaluation.

988    • EBMS (Mollá & Santiago-Martinez, 2011) is a justification verification dataset. We use the entire

         test set of 304 instances for evaluation.

995      sample 300 instances.
996    • Chatdoctor (Li et al., 2023) is based on 100K patient-physician conversations from an online
