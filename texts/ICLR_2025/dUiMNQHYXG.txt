009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Extracting a visual interpretation of a learned representation of a machine learning
012            model applied to image data is a relevant task in eXplainable AI (XAI). Effective
013            visual explanations must reveal how specific features within the learned represen-
014            tation contribute to the model’s predictions. Pixel-level feature attributions are
015            a valuable tool for this, as they highlight the regions in the image that are most
016
               influential in the classification process. The hierarchical Owen approximation
               of the Shapley values has proved to be an effective strategy for this task. How-
017
               ever, existing approaches lack data-awareness, leading to poor alignment between
018
               the pixel-level attributions and the actual morphological features of the classified
019            image. This paper introduces ShapBPT, a novel XAI method that computes the
020            Owen approximation of the Shapley coefficients following a data-aware binary
021            hierarchical coalition structure, generated from the Binary Partition Tree com-
022            puter vision algorithm. By aligning with the morphological features of the image,
023            the proposed method significantly enhances the identification of relevant image
024            regions. Experimental results confirm the effectiveness of the proposed method.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In the field of AI, understanding how a black-box machine learning (ML) model classifies images
029
      is a task of critical importance to extract the representations the model has learned from the data.
030   We consider the problem of attributing importance scores to individual pixels in an image, which
031   reflect their contribution to the model’s decision-making process. This task is commonly referred to
032   as explaining a black-box machine learning (ML) model classifying images.
033
      In recent years, several notable practical approaches were developed to address this task. A pio-
034
      neering approach to this task was LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), which
035
      reformulates the problem of explaining image classifications by leveraging an image segmentation
036   algorithm. This transformation passes from pixel-level attribution values to segment-level scores,
037   computed using a simple linear regression model (Ribeiro et al., 2016). Although lacking theoreti-
038   cal guarantees, the effectiveness of LIME lies in its ability to potentially pre-identify relevant regions
039   through segmentation.
040
      Another influential method is SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), which applies game-theoretic
041
      principles to ML explainability. SHAP combines a feature removal (masking) technique (Lund-
042   berg & Lee, 2017) together with the use of a simple hierarchical image partitioning (Lundberg,
043   2020). Providing explanations over hierarchical image structures leverages multi-scale image fea-
044   tures, which provides better approximations of the representations learnt by the classification model.
045
      In general, it is reasonable to assume that in any image classification task, an effective ML model
046
      needs to learn some form of structured representation that combines some arbitrarily complex but
047
      distinct morphological characteristics of the classified objects (shape, texture, color continuity, etc),
048   as we assume that the model has learned to recognize structured patterns from the image data.
049   Consequently, adopting hierarchical partitions that are adaptive and data-aware can improve the
050   model’s interpretability by aligning more closely with the learned representations, as long as the
051   partitions are flexible and adaptive and not imposed a-priori (as we cannot assume which structured
052   representation the model has learnt). Such an approach ensures that the explanations reflect the
053   underlying features in a way that is both accurate and interpretable, without distorting the model’s
      internal hierarchy of representations.


                                                          1
054
      This paper provides the following contributions:
055
056             1. A novel hierarchical model-agnostic eXplainable AI (XAI) strategy that integrates an adap-
057                tive multi-scale partitioning algorithm with the Owen approximation of the Shapley coef-
058                ficients. We identify in the BPT (Binary Partition Tree) algorithm of Salembier & Garrido
059                (2000) a highly valuable candidate for such task. This approach overcomes the limitations
060                of the inflexible hierarchies adopted by existing state-of-the-art methods like SHAP.
061             2. An empirical assessment of the proposed method showcasing its efficacy across various
062                scoring targets, in comparison to established state-of-the-art XAI methods.
063
064
      We show that the proposed approach surpasses existing Shapley-based model-agnostic XAI meth-
      ods that do not leverage on data-awareness, and at the same time it achieves a significantly faster
065
      convergence rate. This efficiency stems from the fact that, on average, fewer recursive applications
066
      of the Owen formula (i.e. expansions of the partition hierarchy) are needed to accurately localize
067   objects when using a data-aware partition hierarchy, such as the proposed BPT hierarchy, compared
068   to other hierarchies. As far as we know, this is the first XAI method that combines the Owen formula
069   with a data-aware partition hierarchy for image data, and with this paper we prove the effectiveness
070   of this combined strategy for interpreting ML classifiers.

      2       M ETHODOLOGY
073
074
      A fundamental ML objective is to discover a function, denoted as f : X → Y, that effectively
075
      approximates a response y ∈ Y corresponding to a given input x ∈ X . For the sake of simplicity,
076   we will assume Y ⊆ R and X ⊆ Rn . In many practical cases only a subset of x significantly
077   influences the resulting response y = f (x). Understanding the relative importance, or contribution,
078   of each component xi of x in determining the value of y by f is a central problem in XAI. One
079   important approach proposed by Covert et al. (2021) for assessing these contributions is through a
080   technique known as feature removal or masking, wherein certain values of x are replaced with values
081   from a specified context-dependent background set. Let νf,x : 2|X | → Y be a masking function for
082   f (x), where νf,x (S) represents the resulting model evaluation when only the elements in the subset
083   S of x are retained, while the remainders are masked. Hereafter we will denote νf,x as ν.
084

151   3     H IERARCHICAL COALITION STRUCTURES FOR IMAGE DATA
152
153   Calculating Owen coalition values for image data necessitates a well-defined hierarchical structure
154   that captures both spatial relationships and image semantics. Our approach is aimed at addressing
155   limitations in existing methods, by emphasizing the importance of these factors in coalition forma-
156   tion. We therefore consider and compare both data-agnostic and data-aware approaches.
157   In a data-agnostic approach, partitions are created based on simple geometric divisions, like grids
158   or quadrants. The Axis Aligned hierarchy (AA hereafter) is one such approach to building hier-
159   archical coalition structures, adopted by the SHAP’s Partition Explainer (Lundberg, 2020) and by
160   h-SHAP (Teneggi et al., 2022) In an AA hierarchy, each partition T corresponds to a rectangular re-
161   gion within the image, and T ↓ splits the rectangular region of T in half along the longest axis. This
      splitting process continues until individual, indivisible regions (unitary regions, with a single pixel)

175
      within an image may require a large number of recursive evaluation of the Owen’s formula (4), and
176   this evaluation follows the O(4d ) time cost of Theorem 1.
177
178   In a data-aware approach, morphological features within the image guide the partitioning process.
179
      This approach, pioneered by Ribeiro et al. (2016) with LIME, utilizes a pre-defined segmentation
      algorithm to divide the image into regions (patches). Although effective, the main limitation is the
180
      lack of an effective feedback loop within the explanation method. If the segmentation is inaccurate,
181
      the resulting explanation is poor, and there is no opportunity for refinement.
182
183   A notable algorithm for hierarchical segmentation, that fits well with Eq. (4), is the Binary Partition
184   Tree (BPT) (Randrianasoa et al., 2018), originally developed for multiscale image representation in
185
      MPEG-7 encoding (Salembier & Garrido, 2000). The intuitive principle is that portions of an image
      with similar color and coherent shape are highly likely to have similar Shapley values, thereby
186
      maximizing the effectiveness of Eq. (4).
187
188   Theorem 1 shows that the Owen approximation cost increases rapidly if a large number of coalitions
189   need to be evaluated recursively. Therefore, an effective BHCS needs to satisfy these requirements:
190
191
               R1 As few recursive cuts as possible to reach the relevant regions, as each cut increases the
                  required evaluation budget b exponentially;
192
193            R2 Partitions should not be fixed, since the relevant regions are not known in advance.
194
195   AA hierarchies do no satisfy R1, and most a-priori segmentation algorithms do no satisfy R2. The
196
      solution that we propose, which constitutes the main contribution of this paper, is a novel hybrid
      method that finally statisfies the two aforementioned requirements by combining a dynamic a-priori
197
      hierarchical coalition structure (the BPT) aligned with the morphological features of the image (e.g.,
198
      color uniformity, pixel locality) together with an a-posteriori splitting strategy based on the distri-
199   bution of Shapley values (as in the Partition Explainer). This combination results in fewer recursive
200   applications of the Owen formula needed to accurately localize objects, compared to data-agnostic
201   coalition structures. As we shall see in the experimental section, this approach gets a significantly
202   faster convergence than other Shapley-based methods, paired with accurate shape recognition of the
203   classified objects.

209   up, starting from an initial coalition structure T[1] = T1 ={1} . . . Tn ={n} made by n unitary and
210   indivisible partitions, where the features 1 . . . n represents the individual pixels of the image. Two
211
      partitions Ti , Tj ∈ T[k] are adjacent if there is at least one pixel of Ti that is adjacent to a pixel of

212

213

      5    D ISCUSSION ON LIMITATIONS AND OTHER RELATED WORKS
433
434
      The proposed method, as repeatedly mentioned in the text, combines the SHAP Partition Explainer
435   of Lundberg (2020) with the partition hierarchy of the BPT algorithm of Salembier & Garrido
436   (2000). The rationale behind this combination lies in the notion that image regions sharing sim-
437   ilar morphological characteristics are likely to exhibit comparable Shapley contributions. If this
438   rationale is not satisfied for a partition, such partition can be further subdivided, progressively local-
439   izing the regions activating model recognition. The proposed approach is general, but it is particu-
440   larly useful for the Visual Recognition Challenge (Russakovsky et al., 2015) problem. However, the
441   effectiveness of this approach is based on the assumption of a correlation between image morpho-
442   logical features and their corresponding explanations. While the assumption may appear reasonable,
443
      assessing its complete impact is challenging.
      A close concern is related to the introduction of potential biases. After extensive experimentation
444
      with the proposed method, we hypothesize that BPT partitions do not introduce significant biases
445
      w.r.t. other Owen approximations. However, we have not formally quantified this assertion, leaving
446   it as a subject for future research.
447
448   We considered also the h-Shap approach of Teneggi et al. (2022), which exhibits faster convergence
449
      than the one derived in Theorem 1. Unfortunately, the different definition of the object recognition
      task makes the comparison challenging, and we have not included it in the evaluation. However, we
450
      believe that also h-Shap would greatly benefit from using BPT partitions.
451
452   We used the quickshift algorithm to generate the fixed a priori partitions for LIME. We also evaluated
453   the more recent SegmentAnything partitioning algorithm, which offers some improvements over
454   quickshift, albeit at the cost of being significantly slower. However, the rigidity of working with a
455
      priori partitions that may not align with the model’s internal representation persists, a limitation that
      is addressed by the proposed BPT approach.
456
457   We initially considered incorporating the relevance mass and rank accuracy scores from Arras et al.
458   (2022) into our analysis of the experiment results. However, we ultimately decided against it, as
459   these metrics rely on non-negative values, which are incompatible with Shapley values.
460   While Eq. (5) provides reasonable partitionings in the experimental setup, it is also well recognised
461   to be a critical (Randrianasoa et al., 2021) component of the BPT algorithm. A complete analysis
462   and optimization of this heuristic equation has not beed carried out, and it is left for a future work.
463
      As a side note, we empirically observed that the AUC + and AUC − scores (Petsiuk et al., 2018),
464
      which are considered among the gold standards for XAI evaluation (Nauta et al., 2023), do not al-
465   ways align with our intuition. For example, in Figure 3/B, AUC + shows a significant overshoot
466   above the ν(N ) prediction value. While this is beyond the goal of this paper, we believe further
467   investigation into this class of XAI scores is needed, particularly regarding the behavior of over-
468   shooting beyond the prediction range of ν(N ) and ν(∅) (more details in Appendix A.8).

      6    C ONCLUSIONS
471
472
      This paper introduces a novel eXplainable AI method, named ShapBPT, that generates image ex-
473
      planations by computing the the Owen approximation of the Shapley coefficients following a data-
474   aware Binary Partition Tree hierarchy. We provide the formulation of the method, including the
475   approximation at the indivisible partitions, its computational cost, and the algorithms. An evalua-
476   tion is performed on multiple settings, models and datasets, with a full scale comparison with other
477   state-of-the-art XAI methods. We believe that our method produces explanations that are noticeably
478   better both visually and quantitatively compared to existing methods, as they are built following a
479   coalition structure that is hierarchically and adaptively expanded to better follow the morphological
480   features of the image data, which are assumed to be the representation learnt by the model.

728        A.4    P SEUDO - CODE OF THE BPT ALGORITHM
729
730        Detailed pseudo-code for the BPT algorithm can be found in (Salembier & Garrido, 2000; Randri-
731
           anasoa et al., 2018; 2021), but a pseudo-code is provided in Algorithm 2. The algorithm is made by
           three functions:
732
733                • init bpt: initializes the unitary partitions i of the BPT hierarchy from the individual pixels
734                  px of the input image x, and creates the heap of all the pairs of adjacent pixels.
735                • get dist: computes the distance between two (adjacent) partitions i and j using Eq. (5).
736
                   • build bpt: ieratively merges adjacent partitions in distance-order, each time creating a new
737
                     merged partition k, and updates the weights in the heap accordingly. The function proceeds
738
                     as long as there are adjacent partitions, i.e. it stops when all pixels are merged into a single
739                  root partition.
740
741        Once Algorithm 2 has generated a merging sequence, it can be efficiently stored into 6 arrays:
742
743
                   • leaf idx [i]: the image pixel of unitary coalition i, with i ∈ [1, n];
744                • left branch[k] and right branch[k]: the two partition indexes resulting from the split Tk ↓
745                  of each non-unitary coalition k, with k ∈ [n + 1, 2n − 1];
746                • start[k] and end [k]: the index interval of pixels for the non-unitary partition k;
747                • pixels: the sorted array of pixel indexes, indexed by start and end .
748
749        Therefore, the space needed to store the BPT hierarchy in memory is Θ(6n) integers.
750        The core data structure is a graph of the partitions (nodes), paired with the list of adjacencies (edges).
751        The adjacency list needs to be sorted efficiently in order to extract the edge adj = (i, j) having the
752        smallest dist(i, j), as defined by Eq. (5) and computed by function get dist. To do so, a heap data
753        structure is a reasonable choice. Merging coalitions therefore requires to both modify the nodes and
754        update the edges. This process, described at line 11 of build bpt and depicted in Figure 2/B, shows
755        that each merge operation requires to traverse the adjacency list of the merged partitions. Further
           details are provided in the paper of Randrianasoa et al. (2018).

      11      merge linked lists of adjacencies of i and j into a single linked list for partition k, updating the heap

                weights using get dist since partitions i and j are now merged together.

       A.14         R ANDOMIZATION T EST
1230
1231   In all previous experiments, we assume that the BPT hierarchy is constructed using the same input
1232   image, making it inherently data-aware. However, a question not addressed in these tests is: What
1233   happens if the target object(s) is not well-aligned with such a hierarchy? To evaluate performance in
1234   this scenario, we designed a randomization test. In this test, the BPT hierarchy is constructed using
1235   a different image than the one being explained, resulting in a hierarchy that is not aligned with the
1236   actual data being explained. We refer to this ”misaligned” BPT hierarchy as BPTR (Random). The
1237   goal is to compare the performance of BPTR against a baseline (AA), assessing whether the results
1238
       remain at least comparable despite the misalignment.
1239   Figure 20 shows on the first line the image used for constructing the BPT hierarchy, used then to
1240   generate the explanations (in the E2 context) for the other three sample images (a), (b) and (c) in the
1241   BPTR columns. Results are compared against the standard data-aware BPT, and the AxisAligned
       strategy of SHAP.
