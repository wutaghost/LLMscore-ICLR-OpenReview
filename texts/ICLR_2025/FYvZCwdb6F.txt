009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Warning: This paper contains examples that may be offensive.
012             Predicting which social media posts will go viral is a critical but complex task in
013             the field of computational social science. Previous studies have utilized various
014             measures to forecast the virality of tweets or Facebook posts, but these approaches
015             exhibit limitations, particularly in the absence of a virality metric that specifically
016             considers social biases. In this paper, we test existing metrics and introduce a
017             new metric, ViralTweet Score (VTS), inspired by principles of momentum from
018             physics to better predict a tweet’s virality given that it consists of social biases.
019             We compare this new metric with others, highlighting the advantages and dis-
020
                advantages of each of them as a virality measurement metric. We release the
                ViralTweets Dataset with 88.8k Hindi tweets and corresponding virality labels
021
                based on our VTS metric. We also show how social biases in posts can influence
022
                their potential to go viral. We test our hypothesis that VTS is a better metric using
023             two methodologies and we show how VTS achieves an F1 score of 0.87 based
024             on pairwise evaluation methodology and an overall F1 score of 0.58 based on our
025             clustering-based verification methodology. Our work offers a novel metric for un-
026             derstanding tweet virality for biased tweets and opens the door for more equitable
027             and effective social media analytics by considering the role of social biases in
028             virality.1

035   as a prominent arena for public discourse, facilitating real-time communication and interaction on a

040   practitioners, and policymakers Han (2020).

      et al. (2021). By analyzing viral tweets and introducing a metric (VTS) that accounts for social bias

046   virality and broader public discourse.

      3.1       S OCIAL N ETWORKS DYNAMICS
122
123   The nature of Twitter as both a social network and a news media platform has significant implica-
124   tions for information spread. Early studies of Twitter have shown that over 85% of trending topics
125   on Twitter relate to headline or persistent news Java et al. (2007); Kwak et al. (2010). This dual
126   nature drives virality mechanisms. Pan et al. (2019) highlight the role of social network homophily
127   in enhancing user occupation predictions through network-based features. Duan et al. (2012) assess
128   how social influence and content quality affect Twitter topic summarization, emphasizing user inter-
129
      actions’ importance. Rahimi et al. (2015) explore user geolocation by leveraging text and network
      context, illustrating how social ties assist in profiling. Together, these studies underscore the com-
130
      plex interplay between social relations and content properties in shaping social media information
131
      dissemination.
132

      3.2       C ONTENT V IRALITY
134
135   Content virality has been extensively studied, particularly in the context of images and news. Key
136   studies have proposed various metrics and models to understand, predict content virality:
137
138             • Image Virality: Works by Deza & Parikh (2015); Dubey & Agarwal (2017) and Guerini
139               et al. (2013) focus on understanding the virality of images. These studies explore the
140               visual characteristics that contribute to virality and propose metrics such as average score
141               or resubmissions to quantify it.
142             • News Virality: The prediction of news virality has been tackled by Lu & Szymanski
143
                  (2018); Benson (2020), who use various machine learning approaches to predict the spread
                  of news articles based on community structures and textual content, respectively.
144
                • Social Media Content: The diffusion of content such as memes and videos has been
145
                  analyzed in studies like Ling et al. (2021; 2022), which dissect the elements that make such
146               content go viral on platforms like TikTok.

      3.3       V IRALITY M ETRICS
149
150   The definition and quantification of virality are crucial for both theoretical and practical applications:
151
152             • General Metrics: Studies such as Kwak et al. (2010) have examined the role of influencers
153               and the importance of metrics like retweets and PageRank in determining the spread of
154               information.
155             • Multimodal Metrics: Research by Wang et al. (2018) and Wong et al. (2023) has de-
156               veloped deeper insights into the virality of content by considering multimodal aspects,
157
                  integrating both textual and visual data.
                • Emotional and Psychological Aspects: The impact of emotions on virality is explored in
158
                  Pröllochs et al. (2021), which links the emotional content of online rumors to their spread
159
                  and influence.

      The ViralTweet Score Metric is introduced to quantify the virality of tweets based on their engage-
301   ment dynamics over time. Unlike traditional metrics that may consider static counts of likes or
302   retweets, this metric emphasizes the rate of change in engagement, capturing the momentum of a
303   tweet’s spread. This approach is particularly useful for understanding how quickly a tweet gains
304   popularity, which is a critical aspect of virality in fast-paced social media environments.
305
306
      5.1     D EFINITION
307
308   Given a tweet T weet[i] in the dataset, we have time-series data for T + 1 timestamps detailing
309   various engagement metrics and user account information. Each metric offers insight into how the
310   tweet is being received and shared among users:
311
312           • likes (favourite count): Total number of times the tweet has been liked by users.
313           • retweet count: Total number of times the tweet has been retweeted without modifications.
314           • follower count: Total number of followers of the tweet creator on the platform. This is the
315             only metric used from user account information.
316

392   6.1      PAIR - WISE C OMPARISON OF T WEETS
393
394   The first analytical method to test performance of virality metrics in this study is the pair-wise
395   comparison of tweets. This approach allows us to directly compare the ViralTweet Score of two
396   tweets and determine which one exhibits greater virality under similar conditions. Each pair of
397   tweets is selected based on having comparable initial conditions such as similar posting times, initial
398
      user engagement, or demographic reach. Figure 1 gives an overview of how we compare two models
      finetuned to predict two different metrics using the test data, to assess the “goodness” of a metric for
399
      virality prediction.
400
401
402   6.1.1      S ELECTION C RITERIA
403
      Tweets are paired using the following criteria to ensure fairness and relevance in comparisons:
404
405
               • Temporal Proximity: Tweets posted within similar time frames are compared to control
406
                 for variations in user online activity.
407            • Initial Engagement: Tweets with similar initial engagement metrics (likes, retweets within
408              the first hour of posting) but an order of magnitude difference, after one day period are
409              paired to normalize starting popularity.

      6.2        U NSUPERVISED PREDICTION OF V IRALITY
433
434   The second analytical method to test the performance of virality metrics in this study is the unsuper-
435   vised prediction of bias and virality as a classification task.
436
      For this analysis, we divided our data into training and testing sets. We employed different multilin-
437
      gual models (detailed in Section 7) to encode the tweets. All nine bias categories were consolidated
438   into two categories: ‘biased’ for any tweet falling into one of the bias categories and ‘no-bias’ for
439   tweets without identified biases. Similarly, we classified the top K% (K can be 10, 15, 20, 25
440   as shown in Table 4) of tweets as ‘viral’ based on each virality metric, with the rest categorized
441   as ‘non-viral’. We then utilized clustering techniques on the tweet embeddings to identify cluster
442   centers. For the test data, tweets were assigned to one of these four clusters based on their closest
443   similarity to the cluster centers. The motivation behind this approach is to explore whether unsuper-
444   vised methods, which do not rely on predefined labels, can effectively distinguish between viral and
445   non-viral content and how they behave for biased and unbiased tweets using different metrics. This
446
      methodology allows us to evaluate the robustness and generalizability of each metric across various
      contexts without the potential biases introduced by supervised learning labels.

521   8      C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523
524   This study rigorously evaluated various virality metrics, with a special focus on the ViralTweet Score
525   (VTS), across multiple advanced NLP models. Our findings demonstrate that VTS is superior in pre-
526   dicting tweet virality, surpassing traditional metrics like Likes, Retweets, and Retweets per Follower
527   count. This metric’s capacity to capture the dynamic changes in social media engagement makes it
528   a more precise indicator of a content’s potential to go viral. Its robust performance across diverse
529   models, including XLM-Roberta, mT0, and sarvam-2b, confirms its effectiveness and applicability
530
      in practical scenarios where rapid and accurate assessment of social media content is essential.
531   Our analysis confirms that the ViralTweet Score (VTS) most effectively captures tweet virality, out-
532   performing other metrics in accounting for dynamic social media interactions and biases, confirming
533   its utility in addressing RQ1. Moreover, VTS effectively differentiates the virality of biased versus
534   unbiased tweets, providing nuanced insights into how social biases impact virality, addressing RQ2.
535   As a future work, we can incorporate more complex data sources such as user demographic de-
536   tails and temporal engagement patterns to refine virality. Additionally, investigating the influence
537   of external events on social media dynamics could provide deeper insights into how real-world phe-
538   nomena drive online interactions. These areas not only promise to enhance the predictive power of
539   virality metrics but also offer potential to improve strategies for content management and dissemi-
      nation in digital platforms.
