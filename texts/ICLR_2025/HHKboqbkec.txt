                                                 A BSTRACT
 1            Theory-of-Mind (ToM) enables individuals to the mental states of the others, such
 2            as thoughts, beliefs, and desires. To replicate this cognitive ability in machines,
 3            especially under complex multimodal environments, recent advances combine
 4            Bayesian-based state inference with deep learning models to estimate mental states,
 5            where the Bayesian model handles state transitions and a language model (LM)
 6            estimates the likelihood of intermediate states. However, while post-training an LM
 7            to specialise in ToM tasks improves performance, the computational cost increases
 8            as the LM scales, limiting the model size to 7 billion parameters. Despite this post-
 9            training process, smaller LMs still struggle with the physical and mental modelling
10            demands of ToM due to their limited world knowledge and reasoning capacity. To
11            address this, we propose a scalable solution that leverages the strengths of larger
12            LMs (up to 70 and 405 billion parameters, respectively), including their vast world
13            knowledge and atomic-level reasoning capabilities, without increasing post-training
14            resource requirements. Our method transfers ToM-specific behaviours from a post-
15            trained small LM to guide the latent reasoning of a larger LM during test time. This
16            weak-to-strong control mechanism enables the larger LM to improve Bayesian
17            likelihood estimation at each inference step, harnessing its reasoning power in
18            ToM scenarios while reducing the need for additional training resources. Extensive
19            experiments demonstrate the significant effectiveness of our scaled approach. It is
20            better at inferring human mental states in complex and interactive environments,
21            outperforming the state-of-the-art solution by ∼ 4.6% across multiple tasks on the
22            multimodal ToM benchmark and unseen scenarios.


23   1    I NTRODUCTION
24   A key aspect of human social cognition is Theory-of-Mind (ToM)—the ability to comprehend and
25   attribute mental states such as beliefs, desires, and intentions to ourselves and others. This capacity
26   allows individuals to recognize that others may have perspectives and motivations distinct from
27   their own, forming the foundation of social understanding and interaction (Dennett, 1988; Gopnik &
28   Wellman, 2012). Building on this concept, a critical challenge lies in enabling artificial intelligence
29   (AI) to acquire human-level ToM capabilities. Equipping AI systems with such abilities could
30   significantly enhance their potential for human-like interactions and unlock broader capacities for
31   commonsense and context-aware reasoning (Lake et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2023).
32   Among the ongoing efforts to advance ToM abilities in machines, two broadly classed strategies are
33   the highly structured Bayesian approaches and the less structured end-to-end approaches, respectively:
34   (i) Bayesian ToM models use cognitively structured probabilistic frameworks to represent causal
35   relationships between the mind and the world, enabling inverse inference of mental states from sparse
36   behavioral observations (Baker et al., 2017; Jara-Ettinger, 2019; Shu et al., 2021). These models are
37   highly interpretable and support key ToM functions like explanation and inductive learning, excelling
38   in well-defined domains with precise predictions and few-shot generalization (Shum et al., 2019;
39   Zhi-Xuan et al., 2022). However, they require extensive inductive constraints (e.g., abstractions,
40   priors, and causal relationships) provided by experts, limiting their scalability to complex and
41   unconstrained practical environments. (ii) End-to-end models of ToM involve training deep models
42   directly on ToM data (Rabinowitz et al., 2018; Shu et al., 2021; Sclar et al., 2022). These models,
43   such as ToMnet (Rabinowitz et al., 2018), autonomously learn complex patterns and relationships
44   from data without explicitly concepts like agents, beliefs, and goals. However, because these models

45   do not explicitly encode principles of physics or theories of psychology, they lack transparency and
46   may draw conclusions that violate physical laws, logical coherence, or commonsense reasoning.
47   Additionally, their data-driven nature makes them less reliable in, and adaptable to, dynamic or
48   novel environments, particularly where data is sparse or unrepresentative (Sap et al., 2022; Zhi-Xuan
49   et al., 2022; Ullman, 2023; Strachan et al., 2024).
50   The recent interdisciplinary milestone, BIPALM (Bayesian inverse planning accelerated by language
51   model), merges the strengths of Bayesian ToM models and deep learning to facilitate robust reasoning
52   in complex multimodal scenarios (Jin et al., 2024). It uses a Bayesian ToM model to predict agents’
53   mental states in multimodal scenarios, with a language model (LM) estimating the probabilities
54   of these Bayesian intermediate states based on multimodal inputs. For accurate Bayesian ToM
55   inference, the LM undergoes an additional post-training process tailored to the target multimodal
56   contexts. However, this reliance on post-training significantly increases computational demands
57   and limits the scalability of likelihood models, restricting the size of LMs to around seven billion
58   parameters. ToM intricately intertwines open-domain world knowledge and implicit reasoning to
59   ground human mental states within their corresponding physical environments. As environments and
60   their associated queries evolve, reliance on smaller post-trained LMs introduces a critical trade-off:
61   while smaller LMs excel in adapting to specific ToM tasks within particular environments, larger
62   LMs are indispensable for harnessing broader world knowledge and advanced reasoning capabilities.
63   This inherent tension poses a significant challenge, requiring us to improve the scalability of
64   Bayesian ToM methods in handling dynamically evolving, multimodal ToM scenarios
65   Motivated by the scalability challenge of current Bayesian ToM methods, we propose a scalable
66   Bayesian inference solution that generalises to complex and dynamic environments (Tab.5 in App.A
67   compares our technical contributions with other solutions). Our approach introduces a weak-to-strong
68   control mechanism where post-trained smaller LMs specialise in ToM-specific tasks by capturing
69   likelihood inference patterns during Bayesian ToM reasoning. These ToM behaviours are then
70   transferred to larger LMs during test time, aligning the larger models’ reasoning trajectories with
71   the structured requirements of Bayesian inverse planning. In this framework, the larger LM acts
72   as the primary policy model, leveraging its extensive world knowledge and reasoning capabilities.
73   Importantly, the reasoning trajectory of the larger LM is structured by the Bayesian framework,
74   ensuring consistency, robustness, and interpretability. This design avoids additional post-training
75   costs for larger LMs while enabling scalable use of large models up to 70B or 405B parameters.
76   In particular, we focus on the pattern shifts observed in smaller LMs before and after ToM post-
77   training, treating these shifts as ToM behaviours that guide larger LMs. This ToM-behaviour transfer
78   ensures that larger LMs, redirected as aligned policy models, follow reasoning trajectories primarily
79   inferenced by the Bayesian framework. The experiments demonstrate that this weak-to-strong control
80   mechanism significantly enhances the generalizability of Bayesian inference, achieving a ∼ 4.6%
81   improvement in accuracy over state-of-the-art methods, even in dynamic and unseen environments.


82   2     M ETHODOLOGY: S CALED BAYESIAN R EASONING ON M ULTIMODAL T O M

83   Our scaled Bayesian reasoning infers an agent’s mental state based on video and text inputs. While
84   the extension of Bayesian methods to deep models can reverse engineer human ToM reasoning in
85   multiple domains, it also includes LMs for multimodal inputs: (1) building unified representations
86   about a scene, a person’s actions, and the mental state hypotheses from multimodal inputs, and (2)
87   post-training an LM to conduct contextual inverse symbolic planning, based on unified symbolic
88   representations (Jin et al., 2024). Then, as shown in Fig.1, the LM used in our scaled Bayesian
89   reasoning is from 7B up to 405B parameters at test-time compute, avoiding additional post-training.

109   2.2         I NFERRING H UMAN M ENTAL S TATES

110   To infer human mental states, we generate and evaluate hypotheses about human intentions by
111   employing Bayesian inverse planning. These hypotheses are assessed through action likelihoods,
112   which directly inform the posterior probabilities of different mental state explanations. To compute
113   these crucial likelihoods, an LM is controlled by our weak-to-strong mechanism to serve as the policy
114   model. This approach harnesses the world knowledge and reasoning capabilities of large LLMs while
115   aligning their behaviour patterns to ToM tasks without extensive computational requirements.

116   Behaviour modelling: a Markov decision process formulation The behaviour of an agent can be
117   formulated as a forward generative model based on a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process
118   (POMDP), defined by the tuple ⟨S, A, T , G, R, Ω, O, γ⟩ (Kaelbling et al., 1998; Jin et al., 2024).
119   Here, st ∈ S and at ∈ A represent the state and action at time t, respectively. T (st |s, a) denotes
120   the state transition probabilities. The goal g ∈ G determines the reward rt = R(st , at , g). The
121   agent’s observation ot ∈ Ω is obtained via the observation function ot = O(st ). The discount factor
122   is γ ∈ (0, 1]. Crucially, the agent’s belief, b(s), is a probability distribution over the state. This
123   belief is dynamically updated during belief evolution P (bτ | bτ −1 , sτ ), where b(s) is factorized into
124   probabilities over the possible locations of individual objects.

125   Inverse inference: from observed behaviours to the mental states While the POMDP formulates
126   a forward model of agent behaviour, the heart of this Bayesian approach is to invert this process –
127   inferring the agent’s goals and beliefs from observed behaviours, i.e., actions (Baker et al., 2017).
128   Assuming deterministic state transitions for simplicity, we jointly infer the agent’s goal and belief
129   based on observed states and actions. The posterior probability of an agent’s goal g and belief bt
130   given a sequence of observed states s1:t and actions a1:t−1 is expressed as:

183   3     E XPERIMENTS

184   We examine the scaling benefits of this Bayesian weak-to-strong reasoning in multimodal ToM tasks.
185   For the strong component, we scale up the large LMs to 70B and 405B parameters. In contrast, for
186   the weak component, we reduce the size of the small LMs from 8B to 4B parameters. First, the
187   results reveal a positive correlation between model size and ToM capabilities, especially when the
188   larger models are guided by the post-trained behaviours of the smaller models. Interestingly, these
189   post-trained behaviours are also effectively captured by smaller LMs. We also illustrate how the large
190   LMs are progressively redirected to the answer space during the Bayesian process.

191   3.1    S ETUP

192   Datasets (i) For post-training, we use MMToM sampled from an apartment environment simulator,
193   Virtual Home (Puig et al., 2018), using the procedural methods described by Jin et al. (2024). The
194   dataset comprises 1,000 procedurally synthesized videos within a realistic household simulator, each
195   annotated with ground-truth labels for states, goals, beliefs, and actions. These precise annotations
196   are used to train our conditional prediction model for action ai given state si , belief bi , and goal gi :
197   P (ai | si , bi , gi ). By leveraging this synthetic dataset, the inverse symbolic planner—comprising a
198   large base LM guided by a smaller, post-trained LM—acquires robust exposure to diverse scenarios.
199   (ii) For evaluation, we use the MMToM-QA (Jin et al., 2024), an evaluation benchmark aimed at
200   evaluating ToM reasoning over multimodal situations. The dataset consists of 134 videos, each
201   showing a person searching for household objects, with an average of 1,462 frames per video
202   representing approximately 36 human actions. These videos are accompanied by 600 questions
203   (detailed in appendix §D.1), evenly divided between the categories of belief inference (with 1.1,
204   1.2, and 1.3 subtasks) and goal inference (with 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, and 2.4 subtasks). Each question is
205   paired with a video clip and a detailed textual description The questions are designed to assess the
206   ability of models to infer goals and beliefs jointly, providing a richer assessment of multimodal ToM
207   capabilities. It supports three setups, including multimodal, text-only, and video-only inputs.

208   Baselines We include three types of baselines in our evaluation to benchmark our model’s per-
209   formance on the MMToM-QA dataset. For text-only evaluation, we compare performance in the
210   text-only subset of MMToM-QA using various large language models (LLMs), including GPT-4
211   (OpenAI, 2023a), GPT-3.5, GPT-J-6B (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021), and Llama-2-7B (Touvron
212   et al., 2023). Advanced prompting methods, such as SimToM (Wilf et al., 2024) and SymbolicToM
213   (Sclar et al., 2023), which enhance GPT-4’s reasoning capabilities, provide additional baselines
214   (e.g., SimToM with GPT-4 and SymbolicToM with GPT-4). For multimodal evaluation, we include
215   GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023a), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023), Video-Llama-2 (Zhang et al., 2023), and
216   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), BIPALM (Jin et al., 2024). These methods employ sampled frames from
217   each video to evaluate their proficiency in multimodal ToM reasoning. For human, 180 participants
218   answer 120 randomly sampled questions, covering all question types, as reported by Jin et al. (2024).

224   3.2            M AIN RESULTS

225   Tab.1 uses human performance as the gold standard. Humans clearly outperform all models (with
226   93.0% accuracy) when provided with multimodal input. This result highlights the critical role
227   of integrating both visual and textual modalities in achieving an immersive and context-aware
228   perception of the ToM situation. Accordingly, our ToM method also incorporates multimodal inputs.
229   In belief inference, which is strongly linked to world knowledge, models like GPT-4 and GPT-3.5

234   and dynamic environments is crucial. This highlights the need for models to be better aligned
235   with the specific requirements of ToM scenarios. Models with smaller scales, such as those with

242   compensating for the lack of large-scale pretrained knowledge. Our approach goes beyond seesaw

249   In the Bayesian framework, we explore the role of LMs in likelihood estimation and examine how
250   their scale and post-training affect performance across various ToM tasks. According to Tab.2, (i)
251   our results demonstrate a positive correlation between LM size and ToM task performance.
252   For instance, in the zero-shot setting of Llama-3.1, the 405B model achieves an accuracy of 69.43%,
253   outperforming both the 8B model (65.19%) and the 70B model (66.62%). Notably, the performance
254   of the 405B model approaches that of the post-trained Llama2-7B. Furthermore, the improvement
255   from 70B to 405B suggests that the benefits of scaling have not yet reached saturation, indicating
256   potential for further gains with larger models. (ii) Post-training significantly enhances LMs’
257   performance on ToM tasks, even when Larger LMs already perform well in zero-shot scenarios.
258   This effect is consistent across model sizes, from smaller models such as 7B/8B to larger models up to
259   70B, regardless of the specific version (Llama-2, Llama-3, or Llama-3.1). For belief inference tasks,
260   which are closely tied to world knowledge, post-training helps align the large models’ knowledge
261   more precisely with the input questions. For goal inference tasks, which are linked to environmental
262   dynamics, post-training refines the models’ atomic-level reasoning (i.e., predicting a based on s, b, g),
263   resulting in greater improvements compared to belief inference. This suggests that post-training
264   provides a more substantial benefit for tasks that require dynamic reasoning. (iii) Our weak-to-strong
265   control approach approximates the benefits of direct post-training in Bayesian inference. When
266   comparing models such as Llama-2, Llama-3, and Llama-3.1, we find that direct post-training on the
267   70B model, even with adjusted hyperparameters from the 8B model (e.g., reducing the alpha value
268   from 32 to 16), does not produce results as robust as our method. We attribute this to the difficulty of
269   finding optimal hyperparameters for larger models, which require more extensive tuning. In contrast,
270   our weak-to-strong control, which uses a well-trained smaller LM to guide the larger LMs, allows for
271   more consistent improvements without the need for extensive hyperparameter trials.

290   3.5      T RANSFERABILITY OF SCALED BAYESIAN INFERENCE

291   Although the small LMs are post-trained on the apartment, our overall framework is expected to
292   be stable and generalizable across various unseen scenarios since it has the structured Bayesian
293   framework and redirected larger LMs. To evaluate the transferability, Tab.4 compares our method
294   with baseline models in five previously unseen scenarios: Andersen fairy tales, ancient Egyptian,
295   outer space, wild west, and medieval castle. These diverse settings assess the generalisability of
296   our approach beyond the post-training scenario. When scaling the strong component (i.e., the large
297   controlled LMs) from 70B to 405B across these new scenarios, there are continuous improvements
298   in ToM understanding. This demonstrates that the increased capacity of our scaled solution
299   enhances the transferability of ToM reasoning across multiple dynamic and unseen environ-
300   ments. Furthermore, when the weak controller component is reduced from 8B to 4B, performance
301   remains stable, ranging between 78.0% and 79.15%. This result is comparable to the 79.05% accuracy
302   achieved in the original apartment scenario and also remains close to the performance of the 8B
303   LMs. This consistency suggests that downsizing the weak component does not significantly affect
304   performance, even in new and diverse test environments. These results indicate that our approach
305   has strong potential for continually downsizing smaller LMs as controllers since they also are
306   capable of capturing the post-trained behaviours. It allows saved resources to be potentially
307   allocated to stronger controlled LMs, while still keeping stable to scenarios unseen previously.

378   4    R ELATED W ORK

379   Modelling human mental states There are many studies on understanding human behaviour by
380   classifying and predicting physical motion patterns (Aggarwal & Ryoo, 2011; Caba Heilbron et al.,
381   2015; Choi & Savarese, 2013; Shu et al., 2015). Beyond physical behaviour, some studies focus
382   specifically on modeling human mental states, i.e. ToM. ToM models have followed two broad
383   approaches: Bayesian methods and end-to-end deep learning. Bayesian ToM models (Baker et al.,
384   2017; Jara-Ettinger, 2019; Shu et al., 2021) rely on structured probabilistic frameworks to infer
385   mental states from sparse observations of human behaviour. On the other hand, end-to-end models
386   such as ToMnet (Rabinowitz et al., 2018; Shu et al., 2021; Sclar et al., 2022) have been trained
387   directly on ToM tasks, learning relationships between data patterns without explicit causal models of
388   mental states (Sap et al., 2022; Zhi-Xuan et al., 2022; Ullman, 2023). More recently, neurosymbolic
389   reasoning systems use the neural models for feature extraction, while also incorporating probabilistic
390   models for structured reasoning (Wong et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024; 2023). They face challenges in
391   dynamic and multimodal environments, where both physical and mental state reasoning are required.
392   Different from prior studies, our work operates in more complex and dynamic multimodal ToM
393   environments, where physical actions and mental state reasoning are intertwined.

394   Post-training LLMs for downstream tasks Post-training can project the pre-trained capabilities
395   of LMs into downstream tasks such as dialogue generation (Ouyang et al., 2022), human value
396   alignment (Bai et al., 2022), and multimodal tasks (OpenAI, 2023b; Liu et al., 2023). Previous
397   approaches have also used reweighting techniques to adjust the output predictions of fine-tuned
398   LLMs, interpolating the effects of fine-tuning with pre-trained knowledge to achieve human-centered
399   and trustworthy text generation (Liu et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). More
400   recently, LLMs are post-trained as large action/policy models for decision-making in embodied
401   agents, allowing them to interact with and explore environments (Kim et al., 2024; Szot et al., 2024;
402   Li et al., 2024). Our study differs by framing LMs as policy models in the context of Bayesian inverse
403   inference, specifically to model human mental states. We address the limitations of existing ToM
404   methods by scaling large policy models at test time using a likelihood redirection strategy, reasoning
405   more accurately in complex ToM scenarios. See App.A.1 for additional discussions.


406   5    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION

407   This study investigates scalable Bayesian inference in complex and dynamic ToM environments.
408   Existing methods based on normal-sized LMs often fail to provide sufficient reasoning capabilities
409   and world knowledge, particularly when used as likelihood estimators in diverse challenging ToM
410   scenarios. To overcome these limitations, we propose a method that abstracts and transfers the
411   post-trained behavioral patterns of smaller LMs. This approach allows the extensive world knowledge
412   of large LMs to be progressively redirected towards ToM reasoning tasks. The weak-to-strong control
413   mechanism enables scalable reasoning by using small, post-trained LMs to guide large LMs at test
414   time. This approach avoids additional post-training resources for large models, yet allows effective
415   test-time scaling of Bayesian ToM reasoning even in dynamic and complex scenarios.

600   A.1     O UR T HEORETICAL R ATIONALES IN S CALED T O M I NFERENCE AND R ELATED W ORK

601   Our approach is based on a high-level principle derived from Theorem 1 and its proof, which implies
602   that smaller models can approximate the scaled gradient of the loss function for larger models. This
603   mechanism bypasses direct parameter updates in the larger model, capturing the primary adjustments
604   needed for fine-tuning while exploiting the innate generalisation capacity of the larger model. By
605   relying on the approximate knowledge provided by the smaller model, our framework reduces
606   computational overhead and improves scalability.
607   This principle is related with previous studies that have explored reweighting mechanisms for various
608   applications (where not necessarily the same as our perspective of scaling or embodied policy model),
609   including avoiding toxicity in text generation (Liu et al., 2021), mitigating harmful outputs in aligned
610   models (Zhou et al., 2024), adjusting code generation (Mitchell et al., 2024), controlling sentiment
611   in text (Han et al., 2024), and reducing hallucination or degeneration in neural text (Chuang et al.,
612   2024; Su et al., 2022). These works demonstrate how reweighting can approximate the behaviour
613   of large language models, mimicking direct fine-tuning in specific contexts. In contrast, our scaled
614   ToM inference extends this principle beyond text generation tasks into the domain of social cognitive
615   reasoning. Our framework uses language models to approximate policy behaviours for probability
616   estimation in embodied simulators, based on the cognitive science-inspired Bayesian ToM framework.
617   Unlike previous work focusing on text-based tasks such as sentiment or factuality control, our method
618   addresses the unique challenges of ToM tasks, which require complex reasoning and the integration
619   of world knowledge. These tasks involve multimodal scenarios that require understanding of agents’
620   beliefs, goals and actions - a domain distinct from the text generation problems addressed in previous
621   studies.

629   B.2     DATA P REPROCESSING : U NIFIED S YMBOLIC R EPRESENTATIONS

630   To enable Bayesian ToM inference at scale, following established methods mentioned in MMToM (Jin
631   et al., 2024; Blukis et al., 2022), multimodal data (video and textual descriptions) are transformed into
632   structured symbolic representations. This process involves three key components: visual perception,
633   text parsing, and information fusion. Together, these components provide a unified representation
634   of states, actions, and hypotheses required for ToM tasks.

635   Visual Perception. The visual perception module is designed to process video frames and extract
636   symbolic representations of the environment. For each frame, a scene graph is generated to capture
637   the spatial and relational properties of objects and agents with the scene graph generator(Blukis et al.,
638   2022). Following established methods in MMToM (Jin et al., 2024), voxel maps and 3D bounding
639   boxes are utilized to infer object positions, containment relationships, and human poses. For instance,
640   objects such as pear and basket are represented by predicates like In(pear, basket). These
641   predicates effectively summarize the physical state of the environment, serving as critical inputs for
642   subsequent reasoning steps.

643   Text Parsing. To extract symbolic representations from textual descriptions, one LLM (e.g., GPT-4)
644   processes the text into three distinct components: (i) the initial state of the environment, (ii) human
645   actions, and (iii) the question. Each component is translated into symbolic predicates. For example:

646           • The state is represented as predicates like In(pear, basket).
647           • The action is represented as commands such as walk towards kitchen.
648           • The question is decomposed into two hypotheses, each comprising a goal (e.g., pear) and
649             a belief (e.g., In(pear, basket) or its negation, ¬In(pear, basket)).

650   This symbolic parsing ensures compatibility with the structured reasoning framework.

651   Fusion. The fusion module integrates symbolic information from video and text into a unified
652   representation. First, predicates extracted from video inputs (e.g., spatial relationships) are aligned
653   with those parsed from text to form the initial state. Next, human actions detected from the video
654   are matched with text-based actions, and the video sequence is segmented into discrete time steps
655   corresponding to these actions. Starting from the initial state, the symbolic representation of the
656   environment is updated at each time step based on newly detected predicates. This process results in a
657   sequence of symbolic states and actions, which serve as the input for Bayesian inference. Additionally,
658   the parsed question provides two hypotheses—goal and belief—that guide the reasoning task.

683   For theoretical implications for practical applicability, this analysis demonstrates that the weak-
684   to-strong control mechanism relies on the learned ∆s to approximate the scaled gradient

694   strict fine-tuning and maximizes computational efficiency, ensuring the approach is both scalable and

763   D.4    I MPACT OF P RE -T RAINING Q UALITY ON MMT O M TASKS

764   The differences in performance between the Llama-2, Llama-3 and Llama-3.1 models provide insight
765   into the role of pre-training quality, especially at large model scales. Based on the experimental results
766   in Table 2 and Table 3, the influence of pre-training quality diminishes primarily due to a ceiling
767   effect, but this is only observed when comparing models within the same scale, such as the 70B
768   parameter range. However, when comparing smaller models to larger ones, the effect of pre-training
769   is more pronounced. For example, moving from Llama-2 7B to Llama-2 70B after ToM-specific
770   post-training leads to a 6% improvement in belief inference accuracy (from 76.33% to 82.33%) and a
771   2% improvement in goal inference accuracy (from 70% to 72%), highlighting the role of scaling in
772   encoding richer representations.
773   When examining why pre-training becomes less effective at larger scales, such as comparing Llama-
774   2-70B (pre-trained with 2.2 trillion tokens) to Llama-3.1-70B (pre-trained with 15 trillion tokens),
775   the results suggest that larger pre-training corpora improve performance primarily for tasks that rely
776   heavily on world knowledge: Tasks involving belief inference, which rely on short-term reasoning and
777   general world knowledge, show significant improvements due to improved representations learned
778   during pre-training. For example, Llama-3.1 achieves a 3.67% improvement in belief inference
779   accuracy over Llama-2 (from 83.00% to 85.67%). These tasks benefit from richer pre-training
780   datasets that refine the model’s understanding of common human behaviours and object interactions.
781   In contrast, goal inference tasks that rely on long-term reasoning, including integrating temporal
782   observations and dynamically updating beliefs, show smaller gains from larger pre-training corpora.
783   For example, Llama-3.1 improves goal inference accuracy by only 1.67% over Llama-2 (from 72.33%
784   to 74.00%). Such tasks are more dependent on the fine-tuning stage and the use of task-specific
785   reasoning frameworks, such as weak-to-strong control. These results suggest that for complex
786   reasoning tasks, the primary performance bottleneck shifts from pre-training quality to the reasoning
787   strategies employed during fine-tuning.
788   In summary, pre-training quality has a significant impact on smaller models and tasks that rely heavily
789   on world knowledge, such as belief inference. However, as models scale up to 70B parameters, the
790   influence of pre-training diminishes due to ceiling effects, and logical reasoning tasks such as goal
791   inference rely more on task-specific adaptations during fine-tuning.

809   D.6    O N THE ROLE OF THE W EAK - TO -S TRONG F RAMEWORK

810   The weak-to-strong framework presented in this paper focuses on aligning the larger model’s distribu-
811   tion with ToM-specific beliefs and task structures while preserving its general reasoning capabilities,
812   rather than primarily relying on the smaller model’s reasoning abilities. This design enables efficient
813   transfer of ToM-specific task structures without compromising the broader capabilities of the larger
814   model.
815   The smaller model (e.g., 4B or 8B parameters) undergoes ToM-specific post-training to encode
816   task-relevant priors, such as belief states and potential goals, without requiring advanced independent
817   reasoning capabilities. During inference, the smaller model functions as an assistive scaffold,
818   conditioning the larger model’s likelihood estimation in a Bayesian framework. This role is formalized
                                        E
819   through the adjustment ratio: ππN , where π E is the post-trained smaller model’s task-specific policy,
820   and π N is the naive pre-trained smaller model’s policy.
821   The larger model (e.g., 70B parameters) integrates this adjustment ratio to refine its likelihood
                                                                                   E
822   estimation dynamically. The overall policy distribution is computed as π L ππN , where π L is the policy
823   from the larger model. This mechanism allows the larger model to retain its broad reasoning and
824   world knowledge, ensuring its capacity for generalization while aligning with ToM-specific task
825   structures.

827   trained model across five unseen themes, including Andersen Fairy Tales, Ancient Egyptian, and Outer

829   all ToM tasks, demonstrating its ability to preserve and transfer the larger model’s general reasoning
