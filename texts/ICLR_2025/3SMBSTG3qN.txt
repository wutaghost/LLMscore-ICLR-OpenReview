011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In domains such as finance, healthcare, and robotics, managing worst-case scenarios
014            is critical, as failure to do so can lead to catastrophic outcomes. Distributional
015            Reinforcement Learning (DRL) provides a natural framework to incorporate risk
016
               sensitivity into decision-making processes. However, existing approaches face two
               key limitations: (1) the use of fixed risk measures at each decision step often results
017
               in overly conservative policies, and (2) the interpretation and theoretical properties
018
               of the learned policies remain unclear. While optimizing a static risk measure
019            addresses these issues, its use in the DRL framework has been limited to the simple
020            static CVaR risk measure. In this paper, we present a novel DRL algorithm with
021            convergence guarantees that optimizes for a broader class of static Spectral Risk
022            Measures (SRM). Additionally, we provide a clear interpretation of the learned
023            policy by leveraging the distribution of returns in DRL and the decomposition
024            of static coherent risk measures. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
025            model learns policies aligned with the SRM objective, and outperforms existing
026            risk-neutral and risk-sensitive DRL models in various settings.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In traditional Reinforcement Learning (RL), the goal is to find a policy that maximizes the expected
032   return (Sutton and Barto, 2018). However, considering the variations in rewards and addressing the
033
      worst-case scenarios are critical in some fields such as healthcare or finance. A risk-averse policy
      can help address the reward uncertainty arising from the stochasticity of the environment. This risk
034
      aversion can stem from changing the objective from expectation to other risk measures such as the
035
      expected utility function (Howard and Matheson, 1972), Conditional Value-at-Risk (Bäuerle and Ott,
036   2011), coherent risk measures (Tamar et al., 2017), or convex risk measures (Coache and Jaimungal,
037   2023). Another approach is limiting the worst-case scenarios by using constraints such as variance
038   (Tamar et al., 2012) or dynamic risk measures (Chow and Pavone, 2013) in the optimization problem.
039
      Another area of research that has gained attention for risk-sensitive RL (RSRL) is Distributional RL
040
      (DRL) (Morimura et al., 2010; Bellemare et al., 2017). This paradigm diverges from the traditional
041
      RL by estimating the return distribution instead of its expected value. DRL algorithms not only
042   demonstrate notable improvements compared to conventional RL methods but also enable a variety
043   of new approaches to risk mitigation. In this context, a few risk measures such as CVaR (Stanko
044   and Macek, 2019; Keramati et al., 2020), distortion risk measure (Dabney et al., 2018a), Entropic
045   risk measure (Liang and Luo, 2024), or static Lipschitz risk measure (Chen et al., 2024) have been
046   explored.
047
      In the DRL framework, applying a fixed risk measure at each step leads to policies that are optimized
048   for neither static nor dynamic risk measures (Lim and Malik, 2022). In this case, action selection
049   at different states are not necessarily aligned with each other, which can lead to policies that are
050   sub-optimal with respect to the agent’s risk preference. This issue, known as time inconsistency,
051   is a common challenge in risk-sensitive decision-making (Shapiro et al., 2014). Intuitively, this
052   misalignment can be understood as the fact that finding optimal policies starting from different states
053   can yield different and inconsistent policies. To mitigate this, dynamic risk measures were introduced
      (Ruszczyński, 2010), which evaluate risk at each time step, unlike static risk measures that assess


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      risk over entire episodes. However, dynamic risk measures are difficult to interpret, limiting their
055   practical applicability (Majumdar and Pavone, 2020; Gagne and Dayan, 2022).
056
057   Optimizing static risk measures is more interpretable since it can be described as finding the policy that
058
      gives the best possible outcome in the worst-case scenario. However, unlike dynamic risk measures,
      the risk preference that the policy optimizes for at later stages is unclear. Traditionally, calculating
059
      these risk preferences has been limited to CVaR due to computational complexity (Bäuerle and
060
      Ott, 2011; Bellemare et al., 2023). However, we demonstrate that by leveraging the decomposition
061   of coherent risk measures (Pflug and Pichler, 2016) and the return distribution within the DRL
062   framework, these evolving risk preferences can also be computed for more general spectral risk
063   measures. The intuition behind this approach is that a decision maker selects an initial risk preference,
064   but it may change as new information becomes available over time. It is important to emphasize that,
065   unlike previous works (Chow et al., 2015; Stanko and Macek, 2019) that use the decomposition of
066   CVaR to derive optimal policies, we utilize this decomposition only to explain the behavior of the
067   optimal policy, not for policy optimization. In fact, Hau et al. (2023) have demonstrated that the
068   decomposition of coherent risk measures cannot be reliably applied for policy optimization, and the
069
      optimality claims in those works are inaccurate.
070   The contributions of our work are as follows:
071
072          • We propose a novel DRL algorithm with convergence guarantees that optimizes static
073            Spectral Risk Measures (SRM). SRM, expressed as a convex combination of CVaRs at
074
               varying risk levels, provides practitioners with the flexibility to define a wide range of risk
               profiles, including the well-known Mean-CVaR measure.
075
076          • We demonstrate that the return distributions in the DRL framework allow for the temporal
077            decomposition of SRM, offering insights into the agent’s evolving risk preferences over
078            time.
079          • Through extensive evaluations, we show that our model accurately learns policies aligned
080            with the SRM objective and outperforms both risk-neutral and risk-sensitive DRL models in
081            various settings.

      instance, a convex conditional risk measure such as ρt = CVaRα (· | Ft ) can be used as the one-step
      mapping and establish a decomposable and recursive preference system5 . At last, in both of these
932
      cases, whether the preference mappings of a recursive preference system or the one-step mappings of
933
      a decomposable preference mapping are monotone, the preference system is dynamically consistent.
934
935   As mentioned before, the dynamic consistency of the preference system only implies the time-
936   consistency of a unique optimal policy. To guarantee the time-consistency of all optimal policies,
937
      Shapiro and Ugurlu (2016) shows that the preference system has to be strictly dynamically consistent.
      This requires the preference mappings of a recursive preference system or the one-step mappings of a
938
      decomposable preference mapping to be strictly monotone, i.e, the following implication must hold:
939
940                                  Z ≻ Z ′ =⇒ ρt,T (Z) ≻ ρt,T (Z ′ ) , Z, Z ′ ∈ ZT .
941
942   The Spectral risk measure is an example of strictly monotone preference mappings only if the risk
943   spectrum ϕ(u) is positive on the interval (0, 1). Consequently, CVaRα is not strictly monotone for
944   α ∈ (0, 1) and we cannot deduce that, for the preference system characterized by nested Conditional
945   Value-at-Risk, every optimal solution of the corresponding reference problem is time-consistent. An
                                        R1
946   easy way to ensure that ϕ(u) = u µ(dα) > 0 for u ∈ (0, 1) is to check whether µ(dα)|α=1 is
947   non-zero or not. In other words, if the risk measure assigns a non-zero weight on the expectation
948   (CVaR1 ), the resulting SRM is strictly monotone.
949   The decomposition theorem shows that the preference mappings ρt,T can also be provided for SRM.
950   It also shows that these preference mappings are strictly monotone if the initial risk measure is a
951   strictly monotone SRM. This property is evident since for α = 1, ξ α and consequently αξtα is also 1.
952   Therefore, the weight of CVaR1 in the preference mappings would also be non-zero. Intuitively, if
953   the risk measure takes into account the entire distribution to calculate the risk-adjusted value, i.e. has
954   a non-zero weight for the expected value, the resulting preference mappings also have this property.
955   Additionally, the goal of analyzing the evolution of risk preferences over time can be achieved with
956   the preference mappings, without the need for deriving the one-step mappings. The intermediate
957   random variable ξt,t−1 in the one-step mappings shows how the risk preference at time t changes
958   compared to the previous risk preference at time t − 1, however, ξtα in the decomposition theorem
959   shows how the risk preference at time t changes compared to the initial risk preference. For example,
960   the CVaR risk level at time t, αt , can be written as both αt−1 ξt,t−1 or αξt . Similarly, in the CVaR
961   case, the risk parameter Bt+1 can be written as both (Bt − Rt )/γ or (B0 − St )/Ct .

       H.1                              C LIFF WALKING

       This environment is a simple 4×8 grid-world in which the agent has to reach the goal state and
1260   avoid the cliff states. Inspired by the work of Delétang et al. (2021), we add stochasticity to this
1261   environment by adding a wind that blows with 50% chance at each time step and moves the agent
1262   randomly to any of the four nearby positions, regardless of the agent’s action. In our environment,
1263   going into the cliff positions and reaching the goal has a reward of −1 and 10, respectively. The
1264   maximum number of steps allowed is 50 and the discount factor is set to γ = 0.95.

1266   H.2                              A MERICAN P UT O PTION T RADING
1267
1268   In this environment, we assume that the price of the underlying asset follows a Geometric Brownian
1269   Motion, characterized by the differential equation dPt = ζPt dt + σPt dWt , where ζ = 1 is the
1270   drift, σ = 1 is the volatility, the initial price is P0 = 1, and Wt is a standard Brownian motion.
1271
       The strike price of the put option is assumed to be K = 1. At each time step, the agent can either
       exercise the option and receive rt = max{0, K − Pt } or hold the option to receive a reward at
1272
       future steps. At maturity, if the option hasn’t been exercised yet, the agent automatically receives
1273
       rT = max{0, K − PT }.

       H.3                              M EAN - REVERSION T RADING S TRATEGY
1276
1277   In this algorithmic trading framework, the asset price follows an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process,
1278   characterized by the differential equation dPt = κ(ζ − Pt )dt + σdWt , where ζ = 1 is the long-term
1279   mean level, κ = 2 determines the speed of reversion to mean. At each time step t = 0, · · · , T − 1,
1280   the agent takes an action at ∈ (−amax , amax ), corresponding to trading quantities of the asset and
1281   changes its inventory qt ∈ (−qmax , qmax ). The reward is defined as rt = −at Pt − φ(at )2 for
1282   0 ≤ t ≤ T − 2 and rT −1 = −aT −1 PT −1 − φ(aT −1 )2 + qT Pt − ψqT2 for the final time step. Here,
1283   φ = 0.005 represents the transaction cost and ψ = 0.5 signifies the terminal penalty. In our setup,
1284
       the agent faces penalties for holding any assets at the final time step T . Consequently, the reward at
       time step T − 1 has an additional term for the agent’s inventory at time step T . In our example, we
1285
       consider T = 10, qmax = 5, amax = 2, γ = 0.99, and discretize the action space into 21 actions.

       H.4                              W INDY L UNAR L ANDER
1289
       The Lunar Lander environment is a classic rocket trajectory optimization problem, involving an
1290   8-dimensional state space and four actions: firing the left or right orientation engines, firing the main
1291   engine, or doing nothing. To introduce stochasticity, we enable the wind option. The objective is
1292   to guide the lander from the top of the screen to the landing pad. Successful landings yield around
1293   100–140 points. If the lander moves away from the pad, it loses points, while a crash results in an
1294   additional penalty of -100 points. Landing safely adds a bonus of +100 points, and each leg that
1295   makes contact with the ground earns +10 points. Firing the main engine incurs a penalty of -0.3
       points per frame, while firing the side engines costs -0.03 points per frame.
