009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) enable energy-efficient computation through
012            event-driven computing and multiplication-free inference, making them well-
013            suited for processing sparse events. Recently, deep Spiking Convolutional Neural
014            Networks (CNNs) have shown energy efficiency advantages on event-based ob-
015            ject detection. However, spiking CNNs have been limited to local and single-scale
016            features, making it challenging for them to achieve better detection accuracy. To
017            address this challenge, we propose a hierarchical Spiking Transformer-CNN (i.e.,
018
               Spike-TransCNN) architecture, which is the first attempt to leverage the global
               information extraction capabilities of Spiking Transformers and the local infor-
019
               mation capture abilities of Spiking CNNs for event-based object detection. Tech-
020
               nically, we first propose using the Spiking Transformer to extract global features
021            and employ a multi-scale local feature extraction CNN module to complement the
022            Spiking Transformers in local feature extraction. Then, we design intra-stage and
023            inter-stage feature fusion modules to integrate global and multi-scale local fea-
024            tures within the network architecture. Experimental results demonstrate that our
025            Spike-TransCNN significantly outperforms existing SNN-based object detectors
026            on the Gen1 dataset, achieving higher detection accuracy (mAP 0.336 vs. 0.321)
027            with lower energy consumption (5.49 mJ vs. 7.26 mJ). Our code can be available
028            in the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Object detection is essential in computer vision and robotics applications. Nevertheless, conven-
      tional cameras operating at fixed frame rates struggle in challenging conditions, such as fast motion,
034
      over-exposure, and low light, leading to a significant decline in object detection performance Liu
035
      et al. (2020); Sayed & Brostow (2021). Recently, event cameras like DVS Lichtsteiner et al. (2008)
036   and ATIS Posch et al. (2010) have surpassed RGB cameras in dynamic range, temporal resolution,
037   and energy efficiency. These advanced capabilities make them especially well-suited for object de-
038   tection Peng et al. (2023a;b); Wang et al. (2023a; 2024); Zubic et al. (2024); Yuan et al. (2024);
039   Hamaguchi et al. (2023); Zubić et al. (2023); Tomy et al. (2022) in these challenging scenarios.
040
      Most current event-based object detectors rely on Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) Perot et al.
041
      (2020); Li et al. (2022a), which deliver high performance but come with high computational com-
042   plexity and energy consumption. In contrast, Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) Maass (1997); Zhu
043   et al. (2022) present a novel approach inspired by the brain’s temporal information processing dy-
044   namics. SNNs propagate information through binary spike sequences, allowing for energy-effective
045   computing with event-driven computation and multiplication-free inference. This makes SNNs a
046   more efficient and biologically inspired alternative for event-based object detection.
047
      Early SNN-based object detectors are often derived from existing ANNs through conversion, which
048   introduces several limitations. The most critical problem is that most conversion methods are de-
049   signed for static images and may not be able to handle sparse temporal event data effectively. This
050   is because these methods focus on approximating the activation patterns of ANNs, often neglecting
051   the spatiotemporal information inherent in event data. Furthermore, conversed models like Spiking-
052   YOLO Kim et al. (2020) require a large number of time steps to match the performance of the
053   original ANN. Although Spike Calibration Li et al. (2022b) can reduce this to hundreds of time
      steps, its effectiveness still hinges on the quality of the original ANN model.

054
      Directly-trained Spiking Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) could be trained with much fewer
055   steps Su et al. (2023), but they primarily focus on local features, which can limit the overall detec-
056   tion performance. For instance, Spiking-DenseNet Cordone et al. (2022) employs a multi-layered
057   approach to process features at various local scales, and SFOD Fan et al. (2024) introduces a fusion
058   mechanism to combine spike features across different local scales. Despite these efforts to opti-
059   mize spike features across multiple local scales to capture the local features, these Spiking CNNs
060   still face challenges in incorporating global and high-level semantic information, constraining their
061   overall performance.
062   Transformer architectures have increasingly been integrated into SNNs, such as Spikformer Zhou
063   et al. (2023), Auto-Spikformer Che et al. (2024), and Attention-free Spikformer Wang et al. (2023b).
064   These models have demonstrated superior performance over spiking CNNs in various tasks, primar-
065   ily due to the Transformer’s capability for global attention and parallel computation. However, most
066   current research focuses on classification, with limited exploration of Spiking Transformers in the
067   regression task of object detection. Additionally, recent ANN studies Fang et al. (2022); Chen et al.
068   (2023) suggest that combining the global modeling strengths of Transformers with the local fea-
069
      ture extraction capabilities of CNNs can further enhance network performance. Nevertheless, this
      potential has yet to be thoroughly investigated within the context of SNNs.
070
071   To address these challenges, we propose a hierarchical Spiking Transformer-CNN (i.e., Spike-
072   TransCNN), which is a directly-trained deep SNN designed to extract both global and multi-scale
073   local features for event-based object detection. Our model is the first attempt to leverage the global
074   information extraction capabilities of Spiking Transformers and the local information capture abil-
075
      ities of Spiking CNNs. We employ spike-driven token selection to selectively capture tokens, and
      spike self-attention for holistic perception of spike features. Specifically, we first present a multi-
076
      scale local feature extraction module to compensate for the limitations of Spiking Transformers in
077
      local feature extraction. Furthermore, we design intra-stage and inter-stage feature fusion modules
078   to integrate global and multi-scale local features within the architecture. The results show that our
079   Spike-TransCNN reduces energy consumption by 4.7× compared to the same ANN architecture.
080   Moreover, it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on the Gen1 dataset, achieving a
081   higher mAP and lower energy consumption.
082
      In summary, the main contributions of this work are:
083
084
             • We propose Spike-TransCNN, a novel hierarchical Spiking Transformer-CNN, which is the
085
               first hybrid spiking architecture that combines Spiking CNNs and Spiking Transformers to
086            leverage their complementary strengths, yielding both high-accuracy and energy-efficiency
087            in event-based object detection.
088
089          • We present spike-driven token selection to select tokens and spike self-attention for global
090
               spike feature perception, along with spiking dilated convolution for extracting local multi-
               scale features and optimizing them with temporal-channel joint attention.
091
092          • We design intra-stage spike feature fusion and inter-stage spike feature fusion modules that
093            effectively aggregate features extracted from different architectures and multi-scale features
094            from the event stream to improve object detection performance.

      Event-based Object Detection. Most event-based object detection methods use ANN approaches,
      such as RED Perot et al. (2020), ASTMNet Li et al. (2022a), and RVT Gehrig (2023), which demon-
100
      strate impressive detection performance but come with high energy consumption. More recently,
101
      some works have explored achieving energy-efficient object detection for event data using SNNs.
102   For example, Hybrid-SNN Kugele et al. (2021) combines an SNN backbone for efficient event-
103   based feature extraction with an ANN head for object detection tasks. Spiking-DenseNet Li et al.
104   (2022a) is notable for applying SNNs to event-based object detection using the SSD architecture. A
105   feature pyramid structure Zhang et al. (2023) is introduced to support multi-scale feature extraction.
106   SFOD Fan et al. (2024) introduces a fusion mechanism to combine spike features across different
107   scales. Despite these efforts, Spiking CNNs excel at capturing local features but face challenges in
      integrating global information, which limits overall detection performance.

133   Spiking CNNs. Current Spiking CNN-based models for object detection can be broadly categorized
134   into two types. The first type involves ANN-to-SNN methods, which convert pre-trained ANNs into
135   SNNs by replacing continuous activation functions with spiking neurons. For example, Spiking-
136
      YOLOv4 Wang et al. (2023c) implements a converted CNN model for fast and accurate object
      detection from event streams. However, these methods face several limitations, including the need
137
      for a large number of time steps to match the performance of the original ANN. The second type
138
      refers to directly-trained methods, which use surrogate gradients to train deep and large-scale SNNs
139   for object detection. For instance, EMS-YOLO Su et al. (2023) is a directly-trained SNN that
140   surpasses ANN-to-SNN conversion methods, requiring only a few time steps for real-time inference.
141   Besides, a training scheme for SNNs Caccavella et al. (2023) deployed on the neuromorphic chip
142   achieves low-power face detection. These directly-trained SNN models can achieve comparable
143   performance to ANNs with the same architecture while significantly reducing energy consumption.
144
      Spiking Transformers. Transformers have been integrated into SNN models with notable suc-
145   cess across various tasks. For example, Spikformer Zhou et al. (2023) introduces a pioneering
146   Spiking Self-Attention (SSA) version of the self-attention mechanism. Meanwhile, Spike-Driven
147   Self-Attention (SDSA) Yao et al. (2024) employs mask and addition operations to avoid multipli-
148   cation, significantly reducing computational energy compared to conventional self-attention mech-
149   anisms. Besides, a spatiotemporal self-attention mechanism Wang et al. (2023d) has been proposed
150   for SNNs, effectively capturing feature dependencies while preserving the asynchronous transmis-
151   sion property of SNNs. QKFormer Zhou et al. (2024) utilizes sparse matrices to filter tokens and
152   channels. Despite these advancements in Transformer-based SNN models, the exploration of object
153
      detection tasks using event data remains relatively limited.

157   3.1      OVERVIEW
158
159   This work aims at designing a novel hierarchical Spiking Transformer-CNN, termed Spike-
160   TransCNN, which combines Spiking CNNs and Spiking Transformers to leverage their comple-
161   mentary strengths for high-accuracy and energy-efficient object detection. As depicted in Fig. 1,
      our framework consists of four key parts: Spiking Transformer Block (STB), Spiking CNN Block


                                                                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (SCB), intra-state spike feature fusion module, and inter-stage spike feature fusion module. More
163   precisely, our Spike-TransCNN starts by processing the event stream through a hybrid backbone that
164   combines Spiking Transformers and Spiking CNNs. We then extract features at four different scales
165   using patch embeddings (PE) across four stages in the hybrid architecture, integrating these features
166   with both intra-stage and inter-stage fusion modules. Finally, we use the YOLOX head to predict
167   detection results from the fused features.

      methodology outlined in RVT-B Gehrig (2023).

531
532
      This paper proposes a novel hybrid network that takes advantage of both Spiking Transformers and
533
      Spiking CNNs for event-based object detection. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first use
534   of a hierarchical hybrid network that includes intra-stage and inter-stage spike feature fusion mod-
535   ules to ensure comprehensive integration of global and multi-scale local information. Experimental
536   results demonstrate that our Spike-TransCNN significantly outperforms existing SNN-based object
537   detectors on the Gen1 dataset, achieving higher mAP and lower energy consumption. We believe
538   our work presents a conceptual hybrid framework that integrates Spiking Transformers and Spiking
539   CNNs, offering potential for various event-based vision applications and feasibility for deployment
      on neuromorphic hardware.

760   The output of an event camera with a resolution of H × W can be represented as an event sequence,
761   denoted as E = ei 1N , where ei = (xi , yi , ti , pi ). Here, pi ∈ −1, 1 represents the polarity of a
762   brightness change that occurs at time ti and pixel position (xi , yi ). The change is triggered for the
763   pixel (xn , yn ) at timestamp tn when the log-intensity ln L changes beyond the pre-defined threshold
764   θ. This dynamic visual sensing mechanism is depicted by the inequality:
765
766                               ln L(xn , yn , tn ) − ln L(xn , yn , tn − ∆tn )pn θ,                   (17)
767
768   Here, the polarity pn ∈{1, −1} indicates whether the brightness is increasing or decreasing, and ∆tn
769   represents the temporal sampling interval of DVS at a pixel.
770   In our investigation, we have implemented the technique presented in Gehrig (2023) for preprocess-
771   ing event data. Our preprocessing generates a 4-dimensional tensor E from discrete event data. The
772   first dimension consists of T components associated with T discretization steps of time. The second
773   dimension includes two components signifying polarity. The third and fourth dimensions represent
774   the height and width of the event camera. We process a set of events ε within the time duration
775   [ta , tb ) in the following manner:
776                                                 X
777                            E(τ, p, x, y) =          δ(p − pn )δ(x − xn )δ(τ − τn ),
778                                                 ε
