011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-driven 3D shape generation still faces key challenges, especially in achieving
014            high levels of control over the generated outputs. Paticularly, existing text-to-
015
               shape methods ignore the explicit modeling of hierarchical structures in the text
               and 3D shapes, which makes it hard for using long text descriptions with multiple
016
               prompts to guide the coherent part-level 3D shape generation. In this work, we
017
               introduce HierT2S, a framework that integrates a hierarchical tree representation
018            with a conditional diffusion model, to enhance the generation of 3D shapes with
019            coherent structures induced by the hierarchical and structured text representations.
020            The key idea is to first segment the input text into several clusters and construct a
021            hierarchical tree representation, with each node representing a parent entity or the
022            fine-level part components. Then, we process the lower-level clusters of the tree
023            with a relation graph module which uses self-attention mechanism to aggregate the
024            relationships of the clusters, and generate a new sequence containing the processed
025            text features. Finally, the text features are embedded into the 3D feature space and
026
               used for learning the 3D shape generation by a conditional diffusion model, where
               the sparsely implicit parsed hierarchical tree graph further enhances the structural
027
               details of the generated 3D shapes, leading to results that are close to structure-
028
               aware generation. We conducted comprehensive experiments on the existing text-
029            to-shape pairing dataset Text2Shape, and the results demonstrate that our model
030            significantly outperforms current state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, our method
031            can enable progressive part-level 3D shape manipulation and modification guided
032            by the partially modified text prompt.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The text-to-3D shape generation field has advanced notably with the use of generative models like
038   diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), enabling the creation of high-quality and detailed shapes (Chen
039
      et al., 2024b; Chu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Cheng et al., 2023). While text-based input provides
      a flexible and intuitive way for guiding 3D shape generation, accurately producing geometries that
040
      match the text remains challenging, especially for objects with hierarchical structures like chairs and
041
      tables. Since both text and 3D shapes have inherent hierarchical features, we believe it is critical to
042   capture the relations between the hierarchical structures of those two modalities to ensure consistent
043   text-to-3D generation.
044
045

      jects’ appearance, structure, and function (Baillargeon, 1996). This intuitive ability allows human to
046

047
      derman, 1987). For intelligent algorithms, however, understanding the structural connections be-

050   tem which only utilizes global-level priors from the training data. Specifically, some high-frequent
051   co-occurring patterns such as “straight legs” may affect the learning and generation of “wheeled
052   legs” specified in the given text. This example underscores the importance of understanding and
053   leveraging the hierarchical structures of both text and shapes to enhance the fidelity and specificity
      of generated 3D shapes. While existing works (Achlioptas et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019) seek to learn

      2.1     T EXT-G UIDED 3D S HAPE G ENERATION
102
103   Recent advances in deep learning have revolutionized text-guided 3D shape generation, leading to
104   various methods designed to produce 3D shapes and scenes based on text input. Early efforts pri-
105   marily focused on modeling joint text-shape embedding spaces. However, generating high-quality
106   3D shapes directly from text remains challenging, primarily due to the difficulties in aligning textual
107   descriptions with shapes and the scarcity of well-annotated paired datasets. The Text2Shape dataset
      (Chen et al., 2019) was a pioneering effort in this area, employing a GAN-based approach for shape


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generation. Nonetheless, this method encountered limitations regarding resolution, quality, and
109   cross-modal consistency. To address these challenges, subsequent methods have incorporated ad-
110   vanced techniques. For instance, recent approaches have leveraged pre-trained models such as CLIP
111   (Abdelreheem et al., 2022b) and diffusion-based strategies (Abdelreheem et al., 2022a) to enhance
112   the fidelity, visual realism, and structural accuracy of generated shapes. Additional works, such
113   as (Li et al., 2023b; Cheng et al., 2022; Qian et al., 2024), utilize discrete autoencoders to capture
114   block-based shape priors, which are processed by transformers for autoregressive shape generation.
115   More recent diffusion-based methods (Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Cheng et al., 2023) fur-
116   ther improve this framework by generating latent features that align more closely with VQ-VAE
117
      embeddings, resulting in shapes of higher accuracy and fidelity.
118   Despite these advancements, most previous studies have treated shapes as unified entities, primarily
119   relying on text representations that focus on linguistic features (such as sentences or words). This
120   approach often fails to effectively convert sentences containing multiple descriptors into 3D shapes
121   with complex structural details due to trivial attention mechanisms. Our research promotes better
122   enhancement of 3D shapes by transforming text into structure-awareness.
123
124   2.2   3D S TRUCTURE -AWARE R EPRESENTATION
125
126   Structure-aware representation encompasses techniques that capture and utilize hierarchical and re-
127
      lational information within 3D shapes, facilitating more nuanced and accurate shape generation.
      This approach is essential for effectively decomposing complex shapes into their constituent parts
128
      and understanding their geometric relationships. Recent research has introduced several methods
129
      for learning structure-aware 3D shape representations. For instance, in supervised learning, some
130   methods (Chen et al., 2022; Mou et al., 2024) advocate using symmetry hierarchies to represent
131   hierarchical shape structures . Recent progress has also been made in semantic-based shape decom-
132   position, with methods like those presented in (Cai et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024b) using learned
133   operations to identify grammar-level shape components. In the realm of unsupervised learning, re-
134   cent studies such as (Ouasfi & Boukhayma, 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2024) utilize implicit
135   neural representations as a framework to capture complex data modalities, preserving structured
136   features through enhanced boundary sampling and stabilization of the optimization process. Alter-
137   natively, DAE-Net (Chen et al., 2024a) employs a branched autoencoder to learn a set of deformable
138
      part templates and achieve part segmentation of shapes through affine transformations. However,
      most methods focus directly on the structural aspects of 3D shapes without considering how to fur-
139
      ther enhance 3D shape generation through the structure of text. Our approach aims to address this
140
      gap by introducing text structure awareness in the text-to-shape generation process.
141
142
      2.3   G RAPH N ETWORK G UIDANCE
143
144
      Graph networks are essential for modeling intricate relationships and dependencies within data. Re-
145   cent works (Yang et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2024) leverage graph priors to facilitate the transfer
146   of commonalities and bridge the gap between visual and linguistic domains. Other studies (Wu
147   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) utilize scene graphs—composed of nodes and relationships—to
148   analyze and interpret 3D scenes. Scene graphs, generated from textual descriptions, capture ex-
149   pressive structural relationships among entities, enhancing the alignment between textual inputs and
150   graphical models through the application of graph networks. They have been successfully employed
151   in various tasks, including text-image matching (Huang et al., 2024), image processing (Gu et al.,
152   2024), and caption generation (Luo et al., 2024). Recognizing that both shapes and texts are com-
153
      posed of structural elements, we choose to incorporate textual graphs as supplementary guidance
      alongside the generation of structural parts.

157
158   3D Shape VQ-VAE. Modeling 3D shapes is challenging due to their high dimensionality. To ad-
159   dress this, we compress 3D shapes from ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015) into a lower-dimensional
160   latent space using a 3D VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). The 3D VQ-VAE consists of an
161   encoder Eϕ that maps 3D shapes into latent vectors, and a decoder Dτ that reconstructs the origi-
      nal 3D shapes from these vectors. Specifically, for an input shape X, represented by a volumetric


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Truncated-Signed Distance Field (T-SDF) with dimensions X ∈ RD×D×D , the encoding process
163   is defined as: z = Eϕ (X) where z ∈ Rd×d×d represents the lower-dimensional latent space, with
164   d < D. The quantization step (VQ) maps z to the nearest entry in a learned codebook Z, and the de-
165   coder reconstructs the shape as: X ′ = Dτ (VQ(z)). The encoder, decoder, and codebook are trained
166   jointly to minimize the reconstruction loss, commitment loss (which encourages encoder alignment
167   with codebook entries), and the VQ objective to improve quantization.
168
      Forward Process of the Latent Diffusion Model. The diffusion model operates on the lower-
169   dimensional latent variable z0 = Eϕ (X), where the model learns to generate samples by reversing
170   a noise addition process, as described in (Ho et al., 2020). In the forward process, starting with the
171   clean latent representation z0 , Gaussian noise is incrementally added over a series of time steps to
172   produce a sequence of latent variables {zt }Tt=1 . At each step t, the latent variable zt is obtained by
173   diffusing the previous state:

221   4.2   H IERARCHICAL T REE OF A S ENTENCE
222
223   We draw inspiration from the Tree-Transformer (Wang et al., 2019) and recursively parse the input
224   text sequence into a hierarchical tree from top to bottom. However, unlike the Tree-Transformer,
225   which directly computes associations for all entities, we first calculate the entity correlation proba-
226
      bility using a matrix A ∈ Rm×m to cluster entities by calculating association probabilities, where m
      is the length of the entity sequence. This approach enables us to segment the sequence into several
227
      clusters based on different parent entities, thereby facilitating subsequent top-down implicit parsing
228
      of the relevant internal components of these clusters using attention layers.
229
230   Building on this hierarchical parsing strategy, we aim to ultimately align the semantic hierarchy with
231   the entity hierarchy of 3D shapes, where segmented clusters represent parent clusters of entities.
232

371

372   T-SDF into a mesh, followed by sampling 2048 points from each mesh. The dimensions of the latent
373   features Z ∈ R16×16×16×3 . Then, we train a text graph node with the Hierarchical Tree G. The
374   number of channels for the text encoder is dh = 512, and for inner layer features in the feed-forward
375   network it is 2048. The Adam optimizer is used. The learning rate is initialized to 1 × 10−5 and
376   decays by 0.8 every 5 epochs, with 20,000 epoch. Next, we employ the 3D VQ-VAE decoder and
377   trained a conditional diffusion model using the provided pretrain relational text embeddings. Our
      work utilizes the Adam optimizer to train the DDPM sampler for 200 steps with an initial learning

393   various conditioning inputs, our evaluation focuses exclusively on the text-to-3D representation task,

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We presented HierT2S, a novel framework for text-to-shape generation and modification that ex-
532
      ploits hierarchical structures inspired by human reasoning. The key contribution of this work is the
533
      use of a graph structure to impose a hierarchy on text, corresponding to the structure of 3D shapes
534   and embedding relational features into a conditional diffusion model for structure-aware generation.
535   Specifically, we employ the Hierarchical Tree to segment text into clusters and capture the relational
536   embeddings of entities, which are then utilized in the conditional diffusion model to generate high-
537   quality 3D shapes through joint training. Our approach surpasses existing methods in its ability to
538   create structure-aware 3D shapes and facilitate precise, step-by-step shape manipulation using text.
539   Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method improves generation quality and preserves the
      hierarchical characteristics of the shapes.
