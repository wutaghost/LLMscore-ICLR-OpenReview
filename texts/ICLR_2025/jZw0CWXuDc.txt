009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training data attribution (TDA) quantifies the contribution of individual training ex-
012            amples to model predictions, enabling a range of applications such as data curation,
013            data citation, and model debugging. However, applying existing TDA methods to
014            recent large models and training datasets has been largely limited by prohibitive
015            compute and memory costs. In this work, we focus on influence functions, a
016            popular gradient-based TDA method, and significantly improve its scalability with
017            an efficient gradient projection strategy called L O G RA that leverages the gradient
018            structure in backpropagation. We then provide a theoretical motivation of gra-
019            dient projection approaches to influence functions to promote trust in the TDA
020
               process. Lastly, we lower the barrier to implementing TDA systems by introducing
               L OGIX, a software package that can transform existing training code into TDA
021
               code with minimal effort. In our TDA experiments, L O G RA achieves competitive
022
               accuracy against more expensive baselines while showing up to 6,500× improve-
023            ment in throughput and 5× reduction in GPU memory usage when applied to
024            Llama3-8B-Instruct and the 1B-token dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent research has increasingly shown the critical role of training data in advancing the capabilities
031   of foundation models (Chan et al., 2022; Grosse et al., 2023; Kaplan et al., 2020). Following these
032   observations, training data attribution (TDA) (Bae et al., 2024; Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023)
033
      has gained attention as a method to enable the data-centric understanding of the model by quantifying
      the contribution of each training example to its output, which can expedite the data curation process
034
      for reinforcing specific capabilities or mitigating biases. In addition, TDA has been discussed as a
035
      technical solution to newly emerging societal problems that have come with the rise of foundation
036   models, including data author compensation/crediting and legal compliance (Huang & Chang, 2023;
037   Jia et al., 2019; Worledge et al., 2023).
038
039
      At a high level, most TDA algorithms follow the counterfactual prediction framework, and evaluate
      the contribution of each example based on its influence on the model output when included or
040
      excluded from the training dataset (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Ilyas et al., 2022; Koh & Liang, 2017). If
041
      an inclusion of a specific training example consistently improves model performance, a high score can
042   be assigned to this example for its contribution. However, applying existing TDA methods to recent
043   large models and training datasets has faced significant scalability challenges to date. For instance,
044   sampling-based methods, such as the Shapley value (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2021) or
045   Datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022), require retraining the model multiple times with varied combinations
046   of data subsets to directly model the effect of in/excluding each data. Unfortunately, such repeated
047   retraining is hardly affordable even for small models. To overcome this issue, gradient-based methods,
048   including influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023), approximate the effect of data
049   in/exclusion on the model output using gradient information without costly retraining. Even so,
050
      scaling gradient-based methods to recent foundation models is hindered by prohibitive compute and
      memory costs originating in the high-dimensional nature of the gradient.
051
052   Consequently, the main objective of this work is to bridge the gap in scaling existing TDA methods
053   to recent large models and training datasets. Toward this goal, we focus on influence functions (Koh
      & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023), a popular gradient-based TDA method, and significantly improve

      One popular instantiation of Eq. 1 is the leave-one-out error (Koh & Liang, 2017), which only
109   considers S with |S| = |D| − 1 (i.e., leaving one example x from the entire dataset D). However,
110   naively computing the leave-one-out-error requires retraining the model multiple times for each
111   x ∈ D, which is hardly affordable even in small-scale setups. To overcome this issue, influence
112   functions, a representative gradient-based method, efficiently simulates the effect of model retraining
113   without an example xtr on the utility using gradient information as:
114
                                                                 ⊤ −1
115                                    I NFLUENCE(xtr , xte ) = gte H gtr                                 (2)
116
117   where gtr and gte are train and test gradients respectively, and H is the Hessian matrix. Concretely,
118   influence functions approximate the effect of removing xtr by updating the model parameters with
119   a Newton step in the direction of H −1 gtr , and uses a first-order Taylor approximation to estimate
120   how this update will affect the test utility. In practice, computing influence functions involves two
121   key steps of (1) solving the inverse Hessian-vector product (iHVP) with gte , and (2) taking the dot
122   product of this iHVP with the gradient gtr for each training example.
123   Despite their comparative efficiency, influence functions remain difficult to scale to recent foundation
124   models and their vast training datasets, due to the high compute and memory costs associated with
125   both steps. First, space and time complexity of naive iHVP are respectively O(n2 ) and O(n3 ), both of
126   which are impractical in recent models with n > 109 parameters. To address this issue, various tricks
127   for efficiency, such as iterative methods (Koh & Liang, 2017) or EKFAC approximation (Grosse et al.,
128   2023), have been proposed. Second, to ensure fair attribution, one must compute influence scores
129   with all training data, which requires access to their gradients. However, computing gradients for all
130
      training data approximately amounts to one-epoch training, the cost of which often exceeds $1M in
      the context of large-scale (pre)training. If training gradients were to be recomputed frequently for
131
      regular TDA, the total cost can quickly become astronomical. Thus, while it is technically possible
132
      to run a few influence function analyses to interpret interesting outputs of foundation models using
133   efficient iHVP tricks (Grosse et al., 2023), doing it in a scalable and sustainable fashion to build a
134   practical TDA system remains a challenge.
135
136
      In an attempt to mitigate the aforementioned cost issues, Arnoldi IF (Schioppa et al., 2022) and
      TRAK (Park et al., 2023) recently explored the strategy of projecting gradients onto a low-dimensional
137
      space and computing influence scores on the subspace spanned by the projection matrix as follows:
138
139                                                               ⊤             −1
                            I NFLUENCE(xtr , xte ; P ) = P gte        P HP ⊤                              (3)

140                                                                                  P gtr
141
142   where P ∈ Rk×n is the projection matrix given the model and projection dimensions of n and
143   k. Under this strategy, the iHVP operation also occurs in a low-dimensional space, meaning that
144   n in memory and compute complexity of iHVP gets replaced with k ≪ n. Furthermore, low-
145   rank projection enables writing projected gradients for all training data to disk once and simply
146   reading them as new test data arrives without costly re-computations. This converts an influence
147
      function problem into a vector similarity search problem, for which various system optimizations
      exist (Johnson et al., 2019).
148
149   In essence, this strategy significantly reduces both iHVP and training gradient recomputation costs
150   by introducing an additional process of low-rank gradient projection P g. However, the additional
151   compute/memory costs and accuracy degradation incurred from low-rank gradient projection has not
152   been thoroughly studied to date. First, assuming that the batch size is b, the compute cost of naive
153
      batched gradient projection is O(bkn). Noting that the compute cost of backpropgation is O(bn) (or
      O(btn) if we consider the time dimension), the cost of gradient projection is usually larger than that
154
      of backpropagation given a reasonably large k for the expressivity. Second, the memory costs for
155
      full per-sample gradient and the projection matrix are O(bn) and O(kn). If an 8B model were to be
156   used, each of these costs amounts to 32GB×b (or ×k) GPU memory. While Arnoldi IF and TRAK
157   attempt to address the memory costs of the per-sample gradient and projection matrix respectively
158   with forward-mode Jacobian-vector products and a custom CUDA kernel trick, neither of them are
159   able to solve both issues altogether. This leads Arnoldi IF and TRAK to use very small k and b,
160   each of which results in decreased accuracy of influence scores due to limited expressivity and poor
161   efficiency from low GPU utilization. Since accuracy and efficiency are both critical for effective
      TDA, we deduce that further advancements in the gradient projection approach are necessary.

      To quantitatively assess accuracy of TDA algorithms, we adopt two counterfactual evaluation methods:
311
      brittleness test (Ilyas et al., 2022) and linear datamodeling score (LDS) (Park et al., 2023). First, the
312   brittleness test focuses on accuracy in successfully identifying top influential data. To this end, it
313   first removes the top-k influential data identified by each algorithm, retrains the model without them
314   multiple times with different random seeds, and measures the overall change in the model output.
315   The larger the output change is, the more accurate the algorithm is in identifying top influential
316   data. Second, LDS measures general attribution accuracy of all training data under the additivity
317   assumption. Specifically, given multiple data subsets {Si } of the fixed size (e.g., |Si | = |D|/2), LDS
318   estimates the test performance of the model trained on Si by summing the values of all examples in
319   Si returned by each algorithm, and compares it against the gold performance obtained by actually
320
      training the model on Si using the Spearman correlation.
321   We perform these counterfactual evaluations on three benchmarks where many rounds (up to 1800) of
322   retraining is feasible: (1) MLP with FMNIST, (2) ResNet-9 (He et al., 2016) with CIFAR-10, and (3)
323   GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019) with WikiText. On these benchmarks, we compare accuracy of L O G RA
      against four popular TDA baselines, including gradient dot product (Pruthi et al., 2020), TRAK (Park


                                                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      et al., 2023), EKFAC influence (Grosse et al., 2023), and representation similarity (Hanawa et al.,
325   2020). With the aim of bearing relevance to a large-scale setting with LLMs and their vast training
326   data, we have only considered baseline methods that satisfy the following two conditions. First, the
327   method cannot retrain the model multiple times for identifying top-k influential data.1 Second, the
328   method only has access to the final model checkpoint, which is the case for most LLMs. Given the
329   above setup, we present our experiment results in Figure 4.
330
      We observe that L O G RA slightly underperforms EKFAC influence, which is a few orders of magnitude
331   slower in our large-scale experiments (Section 4.2), while noticeably outperforming other baselines.
332   We attribute competitive accuracy of L O G RA to two factors. First, unlike TRAK of which projection
333   dimension is limited by the huge projection matrix, L O G RA can efficiently afford a higher projection
334   dimension thanks to its sublinear memory/compute costs for gradient projection, and thus achieve the
335   higher expressivity. Second, gradient projection enables L O G RA to compute raw projected Fisher
336   information matrix (or Hessian) without an approximation as in EKFAC influence. We expect that a
337   more accurate computation of the Hessian generally leads to more accurate TDA results.
338   Comparing the initialization schemes for L O G RA (PCA vs. random), we observe that L O G RA-PCA
339   outperforms L O G RA-random on the FMNIST and CIFAR benchmarks. Hence, we hypothesize
340   that it is generally more accurate to compute influence functions with larger components, similar
341   to the spectral gradient sparsification effect of a damping term we discussed in Section 3.2. To
342   understand a relatively poor performance of L O G RA-PCA on WikiText+GPT2, we point out that the
343   Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) used in this benchmark lacks the specialized KFAC
344   Hessian approximation, unlike naive MLP (Martens & Grosse, 2015) or convolutional (Grosse &
345
      Martens, 2016) architectures in other benchmarks. Subsequently, our ad-hoc implementation of the
      PCA initialization based on the naive MLP architecture (i.e., no weight sharing) may not successfully
346
      keep larger components of the GPT2 Hessian, failing to deliver its benefit. As a result, we decide to
347
      use L O G RA-random for our LLM experiments in the next subsection.

350   4.2    S CALING TO B ILLION -S CALE M ODELS & DATASETS
351
      Given competitive accuracy of L O G RA, we now evaluate its practical utility in valuing billion-scale
352
      training data for billion-scale models. Specifically, we adopt GPT2-XL (1.5B) (Radford et al., 2019),
353   Pythia-1.4B (Biderman et al., 2023), and Llama3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024) as our models, and
354   conduct TDA on a random 1B-token subset of the OpenWebText (OWT) dataset (Gokaslan et al.,
355   2019). The major motivations behind choosing OWT as our TDA dataset are twofold. First, we
356   observe that OWT consists of relatively higher-quality data compared to other LLM training datasets
357   like C4 (Raffel et al., 2020) or Dolma (Soldaini et al., 2024) while maintaining the diversity unlike
358   other high-quality datasets like WikiText (Merity et al., 2016). Second, we anticipate that OWT
359   largely overlaps with training datasets of all our models. In detail, GPT2-XL is trained on the
360   WebText dataset that shares the same data curation process with OWT, Pythia-1.4B is trained on
361
      the Pile dataset (Gao et al., 2020) that includes an extension of OWT (i.e., OpenWebText2), and we
      suppose a majority of OWT would be a part of Llama3’s massive 15T-token pretraining dataset. We
362
      also note that our OWT subset size (i.e., 1B tokens) was mainly limited by the available storage, not
363
      by compute (see Table 1). If we had access to a storage size of 1PB, performing TDA with a dataset
364   size of 100B+ tokens would be readily feasible using the same compute resource.
365
366   Efficiency. To begin with, we compare memory and compute efficiency of L O G RA against EKFAC
367
      influence (Grosse et al., 2023), the state-of-the-art and only algorithm that can run on billion-scale
      models without CUDA out-of-memory (OOM) errors. Indeed, we confirm that running TRAK or
368
      Arnoldi IF with billion-scale models results in CUDA OOM errors even on A100 GPUs with 80GB
369

370   of both logging (one-time) and influence computation (recurring) phases for the Llama3-8B-Instruct

373   storing raw gradients for all training data to disk. As a result, EKFAC needs to recompute all training

402   Qualitative Accuracy. Next, we analyze qualitative similarities between queried LLM outputs

406
      functions that normalizes the original influence score with the self-influence score of each training
407
      data to penalize such outlier effects (Barshan et al., 2020). Our experimental results are provided in
408   Figure 5 (concise) and in Appendix A (extensive).
409
410   We observe that most influential data identified by L O G RA, especially for Llama3-8B-Instruct and
411

      outputs. For instance, given Llama3’s response on the dream manipulation product, L O G RA identifies
412
      a scientific article that studies manually inducing the lucid dream as most influential data in Figure 5a.
413
      In Figure 5b, both the GPT2-XL output and the corresponding most influential data discuss the
414   need for reducing emissions in the coal industry and its connection to the specific administration.
415   In Figure 5c, the concept of “lifting barbell or dumbells” appear in the model output and the most
416   influential data.
417

418
      not share qualitative similarities with the LLM output, especially with Pythia-1.4B (Appendix A.3).
419
      We here provide three potential explanations on these failing examples based on our experiments.
420   First, attributed data may lack qualitative similarities when the queried LLM output itself is incoherent
421   that its gradient does not encode meaningful information. This aligns with our observation that the
422   failure case occurs more frequently with lower-tier models like Pythia-1.4B whose outputs generally
423   are of lower quality. Second, since we only used a 1B-token subset for TDA, it is possible that our
424   attribution dataset may lack similar data to some queries. As noted above, our experiment was largely
425   limited by the storage of our cluster (not by the compute), so exploring TDA on an industry-scale
426   cluster would be interesting future work. Third, we posit that train/test gradients in influence functions
427   may encode diverse information including features that are hardly perceptible to humans (Ilyas et al.,
428
      2019). Therefore, it is possible that attributed data are indeed influential for increasing the likelihood
      of the queried output by contributing to these other aspects while sharing little qualitative similarities.
429
      A more extensive argument on this final point can be found in Appendix A.3.

463   5    R ELATED W ORK
464
465   Training Data Attribution. Measuring the contribution of training data on the model outputs has
466   gained lots of attention recently. Exemplified by Data Shapley (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019), a flurry of
467   prior work (Jia et al., 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2021; Wang & Jia, 2023) proposed exploiting the Shapley
468   value or concepts from cooperative game theory to address the data attribution problem. However,
469   most existing approaches in this line require repeated retraining of the model, a cost of which is
470   hardly affordable even with small models. In addition to game-theoretic approaches, data attribution
471
      has also been tackled using reinforcement learning (Yoon et al., 2020), meta learning (Choe et al.,
      2024), and training-free methods (Nohyun et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022). Nevertheless, these works
472
      either suffer from high complexity from the need to train other models (Choe et al., 2024; Yoon et al.,
473
      2020) or high computational costs (Nohyun et al., 2023).
474
475   Influence Functions. Influence functions, a classic concept from robust statistics (Hampel, 1974),
476   estimate the infinitesimal effect of removing or adding a training data point without model retraining.
477
      They have various applications in machine learning, such as interpreting the model’s behavior (Han
      et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023; Grosse et al., 2023) and curating training datasets (Liu et al., 2021;
478
      Engstrom et al., 2024). However, when applied to large neural networks, the computation of the
479
      iHVP and its dot product with all training examples introduce scalability challenges. Besides
480   gradient projection, past works have explored computing influence functions only on the last (few)
481   layers (Koh & Liang, 2017; Schioppa et al., 2022) to mitigate these challenges. However, subsequent
482   works (Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Grosse et al., 2023) have shown that the influence on only a subset
483   of layers is insufficient to capture the overall influence of a training data point. To avoid computing
484   the gradient of all training examples, various filtering strategies, such as using the similarity in the
485   model’s representation space (Guo et al., 2020) or TF-IDF (Yeh et al., 2022; Grosse et al., 2023),
      have also been proposed. While it is possible to adopt these filtering strategies for L O G RA, they may


                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      introduce bias in the selection of the most influential sequences. For example, filtering candidate
487   training sequences with TF-IDF might miss interesting influential sequences that do not share many
488   tokens but are semantically related. Recently, similarly to L O G RA, DataInf (Kwon et al., 2024) and
489   LESS (Xia et al., 2024) proposed using LoRA to efficiently compute influence functions. However,
490   these approaches are only applicable in finetuning settings, whereas L O G RA also supports influence
491   analyses for pretraining.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this work, we explored scaling TDAs with influence functions to billion-scale models and datasets.
496
      Toward this goal, we developed a novel gradient projection algorithm that can significantly improve
497   the scalability of influence functions, and designed a simple and interoperable software. Our exper-
498   iments showed that L O G RA achieves competitive accuracy to other more expensive baselines on
499   counterfactual evaluations, while efficiently scaling to billion-scale models and datasets, thereby
500   demonstrating the initial potential of the practical TDA system. Last but not least, we discuss broader
501   impacts and limitations of our work in Appendix F.

       In this section, we discuss several key differences between L OGIX and other interpretability tools,
       and optimizations we implemented in L OGIX.
1732
1733
1734   E.1    D IFFERENCES WITH OTHER T OOLS
1735
1736   Influence functions have been extensively studied as an interpretable AI method. Accordingly,
1737   there have been several tools originating in the AI interpretability field that implement influence
1738   functions, with most notable examples including Captum Kokhlikyan et al. (2020), TRAK Park et al.
1739   (2023), and Kronfluence Grosse et al. (2023). Overall, the software design of these tools aim at
1740
       easing the from-scratch implementation of influence functions by introducing a lot of abstraction,
       following the philosophy of high-level frameworks. In fact, such software designs were well-received
1741
       in the pre-LLM era. Nonetheless, as scaling has become a key aspect of AI research, the (LLM)
1742
       development ecosystem has become complicated and being able to compatibly work with other
1743   tools in the ecosystem has become a core aspect in the ML software design. Hence, unlike existing
1744   software, the design of L OGIX aims at enabling the easy conversion of users’ (already efficient)
1745   training codes into TDA codes. This design is also motivated by the observation that gradient is
1746   simply a by-product of the training procedure so that we can reuse most of the training code for TDA
1747   without needing to write the gradient computation code from scratch as in other tools.
1748
       Recently, there have been active developments in (mechanistic) interpretability software, represented
1749   by TransformerLens Nanda & Bloom (2022) and pyvene Wu et al. (2024). Interestingly, these software
1750   also extensively use PyTorch hooks, similarly to L OGIX, probably due to its high compatibility
1751   with other features such as autocast, distributed data parallelism, fully-sharded data parallelism, and
1752   gradient checkpointing. Nevertheless, we point out two major differences between these (mechanistic)
1753   interpretability software and L OGIX. First, support for dataset-level statistics computations in L OGIX
1754   is largely missing in these tools. In TDA, we often need to compute several dataset-level statistics
1755   such as the Hessian (or Fisher information matrix) for accurate influence computations, and therby
1756   supporting these computations seamlessly was an important design principle behind L OGIX. However,
1757
       analyses in (mechanistic) interpretability research typically focuses on each instance and computing
       dataset-level statistics is typically not supported. Second, support for efficient data IO in L OGIX
1758
       is not a priority in other tools. As we propose to convert the TDA problem into a vector similarity
1759
       search problem with gradient projection, we put efforts into improving efficiency of data IO (see the
1760   next subsection for details), whereas this issue is rarely considered in other interpretability tools. We
1761   hope to explore the possibility of supporting both TDA and other interpretability research in a unified
1762   way with L OGIX as our future work.
1763
1764
1765   E.2    O PTIMIZATIONS
1766
1767
       Efficient Data IO With L O G RA, we propose to save projected gradients for all training data to
       disk, and frequently load them as a new test batch arrives. As a result, reducing latency from data IO
1768
       renders to be critical in realizing efficient TDA. In particular, as the total size of all training gradients
1769
       is usually far beyond the limit of CPU memory, we should optimize data transfer between disk and
1770   CPU (or GPU). To address this issue, we adopted the memory-mapped files that bypasses the need
1771   for intermediate copying between kernel space and user space, reducing the overhead associated
1772   with data IO operations. The use of the memory-mapped files is also motivated by the observation
1773   that, given each query batch, TDA often requires computing influence scores with all training data.
1774   Therefore, we can access training gradients in a predefined or sequential order instead of in a random
1775   order, which can be done efficiently with memory-mapped files (sequential access is faster than
1776   random access).
1777   Moreover, we overlap memory-mapped-file-based data IO with computations to further enhance TDA
1778   efficiency. In the logging phase, we overlap the process of saving gradients extracted from the current
1779   training batch to disk with computations for the next training batch using Python multiprocessing. In
1780   the influence computation phase, we overlap the process of loading saved training gradients from
1781   disk with computing a dot product with the query batch using the pre-fetching feature of PyTorch
       DataLoader.

1782
       We also note that more efficient data IO can be achieved by the use of more advanced techniques
1783   like GPU-accelerated vector database, especially in the production setting. While we considered
1784   supporting this feature, we decided to focus on the memory-mapped-file-based data IO in our initial
1785   version of L OGIX, as it offers more flexibility to explore different algorithms in the research setting.
1786
       Memory Optimization When dealing with LLMs, GPU memory is often a major scaling bottleneck.
1787
       To alleviate this issue, we support CPU offloading of dataset-level statistics by utilizing the sequential
1788
       nature of backpropgation. When this feature is enabled, we by default keep all dataset-level statistics
1789   (e.g., gradient covariance) on CPU, move it to GPU when the corresponding module is called during
1790   forward/backward passes, and then move it back to CPU asynchronously as soon as updating statistics
1791   for the module is done. Depending on the CPU-GPU communication bandwidth, this feature may
1792   slow down the logging process.
1793
       Communication Optimization If training data are split across multiple processes with distributed
1794
       training, we need to aggregate dataset-level statistics across processes for consistency. To minimize the
1795   communication cost, we delay the synchronization process until the training loop (one epoch) is over,
1796   and perform synchronization only once at the end. Following the similar logic, users can maximize
1797   the efficiency of the logging phase by disabling gradient synchronization (e.g., torch.no sync).

1846   F.2   L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
1847
       We generally observed that influence function approaches are susceptible to outlier data with large
1848
       gradient norms. This outlier issue is particularly severe for language modeling tasks due to the fact
1849
       that the gradient of each sequence is the sum of gradients for all tokens in that sequence. If a few
1850   tokens in the sequence have large gradient norms, their gradients may dominate the total gradient
1851   for the sequence and hurt TDA accuracy. While our work tried to reduce the outlier effect with
1852   (self-influence) normalization, exploring other filtering heuristics (e.g., L2 /L1 norm ratio Grosse
1853   et al. (2023)) may be an interesting research direction.
1854
       We attempted to lay the software foundation for TDA with L OGIX, but did not implement extensive
1855
       system support, such as high-performance vector database (e.g., Faiss Johnson et al. (2019)). We
1856   expect further system optimizations would enable significantly more efficient TDA. To reduce the
1857   cost of influence functions, our work mostly explored low-rank gradient projection, which compresses
1858   the gradient in a spectral domain in essence. Noting that gradient compression has been extensively
1859   studied in the efficient distributed training literature, it is worth exploring (or combining) different
1860   gradient compression strategies, e.g., top-k compression Shi et al. (2019) or low-bit compression Wen
1861   et al. (2017), to further reduce the compute/memory/storage costs for influence functions.
