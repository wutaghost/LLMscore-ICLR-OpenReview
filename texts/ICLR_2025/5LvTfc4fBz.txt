009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurate simulation of turbulent flows is of immense importance in a variety of
012            scientific and engineering fields, including climate science, freshwater science,
013            and the development of energy-efficient manufacturing processes. Within the
014            realm of turbulent flow simulation, direct numerical simulation (DNS) is widely
015            considered to be the most reliable approach, but it is prohibitively expensive and
016            thus has limited applicability to long-term and fine-scale simulation over various
017            configurations. Given the pressing need for efficient simulation, there is an in-
018
               creasing interest in building machine learning models for simulating turbulence,
               either by reconstructing DNS from alternative low-fidelity simulations or sequen-
019
               tially predicting DNS from historical data. However, conventional machine learn-
020
               ing models are not designed for capturing complex spatio-temporal characteristics
021            of turbulent flows. This results in their limited performance and generalizability,
022            especially when applied to complex flow data and different flow configurations.
023            This paper presents a novel physics-enhanced neural operator (PENO) that effi-
024            ciently models the complex flow dynamics while leveraging physical knowledge
025            of partial differential equations (PDEs) to enhance simulation process. we fur-
026            ther introduce a self-augmentation mechanism to reduce the accumulated errors
027            in long-term simulations. The proposed method is evaluated through its perfor-
028            mance on multiple turbulent flow datasets, showcasing the model’s capability to
029
               reconstruct high-resolution DNS data, maintain the inherent physical properties of
               flow transport, and transfer across various resolution settings and simulation con-
030
               figurations. These encouraging results confirms its applicability to a wide range
031
               of real-world scenarios in which extensive simulations are needed under diverse
032            settings.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Advances in computational fluid dynamics (CFD) have significantly impacted various scientific and
038   engineering domains. In the clean energy sector, CFD is essential for enhancing power generation
039   and distribution, including the design of high-efficiency wind turbines and their strategic position-
040   ing to maximize energy capture. In the aerospace industry, CFD plays a critical role in analyzing
041   aerodynamic forces and thermal effects on aircraft, rockets, and spacecraft. In particular, efficient
042   CFD techniques are crucial for modeling and refining airflow around wings, fuselages, and engine
043   components, which can help improve fuel efficiency, reduce drag, and enhance maneuverability
044
      and safety. Furthermore, CFD is indispensable in climate science for predicting pollution patterns,
      enhancing emission controls, and assessing the environmental impact of infrastructure projects.
045
046   In the field of computational fluid dynamics (CFD), the simulation of turbulent flows, particularly
047   at high spatial resolutions and over long periods, is a crucial task for many scientific applications.
048   Direct numerical simulation (DNS) is widely considered to be the most reliable approach to produce
049   detailed turbulence simulations of high fidelity. However, DNS requires substantial computational
050
      resources, which limits its practicality for long-term simulations at fine spatial scales (Givi, 1994).
051   There are two common approaches to address this issue using data-driven methods. The first ap-
052   proach aims to reconstruct DNS data from the low-fidelity large eddy simulation (LES) (Fukami
053   et al., 2019; 2021; Liu et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Specifically, LES filters out
      the smaller scales of turbulent transport (Sagaut, 2005), and consequently, it only generates low-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      fidelity simulations on coarser grids (Nouri et al., 2017). Most of these approaches are based on
055   super-resolution (SR) techniques (Park et al., 2003), which have been highly successful in gener-
056   ating high-resolution data in various commercial applications. Predominantly, SR models employ
057   convolutional network layers (CNNs) (Albawi et al., 2017) to identify and transform spatial fea-
058   tures into high-resolution images through non-linear mappings. From the initial end-to-end SRCNN
059   model (Dong et al., 2014), researchers have utilized additional structural elements, including skip-
060   connections (Zhang et al., 2018b; Ahn et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2019; Van Duong et al., 2021), chan-
061   nel attention (Zhang et al., 2018a), adversarial training objectives (Ledig et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
062   2018a;b; Karras et al., 2017; Upadhyay & Awate, 2019; Cheng et al., 2021; Wenlong et al., 2021),
063
      and more recently, Transformer-based structures (Fang et al., 2022a; Lu et al., 2022; Fang et al.,
      2022b; Wang et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2022), and the implicit neural representation
064
      methods (Chen et al., 2022). Despite their popularity, these methods remain limited in their accuracy
065
      for reconstructing detailed flow patterns, which is primarily due to the lack of physical information
066   about the small-scale flow transport in the low-resolution LES data.
067
068   To retain detailed turbulence patterns and capture temporal dynamics in turbulence flows, the se-
069
      quential prediction method has been developed for generating high-resolution DNS data directly
      from historical high-resolution DNS data. Specifically, the sequential prediction method employs
070
      temporal modeling structures to capture underlying flow dynamics, which can be further enhanced
071
      by integrating governing partial differential equations (PDEs) (Omori & Kotera, 2007). This can be
072   achieved by incorporating PDEs into the neural network’s learning process (Cai et al., 2021; Eivazi
073   et al., 2022; Kag et al., 2022; Yousif et al., 2022) or by directly encoding PDEs within a recurrent
074   unit (Bao et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Recently, neural operator-based methods (Lu et al., 2019;
075   Li et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2022; Equer et al., 2023; Boussif et al., 2022) have also shown promise
076   in sequential prediction for the Navier-Stokes equation (Foias et al., 2001). The main advantage of
077   neural operator-based methods is their generalizability to different boundary and initial conditions,
078   and their efficiency in generating simulations. Among these methods, the Fourier neural operator
079   (FNO) (Li et al., 2020) also allows the generation of DNS at higher resolutions in a zero-shot fash-
080
      ion, reducing the need for costly high-resolution training data. However, these approaches are not
      designed to explicitly leverage the knowledge of PDEs, leading to two major drawbacks. First, it is
081
      challenging for these approaches to capture complex flow dynamics, especially when training data
082
      are scarce. This becomes a critical issue in the context of complex 3D flows, where these methods
083   often exhibit degraded performance. Their prediction errors also accumulate quickly for continu-
084   ously modeling complex flows over long periods. Second, they remain limited in generalizing to
085   a heterogeneous set of flow datasets governed by different PDE settings, which if often needed for
086   many manufacturing tasks. In the absence of underlying physics, the model is unable to fully dis-
087   tinguish between different flow behaviors. Even though the model could be fine-tuned towards each
088   new flow dataset, it requires additional cost to generate initial simulations needed for fine-tuning.
089   In this paper, we propose a novel method, physics-enhanced neural operator (PENO), for enhancing
090   the simulation of turbulent transport over long periods and different flow datasets. This proposed
091   method incorporates the physical knowledge of PDE into the FNO (Li et al., 2020) to effectively
092   model turbulence dynamics and also introduces a new self-augmentation mechanism to mitigate
093   the accumulated errors in long-term simulation. In particular, we complement the Fourier layers
094   in FNO with an additional network branch, which gradually estimates the temporal gradient of
095   target flow variables following the underlying PDE. This combined model structure leverages the
096   physical knowledge to better capture complex flow dynamics even in 3D space while also keeping
097
      the power and efficiency of data-driven FNO. Next, we identify a key limitation of the standard
      Fourier layers in preserving informative high-frequency signals, which degrades the performance
098
      in long-term simulation. Hence, we augment the input data at each time through zero-shot super-
099
      resolution and random perturbation. By introducing additional high-frequency signals at each time
100   step, this self-augmentation mechanism can help prevent Fourier layers from filtering out important
101   high-frequency information during long-term simulation.
102
103
      The PENO method has undergone thorough assessments using two sets of data, (i) modeling com-
      plex flow dynamics on 3D turbulence data, and (ii) generalization over different flow datasets. For
104
      test (i), we utilize two datasets: the forced isotropic turbulent (FIT) flow (Minping et al., 2012),
105
      and the Taylor-Green vortex (TGV) flow (Brachet et al., 1984). These assessments demonstrate the
106   PENO’s consistent ability to reconstruct data effectively over time and across different resolutions.
107   The effectiveness of each component in the proposed method has been highlighted through both

      qualitative and quantitative analyses. For test (ii), we conduct experiments on multiple 2D turbulent
109   flow series to confirm the PENO method’s transferability and generalizability. Our implementation

      2.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
116
      This study focuses on the transport of unsteady turbulent flows. In every scenario, the flow is treated
117   as Newtonian and incompressible, characterized by a uniform density. Spatially, the coordinates
118   are denoted by the vector x ≡ {x, y, z} in a 3D space or x ≡ {x, y} in a 2D space, while time
119   is indicated by t. We denote by Q(x, t) the target flow variables (e.g., velocity or vorticity) to
120   be simulated. The pressure, density, and dynamic viscosity in the flow are expressed as p(x, t),
121   ρ(x, t), and ν, respectively. As a neural operator-based approach, the proposed PENO aims to
122   create mappings between infinite-dimensional functional spaces, and it treats solutions to PDEs as
123   functions rather than discrete sets of points. Henceforth, we represent flow variables, pressure, and
124   density as time-dependent functions Q(t), p(t), and ρ(t), respectively, in describing PENO, without
125
      explicitly showing the spatial coordinates x.
126   During the training process, the provided DNS data are obtained at regular time intervals δ, denoted
127   as Q = {Q(t)}, where t belongs to the time range {t0 , t0 + δ, . . . , t0 + Kδ}. The goal is to
128   forecast high-resolution DNS data for future time points, specifically at {t0 + (K + 1)δ, . . . , t0 +
129   M δ}. Additionally, we have access to the large eddy simulation (LES) data at lower resolutions,
130   represented as Ql = Ql (t) for t ∈ [t0 , t0 + M δ]. Since LES data require less computational effort
131   to generate, we assume that they are available for both training and testing phases.

185   3.1   P HYSICS - ENHANCED N EURAL O PERATOR
186
      The PENO method sequentially processes input DNS data Q(t) at each time step and predicts the
187
      DNS data for the next step Q̂(t+δ). As shown in Figure 2, the prediction at each time step combines
188
189
      the outputs from two network branches, i.e., Q̂FNO (t+δ) and Q̂PDE (t+δ), as Q̂(t+δ) = wf Q̂FNO (t+
190   δ) + wp Q̂PDE (t + δ), where wf and wp are learnable parameters. The first branch consists of several
191
      Fourier layers, each of which contains a Fourier transformation, a linear transformation layer, and
      an inverse Fourier transformation. Although the Fourier layers are based on the Green function
192
      method for solving PDEs, they are agnostic of physical knowledge for the target dataset and remain
193
      a purely data-driven approach. This leads to the limitation in capturing complex flow dynamics given
194   scarce training data. Hence, we introduce an additional PDE-enhancement branch to complement
195   the simulation by FNO by leveraging underlying PDEs.
196
197
      Several methods have been developed to incorporate PDEs into the learning process, including the
      physics-based loss function (Chen et al., 2021; Yousif et al., 2022; Bode et al., 2021; Yousif et al.,
198
      2021; Pawar, 2022) and physics-based model structures (Bao et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021). In this
199
      work, the design of the PDE-enhancement network branch is inspired by (Bao et al., 2022), which
200   introduces a new recurrent unit to gradually estimate the temporal gradients over time based on the
201   PDE. The key idea is to leverage the continuous physical relationships depicted by the underlying
202   partial differential equation (PDE) to bridge the gap between discrete data samples and the contin-
203   uous dynamics of the flow. It also does not require modification of the loss function, which often
204   leads to the training instability for complex PDEs (Sun et al., 2022) and also may not guarantee
205   consistency with PDE in the testing phase (e.g., future simulations).
206   Specifically, the PDE-enhancement network branch utilizes the Runge–Kutta (RK) discretization
207   method (Butcher, 2007) for PDEs. The PDE for the target variables Q can be formulated as: Qt =
208   f(t, Q; θ), where Qt denotes the temporal derivative of Q, and f(t, Q; θ) is a non-linear function
209   determined by the parameter θ. This function summarizes the present value of Q along with its
210   spatial fluctuations. The turbulent data adheres to the Navier-Stokes equation for an incompressible
211   flow. For example, the dynamics of the velocity field can be expressed by the following PDE:

245   3.2   S ELF - AUGMENTATION M ECHANISM
246
247   Here we re-examine the frequency spectrum obtained through PENO for modeling turbulent trans-
248   port. In most PDEs, the large-scale, low-frequency components usually possess larger magnitudes
249   than the small-scale, high-frequency components. Therefore, as regularization, the Fourier layers
250   incorporate a frequency truncation process in each layer, allowing only the lowest K Fourier modes
251   to propagate input information. This truncation process encourages the learning of low-frequency
252   components in PDEs, a phenomenon closely related to the well-known implicit spectral bias (Cao
253   et al., 2019). This bias indicates that neural networks when trained using gradient descent, tend to
254   prioritize learning low-frequency functions (Rahaman et al., 2019). It provides an implicit regular-
255
      ization effect that encourages a neural network to converge to a low-frequency and ’simple’ solution.
256   However, in turbulent flow simulations, it is of-
257   ten observed that high-frequency components
258   carry important flow patterns necessary for
259   accurate prediction. The absence of high-
260   frequency information makes it challenging to
261
      adequately represent vital flow data details,
      leading to inaccurate simulations. As demon-
262
      strated in Figure 4 (a) and Figure 4 (c), there
263
      is a noticeable difference in the frequency dis- Figure 4: The frequency distribution of the FNO
264   tribution between the FNO’s reconstructed data and PENO predictions, and the target DNS.

266   flow. It is evident that high-frequency information is missing when the frequency exceeds 0.25. This

268   illustrated in Figure 1. Therefore, to address the limitation of FNO, a new self-augmentation pro-

      during the Fourier layer processing.

      Specifically, few-shot and zero-shot tests aim to testify the model generalizability across turbulent
418
      flows governed by different PDEs. They both utilize 50 complete flow sequences (15 seconds over
419
      50 time steps) for training with the viscosity value (a parameter in the Navier-Stokes equation)
420   sampled uniformly from 1e−5 to 1.25e−5 , followed by testing on 10 additional sequences with the
421   viscosity value sampled uniformly from 1.25e−5 to 1.5e−5 . The zero-shot test directly applies the
422   sequential prediction models obtained from training sequences to predict vorticity for 20 time steps
423   on each testing sequence. In contrast, the few-shot test also utilizes the first 10 time steps of data
424   from each testing sequence to fine-tune the trained model before proceeding with sequential predic-
425   tions in the next 20 time steps. Different from both zero-shot and few-shot tests, the sequential test
426   aims to testify the model generalizability over time. It trains the models using the first 20 time steps
427   from 50 complete training sequences and then applies the obtained models to create simulations for
428
      the following 20 time steps in the same set of flow sequences.

530
531
      A novel physics-enhanced neural operator (PENO) has been developed to improve the simulation
      of turbulent transport over long-term simulations and various flow datasets. PENO is particularly
532
      applicable in the domain of unsteady, incompressible, Newtonian turbulent flows under conditions
533
      of spatial homogeneity. Specifically, PENO integrates physical knowledge of PDEs with the FNO
534   framework to effectively model turbulence dynamics. Additionally, PENO introduces a novel self-
535   augmentation mechanism designed to reduce the accumulation of errors in long-term simulations.
536   The efficacy of the model is assessed through three turbulent flow configurations, employing both
537   flow visualization and statistical analysis techniques. The experimental results confirm PENO’s
538   enhanced capabilities in long-term simulations. More significantly, the PENO method shows po-
539   tential for broad applicability in scientific problems characterized by complex temporal dynamics,
      particularly where generating high-resolution simulations is prohibitively expensive.

      The principal idea of the Runge-Kutta (RK) discretization method (Butcher, 2007) is to use the
722   continuous relationships outlined by the underlying PDEs to connect discrete data points with the
723   continuous flow dynamics. This approach is adaptable to any dynamic system that is defined by
724   deterministic PDEs. The PDE that describes the target variables Q as expressed by:
725
726
                                                  Qt = f(t, Q; θ),                                         (3)
727   where Qt denotes the temporal derivative of Q, and f(t, Q; θ) is a non-linear function determined by
728   the parameter θ. This function summarizes the present value of Q along with its spatial fluctuations.
729   The turbulent data adheres to the Navier-Stokes equation for an incompressible flow. For example,
730   the dynamics of the velocity field can be expressed by the following PDE:
731
                                              1
732                                   f(Q) = − ∇p + ν∆Q − (Q · ∇)Q,                                        (4)
                                              ρ
733
734   where the term ∇ represents the gradient operator, and ∆ = ∇ · ∇ acts on each com-
735   ponent of the velocity vector. We omit the independent variable t in the function f(·) be-
736   cause f(Q) in the Navier-Stokes equations refers to a specific time t, analogous to the t in
737   Qt . Figure 9 illustrates the overall structure, which involves a series of intermediate states
738
      {Q(t, 0), Q(t, 1), Q(t, 2), . . . , Q(t, N )}, where Q(t, 0) ≡ Q(t). The temporal gradients are esti-
      mated at these states as {Qt,0 , Qt,1 , Qt,2 , . . . , Qt,N }. Beginning with Q(t, 0) = Q(t), we estimate
739
      the temporal gradient Qt,0 , then progresses Q(t) in the direction of this gradient to generate the
740
      subsequent intermediate state Q(t, 1). This procedure is iterated for N intermediate states. For the
741
      fourth-order RK method, which is applied here, we have N = 3.
742
743   To initiate with the data point Q(t), we employ an augmentation by integrating LES with DNS data,
744   formulated as Q(t) = W d Q(t) + W l Ql (t), where W d and W l are trainable model parameters, and
745   Ql (t) is the up-sampled LES data with the same resolution as DNS. We estimate the first temporal
746   gradient Qt,0 = f(Q(t)) using the Navier-Stokes equation and computes the next intermediate state
747   variable Q(t, 1) by moving the flow data Q(t) along the direction of temporal derivatives. Given
748   frequent LES data, the intermediate states Q(t, n) are also augmented by using LES data Ql (t, n),
749   as Q(t, n) = W d Q(t, n) + W l Ql (t, n). This iterative method progresses Q(t) along the computed
750   gradient Qt,n to compute the next intermediate states Q(t, n + 1), expressed as:
