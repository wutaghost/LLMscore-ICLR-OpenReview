009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recommender systems aim to deeply understand users’ complex preferences based
012            on their past interactions. Deep collaborative filtering paradigms, leveraging ad-
013            vanced neural architectures like Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), excel at capturing
014            collaborative relationships among users. However, limitations emerge when deal-
015            ing with sparse data or zero-shot learning from unseen datasets, due to the design
016
               constraints of ID-based embedding functions in existing solutions. These chal-
               lenges hinder robust generalization and adaptability. To address this, we propose a
017
               model-agnostic recommendation instruction-tuning paradigm that integrates large
018
               language models with collaborative filtering. Our Recommendation Language
019            Model (RecLM) is introduced to enhance the capability of capturing user prefer-
020            ence diversity. We design a reinforcement learning reward function to facilitate self-
021            augmentation of our language models. Comprehensive evaluations demonstrate
022            significant advantages of our approach across various settings. It can be integrated
023            as a plug-and-play component with state-of-the-art recommender systems, resulting
024            in notable performance enhancements. We have made our RecLM available anony-
025            mously at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/RecLM-A1BE/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recommendation systems play a vital role in web applications, assisting users in navigating the vast
030   amount of information accessible online. These systems deliver personalized recommendations of
031   items that users may find interesting, including products on e-commerce platforms Wang et al. (a);
032   Wu et al., posts on social networking sites Jamali & Ester; Zhang et al. (2021), and videos on sharing
033   platforms Wei et al. (a); Zhan et al.. One of the most widely used methods for generating these
034   recommendations is Collaborative Filtering (CF). This approach leverages the preferences of similar
035   users to suggest new items to a specific user He et al. (2017).
036   However, it is essential to emphasize that most of current recommender systems primarily rely on
037   the user/item ID paradigm, where the training data largely consists of mapped user and item indices.
038   While this approach has significantly advanced recommendation, particularly in scenarios with ample
039   training data Yuan et al., it also presents notable limitations. Key challenges include suboptimal
040   performance in cold-start scenarios and difficulties in generalizing to zero-shot learning situations. In
041   completely cold-start settings, ID-based recommenders struggle to generate effective representations
042   for new items, often leading to failures in providing valid recommendations.
043   To address the cold-start challenge in the ID-based recommendation paradigm, a promising approach
044   is to leverage external features (e.g., textual or visual information) associated with users and items
045   to generate their representations, rather than relying on ID-based embeddings. However, real-world
046   scenarios often lack complete modal features. For instance, many users may withhold personal infor-
047   mation due to privacy concerns, resulting in incomplete data. Additionally, these external features
048   often contain noise, which can distort modeling of user preferences. For example, misleading tags or
049   inaccurate specifications in an item’s description may lead to misguided recommendations. Conse-
050
      quently, extracting accurate, relevant, and high-quality external features from noisy and incomplete
      data has become a critical challenge for generalizing recommenders under data scarcity.
051
052   Contribution. Inspired by the robust generalization and reasoning capabilities of Large Language
053   Models (LLMs), we propose the development of effective language models as profiling systems
      specifically designed for recommendation tasks, aimed at enhancing performance in cold-start

054
      recommendation scenarios. Utilizing LLMs for profile generation involves addressing two primary
055   challenges: (i) How can LLMs generate profile text that accurately reflects the recommendation
056   characteristics for users or items lacking external features? (ii) How can LLMs produce high-quality
057   profiles from noisy features while effectively capturing user-item interaction behavior context?
058
      To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that involves performing message passing
059
      among users and items to enrich user and item profiling with information from their interactions. This
060
      method allows users and items with insufficient external features to be effectively profiled through
061   their interaction dependencies from a global perspective. Additionally, we introduce an innovative
062   recommendation instruction tuning paradigm that integrates behavioral signals into LLMs. This
063   paradigm enables LLMs to not only incorporate external features from users and items but also
064   to understand user preferences in the context of user-item interaction data. By guiding LLMs to
065   consider collaborative relationships, this approach addresses the lack of direct supervision signals in
066   profile generation tasks through self-supervised learning. Furthermore, to mitigate the extraneous
067   noise introduced by this instruction tuning paradigm and counteract the over-smoothing caused
068   by collaborative relationships, we propose a reinforcement learning-based personalized feature
069
      enhancement method. This technique aims to further improve the accuracy and personalization of the
      generated profiles. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
070
071   • Model-Agnostic Framework. We introduce a model-agnostic instruction tuning framework
072     RecLM. It can be seamlessly integrated into existing recommender systems as a plug-and-play
073     component, significantly enhancing their generalization capacity in scenarios with limited data.
074   • Enhancing Profiling System. In this work, we seamlessly integrate large language models with
075     collaborative filtering to enhance user profiling, particularly in cold-start scenarios, where current
076     methods often struggle. Additionally, our approach employs reinforcement learning to refine profile
077     quality, effectively addressing challenges associated with data noise and over-smoothing.
078   • Comprehensive Evaluation. We integrate RecLM with a range of state-of-the-art recommenders to
079     assess the effectiveness of our approach across various settings. This includes conducting ablation
080     studies and efficiency evaluations. Additionally, we carry out extensive experiments in real-world
081     industrial recommendation scenarios, demonstrating the practicality and scalability of RecLM.

085   2.1   ID- BASED R ECOMMENDER S YSTEMS
086
087   In recommender systems, numerous collaborative filtering models have been proposed to map users
088   and items into latent representations based on user/item IDs Koren et al. (2021); Su & Khoshgoftaar
089   (2009). These methods have evolved significantly, starting from early matrix factorization techniques,
090   such as BiasMF Koren et al. (2009), to the introduction of Neural Collaborative Filtering (NCF)
091
      with the advent of neural networks He et al. (2017). Recently, advancements in Graph Neural
      Networks (GNNs) have opened promising avenues for constructing bipartite graphs based on user-
092
      item interaction history, allowing for the capture of high-order collaborative relationships. GNN-based
093
      methods, including NGCF Wang et al. (2019), GCCF Chen et al., and LightGCN He et al. (2020),
094   have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance, enhancing the effectiveness of recommendation.
095
096   Additionally, researchers have incorporated self-supervised learning (SSL) techniques as supple-
097
      mentary learning objectives to improve the robustness of recommenders and address challenges
      related to data sparsity and noise Yu et al. (2023). Contrastive learning (CL), a widely adopted
098
      SSL technique, has been effectively applied in CF research through approaches such as SGL Wu
099
      et al. (2021), SimGCL Yu et al. (2022), NCL Lin et al., and AdaGCL Jiang et al.. Despite these
100   advancements, ID-based recommenders still face significant limitations, particularly in completely
101   cold-start scenarios and in terms of model transferability Yuan et al..
102
103   2.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (LLM S ) FOR R ECOMMENDATION
104
105   The application of large language models (LLMs) in recommender systems has garnered significant
106   attention Fan et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023). Current approaches can be categorized
107   into two main types. The first category includes methods such as P5 Geng et al. and Chat-REC Gao
      et al. (2023), which emphasize designing prompts aligned with recommendation tasks, utilizing the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      LLM directly as the inference model. The second category enhances existing recommenders by
109   integrating LLMs while still relying on traditional collaborative filtering methods Wang et al. (b).
110   For instance, LLMRec Wei et al. (b) strengthens the user-item interaction graph through LLM-based
111   graph augmentation, while RLMRec Ren et al. (2023) combines LLM-enhanced text embeddings
112   with GNN-based user/item representations. However, these approaches often lack fine-tuning tailored
113   to specific recommendation tasks, primarily focusing on full-shot scenarios.
114
      In contrast, our work introduces a novel instruction-tuning technique for an open-source LLM,
115   allowing it to adapt to specific recommendation tasks and effectively capture collaborative information
116   for profile generation. While methods like InstructRec Zhang et al. (2023) and TALLRec Bao et al.
117   align LLM capabilities with recommendation tasks, they struggle with scalability due to instruction-
118   question-answering prompts and exhibit poor generalization on sparse data. Our approach enhances
119   the generalization ability of existing recommender systems in the face of data scarcity and noise,
120   while maintaining efficiency in handling large-scale data in practical scenarios.

124   3.1   ID- BASED C OLLABORATIVE F ILTERING
125
126   In the ID-based collaborative filtering (CF) paradigm, the main goal is to optimize the ID embeddings
127   of users and items. This optimization aims to accurately capture and represent user preferences for
128   items, while considering the interaction patterns of users and items that are similar. Formally, we
129   have a set of users denoted as U = {u1 , · · · , uI }, and a set of items denoted as V = {v1 , · · · , vJ }.
130
      Each user and item is assigned initial ID embeddings, represented as xu and xv ∈ Rd respectively.
      The objective is to obtain optimized user and item representations, denoted as eu , ev ∈ Rd , through a
131
      recommender model R(xu , xv ). This model aims to maximize the posterior distribution p(e|X ) ∝
132
      p(X |e)p(e). The predicted likelihood of user-item interaction, denoted as ŷu,v , is derived by
133
      performing a dot product between the user and item representations, as follows: ŷu,v = e⊤   u · ev .
134
135   Although many state-of-the-art recommender systems operating within the ID-based collaborative
136   filtering paradigm have demonstrated remarkable performance, they face significant challenges when
137   it comes to handling item cold-start scenarios, especially in situations where data scarcity is prevalent.
138
      The primary hurdle arises from the lack of past interaction history for these new items, which disrupts
      the optimization paradigm mentioned earlier. As a consequence, ID-based recommenders may
139
      encounter difficulties in generating accurate representations for these new items, leading to a notable
140
      decline in the overall performance of recommender systems, particularly in zero-shot scenarios.

144   To handle cold-start items in zero-shot recommendation, we propose to leverage textual side features
145   for user and item representation learning. Specifically, we propose to replace the aforementioned
146   ID embeddings with the side information associated with items, concretely items’ text descriptions
147   F ∈ R|V|×dt . A multi-layer perceptron Traw is utilized to project the raw textual features f ∈ Rdt into
148   the latent space Rd . The resulting representation f̂ ∈ Rd is then used for initial item representation:

151   This enables items to have meaningful representations with textual semantics that go beyond simple
152   ID embeddings. After using textual features as item representations, the recommender system
153   optimizes the user ID embedding using observed item interactions, capturing user preferences for
154   text-based items and enabling zero-shot predictive capabilities for cold-start items.
155

188   3.3           E NHANCING C OLLABORATIVE F EATURES VIA R ECOMMENDATION I NSTRUCTION T UNING
189
190
      To enhance collaborative features, our RecLM proposes to integrates users’ collaborative relation-
      ships into the aforementioned LLM-based profiling process, through an innovative recommendation
191
      instruction tuning paradigm. This approach improves the generated user profiles by employing knowl-
192
      edge distillation and a dialogue-based instruction tuning method, effectively preserving high-order
193   collaborative similarities between users and items. Once we have successfully generated high-quality
194   user profiles, we can proceed to generate item profiles by leveraging the associated user profiles,
195   ensuring semantic alignment and resulting in enhanced features for both users and items.
196
197   3.3.1               LLM Fine-Tuning via Knowledge Distillation
198
199   For our profiling system, using state-of-the-art LLMs like ChatGPT can be costly and inefficient, with
200   data security concerns. Instead, fine-tuning open-source LLMs is more common, granting flexibility
201
      to align with computational resources and business needs. This allows designing cost-effective and
      efficient batch inference methods while ensuring data security. Here, we utilize llama2-7b-chat as
202
      the base model. To tailor it to our business, we design prompt templates and sample users and items
203
      to construct input prompts for ChatGPT-3.5. After obtaining the inference results, we fine-tune
204   llama2-7b-chat using the input-output prompts. This distills knowledge from the large-scale ChatGPT
205   into the open-source llama2-7b-chat, yielding a fine-tuned LLM Mkd that meets our requirements.
206
207   3.3.2               C OLLABORATIVE I NSTRUCTION T UNING
208
209   Indeed, the reliance solely on historical item information from user interactions may not effectively
210   harness the collaborative relationships among users. Therefore, we have devised a solution to this
211   issue by introducing a two-turn dialogue-based instruction tuning paradigm. This paradigm not only
212
      aids LLMs in generating higher-quality profiles by considering the collaborative relationships among
      users but also tackles the challenge of lacking direct supervision in the profile generation task.
213
214   Profile Generation with Two-turn Dialogue. The challenge of evaluating generated profiles without
215   readily available ground truth hinders the guidance of LLMs in producing high-quality profiles.
      Typically, the quality assessment is indirectly conducted through downstream recommendation tasks

216
      where the profiles are utilized. To tackle this challenge, we have devised a two-turn dialogue-based
217   instruction-tuning paradigm.
218
219   In the first turn, the input query (i.e., Q) encompasses the historical item lists of the target user and
220
      similar users. The output response (i.e., R) is the profiles of users. Our aim in this turn is guiding
      LLMs to consider both the collaborative relationships among users and their historical interactions
221
      when generating the user’s profile. However, the profile generated in the first turn is solely based on
222
      the distilled knowledge of Mkd and lacks sufficient supervision signals for effective guidance.
223
224   To address this, we introduce the second turn where we utilize the user’s historical interaction records
225   as supervision signals to guide the profile generation process. In this second turn, the input Q consists
226
      the target user ut and a target item vt . The output R is a prediction of whether ut will interact
      with vt . This approach bridges the gap between the profile generation task and the recommendation
227
      optimization objective, guiding LLMs to consider the collaborative relationships among users during
228
      profile generation and allows for supervision signals from the recommendation task.
229
230   Instruction Design. In the first turn, LLMs receive the historical item list Vt ∈ V of the target
231   user ut ∈ U and the historical item lists Vn ∈ V of several users un ∈ U with similar preferences.
232
      To identify these similar users {un }, we employ traditional ID-based recommenders to obtain user
      embeddings. By calculating the cosine similarity between user embeddings, we can obtain users who
233
      exhibit comparable preferences. Alongside Vn , the LLM is also provided with the user profiles of
234
      these similar users via Mkd . The output R of LLMs in this turn is the profiles of both ut and {un }.

252   Tuning Strategy. In the process of multi-turn dialogue instruction-tuning, the object is to utilize
253   the R generated by LLMs for weight updates, while excluding the Q from these updates. If simply
254   employing the conventional single-turn dialogue tuning approach on our paradigm, the inputs to the
255   LLM include Qf ir. , Rf ir. , and Qsec. , with only Rsec. being the predicted part. Therefore, only the

257   for multi-turn dialogues. In our designed paradigm, Rf ir. contains valuable textual information in
258   the form of profiles of multiple users. This rich information guides the generation of Rsec. in the
259   subsequent turn. On the other hand, Rsec. is the relatively simple text, usually a binary choice such
260
      as yes or no. If we disregard the information in Rf ir. and solely use Rsec. for fine-tuning LLMs, it
      is evident that we would not be able to achieve the desired effect.
261
262   To address this issue, we have devised a more efficient method for two-turn dialogues tuning.
263   Our approach involves concatenating the data from the two-turn dialogues and utilizing masking
264   techniques to distinguish between Q and R. When updating the weights of LLMs, only the loss from
265   the part marked as R is taken into account for weight updates. By adopting this method, both Rf ir.
266
      and Rsec. in the two-turn dialogue are able to actively contribute to the training process, allowing for
      the full utilization of the dialogue data. This approach is instrumental in guiding LLMs within our
267
      designed paradigm to effectively learn the collaborative relationships among users.

274   3.4   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING - BASED P ERSONALIZED F EATURE E NHANCEMENT
275
      Despite the LLM’s improved ability to infer profiles through the instruction-tuning, there are still
276
      challenges to address. The inconsistency between the prompt instruction during inference (i.e.,
277   generates a specific user’s profile) and the first turn of the dialogue during fine-tuning (i.e., generates
278   multiple users’ profiles) introduces noise into the generated profiles. Additionally, while considering
279   user collaborative relationships enhances performance, it compromises profile precision in terms of
280   personalization, similar to the over smoothing problem in GNN-based CF methods.
281
      To address above challenges, we draw inspiration from the RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from
282
      Human Feedback) technique Stiennon et al., and develop a RL-based fine-tuning paradigm to further
283   enhance the previously tuned LLM. In this approach, we train a reward model to evaluate the quality
284   of the profiles generated by the LLM. Subsequently, we employ the Proximal Policy Optimization
285   (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017) to update the weights of the LLM using the scores provided by the
286   reward model. This iterative process enables us to progressively refine the LLM’s performance,
287   resulting in the generation of more accurate and personalized profiles.
288

336   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
337
      To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct extensive experiments using two
338
      public datasets: MIND1 Wu et al. (2020) and Netflix2 , along with a large-scale dataset derived from
339
      real-world industrial data (referred to as Industrial for anonymity).
340
341   We assess the accuracy of the top-K recommendation results using two widely adopted metrics:
342   Recall@K (R@K) and NDCG (N@K), with K set to 20 by default. To reduce bias, we employ an
343
      all-rank evaluation strategy, where positive items in the test set are ranked alongside all non-interacted
      items for each user. The final metric is reported as the average score across all users in the test set.
344

528   In this work, we introduce RecLM, a groundbreaking model-agnostic recommendation instruction-
529   tuning paradigm that seamlessly integrates large language models (LLMs) with collaborative filtering
530   techniques to significantly enhance user profiling, especially in cold-start scenarios. This innova-
531   tive approach leverages LLMs to generate rich user and item profiles by harnessing collaborative
532
      relationships and textual features, effectively tackling the critical challenges of data sparsity and
      noise. Furthermore, we incorporate a unique reinforcement learning mechanism to refine profile
533
      quality and optimize recommendation outputs, enabling substantial performance gains across diverse
534
      recommender systems. This combination of techniques not only enhances the robustness of the
535   recommendations but also ensures scalability and adaptability in real-world applications.

      This section gives a brief introduction of the selected base models in this work.
681
682   • BiasMF Koren et al. (2009): It is a matrix factorization method that aims to enhance user-specific
683     preferences for recommendation by incorporating bias vectors for users and items.
684
      • NCF He et al. (2017): It is a neural network-based method that replaces the dot-product operation
685     in conventional matrix factorization with multi-layer neural networks. This allows the model to
686     capture complex user-item interactions and provide recommendations. For our comparison, we
687     utilize the NeuMF variant of NCF.
688
689
      • LightGCN He et al. (2020): This model leverages the power of neighborhood information in the
        user-item interaction graph by using a layer-wise propagation scheme that involves only linear
690
        transformations and element-wise additions.
691
692   • SGL Wu et al. (2021): The model enhances LightGCN by integrating contrastive learning with
693     self-supervision. It employs data augmentation strategies, including random walks and node/edge
694     dropout, to corrupt graph structures.
695   • SimGCL Yu et al. (2022): This work introduces a straightforward contrastive learning (CL) method
696     that eliminates graph augmentations. Instead, it adds uniform noise to the embedding space to
697     generate contrastive views.

738   an extensive hyperparameter search, and also explored the optimal approach for incorporating profile
739   features for each recommendation methods, ensuring a fair comparison. Specifically, each base model
740   is implemented with PyTorch, using Adam optimizer and Xavier initializer with default parameters.

742   learning rate is set as 1e − 3. The coefficient for controling L2 regularization term is searched

744
      the temperature coefficient is searched in {0.1, 0.5, 1.0}.
