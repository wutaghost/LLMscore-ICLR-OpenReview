011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurately identifying the site of origin (SoO) of early ventricular activation is
014            crucial for catheter ablation, an effective therapeutic option for treating ventric-
015
               ular arrhythmia. However, due to the limited availability of clinical data and
               the errors introduced during data preprocessing, achieving precise localization
016
               remains a challenge. While deep learning models offer an end-to-end approach
017
               for data input in the ECG field, they often suffer from overfitting caused by lim-
018            ited training data, hindering continuous performance improvement. This paper
019            proposes a Simple data-parameters Balancing framework for early ventricular ac-
020            tivation Origin Localization (SimBOL). By using onset-based data augmentation,
021            the SimBOL method expands the training data derived from clinical samples. The
022            framework utilizes a small-scale 1D convolution model that balances the relation-
023            ship between available training data and model complexity, effectively mitigating
024            overfitting and eliminating the need for extensive data preprocessing. SimBOL
025            achieves a localization error as low as 9.83 mm, which meets clinical acceptable
026
               localization error ¡ 10 mm and outperforming existing methods in predicting the
               SoO of early ventricular activation. The discussion about data augmentation and
027
               model architecture on ECG signal processing, offering new insights into optimiz-
028
               ing deep learning applications for ECG-based tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Catheter ablation has emerged as a useful therapeutic option for treating ventricular arrhythmias
034   Levine et al. (2016), including ventricular tachycardia (VTs) and premature ventricular complexes
035   (PVCs) Sapp et al. (2016); Cronin et al. (2019), both of which are major causes of sudden cardiac
036   arrest (SCA). Accurately identifying the site of origin (SoO) of early ventricular activation is criti-
037   cal for electrophysiologists to focus their mapping and targeting efforts on a specific region during
038   catheter ablation Asirvatham & Stevenson (2016). Currently, pace-mapping is one of the primary
039
      clinical mapping approaches for localizing the SoO Josephson et al. (1982). As illustrated in Figure
      1, this method involves template-matching analysis and a “trial and error” approach, where intracar-
040
      diac pace-mapping ECG morphologies (12-lead pacing ECGs) are compared to those of the targeted
041
      VT/PVC 12-lead ECGs using commercial modules Guenancia et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2020b;
042   2021). Despite their utility, these systems do not provide direct localization information, pace-
043   mapping is time-consuming and heavily dependent on the operator’s expertise Zhou et al. (2023).
044
045
      With the recent advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) in medicine Singhal et al. (2023);
      Lakkaraju et al. (2022); Alowais et al. (2023), several studies Yang et al. (2017); Pereira et al. (2019);
046
      Gyawali et al. (2019); Missel et al. (2020); Nakamura et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Chang et al.
047
      (2022) have explored the use of deep learning methods to identify the SoO of early ventricular acti-
048   vation, offering end-to-end signal processing and automatic extraction of complex patterns in ECG
049   signals. In studies such as Gyawali et al. (2017; 2019); Missel et al. (2020), the real pacing-site data
050   (ECG and corresponding pacing-site coordinates) available is relatively small (in the thousands)
051   compared to the vast datasets used in deep learning (often in the millions or billions). The over-
052   parameterization characteristic of deep learning models means that their effectiveness heavily de-
053   pends on the number of training parameters and the size of the training data Allen-Zhu et al. (2019).
      When the number of training parameters is comparable to or far exceeds the available training data,

078   In this work, we propose a Simple data-parameters Balancing framework for ventricular arrhythmia

080

081

082

083   within specific regions determined by onset points in ECG signals. Unlike previous models Yang
084   et al. (2017); Pereira et al. (2019); Gyawali et al. (2019); Nakamura et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021);
085   Chang et al. (2022); Missel et al. (2020), where the number of model parameters greatly exceeded
086   the available training data, SimBOL provides a small-scale model that maintains a balance between
087   data and parameters. It combines 1D convolution and fully connected layer, exploiting the robust-
088   ness of 1D convolution in extracting features from time-series signals to handle augmented, irregular
089   data effectively during training. The balance significantly reduces the model’s overfitting and sta-
090   bilizes its localization performance. Furthermore, SimBOL predicts site coordinates to calculate
091
      distance loss instead of traditional label prediction loss, reducing the potential biases introduced by
      operations like SoftMax in model training. Experimental results demonstrate that SimBOL achieves
092
      the SoO localization within 10mm, significantly better than the current most accurate method, SVR
093
      Zhou et al. (2019), by over 2mm. The discussion about data augmentation and model architecture
094   on ECG signal processing, offer- ing new insights into optimizing deep learning applications for
095   ECG-based tasks. In summary, the contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
096
097          • This work introduces Onset-based data augmentation, a method that leverages physical
098            characteristics of ECG signal. By resampling specific regions of the ECG data, this method
099            effectively increases the amount of training data for deep learning models, providing critical
100            data support for their application in the ECG field.
101          • This work proposes a data feature extraction model specifically designed for ECG signals.
102            By appropriately stacking 1D convolution and fully connected layers, the model effectively
103            extracts features from the augmented, irregular ECG signals. Its small scale offers a new
104            direction to mitigating overfitting during model training in the ECG field.
105          • The SimBOL training framework achieves a coordinate prediction distance error of less
106            than 10mm, significantly improving upon the current best localization method, which has
107            an error of 12mm. Its minimal requirements for input data processing not only increase
               accuracy but also enhance convenience and efficiency in clinical settings.

      2.1   E ARLY V ENTRICULAR ACTIVATION O RIGIN L OCALIZATION
112
      Using 12-lead ECGs data to guide clinicians in locating the SoO of early ventricular activation
113
      has long been a focus of clinical and biomedical research. In 2017, Sapp et al. (2017) proposed
114   subdividing the 16 segments of a generic left ventricle (LV) into 238 triangles. They collected a
115   pacing-site database that included pacing 12-lead ECGs and corresponding pacing locations. By
116   applying a multiple linear regression (MLR) method Zhou et al. (2019), they derived coefficients
117   linking the pacing ECGs to their corresponding pacing locations. These coefficients were then
118   used to predict the SoO of early LV activation when having a pacing ECG or VT/PVC ECG. In
119   this approach, the ECG for each lead was represented by a single value based on the 120 ms QRS
120   integral. However, this method is highly sensitive to the selection of the QRS onset and by focusing
121   only on the integral of the first 120 ms of the QRS complex, it may provide a limited understanding
122
      of ventricular contraction, without fully capturing the dynamics of ventricular relaxation.
123   Due to AI’s powerful data analysis and feature extraction capabilities, some studies Lai et al. (2021);
124   Haq et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023) are exploring the use of deep learning (DL) to process clinically
125   relevant but “hidden” patterns in ECG signals, thereby assisting in identifying the SoO of early ven-
126   tricular activation. Yang et al. (2017) employed a comprehensive DL approach using two CNNs
127   (Segment CNN and Epi-Endo CNN) to localize PVC origin onto 25 specific ventricular segments
128
      (17 LV segments, 8 RV segments) and distinguish between endocardial and epicardial activation
      origins. Pereira et al. (2019) employed a shallow neural network for PVC detection, focusing on
129
      distinguishing between left and right ventricles. Tested on three datasets with 328 patients, the
130
      network demonstrated high specificity but relatively low sensitivity. Gyawali et al. (2019); Missel
131   et al. (2020) used a generation task with GRU models, followed by fine-tuning for localization. The
132   model achieved a mean localization accuracy of 12.84 mm within ten segments of a generic LV en-
133   docardium. Li et al. (2021) developed an optimized ResNet-18 network for localizing PVC origins.
134   Chang et al. (2022) developed a DL model with two sets of CNN layers for predicting the Ventric-
135   ular Arrhythmia origin. However, the performance of these models is significantly constrained by
136   overfitting due to the limited availability of clinical data and the over-parameterized of deep models.

      2.2   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN ECG AND S PEECH SIGNALS
139
140   Many speech-related tasks Dureja & Gautam (2015); Wali et al. (2022) use the spectral information
141   of speech signals as input for model training. This is mainly because the content of human speech
142   is primarily conveyed through frequency information, while amplitude and timbre only represent
143   the loudness and quality of the sound Fitch (2000). In contrast, ECG data, being electrical signals,
144   presents unique challenges; its amplitude, as a crucial component that carries important information
145   about the strength of heart contractions Malmivuo & Plonsey (1995). Additionally, the frequency
146   in ECG data reflects the distinct beating patterns of the heart’s chambers. Furthermore, since heart
147
      signals are influenced by the body when transmitted to the body surface, 12-lead ECG data often
      suffers from significant noise interference Clifford et al. (2006), making it more difficult to capture
148
      heart rate frequency information accurately. As a result, directly applying speech-domain models to
149
      ECG data processing is not ineffective.

207   4.1      O NSET- BASED DATA AUGMENTATION
208
      In the preprocessing of pacing ECG data, not all pacing beats are clinically usable due to factors such
209
      as motion artifacts, ectopic beats (highlighted by the red dashed box in Figure 4), and non-captured
210   beats where the stimulus-QRS delay exceeds 40 ms. Previous studies, such as Zhou et al. (2020a),
211   used a single beat per pacing site and represented each lead’s ECG using a 120 ms QRS integral
212   to identify the SoO of early LV activation. While this approach showed strong localization ability
213   Zhou et al. (2020a), it may miss important information beyond the initial 120ms of the QRS com-
214   plex, limiting usable training data and introducing subjectivity in determining the optimal 120-ms
215   QRS interval. To overcome these limitations, SimBOL produced Onset-based Data Augmentation
      strategy. This strategy aims to increase the availability of training data by generating additional

      samples and simplifies the selection of QRS onset regions. By enhancing the dataset size and reduc-
217   ing the complexity of data preprocessing, SimBOL improves the robustness of ECG analysis and
218   facilitates more accurate clinical assessments.

237   Assuming the sampling rate of a sample is F , the duration of the QT interval is less than ∆, and
238   time interval between two spikes is Γ, the actual number of sample points P within a QT interval
239   is P = F × ∆ and the actual number of sample points L between two spikes is L = F × Γ. To
240   determine the optimal onset time, which corresponds to point t, we define the interval β = [ t- P2 ,
241   t ] as the range for selecting the resampling start point. SimBOL augments the training dataset by
242
      randomly choosing the start point τ from this β interval and then sampling L points from τ , forming
      a single training data sample. As shown in Figure 4, since L > P , each sample will include a
243
      portion or the entirety of the QT interval. Any portion of P that is not captured within the sample is
244
      filled by adjacent QT interval data. By performing N resamplings at each onset point, SimBOL×N
245   generates a training dataset that is N -fold larger than the original training dataset.

260   By leveraging the physical characteristics of ECG signals, we developed a compact training model
261
      that combines 1D convolution with activation functions. This model has a limited number of pa-
      rameters, helping to balance the scale of training data and model complexity, while effectively
262
      maintaining the ability to extract features from ECG signals. The detailed model architecture is
263
      illustrated in Figure 5. Based on actual clinical ECGs, SimBOL sets the input length of the resam-
264   pled signals, denoted as L (detailed in Section 4.1), to 800. Consequently, the model first employs
265   a 7 × 7 1D convolution to extract coarse-grained features from the data, followed by a 3 × 3 1D
266   convolution to capture finer-grained features. The most significant single-sample temporal features
267   within each sample are then extracted using activation functions and pooling layers. To ensure that
268   the model captures both the temporal features of the ECG signals and the relationships among the
269   12 leads, SimBOL transposes the obtained data representation and then applies a 3 × 3 1D con-
      volution to extract the sequential relationships between the leads. The final representation, F , is a

284   5.1     E XPERIMENT DATASETS
285
286   In this study, we utilized a pacing-site dataset containing 1,012 LV pacing sites, as referenced in
287   Sapp et al. (2017). Each pacing site includes paired data: electrocardiograms (ECGs) and their
288   corresponding pacing locations. The ECG recordings consist of approximately 15 seconds of con-
289   tinuous 12-lead pacing signals, captured at a sampling rate of 1,000 Hz during routine pace-mapping
290
      procedures on the LV endocardium. The pacing locations were mapped onto the generic LV com-
      posed of 238 triangular segments. Each site is defined by the (x, y, z) coordinates of the center of
291
      one of these triangles. Notably, the 1,012 pacing sites do not cover all 238 triangle labels in the
292
      model. On average, each triangle is associated with fewer than 10 clinical samples, and some tri-
293   angles have as few as one or even no samples. Specifically, 25 triangle labels have no associated
294   clinical samples, and 27 labels have only one corresponding clinical sample. Details of the dataset
295   are provided in the Appendix A.1.

      5.2     E XPERIMENTS S ETTING
298
299   5.2.1    DATASET D IVISION S TRATEGY
300
301   To ensure a reasonable division between the training and test sets, for triangle labels with more
302
      than two samples, we randomly selected 20% of the samples (rounded up) as the test set, with the
      remaining 80% used as the training set. For triangle labels with only two samples, we randomly
303
      selected one as the training sample and the other as the test sample. For triangle labels with only
304
      one sample, to increase the amount of training data, we included these samples exclusively in the
305   training dataset. This approach resulted in 231 test samples and 781 training samples. SimBOL
306   resamples each training sample using onset-based data augmentation.
307
308   5.2.2    DATA AUGMENTATION WITH S IM BOL
309
310   SimBOL applies onset-based data augmentation by resampling each training sample multiple times.
311   The number of resample N for each training sample is determined by a resampling rate, ×N . Ad-
312   ditionally, SimBOL resampled each test sample to enhance the generalization capability of the test
313   set. In all experiments, we fixed the resamples rate for the test set at ×10, resulting in 2,3,10 test
314
      samples for evaluation. We did not apply onset-based data augmentation to the original 1,012 clini-
      cal samples before splitting them into training and test sets. Doing so would have made the domains
315
      of the training and test sets identical, which would prevent the test results from accurately reflecting
316
      the model’s generalization and potential overfitting.

      This paper presents a Simple data-parameters Balancing framework for ventricular arrhythmia
      Origin Localization (SimBOL) in ECG filed. The proposed onset-based data augmentation method
532
      offers a new strategy for expanding training data in the ECG domain for deep learning. The pro-
533
      duced small-scale model, built using stacked 1D convolutions, ensures effective feature extraction
534   from ECG data while reducing the preprocessing demands of the signals. By balancing training data
535   and model parameters, SimBOL achieves a localization accuracy of under 10 mm on the unified test
536   set, which meets clinically-accepted accuracy and improved accuracy by 2 mm compared to the cur-
537   rent best method, SVR. The experimental discussion demonstrates the performance stability of the
538   SimBOL model and emphasizes the importance of balancing training data and model parameters.
539   The discussion about data augmentation and model architecture on ECG signal processing, offer-
      ing new insights into optimizing deep learning applications for ECG-based tasks.

872     input: 12-lead ECG signals X, noise ranges R.

        end for
        return X

884     return X
