009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural scaling laws highlight the trade-off between test error reduction and in-
012            creased resources in machine learning, revealing diminishing returns as data vol-
013            ume, model size, and computational power increase. This inefficiency poses
014            sustainability challenges, as marginal performance gains necessitate exponential
015            resource consumption. Recent works have investigated these laws from a data-
016            efficient standpoint, primarily concentrating on sample optimization, while largely
017            neglecting the influence of target. In this study, we first demonstrate that, given an
018
               equivalent training budget, employing soft targets on a 10% subset can outperform
               the use of one-hot targets on the full dataset. Building on this observation, we
019
               review existing paradigms in the sample-target relationship, categorizing them into
020
               distinct sample-to-target mapping strategies. Subsequently, we propose a unified
021            loss framework to assess their impact on training efficiency. Finally, we conduct a
022            comprehensive analysis of how variations in target and sample types, quantities,
023            and qualities influence training efficiency across three training strategies, providing
024            six key insights to enhance training efficacy.

085   2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087   To assess the impact of data—consisting of samples X and targets Y —on the learning, we revisit
088   three distinct paradigms in deep learning and examine their respective data structures.
089   Conventional representation learning. Representation learning (Zhang et al., 2018a) enables
090   models to automatically discover and extract meaningful features from raw data, facilitating tasks
091   such as classification (Li et al., 2022), clustering (Ren et al., 2024), and segmentation (Minaee et al.,
092   2021). These tasks typically rely on datasets with explicit sample-target pairs, such as ImageNet-
093   1K (Deng et al., 2009). Recent studies (Radford et al., 2021; Hafner et al., 2021) have extended this
094   framework by utilizing text-image pairs to train robust representation models. In these approaches,
095   text is implicitly used as a target through contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020b; Khosla et al., 2020).
096
      Moreover, self-supervised learning methods (Grill et al., 2020; Zbontar et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021;
      Caron et al., 2021) have been developed to extract representations from unlabeled data by generating
097
      supervision signals through mechanisms like momentum models.
098
099   Summary. Current representation learning techniques construct learning targets Y from samples X,
100   explicitly or implicitly, to guide models in learning the nonlinear mapping between them.
101
      Soft label learning via knowledge distillation. Knowledge distillation, introduced by Hinton
102   et al. (2015), is a prominent technique for model compression in which a smaller student model
103   learns from the outputs, or soft labels, of a larger teacher model. This approach effectively trans-
104   fers knowledge with minimal performance degradation, enabling smaller, efficient models while
105   maintaining capability (Gou et al., 2021). Subsequent research has expanded its application to
106   enhance model robustness (Xu et al., 2020), facilitate domain adaptation (He et al., 2020b), and
107   support semi-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020a). Moreover, Yim et al. (2017) demonstrates that
      knowledge distillation can accelerate the optimization process during training.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Summary. Knowledge distillation generates unique soft targets Y for each sample via a pre-trained
109   teacher model, embedding rich information that enables the student model to closely mimic the
110   behavior of the teacher.
111
112   Data-efficient learning through condensed data. Traditional dataset distillation aims to replicate
113   the original dataset’s behavior within a distilled dataset by minimizing discrepancies between surro-
114
      gate neural network models trained on both synthetic and original data. Key metrics in this process
      include matching gradients (Zhao et al., 2020), features (Wang et al., 2022), distributions (Zhao &
115
      Bilen, 2023), and training trajectories (Cui et al., 2022). However, these methods incur substantial
116
      computational overhead due to the ongoing calculation of discrepancies, requiring numerous iter-
117   ations for optimization and convergence. This makes them challenging to scale to large datasets,
118   such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). An effective strategy involves identifying metrics that capture
119   critical dataset information (Yin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024b), thereby eliminating the need for
120   exhaustive comparisons between original and distilled datasets. Consequently, these methods scale
121   efficiently to large datasets, such as ImageNet-1K. For instance, SRe2 L (Yin et al., 2023) compacts
122   the entire dataset into a model, such as pre-trained neural networks like ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016),
123   and subsequently extracts knowledge into images and targets, forming a distilled dataset. Recently,
124   RDED (Sun et al., 2024b) suggests that images accurately recognized by strong observers, such as
125
      humans and pre-trained models, are pivotal for effective learning.
126   Summary. Dataset distillation methods extract key information from the original dataset, forming
127   more informative samples X and targets Y that facilitate data-efficient learning.

131   This section begins by formally defining three mapping strategies between samples X and targets Y .
132   Subsequently, we critically analyze the limitations of existing loss functions in evaluating the impact
133   of these mapping strategies on the representational capacity of models. We then introduce a novel
134   unified loss function.
135   3.1   D EFINITION OF M APPING S TRATEGIES
136
137   Previous studies have demonstrated that data-efficient techniques, such as knowledge distillation
138   and dataset distillation, which reconstruct the relationship between samples X and targets Y , can
139
      significantly accelerate model training. For instance, knowledge distillation leverages soft targets
      generated by teacher models to expedite the training process (Yim et al., 2017). To thoroughly
140
      examine the relationship between samples X and targets Y , we define mapping strategy, denoted as
141
      ψ : X → Y, where X represents the data space and Y the target space. Importantly, augmentations
142   are crucial in deep learning, significantly boosting training effectiveness (Zhang et al., 2018b; Geiping
143   et al., 2022). Consequently, this paper emphasizes the use of augmented samples.
144
145   S TRATEGY A: Mapping multiple augmented samples within the same class into a one-hot target.
146   In conventional representation learning, data pairs (X, Y ) represent a non-injective mapping, denoted
147   as S TRATEGY A, as shown in Figure 2a . For example, for a classification task, one class c may
148
      include multiple samples Xc , indicating that multiple samples are mapped to a same class. The
                                                (j)
149   mapping strategy is formalized as ψ A (xi ) = c, where j denotes the j-th augmented sample of the
150   original sample xi .
151   S TRATEGY B: Mapping each augmented sample within the same class into a unique soft target.
152   For efficient knowledge distillation and dataset distillation, these approaches reconstruct the sample-
153   to-target relationship using more informative soft targets. Each augmented sample corresponds
154   to a unique soft target generated by a pre-trained model, establishing an injective mapping from
155   augmented samples to soft labels, as shown in Figure 2b . Specifically, the mapping strategy is

      formalized as: ψ B (xi ) = yi , where yi represents the soft target generated by a pre-trained

158

190   3.2     D ESIGN A U NIFIED L OSS F UNCTION
191
192   Having defined three distinct sample-to-target mapping strategies, we subsequently investigate their
193   impact on the representational capacity of models. Following previous benchmarks and research (He
194
      et al., 2020a; Chen et al., 2020b; Grill et al., 2020), the representation ability of a trained model can
      be evaluated using an offline linear probing strategy, which involves decoupling the model into a
195
      backbone and a classifier. However, two inherent challenges emerge when employing conventional
196
      loss functions: (1) They inherently integrate these mapping strategies during training, complicating
197   the evaluation of individual strategies. (2) They obscure the backbone’s representational ability due
198   to classifier influence.
199
200
      Therefore, in this subsection, we first review conventional loss functions and analysis and their
      limitations. Subsequently, we propose a novel unified loss function to mitigate these challenges.
201
202
203
      3.2.1    C ONVENTIONAL L OSS F UNCTIONS
204   Cross-entropy (CE) loss is widely recognized as a standard objective for training deep neural networks
205   on classification tasks. However, its inability to exploit information from soft targets, which limits
206   its application in mappings S TRATEGY B and S TRATEGY C. To use soft targets, Yang et al. (2022)
207   introduces the Distillation Loss paradigm, which augments the CE loss with a soft-target matching
208   component, thereby integrating soft label information into the model.
209
      Despite this, the original CE loss continues to propagate one-hot target information to the model’s
210   backbone, implicitly introducing the multiple-to-one mapping to S TRATEGY A. This characteristic
211   complicates the independent analysis of each mapping strategy with respect to representation ability,
212   which is unsuitable for our objectives. Furthermore, most knowledge distillation approaches (Gou
213   et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) treat the backbone and classifier as a unified entity
214   during training. This coupling potentially obscures the backbone’s feature extraction capabilities, as
215   they may be inevitability affected by the classifier’s performance. In summary, evaluating the impact
      of mapping strategies on representation ability of backbone remains challenging.

      3.2.2    S EPARATION L OSS F UNCTION D ESIGN
217
218   To address the challenges previously outlined, we propose a novel loss function designed to
219   evaluate the influence of various strategies on representation ability. Specifically, we employ the
220   Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence to leverage the information embedded in soft targets during
221   the training of the backbone. For the classifier, we decouple it from the entire model and train it
222   independently using cross-entropy (CE) loss. This approach prevents the transmission of one-hot
223
      target information from the classifier to the backbone, thus maintaining the separation between
      these mapping strategies. Furthermore, this decoupled training strategy ensures that the classifier
224
      performance does not adversely affect the representational capacity of backbone.

      We define the data augmentation techniques mentioned throughout the paper as follows:
744
745   No Augmentation (NoAug): Only normalization is applied based on the corresponding dataset.
746
      Standard Augmentation (StdAug):
747
748          • ColorJitter (or Color in short): Apply random changes to the brightness, contrast, satura-
749            tion with a probability of 0.8 to each image.
750
             • RandomGrayscale: Converts the image to grayscale with a 0.2 probability.
751
752          • RandomHorizontalFlip: Flips the image horizontally with a 50% chance.
753          • RandomResizedCrop (or Crop in short): Resizes and crops the image to the input size
754            with a scaling factor between 0.08 and 1.0.
755
      Mix-based Augmentation (not include StdAug in our settings):


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
             • MixUp: Perform a linear interpolation between two randomly selected images, where the
757            interpolation factor, λ, is drawn from a Beta distribution with parameters (α, β) = (0.8, 0.8)
758            which controls the degree of interpolation between the two images.
759
760          • CutMix: A bounding box is randomly generated within one image, and the pixel values
761            inside the box are replaced by the corresponding region from a second randomly selected
762            image. The area of the bounding box is determined by a parameter sampled from a Beta
763            distribution with parameters (α, β) = (1.0, 1.0) which controls the relative size of the cutout
764            region and the pasted-in region.
765
766   For all experiments except in Section 4.4 , Section 5.2 and Tabel 3, 8, Figure 9, 16, 18, we employed
767   the Standard Augmentation (StdAug) and normalization for both teacher models and student models.
768   For the experiments in Figure 5a, 16, and Table 8, we applied StdAug to the student model. For the
769   experiments in Figure 7 and 18, StdAug was applied to the teacher model.
