009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            LLM-as-a-judge models have been used for evaluating both human and AI gener-
012            ated content, specifically by providing scores and rationales. Rationales, in addition
013            to increasing transparency, help models learn to calibrate its judgments. Enhancing
014            a model’s rationale can therefore improve its calibration abilities and ultimately
015            the ability to score content. We introduce Self-Rationalization, an iterative process
016            of improving the rationales for the judge models, which consequently improves
017            the score for fine-grained customizable scoring criteria (i.e., likert-scale scoring
018
               with arbitrary evaluation criteria). Self-rationalization works by having the model
               generate multiple judgments with rationales for the same input, curating a prefer-
019
               ence pair dataset from its own judgements, and iteratively fine-tuning the judge via
020
               DPO. Intuitively, this approach allows the judge model to self-improve by learning
021            from its own rationales, leading to better alignment and evaluation accuracy. After
022            just two iterations – while only relying on examples in the training set – human
023            evaluation shows that our judge model learns to produce higher quality rationales,
024            with a win rate of 62% on average compared to models just trained via SFT on
025            rationale . This judge model also achieves high scoring accuracy on BigGen Bench
026            and Reward Bench, outperforming even bigger sized models trained using SFT
027            with rationale, self-consistency or best-of-N sampling by 3% to 9%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities in natural language understanding
033
      and generation (Radford et al., 2019). However, aligning these models with human preferences,
      values and reasoning has posed significant challenges (Amodei et al., 2016). Consequently, two key
034
      approaches have emerged as powerful solutions to address these challenges - Reinforcement Learning
035
      from Human Feedback (Christiano et al., 2023), known as RLHF, and its more scalable extension
036   Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (Bai et al., 2022), known as RLAIF. Both approaches
037   represent a significant shift in how LLMs are trained, focusing on feedback-driven learning to align
038   models more closely with human preferences.
039
      At the core of RLHF is the concept of learning through interaction with human evaluators who provide
040
      feedback on model generated content by ranking or scoring outputs based on quality, correctness
041
      or alignment with desired outputs. This feedback allows LLMs to learn more directly from human
042   values, making them more aligned with real-world expectations. However, relying exclusively on
043   human feedback can be resource-intensive and difficult to scale. To overcome this, RLAIF introduces
044   a new paradigm where AI systems provide feedback instead. In this setting, LLMs can act as
045   evaluators of their own or other model generated content. This method leverages the power of LLMs
046   to perform the role of Judges, an LLM-as-a-Judge (Vu et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023) which provide
047   judgements on content quality, coherence and alignment. It has become a core component in RLAIF,
048   where LLMs are tasked with evaluating AI generated content providing not only scores but also
049   detailed rationales that explain their decisions.
050   Rationales are critical as they offer insight into the model’s reasoning process, helping both the
051   model developers as well the model itself to assess the quality of judgements. Moreover, rationales
052   are more than just explanations; they are learning mechanism for the model itself. By generating
053   and self-reflecting on rationales —what we term “Self-Rationalization” – LLMs can improve their
      scoring abilities. The iterative process of Self-rationalization, leads to better aligned judgements and

083   To harness this potential, we introduce Self-Rationalizing Evaluators (SRE) - a new approach of
084   improving LLM-as-Judges through iterative preference optimization focusing on enhancing generated
085   rationales. In other words, the model generates multiple judgements with accompanying rationales

087

      tion (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) which enables it to self-improve both its rationale generation and
088

089
      and other existing training methods.

091   Finally, through experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the SRE approach. In just two iter-
092   ations of self-rationalization–relying only on examples from the training data, our model significantly
093
      improves both its rationale quality and its scoring accuracy. When evaluated against models trained
      via supervised fine-tuning (SFT), our SRE model consistently outperforms them in terms of rationale
094
      coherence and scoring accuracy.
095
096   Furthermore, our experiments show that Self-Rationalizing Evaluators outperform similar sized

100
      achieves better judging results. Additionally, human evaluations provide strong evidence that self-
101
      rationalization improves the quality of rationales.

104   2     BACKGROUND
105
106   We consider a general LLM-as-judge (Vu et al., 2024). In particular, we consider training such
107   an LLM-as-judge on a multi-task setting comprising of point-wise and pairwise assessments. This
      design is inspired by empirical evidence that linearly merging these assessment formats results in


                                                                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      superior evaluator performance by leveraging their complementary strengths (Kim et al., 2024c).
109   Below, we formalize the two assessment types:
110
111          • Pointwise Assessment: In a pointwise assessment, the judge evaluates a single response
112            given a context. Formally, the judge model J can be represented as a function:
113                                               J(c, a, e, ipoint ; θ) → (s, r)
114
               where c is the conversation context, a ∈ A is the response, e ∈ E is the scoring criterion
115            (e.g., safety, factuality, helpfulness), ipoint is the input instruction for the pointwise task, θ
116            represents the model parameters, s ∈ S is the score (on a Likert scale, from 1 to 5), and
117            r ∈ R is the rationale explaining the reasoning behind the score.
118          • Pairwise Assessment: In a pairwise assessment, the judge evaluates two responses and se-
119            lects the better one. Formally, the judge model J for pairwise assessment can be represented
120            as a function:
121                                             J(c, a1 , a2 , e, ipair ; θ) → (p, r)
122            where c is the conversation context, a1 , a2 ∈ A are the two responses being compared, e ∈ E
123            is the scoring criterion for choosing the better response(e.g., safety, factuality, helpfulness),
124            ipair is the input instruction for the pairwise task, θ represents the model parameters,
125            p ∈ {1, 2} is the index of the preferred response, and r ∈ R is the rationale explaining the
126            reasoning behind the preference.
127
      Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) approaches for training LLM-as-judge models have inherent limitations.
128
      SFT typically exposes the model to positive examples—teaching it to generate “correct” responses
129   or judgments—but it does not explicitly show the model what constitutes an incorrect response.
130   As a result, models trained solely with SFT may struggle with generalization, especially when
131   they encounter ambiguous or edge-case inputs where multiple interpretations exist, or where the
132   “correctness” of a response may not be binary. Wang et al. (2023) introduced Self-Consistency,
133   an approach that explores multiple reasoning paths to arrive at a final judgment. By aggregating
134   responses across various paths, we can achieve more robust and reliable outputs. Another useful
135   strategy is the Best-of-N approach, where we sample multiple outputs (N) from the base SFT JSFT
136   model, select the most appropriate responses, and use them for further fine-tuning. By doing so, we
137
      expose the model to better judgements that can theoretically mitigate the limitations of relying on a
      single response.
138
139   While both Self-Consistency and Best-of-N help improve the diversity and robustness of model
140   outputs by considering multiple responses, they share a critical shortcoming with SFT: they focus
141   primarily on identifying the best or correct outputs and do not address how models should learn
142   from negative or incorrect responses. To address this, we follow the SFT step with Direct Preference
143
      Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). In this setting, we train the model on pairs of judgments,
      where one is preferred over the other, providing more diverse learning signals. For each input {c, a, e}
144
      the model is presented with two contrasting judgments: a superior well-reasoned judgement and an
145
      inferior judgement.

148   3    T RAINING S ELF -R ATIONALIZING E VALUATORS
149
150   We propose a new training recipe to enhance the performance of LLM-as-Judge through an iterative
151
      alignment process using synthetic data generated via self-rationalization. Our iterative approach
      consists of several stages: the creation of base judge model, the utilization of generated rationales
152
      by model to refine its judgement capabilities (self-rationalization), the selection of preference data
153
      through different curation methods and finally performing alignment via Direct Preference Optimiza-
154   tion (DPO). This iterative process is depicted in Figure 1 consists of:
155
156         1. Seed Initialization: We begin with a base supervised fine-tuned judge model JSFT , trained
157            on an initial labeled dataset (X, Y ) using supervised learning. This serves as the starting
158            point for our iterative improvement process.
159         2. Self-Rationalization: Given an input xi ∈ X (e.g., a conversation context and response to
160            evaluate), we generate N judgments from the current model Jt , each comprising a score
161            si and rationale ri . This step allows the model to produce diverse evaluations of the same
               input.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
            3. Preference Data Curation: The N generated judgments undergo a selection process to
163            create preference pairs (yic , yir ). The chosen output yic represents a higher quality judgment,
164            while yir is the rejected, lower quality judgment. This step is crucial for identifying the most
165            promising rationales and scores.
166
            4. Preference Optimization: Finally, the model is fine-tuned on these preference pairs using
167
               Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), resulting in an improved
168
               judge model Jt+1 .
169
170
      This process is repeated iteratively, starting with J1 , producing J2 , and continuing through each
171   iteration t. We refer the final model (J2 ) also as JSRE . Each cycle aims to refine the model’s ability to
172   generate high-quality rationales and accurate scores. In the following subsections, we will describe
173   this process in detail.
174
175
      3.1   BASE J UDGE C REATION
176
177   Our approach assumes access to a pre-trained seed model with instruction-following capabilities and
178   a labeled training dataset. The input data X consists of conversation context and a response (C, A),
179   while the output includes both S and rationale R. Based on the findings of Kim et al. (2024a), we aim
180   to train a base judge that can perform pointwise (e.g., Likert-scale ratings) and pairwise evaluations.
181   Accordingly, we assume that the labeled dataset consists of both pairwise and pointwise data. With
182   this, as a first step, we fine-tune a base judge model through supervised fine tuning (SFT) and the
183
      resulting model is subject to further calibration in the downstream steps.
184
185   3.2   S ELF -R ATIONALIZATION
186
187   To enable the model to learn from its own reasoning process, we generate multiple judgments with the
188
      tth iteration of the judge Model i.e. Jt for the same input xi . Each judgment jk = (rk , sk ) comprises
      a rationale, followed by a score that is conditioned on the provided rationale. We refer this process
189
      as Self-rationalizing, encourages the model to refine its own decision-making by linking reasoning
190
      with the the judgment score. Notably, the model first generates the rationale, followed by the score,
191   ensuring that the score is conditioned on the rationale. We hypothesize that as the quality of the
192   rationales improves, the accuracy of the scores will also improve.
193
194
      On each iteration we sample p% of the seed dataset, perform self-rationalization for each input N
      times to get multiple judgements [Ψ] = {j1 , . . . , jN }, which will then be subject to preference data
195
      selection, thus refining the reasoning ability in each iteration.
196
197
198   3.3   P REFERENCE DATA C URATION
199
      At each iteration, judgements [Ψ] generated in Self-Rationalization is used to construct Preference
200
      Pairs (jm , jn ) where jm and jn are chosen and rejected judgements respectively. We apply several
201
      methods to guide the creation of these pairs, allowing flexibility based on task-specific objectives and
202   data characteristics.
203
204
      Correct-Answer Preference Pairing In this method, once the judgements with rationales and
205
      scores for the inputs are obtained, preference pairs are constructed by designating a judgement with a
206   score that matches the ground truth as the chosen judgement, while one of the other judgements as
207   the rejected judgement. Additionally, to facilitate the model’s learning and enable it to effectively
208   contrast correct and incorrect pairs, further filtering can be done based on the margin i.e. difference
209   between the scores of chosen and rejected score.
210
211   Meta-Judge In this method, we employ a meta-judge Wu et al. (2024) to evaluate all possible
212   pairs based the quality of the judgments. The criteria for assessment for the LLM-as-Meta-Judge are
213   the correctness of the score and also the quality of rationales . On the basis of the score from the
214   meta-judge, all possible preference pairs are constructed wherein the chosen judgement jm is ranked
215   higher than rejected judgement jn by the meta-judge, to create the (jm , jn ) pairs from the judgment
      pool [Ψ].

216
      Majority-voting/Self-consistency During the self-rationalization phase, we analyze the score
217   distribution generated by multiple judgements [Ψ], for each input xi . The majority score within this
218   distribution is designated as the chosen judgment, while the scores that do not constitute the majority
219   are classified as rejected pairs.
220

      3.4    I TERATIVE R ATIONALIZING P REFERENCE O PTIMISATION
223
224
      For each iteration, we sample p% of the training dataset, generate synthetic preference pairs from
225
      the selected subset and apply DPO to obtain the tth iteration of the judge model. In summary, our
226
      proposed methodology begins with performing SFT on a seed pre-trained model using labeled data
227   Dseed to obtain JSFT . We then apply DPO for T iterations, enhancing the judgment capabilities of
228   model at each iteration.
229
230       Base : Fine-tune the seed pre-trained model on Dseed using SFT to get JSFT .
231       Iter 1 : Initialize with JSFT , create synthetic preference-data D1 using p1 % of Dseed and perform
232              DPO to obtain J1 .
233
          Iter 2 : Initialize with J1 , create synthetic preference-data D2 using p2 % of Dseed and apply DPO
234              to obtain J2 termed as JSRE .

295   4.3   BASELINES
296
297   We compare our model against several popular open-source general-purpose judge models trained for
298   various evaluation tasks. Our comparisons include models of comparable sizes such as Prometheus2-
299   7B (Kim et al., 2024c) , Auto-J 13B (Li et al., 2023) as well as larger models like the MoE Prometheus-
300
      2 8x7B (Kim et al., 2024c) and Prometheus-2-BGB 8x7B (Kim et al., 2024b). Notably, all variants
      of of Prometheus and Auto-J 13B were specifically trained for performing fine-grained custom
301
      evaluation. We do not include pairwise judge models such as Skywork-Critic-Llama-3.1-8B in
302
      the comparison, as it was only trained for preference selection and are not equipped for the more
303   challenging task of fine-grained pointwise evaluation.
304
305   Additionally, we also perform comparisons with extensions of SFT methods. One of the these
306
      methods is Self-Consistency (Majority Voting) where we perform inference from the SFT model
      JSFT with N = 5 and select the most consistent answer as the final output. Another baseline method
307
      is Best-of-N or rejection sampling which involves generating N generations and select the one that
308
      scores high according to a reward model and perform SFT on those generations. In our case, we
309   simply use the ground truth score to guide this selection.

      6       R ELATED W ORK
461
462
463   Rationales: In the context of language models, rationales can refer to chain of thought reasoning
464   (Kojima et al., 2023) or simply natural language feedback for a model’s output (Wang et al., 2024b).
465
      The former refers to a sequence of logically dependent arguments that reach a conclusion (for
      instance, a mathematical proof), whereas the latter involves an analysis which could involve logically
466
      independent arguments (for example, explaining why a movie received a bad review). Rationales
467
      have been explored in various flavors: generated by humans (Zaidan et al., 2007; Ross et al., 2017),
468   or AI models (Kojima et al., 2023); introduced during inference (Wang et al., 2023), or in the prompt
469   during training (Rajani et al., 2019).
470
471
      In the context of an LLM-as-a-judge, a chain of thought rationale “improves data efficiency, ac-
      celerates convergence to higher-performing models, and reduces verbosity bias and hallucination”
472
      (Just et al., 2024). The same authors find that enriching preexisting datasets with machine generated
473
      rationales is effective for training. According to Kim et al. (2024a), fine-tuning on rationales can
474   improve capabilities for evaluation. To the best of our knowledge no research has explored using
475   DPO to specifically and automatically enhance the quality of rationales as a means to improve the
476   scores of a judge. This gap provides an opportunity to explore methods for improving rationale
477   quality, and potentially the scoring capabilities of a judge.
478
      Recent approaches in classical reward modeling have also shown that leveraging rationales improves
479
      reasoning leading to better evaluation accuracy. Critique-out RMs (Ankner et al., 2024) demonstrate
480   that generating rationales before the scalar reward enhances the model’s capabilities. However, these
481   models are constrained by their reliance on predefined evaluation task(s), lacking the flexibility to
482   adapt to custom metrics and the ability to do fine-grained Likert scale evaluation. Generative verifiers
483   (Zhang et al., 2024), on the other hand, outperform LLM-as-a-judge approaches in algorithmic and
484   mathematical tasks, leveraging pure chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning rather than critique-based
485   evaluation. Despite this advantage, their demonstrated effectiveness is limited to these specific
      domains.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      LLM-as-a-judge and reward models: LLM-as-a-judge is now a common approach within the
487   industry to evaluate language models (Fernandes et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Saha et al., 2024; Li
488   et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024). It involves using an LLM to provide feedback on content, performance,
489   or responses from human users or other AI models, in the form of a score (reward model) and
490   optionally a rationale. These models can be used to align other models through RLAIF which has
491   been shown to be both less expensive and time-consuming compared to RLHF (Li et al., 2024). As a
492   consequence, it is imperative to develop efficient methods to train a judge. Multiple methods have
493   been developed which create judges via training or prompting (Bai et al., 2022). However, these
494   methods do not apply DPO to improve the performance on a judge that provides both a rationale and
495
      score, to iteratively improve the performance of those models. (Wu et al., 2024) present a method that
      in part creates a judge, using DPO, to improve their conversational and reward model. Nonetheless,
496
      the main focus of the authors is on improving the conversational model’s capabilities, rather than the
497
      judge’s abilities to provide high quality rationales, and their results represent that emphasis. Research
498   is therefore required to assess whether one can improve the accuracy of the score and rationale
499   produced by a judge, using DPO.
500
501
      Self-curation for model improvement: Many studies have investigated approaches to improving
      models by training on the self-curated generations, a technique where a model is trained on its
502
      own outputs. Yuan et al. (2024) propose a methodology in which a language model generates both
503
      a response and a reward signal, which are used to create a preference dataset and train via DPO.
504   Pace et al. (2024) suggest training a model on a labelled dataset, using it to augment the data by
505   labelling an unlabelled dataset, and finally training a model on the augmented dataset. Another line
506   of research explores using a seed model to create chain-of-thought rationales and answers to generate
507   a preference dataset and train using DPO (Pang et al., 2024). Furthermore, another study focused
508   on a judge for pairwise (preference) evaluation and adding noise to prompts to self-curate datasets
509   for DPO training (Wang et al., 2024a). These papers have shown promising results for the use of
510   self-curating datasets and utilizing them for DPO. The difference between some of these methods
511   and Self-Rationalization are explained in Table 1.
512   Best-of-N sampling, sampling N times from the model and taking the best result, is also helpful
513   in improving the performance of models (Gui et al., 2024). In a similar spirit, Wang et al. (2023)
514   introduce a promising sampling method called self-consistency, where one samples n times from
515   the judge model, and then outputs the average score. These methods are useful in the context of
516   self-curating datasets, as they potentially increase dataset quality.
517   Despite the success of these methods, there is a lack of research on how self-curated datasets,
518   especially those enhanced through sampling techniques, affect the quality of rationales and scoring
519   in LLM-as-a-judge models. Table 1 shows the comparison of current judge training methods,
520   emphasizing their respective limitations. As a consequence, there are opportunities to explore the
521   potential benefits of combining DPO with advanced sampling methods to improve both rationale
522   quality and overall model performance.

525   7   C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   This paper presents the novel methodology Self-Rationalization, in which the judge model generates
529   multiple rationales with judgments for the same input. A preference pair dataset is synthetically
530   curated from these judgments, and the model is iteratively fine-tuned using DPO. The main benefits
531
      are that we do not require extra data labelling, it improves rationale quality, and it can evaluate
      based on customizable scoring criteria, while having less than 10B parameters. Our results show that
532
      Self-Rationalizing Evaluators obtained by performing iterative DPO outperform similar sized models
533
      and even larger sized models on evaluations leaderboards. It also outperforms regular SFT and other
534   common post-SFT methods such as self-consistency and best-of-N . Furthermore, we found that
535   rationale quality increases, and consequently, rationales improve the scoring performance, under
536   the condition that the model is trained via DPO. Self-Rationalizing is thus an effective approach
537   to improve performance of judges. These judges have great practical applications, as they can be
538   used to improve the performance of conversational models, or even evaluate human performance.
539   Future work could explore whether enhancing the judge’s capacity to better generate and differentiate
      between good and bad responses would improve its evaluation capabilities.
