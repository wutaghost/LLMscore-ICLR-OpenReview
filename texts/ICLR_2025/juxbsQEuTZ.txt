010
011
012
               Neural networks often favor shortcut heuristics based on surface-level patterns.
               Language models (LMs), for example, behave like n-gram models early in training.
013
               However, to correctly apply grammatical rules, LMs must instead rely on hierarchi-
014
               cal syntactic representations rather than on surface-level heuristics derived from
015            n-grams. In this work, we use cases studies of English grammar to explore how
016            latent structures in training data drives models toward improved out-of-distribution
017            (OOD) generalization. We then investigate how data composition can lead to
018            inconsistent behavior across random seeds. Our results show that models stabilize
019            in their OOD behavior only when they commit to either a surface-level linear rule
020            or a hierarchical rule. The hierarchical rule, furthermore, is induced by gram-
021            matically complex sequences with deep embedding structures, whereas the linear
022            rule is induced by simpler sequences. When the data contains a mix of simple
023
               and complex examples, potential rules compete; each independent training run
               either stabilizes by committing to a single rule or remains unstable in its OOD
024
               behavior. We also identify an exception to the relationship between stability and
025
               generalization: Models which memorize patterns from homogeneous training data
026            can overfit stably, with different rules for memorized and unmemorized patterns.
027            While existing works have attributed similar generalization behavior to training
028            objective and model architecture, our findings emphasize the critical role of training
029            data in shaping generalization patterns and how competition between data subsets
030            contributes to inconsistent generalization outcomes.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
      Neural networks often learn shortcut heuristics which reflect simple, surface-level patterns in data.
035   In the case of language models (LMs) trained on next-token prediction objectives, this simplicity
036   bias can lead models to behave like n-gram models, relying heavily on local dependencies without
037   fully capturing the deeper, more complex structures of language (Choshen et al., 2022; Geirhos et al.,
038   2020; Saphra & Lopez, 2018). However, LMs are also capable of breakthroughs in generalization,
039   shifting from these simple heuristics to more sophisticated behaviors (Choshen et al., 2022; Chen
040   et al., 2023; McCoy et al., 2020). Such transitions suggest that under certain training conditions, LMs
041   can eventually overcome spurious shortcuts and use linguistic structures to generalize beyond surface-
042   level patterns. Previous works often attribute this ability to model architecture and training objectives
043
      (Ahuja et al., 2024; McCoy et al., 2020). In this work, we investigate how data characteristics
      influence the generalization rules learned, especially when multiple solutions fit the training data
044
      equally well. We also examine the instabilities associated with generalization behaviors.
045
046
      To understand when and why a model favors learning latent structures over surface-level heuristics,
047
      we use case studies in learning English grammar rules. Grammatically correct sentences in English
048   must follow a set of rules that operate on a sequence’s latent tree-like structure (Chomsky, 2015; Crain
049   & Nakayama, 1987). When trained on next-token prediction, an LM may approximate these rules
050   from surface-level statistics, acting as an n-gram model by applying a linear rule. However, such a
051   model struggles to generalize to unseen grammatical patterns. Figure 1 (bottom right) shows that a
052   LM can use a linear bigram model to capture the relationship between a subject noun and its verb
053   by inflecting the verb with the same plurality as the subject. This LM would fail to generalize when
      a distractor noun, e.g., from a prepositional phrase, appears between subject and verb. In contrast,

      Building on previous work (McCoy et al., 2018; 2020; Ahuja et al., 2024), we use two tasks—
      question formation and tense inflection—to investigate whether the model learns the hierarchical rule
086
      or defaults to the surface-level linear rule. We train models on ambiguous data, which is compatible
087
      with both rules, and evaluate them on out-of-distribution (OOD) data which is compatible only with
088   the hierarchical rule. We first find that a preference for OOD hierarchical generalization is induced
089   by training on samples with center embeddings, where the subject is modified by an relative clause.
090   This result mirrors a celebrated claim from linguistics (Wexler, 1980) that center embeddings are
091   responsible for human syntax acquisition.
092
093   Models trained on the same data exhibit inconsistent OOD behaviors across random seeds. By exam-
094   ining training dynamics, we identify a connection between training stability and rule commitment:
095   Only runs that commit to a rule can exhibit stable OOD performance during training. Connecting back
096
      to data, we show that training dynamics can be categorized into three distinct regimes that depend on
      data complexity and diversity, illustrated in Figure 1 left. Data diversity determines whether a model
097
      learns a general rule, while data complexity determines which rule is preferred. Models trained on a
098
      mix of hierarchical-inducing samples and linear-inducing samples are most unstable during training
099   and exhibit the largest inconsistency across random seeds.
100
101   Taken together, our findings demonstrate that data composition plays a critical role in shaping model’s
102   OOD generalization behaviors. Our contributions are as follows:
103
104   • We show that sentences with complex grammatical structure—specifically center embeddings—
105     drive LMs to favor hierarchical syntactic representations over surface-level n-gram heuristics,
106     enabling correct OOD generalization of grammatical rules (see Section 4).
107   • We demonstrate that models stabilize in OOD performance only when they commit to either a
        surface-level heuristic or a hierarchical rule (see Section 5).

      • We show that when the training data mixes complex and simple grammatical structures, the
109     resulting rules are inconsistent across random seeds and many models fail to stabilize OOD
110     behavior by the end of training (see Section 5).
111   • We identify an exception to the relationship between stability and rule learning: Models trained
112     on insufficiently diverse data stabilize in a memorization regime without learning either rule,
113     highlighting another way that data can drive generalization failures (see Section 6).

      2.1   S YNTAX AND H IERARCHICAL G ENERALIZATION
122   McCoy et al. (2018) first used the question formation task to study hierarchical generalization in
123   neural networks, showing that RNNs trained with a seq-to-seq objective exhibit limited hierarchical
124   generalization. However, adding attention mechanisms improved performance on the generalization
125   set. Later, McCoy et al. (2020) found that tree-structured architectures consistently induce hierarchical
126   generalization. Petty & Frank (2021) and Mueller et al. (2022) further investigated inductive biases
127   and concluded that, like RNNs, transformers tend to generalize linearly. This view was challenged by
128
      Murty et al. (2023), who attributed the failure of prior attempts to insufficient training, demonstrating
      that decoder-only transformers can generalize hierarchically, but only after in-distribution perfor-
129
      mance has plateaued. Expanding on this, Ahuja et al. (2024) showed that hierarchical generalization
130
      is achieved only with models trained on a language modeling objective. Previous work primarily
131   attributed the source of inductive bias toward hierarchical rule to model architecture, whereas our
132   study highlights the impact of data, and we further provide a precise measure of data complexity and
133   data diversity. In addition, the inconsistency in generalization behaviors are observed in (McCoy
134   et al., 2018; McCoy et al.). While they have pointed out that models trained on different random seeds
135   can manifest very different generalization behaviors, they did not further studies the distributions of
136   model behaviors.
137
138   Similar to our work, Papadimitriou & Jurafsky (2023) and Papadimitriou & Jurafsky (2020) also
139   studied how training data could introduce an inductive bias to affect language acquisition. specifically
140   identified that by pretraining models on data with a recursive structure, finetuning them on natural
141
      language yields superior performances. This finding is closely related to our conclusions around
      center embeddings since the center embedding structure in language is recursive in nature.
142

144   2.2   T RAINING DYNAMICS AND G ROKKING
145   Grokking refers to the phenomenon where a neural network, after achieving seemingly poor per-
146   formance for a long period, suddenly generalize on unseen data. Power et al. (2022) first observed
147   this behavior in simple arithmetic tasks. Since then, the exact mechanism of grokking has been
148   widely studied. Different from existing grokking work, we studied a different types of grokking:
149   “structural grokking" (Murty et al., 2023). In classic grokking, the model transitions from memoriza-
150   tion to generalization, allowing it to achieve non-trivial performance on unseen data. In structural
151   grokking, a model transitions from the simple linear rule to the hierarchical rule, leading to non-trivial
152   performance on OOD data. However, the findings of this study is potentially related to those in
153
      classic grokking. Zhu et al. (2024) studies the role of data and finds that grokking only occurs when
      training data is sufficiently large. Berlot-Attwell et al. (2023) broadly studies how data diversity and
154
      complexity leads to different generalization behaviors. Liu et al. (2022) shows that grokking can be
155
      induced with different weight norms, associating generalization with a specific goldilocks zone weight
156   norm value. Huang et al. (2024) and Varma et al. (2023) have shown that during training, different
157   circuits compete, and models trained on different random seeds can lead to distinct generalization
158   behaviors depending on which circuits dominate. While these works primarily attribute generalization
159   differences to circuit formation, our findings highlight that this competition between circuits can also
160   destabilize learning dynamics. Importantly, we characterize the unstable regime in both data diversity
161   and data complexity and we address connections between training stability and consistency under
      random variation.

178   2.3 R ANDOM VARIATION
179   Although choices like hyperparameter settings, architecture, and optimizer all shape model outcomes,
180   training remains inherently stochastic. Models are sensitive to random initialization and the order of
181   training examples. Several studies (Zhou et al., 2020; D’Amour et al., 2022; Naik et al., 2018) have
182   reported significant performance differences across model checkpoints and Zhou et al. (2020) noted
183
      that instability extends throughout the training curve. Dodge et al. (2020) found that both weight
      initialization and data order contribute equally to out-of-sample performance variation. Unlike prior
184
      work, which focuses on the experimental implications of random variations, we investigate the source
185
      of these training inconsistencies and link them to characteristics of the training data.

188
189   The question formation task and the tense inflection task are first proposed by Frank & Mathis (2007)
190   and Linzen et al. (2016) as canonical tasks to assess a model’s language modeling ability. In this
191   study, we use the synthetic dataset constructed by McCoy et al. (2018) (for question formation) and
192
      McCoy et al. (2020) (for tense inflection).
193   3.1 Q UESTION F ORMATION TASK
194
      In the question formation (QF) task, a declarative sentence is transformed into a question (see
195
      Table 1) by moving the main auxiliary verb (such as “does” in “does move”) to the front. Our training
196
      data permits two strategies for choosing which verb to move: (1) move first: a linear rule that moves
197   the first auxiliary, or (2) move main: a hierarchical rule—the correct rule in English grammar—based
198   on the sentence’s syntactic structure. This syntactic structure links each word into a tree-like structure
199   in which edges specify syntactic dependencies (e.g., subject, preposition, object), as shown in Figure
200   2. The model leverages this tree representation to determine which auxiliary to move.
201
202   In Table 1, the first example is considered ambiguous because both the hierarchical and linear rules
203   produce the correct outcome. In contrast, the second example is unambiguous because only the
204
      hierarchical rule produces the correct outcome. The training data contains only ambiguous samples,
      while the OOD generalization set includes only unambiguous samples. If a model uses a hierarchical
205
      representation of syntax, it should achieve 100% accuracy on both the in-distribution (ambiguous
206
      questions) and OOD generalization (unambiguous questions) sets. Conversely, if a model rise on
207   linear rules, it will score 0% on the OOD generalization set, but still score 100% accuracy on the
208   in-distribution set. We therefore use the model’s accuracy on the OOD generalization set as a metric
209   for hierarchical generalization.
210

236   3.3 M ODEL , DATA AND T RAINING
237   We use a decoder-only Transformer architecture with 12M parameters: 6 layers of 8 heads with
238   a 512-dimensional embedding for QF. For TI, we use the same transformer architecture but with
239   4 layers. All models are trained from scratch on a causal language modeling objective for 300K

241   schedule. All the hyperparamter settings are directly adopted from existing works (Ahuja et al., 2024;
242   Murty et al., 2023). We run all experiments on the same 50 random seeds. We use the original
243
      training, validation and OOD generalization data proposed by McCoy et al. (2018) and McCoy et al.

244
      original QF and TI task. Specifically, the original TI and QF data are generated with Context-Free
245
      Grammar (CFG) using a simplified set of grammatical rules, and we reuse the same CFG rules to
246   create variations of the training data. We use a word-level tokenizer with a vocabulary of size 72.

252   4.1 C ENTER E MBEDDING
253
      Center embedding occurs when a clause—often acting as a modifier—is placed within another clause
254   or phrase. Figure 2 (left) illustrates two examples of center-embedded sentences, where the embedded
255   clause disrupts syntactic dependencies, such as the subject-verb-object relationship. Moreover, center
256   embeddings exhibit a recursive structure: inside the relative clause, one can find the same structure as
257   the entire sentence. In contrast, sentences without center embeddings are exclusively right branching.
258   Right-branching structures may also include modifying clauses, but these can only be appended at
259   the end of the main clause, maintaining its linear flow (see Figure 2, right). Center embedding has
260   been central to linguistic studies on the types of data required to learn grammatical rules. According
261   to Chomsky’s generative grammar framework (Chomsky, 2015), center-embedded clauses give rise
262
      to hierarchical, tree-like syntactic structures. Additionally, Wexler (1980) posits that all English
      syntactic rules can be learned from “degree 2" sentences, which contain exactly one embedded clause.
263
264   While center embeddings are crucial for human language acquisition, in this study we investigate
265   whether the same type of data can lead a LM to acquire the hierarchical grammar rule. To correctly
266   predict the distribution of next tokens, LMs must track dependencies between sentence components.
267   In right-branching sentences, LMs can rely on linear proximity to identify dependencies; for example,
268   as shown in Figure 2, a simple bigram model suffices to capture the subject-verb relationship. In
269   contrast, center embeddings introduce relative clauses of various lengths, making linear n-gram
      models inefficient for capturing subject-verb dependencies. Furthermore, the recursive nature means

      4.2 Q UESTION F ORMATION
293
      As specified in Section 3.1, the training data for QF must be ambiguous between the linear rule
294
      (i.e., moving the first auxiliary) and the hierarchical rule (i.e., moving the main auxiliary). Center-
295
      embedded sentences do not meet this ambiguity requirement and, therefore, cannot appear in question
296   formation training samples. To ensure the model is exposed to diverse sentence types, McCoy
297   et al. (2018) introduces a secondary task to the QF training dataset: declaration copying. Like
298   question formation, the declaration-copying sample starts with a declarative sentence, but instead of
299   transforming it, the model simply repeats it. Since the ambiguity requirement does not apply to the
300   declaration-copying task, center-embedded sentences are included in this secondary task. Concrete
301   examples of both tasks can be found in Appendix B.
302
303
      We train models on three subsets of the original training data, varying the composition of the
      declaration-copying examples. In Quest Only, we remove all declaration copying examples. In Center
304
      embed, we only keep center-embedded examples. In Right branch, we only keep right-branching
305
      examples. For all three subsets, the question formation samples remain unchanged. Each setup
306   reaches 100% in-distribution validation accuracy; however, the OOD generalization performance,
307   shown in Figure 3 (left), differs significantly across the subsets. When the declaration-copying task is
308   removed, none of the 50 runs achieve an OOD generalization accuracy above 75%, indicating that
309   declaration copying examples are essential for inducing the hierarchical rule. When trained solely on
310   center-embedded sentences in the declaration-copying task, models exhibit a strong preference for
311   the hierarchical rule. In contrast, training only on right-branching sentences leads to poor hierarchical
312   generalization. This evidence suggests that center-embedded sentences direct a model towards the
313   hierarchical rule.

458

460   three distinct regimes. In the low-diversity regime, training is stable but the model fails to commit to

463   to those structures but it cannot extrapolate the rule to unseen syntax structures. In the mid-diversity
464

465

      generalization ratio.

      7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
484
485   By exploring the role of data structure in determining OOD generalization rules, we have also revealed
      which settings allow us to predict model behavior. We show that complex grammatical structures


                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      guide models toward hierarchical rules, while mixed data compositions lead to unstable dynamics
487   and inconsistent rule commitment. These findings emphasize the importance of understanding how
488   data diversity shapes both stability and generalization in neural networks. Our findings have a number
489   of implications across machine learning and even formal linguistics.
490
491
      Clusters of generalization behavior across seeds While errors are often treated as Gaussian noise
      in the theoretical literature, our findings suggest that errors may only be distributed unimodally for a
492
      given compositional solution. Our work joins the growing literature that suggests random variation not
493
      only has an effect, but can create clusters of OOD behaviors. Previously, clustered distributions have
494   been documented in text classification heuristics (Juneja et al., 2022) and training dynamics (Hu et al.,
495   2023). In our case, we note that generalization accuracy is only clearly multimodally distributed when
496   specifically considering stable training runs. We suggest that research on compositional variation in
497   training consider training stability in the future.
498
      Implications for formal linguistics Our findings have potential implications for linguistics debates
499
      about the poverty of the stimulus (McCoy et al., 2018; Berwick et al., 2011). Linguists have
500
      extensively studied the question of what data is necessary and sufficient to learn grammatical rules.
501   In particular, Wexler (1980) argue that all English syntactic rules are learnable given “degree 2” data:
502   sentences with only one embedded clause nested within another clause. Our mixed scoping results
503   show that without a stronger architectural inductive bias—the very subject of the poverty of the
504   stimulus debate—degree 1 data alone cannot induce a preference for hierarchical structure. However,
505   our work also supports the position of Lightfoot (1989) that lower degree data is adequate for a child
506   to learn a specific rule, as the LM generalizes ID degree 1 QF rule examples to OOD degree 2 by
507   using the hierarchical inductive bias induced by declaration examples.
508   Grokking, instability, and latent structure Murty et al. (2023), exploring the same data setting
509   we do, call the transition from linear generalization to hierarchical generalization rules during training
510   structural grokking. Classic grokking (Power et al., 2022), however, is different: Rather than a
511   transition between generalization rules, it describes a transition from memorization to generalization.
512
513   Our findings clarify both scenarios. We link structural grokking to the instability formed by com-
514
      petition between linear- and hierarchical-inducing training subsets. Without competing subsets, the
      model immediately learns either the linear or the hierarchical rule without the gradual transition of
515
      structural grokking. This instability could represent the same phenomenon of circuit competition
516
      described by Ahuja et al. (2024). We find a similar pattern of instability in our study of data diversity,
517   with implications for classic grokking. In this case, the competition is not between two rules, but
518   instead between memorized heuristics—sufficient for modeling syntactically homogeneous training
519   data—and simple OOD rules—required to efficiently model diverse training data. Yet again, while a
520   strict memorization regime is relatively stable, the regime between memorization and generalization
521   is unstable, leading to potential grokking.
522
523   Our findings suggest that memorization is just another rule that the model can adopt when it is
524
      the simplest way of capturing the training distribution. Such a framework unifies the grokking
      literature with other phenomena such as emergence (Schaeffer et al., 2023) and benign interpolation
525
      (Theunissen et al., 2020).

      A     R ELATED W ORK E XTENDED
757
758   A.1   S YNTAX AND H IERARCHICAL G ENERALIZATION
759   While works mentioned in Section 2.1 focused on models trained from scratch, another line of
760   research examined the inductive bias of pretrained models. Mueller et al. (2024); Mueller & Linzen
761   (2023) pretrained transformers on text corpora such as Wikipedia and CHILDES (MacWhinney,
762   2014) before fine-tuning them on the question formation task. They found that exposure to large
763   amounts of natural language data enables transformers to generalize hierarchically.
764
765
      Instead of using the question formation task as a probe, Hewitt & Manning (2019); Murty et al. (2022)
      directly interpreted model’s internal representation to understand whether transformers constrain
766
      their computations to to follow tree-structure patterns. Hewitt & Manning (2019) demonstrated that
767
      the syntax tress are embedded in model’s representation space. Similarly, Murty et al. (2022) projects
768   transformers into a tree-structured network, and showed that transformers become more tree-like over
769   the course of training on language data.
770
771   A.2   R ANDOM VARIATION
772   Specific training choices, such as hyperparameters, are crucial to model outcomes. However, even
773   when controlling for these factors, training machine learning models remains inherently stochas-
774   tic—models can be sensitive to random initialization and the order of training examples. Zhou et al.
775   (2020); D’Amour et al. (2022); Naik et al. (2018) reported significant performance differences across
776   model checkpoints on various analysis and stress test sets. Zhou et al. (2020) further found that
777   instability extends throughout the training curve, not just in final outcomes. To investigate the source
778
      of this inconsistency, Dodge et al. (2020) compared the effects of weight initialization and data order,
      concluding that both factors contribute equally to variations in out-of-sample performance.
779
780
      Similarly, Sellam et al. (2021) found that repeating the pre-training process on BERT models can result
781   in significantly different performances on downstream tasks. To promote more robust experimental
782   testing, they introduced a set of 25 BERT-BASE checkpoints to ensure that experimental conclusions
783   are not influenced by artifacts, such as specific instances of the model. In this work, we also
784   observe training inconsistencies across runs on OOD data, both during training and at convergence.
785   Unlike prior studies that focus on implications of random variations on experimental design, we
786   study the source of training inconsistencies and link these inconsistencies to simplicity bias and the
787   characteristics of the training data.
788
      A.3   S IMPLICITY B IAS
789
790   Models often favor simpler functions early in training, a phenomenon known as simplicity bias
791
      (Hermann & Lampinen, 2020), which is also common in LMs. Choshen et al. (2022) found that
      early LMs behave like n-gram models, and Saphra & Lopez (2019) observed that early LMs learn
792
      simplified versions of the language modeling task. McCoy et al. (2019) showed that even fully trained
793
      models can rely on simple heuristics, like lexical overlap, to perform well on Natural Language
794   Inference (NLI) tasks. Chen et al. (2023) further explored the connection between training dynamics
795   and simplicity bias, showing that simpler functions learned early on can continue to influence fully
796   trained models, and mitigating this bias can have long-term effects on training outcomes.
797
798   Phase transitions have been identified as markers of shifts from simplistic heuristics to more complex
799   model behavior, often triggered by the amount of training data or model size. In language models,
800   Olsson et al. (2022) showed that the emergence of induction heads in autoregressive models is linked
801
      to handling longer context sizes and in-context learning. Similar phase transitions have been studied
      in non-language domains, such as algorithmic tasks (Power et al., 2022; Merrill et al., 2023) and
802
      arithmetic tasks (Nanda et al., 2023; Barak et al., 2022).
803
804   In the context of hierarchical generalization, Ahuja et al. (2024) used a Bayesian approach to analyze
805   the simplicity of hierarchical versus linear rules in modeling English syntax. They argued that
806   transformers favor the hierarchical rule because it is simpler than the linear rule. However, their
807   model fails to explain (1) why learning the hierarchical rule is delayed (i.e., after learning the linear
808   rule) and (2) why hierarchical generalization is inconsistent across runs. In this work, we offer a
809   different perspective, showing that a model’s simplicity bias towards either rule is driven by the
      characteristics of the training data.

1063   We investigate model behavior when trained on data with limited diversity. By analyzing a model’s
1064   generalization accuracy across different syntactic types, we aim to distinguish patterns indicative of
1065   either memorization or generalization.
1066   Measuring data similarity Building on the diversity measure from Section 6.1, we now use Tree-
1067   Edit Distance (TED) as a measure of sentence similarity. As before, we first construct syntax trees
1068   using CFG rules, then calculate TED using the Zhang-Shasha Tree-Edit Distance algorithm (Zhang &
1069   Shasha, 1989). We define TED=0 for sentences that share the same syntax structure but differ only in
1070   vocabulary. This similarity measure allows us to quantify, for each sample in the OOD generalization
1071   set, the closest matching sentence type in the training data. In the memorization regime, where the
1072   model encounters only a few syntax types, we suspect it cannot extrapolate rules to syntactically
1073   distinct OOD sentences. In contrast, with a more diverse syntax exposure, rule extrapolation may
1074
       enable the model to apply rules even to OOD sentence types.
1075   Experiment To verify our intuition about memorization and generalization, we train models on
1076   two variations of the QF data. In the first variation, the declaration-copying task has data diversity set
1077   to 1, meaning only one syntax type appears, and we specifically choose one with center embedding.
1078   In the second variation, the declaration-copying task has diversity set to 5, with all 5 types containing
1079   center embeddings. For both datasets, the question-formation task remains unchanged, consisting
       solely of right-branching sentences. For the diversity=1 dataset, we calculate TED for each unique


                                                                                                  20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       syntax type in the OOD set against the single syntax type in the declaration-copying task. For the
1081   diversity=5 dataset, we compute TED between each OOD sample and the five syntax types in the
1082   declaration-copying task, taking the minimum. This TED score provides a measure of similarity
1083   between the OOD samples and those encountered during training. Our goal is to determine, based on
1084   training with these datasets, which OOD syntax types the model applies the hierarchical rule to.

       Result In Figure 15, we visualize the final generalization accuracy for each OOD syntax type
1086
       against its TED relative to the training data. When trained on low-diversity data (Figure 15, left),
1087
