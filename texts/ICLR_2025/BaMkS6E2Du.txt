009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Enhancing the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) remains
012            a key challenge, especially for tasks that require complex, multi-step decision-
013            making. Humans excel at these tasks by leveraging deliberate planning with an
014            internal world model to simulate the potential outcomes of various actions. In-
015            spired by this, we propose a novel multi-step reasoning framework for LLMs,
016
               referred to as Structure-aware Planning with Accurate World Model (SWAP). Un-
               like previous approaches that rely solely on Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning in
017
               natural language, SWAP incorporates structural information to guide the reason-
018
               ing process via a world model and provides a soft verification mechanism over the
019            steps. Moreover, SWAP overcomes the challenge of accurate world state predic-
020            tions in complex reasoning tasks by introducing a Generator-Discriminator archi-
021            tecture, which enables more reliable world modeling. Specifically, the generator
022            predicts the next state, and the discriminator ensures alignment with the logical
023            consistency required by the problem context. SWAP also encourages the policy
024            model to explore a broad range of potential actions to prevent premature conver-
025            gence. By resolving the bottlenecks of generation diversity for both actions and
026            states using diversity-based modeling (DBM) and improving discrimination accu-
027
               racy through contrastive ranking (CR), SWAP significantly enhances the reason-
               ing performance of LLMs. We evaluate SWAP across diverse reasoning-intensive
028
               benchmarks including math reasoning, logical reasoning, and coding tasks. Ex-
029
               tensive experiments demonstrate that SWAP achieves substantial improvements
030            over the baselines and consistently outperforms existing methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI & et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024) have made remark-
036   able progress in many fields. However, their ability to perform complex reasoning remains limited
037   (Huang & Chang, 2023). Achieving human-level problem solving is viewed as the next milestone in
038   Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) (OpenAI, 2024). Unlike human cognition, the inference time
039
      of LLMs for reasoning tasks primarily depends on the number of input and output tokens rather than
      the complexity of the problem. For instance, while humans require multiple attempts, calculations,
040
      and verification to solve difficult math problems, LLMs immediately begin generating responses
041
      after reading the question. This indicates that they are not actually “thinking” but merely using intu-
042   ition, i.e., predicting the next token based on previous ones. In fact, there are two systems of thinking
043   in human mind (Kahneman, 2011): System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little effort
044   and no sense of voluntary control; and System 2 allocates attention to the effortful mental activities
045   that demand it. In this paper, we aim to enhance the complex reasoning capabilities of LLMs, i.e.,
046   turning thinking time into better outcome, with a planning-based approach that emulates System 2.
047
      Recently, planning and decision-making frameworks (Yao et al., 2022) have been introduced into
048   reasoning tasks for LLMs, where the model is required not only to propose actions but also to make
049   adjustments based on feedback from the environment. However, in many real-world scenarios, envi-
050   ronment feedback is either unavailable or difficult to scale. Inspired by human perception (Johnson-
051   Laird, 1983; 2010), an internal world model is introduced to enable the model to simulate actions
052   and their effects on the world state for deliberate planning (LeCun, 2022). Some recent approaches
053   have demonstrated success in planning and reasoning tasks with a world model (Guan et al., 2023;
      Hao et al., 2023), which is implemented by prompting the same LLM with in-context demonstra-

054
      tions. However, their system performance still falls short of expectations in complex tasks, since
055   constructing an accurate world model is inherently challenging. The predicted future state from an
056   inaccurate world model may lead to sub-optimal or even incorrect decisions. To address this limita-
057   tion, we fine-tune the model using a Generator-Discriminator architecture. Furthermore, we achieve
058   substantial improvements on a diverse set of reasoning benchmarks by resolving the bottlenecks of
059   generation diversity and discrimination accuracy.
060
      On the other hand, the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) approach (Wei et al., 2022), due to its high flexibility
061   and scalability, is widely adopted to enhance the reasoning capability of LLMs. However, it relies
062   purely on natural language, lacking an effective verification mechanism. To address this issue,
063   formal methods have been proposed, such as using first-order logic (Pan et al., 2023) or programs
064   (Chen et al., 2022). Nevertheless, these formal methods are often limited in their expressiveness for a
065   variety of tasks (Yang et al., 2024). In this paper, we propose a semi-formal approach that introduces
066   structural information into the reasoning process, which provides a soft verification mechanism
067   for CoTs. These structures (Dalvi et al., 2021) describe how given premises are used to generate
068   intermediate conclusions that help validate the correctness of a particular answer. In our framework,
069
      the multi-step reasoning process involves constructing a structure, i.e., the policy model proposes
      actions, and the world model predicts the next state and updates the structure. Specifically, new
070
      statements in the next state are introduced and linked to existing ones through entailment relations.
071
      When the reasoning is complete, the system has built an entailment graph from the given premises
072   to the final answer, which itself serves as a justification of the reasoning process.
073
074   Specifically, our contributions mainly include:
075
             • We introduce structure-aware planning, which incorporates entailment graphs into multi-
076
               step reasoning tasks. These graphs demonstrate how premises lead to intermediate conclu-
077            sions and validate the correctness of the final answer, adding coherence and logical verifi-
078            cation to the reasoning process.
079
             • Our framework, SWAP, augments the LLM with an accurate world model, which is im-
080
               plemented using a Generator-Discriminator architecture. In addition, we resolve the bottle-
081
               necks of generation diversity and discrimination accuracy with diversity-based modelling
082            and contrastive process supervision, respectively.
083
             • Experiments on a diverse set of benchmarks, including math reasoning, logical reasoning
084
               and coding, show that SWAP is a general framework that achieves substantial improve-
085
               ments over recent popular reasoning and planning methods for LLMs.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   Existing works that use advanced planning methods to enhance the multi-step problem-solving ca-
091   pabilities of LLMs can be categorized into three types: re-ranking (Ni et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
092   2023b; Li et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2024), iterative correction (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023;
093
      Yao et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024a) and tree search (Chaffin et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023; Hao et al.,
      2023; Yao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). Despite differences in their design, all these methods fun-
094
      damentally rely on a discriminator to evaluate the planning steps. Recent research (Huang et al.,
095
      2023; Chen et al., 2024b) has demonstrated that the discriminator plays a more crucial role than the
096   planning methods themselves. Consequently, using in-context learning to prompt the same LM as
097   both generator and discriminator may not sufficiently improve the model performance on complex
098   reasoning tasks.
099
      To address this issue, prior research has explored various methodologies for designing the discrim-
100
      inator (or reward model). There are two primary types of reward models: Outcome Reward Model
101
      (ORM) and Process Reward Model (PRM). The ORM evaluates the fully generated solution by
102   assigning a single scalar confidence score. Its training relies on outcome supervision by compar-
103   ing generated answers with the ground truth. In contrast, the PRM (Lightman et al., 2023; Yuan
104   et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024) provides stepwise rewards throughout the reasoning process, assign-
105   ing a scalar confidence score to each intermediate steps. Empirical evidence shows that, compared
106   with outcome supervision, process supervision ensures the correctness of each step, providing more
107   benefits to multi-step reasoning (Lightman et al., 2023). However, the training of PRM requires
      process supervision, which is hard to obtain, e.g., collecting process annotation from humans is


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      inherently not scalable. Although recent research (Wang et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2024) has increas-
109   ingly explored automatic process annotations using tree search, training an effective PRM remains
110   challenging, as from a mathematical perspective, it assigns a numerical value within [0, 1] to each
111   state independently. To overcome this problem, we propose a novel strategy for automatic ranking
112   annotation, i.e., given the current context and a set of candidate options, selecting the best option
113   based on relative quality. Our ranking strategy offers significant advantages over traditional PRMs:
114   1) it emphasizes relative quality, making it more robust to noise; 2) it simplifies optimization and en-
115   hances generalization. Notably, our high-quality automatic ranking annotation method is non-trivial
116   as it systemically incorporates three key factors: 1) structural information; 2) correctness; and 3)
117
      semantical equivalence.
118   Furthermore, we notice that although some reasoning processes are inherently non-linear, existing
119   methods mainly follow a linear problem-solving manner. Language models are expected to im-
120   plicitly infer the non-linear structure from the linear representation of the reasoning process, which
121   proves challenging for complex reasoning tasks (Ribeiro et al., 2023). To help the model, we inte-
122   grate structural information into the reasoning process which explicitly represents the reasoning
123
      structure within the context. These structures provide the language model with additional guidance
      and control, enabling extra capabilities such as symbolic learning and verification.

128   3.1   TASK FORMULATION
129
      When solving complex reasoning tasks that require multiple steps, LLMs must plan intelligently,
130
      anticipating future state and guiding their reasoning towards the desired outcome. We formulate this
131
      task as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) represented by (S, A, P, score) in which:
132
133          • State st ∈ S: Represents the current state, i.e., all known or inferred information in the
134            reasoning process. The initial state s0 is extracted from the given context.
135          • Action at ∈ A: Denotes a single action (produced by policy generator PπG ), i.e., deriving
136            new information or making inference based on current state, resulting in a state transition.
137          • Transition probability P(st+1 |st , at ): Describes the probability of transitioning to the
138            next state st+1 after taking action at in state st . We construct an enhanced world model
139            (with generator PwmG and discriminator PwmD ) to simulate the state change.
140          • Scoring function score(at |st ): Quantifies the quality of an action at given current state
141            st . This function guides the reasoning process by prioritizing actions that are more likely to
142            yield correct final answers. We adopt a ranking-based approach (with policy discriminator
143            PπD ) instead of assigning explicit numerical scores (with PRM).
144
145   This MDP framework provides a foundation for applying planning methods to enhance the multi-
146
      step reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Each reasoning step is viewed as a decision-making process,
      where the model generates the next action based on current state. By updating their parameters,
147
      the models gradually learn the optimal policy for each state, improving the overall performance of
148
      reasoning. Additionally, the policy must balance exploiting known optimal actions and exploring
149   new action spaces, guided by the scoring function to help the model make the best choices.
150
151   3.2   S TRUCTURED REASONING AS ENTAILMENT GRAPH CONSTRUCTION
152
153   The key innovation that distinguishes our approach from related work is conceptualizing the multi-
154   step reasoning process (s0 , a0 , s1 , · · · , aT −1 , sT ) as entailment graph (Dalvi et al., 2021) con-
155   struction (Figure 1), which outlines how the premises in s0 lead to intermediate conclusions, ulti-
156   mately validating the final answer in sT . Formally, let G = (V, E) represent the structure, where V
157   is the set of nodes, with each node v ∈ V representing a statement, e.g., evidence, an assumption,
158
      or a lemma/rule; E is the set of directed (hyper) edges, where each (hyper) edge e = (Vsrc , Vtgt ) ∈ E
      represents an entailment relation from a source node set Vsrc ⊆ V (the premises) to a target node set
159
      Vtgt ⊆ V (the conclusions).
160

      ments and their relations. During the reasoning, PwmG incrementally grows the graph by adding new

335   issue, our discriminator employs Contrastive Ranking (CR) to evaluate multiple candidate options

337
      cies between options, particularly erroneous parts, thereby simplifying the task.

      5     E XPERIMENTS
411
412
      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
413
      We conduct experiments on various types of reasoning tasks. Dataset statistics and examples are
414
      provided in Appendix B. For each dataset, we use different types of models (GPT-4o (OpenAI &
415   et al., 2024), DeepSeek-V2 (DeepSeek-AI & et al., 2024), LLaMA3 (Dubey et al., 2024)) to generate
416   multiple trajectories for the training and validation sets. We label the trajectories as positive or
417   negative based on their final answers. To improve the model stability, we augment training questions
418   using GPT-4o. Given the positive and negative trajectories of the same question, we automatically
419   generate contrastive process annotations (Figure 3) using DeepSeek-V2. Additionally, to address the
420   class imbalance in contrastive ranking data, we apply pre-processing and post-processing techniques
421   (see Appendix D for details). With the complete training data, SWAP is fine-tuned from LLaMA3-
422   8B-Instruct using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). The parameter settings are as follows: For DBM, γ0 = 0.7
423
      and α = 0.95. For CR, Nveri = 3; we choose K = {2, 3} for discriminator training, and during
      inference, multiple options are divided into groups of size 2 or 3; for meta knowledge, we use
424
      M = 5. To ensure the effectiveness of training, we also employ specialized strategies such as
425
      curriculum learning and self-improving training (details in Appendix D). During evaluation, we
426   compare our SWAP against popular strategies, CoT, Self-consistency (SC) (Wang et al., 2023b), ToT
427   (Yao et al., 2023), and RAP (Hao et al., 2023)) as well as SFT on CoTs and verification with PRMs
428   (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), using different base models (LLaMA3-8B-Instruct and
429   Mistral-7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023)). The number of candidate solutions for self-consistency and
430   PRMs is set to 8. For SWAP, ToT, and RAP (utilizing MCTS), the generation number and step limits
431   are set to 5 and 10, respectively. The number of rollouts (breadth limit) is set as 8. More details
      about data generation, model training and evaluation are provided in Appendix C, D.

      5.2   M AIN RESULTS
433
434   Overall performance is shown in Table 1, with fine-grained results and examples provided in Ap-
435   pendix E and Appendix G, respectively. We summarize the key findings as follows:
436
      SWAP consistently achieves the best or comparable performance among different methods.
437
      One-pass CoT and verification methods, such as self-consistency and PRMs, do not involve search-
438
      ing through intermediate steps during the reasoning process. In contrast, our framework empowers
439   the model to reason more like humans’ conscious planning, which significantly improves perfor-
440   mance on multi-step reasoning tasks. This planning ability becomes especially crucial in more chal-
441   lenging tasks, where deliberate reasoning is necessary to avoid intermediate errors. For instance,
442   our framework with LLaMA3-8B-Instruct achieves a 14.7% better accuracy compared to CoT (53%
443   relative improvement) on the more difficult MATH dataset, and a 10.3% improvement on GSM8k.
444
      Structure-aware planning and an accurate world model further enhance the effectiveness of
445   planning in LLMs. Methods such as ToT and RAP, which also incorporate planning or search-based
446   strategies, do not match our approach in performance. They lack the deeper structural understanding
447   and precise state modeling that our framework provides. SWAP explicitly introduces the structure
448   that describes the relationship between key statements, which facilitates both action generation and
449   state prediction. In addition, Diversity-based Modeling (DBM) enables the generator to explore
450   a broader solution space, increasing the likelihood of finding optimal steps. Contrastive Ranking
451   (CR), on the other hand, significantly improves the accuracy of the discriminator by focusing on
452   relative comparisons between candidate solutions. This combination of enhanced exploration and
453
      more precise discrimination is key to the substantial performance improvements observed in our
      experiments, especially on challenging datasets like MATH.

456   5.3   A NALYSIS
457
458   We investigate the effect of search tree width and depth on overall accuracy, providing insights for
459   both parameter selection and dynamic evaluation across various tasks.
460   The benefit of increasing search tree width, i.e., the number of search attempts per step, be-
461   comes marginal after a certain point. For planning-based approaches, the width of the search
462   tree directly influences the thoroughness of exploring the solution space at each step. We analyze
463   the effect of search width on accuracy in SWAP (Figure 4). As shown, there is a consistent upward
464   trend across all datasets. However, the benefits diminish beyond a certain point, e.g., after 5-7 search
465   attempts in FOLIO and GSM8K, since most of the promising options have already been explored.
466
      We found that using a search width of 5 offers the best trade-off between computational cost and per-
      formance. We also observed some variability between datasets. GSM8K and MATH show a sharper
467
      initial increase in accuracy with fewer search attempts, while FOLIO and HumanEval exhibit a
468
      more gradual improvement. This discrepancy likely arises from the variations in task complexity
469   and dataset size.
470

      searched steps in the trajectory. Another important factor is the search tree depth, which refers to

      We analyze the impact of the key components proposed in this paper (Table 2). The complete

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce SWAP, a novel framework for enhancing the multi-step reasoning ca-
533
      pabilities of LLMs through structure-aware planning with an accurate world model. Our approach
534   consistently outperforms existing methods in extensive experiments, demonstrating significant im-
535   provements on reasoning-heavy benchmarks, including math, logical reasoning, and coding tasks.
536   In this work, we primarily adopt a re-ranking strategy, as it provides a good balance between com-
537   putational cost and model performance. For future research, exploring reinforcement learning (RL)
538   methods to enable dynamic interaction with the world model could further optimize LLMs for long-
539   term rewards. Additionally, teaching the model to recognize and correct its own mistakes represents
      another promising direction, potentially leading to even more robust reasoning capabilities.

      The meta knowledge Kmeta , which helps verify answers and identify errors, is derived from training
759
      questions. Formally, Kmeta = concatm (Km ), where Km represents stored knowledge from the m-
760   th training sample, and we select the top M samples based on the cosine similarity between the
761   training query embedding qm and the test query embedding q.
762
763
      We fine-tune our discriminator using the contrastive process annotations, which helps it accurately
      identify subtle differences between trajectories and improve its ability to distinguish between valid
764
      and invalid reasoning steps. To further enhance robustness during inference, we randomly group
765
      the candidate options and reorder them, then apply a voting strategy to determine the final ranking
766   to decide the top b candidates as mentioned in Algorithm 1, 2. This approach ensures that the model
767   is not biased by specific sequences and provides a more reliable assessment of the best candidate.
768
769
      In addition, we introduce structural verification for generated entailment graphs G to further en-
      hance discrimination. Key steps involves: 1) Syntax Verification: Validates the format of nodes and
770
      edges. 2) Node Dependency Analysis: Examines the dependencies between nodes (assumptions,
771
      lemmas, facts, or inferences derived from prior nodes). 3) Cycle Detection: Ensures acyclic struc-
772   tures to maintain logical consistency. 4) Redundancy Check: Detects redundant or disconnected
773   nodes. All of them are implemented according to standard graph algorithms.

1555   consists of three main components: the policy model Mπ , the world model Mwm , and the controller
1556

1558
       st+1 and updates the entailment graph Gt+1 . Finally, based on G and the updated state st+1 (Gt+1 ),
1559   the controller Mc decides whether to continue the reasoning process or output the final answer.
