                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Triggered by the realization that AI emulators can rival the performance of tra-
010            ditional numerical weather prediction models running on HPC systems, there is
011            now an increasing number of large AI models that address use cases such as fore-
012            casting, downscaling, or nowcasting. While the parallel developments in the AI
013            literature focus on foundation models – models that can be effectively tuned to
014            address multiple, different use cases – the developments on the weather and cli-
015            mate side largely focus on single-use cases with particular emphasis on mid-range
016            forecasting. We close this gap by introducing Prithvi WxC, a 2.3 billion parameter
017            foundation model developed using 160 variables from the Modern-Era Retrospec-
018
               tive Analysis for Research and Applications, Version 2 (MERRA-2). Prithvi WxC
               employs an encoder-decoder-based architecture, incorporating concepts from var-
019
               ious recent transformer models to effectively capture both regional and global
020
               dependencies in the input data. The model has been designed to accommodate
021            large token counts to model weather phenomena in different topologies at fine
022            resolutions. Furthermore, it is trained with a mixed objective that combines the
023            paradigms of masked reconstruction with forecasting. We test the model on a
024            set of challenging downstream tasks namely: Autoregressive rollout forecasting,
025            downscaling, gravity wave flux parameterization, and extreme events estimation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Deep learning is increasingly transforming weather applications by delivering highly accurate fore-
030
      casts with reduced computational costs compared to traditional numerical weather prediction meth-
031   ods (Bi et al., 2023; Lam et al., 2023; Mukkavilli et al., 2023). Unlike the traditional physics-based
032   approaches, deep learning models do not directly simulate the underlying physics. Instead, they
033   capture this through probability distributions derived from model training, a method adapted from
034   natural language processing and computer vision. This technique has proven surprisingly effective
035   in approximating complex physical systems such as the weather. However, most current deep learn-
036   ing models for weather are task-specific forecast emulators, which focus solely on the forecasting
037   problem. (See, however, Koldunov et al. (2024).) Key examples include FourCastNet (Pathak et al.,
038   2022), Pangu-Weather (Bi et al., 2022), GraphCast (Lam et al., 2022), FengWu (Chen et al., 2023),
039
      Stormer (Nguyen et al., 2023b) and AIFS (Lang et al., 2024). Machine learning models also show
      promise for longer-term subseasonal-to-seasonal forecasts (Weyn et al., 2021). Additionally, ML-
040
      based approaches are being explored to enhance climate predictions (see Mansfield et al., 2023;
041
      Eyring et al., 2024, for a review), with a focus on the development of ML-driven parameterizations
042   (Rasp et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019; Espinosa et al., 2022; Yuval & O’Gorman, 2023; Henn et al.,
043   2024; Gupta et al., 2024), bias corrections (Bretherton et al., 2022; Gregory et al., 2024), and assess-
044   ments of climate change impacts (Davenport & Diffenbaugh, 2021; Diffenbaugh & Barnes, 2023,
045   among others). There is fascinating emerging work that combines the strengths of the data-driven
046   and physics-based approaches (Kochkov et al., 2024; Husain et al., 2024; Roy et al., 2024). Fi-
047   nally, there are further large, task-specific models for nowcasting (Andrychowicz et al., 2023) and
048   downscaling (Mardani et al., 2024).
049   Looking beyond atmospheric sciences at developments in AI in general and language models in
050   particular, the last few years have been dominated by the emergence of foundation models. That
051   is, large AI models pretrained in a task-agnostic manner that can be effectively fine-tuned to ad-
052   dress a number of specific use cases. Despite the mirroring successes of large AI models in both
053   fields, applications of the foundation model principle to atmospheric sciences have been rare. Cli-
      maX (Nguyen et al., 2023a) and AtmoRep (Lessig et al., 2023) considered problems ranging from

      nowcasting to downscaling and bias corrections; Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024) focusses a number of
055   different forecasting problems.
056
057   To address this gap, we introduce Prithvi WxC, a large-scale foundation model for weather and cli-
058
      mate applications trained on the Modern-Era Retrospective analysis for Research and Applications,
      Version 2 (MERRA-2) (Gelaro et al., 2017). Prithvi WxC is a transformer-based deep learning ar-
059
      chitecture which combines ideas from several recent transformer architectures in order to effectively
060
      process regional and global dependencies of the input data and to capture longer sequence lengths
061   of tokens. Moreover, the model is capable of running in different spatial contexts. In addition we
062   introduce a new pretraining objective that blends masking and forecasting.
063
064
      The validation of Prithvi WxC extends from zero shot evaluations for reconstruction and forecasting
      to downstream tasks such as downscaling of weather and climate models, the prediction of hurricane
065
      tracks and atmospheric gravity wave flux parameterization.

068   2     P RITHVI W X C
069
070   Prithvi WxC has been designed to address several questions that arise when considering the meaning
071   of foundation models for atmospheric physics: Since weather models can run on the entire earth or
072   in a regional context, do we need specialized architectures for global and local problems? Do we
073   need to differentiate between models with zero and non-zero lead time? If we do consider tasks with
074   zero and non-zero lead time, what is a suitable pretext task for pretraining?
075
076   2.1    P RETRAINING OBJECTIVE
077
078   Forecast emulators are typically trained by predicting the state of the atmosphere Xt+δt at time
079   t + δt given the state at times t and t − δt. Some do so by directly regressing on physical quantities.
080
      For most the output is the tendency Xt+δt − Xt . Foundation models for vision on the other hand
      are frequently based on masked autoencoders (He et al., 2022). This is notable since masking is a
081
      natural pretext task for weather and climate as observational data is ungridded and sparse. Indeed,
082
      note that the emerging literature on models working directly on observation makes heavy use of
083   masking (Vandal et al., 2024; McNally et al., 2024). Moreover, the forecasting task breaks down for
084   δt = 0 while foundation model use cases satisfy no such constraint.
085
086
      In the end our pretraining objective combines masking with forecasting. Moreover, while our lack
      of constraints on δt makes it impossible to predict tendencies, we generalize the objective of Lam
087
      et al. (2022) to another source of free information, namely Climatology. Instead of predicting the
088
      difference from the current time stamp Xt , we model the deviation from historical climate at this
089   time, Ct . All in all, our pretraining objective is

114   2.3         A RCHITECTURE
115
116   At it’s core, Prithvi WxC is a 2D vision transformer. To keep it as flexible as possible, we aimed
117   not to use architecture elements that restrict to “rectangular” topologies for data. (Even though we
118   train on MERRA-2 data on a rectangular lat/lon grid, one can envision training or running inference
119
      directly on Gaussian grids.) Vanilla ViTs would satisfy this requirement, yet do not scale to large
      token counts. Considering the different flavors of scalable transformers we notice the findings of
120
      “Hiera” (Ryali et al., 2023). Here the authors show that it is possible to surpass the performance of
121
      Swin transformers (Liu et al., 2021) with a more flexible and simplified architecture. Turning back to
122   AI models for weather, Andrychowicz et al. (2023) made use of MaxViT (Tu et al., 2022) leverages
123   axial attention. In the end, our core idea is that if we pretrain the model using only attention, we keep
124   the core of the model flexible and can add convolutions at fine-tuning time to increase performance
125   when suitable. We do so by joining the approaches of Hiera and MaxViT.

148   In detail, the only constraint we impose on the data is the ability to structure tokens into windows
149   – akin to Swin, Hiera and MaxViT. After tokenization, our data takes the shape (batch, windows,
150   tokens, features), where the second dimension enumerates windows and the third tokens within
151   each window. Subsequently we alternate attention within a window and across windows. Modulo
152   masking, the latter is similar to (Tu et al., 2022). In what follows we will refer to attention within a
153   window as “local” and attention across windows as “global”. When masking, we can either mask out
154   entire global windows or individual tokens within a window. A byproduct of the latter is that global
155
      attention no longer connects the same token in each window. For an illustration of the attention and
      masking pattern see figure 1.

      2.4   P RETRAINING
172
173   We train Prithvi WxC in two phases. The first uses a 50% masking ratio and alternates “local”
174   and “global” masking. Moreover, we randomize the time steps for targets and inputs (δt and δτ )
175   choosing among 0, 6, 12 and 24 as well as 3, 6, 9 and 12 respectively. This phase uses 64 A100
176   GPUs and results in a highly flexible model which we use for our downscaling and gravity wave
177   parametrization experiments as well as for the zero-shot reconstruction evaluations.
178   The second phase makes a few optimizations to attune the model for forecasting applications: We
179   reduce the masking ratio to 50%, fix δt = δτ = 6 and introduce a Swin-shift (Liu et al., 2021)
180   in the now dense encoder. Following Lam et al. (2022) we introduce weights to training objective
181   equation 1, yet weigh u10m and v10m higher than t2m. See appendix B.4 for details. This version
182   of the model is used for the forecast evaluation as well as the hurricane-forecasting use case.

406   3.2   C LIMATE M ODEL PARAMETERIZATION FOR G RAVITY WAVE F LUX
407
408   Atmospheric gravity waves (GWs) are intermittent, small-scale (O(1) to O(1000) km) perturbations
409   generated around thunderstorms, jet disturbances, flows over mountains, etc. They belong to a class
410   of physical processes crucial to the earth’s momentum budget but only crudely represented in coarse-
411
      climate models which rely on inadequate physical parameterizations instead of resolving them. As
      such, the fine-tuning task is to use the latent space of Prithvi WxC to develop data-driven physical
412
      parameterizations to provide missing sub-grid scale variability in coarse-climate models at zero-lag.
413
      For this task, the model is fine-tuned using high-fidelity, high-resolution gravity wave data extracted
414   from ERA5 (which resolves a substantial portion of the atmospheric gravity waves).
415
416   We use four years of ERA5 global reanalysis on the 122 lowest vertical model levels and 30 km
417
      horizontal resolution at hourly-frequency to prepare the training data for fine-tuning. The model
      takes the zonal wind speed (u), meridional wind speed (v), temperature (T ), and pressure (p), along
418
      with positional variables latitude, longitude, and surface height as input. The model outputs the
419
      directional momentum fluxes carried by gravity waves mathematically expressed as the covariances
420   (u′ ω ′ , v ′ ω ′ ), and are computed using Helmholtz decomposition using the horizontal (u,v)=(U,V)
421   and vertical wind speeds (ω=OMEGA). Both the input and output are conservatively coarse-grained
422   to a 64×128 (≈ 300 km) latitude-longitude grid to be consistent with a typical coarse-climate model
423   and to remove phase dependencies of the calculated fluxes.
424
      The architecture schematic for the fine-tuning is a U-net like architecture shown in Figure 14: The
425
      frozen encoder is preceded by 4 learnable convolution blocks each with an increasing number of
426   hidden channels. Likewise, the frozen decoder is succeeded by 4 new learnable convolution blocks.
427   Since gravity wave flux prediction is an instantaneous flux calculation task, we fix the lead time δt
428   to zero. The model input now has shape [1, 488, 64, 128] where the 488 channels comprise the four
429   background variables u, v, t and p on 122 vertical levels each, and on a 64 × 128 horizontal grid,
430   as discussed above. The model was fine-tuned to produce an output with shape [1, 366, 64, 128]
431   comprising of the potential temperature, u′ ω ′ , and v ′ ω ′ on 122 vertical levels each. We emphasize
      that Prithvi WxC was pretrained on MERRA-2 data but is now fine-tuned with ERA5 data.

458   As a straightforward test, we look at the climatological distribution, i.e., the monthly-averaged mo-
459
      mentum fluxes in the upper troposphere, and compare the spatial distribution of the predicted di-
      rectional fluxes with the validation data from ERA5 (Figure 9). The prediction from the model
460
      closely agrees with the true flux distribution in the upper troposphere. The nature and properties
461
      of the waves over land can be significantly different from waves over the ocean. Therefore, get-
462   ting a strong agreement over both the ocean and the land indicates effective learning. For instance,
463   enhanced fluxes over the Rocky Mountains, the Andes, and the Himalayas indicates the fine-tuned
464   model skillfully predicts the stationary waves generated over mountain ranges. Likewise, the trop-
465   ical band of positive flux (in Figure 9b) in the tropics points to effective learning of non-stationary
466   gravity waves generated around intense convective and precipitation systems. In fact, the fine-tuning
467   model outperforms task-specific baselines created using MLPs and Attention U-Nets.
468   Without loss of generality, the same finetuning procedure can be applied to develop parameteriza-
469   tions for other sub-grid atmospheric processes of relevance to climate; albeit with some tweaks. A
470   coarse climate model with a typical resolution of O(100) km fails to capture most gravity wave
471   effects (or clouds, or fine-scale turbulence) due to its inability to resolve the smaller-scales. Owing
472   to periodic data assimilation and higher-resolution, numerical weather prediction models are largely
473   unaffected by these biases. Running climate models at a high-resolution over multiple centuries,
474   however, is computationally not so feasible. To address this, we have proposed one climate-focused
475   application of Prithvi WxC and demonstrated its effectiveness. This model can subsequently be in-
476
      tegrated with coarse-resolution climate models of varying complexity to account for the “missing”
      gravity wave physics and correct the physics tendencies. The accuracy of the predicted fluxes also
477
      points to the remarkable effectiveness of the fine-tuning process in blending task-specific data from
478
      heterogenous sources.

481   4   C ONCLUSIONS
482
483   This study introduces Prithvi WxC, a 2.3 billion parameter foundation model designed for weather
484   and climate applications. Trained on 160 atmospheric variables from the MERRA-2 dataset, Prithvi
485   WxC leverages a scalable and flexible transformer-based architecture to capture both regional and
      global dependencies in atmospheric data. Prithvi WxC addresses a diverse set of downstream tasks,


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      aligning with the foundation model paradigm prevalent in AI research. To achieve this, the model
487   introduces a new architecture and novel objective function. The latter combines masked reconstruc-
488   tion with forecasting, incorporating climatological information to enhance its generalizability.
489
490
      The zero-shot evaluation introduces reconstruction as a new benchmark and reveals that the model
      excels in forecasting at shorter lead times. We hypothesize that this strength stems from the masking
491
      objective, which encourages Prithvi WxC to grasp atmospheric dynamics with limited temporal
492
      progression.
493
494   When it comes to fine-tuning, it is important to highlight the diversity of datasets, parameters,
495   and resolutions addressed in the downscaling and parameterization examples. In both cases, we
496
      demonstrate that a pretrained, frozen transformer trained on a single dataset can be effectively com-
      bined with additional architectural components to achieve strong results on new tasks with different
497
      datasets. Furthermore, the CORDEX downscaling case showcases the model’s ability to operate
498
      in both global and regional contexts, a characteristic that we attribute to the heavy use of “global”
499   masking during pretraining.
500
501   Even though there is no previous work on AI-based downscaling using MERRA-2, we chose this
502
      example to isolate the model’s and architecture’s downscaling performance from questions of dis-
      tribution shift when changing datasets. Here, we found that the fine-tuned Prithvi WxC model
503
      improves by more than a factor of 4 over interpolation baselines. This 6x downscaling compares
504
      to an improvement factor of 2 when doing 2x downscaling with ERA5 data in the ClimateLearn
505   benchmarks. That is, we have doubled the performance for a threefold resolution increase, evidence
506   of strong performance. This is mirrored by the more applicable CORDEX example which compares
507   favorably to the results of Doury et al. (2023).
508
      Finetuning Prithvi WxC also demonstrates that large transformer-based foundation models can ef-
509
      fectively learn mesocale atmospheric evolution, helping to streamline, enhance, and accelerate the
510
      development of physical parameterizations in climate models, which in turn improves prediction
511   accuracy on interannual timescales. The fine-tuned model produces strong predictions across all
512   six hotspots, including both the relatively smoother fluxes over the Andes, Southern Ocean, New-
513   foundland, and the Scandinavian Mountains, as well as the more turbulent fluxes over the Pacific
514   Ocean and Southeast Asia. Notably, for the Andes (mountain waves) and the Southern Ocean (non-
515   mountain waves), the fine-tuned model achieves correlation coefficients of 0.99 and 0.97, respec-
516   tively, when compared to the observed fluxes.
517   The latent encoder-decoder space of Prithvi WxC foundation model captures a comprehensive un-
518   derstanding of atmospheric evolution by training on vast amounts of data, including winds, tem-
519   perature, humidity, radiation, and soil moisture. Instead of building task-specific ML-models from
520   scratch, these pretrained encoders can be used to develop more precise data-driven models of atmo-
521   spheric processes.

860
861   The climatology appearing in equation 1 was computed from 20 years of MERRA-2 data following
862   the methodology of the ERA-Interim climatology (Janoušek, 2011). That is, for each Julian day and
863   each hour of the day we aggregate all data across the last 20 years. Subsequently we apply a 61-day
      rolling window weighted average to this. The weights are given by a second order polynomial. Thus

      the climatology resolves the day-night cycle. There are 365 × 8 timestamps and each pixel is based
865   on 20 × 61 = 1220 data points. We used the same 20 year period that we used for training; that is
866   1980-2019.

871   constants σ and σC unconstrained leads to instabilities during training. This is essentially due to the
872

      QL at high model levels can be as small as 10−26 at level 34. To avoid such extreme values upsetting

977
978    Handling of lead time signal Our model has both an encoder and decoder component. The two
979
       are architecturally identical except that data in the encoder is masked. Once is encoded, mask tokens
       are introduced for the much shallower decoder where data is now dense. This follows the paradigm
980
       of (He et al., 2022) exactly.
981
982    Given the objective of equation 1, one can wonder whether one should introduce the lead time signal
983    at the encoder stage or only at the decoder stage. The rationale for doing so would be for the encoder
984    to learn a representation of the data independent of lead time. Once again we trained on 16 GPUs for
985
       24 hours. Table 8 shows the result. By introducing the lead time already in the encoder pretraining
       loss improves by 3%. However, one has to be careful with this result: In principle we are interested
986
       in performance for downstream tasks. So it is perceivable that a more difficult pretext task where
987
       lead time information is only introduced in the decoder leads to more powerful representations in the
988    encoder. Still, given the result of this particular ablation, we chose to introduce a lead time signal
989    already in the encoder. Incidentally one should note the results of (Nguyen et al., 2023b) which
990    found optimal results when using adaptive layer normalization to handle varying lead times. This
991    follows similar findings in other domains. See e.g. (Peebles & Xie, 2023).

1054
       In its pretraining configuration Prithvi WxC comprises 25 encoder and 5 decoder blocks. As both
1055   the encoder and decoder start and end with local attention, 13 (3) of these blocks perform local and
1056   12 (2) global attention respectively. The internal dimension is 2,560. With 16 attention heads and an
1057   MLP multiplier of 4 this results in 2.3 billion parameters. We use a token size of 2 by 2 pixel. Each
1058   window measures 30 by 32 pixel or 15 by 16 tokens. With these choices we are dealing with 51,840
1059   tokens per sample yet are keeping the length of the global and local sequence roughly balanced.
1060   Note that both token and window size can be changed when tuning the model and we will do so
1061   repeatedly below. The model now consumes a bit more than 43 GB of GPU memory in pretraining.
1062   If we keep masking at 50% we are able to backpropagate through 4 autoregressive steps on a 80 GB
1063
       A100. (Masking only applies to the first autoregressive step.) If the data becomes dense (i.e. 0%
       masking) this reduces to 3 steps. Since the data becomes dense in the decoder and our pretraining
1064
       data does live on a rectangular grid, we add a Swin-shift to the decoder layers. The overall scale was
1065
       chosen to ensure that autoregressive “rollout” training is still possible.
1066
1067   We make use of Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP) as well as flash attention (via scaled dot
1068   product attention). We train the model with bfloat16 precision. However, to ensure numeric stabil-
1069
       ity we only use bfloat16 for the transformer layers. The input and output layers remain at float32.
       Finally, we use activation checkpointing. For validation-time inference we tested both float32 and
1070
       bfloat16. While float32 comes with a considerable speed penalty, we observed no signifidant accu-
1071
       racy gains.

       B.4   P RETRAINING PROTOCOL
1074
1075   We train Prithvi WxC in two phases. The first phase uses 5% drop path, a 50% masking ratio
1076   and alternates “local” and “global” masking from gradient descent step to gradient descent step.
1077   Moreover, for each sample we select a random forecast lead time (among 0, 6, 12 and 24 hours
1078   ahead) as well as a random delta between inputs (-3, -6, -9, -12). With this randomization, we train
1079   the model on 64 A100 GPUs and batch size 1 for 100,000 gradient descent steps. After 2,500 steps
       of linear warm-up we perform cosine-annealing from 10−4 to 10−5 . This results in a highly flexible


                                                        20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       model that we use for our downscaling and gravity wave parametrization experiments as well as for
1081   the zero-shot reconstruction evaluations.
1082
1083   To further attune the model to forecasting applications, we make a few changes: We reduce the
1084
       masking ratio to 0% and add a Swin-shift to the encoder. Also, we set drop path to 0%. In addition,
       we fix both the forecast lead time and input delta to six hours so that there is no more randomiza-
1085
       tion. Keeping the learning rate constant at 10−5 , we tune the model with 1, 2 and 3 autoregressive
1086
       steps on a varying compute footprint ranging from 16 to 48 GPUs. In this phase we also modify
1087   the training objective equation 1 by using additional weights. For the vertical parameters, weights
1088   depend linearly on pressure level (in hPa). In addition, we weight H, ω, T, U and V with 1 yet cloud,
1089   PL, QI, QL with 0.1. For the surface parameters, we weigh u10m and v10m with 1, SLP and t2m
1090   with 0.1 and the remaining parameters with 0.01. Essentially this follows (Lam et al., 2022) with the
1091   exception that we found it beneficial to swap the weights for t2m and u10m as well as v10m while
1092   suppressing all variables which are not standard in the AI-forecast emulation literature by a factor of
1093   ten. This version of the model is used for the forecast evaluation as well as the hurricane-forecasting
1094   use case.

       D.2     C LIMATE M ODEL PARAMETERIZATION FOR G RAVITY WAVE F LUX
1300
1301   This task uses the pretrained Prithvi WxC to create a fine-tuned model for climate applications.
1302   The fundamental question being: can we (re-)use large AI models to develop improved, data-driven
1303   climate model parameterizations for small-scale atmosphere-ocean processes?
1304
       Background: Atmospheric gravity waves (GWs) are intermittent, small-scale (O(1) to O(1000)
1305   km) perturbations generated around thunderstorms, jet disturbances, flow over mountains, etc.
1306   (Fritts & Alexander, 2003; Achatz et al., 2023). Gravity waves couple the different layers of the
1307   atmosphere by carrying surface momentum to stratospheric and even mesospheric heights. Yet,
1308   most climate models fail to resolve them owing to limited resolution. Thus, they belong to a class
1309   of key physical processes crucial to the earth’s momentum budget but only crudely represented in
1310   coarse-climate models using inadequate physical parameterizations.
1311   An improved parametric representation of gravity waves in comprehensive climate models can po-
1312   tentially improve the representation of the seasonal transitions (McLandress et al., 2012), clear air
1313   turbulence (Plougonven & Zhang, 2014), Antarctic extreme heat (Choi et al., 2024), and tropical
1314   predictability (Baldwin et al., 2001); leading to more certain climate predictions and advancements
1315   in mechanistic understanding.
1316
       From an AI perspective, this downstream prediction task moves from predicting the large-scale
1317   atmospheric state prediction to smaller-scale state prediction, and leverages the cross-scale learn-
1318   ing from pre-training. As such, the finetuning task is defined to use the latent space of Prithvi
1319   to develop data-driven physical parameterizations to provide missing sub-grid scale variability in
1320   coarse-climate models at zero-lag. This is somewhat akin to the downscaling task where CORDEX
1321   is used to augment missing sub-grid information during fine-tuning. For this task, the model is fine-
1322   tuned using high-fidelity, high-resolution gravity wave data extracted from ERA5 (which resolves a
1323   substantial portion of the atmospheric gravity waves, if not all).
1324
1325   D.2.1    E XTRACTING GW DATA FOR FINETUNING .
1326
       The goal is to accurately predict the momentum fluxes carried by waves generated in different parts
1327   of the globe by different processes, given the background atmospheric state. The approach is similar
1328   to that followed by traditional single-column parameterizations (Lott & Miller, 1997; Scinocca,
1329   2003; Kim et al., 2003). Here, we do so by learning from high-resolution data. In very simple
1330   terms, given the background atmospheric state around a mountain (e.g., Andes), or around tropical
1331   storm, can our ML model predict whether the waves are spontaneously generated, and if they are,
1332   calculate the net momentum fluxes they carry; not unlike predicting the cloud cover for a given set
1333   of atmospheric conditions.
1334   We use four years of ERA5 global reanalysis on 137 model vertical levels and 30 km horizontal
1335   resolution at hourly-frequency to prepare the training data for fine-tuning. The top 15 levels, i.e.,
1336   levels above 45 km are removed due to artifical sponge damping in effect, so effectively 122 vertical
1337   levels. The model takes the zonal wind speed (u), meridional wind speed (v), the temperature
1338   (T ), and pressure (p), along with positional variables latitude, longitude, and surface height as input.
1339   These variables collectively describe the background state of the atmosphere. The model outputs the
1340   directional momentum fluxes carried by gravity waves. These fluxes describe the net instantaneous
1341
       momentum the gravity waves carry. These directional fluxes are mathematically expressed as the
       covariances (u′ ω ′ , v ′ ω ′ ), and are computed using Helmholtz decomposition using the horizontal
1342
       (u,v)=(U,V) and vertical wind speeds (ω=OMEGA). Both the input and output are conservatively
1343
       coarse-grained to a 64×128 (≈ 300 km) latitude-longitude grid to be consistent with a typical coarse-
1344   climate model and to remove phase dependencies of the calculated fluxes.
1345
