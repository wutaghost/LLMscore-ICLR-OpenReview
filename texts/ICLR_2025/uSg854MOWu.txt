                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Similar to surprising performance in the standard deep learning, deep nets trained
015            by adversarial training also generalize well for unseen clean data (natural data).
016            However, despite adversarial training can achieve low robust training error, there
017            exists a significant robust generalization gap. We call this phenomenon the Clean
018            Generalization and Robust Overfitting (CGRO). In this work, we study the CGRO
019            phenomenon in adversarial training from two views: representation complexity and
020            training dynamics. Specifically, we consider a binary classification setting with N
021            separated training data points. First, we prove that, based on the assumption that
022            we assume there is poly(D)-size clean classifier (where D is the data dimension),
023            ReLU net with only Õ(N D) extra parameters is able to leverages robust mem-
024            orization to achieve the CGRO, while robust classifier still requires exponential
025
               representation complexity in worst case. Next, we focus on a structured-data case
               to analyze training dynamics, where we train a two-layer convolutional network
026
               with Õ(N D) width against adversarial perturbation. We then show that a three-
027
               stage phase transition occurs during learning process and the network provably
028
               converges to robust memorization regime, which thereby results in the CGRO.
029            Besides, we also empirically verify our theoretical analysis by experiments in
030            real-image recognition datasets.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Nowadays, deep neural networks have achieved excellent performance in a variety of disciplines,
036   especially including in computer vision (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Kirillov
037   et al., 2023) and natural language process (Devlin et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
038
      2022). However, it is well-known that small but adversarial perturbations to the natural data can
      make well-trained classifiers confused (Biggio et al., 2013; Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al.,
039
      2014), which potentially gives rise to reliability and security problems in real-world applications and
040
      promotes designing adversarial robust learning algorithms.
041
042   In practice, adversarial training methods (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Madry et al., 2017; Shafahi et al.,
043   2019; Zhang et al., 2019; Pang et al., 2022) are widely used to improve the robustness of models by
044
      regarding perturbed data as training data. However, while these robust learning algorithms are able
      to achieve high robust training accuracy (Gao et al., 2019), it still leads to a non-negligible robust
045
      generalization gap (Raghunathan et al., 2019), which is also called robust overfitting (Rice et al.,
046
      2020; Yu et al., 2022).
047
048   To explain this puzzling phenomenon, a series of works have attempted to provide theoretical
049   understandings from different perspectives. Despite these aforementioned works seem to provide
050
      a series of convincing evidence from theoretical views in different settings, there still exists a gap
      between theory and practice for at least two reasons.
051
052   First, although previous works have shown that adversarial robust generalization requires more data
053   and larger models (Schmidt et al., 2018; Gowal et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Bubeck & Sellke, 2023), it
      is unclear that what mechanism, during adversarial training process, directly causes robust overfitting.

096   2    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Empirical Works on Robust Overfitting. One surprising behavior of deep learning is that over-
099   parameterized neural networks can generalize well, which is also called benign overfitting that deep
100   models have not only the powerful memorization but a good performance for unseen data (Zhang
101   et al., 2017; Belkin et al., 2019). However, in contrast to the standard (non-robust) generalization, for
102   the robust setting, Rice et al. (2020) empirically investigates robust performance of models based on
103   adversarial training methods, which are used to improve adversarial robustness (Szegedy et al., 2013;
104
      Madry et al., 2017), and the work Rice et al. (2020) shows that robust overfitting can be observed on
      multiple datasets, including CIFAR10 and ImageNet.
105
106   Theoretical Works on Robust Overfitting. A list of works (Schmidt et al., 2018; Balaji et al.,
107   2019; Dan et al., 2020) study the sample complexity for adversarial robustness, and their works
      manifest that adversarial robust generalization requires more data than the standard setting, which


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      gives an explanation of the robust generalization gap from the perspective of statistical learning
109   theory. And another line of works (Tsipras et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019) propose a principle called
110   the robustness-accuracy trade-off and have theoretically proven the principle in different setting,
111   which mainly explains the widely observed drop of robust test accuracy due to the trade-off between
112   adversarial robustness and clean accuracy. Recently, Li et al. (2022) investigates the robust expressive
113   ability of neural networks and shows that robust generalization requires exponentially large models.
114
      Feature Learning Theory of Deep Learning. The feature learning theory of neural networks
115   (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2020a;b; 2022; Shen et al., 2022; Jelassi & Li, 2022; Jelassi et al., 2022; Chen
116   et al., 2022; Chidambaram et al., 2023) is proposed to study how features are learned in deep learning
117   tasks, which provide a theoretical analysis paradigm beyond the neural tangent kernel (NTK) theory
118   (Jacot et al., 2018; Du et al., 2018; 2019; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019). In this work,
119   we make a first step to understand clean generalization and robust overfitting (CGRO) phenomenon
120   in adversarial training by analyzing feature learning process under our theoretical framework about
121   structured data.
122   Memorization in Adversarial Training. Dong et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2021) empirically and theo-
123   retically explore the memorization effect in adversarial training for promoting a deeper understanding
124   of model capacity, convergence, generalization, and especially robust overfitting of the adversarially
125   trained models. However, different from their works, the concept clean generalization and robust
126   overfitting (CGRO) proposed in our paper focuses on both robust overfitting and high clean test
127   accuracy, which means that there is surprisingly no clean memorization or clean overfitting.

179   4    A NALYZING THE CGRO P HENOMENON FROM THE V IEW OF
180        R EPRESENTATION C OMPLEXITY
181
182   In this section, we provide a theoretical understanding of the CGRO phenomenon from the view of
183   representation complexity. First, We present the data assumption as follow.
184
      For the data distribution D and ℓp -robust radius δ > 0, the supporting set of the data input X can be
185
      divided into two sets X+ and X− that correspond to the positive and negative classes respectively, i.e.
186
      X+ := {X ∈ X : (X, y) ∈ D, y = 1} and X− := {X ∈ X : (X, y) ∈ D, y = −1}.
187
188
      Assumption 4.1. (Bounded) There exists a absolute constant R > 0 such that, with high probability
      over the data distribution D, it holds that the data input X ∈ [−R, R]D .
189
190   Recall the definition of CGRO classifier (Definition 3.4). W.l.o.g., we can only focus on the case
191   X ⊂ [−R, R]D .
192
      Assumption 4.2. (Well-Separated) we assume that the separation between the positive and negative
193   classes is large than twice the robust radius, i.e. distp (X+ , X− ) > 2δ.
194
195   This assumption is necessary to ensure the existence of a robust classifier, and which is indeed
196   empirically observed in real-world image classification data (Yang et al., 2020).
197   Assumption 4.3. (Polynomial-Size Clean Classifier Exists) we assume that there exists a clean
198   classifier fclean : X → R represented as a ReLU network with poly(D) parameters such that
199   LD (fclean ) = o(1) but Lp,δ
                               D (fclean ) = Ω(1).
200
201   In Assumption 4.3, the asymptotic notations o(1) and Ω(1) are defined w.r.t. the data dimension D.
202   It means that the clean classifier with moderate size can perfectly classify the natural data but fails
203   to classify the adversarially perturbed data, which is consistent with the common practice that well-
204
      trained neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2013; Raghunathan
      et al., 2019).

530
531
      To summarize, we study the puzzling clean generalization and robust overfitting (CGRO) phenomenon
532
      in adversarial training and present theoretical explanations: from the perspective of representation
533
      complexity, we prove that the CGRO classifier is efficient to achieve by leveraging robust memo-
534   rization regarding the training data, while robust generalization requires excessively large model
535   capacity in worst case, which may lead adversarial training to the CGRO regime; from the perspective
536   of training dynamics, we propose a three-stage analysis technique to analyze the feature learning
537   process of adversarial training under our structured-data framework, and it shows that two-layer
538   neural network trained by adversarial training provably learns the partial true feature but memorizes
539   the random noise from training data, which thereby causes the CGRO regime. On the empirical side,
      we confirm our theoretical findings above by real-world vision datasets and synthetic data simulation.

847   datasets, MNIST and CIFAR10. During adversarial training, we use cyclical learning rates and mixed
848
      precision technique (Wong et al., 2020). For the MNIST dataset, we use a LeNet architecture and

851   perturbation radius ϵ0 , and then we compute empirical average of maximum gradient norm and

854

855   Learning Process. We also focus on the dynamics of loss landscape over input during the adversarial
856   learning process. Thus, we compute empirical average of maximum gradient norm within different

      robustly memorize training data. However, the test curve of CIFAR10 ascents within training radius
