009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Despite the strong performance of Transformers, quadratic computation complex-
012                ity of self-attention presents challenges in applying them to vision tasks. Lin-
013                ear attention reduces this complexity from quadratic to linear, offering a strong
014                computation-performance trade-off. To further optimize this, automatic pruning
015                is an effective method to find a structure that maximizes performance within a
016                target resource through training without any heuristic approaches. However, di-
017
                   rectly applying it to multi-head attention is not straightforward due to channel mis-
                   match. In this paper, we propose an automatic pruning method to deal with this
018
                   problem. Different from existing methods that rely solely on training without any
019
                   prior knowledge, we integrate channel similarity-based weights into the pruning
020                indicator to preserve the more informative channels within each head. Then, we
021                adjust the pruning indicator to enforce that channels are removed evenly across all
022                heads, thereby avoiding any channel mismatch. We incorporate a reweight mod-
023                ule to mitigate information loss due to channel removal and introduce an effective
024                pruning indicator initialization for linear attention, based on the attention differ-
025                ences between the original structure and each channel. By applying our pruning
026                method to the FLattenTransformer on ImageNet-1K, which incorporates original
027                and linear attention mechanisms, we achieve a 30% reduction of FLOPs in a near
028
                   lossless manner. It also has 1.96% of accuracy gain over the DeiT-B model while
                   reducing FLOPs by 37%, and 1.05% accuracy increase over the Swin-B model
029
                   with a 10% reduction in FLOPs as well. The proposed method outperforms pre-
030
                   vious state-of-the-art efficient models and the recent pruning methods.

033   1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Transformer has achieved remarkable success in various computer vision tasks based on attention
036   mechanisms that effectively capture long-range dependencies. The attention module generates an
037   attention map by utilizing the query Q ∈ RN ×C and key K ∈ RN ×C to extract the relationships
038   between tokens, and then projects the value V ∈ RN ×C to obtain a feature map with global infor-
039   mation. It requires a computational cost of O(N 2 C), which is quadratic with respect to the number
040   of tokens N . Despite providing excellent performance, the quadratic complexity with respect to N
041   poses significant challenges for deployment on mobile and edge devices.
042   Recent research attempts to mitigate this issue by designing efficient transformers. Some approaches
043   propose network architectures that limit the number of tokens (Wang et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021);
044   Hassani et al. (2023)). By reducing the number of tokens, they cut down computation cost while
045   trying to maintain the performance. However, this results in limitations of the receptive field, con-
046   straining the capture of global dependencies. Other approaches propose a new attention mechanism
047   that can replace original attention (Kitaev et al. (2020); Shaker et al. (2023); Han et al. (2023)).
048
      Linear attention is a method that maintains the ability to capture long-range dependencies while re-
      ducing computational complexity linearly. They first approximate Softmax function by replacing it
049
      with a simple activation or a tailored function. By changing the computation order from (Q·K T )·V 1
050
      to Q · (K T · V )2 , they obtain an attention mechanism with computational complexity of O(N C 2 ),
051   which is quadratic with respect to channels instead of tokens.

075   Despite the strength of automatic pruning, applying it directly to Transformer is not trivial. The
076   original attention mechanism is structured with multiple heads, as shown in Fig. 2(a). It enables
077   the model to collectively attend to information from various representation subspaces at different
078   positions (Vaswani et al. (2017)). Fig. 2(b) illustrates the issues when applying automatic pruning
079   without considering multi-head attention. Removing all channels from a specific head eliminates its
080
      representation subspace, which restricts the range of features that can be extracted. Consequently,
      this leads to a notable reduction in the expression capacity compared to the original network. It
081
      can induce mixed channels from different representation subspaces, resulting in the extraction of
082
      features that are entirely different from those of the original network. We refer to these issues as the
083   channel mismatch problem.
084
085   Most Transformer pruning research focuses on the original attention mechanism used in DeiT or
086
      SwinTransformer. Since linear attention has a computation complexity of O(N C 2 ), however, prun-
      ing the same number of channels results in a greater reduction in computational cost compared to the
087
      original attention. Based on this advantage, we propose an automatic multi-head attention pruning
088
      method that can be effectively applied to both original and linear attention mechanisms. As in previ-
089   ous approaches (Xiao et al. (2019); Li et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2024)), we apply a pruning indicator
090   to identify and eliminate redundant elements through a learning process. Existing automatic pruning
091   methods rely solely on training without considering the expression capacity of the original network.
092   In contrast, we assign weights according to head-wise similarity when training the pruning indicator
093   to retain the most informative factors for each head. This approach ensures that the representation
094   subspaces closely resemble the original attention mechanism, minimizing the loss in expression ca-
095   pacity. To address the channel mismatch issue that arises during reconfiguration as in Fig. 2(b),
096   we introduce a pruning indicator adjustment process, which involves head-wise ranking followed by
097
      rank-wise averaging. It handle the problem by balancing the pruning ratio for each head, as shown in
      Fig. 2(c). Our approach leverages the similarity between channels during the training of the pruning
098
      indicator, enabling the representation of removed channels through a combination of the remaining
099
      ones. Consequently, we compensate for the information loss by incorporating a reweighting module
100   to adjust the scale of the remaining channels. Additionally, we introduce a method to initialize the
101   pruning indicator for linear attention, ensuring that channel removal is not excessive.
102

142   2.1   E FFICIENT V ISION T RANSFORMER
143
144   Transformer (Vaswani et al. (2017)) exhibits exceptional performance in the NLP field due to their
145   ability to effectively capture long-range dependencies. The Vision Transformer (ViT) has success-
146   fully adapted these models for image classification, achieving outstanding performance (Dosovitskiy
147
      et al. (2021)). However, the quadratic computational complexity of the original attention module in
      Transformers poses challenges for various vision applications. Several methods address this concern
148
      by limiting the number of tokens. Pyramid Vision Transformer (PvT) (Wang et al. (2021)) progres-
149
      sively limits the number of tokens using spatial reduction attention, which controls the feature map
150   size in the patch embedding. Deformable Attention Transformer (DAT) (Xia et al. (2022)) selects
151   the positions of keys and values in a data-dependent manner, considering only attentive tokens.
152   SwinTransformer (Liu et al. (2021)) divides the input into windows and performs original attention
153   within these limited regions. Neighborhood Attention Transformer (NAT) (Hassani et al. (2023))
154   localizes original attention by considering only the nearest neighboring pixels related to the query.
155   Other researches improve efficiency by integrating convolution operations into transformer models.
156   Convolutional vision Transformer (CvT) (Wu et al. (2021)) uses convolutional projections instead
157   of linear projections to control efficiency. CMT (Guo et al. (2022)) proposes a hybrid network of
158
      transformers and convolutions, achieving a better trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.
159   Another approach to achieve efficient vision transformers is to approximate the original attention
160   with linear complexity operations. EdgeNeXt (Maaz et al. (2022)) applies transposed attention along
161   the channel dimension instead of the spatial dimension, achieving linear complexity with respect to
      tokens. Reformer (Kitaev et al. (2020)) replaces the dot-product operation with locality-sensitive


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      hashing, reducing the computation to O(n log n). LinFormer (Wang et al. (2020)) approximates the
163   original attention matrix using low-rank matrix factorization, achieving linear complexity. Swift-
164   Former (Shaker et al. (2023)) demonstrates that key-value interaction can be replaced with a linear
165   layer without performance degradation, effectively reducing computation. A different line of re-
166   search involves changing the order of original attention computations to achieve a complexity of
167   O(N C 2 ). This requires effectively replacing Softmax. CosFormer (Qin et al. (2022)) replaces
168   Softmax with ReLU activation and cosine-based distance re-weighing. SOftmax-Free Transformer
169   (SOFT) (Lu et al. (2021)) uses a Gaussian kernel function to replace Softmax and achieves lin-
170   ear complexity through low-rank decomposition. Castling-ViT (You et al. (2023)) extracts spectral
171
      similarity between Q and K using a linear angular kernel. FLattenTransformer (Han et al. (2023))
      reduces computation to linear complexity with focused linear attention, effectively maintaining the
172
      expressiveness of original attention.

175   2.2   N ETWORK P RUNING
176
177
      Pruning involves identifying and removing redundant components to reduce the network size. Prun-
      ing has proven highly effective in original Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Lin et al. (2020);
178
      Hou et al. (2022); Gao et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023)). Building on the strong performance of
179
      CNNs, there has been researches aimed at enhancing the efficiency of Vision Transformers through
180   pruning. One approach, token pruning, involves finding and removing unnecessary tokens, effec-
181   tively reducing the computational load of the attention mechanism (Bolya et al. (2022); Wei et al.
182   (2023); Tang et al. (2023)). Another approach is to reduce the size of the Transformer model itself.
183   SViTE (Chen et al. (2021b)) determines redundant components via sparse training and prunes ac-
184   cordingly. Unified Visual Transformer Compression (UVC) (Yu et al. (2022b)) reduces network size
185   by combining pruning methods with various compression techniques. Novel ViT (NViT) (Yang et al.
186   (2023)) enables global structural pruning based on a Hessian-based structural pruning criterion. It
187   uses only their heuristic criterion when pruning multi-heads. In contrast, the proposed method takes
188
      into account the representation space of each head, minimizing the information loss of the original
      model’s attention mechanism.
189
190   To address these issues, there are automatic pruning methods that identify redundant components
191   through training. AutoPrune (Xiao et al. (2019)) determines the necessity of weights based on
192   trainable auxiliary parameters. MetaPruning (Liu et al. (2019)) involves training a meta-network,
193   PruningNet, to find the optimal structure for a given target network. DHP (Li et al. (2020)) ap-
194
      plies differentiable hypernetworks to determine the configuration of each channel in the backbone
      network. Instead of using a hypernetwork, another approach involves a pruning module to each
195
      component, learning the importance and removing components (Li et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2024)).
196
      VTP (Zhu et al. (2021)) applies automatic pruning to Transformers by using trainable gate variables
197   to identify and remove unnecessary components through learning. Automatic pruning can identify
198   the efficient network structure without heuristics, but directly applying them to Transformers is not
199   trivial. Unlike the traditional CNN structure, an automatic pruning method should take into account
200   the multi-head structure in Transformers without losing expression capacity. In this paper, we pro-
201   pose a multi-head-aware automatic pruning method, which can be applied to both original attention
202   and linear attention mechanisms.

289   3.2   R EWEIGHT M ODULE
290
      The removal of channels results in information loss, requiring a method to compensate for this to
291
      minimize performance degradation. As in Fig. 4(a), our method learns indicators by considering the
292   similarity of channels for each head, and subsequently removes channels based on these values. The
293   channels being removed can be represented by the weighted sum of the remaining channels, due to
294   their high similarity with other channels.
295
      Fig. 4(b) illustrates the reweight module. The reweighting method employes a simple channel atten-
296
      tion mechanism inspired by SENet (Hu et al. (2018)). The input token F ∈ RN ×C is compressed
297
      into a feature map FC ∈ R1×C by taking the mean. The compressed feature map is encoded into
298   weights to compensate for each channel through a linear layer and T anh activation function. This
299   is multiplied with the query, allowing the passage of information from the removed channels to
300   the remaining channels. By applying this module, compensation can be provided for the removed
301   channels, minimizing performance degradation.

      To verify the proposed method, we apply our Automatic Multi-head Attention Pruning (AMAP)
353   to the FLattenTransformer (Han et al. (2023)). This network is composed of original attention
354   blocks from the SwinTransformer (Liu et al. (2021)) and linear attention blocks. Unlike previous
355   pruning methods, the proposed approach can be applied to both types of attentions without specific
356   heuristics. The largest model, AMAP-L, is a compressed model of FLatten-Swin-B, with a size
357   of 11.1G. AMAP-B compresses the FLatten-Swin-S model to 6.2G, while AMAP-S and AMAP-T
358   compress the FLatten-Swin-T model to 4.2G and 1.3G, respectively. While other models follow
359   the hybrid structure of FLattenTransformer, the smallest model, AMAP-T, consists solely of linear
360   attention to achieve a higher compression ratio. We evaluate the performance of the compressed
361
      models on the classification task using the ImageNet-1K dataset (Deng et al. (2009)). To compress
      the network, we employ a search and refine process. For more detailed information on the training,

535   In this paper, we introduce an AMAP (Automatic Multi-head Attention Pruning) method. Integrat-
536   ing similarity weights into the trainable scheme allows us to progressively achieve a more optimal
537   structure compared to other pruning methods that rely on deterministic metrics. Through an ablation
538   study, we validate the impact of the proposed similarity-based pruning indicator, reweight module,
539   and initialization method. Comparative analysis against vision transformer variants and previous
      pruning methods demonstrates the superior efficiency and performance trade-off of AMAP.

744   The proposed method for compressing the network involves a two-step process: a search to find
745   the efficient structure and a refinement to restore the loss information. Fig. 8 illustrates the search
746
      pipeline. First, the pruning indicator is initialized (Sec. 3.3). Then, the proposed similarity-based
      weights are computed, and an automatic pruning method is applied to reassign the scores for all
747
      heads using the pruning indicator, masking unnecessary channels (Sec. 3.1). During the search
748

749   are used to train the network (Sec. 3.4).

      search process. Reweight modules are added to compensate for the information loss caused by

      pruning (Sec. 3.2). Subsequently, the network is trained using cross-entropy loss.
