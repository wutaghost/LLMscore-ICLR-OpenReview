                                                A BSTRACT
033
              We propose MeshLRM, a novel LRM-based approach that can reconstruct a high-
034
              quality mesh from merely four input images in less than one second. Different
035
              from previous large reconstruction models (LRMs) that focus on NeRF-based re-
036           construction, MeshLRM incorporates differentiable mesh extraction and render-
037           ing within the LRM framework. This allows for end-to-end mesh reconstruction
038           by fine-tuning a pre-trained NeRF LRM using mesh rendering. Moreover, we
039           improve the LRM architecture by simplifying several complex designs in pre-
040           vious LRMs. MeshLRM’s NeRF initialization is sequentially trained with low-
041           and high-resolution images; this new LRM training strategy enables significantly
042           faster convergence and thereby leads to better quality with less compute. Our ap-
043           proach achieves state-of-the-art mesh reconstruction from sparse-view inputs and
044
              also allows for many downstream applications, including text-to-3D and single-
              image-to-3D generation. https://meshlrm.github.io/

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   High-quality 3D mesh models are the core of 3D vision and graphics applications that are specif-
050   ically optimized for them, such as 3D editing, rendering, or simulation tools. 3D mesh assets are
051   usually created manually by expert artists or reconstructed from multi-view 2D images. Tradition-
052   ally this has been done with complex photogrammetry systems (Snavely et al., 2006; Furukawa
053   & Ponce, 2009; Schonberger & Frahm, 2016; Schönberger et al., 2016), though recent neural rep-
      resentations, such as NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020), offer a simpler end-to-end pipeline through


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      per-scene optimization. These neural representations are however typically volumetric (Mildenhall
055   et al., 2020; Müller et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Yariv et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
056   2021a) and converting them into meshes requires additional post-optimization (Tang et al., 2022;
057   Yariv et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Rakotosaona et al., 2023). Furthermore, both traditional and
058   neural reconstruction approaches require a large number of input images and often long processing
059   time (up to hours), limiting their interactivity.
060
      Our goal is efficient and accurate 3D asset creation via sparse-view mesh reconstruction with direct
061   feed-forward network inference and no post-optimization. We base our approach on recent large
062   reconstruction models (LRMs) (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
063   2023a) for 3D reconstruction and generation. Existing LRMs use triplane NeRFs (Chan et al.,
064   2022) as the 3D representation for high rendering quality. While these NeRFs can be converted into
065   meshes via a Marching Cube (MC) (Lorensen & Cline, 1998) post-processing step. If done naively,
066   this typically leads to a significant drop in rendering quality and geometric accuracy.
067   We propose to address this with MeshLRM, a novel transformer-based large reconstruction model,
068   designed to directly output high-fidelity 3D meshes from sparse-view inputs. Specifically, we incor-
069   porate differentiable surface extraction and rendering into a NeRF-based LRM. We apply a recent
070   Differentiable Marching Cube (DiffMC) (Wei et al., 2023) technique to extract an iso-surface from
071   the triplane NeRF’s density field and render the extracted mesh using a differentiable rasterizer
072   (Laine et al., 2020). This lets us train MeshLRM end-to-end using a mesh rendering loss, optimiz-
073   ing it to produce density fields which are more compatible with the Marching Cube step, leading to
074   more realistic and high-quality meshes.
075   We train MeshLRM by initializing the model using volumetric NeRF rendering; since our meshing
076   components do not introduce any new parameters these pre-trained weights are a good starting point
077   for our fine-tuning. We however find that fine-tuning a mesh-based LRM with differentiable MC
078   (DiffMC) remains challenging. The primary challenge lies in the (spatially) sparse gradients from
079   the DiffMC operation, that only affect surface voxels and leave the vast empty space untouched.
080   This leads to poor local minima for model optimization and manifests as floaters in the reconstruc-
081
      tions. We address this with a novel ray opacity loss that ensures that empty space along all pixel
      rays maintains near-zero density, effectively stabilizing the training and guiding the model to learn
082
      accurate floater-free surface geometry. Our end-to-end training approach reconstructs high-quality
083
      meshes with rendering quality that surpasses NeRF volumetric rendering (as shown in Tab. 5).
084
085   Our approach leverages a simple and efficient LRM architecture consisting of a large transformer
086   model that directly processes concatenated multi-view image tokens and triplane tokens with self-
087
      attention layers to regress final triplane features that we use for NeRF and mesh reconstruction.
      In particular, we simplify many complex design choices used in previous LRMs (Li et al., 2024;
088
      Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), including the removal of pre-trained DINO modules in image
089
      tokenization and replacing the large triplane decoder MLP with small two-layer ones. We train
090   MeshLRM on the Objaverse dataset (Deitke et al., 2023) with a progressive resolution strategy with
091   low-resolution early training and high-resolution fine-tuning. These design choices lead to a state-
092   of-the-art LRM model with faster training and inference and higher reconstruction quality.
093
      In summary, our key contributions are:
094
095   • A LRM-based framework that integrates differentiable mesh extraction and rendering for end-to-
096     end few-shot mesh reconstruction.
097   • A ray opacity loss for improved DiffMC-based training stability.
098   • A simplified LRM architecture and enhanced training strategies for fast and high-quality recon-
099
        struction.
100   We benchmark MeshLRM for 3D reconstruction (on synthetic and real datasets) and 3D generation
101   (in combination with other multi-view generation methods). Fig. 1 showcases high-quality mesh
102   outputs from MeshLRM that were each reconstructed in under one second.

107   Mesh Reconstruction. Despite the existence of various 3D representations, meshes remain the most
      widely used format in industrial 3D engines. Reconstructing high-quality meshes from multi-view


                                                         2

108
      images is a long-standing challenge in computer vision and graphics. Classically, this is addressed
109   via a complex multi-stage photogrammetry pipeline, integrating techniques such as structure from
110   motion (SfM) (Snavely et al., 2006; Agarwal et al., 2011; Schonberger & Frahm, 2016), multi-
111   view stereo (MVS) (Furukawa & Ponce, 2009; Schönberger et al., 2016), and mesh surface extrac-
112   tion (Lorensen & Cline, 1998; Kazhdan et al., 2006). Recently, deep learning-based methods have
113   also been proposed to address these problems for higher efficiency and accuracy (Vijayanarasimhan
114   et al., 2017; Tang & Tan, 2018; Yao et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2018; Im et al., 2019; Cheng et al.,
115   2020). However, the photogrammetry pipeline requires dense input images and long processing
116   time, aiming at exact object reconstruction, but often suffers from challenging calibration or appear-
117
      ance effects on the captures object. As opposed to this classic many-image acquisition process, our
      approach enables sparse-view mesh reconstruction through direct feed-forward network inference
118
      in an end-to-end manner.
119
120
      Neural Reconstruction. Neural rendering techniques have recently gained significant attention
121
      for their ability to produce high-quality 3D reconstructions for realistic rendering (Zhou et al., 2018;
122   Lombardi et al., 2019; Mildenhall et al., 2020; Kerbl et al., 2023). Most recent methods are based on
123   NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and reconstruct scenes as various volumetric radiance fields (Milden-
124   hall et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022)
125   with per-scene rendering optimization. Though radiance fields can be converted into meshes with
126   Marching Cubes (Lorensen & Cline, 1998), the quality of the resulting mesh is not guaranteed. Pre-
127   vious methods have aimed to transform radiance fields into implicit surface representations (SDFs)
128   (Wang et al., 2021a; Yariv et al., 2021; Oechsle et al., 2021), enhancing mesh quality from Marching
129   Cubes. In addition, some methods (Yariv et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2022; Rakoto-
130
      saona et al., 2023) attempt to directly extract meshes from radiance fields using differentiable surface
      rendering. However, these methods all require dense input images and long per-scene optimization
131
      time, which our approach avoids.
132
133   On the other hand, generalizable neural reconstruction has been explored, often leveraging MVS-
134   based cost volumes (Chen et al., 2021; Long et al., 2022; Johari et al., 2022) or directly aggregating
135   multi-view features along pixels’ projective epipolar lines (Yu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021b;
136
      Suhail et al., 2022). While enabling fast and few-shot reconstruction, these methods can only handle
      small baselines, still requiring dense images with local reconstruction in overlapping windows to
137
      model a complete object. More recently, transformer-based large reconstruction models (LRMs)
138
      (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023) have been proposed and
139   achieved 3D NeRF reconstruction from highly sparse views. Inspired by this line of work, we
140   propose a more efficient LRM architecture, incorporating DiffMC techniques (Wei et al., 2023) to
141   enable high-quality sparse-view mesh reconstruction via direct feed-forward inference.
142
143   3D Generation. Generative models have seen rapid progress with GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014)
144   and, more recently, Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) for image and video generation.
145   In the context of 3D generation, many approaches utilize 3D or multi-view data to train 3D genera-
146   tive models with GANs (Wu et al., 2016; Henzler et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2022; Chan et al., 2022)
147   or diffusion models (Anciukevičius et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c; Chen et al.,
148   2023a; Liu et al., 2023b), which are promising but limited by the scale and diversity of 3D data.
149   DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) proposed to leverage the gradient of a pre-trained 2D diffusion
150
      model to optimize a NeRF for text-to-3D generation with a score distillation sampling (SDS) loss,
      achieving diverse generation results beyond common 3D data distributions. This approach inspired
151
      many follow-up approaches, to make the optimization faster or improve the results quality (Lin et al.,
152
      2023; Wang et al., 2023d; Tang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Metzer et al., 2022). However, these
153   SDS-based methods rely on NeRF-like per-scene optimization, which is highly time-consuming,
154   often taking hours. More recently, a few attempts have been made to achieve fast 3D generation by
155   utilizing pre-trained 2D diffusion models to generate multi-view images and then perform 3D recon-
156   struction (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024b; Tochilkin et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
157   2024c). In particular, Instant3D (Li et al., 2024) leverages an LRM to reconstruct a triplane NeRF
158   from four sparse generated images. In this work, we focus on the task of sparse-view reconstruction
159   and propose to improve and re-target a NeRF LRM to directly generate meshes with higher quality.
160   Unlike MeshGPT (Siddiqui et al., 2024), which sequentially predicts triangles using a transformer,
161
      we incorporate differentiable marching cubes and rasterization into the LRM framework to gen-
      erate meshes. Concurrently, InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024) makes a similar attempt by combining

184   3       M ETHOD
185
186   We present MeshLRM enabling the reconstruction of high-quality meshes in under 1 second. We
187   start with describing our backbone transformer architecture (Sec. 3.1), which simplifies and im-
188   proves previous LRMs. We then introduce our two-stage framework for training the model: we
189   first (Sec. 3.2) train the model to predict NeRF from sparse input images by supervising volume
190   renderings at novel views, followed by refining the model for mesh surface extractions (Sec. 3.3) by
191   performing differentiable marching cubes on the predicted density field and minimizing a surface
192
      rendering loss with differentiable rasterization.
193
194   3.1       M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
195
196
      As shown in Fig. 2, we propose a simple transformer-based architecture for MeshLRM, mainly
      consisting of a sequence of self-attention-based transformer blocks over concatenated image tokens
197
      and triplane tokens, similar to PF-LRM (Wang et al., 2023a). In contrast to PF-LRM and other
198
      LRMs (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023), we simplify the designs for both image
199   tokenization and triplane NeRF decoding, leading to fast training and inference.
200
201   Input posed image tokenization. MeshLRM adopts a simple tokenizer for posed images, inspired
202   by ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). We convert the camera parameters for each image into Plücker
203   ray coordinates (Plücker, 1865) and concatenate them with the RGB pixels (3-channel) to form a
204   9-channel feature map. We then split the feature map into non-overlapping patches, and linearly
205   transform them to input our transformer. With this process, the model does not need additional
206   positional embedding as in ViT, since Plücker coordinates contain spatial information.
207   Note that our image tokenizer is much simpler than previous LRMs that use a pre-trained DINO
208   ViT (Caron et al., 2021) for image encoding. We find that the pre-trained DINO ViT is unnecessary,
209   possibly because it is mainly trained for intra-view semantic reasoning, while 3D reconstruction re-
210   quires inter-view low-level correspondences. Vision transformer models (like DINO) often lack the
211   zero-shot ability to handle untrained higher resolutions and require extensive fine-tuning to general-
212   ize (see Fig. 8). By dropping this per-view DINO encoding, the model becomes shallower, enabling
213   a better connection between raw pixel information and 3D-related processing.
214
215   Transformer. We concatenate multi-view image tokens and learnable triplane (positional) embed-
      dings, and feed them into a sequence of transformer blocks (Vaswani et al., 2017), where each block


                                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      is comprised of self-attention and MLP layers. We add layer normalization (Ba et al., 2016) be-
217   fore both layers (i.e., Pre-LN architecture), and use residual connections. This deep transformer
218   network enables comprehensive information exchange among all the tokens, and effectively models
219   intra-view, inter-view, and cross-modal relationships. The output triplane tokens, contextualized by
220   all input views, are then decoded into the renderable triplane NeRF while the output image tokens
221   are dropped. More specifically, each triplane token is unprojected with a linear layer and further un-
222   patchified to 8×8 triplane features via reshaping. 1 All predicted triplane features are then assembled
223   into the final triplane NeRF.
224
225   Tiny density and color MLPs. Previous LRMs (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023;
226   Wang et al., 2023a) use a heavy shared MLP (e.g., 9 hidden layers with a hidden dimension of 64) to
227
      decode densities and colors from triplane features. This design leads to slow rendering in training.
      This is a bottleneck for MeshLRM since we need to compute a dense density grid, using the MLP
228
      and triplane features, for extracting the mesh with DiffMC. Therefore, we opt to use tiny MLPs with
229
      a narrower hidden dimension of 32 and fewer layers. In particular, we use an MLP with one hidden
230   layer for density decoding and another MLP with two hidden layers for color decoding. Compared
231   to a large MLP, our tiny MLPs lead to 50% speed-up in training without compromising quality
232   (see Tab. 4). We use separate MLPs since the density MLP and color MLP are used separately in
233   the Marching Cubes and surface rendering processes, respectively. We empirically find this MLP
234   separation largely improves the optimization stability for DiffMC fine-tuning (described in Sec. 3.3),
235   which is critical to large-scale training.
236
237   While being largely simplified, our transformer model can effectively transform input posed images
238   into a triplane NeRF for density and color decoding. The density and color are then used to achieve
239   both radiance field rendering for 1st-stage volume initialization (Sec. 3.2) and surface extraction &
240   rendering for 2nd-stage mesh reconstruction (Sec. 3.3).
241
242   3.2      S TAGE 1: E FFICIENT T RAINING FOR VOLUME R ENDERING
243
244
      We train our model with ray marching-based radiance field rendering Mildenhall et al. (2020) to
      bootstrap our model, providing good initialized weights for mesh reconstruction. Instead of training
245
      directly using high-res (512×512) input images like prior LRM works (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al.,
246
      2024), we develop an efficient training scheme, inspired by ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). Specifi-
247   cally, we first pretrain our model with 256×256 input images until convergence and then finetune it
248   for fewer iterations with 512×512 input images. This training schedule leads to significantly better
249   quality than training at 512-res from scratch given the same amount of compute (see Tab. 1).
250
251   256-res pretraining. The pre-training uses 256-resolution images for both model input and output.
252   We use a batch size of 8 objects per GPU and sample 128 points per ray during ray marching. In
253   contrast to training with 512-res images from scratch (like previous LRMs), our training efficiency
254   benefits from the low-res pre-training from two factors: shorter sequence length for computing self-
255   attention and fewer samples per ray for volume rendering (compared to our high-res fine-tuning).
256
257   512-res finetuning. For high-res fine-tuning, we use 512-resolution images for the model input and
258   output. We use a batch size of 2 per GPU and densely sample 512 points per ray. Here, we com-
259
      pensate for the increased computational costs (from longer token sequences and denser ray samples)
      by reducing the batch size 4 times, achieving a similar per iteration time to the low-res pretrain-
260
      ing. It’s also worth noting that our resolution-varying pretraining and finetuning scheme benefits
261
      from the use of Plücker coordinates for camera conditioning. It avoids the positional embedding
262   interpolation (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021) and has inherent spatial smoothness.
263
264
      Loss. We use an L2 regression loss Lv,r and a perceptual loss Lv,p (proposed in Chen & Koltun
265   (2017)) to supervise the renderings from both phases. Since rendering full-res images is not afford-
266   able for volume rendering, we randomly sample a 128×128 patch from each target 256- or 512-res
267   image for supervision with both losses. We also randomly sample 4096 pixels per target image for
268   additional L2 supervision, allowing the model to capture global information beyond a single patch.

      3.3   S TAGE 2: S TABLE T RAINING FOR S URFACE R ENDERING
276
277   Once trained with volume rendering, our model already achieves high-fidelity sparse-view NeRF
278   reconstruction, which can be rendered with ray marching to create realistic images. However, di-
279   rectly extracting a mesh from the NeRF’s density field results in significant artifacts as reflected
280   by Tab. 2. Therefore, we propose to fine-tune the network using differentiable marching cubes and
281   differentiable rasterization, enabling high-quality feed-forward mesh reconstruction.
282
283   Mesh extraction and rendering. We compute a 2563 density grid by decoding the triplane features
284   and adopt a recent differentiable marching cubes (DiffMC) technique (Wei et al., 2023) to extract
285   mesh surface from the grid. The DiffMC module is based on a highly optimized CUDA implemen-
286
      tation, much faster than existing alternatives (Shen et al., 2023; 2021), enabling fast training and
      inference for mesh reconstruction.
287
288   To compute the rendering loss, we render the generated mesh using the differentiable rasterizer
289   Nvdiffrast (Laine et al., 2020) and utilize the triplane features to (neurally) render novel images from
290   our extracted meshes. This full rendering process is akin to deferred shading where we first obtain
291   per-pixel XYZ locations via differentiable rasterization before querying the corresponding triplane
292
      features and regressing per-pixel colors using the color MLP. We supervise novel-view renderings
      with ground-truth images, optimizing the model for high-quality end-to-end mesh reconstruction.
293
294   However, using the rendering loss alone leads to high instability during training and severe floaters
295   in the meshes (see Fig. 3). This is caused by the sparsity of density gradients in mesh rendering:
296   unlike volume rendering which samples points throughout the entire view frustum, mesh rendering
297   is restricted to surface points, lacking gradients in the empty scene space beyond the surface.
298
299   Ray opacity loss. To stabilize the training and prevent the formation of floaters, we propose to use
300
      a ray opacity loss. This loss is applied to each rendered pixel ray, expressed by:
301                              Lα = ||αq ||1 ,   αq = 1 − exp(−σq ||p − q||)                            (2)
302
303
      where p represents the ground truth surface point along the pixel ray, q is randomly sampled along
      the ray between p and camera origin, and σq is the volume density at q; when no surface exists for
304
      a pixel, we sample q inside the object bounding box and use the far ray-box intersection as p.
305
306   In essence, this loss is designed to encourage the empty space in each view frustum to contain near-
307   zero density. Here we minimize the opacity value αq , computed using the ray distance from the
308   sampled point to the surface. This density-to-opacity conversion functions as an effective mecha-
309
      nism for weighting the density supervision along the ray with lower loss values for points sampled
      closer to the surface. An alternative approach would be to directly regularize the density σq using an
310
      L1 loss, but we found this to lead to holes in surfaces as all points contribute the same, regardless of
311
      their distance to the surface. A more detailed comparison between our loss and the standard density
312   and depth losses can be found in Appendix A.
313
314
      Combined losses. To measure the visual difference between the results renderings after our DiffMC
315   step and ground-truth (GT) images, we use an L2 loss Lm,r and a perceptual loss Lm,p similar to
316   stage 1. We still supervise the opacity using Lα , a ray opacity loss around the surface points using
317   the GT depth maps. In addition, to further improve our geometry accuracy and smoothness, we
318   apply an L2 normal loss Ln to supervise the face normals of our extracted mesh with GT normal
319   maps in foreground regions. Our final loss for mesh reconstruction is
320
                               Lm = Lm,r + wm,p ∗ Lm,p + wα ∗ Lα + wn ∗ Ln                                (3)
321
322   where we use wm,p = 2, wα = 0.5 and wn = 1 in our experiments. Since mesh rasterization is
323   significantly cheaper than volume ray marching, we render the complete images (512×512 in our
      experiment) for supervision, instead of the random patches+rays used in Stage 1 volume training.

345   geometry quality (more details in Appendix H).

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   We present MeshLRM, a novel Large Reconstruction Model that directly outputs high-quality
531
      meshes. Our model leverages the Differentiable Marching Cubes (DiffMC) method and differen-
      tiable rasterization to fine-tune a pre-trained NeRF-based LRM, trained with volume rendering. As
532
      DiffMC requires backbone efficiency, we propose multiple improvements (tiny shared MLP and
533
      simplified image tokenization) to the LRM architecture, facilitating the training of both NeRF and
534   mesh targeting models. We also find that a low-to-high-res training strategy significantly acceler-
535   ates the training of the NeRF-based model. Compared with previous work, our method provides
536   both quality and speed improvements and generates high-quality meshes. Finally, we show that our
537   method can be directly applied to applications such as text-to-3D and image-to-3D generation. As
538   meshes are the most widespread format for 3D assets in the industry, we believe our method takes
539   a step towards better and faster integration of 3D asset creation networks in existing 3D workflows
      and manipulation tools.

887   A    D IFFERENT BETWEEN RAY OPACITY LOSS AND OTHER REGULARIZATIONS
888
889   The main difference between our ray opacity loss and previous approaches lies in the selection of
890   supervision points. Standard density regularization uniformly supervises all points in space, includ-
891   ing those on the mesh surface as well as in empty regions, which can lead to gradients that are less
892   effective at guiding the model toward a clean surface. On the other hand, standard depth loss only
893   supervises points on the mesh surface; once floaters appear during training, its gradients can move
894   these floaters but cannot easily eliminate them. In contrast to these standard losses, our opacity loss
895
      supervises the entire space between the surface and the camera, effectively preventing floaters and
      stabilizing the training process.

1066   H                            A DDITIONAL I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1067
1068   The transformer size follows the transformer-large config (Devlin et al., 2019). It has 24 layers and
1069   a model width of 1024. Each attention layer has a 16 attention head and each head has a dim of 64.
1070   The intermediate dimension of the MLP layer is 4096. We use GeLU activation inside the MLP and
1071   use Pre-LN architecture. The layer normalization is also applied after the input image tokenization,
1072   and before the triplane’s unpachifying operator.
1073
       For the training of both stages, we use AdamW with β2 = 0.95. Other Adam-specific parameters
1074   are set as default and we empirically found that the model is not sensitive to them. A weight decay
1075   of 0.05 is applied to all parameters excluding the bias terms and layer normalization layers. The
1076   learning rate for stage 1 is 4e − 4. We also use cosine learning rate decay and a linear warm-up in
1077   the first 2K steps. For stage 2, we use a learning rate of 1e − 5, combined with cosine learning rate
1078   decay and a linear warm-up during the first 500 steps. In total, there are 10k fine-tuning steps. The
1079   resolution of DiffMC is 256 within a [−1, 1]3 bounding box, which is consistent with the triplane
       resolution.

       For the CD metric, we sample points only on the visible surface from the testing views. Since
1081   the ground-truth meshes from GSO Downs et al. (2022) and OmniObject3D Wu et al. (2023) are
1082   obtained through scanning, their interior structures cannot be considered reliable. For example, we
1083   found that the interiors of shoes in GSO are incorrectly represented, with a face filling the interior,
1084   which contradicts common sense. To address this, we cast rays from all testing views and sample
1085   100,000 points at the ray-surface intersections for each object, following a strategy similar to that
1086   used in Tang et al. (2022).

1089
1090   For the GSO dataset, the single-view input is the first thumbnail image, while for the OmniObject3D

1092   evenly distributed around the object to capture a full 360-degree view and use BlenderProc to render
1093   images at a resolution of 320×320. The object is normalized to fit within a [−1, 1]3 bounding box.

1095   and elevation angles of [60, 90, 120] degrees.
