009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained vision foundation models have transformed many computer vision
012            tasks. Despite their strong ability to learn discriminative and generalizable features–
013            crucial for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, their impact on this task remains
014            underexplored. Motivated by this gap, our study investigates vision foundation
015            models in OOD detection. Our findings show that even without complex designs, a
016
               pre-trained DINOv2 model, utilizing a simple scoring metric and no fine-tuning,
               outperforms all prior state-of-the-art models, which typically depend on fine-
017
               tuning with in-distribution (ID) data. Furthermore, while the pre-trained CLIP
018
               model struggles with fine-grained OOD samples, DINOv2 excels, revealing the
019            limitations of CLIP in this setting. Building on these insights, we explore how
020            foundation models can be further optimized for both ID classification and OOD
021            detection when ID data is available for fine-tuning. From a model perspective, we
022            propose a Mixture of Feature Experts (MoFE) module, which partitions features
023            into subspaces. This mitigates the challenge of tuning complex data distributions
024            with limited ID data and enhances decision boundary learning for classification.
025            From a data perspective, we introduce a Dynamic-β Mixup strategy, which samples
026            interpolation weights from a dynamic beta distribution. This adapts to varying
027
               levels of learning difficulty across categories, improving feature learning for more
               challenging categories. Extensive experiments and ablation studies demonstrate
028
               the effectiveness of our approach, significantly outperforming baseline methods.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The task of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Sehwag et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2018; Hsu et al.,
034   2020; Lee et al., 2018b) aims to equip models with the capability to discern whether input images
035   originate from unknown OOD classes or belong to in-domain (ID) classes. Mainstream OOD
036   detection methods (Du et al., 2023; Tao et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2018a) focus on
037   learning features and classifiers (Sun & Li, 2022; Liang et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
038   2022a) from ID data and then develop a score metric (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2022; Liu
039
      et al., 2020; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017) to determine whether a sample belongs to ID or OOD.
      Despite significant advancements, the fundamental challenge in OOD detection is establishing a
040
      feature space with high discriminative capacity that can effectively distinguish OOD samples from ID
041
      samples, which is still unresolved. Recently, vision foundation models (Maxime et al., 2023; Radford
042   et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023) trained on large-scale datasets have demonstrated
043   the ability to learn robust and generalizable features, benefiting numerous tasks (Yang et al., 2024;
044   Zhang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2021). This raises the question: with such powerful
045   models and feature representations, does OOD detection remain a problem?
046
      Although some studies have explored the use of CLIP for OOD detection, a comprehensive compara-
047
      tive analysis of vision foundation models remains lacking. To address this, we begin by investigating
048   whether vision foundation models can effectively serve as OOD detectors and their limitations. In
049   this study, we focus on two representative models: CLIP, trained on 400 million text-image pairs
050   using a text-image contrastive loss, and DINOv2, trained on 142 million web-curated images using
051   self-supervised learning. We conduct an analysis using ImageNet-1K as ID data and OOD data
052   from four datasets (see Tab. 1). Without any model tuning, we directly utilize the features from the
053   models for OOD evaluation. Our results reveal that with a simple KNN metric, DINOv2 surpasses
      leading OOD detectors that have been fine-tuned on ID data across all evaluated OOD datasets (see


                                                          1

054
      Tab. 1). Additionally, our study highlights that CLIP’s pre-trained feature space is less effective for
055   fine-grained tasks like iNaturalist, where DINOv2 performs significantly better (see Tab. 1 and Fig. 1).
056   We hope these findings will inspire further research in this area.
057
058
      While vision foundation models have achieved impressive performance in OOD detection, there is
      still room for improvement, particularly on in-domain data with large semantic spaces (e.g., 29.27%
059
      FPR95 on the ImageNet-1K OOD benchmark). This prompts us to investigate whether foundation
060
      models can be further optimized, leveraging available ID data to improve both ID classification
061   and OOD detection. However, as the number of semantic classes increases, the complexity of
062   the decision boundaries required to distinguish between ID and OOD data grows as well. This
063   heightened complexity creates challenges when fine-tuning foundation models on limited ID data,
064   often forcing a trade-off between model fitting on ID data and preserving generalizable features. Our
065   empirical experiments support this observation: when fine-tuning the DINOv2 pre-trained model on
066   ImageNet-1K ID data, its performance declined on three out of four OOD datasets (see Tab. 1). This
067   suggests that as the model adapts to the ID data distribution, fine-tuning on a complex in-domain
068   distribution with limited ID data can compromise the discriminative and generalizable features crucial
069
      for effective OOD detection.
070   To address these challenges, we propose a Mixture-of-Feature-Expert (MoFE) module, which divides
071   the complex semantic space into multiple subspaces, with each expert specializing in a specific
072   subspace. This approach reduces the difficulty of fitting complex data distributions from limited data
073   by breaking the problem into smaller subproblems. This division eases the optimization process while
074   maintaining the generalizability of features (see Fig. 4 in Appx. A). MoFE operates by partitioning
075
      the original feature space into K subspaces based on feature similarities within the ID dataset. Each
      subspace is assigned to a dedicated expert, and a router assigns samples to the appropriate expert
076
      based on these partitions. During training, the router is supervised by the partition assignments to
077
      ensure accurate sample-to-expert mapping. Importantly, in our design, each expert focuses solely on
078   optimizing features within its designated partition. This helps prevent interference between features
079   from different partitions, preserving feature diversity and generalizability. Additionally, given that
080   data augmentation has been shown to enhance generalization during fine-tuning, we introduce a
081   novel Mixup data augmentation strategy to further improve feature learning for ID classification and
082   OOD detection with vision foundation models. Our design is based on the observation that different
083   categories exhibit varying levels of learning difficulty. In the raw feature space of vision foundation
084   models, some categories shows great discriminativeness (see Fig. 1d and Fig. 1e), while others do not
085   (see Fig. 1f). For categories that are already well-represented, synthesizing dissimilar samples via
086
      vanilla Mixup can blur the decision boundary between ID and OOD, leading to degraded performance
      (see Fig. 3 in Appx. A). Thus, unlike existing Mixup strategies that treat all categories equally, our
087
      approach makes Mixup weight sampling category-dependent by adjusting the sampling distribution
088
      (i.e. beta distribution) dynamically, taking into account their learning difficulties.
089
090   Extensive experiments on existing benchmark datasets demonstrate consistent performance improve-
091   ments. Notably, we achieve a significant performance boost of +11.33% in FPR95 on the challenging
092
      ImageNet-1K dataset, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach in enhancing vision foundation
      models. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
093
094
095   • We are the first to conduct a comprehensive study on pre-trained foundation models for OOD
096     detection. The promising results emphasize that discriminative and generalizable features are
097
        the most important factors for effective OOD detection. Notably, DINOv2 and CLIP exhibit
        distinct behaviors when handling fine-grained datasets, underscoring the potential advantages of
098
        the learning paradigm of DINOv2 .
099
100   • To tailor pre-trained vision foundation models for improved ID classification and OOD detection
101     during fine-tuning, we introduce a novel MoFE module to effectively fit ID data distribution and
102     preserve generalizable features, along with a Dynamic-β Mixup strategy to enhance generalization
103     and boost OOD detection performance.
104
105   • We establish new baselines for OOD detection using foundation models. Our extensive experimental
106     results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, achieving significant improvements
107     over several competitive baseline methods.

151   Out-of-distribution Detection. When deploying a machine learning model in real-world scenarios,
152   it is crucial for a reliable classifier not only to accurately classify known in-distribution (ID) samples,

154   by incorporating an OOD detector alongside the classification model f . OOD can be formulated as
155   a binary classification task. During testing, the objective is to determine whether a sample x ∈ X
156
      belongs to Pin (ID) or not (OOD). This decision can be made using a scoring metric S(x):

      2.2                    I S OOD D ETECTION STILL A P ROBLEM IN THE ERA OF F OUNDATION M ODELS ?
189
190
      Most previous studies have primarily focused on fine-tuning ImageNet-pretrained model (Rus-
191
      sakovsky et al., 2015) on ID data for OOD detection. However, ImageNet pre-training has been
192   shown to be relatively outdated in the contemporary development of vision foundation models. In
193   particular, vision foundation models (e.g., DINOv2), which are trained on vast amounts of data, have
194   demonstrated their ability to generate discriminative and generalizable features. This development
195   has inspired us to reexamine this issue within the context of large foundation models. In this section,
196   we aim to investigate and analyze the potential of pre-trained vision foundation models as effective
197   OOD detectors without fine-tuning.
198   Experimental Setup. We perform our evaluation on a challenging OOD detection benchmark that
199   utilizes ImageNet-1K as ID data and selects samples from iNATURALIST, SUN, PLACES, and
200   TEXTURES as OOD samples. We choose two representative vision foundation models, namely
201   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and DINOv2 (Maxime et al., 2023). Without any model tuning, we
202   directly use the features extracted from these models for OOD detection evaluation to assess whether
203   they are already sufficiently capable of OOD detection. To emphasize the significance of our findings,
204   we also compare them with state-of-the-art methods that involve fine-tuning an ImageNet pre-trained
205   model on the ID dataset.
206   Result Analysis. As shown in Tab. 1, we compare the OOD detection performance of foundation
207   models without fine-tuning and SOTA ImageNet pre-trained models with fine-tuning, We observe:
208
      (1) When using traditional metric scores (i.e., Energy, MSP, and Maxlogit), CLIP does not exhibit supe-
209
      rior performance. However, after implementing negative prompts, CLIP-based methods (Wang et al.,
210   2023; Nie et al., 2024) (e.g., LSN (Nie et al., 2024)) outperformed the method (e.g., MOS (Huang
211   & Li, 2021)) by over 9%. Specifically, negative prompts learn the concept of “not this category”,
212   opposite to the normal positive prompt “a photo of this category”. Since CLIP is trained by a vast
213   amount of categories, it can summarize the concept of “not this category” in its feature space through
214   negative prompts, which helps to establish a more discriminative decision boundary. Please refer
215   to the papers (Wang et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2024) for more details. These results indicate that the
      raw feature space exhibits good OOD performance since it outperforms the finetuning-based method

237
      capture image details, and DINOv1 (Caron et al., 2021) that enhances the feature discriminativeness
      by contrastive learning objectives.
238
239   Further Discussion. In summary, DINOv2, without requiring any fine-tuning, can already function
240   as a high-performing OOD detector, surpassing previous approaches and underscoring the importance
241   of discriminative and generalizable features for OOD detection. Besides, as shown in Fig. 1c and
242   Fig. 1f, we also show some failure examples, where the models exhibit particularly poor feature
243
      discriminability. It demonstrates that foundation models, even DINOv2, still have room to improve
      and cannot generalize well across the entire feature space. Moreover, though there is a consensus
244
      that fine-tuning on the ID data can improve OOD performance (Vaze et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020;
245
      Hendrycks et al., 2019; Tack et al., 2020), we find that this doesn’t hold in the context of foundation
246   models, particularly on in-domain data with large semantic spaces. For instance, when we fine-tune
247   DINOv2 on ImageNet-1K ID data and evaluate the fine-tuned model, the performance declines on
248   three out of the four OOD datasets (see naive finetuning in Tab. 1). The implementation details of this
249   finetuning can be referred to Appx. A.2. These findings motivate us to design specialized fine-tuning
250   methods for vision foundation models to achieve better OOD performance.

361   of xi are discriminative enough, a small λ, which leads to a dissimilar sample, is not necessary

499   5    R ELATED W ORK
500
501   Out-of-Distribution Detection The goal of OOD detection is to detect OOD images from the
502   test dataset (containing both ID and OOD images). Designing the score function is the most
503   popular method in OOD detection tasks. The scores are mainly derived from three sources: the
504   probability (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Hendrycks et al., 2019), the logits (Hendrycks et al., 2019;
505   Liu et al., 2020), and the feature (Lee et al., 2018b; Ndiour et al., 2020). Some studies (Khalid et al.,
506   2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Sehwag et al., 2021) focus on leveraging contrastive learning to enhance
507   the feature representation. Other studies show that synthesizing pseudo samples (Du et al., 2022;
508
      Sehwag et al., 2021; Tack et al., 2020;?) as OOD instances is also a promising approach to make the
      feature space more compact.
509
510   OOD Detection with Foundation Models There are some existing OOD detection methods (Wang
511   et al., 2023; Esmaeilpour et al., 2022; Ming et al., 2022; Ming & Li, 2024; Nie et al., 2024) leveraging
512   foundation models. Maximum Concept Matching (MCM) (Ming et al., 2022) proposes a simple yet
513   effective zero-shot OOD detection method by aligning visual features with textual concepts. Some
514
      other studies (Wang et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2024) explore negative prompts to learn the diversity of
      negative features, enabling more accurate detection of OOD samples. Although these studies have
515
      made great progress by leveraging CLIP to enhance the performance in existing benchmarks, they
516
      only explore and fine-tune CLIP. In our studies, we explore different foundation models and explore
517   a better fine-tuning paradigm.
518
519   Mixture of Experts Mixture of Experts has been studied independently in both computer vi-
520
      sion (Riquelme et al., 2021; Lou et al., 2021; Mustafa et al., 2022) and natural language process-
      ing (Shazeer et al., 2017; Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2021; Komatsuzaki et al., 2022). These
521
      works are studied in the context of conditional computation, which is to increase the number of model
522
      parameters without a proportional increase in computational cost. Currently, some studies (Chen
523   et al., 2024; Krishnamurthy et al., 2023) explore improving expert specialization and leveraging MoE
524   to mitigate data conflict problems, where some data might interfere with each other. In our study,
525   we introduce MoFE to the out-of-distribution task in the context of foundation models and build
526   specialized OOD detectors for different feature subspaces.

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper studies the OOD detection task within the context of foundation models. Our study
      shows that vision foundation models (e.g., DINOv2) are effective OOD detectors, suggesting high-
532
      quality and generalizable feature space is essential for OOD detection. our study highlights that
533
      CLIP’s pre-trained feature space is less effective for fine-grained tasks like iNaturalist18, where
534   DINOv2 performs significantly better, which worths further exploration. Second, we find that simply
535   fine-tuning foundation models on ID data will result in performance degradation due to the loss
536   of generalization ability. Thus, we propose MoFE and a Dynamic-β Mixup data augmentation to
537   enhance the feature learning during fine-tuning. We conduct extensive experiments and ablation
538   studies to validate the effectiveness of our approach, significantly surpassing baseline methods. We
539   believe enhancing the discriminativeness and generalization ability of learned features is the key to
      OOD detection. We hope our investigation could inspire more future studies.

      A.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
705
706   We adopt ViT-Base (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as the backbone. When using pre-training paradigms
707   of CLIP and DINOv2, we directly initialize ViT from their weights. Besides, when using CLIP, we
708   leverage CLIPN (Wang et al., 2023) as the baseline method and we follow their scoring metric. For
709   DINOv2, we use DINOv2 with standard cross-entropy loss as the baseline method and the scoring
710   metric is KNN (Sun et al., 2022). When using DINOv2, we first conduct linear probing for 3 epoches
711
      to ensure its training stability. Our models are trained with AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter,
      2019) with βs = {0.9, 0.95}, with an effective batch size of 1024 on 8 NVIDIA 3090 GPUs. The
712
      values for weight decay and layer decay are 0.05 and 0.75, The training epochs are set to 40. We set a
713
      cosine learning rate schedule and the minimum learning rate is 1e-6.

      A.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF NAIVE FINETUNING

724   A.3    L IMITATION
725
726
      We summarize the limitations of our research as follows: Although CLIP and DINOv2 are currently
      the top foundation models, they still have inherent shortcomings. For instance, CLIP only utilizes
727
      image-text pairs for contrastive learning between text and images, lacking self-supervised learning on
728
      images. This results in its inability to capture fine-grained image details, leading to poor performance
729   on granularity. On the other hand, DINOv2 employs a large number of images for self-supervised
730   learning, yet it still performs poorly on certain categories, indicating potential long-tail distribution
731   issues in its pre-training data. The current benchmarks for OOD (Out-of-Distribution) detection have
732   significant limitations. While they utilize datasets like ImageNet-1K, which cover a wide range of
733   categories, the OOD data itself is relatively limited.
