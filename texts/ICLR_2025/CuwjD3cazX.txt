009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is widely utilized in the Reinforcement
012            Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) phase to align Large Language Models
013            (LLMs) with human preferences, thereby enhancing both their harmlessness and
014            efficacy. However, it has been observed that DPO tends to over-optimize for
015            verbosity, which can detrimentally affect both performance and user experience.
016            In this paper, we conduct an in-depth theoretical analysis of DPO’s optimization
017
               objective and reveal a strong correlation between its implicit reward and data
               length. This correlation misguides the optimization direction, resulting in length
018
               sensitivity during the DPO training and leading to verbosity. To address this
019
               issue, we propose a length-desensitization improvement method for DPO, termed
020            LD-DPO. The proposed method aims to desensitize DPO to data length by
021            decoupling explicit length preference, which is relatively insignificant, from
022            the other implicit preferences, thereby enabling more effective learning of the
023            intrinsic preferences. We utilized two settings (Base and Instruct) of Llama2-13B,
024            Llama3-8B, and Qwen2-7B for experimental validation on various benchmarks
025            including MT-Bench and AlpacaEval 2. The experimental results indicate that
026            LD-DPO consistently outperforms DPO and other baseline methods, achieving
027            more concise responses with a 10-40% reduction in length compared to DPO.
028
               We conducted in-depth experimental analyses to demonstrate that LD-DPO can
               indeed achieve length desensitization and align the model more closely with
029
               human-like preferences.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized Natural Language Processing (NLP) by em-
038   powering machines to generate human-like text, comprehend intricate context, and execute a
039   wide range of linguistic tasks with unprecedented accuracy(Ouyang et al., 2022; Chang et al.,
040   2024; Liu et al., 2023). Aligning LLMs with human values and preferences through learning
041   from human feedback is crucial to ensuring these mod-
042   els are helpful, honest, and harmless. Among the various
043   methods to achieve effective alignment (Dai et al., 2024;
044   Yuan et al., 2024a), Direct Preference Optimization
045
      (DPO) has emerged as a promising technique (Rafailov
      et al., 2024), giving rise to numerous derivative algo-
046
      rithms (Hong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Ethayarajh
047
      et al., 2024). DPO eliminates the reliance on online
048   Reward Models (RMs) by reparameterizing the reward
049   function in Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-
050   back (RLHF), thereby implementing a simple and stable
051   offline preference learning paradigm. Among the dimen- Figure 1: Performance of iterative DPO
052   sions of human language preferences, detailedness is one model(Chen et al., 2024d) on Arena-Hard
053   of the most straightforward categories that current align- and Alpacaeval 2.
      ment algorithms can effortlessly capture, as longer texts tend to be richer in content. However, it has

054
      been demonstrated that DPO is susceptible to an over-optimization issue in this particular preference
055   dimension (Xu et al., 2024). As illustrated in Fig.1, the model post-DPO tends to generate longer
056   responses. However, excessively long responses can result in performance degradation, particularly
057   impacting its instruction-following and reasoning capabilities (Ding et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024b).
058
      The phenomenon of verbose response caused by DPO is often attributed to the presence of length
059
      bias in the training data (Park et al., 2024; Singhal et al., 2023). This bias arises from an inherent
060
      length preference in offline RMs (Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c), which results in most pre-
061   ferred responses (chosen) being significantly longer than the dispreferred ones (rejected). Based on
062   this assumption, Yuan et al. (2024b) proposed LIFT-DPO to mitigate the length bias in the train-
063   ing data through a prompt enhancement strategy. Recently, more researchers have questioned the
064   efficacy of the DPO algorithm itself. Park et al. (2024) introduce a regularization term in the opti-
065   mization objective to adjust the weight of the gradient according to the length difference between
066   the preference pairs. Meng et al. (2024) propose a reference-model-free method SimPO, which used
067   the likelihood averaged by length to eliminate the effect of data length. Lu et al. (2024) introduce
068   a down-sampling approach on KL divergence to eliminate the length reliance of DPO. Though Lu
069
      et al. (2024) have conducted a statistical analysis of the implicit rewards during the DPO process
      and found that the rewards might be overestimated or underestimated due to length, the theoretical
070
      explanation of why DPO encounters this issue remains inadequately explored. Meanwhile, exper-
071
      imental results demonstrate that these methods either fail to achieve significant length control or
072   compromise the performance to some extent.
073
074   In this paper, we attribute the verbosity problem to the length sensitivity of DPO. Specifically, the
075
      partial derivatives of the optimization objective of DPO with respect to the chosen and rejected re-
      sponses are inversely proportional to their respective likelihood (Feng et al., 2024a). Since the like-
076
      lihood, which is calculated as the product of the conditional probabilities of each token, decreases
077
      rapidly with increasing sequence length, longer chosen or rejected responses are disproportionately
078   favored in the optimization process. Moreover, the length disparity between the chosen and re-
079   jected responses will substantially skew the optimization objective, ultimately biasing the direction
080   of optimization. Since decreasing the likelihood of any rejected may not affect response length, the
081   primary cause of the verbosity problem is the model’s tendency to increase the likelihood of longer
082   chosen responses while ignoring shorter ones.
083
      To address this issue, we propose an offline optimization algorithm for Length Desensitization of
084   DPO, termed LD-DPO. In this approach, we decompose the likelihood of the longer response in
085   a preference pair into the product of the likelihood of the public-length portion and the likelihood
086   of the excessive portion. The excessive portion is further broken down into verbosity preference
087   (due to excess length) and other preferences. LD-DPO aims to mitigate the verbosity preference
088   caused by excessively long responses, thereby smoothing the relationship between the likelihood
089   and response length. This adjustment reduces the influence of length on the optimization direction
090   in DPO, effectively achieving length desensitization.
091   We employ two settings (Base and Instruct) of Llama2-13B (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama3-8B
092   (AI@Meta, 2024), and Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024) for experimental validation on various bench-
093   marks including MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024) and AlpacaEval 2 (Dubois et al., 2024). The ex-
094   perimental results indicate that LD-DPO consistently outperforms DPO and other baseline methods,
095   achieving more concise responses with a 10-40% reduction in length compared to DPO. Moreover,
096   experiment on reasoning-specific benchmarks shows that LD-DPO significantly improves models’
097   reasoning performance. An interesting phenomenon is also observed: the length sensitivity during
098
      DPO training exhibits a negative correlation with the underlying model capability. we then define
      γ as the length sensitivity coefficient and conduct a detailed analysis of the DPO length sensitivity
099
      across models of varying capabilities, we believe γ is instructive for the entire preference optimiza-
100
      tion process. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
101
102          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to define the length sensitivity of DPO and
103            provide theoretical validation for this phenomenon.
104
             • We propose LD-DPO, a length-desensitization preference optimization algorithm that mit-
105
               igates length sensitivity by decoupling length preference from the reward.
106
107          • We experimentally verify that LD-DPO enables the model to achieve superior results with
               more concise responses, reducing response length by 10-40% compared to DPO.

116
117   The standard pipeline of RLHF aligns LLMs with human preferences in three stages:
118   Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) stage: In this stage, labeled data is used to fine-tune the pre-trained

120   obtain model π SF T (y|x).

122   generate pairs of responses (y1 , y2 ) ∼ π SF T (y|x), which are then labeled by human annotators as a
123
      preferred answer yw and a dispreferred answer yl , denoted as yw > yl . To predict these preferences,
      previous works employ the Bradley-Terry (BT) RM (Bradley & Terry, 1952), which essentially

223   DPO primarily originates from the substantial influence text length exerts on the likelihood πθ (y|x).

225   advantage. To address this issue, Length-Desensitization DPO (LD-DPO) is employed to diminish
226   the impact of length on the likelihood. This adjustment allows the optimization process to focus
227
      more on the substantive content of the text, thereby better aligning with human preference.

275   Models and training settings. We perform preference optimization using three families of models:
276   Llama2-13B (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) and Qwen2-7B (Yang et al.,
277
      2024) under two setups: Base and Instruct/Chat.
278   For the Base setup, we train a base language model on the UltraChat-200k dataset (Ding et al.,
279   2023) to obtain an SFT model, which possesses a basic capability for conversation. For the Instruct
280   setup, we select their corresponding instruct models (i.e., Llama2-13B-Chat, Llama3-8B-Instruct,
281   and Qwen2-7B-Instruct) as initial models. These models are more powerful and robust compared to
282   the base models. Both setups ensure a high level of transparency as the models and training data are
283
      open source.
284   In the preference optimization phase, we utilize UltraFeedback(Cui et al., 2023) as the human pref-
285   erence dataset. This dataset consists of 60,000 high-quality data pairs (x, yw , yl ) designed to align
286   with human conversational preferences and emphasize helpfulness.
287   Evaluation benchmarks. We primarily evaluate our models using three of the most popular open-
288   ended evaluation benchmarks: MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), AlpacaEval 2 (Dubois et al., 2024)
289   and Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024). These benchmarks assess the model’s versatile session capabilities
290   across a wide range of queries and have been widely adopted by the community.
291
      MT-Bench comprises 80 questions spanning 8 categories, whereas AlpacaEval 2 encompasses 805
292
      questions derived from 5 datasets. We present the results in accordance with the evaluation protocol
293   designated for each benchmark. For MT-Bench, we present the average score and provide a detailed
294   breakdown of the scores for each capability item in Appendix B.3. In the case of AlpacaEval 2, we
295   report the length-controlled (LC) win rate against GPT-4-preview-1106, a metric specifically engi-
296   neered to be resistant to model verbosity. For space reasons, we present the analysis of Arena-Hard

298   model. Furthermore, we calculate the average response length on each benchmark to compare the
299   effects of different methods on response length.

304   process by adding weights to the optimization objective of DPO. SimPO introduces an optimization

449   To verify the effect of the relative lengths of yw (chosen) and yl (rejected) in the training data on

451   was applied to yw and yl separately or simultaneously.
452   As shown in Table.3, the performance of the LLMs on MT-Bench, AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard,
453   as well as the length control effect, is inferior to that of LD-DPO under both the chosen and rejected
454   setups. This suggests that length decoupling is necessary for both chosen and rejected. For more
455   detail, we have:
456
457          • If yw is longer: According to the previous analysis, it is known that DPO is more inclined
458            to optimize in the direction of yw under the effect of length bias, which results in redundant
459            output. At this point, length decoupling for yw can alleviate this tendency.
460          • If yl is longer: DPO tends to block the output of yl . In this case, the decoupling of the
461            length of yl may redirect the optimization towards a shorter yw , thus reducing redundancy
462            and improving the quality of the model’s answers.
463
464   Therefore, our length desensitization strategy can encourage the model to identify and leverage more
465   human-like preference gaps in the data samples, rather than optimizing the training process based
466   on superficial length differences.

502   6     R ELATED W ORKS
503
504   6.1   O FFLINE P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
505
506   In practice, traditional RLHF paradigms are more complex in terms of coding and hyperparame-
507   ter tuning, requiring four models simultaneously, which makes them more resource-intensive and
508   difficult to train stably. Due to the lack of online reward models, DPO needs to construct artificial
509
      preference datasets in advance, and many works have proposed different data construction strategies
      to enable the model to better learn human preferences (An et al., 2023; Gallego, 2024; Khaki et al.,
510
      2024). Meanwhile, another research direction is to improve the preference optimization objective,
511
      including the necessity of the reference model, the selection of the reward fitting function, and the
512   adjustment of the update weights, and derive a variety of offline optimization strategies, including
513   ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), NCA (Chen et al., 2024b), IPO (Azar
514   et al., 2024), WPO (Zhou et al., 2024), etc.
515
516   6.2   L ENGTH C ONTROL S TRATEGY
517
518   Recent research has shown that DPO may lead to biased results, such as models producing lengthy
519   outputs, which affects the model’s ability to follow instructions and reasoning. To address this prob-
520   lem, Park et al. (2024) proposed R-DPO, which suppresses the model from producing excessively
521   long answers by introducing a rule term, which is a intuitive scheme followed by several algorithms
522
      (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). Meng et al. (2024) proposed an optimization strategy that
      does not rely on the reference model, called SimPO, which uses length-normalized rewards to pre-
523
      vent the model from generating excessively long but low-quality answers. Lu et al. (2024) argued
524
      that the phenomenon of lengthy outputs in DPO is due to the overestimation or underestimation of
525   implicit rewards caused by the length of the training data. Based on this, they proposed SamPO,
526   which mitigates the length bias by down-sampling the KL divergence to ensure that implicit rewards
527   are not affected by length.
528

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we propose for the first time that the optimization process of DPO is length-sensitive
532
      and provide a theoretical proof. Based on this, we design a length-desensitization algorithm based
533
      on DPO: LD-DPO, which achieves length desensitization by reparameterizing the likelihood to de-
534   couple verbosity preferences from complete information while preserving human-like preferences.
535   Through extensive experimental analysis, LD-DPO consistently outperforms existing algorithms in
536   various training settings, achieving performance improvements with a 10-40% reduction in output
537   length, especially in reasoning ability. This suggests that previous optimization algorithms have
538   overemphasized length at the expense of quality, validating the value of our work. Furthermore, we
539   perform a comparative analysis of length sensitivity across models with different capabilities, which
      may provide new insights into the preference optimization process.
