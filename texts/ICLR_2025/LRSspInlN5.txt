010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Given the rising popularity of AI-generated art and the associated copyright con-
013            cerns, identifying whether an artwork was used to train a diffusion model is an
014            important research topic. The work approaches this problem from the membership
015            inference attack (MIA) perspective. We first identify the limitation of applying
016            existing MIA methods for proprietary diffusion models: the required access of
017
               internal U-nets. To address the above problem, we introduce a novel member-
               ship inference attack method that uses only the image-to-image variation API and
018
               operates without access to the model’s internal U-net. Our method is based on
019
               the intuition that the model can more easily obtain an unbiased noise prediction
020            estimate for images from the training set. By applying the API multiple times to the
021            target image, averaging the outputs, and comparing the result to the original image,
022            our approach can classify whether a sample was part of the training set. We validate
023            our method using DDIM and Stable Diffusion setups and further extend both our
024            approach and existing algorithms to the Diffusion Transformer architecture. Our
025            experimental results consistently outperform previous methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recently, there has been a surge in the popularity of generative models, with diffusion models in
031   particular, gaining huge attention within the AI community (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song &
032   Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020b). These models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across
033   various tasks, including unconditional image generation (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a), text-to-
034   image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021) and image-to-image
035   generation (Saharia et al., 2022a). This surge has given rise to powerful AI art models such as
036   DALL-E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), and Imagen (Saharia et al.,
037   2022b). AI-generated art holds a promising future and is expected to have a widespread impact.
038   Effective training of diffusion models requires high-quality data. It is thus crucial to design an
039   algorithm that can identify whether a specific artwork has been used during the training of a model,
040   thereby providing protection for these artworks and detecting misuse of data. This is especially
041   important due to the rapid growth of generative models, which has raised concerns over intellectual
042   property (IP) rights, data privacy, and the ethical implications of training on copyrighted or proprietary
043   content without consent. As these models are increasingly deployed across industries, detecting
044
      whether a specific piece of content was used in training can help prevent unauthorized use of artistic
      works, protecting creators’ copyrights and ownership rights. This is a classic problem in the field of
045
      machine learning, first introduced by Shokri et al. (2017) and named “membership inference attack”.
046
047   A series of studies have been conducted on membership inference attacks against diffusion models.
048   Hu & Pang (2023) was the first to examine this issue, utilizing the loss function values of diffusion
049   models to determine whether an image is in the training set. Duan et al. (2023) and Kong et al. (2023)
050
      extended this work by relaxing the assumptions about model access requirements.
051   Despite great progress, previous methods are not yet ready for MIA in proprietary diffusion models.
052   Most existing approaches heavily rely on checking whether the U-net of the model predicts noise
053   accurately, which is not practical since most commercial diffusion models available today offer only
      API access, while the U-net remains hidden.

      To address the above issue, we propose a membership inference attack method that relies on the
055   variation API and does not require access to the denoise model (e.g., U-net). We observe that if
056   we alter an image using the target diffusion model’s variation API, the sampling process will be
057   captured by “a region of attraction” if the model has seen this image during training (illustrated in
058   Figure 2). Based on the above observation, we propose the R E D IFFUSE algorithm for MIA with
059   image-to-image variation API, and can detect member images without accessing the denoise model.
060   Our main contributions are listed as follows:
061
062         1. We propose a membership inference attack method that does not require access to the
063
               model’s internal structure. Our method only involves using the model’s variation API to
               alter an image and compare it with the original one. We name our method R E D IFFUSE.
064
065         2. We evaluate our method using DDIM (Song et al., 2020a) and Stable Diffusion (Rombach
066
               et al., 2022) models on classical datasets, including CIFAR10/100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009),
               STL10-Unlabeled (Coates et al., 2011), LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), etc. Our
067
               method outperforms the previous methods.
068
069         3. We extend both existing algorithms and our own algorithm to the Diffusion Transformer (Pee-
070
               bles & Xie, 2023) architecture, implementing the membership inference attack within this
               model framework for the first time. Experimental results demonstrate that our algorithm is
071
               consistently effective.

074   2    R ELATED W ORKS
075
      Diffusion Model The diffusion model, initially proposed by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015), has
076
      achieved remarkable results in producing high-quality samples across a variety of domains. This
077
      ranges from image generation (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020b; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021),
078   audio synthesis (Popov et al., 2021; Kong et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022), and video generation (Ho
079   et al., 2022a;b; Wu et al., 2023), etc. Among existing diffusion models, the Denoising Diffusion
080   Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) is one of the most frequently adopted. This approach
081   introduces a dual-phase process for image generation: initially, a forward process gradually transforms
082   training data into pure noise, followed by a reverse process that meticulously reconstructs the original
083   data from this noise. Building on this model, there have been numerous follow-up studies, such
084   as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), which compresses images into a latent space and
085   generates images based on text, and the Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (Song et al.,
086
      2020a), which removes Gaussian randomness to accelerate the sampling generation process. These
      advancements demonstrate the versatility and potential of diffusion models.
087
088   Data Safety and Membership Inference Attack In the era of big data, preserving data privacy
089   is paramount. The training of diffusion models may involve sensitive datasets like artists’ artworks,
090   which are protected by copyright laws. Membership inference attacks, initially introduced by Shokri
091   et al. (2017), serve as an effective means to detect potential misuse of data without proper authorization.
092   Its objective is to ascertain whether a particular data sample participated in the training phase of
093
      a target model. This approach is instrumental in probing privacy breaches and identifying illicit
      data utilization. Researchers primarily focus on membership inference attacks for classification
094
      models (Salem et al., 2018; Yeom et al., 2018; Long et al., 2018; Li & Zhang, 2021), embedding
095
      models (Song & Raghunathan, 2020; Duddu et al., 2020; Mahloujifar et al., 2021), and generative
096   models (Hayes et al., 2017; Hilprecht et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020).
097
098   In the domain of membership inference attacks against diffusion models, Wu et al. (2022); Hu &
099
      Pang (2023) use a white-box approach, which assumes access to the entire diffusion model and
      utilizes loss and likelihood to determine whether a sample is in the training set. Duan et al. (2023);
100
      Kong et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023) have relaxed these requirements, eliminating the need for the
101
      entire model. They leverage the insight that samples within the training set yield more accurate noise
102   predictions, thereby achieving high accuracy in membership inference attack tasks. However, they
103   also require the outputs of the U-net, as it is necessary to obtain the noise predictions of intermediate
104   steps. Recently, Pang & Wang (2023) proposed a black-box membership inference attack method
105   against diffusion models. Their method identifies whether a specific image is in a finetuning dataset
106   of 100 images by calculating the difference between the generated image and the target image, using
107   the corresponding prompt as input. Their approach leverages the model’s tendency to memorize
      finetuning images and generate similar outputs. In contrast, we focus on detecting whether an image

108
      is in the pretraining dataset, where a pre-trained model often produces diverse outputs for the same
109   prompt, making detection more challenging.

114   We first introduce the Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020). Then,
115   we extend to the Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020a) and Stable
116   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), which are variants of DDPM used to accelerate image generation or
117   generate images grounded in text descriptions. Lastly, we discuss Diffusion Transformer (Peebles &
118
      Xie, 2023), a model that replaces the U-net architecture with a transformer and achieves higher-quality

525   7    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
526
527   In this work, we introduce a novel membership inference attack method specifically designed for
528   diffusion models. Our approach only requires access to the variation API of the model, bypassing
529   the need for internal network components such as the U-net. This represents an advancement
530
      for commercial diffusion models, which typically restrict internal access. We demonstrate the
      effectiveness of our approach across various datasets, showing that it achieves high accuracy in
531
      identifying whether an image was included in the training dataset. Our algorithm can detect data
532
      misuse by the model, representing a step forward in protecting the copyright of artworks.
533
534   However, our method has certain limitations, particularly the requirement for a moderate diffusion
535   step t in the variation API. The algorithm’s accuracy declines when the diffusion step is excessively
536
      high. As such, we propose our method as an initial step towards black-box MIA, with a more
      comprehensive solution left for subsequent exploration.
537
538   Future work could focus on developing more robust algorithms capable of handling a broader range
539   of diffusion steps. Improving the interpretability of our method and extending it to other generative
      models are also valuable directions for further research.
