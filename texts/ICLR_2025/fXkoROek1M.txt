009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Fine-tuning foundation models via reinforcement learning (RL) has proven promis-
012            ing for aligning to downstream objectives. In the case of diffusion models (DMs),
013            though RL training improves alignment from early timesteps, critical issues such
014            as training instability and mode collapse arise. We address these drawbacks by
015            exploiting the hierarchical nature of DMs: we train them dynamically at each
016            epoch with a tailored RL method, allowing for continual evaluation and step-by-
017            step refinement of the model performance (or alignment). Furthermore, we find
018            that not every denoising step needs to be fine-tuned to align DMs to downstream
019            tasks. Consequently, in addition to clipping, we regularise model parameters at
020
               distinct learning phases via a sliding-window approach. Our approach, termed
               Hierarchical Reward Fine-tuning (HRF), is validated on the Denoising Diffusion
021
               Policy Optimisation method, where we show that models trained with HRF achieve
022
               better preservation of diversity in downstream tasks, thus enhancing the fine-tuning
023            robustness and at uncompromising mean rewards.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Diffusion models (DMs) are the de facto state of the art in prompt-based generative modelling across
030   various tasks including text-to-image, text-to-video, molecular graph modelling and medical image
031   reconstruction (Ramesh et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2023; Jing
032   et al., 2022; Song et al., 2022). Most of these applications build on the original Denoising Diffusion
033
      Probabilistic Model (DDPM) by Ho et al. (2020), but the extension to other formulations and variants
      is fast growing. It includes Score-based and Flow matching generative models, among others (Song
034
      and Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020; Lipman et al., 2022).
035
036   Recent works use Reinforcement Learning (RL) to align DMs to downstream tasks that are otherwise
037   difficult to address, e.g., via explicit class labelling, such as generating aesthetic images following a
038   specific prompt, or producing images admitting significant JPEG compression rates. These methods,
039
      such as the Denoising Diffusion Policy Optimization (DDPO) (Black et al., 2024) and others (Deng
      et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023), formulate the denoising steps as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
040
      controlled through a reward function. Despite recent works (Ouyang et al., 2022; Schulman et al.,
041
      2017) making RL fine-tuning more stable and accurate, the consequence for alignment in reward-
042   based DDPM extensions are skewed generated samples, which lead to mode collapse, i.e., when the
043   model’s inherent diversity vanishes. Fig. 1 shows samples from DDPM and DDPO to illustrate that,
044   although DDPO provides better samples than DDPM (in terms of the LAION aesthetic score in this
045   case), it sacrifices sample diversity: the images look the same.
046
      We build on the hierarchical interpretation of DDPMs (Sclocchi et al., 2024) and propose a method-
047
      ology called Hierarchical Reward Fine-tuning (HRF), which performs reward-based learning from
048   different timesteps in the diffusion using a sliding window approach. In DDPM, the HRF window
049   selection mechanism fixes high-level features and generates variations in low-level features, facilitat-
050   ing exploration while preserving the sample’s semantics, as illustrated in Fig. 3. To achieve this, we
051   identify distinct stages in the learning process, distinguishing between high- and low-level features,
052   and apply online RL at each stage, thus effectively training intermediate steps in the diffusion. Our
053   proposal thus enables a controlled learning scheme promoting sample diversity, mitigating mode
      collapse, and thus successfully aligning diffusion models (DMs) to downstream tasks.

      The proposed HRF is implemented and experimentally assessed in the generation of RL-optimised
055   diversity-preserving samples within DDPM. To this end, we consider a DM pre-trained on CelebaHQ
056   (Karras et al., 2018), and fine-tuned over three downstream tasks originally considered in (Black
057   et al., 2024): compressibility, incompressibility and LAION aesthetic score. Fig. 4 shows a succinct
058   illustration of the results of our method for each of these tasks.
059
      The key contributions of our work are:
060
061         • Insights into the learning dynamics of DDPMs when fine-tuned via online RL.
062         • A critical assessment of the state of the art, in terms of its inability to preserve sample
063           diversity due to an overoptimisation in noisy (early) stages of the diffusion chain.
064
            • A novel framework for preserving sample diversity in DDPMs fine-tuned with RL, based on
              a hierarchical interpretation of diffusion models, called Hierarchical Reward Fine-tuning
065
              (HRF).
066
            • An experimental validation of HRF demonstrating: i) advantages in controlled learning, ii)
067           reduced need for reward handcrafting, and iii) improved preservation of sample diversity
068           compared to DDPO.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110   2.1   H IERARCHICAL FEATURES OF DIFFUSION MODELS
111
112   Recent studies have highlighted the hierarchical nature of data generation in diffusion models,
113   particularly in the case of images. For instance, Sclocchi et al. (2024) found that a phase transition
114   occurs during the backward diffusion at a specific timestamp. Beyond this point, the probability of
115   reconstructing high-level features drops rapidly, while low-level features (details) change slower.
116   High-level features typically refer to global attributes, such as overall face structure or hair type,
117
      whereas low-level features involve finer details, like skin texture or small facial details. This implies
      that high-level features are more susceptible to temporal changes in the diffusion process, whereas
118
      low-level features remain relatively stable across the diffusion. This observation is pivotal in our work,
119
      as we will develop a methodological approach to learn favourable representations for downstream
120   tasks at distinct steps in the diffusion process. The main rationale behind our diversity-preserving
121   approach is to target the early steps of the diffusion process trajectory we are training on.
122
123   2.2   D IFFUSION MODEL TRAINING AT DIFFERENT NOISE LEVELS
124
125   The learning dynamics and convergence properties of DDPM at different stages of the diffusion
126   process have been studied by Hang et al. (2023), finding that convergence speed is related to the
127   learning difficulty associated with each timestep. Additionally, Xu et al. (2024) adopted a perspective
128   of curriculum learning and found that DDPMs learn the early steps easier than the later ones across the
129
      denoising procedure. This curriculum learning approach allows for the design of learning strategies
      tailored for each step of the diffusion, thus improving the convergence speed.
130
131
132
      2.3   T RAINING DIFFUSION MODELS WITH REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
133   Approaches to train DDPMs by modelling the diffusion chain as an MDP leverage different loss
134   formulations and regularization techniques (Black et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024).
135   However, they all build upon the same RL setup: they refine loss definitions and mitigate the
136   degeneration of sample diversity through regularization and hyperparameter tuning, but they do
137   not exploit the hierarchical nature of data generation inherent in diffusion models. This means that
138   although these approaches successfully fine-tune the models for downstream tasks in terms of their
139   achieved rewards, they do so struggling to propagate learning across all diffusion steps in a controlled
140   manner, ultimately compromising diversity. Our contribution aims to address this limitation.

      3.3   D IVERSITY LOSS DUE TO OVERPARAMETERIZATION IN THE EARLY PHASES OF DENOISING
217
218   As discussed in Secs. 2.1 and 2.2, learning in DDPM models is more effective during the noisy stages
219   of diffusion and becomes increasingly difficult as noise is reduced. We confirm that this trend also
220   applies to RL-based diffusion via a succinct experimental example. By employing injection sampling,
221   a process where sampling is started with the fine-tuned model and then switched to the base model
222   at step t to continue the sampling, we observed that interventions made in the noisier stages of the
223   denoising process have the most significant impact on the resulting samples. A detailed description
224
      of the experiment can be found in Appendix A.1.

      4.1   H IERARCHICAL REPRESENTATION OF TRAINING
263
      Our approach, HRF, is underpinned by the hierarchical nature of sample generation in diffusion
264   models as identified by Sclocchi et al. (2024). With this in mind, we propose the training diagram
265   illustrated in Figure 3. Each training step starts at a noisy step t using an image prior, guiding the
266   model to learn from favorable policies at that stage. This is achieved by computing the trajectories
267   and their corresponding rewards for every chosen initial step and then propagating the learning to
268   those starting points. This approach leverages the fact that structural changes in generated samples
269   occur at different stages of the diffusion chain, where the rate of change varies depending on the
      learning difficulty (Xu et al., 2024; Hang et al., 2023). The methodology remains flexible enough to


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      allow evaluation on fully denoised samples for each step t, while also facilitating the assessment of
271   reward functions at intermediate steps. This capability helps in selecting better policies throughout
272   the diffusion chain.
273
274
      We propose two variants of the aforementioned methodology based on the window selection mech-
      anism: HRF, which uses manually defined windows for selecting initial steps, and HRF-D, which
275
      employs a dynamic window sampling strategy. Refer to Section 4.3 for more details about the window
276
      selection schemes.
277
278   A major benefit of our proposed methodology is the control over when and where to optimize the
279   DMs distinct phases, particularly for training using RL on downstream rewards that are hard to
280
      describe explicitly. Furthermore, HRF is independent of the chosen loss definitions since it still uses
      the final sample to represent the information conveyed in the segment of the denoising trajectory
281
      under evaluation. Another significant benefit of HRF comes from splitting the learning task into
282
      multiple training steps (abiding by the hierarchical nature of training), which allows us to optimize
283   hyper-parameters or skip phases in a controlled manner.

      5.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
402
403   We train our base model using DDPO and our proposed DDPO-based methods with three random
404   seeds each, applying early stopping at a common stable point. First, we train DDPO to a target
405   reward, then fine-tune our hierarchical models to achieve similar scores, resulting in 16 models: one
406   baseline, one DDPO per task, three HRF per task, and one HRF-D per task. We evaluated these
407   models via the following performance indices:
408         1. Inception Score (IS) Salimans et al. (2016): Measures both sample quality and diversity by
409            evaluating the probability of generated samples belonging to distinct classes. A higher score
410            indicates better visual quality and diversity, while a score closer to baseline score reflects
411            preserved sample diversity and distribution.
412         2. Vendi Score (Dan Friedman and Dieng, 2023), which measures diversity as the exponential
413            of the Shannon entropy of a similarity matrix. It approximates the effective number of
414
               distinct classes in a sample based on a distance metric. For our evaluation, scores closer to
               baseline indicate better sample diversity preservation.
415
            3. Reward Score: Evaluates a model’s adaptability to a given downstream task by assigning
416
               a numerical value to task-specific performance. Higher scores indicate better alignment
417            with the task, providing an intuitive measure for comparing model behavior under similar
418            conditions.

      The evidence shows a trade-off between optimizing for a downstream objective and maintaining
501

      In summary, our findings suggest that our RL-based training approach achieved comparable perfor-
      mance to DDPO in reward optimization, while better preserving model diversity. This highlights
506
      the robustness and efficacy of HRF and HRF-D as a method, showcasing its potential for enhancing
507
      model performance across various tasks while avoiding mode collapse.

510   6     C ONCLUSIONS
511
512   We have presented HRF, a novel methodological framework that leverages the hierarchical nature of
513   data generation in diffusion models and improves the multi-step decision-making interpretation. In
514
      this line, we have also introduced a phase training scheme that enhances specific parts of the diffusion
      model during fine-tuning. The method provides new insights into learning via reinforcement learning
515
      and the effects on each diffusion step. Our hierarchical formulation has proven to be an effective
516
      method for fine-tuning diffusion models on downstream tasks while successfully preserving the
517   inherent models’ diversity. Our evaluation utilized known and validated metrics such as the Inception
518   Score (IS) and Vendi Score to measure the performance of the generative models considered, thus
519   providing a trustworthy assessment of the
520
521   Future work. Some future work directions include optimizing cluster selection and hyperparam-
522   eters to balance learning and sample diversity. Also, exploring alternative RL formulations within
523   this framework could yield interesting results. While we showed that manual step selection improves
524   performance, finding optimal training scenarios remains an open question that we initially explored
525   with HRF-D, which employs an algorithm to identify optimal training steps, but it is not yet fully
526   optimized and requires further research. Finally, we find it extremely necessary to define benchmarks
527
      with specific pre-trained models and tasks to measure and compare different post-training strategies,
      as this is an area rather unexplored. Methodologies like the one proposed in this paper would benefit
528
      from better and more concise evaluation.
529
530
      Limitations. Our method aims to control diffusion models to align with downstream tasks via
531   reward functions, offering benefits like avoiding mode collapse. However, risks include generating
532   explicit content, and more research is needed to understand its broader impact.

      A.1     I NJECTION S AMPLING
651
652   When investigating the impact of learning with the MDP formulation we propose that most of the
653   learning prowess is being done at the early steps of the diffusion model (the noisier states). To verify
654   this experimentally we propose the following method:
655         1. Sample 15 trajectories with all fine-tuned models using DDPO on three tasks.
656         2. Select initial injection steps: [38, 35, 30, 25, 20, 10], where 40 is the noisiest and 0 is the
657            final sample.
658         3. Resample 15 trajectories, switching to the base model at selected injection steps, and
659            compute all trajectory variations.
660         4. Compute cosine distances between denoised images of the original and fine-tuned models
661            for all starting timesteps for the same starting seed. This is between the estimated noiseless
662            image for each timestep.
663
            5. Plot the distances for all three models.
664   Following these steps we get the differences that each model makes for the denoising trajectory at
665   different timesteps. We confirm that most of the differences in the final image come from the early
666   stages of the diffusion model 6. This insight further solidifies the fact that almost all of the learning
667   tends to happen at the early stages of the diffusion model due to the training inherent dynamics.
