                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Diffusion models (DMs) are a powerful type of generative models that have
               achieved state-of-the-art results in various image synthesis tasks and have shown
011
               potential in other domains, such as natural language processing and temporal data
012
               modeling. Despite their stable training dynamics and ability to produce diverse
013            high-quality samples, DMs are notorious for requiring significant computational
014            resources, both in the training and inference stages. Previous work has focused
015            mostly on increasing the efficiency of model inference. This paper introduces, for
016            the first time, the paradigm of sparse-to-sparse training to DMs, with the aim of
017            improving both training and inference efficiency. We focus on unconditional gener-
018            ation and train sparse DMs from scratch (Latent Diffusion and ChiroDiff) on six
019            datasets using three different methods (Static-DM, RigL-DM, and MagRan-DM)
020            to study the effect of sparsity in model performance. Our experiments show that
021
               sparse DMs are able to match and sometimes outperform their Dense counterparts,
               while substantially reducing the number of trainable parameters and FLOPs. We
022
               also identify safe and effective values to perform sparse-to-sparse training of DMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Diffusion models (DMs) are a class of deep generative models that exhibit extraordinary performance
028
      to produce diverse and high-quality data. DMs currently dominate the generative field in computer
029
      vision, having been applied to a wide range of tasks such as (un)conditional image generation (Ho
030   et al., 2020b; Rombach et al., 2021; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Nichol
031   et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2022; Das et al., 2023), image super-resolution (Saharia et al., 2021;
032   Chung et al., 2022), and image inpainting (Nichol et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
033   2022), among others. DMs have also shown incredible potential in other domains, including speech
034   generation (Liu et al., 2023a), text generation (Li et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2023), and time-series
035   prediction and imputation (Rasul et al., 2021; Tashiro et al., 2021).
036   Despite these advantages, DMs are notorious for their slow training, demanding significant computa-
037   tional resources and resulting in a considerable carbon footprint (Strubell et al., 2020). Due to the
038   extensive number of diffusion timesteps required to produce a single sample (e.g., Rombach et al.
039   (2021) mentioned up to 500 steps), DMs also suffer from slow sampling speed (Song et al., 2021).
040   Even though progress has been made in improving inference speed, DMs are still considerably slower
041   than other generative approaches such as GANs and VAEs (Rombach et al., 2021). This inefficiency
042   impacts not only end users, but also the research community, by hindering further developments due
043   to the time-consuming process of model training and evaluation.
044   Reducing the computational costs and memory requirements of DMs is a critical challenge for the
045   broad implementation and adoption of these models, and an active field of research. Much of the
046   existent literature has addressed this challenge through improvements to the inference stage (Song
047   et al., 2021; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Fang et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Salimans
048   & Ho, 2022; Meng et al., 2023). Efforts have also been made in the direction of training efficiency,
049   exploring different architectures and training strategies (Wang et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Rombach
050
      et al., 2021; Phung et al., 2022), but training DMs is still an extensive and costly process.
051   In the last few years, sparse-to-sparse training has emerged as a promising approach to reduce the
052   computational cost of deep learning models, by training sparse networks from scratch (Mocanu et al.,
053   2018; Bellec et al., 2018; Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2019; Evci et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024b).
      Interestingly, sparse neural networks have been shown to match, or even outperform, their Dense


                                                         1

054
      counterparts in classification tasks (Mocanu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021a), generative modeling
055   using GANs (Liu et al., 2023b), and Reinforcement Learning (Sokar et al., 2022), all while requiring
056   less memory and reducing the number of floating-point operations (FLOPs). We should note that,
057   currently, most sparse neural networks require roughly the same amount of time to train as their dense
058   counterparts, since today’s hardware is optimized for dense matrix operations. However, growing
059   interest in sparse models is reshaping the landscape; see Appendix A for a discussion in this regard.
060
      We propose to lower the computational cost of DMs by incorporating, for the first time, the paradigm
061   of sparse-to-sparse training for unconditional generation. As such, we introduce three different
062   methods, Static-DM (static strategy), RigL-DM, and MagRan-DM (both dynamic strategies), that
063   can be easily integrated with existing DMs. Since our goal is to study the effect of these techniques
064   on the performance of DMs, we experiment using two state-of-the-art DMs in two domains: Latent
065   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) for image generation (continuous, pixel-level data) and ChiroD-
066   iff (Das et al., 2023) for sketch generation (discrete, spatiotemporal sequence data). In sum, we make
067   the following contributions:
068
             • We introduce sparse-to-sparse training to DMs, with both static and dynamic strategies. We
069
               consider various sparsity levels (from 10% to 90%), two state-of-the-art models (Latent
070
               Diffusion and ChiroDiff), and six datasets in total.
071
072
             • Our experiments show great promise of sparse-to-sparse training for DMs, as we were able
               to train a sparse DM for each model/dataset case with comparable performance to their
073
               respective Dense counterpart, while significantly reducing the parameters count and FLOPs.
074
               In most cases, at least one sparse DM outperformed its Dense version.
075
076
             • We identify safe and effective values to perform sparse-to-sparse training of DMs. Higher
               performance is achieved using dynamic sparse training with 25–50% sparsity levels and a
077
               conservative 0.05 pruning rate.

082   2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
083
      DMs (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020a; Song et al., 2020) are probabilistic models
084
      designed to learn a data distribution q(x) through two processes: a forward noising process and a
085
      reverse denoising process. The forward process is defined as a Markov Chain of length T in which
086   Gaussian noise is added at each timestep t, producing a sequence of increasingly noisier samples:
087                                                       p
088                                 q(xt |xt−1 ) = N (xt ; 1 − βt xt−1 , βt I)                        (1)
089                                                         T
                                                            Y
090                                        q(x1:T |x0 ) =         q(xt |xt−1 )                              (2)
091                                                         t=1
092   where x0 is the original data point, xt is the data point at timestep t, and βt is the pre-defined amount
093   of noise added at timestep t.
094
095
      The reverse denoising process q(xt−1 |xt ), attempts to recover the original data, but it is intractable
      as it depends on the entire data distribution q(x). As such, we need to parameterize a neural network
096
      pθ to approximate it. This network pθ can be optimized by training with the simplified objective:
097
098                                 L = Et∼[1,T ],x0 ,ϵ∼N (0,1) ||ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t)||2                        (3)
099
      where xT is a noisy version of input x at the final timestep T , and ϵθ the prediction of the neural
100   network pθ .

      2.2   E FFICIENCY IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
103
104   Increasing the efficiency of DMs has been primarily addressed through accelerating the sampling
105   process, by reducing the number of diffusion steps through faster sampling (Song et al., 2021; Karras
106   et al., 2022) and model distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Meng et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024).
107   As for training acceleration, some works have proposed shifting the diffusion process to the latent
      space (Rombach et al., 2021; Vahdat et al., 2021). Interestingly, Phung et al. (2022) used discrete


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      wavelet transforms to decompose images into sub-bands, employing these sub-bands to perform the
109   diffusion more efficiently.
110
111   Previous studies have also presented refinements to the training process of DMs. For example, Wang
112
      et al. (2023) introduced a plug-and-play training strategy that utilizes patches instead of the full
      images, to improve training speed. Hang et al. (2024) proposed treating DMs as a multitask learning
113
      problem and introduced a weighting strategy to balance the different timesteps, achieving a significant
114
      improvement in training convergence speed.
115
116   From the perspective of network compression, prior works have explored techniques such as structural
117   pruning (Fang et al., 2023), post-training quantization (Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), knowledge
118
      distillation (Yang et al., 2023), and the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019; Jiang et al.,
      2023). Very recently, Wang et al. (2024) proposed the incorporation of sparse masks into pre-trained
119
      DMs before fine-tuning, and achieved a 50% reduction in multiply-accumulate operations (MACs)
120
      with only a slight average decrease of image quality (as measured by the FID score). Although
121   these techniques work in increasing efficiency, they still require pre-training of full DMs. Our work
122   proposes training sparse DMs from scratch, which has the potential to both accelerate training and
123   inference, and reduce the memory footprint.

126   2.3   S PARSE - TO -S PARSE T RAINING
127
128   Nowadays most computational models are what is referred to as Dense networks, comprising a
129
      stack of layers containing multiple neurons, each connected to all neurons in the following layer.
      Sparse-to-sparse training techniques aim to train sparse neural networks from scratch, thus reducing
130
      the number of parameters and computations. If we define the connectivity graph of a Dense neural
131
      network as G(V, E), where V represents the set of neurons (vertices), and E the set of connections
132   between them (edges), a sparse version of that neural network would be defined as G(V ′ , E ′ ), with V ′
133   and E ′ being a subset of the neurons and connections of the Dense network. Sparse networks can be
134   obtained using structured methods, where V ̸= V ′ , and unstructured methods, where V = V ′ .
135
      Overall, sparse-to-sparse training techniques can be divided into static sparse training (SST) and
136
      dynamic sparse training (DST), according to whether or not the connections between neurons change
137
      during training. In the following, we provide a comprehensive overview of these.
138
139
140   Static Sparse Training: In SST methods, the connectivity pattern between neurons is set at
141   initialization, and remains fixed during training. This concept was first introduced by Mocanu et al.
142   (2016), who proposed a non-uniform scale-free topology for Restricted Boltzmann machines, with
143   the sparse models achieving better results than their Dense counterparts. Later, Liu et al. (2022)
144
      investigated the efficacy of random pruning at initialization, and found that, using appropriate layer-
      wise sparsity ratios, a randomly pruned subnetwork of WideResNet-50 can outperform a dense
145
      WideResNet-50 on ImageNet. Many other criteria have been proposed to set layer-wise sparsity ratios
146
      before training, by trying to identify important connections using information such as connection
147   sensitivity, as in SNIP (Lee et al., 2019), gradient flow (Wang et al., 2020), as in GraSP. Very recently,
148   two new initialization criteria have been proposed that utilize concepts from network science theory:
149   Bipartite Scale-Free and Bipartite Small-World (Zhang et al., 2024a;b).
150
151
152   Dynamic Sparse Training: In DST methods, the network is initialized with a connectivity pattern
153
      and dynamically explores different connections throughout training (Mocanu et al., 2018; Bellec
      et al., 2018). This was first proposed by Mocanu et al. (2018) through Sparse Evolutionary Training
154
      (SET), an algorithm that adjusts the connections using a prune-and-grow scheme every N training
155
      steps. In SET, weights are dropped based on their magnitude (ensuring an equal amount of positive
156   and negative weights) and regrown randomly. RigL (Evci et al., 2021) proposes an alternative method
157   that prunes the weights based on the absolute magnitude, and regrows them based on the gradients
158   by calculating the dense gradients only at the update step. Although further pruning methods have
159   been proposed (Lee et al., 2019; Yuan et al., 2021), a study by Nowak et al. (2023) found only
160   minor differences between the tested criteria. The contrast was higher in lower density patterns, with
161   magnitude pruning giving the best performance. Other growing criteria have been proposed based on
      randomness (Mostafa & Wang, 2019) and momentum (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2019).


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Recently, Zhang et al. (2024b) proposed Epitopological Sparse Meta-deep Learning (ESML), a brain-
163   inspired, gradient-free method to evolve the sparse network topology. ESML evolves the network
164   through magnitude pruning, network percolation, and weight regrowth through link prediction, and
165   aims to shift the focus from the weights to the network topology. By leveraging ESML, the authors
166   train a sparse network that using just 1% of the connections, is able to surpass dense networks, as
167   well as other DST methods, in several image classification tasks.
168
      DST has also been applied to the field of generative modelling. For example, Liu et al. (2023b)
169   proposed STU-GAN, comprised of a generator with high sparsity and a denser discriminator. STU-
170   GAN was able to outperform a dense BigGAN on CIFAR-10 with a 80% sparse generator and 70%
171   sparse discriminator.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
174
175
      Our study aims to understand the effect of sparse-to-sparse training techniques on DMs. We focus
176
      on unstructured sparsity due to its ability to maintain high performance even at very high levels of
177   sparsity (Evci et al., 2021). Thus, our experiments cannot rely on current hardware to accelerate
178   sparse computations; for example, NVIDIA A100 and Ampere cards only support 2:4 structured
179   sparsity, which requires to enforce a fixed sparsity level of 50%. In the following sections, we
180   present three methods of introducing sparsity in DMs: one SST technique, Static-DM, and two DST
181   techniques, MagRan-DM and RigL-DM.
182
183   3.1   S TATIC S PARSE T RAINING : S TATIC -DM
184
185   Static-DM is a sparse DM trained from scratch, with fixed connectivity between neurons. The
186
      pseudocode for Static-DM is shown in Algorithm 1. The training process closely resembles that of
      a dense DM, with the addition of a sparse initialization step. In this step, the graph underlying the
187
      neural network is sparsified by setting a fraction of the neuron connections to zero.
188

194    5:   t ∼ U({1, 2, . . . , Td })
195    6:   ϵ ∼ N (0, I)
196    7:   θi = AdamW(▽θ , LDIF (fθ (x0 , t), ϵ))
197    8: end for

201
      layers. With this strategy, larger layers get assigned higher sparsity than smaller layers. The sparsity
      of each layer scales with:

213   3.2   DYNAMIC S PARSE T RAINING : M AG R AN -DM AND R IG L-DM
214
215   The key aspect of DST algorithms lies with the process of pruning and regrowing weights. We opted
      to test the two most common regrowth methods, random growth and gradient growth, combined


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      with the magnitude pruning criteria. Magnitude pruning is a simple criteria, that has been shown to
217   perform well in high sparsity regimes for supervised classification, as well as in other generative
218   models (Nowak et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b)
219
220
      RigL, proposed by Evci et al. (2021), combines gradient growth and magnitude pruning, thus the
      name of our model RigL-DM. The combination of random growth and magnitude pruning closely
221
      resembles the SET algorithm (Mocanu et al., 2018), and has been studied before for other types
222
      of models (Nowak et al., 2023), although it has never been named. For simplicity, we refer to this
223   method as MagRan-DM.
224
225   Algorithm 2 RigL-DM and MagRan-DM
226
       1: Input: Dataset D, Network fθ , Number of Epochs N , Diffusion steps Td , Sparsity ratio S,
227       exploration frequency ∆Te , Pruning rate p, Sparse method METHOD
228    2: θ ← sparse initialization using S // Equations 4 and 5
229    3: for i = 1 to N do
230    4:   x0 ∼ D
231    5:   t ∼ U ({1, 2, . . . , Td })
232    6:   ϵ ∼ N (0, I)
233    7:   θi = AdamW(▽θ , LDIF (fθ (x0 , t), ϵ))
234    8:   if i mod ∆Te then
235
       9:       θip = TopMag(|θi |, 1 − p) // Magnitude pruning
      10:       if METHOD is RigL-DM then
236
      11:          θig = TopGrad(|▽θ LDIF |, p) // Gradient growth
237
      12:       else if METHOD is MagRan-DM then
238   13:          θig = Random(p) // Random growth
239   14:       end if
240   15:       θi ← update activated weights using θig and θip
241   16:   end if
242   17: end for
243
244   The full pseudocode for the training process of MagRan-DM and RigL-DM can be found in Algo-
245   rithm 2. At the start of the training process, the network is sparsely initialized using the same strategy
246   as described for Static-DM. After every ∆Te training iterations, a cycle of connection pruning and
247   growth is performed. First, we drop (i.e. set to zero) a fraction of the activated weights with the
248
      lowest magnitude from the network, determined using TopMag(|θi |, 1 − p), which returns the indices
      of the top 1 − p of weights by magnitude. After pruning, we regrow new weights in the same
249
      proportion in order to maintain the sparsity level. For RigL-DM, the connections to regrow are given
250
      by TopGrad(|▽θ LDIF |, p), that returns the indices of the top p of weights with highest magnitude
251   gradients. For MagRan-DM the regrowth is determined by Random(p), which outputs the indices of
252   random p of connections.

266   architectures and choice of datasets can be found in Appendix B.

      A       H ARDWARE AND S OFTWARE S UPPORT
757
758
      One of the main challenges in sparse neural networks research is that most hardware optimized for
759
      deep learning is designed for dense matrix operations. As a result, most of current research attempts
760   to mimic sparsity by using a binary mask over weights, which results in sparse networks offering, in
761   practice, no better training efficiency than dense networks. However, industry is catching up and so it
762   is a matter of time for hardware to truly leverage sparse operations.
763
      There is a growing trend towards developing hardware that better supports sparse operations. In
764
      2021, NVIDIA released the A100 GPU, which supports accelerating operations in a 2:4 sparsity
765
      pattern. Several works have already leveraged this feature (Zhou et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024). In
766   order to use this capability, the sparse matrices must follow a specific structure: among each group
767   of four contiguous values, two values must be zero, thereby fixing the sparsity level at 50%. While
768   this structure enables significant acceleration, it supports only one static sparsity level, and makes it
769   impossible to vary the sparsity ratio between layers.
770
      More recently, Cerebras introduced the CS-3 AI accelerator (Lie, 2022), capable of accelerating sparse
771
      training and supporting unstructured sparsity. Using Cerebras’ CS-3 AI to accelerate training, and
772   Neural Magic’s inference server to accelerate inference, Agarwalla et al. (2024) trained an accurate
773   sparse Llama-2 7B model. Its accelerated training closely matched the theoretical speedup, while
774   achieving 91.8% accuracy recovery of Llama Evaluation metrics, with 70% sparsity. This significant
775   finding underscores the potential of sparse training to produce more efficient neural networks in
776   practice, not just in theory.
777
      In parallel, there have also been advancements in creating software implementations that support truly
778   sparse-to-sparse neural network training, mostly for supervised learning tasks (Liu et al., 2021b; Curci
779   et al., 2022). In addition, a sparse-to-sparse denoising autoencoder has been developed by Atashgahi
780   et al. (2020), to perform fast and robust feature selection.
781
      These developments in both hardware and software point towards a future where sparse-to-sparse
782
      training may become the de facto approach for developing neural networks, enabling faster, more
783
      memory-efficient, and energy-efficient deep learning models.

      Latent Diffusion on CelebA-HQ: We use a batch size of 12, AdamW optimizer with weight decay

      1e-2 and static learning rate 2.0e-06. We train for 150 epochs. We use 1000 Denoising steps (T),
821
      linear noise schedule from 0.0015 to 0.0195, and sinusoidal embeddings for the timestep.

823   Latent Diffusion on Imagenette: We use a batch size of 12, AdamW optimizer with weight decay
824   1e-2 and static learning rate 2.4e-5. We train for 150 epochs. We use 1000 Denoising steps (T), linear
825
      noise schedule from 0.0015 to 0.0195, and sinusoidal embeddings for the timestep.
826   ChiroDiff on QuickDraw: We use a batch size of 128, AdamW optimizer with weight decay 1e-2
827   and static learning rate 1e-3. We train for 600 epochs. We use 1000 Denoising steps (T), linear noise
828   schedule from 1e-4 to 2e-2, and random Fourier features for the timestep embedding.
829   ChiroDiff on KanjiVG: We use a batch size of 128, AdamW optimizer with weight decay 1e-2 and
830   static learning rate 1e-3. We train for 600 epochs. We use 1000 Denoising steps (T), linear noise
831   schedule from 1e-4 to 2e-2, and random Fourier features for the timestep embedding.

      ChiroDiff on VMNIST: We use a batch size of 128, AdamW optimizer with weight decay 1e-2 and
833
      static learning rate 1e-3. We train for 600 epochs. We use 1000 Denoising steps (T), linear noise
834
      schedule from 1e-4 to 2e-2, and random Fourier features for the timestep embedding.
