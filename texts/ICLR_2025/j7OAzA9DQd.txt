009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Effectively modeling multimodal longitudinal data is a pressing need in various
               application areas, especially biomedicine. Despite this, few approaches exist in
013
               the literature for this problem, with most not adequately taking into account the
014
               multimodality of the data. In this study, we developed multiple configurations of
015            a novel multimodal and longitudinal learning framework, Longitudinal Ensemble
016            Integration (LEI), for sequential classification. We evaluated LEI’s performance,
017            and compared it against existing approaches, for the early detection of dementia,
018            which is among the most studied multimodal sequential classification tasks. LEI
019            outperformed these approaches due to its use of intermediate base predictions aris-
020            ing from the individual data modalities, which enabled their better integration over
021            time. LEI’s design also enabled the identification of features that were consistently
022            important across time for the effective prediction of dementia-related diagnoses.
023
               Overall, our work demonstrates the potential of LEI for sequential classification
               from longitudinal multimodal data.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Data that are both longitudinal/temporal and multimodal are increasingly being used in combination
029
      with machine learning for forecasting, especially in medical diagnosis (Brand et al., 2019; Zhang
030
      & Shen, 2012; Feis et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023). Recently, a number of promising approaches for
031   sequential classification from such data have been introduced (Eslami et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2011;
032   Wang et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2024). For instance, some approaches have used recurrent neural
033   network (RNN)-based models applied to data sequences where the modalities at each time point have
034   been concatenated into a long feature vector, sometimes referred to as early fusion (Nguyen et al.,
035   2020; Olaimat et al., 2023; Maheux et al., 2023). Generally, due to the complexity of this modeling,
036   these approaches generally consider only a limited set of modalities, samples, or time points. In
037   addition to computational issues, the early fusion of data across modalities may obfuscate signals
038   local to the individual data modalities (Zitnik et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022). This may hinder model
039
      performance, as the differing semantics and scales of the various modalities are not adequately
      accounted for. This represents a major challenge for the automated classification of multimodal
040
      longitudinal data, as the consensus and complementarity among different modalities may not be
041
      sufficiently leveraged (Zitnik et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022).
042
043   Recently, the Ensemble Integration (EI) framework (Figure 1) was developed to leverage modality-
044   specific information during data integration and predictive modeling (Li et al., 2022). EI accom-
045
      plishes this by first capturing information local to the individual modalities into effective base pre-
      dictors of the target outcome. These base predictors are then aggregated into final heterogeneous
046
      ensemble-based predictive models using methods such as stacking (Sesmero & Ledezma, 2015).
047
      Due to its flexibility, EI has been effective for a variety of biomedical prediction problems, such as
048   protein function prediction and prognosis of COVID-19 patients in Li et al. (2022), detection of cell
049   division events in Bennett et al. (2024), screening of diabetes among youth in McDonough et al.
050   (2023), and the early detection of dementia from only snapshot baseline data in Cirincione et al.
051   (2024). Additionally, EI has provided insights into the prediction problems in the applications listed
052   above, e.g., risk factors of diseases, through its model interpretation capability (Li et al., 2022).
053   Despite the successes of EI, it has thus far only been applicable in its design to non-longitudinal
      multimodal data.

074   In the current work, we extended the capabilities of EI to multimodal longitudinal data by combining
075   its modality-specific base predictors with temporal deep learning methods like RNNs. Specifically,
076   in the new Longitudinal Ensemble Integration (LEI) framework, we first derive base predictors from
077   individual modalities at every time point and then stack these predictions using a Long Short-Term
078   Memory (LSTM) Network (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). We also leveraged EI’s interpreta-
079   tion strategy found in Li et al. (2022) to identify the most predictive features at different time points,
080   as well as longitudinal patterns among these features.
081   We tested the capabilities of LEI for the early and accurate prediction of the progression of demen-
082   tia, due to this being one of the most commonly tackled problems in the sequential classification of
083   multimodal data (most of the references cited above), as well as for its real world importance (Chen
084   et al., 2021; Bredesen, 2014). For this, we used data from The Alzheimer’s Disease Prediction of
085   Longitudinal Evolution (TADPOLE) Challenge, Marinescu (2019), a structured dataset derived from
086   The Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) (Petersen, 2010). TADPOLE contains
087
      multiple data modalities, e.g., demographics and cognitive test scores, as well as domain-relevant
      features derived from MRI images, such as volumes of brain regions, collected over the course of
088
      ADNI participants’ enrolment in the study. Our specific task was to use the TADPOLE data to pre-
089
      dict whether a patient would be diagnosed with different stages of this disorder, namely cognitively
090   normal (CN), mildly cognitively impaired (MCI), or dementia, at the next TADPOLE/ADNI visit,
091   given data up to and including the current one. We evaluated the performance of various config-
092   urations of LEI for this task, as well as compared its performance to those of existing approaches
093   for multimodal sequential classification. We also leveraged EI’s interpretation strategy introduced
094   in Li et al. (2022) to identify the most predictive features for this problem at different time points,
095   as well as temporal patterns among these features. Although demonstrated here for early dementia
096   detection, our approach is general with respect to applications, modalities, and constituent models,
097   and can be adapted for other data integration-based longitudinal prediction problems as well.

173   2.2 LEI C ONFIGURATIONS
174   Although the basic design of LEI is straightforward, (Figure 2), the availability of several base
175   predictors at multiple time points, as well as the architecture of the LSTM stacker, lend the design to
176   be implemented in multiple configurations. Four such configurations were developed and evaluated.
177   They are distinguished by the modeling approaches taken at the beginning base predictor step and
178   the final classification. In the regime of sequential classification with machine learning, three broad
179   strategies are common.
180
             • What we refer to as time-dependent modeling involves setting up and solving a separate
181
               modeling problem at every time point within a sequence of data and therefore only uses a
182
               cross section of the data for model training and evaluation of all models involved.
183
184
             • In the time-distributed modeling approach, a single static model is trained and evaluated on
               all of the cross sections of data from all time points within a sequence of data but separate
185
               time points are treated as independent.
186
187          • In longitudinal modeling, a model capable of leveraging longitudinal patterns in a sequence
188
               of data is used to classify the elements of the sequence, such as with RNNs or LSTMs.
189
      In this work, different combinations of these three strategies were used at different steps in the LEI
190   algorithm to yield the four combinations evaluated:
191
192         1. Time-dependent base predictors (BPs) that are stacked by an LSTM with a time-distributed
193            classification head.
194         2. Time-dependent BPs that are stacked by an LSTM with a longitudinal classification head.
195
            3. Time-distributed BPs that are stacked by an LSTM with a time-dependent classification
196
               head.
197
198         4. Time-distributed BPs that are stacked by an LSTM with a longitudinal classification head.
199
      Below, we describe each of these configurations of LEI in detail.
200
201
      2.2.1 C ONFIGURING BASE P REDICTION G ENERATION /AGGREGATION
202
      For generating base predictions in the first step of LEI (top half of Figure 2), both time-dependent
203
      modeling (Configurations 1 and 2) and time-distributed modeling (Configurations 3 and 4) were
204   explored. In the time-dependent approach, separate sets of base predictors were trained and used
205   to generate predictions at each time point, followed by their concatenation for the final ensemble
206   (Figure 3). This approach has the potential advantage of generating predictions for the stacker that
207   are optimized for the time point corresponding to the original data.
208
      In the time-distributed approach, the longitudinal data were flattened across the time dimension and a
209
      single instance of each of the base predictors was trained on all the data across all time points (Figure
210   4). A numerical feature indicating the time point corresponding to a sample was concatenated to the
211   original data as a form of positional encoding for this approach as well. In all data splitting steps,
212   feature vectors of the same sample were always kept in the same split to prevent data leakage. A
213   practical advantage of this approach is that each base predictor is trained on T · N samples, as
214   opposed to N in the time-dependent case. Similarly, a factor of T fewer total models are trained in
215   the time-distributed base predictor approach than in the time-dependent setting, which can reduce
      computational complexity. A more subtle potential strength of time-distributed base predictors is

266   2.2.2 C ONFIGURING THE S EQUENTIAL C LASSIFICATION H EAD WITHIN LEI
267   While LSTMs were used in all configurations of LEI, different approaches were taken for the final
268   sequential classification of the data. The standard approach, as in Foumani et al. (2024), employs
269   time-distributed modeling where the hidden states output at every time point by the LSTM are
      processed by a classifying MLP (Configurations 1 and 3). This approach has the possible benefit


                                                                            5

270
      of consistently strong performance across time due the model’s focus on feature level information
271   independent of time point. There is also the general possibility of modest gains across time due to
272   the richer temporal information stored in hidden states from later time points.
273
274
      We also employed a longitudinal modeling approach at this stage, where a multi-layered LSTM
      was used. The softmax activated hidden states from the last layer, equal in length to the number
275
      of classes, were output to the loss function (Configurations 2 and 4). This approach may have the
276
      advantage of improved performance across time as the classifier can capture temporal dependencies
277   and use them to make a classification.
278
279   A time-dependent sequential classification approach was also explored. However, this approach did
280
      not perform on the same level as the other approaches due to the limited training data available to
      the classifiers at each time point.
281
282   A final variation tested for LEI concerned the labels the base predictors were trained on, given the
283   goal of making predictions at the next time point. Two reasonable choices would be to match the
284   data from time point t either to the corresponding label at time t or to the label at time point t+1. We
285   found that the t to t approach outperformed the t to t+1 approach in all LEI configurations, possibly
286
      because the LSTM stacker covered the t to t + 1 dependence. Thus, in all the results presented, base
      predictors were trained with the labels at the same time point, i.e., the t to t approach.

289   2.3 I NTERPRETATION OF L ONGITUDINAL EI M ODELS
290   The original EI framework enabled the identification of the most predictive features at individual
291   time points. Since deep learning methods like LSTMs are well-known to be hard to interpret, as
292   demonstrated by Zhang et al. (2021), we adopted an alternate approach based on the interpretation
293   of static EI models (Li et al., 2022). Specifically, we used the latter’s algorithm to identify the
294
      ten most predictive features at each time point, and then analyzed how these sets varied over the
      temporal trajectory. To make the interpretations consistent with the LEI algorithm, the stacking
295
      algorithms used in static EI were trained with the labels at time t + 1.

357   3.2 T RAINING AND E VALUATION
358   We translated the nested cross-validation setup for the training and evaluation of the static EI frame-
359   work introduced in (Li et al., 2022) to the various configurations of LEI as well. Specifically, the
360   overall evaluation of LEI was conducted in a five-fold cross-validation (CV) setup, where 80% of
361   the cohort was used for training the models, and the remaining 20% for evaluation. Furthermore,
362   the base predictors at each time point were trained using the diagnosis labels at the same time point,
363
      and used to make predictions in our inner five-fold CV setup within the training split of each cross-
      validation round. These base predictions were then input to the LSTM stacker for training, and the
364
      predicted outputs over the five outer CV folds were concatenated for evaluation. The accuracy of
365
      LEI’s predictions was measured in terms of the multi-class version of the F-measure, also known
366   as the argmax measure (Opitz & Burst, 2021). Specifically, at each time point, we evaluated the
367   model in terms of the arithmetic mean of the F-measure of the three classes (CN, MCI, or De-
368   mentia) after assigning the class predicted with the highest probability (argmax) as the most likely
369   diagnosis (Berger & Guda, 2020). We also repeated the CV process 20 times to calculate the me-
370   dian F-measure as the representative metric, along with the standard errors indicating the statistical
371   robustness of the performance assessments.
372

      5   D ISCUSSION
508
509
      This work investigated the potential of our novel Longitudinal Ensemble Integration (LEI) frame-
510
      work, for sequential classification from temporal multimodal data. LEI builds upon the success of
511   the existing EI framework by integrating base predictors inferred from the multimodal data over
512   time through an LSTM stacker. We tested LEI on longitudinal multimodal clinical data from the
513   ADNI-derived TADPOLE cohort to predict the likelihood of patients’ progression to dementia. LEI
514   performed better than several other approaches that have been used for predicting Dementia progres-
515   sion, such as an LSTM and PPAD applied to the raw TADPOLE data. The framework also identified
516   several predictive features and their variations over time that could expand our knowledge of the key
517   characteristics of progression to dementia. In conclusion, our work demonstrates the potential of
518   LEI for effectively integrating longitudinal multimodal data for sequential classification.
519   However, our work also had some limitations. During our processing of the TADPOLE data, any
520   feature with missing values for over 30% of patients at any time point was eliminated. This re-
521   sulted in several clinically useful imaging modalities, such as FDG PET, AV45 PET, AV1451 PET
522   and DTI, being removed, potentially deteriorating prediction performance. The exclusion of the
523   non-longitudinal features within TADPOLE data may also have caused a reduction in predictive
524   performance. Some limitations related to the label set (CN, MCI and Dementia) were notable as
525
      well. The considerable imbalance in the class labels and its variation across time (Figure 5) could
      have biased our predictive models in favor of the more prevalent classes/diagnoses. Although we
526
      attempted to address this challenge by designing a weighted ordinal cost function for training the
527
      LSTM within LEI, the problem still affected performance.
528
529   Additionally, although the general design of LEI makes it capable of being used for both structured
530   and unstructured data, our work only considered structured modalities in the TADPOLE data. Future
531
      work can include the development and validation of LEI for collections of structured and unstruc-
      tured data, such as those available from ADNI. Future work can also include the consideration of
532
      variable length sequences of longitudinal data, which are typical in large cohorts like ADNI, and
533
      which LSTMs and other longitudinal deep learning models are capable of processing. Other poten-
534   tial configurations of the LEI framework can be explored as well. The use of longitudinal modeling
535   at the base prediction step could further enhance the benefits of LEI by better leveraging the tempo-
536   ral information within modalities. Similarly, in both the time-dependent and time-distributed base
537   predictor formulations, training base predictors on feature vectors that are not necessarily part of
538   an unbroken sequence of data may be useful in strengthening base predictions, as these modeling
539   approaches do not require sequential data, even if these samples can not be used in the ultimate
      sequential classification task.
