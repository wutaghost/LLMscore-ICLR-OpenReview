011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures face challenges such as high memory
014            consumption and redundancy in experts. Pruning MoE can reduce network
015            weights while maintaining model performance. Motivated by the recent obser-
016            vation of emergent large magnitude features in Large Language Models (LLM)
017            and MoE routing policy, we propose MoE-Pruner, a method that prunes weights
018            with the smallest magnitudes multiplied by the corresponding input activations
019
               and router weights, on each output neuron. Our pruning method is one-shot,
               requiring no retraining or weight updates. We evaluate our method on Mixtral-
020
               8x7B and Mixtral-8x22B across multiple language benchmarks. Experimental
021
               results show that our pruning method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
022            LLM pruning methods. Furthermore, our pruned MoE models can benefit from
023            a pretrained teacher model through expert-wise knowledge distillation, improv-
024            ing performance post-pruning. Experimental results demonstrate that the Mixtral-
025            8x7B model with 50% sparsity maintains 99% of the performance of the original
026            model after the expert-wise knowledge distillation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Scaling neural network models is one of the main drivers of better performance in deep learning.
033
      From BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) to GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) to Llama 3.1 405B (Dubey et al.,
      2024) in natural language processing, or from ResNet (He et al., 2016) to ViT (Dosovitskiy et al.,
034
      2021) in computer vision, breakthroughs in performance have been obtained from larger models,
035
      datasets, and computational resources for training (Kaplan et al., 2020). However, the cost of train-
036   ing state-of-the-art models grows exponentially. For instance, BERT-Large (345M parameters, pro-
037   posed in 2018) requires an estimated 5 × 1020 FLOPs (Devlin et al., 2019) to train, GPT-3 (175B
038   parameters, from 2020) requires 3.14 × 1023 FLOPs (Brown et al., 2020), while Llama 3.1 (405B,
039   released in 2024) requires 3.8 × 1025 FLOPs (Dubey et al., 2024) to train. This exponential growth
040   motivates researchers to seek more efficient and effective training approaches.
041
      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures (Jacobs et al., 1991; Shazeer et al., 2017) have been pro-
042   posed to reduce the computing cost while enabling efficient scaling of network capacity. It has been
043   successfully employed to scale both vision (Ruiz et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2023) and language (Lep-
044   ikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022) models. In addition, these models provide other advantages,
045   including sparsity that can mitigate catastrophic forgetting in continual learning and an inductive
046   bias that can enhance performance in multitask learning (Collier et al., 2020; Komatsuzaki et al.,
047   2023). Overall, MoE has proven to be a promising strategy for scaling deep learning models across
048   various domains.
049   However, several crucial limitations persist in MoE for expanding its capacity. First of all, the static
050   parameters, particularly those required for constructing the MoE architecture, introduce substantial
051   memory overheads and constraints for deployment. For example, Mixtral-8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024)
052   expert layers account for 96% of model parameters (45B out of 47B), which demands considerable
053   memory and storage during inference. Moreover, MoE has a poor utilization of its experts. The
      conventional learning-based routing policy for MoE suffers from representation collapse issues since


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      it encourages token embeddings to be clustered around expert centroids (Chi et al., 2022) and results
055   in redundant experts (Mittal et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022).
056
057   One possible solution to address those drawbacks and fully unleash the power of MoE is consol-
058
      idating information from insignificant experts, aiming to establish a more compact MoE without
      hurting performance. Another solution is pruning experts that yield the lowest token reconstruc-
059
      tion loss. Nevertheless, naively combining existing model merging mechanisms or expert pruning
060
      leads to performance degradation in the MoE architectures. We raise the following pivotal questions
061   for MoE LLM pruning: (i) How do we formulate and devise comprehensive pruning metrics that
062   leverage existing methods? (ii) How do we find the optimal pruning metric tailored for MoE Large
063   Language Models?
064
      In this paper, we systematically explore MoE LLM pruning and target a high-quality compressed
065
      MoE model in downstream fine-tuning scenarios. Specifically, we first analyze the open-source MoE
066
      model’s expert activation frequency and observe that different MoE expert initialization methods
067   result in different expert activation frequencies and expert similarities. We leverage existing LLM
068   pruning methods such as SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023b) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2024),
069   and design a novel pruning metric that incorporates MoE router weights information to identify
070   and remove unimportant weights in expert layers. Since the pruning process is one-shot and only
071   requires a small set of calibration data, the MoE model suffers from performance degradation. To
072   recover MoE model performance, we further propose an expert-wise knowledge distillation method
073   that utilizes the pretrained model as a teacher model, facilitating the recovery of the pruned model’s
074   performance.
075   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
076
077          • We propose a novel framework, MoE-Pruner, that is efficient and effective for pruning MoE
078            models with minimal performance degradation.
079          • We design an innovative expert-wise knowledge distillation method that leverages the pre-
080            trained MoE model as a teacher model to recover pruned MoE student model performance.
081          • Experimental results on Mixtral MoE models across nine zero-shot evaluation benchmarks
082            demonstrate the effectiveness of our MoE-Pruner algorithm. MoE-Pruner achieves minimal
083            performance drop even at 50% sparsity with only a small set of calibration data compared
084            with existing pruning methods. The pruned model maintains 99% of the performance of
085            the original model after the expert-wise knowledge distillation.

      2   P RELIMINARIES
088
089
      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE). Scaling model size increases learning capacity and enhances general-
090   ization (Kaplan et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). MoE (Jacobs et al., 1991;
091   Shazeer et al., 2017; Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022) is an efficient approach that enables
092   significantly more compute-efficient pretraining and inference. It replaces the feed-forward network
093   (FFN) layers in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) with expert layers, where different experts are
094   activated for different input tokens instead of utilizing the full network parameters. Sparse MoE
095   architecture can dramatically scale the model with the same compute budget as a dense model.
096   MoE Expert Initialization. MoE expert initialization uses different strategies, which can be clas-
097   sified into two categories: sparse upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023) and training from scratch.
098   The sparse upcycling method starts from a dense model checkpoint and copies all parameters, ex-
099   cept the MoE router, which does not exist in the original dense model. In particular, each expert
100   in the new MoE layer is an identical copy of the original MLP layer that is replaced. Some open-
101   source MoE models such as Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024), Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B (Team, 2024), and
102   MiniCPM-MoE (Hu et al., 2024) all employ the upcycling approach to reduce the total training
103   costs. While some MoE models like DeepSeek-V2 (Liu et al., 2024), OLMoE (Muennighoff et al.,
104
      2024), and Yuan2.0-M32 (Wu et al., 2024) use the training from scratch approach to help expert
      diversification.
105
106   Large Language Model Pruning. Magnitude pruning (Han et al., 2016) is a standard approach to
107   induce sparsity in neural networks. It removes individual weights with magnitudes below a certain
      threshold. However, magnitude pruning fails dramatically on LLMs even with relatively low levels


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of sparsity (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023b). SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023b) proposes a one-shot,
109   post-training pruning method that prunes LLM weights and uses Hessian matrix and calibration
110   data to update the remaining weights without any retraining. Wanda (Sun et al., 2024) is a simple
111   method that prunes LLM weights with the smallest magnitudes multiplied by the corresponding
112   input activations without any additional weight update.

207   When it comes to pruning MoE, the expert layers constitute the majority of model parameters. For
208   example, the Mixtral-8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024) has a total of 47B parameters where 1.3B belongs to
209   attention modules and 45B is used for expert layers (2 out of 8 experts are activated, 12.5B active
210   parameters during inference). Only a subset of experts are activated for different input tokens, so
211   there is a large space of expert redundancy.
212   Motivation. Consider a simple Mixture-of-Experts with two experts and each with only one weight:
213   y = Gate(x)1 · E1 (x) + Gate(x)2 · E2 (x) = Gate1 · w1 · x + Gate2 · w2 · x, where |w1 | ≤ |w2 |.
214   If we want to remove one weight without incurring significant change on the output, traditional
215   magnitude pruning (Han et al., 2016) will remove weight |w1 |. However, in MoE architecture, the
      router weights Gatei is an important part as it assigns different values to different experts. Especially


                                                                              4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      when we consider a top-k setting that only a subset of experts are activated, the router weights Gate1
217   could be a large value close to 1, while router weights Gate2 could be 0 if it is not activated. As a
218   results, |Gate1 · w1 · x| ≫ |Gate2 · w2 · x|, and therefore we should remove weight w2 instead to
219   minimize change on the output.
220
      This motivating example shows that for MoE architecture, we need to consider the importance of
221
      router weights. Previous pruning methods for LLMs do not consider the router weights which only
222
      exist in MoE architecture and may result in lower performance after pruning MoE. We propose a
223   pruning metric designed explicitly for MoE LLMs to handle such a limitation while maintaining the
224   simplicity of Wanda’s pruning metric.

312
313   Models, Datasets, and Evaluation. We conduct pruning experiments on widely adopted open-
314   source MoE models: the base and instruct version of Mixtral-8x7B and Mixtral-8x22B (Jiang et al.,
315
      2024). We use samples from the pretraining dataset C4 (Raffel et al., 2020) as calibration data for
      one-shot pruning since pretraining datasets are often more comprehensive and not dominated by
316
      knowledge specific to any particular domain. We use the exact same 128 sequences of calibration
317
      data for all one-shot pruning experiments to control this variable factor. We evaluate the perplexity
318   on the WikiText (Merity et al., 2017) validation set. Our expert-wise knowledge distillation method
319   uses a subset of the C4 (Raffel et al., 2020) as the training set. We measure the performance of
320   pruned models on zero-shot tasks and language modeling. For zero-shot evaluation, we use nine
321   popular tasks from EleutherAI LM Harness (Gao et al., 2023). The nine evaluated zero-shot tasks
322   are: ARC-easy, ARC-challenge (Clark et al., 2018), Boolq (Clark et al., 2019), HellaSwag (Zellers
323   et al., 2019), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), OpenBookQA (OBQA) (Mihaylov et al., 2018),
      PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), RTE (Wang et al., 2018), and WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021).

477   5              R ELATED W ORKS
478
479   Pruning and Sparsity. Pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Hassibi et al., 1993; Han et al., 2015) is an
480   important approach for compressing neural networks through eliminating weights (Han et al., 2016)
481   or activations (Rao et al., 2021), yielding sparse networks. It can be mainly classified into two
482
      categories based on the granularity: unstructured and structured pruning.
483   Unstructured pruning such as magnitude pruning (Han et al., 2015; 2016) removes individual
484   weights to introduce sparsity while preserving accuracy even at high sparsity. Existing methods
485   either require retraining or fine-tuning the pruned models (Liu et al., 2019) or the whole iterative
      retraining process (Frankle & Carbin, 2019). However, in the era of LLMs, these methods fail as


                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      retraining LLMs demands substantial computational resources. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh,
487   2023b) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2024) propose efficient post-training pruning method that prunes
488   LLM weights in a layer-wise manner without retraining the model.
489
490
      Structured pruning eliminates weights as a group, such as channel pruning (He et al., 2017), ker-
      nel pruning (Zhong et al., 2022), attention head pruning (Wang et al., 2021), token pruning (Rao
491
      et al., 2021), and layer pruning (Elhoushi et al., 2024). Unlike unstructured pruning, it leads to more
492
      hardware-friendly, dense blocks of computation, which facilitates acceleration on modern hardware
493   platforms. Some methods explore structured pruning based on sparsity on the structural components
494   of LLMs, such as attention heads (Wang et al., 2021) and FFN channels (Ma et al., 2023). Muralid-
495   haran et al. (2024) uses both structured pruning and knowledge distillation to compress LLM models
496   and shows improvement over models trained from scratch. Due to the constraint of removing regu-
497   lar components, structured pruning usually has low sparsity ratios and high accuracy loss. NVIDIA
498   proposes N:M semi-structured sparsity (Mishra et al., 2021), which can preserve model performance
499   by retraining and leverage GPU tensor core acceleration.
500   Pruning for MoE Models. Most of the works for MoE pruning focus on structured expert prun-
501   ing. Chen et al. (2022) and Koishekenov et al. (2023) prune experts based on their utilization to
502   save memory. However, this usually leads to degraded performance. Lu et al. (2024) enumerates
503   expert combinations based on the required expert number and uses calibration data to find a set of
504   remaining experts that has the minimum reconstruction loss. Chowdhury et al. (2024) prunes ex-
505   perts based on the change in the router’s norm and proves that the generalization accuracy can be
506   preserved. However, expert pruning sometimes removes experts with certain knowledge and results
507
      in the loss of model performance. Therefore, Li et al. (2024) and Zhang et al. (2024) both leverage
      expert merging techniques to compress the expert layer while also preserving expert knowledge.
508
      He et al. (2024) proposes a unified framework to compress MoE models. The framework consists
509
      of two perspectives: (i) expert slimming that compresses individual experts by weight pruning and
510   quantization, and (ii) expert trimming that removes whole structured modules by layer drop and
511   block drop.
512
513
      Efficiency for MoE and Existing Solutions. MoE models require huge memory to host expert
      layers, while many experts have low utilization during inference. To address this, Gao et al. (2022)
514
      uses a tensor decomposition method to share the central tensor’s parameters across experts and keep
515
      different auxiliary tensors for each expert. MoQE (Kim et al., 2023) and QMoE (Frantar & Alistarh,
516   2023a) both study extreme low-bit quantization for compressing MoE model size. Moreover, some
517   works employ knowledge distillation (Fedus et al., 2022; Artetxe et al., 2021) to create either a
518   smaller dense model or a MoE model with fewer layers. However, they also overlook the existing
519   redundancy within MoE expert layers. Yadav et al. (2023) shows that experts can be compressed to
520   a huge degree without any performance loss.
521

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      We propose a simple and effective pruning method for MoE models, MoE-Pruner. We prune weights
      with the smallest magnitudes multiplied by the corresponding input activations and router weights,
526
      on each output neuron. Our pruning method is one-shot and fast, without the need for any retrain-
527
      ing or weight update procedures. Pruning MoE LLM with high sparsity will incur performance
528   degradation, so we also propose a fine-tuning method that leverages the unpruned pretrained MoE
529   model as a teacher to guide the pruned student model through expert-wise knowledge distillation.
530   The fine-tuned MoE models could maintain 99% of the performance of the original model after the
531   expert-wise knowledge distillation, using only a small set of training data and low GPU hours. In
532   the future, MoE-Pruner could also be extended to structured pruning of MoE LLMs, such as channel
533   pruning and expert pruning, for better hardware acceleration.
