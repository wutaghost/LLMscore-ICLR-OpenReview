009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, time series forecasting, which predicts future signals, and time series
012            anomaly detection, which identifies abnormal signals in given data, have achieved
013            impressive success. However, in the real world, merely forecasting future signals
014            or detecting anomalies in existing signals is not sufficiently informative to prevent
015            potential system breakdowns, which lead to huge costs and require intensive
016
               human labor. In this work, we tackle a challenging and under-explored problem
               of time series anomaly prediction. In this scenario, the models are required to
017
               forecast the upcoming signals while considering anomaly points to detect them.
018
               To resolve this challenging task, we propose a simple yet effective framework,
019            Anomaly to Prompt (A2P), which is jointly trained via the forecasting and anomaly
020            detection objectives while sharing the feature extractor for better representation.
021            On top of that, A2P leverages Anomaly-Aware Forecasting (AAF), which derives
022            the anomaly probability by random anomaly injection to forecast abnormal time
023            points. Furthermore, we propose Synthetic Anomaly Prompting (SAP) for more
024            robust anomaly detection by enhancing the diversity of abnormal input signals for
025            training anomaly detection model. As a result, our model achieves state-of-the-
026            art performances on seven real-world datasets, proving the effectiveness of our
027
               proposed framework A2P for a new time series anomaly prediction task.

092

121   2    R ELATED W ORK
122
123   Time Series Forecasting. Time series anomaly detection, which is the task of forecasting future
124   signals based on historical observations, is important in terms of practicality, such as network monitor-
125   ing, weather forecasting, economics and finance, and electricity forecasting. Previous works on time
126   series have achieved strong prediction performance by leveraging advances in sequence modeling
127   machine learning methods and deep neural networks such as RNN (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
128   1997; Tokgöz & Ünal, 2018; Abdel-Nasser & Mahmoud, 2019), GNN (Jiang & Luo, 2022; Wang
129   et al., 2022b; Panagopoulos et al., 2021), and CNN (Bai et al., 2018; Livieris et al., 2020) to capture
130
      temporal dependencies. Recently, Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) have begun to be actively used
      for time series forecasting since it has become very prominent in natural language processing (Zhou
131
      et al., 2021; 2022; Wu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Cirstea et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2022; Nie
132
      et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). However, existing forecasting models are trained with only normal
133   signals, excluding the possibility of abnormal events in the signals. Therefore, even though they can
134   effectively learn the features of normal signals, they fail to predict anomalous signals appropriately.
135   Moreover, the forecasting model alone cannot give useful information for any decision-making
136   directly because a human being should investigate the inherited meanings of predicted signals to
137   make use of them, which is time-consuming and labor intensive. Therefore, in this paper, we address
138   this issue by introducing a new task that a model should forecast from given signals and detect
139   anomalies in them.
140   Time Series Anomaly Detection. Multivariate time series anomaly detection is a crucial problem for
141   many applications and has been widely studied. Most of the previous studies are mainly performed
142   in an unsupervised manner considering the restriction on access to abnormal data. Traditional
143   approaches include the density-estimation and clustering-based methods. However, since they cannot
144   consider the complex temporal dynamics most of the recent works focus on deep learning-based
145   approaches. Among them, reconstruction-based approaches (Shen et al., 2021; 2020; Li et al.,
146   2019; Su et al., 2019c; Zhou et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2023) find latent representations of normal
147
      time series data for reconstruction. Recently, Xu et al. (2022) proposes a new association-based
      method, which applies the learnable Gaussian kernel to introduce the adjacent-concentration bias
148
      for better reconstruction. Another recent reconstruction-based model DCdetector (Yang et al., 2023)
149
      achieves a similar goal in a much more general and concise way with a dual-attention self-supervised
150   contrastive-type structure. The existing anomaly detection models focus on detecting the time points
151   of anomalies in already elapsed time, which has limited application in real-world. In this paper, we
152   address a more practical and challenging scenario, Anomaly Prediction to predict the anomalies in
153   future signals which have not arrived yet.
154
      Synthetic Anomalies for Time Series. The intuitive approach to overcome the absence of abnormal
155
      data at training time is to artificially generate them during training time. However, synthetic anomalies
156   and their associated data augmentation techniques have not been widely studied in time series anomaly
157   detection field. Zhang et al. (2022) proposes a data augmentation module to increase the robustness
158   of the network by generating various sequence anomalies. However, it does not consider the specific
159   abnormal time points of augmented signal at training time although it is crucial to distinguish specific
160   abnormal time points at test time. Another approach for synthetic anomaly is introduced in Carmona
161   et al. (2021), which injects synthetic abnormal points into a portion of normal signal. Then it trains
      the contrastive classifier to create embeddings where the part of the signal without injected anomalies

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is distant from the entire signal with anomalies. However, it operates only at the embedding level,
163   making it difficult to precisely identify the exact abnormal points, whereas ours considers both
164   specific anomaly time points and their embedding outputs. Recently, Goswami et al. (2023) injects 9
165   types of anomalies to select the most accurate model for a given dataset without labels. However, all
166   the methods above can only simulate limited forms of anomalies without considering the inherent
167   features of the input signal. In this paper, we design a simple yet effective Synthetic Anomaly Pool
168   method to infuse signal-adaptive learnable anomalies, considering the abnormality of specific time
169   points. Moreover, we propose an energy score-based loss function, Intra-Signal Anomaly Discrepancy

      effectively imitate abnormal characteristics.

184   3.1   S CENARIO D ESCRIPTION : T IME S ERIES A NOMALY P REDICTION
185   Time series anomaly prediction is a novel scenario that aims to pinpoint the exact time steps of
186   anomaly points in the upcoming signals. Specifically, for a given input signal Xin ∈ RLin ×C ,
187   the final goal is to obtain the binary results of anomaly detection O ∈ RLout from the predicted
188   signal X̂out ∈ RLout ×C , where Lin and Lout are the lengths of the input and predicted signals,
189   respectively, and C is the number of channels in the signal. To perform anomaly prediction, we
190   need a network for time series forecasting denoted as ΘF , and a network for time series anomaly
191   detection denoted as ΘAD . Therefore, O can be written as O = ΘAD ◦ ΘF (Xin ) = ΘAD (X̂out ),
192   where X̂out = ΘF (Xin ). For the evaluation of anomaly prediction performance, the F1-score is
193   used as existing time series anomaly detection methods do. The difference in the measurement from
194   the existing methods is that the Point Adjustment (PA) proposed in Audibert et al. (2020) cannot be
195   adopted in original way, since in anomaly prediction, it is important to identify specific time points
196   rather than to identify the existence of anomaly segments. Therefore, we alleviate PA for our metric,
197   which is explained in section 4.
198
199   3.2   U NIFIED A RCHITECTURE FOR A NOMALY P REDICTION
200
201   Existing time series forecasting models such as Nie et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022a); Zhou et al.
202
      (2023); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2021; 2022); Liu et al. (2021) and anomaly detection models
      like Xu et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2023) consider the train set comprised of only normal signals.
203
      Accordingly, although the target task of each model is different, both networks for time series
204
      forecasting and anomaly detection attempt to capture the characteristics of normal time series data in
205   common. Inspired by this point, we adopt a shared backbone to establish a unified architecture to
206   learn the representations of normal signals for both the forecasting and anomaly detection models, as
207   shown in Figure 4.
208
      Specifically, in our framework, several base layers of transformer blocks denoted as θ are shared,
209
      while other specific parts, the embedding layers (eF and eAD ) and output layers (oF and oAD ) to
210
      construct ΘF and ΘAD , exist separately, i.e., ΘF = {eF , oF , θ} and ΘAD = {eAD , oAD , θ}. By
211   sharing the backbone network, our model can accumulate general knowledge for both time series
212   forecasting and anomaly detection effectively, resulting in rich representations and performance
213   improvements. We analyze the effectiveness of our unified framework in Section 4.3 with details.

      Signal-Adaptive Anomaly Prompting. To accurately predict anomaly points, forecasting the future
249
      signal from a prior signal while considering the existence of anomaly points is crucial. To tackle this
250   challenge, we propose a novel approach, named Synthetic Anomaly Prompting (SAP), which utilizes
251   synthetic anomaly prompts for our model to predict future abnormal signals effectively. For SAP, we
252   integrate a new Anomaly Prompt Pool (APP) into our unified architecture, as shown in Figure 4. The
253   purpose of APP, which is a set of additional trainable parameters P , is to guide an input signal to
254   behave like an abnormal signal, by infusing the anomaly prompts in the pool into the original signal.
255
      In detail, APP is defined as P = {(k1 , Z1 ) , (k2 , Z2 ) , · · · , (kM , ZM )}, where Zm ∈ RLz ×D and
256   km ∈ RD denote the m-th anomaly prompt and its corresponding key, respectively, Lz and D are
257   the token length of single anomaly prompt and the embedding dimension, and M is the number of
258   anomaly prompts. Moreover, to select the number of N best-matched prompts with the input signal
259   Xin in the pool, we introduce a feature extractor ff tr (·), which is a simple three-layer transformer
260   architecture with a [CLS] token, as a query function, i.e., q(Xin ) = ff tr ([CLS; Xin ]).

367   Inter-Signal Anomaly Divergence loss and Intra-Signal Anomaly Discrepancy loss, where λ =
368   {λR , λF , λIntra , λInter } is a set of coefficients for weighting each loss term. We used all four
369   coefficients of 1 as default values for experiments. Note that only abnormal signals are used for
370   training during APT, whereas only normal signals are used in the main training phase. After the
371   pre-training phase, the proposed APP is frozen during the main training phase.

524   5      C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we first addressed a novel scenario, named Anomaly Prediction (AP), where the
527   model needs to detect abnormal time points from unarrived future signals. We tackled this issue
528   by establishing a unified architecture that shares the feature extractor for forecasting and anomaly
529
      detection models. In addition, we employed to use synthetic anomalies in train time, whereas
      traditional time series forecasting and anomaly detection models were trained with only normal
530
      time series features, limiting their generalizability to abnormal signals. We proposed two effective
531
      approaches, Anomaly-Aware Forecasting (AAF) and Synthetic Anomaly Prompting (SAP). In AAF,
532   we designed an Anomaly-Aware Forecasting Network to help the model forecast time steps with
533   anomalies. In SAP, we defined an anomaly prompt pool which learns how to prompt the input
534   signals to have anomalous features. In addition, we devised two novel loss terms, energy loss and
535   Inter-Signal Anomaly Divergence loss, to make the anomaly-prompted features more anomalous. We
536   achieved state-of-the-art performances on the Anomaly Prediction task in seven real-world datasets,
537   demonstrating the effectiveness of our methods through comprehensive experiments. We hope
538   our pioneering attempt to predict future anomalies provides an opportunity to anticipate potential
539   breakdowns, while also opening up a new direction for research.
