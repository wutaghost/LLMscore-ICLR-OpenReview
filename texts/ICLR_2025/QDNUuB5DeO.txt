009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conflicting objectives present a considerable challenge in interleaving multi-task
012            learning, necessitating the need for meticulous design and balance to ensure effec-
013            tive learning of a representative latent data space across all tasks without mutual
014            negative impact. Drawing inspiration from the concept of marginal and condi-
015            tional probability distributions in probability theory, we design a principled and
016
               well-founded approach to disentangle the original input into marginal and condi-
               tional probability distributions in the latent space of a variational autoencoder. Our
017
               proposed model, Deep Disentangled Interleaving Variational Encoding (Deep-
018
               DIVE) learns disentangled features from the original input to form clusters in
019            the embedding space and unifies these features via the cross-attention mechanism
020            in the fusion stage. We theoretically prove that combining the objectives for re-
021            construction and forecasting fully captures the lower bound and mathematically
022            derive a loss function for disentanglement using Naı̈ve Bayes. Under the assump-
023            tion that the prior is a mixture of log-concave distributions, we also establish that
024            the Kullback-Leibler divergence DKL between the prior and the posterior is up-
025            per bounded by a function minimized by the minimizer of the cross entropy loss,
026            informing our adoption of radial basis functions (RBF) and cross entropy with
027
               interleaving training for DeepDIVE to provide a justified basis for convergence.
               Experiments on two public datasets show that DeepDIVE disentangles the original
028
               input and yields forecast accuracies better than the original VAE and comparable
029
               to existing state-of-the-art baselines.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In multi-objective deep learning, gradients from different objectives can conflict, when the different
035   loss terms induce competing gradient directions during training of the network. Balancing these
036   gradients to ensure stable and effective learning is a significant challenge prompting the development
037   of methods to mitigate this issue, such as Liu et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2020); Sener & Koltun (2018)
038   which solve an additional optmization problem before each gradient update step, to manipulate
039
      conflicting gradients before the update. In contrast to such methods which usually involve some
      additional computations to search for non-conflicting gradient updates, we derive the overall loss
040
      function from the log likelihood of a pre-existing data point, hence it follows that these objectives
041
      exhibit no mutual conflict.
042
043   Contribution. In this paper, our primary contribution lies in extending the original VAE architecture
044   to include the use of labelled data for a forecasting use case. We motivate the use of semi-supervised
045
      learning considering the fact that while it is common to employ a sequence as input to a time se-
      ries forecasting model, other available labels that classify the entire sequence are often overlooked.
046
      For this task, the benefits of our proposed Deep Disentangled Interleaving Variational Encoding
047
      (DeepDIVE) architecture are two-fold. (i) Building upon the statistical foundations provided by the
048   Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) for learning deep latent-variable models
049   and corresponding inference models for reconstruction, DeepDIVE also provides a principled and
050   well-founded semi-supervised framework for learning deep latent-variable models and correspond-
051   ing inference models which is useful for reconstruction, forecasting, and classification. (ii) We also
052   believe that probabilistic latent variable models such as VAEs hold great potential for facilitating
053   autonomous planning and resource allocation in situations characterized by uncertainty, mirroring
      real-world scenarios.

054
      To address this task, DeepDIVE learns a disentangled representation space without conflicting ob-
055   jectives. The main contributions of our study are summarized as follows:
056
057          • To obtain objectives that are not mutually conflicting, we theoretically extend the VAE
058            architecture originally intended for generative reconstruction tasks to a generative forecast-
059            ing case by proving that combining the objectives for reconstruction and forecasting fully
060            captures the lower bound.
061          • Drawing inspiration from the concept of marginal and conditional probability distribu-
062            tions in probability theory, we mathematically derive a loss function to disentangle the
063            model input in the latent space by applying Bayes’ theorem with the “naı̈ve” assumption
064            of independence among the marginal dimensions conditional on the original input. This
065
               marginalisation approach decomposes the input into its more manageable constituent ele-
               ments, yielding univariate distributions that can be useful for human-interpretable analysis
066
               of data representations while maintaining the absence of conflict across the different objec-
067
               tives.
068
             • Following these derivations, we design DeepDIVE to learn disentangled features from the
069
               original input, and further propose to unify these features via the cross-attention mechanism
070
               in the fusion stage. The disentangled embedding space easily lends itself to exploration of
071            how important features of the data is encapsulated and encoded.
072
073   Paper Organization. We structure our paper as follows: In Section 2 we introduce notation related
074   to the VAE loss function and review related works for disentangling the latent space. In Section 3 we
075   derive the loss function for extending the VAE to accommodate a forecasting scenario, and establish
076   that the minimizer of the cross entropy loss also minimizes the DKL under certain assumptions.
077   Following this, in Section 4 we propose DeepDIVE and empirically validate its benefits in Section
078
      5. Finally, we summarize our main contributions and provide further details and more complete
      proofs in the Appendix.

083   2.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER AND THE R EPARAMETERIZATION T RICK
084
      Our work is built upon the VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014), originally intended for reconstruction
085
      tasks. In their paper, Kingma & Welling (2014) assume that each arbitrary data sample x(i) is
086
      generated from some random process, where the likelihood pθ∗ (x(i) |z (i) ) involves a hidden random
087
088
      variable z (i) generated from some prior pθ∗ (z), and pθ∗ (x|z) and pθ∗ (z) come from parametric
      families of distributions pθ (x|z) and pθ (z) with the true parameters θ∗ ∈ Θ unknown. In the
089
      following we omit index i for convenience. To learn an approximation of the (intractable) true
090
      posterior pθ∗ (z|x), the authors proposed the VAE architecture, which we can view as a combination
091   of 3 components:
092
093          • Probabilistic decoder pθ (x|z) parameterized by learned generative model parameters θ ∈ Θ
094            approximates the likelihood and maps latent random variable z to a conditional distribution
095            over the data x.
096          • Prior pθ (z) over the latent random variables z, where any learnable parameters are also
097            considered to be part of θ.
098          • Probabilistic encoder qϕ (z|x) parameterized by learned recognition model parameters
099            ϕ ∈ Φ approximates the intractable true posterior, ie. qϕ (z|x) ≈ pθ (z|x), and maps data
100            observations x to a distribution over the possible values of z in the latent space.
101
102
      Observing that the log likelihood of the model can be expressed as the following sum:
103                           log pθ (x) = L(θ, ϕ; x) + DKL (qϕ (z|x) ∥ pθ (z|x)),                       (1)
104   where the second RHS term representing the DKL between the approximate and true posterior
105   is always non-negative, we have that log pθ (x) is lower bounded by the variational lower bound
106   L(θ, ϕ; x):
107
                         L(θ, ϕ; x) = Eqϕ (z|x) [log pθ (x|z)] − DKL (qϕ (z|x) ∥ pθ (z))                 (2)

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      Thus, since direct maximization of the log likelihood Requation 1 is not possible due to the intractable
109   term pθ (z|x) = pθ (x|z)pθ (z)/pθ (x) where pθ (x) = Z pθ (x|z)pθ (z) dz, we instead jointly train the
110   generative model parameters θ and recognition model parameters ϕ to maximize the lower bound.

      The reparameterization trick was proposed as a solution to the high variance of the expected recon-
      struction error (first RHS term) in equation 2. By introducing a new random variable ϵ, the originally
113
      random z can thus be formulated as a deterministic function of ϵ, instead of directly sampling z from
114
      Z ∼ qϕ (z|x) as in the usual Monte Carlo estimation.

117   of the DKL .

121   2.2.1     W EIGHTED DKL
122
123   Viewing the latent space as an embedding space and the outputs of the encoder as embedded data,
124   the latent space of a VAE can be made interpretable when restricted to low dimensions, or by pro-
125   jecting a multi-dimensional latent space into a lower dimension via PCA. The prevalent methods
126   to disentangle the VAE encourage the model to learn these independent factorizations directly by
127
      simply increasing the influence of the regularization term (second RHS term) in equation 2. Higgins
      et al. (2017) proposed the β-VAE by constraining the divergence between the prior and approximate
128
      posterior to be less than some ϵ > 0, which the authors reformulated as a Lagrangian under KKT
129
      conditions. Since the divergence between the distributions is at most ϵ, and the prior is chosen with
130   0 covariance, thus the covariance between dimensions in the latent space is also close to 0. The
131   Lagrangian dual problem:
132
133                  min max Eqϕ (z|x) [log pθ (x|z)] − β ∗ DKL (qϕ (z|x) ∥ pθ (z)) + β ∗ ϵ               (3)
                       β   θ,ϕ
134
135                          st.β ≥ 0                                                                     (4)
136
137   has β ∗ ϵ ≥ 0 by complimentary slackness. Since the optimal value of β depends on the hyperpa-
138   rameter ϵ, hence ϵ is removed from the equation and β is treated as a hyperparameter. Thus, the
139
      model is trained by maximizing the lower bound Eqϕ (z|x) [log pθ (x|z)] − β ∗ DKL (qϕ (z|x) ∥ pθ (z))
      of the Lagrangian formulation, where the authors set hyperparameter β > 1 to drive the divergence
140
      to be closer to 0.
141
142   Burgess et al. (2018) and Sankarapandian & Kulis (2021) extend this idea to learn factors in order of
143   importance by encouraging the values of the regularization term to increase as training progresses.
144   The former’s Bottleneck VAE pressures the divergence to be close to a controllable value C that is
145
      gradually increased from zero, while the latter’s β-annealed VAE proposed a gradual decrease of β,
      from β-VAE’s loss formulation. Bae et al. (2023) proposed a separate extension with the Multi-Rate
146
      VAE, which constructs the rate-distortion curve to learn the optimal parameters corresponding to
147
      various values of β in a single training run.

410   4     D EEP D ISENTANGLED I NTERLEAVING VARIATIONAL E NCODING
411         (D EEP DIVE)
412

      The derived loss function in theorem 1 plays a pivotal role in informing the architecture and training
414   paradigm of DeepDIVE, specifically: (i) Employing cross entropy loss in place of DKL for the
415   marginal dimensions, (ii) Utilizing interleaving training to optimize model performance, since cross
416   entropy loss is only a bound on the DKL , and (iii) Integrating a Gaussian radial basis function layer
417   into the model, which satisfies the assumptions made in the sufficiency corollary.
418
      Our latent space consists of n1 + n2 latent dimensions, where n2 dimensions represent the marginal
419
      embedding features and are inputs to classifiers, and the remaining n1 dimensions represent the other
420   conditional embedding features. The classification from the latent space is employed as an auxiliary
421   task in order to encourage clustering in the relevant dimension in the latent space, from which we
422   can obtain the marginal probability distribution in each dimension conditional on the input data
423   point. Jointly, the n2 latent dimensions capture the marginal distribution of the input data patterns
424   conditioned on the marginal embedding features. This results in disentangling of the original input
425   data into individual factors of variation, where data presented in this univariate form would be more
426   meaningful for visualization and analysis from a user perspective.
427   Furthermore, substituting the DKL term with the cross entropy loss eliminates the need for manual
428   crafting of a prior, especially considering the case of a mixture model. The usual approach of a
429   standard normal prior centers the latent embeddings at the origin. Alternatively, allowing K classes
430   and specifying K means or centroids is also undesirable as this may inhibit the emergence of patterns
431   inherent in the data. Moreover, while the DKL can be calculated for a Normal prior, this term is
      intractable when the probability distribution is a mixture model. In contrast, learning the parameters


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      that define the prior of each class in the mixture would allow the model to better capture and reflect
433   relationships between and within classes.

452
      the marginal dimensions or Ltotal for the conditional dimensions. Formally, for the main network

453

454

455   classification network pass, or to the conditional embedding features during the main network pass.
456   To allow the model to learn conditional dimensions a conditioned on marginal dimensions b, during
457   main network backpropagation we freeze the RBF layers for the marginal dimensions, together with
458   the weights in the last layer of the encoder that affect the marginal latent dimensions. The key here
459   is to decouple the learning process of the marginal and conditional latent dimensions in an interleav-
460   ing training scheme. Intuitively, this is similar to the alternating least squares algorithm (Zachariah
461   et al., 2012), where the algorithm alternates between fixing the first factor when updating the second,
462   and fixing the second factor when updating the first. However, one main difference is that in our
463
      case it is always the marginal dimensions that are fixed when the conditional dimensions are trained,
      so that it is always the conditional dimensions that are conditioned on the marginal ones.

581
582
583   In this paper, we present a theoretical extension of the VAE to accommodate forecasting scenar-
584   ios, with disentanglement via the application of Bayes’ theorem with the “naı̈ve” assumption of
585
      independence among the supervised dimensions conditional on the original input. Despite being
      multi-objective in nature, our overall loss function was derived from the log likelihood of a pre-
586
      existing data point, hence it follows that these objectives exhibit no mutual conflict. Leveraging
587
      this mathematical basis, we present Deep Disentangled Interleaving Variational Encoding (Deep-
588   DIVE), designed for learning a disentangled representation space without conflicting objectives in
589   interleaving multi-task learning. Experimental validation across 2 datasets confirms the benefits of
590   DeepDIVE, which utilizes the shared representation space to achieve results superior to both the
591   original VAE and β-TCVAE, and comparable to existing state-of-the-art. Exploring the capabili-
592   ties of the shared representation space encoded by DeepDIVE in other downstream tasks such as
593   anomaly detection and augmenting large language models (LLMs) offers a compelling direction for
      future research.

718   A.1   P ROBABILISTIC C ORRELATED T IME -S ERIES F ORECASTING
719
720   Probabilistic time-series forecasting aims to learn the correlation between historical signals and
721   future outcomes to obtain a probability distribution over possible future outcomes. The classical
722   methods for probabilistic multivariate forecasting are mainly dedicated to AR models, which can be
723   represented in state space form, and Bayesian forecasting methods (Rangapuram et al., 2018; Qiu
724
      et al., 2018; Dorfman & Havenner, 1992). In more recent years, the advancement of data collection
      and increasing power of computing facilities have increased the feasibility of deep networks, which
725
      have gained popularity in multivariate probabilistic time-series forecasting due to their ability to
726
      capture non-linear temporal dependencies in the data. For example, DeepAR (Salinas et al., 2020)
727   is a popular univariate forecasting method which fits a shared global RNN across multiple related
728   time-series after scaling, where the network is adapted to probabilistic forecasting by Monte Carlo
729   sampling methods. Closer to our work, the Temporal Latent Autoencoder (TLAE) (Nguyen &
730   Quanz, 2021) enhances the DeepGLO (Sen et al., 2019), which uses low-rank matrix factorization
731   to obtain a set of basis time-series that captures global properties, and a Temporal Convolutional
732   Network (TCN) on these basis time-series to generate global forecasts. The global forecasts are then
733   concatenated with other variables and fed into another TCN, viewed as the local TCN, to forecast
734   for each individual time-series. Building on this study, TLAE replaces the global TCN with an
735
      encoder and local TCN with a decoder in an autoencoder architecture, with a temporal deep neural
      network model in the latent space to encourage evolution of the embeddings over time. Since TLAE
736
      introduces non-linearity into the global encoder and local decoder and is expected to perform better
737
      in cases when there are sufficient training data, from among this group we only compare against
738   TLAE in our experiments.
739
740   However, we note that the use of an RNN within the latent space may overly reduce the flexibility
741
      of the model, since the temporal trends are learnt within the bottleneck section of the model. With a
      similar idea of global and local forecasting, DSANet (Huang et al., 2019) uses a dual-branch TCN
742
      where one branch operates on a global level and the other operates on a local level, before fusing the
743
      features from both branches with a fully connected MLP to capture complex linear combinations
744   of both global and local temporal patterns. The authors of AutoCTS (Wu et al., 2021) introduce a
745   design with both micro and macro search spaces that model possible architectures, and uses Neural
746   Architecture Search (NAS) (Elsken et al., 2019) to jointly explore the search spaces and automati-
747   cally discover highly competitive models. Models identified by the framework has been shown to
748   outperform state-of-the-art human-designed models, thus eliminating the need for manual design.
749   LightCTS (Lai et al., 2023), with its adoption of plain stacking of TCN and transformer, can be
750   viewed as an autoencoder with a TCN encoder and transformer decoder and an interpretable latent
751   space, that is capable of state-of-the-art accuracies.

753   and we also report the standard deviation of the RRSE across 30 runs. Table 3 shows that despite
754   being trained on multiple objective functions, derived from the loss function L(θ, ϕ; x, y) that is
755   only a lower bound of the true negative log-likelihood loss log pθ (x, y), DeepDIVE still produces
      results comparable to the existing state-of-the-art trained on only the forecasting task.
