011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Point cloud learning, especially in a self-supervised way without manual labels,
014            has received emerging attention in both vision and learning communities, with
015            its potential utility in wide areas. Most existing generative approaches for point
016            cloud self-supervised learning focus on recovering masked points from visible
017
               ones within a single view. Recognizing that a two-view pre-training paradigm
               inherently introduces greater diversity and variance, it could thus enable more
018
               challenging and informative pre-training. Inspired by this, we explore the po-
019
               tential of two-view learning in this domain. In this paper, we propose Point-
020            PQAE, a cross-reconstruction generative paradigm that first generates two decou-
021            pled point clouds/views and then reconstructs one from the other. To achieve this
022            goal, we develop a crop mechanism for point cloud view generation for the first
023            time and further propose a novel positional encoding to represent the 3D relative
024            position between the two decoupled views. The cross-reconstruction significantly
025            increases the difficulty of pre-training compared to self-reconstruction, which en-
026            ables our method to achieve new state-of-the-art results and surpasses previous
027            single-modal self-reconstruction methods in 3D self-supervised learning by a mar-
028
               gin. Specifically, it outperforms self-reconstruction baseline (Point-MAE) 6.5%,
               7.0%, 6.7% in three variants of ScanObjectNN with M LP -L INEAR evaluation pro-
029
               tocol. Source code will be released.

034   3D vision is gaining increasing attention for its wide applications such as autonomous driving (Qian
035   et al., 2022b) and robotics (Sergiyenko & Tyrsa, 2020; Enebuse et al., 2021), owing to its ability to
036   understand the human world. The point cloud is the most popular representation form of data in 3D
037   vision and many analyses based on point cloud are explored to solve various tasks such as object
038   classification (Qi et al., 2017a;b; Wang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018b; Guo et al., 2021), object detec-
039
      tion (Misra et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2019; Zhou & Tuzel, 2018) and segmentation (Qi et al., 2017a;
      Wang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018b; Guo et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023), they typically necessitate
040
      fully-supervised training from scratch. Compared to 2D data, point cloud generally requires more
041
      expensive and labor-intensive efforts to collect and annotate, which refrains the development of the
042   fully supervised 3D representation learning method. Self-supervised learning is one of the predom-
043   inant approaches to address this issue and is proven to be effective in 2D vision (Chen et al., 2020;
044   He et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; He et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020). Inspired by
045   this, self-supervised learning has been widely studied in the 3D field in recent years.
046
      Self-supervised learning in 3D can be mainly divided into two categories, i.e., generative meth-
047
      ods (Chen et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2018a; Achlioptas et al.,
048   2018; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) and contrastive methods (Afham et al., 2022; Xie et al.,
049   2020; Dong et al., 2022; Qi et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2021; Sanghi, 2020; Du et al., 2021). Similar
050   to 2D, contrastive methods in 3D aim to learn global-discriminative information by maximizing
051   the mutual information across views (Afham et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2022) with
052   intra-/inter- modal information. PointContrast (Xie et al., 2020) is the first to learn transformation
053   invariance across views through the extension of InfoNCE objective (Oord et al., 2018; Xie et al.,
      2020). Different from contrastive methods, The core idea of the generative methods (Pang et al.,

076   MAE (Pang et al., 2022) is one of the conventional methods, which learns point-wise information

081   Existing generative methods mostly focus on the masked reconstruction in a single view, where
082   single-view learning has been proven less difficult and informative than two views (Chen et al.,
083   2020; Grill et al., 2020) since two views would bring more variance than the single case. To bring
084   the benefits of the two-view learning paradigm to the generative learning field, one straightforward
085   assumption is adopting the two-view learning paradigm in the 3D generative pertaining tasks to
086   increase the difficulty of point-wise reconstruction. However, unlike 2D vision data, constructing
087
      two views in the 3D point cloud is more challenging, as point cloud data is less structured. Be-
      sides, cross-reconstruction (reconstructing one view from the other view) is much more difficult
088
      than self-reconstruction (reconstructing the masked single view), which involves learning both the
089
      inter-positional relation between two views and the inner spatial information within the view. The
090   differences between these two paradigms can be seen in Figure 1. Aimed at solving the above issues,
091   we propose Point-PQAE (Position Query based AutoEncoder for Point Cloud), a novel framework
092   aimed at addressing two primary challenges: i) how to construct two views in point cloud data, and
093   ii) how to perform cross-reconstruction between two views. As shown in Figure 2, for each point
094   cloud, we first apply a custom-designed point cloud crop mechanism, which randomly selects two
095   points as the central points of two views and records their positions. Following this, for each central
096   point, the nearest points according to a specified ratio are incorporated into the view. Subsequently,
097   we normalize the cropped point cloud and apply random augmentation (e.g., rotation) to each point
098
      cloud to achieve view decoupling. Then we calculate the relative positional embedding (RPE) for
      these two decoupled views. Finally, we take the RPE and one view of the point cloud as input to
099
      predict the point-wise input of the other view. The highlights of this paper are:
100
101   i) New generative framework: We propose Point-PQAE, the first framework that successfully
102   brings cross-reconstruction to 3D generative field for point cloud self-supervised learning, with
103   three modules: 1) decoupled views generation, 2) RPE generation, and 3) positional query block.
104
      To our knowledge, we are the first to design and apply crop mechanism to point cloud self-supervised
      learning. Our framework, Point-PQAE, enables more informative and challenging self-supervised
105
      pre-training compared to existing traditional self-reconstruction methods.
106
107   ii) The first relative position-aware query module for point cloud: We introduce a positional
      query block after the encoder to capture relative position information between decoupled views.

      We briefly review the literature on self-supervised learning in vision and point cloud.
142   SSL in 2D Vision. Self-supervised learning has been well-explored in 2D vision. These meth-
143   ods can be roughly divided into two categories, i.e., Masked-Image-Modeling-based (MIM-based)
144   and Contrastive-based. Contrastive methods usually learn discriminative information by crafting
145   two different views through random augmentation and learning the invariance of two views. Sim-
146   CLR (Chen et al., 2020) is one of the conventional methods, which takes the views augmented from
147
      the same image as the positive pair, and the views augmented from different images in the same
      mini-batch as the negative pairs. MoCo (He et al., 2020) utilizes a memory bank to store these neg-
148
      ative samples and further proposes a momentum-updated mechanism to avoid model collapse. To
149
      further reduce the effect of negative pairs, BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) borrows the idea in reinforce-
150   ment learning, using online and target networks and Exponential Moving Average (EMA) to update
151   the target network. BYOL directly adds the l2 objective on the target output and online output, avoid-
152   ing the use of negative pairs. Depart from contrastive learning, MIM-based methods usually mask
153   the input image and make models to reconstruct the masked area, which could enable models to
154   capture fine-grained information. MAE (He et al., 2022) and SimMIM (Xie et al., 2022) are the two
155   widely-known methods belonging to the MIM-based family. MAE divides the network into encoder
156   and decoder parts. Then, MAE masks the image and directly drops this masked area, followed by
157   the encoder-decoder to reconstruct. Different from MAE, SimMIM (Xie et al., 2022) replaces these
158
      masked patches with a learnable parameter to reconstruct these masked patches. Following MAE
      and SimMIM, variant directions are well explored, e.g., masking strategy (Zhang et al., 2023c; Li
159
      et al., 2022) and reconstruct feature map (Zhou et al., 2021).
160
161   Representation Learning for Point Clouds. It is a challenging task due to its irregular and sparse
      nature when compared to other modalities such as images which are well-structured and uniform.


                                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      PointContrast (Xie et al., 2020) firstly borrows the idea of contrastive learning in 2D and per-
163   forms point-level invariant mapping on two transformed views of the given point cloud. On the
164   basis of PointContrast, CrossPoint (Afham et al., 2022) further proposes inter and intra-modal con-
165   trastive objectives to enhance the global-discriminative capability. To better model multi-modal data,
166   ACT (Dong et al., 2022) employs cross-modal auto-encoders as teacher models to acquire knowl-
167   edge from other modalities. Depart from contrastive objectives, Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022)
168   proposes to randomly mask points, and reconstruct the masked part through the visible part. Fol-
169   lowing Point-MAE and CrossPoint, ReCon (Qi et al., 2023) harmoniously incorporates the genera-
170   tive method Point-MAE and the contrastive method. Almost all the existing generative methods
171
      in 3D rely on self-reconstruction. To this end, we propose a new cross-reconstruction generative
      framework, named Point-PQAE, which increases the difficulty of pre-training by introducing recon-
172
      struction between two decoupled views into training, bringing more variance to the training samples,
173
      as opposed to within a single view. This requires the model to effectively learn both intra-view and
174   inter-view knowledge, thereby enabling the model to learn more semantic representations.

178   3.1   P OINT C LOUDS V IEWS AND E MBEDDINGS G ENERATION
179
      Decoupled Views Generation. 2D image Random Crop has been widely used in both the su-
180   pervised domain (He et al., 2016; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and the
181   self-supervised domain (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2021), and it has been proved
182   to be effective in practice. This method typically involves randomly selecting a rectangular region
183   from an image and cropping it. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no corresponding
184   crop mechanism proposed in 3D, especially for self-supervised representation learning. Instead of
185   directly bringing the crop algorithm in 2D to 3D (directly choose a random cube in space), which
186   will bring inconsistent problems (the points contained by the cubes of the same size may change
187   intensively due to the variety of point cloud distribution in 3D), we design in a smarter way: For
188
      a randomly selected crop ratio, we first select a center point randomly and then choose the corre-
      sponding number of points in the point cloud that are closest to the center point.
189
190   Formally, given an input point cloud with p points X ∈ Rp×3 and a pre-defined minimum crop ratio
191   rm where 0 < rm < 1, we first randomly select the crop ratios r1 and r2 uniformly from [rm , 1].
192   Then, we randomly sample two center points C1 ∈ R1×3 and C2 ∈ R1×3 and get X1 and X2
193   by expanding nearest r1 × p and r2 × p points to the center point C1 and C2 respectively in X.
194
      Meanwhile, we record the absolute coordinates of cropped point clouds’ geometric centers, which
      are denoted as L1 ∈ R1×3 and L2 ∈ R1×3 , respectively. The process can be formulated as:
195
196                  r1 , r2 = UniformSample([rm , 1]), C1 , C2 = RandomSelect(X),                       (1)
197                         X1 = N-Nearest(C1 , r1 , X), X2 = N-Nearest(C2 , r2 , X)                     (2)
198
199   where X1 ∈ Rr1 p×3 and X2 ∈ Rr2 p×3 mean the two cropped views. The X1 and X2 are nor-
200   malized using the min-max normalization, centered on L1 and L2 , respectively. Subsequently, we
201   apply additional augmentation to further enhance the variance between two views. Specifically, we
202   apply random rotations to the cropped views. The distinct normalization centers, along with random
203
      rotation, effectively isolate the coordinate systems of the two views and alter their fixed relative
      relationships. This process decouples the two point clouds, resulting in two independent views.
204
205   Point Patches Generation. Following Point-BERT (Yu et al., 2022), we divide the input point
206   cloud into irregular point patches (may overlap) via the Farthest Point Sampling(FPS) and K-Nearest
207   Neighborhood (KNN) algorithm. Formally, given two point clouds (views) X1 and X2 , we first use
208   the FPS algorithm to sample pre-defined n points as group centers, which we called G1 ∈ Rn×3
209
      and G2 ∈ Rn×3 , respectively. Then, based on the center points, we use KNN to choose the pre-
210
      defined k-nearest points for each group center G(i) (0 ≤ i ≤ n) and finally get n groups (each
      group contains k points). We apply the FPS and KNN on X1 and X2 , respectively:
211
212                                  G1 = FPS(X1 ), G2 = FPS(X2 )                                        (3)
213                              P1 = KNN(X1 , G1 ), P2 = KNN(X2 , G2 )                                  (4)
214
215   where G1 , G2 ∈ Rn×3 and P1 , P2 ∈ Rn×(k·3) . Then, the P1 and P2 both are treated as a
      sequence of length n and dimension k · 3 as the input.

      3.2   BACKBONE WITH P OSITIONAL Q UERY
217
218   Following (Pang et al., 2022), we adopt a lightweight PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a), followed by the
219   standard Transformer with an asymmetric encoder-decoder structure in the Point-PQAE. After the
220   encoder projects the input embedding into the latent space, we use the latent representation of one
221   view and relative positional embedding (RPE) between two views to implement the position query
222
      through cross-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017). We then directly feed the output of the position query
      block to the decoder to reconstruct another view without using extra positional embedding. The last
223
      layer of the autoencoder adopts a simple prediction head to achieve the cross-reconstruction target.
224
225   Encoding. The encoder of Point-PQAE consists of the lightweight PointNet and a standard Trans-
226   former blocks (Vaswani et al., 2017), which projects P1 and P2 to latent space, respectively:
227                          H1 = Encoder(P1 , G1 ), H2 = Encoder(P2 , G2 ),                             (5)
228
229   where H1 ∈ Rn×D and H2 ∈ Rn×D mean the latent representations of the view 1 and view 2. Note
230   that D is the hidden dimension of the networks.
231   Positional Query Block. After obtaining latent representations H1 and H2 , we feed them into the
232   proposed positional query block to extract cross-information. The module can be mainly divided
233   into 3 sub-stages: attain relative information, RPE generation, and cross-attention.
234
      Attain Relative Information. Take reconstructing view 2 from view 1 as an example. To reconstruct
235   view 2, we have to know the latent representations of view 1 and the relative positions of view 2
236   centered on view 1, where the latent representations of view 1 can be obtained through the encoder
237   network. Therefore, the key to reconstructing view 2 is to obtain the relative positions of view 2
238   centered on view 1. Back to the crop operation, we have recorded the geometric centers of views,
239   denoted as L1 and L2 , respectively. We define the relative position of view 2 centered on view 1 as
240   RL1→2 = L1 − L2 where RL1→2 ∈ R1×3 . To better align the dimension in the latter operation, we
241   expand the RL1→2 to Rn×3 by repeating the content for n times. By obtaining the relative positions
242   between the two views, we further add the group center location to help the PQ modules reconstruct
243
      each group of the inputs. Specifically, we define the group-/patch- wise relative positions as:
244                 RP1→2 = ConCat(G2 , RL1→2 ), RP2→1 = ConCat(G1 , RL2→1 )                             (6)
245
      where RP ∈ Rn×6 (as G ∈ Rn×3 and RL ∈ Rn×3 ) and ConCat(·, ·) is the concatenate operation.
246
247   Relative Positional Embedding (RPE). Instead of simply using learnable positional encoding, which
248   could hurt the expression of the relative positions between the two views (see ablation in Sec. 4.3),
249   we use the fixed positional encoding of relative information to cut the uncertainty. Specifically, for
250   elements in the second dimension of RP1→2 , we use a popular form (He et al., 2022) in Transform-
251
      ers to generate relative positional embedding RPE1→2 ∈ Rn×D :

      once as stated in Section 3.2, and the number of heads is set to 6 and remains 384 hidden dimensions.

502   Effectiveness of the Crop Mechanism. Random crop has been shown to be highly effective in
503
      2D self-supervised learning (SSL), particularly for contrastive learning (Grill et al., 2020; Chen
      et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2021), and is also vital for generative learning (He et al., 2022). However,
504
      its potential in 3D SSL remains largely unexplored. Even contrastive learning methods for point
505
      cloud understanding have not explored the power of it (Afham et al., 2022; Pang et al., 2023). To
506   address this gap, we first introduce a custom-designed random crop mechanism for point cloud
507   data, using it as the foundation for generating decoupled views. Without the crop augmentation,
508   the two views will contain exactly the same shape though with normalization and rotation applied.
509   The model would only need to infer the augmentation rather than the inter-view relationship,
510   which means the views are not decoupled. Conversely, given two isolated cropped point clouds,
511   followed by min-max normalization and rotation for further decoupling, there remain overlapping
512   and non-overlapping parts, requiring the model to effectively encode the intra-view parts in order to
513   infer the unknown inter-view points in our Point-PQAE. However, directly applying random crop
514
      as an additional augmentation to the self-reconstruction method (Point-MAE) does not improve
      the performance much, as shown in Table 4c. In other words, it means our Point-PQAE fits
515
      crop mechanism better by harmoniously incorporating it into the decoupled views generation
516
      process. The experiment results in Table 4c demonstrate the critical role of random crop in our
517   method. We set minimum crop ratio rm = 1.0 when removing the crop in our Point-PQAE.
518
519   Analysis on Decoupled Views Generation. While crop plays an important role in our method,
520
      relying solely on the random crop mechanism to generate new views would degenerate cross-
      reconstruction to simply reconstructing one part from another. This is similar to the block mask
521
      self-reconstruction used in Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022), where visible blocks are used to recon-
522
      struct masked blocks, which is also trivial for pre-training. The only difference between them is
523   that the cropped parts can overlap somewhat, whereas the block mask does not. What makes the
524   cross-reconstruction different from block mask self-reconstruction are the subsequent augmentations
525   including normalization and rotation that decouple the cropped parts. By normalizing cropped point
526   clouds centered on the geometric centers, the coordinate systems of the two point clouds become
527   isolated and independent from each other, with rotation further amplifying the variance between the
528   two views, thus enabling the generation of two decoupled views. The importance of the augmenta-
529   tions applied after the random crop can be validated through experiments, as shown in Table 4d.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
      We have proposed Point-PQAE, a novel cross-reconstruction generative framework for self-
533
      supervised learning on 3D point clouds. In contrast to well-studied self-reconstruction schemes,
534   Point-PQAE reconstructs one cropped point cloud via the other decoupled point cloud. To supply
535   sufficient information to perform cross-reconstruction, we further propose a 3D relative positional
536   embedding and corresponding position-aware query module. Compared to self-reconstruction, the
537   cross-reconstruction task carefully designed by us is much more challenging for pre-training, pro-
538   moting the learning of richer semantic representations during pre-training. This enables our pro-
539   posed Point-PQAE to outperform previous single-modal self-reconstruction methods by a margin
      and to perform on par with or better than cross-modal methods.

815   A    A DDTIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
816
817   Our Point-PQAE pioneers the cross-reconstruction paradigm in 3D point cloud self-supervised
818   learning (SSL). There are two essential components in our proposed cross-reconstruction frame-
819   work: 1) Two isolated/decoupled views, rather than two parts of the same instance that maintain a
820   fixed relative relationship. 2) A model that achieves cross-view reconstruction using relative posi-
821   tion information. To our knowledge, there are no similar previous methods in this domain. To better
822
      position our methodology, we compare it to similar SSL methods, including SiamMAE (Gupta et al.,
      2023) and CropMAE (Eymaël et al., 2025), proposed in the image domain. SiamMAE operates on
823
      pairs of randomly sampled video frames and asymmetrically masks them, utilizing the past frame to
824
      predict the masked future frame. CropMAE relies on image augmentations to generate two views,
825   using one to reconstruct the other.
826
827   Relation of SiamMAE and CropMAE to our Point-PQAE: All are cross-reconstruction methods.
828
      Both SiamMAE and CropMAE have two essential components for cross-reconstruction-framework
      including multi-view (different frames sampled from one video for SiamMAE and isolated aug-
829
      mented images for CropMAE) and cross-view reconstruction model. They can be treated as cross-
830
      reconstruction methods, similar to our Point-PQAE.
831
832   Difference of SiamMAE and CropMAE to our Point-PQAE: 1) Different domain: SiamMAE
833   and CropMAE focus on the 2D SSL domain. Our Point-PQAE is the first method for cross-
834
      reconstruction in the 3D SSL domain. 2) Asymmetric/symmetric reconstruction: SiamMAE uses
      the past to predict the future, which is asymmetric. CropMAE performs asymmetric reconstruc-
835
      tion and doesn’t explore siamese cross-reconstruction. In contrast, our Point-PQAE is inherently
836
      symmetric, and the siamese loss brings a performance gain. 3) No relative information utilized:
837   SiamMAE and CropMAE do not incorporate relative information into training but rely on non-fully
838   masking to guide the cross-reconstruction. The RPE adopted by our Point-PQAE provides explicit
839   guidance, making training more stable and improving explainability. 4) No tuned-needed mask
840   ratio exists in our framework. There is a hyperparameter mask ratio that needs to be tuned in both
841   SiamMAE and CropMAE, but this is not the case in our Point-PQAE framework.

847   Related Relative Positional Embedding (RPE) Methods. To better position the Relative Posi-
848
      tional Embedding proposed by us for point cloud cross-view reconstruction, we discuss the differ-
      ence between our RPE and other RPE methods. In the fields of Natural Language Processing (NLP)
849
      and 2D vision, RPE techniques have been widely adopted (Wu et al., 2021; Yang, 2019; Raffel
850
      et al., 2020). For instance, Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) is an emerging
851   RPE technique gaining traction in the realm of large language models (LLMs). RoPE integrates
852   rotational transformations to encode relative token positions, enabling more efficient extrapolation
853   over unseen sequences. iRPE (Wu et al., 2021) first reviews existing relative position encoding
854   methods, and then proposes new RPE methods dedicated to 2D images. The work (Qu et al., 2021)
855   investigates the potential problems in Shaw-RPE and XL-RPE, which are the most representative
856   and prevalent RPEs, and proposes two novel RPEs called LRHC-RPE and GCDF-RPE. Generally,
857   in NLP and 2D vision, RPE captures the relative distances or orientations between tokens or pixels
858   to enhance the model’s capacity to understand relationships between paired elements. This approach
859
      often leads to improved generalization, especially when handling out-of-distribution data.
860   In contrast, our proposed RPE is designed with a view- or instance-based focus, rather than a token-
861   based one. Rather than capturing relationships between individual tokens or pixels, our RPE encodes
862   the positional relationships between two decoupled views. Our approach is not aimed at improving
863   extrapolation or generalization. Instead, it is tailored to model the geometric and contextual infor-
      mation between different views to facilitate accurate cross-view reconstruction. This shift in focus

      Related Positional Query (PQ) Methods. The positional query is also used in 2D self-supervised
883
      learning (SSL) and AI-generated content (AIGC). PQCL (Zhang et al., 2023b) pioneered the intro-
884
      duction of positional query, aiming to represent geometric relationships between multiple cropped
885   views. PQDiff (Zhang et al., 2024) advanced this concept by devising a contiguous relative posi-
886   tional query module, applying it to image outpainting to achieve arbitrary location and contiguous
887   expansion factor outpainting. Positional query has also found applications in 2D segmentation tasks.
888   For example, DFPQ (He et al., 2023) generates positional queries dynamically by leveraging cross-
889   attention scores from the previous decoder block and the positional encodings of the image features,
890   which together enhance the effectiveness of semantic segmentation. Our method, however, distin-
891   guishes itself from these existing positional query approaches by focusing on the 3D world, which
892   presents significantly greater complexity (one more dimension) and challenges compared to 2D im-
893
      age domains. By leveraging the obtained RPE to query the target view from the source view, our PQ
      technique successfully achieves decoupled view reconstruction.

949   Integrate Positional Query (PQ) Scheme into Knowledge Distillation. The knowledge dis-
950   tillation (Hinton et al., 2015) typically involves inputting the same instance into both the student
951   model and the frozen teacher model, then maximizing the mutual agreement between their outputs
952   to distill knowledge from the teacher to the student. Our positional query block can be seamlessly
953   integrated into knowledge distillation, allowing for cross-view distillation rather than being con-
954   fined to distillation within the same view. For example, view 1 is fed to the student, view 2 is fed
955   to the teacher, and a positional query block is added after the backbone to model relative relations
956
      and recover the latent representation of view 2. We conduct experiments on distilling the pre-trained
      model ReCon (Qi et al., 2023), and the results are reported in Table 6, indicating that our PQ scheme
957
      successfully learns knowledge from the ReCon teacher and performs much better than the baseline.
958
      It shows that the PQ scheme can be easily utilized as a plug-in tool for knowledge distillation.

      2017) (CD-l1), and the l2-form Chamfer distance (CD-l2). The results show the CD-l2 is more
      suitable for Point-PQAE.
