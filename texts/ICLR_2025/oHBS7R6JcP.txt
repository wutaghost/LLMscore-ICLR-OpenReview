010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            By employing rotation, outliers in activations can be effectively mitigated without
013            altering the output, thereby facilitating the quantization of large language mod-
014            els (LLMs). However, existing rotation-based methods only consider global ac-
015            tivation distributions, leaving the finer-grained distributions underexplored. Ad-
016            ditionally, these methods predominantly rely on the Walsh–Hadamard transform
017            (WHT) to accelerate online rotation operations, while not fully considering per-
018            formance between matrix multiplication (Matmul) and WHT in actual runtime.
019            These limitations hinder the rotation’s ability to effectively reduce quantization
020
               errors and decrease inference speed. Therefore, improvements are needed in their
               performance regarding both accuracy and speed. In this paper, we propose a dual
021
               rotation method for rotation matrices, dubbed DuaRot, based on reparameteriza-
022
               tion. During training, DuaRot sequentially refines global and local features to
023            achieve effective outlier mitigation. During inference, global and local rotations
024            can be merged, which maintains rotational invariance without introducing addi-
025            tional computational overhead. Meanwhile, we propose a hardware-aware ma-
026            trix configuration strategy, which determines whether the online Hadamard ma-
027            trix should be expanded into a trainable parameter space by taking the runtime
028            of the WHT and Matmul into account. This approach further enhances the re-
029            duction of quantization errors in online rotation operations without compromising
030            inference speed. Extensive experiments demonstrate that DuaRot outperforms
031
               existing methods across various models and quantization configurations. For in-
               stance, when applied to LLaMA3-8B, DuaRot achieves WikiText-2 perplexities of
032
               7.49 and 7.41 under W4A4KV4 and W4A4KV16 configurations with Round-to-
033
               Nearest (RTN), improving by 0.51 and 0.41 over the state-of-the-art, respectively.
034            The code will be publicly available soon.

      In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020; Jiang et al., 2023;
040
      AI@Meta, 2024; Yang et al., 2024) have rapidly emerged, demonstrating remarkable effectiveness
041
      across various fields (Zellers et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
042   2023; Mo et al., 2024). Nevertheless, the performance of LLMs heavily relies on a large number
043   of parameters, which always leads to significant memory, computational overhead and high energy
044   consumption during deployment. To reduce overhead while retaining performance for LLMs, it
045   is vital to study the network compression. Among many compression methods (Yao et al., 2022;
046   Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Lin et al., 2024a), model quantization has garnered significant attention
047   from both industry and academia. The goal of model quantization is to convert high-precision
048   weights/activations to low-precision and replace high-precision operations with low-precision ones,
049   thereby reducing the memory footprint and computational resources needed deploying these models.
050   However, the presence of outliers in activations often leads to a significant accuracy drop when di-
051   rectly quantizing LLMs. Although LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022) separates outliers and uses
052   mixed-precision matrix multiplication to minimize quantization errors, this fine-grained approach
053   often decreases the model’s inference speed. As one of the most representative works in LLM quan-
      tization, SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) handles outliers through scale invariance. It shifts the

134
135   Eliminating outliers is crucial for reducing quantization errors and improving accuracy for LLM
136   quantization. Currently, outlier elimination methods can be primarily categorized into two types:
137   scaling-based methods and rotation-based methods.
138
139   2.1   E LIMINATING O UTLIERS THROUGH S CALING
140
      SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) is one of the most representative methods in LLM quantization,
141
      being the first to propose using scale invariance to transfer outliers from activation to weights. This
142
      approach reduces quantization error and enhances the network’s performance in W8A8. Outlier
143   Suppression+ (Wei et al., 2023) identifies that outliers tend to cluster in specific channels and exhibit
144   asymmetry across different channels. It proposes channel-wise shifting and scaling to address this
145   issue, migrating these operations into subsequent modules to maintain equivalence. Furthermore,
146   OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) introduces learnable weight clipping and equivalent transformations,
147   operating within a differentiable framework through block-wise error minimization. QQQ (Zhang
148   et al., 2024) proposes adaptive smoothing, which achieves higher-quality activation quantization
149   and improved accuracy. However, scaling-based methods merely transfer the quantization challenge
150   from activations to weights, failing to fundamentally address the overall outlier problem. These
151
      methods can complicate the quantization of weights, particularly when outliers in activation are
      exceptionally large (Sun et al., 2024). Thus, how to effectively and efficiently address the outlier
152
      problem in LLM quantization still remains a significant challenge.
153
154
      2.2   E LIMINATING O UTLIERS THROUGH ROTATION
155
156   Recent research has shown that employing rotation matrices can effectively mitigate outliers in
157   LLMs. A pioneering work in this field is QuIP (Chee et al., 2024), which suggests that incoherence
158   processing serves as a technique for mitigating outliers in both the weight and activation spaces.
159   QuIP enhances the incoherence of weight and Hessian matrices by multiplying them with a ran-
160   dom orthogonal matrix generated using the Kronecker product. Subsequently, QuIP# (Tseng et al.,
161   2024) employs a randomized Hadamard transform, which is faster and demonstrates better theoret-
      ical properties than previous QuIP. Building on prior research, QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024b) is

226   3.2   D UAL ROTATION

228   In recent years, reparameterization (Ding et al., 2021) has been proven to be an effective technique
229   and has been widely applied in various fields of computer vision, achieving significant performance
230   improvements. During training, reparameterization introduces multiple branches to capture diverse
231   features and enhance representational capacity. During inference, these branches are mathematically
232   merged into a single equivalent layer, simplifying the model to achieve VGG-like efficiency while
233
      retaining the learned performance.
234   Inspired by reparameterization techniques, we propose Dual Rotation (DuaRot), a novel reparame-
235   terization method for rotation which aims at enhancing both the accuracy and efficiency of models.
236   DuaRot involves a global rotation matrix RG ∈ Rd×d and a local rotation matrix RL ∈ Rd×d ,which
237   contains n groups. RL is a block diagonal matrix:

499   5   C ONCLUSION
500
501   In this paper, we propose a Dual Rotation method, DuaRot, to achieve more advanced elimination
502   for activation outliers while retaining the efficiency of quantized models. DuaRot follows a repa-
503   rameterization method for rotational matrices. During the training process, both global and local
504   rotational matrices are trained separately, with the former refine broader activation distributions and
505   the latter focusing on finer-grained details. During inference, these matrices can be merged without
506   introducing any computational overhead, while still maintaining the rotational invariance. More-
507   over, DuaRot employs a hardware-aware matrix configuration strategy. By comparing the runtime
508
      performance of WHT and Matmul across different matrix sizes, DuaRot extends the matrices requir-
      ing online computation into a trainable space. This approach achieves better accuracy performance
509
      without sacrificing inference speed. Extensive experiments have demonstrated the effectiveness of
510
      DuaRot across various models, quantization configurations, and weight quantization techniques. For
511   example, DuaRot achieves 7.49 and 7.41 PPL to LLaMA3-8B under W4A4KV4 RTN quantizations,
512   improving by 0.51 and 0.41 over the state-of-the-art respectively.
513
514
515   6   L IMITATIONS
516
517   In this study, we introduce a dual rotation and hardware-aware matrix configuration strategy. This
518   method achieved significant improvements in the target dataset by fine-tuning the rotation matrix
519   on the calibration set. However, this method still has many issues expect calibration dataset as
520   mentioned in Section 4.3:
521
522   Training Cost. Since each MHA and FFN is affected by R1 , we can’t optimize R1 using a layer-
523   by-layer or block-by-block approach. Optimizing R1 directly via gradient is a straightforward
524   method. Although the optimizer needn’t to store weight state and the memory overhead required
525   for training is significantly reduced compared to QAT methods, both SpinQuant and DuaRot still
526
      require loading the entire model into the GPU and updating R1 using gradient methods. Compared
      to GPTQ, training-based methods are still expensive. For example, training LLaMA2-70B requires
527
      at least four NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs. In the future, reducing the training cost remains a direction
528
      worth exploring. For instance, calibrating R1 in a cheap and efficient way, and then using a block-
529   by-block approach to train R2 , R3 , and R4 . This approach can bring the cost of LLaMA2-70B
530   down to the same level as GPTQ.
531
532
      Optimize Efficiency. Although Cayley optimization can retain the orthogonality of the matrix
533
      during training, it is achieved through a fixed-point iteration method. This iterative approach in-
534   evitably increases the training overhead. Additionally, during training, we find that the rotational
535   matrix R1 after training is only optimized around the randomized Hadamard matrix, which denotes
536   that the gradients obtained using Cayley optimization are essentially close to the identity matrix I.
537   It will make the rotational matrix highly dependent on initialization and cannot optimize efficiently
538   over the entire orthogonal group SO(n), reducing the optimization efficiency of the model. In the
539   future, studying how to learn the rotational matrix more efficiently over the entire orthogonal group
      will further help reduce training overhead.

1230   We further discuss the essential difference between DuaRot (QuaRot, SpinQuant) and DuQuant in
1231   eliminating outliers and massive activation, as a way of explaining why we do not compare DuaRot
1232
       with DuQuant.
1233   As shown in Figure 15 and Figure 16, DuaRot (QuaRot, SpinQuant) is based on rotational invari-
1234   ance, while DuQuant is not. By applying an equivalent transformation to the network, rotational
1235   invariance does not change to the computational graph. which denotes that rotational invariance
1236   does not introduce any additional computational cost. However, in Figure 15(b) and Figure 16(b),
1237   we can see that DuQuant is not based on rotational invariance. DuQuant inserts R1 R1T in the middle
1238
       of XW and we can get XR1 R1T W = XR1 (R1T W ), although R1T can be folded into W , R1 must
       be computed online and will inevitably introduce additional computational cost.
1239
1240   On the other hand, in terms of optimization difficulty, since rotational invariance employs the same
1241   R1 throughout the network (including each MHA and FFN), i.e. optimizing R1 will lead to changes
       in the quantization results of each block, which will lead to the optimization of R1 to be very

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       difficult. On the contrary, DuQuant uses different R1 for each MHA and FFN, which greatly reduces
1243   the optimization difficulty of R1 , since the optimization of R1 can be optimized in a Block by Block
1244   manner.
1245
1246
       As shown in Table 15, from the perspective of ease of deployment, rotational invariance can be
       seamlessly integrated into existing inference frameworks. However, DuQuant requires modifications
1247
       to the inference framework and introduces mixed-precision rotation matrix multiplications (XR1 ).
1248
       Correspondingly, DuQuant usually can achieve better accuracy. Since the quantization error can be
1249   optimized in a block-by-block manner, the quantized model can be effectively improved.
