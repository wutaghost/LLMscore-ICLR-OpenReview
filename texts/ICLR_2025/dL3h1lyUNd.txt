009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Edge detection has made great progress under the development of Artificial Neu-
012            ral Networks (ANNs), particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and
013            Transformers, some of them even have achieved a beyond human-level perfor-
014            mance. However, these methods come with complex designs and high energy
015            consumption. Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), with their low energy consump-
016            tion and biological interpretability, offer a promising solution to address these is-
017            sues. In this work, we propose the first SNN-based method named EDSNN (Edge
018
               Detection with Spiking Neural Network) for edge detection. We construct a novel
               Spiking Multi-Scale Block (SMSB) to effectively utilize multi-scale information,
019
               thereby helping the network generate precise and clean edge maps. In addition, to
020
               more accurately decode spike trains, we present a Membrane Average Decoding
021            (MAD) method in the prediction block. Our method has the advantages of remark-
022            able efficiency and high performance across multiple datasets. It surpasses the
023            human-level performance on BSDS500 (ODS=0.804 vs. ODS=0.803) while con-
024            suming only 14.64 mJ, remains competitive performance among top-performing
025            ANN-based approaches on NYUDv2 (ODS=0.750), and achieves state-of-the-art
026            performance on BIPED (ODS=0.891). Our codes are publicly available in sup-
027            plementary materials.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Edge detection is a longstanding and fundamental task in computer vision, aiming to identify se-
033
      mantically meaningful object boundaries Deng et al. (2018). It is crucial in many high-level tasks
      such as object detection Zhou et al. (2022a); Yao & Wang (2023) and image segmentation Wang
034
      et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2023). In recent years, edge detection has attracted significant attention
035
      from researchers and has achieved remarkable progress. Some state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods
036   based on Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have achieved a beyond human-level performance Liu
037   et al. (2024). However, these high-performing edge detection methods are usually associated with
038   complex designs and extremely high energy consumption Zhou et al. (2024a). We believe that as a
039   foundational task, edge detection needs simpler and more energy-efficient solutions.
040
      Spiking neural networks (SNNs) offer a potential solution to this problem. As the third generation
041
      of neural networks, SNNs compute and transmit information using spike signals Maass (1997). The
042   characteristics of their binary which involves all-or-nothing computations, make them particularly
043   well-suited for binary classification tasks like edge detection. Moreover, because SNNs transmit
044   and compute information solely through spikes, the operations within the network are limited to
045   addition, leading to a significant reduction in energy consumption. With these advantages, SNNs
046   have demonstrated excellent performance in some high-level computer vision tasks such as image
047   classification Zhou et al. (2024b; 2022b), object detection Fan et al. (2024), and depth estimation
048   Rançon et al. (2022). However, the effective application of SNNs to edge detection remains a topic
049   that requires further exploration.
050   To fill this gap, we propose an SNN-based edge detection method called EDSNN, marking the first
051   attempt of the SNNs for edge detection. Specifically, the EDSNN employs an encoder-decoder
052   architecture, which facilitates the preservation and utilization of high-resolution spatial information
053   throughout the network. For the encoder, we convert the VGG network Simonyan & Zisserman
      (2014) into an SNN version, enabling multi-scale feature extraction in the spiking domain.

054
      Then, in edge detection, precisely locating edge pixels and generating clean edge maps have long
055   been major challenges Deng et al. (2018). To address this issue, we propose the Spiking Multi-Scale
056   Block (SMSB) in the decoder. The SMSB employs parallel convolutions with different kernel sizes
057   and dilation rates to capture multi-scale features. Such a strategy can integrate local precision with
058   long-range context, enabling the network to consider both detailed edge characteristics and their sur-
059   rounding noise, thereby helping to comprehensively understand edge characteristics across different
060   scales, consequently enhancing noise suppression. Subsequently, utilizing the proposed SMSB and
061   Nearest-Neighbor Interpolation, we perform spike-friendly upsampling operations Rançon et al.
062   (2022) to restore the features to the original resolution.
063   Furthermore, in SNNs, both computation and information transmission are carried out using spike
064   trains. Consequently, efficient and accurate decoding of these spike trains at the final output stage
065   has become a crucial issue in the SNN field. Currently, common decoding methods include Spiking
066   Rate Decoding (SRD), Spiking Count Decoding (SCD), and Last Membrane Potential Decoding
067   (LMPD). While Fan et al. (2024) have demonstrated that SRD is more conducive to convergence
068   than SCD, we argue that SRD merely decodes spike trains into a few fixed discrete values, poten-
069
      tially limiting the model’s expressive power. On the other hand, LMPD suppresses spike firing and
      uses the final accumulated membrane potential for decoding, which may diminish the impact of
070
      earlier time steps. To address the limitations of these existing decoding approaches, we present the
071
      Membrane Average Decoding (MAD) method. The MAD modifies the last layer of neurons to apply
072   a decay function to the membrane synaptic inputs and then accumulate them over time, ultimately
073   outputting the accumulated membrane potential. This process essentially averages the membrane
074   synaptic inputs, hence the name Membrane Average Decoding. Finally, we accurately predict the
075   edge map at the final stage of our model using the proposed MAD method.
076
      The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
077
078   • To enable the model to generate clean edge maps in a spike-friendly manner, we propose the
079     Spiking Multi-Scale Block (SMSB). By integrating convolutions with varying receptive fields, this
080     block can suppress the false positive edge pixels and improve the accuracy of true edge location.
081   • To enhance the efficiency of spike decoding, we propose the Membrane Average Decoding (MAD)
082     method. This approach not only improves the model’s expressive ability but also fully considers
083     information from all time steps.
084   • We propose EDSNN, the first SNN-based edge detection method, which adopts a simple encoder-
085     decoder network architecture. Extensive experiments demonstrate the remarkable performance of
086     our method. Specifically, it surpasses human-level performance on BSDS500 and achieves SOTA
087     performance on BIPED. All the experiment results suggest that SNNs have a promising potential
088     for edge detection.

092   2.1   E DGE D ETECTION
093
094   Edge detection is a fundamental research task in computer vision that has seen significant devel-
095   opments over the years. Early methods primarily rely on calculating image derivatives information
096   which includes Sobel Sobel (1970), Laplacian Jain et al. (1995), and Canny Canny (1986). The
097   advent of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), especially Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
098   and Transformers, has brought about revolutionary progress in this field, giving rise to many ANN-
099
      based methods. HED Xie & Tu (2015) utilizes a fully convolutional neural network to perform
      end-to-end edge detection. RCF Liu et al. (2017) exploits multi-scale and multi-level features to
100
      enhance edge detection performance. BDCN He et al. (2019) introduces a bi-directional cascade
101
      structure to refine edge predictions progressively. DexiNed Poma et al. (2020) employs a dense
102   extreme inception architecture for improved edge localization. PiDiNet Su et al. (2021) utilizes
103   pixel difference convolutions for high-efficiency edge detection. EDTER Pu et al. (2022) proposes
104   a two-stage Transformer-based architecture for accurate edge detection. As for precise edge detec-
105   tion, some novel loss functions are proposed, such as LPCB Deng et al. (2018) and DSCD Deng
106   & Liu (2020). Recent methods focus on exploring the uncertainty arising from multi-annotators
107   in datasets, including UAED Zhou et al. (2023), RankED Cetinkaya et al. (2024), and BetaNet Li
      et al. (2023). These methods provide a new perspective on edge detection. However, ANN-based

      methods with high computational demands translate to significant energy consumption, which can
109   be problematic for deployment on edge devices or in energy-sensitive scenarios.

113   2.2   S PIKING N EURAL N ETWORK
114
115   SNNs are biologically inspired models that process information through discrete spikes, offering
116   advantages in energy efficiency and temporal data processing over traditional ANNs. To realize
117   these advantages of SNNs, numerous researchers have proposed various SNN neuron models, such
118
      as the Hodgkin-Huxley (H-H) model Hodgkin & Huxley (1952), the Izhikevich model Izhikevich
      (2003), the Integrate-and-Fire (IF) model Burkitt (2006), the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) model
119
      Abbott (1999), and the Parametric Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (PLIF) model Fang et al. (2021). In this
120
      work, we adopt the IF model due to its balance of computational efficiency and ability to capture
121   essential neuronal dynamics.
122
123   Currently, most SNN-based approaches are primarily employed to address various vision prob-
124
      lems, including object detection, image classification, and image segmentation. SFOD Fan et al.
      (2024) proposes a novel multi-scale feature fusion and optimized spiking decoding strategies for
125
      high-performance object detection. Meta-SpikeFormer Yao et al. (2024) combines convolution and
126
      Transformer blocks with spike-driven self-attention, which achieves state-of-the-art performance
127   on top-1 accuracy classification. Spiking U-Net Li et al. (2024) achieves comparable accuracy to
128   traditional CNNs while consuming significantly less energy, demonstrating the potential of neuro-
129   morphic computing for efficient image processing. These researches fully demonstrate the potential
130   of SNNs in computer vision. However, research on SNNs in edge detection remains notably limited.
131   Given that edge detection is a task highly analogous to semantic segmentation, we believe that SNNs
132   could be equally effective in this field.

141
      The whole architecture of our EDSNN is shown in Fig. 1. We adopt a simple encoder-decoder
      structure to construct the EDSNN and such a simple structure has been proven to have strong ca-
142
      pabilities in ANNs Liu et al. (2024); Deng et al. (2018). First, we use the direct coding method to
143
      convert static RGB images into spike trains. Unlike the rate coding method, which combines fixed
144   probability model sampling, direct coding introduces a learnable encoding layer Kim et al. (2022).
145   After passing through this encoding layer, the output is repeated T times and fed into IF neurons,
146   generating spike trains. This enhances the learning capability of the model. To further optimize
147   the encoding process, we improve it by directly repeating the RGB image T times and then feeding
148   it into a Conv7×7-tdBN-IF structure to generate spike trains. By introducing tdBN, the gradient
149   propagation within the encoding layer is effectively stabilized Zheng et al. (2021).
150   After the coding process, the generated spike trains are fed into the encoder for multi-scale feature
151   extraction. The extracted features at different levels are then passed through the Skip Module to fur-
152   ther refine feature representations at the same resolution. Specifically, the Level 1 encoder features
153   are processed by a Conv3×3-tdBN-IF block, Level 2-5 features are processed by a Conv1×1-tdBN-
154   IF block, and Level 6 features are processed by two sequential Spiking Resblocks Zheng et al.
155   (2021). Then, the Level 6 features from the Skip Module are sent to the decoder as the primary
156   features for upsampling. The output from the other level of Skip Modules is used as supplemen-
157   tary information and is combined with the corresponding decoder features through membrane-based
158
      addition.
159   Finally, during the training process, we use n×Up Prediction Blocks to restore the decoder output
160   of each layer to the original resolution, enabling deep supervision. During inference, we take the
161   output from Level 1 as the final edge map. Notably, in the n×Up Prediction Block, we adopt the
      Membrane Average Decoding (MAD) method to decode information across multiple time steps.

      3.2       E NCODER
196
      As the first attempt to apply SNNs to edge detection and to facilitate comparisons with other net-
197
      works, we propose the Spiking VGG network, based on the widely used VGG network Simonyan &
198   Zisserman (2014) in ANNs for edge detection Liu et al. (2019); Xie & Tu (2015), and employ it as
199   the encoder. Additionally, in ANNs, large-scale pre-trained networks on ImageNet are typically used
200   as encoders to enhance feature extraction capabilities for edge detection Liu et al. (2024). However,
201   we believe that such large-scale pre-trained weights not only waste resources but also against the
202   simplicity and energy efficiency that should be prioritized for foundational tasks like edge detection.
203   Therefore, we do not pre-train our encoder.
204   The vanilla VGG network is a deep convolution neural network composed of multiple stacked small
205   convolution layers. To convert it into a spiking version, we replace its activation functions with IF
206   neurons, enabling computation and information transmission through spikes. Additionally, tdBN is
207   introduced between the convolution layers, IF neurons are employed to stabilize gradient propaga-
208   tion and accelerate convergence.

242   In the decoder, there is a need to aggregate information from the Skip Module and the higher-
243
      level upsampled features within the decoder. Consequently, finding out how to optimally fuse these
      features in each block of the decoder becomes a significant challenge. Furthermore, generating
244
      precise edge maps has long been a focal point in edge detection. In our view, the coarseness of edge
245
      inference results stems from an abundance of edge artifacts (false positives) surrounding true edges
246   (true positives). These artifacts significantly interfere with the inference of true edges, inevitably
247   leading to thick edge predictions.
248
249
      To address these issues, we propose the Spiking Multi-Scale Block (SMSB), as illustrated in Fig.
      2 (b). Within the SMSB, we integrate five convolutions with various kernel sizes and dilation rates.
250
      These multi-scale features are then aggregated through concatenation and 1×1 convolution. In edge
251
      detection, true edge pixels are typically associated with objects or structures, while noise edges
252   lack semantic coherence. Therefore, the SMSB can effectively integrate local precise spatial cues
253   from smaller receptive fields with long-range context information from larger receptive fields. This
254   integration is crucial because it can provide structural information to enhance the network’s ability
255   to distinguish true edge pixels from noise pixels, resulting in the production of refined edge maps.

      3.4    M EMBRANE AVERAGE D ECODING
259
260
261
      Currently, the most commonly used spiking decoding methods are Spiking Rate Decoding (SRD)
      and Last Membrane Potential Decoding (LMPD). Specifically, SRD involves summing the emitted
262
      spikes from the last layer of spiking neurons and dividing by the number of time steps T to calculate
263
      the spiking rate, which represents the decoding result of the model. While this method preserves
264   the spiking characteristics of the network, it significantly compromises its expressive capacity. This
265   is because it only represents the output of the model as a few fixed discrete values, diminishing
266   the expressive power of the model. LMPD suppresses spike firing in the final layer and uses the
267   accumulated membrane potential as the final output. Although this method enhances the expressive
268   capacity of the model, it disregards the influence of earlier time steps. Furthermore, the continuous
269   accumulation of membrane potential for output can lead to an amplification of subtle edge artifacts,
      which is harmful to generating clean edge maps.

308   4.1   DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION
309
310   Datasets. We select three widely used datasets to evaluate the performance of our EDSNN:
311   BSDS500 Arbelaez et al. (2010), NYUDv2 Silberman et al. (2012), and BIPED Poma et al. (2020).
312   BSDS500 comprises 200 training images, 100 validation images, and 200 test images. Each image
313   is annotated by multiple annotators (around 5 to 7). Following previous works Deng et al. (2018),
314   we also incorporate the PASCAL VOC Context dataset Mottaghi et al. (2014), containing 10103
315
      images, as additional training data to further enhance the model’s performance. NYUDv2 dataset
      contains 1449 pairs of images, each pair comprising an RGB image and its corresponding depth
316
      map. These image pairs are split into 381 for training, 414 for validation, and 654 for testing.
317
      BIPED is a high-quality dataset with 250 high-resolution images (1280×720) captured in outdoor
318   scenes. All images are divided into a training set of 200 images and a test set of 50 images. During
319   the training phase, we merge the training and validation images of BSDS500 into a single subset.
320   The same procedure is applied to the NYUDv2. For BIPED, we adopt their default configuration.
321   As for data augmentation, we follow the protocols established in previous works Deng et al. (2018).
322   The strategy involves first applying three-way flipping to the images (horizontal, vertical, and both),
323   followed by rotating each flipped image through 24 different angles. This data augmentation strategy
      is consistently applied across all three datasets.

      Implementation Details. We adopt the SpikingJelly Fang et al. (2023) deep-learning framework to
325   implement our network. Specifically, we set the mini-batch size to 8 in BSDS500 and 4 in NYUDv2,
326   respectively. We randomly crop the images in BIPED to 320 × 320 for training, as the original
327   resolution is relatively large, and the mini-batch size is set to 8. The initial learning rate is set to
328   1 × 10−4 and the learning rate decay is 0.1. We decay the learning rate every 3 epochs and adopt
329   the Adam for optimization. The number of total training epochs is set to 21. All the experiments are
330   performed using a single Tesla A40 GPU.
331   Evaluation Metrics. To evaluate the performance of EDSNN, we employ widely adopted metrics
332   in edge detection, including ODS (Optimal Dataset Scale), OIS (Optimal Image Scale), and AP
333   (Average Precision). Before metric computation, we process the predicted edge maps using non-
334   maximum suppression and morphological thinning. During the evaluation phase, the localization
335   tolerance is set to 0.0075 for BSDS500 and BIPED, while for NYUDv2, it is set to 0.011.

337   which is frequently utilized in Spiking Neural Networks. SNN energy efficiency stems from per-

      5     C ONCLUSION
516
      In this work, we propose the EDSNN network which is the first SNN-based method for edge detec-
517
      tion. We build a novel Spiking Multi-Scale Block (SMSB) into the decoder to enhance its multi-
518
      scale ability, thereby suppressing the false edge pixels near the true edge pixels. This strategy can
519   facilitate more accurate localization of edge pixels by our network. In addition, to more accurately
520   decode spike sequences, we propose the Membrane Average Decoding (MAD) method. Our method
521   is simple yet effective for edge detection, and the training process without relying on any large-scale
522   pre-trained weights. EDSNN offers a highly efficient solution for edge detection, showcasing a
523   well-balanced trade-off between energy consumption and performance. However, as a pioneering
524   attempt at applying SNNs to edge detection, our proposed method still has room for improvement.
525   In future work, we will explore the impact of various SNN architectures on edge detection, thereby
526   developing more powerful SNN-based edge detection methods.
