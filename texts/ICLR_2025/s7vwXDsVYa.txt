009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating image variations, where a model produces variations of an input image
012            while preserving the semantic context has gained increasing attention. Current im-
013            age variation techniques involve adapting a text-to-image model to reconstruct an
014            input image conditioned on the same image. We first demonstrate that a diffusion
015            model trained to reconstruct an input image from frozen embeddings, can recon-
016            struct the image with minor variations. Second, inspired by how text-to-image
017            models learn from web-scale text-image pairs, we explore a new pretraining strat-
018            egy to generate image variations using a large collection of image pairs. Our dif-
019
               fusion model Semantica receives a random (encoded) image from a webpage as
               conditional input and denoises another noisy random image from the same web-
020
               page. We carefully examine various design choices for the image encoder, given
021
               its crucial role in extracting relevant context from the input image. Once trained,
022            Semantica can adaptively generate new images from a dataset by simply using
023            images from that dataset as input. Finally, we identify limitations in standard im-
024            age consistency metrics for evaluating image variations and propose alternative
025            metrics based on few-shot generation.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Machine learning initially focused on optimizing and improving models on small datasets. The
031   field has transitioned to training general purpose models on web-scale data and then finetuning
032   them for specific tasks on smaller datasets. This paradigm shift has lead to state-of-the-art results
033   on a number of different domains. In this paper, we focus on the relatively underexplored task
034   of adapting an image generative model to different datasets. One approach is to simply train a
035   generative model on a large dataset of unlabelled images and finetune them on smaller datasets.
036   While this approach is straight-forward in theory, it requires clever architecture or regularizer design
037   to prevent overfitting in practice (See Sec.2.2). As models scale up, finetuning for every dataset also
038   just becomes increasingly impractical.
039   Image-conditioned diffusion models are now increasingly used to adapt generative models to new
040   datasets, also known as image variations (Ye et al., 2023; Pinkney, 2022; Xu et al., 2023b). First, an
041   image encoder trained on a different upstream task, such as self-supervised learning (DINO (Oquab
042   et al., 2023)) or contrastive-learning on web-image text pairs (CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) produces
043   frozen embeddings. The frozen embeddings then condition a diffusion model usually pretrained on
044   text-to-image, which is finetuned to reconstruct the original image. However in these models, the
045
      study of image variations often remains a secondary objective. In this paper, we take a step back and
      directly analyze image variations in isolation. To avoid ambiguity involved in generating multiple
046
      objects in an image, we focus our evaluation on datasets with a single dominant object. We start with
047
      a vanilla image-conditional diffusion architecture that is composed of a frozen image encoder and
048   conditions the diffusion model with cross-attention. As done in prior works, we train the diffusion
049   model to reconstruct images from frozen embeddings. We demonstrate that without text-to-image
050   pretraining or co-training, this model qualitatively achieves near-perfect image reconstruction. This
051   suggests that the capacity to generate image variations via reconstruction is due to the implicit reg-
052   ularization inherent in the pre-trained or co-trained text-to-image model. While empirically this
053   may be sufficient to generate plausible image variations, the relationship between the text-to-image
      model, image variations and scale remains unclear.

091   In this paper, we explore a different pretraining strategy with the same image-conditioned diffusion
092   architecture. We train our model Semantica using image episodes, which are image pairs that belong
093   to the same web page. Therefore, training relies exclusively on the hypothesis that images from the
094   same web page should have some common semantic attributes. For example, it is probable that
095   images scraped from a Wikipedia page on dolphins, contain pictures of dolphins. To generate image
096   variations, the model receives an image and then generates another image that preserves semantic
097   information. Under this pre-training strategy, our experiments demonstrate that scaling both the
098
      image encoder and the diffusion decoder steadily improve image variation quality. In Fig. 1, we
      compare Semantica to state-of-the-art image variation models. Semantica is capable of generating
099
      high quality and diverse images, reflective of semantic information from the conditioning image.
100
101   Evaluating image variations is non-trivial. Unlike standard image generative modeling where a
102   model generates images from scratch, a model has access to the entire test set of images via condi-
103   tioning when generating image variations. This means a model could simply copy the conditioning
104
      image and achieve high scores both at distribution-level and instance-level metrics. To bridge this
      limitation in existing metrics, we instead propose to evaluate image-variations exclusively in the
105
      few-shot setting. Concretely, we limit the number of conditioning images available to the model and
106
      then measure its ability to model the test distribution.

125   2.1   I MAGE VARIATIONS
126
      SD-V2 Image Variations (Pinkney, 2022), IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023), MultiFusion (Bellagente
127
      et al., 2024) and Verstatile Diffusion (Xu et al., 2023b) generate image variations via image re-
128
      construction. Specifically, SD-V2, IP-Adapter and MultiFusion adapt a pretrained Stable Diffusion
129   model. SD-V2 swaps the frozen CLIP text embedding with the CLIP image embedding, first only
130   finetunes the cross-attention layers of the SD model to attend to the image embedding and then the
131   entire backbone. IP-Adapter trains a adapter layer to the output of the clip image embedding and ad-
132   ditional decoupled cross-attention layers. MultiFusion finetunes a LLM to accept additional image
133   inputs. The resultant LLM embeddings than condition a pretrained Stable-Diffusion model. Versa-
134   tile Diffusion trains a single model to perform both text-to-image and image variations, with some
135   decoupled components such as cross-attention. All these methods use the same input image as the
136   target image, and rely on regularization for variations. Bordes et al. (2021) analyze the reconstruc-
137
      tions generated by diffusion models conditioned on just the global embedding from self-supervised
      models and show that they can reconstruct image semantics. Here we show, with cross-attention
138
      based conditioning, near perfect reconstruction can be achieved.

141   2.2   G ENERATIVE T RANSFER
142
      Prior to image variations, there has been research that studies the adaptation of source-pretrained
143
      generative models to a target dataset with adaptation of weights. Initial works study the transfer of
144
      discriminators and generators in GANs from a source dataset to a target dataset (Wang et al., 2018).
145   Further, Grigoryev et al. (2022) show that ImageNet pretraining on a large GAN model is beneficial
146   for transfer to small datasets. A number of papers focus on improving generation quality by adapting
147   only a subset of parameters. These include scale and shift parameters (Noguchi & Harada, 2019),
148   updating only the higher discriminator layers (Mo et al., 2020), linear combinations of scale and
149   shift parameters (Shahbazi et al., 2021), modulating kernels or convolutions (Zhao et al., 2022a;
150   2020; Cong et al., 2020; Alanov et al., 2022) and singular values (Robb et al., 2020), mapping
151   networks from noise to latents (Wang et al., 2020; Mondal et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) and latent
152   offsets (Duan et al., 2024). Various works apply regularization losses by enforcing constraints to
153
      samples/weights by the source generator including elastic weight regularization (Li et al., 2020),
      domain correspondence (Ojha et al., 2021; Gou et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2022), contrastive learning
154
      (Zhao et al., 2022b), spatial alignment (Xiao et al., 2022), inversion (Wu et al., 2022; Kato et al.,
155
      2023; Thopalli et al., 2023), random masks on discriminators (Zhu et al., 2022) and alignment free
156   spatial correlation (Moon et al., 2023). Given the increasing popularity of VQ-VAE and diffusion
157   based models, recent work (Sohn et al., 2023) and (Zhu et al., 2022) explore few-shot finetuning
158   on VQ-VAE tokens and diffusion models. We defer to Abdollahzadeh et al. (2023) for a detailed
159   exposition of all these methods. In contrast to these works, we explore training a generator on web-
160   scale images and study their transfer to standard small-scale image datasets. Retrieval augmented
161   models (Casanova et al., 2021; Blattmann et al., 2022) compute nearest neighbours for a query image
      across a bank of memory images. These retrieved neighbors facilitate the training or generation

166   2.3   D IFFUSION
167
168
      Diffusion and score-based generative models have become increasingly successful in modelling im-
      ages (Ho et al., 2022b; Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Balaji et al., 2022), videos (Ho
169
      et al., 2022a; Singer et al., 2022) and audio (Kong et al., 2020). As generation quality has steadily
170
      improved, they have been used in contexts with more and more conditioning variables. Well-known
171   examples are text-to-image and text-to-video modelling, where the conditioning variable is text. In
172   this case, the conditioning variable can be seen as a sequence from which cross-attention layers
173   communicate to the feature maps of the image or video, i.e. what the diffusion model is learning
174   to generate (Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022). As the desire for controllable generation in-
175   creases, solutions such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) have been developed. ControlNet takes
176   in conditioning images of the same size as the generations, and uses a copy of the UNet to learn an
177   encoder for the conditioning signals. Although this encoder trains fast due to parameter initialization
178   from a pretrained diffusion UNet, it is difficult to deal with different sized inputs. In those cases,
179
      only conditioning via cross-attention as done in (Xu et al., 2023a) overcomes the in-place additions
      between the ControlNet encoder and the base UNet. Conditioning on images as context has pro-
180
      duced impressive results, turning scribbles or edge detections into high quality image generations
181
      (Wang et al., 2023; Najdenkoska et al., 2023) and discriminative tasks (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al.,
182   2023). In contrast with the above mentioned techniques, our framework relies on general web-based
183   pretraining for semantic-based adaptive image generation. While (Giannone et al., 2022) study the
184   transfer of few-shot diffusion models between small datasets (CIFAR-100 → miniImageNet, we see
185   in Sec. 8.3 that this can lead to sub-optimal transfer. (Liu et al., 2023) employ test-time guidance
186   using similarity scores with a reference image, to steer unconditional generative models. However,
187   they still require training a separate unconditional generative model for each domain.

      3.2   I MAGE E NCODER
207
      Training an image-conditioned diffusion model requires an image encoder that extracts semantic
208
      information from a conditioning image. We could train a separate ViT end-to-end as an image en-
209
      coder with the diffusion model to learn useful conditioning representations. Instead, we leverage
210   pre-trained image encoders and condition our diffusion model on their “frozen” representations.
211   This offers two advantages. First, we can precompute representations for all images in the dataset
212   that eliminates expensive forward passes through the encoder during training. Second, we can use
213   different scales of readily available pre-trained encoders and just focus on scaling the diffusion
214   model. We investigate ViT image encoders trained with two pretraining strategies, contrastive learn-
215   ing (SigLIP) (Zhai et al., 2023) and self-supervised learning (DINOv2) (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab
      et al., 2023).

      3.3   D IFFUSION D ECODER
217
218   In early days, diffusion literature typically used UNets that consisted of ResNet blocks, with optional
219   self-attention layers. More recent architecture either use full Transformers (DiT (Peebles & Xie,
220   2023), StableDiffusion) or UNeTs with transformer backbones (UViTs) ((Hoogeboom et al., 2023).
221   The transformer backbone makes it especially easy to use conditioning signals in these architecture
222   via cross-attention layers. To be precise, the denoising neural network takes in a noised image at
223   a certain timestep zt ∈ RH×W ×3 , timestep t ∈ R and contextual information c ∈ RTc ×Dc . In
224   principle it does not matter which diffusion or generative model we use to generate images. In
225   practice we use the simple diffusion framework (Hoogeboom et al., 2023) because it can learn to
226
      generate high resolution images end-to-end without the need of a separate autoencoder.
227
228   3.4   C ONDITIONING I NFORMATION
229
230   Formally, the image encoder encodes the context image Xc ∈ RH×W ×C into a sequence of tokens
231   XC ∈ RTc ×Dc , We follow the encoder-decoder framework in the original Transformer (Vaswani,
232   2017) to condition the diffusion decoder with context tokens. We employ conditioning only in the
233   lowest-resolution transformer in UViT. Every self-attention block in the transformer backbone is
234   followed by a cross-attention block, where the diffusion decoder cross-attends to Xc . In addition to
235   conditioning via cross-attention, we also explore conditioning only using global features using the
236
      CLS token. Specifically, we normalize the CLS token, embed it with a dense projection and add it
      to the timescale embedding. The resultant embedding then conditions the diffusion model via FiLM
237
      layers (Perez et al., 2018).

252   The objective amounts to reconstruction where the diffusion model is trained to reconstruct the
253   context image in pixel space from frozen embeddings. The generation of diverse image variations
254   via reconstruction can be attributed to two primary factors.
255
256         1. The frozen embeddings from the image encoder retain information only useful for the up-
257            stream task it is trained on. This in turn, leads to a lossy representation of the original
258            image and the diffusion model has to fill in the missing details.
259
            2. The pretrained diffusion decoder provides implicit regularization that prevents the model

350   We first compare two choices of conditioning the diffusion model on frozen image embeddings.

353   SigLIP and DINO encoders. The result highlights the importance of local features for image varia-

365   7     M ETRICS FOR I MAGE VARIATIONS
366
367   Previous methods assess image variations using the following approach. For each reference image in
368   a test set, the model generates a new image. To quantify the quality of image variations, these meth-
369   ods employ LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) for individual image comparisons and FID (Heusel et al.,
370   2017) or Precision/Recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) to compare test to generated distributions.
371   These metrics can be sufficient to compare variations of our model on our episodic dataset since we
372   explicitly filter out near duplicates, and in theory, the model is unlikely to repeat the same image.
373   However, one major drawback is its inability to measure how diverse the generates samples are with
374
      respect to the input image. For instance, a model that just copies the input image or produces very
      minor variations will have near perfect LPIPS and FID. Thus, these metrics are not ideal for image
375
      variation baselines that rely on reconstruction.
376
377   To address this limitation, we propose a few-shot approach to assess image variations. Given a set
      of N test images, we randomly select K images and generate N/K samples for each, resulting in


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      a total of N samples. We then evaluate FID, recall and precision between the N test images and
379   N samples. Precision measures the quality of the generated samples while recall measures sample
380   diversity.
381
382
      We provide a short recap on recall a widely used metric to capture diversity between a real and
      generated dataset. For each generated sample, we store the distance to its Kth nearest neighbor,
383
      which serves as a per-sample threshold. A real image has recall 1.0 if its distance to any generated
384
      image is less than its corresponding threshold. If a model simply copies a real image, the distance
385   between the original and the copy is zero, which will always be less than its threshold.
386
387   Imagine a toy real dataset with 1 class and ten images. To measure 1-shot recall, we condition the
388
      image variation model on just one image randomly sampled from the ten images and generate ten
      samples. If the model just copies the same image ten times, then its nearest neighbour threshold per
389
      image is zero. However, the distance between each of the nine other images and the conditioning
390
      image is greater than zero, which is greater than the threshold of zero. Thus each of the nine images
391   have a recall of 0.0.

499   8.3   L ABEL G ROUPED BASELINE
500
501   Here we compare Semantica to a baseline that has direct label supervision (for example, ImageNet)
502   on lower resolution images 256 × 256. Recall that the conditioning and target image belong to
503   the same webpage. However, in the presence of label supervision (as in ImageNet), the target
504
      and conditioning image can just belong to the same class label. So as a supervised baseline, we
      group images on ImageNet as per their label and train Semantica on this dataset. Table 2 compares
505
      the FID of the Label Grouped baseline (LG) to Semantica. Since LG is trained on ImageNet, it
506
      significantly outperforms Semantica (FID 4.8 vs FID 18.4). However, this trend reverses on all other
507   datasets, where Semantica outperforms LG. Both the supervised baseline and Semantica rely on the
508   DINOv2 encoder which was trained on a wide variety of data sources. Therefore the encoder itself
509   may provide useful representations on a number of datasets. But training LG just on ImageNet,
510   might limit the diffusion model’s exposure from non ImageNet images, potentially explaining its
511   significant performance drop on all other datasets.

514
      9     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
515
516
      Our paper explores a new method for training image-conditioned diffusion models to generate image
      variations. Instead of the typical image reconstruction approach, we condition the model on one
517
      random image from a webpage and train it to denoise another random image from the same webpage.
518
      Through rigorous evaluations, DINOv2 as the image encoder produces better image variations than
519   the popular CLIP model. Finally, we emphasize the difficulty in measuring image variations, and
520   propose new metrics that are applicable in the one-shot setting.
521
522
      In this work, we focus on evaluating image variations on datasets consisting of mainly a single ob-
      ject. When multiple objects are present in an image, additional supervision in the form of bounding
523
      boxes or text can allow for fine-grained control of generations. Further, as in prior works, we focus
524
      on frozen image encoders to efficiently encode representations and filter data as opposed to training
525   an image encoder end-to-end. Thus Semantica can inherit the biases of the frozen image encoder.
526   We leave studying the tradeoffs between finetuning and using frozen representations to future work.

853
854   Here, we investigate the impact of semantic data filtering. We first manually looked at pairs of
855
      images from the Episodic Webli training set and computed their similarites in DINO embedding
      space. We found a lower threshold of 0.3 to be sufficient to filter out completely unrelated images
856
      and 0.9 to filter out near duplicates. Interestingly. we also found that the distribution of similiarities
857
      to be dependent on the embedding space used. For example, DINOv2 (Fig. 8 Center) assigns more
858   examples a lower similarity as compared to SigLIP (Fig. 8 Right). So we set the lower threshold
859   of CLIP and DINOv2 models such that, the total number of examples are roughly the same. This
860   lead to a lower threshold of 0.65 for CLIP. Fig. 8 middle shows the FID of the DINO-v2 B/14
861   + Cross Attention with and without data filtering. Similarity-based data filtering in DINO feature
862   space positively impacts the generation quality and improves FID by greater than 10. In future
863   work, we can explore tuning these thresholds for a desired quality-diversity tradeoff or even directly
      conditioning the diffusion model on the desired similarity with the conditioning image.

       Fig. 14, displays five conditioning images from ImageNet and the generated samples at different

1011

1012   kid, Semantica stars with a sample of a dog. The specific breed of the dog and the child in the image
1013   appear as we amplify the guidance. Fig. 15 showcases samples for each small dataset across various
1014   guidance factors. In row four, the bed (main object) persists across all guidance levels, while the
1015   chair and the fence appear at high guidance levels. Row five exhibits a similar effect: the number of
1016   minarets in the generated church increases from one to two and the shape of the main dome begins
1017   to resemble the conditioning image. In row one, the sample resembles toys with zero guidance, the
1018   sample resembles toys but transforms into a crowded convention as guidance increases.
