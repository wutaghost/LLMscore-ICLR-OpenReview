011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Parameter-efficient transfer learning (PETL) has attracted significant attention
014            lately, due to the increasing size of pre-trained models and the need to fine-tune
015            them for superior downstream performance. This community-wide enthusiasm has
016            sparked a plethora of approaches. Nevertheless, a systematic study to understand
017
               their performance and suitable application scenarios is lacking, leaving questions
               like “when to apply PETL” and “which approach to use” largely unanswered, es-
018
               pecially in visual recognition. In this paper, we conduct a unifying empirical study
019
               of representative PETL approaches in the context of Vision Transformers (ViT).
020            We systematically tune their hyper-parameters to fairly compare their accuracy on
021            downstream tasks. Our study not only offers a valuable user guide but also unveils
022            several new insights. First, if tuned carefully, different PETL approaches can
023            obtain quite similar accuracy in the low-shot benchmark VTAB-1K. This includes
024            simple approaches like fine-tuning the bias terms that were reported inferior. Sec-
025            ond, though with similar accuracy, we find that PETL approaches make different
026            mistakes and high-confidence predictions, likely due to their different inductive
027            biases. Such an inconsistency (or complementariness) opens up the opportunity for
028
               ensemble methods, and we make preliminary attempts at this. Third, going beyond
               the commonly used low-shot tasks, we find that PETL is also useful in many-shot
029
               regimes — it achieves comparable and sometimes better accuracy than full fine-
030
               tuning, using much fewer learnable parameters. Last but not least, we investigate
031            PETL’s ability to preserve a pre-trained model’s robustness to distribution shifts
032            (e.g., a CLIP backbone). Perhaps not surprisingly, PETL approaches outperform
033            full fine-tuning alone. However, with weight-space ensembles, the fully fine-tuned
034            model can better balance target (i.e., downstream) distribution and distribution shift
035            performance, suggesting a future research direction for PETL.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
      Pre-training and then fine-tuning has become the standard practice to tackle visual recognition
040
      problems (Bommasani et al., 2021). The community-wide enthusiasm for open-sourcing has made
041
      it possible to access large, powerful pre-trained models learned from a gigantic amount of data,
042   e.g., ImageNet-21K (Ridnik et al., 2021) or LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022). More research
043   focus has thus been on how to fine-tune such large models (Yu et al., 2023a). Among existing
044   efforts, parameter-efficient transfer learning (PETL), a.k.a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT),
045   has attracted increasing attention lately (Han et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2023). Instead of fine-tuning
046   the whole model (i.e., full fine-tuning) or the last fully connected layer (i.e., linear probing), PETL
047   approaches seek to update or insert a relatively small number of parameters to the pre-trained model
048   (Xin et al., 2024). Doing so has several noticeable advantages. First, as named, PETL is parameter-
049   efficient. For one downstream task (e.g., recognizing bird species or car brands), it only needs to learn
050
      and store a tiny fraction of parameters on top of the pre-trained model. Second, accuracy-wise, PETL
      has been shown to consistently outperform linear probing and often beat full fine-tuning, as reported
051
      on the commonly used low-shot image classification benchmark VTAB-1K (Zhai et al., 2019).
052
053   To date, a plethora of PETL approaches have been proposed, bringing in inspiring ideas and promising
      results. Along with this come several excellent surveys that summarize existing PETL approaches

        For example, with so many PETL approaches, there is a lack of unifying references for when and

081
       Attempting to answer these questions, we conduct a unifying empirical study of representative
082
       PETL approaches in the context of Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). This
083
       include Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al.,
084    2022), Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019), and ten other approaches. We systematically tune their hyper-
085    parameters to fairly compare their accuracy on the low-shot benchmark VTAB-1K. This includes
086    learning rate, weight decay, and approach-specific parameters like the size of PETL parameters.
087    Besides VTAB-1K, we examine PETL approaches on full-size downstream datasets such as CIFAR-
088    100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), RESISC for remote sensing image scene classification (Cheng et al.,
089    2017), and Clevr-Distance for depth classification with synthetic data (Zhai et al., 2019; Johnson
090    et al., 2017). We also conduct a study on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and its variants with domain
091    shifts (Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Gao et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Recht et al., 2019).
092    We summarize our key findings and extended analyses as follows.
093
        Representative PETL approaches perform similarly on VTAB-1K, if properly implemented.
094
        This includes methods previously considered less effective, such as fine-tuning the bias terms (Zaken
095
        et al., 2022) in the pre-trained backbone and methods originally proposed for NLP, like Adapter
096     (Houlsby et al., 2019). Among all the hyper-parameters, we find the drop path rate (Huang et al., 2016)
097     quite important. Ignoring it (i.e., setting it to 0) significantly degrades the performance. Overall, PETL
098     approaches consistently outperform linear probing and full fine-tuning on all 19 image classification
099     tasks (each with 1, 000 training examples) in VTAB-1K.
100
       While similarly accurate on average, PETL approaches make different predictions. The above
101
       finding seems daunting: if existing PETL approaches all perform similarly in terms of accuracy, do
102    we learn anything useful beyond a single approach? This is particularly worrisome given that they
103    fine-tune the same backbone using the same downstream data. Fortunately, our analysis shows that
104    different PETL methods learn differently from the same data, resulting in diverse prediction errors
105    and confidence. We attribute this to their difference in inductive biases (Neyshabur et al., 2014) —
106    they explicitly specify different parameters to be updated or inserted. This opens up the door to
107    leverage their discrepancy for improvement, e.g., through ensemble methods (Dietterich, 2000; Zhou,
       2012) or co-training (Blum & Mitchell, 1998; Balcan et al., 2004) and we provide preliminary studies.

108
      PETL is also effective in many-shot regimes. We apply PETL beyond the low-shot regime and find
109   it effective even with ample downstream training data — PETL can be on par or outperform full fine-
110   tuning. This suggests that varying a fraction of parameters of a properly chosen pre-trained backbone
111   (e.g., pre-trained on ImageNet-21K (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)) could already offer a sufficient effective
112   capacity (Zhang et al., 2021) to reach a performant hypothesis for downstream tasks.
113
      PETL is more robust than full fine-tuning to distribution shifts, but with weight-space ensembles,
114
      the observation is overturned. We also evaluate PETL’s robustness to distribution shifts, inspired
115   by (Wortsman et al., 2022). We consider a CLIP backbone (Radford et al., 2021), known for its
116   superior generalizability to distribution shifts, and apply PETL to fine-tune it with ImageNet-1K. We
117   find that PETL preserves CLIP’s generalizability (e.g., to samples from ImageNet-Sketch or ImageNet-
118   Rendition) better than full fine-tuning. This may not be surprising. What is interesting is that the
119   weight-space ensembles (WiSE) between the fine-tuned and pre-trained models (Wortsman et al.,
120   2022) apply to PETL as well to further improve the robustness without sacrificing the downstream
121   accuracy. Nevertheless, full fine-tuning with WiSE can achieve even higher accuracy in both
122   downstream and distribution shift data than PETL, suggesting a further research direction in PETL.
123   What lead to PETL’s success? We attempt to answer this fundamental question by analyzing the
124   results of our study. On VTAB-1K with 19 tasks, we find two cases: 1) in some tasks, full fine-tuning
125   outperforms linear probing, suggesting the need to update the backbone; 2) in some tasks, linear
126   probing outperforms full fine-tuning, suggesting either the backbone is good enough or updating it
127   risks over-fitting. The superior accuracy of PETL in both cases suggests that PETL acts as an effective
128   regularizer during low-shot training. Still using VTAB but with ample training data, we find that for
129
      tasks in case 1), PETL is on par with full fine-tuning, suggesting that its regularization role does not
      prevent the fine-tuned model from learning sufficiently from the data. For tasks in case 2), PETL
130
      can surprisingly still outperform full fine-tuning, suggesting that it effectively transfers (or preserves)
131
      some useful pre-trained knowledge that full fine-tuning may wash away. In sum, PETL succeeds as a
132   high-capacity learner equipped with an effective regularizer.
133
134   Contributions. Instead of chasing the leaderboard, we systematically understand existing approaches
135
      via a unifying study. Our contribution is thus not a technical novelty, but: (1) a systematic frame-
      work enabling consistent and reproducible evaluations of PETL methods; (2) a set of empirical
136
      recommendations on when and how to use PETL methods for practitioners; (3) new insights for
137
      future research including leveraging PETL’s prediction differences and exploring robust fine-tuning.
138
139   What do we not investigate? There are many aspects that one can ask about PETL. Our study does
140   not consider computation-specific properties like memory usage and FLOPS.

145   2.1   L ARGE PRE - TRAINED MODELS
146
147   Pre-trained models have become an indispensable part of modern AI development (Bommasani
148   et al., 2021). Building upon neural networks with millions if not billions of parameters and gigantic
149
      amounts of training data, these large pre-trained models have led to groundbreaking results in various
      downstream tasks (Liang et al., 2024; Moor et al., 2023) and shown several emerging capabilities not
150
      observed previously (Khan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024; Bommasani et al., 2021). For example, in
151
      computer vision, a Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) trained with ImageNet-21K
152   (around 14M images) leads to consistent gains v.s. a ViT trained with ImageNet-1K (around 1.3M
153   images) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). ViTs pre-trained with millions of image-text pairs via a contrastive
154   objective function (e.g., a CLIP-ViT model) (Radford et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023) show an
155   unprecedented zero-shot capability and robustness to distribution shifts (Radford et al., 2021). In this
156   paper, we focus on the ImageNet-21K-ViT and use the CLIP-ViT in a robustness study.
157

      2.2   PARAMETER EFFICIENT TRANSFER LEARNING (PETL)
184
185   Fine-tuning is arguably the most common way to tailor a pre-trained model for downstream tasks. As
186   the size of pre-trained models gets larger, copying and updating all the parameters for one downstream
187   task becomes inefficient. PETL has thus emerged as a promising paradigm.
188
      PETL was originally developed in NLP (He et al., 2021a; Lester et al., 2021; He et al., 2022b; Mao
189
      et al., 2022; Sung et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022; Asai et al., 2022; Vu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Su
190   et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2022) and has attracted increasing attention in vision (Jia et al., 2022; Chen
191   et al., 2022b; Jie & Deng, 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b; Lian et al., 2022). Existing
192   approaches can generally be categorized into four groups: prompt-based, adapter-based, direct
193   selective parameter tuning, and efficient selective parameter tuning. We focus on visual recognition
194   and compare representative PETL approaches applicable to ViTs. During fine-tuning, all approaches
195   learn a new FC layer for prediction.
196   Prompt-based approaches. Prompt-based learning emerged in NLP (Liu et al., 2023; Lialin et al.,
197   2023). The core concept is to augment the input data with task-specific hints (prompts). Visual
198   Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022) adapts such an idea to ViTs. Specifically, its deep version
199   (VPT-Deep) prepends a set of soft prompts to the input tokens of each Transformer layer (i.e.,
              −1
200   {Zm }Mm=0 ) and only optimizes the prompts during fine-tuning. Other representative works in this
201   category include (Yu et al., 2023b; Tu et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023).
202
      Adapter-based approaches. This category typically introduces additional trainable parameters (e.g.,
203
      an MLP block) to the frozen pre-trained model (Lialin et al., 2023). It was initially developed for
204   multi-domain adaptation (Rebuffi et al., 2017; 2018) and continual learning (Rosenfeld & Tsotsos,
205   2018; Mai et al., 2022), and was subsequently extended to the NLP and vision domains to adapt
206   Transformer-based models (Houlsby et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2023b).
207
      We consider five popular adapter-based methods. Houl. Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019) is the first
208
      adapter-based PETL approach. It inserts two Adapters — a two-layer bottleneck-structured MLP
209
      with a residual link — into each Transformer layer, one after the MSA block and the other after the
210   MLP block. Pfeif. Adapter (Pfeiffer et al., 2021) inserts the Adapter solely after the MLP block,
211   a strategy shown effective in recent studies (Hu et al., 2021). AdaptFormer (Chen et al., 2022b)
212   inserts the Adapter in parallel with the original MLP block in a Transformer layer, different from
213   the sequential design of Houl. and Pfeif. Adapter. One can view it as an ensemble, summing the
214   task-specific features (by the Adapter) and the task-agnostic features (by the original MLP) to form
215   Zm in Equation 2. ConvPass (Jie & Deng, 2022) introduces a convolutional-based bottleneck module
      (without a skip link) that explicitly encodes visual inductive biases: the 2D convolution is performed

216
      over tokens of nearby patches. The module is inserted in parallel with the MSA and/or MLP block.
217   RepAdapter (Luo et al., 2023) introduces a linear Adapter with group-wise transformations (Luo
218   et al., 2022) and sequentially inserts two such modules after both MSA and MLP blocks.
219
220
      Direct selective parameter tuning. This category selectively updates a subset of parameters
      of the pre-trained model, seen as a trade-off between full fine-tuning and linear probing. We
221
      consider three approaches. BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022) updates the bias terms, including those in
222
      the Q/K/V projections, the MLP blocks, the LN blocks, and the projection for patch embeddings.
223   LayerNorm (Basu et al., 2023) updates the trainable parameters of the LN blocks in each Transformer
224   layer. DiffFit (Xie et al., 2023) updates both the bias terms and the LN blocks and inserts learnable
225   factors to scale the features after the MSA and the MLP blocks. Instead of updating parameters,
226   SSF (Lian et al., 2022) linearly adapts intermediate features, motivated by feature modulation (Huang
227   & Belongie, 2017; Perez et al., 2018). For an intermediate feature Z ∈ RD×(N +1) , SSF learns a
228   D-dimensional scaling vector and a D-dimensional additive vector broadcasting to the tokens.
229
      Efficient selective parameter tuning. Unlike the above category which directly updates parameters,
230   this category learns additive residuals (e.g., ∆W ) to the original parameters (e.g., W ). By injecting
231   a low-rank constraint to the residuals, this category effectively reduces the learnable parameters.
232   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), arguably the most well-known approach, parameterizes the residuals by
233   low-rank decomposition to update the Query/Value projection matrices WQ/V ∈ RD×D . Concretely,
234   to update a W ∈ RD×D matrix, LoRA learns Wdown ∈ Rr×D and Wup ∈ RD×r with r ≪ D,
235   and forms the additive residual by ∆W = Wup Wdown ∈ RD×D . Factor Tuning (FacT) (Jie &
236   Deng, 2023) extends the idea of matrix decomposition into tensor decomposition. It stacks the
237   D × D learnable matrices in all the Transformer layers into a 3D tensor and learns an additive
238   residual parameterized by the well-established Tensor-Train (TT) (Oseledets, 2011) and Tucker
239   (TK) (De Lathauwer et al., 2000) formulations.

      2.3   R ELATED WORK AND COMPARISON
246
247   The community-wide enthusiasm for PETL has led to multiple survey articles (Yu et al., 2023a; Xin
248   et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024). Meanwhile, several empirical, integrative, and theoretical studies were
249   presented, mostly based on NLP tasks, attempting to provide a holistic understanding. (He et al.,
250   2021a; Mao et al., 2021) provided unified views to methodologically connect PETL approaches.
251   (Chen et al., 2022a; Ding et al., 2023; He et al., 2021b) and (He et al., 2022a) empirically compared
252   PETL approaches on NLP and vision tasks, respectively, while (Fu et al., 2023) offered a theoretical
253   stability and generalization analysis. Accuracy-wise, (Chen et al., 2022a; Ding et al., 2023; He et al.,
254   2021b) found that PETL is robust to over-fitting and quite effective in NLP tasks under low-data
255
      regimes. This is, however, not the case for vision tasks: (He et al., 2022a) showed that representative
      PETL approaches like LoRA and Adapter cannot consistently outperform either full fine-tuning or
256
      linear probing. In terms of why PETL works, (Fu et al., 2023) framed PETL as sparse fine-tuning
257
      and showed that it imposes a regularization by controlling stability; (Ding et al., 2023; He et al.,
258   2022a) framed PETL as (subspace) optimization; (Ding et al., 2023) further discussed the theoretical
259   principle inspired by optimal control.
260
261
      Our study strengthens and complements the above studies and offers new insights. First, we compared
      over ten PETL approaches, more than any of the above. We carefully tune the hyper-parameters,
262
      aiming to reveal the faithful accuracy of each approach. This is particularly important for the vision
263
      community because there have been no unifying references for PETL accuracy; simple approaches
264   like BitFit have often been reported as quite inferior; the effectiveness of other approaches was
265   reported quite discrepant from the study in NLP. Second, we go beyond a competition perspective
266   to investigate a complementary perspective of PETL approaches. We show that different PETL
267   approaches offer effective base learners for model ensembles. Third, we go beyond downstream
268   accuracy to investigate PETL’s effectiveness in maintaining out-of-distribution robustness. Fourth,
269   we systematically analyze the results from low-shot and many-shot regimes and identify two distinct
      patterns among PETL, full fine-tuning, and linear probing, extending the understanding of PETL.

301   Pre-trained models are meant to ease downstream applications. One representative scenario is low-
302
      shot learning: supervised fine-tuning with a small number of examples per class. Indeed, low-shot
      learning has been widely used to evaluate PETL performance.
303
304   Dataset. VTAB-1K (Zhai et al., 2019) consists of 19 image classification tasks from three groups.
305   The Natural group comprises natural images captured with standard cameras. The Specialized group
306   contains images captured by specialist equipment for remote sensing and medical purposes. The
307   Structured group evaluates the scene structure comprehension, such as object counting and 3D depth
308
      estimation. Following Zhai et al. (2019), we perform an 80/20 split on the 1000 training images in
      each task for hyperparameter tuning. The reported top1 accuracy is obtained by training on the 1000
309
      training images and evaluating on the original test set.
310
311   Methods. We consider linear probing, full fine-tuning, and 14 PETL methods including 2 prompt-
312   based (Jia et al., 2022), 5 adapter-based (Houlsby et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b;
313   Jie & Deng, 2022; Luo et al., 2023), 4 Direct selective (Zaken et al., 2022; Basu et al., 2023; Xie
314
      et al., 2023; Lian et al., 2022), and 3 Efficient selective (Hu et al., 2021; Jie & Deng, 2023). Please
      refer to subsection 2.2 for details.

318   are resized to 224 × 224. We systematically tune 1) learning rate, 2) weight decay, and 3) approach-
319   specifics like the size of PETL parameters which are often left intact. We set a cap for 3), ≤ 1.5% of

      ViT-B/16. We also turn the drop path rate (Huang et al., 2016) on (e.g., 0.1) or off (i.e., 0). A detailed

      The previous section demonstrates that all PETL methods perform similarly across various domains.
363   Given that different PETL methods are trained on the same downstream data using the same backbone
364   and achieve comparable accuracy, one might expect them to learn similar knowledge from the data,
365   resulting in similar predictions. Contrary to this expectation, our findings below reveal that different
366   PETL methods acquire distinct and complementary knowledge from the same downstream data,
367   even when built upon the same backbone, leading to diverse predictions.
368
      We start by analyzing their prediction similarity on the same dataset in VTAB-1K. It is expected
369   that their predictions are similar for datasets with very high accuracy, such as Flowers102 (avg
370   99.1%) and Caltech101 (avg 91.4%). Beyond them, we find that most PETL methods show diverse
371   predictions in other datasets in VTAB-1K. Figure 3 shows the prediction similarities between 14 PETL
372   methods in DTD, Retinopathy, and DMLab, which belong to natural, specialized, and structured
373   groups, respectively. In DTD and Retinopathy, most methods differ in about 20% of their predictions,
374   while in DMLab, this difference increases to approximately 35%, even though they achieve similar
375   accuracies. This prediction diversity may be attributed to the different inductive biases (Neyshabur
376   et al., 2014) of PETL methods — they explicitly select specific parameters to update or insert different
377
      modules at various locations within the model. More analyses and details about Figure 3 are offered
      in Appendix C.

411   scene classification with 25.2K training samples across 45 classes; and (3) Clevr-Distance Zhai et al.
412   (2019); Johnson et al. (2017), a synthetic image dataset for predicting the depth of the closest object

423   larger domain gaps suggest the need to update, perhaps many, parameters to obtain high accuracy.
424   With sufficient downstream data, full fine-tuning is less prone to over-fitting and indeed attains a high
425   accuracy. But interestingly, PETL methods, with only 2 ∼ 5% of tunable parameters, achieve similar
426   accuracy, suggesting that its design principle does offer sufficient effective capacity for the model to

505   Dataset. We use 100-shot ImageNet-1K as our target distribution, with each class containing 100

507   ImageNet-V2 Recht et al. (2019), a new ImageNet test set collected with the original labeling

511   Setup. We focus on the CLIP ViT-B/16 model, which comprises a visual encoder and a text encoder,
512   pre-trained via contrastive learning on image-text pairs. Following Wortsman et al. (2022), we add
513   an FC layer as the prediction head with zero-initialized bias and initialize weights using the class
514
      label text embedded by the text encoder. Subsequently, we discard the text encoder and apply PETL
      methods to the visual encoder, fine-tuning only the PETL modules and the head. More details about
515
      the CLIP model and experiment setup can be found in Appendix A.1.

517   Results. As shown in Table 2, while some PETL methods may not surpass full fine-tuning on the
518   target distribution, they consistently demonstrate more robust performance on distribution shift data.
519   This is likely because PETL updates only a small fraction of the parameters, thus preserving the
520
      robust features of the foundation models. Given the similar target distribution performance, should
      we blindly use PETL methods for more robustness?
521
522   Weight-space ensembles (WiSE) for PETL. WiSE (Wortsman et al., 2022), which linearly interpo-
523   lates the full fine-tuned and original models, is a popular fine-tuning approach to enhance robustness.
524   We explore whether WiSE can enhance the robustness of PETL. To apply WiSE to PETL, we first
525   linearly interpolate the prediction head with a mixing coefficient α. For direct selective tuning
526
      methods (e.g.BitFit), we directly interpolate with the original model. Since most Adapter-based
      methods have residual connections, we can multiply the adapter modules with α to control their
527
      strengths. A similar approach can be applied to efficient selective methods (e.g.LoRA) as they learn
528
      additive residuals to the original parameters. As shown in Figure 1b (more results in Appendix C),
529   WiSE improves both fine-tuning and distribution shift performance of PETL methods. Interestingly,
530   even though full fine-tuning is generally less robust than PETL methods, applying WiSE allows it to
531   achieve better performance in both target distribution and distribution shift data, which suggests a
532   promising research direction for robust PETL.

537   We conduct a unifying empirical study of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PETL), an emerging topic

      our study to open new research directions and serve as a valuable user guide in practice.
