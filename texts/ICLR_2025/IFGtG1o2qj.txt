011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, there has been a growing interest in Long-term Time Series Forecasting
014            (LTSF), which involves predicting long-term future values by analyzing a large
015
               amount of historical time-series data to identify patterns and trends. There exist
               significant challenges in LTSF due to its complex temporal dependencies and high
016
               computational demands. Although Transformer-based models offer high forecast-
017
               ing accuracy, they are often too compute-intensive to be deployed on devices with
018            hardware constraints. On the other hand, linear models aim to reduce the com-
019            putational overhead by employing decomposition methods in the time domain or
020            compact representations in the frequency domain. In this paper, we propose Mix-
021            Linear, an ultra-lightweight multivariate time series forecasting model specifically
022            designed for resource-constrained devices. MixLinear effectively captures both
023            temporal and frequency domain features by modeling intra-segment and inter-
024            segment variations in the time domain and extracting frequency variations from a
025            low-dimensional latent space in the frequency domain. By reducing the param-
026
               eter scale of a downsampled n-length input/output one-layer linear model from
               O(n2 ) to O(n), MixLinear achieves efficient computation without sacrificing ac-
027
               curacy. Extensive evaluations with four benchmark datasets show that MixLin-
028
               ear attains forecasting performance comparable to, or surpassing, state-of-the-art
029            models with significantly fewer parameters (0.1K), which makes it well-suited
030            for deployment on devices with limited computational capacity.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Time-series modeling is crucial for various fields, including climate science (Moon & Wettlaufer,
036   2017), biological research (Watson et al., 2021), medicine (Kim et al., 2014), retail (Nunnari &
037   Nunnari, 2017), and finance (Sezer et al., 2020). Accurate time series forecasting is essential for
038   informed decision-making and strategic planning in these domains. Traditional approaches, such
039
      as Autoregressive (AR) models (Nassar et al., 2004), exponential smoothing (Gardner Jr, 1985),
      and structural time-series models (Harvey, 1990), have established a strong foundation for time-
040
      series forecasting. In recent years, there has been a growing interest in Long-term Time Series
041
      Forecasting (LTSF), which aims to predict long-term future values by identifying patterns and trends
042   in large amounts of historical time-series data. Recent research has demonstrated that leveraging
043   advanced machine learning techniques, such as Gradient Boosted Regression Trees (GBRT) (Mohan
044   et al., 2011), and deep learning models, including Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) (Salehinejad
045   et al., 2017) and Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) (He & Zhao, 2019), yields significant
046   performance improvements over traditional methods.
047
      Over the last few years, significant efforts have been made to explore the use of Transformers for
048   LTSF and produced many good models, such as LogTrans (Nie et al., 2022), Informer (Zhou et al.,
049   2021), Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2021), Triformer (Cirstea et al., 2022),
050   FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022b), and PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023). Those models achieve good fore-
051   casting performance at the cost of introducing significant computation overhead due to the use of
052   the self-attention mechanism, which scales quadratically with sequence length L. The high compu-
053   tational demands and large memory requirements of these models hinder their deployment for LTSF
      tasks on resource-constrained devices. To address this limitation and facilitate low-resource usage,

094   smaller segments. In the frequency domain, it captures frequency domain variations by mapping
095   the decoupled time series subsequences (trend) into a latent frequency space and reconstructing the

      2     P RELIMINARIES
120
121   Long-term Time Series Forecasting. The task of LTSF involves predicting future values over
122   an extended horizon using previously observed multivariate time series data. It is formalized as
123   x̂t+1:t+H = f (xt−L+1:t ), where xt−L+1:t ∈ RL×C and x̂t+1:t+H ∈ RH×C . In this formulation,
124
      L denotes the length of the historical observation window, C represents the number of distinct
      features or channels, and H denotes the length of the forecast horizon. The main goal of LTSF is to
125
      extend the forecast horizon H as it provides rich and advanced guidance in practical applications.
126
      However, an extended forecast horizon H often requires more parameters and significantly increases
127   the parameter scale of the forecasting model.
128
129
130   Lightweight Time Series Forecasting. Recently, there has been a growing interest in developing
131   lightweight models for LTSF. DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) demonstrates that simple linear models
132
      can effectively capture temporal dependency and outperform transformer-based models. DLinear
      shares the weights across different variates, does not model spatial correlations, and transforms the
133
      multivariate input xt−L+1:t ∈ RL×C to the output x̂t+1:t+H ∈ RH×C by reformulating it into
134
      a univariate mapping xt−L+1:t ∈ RL to x̂t+1:t+H ∈ RH . On the other hand, FITS (Xu et al.,
135   2024) employs a harmonic content-based cutoff frequency selection method that reformulates the
136   univariate input xt−L+1:t ∈ RL to output x̂t+1:t+H ∈ RH by mapping it to the frequency domain
137   and reduces the input length from L to nCOF , where nCOF is the cutoff frequency and nCOF << L.
138   FITS significantly reduces the parameter scale (from 140K to 10K). SparseTSF takes a different
139   approach by decoupling periodicity and trend components in time series data through aggregation
140   and downsampling and reformulates the univariate input xt−L+1:t ∈  RL to output      x̂t+1:t+H   ∈ RH
                                                                              L
                                                                                 ,m= H

141   by mapping the trend component xt−n+1:t to x̂t+1:t+m , where n = w                   w   , and w is the
142   period. SparseTSF reduces the parameter scale to as low as 1K.

147   3.1    OVERVIEW
148
149   Current research in LTSF focuses on efficiently decomposing and capturing temporal dependencies
150   from the time or frequency domain. The key innovation of MixLinear lies in its ability to extract
151   features from both domains while minimizing the number of neural network parameters. However,
152   combining time and frequency domain models can significantly increase the parameter scale. Mix-
153
      Linear addresses such an issue by substantially reducing the parameter count without compromising
      prediction performance. Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of MixLinear, which consists of
154
      two key processes: Time Domain Transformation and Frequency Domain Transformation. Unlike
155
      the existing linear models that apply pointwise transformations, our Time Domain Transformation
156   captures inter-segment and intra-segment dependencies by splitting the decoupled time series (trend)
157   into segments. Such a method significantly reduces the model parameter scale and enhances the lo-
158   cality, which is unavailable in the pointwise methods. In contrast to the existing frequency-based
159   models that perform transformation on the entire series, our Frequency Domain Transformation fo-
160   cuses on transforming more compact trend components in a lower-dimensional latent space, which
161   reduces the model complexity by learning frequency variations more effectively. The overview
      workflow of MixLinear can be found in Appendix A.1.

445   5.1   L ONG - TERM T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
446
447   LTSF aims to predict future values over extended horizons, which is challenging because the time
448   series data is complex and high-dimensional Zheng et al. (2024; 2023). The traditional statistical
449   methods, such as ARIMA (Contreras et al., 2003) and Holt-Winters (Chatfield & Yar, 1988), are
450
      effective for short-term forecasting but often fall short for long-term predictions. Machine learning
      models, such as SVM (Wang & Hu, 2005), Random Forests Breiman (2001), and Gradient Boosting
451
      Machines (Natekin & Knoll, 2013), have improved performance by capturing non-linear relation-
452
      ships but require extensive feature engineering. Recently, deep learning models, such as RNNs,
453   LSTMs, GRUs, and Transformer-based models including Informer and Autoformer have excelled
454   in efficiently modeling long-term dependencies. The hybrid models that combine statistical and
455   machine learning or deep learning techniques have also shown enhanced accuracy. State-of-the-
456   art models like FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022b), FiLM (Zhou et al., 2022a), PatchTST Nie et al.
457   (2023), and SparseTSF incorporate advanced mechanisms like frequency domain transformations
458   and efficient self-attention to achieve remarkable performance.
459   Recently, there has been a notable trend towards designing lightweight LTSF models. DLinear (Zeng
460   et al., 2023) shows that even simple models can capture significant temporal periodic dependencies
461   effectively. LightTS (Campos et al., 2023), TiDE (Das et al., 2023), and TSMixer (Chen et al.,
462   2023) show similar conclusions. FITS (Xu et al., 2024) has emerged as a significant advancement in
463   the field and achieved a milestone by scaling LTSF models to around 10K parameters while main-
464   taining high predictive accuracy. FITS accomplishes this by transforming time-domain forecasting
465   tasks into frequency-domain equivalents and employing low-pass filters to minimize parameter re-
466   quirements. SparseTSF (Lin et al., 2024) pushes the boundaries even further by leveraging the
467
      Cross-Period Sparse Forecasting technique.
468
469   5.2   T IME S ERIES DATA D ECOMPOSITION
470
471
      Several decomposition methods in the time domain have been introduced in the literature to handle
      such a task, including STL (Robert, 1990), TBATS (De Livera et al., 2011), and STR (Dokumentov
472
      et al., 2015) for periodic series, as well as ℓ1 trend filtering (Moghtaderi et al., 2011) and mixed
473
      trend filtering (Tibshirani, 2014) for non-periodic data. Although these techniques have gained
474   popularity and proven effective in various applications, they exhibit limitations due to three reasons:
475   the inefficiency in handling time series with long seasonal periods, the frequent seasonal shifts and
476   fluctuations in real-world data, and the lack of robustness to outliers and noise (Gao et al., 2020).
477
      Decomposing time series data in the frequency domain provides compressed representations that
478
      capture rich underlying patterns (Xu et al., 2020). These representations offer a more compact and
479
      efficient depiction of the inherent characteristics within the data (Xu et al., 2024). FEDformer de-
480   composes sequences into multiple frequency domain modes using frequency transforms to extract
481   features (Zhou et al., 2022b). TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022) employs a frequency-based method to
482   separate intraperiod and interperiod variations. FITS (Xu et al., 2024) leverages this property by
483   transforming the time series into the frequency domain, treating the data as a signal that can be
484   expressed as a linear combination of sinusoidal components, a process that ensures no information
485   loss. Each sinusoidal component is defined by its own frequency, amplitude, and initial phase, allow-
      ing for a precise representation of different oscillatory patterns present in the data. Although there


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      are many ways of frequency domain decomposition method, extracting features from the frequency
487   domain requires suitable techniques. There will be many interferences in the signal, and suitable
488   schemes for temporal features must be considered when combining deep learning methods.

492
493
      There has been a growing interest in LTSF, which aims to predict long-term future values by iden-
      tifying patterns and trends in large amounts of historical time-series data. A key challenge in LTSF
494
      is to manage long sequence inputs and outputs without incurring excessive computational or mem-
495
      ory overhead, particularly in resource-constrained scenarios. In this paper, we introduce MixLinear,
496   the first lightweight LTSF model that captures temporal and frequency features from both time and
497   frequency domains. MixLinear applies the trend segmentation in the time domain to capture the
498   intra-segment and inter-segment variations and captures the amplitude and phase information by
499   reconstructing the trend spectrum from a low dimensional frequency domain latent space. Experi-
500   mental results show that MixLinear can achieve comparable or better forecasting accuracy with only
501   0.1K parameters. Besides, MixLinear exhibits strong generalization capability and is well-suited
502   for scenarios where the training data are limited.

      We briefly describe the baseline models we used in this paper:
722
723   (1) Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) is a Transformer-based model that employs self-attention
724   distillation to highlight dominant attention by halving the input to cascading layers, en-
725   abling efficient handling of extremely long input sequences. The source code is available at
726   https://github.com/zhouhaoyi/Informer2020.
727   (2) Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) is a Transformer-based model that introduces the Auto-
728   Correlation mechanism, leveraging the periodicity of time series to discover dependencies
729   and aggregate representations at the sub-series level.   The source code is available at
730   https://github.com/thuml/Autoformer.
731   (3) Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2021) is a Transformer-based model that captures temporal dependen-
732   cies of different ranges in a compact multi-resolution fashion. The source code is available at
733   https://github.com/ant-research/Pyraformer.
734
      (4) FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022b) is a Transformer-based model proposing seasonal-trend decom-
735
      position and exploiting the sparsity of time series in the frequency domain. The source code is
736
      available at https://github.com/DAMO-DI-ML/ICML2022-FEDformer.
737
738   (5) TimesNet (Liu et al., 2023) is a CNN-based model with TimesBlock as a task-general backbone.
739   It transforms 1D time series into 2D tensors to capture intraperiod and interperiod variations. The
740
      source code is available at https://github.com/thuml/TimesNet.
741   (6) SCINet (Liu et al., 2022) is a recursive downsample-convolve-interact architecture that uses mul-
742   tiple convolutional filters to extract distinct yet valuable temporal features from the downsampled
743   sub-sequences or features. The source code is available at https://github.com/cure-lab/SCINet.
744   (7) iTransformer (Wu et al., 2022) is a Transformer based architecture that applies the atten-
745   tion and feed-forward network on the inverted dimensions. The source code is available at
746   https://github.com/thuml/iTransformer.
747
      (8) PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) is a transformer-based model utilizing patching and CI technique. It
748
      also enables effective pre-training and transfer learning across datasets. The source code is available
749
      at https://github.com/yuqinie98/PatchTST.

767   A.4     D ETAILED E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
768
769
      We implement MixLinear in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and train it using the Adam opti-
      mizer (Diederik, 2015) for 30 epochs with early stopping based on a patience of 10 epochs. We
770
      follow the procedures outlined in FITS and Autoformer to split the dataset (Wu et al., 2021). Specif-
771
      ically, the ETT datasets are divided into training, validation, and test sets with a 6 : 2 : 2 ratio.
772   The other datasets are split with a 7 : 1 : 2 ratio. Both our model and baselines use the same
773   normalization method (i.e., StandardScaler).
774
775
      MixLinear has minimal hyperparameters due to its simple design. The period w is chosen based
      on the inherent cycle of the data (e.g., w = 24 for the ETTh1 dataset) or reduced when the dataset
776
      exhibits a longer cycle. The batch size is determined by the number of channels in each dataset. The
777
      batch size is set to 256 for the datasets with fewer than 100 channels (e.g., ETTh1). The batch size
778   is set to 128 for the datasets with fewer than 300 channels (e.g., Electricity). Such a configuration
779   maximizes the GPU parallelism while preventing any out-of-memory issues. In addition, given the
780   small number of learnable parameters in MixLinear, we use a relatively large learning rate of 0.02
781   to accelerate training.
782
      The baseline results reported in this paper come from the first version of the FITS paper, where
783
      FITS uses a uniform input length of 720. To ensure a fair comparison, we also use an input length of
784   720. The input lengths of other baseline models are set according to the values used in their original
785   implementations.

       B.6   M ORE D ETAILED C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
976
977    In this section, we analyze the computational complexity of MixLinear, a lightweight model that
978    combines both time-domain and frequency-domain transformations to enhance efficiency in multi-
979    variate time series forecasting.
980    MixLinear captures both intra-segment and inter-segment variations by isolating channel-specific
981    and periodic information from trend components in the time domain. This approach segments the
982    trend data into smaller parts, allowing efficient handling of multivariate dependencies and reducing
983    parameter complexity from O(n2 ) to O(n)        input and output sequences of length L, with a known
                                                   Lfor
984    period w and a subsequence length n = w        .
985
       To model long-term dependencies in the frequency domain, MixLinear maps the decoupled time
986
       series subsequences (trends) into a latent frequency space, where it reconstructs the trend spectrum.
987    This process allows for effective frequency representation across segments, capturing critical peri-
988    odic variations.
989
990
       To further enhance efficiency, MixLinear applies an aggregation downsampling step using a 1D
       CNN with a kernel size of w, resulting in a computational complexity of O(w). Following this,
991
       in the frequency domain transformation, FFT and iFFT operations are applied to the downsampled
992
       trend segments of length n, introducing an additional time complexity of O(n log n) and space
993    complexity of O(n) (Baxley & Zhou, 2006).
994
995    In summary, the total time complexity of MixLinear is O(w) + O(n log n) + O(n), which simplifies
996
       to O(n log n), the total space complexity of MixLinear is O(w) + O(n) + O(n), which simplifies
       to O(n).
