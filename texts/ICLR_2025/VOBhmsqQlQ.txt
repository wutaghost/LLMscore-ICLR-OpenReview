009                                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                 We propose cognitive prompting as a novel approach to guide problem-solving in
012                 large language models (LLMs) through structured, human-like cognitive opera-
013                 tions such as goal clarification, decomposition, filtering, abstraction, and pattern
014                 recognition. By employing systematic, step-by-step reasoning, cognitive prompt-
015                 ing enables LLMs to efficiently tackle complex, multi-step tasks. We evaluate
016                 the effectiveness of cognitive prompting on Meta’s LLaMA models, comparing
017                 performance on arithmetic reasoning tasks using the GSM8K dataset and on com-
018                 monsense reasoning benchmarks. Our analysis includes comparisons between
019
                    models without cognitive prompting, models with a static sequence of cognitive
                    operations, and models using reflective cognitive prompting, where the LLM dy-
020
                    namically self-selects the sequence of cognitive operations. The results show that
021
                    cognitive prompting, particularly when dynamically adapted, significantly im-
022                 proves the performance of larger models, such as LLaMA3.1 70B, and enhances
023                 their ability to handle multi-step reasoning tasks. This approach also improves
024                 interpretability and flexibility, highlighting cognitive prompting as a promising
025                 strategy for general-purpose AI reasoning.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), especially with large language models (LLMs),
031   have made great progress in emulating human reasoning to solve tasks like text summarization (Sti-
032   ennon et al., 2020), code generation (Guo et al., 2023), and question answering (Lu et al., 2022).
033   While LLMs excel at generating coherent text and handling vast data, their ability to perform multi-
034   step reasoning still falls short of human cognitive processes. Human cognition, marked by its struc-
035
      tured nature, provides a compelling blueprint for guiding AI through complex tasks that require
      layered thinking and adaptability.

      This paper introduces a novel approach called cognitive prompting, designed to enhance problem-
055   solving in LLMs by systematically emulating human cognitive operations (COPs). Cognitive
056   prompting organizes problem-solving into distinct cognitive steps—such as goal clarification, task
057   decomposition, and pattern recognition—allowing LLMs to tackle complex tasks in a more struc-
058   tured and interpretable manner, see Figure 1. Inspired by cognitive psychology and cognitive archi-
059   tectures like ACT-R (Anderson & Lebiere, 1996), this method bridges the gap between human-like
060   reasoning and AI’s computational power, enabling models to handle tasks in fields such as mathe-
061   matics, logic, decision-making, and creativity with greater precision. Our experiments, conducted
062   with Meta’s LLaMA models (Touvron et al., 2023) on the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and a
063
      commonsense benchmark (Shi & Lipani, 2024), demonstrate significant improvements in task per-
      formance when cognitive prompting is applied. In particular, the reflective variant of cognitive
064
      prompting leads to enhanced reasoning capabilities.
065
066   The structure of the paper is as follows: Section 2 introduces the concept of cognitive prompting,
067   detailing its core operations and their application in problem-solving. Section 3 presents experi-
068   mental results on the impact of cognitive prompting on arithmetic reasoning tasks, while Section 4
069
      explores its effectiveness in commonsense reasoning. Section 5 reviews related work on prompting
      engineering strategies. Finally, Section 6 concludes the paper. The appendix contains exemplary
070
      reasoning processes and examples for problem-specific COPs.

073   2    C OGNITIVE P ROMPTING
074
075   Cognitive prompting organizes problem-solving through a structured sequence of human-like COPs,
076   enabling LLMs to tackle complex tasks across domains such as mathematics, logic, creativity, and
077   decision-making. This method, inspired by principles in cognitive psychology, breaks problems into
078   stages like goal clarification, decomposition, filtering, and integration—mimicking the way humans
079
      refine their understanding of tasks. By leveraging this structured approach, cognitive prompting
      enhances clarity, interpretability, and adaptability in LLM reasoning.
080
081   Unlike methods like Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), cognitive prompting offers more
082   general multi-dimensional operational depth, allowing LLMs to approach a wider variety of prob-
083   lems with reasoning progression. This framework, rooted in dual-process and problem-space theo-
084   ries, encourages both intuitive and analytical reasoning, helping models transition between pattern
085
      recognition, abstraction, and integration for more consistent and interpretable solutions. Cognitive
      prompting can be formalized as an optimization problem. Let C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , cn } represent a set
086
      of COPs and S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sk } denote a sequence of k operations from C. The objective is
087
      to find the sequence S ∗ that maximizes task performance S ∗ = arg maxS⊆C f (S) subject to con-
088   straints such as |S| = k, s1 = goal clarification, and sk = integration. Here, f (S) represents task
089   performance, e.g., accuracy, efficiency, coherence.
090
091
      Cognitive prompting follows a structured process that mirrors human problem-solving. Key COPs
      include:
092
093
094
      Goal Clarification: Clearly define the objective of the problem to maintain focus on solving it
      effectively. In the context of COP, goal clarification ensures that the model aligns its reasoning
095
      with the desired outcome, minimizing distractions. Let G represent the goal, and all subsequent
096
      operations should be oriented toward achieving G , helping the model concentrate on the correct
097   direction of reasoning.
098
099   Decomposition: Break down Sthe problem P into smaller, manageable components
100                                        n
      {P1 , P2 , . . . , Pn }, where P = i=1 Pi . This step is crucial in COP as it allows the model
101   to tackle complex, multi-step problems incrementally. Decomposition is particularly useful in
102   mathematical problem-solving and logic tasks, where breaking a problem into sub-problems allows
103   the model to apply specific operations or strategies to each part. Moreover, decomposition helps to
104   identify the core structure of the problem, isolating the critical steps required for a comprehensive
105   solution.
106
107   Filtering: Select the most relevant information from I = {i1 , i2 , . . . , im } using a filtering function
      F (I) = Irel ⊆ I. Filtering is essential in COP to prevent the model from being overwhelmed by

108
      irrelevant details. In complex tasks, the problem statement may include redundant or distracting
109   information, so filtering ensures that the model focuses on the essential data points that directly
110   impact problem-solving. This operation can significantly improve accuracy by narrowing down
111   the scope of attention to the key elements required for a solution. Filtering also helps prioritize
112   conflicting information by selecting the most reliable or impactful inputs for further operations.
113
114
      Reorganization: Rearrange data, variables, or equations D to reveal patterns or simplify the struc-
115   ture, such that Reorder(D) → D′ . In COP, reorganization plays a crucial role by enabling the model
116   to manipulate the structure of the information to expose underlying patterns or simplify the problem-
117   solving process. This operation helps in transforming complex, disordered data into a more logical
118   and interpretable form, allowing the model to focus on solving manageable sub-problems. Reorga-
119   nization can be especially useful in algebraic manipulations, where reordering terms or rearranging
120   equations simplifies solving or leads to the discovery of connections between different parts of the
121   problem.
122
123   Pattern Recognition: Identify recurring relationships or patterns P in the data, which facilitates
124   the application of known solutions. In COP, pattern recognition helps the model detect similar-
125   ities with previously encountered problems, accelerating problem-solving by applying already-
126   established solutions to new contexts. Recognizing patterns not only speeds up problem-solving
127   but also enhances the model’s ability to predict the next steps in a sequence or foresee potential
128   outcomes based on recognized trends. This is particularly beneficial in domains like mathematics
129
      and logic, where identifying structural or numerical patterns allows for the reuse of strategies from
      similar problems, leading to more efficient and elegant solutions. Moreover, it enables the model
130
      to generalize from specific cases to broader principles, laying the groundwork for abstraction and
131
      generalization.
132
133
134   Abstraction: Extract broader principles A from the identified patterns P, and generalize them to
135
      apply across different problems or contexts. In COP, abstraction enables the model to transcend
      specific details and focus on fundamental principles, which enhances its adaptability to new and
136
      unfamiliar tasks by recognizing underlying structures. Abstraction is a key step in solving not just
137
      individual problems but entire classes of problems by deriving rules, formulas, or frameworks that
138   can be applied universally. By focusing on the core ideas underlying a problem, abstraction helps
139   simplify the solution and extends the model’s reasoning capabilities beyond surface-level details,
140   improving its ability to tackle complex and novel tasks that require higher-order thinking.
141
142
      Generalization: Apply abstracted principles A to the broader problem or similar contexts, such
143
      that fgen (A) = {P1 , P2 , . . . , Pk }. Generalization in COP ensures that solutions are not isolated
144   to the specific instance but are scalable across various related problems. This operation allows the
145   model to extend insights gained from the current task to solve new problems with similar structures.
146   By abstracting and generalizing, the model improves its adaptability, enabling it to handle a wide
147   range of tasks beyond the immediate problem and apply the same cognitive framework to different
148   contexts, thereby enhancing its reasoning flexibility and robustness.
149
150   Integration: Synthesize the individual solutions Qi into a cohesive final solution Q, ensuring
151   all components of the problem are addressed and fit together logically. In COP, integration is the
152   culmination of the reasoning process, where the model combines all the previously solved sub-
153   problems into a comprehensive, unified solution, ensuring coherence and completeness.
154
155
      Static and Reflective Cognitive Prompting: This flexible process allows LLMs to dynamically
156   apply the most relevant operations based on the task’s context, enhancing problem-solving perfor-
157   mance across various domains. In static cognitive prompting, a fixed order S = [s1 , s2 , . . . , sk ] of
158   COPs is followed throughout the problem-solving process, ensuring a structured yet rigid approach.
159   In contrast, reflective cognitive prompting allows the LLM to self-select the sequence of COPs,
160   adapting flexibly to the task’s needs, i.e., choosing the next COP si ∈ C in each step. This adapt-
161   ability not only improves the model’s ability to solve complex problems but also offers structured,
      interpretable explanations of the reasoning processes.

      Domain Specific COPs The effectiveness of cognitive prompting is significantly enhanced when
163   the general COPs are adapted to specific problem domains. By tailoring each cognitive operation to
164   the characteristics of a particular domain, the model can better align its reasoning process with the
165   demands of the task. For example, the decomposition operation in scientific inquiry might involve
166   breaking down a complex hypothesis into smaller, testable components, while in ethical decision-
167   making, decomposition could involve identifying and separating conflicting moral principles and
168   stakeholder interests. This domain-specific adaptation ensures that the reasoning process remains
169   relevant and effective for each type of problem. A detailed overview of how COPs are adapted across
170   different domains, such as scientific inquiry and ethical decision-making, can be found in Table 1 in

      the Appendix.

305   5   R ELATED W ORK
306
307   Prompting is a key technique for leveraging pre-trained LLMs to perform tasks by guiding their out-
308   puts through well-crafted instructions. In zero-shot prompting, models generate responses without
309   task-specific examples, while few-shot prompting (Brown et al., 2022) improves performance by
310   including a few task examples. CoT prompting (Wei et al., 2022) breaks down complex reasoning
311   into intermediate steps, enabling systematic problem-solving, while Tree of Thoughts (ToT) (Yao
312   et al., 2023a) extends CoT by enabling LLMs to explore multiple reasoning paths and make de-
313   liberate decisions. Building on CoT, ReAct (Yao et al., 2023b) combines reasoning with real-time
314
      decision-making, enhancing models’ abilities to handle dynamic tasks. This approach allows for
      more flexible handling of unpredictable inputs, mimicking human cognitive processes like adjusting
315
      decisions on the fly.
316
317   Prompt Breeder (Fernando et al., 2023) optimizes prompts using evolutionary computation to iter-
318   atively refine and improve performance. Similarly, self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023) enhances
319   reliability by generating multiple responses and selecting the most consistent one, reducing variabil-
320
      ity in complex tasks. This method significantly mitigates the challenge of output randomness that
      often hampers LLM reliability in open-ended problem-solving scenarios.
321
322   Automated Prompt Engineering (APE) (Zhou et al., 2023) automates prompt optimization through
323   model self-instruction and feedback loops, pushing the boundaries of human-computer collabora-
      tion. Optimization by PROmpting (OPRO) (Yang et al., 2024) uses LLMs to iteratively generate and


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      refine solutions, significantly outperforming human-designed prompts in optimization tasks. These
325   automated approaches open new avenues for improving performance without extensive human in-
326   tervention, allowing models to autonomously evolve their problem-solving strategies.
327
328
      Recent works also explore multi-task learning to generalize prompt strategies across diverse appli-
      cations, further enhancing their adaptability. Techniques like retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
329
      (Lewis et al., 2020) combine prompting with external knowledge sources, offering richer context
330
      and better-informed outputs, demonstrating how prompts can evolve to integrate more human-like
331   reasoning. Recent advancements in parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as decomposed
332   prompt tuning (DePT) (Shi & Lipani, 2024), have demonstrated how efficient prompt-based strate-
333   gies can reduce memory and computational costs in large language models, which can complement
334   the flexibility provided by cognitive prompting in adapting models to complex problem-solving
335   tasks. To the best of our knowledge, no prompt strategies are motivated explicitly by human-like
336   COPs.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
339
340
      Cognitive prompting models human reasoning as a sequence of COPs delivered through prompts.
341   It fosters structured thinking using general COPs or domain-specific adaptations. Unlike example-
342   based approaches that rely on memorized examples, cognitive prompting emphasizes high-level
343   reasoning, making it adaptable across a wide range of tasks. The specialization of these cognitive
344   operations for specific domains allows it to tackle diverse problems effectively. Our experiments
345   demonstrate that cognitive prompting, particularly the reflective variant, is highly effective in guid-
346   ing LLMs through complex tasks such as GSM8K math problems and commonsense reasoning.
347   Reflective prompting significantly enhances the performance of smaller models, consistently out-
348   performing static prompting. However, in larger models like the 70B, cognitive prompting excels in
349
      arithmetic reasoning but suffers in commonsense tasks, where excessive reasoning steps reduce per-
      formance—similar to overfitting—indicating the need for regularization. For future work, we plan
350
      to extend experiments across more domains and models, exploring the effectiveness of cognitive
351
      prompting in areas like legal reasoning, medical decision-making, and strategic planning. This will
352   ensure the robustness of the approach across general and specialized tasks.

362   E THICS S TATEMENT
363
364   Cognitive prompting promotes structured, human-like reasoning, enhancing transparency and con-
365   sistency. However, modeling human-like thinking in sensitive domains, such as ethical decision-
366   making, raises concerns about biased reasoning and harmful outcomes. To mitigate risks, we focus
367
      on well-defined contexts like mathematics and commonsense reasoning, with no access to sensitive
      data. We urge careful consideration of ethical implications when applying cognitive prompting to
368
      more complex tasks.
