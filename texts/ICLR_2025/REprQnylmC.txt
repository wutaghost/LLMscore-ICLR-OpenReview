009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid growth of urban transportation and the continuous progress in
012            autonomous driving, a demand for robust benchmarking autonomous driving
013            algorithms has emerged, calling for accurate modeling of large-scale urban traffic
014            scenarios with diverse vehicle driving styles. Traditional traffic simulators, such
015            as SUMO, often depend on hand-crafted scenarios and rule-based models, where
016
               vehicle actions are limited to speed adjustment and lane changes, making it difficult
               for them to create realistic traffic environments. In recent years, real-world traffic
017
               scenario datasets have been developed alongside advancements in autonomous
018
               driving, facilitating the rise of data-driven simulators and learning-based simulation
019            methods. However, current data-driven simulators are often restricted to replicating
020            the traffic scenarios and driving styles within the datasets they rely on, limiting
021            their ability to model multi-style driving behaviors observed in the real world.
022            We propose LCSim, a large-scale controllable traffic simulator. First, we define
023            a unified data format for traffic scenarios and provide tools to construct them
024            from multiple data sources, enabling large-scale traffic simulation. Furthermore,
025            we integrate a diffusion-based vehicle motion planner into LCSim to facilitate
026            realistic and diverse vehicle modeling. Under specific guidance, this allows for
027
               the creation of traffic scenarios that reflect various driving styles. Leveraging
               these features, LCSim can provide large-scale, realistic, and controllable virtual
028
               traffic environments. Codes and demos are available at https://anonymous.
029
               4open.science/r/LCSim-0C7A.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As global urbanization progresses, the complexity and diversity of urban transportation systems
035   continue to increase. The driving styles of vehicles in various cities often have distinct characteristics
036   (Sagberg et al., 2015), leading to high costs of benchmarking autonomous driving algorithms, which
037   require high levels of safety and reliability and thorough testing and evaluation before actual deploy-
038   ment (Waymo, 2021). This necessitates accurately modeling urban microscopic traffic scenarios
039
      through traffic simulation, enabling the robust assessment of relevant algorithms. Accurate modeling
      of urban traffic scenarios poses two main challenges for simulation systems: the need for realistic
040
      and controllable vehicle models to replicate the complex and diverse driving behaviors in reality, and
041
      the requirement for large-scale traffic scenario data to support traffic simulation.
042
043   Existing simulation methods often have shortcomings in these two aspects. For one, vehicle behavior
044   modeling in simulation systems is typically categorized into three types: rule-based (e.g. IDM
045
      (Brockfeld et al., 2003)) simulation (Behrisch et al., 2011; Li et al., 2022; Wenl et al., 2023; Zhang
      et al., 2019; Gulino et al., 2024; H. Caesar, 2021; Li et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
046
      2024), log-replay of real-world dataset (Gulino et al., 2024; H. Caesar, 2021; Li et al., 2024), and
047
      learning-based methods (Bansal et al., 2018; Isele et al., 2018; Chai et al., 2019; Bergamini et al.,
048   2021; Igl et al., 2022; Bronstein et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023). Rule-based simulations are often
049   too simplistic and fail to replicate real vehicle behaviors accurately. Log replay and learning-based
050   methods excel in replicating real vehicle behavior, but they usually lack controllability and struggle
051   to model different driving styles faithfully. CTG (Zhong et al., 2023) has proposed a controllable
052   traffic simulation method based on a diffusion model. But, its scenario is limited to the nuScenes
053   (Caesar et al., 2020) dataset, and this method has not been integrated into a simulation system for
      algorithm benchmarking. On the other hand, most data-driven simulators rely on public datasets that


                                                          1

054
      only contain fragmented scenarios (H. Caesar, 2021; Gulino et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), limiting the
055   scale of the simulation. Metadrive (Li et al., 2022) offers manual map creation tools. ScenarioNet (Li
056   et al., 2024) does a great job of collecting large-scale real-world traffic scenarios from various driving
057   datasets. However, the driving styles in the simulation environments they provide are still constrained
058   by the given dataset. Therefore, extra efforts are needed to improve traffic scenario construction and
059   enhance vehicle modeling.
060
      We propose LCSim, a Large-scale, Controllable traffic Simulator to address the abovementioned
061   challenges. Our contributions are listed below:
062
063   • We define a unified data format for traffic scenarios and provide tools to construct them from
064     multiple data sources including real-world driving datasets like the Waymo open motion dataset
065     (WOMD) (LLC, 2019) and Argoverse (Wilson et al., 2021) dataset, and hand-crafted scenarios
066     built from public data sources such as OpenStreetMap (OSM)1 (Behrisch et al., 2011; Zhang et al.,
067     2024).
068   • We design and implement a simulation system that integrates a diffusion-based vehicle motion
069     planner to achieve realistic, diverse, and controllable traffic simulation in the constructed traffic
070     scenarios. A Gym-like environment interface is provided to support reinforcement learning
071     algorithm training and benchmarking.
072   • A series of experiments are conducted to validate LCSim’s functionality. Firstly, we demonstrate
073     the ability of the diffusion-based motion planner on WOMD. Next, reinforcement learning agents
074     are trained in environments with different driving styles built by LCSim, showcasing the impact
075     of various driving styles on algorithm benchmarking. Lastly, through the accurate replication
076
        of city-level traffic scenarios, we highlight LCSim’s capability to construct large-scale traffic
        simulations.

079   2       R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Traffic Simulators. The development of traffic simulators has a history of over a decade. Initially,
082   researchers conducted simulations based on hand-crafted traffic scenarios and rule-based vehicle
083   models (Behrisch et al., 2011; Dosovitskiy et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2023; Wenl
084   et al., 2023). However, these rule-based models are often simplistic and unable to accurately model
085
      real and diverse vehicle behaviors. With the continuous advancement of autonomous driving, an
      increasing number of open-source datasets containing real-world traffic scenarios have been released
086
      in recent years(LLC, 2019; Wilson et al., 2021; Caesar et al., 2020; H. Caesar, 2021; Houston et al.,
087
      2020). Consequently, many data-driven simulators based on these datasets have emerged (Kothari
088   et al., 2021; Vinitsky et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Gulino et al., 2024). They utilize log replay to
089   rebuild realistic traffic scenarios and incorporate rule-based models to enable close-looped simulations.
090   Building upon this foundation, DriverGym (Kothari et al., 2021) provides a learning-based vehicle
091   model based on SimNet (Bergamini et al., 2021), while ScenarioNet (Li et al., 2024) integrates
092   various open-source datasets and offers reinforcement learning-based vehicle agent. However, these
093   data-driven simulators often only simulate fragmented scenarios based on the provided data, and
094   their simulation scale is limited only to the scope of the dataset. Metadrive (Li et al., 2022) presents
095   a scenario-creation tool based on the combination of map elements. However, this approach faces
096
      challenges when it comes to constructing large-scale urban road networks. Furthermore, to the best of
      our knowledge, LCSim is the first open-source traffic simulator to provide controllable learning-based
097
      vehicle models to simulate multi-style driving behaviors in the real world.
098
099   Learning-based Traffic Simulation. Various learning-based vehicle simulation methods have
100   emerged with the increasing availability of open-source traffic scenario datasets in recent years.
101   Among them, imitation learning is often employed to learn expert actions from the dataset, thereby
102
      achieving realistic traffic simulation (Xu et al., 2023; Bergamini et al., 2021; Bhattacharyya et al.,
      2018; Zheng et al., 2020; Bhattacharyya et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023). However, this approach is
103
      often plagued by causal confusion (De Haan et al., 2019) and distribution shift (Ross et al., 2011).
104
      Reinforcement learning methods, on the other hand, address the distribution shift issue effectively by
105   interacting with the simulation environment to learn driving behaviors (Kendall et al., 2019; Isele
106   et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2022). However, the design of reward

209   With scenario data constructed from multiple sources, LCSim performs discrete-time simulation
210   based on a given time interval. Figure 3 illustrates the basic components of a simulation step. Each
211   simulation step can be divided into two stages:
212   Prepare Stage. During this stage, the simulator prepares the observation data for each vehicle,
213   as depicted in the blue box in Figure 3. The observation data comprises three components: scene
214   information observed by the vehicle, including road network topology and surrounding vehicles,
215   scene embeddings computed by the scene encoder, which encodes the scene information, and vehicles’
      motion plans either generated by the diffusion decoder or given by the logs from the scenario data.

216
      Update Stage. In this stage, each vehicle’s action is calculated by its policy based on the observation
217   data, and these actions are used to update the vehicles’ states. We implement various control
218   policies in the simulator to handle different simulation scenarios. The ExpertPolicy controls vehicles
219   to strictly follow the given motion plans. At the same time, the BicycleExpertPolicy enhances
220   this by adding kinematic control based on the bicycle model to achieve more realistic simulation
221   effects. Furthermore, we implement the IDMPolicy to enable closed-loop traffic simulation, where
222   vehicles adjust their accelerations based on the objects ahead while following the given motion plans.
223   Additionally, any vehicle in the scenario can be controlled by external input actions, allowing the
224   simulator to serve as a training or testing environment for specific vehicle control algorithms. Details

      about these policies can be found in Appendix B.2.

284   Datasets. We train our diffusion model on WOMD (LLC, 2019), which contains 500+ hours of
285   driving logs collected from seven different cities in the United States. The dataset is further divided
286   into scenario segments of 20s and 9s. In this experiment, we utilize the 9s segments for training, using
287
      the initial 1s as the historical context, and let the model generate vehicle motions in the future 8s. The
      diffusion model is trained on the training set and evaluated for its performance on the validation set.
288
289   Metrics. The evaluation metrics of the model consist of two aspects: first, the motion plans generated
290   for vehicles by the model should adhere to basic traffic rules. We calculate the probabilities of
291   vehicle collisions and off-road incidents in the simulated scenarios and compare these with statistical
292   values from real-world data. Second, we use distance-based metrics to assess the model’s ability to
293
      reconstruct real-world traffic scenarios. For each scenario in the validation set, we sample K times
      for a simulation duration of T and compute the average displacement error across time (ADE) and
294
      the final displacement error (FDE) at the last time step. In the experiment, we set K = 6, and T = 8s,
295
      and choose the best matching sample to calculate minADE and minFDE.
296

298   learning-based traffic simulation methods, TrafficSim (Suo et al., 2021) and SimNet (Bergamini
299
      et al., 2021). SimNet simulates traffic based on behavior cloning, while TrafficSim generates motion
      plans for vehicles using C-VAE. Additionally, to verify the effectiveness of the realistic guidance, we
300
      compare the model’s performance with and without the "no collision" and "no off-road" guidance 2 .
301
      To handle the impact of randomness in the generation process, we conducted repeated experiments
302   and reported the mean and error bars of the results.

477   5       C ONCLUSION
478
479   We proposed LCSim, a large-scale, controllable diffusion-based traffic simulator. With an automated
480   tool to construct traffic scenarios from multiple data sources, LCSim is capable of conducting large-
481   scale traffic simulations. By integrating the diffusion-based motion planner and guide functions,
482   LCSim can build traffic environments with diverse vehicle driving styles.
483   Limitations. LCSim has two main limitations. Firstly, the simulator is implemented in Python using
484   a single-threaded CPU, which limits its performance potential, discussion can be found in Appendix
485
          3
              See Appendix A.4 for details.

      E. Although parallel simulation using multiple processes is currently employed as a solution, it does
487   not fundamentally address the issue. One potential approach to overcome this limitation is to develop
488   a multi-threaded version of the simulator using C++ and deploy the Diffusion model in C++, which
489   is a potential direction for future work. Secondly, the simulator currently provides visualization
490   only from a top-down perspective and lacks the rendering of realistic perceptual data. Integrating
491   the sensor data generation method into the simulation is one of the planned future developments to
492   address this limitation.

      We implemented five different policies to support traffic simulation in various scenarios:
958
959   • ExpertPolicy: The vehicles strictly follow the given action sequences to proceed.
960
      • BicycleExpertPolicy: Based on the expert policy, we impose kinematic constraints on the vehicle’s
961
        behavior using a bicycle model to prevent excessive acceleration and steering. By default, we set
962     max acceleration to 6.0 m/s2 and max steering angle to 0.3 rad.
963
964
      • LaneIDMPolicy: Under this policy, vehicles ignore the action sequences and proceed along the
        center line of their current lane. The vehicle’s acceleration is calculated using the IDM model and
965
        lane-changing behavior is generated using the Mobil model.
966
967   • TrajIDMPolicy: Vehicles move along the trajectories computed based on the action sequence, but
968     their acceleration is controlled by the IDM Mode to prevent collisions.
969   • RL-based Policy: A PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) agent trained based on our simulator, its observa-
970     tion space contains the scene embedding and the action sequence. The action space consists of
971     acceleration and steering values. The training environment of this agent is the second one, enabling
        diffusive simulation with Waymo-style vehicle behavior.

1023   D.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING S ETUP
1024
1025   We constructed a reinforcement learning environment based on the validation set of the Waymo
       dataset. 4,400 scenarios are selected from the validation set and further divided into a training set


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       containing 4,000 scenarios and a test set containing 400 scenarios. We trained a PPO (Schulman
1027   et al., 2017) agent on the training set and evaluated its performance on the test set.
1028
1029   Observation Spec. Observation of the agent consists of two parts:
1030
       • Scene Embedding: Embedding computed by scene encoder of the diffusion model with size of [Nh ],
1031
         by applying cross attention to map polygons and agent states, this feature contains information about
1032     surrounding vehicles, road elements, and the vehicle’s own historical states. In this experiment, we
1033     use Nh = 128 following the setup of the diffusion model.
1034
       • Route: We sampled the vehicle’s trajectory points within the next 1 second at a frequency of 10Hz
1035
         and projected them into a relative coordinate system based on the vehicle’s current position and
1036     orientation. The shape of the route data is [10, 2], representing the reference path of the vehicle’s
1037     forward movement. If the vehicle behavior in the driving environment is generated by a diffusion
1038     model, then this path will be accumulated from the behavior sequences generated by the model for
1039     the vehicle.
1040
1041   Action Spec. We let the agent directly control the throttle and steering angle of the vehicle. The
1042   agent’s output is a two-dimensional vector with a range [−1, 1]. This vector is multiplied by the
1043
       maximum range of acceleration and steering angle, resulting in the final vehicle action. In this
       experiment, the maximum acceleration and steering angle of the vehicle are set to 6.0 and 0.3,
1044
       respectively.
1045
1046   Rollout Setting. To let the agent explore every scenario in the training set, we randomly divided the
1047   4000 scenes in the training set into 20 parts, each containing 200 different scenarios. We used 20
1048   parallel threads to rollout episodes, with each thread pre-loading and pre-calculating map embeddings
1049
       for 200 different training scenarios. During the rollout process, after the current episode ends, the
       environment automatically switches to the next scenario, and this cycle continues iteratively.
1050
1051   Reward Function. Our goal is to make the vehicle progress along the given route while avoiding
1052   collisions and staying within the road. Therefore, we provide the following formula for the reward:
1053
1054                      R = Rf orward + Pcollision + Proad + Psmooth + Rdestination .                         (4)
1055
1056   The meanings of elements in the formula are as follows:
1057
1058   • Rf orward : A dense reward to encourage the vehicle to drive forward along the given route. We
1059     project the current position and last position of the vehicle onto the Frenet coordinate of the route
1060
         and calculate dt , dt−1 , st , st−1 , the value of the reward would be 0.1 × ((st − st−1 ) − (dt − dt−1 )).
1061   • Pcollision : Penalty for collision, When the vehicle collides, the value will be −10, and the current
1062     episode terminates; otherwise, the value is 0.
1063   • Proad : Penalty for driving off the road, when this happens, the value will be −5, and the current
1064     episode terminates; otherwise, the value is 0.
1065   • Psmooth : Following (Li et al., 2024), we implemented Psmooth = min(0, 1/vt − |a[0]|) to avoid a
1066     large steering value change between two timesteps.
1067
       • Rdestination : When an episode ends, we check if the vehicle has reached the destination of the
1068     given route, which means the distance to the endpoint of the route is within 2.5 meters. If yes, the
1069     reward value is 10; otherwise, it’s −5.
