034                                             A BSTRACT
035
036           The success of autoregressive (AR) language models in text generation has in-
037           spired the computer vision community to adopt Large Language Models (LLMs)
038           for image generation. However, considering the essential differences between text
039           and image modalities, the design space of language models for image generation
040           remains underexplored. We observe that image tokens exhibit greater random-
041
              ness compared to text tokens, which presents challenges when training with token
              prediction. Nevertheless, AR models demonstrate their potential by effectively
042
              learning patterns even from a seemingly suboptimal optimization problem. Our
043
              analysis also reveals that while all models successfully grasp the importance of lo-
044           cal information in image generation, smaller models struggle to capture the global
045           context. In contrast, larger models showcase improved capabilities in this area,
046           helping to explain the performance gains achieved when scaling up model size.
047           We further elucidate the design space of language models for vision generation,
048           including tokenizer choice, model choice, model scalability, vocabulary design,
049           and sampling strategy through extensive comparative experiments. Our work is
050           the first to analyze the optimization behavior of language models in vision gen-
051           eration, and we believe it can inspire more effective designs when applying LMs
052
              to other domains. Finally, our elucidated language model for image generation,
              termed as ELM, achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ImageNet 256×256
053
              benchmark.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   In the domain of artificial intelligence generated content (AIGC), text and image generation (Brown,
058   2020; Ho et al., 2022) represent the principal focal points. Despite their shared goal of content gen-
059   eration, these two modalities predominantly employ distinct modeling methods. On the one hand,
060   text generation is commonly facilitated by autoregressive (AR) language models, like LLaMA-3
061   (Touvron et al., 2023a) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), which operate by predicting subsequent
062   tokens based on preceding ones in a sequence. On the other hand, image generation predominantly
063   utilizes diffusion models, such as Dall·E 3 (Betker et al., 2023) and Stable Diffusion v3 (Esser et al.,
064
      2024), which learn to gradually denoise images for all pixels simultaneously.
065   The recent success of large language models (LLMs) has bolstered the research community’s confi-
066   dence in their potential contribution towards achieving artificial general intelligence (AGI). This op-
067   timism has also inspired researchers within the computer vision domain to extend the AR paradigm
068   to applications beyond text, such as image generation (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Tian et al.,
069   2024) and video generation (Kondratyuk et al., 2024). Such explorations open up novel avenues
070
      for leveraging AR in visual content creation. A significant advantage of integrating LLMs into im-
      age generation is the ability to transfer established techniques from text-based applications, such as
071
      generating content that exceeds the input length. In contrast, diffusion models generally exhibit less
072
      flexibility in adapting to such capabilities. Moreover, the scalability of LLMs makes them the pre-
073   ferred foundation for building unified models with multi-modal inference capabilities (Team et al.,
074   2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2024). Gaining a deeper understanding of their potential across domains
075   will aid the community in building more efficient and effective universal models.
076
      Nevertheless, current research in visual generation remains focused on diffusion models (Karras
077
      et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023; Kingma & Gao, 2024), and language models for image generation have
078
      yet to be thoroughly explored. Current efforts (Esser et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022;
079   Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) are mostly preliminary, involving the discretization
080   of images into sequences of tokens with vector-quantization autoencoders, which are then processed
081   by language models trained with token prediction objectives. However, considering that text and
082   images represent fundamentally different modalities, it is essential to thoroughly analyze the training
083   dynamics and elucidate the design space when adapting LLM for image generation tasks.
084
      In this study, we delve into the potential of language models for vision generation tasks. We quanti-
085   tatively analyze the fundamental differences between images and text and conduct a comprehensive
086   exploration of the design space for image generation using language models. Starting with image
087   tokenization, we compare two approaches: VQGAN, which uses a vector quantizer (VQ) to dis-
088   cretize latent codes (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021), and BAE, which employs binary
089   autoencoders for “look-up free” quantization (LFQ) (Wang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023). Our com-
090   parison based on reconstruction ability, scalability, and generation performance shows that BAE
091   consistently outperforms VQGAN across all dimensions. Despite this, current language model-
092   based image generation methods largely rely on vector-quantization auto-encoders (Yu et al., 2022;
093
      Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). We believe that a more powerful quantizer for
      images can lead to significantly better generation performance. We then evaluate the performance
094
      of two primary language modeling approaches for image generation: autoregressive (AR) models
095
      and masked language models (MLMs). Consistent with findings in the language domain (Henighan
096   et al., 2020; Liao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024; Chang & Bergen, 2024), AR models demon-
097   strate superior image generation ability and scalability compared to MLMs. We further leveraged
098   the flexibility of the binary-valued bit codes produced by BAE. Through our exploration of code
099   decomposition strategies, we found that splitting the original code into two subcodes significantly
100   reduces learning complexity, improves performance, and reduces computational costs.
101
      Additionally, we analyze how AR models learn to generate images by examining attention scores
102   across different layers and model sizes. Our findings indicate that AR models effectively learn the
103   importance of local information for image generation. However, larger models also capture global
104   information, which is more difficult for smaller models to learn, helping to explain the performance
105   improvements observed with increasing model size. Our research deepens the understanding of
106   the LLM’s capability and behavior in vision generation. The insights can contribute to the design of
107   more efficient and unified large models handling multi-modalities inference tasks and the exploration
      of general artificial intelligence systems. In conclusion, our main contributions include:

193   in Henighan et al. (2020). However, the models can still generate high-quality images with a low
194   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), indicating that they have learned sufficient
195
      patterns for image generation, although the training loss remains high.

      3.4            L EARNING AND S CALING B EHAVIOR
283
284   To further understand the model’s learned patterns, we visualize the attention maps of different AR
285   models. These visualizations revealed that the attention mechanism were primarily focused on local
286   regions of the image, indicating that the AR transformer models effectively learn the importance of
287   local patterns for image generation (Vaswani et al., 2017). This finding is notable because the model
288   was trained without any inductive biases tailored to image data, highlighting the strong capability of
289   AR transformer models across different domains.
290   Additionally, the scaling law (Henighan et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020) holds for AR models in
291   image generation tasks, as reflected in lower training loss (Figure 14), improved generation perfor-
292   mance, and an enhanced ability to capture global information as the model size increases. As for
293   the attention pattern, models of varying sizes showed subtle differences: the L-sized model mainly
294   focused on local information, struggling to capture long-term information. In contrast, larger models
295   (XL and XXL) exhibited longer-range attention in certain layers, suggesting they had also learned
296   global features (Figure 4). Specifically, in the first layer (layer 0), attention generally captures global
297
      information, while deeper layers show a more localized focus, with recent tokens receiving greater
      attention. In XL and XXL models, which have more layers, some deeper layers still capture global
298
      information. However, in L-sized models, the deeper layers also focus on local tokens, with little
299
      attention to long-term dependencies. The incorporation of global information positively impacted
300   overall generation performance, as evidenced by the lower FID score and better visual image quality
301   as shown in Figure 8.

      code dimension improves reconstruction ability. Recent research also indicates that a stronger tok-
336   enizer leads to a better generation performance in AR models (Tao et al., 2024).
337
338   To address the challenge of large vocabulary sizes, we leverage the flexibility of binary-quantized
339
      codes that allows us to decompose each code into multiple subcodes (Yu et al., 2023). For instance,
      an 8-bit code like [1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1] can be split into two 4-bit codes: [1, 0, 1, 0] and [0, 0, 1, 1].
340
      These two subcodes can then be converted into decimal values to generate corresponding indices.
341
      As a result, we convert the embedding matrix from a size of 28 × Df eature into two matrices of size
342
      24 × Df eature , where Df eature is the feature dimension within the AR model. The final embedding
343   is achieved by concatenating the two indexed embeddings and applying a projection to restore the
344   dimension to Df eature . Separate prediction heads are applied to generate the logits.
345
346
      We conduct experiments using AR models with BAE that have varying code dimensions (D = 16,
      20, 24, and 32). We treat quantizers with and without code decomposition as distinct tokenizers; for
347
      example, for D = 16, “1-16” means the original tokenizer and “2-8” denotes the code is split into
348
      two 8-bit subcodes. The results in Figure 5 reveal several key insights:
349
350   • Optimal decomposition. A decomposition into two subcodes is generally optimal, which also
351   reduces computational costs, leading to more efficient and effective generation (see the detailed
352
      result in Table 10). When dealing with two sub-vocabularies of smaller size, the prediction at
      each position is split into two independent classification tasks, each with a more manageable set of
353
      possible outcomes, largely reducing the cognitive load on the model (Ali et al., 2023; Yang, 2024).
354
      Further increasing the number of subcodes significantly raises the prediction complexity, and the
355   model struggles to optimize across three or more dimensions (Limisiewicz et al., 2023). This is
356   evidenced by the increasing training loss observed when moving from tokenizers 2-8 to 3-8 and 4-8
357   in XL and XXL models (see Figure A.4). The added complexity in managing multiple classification
358   heads impairs the model’s generalization, leading to suboptimal outcomes in image synthesis.
359
      • Vocabulary complexity and model capacity. Larger code dimensions generally lead to improved
360
      generation performance but introduce more complex vocabularies, making it harder for the model
361   to predict the next token. As a result, more complex tokenizers require more powerful models for
362   effective learning (Tao et al., 2024). For example, the 2-10 tokenizer is optimal for L and XL models,
363   while the 2-12 tokenizer performs best with the XXL model.
364
      These findings demonstrate the trade-offs between model scale, vocabulary complexity, and decom-
365
      position strategies, highlighting the potential of the AR model’s ability to effectively handle complex
366
      tokenization while maintaining high performance across model scales.

369   3.6   S AMPLING S TRATEGY
370
371   Sampling strategy plays a crucial role in vision generation, applicable to both diffusion models
372   (Karras et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023) and language models (Chang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024). In
373   this study, we thoroughly explore the sampling strategies for both AR and MLM, including classifier-
374
      free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) (CFG) scale, the introduction of randomness, and the number
      of generation iterations for the MLMs.
375
376   Firstly, regarding the CFG scale, we discover that a gradually increasing CFG scale performs better
377   than a constant one. We test various CFG scale scheduling methods (as illustrated in Figure 16) and
      find that linear scheduling yields the best results (see the result in Table 11).

      Secondly, regarding the introduction of randomness, for the AR model, randomness primarily de-
379   rives from the k value in the top-k filter used when selecting next-token indices based on their confi-
380   dence scores; a larger k introduces more randomness. For the MLM, randomness mainly stems from
381   the coefficient τ of Gumbel noise added to the confidence of the [MASK] token predictions; a larger
382   τ results in greater randomness. We observed that for both methods, a high degree of randomness is
383   crucial during the sampling process (see Figure 6, 7 and Table 12). This finding is consistent with
384   the natural randomness of image token distribution discussed in Section 3.1. Moreover, as model
385   size and vocabulary increase, the need for randomness diminishes, indicating that larger models
386   are capable of capturing a broader range of patterns and making more accurate predictions. This
387
      observation aligns with the attention and scalability analysis discussed earlier, where larger models
      demonstrated enhanced capacity to manage both local and global information, reducing the need for
388
      stochasticity to generate realistic samples.
389

408
409   After thoroughly exploring the design space of language models for image generation, via combin-
410   ing the better design trick, we reach our final Elucidated Language model for iMage generation
411   (ELM). ELM adopts BAE as the image tokenizer and AR as the modeling method. According
412   to our previous results, ELM splits the quantized image code into two subcodes. When choosing
413   the vocabulary, the capacity of the model should be considered. Larger vocabularies require more
414   powerful models to handle next-token prediction (2-12 tokenizer works best for ELM-XXL), while
415   smaller models perform better with simpler vocabularies (ELM-L and XL perform better with 2-10

      tokenizer). For sampling strategy, we choose a high randomness because it will bring in large di-

      (2-10), ELM-XXL (2-12), and ELM-2B (2-12), with parameters ranging from 315M to 1.9B.

      Large Language Models. Language models are foundational tools in natural language processing,
474   designed to predict the likelihood of sequences of words or tokens, using Transformer architectures
475   with self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). There are two primary types: autoregressive
476   (AR) models, like GPT (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron
477   et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024), etc., which generate text one token at a time in a left-to-right
478   fashion, and masked language models (MLM), such as BERT (Devlin, 2018), T5 (Raffel et al.,
479   2020), etc., which predict masked tokens within a sequence using bidirectional context. AR models
480   are particularly effective for text generation due to their sequential nature, while MLMs are bet-
481   ter suited for representation learning by leveraging global context (Chang & Bergen, 2024). The
482
      scaling law (Henighan et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020), which describes the relationship between
      the growth of model parameters, dataset sizes, computational resources, and performance improve-
483
      ments, highlights the immense potential of AR models.
484
485   Vision Generation. Vision generation is a key focus in the current AIGC field, primarily relying on
      diffusion probabilistic models, which generate images by progressively denoising a random Gaus-

      6   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we investigate the use of language models for image generation. We analyze the
530   differences between image and text token distribution, demonstrating how these distinctions affect
531
      training behavior, and offering insights that extend beyond current research on language models
      for image generation. We further elucidate the design space of language models for vision genera-
532
      tion, including tokenizer choice, model choice, model scalability, vocabulary design, and sampling
533
      strategy through extensive comparative experiments. Through our analysis, we have the following
534   findings: (1) binary autoencoder (BAE) demonstrates superior performance as an image tokenizer
535   compared to traditional VQGAN approaches; (2) AR models consistently outperform MLMs and
536   show a strong scaling law, (3) larger vocabulary size and a decomposition design benefit the image
537   generation, (4) sampling strategies should also allow for greater randomness; gradually increased
538   CFG scale, larger top-k are important for a better FID score. By combining these designs, we reach
539   our final ELM model, and it achieves state-of-the-art performance on ImageNet. We hope this work
      will motivate further usage of the AR model across other domains.

       A.6   L IMITATION
1234
1235
       Our work has limitations. While we propose several improvements for AR models, the fundamental
1236   issue of optimizing highly random token distributions remains. Traditional next-token prediction
1237   using classification loss may not be the most optimal training objective for such tasks, suggesting
1238   that more suitable objectives should be explored in future research. For instance, MAR (Li et al.,
1239   2024) has made promising progress by introducing diffusion loss into AR models, while VAR (Tian
1240   et al., 2024) presents a valuable perspective by altering the image tokenization approach. We hope
1241   our analysis will inspire further exploration and innovation in utilizing language models for vision
       generation, as well as other modalities.
