009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            Computational imaging plays a vital role in various scientific and medical appli-
012            cations, such as Full Waveform Inversion (FWI), Computed Tomography (CT),
013            and Electromagnetic (EM) inversion. These methods address inverse problems by
014            reconstructing physical properties (e.g., the acoustic velocity map in FWI) from
015            measurement data (e.g., seismic waveform data in FWI), where both modalities
016            are governed by complex mathematical equations. In this paper, we empirically
017            demonstrate that despite their differing governing equations, three inverse prob-
018
               lems—FWI, CT, and EM inversion—share a hidden property within their latent
               spaces. Specifically, using FWI as an example, we show that both modalities (the
019
               velocity map and seismic waveform data) follow the same set of one-way wave
020
               equations in the latent space, yet have distinct initial conditions that are linearly
021            correlated. This suggests that after projection into the latent embedding space, the
022            two modalities correspond to different solutions of the same equation, connected
023            through their initial conditions. Our experiments confirm that this hidden property
024            is consistent across all three imaging problems, providing a novel perspective for
025            understanding these computational imaging tasks.

053   Computational imaging, encompassing applications such as Full Waveform Inversion (FWI), Com-
      puted Tomography (CT), and Electromagnetic (EM) inversion, is foundational in many scientific


                                                                 1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and medical fields. These methods address inverse problems, which involve reconstructing physi-
055   cal properties from measured data, a process governed by linear or nonlinear mathematical equa-
056   tions (Kirsch et al., 2011). Accurate reconstruction of physical properties is essential for various
057   applications, including medical diagnostics, geophysical exploration, and non-destructive testing of
058   materials. Deep learning methods usually trade these problems as Image-to-Image translation tasks,
059   modeling them via encoder-decoder architectures, and achieve significant improvements (McCann
060   et al., 2017; Wu & Lin, 2019; Ongie et al., 2020; Song et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022;
061   Feng et al., 2024b). However, while these methods construct latent space representations, typically
062   with a bottleneck in the network, they lack a deeper understanding of these latent representations.
063
      Thus, we are curious about the question:
064           Whether an elegant mathematical relationship exists in the latent space, akin to that
065           in the original space?
066
      This curiosity drives us to explore the structure of the latent space, specifically whether a simpler
067   mathematical relationship exists between the two modalities in these inverse problems.
068
069   Recently, Chen et al. demonstrated that, in the latent space, natural images can be described by
070
      a set of one-way wave equations with learnable speeds (Chen et al., 2023b;a), where each image
      corresponds to a unique solution of these wave equations, enabling high-fidelity reconstruction from
071
      an initial condition. While this work links natural images to wave equation-based representations,
072
      it is limited to single-modality image reconstruction. Motivated by this work, we aim to explore
073   the relationship between two modalities in computational imaging. Specifically, our exploration
074   is driven by three key questions: (1) Can two modalities share the same wave equations in the
075   latent space? (2) What is the relationship between their initial conditions? (3) Can this relationship
076   generalize across different computational imaging problems?
077
      This paper answers these three questions above. Firstly, we show that the latent spaces of both
078
      measurement data and target properties are governed by the same set of one-way wave equations,
079   characterized by identical wave speeds. The two modalities can be projected as different initial
080   conditions of these same equations. Secondly, building upon the work of Feng et al. (Feng et al.,
081   2022; 2024b), who discovered a linear correlation between the latent representations of two modal-
082   ities in geophysical inversion problems (e.g., FWI, EM inversion), we further reveal that when the
083   two modalities follow the same wave equations, the corresponding initial conditions also exhibit
084   a strong linear correlation, allowing one to be derived from the other via a linear transformation.
085   Finally, we demonstrate that this hidden property is common across different computational imag-
086   ing problems. As illustrated in Fig 1, we term this hidden property HINT (short for the HIddeN
087
      properTy). The HINT transforms the relationships of physical properties, traditionally described by
      distinct equations in the physical space, into a dual problem in the latent space described by this
088
      common property across various tasks.
089
090   The proposed hidden property can be easily implemented. We propose a unified framework that
091   learns the embedding of measurement data and target property together while simultaneously gen-
092   erating input reconstruction and target property prediction. Our approach begins by encoding the
093
      measurement data P (e.g., waveform data in FWI) into a latent vector, denoted as vP , using a visual
      encoder E. This latent vector vP is then linearly transformed to obtain the latent vector vψ of the
094
      target property ψ (e.g., velocity map in FWI). Both vP and vψ are propagated through the same au-
095
      toregression process (called multi-path FINOLA) governed by one-way wave equations (Chen et al.,
096   2023b;a) to generate larger size feature maps zP and zψ , respectively. Subsequently, decoders DP
097   and Dψ are employed to reconstruct the original input P from zP and to infer the corresponding ψ
098   from zψ . The network is trained with a combination of L1 and L2 loss. This integrated framework
099   captures both cross-domain and within-domain relationships in the latent space, offering a more pre-
100   cise and interpretable understanding of the latent space structure. The discovered hidden property
101   forms the core of the framework, serving as a hard constraint throughout the learning process. Based
102   on this architecture, the wave speed Λ of the hidden wave equations, along with the two solutions
103   (noted as ζP and ζψ ), can be derived from the parameters of FINOLA, as well as the feature maps
104
      zP and zψ . The detailed relationship will be explained in the next section.
105   We validate the proposed hidden property across three tasks: FWI (Deng et al., 2022), EM inver-
106   sion (Alumbaugh et al., 2021), and CT (Flanders et al., 2020). Across these tasks, our approach
107   matches or surpasses the performance of unconstrained methods. These results demonstrate that the
      constrained latent space remains optimal for solving inverse problems, offering a simpler and more

      tractable latent space structure without compromising reconstruction accuracy. By leveraging the
109   hidden property, the proposed framework provides a new perspective on the relationship between
110   physical properties in their latent representations, paving the way for a further understanding of the
111   latent space.

      source term. r = (x, y) is the spatial location for 2D slice data, in which x is the horizontal direction

241   Overall Structure: Combining the above two processes over two modalities, we proposed method
242   HINT (short for the Hidden Property), a unified framework that jointly learns the embeddings of
243   both measurement data and target property, while simultaneously performing input reconstruction
244   and target property prediction. The overall framework is illustrated in Fig. 2 (b). The network is
245   trained by combining both the reconstruction loss and prediction loss.
246   Empirical validation: We empirically validate the two key components of the above extension of
247   two modalities: the shared wave equations and the linear correlation between embeddings. First,
248   we compare using separate versus shared FINOLA layers on the FWI tasks. Results are shown in
249   Fig.3, Section 3.3. We see similar performance between models using two distinct FINOLAs and

252   Section 3.3. A nonlinear converter has no positive effect, affirming that a strong linear correlation

278   Difference with vanilla FINOLA: Unlike vanilla FINOLA, which is designed for single-modality
279   image reconstruction, our method extends to two modalities by sharing parameters across both do-
280   mains. While vanilla FINOLA captures single-domain image invariants, we use FINALO to model
281   the relationship between two domains in computational imaging, enabling the joint representation
282
      of measurement data and target properties with wave equations.

      3.1   DATASETS
293
      FWI: For many scientific problems, like subsurface imaging, real data are extremely expensive and
294   difficult to obtain. Research often relies on full-physics simulations due to the lack of publicly
295   available real datasets. Thus, we verify our method on OpenFWI (Deng et al., 2022), the first
296   open-source collection of large-scale, multi-structural benchmark datasets for data-driven seismic
297   FWI. It contains 11 2D datasets with baseline, which can be divided into four groups: four datasets
298   in the “Vel Family” are FlateVel-A/B, and CurveVel-A/B; four datasets in the “Fault Family” are
299   FlateFault-A/B, and CurveFault-A/B; two datasets in“Style Family” are Style-A/B; and one dataset
300   in “Kimberlina Family” is Kimberlina-CO2 . The first three families cover two versions: easy (A)
301   and hard (B), in terms of the complexity of subsurface structures. The following experiments are
302
      conducted on the ten datasets of these first three families. We will use the abbreviations (e.g., FVA
      for FlatVel-A). More details can be found in (Deng et al., 2022).
303
304   CT: The CT dataset, provided by the Radiological Society of North America (RSNA) and ASNR,
305   includes large volumes of de-identified brain CT scans labeled by expert neuroradiologists (Stein
306   et al., 2019). It focuses on detecting acute intracranial hemorrhage, a critical condition that requires
307   rapid diagnosis. The dataset covers various hemorrhage types to enable AI algorithms to assist
308
      in identifying hemorrhages for quicker and more accurate medical treatment. We randomly select
      47000 samples as the training set and 6000 samples as the test set, with resolution 256 × 256. We
309
      simulate CT measurements (projection) with a stationary head CT (s-HCT) system with three linear
310
      CNT x-ray source arrays (Luo et al., 2021). This design has sparse and asymmetrical scans and
311   a non-circular geometry with a relatively low radiation dose, providing a unique challenge to the
312   reconstruction. An illustration of the geometry has been shown in the Supplementary Material.
313
314
      EM Inversion: We also test our method on the subsurface electromagnetic (EM) inversion task
      on the Kimberlina-Reservoir dataset, which recovers subsurface conductivity from surface-acquired
315
      EM measurements. The geophysical properties were developed under DOE’s NRAP. It is based on
316
      a potential CO2 storage site in the Southern San Joaquin Basin of California (Alumbaugh et al.,
317   2021). In this data, there are 780 EM data for geophysical measurement with the corresponding
318   conductivity. We use 750/30 for training and testing. EM data are simulated by finite-difference
319   method (Commer & Newman, 2008; Feng et al., 2022).

357   with a dimension of 512 and one path. The feature map of velocity maps, ζψ , has the size (7 × 7)
358
      for Sec. 3.3, and (14 × 14) for the rest.
359   For the CT dataset, the size of projection data is 3 × 45 × 1728, and the size of the CT image is
360   256 × 256. We choose patch size (9 × 36) for the three-layer Transformer with the hidden size
361   of 768, and the number of heads is 16. Then it will be pooled with two seeds, i.e., the dimension

365   Evaluation Metrics. We apply three metrics to evaluate the generated geophysical properties:
366   MAE, MSE, and Structural Similarity (SSIM). Following the existing literature (Wu & Lin, 2019;
367   Feng et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022), MAE and MSE are employed to measure the pixel-wise error,
368   and SSIM is to measure the perceptual similarity since the target properties have highly structured
369   information, and degradation or distortion can be easily perceived by a human. We calculate them
370
      on normalized data, i.e., MAE and MSE in the scale [−1, 1], and SSIM in the scale [0, 1].

      4     R ELATED W ORKS
512
513   Recently, data-driven methods for inverse problems have emerged, treating it as an image-to-image
514   translation problem with an encoder-decoder architecture. Wu & Lin (2019); Zhang et al. (2019)
515   utilized a CNN to address FWI, while Jin et al. (2022) combined forward modeling with deep neural
516
      networks in an unsupervised learning framework. Diffusion models have also emerged as competi-
      tive solutions for inverse problems, requiring pre-training of a prior model and integrating the mea-
517
      surement process into the denoising process (Song et al., 2021; Tewari et al., 2023). Unlike them,
518
      our work focuses on uncovering the underlying mathematical relationships within the latent space.
519   Similarly, Feng et al. (2022; 2024a) decoupled the training of the encoder and decoder, demonstrat-
520   ing a strong linear correlation between the latent representations of two modalities in geophysical
521   inversion. We go further by proposing that the linear correlation exists even when both modalities
522   follow the same wave equations in the latent space.
523
      FINOLA (Chen et al., 2023b;a), a recent advancement in modeling image invariance in latent space,
524
      models latent features using a first-order autoregressive process. It focuses on treating each image
525   as a unique solution of the wave equations. This approach not only has the ability for image recon-
526   struction but also extends to self-supervised learning tasks with Masked Image Modeling (MIM).
527   In MIM (Bao et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022), networks are challenged to reconstruct missing parts of
528   an image. Recently, MAE (He et al., 2022) adopts an asymmetric encoder-decoder architecture to
529   recover pixels from highly masked images, demonstrating its ability to learn robust representations.
530   A more detailed comparison of our work with FINOLA is shown in Sec. 2

534
535   In this paper, we empirically reveal a hidden property in the latent space of computational imag-
536   ing. This property, characterized by a shared set of one-way wave equations and a strong linear
537   correlation between the latent representations of measurement data and target properties, enables a
538   unified framework across different computational imaging tasks. Our experiments validate the hid-
539   den property across different computational imaging tasks. It shows that an elegant mathematical
      relationship exists in the latent space, akin to that in the original space.
