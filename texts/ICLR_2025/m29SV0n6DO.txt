                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               We empirically study generative pre-training from videos. This paper does not de-
010
               scribe a novel method, Instead, It studies a straightforward, yet must-know baseline
011            given the recent progress of large language models pre-training for self-supervised
012            vision pretraining. Our approach is conceptually simple and inspired by generative
013            pre-training from text and images. To enable scaling to videos, we make several
014            important improvements along the data, architecture, and evaluation axes. Our
015            model, called Toto, is a causal transformer that generates videos autoregressively,
016            one token at a time. We pre-train our model on a diverse set of videos with over
017            1 trillion visual tokens. Our tokens are quantized patch embeddings, and we use
018            relative embeddings for coarse-to-fine pre-training. We conduct a large-scale study
019
               across a suite of diverse benchmarks, including image recognition, video classifica-
               tion, object tracking, robotic manipulation and scaling behaviours. We find that,
020
               despite minimal inductive biases, our approach achieves competitive performance
021
               across all benchmarks.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In a paper published in 1951, Shannon, having just published the foundational papers of information
027   theory, proposed a “guessing game” of next word prediction to estimate the entropy of English (Shan-
028   non, 1951). Nearly 70 years later, training a high-capacity transformer network (Vaswani et al., 2017)
029   on this task, provided the generative pre-training backbone for Large Language Models (Radford
030   et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020).
031   Less well known is the fact that in 1954, Fred Attneave (Attneave, 1954) proposed an analog of
032   Shannon’s task for images. To quote “We may divide the picture into arbitrarily small elements
033   which we “transmit” to a subject (S) in a cumulative sequence, having them guess at the color of each
034   successive element until they are correct. This method of analysis resembles the scanning process
035   used in television and facsimile systems and accomplishes the like purpose of transforming two
036   spatial dimensions into a single sequence in time”.
037   While Attneave was concerned with images, in the context of 2024, we have to note the “Big Visual
038   Data” is in videos, not images. While there are concerns that most of the text available on the Internet
039   has already been used by the leading language models, in video we are barely started on the journey
040   of Big Data exploitation.
041
      As a step toward that goal, we propose a method for generative pre-training from videos. We build
042   Toto: Token to Token Video models with necessary architectural changes to enable scaling to videos.
043   Figure 1 shows our overall framework, we use multiple data sources, such as internet style exocentric
044   videos, egocentric videos, and images to pre-train our models. Our pre-training objective is simple
045   and follows language modeling, by predicting the next token. We then evaluate our models on various
046   downstream tasks and show that generative video pre-training can lead to strong representations, for
047   image, video understanding, and robot manipulation tasks.
048   In summary, this paper studies two central questions: (1) what is an appropriate architecture for
049   generative pre-training in vision, and (2) what are the benefits of generative pre-trained features for
050   vision tasks? First, we study the effects of various tokenization approaches (e.g. VQGAN (Esser
051   et al., 2020), dVAE (Ramesh et al., 2021), patches (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)) and find that, most
052   of these perform similar to each other. We find that relative positional embeddings are better than
053   absolute ones, allowing us to extend the context length and resolution in a straightforward manner.
      Our architecture enables us to train models on videos and images jointly. We pre-train three model

      2   R ELATED WORK
078
079   Over the years self-supervised pre-training has proven to be effective in many areas including
080   language, vision, and robotics. For vision, there are two main schools of thought, discriminative
081
      vs generative pre-training. Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2018) and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020b) showed
      that instance discrimination training can learn strong discriminative features. Recently, MoCo (He
082
      et al., 2020) and DINO (Caron et al., 2021) show the effectiveness of strong visual representations
083
      on various downstream tasks. Generative pre-training on the other hand, learns to model the data
084   distribution. In language models, generative pre-training has become the de facto standard for training
085   large models. On the vision models, we are still exploring the powers of generative models.
086
087
      Masked Modeling: BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) and MAE (He et al., 2022) follows the BERT (Devlin
      et al., 2018) style masked modeling of images. Compared to BERT, MAE uses asymmetric encoder-
088
      decoders, allowing it to be very efficient at training with high masking ratios. ST-MAE (Feichtenhofer
089
      et al., 2022), VideoMAE (Wang et al., 2023a) apply this masked modeling to videos, by masking a
090   large amount of tokens during pre-training and predict the masked tokens with a light-weight decoder.
091
092   Autoregressive Modeling: For Autoregressive pre-training, PixelCNN (Van den Oord et al., 2016)
093
      and PixelRNN (Van Den Oord et al., 2016) proposed generating pixels one by one using convolution
      and bidirectional LSTMs. With the introduction of the transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), Image-
094
      Transformers (Parmar et al., 2018) showed generating pixels with causal local attention performs
095
      better than previous CNN and RNN-based methods. While all of these methods focused on the
096   generation quality of the pixels, iGPT (Chen et al., 2020a) showed that generative pre-training is also
097   a good way to learn strong visual representations for recognition tasks. AIM (El-Nouby et al., 2024)
098   on the other hand uses patch embedding rather than any pre-trained models for tokenization, however,
099   it trains on Data Filtering Networks (Fang et al., 2023) with clip filtered data. Compared to these
100   works, we do not use any supervision during our pre-training and utilizes image and videos jointly.
101   VisionMamba (Zhu et al., 2024) also showed how to utilize sequence models with bidirectional
102   state-space modeling for supervised vision tasks.
103   Robot Manipulation: Control of action, based on pixel observations gives a good signal on how good
104   the learned representations are at estimating the state of the object being manipulated. MVP (Xiao
105   et al., 2022) showed that manipulation tasks can be learned with better sample efficiency when
106   using pre-trained vision models for encoding pixel observations, and it also generalizes to real-world
107   tasks (Radosavovic et al., 2022). For video based generative pretraining (Wu et al., 2023) showed
      the benefits of video-language pretrained models for learning robot policies.

      3     A PPROACH
109
110
      We train a casual transformer model to predict the next patch-tokens in images and videos. This
111   is equivalent to the next token prediction in large language models. From the vast collection of
112   images and videos, every patch is tokenized into a discrete token, and the transformer is trained to
113   predict the next token, using raster scan ordering. We pre-train our models on over one trillion tokens.
114   Finally, we evaluate the learned representations of these models on various downstream tasks on
115   image classification, action classification, action anticipation, video tracking, object permanence,
116   robotic manipulation tasks and scaling behaviours.

206   pooling (Lin et al., 2013) over t tokens to get the intermediate representation H̃ l = 1t t ht . Then

208
      MAE (He et al., 2022) or BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) have a uniform structure when it comes to which
209
      token attends which tokens, however in language modeling later tokens attend more tokens than
210   the tokens at the beginning. Due to this skewed nature equally weighting all the tokens affects the
211   downstream performance. Attention pooling allows to dynamically weight the tokens, ideally giving
212   more weight to tokens that see more tokens. This requires learning Wk and Wv matrices and a query
213   token q. The query token cross-attends the intermediate tokens and combines them into a single
214   vector. Then we learn a linear layer on top of this representation for downstream tasks. While this
215   whole function is not linear anymore, we argue that for a casual model equally averaging the tokens
      is not fair. This has shown to be effective in recent works as well (El-Nouby et al., 2024).

      4.3   ACTION R ECOGNITION
360
361   We use Kinetics-400 (K400) (Kay et al., 2017) for evaluating our models on action recognition tasks.
362   Similar to ImageNet evaluation, we apply a probe at each layer of the model, with attention pooling,
363   and choose the optimal layer with the highest action classification accuracy. We also fine-tune the
364   pre-trained models on a self-supervised next-patch prediction task while training the probing layers
365   with a classification loss. All our video models are trained with 16 frames, thus with a context length
366   of 4096 tokens per video. When evaluating videos, we follow the protocol in SlowFast (Feichtenhofer
367   et al., 2019). Unlike ImageNet where we evaluate the models at 256x256 resolution, on videos we
368
      only evaluate our models at 128x128 resolution, to keep the number of tokens in a similar budget.
369   Table 8 shows the Kinetics-400 top-1 accuracy of our base, large and 1b models. Similar to
370   ImageNet results in Table 7, we see that discriminately trained models perform better than generative
371   models. Our models achieve comparable performance among generative models, and first to show
372   competitive performance on action recognition with autoregressive generative modeling. All the
373   models are trained and evaluated with 16 frames with a stride of 4 frames.
374   4.4   ACTION F ORECASTING
375

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      We present an approach Toto, for generative pre-training from videos. We build on prior work
532
      on generative pre-training from images and make architectural improvements to enable scaling to
533
      videos, including the use of quantized patch embeddings, relative position information. We collect
534   a large video dataset and conduct a large-scale empirical study across a range of diverse tasks,
535   including image recognition, video classification, object tracking, trajectory prediction, and robotic
536   manipulation. We perform extensive ablation studies to understand different design choices and
537   compare our approach to strong baselines across different tasks. We find that, despite minimal
538   inductive biases, our approach achieves competitive performance across all tasks. Finally, we studied
539   the scaling behaviours of visual next token prediction models, and showed it scales with compute,
      but at a slower rate than text based next token prediction models.
