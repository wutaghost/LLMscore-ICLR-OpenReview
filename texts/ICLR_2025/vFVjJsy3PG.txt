                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Recent advancements in molecular generative models have demonstrated substan-
013
               tial potential in accelerating scientific discovery, particularly in drug design. How-
               ever, these models often face challenges in generating high-quality molecules,
014
               especially in conditional scenarios where specific molecular properties must be
015
               satisfied. In this work, we introduce GeoRCG, a general framework to enhance
016            the performance of molecular generative models by integrating geometric repre-
017            sentation conditions. We decompose the molecule generation process into two
018            stages: first, generating an informative geometric representation; second, gener-
019            ating a molecule conditioned on the representation. Compared to directly gener-
020            ating a molecule, the relatively easy-to-generate representation in the first-stage
021            guides the second-stage generation to reach a high-quality molecule in a more
022            goal-oriented and much faster way. Leveraging EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022)
023            as the base generator, we observe significant quality improvements in uncondi-
024
               tional molecule generation on the widely-used QM9 and GEOM-DRUG datasets.
               More notably, in the challenging conditional molecular generation task, our frame-
025
               work achieves an average 31% performance improvement over state-of-the-art
026
               approaches, highlighting the superiority of conditioning on semantically rich ge-
027            ometric representations over conditioning on individual property values as in pre-
028            vious approaches. Furthermore, we show that, with such representation guidance,
029            the number of diffusion steps can be reduced to as small as 100 while maintaining
030            superior generation quality than that achieved with 1,000 steps, thereby signifi-
031            cantly accelerating the generation process.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent years have seen rapid development in generative modeling techniques for molecule gener-
038   ation (Garcia Satorras et al., 2021; Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Luo & Ji, 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Xu
039
      et al., 2023; Morehead & Cheng, 2024), which have demonstrated great promise in accelerating sci-
      entific discoveries such as drug design (Graves et al., 2020). By modeling molecules as point clouds
040
      of chemical elements embedded in Euclidean space and employing equivariant models as backbone
041
      architectures, such as EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021), these approaches can ensure the O(3)- (or
042   SO(3)-) invariance of the modeled molecule probability and have shown significant promise in both
043   unconditional and conditional molecule generations.
044
045
      Despite the advances, precisely modeling the molecular distribution q(M) still remains a challenge,
      with current models often falling short of satisfactory results. This is especially true in the more
046
      practical scenarios where the goal is to capture the conditional distribution q(M|c) for conditional
047
      generation, with c representing a desired property such as the HOMO-LUMO gap. In such cases,
048   recent models still produce molecules with property errors significantly larger than the data lower
049   bound (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023). Such challenge arises partly because molecules are
050   naturally supported on a lower-dimensional manifold (Mislow, 2012; De Bortoli, 2022; You et al.,
051   2023), yet are embedded in a 3D space with much higher ambient dimensions (N × (3 + d), where
052   N is the number of atoms and d the atom feature dimension). Consequently, directly learning these
053   distributions without additional guidance or conditioning solely on a single property can result in
      substantial errors (Song et al., 2021), often leading to unstable or undesirable molecular samples.

      In this work, we propose GeoRCG (Geometric-Representation-Conditioned Molecule Generation),
071
      a general framework for improving the generation quality of molecular generative models by lever-
072
      aging geometric representation conditions for both unconditional and conditional generation. See
073   Figure 1 for an overview of the framework. At a high level, rather than directly learning the extrinsic
074   molecular distribution, we aim to first transform it into a more compact and semantically meaning-
075   ful representation distribution, with the help of a well-pre-trained geometric encoder E such as
076   Unimol (Zhou et al., 2023) and Frad (Feng et al., 2023). This distribution is much simpler because
077   it does not exhibit any group symmetries, such as O(3)/SO(3) and S(N ) groups which are present
078   in extrinsic molecular distributions. As a result, a lightweight representation generator (Li et al.,
079   2023) can effectively capture this reduced distribution. In the second stage, we employ a standard
080   molecular generator to achieve the ultimate objective: molecular generation. Unlike conventional
081
      approaches, our molecular generator is directly informed by the first-stage geometric representation,
      which encapsulates crucial molecular structure and property information. This guidance enables the
082
      generation of high-quality molecular structures with improved fidelity.
083
084   Our approach is directly inspired by RCG (Li et al., 2023), which, however, focuses on image
085   data with fixed size and positions and does not necessitate handling Euclidean and permutation
086   symmetries—factors that are markedly different in molecular data. Compared to recent work
087
      GraphRCG (Wang et al., 2024), which applies the RCG framework to 2D graph data, we explic-
      itly handle 3D geometry that is more complex due to the additional Euclidean symmetry. Moreover,
088
      we avoid the complicated step-wise bootstrapped training and sampling process proposed in Wang
089
      et al. (2024) that requires noise alignment, sequential training, and simultaneous encoder training.
090   Instead, we employ a simple and intuitive framework that enables parallel training and leverages ad-
091   vanced pre-trained geometric encoders containing valuable external knowledge (Zaidi et al., 2022;
092   Feng et al., 2023), thus achieving competitive results while avoiding complex training procedures.
093   Notably, while Li et al. (2023) primarily focus on empirical evaluation, we also provide theoretical
094   characterizations of general representation-conditioned diffusion models for both unconditional and
095   conditional generation, which offers a rigorous understanding of the improved performance.
096   To illustrate the effectiveness of our approach, we select one of the simplest and most classical
097   equivariant generative models, EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022), as the base molecular generator of
098   GeoRCG. Experimentally, our method achieves the following significant improvements:
099
          • Substantially enhancing the quality (e.g., molecule stability) of the generated molecules on
100
            the widely used QM9 and GEOM-DRUG datasets. On QM9, GeoRCG not only improves
101
            the performance of EDM by a large margin, but also significantly surpasses several recent
102         baselines with state-of-the-art performance (Wu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Morehead &
103         Cheng, 2024; Song et al., 2024a).
104       • More remarkably, in conditional molecule generation tasks, GeoRCG yields an average 31%
105         improvement in performance (i.e., difference of generated molecule’s property with specified
106         conditions), while many contemporary models struggled to achieve even marginal gains.
107       • By incorporating classifier-free guidance in the molecule generator (Li et al., 2023) and
            employing low-temperature sampling for representation generation (Ingraham et al., 2023),

      2      R ELATED W ORKS
117
      Molecular Generative Models Early work has primarily focused on modeling molecules as 2D
118
      graphs (composed of atom types, connection, and edge types), utilizing 2D graph generative models
119
      to learn the graph distribution (Vignac et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2023; Jo et al., 2023; Luo et al.,
120   2023; Zhou et al., 2024). However, since molecules inherently exist in 3D space, where physical
121   laws govern their behavior and geometry provides critical information related to key properties,
122   recent research has increasingly focused on utilizing 3D generative models to directly learn the
123   geometric distribution by modeling molecules as point clouds of chemical elements. Notable early
124   autoregressive models include G-SchNet (Gebauer et al., 2019) and G-SphereNet (Luo & Ji, 2022).
125   More recently, diffusion models have demonstrated effectiveness in this domain, as evidenced by
126   models like EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) and subsequent advancements (Xu et al., 2023; Wu
127   et al., 2022; Morehead & Cheng, 2024) that enhance EDM with latent space, prior information
128
      and more powerful backbones respectively. Furthermore, recent advances in flow methods (Lipman
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b) have inspired the development of geometric, equivariant flow methods
129
      including EquiFM (Song et al., 2024b) and GOAT (Hong et al., 2024), which can provide much
130
      faster molecule generation speed. Beyond these, there are also methods jointly model 2D and 3D
131   information (Vignac et al., 2023; You et al., 2023; Irwin et al., 2024) (also called 3D graph (You
132   et al., 2023)), where a representative method is MiDi (Vignac et al., 2023) which uses a diffusion
133   framework to jointly diffuse and denoise atom type, bond type, formal charges and coordinates.
134
135   Latent Generative Models At a high level, our framework can also be viewed as a latent gener-
136   ative model, where data distributions are learned in a latent space (our stage 1) and decoded back
137   through some decoder (our stage 2). Most prior works in this domain either focus on regular data
138   forms with fixed positions and sizes (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019; Dai & Wipf,
139   2019; Aneja et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023), or on data without Euclidean
140   symmetry and require explicit modeling (Wang et al., 2024). Molecular data, however, presents
141   unique challenges in both aspects. One of the key issues in this context is how to define the latent
142
      space—defining it as “latent coordinates and features” as in GeoLDM (Xu et al., 2023) still results
      in a geometrically structured and thus complex space, while defining it on representations as we do
143
      introduces the challenge of effectively “decoding” a global, non-symmetric embedding back into ge-
144
      ometric objects. LGD (Zhou et al., 2024) trains a diffusion model on a unified Euclidean latent space
145   obtained by jointly training a powerful encoder and a simple decoder, and performs both generation
146   and prediction tasks focusing on 2D graphs. LDM-3DG (You et al., 2023) also adopts representation
147   latent space but employs a cascaded (2D+3D) auto-encoder (AE) framework, where the decoder is
148   designed (or trained) to be deterministic, rendering poor performance on the 3D part as evidenced in
149   our experiments. In contrast, we model the decoder as a powerful generative model, focusing solely
150   on geometric learning while demonstrating superior effectiveness. We leave the related works of
151   pre-trained geometric encoders in Appendix A.

      Geometric Representation Generator To improve the quality of the generated molecules, we
188
      propose first transforming the geometrically structured molecular distribution q(M) into a non-
189
      geometric representation distribution q(r) using a well-pre-trained geometric encoder E that maps
190   each molecule M to its representation r. Learning the representation distribution q(r) is consider-
191   ably easier, since representations do not exhibit any symmetry as in explicit molecular generative
192   models (Xu et al., 2022; Hoogeboom et al., 2022). We thus leverage a simple yet effective MLP-
193   based diffusion architecture proposed in (Li et al., 2023) for the representation generator pφ (r),
194   which adopts the DDIM architecture (Song et al., 2020) with MLP backbones and is optimized via
195   the denoising score matching scheme (Vincent, 2011).
196   One deviation from previous practices (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) is that we additionally con-
197   dition the representation generator on the molecule’s node number N by default1 . This is crucial for
198   ensuring consistency between the size of the representation’s underlying molecule and the size of the
199   molecule it guides to generate. Moreover, molecules with different sizes often have distinct modes
200   in structures and properties (Hoogeboom et al., 2022), which is reflected in their geometric repre-
201   sentations learned by modern pre-trained geometric encoders (Zhou et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023),
202   as shown in Figure 2. From the figures, it is evident that by conditioning on N , the learning process
203   for the representation generator becomes simpler and more effective, leading to the following loss

286   Balancing Quality and Diversity of Molecule Generation In many scientific applications, re-
287   searchers prioritize generating higher-quality molecules over more diverse ones. To address this
288   preference, we introduce a feature that allows fine-grained control over the trade-off between diver-
289   sity and quality in the sampling stage (thus without retraining). This is achieved by integrating two
290   key techniques: low-temperature sampling (Ho & Salimans, 2022) (controlled via the temperature
291   T ) for the representation generator, and classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) (controlled
292
      via the coefficient w) for the molecule generator. We provide more details about the two techniques
      in Appendix B. Combining the two features enables a flexible and explicit control, which we refer
293
      to as “Balancing Controllablility” and demonstrate its effectiveness in Section 4.2.

296   Handling Conditional Molecule Generation The framework discussed thus far focuses on un-
297   conditional molecule generation, where no specific property c (e.g., HOMO energy) is pre-specified.
298
      However, for molecule generation, a more practical and desired scenario is conditional (also called
      controllable) generation, where additional conditions c, such as HOMO-LUMO gap energy, is intro-
299
      duced, and our objective shifts to generating molecules from the distribution  q(M|c). In GeoRCG,
300                                                                           R
      this conditional generation is naturally decomposed as pθ,φ (M|c) = pθ (M|r)pφ (r|c) dr , mean-
301   ing that we first generate a “property-meaningful” molecular representation r, which is then inde-
302   pendently used to condition the second-stage molecule generation. See Figure 3 for illustration. A
303   key advantage of this modeling approach is that, when different properties (e.g., HOMO, LUMO,
304   GAP energy) need to be captured, only the representation generator requires retraining under
305   the new conditions. This retraining is highly efficient due to the lightweight nature of the represen-
306   tation generator. Notably, GeoRCG demonstrates outstanding conditional generation performance,
307   as shown in Section 4.3. Moreover, we theoretically demonstrate that, under mild assumptions, the
308   representation generator can provably estimate the conditional distribution and generate representa-
309
      tions that lead to provable reward improvements toward the target, which subsequently benefits the
      second-stage generation. Further details are provided in Appendix E.2.

524   5      C ONCLUSIONS
525
526
      In this work, we present GeoRCG, an effective framework for improving the generation quality
527
      of arbitrary molecule generators by incorporating geometric representation conditions. We use
528   EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) as the base molecule generator and demonstrate the effectiveness of
529   our framework through extensive molecular generation experiments. Notably, in conditional gener-
530   ation tasks, GeoRCG achieves a 31% performance boost compared to recent state-of-the-art models.
531   Additionally, the representation guidance enables significantly faster sampling with 10x fewer dif-
532   fusion steps while maintaining near-optimal performance. Beyond these empirical improvements,
533   we provide theoretical characterizations of general representation-conditioned generative models,
534   which address a key gap in the existing literature (Li et al., 2023).
535   One limitation of our framework is that its generation quality could depend on the representation
536   quality. For instance, on the GEOM-DRUG dataset, where the encoder was less thoroughly pre-
537   trained, the improvements were less pronounced, and GeoRCG did not surpass SOTA methods.
538   Future work could focus on improving the effectiveness of pre-trained models or exploring enhanced
539   representation regularization techniques. Furthermore, while we employ EDM (Hoogeboom et al.,
      2022) as the base molecule generator, our framework is general and can be applied to any molecular

758   Pre-training for Molecular Encoders Learning meaningful molecular representations is crucial
759   for downstream tasks like molecular property prediction (Fang et al., 2022). The paradigm of pre-
760   training on large-scale datasets followed by fine-tuning on smaller datasets has been shown to sig-
761   nificantly enhance model performance in both vision and language domains (Kenton & Toutanova,
762   2019; Brown, 2020; Dosovitskiy, 2020), and building on this success, recent studies have proposed
763   self-supervised pre-training methods for point-cloud-formatted molecules, aiming to achieve similar
764   performance gains (Zhou et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a; Fang et al., 2022; Jiao
765   et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024). Typical proxy tasks involve masking and recovering atom types, bond
766
      lengths, and bond angles (Fang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). However, since molecules reside
      in continuous 3D space, a more effective approach is to introduce carefully designed noise to the
767
      coordinates and train the model to denoise it. Examples include isotropic Gaussian noise (Zaidi
768
      et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), Riemann-Gaussian noise (Jiao et al., 2023), and complex hybrid
769   noise (Ni et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2024). Notably, Zaidi et al. (2022) demonstrated
770   that denoising equilibrium structures corresponds to learning the force field, thereby producing rep-
771   resentations that are both physically and chemically informative.

      Model Architectures, Hyperparameters and Training Details
919
920         • Representation Generator. We use the same architecture for the representation generator
921           as the MLP-based diffusion model proposed in Li et al. (2023). We use 18 blocks of
922           residual MLP layers with 1536 hidden dimensions, 1000 diffusion steps, and a linear noise
923           schedule for βt . The representation generator is trained for 2000 epochs with a batch size
924           of 128 for both the QM9 and DRUG datasets. Training on QM9 takes approximately 2.5
925           days on a single Nvidia 4090, while training on DRUG takes around 4 days on a single
926
              Nvidia A800. Training time can indeed be further reduced, as the model shows minimal
              progress after approximately half of the reported time.
927
928         • Molecule Generator. We adopt EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) as the base molecule gen-
929           erator, using the same EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021) architecture, with the exception of the
930
              conditioning module. Specifically, we incorporate a cross-attention (Vaswani, 2017) mod-
              ule for better conditioning on representations, placing it between every block of the EGNN
931
              to enhance regularization and increase expressiveness. For the EGNN hyperparameters, we
932
              use 9 layers with 256 hidden dimensions for QM9 and 4 layers with 256 hidden dimensions
933           for DRUG. The number of diffusion steps is set to 1000 (except for cases in Table 4 that
934                                                                                                  (M)
              generate molecules with fewer steps), and we employ the polynomial scheduler for αt .
935
              Notably, all model hyperparameters are identical to those in EDM for fair comparison.
936           During training, we use a batch size of 128 and 3000 epochs on QM9, and a batch size of
937           64 and 20 epochs on DRUG. The representation perturbation values are set to σrep = 0.3
938           on QM9 and σrep = 0.5 on DRUG. Training takes approximately 6 days on QM9 using a
939           single Nvidia 4090, and around 10 days on DRUG using two Nvidia A800 GPUs.
940         • Sampling Details. During representation sampling, we use the predictor-corrector method
941           from Song et al. (2021) for VP SDE to achieve better equilibrium distributions, which
942           facilitates low-temperature sampling. For molecule sampling, the process remains the same
943           as in EDM, with the addition of classifier-free guidance. For the unconditional generation
944           results in Table 1, we use (w, T ) = (1.0, 1.0) in the QM9 dataset, except for the results
945           in Figure 4, and (w, T ) = (0.0, 0.5) in the GEOM-DRUG dataset (note that w = 0.0
946           indicates that we do not use classifier-free guidance, not that the representation condition
947
              itself is not applied). Further tuning of these two hyperparameters may lead to improved

953         • For the unconditional results in Table 1, we utilize the 3D conformations unconditionally
954           generated by LDM-3DG (You et al., 2023) and compute the bond information using the
955           look-up table method from EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022). Notably, although LDM-3DG
956           predicts both the 2D molecular graph and the 3D conformation, we do not use the bond
957           information it predicts for the following reasons:
958             1. For the calculation of 3D geometry statistics, we observe significant inconsistencies
959                between the generated 2D graphs and 3D geometries (e.g., valid molecules with bond
960                lengths exceeding 100 m), leading to unreliable statistics (e.g., BondLengthW1 ex-
961                ploding to 3900).
962             2. For stability and validity metrics, which are fundamentally 2D and computed based
963                on molecular graphs (atoms and bond types), using the generated 2D graph would
964                ignore the contribution of the 3D module, preventing an evaluation of its 3D learning
965                performance.
966             3. Most critically, their 2D module is explicitly designed to filter out invalid (sub-
967                )molecules during generation using the RDKit method. This means that if invalid
968                molecules or sub-molecules are generated, they are regenerated. This explicit filtering
969
                   deviates from our standard evaluation criteria and is unsuitable for a fair comparison.
970         • For the conditional results in Table 3, we first note a potential issue with LDM-3DG (You
971           et al., 2023): The model cannot explicitly specify the node number N during molecule
              generation, as it uses an auto-regressive 2D generator that automatically stops adding

973             ing both N and property c, following the ground-truth distribution q(N, c) from the training

975             distribution. As the authors claim the model can implicitly learn q(N ) and thus q(N |c), we

       In this section, we provide rigorous theoretical analysis on representation-conditioned diffusion
1068   models. Our theory is not limited to molecule generation, and is the first theoretical breakthrough
1069   for the RCG framework (Li et al., 2023).
1070
1071
       Our analysis is organized as follows. In Appendix E.1, we analyze the generation bound of
       representation-conditioned diffusion models in unconditional generation tasks by showing: (i) the
1072
       representation can be well generated by the first-stage diffusion model with mild assumptions (Ap-
1073
       pendix E.1.1); (ii) the second-stage representation-conditioned diffusion model exhibits no higher
1074   generalization error than traditional one-stage diffusion model, and can arguably achieve lower er-
1075   ror leveraging the informative representations (Appendix E.1.2). Then in Appendix E.2, we analyze
1076   conditional generation tasks as follows: (iii) under mild assumptions of representations and targets,
1077   we provide novel bound for score estimation error (Appendix E.2.1); (iv) generated representations
1078   have provable reward improvement towards the target, with the suboptimality composed of offline
1079   regression error and diffusion distribution shift (Appendix E.2.2), thus would improve the second
       stage of conditional generation (Appendix E.2.3).

       To conclude this subsection, we provide a detailed characterization of the generalization error of
1301
       representation-conditioned diffusion models. It is important to note that some parameters in our
1302
       assumptions, such as Lipschitz scores and estimation errors, are not constants; they are functions
1303   of the SDE total time T and the number of diffusion steps N . Our conclusions also explain
1304   why representation-conditioned generation methods remain competitive even when the number of
1305   second-stage diffusion steps N is decreased for faster generation. This is because the score estima-
1306   tion error can remain small even when the number of diffusion steps is reduced, given the guidance
1307   from representations. As a result, reducing N causes a slower increase in ϵφ,θ,cond (N ) compared to
1308   the score estimation error without representation conditioning, leading to representation-conditioned

       E.2.3    S ECOND S TAGE OF C ONDITIONAL G ENERATION
1486
1487   Now that we have proved that: (i) the first-stage diffusion model can estimate the score function
1488   with provable error bound (Appendix E.2.1); and (ii) the generated representations have provable
1489   reward improvement (Appendix E.2.2). We continue to show that the ultimate generated samples
1490   also have distribution shift towards the desired target in the following contexts. Particularly, we want
1491
       to answer the question: why utilizing the conditionally generated representations is enough for the
       second stage generation?
1492
1493   First, when we use the generated representations as the only condition of the second stage diffusion
1494   model, the generation process is identical to the second stage of unconditional generation. There-
1495   fore, the results in Appendix E.1.2 can be directly applied to analyze the second stage generation of
1496   conditional generation, which states that the generation conditioning on representations has small
1497
       TV distance error compared with ground truth conditional distribution. Thus, when we have high-
       quality first-stage generation, the corresponding second stage generation would introduce almost no
1498
       additional error, which implies provable reward improvements towards the desired target. In addi-
1499
       tion, the well-pretrained encode ensures that the correspondence between representations and data
1500   points is good, which makes it possible to rigorously construct the data points given the representa-
1501   tions (a special case would be r is the complete representation of x).
1502
1503
       We then partially answer this question from the information theoretic perspectives. We use H(·) to
       denote the information entropy, and I(·; ·) to denote the mutual information between two variables.
1504
1505
               • I(x; r) ≥ I(x; y). On the one hand, r contains enough information to recover the targets
1506             y thanks to the results in Appendix E.2.2, thus we do not explicitly need y for the second
1507             stage. One the other hand, benefit from the pretraining task, the representations obviously
1508             contains more information in addition to y. This assumption is valid if w∗ in previous anal-
1509             ysis is sparse (there are components in r independent of y), i.e., H(r) > H(y). Therefore,
1510             generating x conditioning on r is much easier than generating conditioning on y (traditional
1511             one stage generation), as the score estimation error of the former one would obviously be
                 much smaller than the latter.


                                                          28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
              • I(x, r; y) ≥ I(x; y). Recall Equation (44) which states the target property y depends on
1513            both x and r. Hence, r may contain additional information of y obtained from pretrained
1514            tasks that is hard to (or cannot) be directly extracted from x - the complex pretrained model
1515            assists in extracting relevant information in our two-stage generation, while one-stage gen-
1516            eration solely relies on the single denoising model to do so. Therefore, by leveraging
1517            representations with provable error bounds, we can better shift the distribution towards the
1518            target.
1519
       In summary, r is an ideal middle state connecting x and y - it is easy to recover r from y (Ap-
1520
       pendix E.2.1) and to recover x from y (Appendix E.1.2), and vice versa. In comparison, it is some-
1521
       what more difficult to directly recover x from y or predict y from x. Consequently, r may be a better
1522   indicator of y compared with x due to the aforementioned reasons.
1523
1524   Indeed, one-stage diffusion models generate x directly from conditions y need to optimize a highly
1525
       complex score ∇x log p(x|y) where x and y are highly non-linearly mapped. As Theorem E.4 in
       (Yuan et al., 2023) points out, the nonparametric SubOpt of x generated by deep neural networks is
1526
       larger than our results in Appendix E.2.2, which further validates the advantage of first generating r
1527
       that can be well mapped from y.
