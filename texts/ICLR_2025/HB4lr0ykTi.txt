009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative modeling typically concerns the transport of a single source distribu-
012            tion to a single target distribution by learning (i.e., regressing onto) simple proba-
013            bility flows. However, in modern data-driven fields such as computer graphics and
014            single-cell genomics, samples (say, point-clouds) from datasets can themselves be
015            viewed as distributions (as, say, discrete measures). In these settings, the stan-
016            dard generative modeling paradigm of flow matching would ignore the relevant
017            geometry of the samples. To remedy this, we propose Wasserstein flow matching
018
               (WFM), which appropriately lifts flow matching onto families of distributions by
               appealing to the Riemannian nature of the Wasserstein geometry. Our algorithm
019
               leverages theoretical and computational advances in (entropic) optimal transport,
020
               as well as the attention mechanism in our neural network architecture. We present
021            two novel algorithmic contributions. First, we demonstrate how to perform gen-
022            erative modeling over Gaussian distributions, where we generate representations
023            of granular cell states from single-cell genomics data. Secondly, we show that
024            WFM can learn flows between high-dimensional and variable sized point-clouds
025            and synthesize cellular microenvironments from spatial transcriptomics datasets.
026            Code is available at WassersteinFlowMatching.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Today’s abundance of data and scalability of training massive neural networks has made it possible
032   to generate hyper-realistic images on the basis of training examples (OpenAI, 2022), as well as video
033
      and audio clips (Vyas et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2023), and, of course, text (Bubeck et al., 2023). All of
      these are instances of generative modeling: given access to finitely many samples from a distribution,
034
      devise a scheme which generates new samples from the same distribution. Generative modeling
035
      has also been revolutionary in the biomedical sciences, for drug design (Jumper et al., 2021) and
036   single-cell genomics (Lopez et al., 2018). Nearly all frameworks exploit the notion that datasets (of,
037   say, genomic profiles of cells, images, videos, or corpora of text documents) are instantiations of
038   probability measures, and the task is to transform a point sampled from random noise to generate a
039   data point that obeys the distribution of interest.
040
      Among the zoo of available generative models, one approach noted for its flexibility and simplicity
041
      is Flow Matching (FM) (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022).
042   For a fixed target probability measure, FM learns an implicitly defined vector field that can transform
043   a source measure (e.g., the standard Gaussian) to the target. Unlike discrete time and probabilistic
044   generative models (such as Denoising Diffusion Models by Song et al. (2020)), FM learns a deter-
045   ministic, continuous normalizing flow by regressing onto a simple conditional probability flow. This
046   approach, while originally designed for Euclidean domains, can be readily adopted to Riemannian
047   geometries (Chen & Lipman, 2023). Riemannian flow matching is widely used for generating sam-
048   ples over geometries such as spheres, tori, translation/rotation groups, simplices, triangular meshes,
049   mazes, and molecular positions and structures.
050   The Wasserstein geometry, a canonical geometry over distributions, does not easily fit into any of
051   these existing frameworks and has not been successfully adapted for flow matching. This geometry
052   is useful, for example, in computational graphics where collections of 3D shapes are represented
053   as empirical distributions (point-clouds). Likewise, recent developments in single-cell genomics
      analysis have demonstrated that gene-expression profiles from groups of cells aggregated via their

070
      Contributions. We introduce Wasserstein Flow Matching (WFM), a principled extension of the
071
      FM framework lifted to the space of probability distributions. As illustrated in Figure 1, a single
072
      point in our source and target datasets is itself a distribution (e.g., a single discrete measure or a
073   single Gaussian), and our aim is to learn vector fields acting on the space of probability distributions
074   and match the optimal transport map, which is the geodesic in Wasserstein space. WFM is an
075   instantiation of Riemannian FM (Chen & Lipman, 2023), where we train a neural model to learn
076   a continuous normalizing flow (CNF) between distributions over distributions.
077
      We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach for generative modeling between distributions
078
      over Gaussian distributions and distributions over point-clouds. The former task is motivated by
079   recent directions in single-cell and spatial transcriptomics (Haviv et al., 2024b; Persad et al., 2023),
080   where we consider matching problems over the Bures–Wasserstein space (BW), the Gaussian sub-
081   manifold of the Wasserstein space. In this case, we show that WFM can be further modified, re-
082   sulting in the Bures–Wasserstein FM (BW-FM) algorithm. We validate BW-FM on a variety of
083   Gaussian-based datasets, where we observe that samples generated by our algorithm are signifi-
084   cantly more robust than naı̈ve approaches which do not fully exploit the underlying geometry of the
085   data. In turn, we present a generative model for cell states and niches from single-cell genomics data.
086   Point-cloud generation is made possible by two distinct, yet crucial, algorithmic primitives: (1) in-
087   corporating transformers in our neural network architecture (Vaswani, 2017; Lee et al., 2019), and
088   (2) recent algorithmic advances in entropic optimal transport (Pooladian & Niles-Weed, 2021). In-
089   deed, our WFM algorithm performs generative modeling in the Wasserstein space, where geodesics
090   are given by pushforwards of optimal transport (OT) maps; see Section 2.3 for more information.
091   Both the transformer architecture and entropic optimal transport are crucial to approximating the OT
092   map between independent point-clouds. Indeed, the permutation equivariance of attention makes
093
      the transformer a natural basis for our model, inherently modeling the equivariance feature of the
      Wasserstein geometry while maintaining scalability in high-dimensions.
094
095   For datasets of 3D point-clouds with uniform sizes, the performance of WFM is comparable to other
096   current generative models. However, due to their particular training paradigms (namely the voxeliza-
097   tion of 3D spaces), contemporary approaches cannot scale to high-dimensional point-clouds and fail
098   on datasets with variable sized examples. Conversely, WFM succeeds in the high-dimensional and
099
      inhomogeneous settings, unlocking generative modeling to new, previously uncharted domains such
      as synthesizing niches from spatial genomics data. The ability to model tissue biology in this gener-
100
      ative manner could enhance our understanding of how environment is associated with cell state. In
101
      the context of many diseases, most notably cancer and its tumor-immune microenvironment, these
102   insights are critical for developing novel therapeutics (Binnewies et al., 2018).

120   Let p0 be the source distribution and p1 be the target distribution over a Riemannian manifold M,

123   equation with respect to the metric g, abbreviated to (γt , wt ) ∈ Cg if
124                                              ∂t γt + ∇g ·(γt wt ) = 0 ,                                   (1)
125
      where ∇g · is the Riemannian divergence operator.

145   2.2   R ELATED WORK
146
147   Generative models for point-clouds. Paralleling the progress in generative models for natural
148   images, the field of point-cloud generation is rapidly expanding. Many different models have been
149   used from this task, namely generative-adversarial-nets (Achlioptas et al., 2018), variational autoen-
150   coders (Gadelha et al., 2018), normalizing flows (Yang et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020; Klokov et al.,
151
      2020), diffusion (Zhou et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2020) and even euclidean FM (Wu et al., 2023).
      Thus far, these approaches are limited to uniformly sized point-clouds in 2D & 3D, and fail on
152
      high-dimensional spaces which cannot be voxelized.
153
154
      Generative models over families of distributions. Our work is not the first to instantiate Rieman-
155
      nian FM with a manifold of probability measures. Two notable works are Fisher FM (Davis et al.,
156   2024) and Categorical FM (Cheng et al., 2024), which consider the FM algorithm with respect to
157   the Fisher–Rao geometry Amari (2016); Nielsen (2020) over the d-dimensional simplex ∆d . The
158   work of Stark et al. (2024) is similar in spirit, where they focus on the Dirichlet distribution for gen-
159   eration of discrete data. Another related work is that of Atanackovic et al. (2024), called Meta FM.
160   Their approach requires pairs of distributions which are already coupled, with the goal of solving
161   FM between a distribution over pairs. In contrast, we emphasize that our proposed Wasserstein FM
      applies between two separate uncoupled distributions over distributions.

      Generative models for single-cell genomics. Deep learning based generative models have trans-
163   formed single-cell genomics through various approaches. Variational auto-encoders (Lopez et al.,
164   2018; Gayoso et al., 2022) have successfully addressed technical artifacts, integrating multi-moodal
165   data and imputing missing features in scRNA-seq data. More recently, Transformer-based foun-

      relevant for spatial genomics, where cellular microenvironments are naturally represented as point-

172
      modeling for the single-cell field.

      4.1     F LOW MATCHING BETWEEN FAMILIES OF G AUSSIANS
390
391   We first demonstrate our flow matching framework between measures of Gaussian distributions on
392   a synthetic and real datasets. For comparable baselines in each scenario, we construct two simpler
393   flow matching approaches for Gaussian generation: (1) Frobenius FM, which concatenates the mean
394   and covariances values, and trains on Σ̇E
                                              t with respect to the squared-Frobenius norm, and (2) BW-
395   FM (Euclidean), which tries to match Σ̇Et but still under the BW geometry.
396
397   In all cases, we assess the quality of the learned flows by computing the the minimum distance be-
398
      tween each generated Gaussian and the dataset using the (squared) 2-Wasserstein distance. Notably,
      the flows generated by Frobenius FM and BW-FM (Euclidean) do not strictly adhere to the geom-
399
      etry of the Bures-Wasserstein manifold, requiring synthesized covariance matrices to be artificially
400
      projected onto the space of positive semi-definite (PSD) matrices via eigenvalue truncation. In con-
401   trast, the Riemannian BW-FM algorithm consistently produces valid and accurate results across all
402   dimensions and datasets.

452   When the two measures are point-clouds, we turn to entropic optimal transport to estimate the Mc-
453   Cann interpolation (Cuturi, 2013). When the dataset consists of source and target point-clouds of the
454   same size, we use a GPU-efficient rounding scheme to approximate the OT map using the entropic
455   OT coupling; see Appendix A. When the support size of the source and target point-clouds vary, it
456   is worth mentioning that an OT map may not even exist. Nevertheless, we approximate these curves
457   using the entropic transport map of Pooladian & Niles-Weed (2021). Together, these approaches
458   offer a computationally feasible solution while maintaining accuracy in transport map estimation.
459   We compare WFM to many other point-cloud generation algorithms. Following in their footsteps,
460   we measure generation quality based on the 1-Nearest-Neighbour accuracy metric between gen-
461   erated and test-set point-clouds. On uniform, 3D datasets, WFM is competitive with current ap-
462   proaches (Table 3), but exemplifies itself with its unique ability to generate point-clouds with varying
463   sizes and in high-dimensions (Table 2).

      5   C ONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK
531
532
      This work shows how to appropriately lift the Riemannian flow matching paradigm of Chen & Lip-
533
      man (2023) to the Wasserstein space, resulting in Wasserstein flow matching. Our motivations stem
534   from modern datasets, where each sample of data can itself be viewed as a probability distribution,
535   necessitating this extension for generative modeling purposes. Our contributions are algorithmic in
536   nature, which incorporate various elements, such as estimating optimal transport maps via entropic
537   optimal transport, closed-form expressions over the Bures–Wasserstein space, and attention mech-
538   anisms in neural network architectures. Our algorithm is capable of generating realistic data from
539   Gaussian and variable-size or high-dimensional point-clouds. Both contexts are highly relevant in
      single-cell and spatial transcriptomics for synthesizing of microenvironments and cellular states.

       E.2   WFM ON POINT- CLOUDS
1061
1062   WFM is designed to estimate the optimal transport (OT) map for a given pair of interpolate
1063   point-cloud and time (Xt , t). Here too the time component t is first converted into Fourier features.
1064   The model’s architecture begins with an embedding layer, followed by a series of alternating
1065   multi-head attention and fully-connected layers. Skip connections and layer-norm are applied after
1066   each operation. The final layer projects the embeddings back to X’s original space using a dense
1067   layer with zero initialization. The model is trained by minimizing the squared distance between the
1068   predicted and true OT maps.
1069   By default, the entropic OT map is constructed with regularization weight of ε = 0.002 and 200
1070   Sinkhorn iterations, which we found to be sufficient for convergence. Whenever the dataset consists
1071   of uniformly sized point-clouds, we use rounded matching (Appendix A.1), otherwise we apply
1072   the out-of-sample estimator (Appendix A.2) which can calculate maps between point-clouds with
1073   different sizes. The transformer network is composed to 6 multi-head attention blocks, with an
1074   embedding dimension of 512 and 4 heads. Our model is optimizer with Adam (Kingma, 2014)
1075   using an exponential learning rate decay and batch size of 64.
1076   WFM relies on JAX and OTT-JAX (Bradbury et al., 2021; Cuturi et al., 2022) and enjoys seam-
1077   less optimization via end-to-end just-in-time compilation. For the ShapeNet experiments (Table 3),

       The ShapeNet dataset consists of 3D point-clouds of 55 different classes, each one comprised of
1157   15, 000 points. Emulating the benchmarking effort in (Wu et al., 2023), we apply WFM to generate
1158   n = 1000 sized examples from the plane, car and chair classes. At each gradient descent step, we
1159   sample 64 point-clouds from the training set for each class, and randomly select n = 1000 points
1160   from each. To evaluate generation quality, we synthesize point-clouds to much the size of the test
1161   set, and calculate the real or generated 1 − N N accuracy based on EMD and CD metrics.
1162
       ModelNet has 40 classes of point-clouds, with 2048 points in each. Conditioned on class label,
1163   WFM is trained to generate n = 1000 sized point-clouds here too. In this setting, the noise measure
1164   is the standard normal and we did not use multi-sample matching. According to nearest-neighbour
1165   classification from OT preserving Wormhole embeddings, generated samples match their class with
1166   an accuracy of 77.66%, approaching the 79.98% purity of test set samples from real-data.
