011                                            Abstract
012
013           Quantizing the weights of a neural network has two steps: (1) Finding a
014           good low bit-complexity representation for weights (which we call the quan-
015           tization grid) and (2) Rounding the original weights to values in the quan-
016           tization grid. In this paper, we study the problem of rounding optimally
017
              given any quantization grid. The simplest and most commonly used way to
              round is Round-to-Nearest (RTN). By rounding in a data-dependent way
018
              instead, one can improve the quality of the quantized model significantly.
019
020
              We study the rounding problem from the lens of discrepancy theory, which
              studies how well we can round a continuous solution to a discrete solu-
021
              tion without affecting solution quality too much. We prove that given
022
              m = poly(1/ε) samples from the data distribution, we can round all but
023           O(m) model weights such that the expected approximation error of the
024           quantized model on the true data distribution is ≤ ε as long as the space
025           of gradients of the original model is approximately low rank (which we
026           empirically validate).
027           Our proof, which is algorithmic, inspired a simple and practical round-
028           ing algorithm called DiscQuant. In our experiments, we demonstrate that
029           DiscQuant significantly improves over the prior state-of-the-art rounding
030           method called GPTQ and the baseline RTN over a range of benchmarks
031           on Phi3mini-3.8B and Llama3.1-8B. For example, rounding Phi3mini-3.8B
032           to a fixed quantization grid with 3.25 bits per parameter using DiscQuant
033
              gets 64% accuracy on the GSM8k dataset, whereas GPTQ achieves 54%
              and RTN achieves 31% (the original model achieves 84%).

      1   Introduction
038
039
      Modern deep learning models continue to grow in size, incurring greater challenges to train
      and serve these models. Post training compression methods have emerged which aim to
040
      make model inference faster and cheaper. Compressing after pretraining is desirable among
041
      practitioners who either cannot afford to train models themselves, or do not want to change
042   the expensive training process too much. In this paper, we study post training quantization
043   (PTQ) of the model weights. Quantization reduces the memory requirements of the model,
044   and speeds up inference for LLMs under memory-bound settings such as the generation
045   phase (as opposed to prefilling phase which is compute-bound) (Kwon et al., 2023).
046
      The quantization problem can be divided into two overall steps: (1) Construct a good low
047
      bit-complexity representation for the weights (we colloquially call this the quantization grid),
048   and (2) Round the original weights to values in the quantization grid. Within step (1), we
049   also consider those methods which apply a transformation on the weights to better match
050   the encoding format. There has been much recent work on weights-only PTQ for LLMs. To
051   date, the vast majority of such research has been focused on step (1): constructing good low
052   bit representations (Shao et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2024a; Egiazarian et al., 2024). However,
053   work on rounding methods is under-explored. To the best of our knowledge, Round-to-
      Nearest (RTN) and GPTQ (Hassibi et al., 1993; Frantar et al., 2022; 2023) are the primary

054
      rounding methods for LLM weight quantization. RTN is a simple baseline, and GPTQ is a
055   data dependent method which aims to match the activations of the quantized model with
056   that of the original model layer-by-layer.

058
      Let f (w; s) be the loss function of a neural network where w are original pretrained weights
      and s is an input sample; for example f can be the usual cross-entropy loss on input s.
059
      To find a good rounding solution, we are looking for perturbations of the original weights
060
      w ∈ Rn that correspond to values in the quantization grid, and do not increase the loss
061   f too much. We further impose the constraint that we only round each parameter up or
062   down, this ensures that we are not changing the original model weights too much. Then the
063   set of allowed quantization points can be pictured as vertices of a hypercube H around w.
064   Let ŵ ∈ Rn be these perturbed weights, and ∆f = f (ŵ; s) − f (w; s) be the resulting change
065   in loss function for a sample s. We approximate ∆f via a first order Taylor expansion:
066   ∆f ≈ ⟨∇w f (w; s), ŵ − w⟩. Some prior works such as Nagel et al. (2020); Hassibi et al.
067   (1993) assume the gradients of a pretrained model to be nearly zero, and focus on the
068   second order terms. We show that this assumption is not always true, the average gradients
069
      are close to zero but per-sample gradients can be big; in fact the first order term is a good
      approximation to ∆f (see Figure 3).

146   We summarize our main contributions:
147
148   • Theoretical developments: We prove that it is possible to achieve generalization error
149     ≤ ε on the true data distribution by rounding all but poly(log n/ε) weights, so long as
150     the gradients of the original model are approximately low rank.
151   • Practical algorithm: We develop a simple and practical algorithm DiscQuant guided by
152     our theoretical analysis. We perform extensive experiments on Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct and
153     Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, over block scaling and incoherence processing quantization
154     formats, and a variety of evaluation tasks. Our method DiscQuant achieves superior or
155
        comparable quantization to the baselines GPTQ and RTN as can be seen from Figure 2.

157   2               Related Work
158
159   In this paper we focus on weights-only PTQ. Quantization can also be applied to the activa-
160   tions or KV-cache (Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b). Other compression method
161   such as pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023) are also outside the scope of
      this work. As discussed in the introduction, post training quantization can be divided into

                                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      two overall steps: (1) Construct a good low bit-complexity representations for the weights
163   (the quantization grid), and (2) Round the original weights to the values in the quantization
164   grid. To this date, the vast majority of PTQ research for LLMs has focused on step (1).
165   Note that determining a good compressed representation can involve both encoding formats,
166   as well as transformations to ensure the weights better match the encoding format.
167
168   2.1   Quantization Grids
169
170   One of the more common quantization formats is called block scaling, or group-wise quan-
171   tization (Frantar et al., 2023). In addition to the bits parameter determining the number
172   of representable points, each groupsize parameters share a unique scaling parameter. An-
173   other successful encoding is to identify a small set of important weights and keep them in
174   high precision (Dettmers et al., 2022; 2024; Kim et al., 2024). Shao et al. (2024) learns
175
      quantization parameters. Other works apply transformations to make quantization easier,
      either relatively simple invariant scalings (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024), or more com-
176
      plicated random orthogonal transformations (Chee et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Beyond
177
      block scaling, there has been work quantizing multiple parameters together using vector
178   quantization (Tseng et al., 2024a; Egiazarian et al., 2024; van Baalen et al., 2024) or trellis
179   quantization (Tseng et al., 2024b).
180
181
      2.2   Rounding
182
183   To the best of our knowledge, GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) is the main rounding method for
184   LLMs. It is based on the Optimal Brain Surgeon (Hassibi et al., 1993), which was adapted
185   for pruning and quantization in Frantar et al. (2022) and then refined for quantization in
186   GPTQ. GPTQ works by minimizing a layer-wise objective ∥W X − Ŵ X∥22 , where W is
187   the weight matrix of a linear layer and X is the matrix of input activations to that layer
188   (stacked as columns). Two other LLM rounding methods both use coordinate descent: Nair
189   & Suggala (2024) only has results on the closed source PaLM-2 models with no released code,
190   and Behdin et al. (2023) has results on the OPT, BLOOM, and Falcon model families.
191   There was more work on rounding methods several years ago, before the LLM boom.
192   These papers were typically on smaller vision models. The line of work was started by
193   AdaRound (Nagel et al., 2020) and continuing to AdaQuant (Hubara et al., 2021) and
194   BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) employ a similar approach to ours, optimizing essentially inter-
195   polation variables between the closest up(wup ) and down(wdown ) quantization grid points,
196   while adding a concave regularization term to encourage rounding and using a rectified sig-
197   moid to interpolate between wup and wdown . They also do rounding layer by layer. However
198
      our method uses a linear term as a regularizer inspired from our theoretical insights using
      discrepancy theory and uses simple linear interpolation between wup and wdown and we
199
      round the entire model at once.
200

202   2.3   Discrepancy Theory
203
      Discrepancy theory is a deep branch of mathematics and theoretical computer science, and
204
      we refer the readers to standard textbooks for more details (Matousek, 2009; Chazelle et al.,
205   2004; Bansal, 2022) To our knowledge, only Lybrand & Saab (2021) makes the connection
206   between discrepancy theory and quantization. However, besides the high level motivational
207   similarities, their work is not directly relevant to ours. Lybrand & Saab (2021) reduce the
208   problem of understanding the error introduced by quantization on the output of a single
209   neuron to a problem in discrepancy, and construct an algorithm for quantizing a single
210   neuron. Their theoretical analysis on the generalization error only applies to quantizing the
211   first layer of a neural network. On the other hand, we use discrepancy theory to understand
212   when the whole network f (w; s) can be approximated by f (ŵ; s) with ŵ in the quantization
213
      grid, and our theory holds for any network as a whole as long as our assumptions are true.

301   almost integral x̂—a vertex of K—is good enough if we apply RTN to the few remaining
302   fractional parameters; we observe that the linear constraints are all approximately satisfied.

      4     DiscQuant: Algorithm
348
349
      In this section, we will present DiscQuant, a simple and practical algorithm for rounding
350   inspired by the theoretical insights in Section 3. Instead of trying to approximate the loss
351   function of the pre-trained model, i.e., f (ŵ; s) ≈ f (w; s), we will instead take a distillation
352   approach and try to minimize the KL divergence between the next token distribution of the
353   original model and the quantized model. Let pw (·|z<i ) be the distribution of the next token
354   predicted by the original model given prefix z<i where z ∼ Ddata is a sample from the data
355   distribution. We want error(ŵ) = Ez∼Ddata Ei DKL (pw (·|z<i ) ∥ pŵ (·|z<i )) ≈ 0.
356   Expanding error(ŵ) using Taylor series, we can see that first order term vanishes exactly
357   and so the second order term is the dominant term (see Appendix D). By Lemma D.1,
358   Hessian of error(ŵ) can be written as a covariance of gradients as:

395   5     Experiments
396
397   We evaluate our method on the Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct (Abdin et al., 2024) and Meta-
398   Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) models, and compare against GPTQ and greedy
399   rounding (i.e. round-to-nearest, or RTN). We use the lm-evaluation-harness Gao et al. (2023)
400
      to evaluate on the Wikitext, GSM8k cot 8-shot, MMLU 5-shot, ARC Challenge 0-shot,
      PIQA 0-shot, HellaSwag 0-shot, and Winogrande 0-shot tasks. We report standard errors
401
      from lm-evaluation-harness. Wikitext measures perplexity, GSM8k is a generative task,
402
      and the remaining are multiple choice tasks. Note that generative tasks are typically more
403   difficult than multiple choice tasks, and better reflect how the models are used in practice.
404   See Appendix A for details on the hardware used, and hyper-parameter settings. Our
405   method has similar memory requires as knowledge distillation, which also requires two copies
406   of the model. We do not perform inference timing experiments; DiscQuant can optimize over
407   a given quantization grid, so that we can utilize any pre-existing inference optimizations. For
408   example, there are inference kernels for block scaling (Frantar et al., 2024) and incoherence
409   processing (Tseng et al., 2024a). Ablations on the loss formulation are in Appendix A.
410
411   5.1   Block Scaling
412
      Our first experiments use standard block scaling quantization, determined by a bits and
413
      groupsize parameter. There are 2bits unique points, and every groupsize parameters
414   share a unique 16-bit scale parameter. For example, 3.25 bits is achieved with bits=3,
415   groupsize=64. We use the block scaling implementation from Frantar et al. (2024) which is
416   symmetric linear quantization. Table 2 shows the results quantizing Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct.
417   Across all tasks and all bit settings, our method DiscQuant achieves superior or comparable
418   compression over the baseline GPTQ and RTN methods. The gap between DiscQuant and
419   the baselines is greater at lower bits. On the ARC Challenge, PIQA, and WinoGrade tasks,
420   DiscQuant achieves full recovery with at least 0.25 fewer bits per parameter than GPTQ and
421   RTN. For example on ARC Challenge, DiscQuant achieves full recovery at 4 bits per weight,
422
      whereas GPTQ requires 4.25 bits, and RTN 4.5 bits. DiscQuant achieves better compression
      on the more difficult generative GSM8k task: at 4 bits DiscQuant gets 77.3% accuracy,
423
      while GPTQ gets 71.5%, and RTN gets 62.2%. Table 3 shows the results quantizing Meta-
424
      Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. Overall the story is the same. Our method DiscQuant achieves
425   improved compression on the majority of quantization levels and tasks. For example at 4
426   bits, DiscQuant gets 66.5% GSM8k accuracy, while GPTQ gets 63.2%, and RTN gets 50.8%.

732
733   The experiments for the Phi-3-mini model were conducted on either a single 80GB Nvidia
734
      A100 GPU, or 2x40GB A100 GPUs, while the Llama-3.1-8B model used either 2x80GB

735
      uniformly at random, and used AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a cosine
736
      learning rate schedule. We multiply the regularization coefficient λ with the KL loss
737   term, and perform entry-wise gradient clipping on the KL loss term. For DiscQuant,
738   we tuned the hyper-parameters for each model and bit setting. The hyper-parameters
739   clamp, λ, lr, batch size, num iter and warmup were tuned. In the block scaling
740   setting we found that clamp={1.0, 0.5}, λ=200, lr={0.1, 0.05}, batch size={4,8},
741   num iter=1024, warmup=128 worked well for both models. In the incoherence processing
742   setting we found that clamp={0.05,0.01}, lr={0.05,0.01} worked well for both mod-
743   els, all other parameters being the same as before. For GPTQ, we used the actorder,
744   true sequential heuristics, and as tuned the number of samples over {1024, 4096,
745
      8192} for each model and bit setting. Our quantization dataset is constructed from the
      RedPajama-1T-Sample training set (Computer, 2023). We concatenate random samples
746
      until up to 2048 sequence length, truncating the last sample if necessary. Greedy or round-
747
      to-nearest requires no data, and no hyper-parameter tuning.

849
850
       A seminal result by Lovett and Meka Lovett & Meka (2012) works as follows: we are given
       a point y ∈ [0, 1]n in the hypercube, vectors v1 , . . . , vm ∈ Rn with ∥vj ∥2 = 1 and parameters
851                        Pm       2

852    cj ≥ 0 so that j=1 e−cj /16 ≤ 16       n
                                                . Then in randomized polynomial time one can find a
853    point x ∈ [0, 1] so that | ⟨vj , x − y⟩ | ≤ cj for all j and at least half the coordinates of x are
                         n

854    integral. Their algorithm is simple and elegant: we construct x as the outcome of a random
855
       walk starting at y. Then iteratively, for some small step size δ > 0 we add the outcome
       of a random Gaussian times δ to the current point. After hitting some constraint xi = 0,
856
       xi = 1 or | ⟨vj , x − y⟩ | = cj , the Gaussian updates will be taken orthogonal to those normal
857
       vectors. In other words, the random walk will continue in the face of the described polytope.
858    Still Lovett & Meka (2012) prove that performing the updates for O( δ12 ) iterations the walk
859    will cover enough distance so that on average Θ(n) box constraints must become tight.
860

       Lovett-Meka algorithm that are not explicitly stated elsewhere. Here we denote ∥M ∥S(1)
