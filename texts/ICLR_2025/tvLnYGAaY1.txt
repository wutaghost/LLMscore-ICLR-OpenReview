009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In time-series unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA), the adaptation between
012            temporal and frequency domain features has been relatively underexplored. To
013            address this gap, we conduct a comprehensive series of experiments to revisit
014            the roles of these domains in source-free UDA (SFUDA), a branch of the UDA
015            task. Our findings reveal that the temporal domain contains more diverse features,
016
               offering higher discriminability, while the frequency domain is more domain-
               invariant, providing better transferability. Combining the strengths of both domains,
017
               we propose TidalFlow, a SFUDA framework that synergistically integrates temporal
018
               and frequency domain features. TidalFlow enhances feature extraction and captures
019            subtle, class-specific features without relying on traditional alignment strategies.
020            By utilizing simple hyperparameter adjustments and using frequency embeddings
021            from the source domain as reference points for domain adaptation, TidalFlow
022            achieves nearly a 10% improvement across five benchmark datasets in time-series
023            UDA. This research highlights the unique strengths of both domains and marks a
024            paradigm shift in SFUDA methods, showcasing TidalFlow’s robust performance
025            in real-world applications. Code is available at the anonymous link: https:
026            //anonymous.4open.science/r/TidalFlow-42B0/.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Time series datasets showcase the prowess of neural networks Ravuri et al. (2021); Lundberg et al.
031   (2018), but their vulnerability to domain shifts poses deployment challenges Singhal et al. (2023);
032   Painblanc et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2021). These shifts, stemming from nuanced differences in test
033   distributions, hinder model generalization Koh et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2013).
034   Addressing this, domain adaptation (DA) techniques, such as leveraging unlabeled data Garg et al.
035   (2021); Ganin et al. (2016), emerge as essential to ensure robust model performance in real-world
036   scenarios. In addition, DA for time series is even more difficult Wilson & Cook (2020); Ozyurt
037   et al. (2023); He et al. (2023), as it has to deal with both the domain discrepancy and the temporal
038
      dynamics that may cause feature shift and label shift.
039   Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) is pivotal for enhancing the generalization of machine
040   learning models, aiming to train a model on a labeled source domain that can effectively perform on
041   a related yet unlabeled target domain Garg et al. (2021); Ganin et al. (2016). While UDA methods
042   have flourished in computer vision Huo et al. (2022); Tang et al. (2021); Pan et al. (2020); Tzeng
043   et al. (2019), their application to time series, though feasible with feature extractor adjustments, often
044
      falls short in fully harnessing time-series properties. In the domain of time series, a limited number
      of works have explicitly addressed UDA, they most focus on temporal information. Even when the
045
      frequency domain is considered, it is typically combined with temporal features and treated as general
046
      information during training.
047
048   To clarify the characteristics of the time and frequency domains, this research conducted a series
049   of experiments leading to the following conclusions: the temporal domain provides broader
050
      information with stronger classification discriminability, while the frequency domain, though
      simpler, offers more domain-invariant features that serve as reference points between the source
051
      and target domains (Section 3).
052
053   Our research integrates the strengths of both the temporal and frequency domains, moving beyond
      the prior focus on “how to align two inconsistent distributions” to explore “how to identify features

054
      that represent classes across domains.” The difference lies in that the former approach pays little
055   attention to the features extracted by the model, focusing instead on alignment methods and classifier
056   performance. This overemphasis on alignment leads to overly sensitive and inflexible classifiers,
057   particularly when dealing with data with large domain gaps or longer time series. The latter approach
058   avoids these pitfalls by enabling the model to utilize class-representative features early in training,
059   ensuring more robust performance.
060
      We propose TidalFlow, a simple framework for SFUDA in time series that leverages both temporal
061   and frequency domain characteristics to achieve strong performance. Our model integrates informa-
062   tion from both domains to capture subtle, class-specific features, enhancing feature extraction. By
063   focusing on the domain-invariant properties of the frequency domain, we use a frequency embeddings
064   table from the source domain as reference points, along with simple hyperparameter adjustments,
065   to enable the model to find the most suitable embeddings for target domain data during adaptation,
066   ultimately assigning the appropriate class labels. This straightforward training framework show-
067   cases the complementary strengths of the temporal and frequency domains, resulting in exceptional
068   performance across five different real-world datasets.

      2.1   U NSUPERVISED D OMAIN A DAPTATION
086
087   Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) involves utilizing labeled data from a source domain to
088   predict labels for an unlabeled target domain. The primary objective of UDA methods is to minimize
089   domain discrepancy, thereby reducing the lower bound of target error. Existing UDA approaches can
090   be broadly categorized into three groups: (1) Metric-based methods, like DDC (Tzeng et al., 2019),
091   Deep CORAL (Sun & Saenko, 2016), DeepJDOT (Damodaran et al., 2018), HoMM (Chen et al.,
092   2020), and MMDA (Rahman et al., 2020), minimize domain discrepancy by imposing restrictions
093   using a distance metric (e.g., maximum mean discrepancy). (2) Adversarial-based methods employ
094   domain discriminator networks, such as DANN (Ganin et al., 2016), CDAN (Long et al., 2018), and
095
      DIRT-T (Shu et al., 2018), to enforce the feature extractor in learning domain-invariant representations.
      (3) Contrastive methods reduce domain discrepancy through a contrastive loss, aligning embeddings
096
      of source and target samples of the same class. Pseudo-labels, generated by clustering algorithms,
097
      are used for target samples, as their actual labels are unknown. Examples include CAN (Kang et al.,
098   2019), CLDA (Singh, 2021), and IDCo (Zhang et al., 2023). While UDA has been extensively
099   explored in computer vision, limited research has been conducted on UDA for time-series data.

      2.3   V ECTOR Q UANTISED VARIATIONAL AUTO E NCODER (VQ-VAE)
149
150   Conceptualized as a communication system, the VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) model
151   comprises an encoder and a decoder. The encoder involves a non-linear mapping from the input
152   space to a vector, which is then quantized by determining its nearest prototype vector in a shared
153   codebook. The quantized vector, essentially the index of the closest prototype vector, is transmitted
154   to the decoder. Despite the potential loss, the decoder maps these indices back to their corresponding
155   vectors in the codebook, reconstructing the data through another non-linear function. Learning
156   involves back-propagating the gradient of the reconstruction error through the decoder and to the
157   encoder, utilizing the straight-through gradient estimator.
158   A key benefit of VQ-VAE is its discrete representation, which proves useful in obtaining effective
159   features. In UDA, data distribution from the target domain is indirectly captured through self-
160   supervised learning. Notably, VQ-VAE is less susceptible to model degeneration issues, enabling it
161   to effectively capture both temporal and frequency domain information during adaptation without the
      associated concerns.

190   3.2   P RELIMINARY S TUDY
191
192   We design a series of experiments on both the temporal and frequency domains. To minimize model
193   influence, we follow prior research (Liu & Xue, 2021b; Cheng et al., 2024) by constructing a 3-layer
194   CNN as a temporal feature extractor and a frequency feature extractor that combines a fast Fourier
195   transform with a 1-layer linear network. Both are followed by a 1-layer linear classifier for simplicity.
196
      The key question we explore is: What kind of feature information do the temporal and frequency
197
      domains provide? We pre-train three models on the source domain until until they converge and
198   observe their performance on the target domain. During the temporal model experiments, we observe
199   a noteworthy phenomenon: despite achieving nearly 100% accuracy in the source domain (Fig.
200   1(a)) with different model parameters, the performance on the target domain exhibits considerable
201   fluctuation. As shown in Fig. 1(b), the performance variance of the three temporal models is
202   larger than that of the frequency models. A t-test confirms a statistically significant difference in
203   performance variance between the temporal and frequency models (p-value = 0.0133).
204   Transferability. When we examine transferability, Fig. 1(c) shows that the temporal models
205   experience a more significant performance drop, with a statistically significant difference from the
206   frequency models (p-value = 0.0491). We hypothesize that this is because the temporal domain
207   contains a wider variety of information, enabling the model to classify based on multiple dimensions.
208   Nevertheless, this diverse information also includes more features specific to the source domain or
209   confounders, meaning that when domain shifting occurs, the model’s focus may no longer be on the
210   relevant class features of the target domain, resulting in poorer transferability.
211   In contrast, the frequency domain, after undergoing Fourier transformation, filters out much of
212   the extraneous information, such as signal start and end points or noise, resulting in fewer feature
213   dimensions. However, this allows the frequency models to focus more on the overall structure of the
214   information, making them more domain-invariant. Fig. 1(c) supports this, showing that although the
215   frequency models do not perform as well as the temporal models in source domain classification,
      their transferability is superior.

      This raises another concern: Is the frequency domain truly more domain-invariant? To investigate,
217   we design another experiment where we only adjust the extent of feature updates (here, we choose
218   to adjust the learning rate) during the fine-tuning phase. Our assumption is that if merely tweaking
219   the learning rate significantly improves model performance, it indicates that the frequency domain
220   contains domain-agnostic features that are specific to each class of data, rather than just irrelevant
221   features that do not contribute to the model’s effectiveness.
222
      As shown in Fig. 1(d), the frequency models require a very small learning rate to fine-tune correctly.
223   Larger learning rates prevent the frequency models from converging to the optimal point. Interestingly,
224   the temporal models are much less sensitive to hyperparameter adjustments compared to the frequency
225   models. In Fig. 1(d), despite averaging accuracy across 15 source 7→ target experiments, the temporal
226   models fine-tune to 100% accuracy across learning rates ranging from 1 × 10−4 to 1 × 10−8 . This
227   could be explained by the high feature diversity in the temporal domain, allowing different model
228   parameters to reach optimal solutions depending on the learning rate. Meanwhile, the frequency
229   models retain robust domain-invariant features between source and target domains, making them
230   better suited to fine-tuning with smaller steps.
231   Empirical insights. The analysis reveals two key insights regarding time-series domain adaptation:
232   (1) the time domain excels at classification, but its transferability is hindered by an excess of
233   confounding factors, and (2) the frequency domain, though containing more uniform and less diverse
234   information, offers better domain-invariant features, leading to stronger transferability. Based on
235   these observations, we design a simple model framework that leverages the rich features of the time
236   domain while using the frequency domain as a reference point to bridge the source and target domains.
237
      Our experimental results demonstrate that combining the strengths of both domains yields improved
      performance.

328
329   In TidalFlow, we utilize three types of objective functions during the training phase: (1) classification
330   loss, (2) dissimilarity loss, and (3) feature-embedding consistency loss. While there are two types of
331   objective functions during the adaptation phase: (1) reconstruction loss and (2) feature-embedding
332   consistency loss.
333   Classification loss LCE . We utilize cross-entropy loss as the loss function for our classification task
334   during training.
335
336
      Dissimilarity loss LD . This objective function is designed to prevent the model from generating
      nearly identical embeddings among categories during the training phase. To achieve this, we identify
337
      the closest embedding to zf req from all embeddings in the frequency block, which is more domain-
338
      agnostic than temporal features and does not belong to the same category as yis . The repulsive effect
339   is introduced by calculating the dissimilarity loss. It is worth noting that, while TidalFlow searches
340   for the closest representative in the embedding table within the same category as xsi , this approach
341   may result in the model learning a common feature across all categories, neglecting latent features
342   that distinguish between different categories. To address this, we utilize the following equation (Eq.
343   4) to guide the model explicitly in generating a better latent representation.

390
      Datasets. We employ a comprehensive evaluation strategy, consisting of two main aspects. First,
391
      extensive experiments are conducted using five well-established benchmark datasets in UDA tasks,
392   from three distinct problem types: (1) Human Activity Recognition: HAR (Anguita et al., 2013),
393   HHAR (Stisen et al., 2015), WISDM (Kwapisz et al., 2011); (2) Sleep Stage Classification: Sleep-
394   EDF (Goldberger et al., 2000); (3) Machine Fault Diagnosis: MFD (Lessmeier et al., 2016). In human
395   activity recognition datasets, we treat sensor measurements from each participant as distinct domains.
396   To ensure robust assessment, we randomly select 10 source-target domain pairs for evaluation, a
397   methodology widely adopted in previous works on UDA in time-series research (He et al., 2023;
398   Ozyurt et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2021; Wilson et al., 2020). For the sleep stage classification task,
399   following the approach of (Ragab et al., 2023a), we utilize the Sleep-EDF dataset, comprising EEG
400
      readings from 20 healthy subjects, and we specifically choose EEG in alignment with previous studies
      (Eldele et al., 2021). The machine fault diagnosis dataset has been collected under four different
401
      operating conditions, and we treat them as separate domains. In contrast to datasets used for human
402
      activity recognition being multi-variate, the data used in Sleep-EDF and MFD consist of a single
403   univariate channel following previous works. (He et al., 2023; Ragab et al., 2023a) Further details on
404   datasets are given in Appendix B.
405
406
      Baselines. We evaluate nine domain adaptation methods, including general UDA approaches: deep
      correlation alignment (Deep Coral) (Sun & Saenko, 2016), decision boundary iterative refinement
407
      training with a teacher (DIRT-T) (Shu et al., 2018), HoMM (Chen et al., 2020), and CDAN (Long
408
      et al., 2018). Additionally, we include four UDA methods specifically designed for time series:

454   We further analyze the impact of different learning rates for the temporal and frequency blocks of
455   TidalFlow during the adaptation phase. We conduct experiments using the MFD and Sleep-EDF
456   datasets due to their large data volumes, which make performance differences more pronounced, as
457   shown in Fig. 4. We discover some valuable findings:
458
              1. When the learning rate for the frequency block is smaller, TidalFlow’s adaptability improves.
459
                 This trend aligns with the observations of our insights in Section 3.
460
461           2. When the learning rate for the temporal block is larger, the model’s performance deteriorates.
462              We speculate that this is due to the interaction between the encoder and the HET within
463
                 TidalFlow architecture. Specifically, when the learning rates of the temporal and frequency
                 blocks differ by four orders of magnitude, it indirectly hinders the adjustment range of one
464
                 of the blocks through the encoder.
465
466   Therefore, we recommend setting the learning rates of the temporal and frequency blocks to the same
467   value during the adaptation phase for optimal performance.

536   6    C ONCLUSION
537
538   This research uncovers the distinct and complementary strengths of the temporal and frequency
539   domains in the context of time-series Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA). Our initial experi-
      ments show that the temporal domain captures a wider range of discriminative features, while the


                                                          10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      frequency domain focuses on domain-agnostic features that improve transferability between the
541   source and target domains. Building on these findings, we introduce TidalFlow—an innovative
542   SFUDA framework that effectively combines frequency embeddings and uses simple hyperparameter
543   adjustments to adapt to new domains without relying on traditional alignment methods.
544
      TidalFlow demonstrates significant performance improvements, achieving nearly a 10% gain across
545
      five benchmark datasets, highlighting its practical utility and robustness in real-world applications.
546
      By moving beyond conventional alignment-focused approaches, this work shifts the focus toward
547   extracting class-specific features that remain consistent across domains. The methodologies and
548   insights presented in this study represent a paradigm shift in time-series SFUDA, offering a more
549   flexible and resilient framework that is better equipped to handle diverse and challenging domain
550   adaptation scenarios.
551
      Limitation and future work. Addressing issues related to class imbalances would be urgent for
552
      future research. Additionally, mitigating the frequency leakage problem, which can arise due to the
553   integration of information from both time and frequency domains, is essential for further enhancing
554   the model’s performance. These endeavors will not only bolster TidalFlow’s capabilities but also
555   contribute valuable insights to the broader landscape of time-series SFUDA.

      We assess the performance of TidalFlow on five distinct UDA benchmark datasets, each characterized
939
      by its unique features. The datasets considered include:
940
941        1. HAR Anguita et al. (2013): This dataset incorporates measurements from a 3-axis ac-
942           celerometer, 3-axis gyroscope, and 3-axis body acceleration. Data is collected from 30
943           participants at a sampling rate of 50 Hz and uses non-overlapping segments of 128-time
944           steps to predict activity labels. The objective is to classify time series into six activities:
945           walking, walking upstairs, walking downstairs, sitting, standing, and lying down.
946        2. HHAR Stisen et al. (2015): Comprising 3-axis accelerometer measurements from 9 partici-
947           pants at a frequency of 50 Hz, this dataset employs non-overlapping segments of 128-time
948           steps for classification. Activity labels include biking, sitting, standing, walking, walking
949           upstairs, and walking downstairs.
950        3. WISDM Kwapisz et al. (2011): Featuring 3-axis accelerometer measurements from 36
951           participants at a frequency of 20 Hz, similar to the HAR dataset, we use non-overlapping
952           segments of 128-time steps for classification. The dataset includes six activity labels:
953           walking, jogging, sitting, standing, walking upstairs, and walking downstairs.
954        4. Sleep-EDF Goldberger et al. (2000): This task involves classifying electroencephalography
955           (EEG) signals into five stages (Wake, N1, N2, N3, REM). Comprising EEG readings from
956           20 healthy subjects, we select a single channel (Fpz-Cz) as Ragab et al. (2023a).
957        5. MFD Lessmeier et al. (2016): Collected by Paderborn University to identify incipient faults
958           using vibration signals, this dataset consists of data collected under four different operating
959           conditions. Each condition is treated as a separate domain, and we use five cross-condition
960           scenarios to evaluate domain adaptation performance. Each sample in the dataset comprises
961           a single univariate channel with 5120 data points.

1298
       Two main factors affect TidalFlow’s performance: (1) the size of the hierarchical embedding table
1299   and (2) the number of classification categories. The following will elaborate on these two aspects:
1300
1301
       E.1   S IZE OF THE HIERARCHICAL EMBEDDING TABLE
1302
1303   During the training phase, as the source domain has labels, we only need to calculate K nearest
1304   neighbors for each category, where K represents the number of embeddings per category (Fig. 6). We
1305   determine the appropriate value of K through experimentation, considering both Mean accuracy and
1306   macro F1 score. We found that for the majority of datasets, setting K to 8 yielded better performance,
1307   excluding MFD dataset in mean macro F1 score. Accordingly, we speculate that other parameters of
1308   the model contribute to its superior performance at K=8.
1309

1313   is directly influenced by the number of categories, leading to a significant impact.
