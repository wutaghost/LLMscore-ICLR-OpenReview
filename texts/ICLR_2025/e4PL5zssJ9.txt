009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular dynamics simulations are crucial for understanding complex biomolec-
012            ular systems, but they are often hindered by the high dimensionality of the con-
013            figurational space. This paper introduces two novel approaches for discovering
014            effective degrees of freedom (DoF) in molecular dynamics simulations by lever-
015            aging approximate symmetries of the energy landscape. We present a scalable
016
               symmetry loss function compatible with existing force-field frameworks and a
               Hessian-based method efficient for smaller systems. Both approaches enable sys-
017
               tematic exploration of conformational space by connecting structural dynamics to
018
               energy landscape symmetries. Applied to alanine dipeptide, our methods com-
019            prehensively sample the Ramachandran plot, including shallow minima. Simu-
020            lations initiated from our DoF-sampled points converge to all important confor-
021            mations, demonstrating the methods’ effectiveness in navigating complex energy
022            landscapes. These approaches offer powerful tools for efficient exploration in
023            molecular simulations, with potential applications in protein folding and drug dis-
024            covery.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Molecular dynamics (MD) is an essential tool for a wide range of applications, including drug dis-
029
      covery, protein folding, and understanding the physics of biological systems at the molecular level
030   . By simulating the motion of atoms and molecules over time, MD allows researchers to investigate
031   the structural and functional properties of complex biomolecules and materials. The configuration
032   space of molecules, however, is extremely high-dimensional, with each atom contributing three de-
033   grees of freedom (xyz coordinates). Despite this complexity, the physically relevant conformations
034   of molecules typically occupy a much lower-dimensional subspace. This space corresponds to re-
035   gions of low free energy, which can be thought of as a negative log-likelihood of the system’s state.
036   The most likely conformations correspond to the minima of this energy landscape.
037   Sampling from this low-energy subspace is crucial for understanding the function and stability of
038   biomolecules, but it presents significant computational challenges due to the vast number of possible
039   configurations and the presence of energy barriers separating different conformational states. Exist-
040   ing methods for sampling these low-energy conformations include enhanced sampling techniques
041   such as metadynamics Laio & Parrinello (2002), umbrella sampling Torrie & Valleau (1977), and
042   replica exchange molecular dynamics (REMD) Sugita & Okamoto (1999), which aim to overcome
043   energy barriers and improve exploration of the conformational space. However, these methods often
044
      require careful tuning and can be computationally expensive.
045   In this work, we present a novel approach for discovering degrees of freedom (DoF) that effectively
046   move the system along the low-energy manifold, enabling more efficient exploration of relevant
047   conformations in the molecular landscape. Our key observation is that low-energy DoF (LEDoF)
048   can be related to approximate symmetries of the energy function. Recent works in machine learning
049   have made progress in discovering symmetries in data Benton et al. (2020); Dehmamy et al. (2021);
050
      Yang et al. (2023b;a). Our setting is different in that we do not have data, but an energy function,
      which can be used to construct a symmetry, loss. This problem was addressed in LieGG Moskalev
051
      et al. (2022), where we discover infinitesimal symmetry generators, i.e. Lie algebra elements.
052
053   We find approximate symmetries by considering small transformations of the original DoF and
      deriving conditions for the near-invariance of the energy. This process yields a symmetry loss, which


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      we then minimize. In the case of small molecules, we show that the problem can be formulated
055   as finding symmetries of the Hessian. Aside from the optimization approach, we also provide an
056   analytic solution based on degenerate Hessian eigenspaces.
057
058
      Our method connects the structural dynamics of molecules to the approximate symmetries of the
      energy landscape. This enables a systematic exploration of conformational space that is both com-
059
      putationally efficient and physically insightful. We implement this technique in the study of Alanine
060
      dipeptide and demonstrate how these effective DoFs can correspond to significant chemical features
061   such as dihedral angles. Hence, these DoFs facilitate targeted exploration and sampling of biolog-
062   ically relevant conformations. WE show that this method of sampling can significantly reduce the
063   computational burden associated with high-dimensional MD simulations. It also enhances the abil-
064   ity to explore areas of the conformational landscape that are difficult to access through conventional
065   methods.
066
      Our contributions can be summarized as:
067
068         1. DoF as Symmetries: We formulate identifying effective DoF (EDoF) as an optimization
069            problem aiming to discover approximate symmetries of the energy.
070
071
            2. Symmetry discovery using the Hessian: We derive the relation between symmetry gen-
               erators and the Hessian near critical points. We also provide a method to construct symme-
072
               tries from the spectrum of the Hessian.
073
074         3. Extraction of internal coordinates for molecules: We show that our method leads to
075            discovering well-known dihedral angles and several additional EDoF.
076         4. Effective exploration of Alanine dipeptide landscape: We demonstrate enhanced ca-
077            pability in sampling diverse conformations, particularly shallow local minima, which are
078            difficult to sample using existing methods.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Exploring the multidimensional energy landscape of molecular systems, such as proteins or
      molecules, poses significant challenges due to the system’s numerous degrees of freedom, which
084
      make the landscape rugged. Traditional molecular dynamics and Monte Carlo methods often strug-
085
      gle to fully map this landscape, as they tend to become trapped in local minima and fail to capture
086   rare conformations. This is largely due to the high energy barriers surrounding the minima or the
087   long timescales required for conformational changes to evolve Bernardi et al. (2015).
088
089
      Space sampling. Monte Carlo methods are advantageous for sampling configurational space due
090   to the absence of inherent timescales, but they struggle to capture transitions between conformations
091   and can become trapped in local minima behind high-energy barriers, leaving some regions of the
092   energy landscape poorly sampled Heilmann et al. (2020). Umbrella sampling, introduced by Torrie
093   & Valleau (1977), addresses this by replacing the standard Boltzmann weighting with a biasing
094   potential, effectively enabling a random walk across energy barriers.
095
      Methods such as replica exchange molecular dynamics (REMD) Hansmann & Okamoto (1999);
096   Sugita & Okamoto (1999) employ molecular dynamics simulations simultaneously on a series of
097   replica systems with different conditions,e.g. temperature, and randomly exchange the states of any
098   two replicas with a regular schedule Qi et al. (2018). By utilizing multiple replicas, REMD enables
099   efficient sampling of the energy landscape and helps overcome high energy barriers. However, the
100   need for numerous replicas can significantly increase computational demands, making the approach
101   challenging to implement in practice Rathore et al. (2005); Liu et al. (2005); Wang et al. (2020).
102   Metadynamics is another approach to improve sampling of the energy landscape of a system by
103   driving it through collective variables (CV), which represent key coordinates defining the landscape
104   Laio & Parrinello (2002); Bussi & Laio (2020). In molecular dynamics simulations, Gaussian biases
105   are periodically added to prevent the system from revisiting previously explored regions, facilitating
106   the discovery of new minima. In many cases, identifying the appropriate set of collective variables
107   (CVs) is non-trivial and typically relies on prior knowledge; additionally, managing a large number
      of CVs can become inefficient.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Coarse graining and machine learning methods can also accelerate molecular dynamics simulations,
109   improving sampling efficiency and expanding accessible phase space Souza et al. (2021); Majewski
110   et al. (2023); Noé et al. (2020).
111
112   Identifying the DoF. Identifying suitable collective variables (CVs) for energy landscape sam-
113   pling is challenging, often introducing biases and facing issues related to data quality and inter-
114   pretability. Principal component analysis (PCA) has been employed to define CVs Hori et al. (2009),
115   effectively capturing broad folding and non-folding features of proteins. However, due to its linear
116   nature, PCA often fails to represent the intricate and nonlinear structure of protein energy landscapes
117   Maisuradze et al. (2009). To address these limitations, autoencoders have been used to automatically
118
      learn nonlinear CVs from data Chen & Ferguson (2018), although this approach may yield CVs that
      are difficult to interpret physically and dependent on data quality. Support vector machines (SVMs)
119
      Sultan & Pande (2018) have also been applied to classify protein properties from short molecular dy-
120
      namics simulations and to identify CVs. Other machine learning techniques, such as self-supervised
121   deep neural networks, have been developed to identify slow CVs or reaction coordinates, further
122   advancing the search for effective descriptors Wehmeyer & Noé (2018).

      3     T HEORY
126
      This section presents our theoretical framework for discovering effective degrees of freedom (DoF)
127
      in molecular systems, based on identifying approximate symmetries in the energy landscape. We
128
      introduce two approaches: a scalable symmetry loss function compatible with existing force-field
129   frameworks, and a Hessian-based method effective for smaller systems. Both methods connect
130   molecular structural dynamics to energy function symmetries, enabling systematic conformational
131   space exploration. We derive the mathematical foundations of these approaches, showing how they
132   lead to the discovery of physically meaningful DoF, such as dihedral angles in peptides.
133
134   3.1   S YMMETRY AND D O F
135
136   While we primarily focus on physical and molecular systems, our approach is general and can be
137   formulated in broader terms by treating the energy as a loss function. Let E : Z → R represent the
138
      potential energy of a physical system, which, analogous to a loss function, we assume to be smooth
      over large regions of the parameter space X , and bounded from below. The parameters z ∈ Z
139
      correspond to the system’s degrees of freedom (DoF). We assume that X is a vector space. In the
140
      context of molecular dynamics (MD), the standard DoF include the 3D positions ⃗xi and momenta
141
      p⃗i = mi d⃗xi /dt = mi ⃗x˙ i of all particles i ∈ {1, . . . , n}, where mi denotes the mass of particle i.
142
143   Temperature and kinetic energy In this work, we are primarily interested in the static confor-
144   mations of the system, and thus, we ignore the kinetic energy term in our formulation. By focusing
145   on the potential energy, we capture the equilibrium properties of molecular systems. While tem-
146   perature induces thermal fluctuations in real systems, our current approach neglects these effects.
147   However, these fluctuations could be incorporated in future extensions, particularly when account-
148   ing for finite-temperature effects and exploring dynamic behavior. Ignoring the kinetic DoF pi , our
149   parameter space reduces to the space X ⊂ X of positions x = {⃗xi }. Hence, we redefine the energy
150   to be just the potential energy E : X → R
151
152   Lifting DoF to a group Our core idea is to use transformations acting on the degrees of freedom
153
      (DoF) instead of the original DoF, thereby lifting the DoF to a group action on X . While not all
      DoF can be lifted in this manner, we demonstrate that this approach enables us to link low-energy
154
      DoF to underlying symmetries of the system. We consider the general linear group GL(X ) acting
155
      on the parameter space X . The translation group can also be included, but since MD potentials are
156   generally translation invariant, it leads to trivial symmetries, which we are not interested in here.
157   Starting from a reference point x0 ∈ X , the orbit of GL(X ), defined as Orbit{x = gx0 | g ∈
158   GL(X )}, generates a manifold of transformed configurations. This manifold effectively describes
159   the set of configurations related to x0 by symmetry transformations. This allows us to replace x with
160   transformations g that reach x from x0 . By focusing on symmetries that preserve or approximately
161   preserve the potential energy, we extract DoF that correspond to motion along low-energy directions,
      thus providing a natural way to explore the low-energy landscape of the system.

                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Group parameters as DoF In order to map g to degrees of freedom, we need a parametriza-
163   tion for g. Since GL(X ) is a continuous group, we use the Lie algebra and the exponential
164   map to parametrize g in terms of the Lie algebra basis La ∈ gl(X ), where the Lie algebra
165   gl(X ) = Tid GL(X ) is the tangent space at the identity of GL(X ). In the case of matrix Lie groups
166   such as GL(X ), the exponential map exp : gl(X ) → GL(X ) can be written in terms of the matrix
167   exponential. Exponentiating an element in the Lie algebra yields a group element g = exp(θ · L),
168   where θ is a vector of parameters. In more general cases, where a group element requires a nontriv-
169   ial path
            Q on the group manifold, the transformation may be expressed as a product of exponentials
170   g = i exp(θi · L). In both cases, small group elements (i.e. near identity) can be expanded as
171   g ≈ I + θ · L + O(θ2 ), where I is the identity matrix. This formulation allows us to define the group
172
      parameters θ as the new DoF, reparametrizing the system in terms of group transformations that
      capture the low-energy dynamics. Next, we define more concretely what we mean by low-energy
173
      dynamics and effective DoF.

      3.2   E FFECTIVE D EGREES OF F REEDOM
176
177   To motivate our definition of effective degrees of freedom (DoF), we begin by considering the poten-
178   tial energy E(z) of a molecular system described by a typical force field, such as AMBER Cornell
179   et al. (1995). The energy landscape comprises quadratic terms representing bond lengths and bond
180   angles, which can be minimized easily, along with non-linear terms such as Lennard-Jones and
181   Coulomb forces that dominate at short distances and decay at larger separations (Appendix A).
182
      Once the bond and angle terms have been minimized, the system navigates a restricted subspace
183   of the full configuration space where bond lengths and angles remain near their equilibrium values.
184   However, within this subspace, the energy can still vary significantly due to non-linear interactions
185   between atoms. To describe the relevant motion in this subspace, we seek directions in which the
186   potential energy changes minimally. These directions correspond to the effective DoF of the system.
187
      Concretely, let x ∈ X be a reference point, corresponding to a configuration where the quadratic
188
      energy terms are minimized. Consider a small perturbation δx, such that the system moves from x to
189   x′ = x + δx. The first-order expansion of the energy around x is E(x + δx) ≈ E(x) + δx · ∇E(x).
190   We are interested in directions δx for which δx · ∇E(x) is small, implying minimal change in the
191   energy. Now, instead of arbitrary perturbations, we focus on transformations generated by elements
192   of the Lie algebra. Let g ≈ I + ϵL be a small group transformation acting on x, so that x = gx and
193   the perturbation is δx = Lx. Substituting into the energy expansion, we have
194
                                         E(gx) ≈ E(x) + Lx · ∇E(x)                                       (1)
195
196   We define the effective DoF as the directions in the Lie algebra for which the directional derivative
197   Lx · ∇E(x) is small. These directions correspond to approximate symmetries of the system, in the
198   sense that they preserve the energy to first order. Next, we discuss how to learn these DoF.
199

523   Discussion about the time advantage For both the Hessian-based approached, we see that we
524   are able to fully recover the main conformations of alanine dipeptide while also sampling other
525
      saddle points or unstable conformations. Although our method uses the openmm simulation in the
      final step, we only need a fraction of the total steps required by openmm to find all conformations.
526
      Furthermore our methods does not need to be run sequentially as the simulations for all the grid
527
      points can be run simultaneously. Thus, in principle, the effective time required for simulating our
528   method could be 105 × less than the time required to get the same results using openmm simulations.
529   The major bottleneck for our approach is the Hessian computation which scales quadratically with
530   the size of the system and does not easily generalize to stochastic forcefields. While the optimization
531   method overcomes the second challenge in principle we find it to be less stable than the Hessian-
532   based approach in practice. As for the quadratic cost of our method, it inherently comes from
533   modelling the pairwise interactions between the particles and it can be pruned to be almost linear by
534   using cut-offs parameters for intra-atom interactions. We plan to explore this further in future work.

      Proposition B.2. Another possible solution is if L has a low-rank structure, particularly if it is a
791
      rank-one or rank-two matrix. In this case, even if H has high rank, the product HL will still result
792
      in a matrix of low rank, potentially minimizing the trace loss:
793                                                     X
794                                         Tr [HL] =        λi vi⊤ Lvi
795                                                       i
796
      where vi are the eigenvectors of H and λi are the corresponding eigenvalues. If L is chosen to have
797
      support in directions orthogonal to the eigenvectors corresponding to large eigenvalues, the trace
798   loss can be reduced.
799
800   In the case where H has degenerate eigenvalues, an additional symmetry arises. Specifically, if
801   H has a degenerate subspace corresponding to k degenerate eigenvalues, this subspace has an in-
802   herent SO(k) symmetry. In such cases, we can choose L to be a generator of rotations within the
803   degenerate subspace, exploiting the symmetry. More formally:
804   Proposition B.3. let Λ be the diagonalized form of H, with QΛQ⊤ = H. If Λ has a set of k-fold
805   degenerate eigenvalues λ1 = λ2 = · · · = λk , the corresponding eigenspace forms a k-dimensional
806   subspace of symmetry. The action of L in this subspace can be viewed as a rotation, and L can be
807   chosen to belong to the Lie algebra of SO(k), the group of rotations in k dimensions.
808   In this case, L takes the form of an antisymmetric matrix restricted to the degenerate subspace:
809
                                            L ∈ so(k),        L⊤ = −L

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      The matrix L generates rotations within the degenerate eigenspace, leaving the overall structure of
811   H invariant. This rotation symmetry, arising due to the degeneracy of the eigenvalues, ensures that
812   the trace loss tr(HL) vanishes. Thus, the commutator condition:
813
814                                               [L, H] = 0
815   is naturally satisfied within this degenerate subspace. The presence of degenerate eigenvalues gives
816   rise to this additional symmetry, and L can be chosen as one of the canonical generators of SO(k).
817
818   This mechanism applies specifically to the case where H has degenerate eigenvalues, as it is the
819   degeneracy that gives rise to the SO(k) symmetry. If the eigenvalues are non-degenerate, such a
820   rotation within an eigenspace does not apply, and the symmetry must be realized in other ways, such
821
      as by ensuring L lies in the approximate null space of H or satisfies an antisymmetry condition.
