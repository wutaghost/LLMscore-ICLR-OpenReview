011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            As embedded devices become increasingly prevalent in intelligent systems, low-
014            power system in resource-constrained environments has emerged as a key chal-
015            lenge. Spiking neural networks (SNNs), with their sparse and event-driven com-
016            putation, have shown great potential as a low-power candidate for embedded de-
017
               vices. In federated learning scenarios, where multiple energy-constrained devices
               collaborate, adopting efficient SNN models with effective training methods is crit-
018
               ical. However, research on training SNNs within federated learning systems is still
019
               very limited, particularly in terms of how to achieve both energy efficiency and ro-
020            bustness under system heterogeneity. This gap presents a significant opportunity
021            for further exploration of SNNs in distributed learning settings. In this paper,
022            we investigate a significant and innovative problem in robust spike-based feder-
023            ated learning, particularly in the presence of noise, and system heterogeneity. We
024            majorly consider two types of system heterogeneity in this study, including data
025            and client participation heterogeneity. To address this, we propose a novel feder-
026            ated learning framework, spike-based decoupled federated information-bottleneck
027            learning (SDFIL), to enable robust, low-power federated learning through SNNs
028
               under system heterogeneity. Specifically, we design a decoupled information bot-
               tleneck principle tailored for local SNN training to maximize the mutual informa-
029
               tion between ground truth and model predictions while minimizing mutual infor-
030
               mation between intermediate representations. This method effectively minimizes
031            the impact of outliers in non-independent and identically distributed (non-IID)
032            data on model updates, thereby enhancing the performance of federated SNNs,
033            resulting in enhanced robustness and reduced sensitivity to outliers. We evalu-
034            ate the proposed SDFIL algorithm across a variety of settings, including different
035            noise levels and varying degrees of system heterogeneity. The experimental re-
036            sults indicate that SDFIL demonstrates superior robustness compared to compet-
037            ing methods and generally achieves an improvement in overall accuracy of 5% to
038            10%. Additionally, it can achieve up to 7.7× higher energy efficiency compared to
039
               traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Neuromorphic computing represents a groundbreaking approach to achieving artificial general in-
045
      telligence, modeled after the brain’s processing mechanisms (Renner et al., 2024). Its event-based
      processing provides several advantages, including low power consumption, low latency, and high
046
      biological plausibility (Frenkel et al., 2023). With advancements in neuromorphic chips, such as
047
      TrueNorth, Loihi, and Tianjic, neuromorphic computing has found applications across various fields
048   (Akopyan et al., 2015; Davies et al., 2018; Pei et al., 2019). A prominent model for neuromorphic
049   computing is the spiking neural network (SNN), which employs spiking neurons as its fundamental
050   unit (Meng et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023). SNNs not only exhibit low-power effi-
051   ciency but also achieve performance on par with deep neural networks (Qiu et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
052   2019; She et al., 2022). Due to this power efficiency, SNNs show great potential for deployment in
053   embedded devices (Kucik & Meoni, 2021; Ottati et al., 2023). However, embedded devices often
      require continuous data collection for training and updates.

055   2022; Nguyen et al., 2022). However, due to data silos and privacy concerns, centralized learning

057   learning has been introduced as a distributed machine learning framework (Zhang et al., 2021).
058   It enables numerous clients to collaboratively train a model with decentralized data, ensuring that
059   private data is not shared with a central server, thus safeguarding data privacy (Zhuang et al., 2021).
060   Although federated learning plays a crucial role in large-scale, distributed edge learning systems
061   that prioritize privacy, current research on SNNs has yet to develop extensive methods for federated
062   learning within a distributed training framework.

068   challenge for federated learning with SNNs. Second, to mitigate the risk of data leakage through
069

070

071

072   are more computationally intensive due to event-driven processing and temporal characteristics, this
073   heterogeneity in client participation exacerbates the challenge of maintaining model consistency and
074   accuracy across a distributed system. In this study, data and client participation heterogeneity are
075   collectively referred to as system heterogeneity. Addressing system heterogeneity is critical for en-
076   suring the robustness and scalability of federated learning in SNN applications. Unlike previous

      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
174
175   In our investigation of federated learning frameworks, we methodically address three predominant
176   challenges: client participation heterogeneity, data heterogeneity, and gradient noise.
177
      In federated learning, client participation heterogeneity poses a significant challenge to the scalabil-
178   ity. This issue mainly arises when the amount of training data Ntrain remains constant but the number
179   of clients K increases, which reduces the amount of private data nk on each client, adversely af-
180   fecting the data diversity in each training round. Although the total number of clients represents
181   one dimension of scalability, the proportion of participating clients in each round, K/P , is also
182   crucial to performance, especially under non-IID datasets. Furthermore, data heterogeneity and pri-
183   vacy concerns complicate model training and scalability. Individual clients, varying in geographic,
184   demographic, or behavioral factors, may have unique data distributions Pk , often with skewed cate-
185   gory Σ representation Dk ∼ Pk (Σ), which challenges the global model’s ability to learn uniformly
186
      from all clients. Here, α serves as a parameter that dictates how unevenly samples are distributed
      among users. A smaller α value leads to more uneven (non-IID) distributions. Moreover, while the
187
      base station B updates the global model using gradients from clients, it cannot access local data
188
      directly. This arrangement, although protective, doesn’t fully prevent potential data inference from
189   exchanged gradients. To enhance data privacy, gradients sent to the base station are typically obfus-
190   cated with noise, addressing the risk of data reconstruction. Clearly, our aims are the following: by
191   minimizing the loss on local datasets, the resulting gradients should exhibit high sample efficiency
192   and maintain good generalization capabilities, while also retaining information even after gradient
193   noise is introduced.

346   4.2   C OMPARISON WITH OTHER M ETHODS
347
      Existing methods (Venkatesha et al., 2021) acknowledge that real-world federated learning systems
348
      require strong scalability. They experimented with SNNs and cross-entropy loss functions across a
349
      range of client combinations, taking into account the imbalance in data distribution. This study used
350   the proposed SDFIL with the same optimizer and parameter settings. Table 1 shows the final valida-
351   tion accuracy after 100 rounds of training. It is evident that our method outperforms the Venkatesha
352   et al. (2021) under various conditions. In scenarios with full participation of clients, SDFIL only
353   shows a slight advantage. However, as the number of clients increases and the proportion of partici-
354   pating clients remains the same, SDFIL maintains a higher accuracy compared to existing methods,
355   with an improvement of 5% to 10%, demonstrating a significant scalability advantage. Furthermore,
356   under conditions of imbalanced data distribution, SDFIL experiences a smaller drop in accuracy
357   compared to when the data is balanced, indicating robustness.
358   Then, we analyze the reasons for this phenomenon. SDFIL optimizes the mutual information be-
359   tween the input and output of the model, helping the network filter out task-relevant information
360   while suppressing redundant or irrelevant data. By introducing a decoupled information bottleneck
361   mechanism, SDFIL enhances the model’s ability to extract useful information, thereby improving
362   generalization and robustness. Particularly in scenarios with imbalanced client data, SDFIL auto-
363   matically adjusts to learn the key features from different clients’ data, effectively addressing data
364
      distribution imbalances and mitigating accuracy degradation. This occurs because irrelevant local
      noise is suppressed under the constraints of information bottleneck in SDFIL, allowing the model to
365
      focus more on global information that contributes to the overall task. Additionally, SDFIL enables
366
      the data features of each client to remain relatively independent within the global model, allowing
367   the model to maintain high accuracy even as the number of clients increases. This independence
368   of client features reduces conflicts between different clients’ data, contributing to the stability and
369   scalability of the model. By compressing irrelevant information, SDFIL can reduce overfitting to
370   specific clients or local data, thus improving the model’s generalization capabilities. By lessening
371   its dependency on particular datasets, the model becomes more robust in the face of noise or imbal-
372   anced data, reducing accuracy loss under extreme conditions. Due to the suppression of irrelevant
373   or redundant features, the SDFIL model maintains high stability.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This study introduces and evaluates a approach to robust, spike-based federated learning within en-
      vironments characterized by noise, data heterogeneity, and client participation heterogeneity. The
532
      proposed SDFIL not only maximizes mutual information between ground truths and model predic-
533
      tions but also minimizes it between intermediate representations. Experimental results demonstrate
534   that the SDFIL framework outperforms existing methods in terms of robustness to noise across
535   various settings and degrees of heterogeneity. By mitigating the adverse effects of noisy and incon-
536   sistent data, SDFIL significantly enhances the feasibility and energy efficiency of federated learning
537   on energy-constrained devices. These findings underscore the promising applications of SNNs in
538   federated learning scenarios. Given the promising effectiveness of SDFIL, it also has limitations,
539   including lack of investigation on different SNN architectures, other types of noises, and tests on
      real-world devices. Limitations and future work are discussed in detail in Appendix A.5.

      Poisson Rate Coding The training process starts by encoding the pixel values into spike trains of
703   length T using Poisson rate coding. In Poisson coding, the relationship between the number of
704   spikes and the pixel intensity is established. At each time step, a random value is sampled from the
705   minimum to maximum possible pixel intensity range Imin to Imax . If this sampled value is below
706   the actual pixel intensity, a spike is generated, rendering the spike occurrence at each time step
707   stochastic. The accumulated number of spikes over time steps proportionally represents the pixel
708   intensity. Thus, when spikes are aggregated over time, the resulting image closely approximates the
709   original.

811
812   Given the promising results demonstrating the effectiveness of SDFIL, it also has limitations.
813
      First, the effectiveness of SDFIL may be highly dependent on the specific SNN architecture and
814
      hyperparameter settings. Different network structures may exhibit varying adaptability under non-
815
      independent and identically distributed (non-IID) data and client heterogeneity conditions. The
816   current research may not have fully explored the diversity of SNN architectures. Future studies
817   could attempt to validate the effectiveness of SDFIL across a wider range of SNN architectures,
818   investigating how different architectures impact the disentanglement results.
819
      Second, while SDFIL demonstrates strong robustness in handling gradient noise, its performance
820
      may degrade in extreme noise environments (such as very high noise levels or adversarial attacks).
821
      Different types of noise, such as asynchronous client updates or data contamination introduced by
822   malicious clients, may require further research and optimization.
823
824   Finally, the current experiments are primarily based on simulated environments, and the framework
825
      has not yet been thoroughly tested in large-scale real-world federated learning systems. In particular,
      energy consumption evaluations may lack rigorous validation on real resource-constrained devices.
826
      Future work could further test SDFIL in real IoT or mobile device scenarios to verify its performance
827
      under actual energy consumption and computational resource constraints.
828
829   Future work can include two aspects.
830   First, the current research primarily focuses on optimizing single-task federated learning. Future
831   work could consider extending SDFIL to multi-task federated learning environments, where impor-
832   tant features can be shared and disentangled across different tasks. This would help improve the
833   model’s generalization ability in multi-task and cross-domain applications.
834
      Second, future research could explore the application of SDFIL in more complex federated learning
835   scenarios, such as asynchronous update mechanisms, dynamic client participation, and data sparsity.
836   These situations are common in real-world applications, and enhancing the algorithm’s adaptability
837   and generalization in these contexts will be an important research direction.
