011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Solving stiff ordinary differential equations (ODEs) through machine learning
014            methods has been quite a popular topic for years as it challenges the recently pro-
015
               posed physics-informed neural network (PINN). Many variations based on PINN
               have been advanced to enhance both the efficiency and the robustness. Nonethe-
016
               less, many of them need to find the trade-off between the precision and speed be-
017
               cause they have to train hundreds or even thousands of parameters if they do not
018            design good or problem-adapt networks. In this scenario, we put forward a sin-
019            gle layer physics-informed neural network with exponential activation functions
020            (EPINN) by implementing the prior knowledge of the solution to the linear stiff
021            ODEs. Under this simple but useful structure, less parameters would be sufficient
022            and the model is easy to train. The model is also extended to solve nonlinear sys-
023            tems by introducing sequential EPINN. The network is tested on six benchmark
024            problems including both linear and nonlinear ones and shows great performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Stiff ordinary differential equations (ODEs) are frequently employed to describe variable phenom-
029
      ena of fields such as atmospheric chemistry, physics and chemical engineering. Formally, stiff refers
030   to the fact that both fast and slow processes exist simultaneously, in which the absolute changes of
031   different components sometimes could cover several orders of magnitude within a relatively nar-
032   row time interval, mathematically called boundary or inner layers. On the one hand, this property
033   makes the numerical solving significantly challenging for both the traditional and machine learning
034   schemes since one has to use very small time steps or a great number of neurons to capture the huge
035   change of the solution. On the other hand, stiff ODE systems like Van Der Pol’s equation and Rober
036   equations are very essential models. As a result, finding highly efficient solver for stiff ODEs is a
037   demanding and formidable work.
038   Generally speaking, ODEs can be integrated either by traditional schemes or machine learning meth-
039   ods. The traditional algorithms, such as finite difference method (FDM), mainly consist of explicit
040   and implicit methods. Nevertheless, solving stiff ODEs is still a tough task because both efficiency
041   and high stability are significantly in need especially for stiff ODEs. The explicit methods often do
042   not satisfy the stability requirement while the implicit ones are computationally intensive most of
043   the time.
044   With the development of machine learning, neural network methods have enabled advances in solv-
045   ing differential equations, among which the recently proposed physics-informed neural network
046   (PINN) embeds physics laws in the training of the network, transferring data-driven methods into
047   knowledge-driven methods Raissi et al. (2019). After being brought up, PINN has been successfully
048   applied to plenty of issues from numerous fields such as solid mechanics Haghighat et al. (2021);
049   Vahab et al. (2022); Niaki et al. (2021); Samaniego et al. (2020); Arora et al. (2022) and fluid me-
050
      chanics Mao et al. (2020); Almajid & Abu-Al-Saud (2022).
051   Despite the great performance deep learning methods have shown in many areas, when facing multi-
052   task or multi-scale problems, regular deep learning schemes are usually not so powerful. For in-
053   stance, it is known that PINN have troubles in solving stiff ODEs Karniadakis et al. (2021). Reasons
      are analyzed Wang et al. (2021) and variations are raised such as stiff PINN, MPINN, vanilla PINN


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and reduced PINN Ji et al. (2021); Weng & Zhou (2022); Baty (2023); Nasiri & Dargazany (2022).
055   Although these models improve the performance of the original network, they all have to train hun-
056   dreds or even thousands of parameters which is time-consuming. PIRPNN observes this issue and
057   introduces a single layer network to reduce the number of parameters and enhance the efficiency
058   by using the technique of random projection, while it still needs to lots of neurons to guarantee the
059   accuracy Fabiani et al. (2023).
060
      In this scenario, our interest is to construct a model with parameters as few as possible while main-
061   taining both the accuracy and efficiency. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
062
063          • We put forward a single neural network with exponential activation functions by imple-
064            menting prior knowledge of the solution to obtain a fast and accurate stiff ODE solver.
065          • Under our structure, very few parameter would suffice to get a satisfactory solution and the
066            loss function is smooth and convex so that the global optimizer can be guaranteed.
067
             • The model is tested on several benchmark stiff cases containing both linear and nonlinear
068            ones and shows great performance.
069
070   The paper is organized as follows. In section 2, we introduce the basic information about our prob-
071   lem settings and preliminaries. In section 3, we discuss the method and construct our model. In
072   section 4, we compare its performance with the famous stiff ODE solver ode15s in Matlab on six
073   benchmark cases. Finally, we draw our conclusions in section 5.

      2.3     R ELATED W ORKS
118
119   After being proposed, PINN has been successfully utilized to solve many classical differential equa-
120   tions including the Burgers’ equation, the Navier-Stokes equation and the Schrodinger equation
121   Raissi et al. (2019); Jin et al. (2021); Naderibeni et al. (2024). However, it has troubles in solving
122   stiff ODEs. Afterwards, reasons are analyzed and an adaptive algorithm is put forward to balance
123   the huge gradient difference among different loss terms Wang et al. (2021). Several variations based
124   on PINN are brought up as well.
125   Different models emphasize on different aspects. Some transfer the original equations to make
126   the training easier: Stiff PINN introduces quasi-steady state assumption to make the equations less
127   stiffer Ji et al. (2021). As a result, the model learns the solution to the milder system rather than
128   the original one. Reduced PINN transfers the governing equation into an integral one Nasiri &
129   Dargazany (2022). However, the numerical solving of the integral has to be implemented in a
130   relatively small time interval, making the method time-consuming. Some adjust the structure of
131
      the network: MPINN uses different networks to learn the fast and slow components separately but
      needs some ground truth data to guarantee the accuracy Weng & Zhou (2022), which is inaccessible
132
      in many cases. Vanilla PINN imposes some strategies like hybrid loss and moving collocation grid
133
      but cannot solve the strongly stiff cases Baty (2023). Some focus on the activation functions chosen:
134   PIRPNN uses a single layer network with Gaussian kernels as its activation functions to approximate
135   the solution Fabiani et al. (2023). It introduces random projection technique to reduce the parameters
136   and shows great performance on nonlinear stiff ODEs.

226   As we already know that the solution to system 11 can be written as the linear combination of 13,

228   activation functions. Afterwards, we apply the linear combination of σ(t) as the output, denoted as
229   N (t, W ) = W σ(t), which we call exponential physics-informed neural network(EPINN). Under
230   the format of PINN, the loss function can be constructed as:

236
      solution. It benefits a lot from constructing EPINN:
237
238           • Accuracy of the solution: By the discussion in section 3.1.1, we know our model can
239             compute the exact solution for linear systems with constant coefficients. Additionally, for
240             linear systems with variable coefficients and nonlinear systems, EPINN can also offer a
241             good approximation.
242
              • Efficiency of the training: We construct a single layer neural network similar to PIRPNN
243             Fabiani et al. (2023), where only a few weight matrix parameters are needed to be opti-
244             mized. Furthermore, less neurons would suffice as we implement the prior knowledge of
245             the solution in the design of the network, which makes the training very fast.
246
              • Flexibility of the coding: For any sample set, L(W ) is a smooth and convex function.
247
                Therefore, quite a few optimization algorithms such as the least square method and gradient
248             descent algorithms can be used and the global minimum is guaranteed. Moreover, the
249             gradient is easy to compute and there is no need to introduce auto differentiation or other
250             alternatives, which would sharply reduce the complexity of the code.

515   5   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we propose a brand new single layer PINN to solve the initial value problem of stiff
518
      ODEs by embedding the prior knowledge of the solution structure into the design of the network
      and the model shows great performance for both the linear and nonlinear stiff ODEs.
519
520   For linear systems, our model not only achieves satisfactory precision but also runs very fast. In
521   our first four linear systems, the time consumed is all almost 0s. For nonlinear ones, our scheme
522   also holds a high calculation speed against regular multi-layer PINN models while maintaining great
523   accuracy.
524   In the future, there is still an urge for designing higher-precision model to solve nonlinear stiff
525   ODEs and extending the model to solve stiff PEDs with solid mathematical foundation and abundant
526   numerical experiments as well. As for the issue this paper focus, one can try to develop the model
527   with adaptive time step techniques to further enhance the efficiency and robustness.
