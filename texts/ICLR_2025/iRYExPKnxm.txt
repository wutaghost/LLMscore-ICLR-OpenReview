009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance in long-
012            context understanding. However, current long-context LLM benchmarks are lim-
013            ited by rigid context length and labor-intensive annotation, and the label leakage
014            issue in LLM training also poses a pressing challenge. Therefore, we propose
015            ACADEMIC E VAL, a live benchmark for evaluating LLMs over long-context gen-
016            eration tasks. ACADEMIC E VAL adopts papers on arXiv to introduce several aca-
017            demic writing tasks with long-context inputs, i.e., T ITLE, A BSTRACT, I NTRO -
               DUCTION , and R ELATED W ORK, which cover a wide range of abstraction lev-
018
               els and require no manual labeling. Moreover, ACADEMIC E VAL integrates high-
019
               quality and expert-curated few-shot demonstrations from a collected co-author
020
               graph to enable flexible context length. Especially, ACADEMIC E VAL features an
021            efficient live evaluation, ensuring no label leakage. We conduct holistic experi-
022            ments on ACADEMIC E VAL, and the results illustrate that LLMs perform poorly
023            on tasks with hierarchical abstraction levels and tend to struggle with long few-
024            shot demonstrations, illustrating the challenge of our benchmark. We also provide
025            insightful analysis for enhancing LLMs’ long-context modeling capabilities.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently achieved tremendous success in natural language
031   processing (NLP) tasks Achiam et al. (2023); AI@Meta (2024). However, when facing long context
032   inputs, LLMs show a sharp decline in performance, which poses a pressing challenge to LLMs
033
      in understanding and capturing key information in long texts Li et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024).
      Therefore, several long-context LLM benchmarks are spawned to evaluate LLMs in various settings,
034
      including question answering, summarizing, and reasoning Shaham et al. (2023); An et al. (2023);
035
      Dong et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024). Despite their success,
036   these benchmarks still suffer from concerns of rigid context length, saturated performance, and being
037   leaked in LLM training.
038
039
      We envision that the next-generation long-context LLM benchmarks should ideally possess three key
      features. (1) Flexible and potentially unlimited context length: existing benchmarks fix the context
040
      for each long-context problem; ideally, the format and length of the context could be flexibly set
041
      based on the LLM’s capability, especially given the release of long-context LLMs Reid et al. (2024)
042   and their capabilities in ingesting multi-modal information, e.g., graphs Dong et al. (2024). (2)
043   High-quality labels derived from real-world data, minimizing human labeling efforts: existing long-
044   context benchmarks often require human labeling Bai et al. (2023b); An et al. (2023); Li et al.
045   (2023); Dong et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024), which is costly and limits the size of the benchmarks
046   to about 2000 samples Xu et al. (2023) (3) Live updates to mitigate information leakage during LLM
047   pretraining and fine-tuning: benchmark data contamination in LLM has gradually become a severe
048   issue Sainz et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2024b;a); Xu et al. (2024); we argue that holding
049   out future data as the val/test set is one of the most effective approaches for open benchmarks.
050   Based on these principles, we propose ACADEMIC E VAL, a live benchmark to evaluate LLMs over
051   long-context generation tasks. ACADEMIC E VAL adopts arXiv as its data source and features a suite
052   of academic writing tasks on each paper without labor-intensive annotation: T ITLE, A BSTRACT,
053   I NTRODUCTION, and R ELATED W ORK, each of which has long-context input and hierarchical ab-
      straction levels. In particular, we construct a co-author graph via arXiv API to conveniently obtain

      Long-context Modeling and LLM Benchmarks LLMs are known to be powerful in language
101
      modeling tasks Achiam et al. (2023); AI@Meta (2024). However, when it comes to long-context
102   input, LLMs show a sharp decline in performance, posing a pressing challenge when benchmark-
103   ing their long-context modeling capabilities Liu et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024). Currently, there are
104   two mainstream technologies for long-context modeling tasks: retrieval-augmented language mod-
105   els (RALM)Ram et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Trivedi et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2023); Asai et al.
106   (2023) and long-context LLMs Bai et al. (2023a); Jiang et al. (2024); Teknium et al.. RALM equips
107   LLMs with a retrieverRobertson et al. (2009); Ramos et al. (2003); Karpukhin et al. (2020); Izac-
      ard et al. (2021) to perform information retrieval on short text chunks, which are then fed to LLMs


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      together with the input query to generate the final output. As a retrieval system, RALM is usually
109   evaluated over retrieval-based benchmarks, including STARK Wu et al. (2024), RGB Chen et al.
110   (2024), ARES Saad-Falcon et al. (2023), etc. In comparison, long-context LLMs expand their con-
111   text window length to accommodate longer inputs and are benchmarked over various tasks, which
112   include long-context QA, summarization, conversations, reasoning, etc Shaham et al. (2023); An
113   et al. (2023); Dong et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024).
114
      Label Leakage in LLM Benchmarks Label leakage has always been a severe issue that bench-
115   marks must attempt to avoid during data collection. However, recent researches Xu et al. (2024);
116   Zhu et al. (2024b;a); Ye et al. (2024) point out that most LLM benchmarks are composed of statically
117   collected data, which may be inevitably included in the large amount of training data of LLMs, caus-
118   ing label leakage. Therefore, some works attempt to measure or detect the extent of label leakage in
119   LLM benchmarks. Benbench Xu et al. (2024) leverages perplexity and N-gram accuracy to quantify
120   potential label leakage, while PAC Ye et al. (2024) detects contaminated data by comparing the po-
121   larized distance of samples before and after augmentation. Even though these approaches propose
122   to measure or detect label leakage, there is little work on mitigating and solving this issue Zhu et al.
123
      (2024b). Dynabench Kiela et al. (2021) and Dynaboard Ma et al. (2021) feature dynamic human-
      in-the-loop dataset creation while avoiding leakage, which is very labor-intensive. DyVal Zhu et al.
124
      (2024b) leverages pre-set constraints and directed acyclic graphs (DAG) to dynamically generate test
125
      cases with diverse complexities, reducing the risk of label leakage. FreshBench Zhu et al. (2024a)
126   and StackMIA Ye et al. (2024) collect the latest data from public websites periodically and simply
127   rely on the chronological split to build a dynamic benchmark.
128
129
      Long-context Summarization Benchmarks Solving ACADEMIC E VAL requires LLM’s long-
      context summarization capability Liu et al. (2024). Existing works include (1) query-based summa-
130
      rization tasks, focusing on the capability of models to position and capture local key information in
131
      long texts given a specific query Litvak & Vanetik (2017); Wang et al. (2022); (2) single-document
132   or multi-document summarization tasks concentrate on evaluating the ability of models to under-
133   stand long texts holistically Cohan et al. (2018); Meng et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2021); Kryściński
134   et al. (2021); Cachola et al. (2020). These long-context summarization benchmarks suffer from the
135   above-mentioned limitations, including requiring human-assisted labeling and concerns about data
136   leakage; moreover, these summarization tasks focus on one-level summarization, failing to consider
137   the summarizations at different abstraction levels.

      ation tasks with hierarchical abstraction levels. We first describe data collection and preprocessing
      in Section 3.1. Then, in Section 3.2, four academic writing tasks with diverse abstraction levels are

148   3.1       DATA C URATION
149
150
      Co-author Graph Construction via arXiv As a public paper preprint platform, arXiv1 has always
      been favored by researchers. It archives a huge amount of papers and updates the latest ones daily,
151
      which serves as an excellent data source and also lays the foundation for the live update of our
152
      benchmark. Thanks to the arXiv API2 , paper files can be obtained in batch without much manual
153   effort. We first collect and construct a co-author graph (edges are established between two author
154   nodes that are co-authors in at least one paper) using the arXiv API through breadth-first search
155   (BFS), where the features of each author node include the first-author papers published by the author.
156   By making the co-author graph the carrier of paper data, we can form an interconnected whole of
157   scattered papers, which provides valuable structural information to be exploited for our benchmark
158   (e.g., as few-shot demonstrations). Furthermore, we can enable efficient live updates on the co-
159   author graph, which will be introduced in Section 3.3.

      3.2   B ENCHMARKING LLM S OVER L ONG - CONTEXT G ENERATION TASKS WITH
198         H IERARCHICAL A BSTRACTION
199
200   Task Description Employing machine learning approaches to automate academic writing has al-
201   ways been a research hotspot with significant practical application value Chen et al. (2022; 2021).
202   Therefore, inspired by the leave-one-out validation, we introduce four academic writing tasks with
203   ultra-long context to evaluate the generation capability of LLMs under different abstraction levels,
204   as shown below:
205
             • T ITLE W RITING . This task takes a paper’s main body and abstract, along with a specific
206
               task prompt as inputs, and then asks LLMs to output a predicted title.
207
208
             • A BSTRACT W RITING . Similar to the above, this task takes a paper’s main body and title,
               along with a specific task prompt as inputs, and then asks LLMs to output a predicted
209
               abstract.
210
211          • I NTRODUCTION W RITING . This task takes a paper’s main body (with the introduction
212            section removed), title, and abstract, along with a specific task prompt as inputs, and then
213
               asks LLMs to output a predicted introduction.
214          • R ELATED W ORK W RITING . This task takes a paper’s main body (with the related work
215            section removed), title, abstract, and citation corpus (introduced in Section 3.1), along with
               a specific task prompt as inputs, and then asks LLMs to output a predicted related work.

      Based on the above task descriptions, we can generate four basic benchmark settings with differ-

      Integration of Few-shot Demonstrations Given the rigid context length of current long-context
      LLM benchmarks and the general effectiveness of in-context learning in LLMs Dong et al. (2022);
221
      Wei et al. (2022a;b); Kojima et al. (2022), we propose to integrate long few-shot demonstrations
222
      to enable flexible and scalable context length, and we have two selection options for each sample
223   in the above four basic benchmark settings: (1) Randomly select papers under the same category.
224   According to the paper categories provided by the arXiv API, we can randomly select several non-
225   duplicate papers under the same category. (2) Randomly Select co-author papers. The motivation is
226   straightforward: the similarity of research directions between co-author papers is more fine-grained.
227   Thanks to the co-author graph, it is convenient to obtain co-author papers of each original paper
228   sample. These selected papers serve as few-shot demonstrations and are utilized as input-output
229   pairs to enrich the input context of the original samples, providing potentially insightful and relevant
230   content while enabling flexible and scalable context length.
231   Consequently, we have completed the construction of benchmark settings, and the data statistics in
232   the initial collection round are shown in Table 2.
233
      Data Statistics As shown in Table 2, ACADEMIC E VAL has four academic writing tasks with hier-
234
      archical abstraction levels, and each task features four settings with diverse input context lengths,
235   some of which are obtained by integrating few-shot demonstrations. For instance, each sample in
236   T ITLE -10K consists of a single paper sample. T ITLE -30K and T ITLE -31K-G are obtained by
237   integrating with two few-shot demonstrations from random papers and co-author papers, respec-
238   tively, while T ITLE -50K-M is obtained by using both of the above integration options. Actually,
239   we can scale context length by increasing the number of few-shot demonstrations to provide more
240   informative references, enhancing task performance.
241   Furthermore, we present the text compression rate (defined as the number of input tokens divided
242   by the number of output tokens) for each benchmark setting in Table 2 to illustrate the diverse
243   abstraction levels in ACADEMIC E VAL. Across the four tasks, a higher compression rate means
244   a higher level of text abstraction in this task. Among several settings within each task, a higher
245   compression rate makes it tougher to exploit information holistically but more likely to produce
246   better outputs (since more references are integrated). These different tasks and settings increase the
247   diversity of the ACADEMIC E VAL benchmark.
248   As for data splitting, we perform a chronological split in ACADEMIC E VAL, which means that the
249   test set always contains the latest papers collected in each collection round, ensuring no label leak-
250   age. Note that Table 2 shows only the data collected in the initial round, which will be updated
251   periodically as described in the next section.

      The daily updates of arXiv provide the basis for the live evaluation of ACADEMIC E VAL: we can
256
      periodically update the benchmark with the latest papers on arXiv. By setting a reasonable update
257   cycle (e.g., monthly or quarterly), we can ensure that the data in the benchmark is not contaminated
258   so that it can be used to evaluate LLMs fairly in a live manner. Therefore, we proposed an efficient
259   incremental update procedure on the co-author graph:
260

261
      published first-author paper through the arXiv API. If so, add it to the corresponding node feature
262
      on the co-author graph.
263
264   (2) Node and Edge Update During the traversal of Node Update, each author’s new co-authors are
265   added to a candidate list, and the number of new papers (including first-author and non-first-author
266
      papers) when searching for the author is used as the priority of the co-authors (co-authors of active
      authors tend to be active as well, and we can efficiently collect the latest papers from active authors).

316   4.1    BASELINES
317
318   We adopt the following three types of baselines to conduct a holistic evaluation of ACADEMIC E VAL.
319
320
      Standard LLMs We choose Gemma Instruct (7B) Team et al. (2024) and LLaMA-3 Chat
      (70B) AI@Meta (2024) as standard LLM baselines, each with a context length of 8K.
321
322   Long-context LLMs We choose Qwen 1.5 Chat (72B) Bai et al. (2023a), Mixtral-8x7B Instruct
323   (46.7B) Jiang et al. (2024), and Nous Hermes 2 - Mixtral 8x7B-DPO (46.7B) Teknium et al. as
      long-context LLM baselines, each with a context length of 32K.


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Retrieval-augmented language models (RALM) First, we consider two sparse retrievers: (1)
325   BM25 Robertson et al. (2009): This is a widely used retrieval model that ranks documents based
326   on the frequency of query terms in each document. (2) TF-IDF Ramos et al. (2003): It scores doc-
327   uments by multiplying the term frequency of each query term by the inverse document frequency.
328   Second, we also consider three dense retrievers: (3) DPR Karpukhin et al. (2020): It uses a bi-
329   encoder to retrieve relevant documents based on dense embeddings. (4) Contriever Izacard et al.
330   (2021): It leverages unsupervised contrastive learning to learn high-quality dense representations.
331   (5) Dragon Lin et al. (2023): It enhances retriever training by employing data augmentation, includ-
332   ing query and label augmentation.
333   We use the inputs of ACADEMIC E VAL as the external corpus of RALM. For text split, we use the
334   RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter from LangChain4 and set chunk size and chunk overlap to 512 and
335   64, respectively. For each retrieval, we recall up to 12 text chunks (limited by the context length
336   of standard LLMs) based on text similarity (semantic similarity based on inner product for dense
337   retrievers or similarity based on word frequency for sparse retrievers).
338
339   4.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
340
341
      For evaluation metrics, we adopt (1) BERTScore5 Zhang et al. (2019): This metric leverages
      BERT-based embedding to measure semantic similarity between predicted and reference texts. (2)
342
      RougeL Lin (2004): This metric evaluates the longest common subsequence between the generated
343
      and reference texts, providing a measure of similarity in terms of sequential matching. For both
344   metrics, higher scores indicate a better match between the predicted and the reference text.

      4.3    M AIN R ESULT A NALYSIS
347
348   We conduct comprehensive experiments on the four academic writing tasks, and the results w.r.t.
349   BERTScore and RougeL are presented in Table 3 and 4, respectively. Note that we do not conduct
350   experiments on -M settings because its context length is too long for most of our selected baselines.
351
      Diverse Task Difficulties and Abstractions The four tasks we proposed are designed to challenge
352   LLMs over long-context generation tasks with different abstraction levels. From Table 3 and 4,
353   we can clearly observe that it provides different difficulties for LLMs to perform well from T ITLE
354   W RITING to R ELATED W ORK W RITING tasks, and the results of all baselines on these four tasks
355   have a relatively obvious trend. For example, the T ITLE W RITING task tends to have a higher score
356   than the A BSTRACT W RITING task, which may indicate that the T ITLE W RITING task is easier
357   than the A BSTRACT W RITING task. Since a title only has a few words, LLMs only need to generate
358   a roughly related theme to achieve a high semantic similarity, while an abstract requires a more
359   detailed description generated to achieve it.
360   Baseline Performance Comparison Among different baselines, RALM with LLaMA frequently
361   delivers the highest scores across various tasks and context lengths, with only a context length of
362   8K. Standard LLMs also achieve competitive performance, which is slightly inferior to long-context
363   LLMs. This exposes the shortcomings of long-context LLMs’ generation capabilities, which is
364   well revealed by ACADEMIC E VAL. Among long-context LLMs, Hermes performs best overall,
365   but is still slightly inferior to RALM with LLaMA. This shows that although the current long-
366
      context LLMs have a longer context window size, they still have great deficiencies in processing
      long text information. In contrast, RALM-based methods generally outperform other baselines. This
367
      is primarily due to the retrieval mechanisms of RALM, which retrieves and processes information
368
      in a few relevant shorter chunks, enabling it to focus on key information.
369
370   Impact of Context Length The impact of context length on performance is evident across all task

463
464
      We conduct extensive experiments on RALM on the A BS -9K setting using standard LLMs Gemma
465   Instruct (7B) and LLaMA-3 Chat (70B), and the results are presented in Figure 2. We can find that
466   the performance of dense retrievers consistently outperforms sparse retrievers, among which con-
467   triever achieves the best results. This is because the summary generation task emphasizes semantic
468   similarity, which can be well measured by the similarity of dense embeddings. However, the sparse
469   retrievers perform text chunk recall based on sparse embeddings, and the results are significantly
470   worse than those of the dense retrievers.

473   5      C ONCLUSION
474
475
476   In this paper, we propose ACADEMIC E VAL, a live long-context LLM benchmark for evaluating
477
      long-context generation tasks with hierarchical abstraction levels. ACADEMIC E VAL adopts arXiv
      as the data source and introduces several long-context academic writing tasks without manual anno-
478
      tation since the papers on arXiv can be regarded as original, high-quality, and expert-curated labels.
479
      Moreover, we integrate few-shot demonstrations from a collected co-author graph to make the con-
480   text length of our benchmark flexible and scalable. An efficient live evaluation is also designed to
481   make ACADEMIC E VAL immune to the label leakage issue and move toward a more fair evaluation.
482   In the experiments, we conduct a comprehensive analysis on ACADEMIC E VAL using several LLM
483   baselines, and the results show that ACADEMIC E VAL is a challenging long-context LLM bench-
484   mark. Insightful findings are also elucidated for potentially strengthening the long-context modeling
485   capabilities of LLMs and inspiring future long-context LLM benchmarks to evaluate LLMs more
      flexibly and holistically.

      A.1       E VALUATION C RITERIA AND H YPERPARAMETERS
705
706   API Access. In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation over ACADEMIC E VAL bench-
707   mark using the LLM API provided by together.ai6 . For each API call, we fix the temperature pa-
708   rameter to 0 (i.e., greedy decoding).
709   Input Truncation. By default, we use a BERT tokenizer to calculate the number of input tokens
710   for ACADEMIC E VAL. However, since the tokenizer of each LLM is usually different, it will cause
711   some inputs to exceed the context length limit of the LLM. Therefore, for the evaluation of each
712   LLM, we additionally download its tokenizer configuration file from the official website at hugging
713   face, which is utilized to ensure correct and accurate truncation of input tokens.
714
      Refinement of LLM Responses. For the T ITLE W RITING task, the responses of LLMs are rela-
715   tively short. If the response contains some extra redundant information, it will have a greater impact
716   on the evaluation metric score (although we have given LLM instructions not to generate irrelevant
717   information). Therefore, for the T ITLE W RITING task, we additionally refine the LLM responses,
718   for example, removing irrelevant information such as “here is the title”. For other tasks, since LLM’s
719   responses are relatively long, occasional small amounts of irrelevant information will not have a sig-
720   nificant impact on the evaluation, so we do not perform any refinement on LLM’s responses in this
721   case.
722   Details of the implementation of RALM. We use the inputs of ACADEMIC E VAL as the external
723   corpus of RALM (such as Target Content and Reference Content introduced in Section F). For text
724   split, we use the RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter from LangChain7 and set chunk size and chunk
725   overlap to 512 and 64, respectively. For each retrieval, we recall up to 12 text chunks (limited by
726   the context length of standard LLMs) based on text similarity (semantic similarity based on inner
727   product for dense retrievers or similarity based on word frequency for sparse retrievers).

      C    L IMITATION AND F UTURE I MPROVEMENT
811
812
      ACADEMIC E VAL is a live benchmark without label leakage, which leverages co-author papers from
813   a collected co-author graph as few-shot demonstrations to make the context length flexible and
814   scalable. ACADEMIC E VAL adopts arXiv as its data source without the need for manual labeling, and
815   the content of the papers on it can naturally serve as high-quality and expert-curated annotations.
816
      However, ACADEMIC E VAL still has some limitations:
817
818         • Task Diversity. ACADEMIC E VAL currently has only four academic writing tasks, which
819           limits the task diversity.
820
            • Independent Evaluation of the Paper Section. In ACADEMIC E VAL, we independently
821
              evaluate the section content extracted from a paper, which may lack a comprehensive eval-
822           uation of the paper as a whole.
823
            • Popularity Bias. ACADEMIC E VAL first collects a co-author graph from arXiv, which con-
824
              tains a subset of all papers on arXiv. Therefore, the collected papers may have some pop-
825
              ularity bias. For example, most of the papers may come from a few active authors, which
826           will cause bias in the evaluation.
827
828   Based on the above limitations, our future improvements will include:
829
830         • Introduce More Data Source. The goal of ACADEMIC E VAL is to make context length
831           flexible and scalable by using few-shot demonstrations and high-quality labels without
832
              manual annotation, so papers on arXiv are a more suitable data source. We will con-
              sider adding other websites as data sources in the future, such as some question-answering
833
              websites (Stack Overflow or Reddit, etc.). In this case, we can use the best answers as
834
              high-quality labels. By modeling the citation relationship between posts into a graph, we
835           can also obtain few-shot demonstrations to enrich the context length.
836
            • K-fold Cross-validation. We can use k-fold cross-validation for a paper, that is, leaving a
837
              section (or fold) as the label, the remaining sections as inputs, and finally calculating the
838
              average of all leave-one-out evaluation scores.
839
840
            • Eliminate Popularity Bias. We will perform probabilistic sampling on papers when col-
              lecting the co-author graph and give a lower sampling probability to active authors to alle-
841
              viate the impact of popularity bias.

      D    S OCIAL I MPACT
865
866
      The proposed benchmark ACADEMIC E VAL will promote the academic community’s exploration
867   of using LLMs to automate academic writing tasks. Here are some key points highlighting its
868   significance:
869
870         • Efficiency and Productivity. LLMs can drastically reduce the time and effort required
871           for various academic writing tasks. These tasks include drafting papers, writing litera-
872           ture reviews, summarizing research articles, and generating bibliographies. By automating
873           these processes, researchers can focus more on high-level thinking, experimentation, and
874
              analysis.
875         • Enhanced Writing Quality. LLMs have the ability to produce coherent and grammatically
876           correct text, which can improve the overall quality of academic writing. They can assist in
877           refining arguments, improving clarity, and ensuring consistency in style and tone, which is
878
              particularly useful for non-native English speakers.
879         • Support for Multidisciplinary Research. Given their training on diverse topics, LLMs
880           can assist researchers in exploring interdisciplinary approaches by providing information
881           and generating content across various fields of study. This can foster innovation and en-
882
              courage collaboration between different academic disciplines.

      E   D ETAILS OF L IVE L EADERBOARD
919
920
      To enhance usability, we aim to create a live leaderboard on Hugging Face to help users easily
921   utilize our benchmarks and compare various models. Our leaderboard will provide the following
922   functionalities:
923
924        1. Live updates and time selection: We will update the dataset periodically to ensure it
925           includes the latest papers from arXiv, which the LLMs have never seen before, to prevent
926           label leakage. Users can choose the version they wish to use.
927        2. Different abstraction tasks: We will create separate leaderboards for each task. Users are
928           welcome to run their models on one or more tasks and report their results.
929        3. Ease of use: We will provide detailed and standardized instructions so that users can easily
930           run the pipeline with their models and obtain the results.
