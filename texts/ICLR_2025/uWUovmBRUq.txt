011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The primary goal of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection tasks is to identify in-
014            puts with semantic shifts, i.e., if samples from novel classes are absent in the
015
               in-distribution (ID) dataset used for training, we should reject these OOD samples
               rather than misclassifying them into existing ID classes. However, we find the
016
               current definition of “semantic shift” is ambiguous, which renders certain OOD
017
               testing protocols intractable for the post-hoc OOD detection methods based on a
018            classifier trained on the ID dataset. In this paper, we offer a more precise definition
019            of the Semantic Space and the Covariate Space for the ID distribution, allowing
020            us to theoretically analyze which types of OOD distributions make the detection
021            task intractable. To avoid the flaw in the existing OOD settings, we further define
022            the “Tractable OOD” setting which ensures the distinguishability of OOD and ID
023            distributions for the post-hoc OOD detection methods. Finally, we conduct sev-
024            eral experiments to demonstrate the necessity of our definitions and validate the
025            correctness of our theorems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection has gained increasing attention in recent years due to its crucial
030
      role in ensuring algorithmic reliability. While deep learning models excel on in-distribution (ID)
031   samples, i.e., those belonging to the same classes as the training set, these models often struggle
032   when faced with unseen OOD classes Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017). For example, a model trained
033   on a fruit classification dataset may generate arbitrary and incorrect results when presented with
034   an image of an animal. Their primary objective of OOD detection is to enable models to identify
035   inputs that fall outside the ID distribution and reject them, rather than blindly classifying them
036   into existing ID classes, thus mitigating potential security risks. Existing OOD detection research
037   has made significant progress Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017); Liang et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2018);
038   Hendrycks et al. (2019); Hsu et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2021). Particularly in
039
      the more extensively studied and widely adopted post-hoc OOD detection methods Hendrycks &
      Gimpel (2017); Liang et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2018); Liu et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2021); Sun
040
      et al. (2022); Sun & Li (2022); Song et al. (2022); Djurisic et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023); Xu
041
      et al. (2024), they leave the original model training unchanged and instead utilize the features or
042   prediction outputs of the test data to assess whether a sample is OOD, making the OOD detection
043   process highly efficient.
044
045
      However, we find the current definition of OOD detection setting to be flawed, which renders certain
      OOD testing protocols intractable for the post-hoc OOD detection methods and thus undermines the
046
      significance of the OOD detection task. In existing OOD literature, OOD samples are typically
047
      defined as data exhibiting semantic shifts relative to the training set Yang et al. (2024a), in which the
048   concept of “semantic shift” lacks clear definition and boundaries. For the example shown in Fig. 1,
049   if an ID dataset contains classes of dog breeds like “collie”, “husky”, and “chihuahua”, while the
050   OOD data includes a novel breed “beagle”, the OOD detection task is well-defined as the model
051   can identify the OOD class by the “breed-specific feature” it learns from the “breed-separated”
052   training setup. However, if the model is trained on an object classification dataset that aggregates
053   the different dog breeds into a single class “dog”, while the OOD data still includes the novel breed
      “beagle” that is absent in the ID data, whether to consider this variation on dog breeds a “semantic

      Therefore, we believe that the scope of “semantic shift” should be more precisely defined based on
084
      the characteristics of the training data, rather than by the object classes as in existing works.
085
086   To address this issue, we first provide a more precise definition for the Semantic Space and the
087   corresponding Covariate Space in this paper. Specifically, we construct the Semantic Space using a
088
      linear span of representative feature vectors from each ID class. These representative feature vectors
      are the centers of each ID class’s distribution, capturing the overall characteristics of the samples
089
      within that class. With the defined Semantic Space, we can represent the Covariate Space using
090
      direct sum decomposition and then identify the range of shifts that a model cannot distinguish when
091   it is exposed solely to the ID data. Our theoretical analysis shows that: “if two classes do not
092   exhibit any shift in the Semantic Space, they will be indistinguishable by a post-hoc OOD detection
093   model based on a classifier trained only on the ID dataset”. This theorem well explains the difficulty
094   of distinguishing ambiguous OOD classes, such as the intractable case in Fig. 1, where the shift
095   occurs only within the Covariate Space for the model trained under the “breed-aggregated” setup.
096   Furthermore, we propose the “Tractable OOD” setting, where the OOD shift must occur within
097   the Semantic Space we define, thereby mitigating the issue of undetectability. Finally, extensive
098   experiments are conducted to effectively demonstrate the necessity of our definitions and the validity
099
      of the theoretical analysis.
100   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
101
102           • We provide more accurate definitions for the Semantic Space and the Covariate Space
103             within the OOD detection tasks. Based on these definitions, we introduce the “Tractable
104             OOD” setting, which ensures the OOD detection task is tractable.
105
106           • We present a theoretical analysis that proves the intractability of detecting OOD classes
107             that exhibit no shift within the defined Semantic Space. The analysis enhances our under-
                standing of the flaw in the current OOD detection definition.

114   Below, we provide the notions for Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection as described in most existing
115   works Morteza & Li (2022); Du et al. (2024b); Yang et al. (2024b).
116   Labeled ID distribution. Let the input space X be a d-dimensional space Rd . The label space for
117   ID data is denoted as YI = {y1 , ..., yk }. The ID data is independently and identically sampled from
118   the joint distribution (x, y) ∼ PI defined over X × YI .
119
      OOD distribution. The input of the OOD distribution is also from the space X but with OOD label
120
      YO = {yo }, where yo ∈ / YI . The OOD data is drawn from the joint distribution (x, y) ∼ PO
121
      defined over X × YO . For OOD detection, only the input x is available for use by the models.
122
123   However, the definition above lacks explicit connections between the input and the label, complicat-
124   ing the analysis of the model’s training process on these data. In real-world tasks, samples sharing
125
      the same label inevitably exhibit common features, e.g., school buses are typically yellow. There-
      fore, following the previous setup in Morteza & Li (2022), we assume that the input of each class can
126
      be modeled as a Gaussian distribution in this paper. To further simplify the theoretical analysis, we
127
      also assume that all the classes in the input space are linearly separable, allowing the model trained
128   on these data to be treated as a linear classifier. The modified definition of the OOD detection task
129   is as follows:
130
131
      OOD Detection in Gaussians. For the class-conditional ID distribution (x, yi ) ∼ PI |yi , we
      assume the input follows a Gaussian distribution x ∼ N (µi , I), where µi denotes the representa-
132
      tive feature vector. In addition, all the representative feature vectors in the input space are linearly
133
      separable. Similarly, the OOD input follows a Gaussian distribution N (µo , I) with µo as the repre-
134   sentative feature vector. There exists a shift between OOD and ID distributions, with the minimum
135   distance between them denoted as δ:
136
137                                      ∥µo − µi ∥22 ≥ δ, i ∈ {1, ..., k}.                               (1)
138
139   Remark. Defining the OOD problem as described above simplifies the analysis by avoiding the
140   complexities introduced by nonlinear transformations during feature extraction in deep neural net-
141   work training. Our experimental results demonstrate that this simplification does not compromise
142   the validity of the conclusions presented in the subsequent sections.
143   Building on the OOD detection setup above, we now introduce our definitions of the Semantic Space
144   and the Covariate Space for the ID distribution.
145
      Definition 1 (Semantic Space). The Semantic Space S based on ID distributions is
146
147                                 S = span({µ1 − µ2 , ..., µk − µk−1 }).                                (2)
148
149   Remark. The definition of the Semantic Space relies on the differences between the representative
150   feature vectors. This approach ensures that each class’s representative feature vector has distinct
151   components in the Semantic Space while removing any common elements shared across all repre-
152   sentative feature vectors.
153
      Definition 2 (Covariate Space). The Covariate Space C is defined using direct sum decomposition
154
155                                                X = S ⊕ C.                                             (3)
156
157   Remark. It is important to note that the defined Covariate Space is determined by the Semantic
158
      Space, meaning the Covariate Space also depends on the ID data in our definition. For instance, in
      object classification tasks, the image background is considered part of the Covariate Space, whereas
159
      it is not in scene recognition tasks.
160
161   To better understand the defined Semantic Space and Covariate Space, we present the following
      proposition which explains the practical significance of the definition.

172   With the definitions of Semantic Space and Covariate Space established, we can briefly analyze
173   the intractable case in Fig. 1. Under the “breed-separated” setup, where different classes represent
174
      distinct dog breeds, the differences between the representative feature vectors of each class capture
      the distinctions between breeds. According to our definition, the “breed-specific feature” belongs to
175
      the Semantic Space. However, under the “breed-aggregated” setup, since different classes represent
176
      different objects, the differences between the representative feature vectors do not reflect breed
177   distinctions, placing the “breed-specific feature” within the Covariate Space. Thus, we conclude
178   that the range of the Semantic Space and the Covariate Space depends on the training setups, and
179   post-hoc OOD detection models should be sensitive only to shifts within the Semantic Space. In the
180   next section, we provide a concrete theoretical analysis to support this conclusion.

      Finally, we can derive the following corollary, which proves the intractability of the post-hoc OOD
      detection models in detecting certain samples, like the case in Fig. 1.
246
247   Corollary 1. If the representative feature vector µo of an OOD distribution N (µo , I) is the same
248   as any of the ID distribution in the Semantic Space S, it becomes intractable for any post-hoc OOD
249   detection method to identify the class.
250   Remark. The corollary can be easily derived from Theorem 1. Since the representative feature vec-
251   tor of the OOD distribution is the same as that of a certain ID distribution in the Semantic Space,
252   Theorem 1 implies that the output distributions of these two after passing through the linear classi-
253   fier f trained on the ID distribution will be identical. Consequently, any post-hoc OOD detection
254   method based on the classifier’s output will be unable to distinguish between these two distributions,
255   rendering the OOD detection in this case an intractable problem.
256   Given the intractability under the current ambiguous definition of OOD classes, as demonstrated in
257   Corollary 1, we propose a more rigorous definition of OOD setting as below, ensuring the OOD
258   detection task is tractable for the post-hoc model trained on the ID dataset.
259
      Definition 4 (Tractable OOD). We define the input from a Gaussian N (µo , I) a δ-OOD distribution
260
      if its representative feature vector µo is greater than δ away from the vector of any ID distributions
261   in the Semantic Space S
262
263                                     ∥so − si ∥22 ≥ δ, i ∈ {1, ..., k},
                                                                                                       (10)
264                               where so = projS (µo ), si = projS (µi ).

266   The definition ensures that the representative feature vector of the OOD distribution differs from any
267   ID distribution within the Semantic Space, thereby avoiding the situation in Corollary 1, where the
268   OOD output distribution is identical to one of the ID output distribution. Additionally, we quantify
269   the degree of the shift using δ. Generally, a larger δ increases the distance between the OOD and the
      known ID distributions, making it easier for models to identify the OOD samples.

527   6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   In this paper, we identify flaws in the existing OOD detection settings and introduce a more precise
530   definition of the Semantic Space and the corresponding Covariate Space for the OOD detection task.
531   Based on this definition, we theoretically analyze cases where OOD detection becomes intractable
532   for post-hoc methods. To address the issue, we further propose the “Tractable OOD” setting, which
533   ensures the distinguishability between OOD and ID distributions during the OOD testing process.
534   Finally, we conduct extensive experiments that validate our theoretical analysis.
535   Limitation. The theoretical analysis is conducted in low-dimensional spaces for simplification,
536   under the assumption of linear separability between Gaussian-like classes. Although experimental
537   results confirm the validity of our theorem with high-dimensional image inputs and nonlinear classi-
538   fiers, there remains a lack of theoretical proof for more general training scenarios. Future work can
539   explore the definitions of Semantic Space and Covariate Space and theoretically study their impact
      on OOD detection tasks without imposing restrictions on the input space and classifier.
