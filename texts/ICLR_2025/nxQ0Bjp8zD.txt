024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   We investigate the in-context learning ability of transformers in addressing the mixture of regression
027   (MoR) problem (De Veaux, 1989; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994). The MoR model is widely applied in
028   various domains, including clustered federated learning, collaborative filtering, and healthcare (Deb
029   & Holmes, 2000; Viele & Tong, 2002; Kleinberg & Sandler, 2008; Faria & Soromenho, 2010; Ghosh
030   et al., 2020), to address heterogeneity in data, often arising from multiple data sources. We consider
031
      linear MoR models where independent and identically distributed samples pxi , yi q P Rd ˆ R, for
      i “ 1, . . . , n, are assumed to follow the model yi “ xβi , xi y ` vi , where vi „ N p0, ϑ2 q represents
032
      observation noise, independent of xi , and βi P Rd is an unknown regression vector. Specifically,
033
      there are K distinct regression vectors tβk˚ uK  k“1 , and each βi is independently drawn from these
034
      vectors according to the distribution tπk uK k“1 The goal for a new test sample, xn`1 , is to predict its
                                                      .
035
      label yn`1 . Specifically, we are interested in the meta-learning setup for MoR (Kong et al., 2020).
036
037   In a recent intriguing work, through a mix of theory and experiments, Pathak et al. (2024) examined
038   the performance of transformers for learning MoR models. However, their theoretical result suffers
039
      from the following major drawback: They only showed that the existence of a transformer architec-
      ture that is capable of implementing the oracle Bayes optimal predictor for the linear MoR problem.
040
      That is, they assume the availability of tβk˚ uK
                                                     k“1 which are in practice unknown and are to be es-
041
      timated. Hence, there remains a gap in the theoretical understanding of how transformers actually
042   perform parameter estimation and prediction in MoR. Furthermore, their theoretical result is rather
043   disconnected from their empirical observations which focused on in-context learning. Indeed, they
044   leave open a theoretical characterization of the problem of in-context learning MoR (Pathak et al.,
045   2024, Section 4). In this work, we show that transformers are actually capable of in-context learning
046   linear MoR via implementing the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, a double-loop algo-
047   rithm, wherein each inner loop involves multiple steps of gradient ascent.
048   The EM algorithm is a classic method for estimation and prediction in the MoR model (Balakrishnan
049   et al., 2017; Kwon et al., 2019; Kwon & Caramanis, 2020; Wang et al., 2024). A major limitation
050   of the EM algorithm is its tendency to converge to local maxima rather than the global maximum
051   of the likelihood function. This issue arises because the algorithm’s performance crucially depends
052   on the initialization (Jin et al., 2016). To mitigate this, favorable initialization strategies based on
053   spectral methods (Chaganty & Liang, 2013; Zhang et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2020) are typically em-
      ployed alongside the EM algorithm. Via our experiments, we empirically demonstrate that trained

076   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
077
      Transformers and optimization algorithms: Garg et al. (2022) successfully demonstrated that
078
      transformers can be trained to perform in-context learning (ICL) for linear function classes, achiev-
079   ing results comparable to those of the optimal least squares estimator. Beyond their empirical suc-
080   cess, numerous studies have sought to uncover the mechanisms by which transformers facilitate
081   ICL. Recent investigations suggest that transformers may internally execute first-order Gradient De-
082   scent (GD) to perform ICL, a concept explored in depth by Akyürek et al. (2023), Bai et al. (2024),
083   Von Oswald et al. (2023a), Von Oswald et al. (2023b), Ahn et al. (2024), and Zhang et al. (2024).
084   Specifically, Akyürek et al. (2023) identified fundamental operations that transformers can execute,
085   such as multiplication and affine transformations, showing that transformers can implement GD
086   for linear regression using these capabilities. Building on this, Bai et al. (2024) provided detailed
087
      constructions illustrating how transformers can implement convex risk minimization across a wide
      range of standard machine learning problems, including least squares, ridge, lasso, and generalized
088
      linear models (GLMs). Further, Ahn et al. (2024) demonstrated that a single-layer linear trans-
089
      former, when optimally parameterized, can effectively perform a single step of preconditioned GD.
090   Zhang et al. (2024) expanded on this by showing that every one-step GD estimator, with a learnable
091   initialization, can be realized by a linear transformer block (LTB) estimator.
092
093
      Moving beyond first-order optimization methods, Fu et al. (2023) revealed that transformers can
      achieve convergence rates comparable to those of the iterative Newton’s Method, which are expo-
094
      nentially faster than GD, particularly in the context of linear regression. These insights collectively
095
      highlight the sophisticated computational abilities of transformers in ICL, aligning closely with clas-
096   sical optimization techniques. In addition to exploring how transformers implement these mecha-
097   nisms, recent studies have also focused on their training dynamics in the context of linear regression
098   tasks; see, for example, Zhang et al. (2023) and Chen et al. (2024). In comparison to the aforemen-
099   tioned works, in the context of MoR, we demonstrate that transformers are capable of implementing
100   double-loop algorithms like the EM algorithm.
101
      EM Algorithm: The analysis of the standard EM algorithm for mixture of Gaussian and linear
102   MoR models has a long-standing history (Wu (1983),McLachlan & Krishnan (2007), Tseng (2004)).
103   Balakrishnan et al. (2017) first proved that EM algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a local
104   region close to the maximum likelihood estimator with explicit statistical and computational rates of
105   convergence. Subsequent works (Kwon et al., 2019; 2021) established improved convergence results
106   for mixture of regression under different SNR conditions. Kwon & Caramanis (2020) extended these
107   results to mixture of regression with many components. Gradient EM algorithm was first analyzed
      by Wang et al. (2015) and Balakrishnan et al. (2017). It is an immediate variant of the standard EM

                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      algorithm where the M-step is achieved by one-step gradient ascent rather than exact maximization.
109   They proved that the gradient EM also can achieve the local convergence with explicit finite sample
110   statistical rate of convergence. Global convergence for the case of two-components mixture of
111   Gaussian model was show by Xu et al. (2016); Daskalakis et al. (2017); Wu & Zhou (2021). The
112   case of unbalanced mixtures was handled by Weinberger & Bresler (2022). Penalized EM algorithm
113   for handling high-dimensional mixture models was analyzed by Zhu et al. (2017), Yi & Caramanis
114   (2015) and Wang et al. (2024), showing that gradient EM can achieve linear convergence to the
115   unknown parameter under mild conditions.

525   6   D ISCUSSION
526
527
      We explored the behavior of transformers in handling linear MoR problems, demonstrating their
      strong in-context learning capabilities through both theoretical analysis and empirical experiments.
528
      Specifically, we showed that transformers can internally implement the EM algorithm for linear
529
      MoR tasks. Our findings also reveal that transformer performance improves in high signal-to-noise
530   ratio (SNR) settings and are less suseptible to initializations. Additionally, we examined the sam-
531   ple complexity involved in pretraining transformers with a finite number of ICL training instances,
532   offering valuable insights into their practical performance.
533
      Our empirical and theoretical findings point to several promising directions for future research.
534
      First, while our results demonstrate that transformers can internally implement the EM algorithm,
535
      investigating the use of looped transformers, as discussed in Giannou et al. (2023), could reduce
536   architectural complexity in in-context linear MoR problems. Next, understanding the training dy-
537   namics of transformers for linear MoR problems remains a highly interesting and challenging task.
538   Finally, extending these results to general non-linear MoR models would be a significant and im-
539   pactful direction for future work.
