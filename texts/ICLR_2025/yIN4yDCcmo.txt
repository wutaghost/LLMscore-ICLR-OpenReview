011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated outstanding per-
014            formance in various general multimodal applications such as image recogni-
015            tion and visual reasoning, and have also shown promising potential in special-
016            ized domains. However, the application potential of LVLMs in the insurance
017
               domain—characterized by rich application scenarios and abundant multimodal
               data—has not been effectively explored. There is no systematic review of multi-
018
               modal tasks in the insurance domain, nor a benchmark specifically designed to
019
               evaluate the capabilities of LVLMs in insurance. This gap hinders the development
020            of LVLMs within the insurance domain. In this paper, we systematically review and
021            distill multimodal tasks for four representative types of insurance: auto insurance,
022            property insurance, health insurance, and agricultural insurance. We propose INS-
023            MMBench, the first comprehensive LVLMs benchmark tailored for the insurance
024            domain. INS-MMBench comprises a total of 8,856 thoroughly designed multiple-
025            choice questions, covering 12 meta-tasks and 22 fundamental tasks. Furthermore,
026            we evaluate multiple representative LVLMs, including closed-source models such
027            as GPT-4o and open-source models like BLIP-2. Our evaluation not only validates
028
               the effectiveness of our benchmark but also provides an in-depth performance
               analysis of current LVLMs on various multimodal tasks in the insurance domain.
029
               We hope that INS-MMBench will facilitate the further application of LVLMs in
030
               the insurance domain and inspire interdisciplinary development. We will release
031            our dataset and evaluation code.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkably powerful semantic
038   understanding and conversational capabilities (Wei et al., 2022; Kasneci et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
039
      2023a; Shen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022), profoundly impacting human work and life. Building
      on this foundation, Large Visual Language Models (LVLMs) have taken a further step by mapping
040
      and aligning visual and textual features, enabling the processing and interaction with multimodal
041
      data (Bai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c; Yin et al., 2023). Researchers have found
042   that LVLMs exhibit exceptional performance in general tasks such as image recognition, document
043   parsing, and OCR processing (Yang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2023). Beyond exploring
044   general capabilities, researchers have also begun to apply LVLMs to various specialized domains such
045   as healthcare (Hu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a), autonomous driving (Dewangan et al., 2023; Li
046   et al., 2024b) and social media content analysis (Lyu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). By exploring
047   the capabilities of LVLMs in specialized domains through qualitative and quantitative methods, these
048   studies have demonstrated various application potentials.
049   Insurance, as a discipline encompassing numerous multimodal application scenarios, involves ex-
050   tensive use of multimodal data and computer vision algorithms in its actual operations (Fernando
051   et al., 2022; Sahni et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2018). This offers vast potential for the
052   integration of LVLMs with the insurance industry. For instance, in auto insurance, analyzing images
053   of damaged vehicles can enable quick assessments and accurate estimations of damage (Mallios
      et al., 2023). Similarly, in property insurance, analyzing images of buildings can help evaluate

      2.1   L ARGE V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
116
117   With the rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Chang et al., 2024; Wei et al.,
118   2022; Huang et al., 2022), researchers are leveraging the powerful generalization capabilities of
119   these pre-trained LLMs for processing and understanding multimodal data (Ye et al., 2023; Zhao
120   et al., 2023b; Deshmukh et al., 2023). A key area of focus is the use of Large Vision-Language
121   Models (LVLMs) for visual inputs. LVLMs employ visual encoders and visual-to-language adapters
122   to encode the visual features from image data and align these features with textual features. The
123   combined features are then processed by pre-trained LLMs, leading to significant advancements in
124
      visual recognition and understanding (Yin et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023).
125   Various open-source and closed-source LVLMs are continuously emerging. In the realm of open-
126   source models, notable examples include LLaMA-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al.,
127   2024), BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023c), MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), and InternVL (Chen et al., 2023).
128   These models have successfully integrated visual and textual modalities, achieving commendable
129   results. In the closed-source domain, representative models include GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), GPT-
130
      4V (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Google, 2024), and Qwen-VL (Team, 2024), all of which have
      demonstrated outstanding performance in numerous tests and evaluations (Yang et al., 2023; Fu et al.,
131
      2023; Li et al., 2023f). We intend to evalute both open-source and closed-source LVLMs to verify the
132
      capability of different models in the insurance domain.

      2.2   B ENCHMARKS FOR L ARGE V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
135
136   As research into LVLMs intensifies, an increasing number of researchers are proposing benchmarks
137   to evaluate the capabilities of models (Ye et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023a; Chen
138   et al., 2024b). Based on the scope of capability evaluation, these studies can be categorized into three
139   types: task-specific benchmarks, comprehensive benchmarks, and domain-specific benchmarks.
140
141   Comprehensive benchmarks are characterized by their breadth and generality. Researchers
142   construct these benchmarks by defining and categorizing the general capabilities and tasks of LVLMs,
143   resulting in a comprehensive and wide-ranging evaluation. Representative studies include LVLM-
144
      eHub (Xu et al., 2023), SEED-Bench (Li et al., 2023b;a), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c), MME, and
      MMT-Bench (Ying et al., 2024).
145
146
      Task-specific benchmarks focus on particular tasks and types of visual data, providing detailed
147
      task definitions. Examples include SciFIBench (Roberts et al., 2024) for scientific images, MMC-
148   Benchmark (Liu et al., 2023b) for charts, MVBench (Li et al., 2023d) (using video frames as input)
149   for videos and SEED-Bench-2-Plus (Li et al., 2024a) for web pages, charts and maps.
150
151   Domain-specific benchmarks are designed for visual tasks within specific professional domain.
152   Due to the specialized knowledge and unique tasks of these domains, general benchmark cannot
153   fully meet the needs of evaluating LVLMs in these areas. As a result, researchers have begun
154   proposing specialized benchmarks for domains such as healthcare (OmniMedVQA (Hu et al., 2024)),
155   mathematics (Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b), autonomous driving (Talk2BEV-Bench (Dewangan
156   et al., 2023)), and geography (Roberts et al., 2023). However, as mentioned previously, the insurance
157   domain and even the finance domain currently lack corresponding domain-specific benchmarks for
158
      LVLMs (Chen et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2023e; Lin et al., 2024). Our work introduces INS-MMBench to
      address this gap, aiming for a significant advancements in the application of LVLMs in the insurance
159
      domain.

      3.1     TASKS
203
      Given the differences in workflows among various types of insurance in practical operations, we
204   select four core types for building this benchmark: auto insurance, commercial/household property
205   insurance, health insurance, and agricultural insurance. Our selection is based on a comprehensive
206   consideration of both the wide coverage these types offer across personal and general insurance,
207   as well as the unique visual tasks associated with each. On the one hand, These categories cover
208   both life and property insurance, which are the most prevalent in the insurance market and highly
209   representative (Weedige et al., 2019; Driver et al., 2018). On the other hand, these insurance types
210   are chosen for their distinct multimodal tasks that are closely aligned with practical applications in
211   the field. For instance, auto insurance involves the assessment of vehicle damage through visual
212
      inspection, while property insurance covers evaluations of damaged buildings or personal property.
213   To ensure that our evaluation tasks closely align with real-world applications in the insurance domain
214   and fully demonstrate the capabilities of LVLMs in this context, we have developed a bottom-
215   up hierarchical task definition methodology. Using this methodology, we construct a systematic
      visual task framework specifically tailored for the insurance sector. As an example, we discuss the

247   information collection, condition verification, and underwriting decision-making. Further, based
248   on these key visual elements, we define the fundamental tasks. For example, the need to extract
249   license plate information led to the definition of the License Plate Recognition task, while the need to
250   monitor risky driving behavior resulted in the In-car Driving Driving Behavior Detection task. By
251   following this process, we define a total of nine fundamental tasks for auto insurance. Finally, we
252   cluster these fundamental tasks based on their characteristics, forming four meta-tasks. Through this
253   approach, we have constructed a comprehensive set of 12 meta-tasks and 22 fundamental tasks across
254   the four types of insurance.

      4.3     E RROR ANALYSIS AND MITIGATION
433
434   To provide further insights into the limitations of LVLMs in the insurance domain, we conduct an
435   in-depth analysis of the errors made by selected models on the INS-MMBench. We examine the error
436   patterns of three models: GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Flash, and Qwen-VL-Max, categorizing the errors into
437   four types: perception errors (where LVLMs do not recognize or detect objects or content within the
438   image), lack of insurance knowledge or reasoning ability (where LVLMs can recognize and perceive
439   visual content but lack the necessary insurance knowledge or reasoning skills to correctly answer the
440
      question), refusal to answer (where LVLMs decline to respond to questions they deem sensitive or
      illegal), and failure to follow instructions (where LVLMs do not adhere to the provided instructions,
441
      resulting in irrelevant responses).

459   The error analysis results for these models are illustrated in Figure 4. The most common error type is
460   the lack of insurance knowledge or reasoning ability, which accounts for 59.5%, 63.6%, and 57.2%
461   of the errors in GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Flash, and Qwen-VL-Max, respectively. Due to insufficient
462   specialized knowledge and analytical skills in the insurance field, LVLMs struggle to accurately
463   assess and judge factors such as risk conditions and the extent of damage. Therefore, optimizing
464   LVLMs for the insurance domain should primarily focus on enriching domain-specific knowledge
465
      and enhancing professional capabilities. Perception errors are the second most significant error
      type. Limited by the capabilities of the visual encoder, LVLMs often fail to fully recognize and
466
      capture detailed content in images, leading to misinterpretations. For instance, GPT-4o misidentifies a
467
      damaged farmland image as ‘an abstract or close-up view of a textured surface with blue and purple
468   hues’. This type of error is common across LVLMs. Additionally, due to built-in safety monitoring
469   functions, GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Flash sometimes incorrectly flag images as illegal and refuse
470   to respond. Qwen-VL-Max, on the other hand, struggles with following instructions, occasionally
471   outputting content in Chinese, which compromises result accuracy.
472
      To address the challenge of insufficient specialized knowledge and analytical skills in the insurance
473
      field, we employ prompt engineering as a mitigation method. Specifically, we integrate additional
474   insurance-related information into the original prompts, such as detailed explanations of damage type
475   and severity assessment criteria, to supplement the model’s knowledge and support its analytical
476   reasoning. To validate the effectiveness of this approach, we select five models and evaluate them on
477   three tasks that require significant domain expertise in insurance: House Damage Type Detection,
478   Crop Growth Stage Detection, and Vehicle Damage Severity Detection. For each task, we randomly
479   sample 100 instances to create a test set.
480   As shown in Table 6, the results demonstrate that model performance significantly improves in
481   most cases when enhanced prompts are used. However, in some instances, particularly in the
482   vehicle damage detection tasks for Qwen-VL-Max and Qwen-VL-Plus, the inclusion of additional
483   information leads to confusion when it conflicts with the model’s existing reasoning, causing a
484   decline in accuracy. This finding highlights both the effectiveness of prompt engineering as a simple
485   and generalizable method and underscores the need to focus on enhancing LVLMs’ specialized
      knowledge and analytical skills in the insurance domain for further performance improvements.

      5      D ISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSIONS
498
499
      In this paper, we introduce INS-MMBench, a multimodal benchmark tailored for the insurance
      domain. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first initiative to systematically review multimodal
500
      tasks within this sector and establish a specialized benchmark specifically for it. INS-MMBench
501
      comprises 8,856 multiple-choice visual questions, covering four types of insurance, 12 meta-tasks,
502   and 22 fundamental tasks, effectively supporting the assessment of LVLMs’ applications in insurance.
503   Additionally, we evaluate several mainstream LVLMs and provide a detailed analysis of the results,
504   offering an initial exploration into the feasibility of employing LVLMs in the insurance sector and
505   providing support for future applications and research directions of LVLMs in this field. We hope
506   our benchmark and findings will guide future research and promote interdisciplinary integration and
507   practical applications within the sector.
508   However, this study has some limitations. A constraint is the lack of open-source image datasets
509   specific to the insurance domain, primarily due to privacy concerns. The image data utilized in this
510   study, sourced from publicly available datasets, undergoes rigorous curation to ensure that it aligns
511   as closely as possible with real-world insurance application scenarios. Nevertheless, since these
512   images do not from actual insurance cases, there remains an inherent potential for some degree of
513   discrepancy. This issue underscores the need for collaborative efforts between insurance companies
514   and the academic community to develop dedicated open-source image datasets for the insurance
515   domain. Another limitation is that INS-MMBench disaggregates the tasks of LVLMs into various
516
      fundamental tasks, assessing LVLM performance from a micro perspective based on task-specific
      accuracy. In reality, visual tasks in insurance often entail complex integration of multiple capabilities
517
      and comprehensive analysis. Addressing this, our next step is to construct a more complex, integrated
518
      application benchmark to enable a deeper evaluation of LVLM applications in the insurance domain.
