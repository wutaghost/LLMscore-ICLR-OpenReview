009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Computer vision is a critical component in a wide range of real-world applications,
012            including plant monitoring in agriculture and handwriting classification in digital
013            systems. However, developing high-quality computer vision systems traditionally
014            requires both machine learning (ML) expertise and domain-specific knowledge,
015            making the process labor-intensive, costly, and inaccessible to many. To address
016
               these challenges, we introduce AutoModel, an LLM agent framework that au-
               tonomously builds and optimizes image classification models. By leveraging the
017
               collaboration of specialized LLM agents, AutoModel removes the need for ML
018
               practitioners or domain experts for model development, streamlining the process
019            and democratizing image classification. In this work, we evaluate AutoModel
020            across a diverse range of datasets consisting of varying sizes and domains, includ-
021            ing standard benchmarks and Kaggle competition datasets, demonstrating that it
022            consistently outperforms zero-shot LLM-generated pipelines and achieves human
023            practitioner-level performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Computer vision has emerged as a powerful tool for addressing many complex real-world problems,
028
      from plant monitoring in agriculture to handwriting classification in digital systems. However, the
029
      process of training computer vision models has grown increasingly complex, involving many differ-
030   ent steps such as data augmentation, architecture selection, and hyperparameter tuning. As a result,
031   developing high-performing models is labor-intensive and often demands machine learning (ML)
032   expertise, as well as domain-specific knowledge. Each component must be manually calibrated
033   based on prior experience and detailed analysis of training statistics.
034
      To simplify this process, tools such as Weights & Biases have been developed, offering ML
035
      practitioners the ability to track, organize, and optimize model training workflows. Similarly,
036   many approaches in Automated Machine Learning (AutoML), such as hyperparameter optimiza-
037   tion (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Bergstra et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; Springenberg et al., 2016;
038   Falkner et al., 2018) and neural architecture search (Elsken et al., 2017; Kandasamy et al., 2018),
039   aim to automate parts of the ML pipeline. While these platforms and methods provide valuable
040   support, they tend to address isolated aspects of the model development process and still require
041   substantial involvement from human experts.
042   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have shown their ability to serve as capa-
043   ble code generators and emulate expert agents in collaborative environments. For instance, Hong
044   et al. (2024) introduced MetaGPT, a multi-agent framework for autonomous software development,
045   where agents assume roles like product managers, software engineers, and quality assurance (QA)
046   engineers. Similarly, Du et al. (2024) proposed Cross-Team Collaboration, where LLM agents work
047   across design, coding, and testing phases in a coordinated multi-team framework.
048   Inspired by these advances, we propose AutoModel - an LLM agent framework designed to fully
049   automate the model generation process for image classification tasks. AutoModel is an end-to-end
050   framework that requires only a dataset as input. It autonomously handles the entire model develop-
051   ment workflow, optimizing various components in parallel, such as data augmentation, architecture
052   selection, and hyperparameter tuning, to produce high-performing models without requiring any
053   user intervention. By assigning specialized roles to LLM agents, AutoModel replicates the col-
      laborative approach of a human expert team, enabling individuals with no ML expertise to train


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      state-of-the-art image classification models. For experienced ML practitioners, AutoModel can also
055   reduce the time and effort required to develop high-performance models.
056
057   Our experiments demonstrate that AutoModel consistently outperforms zero-shot LLM-generated
058
      training pipelines and achieves near-human-level performance on standard datasets such as CIFAR-
      10, TinyImageNet, and various Kaggle competition datasets. This establishes AutoModel as an ideal
059
      solution for both domain experts and ML practitioners who seek a streamlined, efficient approach to
060
      model development.
061
062   Our contributions in this work are as follows
063   • We present AutoModel, an end-to-end image classification framework that leverages multiple
064     specialized LLM agents to achieve human-level performance in model training.
065   • We design a fully automated pipeline for code generation, model training, and performance eval-
066     uation, which iteratively improves models without any human intervention after initialization.
067   • We conduct comprehensive experiments on a wide range of datasets, including standard bench-
068     marks and real-world datasets, demonstrating that AutoModel consistently outperforms zero-shot
069     prompting LLMs and matches the performance of expert human practitioners.

073   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND LLM AGENTS
074
075   Large language models (LLMs) are pre-trained on massive text corpora, often with millions to tril-
076   lions of parameters. Some of the most well-known LLMs today include GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 (Ope-
077   nAI et al., 2024) from OpenAI, Claude 3.5 from Anthropic, Mixtral from Mistral, and Llama 3
078
      from Meta. While LLMs can be used directly after pre-training, they often undergo additional train-
      ing stages, such as supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning from human feedback
079
      (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022). These stages aim to align LLMs with specific behaviors and ob-
080
      jectives, resulting in models capable of performing tasks such as general-purpose chatbots, code
081   generation, and information retrieval.
082
083   A subset of LLMs specializes in code generation. Codex (Chen et al., 2021), for example, is a GPT
084
      model fine-tuned on publicly available GitHub code with a focus on Python programming. Fol-
      lowing Codex, Code Llama was introduced, extending coding capabilities to various programming
085
      languages such as Python, C++, Java, PHP, and TypeScript. More recently, larger natural language
086
      focused LLMs, including GPT-3.5 and Llama 3-8B, have also demonstrated significant coding pro-
087   ficiency across multiple languages, without needing to be explicitly fine-tuned on code.
088
089   LLMs have also been used to create human-like agents capable of executing complex instructions
090
      autonomously. Examples of such LLM-based agents include ReAct (Yao et al., 2023), Reflex-
      ion (Shinn et al., 2023), and SwiftSage Lin et al. (2023), which have demonstrated effectiveness in
091
      complex reasoning and decision-making tasks. Expanding on this, works like AutoGPT (Signifi-
092
      cant Gravitas) enabled LLM agents to move beyond reasoning and autonomously perform actions,
093   such as executing code and receiving feedback from outputs. Another line of research explores the
094   collaboration of multiple LLM agents, each with specialized roles (Hong et al., 2024; Du et al.,
095   2024; Park et al., 2023). These multi-agent frameworks have shown that LLMs can effectively as-
096   sume distinct roles (Tseng et al., 2024), and role specialization enhances their ability to retrieve
097   relevant knowledge, outperforming single-agent systems on tasks requiring reasoning and strategic
098   planning (Sreedhar & Chilton, 2024).
099
100   2.2   AUTOMATED M ACHINE L EARNING
101
102   Automated Machine Learning (AutoML) is a field of research seeking to automate various stages
103   of the machine learning pipeline, making it more efficient to develop ML models. Most AutoML
104
      works focus on automating specific parts of the process, such as data preparation, model architecture
      selection, and hyperparameter optimization (He et al., 2021).
105
106   For instance, works like AutoAugment (Cubuk et al., 2019), Faster AutoAugment (Hataya et al.,
107   2019), DADA (Li et al., 2020), and TrivialAugment (Müller & Hutter, 2021) concentrate on au-
      tomating the data augmentation component of image classification by applying search strategies


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      over predefined search spaces. Another major focus in AutoML is neural architecture search (NAS),
109   which aims to find the most optimal model architecture from a given search space. A prominent
110   example is Elsken et al. (2017), where a hill-climbing algorithm is used to identify the best Con-
111   volutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture. More advanced approaches, such as those by Kan-
112   dasamy et al. (2018), leverage Bayesian optimization and optimal transport to refine this search.
113
      In addition to NAS, hyperparameter optimization (HPO) is a closely related area that seeks to iden-
114
      tify the best set of hyperparameters for a fixed architecture. Random search (Bergstra & Bengio,
115   2012) is a simple yet efficient method that outperforms traditional grid search (Bergstra et al., 2011)
116   by exploring the search space more effectively. To further enhance the search process, many works
117   incorporate Bayesian optimization in HPO (Snoek et al., 2012; Springenberg et al., 2016; Falkner
118   et al., 2018), enabling better use of past information to guide future decisions.
119
120   2.3   AUTO ML WITH LLM S
121
122   With the recent advancement of LLMs, many researchers have started exploring using LLMs to
123   tackle problem in AutoML, as LLMs offer much greater flexibility and in the search space over
124   traditional methods. One of the earliest works in this direction is Yu et al. (2023), which utilizes
125   a fine-tuned Generative Pre-Trained Transformer (GPT) model to create new architectures using
126   crossover, mutation, and selection strategies. Later, many works took advantage of the pre-training
127   of LLMs, which grants them an immense amount of implicit knowledge on neural architectures.
128
      GENIUS (Zheng et al., 2023) is one such work, where NAS is performed by simple prompting GPT-
      4 to generate different configurations, which are evaluated iteratively for a pre-determined number
129
      of iterations. In a different direction, Hollmann et al. (2024) proposed the CAAFE method that uses
130
      LLMs to automate feature engineering by generating semantically meaningful features for tabular
131   dataset. Zhang et al. (2023) worked on a more comprehensive pipeline, attempting to simultaneously
132   tackling many tasks including object detection, object classification and question answering with
133   LLM-based AutoML. Notably, not only did they use LLMs to improve data augmentation and model
134   architecture components, they also did so based on a LLM-predicted training log, although there
135   were no experimental comparison that demonstrates the method’s effectiveness.

      3.1   BACKGROUND
141
      In image classification tasks, the engineering workflow typically begins with a high-level assessment
142
      of the dataset. This initial step focuses on understanding the size and complexity of the dataset,
143   ensuring that the subsequent steps are aligned with the specific characteristics of the data.
144
145   Next, the workflow moves to data processing, a critical phase that prepares the raw data for model
146
      training. This involves standardizing the input images, resizing them to consistent dimensions, and
      possibly augmenting the dataset through techniques such as rotation, flipping, or color adjustments.
147
      These pre-processing steps are essential to ensure that the model can generalize well across different
148
      variations of the input data.
149
150   Once the data is pre-processed, the focus shifts to model selection and engineering. Engineers
151   consider various architectures and select the one with the most potential to achieve good perfor-
152
      mance in the context of the given dataset. This phase may also involve incorporating regularization
      techniques, such as Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), as necessary to better suit the specific charac-
153
      teristics of the dataset.
154
155   Following model selection, the training process is initiated. This involves configuring the training
156   hyperparameters, such as learning rate, batch size, and optimization algorithms.
157   After the model is trained, it is evaluated on a test set to measure its performance. Based on the
158   evaluation results, engineers may revisit earlier stages of the workflow - adjusting data process-
159   ing techniques, exploring alternative model architectures, or refining training parameters - to it-
160   eratively improve the model’s accuracy and robustness. This cyclical process continues until the
161   model achieves satisfactory performance, ensuring that the final output is well-suited for the object
      classification task at hand.

181   3.2   AUTO M ODEL
182
183
      To address the challenges with traditional model tuning, we wish to design and develop an automate
      system that simulates the above process with a team of LLM agents. Thus, we introduce AutoModel,
184
      an end-to-end framework that utilizes a team of specialized Large Language Model (LLM) agents
185
      to autonomously generate a high-performing model, requiring only a dataset as input and no human
186   intervention. We provide a diagram of the workflow of Automodel in 1.
187
188   The framework is designed to streamline the machine learning pipeline by assigning specific roles
189
      to each agent, ensuring every aspect of model development is handled efficiently and effectively.
      Each agent is provided with a detailed profile that includes the overall project goal, team structure,
190
      individual responsibilities, and instructions for collaboration. To further enhance their effectiveness,
191
      each agent is embedded with explicit prompts that guide them in applying advanced techniques and
192   methods that may not be intuitively utilized by LLMs without direct prompting, despite their implicit
193   knowledge of these techniques.
194
195
      The key agents in the AutoModel framework are as follows:
196   • Project Architect: This agent is responsible for the initial analysis of the dataset, evaluating
197     its size, structure, and any supplementary information provided. Based on this analysis, the
198     Project Architect generates a comprehensive technical design that outlines the data processing
199     steps, model architecture, and training strategies. This design serves as the foundation for the
200     entire workflow.
201
      • Data Engineer: The Data Engineer handles the implementation of the data processing pipeline
        according to the specifications laid out by the Project Architect. This involves tasks such as data
202
        standardization, augmentation, and transformation, ensuring that the dataset is optimally prepared
203
        for model training.
204   • Model Engineer: The Model Engineer is tasked with selecting and configuring the model ar-
205     chitecture. This agent chooses the most appropriate model, applies necessary modifications, and
206     incorporates techniques such as regularization to maximize performance.
207   • Training Engineer: This agent is responsible for configuring the model training process. Key
208     tasks include setting the hyperparameters, such as learning rate and batch size, and selecting opti-
209     mization algorithms to ensure efficient training.
210   • Performance Analyst: After the model is trained, the Performance Analyst reviews the entire
211     history of pipeline configurations and training logs. This agent identifies areas for improvement
212
        and provides targeted feedback to the relevant engineering agent, guiding the next iteration of
        model development.
213
214   Each agent in the AutoModel framework is equipped with knowledge of its role, the team’s goals,
215   and how to collaborate effectively. The prompts provided to each agent embed expert-level knowl-
      edge specific to their domain. For example, the Model Engineer is aware of effective pre-trained


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      models like ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022), while the Data Engineer is familiar with advanced data
217   augmentation techniques such as MixUp (Zhang et al., 2018) and CutMix (Yun et al., 2019). Al-
218   though LLMs like GPT-4o implicitly understand these techniques, they rarely apply them unless
219   explicitly prompted. By specifying these techniques in the prompts, AutoModel ensures that the
220   agents select and experiment with highly effective methods when generating the training pipeline.
221
      These specialized agents collaborate to develop and improve models iteratively. The development
222
      process begins with the Project Architect, who reviews the dataset and additional context to produce
223   a technical design. This design is then passed to the Data Engineer, Model Engineer, and Training
224   Engineer, who collaboratively build their respective components according to the plan.
225
226
      Once the data pipeline, model architecture, and training configurations are in place, the system
      integrates these components and trains the model. The Performance Analyst then reviews all past
227
      and current pipeline configurations, along with their associated training logs, to identify areas for
228
      improvement. It provides targeted feedback to the most relevant engineering agent, who adjusts
229   their component accordingly. The updated system is then retrained and re-evaluated.
230
231   This iterative cycle continues, with each round focusing on refining the model based on the feedback
232
      loop. In particular, configurations that lead to performance gains are saved, while those that degrade
      performance will be discarded. The process runs for a predefined number of iterations, after which
233
      the best-performing configuration and its corresponding model checkpoint are selected as the final
234
      output.
235
236   AutoModel optimizes multiple aspects of the training pipeline, including data augmentation, opti-
237   mization strategies, and hyperparameters, all in a single iterative process. Unlike traditional AutoML
238
      methods such as hyperparameter optimization and neural architecture search, which focus on spe-
      cific components, AutoModel’s flexibility allows it to explore a wider search space by adjusting
239
      nearly all components within the training pipeline. Additionally, AutoModel optimizes the com-
240
      ponents sequentially rather than simultaneously. This stepwise approach is essential because many
241   components, such as learning rate and batch size, are interdependent. Adjusting these parameters
242   together without considering their interactions can result in suboptimal performance. By focusing
243   on one component at a time, AutoModel effectively identifies and builds upon successful changes,
244   leading to more reliable improvements in future iterations.
245
246   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION
247
248   With the architecture and roles of the AutoModel framework introduced, we now discuss its imple-
249   mentation. In particular, we will discuss how the framework processes dataset inputs, autonomously
250   generates and executes code, and employs iterative refinements to optimize model performance.
251
      Dataset Information Input A key advantage of the LLM agents framework is its ability to under-
252   stand any natural language description of the dataset, enabling the system to make more informed
253   decisions. Optionally, the user can supply a brief description of the dataset, detailing aspects such as
254   the image domain (e.g., real-life objects, digits, synthetic images), whether the images are grayscale
255   or colored, their dimensions (same or varying sizes), and their quality (high or low). This infor-
256   mation is passed to both the Project Architect and Performance Analyst and is factored into the
257   initial technical design and performance analysis. The impact of including this dataset information
258   is discussed further in Section 4.4.
259   Autonomous Code Execution The AutoModel framework autonomously generates, executes, and
260   refines code over multiple iterations to improve model performance. The code-generating agents
261   (i.e., Data Engineer, Model Engineer, and Training Engineer) operate according to a pre-defined
262   interface. This interface specifies the required input arguments and expected return values for each
263   component. The agents have flexibility in how they implement their code, but they must adhere to
264   these interface guidelines. Once the agents produce the code, it is parsed, extracted, and saved into
265   Python files. The system then uses a pre-defined pipeline to integrate these components and execute
266
      the full model training process. The execution outputs, including model performance metrics, are
      captured at the shell level and stored in a training log for analysis.
267
268   Zero-shot Initialization The Project Architect agent leverages the zero-shot generation capabilities
269   of LLMs to produce a strong baseline training pipeline, ensuring a foundation of good performance
      from the outset. As discussed in the ablation studies in Section 4.3, generating the data, model,


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and training components sequentially in separate calls leads to suboptimal results, even when the
271   LLM has access to previously generated components. To address this issue, the Project Architect
272   generates a single, coherent pipeline in one step, which is then divided into the necessary compo-
273   nents (data, model, and training) required by AutoModel. This approach allows the framework to
274   be more efficient by starting from a solid baseline, reducing the need for extensive corrections and
275   minimizing wasted iterations on poor initial configurations.
276
      Performance Analysis with Summarized History To ensure informed decision-making and pre-
277   vent redundancy, all previous pipeline configurations (e.g., data augmentation methods, model ar-
278   chitectures, hyperparameters) and their corresponding training logs are passed to the Performance
279   Analyst. However, passing the entire history of code and logs could quickly exceed the LLM’s max-
280   imum context length. To address this, after each successful iteration, the system uses an LLM to
281   summarize the key aspects of the data, model, and training code, highlighting critical details such as
282   the methods and hyperparameters used. The summarized configuration history, along with the cor-
283   responding training performance data, is then provided to the Performance Analyst to guide further
284   iterations and improvements.

      Datasets We evaluated AutoModel using two widely recognized object classification datasets:
      CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) and TinyImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). CIFAR-10 con-
292
      tains 60,000 32x32 color images across 10 distinct classes, while TinyImageNet, a subset of Ima-
293
      geNet1K, includes 100,000 images across 200 classes, resized to 64x64 pixels. Both datasets feature
294   commonly seen objects such as vehicles and animals.
295
296   To test AutoModel’s robustness, we also incorporated CIFAR-10-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019),
297
      a variant of CIFAR-10 designed to assess model performance under common corruptions. CIFAR-
      10-C introduces 15 corruption types, grouped into four categories: noise, blur, weather, and digital
298
      distortions. These corruptions are applied at two severity levels (1 and 5), resulting in 30 distinct
299
      versions of the dataset. For experimental efficiency, we created two subsets, CIFAR-10-C-1 and
300   CIFAR-10-C-5, where the number indicates the severity level. These subsets were generated by
301   uniformly sampling one image from each corrupted dataset for every test image.
302
303
      Additionally, we evaluated AutoModel on two smaller datasets from different domains: SVHN and
      dSprites. SVHN is a digit classification dataset containing real-world images of house numbers, with
304
      variations in resolution and background complexity. dSprites is a synthetic dataset of 2D shapes,
305
      with latent factors controlling the shapes’ properties. Specifically, we used the dSprites Orientation
306   dataset, where each shape is rotated into one of 40 possible orientations within the [0, 2π] range.
307   Both datasets are part of the Visual Task Adaptation Benchmark (VTAB) (Zhai et al., 2020).
308
309
      To further assess AutoModel’s versatility across different real-world domains, we also evaluated it

      datasets span a range of sizes and represent various data domains, including:

        Streak Disease, Bacterial Blight Disease, or no disease, with 5 classes in total.
      • Kitchenware Classification (ololo, 2022): This dataset has 9,367 photos of 6 types of common
316
        kitchenware, including cups, glasses, plates, spoons, forks and knives. These photos are taken in
317
        households with various lighting and scene conditions.
318   • Arabic Letters Classification (Khalil, 2023): This dataset contains 53,199 images of 65 writ-
319     ten Arabic letters, each exhibiting positional variations based on their occurrence within a word

322   • 4 Animal Classification (Lee et al., 2022): This dataset comprises a total of 3,529 images, cat-
323     egorized into four distinct animal classes: cats, deer, dogs, and horses. Each class represents a
        diverse range of images capturing various poses, environments, and lighting conditions.

      4.4   U TILIZING DATASET I NFORMATION
451
452   In our experiments, we investigated how providing additional dataset-specific information can in-
453   fluence the effectiveness of data augmentation strategies in AutoModel. When supplied with this
454   information, AutoModel is capable of selecting augmentations that are well-suited to the character-
455   istics of the dataset.
456
      For instance, when training on the SVHN dataset, which consists of real-world images of house
457
      numbers, AutoModel chose to apply the ColorJitter augmentation. The reasoning provided was
458   that ”Color jitter (brightness, contrast, saturation, hue) can help in robustifying the model against
459   variations in lighting conditions.” This augmentation is particularly appropriate for SVHN since
460   real-world images can be subject to inconsistent lighting, and this technique helps improve model
461   robustness in such conditions.
462
      However, not all augmentations are universally beneficial. In some cases, certain augmentations
463
      can harm model performance. For example, in the dSprites Orientation dataset, where orientation
464   is label for classification, applying any flipping augmentation like RandomHorizontalFlip can de-
465   grade performance by altering the class labels and confusing the model. AutoModel’s Performance
466   Analyst identified this issue, noting: ”RandomHorizontalFlip may not be useful for orientation clas-
467   sification tasks,” and ”Orientation-based tasks will not benefit from horizontal flipping; could even
468   confuse the model.”
469
      This demonstrates AutoModel’s ability to intelligently adapt its augmentation strategy based on the
470   specific characteristics of the dataset. By recognizing which augmentations enhance performance
471   and which are detrimental, AutoModel fine-tunes the model more effectively, leading to better and
472   faster optimization.

475   5     C ONCLUSION
476
477   In this paper, we introduced AutoModel, an end-to-end LLM agent framework designed to au-
478   tonomously generate high-performing image classification models. As a part of the framework,
479   we designed an automated code generation and execution pipeline, eliminating the need for human
480   intervention, requiring only a dataset as input. By leveraging the expertise of specialized agents,
481   AutoModel replicates the workflow of human practitioners, to iteratively improve on tasks such as
482
      data augmentation, model selection, and hyperparameter tuning.
483   Our experiments demonstrated that AutoModel consistently outperforms models generated by zero-
484   shot LLM prompting. AutoModel is capable of handling both corrupted datasets and adapt to vary-
485   ing domains, showcased through its evaluation on Kaggle competition datasets, where it performed
      comparably to top human ML practitioners.
