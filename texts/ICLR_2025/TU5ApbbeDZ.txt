009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a framework to discover preference optimization algorithms special-
012            ized to particular scenarios, in a theoretically sound and computationally efficient
013            setting. We start by designing a novel family of PO algorithms based on mirror
014            descent, which we call Mirror Preference Optimization (MPO). MPO recovers
015            existing methods like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and Odds-Ratio
016
               Preference Optimization (ORPO) for specific choices of the mirror map. Given
               specific properties of preference datasets, such as mixed-quality or noisy data, we
017
               show that we can efficiently search the MPO class to find specialized algorithms
018
               that outperform current baselines. Namely, we leverage evolutionary strategies
019            and preference datasets generated on MuJoCo environments to systematically
020            evaluate and optimize MPO algorithms on hand-crafted scenarios. We demon-
021            strate the resulting PO algorithms successfully transfer to a Large Language
022            Model (LLM) alignment task, where they demonstrate superior robustness in
023            handling mixed-quality datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Learning from human feedback is a paradigm that enables the alignment of complex agents to
028
      human preferences, and has been successfully applied to Large Language Models (Team et al.,
029
      2023; Achiam et al., 2023). In particular, fine-tuning pretrained LLMs with human preferences has
030   become a popular strategy to adapt them to specific tasks and to improve their safety and helpfulness.
031
032   Most LLM alignment pipelines begin with a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) step, which involves
033
      supervised next-token prediction on a dataset of high-quality responses and leads to a reference
      policy. The reference policy is further optimized using the human preference data, typically through
034
      either Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) or Direct
035
      Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), or one of their several variants. RLHF con-
036   sists of learning a reward model consistent with human preferences and then using Reinforcement
037   Learning (RL) techniques such as REINFORCE (Sutton et al., 1999) and Proximal Policy Optimi-
038   sation (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to maximize the total expected reward. In contrast, DPO and
039   its variations, e.g. odds ratio preference optimization (ORPO) (Hong et al., 2024), bypass explicit
040   reward models entirely and optimize directly on preference data, implicitly learning the reward.
041
      While RL-based methods offer stronger theoretical guarantees and often lead to higher perfor-
042   mance (Song et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a), offline approaches such as DPO have gained traction
043   due to their simplicity and the ability to leverage preexisting high-quality datasets. In contrast to
044   PPO, where data collection and labeling are performed iteratively after each update, DPO and its
045   modifications allow for more efficient training, avoiding the high computational costs, need of addi-
046   tional sample labelling and complexity of RL methods. Specifically, PPO requires careful parameter
047   tuning (Yuan et al., 2023) and involves simultaneous training of multiple models (the reward model,
048   language model, and critic), which can be prohibitive in most hardware setups.
049   The performance of offline algorithms such as DPO, particularly on noisy or low-quality datasets,
050   has been a subject of debate (Chowdhury et al., 2024), with limited empirical results available.
051   In this work, we provide a comprehensive analysis of PO algorithms, examining their behavior
052   on automatically generated preference datasets. We perform this analysis in MuJoCo environ-
053   ments (Todorov et al., 2012), where the underlying reward structure is well defined and offers a
      clear performance metric to compare agents. In particular, we focus on ORPO, as we have found

      that it largely outperforms DPO in all settings we have considered. Additionally, our findings
055   indicate that ORPO exhibits distinct failure modes when applied to specific low-quality or noisy
056   datasets. These failure modes are present in practical LLM applications, raising concerns about
057   using mixed-quality datasets for PO.
058
      We introduce a novel framework to find algorithms that are capable of dealing with these more diffi-
059
      cult settings. We first define a novel class of Preference Optimization (PO) algorithms based on mir-
060
      ror descent (Nemirovski & Yudin, 1983), which generalizes DPO and ORPO for particular choices
061   of the mirror map. Our framework consists in searching the MPO class using evolutionary strate-
062   gies (ES) and hand-crafted preference datasets generated on MuJoCo environments, with the goal
063   of discovering new and better performing algorithms. We outline our contributions in detail below.
064
065         1. We perform a systematic analysis on the performance of ORPO on automatically generated
066            preference datasets with varying levels of data quality, noise levels, initial policy, and
067            judge temperature.
068         2. We introduce a novel family of offline PO algorithms using mirror descent, named Mirror
069            Preference Optimization (MPO), which can be easily parameterized and explored via ES.
070         3. For each failure mode we identify, we find and describe an algorithm within our framework
071            that outperforms ORPO. Additionally, we show that allowing losses to vary based on the
072            percentage of training progress drastically boosts performance in some settings.
073
            4. We demonstrate that PO algorithms discovered on MuJoCo can generalize to LLM tasks.
074
               In particular, for a highlighted failure setting, we show that our discovered algorithm
075            improves performance w.r.t. the ORPO baseline. This finding showcases the efficacy of
076            analyzing PO algorithms in simpler, less computationally expensive environments, as it is
077            possible to obtain insights relevant for LLM applications.
078
            5. Following our findings, we present a closed form expression for one of our discovered PO
079
               algorithms that can be implemented with one line of code.

236   To search the space of PO algorithms we have defined, we employ a neural network parametrization
237   for both ψ and ϕ−1 , which we optimize using evolutionary strategies.
238   Similarly to Alfano et al. (2024), we parameterize both ψ and ϕ−1 as a one layer neural network with
239   126 hidden units and non-negative kernels, where the activation functions are equally split among:
240                             1/2     1/3
241

243   increasing activation functions guarantee the monotonicity of ψ and ϕ−1 , while the several different

245   the ORPO objective, we add a log(x) and b log(x/(1 − x)) to the final outputs of ψ and ϕ−1 ,

247

      We carry out our experiments on continuous RL tasks in MuJoCo and on LLM fine-tuning. To
278
      address resource constraints, we discover mirror maps in MuJoCo and report their performance
279
      when evaluated on a LLM finetuning task. To maximize computational efficiency, all our MuJoCo

283
      We consider three different preference optimization settings which we describe below. Each setting
284
      includes a starting policy, a set of preference datasets with different properties and an evaluation
285
      method for the trained policy. We apply ES to the first two settings (MuJoCo) and test the discovered
286   objectives on the last one (LLM fine-tuning).

      4.1   DATASET T YPES
289
290   Across all analysed MuJoCo environments, we generate several datasets to test the efficacy and
291   robustness of our discovered loss functions. Our datasets are meant to model common issues of real
292   world data. The dataset types are described below:
293
294          • Base dataset: we compare a trajectory from the expert agent with one of a reference agent
295            with varying skill levels.
296          • Noisy dataset: we flip a varying portion of the rankings in the base dataset.
297          • Shuffled dataset: the comparisons are not always between the expert agent and a second
298            agent. Namely, 25% of the comparisons are between two trajectories from the expert agent,
299            50% of the comparisons are between a trajectory of the expert agent and one of a reference
300            agent, and 25% of the comparisons are between two trajectories of a reference agent.
301          • Poor judge dataset: the judge is more likely to flip labels when the two trajectories have
302            closer reward values. This is implemented as an increase in the temperature of the Bradley-
303            Terry judge.
304

          6                           C ONCLUSION
524
525
          We have introduced a novel framework for Policy Optimization algorithms, as well as a methodol-
526       ogy for the automatic discovery of PO algorithms using evolution strategies. Through a systematic
527       evaluation across diverse settings in MuJoCo environments, we demonstrated that our discovered
528       objective functions consistently match or exceed the performance of existing methods, particularly
529       in noisy and mixed-quality datasets where the ORPO baseline struggles. The introduction of tem-
530       porally aware objective functions further improved performance, allowing the optimization process
531       to vary and adapt during training. Analyzing the landscape of the discovered objectives, we give
532       an intuition that justifies improved performance as well as guidance in the design of new PO algo-
533       rithms. Our results also indicate that the discovered objectives generalize beyond simple reinforce-
534
          ment learning environments, showing promising performance when transferred to LLMs, thereby
          confirming the broader applicability of our approach.

704   Automatic Discovery of Preference Optimization Loss Functions Several works in the litera-
705   ture have shown that it is possible to discover machine learning algorithms that outperform algo-
706   rithms manually designed by researchers (Oh et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022; Jackson et al., 2024;
707   Alfano et al., 2024). An approach particularly relevant to our method is DiscoPOP by Lu et al.
708   (2024), which leverages an LLM to discover objective functions for LLM tuning. They consider a
709   different space of objective functions from us, as they replace the log-sigmoid in (5) with a generic
710   loss function, following the framework built by Tang et al. (2024). Additionally, instead of searching
711   over a space of parametrized functions, they ask the LLM to generate loss functions in code space.
712
      This distinction suggests that our approaches could be complementary, as the model discovered by
      DiscoPOP could be paired with our learned mirror map. Lastly, DiscoPOP optimizes its objective
713
      function directly on the final task, whereas we adopt a two-stage process—optimizing the loss func-
714
      tion on a separate task (MuJoCo) and later transferring it to the LLM setting. This transferability
715   underscores the broader applicability of our approach.
716
717   Generalisations of DPO A generalization of DPO alternative to ours is f -DPO, developed
718   by Wang et al. (2023), which consists in replacing the KL-divergence in (2) with an f -divergence
719   and then apply the same heuristic as DPO to obtain the final objective function. We note that the
720   KL-divergence is the only f -divergence to be also a Bregman divergence, and vice-versa. They
721   empirically demonstrate that different f -divergences lead to different balances between alignment
722   performance and generation diversity, highlighting the trade-offs inherent to this class of algorithms.
723   Huang et al. (2024) further explore this class of PO algorithm and individuate an f -divergence for
724   which f -DPO is robust to overoptimization.

      E    F URTHER DISCUSSION ON O NLINE VS O FFLINE M ETHODS
811
812
813
      In the domain of RL and preference optimization, the choice between online and offline algorithms
814   presents a critical trade-off, influencing computational efficiency, data requirements, and generaliza-
815   tion capabilities. Online methods, such as PPO, iteratively collect and incorporate new data during
816   training. These inherently support exploration of the environment, enabling the discovery of novel
817   strategies or behaviors that are not captured in pre-existing datasets. However, they need feedback
818   for each generated “trajectory” (or response, in the LLM case), which might be expensive to ob-
819   tain. Online methods are also more complex and particularly sensitive to hyperparameters, often
820   requiring meticulous tuning for stability and efficiency.
821   Offline algorithms, such as DPO and its variants, rely entirely on pre-collected datasets. These
822   methods are designed for efficiency and simplicity: they don’t require any additional feedback from
823   users and are therefore particularly effective in scenarios where feedback is delayed or unavailable.
824   However, the reliance on static datasets means offline methods may struggle to generalize beyond
825   the training data, particularly if the distribution shift between the training dataset and test time
826   distribution is significant. Additionally, the performance of the algorithm is closely tied to the quality
827   of the training dataset: noisy, biased, or corrupt datasets can severely degrade performance, as these
828
      methods cannot mitigate such issues through exploration or resampling.
829   In summary, RLHF (i.e., online) is considered the superior approach, particularly when substantial
830   amounts of online labels are accessible. This makes it the industry standard (Xu et al., 2024b). While
831   DPO has been theoretically equated to optimizing using PPO and a reward model trained on an
832   offline dataset, recent empirical research (Tang et al., 2024) has challenged this notion. These studies
833   have demonstrated that online methods, such as PPO, consistently outperform offline methods like
834
      DPO. This superiority is attributed to the benefits of on-policy sampling.
835   While DPO has occasionally outperformed PPO, it’s important to note that several studies (Xu et al.,
836   2024b; Song et al., 2024) have consistently shown PPO’s overall superiority. DPO’s relative strength
837   lies in its simpler training regime, which avoids the complexities associated with reward model
838   inaccuracies. However, DPO’s performance is significantly limited by its sensitivity to distribution
839   shift, especially when the offline preference data lacks diversity (Song et al., 2024). This limitation
840
      becomes particularly evident when querying the model with out-of-distribution data, a common
      challenge for methods relying solely on offline data. To mitigate this issue, DPO-iter (Xu et al.,
841
      2024b), which incorporates online data, has been proposed as a potential solution.

      F    F URTHER DISCUSSION ON M ETA -L EARNING A LGORITHMS
847
848
849
      Meta-learning, or “learning to learn”, has been extensively employed to automate the design of al-
      gorithms that can either adapt rapidly with minimal data samples or generalize effectively to unseen
850
      data, tasks, or environments. The development of broadly applicable algorithms is particularly crit-
851
      ical in the context of preference optimization for LLMs. Here, LLMs are fine-tuned on relatively
852   small datasets of offline data but must generalize to a virtually infinite range of potential user queries.
853   Prior work in meta-learning has demonstrated success in developing generalizable optimization al-
854   gorithms and loss functions (Lu et al., 2022; Jackson et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Goldie et al., 2024;
855   Kirsch et al., 2020).
856
      At its core, meta-learning is defined as a bilevel optimization problem with an inner and an outer
857
      loop. The inner loop consists in an iterative optimization algorithm that trains agents to solve a
858   predetermined task given a set of meta-parameters. The outer loop consists in evaluating the agents
859   trained in the inner loop and update the meta-parameters accordingly, following some optimiza-
860   tion method like second order gradient descent (Finn et al., 2017). The evaluation of the agents is
861   typically done on a held-out dataset in supervised learning or by sampling trajectories on the en-
862   vironment simulator in RL (Lu et al., 2022; Jackson et al., 2024). In our setting, the inner loop is
863   the offline preference optimization algorithm, while the outer loop is the agent evaluation on the
      environment (online) and the update of the meta-parameters ζ.
