009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Safety alignment is a fundamental yet still developing research topic for the real-
012            world applications of AI. Despite the multifaceted nature of safety and trustwor-
013            thiness in AI, current safety alignment methods often focus on a singular notion
014            of safety. By carefully assessing models from the existing safety-alignment meth-
015            ods, we found that, while they generally improved overall safety performance,
016
               they failed to ensure safety in specific categories. Our study first identified the dif-
               ficulty of eliminating such vulnerabilities without sacrificing the model’s helpful-
017
               ness. We found that, while smaller KL penalty parameters, increased training iter-
018
               ations, and dataset cleansing can enhance safety, they do not necessarily improve
019            the trade-off between safety and helpfulness. We discovered that safety alignment
020            can induce undesired effects and result in a model that prefers generating negative
021            tokens leading to rejective responses, regardless of the input context. To address
022            this, we introduced a learning-free method, Token-level Safety-Debiased Infer-
023            ence (TSDI), to estimate and correct this bias during the generation process using
024            randomly constructed prompts. Our experiments demonstrated that our method
025            could enhance the model’s helpfulness while maintaining safety, thus improving
026            the trade-off Pareto-front.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities across various real-
031   world applications, including translation (Zhang et al., 2023), content creation (Yuan et al., 2022),
032   coding (Chen et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023), and summarization (Stiennon et al., 2020). As the use of
033   LLMs extends into high-stakes domains such as medicine (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023), law (Cui
034   et al., 2023), robotics (Shah et al., 2023), and autonomous driving (Chen et al., 2023), the impor-
035   tance of safety in AI systems becomes paramount to maximize benefits while minimizing societal
036   risks (Gehman et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b).
037   Alignment (Ji et al., 2023) has emerged as a promising approach to embed human values into LLMs,
038   thereby improving their helpfulness and safety. Techniques such as reinforcement learning from
039   human feedback (RLHF, Christiano et al. (2017); Ouyang et al. (2022)) and direct preference opti-
040   mization (DPO, Rafailov et al. (2024)) have played a crucial role in making LLMs more helpful and
041   harmless. However, these methods often rely on a single reward metric to determine output quality,
042   which does not consistently ensure high safety levels (Dai et al., 2024).
043   Given the complexity of modeling both helpfulness and safety using a singular reward function, it is
044   natural to formulate the LLM alignment problem using multiple separate functions. Safe RLHF (Dai
045   et al., 2024) is a pioneering approach that introduces the (constrained) safe RL paradigm into the
046   alignment of LLMs. This approach enhances the development of LLMs that effectively balance
047   reward (i.e., helpfulness) and safety (i.e., harmlessness). As computationally efficient approaches of
048   Safe RLHF, Wachi et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2024) respectively proposed SACPO and CAN,
049   in which constrained LLM alignment problems are solved using RL-free algorithms such as DPO.
050   A limitation common to the existing safety alignment methods (e.g., Safe RLHF, SACPO, CAN)
051   is their tendency to focus on a singular notion of safety. This focus obscures specific vulnerabilities
052   and fails to address the distinct safety requirements of different domains. AI safety is inherently
053   multifaceted (Amodei et al., 2016; Bostrom & Yudkowsky, 2018). Ensuring societal acceptance of
      AI systems requires considering diverse metrics such as harmlessness, bias, security, fairness, and

      privacy (Wang et al., 2023a). Moreover, the previous safety alignment studies (Dai et al., 2024;
055   Wachi et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) suffer from misdirected safety evaluations. Using compar-
056   ative evaluations based on preference labels from humans or advanced LLMs, their evaluations may
057   inaccurately deem a model safe if it performs better than a poor comparative model. With poten-
058   tial vulnerabilities in specific topics possibly hidden by insufficient evaluation, existing works lack
059   investigation into the challenges of achieving high safety levels for these topics, and its solution.
060
061   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
062
063   First, we demonstrate the challenges in eliminating the vulnerability of safety-aligned models and
064
      achieving a high level across all safety metrics. Specifically, predicting the vulnerabilities of LLMs
      before the alignment is difficult, even with a thorough analysis of pre-trained LLMs or the alignment
065
      dataset. The alignment process itself is complex, with varying difficulty and data quality across dif-
066
      ferent categories. Moreover, applying safety alignment with various KL divergence penalty param-
067   eters shows that a smaller KL penalty leads to higher safety scores but also decreases the model’s
068   helpfulness. This trade-off highlights the difficulty in maintaining the model’s helpfulness while
069   eliminating these safety vulnerabilities. We also observed that, while data cleansing helps achieve
070   higher safety levels, it does not necessarily improve the trade-off between helpfulness and safety.
071
      To address these challenges, we propose a learning-free method, Token-level Safety-Debiased Infer-
072
      ence (TSDI), to estimate and mitigate the unwanted effects caused by safety alignment. By creating
073   a synthetic dataset with random tokens, we estimate the safety bias for each output position. Inter-
074   estingly, we found that the logits of negative tokens, leading to rejective and unhelpful responses,
075   increase significantly even for randomly constructed prompts after safety alignment. We propose a
076   method that subtracts this bias to adjust the output logits accordingly during the generation process.
077   Our experiments demonstrate that our debiasing method can improve the model’s helpfulness while
078   maintaining its safety, resulting in an improved safety-helpfulness trade-off.

      2.2       V ULNERABILITY OF S AFETY- ALIGNED M ODELS
144
145   Although significant efforts have been made for safety alignment, LLMs still potentially exhibit
146   vulnerabilities in producing harmful generations (Wei et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
147   2023; Yi et al., 2024). Prior research has shown that even if LLMs are trained to be safe and
148   harmless, they can still be misused. Many studies have demonstrated that it is possible to conduct
149   jailbreak attacks that provoke harmful behavior from an aligned LLM (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
150
      2023a). Moreover, Yang et al. (2023) and Yi et al. (2024) have shown that fine-tuning with a few
      malicious examples can easily subvert the model and cause harmful content generation. In this
151
      paper, we highlight vulnerability, in which a model, despite being safety-aligned using Safe
152
      RLHF or SACPO and deemed safe overall, generates harmful responses on specific safety
153   topics. Such vulnerabilities arise from insufficiency in the safety alignment and evaluation as
154   well as inherent vulnerabilities of the reference model or quality of the dataset. Though such
155   vulnerabilities have been well-studied in Wang et al. (2023b) for closed LLMs (e.g., GPTs,
156   Claude), we aim to find vulnerabilities in our safety-aligned model and mitigate them by fine-
157   tuning and evaluating the LLMs ourselves.

200   may oversee specific risks associated with distinct safety categories. In real-world applications,
201

249     3.2                         C HALLENGES IN ACHIEVING BOTH S AFETY AND H ELPFULNESS
250
251     We conduct experiments to reassess the challenges of achieving high safety for specific safety cat-
252     egories. Here, we focus on the Adult Content category (Category 03), the most significant vulner-
253     ability of existing models. We employ SACPO’s stepwise approach, which initially applies DPO
254     to align for helpfulness and then for safety. Our experiment setup is largely similar to that used in
255
        SACPO. We utilized the same SFT model as SACPO and Safe RLHF, a replicated version of Alpaca-
        7B (Taori et al., 2023). We also employed the same preference dataset, namely PKU-SafeRLHF (Ji
256
        et al., 2024b), in which each record contains a pair of responses to a specific prompt, ranked by
257
        helpfulness and harmlessness. We set the KL divergence penalty coefficient β = 0.1 for helpfulness
258     alignment and test various β/λ values for safety alignment. We also vary the training iterations to
259     consider the effect of longer safety alignment.
260
261
        Figure 2 shows the safety score for the Adult Content category and the helpfulness win rate against
        the SFT model across various settings. We observed that higher safety can be achieved using a
262
        smaller KL penalty or increasing training iterations. We note that increasing the training itera-
263
        tions might improve the safety score, but it often eventually plateaus. Conversely, using a smaller
264     KL penalty has a much more pronounced effect in obtaining higher safety levels. However, since
265     fine-tuning these parameters leads to higher safety, it often decreases the model’s helpfulness. In
266     particular, reducing β/λ leads to higher safety scores for the Adult Content category but might
267     significantly decrease the helpfulness win rate versus the SFT model. We also observed that a small
268     β/λ and excessive training iterations sometimes led to generation corruption (see Appendix D.3
269     for examples). These results demonstrate the difficulty in mitigating all safety vulnerabilities while
        preserving the helpfulness of the model.

286   3.3                          C HALLENGES IN I MPROVING THE DATASET
287
288   We discuss the challenges in improving the safety preference dataset. Initially, we observed that
289   there seems to be room for data improvement. We inspect the safety preference dataset by apply-
290   ing the safety evaluator MD-Judge to all samples in the PKU-SafeRLHF dataset. For each data
291   tuple (x, yw , yl ), we assessed the safety probabilities s(x, yw ) and s(x, yl ) for chosen and rejected
292   responses. Figure 3a illustrates the heatmap plot of safety probabilities for chosen and rejected re-
293
      sponses. We observed a decent number of samples where the chosen response had a lower safety
      probability than the rejected one, raising questions about the potential benefits of cleansing the
294
      dataset in our setting.
295
296   First, we found that it is difficult to predict the vulnerabilities a priori by inspecting the reference
297   LLMs or the alignment dataset. Figure 1 shows that the reference LLM is not particularly bad at
298   handling adult content. Moreover, the adult-related samples are neither particularly low in quality
299
      nor lacking in quantity. As shown by (Ji et al., 2024a), the number of adult-related samples is
      comparable to other categories. We further investigate the distribution of safety scores for each
300
      category, using the category information assigned by MD-Judge when a prompt-response pair is
301
      classified as unsafe. We excluded the samples where both responses are classified as safe, as category
302   information can not be identified. Figure 3b shows that the fraction of data where s(x, yw ) >
303   s(x, yl ) is not particularly low for Category 03, indicating that the safety preference data is not of
304   particularly low quality. This difficulty may arise because LLM alignment is not a straightforward
305   procedure, and the hardness of aligning each category may vary. Moreover, these categories are
306   interrelated and may influence each other.
307
      Next, we found that removing the training samples where the safety probability for the chosen
308   response was significantly lower than that for the rejected one does not necessarily improve the
309   safety-helpfulness trade-off. We removed all the samples where s(x, yl ) − s(x, yw ) > 0.25, then
310   conducted safety alignment using the cleansed dataset with the same settings as Section 3.2. This
311   cleansing procedure removed 577 samples (2.14%) among the original 26,872 samples. Surpris-
312   ingly, removing this small data subset significantly improved the safety level when training under
313   identical training settings compared to using the entire dataset. We provide a detailed plot showing
314   the safety levels of two datasets for different β/λ values and training iterations in Appendix B.3.
315   However, Figure 2 shows that data cleansing does not necessarily enhance the trade-off between
316
      safety and helpfulness. The resulting performance using the entire and cleansed dataset typically
      lies on the same Pareto-front, indicating that data cleansing does not fully resolve our challenges.

341   4.1                           O BSERVATION : U NINTENDED S AFETY E FFECTS

343   We first investigate why the model’s helpfulness decreases with stronger safety alignment. We hy-
344   pothesize that while enhancing the model’s ability to handle harmful prompts, safety alignment also
345   introduces unwanted bias. We use the term bias to denote a context-free effect, where the model
346   prefers specific outputs regardless of the input tokens. We observed that the safety-aligned model
347   sometimes falsely refused to answer harmless prompts, especially under strong safety alignment.
348
      Moreover, these unhelpful responses often began with specific tokens like “I’m sorry” or “Unfortu-
      nately,” indicating that such unwanted effects can be assessed at the token level.

385   4.2    P ROPOSED M ETHOD : D E - BIASING
386
387   Here, we propose Token-level Safety-Debiased Inference (TSDI), a learning-free method to estimate
388   and mitigate the bias introduced by the safety alignment. Generating proper tokens at the beginning
389   of a generation is crucial for controlling the safety of the entire response. Zou et al. (2023) demon-
390
      strates that if an adversarial prompt can trick the model into outputting a few harmful tokens at the
      start, it likely leads to an entirely harmful response. Thus, we expect debiasing the initial tokens to
391
      lead to more appropriate and helpful responses.
392
393   Our method first estimates the biases {bi }L
                                                 i=1 in output logits for L output tokens using randomized
394   prompts. In what follows, we let ρ̃ denote the distribution of the aforementioned random prompts.
395   Then, we subtract the estimated bias from the output logits during the generation process. That is,
396
      we subtract bi from the output logit of the i-th output token:
397                          pπθ′ (yi | x ⊕ y1:i−1 ) = SOFTMAX(fπθ (x ⊕ y1:i−1 ) − bi ).                             (7)
398
399   This de-biasing method aims to mitigate the unintended bias of safety alignment, thereby achieving
400   a better balance between safety and helpfulness. By subtracting the estimated biases from the output
401   logits, we expect to reduce the unwanted impact of safety alignment on harmless prompts, resulting
402   in higher helpfulness while maintaining a high level of safety.

454
455   5.1                  E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
456
457   We empirically evaluate the effectiveness of TSDI in improving the trade-off between safety (i.e.,
458   harmlessness) and helpfulness. We utilize the same SFT model as in Safe RLHF and SACPO, a
459   replica of Alpaca-7B. We employ the PKU-SafeRLHF preference dataset, containing over 30,000
460   records of expert evaluations. Each record separately ranks a pair of responses to a specific prompt
461
      based on helpfulness and harmlessness. We conduct the same experiments using the entire dataset
      and the cleansed dataset, in which the samples satisfying s(x, yl ) − s(x, yw ) > 0.25 are removed.
462
463   Implementation. We adopt the stepwise approach of SACPO. We first apply DPO to align for
464   helpfulness, resulting in a model referred to as DPO(H). We then align DPO(H) for safety under
465   various settings. We employed β = 0.1 for the helpfulness alignment and tested a range of β/λ
466   values for the safety alignment. The safety bias is estimated for the first 20 output tokens using 500
467
      randomly constructed (x, y) pairs, as discussed in Section 4. The token pool is constructed from the
      MMLU dataset. For more implementation details (e.g., hyperparameters), see Appendix C.
468
469   Evaluation. We use two metrics to measure helpfulness improvement: compliance rate and help-
470   fulness win rate. The compliance rate assesses if the models refuse to response with expressions
471   like “I’m sorry” or “Unfortunately.” We use 53 keywords, of which 47 keywords are from Zou
472   et al. (2023) (see Appendix C.6 for the completed list). This metric assesses the helpfulness at the
473
      token level, aligning with the intention of the proposed method. On the other hand, we measure the
      win rate against the SFT model using GPT-4 to evaluate the quality of the responses, which cannot
474
      be evaluated by the compliance rate. Our GPT-4 prompt is based on those in the SafeRLHF and
475
      SACPO study, with a minor adjustment in the output format (see Appendix C.5). We use prompts
476   from the AlpacaEval dataset, which are unlikely to elicit harmful content. To evaluate safety, we
477   employ the balanced SALAD-Bench dataset with MD-Judge and Llama Guard 3 to ensure robust
478   results across different evaluators. Finally, we provide several generation examples in Appendix D.

530   6            C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      This paper has demonstrated that the existing safety-alignment methods focused on a singular no-
533
      tion of safety, which often results in unrevealed vulnerabilities in specific safety categories. Our
534   findings indicated that using smaller KL penalty parameters, more training, and dataset cleansing
535   can improve safety but do not necessarily result in a better trade-off between safety and helpfulness.
536   We also found that safety alignment can induce an undesired bias, in which the model tends to give
537   negative or dismissive responses, regardless of the input context. To address this, we proposed TSDI,
538   which estimates the safety bias using random prompts and corrects it during the generation process.
539   Our experiments demonstrated that TSDI improves the safety-helpfulness Pareto front, achieving
      high safety levels across all safety categories while preserving helpfulness.
