009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Handling missing data is a major challenge in machine learning where missing
012            values are common in various datasets. This work introduces a hypergraph rep-
013            resentation directly constructed from datasets containing missing values. The
014            method does not rely on traditional techniques like deletion or data imputations. A
015            hypergraph is directly extracted from the dataset, preserving the relationships be-
016            tween variables and modeling multi-variable interactions. This enables the model
017            to capture the dataset structure in ways other methods may overlook. The pro-
018
               posed hypergraph learning method can be applied to classification and regression
               tasks. For real-world evaluation, we use the MIMIC-III and Adult datasets focus-
019
               ing on classification performance. Additionally, synthetic datasets with controlled
020
               missingness levels are used to evaluate the method’s effectiveness across degrees
021            of missing data. When compared with imputation and prediction techniques, the
022            hypergraph approach achieves competitive or superior performance. Specifically,
023            our method maintains high performance in scenarios with significant levels of
024            missing data. We demonstrate that the hypergraph representation not only offers a
025            more resilient framework for learning from datasets containing missing data. But
026            also scales effectively across diverse datasets and prediction tasks. The method
027            maintains stable performance under various degrees of missingness, demonstrat-
028            ing its potential as a valuable machine learning tool with high data reliability and
029
               prediction quality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Missing data in machine learning datasets is a major issue caused by various causes, such as human
034
      error, data corruption, or refusing to answer, leading to incomplete datasets. Groves et al. (2011),
035
      Yan et al. (2009) and Shih (2002) categorize missing data into three types: Missing Completely
036   at Random (MCAR), Missing at Random (MAR), and Missing Not at Random (MNAR). These
037   causes impact machine learning models in different ways. MCAR occurs when the missingness is
038   unrelated to the data itself, MAR arises when the missingness is related to observed variables but
039   not the missing ones, and MNAR occurs when the missingness is related to the missing data itself,
040   leading to potentially severe biases Allison (2009).
041
      Various approaches have been developed to handle missing data, a common issue across many fields.
042   These methods aim to preserve the validity of statistical analyses and model predictions despite in-
043   complete datasets. One of the most straightforward techniques is deletion, where instances with
044   missing values are removed Little & Rubin (2019). Deletion methods, such as listwise or pair-
045   wise deletion, are the most straightforward approach but often significantly reduce sample size and
046   increase potential bias if the data are not missing completely at random (MCAR) Little & Rubin
047   (2020). This method simplifies data preparation, particularly when the proportion of missing data is
048   low and the dataset is large. However, deletion methods have limitations, as they can introduce bias,
049   reduce the data representativeness and distort relationships between variables. They lead to reduced
050
      statistical power and unreliable results Graham (2009).
051   More sophisticated approaches were introduced to address these issues. The imputation methods
052   replace missing data with substituted values. The benefits of using imputation methods to handle
053   missing data include the ability to retain valuable information by estimating missing values. Single
      imputation techniques, such as mean substitution, are easy to implement but fail to account for


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      some uncertainties. Multiple imputation addresses this issue by creating several imputed datasets
055   and combining results across these datasets. Therefore, they can provide more reliable estimates
056   and valid statistical inferences. Model-based methods, including maximum likelihood (ML) and
057   Bayesian methods, offer a more rigorous approach by modeling the data with the missing values,
058   assuming a particular distribution for the incomplete data Daniels & Hogan (2008).
059
      Even though data imputations were commonly used, they present several problems. First, it may
060
      introduce bias. As the imputed values are based on statistical or prediction models, they may not
061   fully capture the nature of the missing data. Especially when the data is not missing at random Little
062   & Rubin (2019), Eekhout et al. (2012), and Collins et al. (2001). Imputation can reduce variability
063   in data. When simple imputation techniques are used, they may lead to lower deviations and reduced
064   variance Scheffer (2002). Imputation may fail to model relationships between variables, resulting in
065   misleading and flawed interpretations Resche-Rigon & White (2018), and Kang (2013). Moreover,
066   data imputation can foster overconfidence. When large portions of the dataset are missing. As the
067   imputed data are treated as complete, prediction models may lead to wrong conclusions Van Buuren
068   (2018), Dong & Peng (2013).
069   Knowledge graphs and hypergraphs are utilized to model complex relationships in datasets, pro-
070   viding advanced frameworks for representing simple and higher-order interactions. A knowledge
071   graph represents entities as nodes and relationships between them as edges. It is widely adopted
072   in domains such as natural language processing, recommendation systems, and the semantic web
073   Paulheim (2017). Although highly effective for pairwise relationships, knowledge graphs often fall
074   short in representing more complex interactions involving multiple entities, as they are restricted
075
      to binary connections between nodes Ji et al. (2021). To overcome this limitation, hypergraphs ex-
      tend traditional graph models by introducing hyperedges, which can connect more than two nodes
076
      at once. Therefore capturing higher-order relationships within the data Zhou et al. (2006). This
077
      makes hypergraphs particularly useful in domains like bioinformatics and social networks where
078   interactions often involve more than two elements Yadati et al. (2019). For example, in biological
079   networks, a hypergraph can effectively represent complex interactions between multiple genes, pro-
080   teins, or metabolic pathways, providing a richer model than the simple pairwise interactions Ahn
081   et al. (2010).
082
      Hypergraphs have also been successfully applied in machine learning tasks such as classification and
083
      clustering. In multi-label classification, for instance, hypergraph-based methods outperform tradi-
084   tional graph-based approaches by leveraging the multi-way relationships among labels and features
085   Sun et al. (2008). Moreover, hypergraphs are proving to be particularly useful for handling missing
086   data, where traditional methods struggle to capture the underlying structure of incomplete datasets
087   Gao et al. (2020), Liu et al. (2017). By leveraging hypergraphs, researchers can uncover more intri-
088   cate relationships in datasets and provide more accurate and robust analyses than knowledge graphs
089   alone.
090   The previous researches in handling missing data primarily revolves around the complex relation-
091   ship between features. While existing methods, such as imputation techniques and machine learn-
092   ing algorithms, have shown promise in addressing individual missing data, they often fall short of
093   accurately capturing the intricate interdependencies. Moreover, most current techniques do not ad-
094   equately address the variability introduced by missing data, potentially leading to biased estimates
095   and conclusions. There is a need for more sophisticated models that can learn from incomplete data
096   without making strong assumptions about the data mechanism or losing information introduced by
097   missingness. Developing methods that can better understand and utilize the complex relationships
098
      between features in the presence of missing data remains a significant challenge in the field.
099   The structure of this work is organized as follows: Section 2 presents the proposed method. This
100   section details how the hypergraph is constructed, and explains how the hypergraph can be used for
101   inference. Section 3 describes the experimental setup, providing details about the datasets. And
102   outlining the test to compare approaches. In Section 4, the experimental results are presented, and
103   the proposed approach will be compared to other methods. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper
104
      by summarizing the findings and suggesting potential directions for future work.

      2.1    H YPERGRAPH C ONSTRUCTION
135
136   Given a dataset, each node in the hypergraph corresponds to a feature or variable. The hyperedges
137   represent relationships or interactions between subsets of nodes, allowing for more complex connec-
138   tions compared to traditional graph structures. Hypergraphs are particularly useful for representing
139   variables and their interactions in scenarios where relationships involve more than two entities si-
140   multaneously. Formally, a hypergraph is defined as:
141                                                                                                        H = (V, E)
142
143   where:
144
145            • V is a set of nodes (or vertices), i.e., V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn }.
146            • E is a set of hyperedges, where each hyperedge is a subset of V , i.e., ei ⊆ V for each
147              ei ∈ E.
148
149   In other words, each hyperedge ei can connect multiple vertices simultaneously, which distinguishes
150   hypergraphs from traditional graphs. The degree of a vertex v ∈ V is the number of hyperedges that
151   contain the vertex.
152   Each hyperedge represents a subset of dataset samples that share the same number of available
153   parameters. This facilitates the organization of complete and incomplete data, allowing the grouping
154   of samples with similar patterns of missing data. To ensures that subgraphs derived from larger
155   sample sizes have greater influence on the analysis, each hyperedge ei ∈ E is weighted by the
156   number of samples N (ei ) that contribute to it. The weight of hyperedge ei is defined as:
157
158                                                                                                    w(ei ) = N (ei )
159
160   For each pair of variables within a hyperedge, we define edges that represent the regression re-
161   lationships between the variable pairs. The weight of each edge is determined by the correlation
      coefficient ρij between two variables vi and vj . The edge weight is defined as:

166   where ρij is the Pearson correlation coefficient calculated between variables vi and vj . This weight
167
      reflects the strength of the relationship between the variables.
168   As summary, to generate the hypergraph dataset samples are grouped based on identical patterns of
169   missing variables. Each distinct missingness pattern forms a unique hyperedge ei , and the weight
170   of each hyperedge is assigned according to the number of samples contributing to its construction.
171   Thus, each hyperedge weight w(ei ) represents the prevalence of a specific missingness pattern,
172   while the pairwise relationships between variables within each hyperedge are weighted by the cor-
173
      relation coefficient ρij , providing a quantitative measure of the relationship strength.

175   2.2   H YPERGRAPH I NFERENCE
176
177   The hypergraph representation effectively encodes the interdependencies between variables and the
178   missingness characteristics within a dataset. This encoding allows the hypergraph to generalize ob-
179
      served data and infer values for missing or unknown variables. The inference process for predicting
      unseen samples using the hypergraph can be outlined as follows:
180
181   To make predictions, we first identify the hyperedges that contain the unknown target variable. For
182   each selected hyperedge ei ∈ E, the values of its vertices (nodes) are assigned based on the values
183   from the sample for which we are making the prediction. Let the sample values be denoted as
184   x = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }, where each xi corresponds to a value of vertex vi .
185   Once the values from the sample are assigned to the vertices, we traverse the hypergraph from ver-
186   tices that have known values to predict the values of their neighboring vertices (nodes with missing
187   values). The predicted value of a neighboring vertex vj is calculated using the regression coefficients
188   βij , which were derived during hypergraph generation, as follows:

226   3.1     R EAL DATASET

228   3.1.1    MIMIC-III
229
      The MIMIC-III (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care III) dataset is a large, publicly avail-
230
      able database comprising de-identified health data from over 40,000 critical care patients admitted
231   to the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center between 2001 and 2012 Johnson et al. (2016). It
232   includes detailed information such as patient demographics, vital signs, laboratory results, medi-
233   cations, procedures, diagnostic codes, and clinical notes. The dataset is widely used for medical
234   research, particularly in predictive modeling, due to its richness in temporal and multimodal data. It
235   allows researchers to develop and validate machine learning models in a healthcare context.
236
      To preprocess the MIMIC-III dataset for our experiments, we selected features that included de-
237   mographic information, patient monitoring data, and laboratory results. Continuous variables were
238   normalized to ensure consistent scaling across all features, and categorical variables were one-hot
239   encoded to convert them into a numerical format suitable for machine learning models. Finally, the
240   dataset was split into training and testing sets in a 4 to 1 ratio. For our experiment, we perform
241   classification task of predicting whether a patient’s length of stay (LOS) will exceed 3 days in the
242   ICU. This is a common challenge using the MIMIC-III dataset. The complexity of ICU patients and
243   the dynamic nature of their conditions make this task challenging, requiring sophisticated models.
244
245   3.1.2    A DULT
246
      The Adult dataset, also known as the ”Census Income” dataset, is a widely used dataset in machine
247
      learning research and comes from the 1994 U.S. Census Bureau data. It contains demographic and
248
      employment-related attributes for individuals such as age, education level, occupation, marital sta-
249   tus, work hours per week, and native country. The dataset has over 48,000 records with categorical
250   and numerical features, and includes some missing values. Its diversity and real-world nature make
251   it a popular choice for classification tasks.
252
      The prediction task associated with the Adult dataset is to determine whether an individual earns
253
      more than $50K a year based on their demographic and employment features. This binary classifi-
254
      cation problem involves using attributes to predict income level. The task is a benchmark problem
255   for testing classification algorithms, as it requires handling a mix of categorical and continuous data,
256   missing values, and potential biases in the dataset.

      5   C ONCLUSION
359
360
361   This paper presents a novel hypergraph-based approach for handling missing data in machine
362   learning, offering a robust alternative to traditional methods like deletion or imputation. Directly
363
      constructing hypergraphs from datasets, effectively preserves variable relationships and models
      multi-variable interactions, leading to improved performance in classification and regression tasks.
364
      Through comprehensive evaluations on real-world datasets as well as synthetic datasets with con-
365
      trolled missingness, the proposed method demonstrates highly competitive performance compared
366   to other methods. It consistently achieves accuracy that are comparable to, or better than, those
367   obtained using imputation techniques and traditional classifiers. Notably, the hypergraph represen-
368   tation excels in scenarios with substantial missing data. Furthermore, a notable advantage of our
369   hypergraph-based method is its consistency. The results show that it provides reliable performance
370   across all datasets with varying missingness levels. This consistency is crucial for real-world appli-
371   cations.
372   Beyond its demonstrated effectiveness in handling missing data within individual datasets, the pro-
373   posed hypergraph-based method shows great potential for cross-dataset learning, particularly in sce-
374   narios where feature sets differ significantly between datasets. In such cases, merging datasets often
375   results in substantial amounts of missing data due to the absence of overlapping features. Tradi-
376   tional approaches to address this issue, such as imputation or deletion, can lead to information loss
377   or introduce bias. However, the hypergraph representation naturally accommodates missing values
      while preserving the multi-variable relationships inherent to each dataset. This allows the model to

      leverage the complementary information present in different datasets without relying on imputation,
379   making it highly suited for cross-dataset applications. As a result, this method opens up promis-

381   sources often produce similar datasets.
