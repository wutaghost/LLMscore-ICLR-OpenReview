011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Contextual Self-Modulation (CSM) is a potent regularization mechanism for Neural
014            Context Flows (NCFs) which demonstrates powerful meta-learning on physical sys-
015            tems. However, CSM has limitations in its applicability across different modalities
016
               and in high-data regimes. In this work, we introduce two extensions: iCSM, which
               expands CSM to infinite-dimensional tasks, and StochasticNCF, which improves
017
               scalability. These extensions are demonstrated through comprehensive experimen-
018
               tation on a range of tasks, including dynamical systems with parameter variations,
019            computer vision challenges, and curve fitting problems. iCSM embeds the con-
020            texts into an infinite-dimensional function space, as opposed to CSM which uses
021            finite-dimensional context vectors. StochasticNCF enables the application of both
022            CSM and iCSM to high-data scenarios by providing a low-cost approximation of
023            meta-gradient updates through a sampled set of nearest environments. Additionally,
024            we incorporate higher-order Taylor expansions via Taylor-Mode automatic differ-
025            entiation, revealing that higher-order approximations do not necessarily enhance
026            generalization. Finally, we demonstrate how CSM can be integrated into other
027
               meta-learning frameworks with FlashCAVIA, a computationally efficient exten-
               sion of the CAVIA meta-learning framework (Zintgraf et al. 2019). FlashCAVIA
028
               outperforms its predecessor across various benchmarks and reinforces the utility of
029
               bi-level optimization techniques. Together, these contributions reaffirm the power-
030            ful benefits of CSM, and suggest that its spectrum of addressable meta-learning and
031            out-of-distribution tasks is limited to physical systems. Our open-sourced library,
032            designed for flexible integration of self-modulation into contextual meta-learning
033            workflows, is available at AnonymousGitHubRepo.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Meta-learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm in machine learning, addressing the limitations of
039
      conventional approaches that train a single algorithm for a specific task. This innovative technique
      aims to develop models capable of rapid adaptation to novel but related tasks with minimal data,
040
      a process often referred to as “learning to learn” (Wang et al., 2021). By leveraging common
041
      information across multiple training environments (or meta-knowledge), meta-learning algorithms
042   can efficiently adapt to new scenarios without starting from scratch (Hospedales et al., 2021). The
043   success of meta-learning has been demonstrated in various domains, including dynamical system
044   reconstruction (Norcliffe et al., 2021), program induction (Devlin et al., 2017), out-of-distribution
045   (OoD) generalization (Yao et al., 2021), and continual learning (Hurtado et al., 2021).
046
      Recent advancements in meta-learning have focused on reducing the number of adaptable parameters
047
      to a smaller subset known as a “context”, which encodes environment-specific information. This
048   approach, termed “contextual meta-learning”, has shown superior performance in terms of speed,
049   memory efficiency, and accuracy compared to alternative methods (Zintgraf et al., 2019; Garnelo
050   et al., 2018; Gordon et al., 2019; Nzoyem et al., 2024). Given the growing popularity of contextual
051   meta-learning, a comprehensive comparison of some of its methods is essential to guide its users. In
052   this paper, we identify three critical axes of comparison—task modality, task dimensionality, and
053   data regime—necessary for evaluating the general competitiveness and applicability of contextual
      meta-learning approaches.

054
      (R1 - Task Modality) Contextual meta-learning methods have demonstrated remarkable success
055   across various data modalities, including images, meshes, audio, and functas (Dupont et al., 2022).
056   However, recent observations have revealed limitations in their performance on time series data
057   from physical systems (Kirchmeyer et al., 2022). For instance, CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019)
058   with its bi-level optimization algorithm has been reported to overfit when learning the underlying
059   parameter-dependence of dynamical systems (Nzoyem et al., 2024). Conversely, the Neural Context
060   Flow (NCF) (Nzoyem et al., 2024), which has recently achieved state-of-the-art results on several
061   dynamical systems benchmarks, remains untested in decision-making scenarios and other domains
062   where established contextual meta-learning methods excel.
063
064   (R2 - Task Dimensionality) The ability to adapt to infinite-dimensional changes, rather than
065
      fixed-size vector embeddings, is increasingly demanded of contextual meta-learning. Such demands
      are pressing in physical systems learning (Yin et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2017; Nzoyem et al., 2024),
066
      where a common challenge is generalizing to parameter changes in the underlying dynamical system,
067
      such as the time-invariant gravity g of a swinging pendulum. While several recent works have
068   successfully modelled such time-invariant parameter changes through contextual meta-learning (Liu
069   et al.; Day et al., 2021), many approaches have overlooked cases where the changing parameter is
070   itself a function of time, such as the forcing term f (·) of a pendulum.
071
072   (R3 - Data Regime) While meta-learning is designed to require limited data during the meta-testing
073   stage (Hospedales et al., 2021), the optimal amount of data needed for effective meta-training remains
074   an open question. This issue is particularly evident in image completion tasks, where Conditional
075   Neural Processes (CNPs) perform well in low-data regimes but struggle to reconstruct known pixels in
076   high-data scenarios (Gordon et al., 2019). Understanding the necessary adjustments for meta-learning
077
      in both low and high-data regimes is crucial, especially given the potential contradictions between
      neural scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) and monotone learning in some
078
      scenarios (Bousquet et al., 2022).
079
080   Contextual Self-Modulation (CSM) is a recently proposed regularization mechanism for smooth
081   physical systems (Nzoyem et al., 2024) that shows promise in addressing the aforementioned re-
082   quirements. Importantly, it can be combined with other contextual meta-learning techniques. This
083
      work examines several families of contextual meta-learning approaches through the lens of CSM. In
      the following sections, we present a common problem setting followed by a brief summary of the
084
      methods involved.

091   with limited data. Adaptation is qualified as In-Domain (InD) when ptr = pte , and Out-of-Distribution

093   sampled.

      Contextual meta-learning methods aim to modulate the behavior of a main network with latent
119   information. Our work broadly encompasses three families of contextual meta-learning methods: the
120   Neural Processes family, Gradient-Based Meta-Learning (GBML), and Neural Context Flows (NCFs).

      1.3   C ONTRIBUTIONS
142
143   Our primary contribution is the systematic examination of Contextual Self-Modulation (CSM) in
144   various task and data settings, exploring strategies to mitigate issues that arise, and investigating its
145   potential to enhance established meta-learning techniques. Specifically, we contribute the following
146   four points:
147
      (1) We extend the CSM regularization mechanism to infinite dimensions (iCSM) and arbitrarily
148
      high Taylor orders, which we successfully apply to complex dynamical systems with functional
149   parameter changes and beyond. Notably, we show that iCSM performs consistently well, even on
150   finite-dimensional problems, thus generalizing CSM.
151
152
      (2) We propose StochasticNCF, a stochastic generalization of Neural Context Flows (NCF) (Nzoyem
      et al., 2024) designed to handle the computational demands of high-data regimes like curve-fitting
153
      and image completion. The randomness it introduces is compatible with both CSM and iCSM, and
154
      enables robust comparison of NCF against gradient-based meta-learning which has such a randomness
155   feature built-in.
156
157   (3) We present FlashCAVIA, a powerful update of CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019). This implementation
158
      demonstrates the compatibility of CSM with bi-level adaptation rules. Together, they enable more
      efficient use of numerous inner updates, leading to more expressive models.
159
160   (4) We develop a software library that facilitates the application of all aforementioned strategies and
161   the combination thereof. The code, along with several examples, is made open-source and available
      at AnonymousGitHubRepo.

202   Finite dimensions. The original CSM methodology (Nzoyem et al., 2024) proposes Ξ ⊂ Rdξ . In
203   that approach, the context is pre-processed by an optional context network, then concatenated to (a
204   pre-processed) xe before feeding into a main neural network.
205
206   Infinite dimensions. We extend CSM to infinite-dimensional variations by letting Ξ be a function
207   space, naming this approach iCSM (see Fig. 1). This extension allows our model fθ to adapt to a
208   broader range of changes. In our implementation of iCSM, we chose Ξ as the space of multi-layer
209   perceptrons, whose weights are flattened into a 1-dimensional tensor to perform Taylor expansion.
210   The input xe is no longer concatenated to this 1-dimensional tensor ξ e , but rather to its output ξ e (·)
211

212
      networks at 0 since Taylor expansions are local approximations and perform better when ξ e is close
213
      to ξ j . Random initialization of neural networks does not guarantee such proximity, and leads to
214   divergence early in the training process.

      Higher-order Taylor expansions. With Proposition 3.1, Nzoyem et al. (2024) propose a technique
217   to efficiently calculate the second-order Taylor expansion without materializing Hessians. However,
218   their approach is limited to second order, and a naive calculation of higher-order derivatives would
219   incur an exorbitant cost of O(exp(k)) due to redundant recomputations (Bettencourt et al., 2019). In
220   this work, we leverage Taylor-mode automatic differentiation (AD) to compute these derivatives at a
221   much lower cost. Specifically, we adapt the jet API (Bradbury et al., 2018) to derive fˆθj in Eq. (2).
222   While jet requires both a primal value and a series of coefficients to calculate the derivatives of
223   f = g ◦ h, our setting is not concerned with function composition. As such, we set our primal value
224   to ξ j and the demanded series to [ξ e − ξ j , 0, . . . , 0]. The output sequence is then multiplied by the
225   appropriate inverse factorials and summed to produce the Taylor approximation. For the remainder of
226   this work, any Taylor expansion of order k > 2 leverages the jet API.
227
228
      Benefits of self-modulation. A key advantage of both CSM and iCSM is uncertainty quantification
      (Nzoyem et al., 2024). Upon training, we obtain multiple contexts in close proximity, allowing us to
229
      produce an ensemble of candidate predictions in order to ascertain the variance inherent in the task
230
      distribution through the learned contexts (see for example Figs. 11 and 12).
231
232   Perceived limitations. We acknowledge that not all functions can be approximated near ξ 0 ∈ Ξ by
233   Taylor expansion, as such guarantees are only valid for analytic functions within a prescribed radius
234   of convergence R > 0 (Cartan, 1995). In Appendix B.2, we address the question of whether our
235   power series in Eq. (2), modulated through CSM, can recover discrepancies when the underlying
236   parameters are farther apart than R.
237
238   CSM for NCF. In the framework of Neural Context Flows (NCF) (Nzoyem et al., 2024), CSM
239   refers specifically to the Taylor expansion process used to produce candidate predictions. It does
240   not encompass other concepts, such as the 3-networks architecture2 , penalization, or the alternating
241
      optimization scheme. In fact, we introduce a joint-optimization variant termed NCF* in Section 3.4,
      demonstrating improved performance over NCF while keeping the same CSM mechanism.

      2.3     F LASH CAVIA
271
272   To demonstrate the applicability of CSM within other meta-learning frameworks, we redesigned and
273   implemented CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019) from scratch, resulting in FlashCAVIA. This derivative
274   framework is designed for greater performance compared to the original CAVIA implementation,
275   while incorporating CSM at the model level.
276   Some key improvements in FlashCAVIA include:
277
278          (i) Parallelization: We parallelize the sequential task loop (line 6 in Algorithm 1) to process all
279              environments simultaneously, allowing for fairer comparison with the three-way parallelized
280              NCF (Nzoyem et al., 2024).
281
            (ii) Efficient inner updates: For each inner gradient loop, we use the prefix sum primitive
282
                 scan (Bradbury et al., 2018) to perform longer and more efficient inner updates. This
283              follows a recent trend with efficient hardware-aware implementations of state-space models
284              (Gu & Dao, 2023).
285
286
            (iii) Custom optimizer: We leverage a custom optimizer to steer the inner gradient updates,
                  which is particularly important when performing a large number of inner gradient updates
287
                  (e.g., H = 100 in Section 3.1).
288
289
      Although it requires no changes at the model architecture level, our main contribution to FlashCAVIA
290   is the integration of CSM. The forward-mode Taylor expansion step may require a bespoke reverse-
291   mode automatic differentiation depending on the AD mode used at higher levels of the optimization
292   process, and whether custom adjoint rules are involved.
293
      These improvements in FlashCAVIA not only enhance its performance but also provide a versatile
294
      platform for comparing different meta-learning approaches. They also allow the exploration of the
295
      benefits of CSM across various frameworks.

299
300   This section presents a comprehensive analysis of the CSM mechanism’s performance across four
301   distinct problem domains: curve-fitting, optimal control, dynamical system reconstruction, and image
302   completion. We examine how requirements R1 to R3 are met in these varied settings. For each
303   experiment, we describe the dataset and analyze the results and their implications. The training

      hyperparamters are detailed in Appendix B.

306   3.1     S INE R EGRESSION
307
308   The sine regression experiment, a standard benchmark in meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017), serves as
309
      our initial test for the CSM mechanism. This curve-fitting regression problem allows us to assess
      its benefits in NCF. This experiment also evaluates the impact of H ∈ {1, 5, 100} gradient update
310
      iterations on the CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019) and MAML (Finn et al., 2017) baselines, and in our
311
      FlashCAVIA implementation. We generate input-output pairs based on the generalized sinusoid
312   y = A sin (x − α), where amplitude A ∈ [0.1, 5.0] and phase α ∈ [0, π] are sampled from uniform
313   distributions. The same distributions are used for both meta-training and adaptation. We explore
314   low-, medium-, and high-data regimes with N = 250, N = 1000, and N = 12500 environments,
315   respectively.
316

      3.4          I MAGE C OMPLETION

      To assess the impact of bi-level optimization schemes like FlashCAVIA and alternating ones like
458   NCF, we focus on the challenging image completion task from (Garnelo et al., 2018; Zintgraf
459   et al., 2019). Using the CelebA32 dataset (Liu et al., 2018), our objective is to learn the mapping
460   f : [0, 1]2 → [0, 1]3 from pixel coordinates to RGB values. We treat each image as an environment,
461   meta-training on the CelebA Training split and meta-testing on both its Validation (not reported) and
462   Test splits. The support set for each environment consists of a few K = 10, 100 or 1000 labeled
463   pixels, while the query set comprises all 1024 pixels. For this task, we introduce NCF*, a variation of
464   NCF that eliminates the costly proximal alternating gradient descent regularization mechanism and
465   performs joint optimization of both contexts and model weights.
466   Fig. 4 illustrates that Taylor expansion has a smoothing or underfitting effect, particularly noticeable
467   with FlashCAVIA. Contrasting with additional results in Table 6, we observe that visual quality tends
468   to be negatively correlated with the FlashCAVIA MSE metrics, a sign of overfitting on the few-shot
469   pixels, an effect compounded by the algorithmic adjustments detailed in Appendix A. Additionally,
470   Fig. 4 demonstrates the degradation of results for NCF and NCF* with increasing learning shots,
471   potentially explained by massive overfitting and monotone learning (Bousquet et al., 2022). In their
472
      current state, the joint and alternating optimization schemes appear suboptimal for this task, with
      results falling short of the state-of-the-art (Garnelo et al., 2018). Consequently, further investigations
473
      into their generalization capabilities across low and high data regimes are warranted.

483   modality), R2 (task dimensionality), and R3 (data regime) outlined in Section 1. When incorporated

485   extend to CSM embedded into other contextual meta-learning methodologies, warranting further
      investigation.

      Despite its proximal, stochastic, and CSM regularization mechanisms, our experiments on sine
507   regression and image completion reveal limitations in Neural Context Flows (NCF) for high-data
508   regimes. In these scenarios, NCF and its variants struggle to discriminate between environments
509   amid vast data quantities. With higher-order Taylor expansions, we effectively hit diminishing
510   returns. Conversely, NCF demonstrates unparalleled efficacy in physical systems such as linear
511   optimal control and forced pendulum, presumably due to the inherent regularity these systems exhibit
512   compared to computer vision challenges. This dichotomy suggests that NCF is optimally suited for
513   physical systems with clear inter-task commonalities.

      4.2   L IMITATIONS
517
      While our work provides extensive experimental results on CSM, several limitations merit acknowl-
518
      edgment • (i) First, while substantive discussion of the Neural Process family is provided in Ap-
519
      pendix A, our investigation still focuses on Neural Context Flows and Gradient-Based Meta-Learning,
520   and does not encompass all contextual meta-learning frameworks • (ii) Furthermore, our study did
521   not address classification tasks, which have traditionally served as fertile ground for meta-learning
522   experimentation • (iii) Finally, in scenarios precluding the use of forward- or Taylor-mode AD (e.g.,
523   Neural ODEs within FlashCAVIA), reliance on reverse-mode AD renders Taylor approximations
524   of order greater than 3 computationally prohibitive. Focusing on FlashCAVIA, we note that further
525   theoretical work is required to elucidate its benefits.

528   4.3   C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
529
530   This study has substantially advanced the application scope of Contextual Self-Modulation (CSM)
531
      beyond Neural Context Flows (NCF), elucidating its efficacy and constraints across a spectrum
      of tasks and modalities. Our contributions, encompassing the introduction of iCSM for infinite-
532
      dimensional tasks and StochasticNCF for improved scalability in high-data regimes, offer valuable
533
      methodologies for advancing meta-learning in dynamical systems, computer vision challenges, and
534   curve fitting problems. Through extensive empirical evaluation, we have demonstrated iCSM’s
535   capacity to facilitate OoD generalization and, when integrated with bi-level optimization schemes, to
536   enhance prediction quality. These findings underscore the efficacy of iCSM for smooth dynamical
537   systems, where StochasticNCF exhibits superior performance. We also identified several limitations
538   associated with its alternating optimization scheme, notably a low expressiveness in high-data settings.
539   These constraints delineate critical areas for improvement and future research, potentially culminating
      in a general-purpose framework for meta-learning across a broader range of domains.

723   Neural Process Family. Conditional Neural Processes (CNPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018), the progen-
724
      itors of this family, ingeniously combine the test-time flexibility of Gaussian Processes with the
      scalability and expressivity of Neural Networks. Utilizing an encoder network gϕ , CNPs construct
725
      a permutation-invariant representation ξ e for each environment in Dtre . Both input datapoints x and
726
      their corresponding labels y are fed into g, adhering to the Deep Sets theory (Zaheer et al., 2017).
727   A decoder fθ is then introduced, whose predictions (conditional means and standard deviations)
                     e
728   on (x, ·) ∈ Dtest are parametrized by ξ e . Trained by minimizing the resulting negative Gaussian
729   probability, CNPs demonstrate significant advantages in function regression, image completion, and
730   dynamics forecasting (Norcliffe et al., 2021). Their particular appeal lies in their suitability for
731   streaming data, given their adaptation complexity scaling as O(n + m), where n and m represent the
732   number of points in Dtre and Dtee , respectively (cf. Table 1).
733   While CNPs eliminate the need for gradient updates at test time, they tend to underfit. Extensive
734   research has been conducted to address this weakness, including notable contributions from (Gordon
735   et al., 2019) and (Bruinsma et al., 2023). However, this issue remains a topic of significant interest.
736   Another limitation of NPs is their restrictive assumption of finite-dimensional latent variables, a
737   problem addressed by (Gordon et al., 2019) through the introduction of infinite-dimensional latent
738   variables for translation equivariance, and further explored by (Lee et al., 2023), among others.
739
740   Gradient-Based Meta-Learning. Gradient-Based Meta-Learning (GBML) offers an alternative
741   approach to meta-learning through bi-level optimization. These methods optimise shared knowledge
742   θ in an outer loop while adapting to each task in an inner loop. Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning
743   (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017), for instance, searches for an optimal initialization in the outer loop
744
      to facilitate fine-tuning in the inner loop, necessitating the use of second-order derivatives during
      meta-training. While agnostic to model architecture, this approach scales poorly as models grow
745
      larger, a challenge addressed by CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019) whose training algorithm is presented
746
      in Algorithm 15 . CAVIA leverages external context parameters ξ := {ξ e }N e=1 to modulate the model,
747   with these contexts being the sole parameters adapted during meta-testing. Although more efficient
748   than MAML, CAVIA still requires Hessian information in its bi-level approach to optimising {θ, ξ}.
749
750
      It is important to compare this form of contextual meta-learning with other, more computationally
      efficient styles, such as joint or alternating optimization. Moreover, GBML methods are susceptible
751
      to overfitting, necessitating further investigation into the role of dataset size in the training process.
752
      Notably, significant efforts have been made to develop first-order MAML variants, as exemplified
753
754       5
            Our definition of CAVIA differs from Zintgraf et al. (2019) in that we perform meta-updates on the support
755   sets Dtr , rather than on Dte . This way, CAVIA sees the same labelled datapoints as NCF during its meta-training
      process (cf. Algorithm 2). This ensures a fair comparison against NCF and CNP.

      by iMAML and Reptile (Nichol, 2018), with the latter even outperforming MAML in transductive
757   classification settings. However, the benefits of second-order information remain invaluable for
758   the expressivity of contextual methods like CAVIA, making the improvement of scalability while
759   maintaining accuracy a vital, unsolved challenge. The memory and computational complexity of
760   CAVIA’s adaptation rule is presented in Table 1.

762   Neural Context Flows. The original formulation of Neural Context Flow (NCF) (Nzoyem et al.,
763   2024) illustrated in Algorithm 2, optimizes model weights and contexts in an alternating manner,
764   diverging from the bi-level optimization approach. This training scheme mirrors the Multi-Task
765   Learning (MTL) joint training paradigm (Wang et al., 2021). To enable effective modulation
766   of weights, NCF introduces the concept of CSM, facilitating seamless information flow across
767   environments. This approach has been successfully tested on several physical systems with limited
768   number of trajectories. NCF offers several advantages, including interpretability and uncertainty
769
      quantification, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance against CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019)
      and CoDA (Kirchmeyer et al., 2022) in few-shot learning of physical systems across dozens of
770
      environments. Moreover, for linearly parameterized systems, the underlying physical parameters can
771
      be recovered using a simple linear transform (Blanke & Lelarge, 2024). However, the efficacy of
772   CSM in high-data regimes remains largely unexplored.
773
774   Comparative studies between GBML and MTL with multi-head structures by Wang et al. (2021)
775
      reveal that, in addition to sharing similar optimization formulations in certain settings, the predictions
      generated by these two methods are comparable, with the gap inversely proportional to neural network
776
      depth. Furthermore, their research demonstrates that MTL can achieve similar or even superior
777
      results compared to powerful GBML algorithms like MetaOptNet (Lee et al., 2019), while incurring
778   significantly lower computational costs. In our work, we empirically evaluate the performance of
779   these training regimes across various settings.

797   In conclusion, modulating neural network behaviour with contextual information presents a non-
798   trivial challenge. Beyond CNPs, CAVIA, and NCF, numerous approaches have attempted this task
799   using global context vectors (Norcliffe et al., 2021; Massaroli et al., 2020) and other methodologies.

      specific regression tasks, others for physical systems (Day et al., 2021; Nzoyem et al., 2024) or vision

       For the image completion task, we adopted the model hyperparameters from (Zintgraf et al., 2019),
1110
       implementing a 5-layer MLP with 128 hidden units each and a context size of 128. However, we made
1111   one significant modification: the activation function was changed to softplus to promote smoothness
1112   in the model’s behavior. This alteration allows for potentially more nuanced image completions.
1113
1114
       Following Zintgraf et al. (2019), we use an MLP with 5 hidden layers of 128 nodes each, followed by
       ReLU activations. The context vector is directly concatenated to the 2 inputs before being fed into
1115
       the first layer of the MLP. We test various orders of Taylor expansion, from k = 0 to k = 3, for all
1116
       methods. To ensure fair comparison, we adjust the number of epochs/steps to maintain a consistent
1117   training duration of 1.5 hours across all methods.
1118
1119   In our FlashCAVIA implementation, we use 4 inner gradient updates, while for NCF, we employ 20
1120
       in the inner approximation of its proximal operators and a fixed 500 adaptation steps. To expedite
       FlashCAVIA training, we meta-train on |B| = 512 environments simultaneously, which unfortunately
1121
       precluded running FlashCAVIA with high k and/or K values. We set the outer learning rate to 10−4
1122
       and the inner learning rate to 10−1 . For NCF, we used a constant learning rate of 10−3 for both model
1123   weights and contexts. To ensure fair comparison, we kept the number of training steps under control,
1124   with no run lasting longer than 1.5 hours.
