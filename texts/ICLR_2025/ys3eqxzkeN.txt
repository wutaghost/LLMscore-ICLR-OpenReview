009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Object detection in videos is a crucial task in the computer vision domain. Ex-
012            isting methods have explored different approaches to detect objects and classify
013            the videos. However, detecting tiny objects (e.g., gun) in videos has always been
014            a challenging and rigorous task. Moreover, the existing video analysis (detection
015            and classification) models may not achieve high accuracy for gun detection in
016            videos in real-world scenarios due to the lack of a large amount of labeled data.
017            Thus, it is imperative to develop an efficient method to capture the features of tiny
018
               objects and train models that can perform accurate gun detection. To address this
               challenge, we make three contributions. First, we perform an empirical study of
019
               several existing video classification methods to identify the presence of guns in
020
               videos. Our extensive analysis shows that these methods may not achieve high
021            accuracy in detecting guns in videos. Second, we propose a novel gun detection
022            method with image-augmented training and evaluate the technique in real-world
023            settings with different evaluation metrics. Third, our experimental results demon-
024            strate that our proposed domain-specific method can achieve significant perfor-
025            mance improvements in real-world settings compared to the other popular meth-
026            ods. We also discuss emerging challenges and critical aspects of detecting tiny
027            objects, e.g., guns, using existing computer vision techniques, their limitations,
028            and future research opportunities.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Public safety is crucial to ensure the well-being and dignity of every citizen. It is highly essential
033   to protect individuals from threats such as gun violence. Gun violence is one of the major attacks
034   against public security. The manual monitoring systems that mainly rely on a human operator for
035   analyzing CCTV footage to detect guns might lead to human error (Goldmeier, 2023). Moreover,
036   several other issues, e.g., boredom, voyeurism, and biased profiling (Surette, 2005), might deterio-
037   rate the potential shooting detection scenarios. Detecting guns in videos has been a highly important
038   task in the computer vision domain due to several factors, e.g., low video quality, small size of
039
      gun objects, and lack of large datasets to train models effectively. Identifying a tiny object (e.g.,
      gun) from a sequence of frames is complex due to several reasons, e.g., varying lighting conditions,
040
      similarity to other objects, and low video quality (Wu et al., 2021; Leszczuk et al., 2024). Despite
041
      significant efforts, existing methods often struggle with detection accuracy, robustness, and general-
042   ization performance, especially in real-world settings. The shortcomings of the current approaches
043   underscore the requirement for a more accurate solution that is capable of overcoming the limitations
044   of current techniques in detecting guns in videos and performs well in dynamic environments.
045
      To mitigate the aforementioned issues regarding human-monitored CCTV footage analysis, it is cru-
046
      cial to leverage Artificial Intelligence (AI) techniques to identify the guns in videos. Previous studies
047
      have explored various AI-driven approaches to tackle this problem. Most of the existing video analy-
048   sis models (e.g., (Tran et al., 2018; Tong et al., 2022; Kondratyuk et al., 2021; Arnab et al., 2021; Fan
049   et al., 2021)) are intended for action recognition in videos and have been evaluated on benchmark
050   action recognition datasets, which might not be able to perform effective gun detection. Among the
051   detection-oriented models, CNN (Bhatti et al., 2021; Manikandan & Rahamathunnisa, 2022; Elmir
052   et al., 2019; Khin & Htaik, 2024), R-CNN (Olmos et al., 2018), faster R-CNN (Verma & Dhillon,
053   2017; Alaqil et al., 2020), and different versions of YOLO (Warsi et al., 2019; Khin & Htaik, 2024)
      are widely utilized. Deployment of hybrid models, e.g., Faster-RCNN-VGG16 (Olmos et al., 2018),


                                                         1

054
      MobileNetV3-SSD (Ghazal et al., 2020), ResNet-YOLO (Salido et al., 2021), are also gaining at-
055   tention for gun detection in videos. Despite the advancements in weapon detection technologies,
056   current methods may be inadequate in accurately identifying guns, particularly in scenarios involv-
057   ing low-quality video footage and unpredictable real-world conditions. In this paper, in order to
058   address these problems, we propose a novel accurate model that is tailored towards gun detection in
059   videos. To this end, we also conduct a comprehensive empirical analysis of existing solutions. The
060   key contributions of this work are as follows.
061
062          • We perform an empirical study on several video classification approaches for detecting
063
               guns in videos, including 3D CNN approach (Tran et al., 2018), pre-trained video models
               (Kondratyuk et al., 2021), pre-trained Transformer-based models (Tong et al., 2022), and
064
               hybrid architectures. In our empirical analysis, we underscore that none of these meth-
065
               ods might achieve high performance in detecting guns from videos as they do for action
066            recognition.
067
             • We propose a novel gun detection methodology that involves a two-stage training process:
068
               (1) image-augmented training, wherein we utilize gun image datasets to introduce subtle
069            gun features into the image model, facilitating the extraction of spatial representations from
070            video frames that contain guns, and (2) temporal modeling, in which we incorporate a se-
071            quence model in addition to the image model to effectively capture sequential dependencies
072            and temporal information of the video data.
073          • The empirical results illustrate that our proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art ap-
074            proaches, yielding significant improvements in performance on a specially crafted synthetic
075            firearms action recognition video dataset.
076
             • Our analysis investigates multiple application scenarios, drawing from real-world contexts,
077            to highlight the imperative for developing high-performing AI-driven methods for identi-
078            fying guns in video data.
079
             • We also highlight several key challenges and limitations of existing gun detection tech-
080
               niques and discuss potential future research directions.

161   In the real world, machine learning (ML) models learn through a vast amount of data in the training
      phase for different learning tasks. For instance, the state-of-the-art high-performing image classifi-


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      cation models such as ResNet (He et al., 2016), EfficientNet (Tan, 2019), are trained over ImageNet
163   (Deng et al., 2009) which contains more than 1M labeled samples of 1000 categories. Moreover,
164   using (fine-tuning) those models in another domain for any learning task also requires a significant
165   amount of domain-specific labeled data for achieving competitive performance. As discussed, the
166   existing video models are more focused on action recognition (Kondratyuk et al., 2021; Tran et al.,
167   2018; 2015; Ji et al., 2012). Also, the advanced transformer-based pre-trained video recognition
168   models, e.g., VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022), ViViT (Arnab et al., 2021), Slowfast (Feichtenhofer
169   et al., 2019), X3D (Feichtenhofer, 2020), MVit family (Fan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022), which have
170   been trained on labeled action recognition video datasets, thus mostly suitable for action recognition
171
      tasks in videos.
172   In this paper, we consider gun detection in videos as a classification problem. In order to achieve
173   high performance in this domain, we require labeled datasets with a significant amount of samples
174   to train a new model or fine-tune the existing models. There is a huge scarcity of labeled video
175   datasets for such tasks in this specific domain. Therefore, current methods may not achieve high
176   performance either in training or fine-tuning due to the lack of training samples. We illustrate the
177
      performance of several existing representative video classification methods and discuss it in detail
      in the experiment section (Section 5).
178
179   Transfer Learning is a widely used technique that benefits from a pre-trained model and reuses
180   the existing model’s knowledge for a similar task (Zhuang et al., 2020). In order to mitigate the
181   above-mentioned issues, transfer learning can be utilized to perform gun detection by leveraging the
182   pre-trained weights of existing models for a better understanding of the features of guns in videos.
183
      The fundamental idea behind transfer learning is to use the knowledge learned from one task (the
      source task) of any domain and apply it to another related task (the target task) to the same or
184
      different domain (Zhuang et al., 2020; Pan & Yang, 2009). Formally, a domain D contains a feature
185
      space X, (X is a set of instances defined as {x1 , x2 , ...., xn }) and a probability distribution P (X).
186   For a specific domain, D = {X, P (X)}. A task τ is defined as τ = {Y, f }, where Y denotes a
187   label space defined as {y1 , y2 , ...., yn } and f is a prediction function, f : X → Y , which is used to
188   predict the corresponding label f (x) of a new instance x. The task τ is defined as τ = {Y, f (x)},
189   which is learned from the training instances consisting of pairs {xi , yi }, where xi ∈ X, and yi ∈ Y .
190   Given a source domain Ds and source learning task τs and a target domain Dt and target learning
191   task τt (e.g., gun detection), where Ds ̸= Dt and τs ̸= τt , we aim to identify effective transfer
192   learning strategies that can improve the learning of the target prediction function, ft in dt utilizing
193   the knowledge in Ds and τs .

      4.1     E XISTING V IDEO C LASSIFICATION M ETHODS
197
198   The video classification problem has been explored using different approaches in the literature. In
199   this paper, we explore several representative video classification models from the existing studies.
200   Here, we describe them briefly.
201
202   4.1.1    3D-C ONVOLUTONAL N EURAL N ETWORK (3D-CNN)
203   3D-CNN was proposed in order to effectively capture both spatial and temporal dimensions of video
204   data (Ji et al., 2012). The fundamental idea was to build a 3D architecture as a 3D convolutional
205   feature extractor. Tran et al. (2015) proposed Convolutional 3D (C3D) which is basically a 3D
206   kernel size of T × K × K (where T is temporal depth, and K is spatial kernel size) that slides
207   over the entire volume of video in three dimensions to extract spatiotemporal information on large
208   supervised training for different video analysis tasks. Another approach to implementing 3D CNN
209   for video analysis is factorizing 3D kernel into 2D spatial convolution and 1D temporal convolutions
210   introduced by Tran et al. (2018). They proposed an R(2+1)D convolutional block within residual
211
      architecture, which better helps to understand the spatiotemporal features more effectively than pure
      3D-CNN by increasing the nonlinearity between these two operations.
212
213   4.1.2    M OV I N ET
214
215   Mobile video networks (MoViNets) is a family of 3D-CNN video recognition models which is
      memory and computation-efficient (Kondratyuk et al., 2021), optimized for resource-constrained


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      devices. Unlike vanilla 3D-CNN, it doesn’t require high computational resources for training and
217   inference. Essentially, it contains three major components. First, MoViNet search space, which
218   is built based on MobileNetV3 (Howard et al., 2019) and inspired by X3D (Feichtenhofer, 2020),
219   the 2D blocks of MobileNetV3 are expanded to handle the 3D video input. It creates a neural
220   architecture search (NAS) which obtains an optimal neural architecture configuration to trade off
221   the performance and efficiency based on the specific use cases. Second, in order to deal with the
222   memory budget, instead of processing the entire video at once, it splits the entire video into smaller
223   n sub-clips, called Stream-buffer. It mitigates the issues of recomputing the frame activations due
224   to overlapping and preserves long-range dependencies by caching feature activation at each edge
225
      of sub-clips. Once it iterates over n sub-clips, the whole feature map is obtained by concatenating
      the activations cached at each ith < n step. Third, temporal ensembles allows the creation of two
226
      identical models separately by halving the frame rate and offsetting by one frame. Finally, before
227
      the softmax, the arithmetic mean is performed on unweighted logits. Thus, it ensembles two models
228   with the same computational cost, leading to enriched predictions. Several versions (pre-trained on
229   Kinetics-600 (Kay et al., 2017)) are available depending on the capacity of the users’ device.
230
231   4.1.3    V IDEO MAE
232
      Inspired by the success of masked autoencoding in images (He et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2021) and
233   NLP (Devlin, 2018), authors in Tong et al. (2022) proposed video masked autoencoder (VideoMAE).
234   Video MAE is a self-supervised learning technique designed specifically for video pre-training. It
235   overcomes the requirements of large-scale labeled datasets and introduces a vanilla vision trans-
236   former on the video dataset without using pre-trained models or extra data. During the pre-training
237   phase, it randomly masks 90-95% of video frames using the tube masking technique due to the tem-
238   poral redundancy, and it yields great efficiency in terms of computational cost due to the asymmetric
239   encode-decode architecture. It successfully trains the basic ViT backbone (Dosovitskiy, 2020) with
240   relatively small video datasets, e.g., SSV2 (Goyal et al., 2017), UCF101 (Soomro, 2012), HMDB51
241
      (Kuehne et al., 2011). The model’s task is to reconstruct the masked pixels of the downsampled
      video clips from the unmasked ones and this reconstruction process allows it to effectively learn
242
      features and patterns (both spatial and temporal) in the videos. The pre-trained model can further be
243
      fine-tuned with smaller labeled datasets for any downstream tasks, classification, or detection.
244
245   4.1.4    H YBRID A RCHITECTURES
246
      Hybrid architectures are also gaining popularity for different video analysis tasks, e.g., event classifi-
247
      cation in videos (Zhang & Xiang, 2020), facial expression classification (Abdullah et al., 2020), ges-
248
      ture recognition (Hu et al., 2018). The key idea of this twofold architecture is extracting the spatial
249   features from the video frames with deep neural network (DNN) models (e.g., VGG) and handling
250   the temporal features with sequence models (e.g., LSTM, Transformer). Different combinations
251   of these hybrid models are able to achieve high performance for action recognition and earlier-
252   mentioned tasks as state-of-the-art video analysis models on benchmark datasets (Paul, 2021), (Mah-
253   moud, 2024).
254
      Though these advancements have shown their potential towards video classification area, these meth-
255   ods are primarily focused on action recognition with various datasets such as UCF-101 action recog-
256   nition dataset (Soomro, 2012), Kinetics Human Action Video Dataset (Kay et al., 2017), HMDB51
257   (Kuehne et al., 2011), Sports-1M (Karpathy et al., 2014). Thus, these models can achieve high accu-
258   racy in classifying different action classes. However, it’s unclear how these methods will perform in
259   order to find the presence of tiny objects such as guns in videos and, more importantly, in real-world
260   environments.

      features of gun images for the target task τt , i.e., gun detection.

479   6   D ISCUSSION
480
481   The existing models struggle to achieve high performance in detecting guns in videos due to sev-
482   eral reasons. First, existing models are not trained specifically for identifying gun features in video
483   frames, rather these are trained for generic action recognition tasks (Kondratyuk et al., 2021; Tong
484   et al., 2022). Second, while techniques such as 3D-CNN (Ji et al., 2012; Tran et al., 2018), can
485   keep correspondence between spatiotemporal features of the action recognition tasks, they can not
      adequately capture the spatial features of guns in sequences of frames. VideoMAE’s tube masking


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (Tong et al., 2022) technique might mask the portion where the gun appears in the video frames.
487   Masking random cubes with extremely high ratios may cause the elimination of the spatial features
488   of a gun where it appeared in the video. So, for action recognition, it is able to reconstruct the
489   masked parts by finding the corresponding patches in adjacent frames, however, this idea might not
490   work for effectively capturing small object features, e.g., gun. Due to these types of architectural
491   designs, these models fail to learn the subtle gun features or features of similar small objects that
492   appear in the video. Third, while several existing methods demonstrate comparable performance in
493   action classification on synthetic datasets (Ruiz Santaquiteria et al., 2024), their effectiveness may
494   significantly diminish in real-world scenarios. For instance, in the UCF crime dataset (Sultani et al.,
495
      2018), where the instances are taken from real-world shooting violence, the performance might
      severely deteriorate for existing models. Our proposed method addresses these issues by feeding
496
      spatial gun features to the model through image-augmented training. Thus, it is able to effectively
497
      capture the features of tiny objects, i.e., guns, in videos. Further, the sequence model handles the
498   temporal correspondence with respect to the spatial features. According to the empirical analy-
499   sis, without image-augmented training, the combination of MobileNet and GRU leads to 95.04%
500   accuracy (i.e., last row of Table 3) for the firearm action recognition dataset. Further, the image-
501   augmented training may achieve 100% accuracy (i.e., 9th row, second column of Table 4) on the
502   same dataset. Therefore, image-augmented training plays a significant role in terms of performance
503   improvement in order to detect guns in videos. Through our experimental analysis, we demon-
504   strated that our proposed method outperforms the state-of-the-art methods in classification accuracy
505   and exhibits high generalization performance in real-world scenarios like the UCF crime dataset.
506   One potential limitation of our proposed method could be its low performance in more challenging
507   scenarios, e.g., videos with poor quality in low light conditions and heavily rainy or stormy environ-
508   ments, as described in Section 2, where deep ensembles Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017); Liu et al.
509   (2019); Wu et al. (2023) can serve as a potential solution for enhancing the overall robustness under
510   these adverse situations.
511

513
      7   R ELATED W ORK
514
515   Weapon detection in videos has been explored through various approaches in the literature. Very few
516   of them obtained classification-based approaches, e.g., Bhatti et al. (2021) explored the siding win-
517   dow technique through pre-trained image classifiers. Olmos et al. (2018) proposed a pistol detection
518   method in videos guided by the features extracted by the VGG-16 model under the sliding window
519   technique. On the other hand, state-of-the-art detection-based approaches are also used to address
520   this problem. R-CNN (regions with CNN features) (Girshick et al., 2014), faster R-CNN (Ren et al.,
521   2016), different versions of YOLO (You Only Look Once) (Redmon, 2016) family, and Single Shot
522
      Detector (SSD) (Liu et al., 2016) are widely used in literature to detect guns or other handheld
      weapons (e.g., knives, grenades) in videos (Ahmed et al., 2022; Hashmi et al., 2021; Bhatti et al.,
523
      2021; Ingle & Kim, 2022). However, these methods may not be able to achieve robust performances
524
      specifically in more challenging real-world scenarios.

528   8   C ONCLUSION
529

531
      This paper addressed the crucial challenges of gun detection in videos by contributing to three main
      directions. Through an empirical analysis of existing video classification methods, we identified
532
      their drawbacks in terms of gun detection accuracy in videos. We then leveraged this empirical anal-
533
      ysis and developed a novel gun detection model utilizing an image-augmented training technique,
534   which was evaluated using a wide range of datasets, including the synthetic gun action recognition
535   dataset and real-world UCF crime dataset. Our empirical results verify that our approach not only
536   outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of classification accuracy but also demonstrates robust
537   performance in real-world scenarios. Further, we highlighted other challenges associated with de-
538   tecting small objects in videos, such as guns, showcasing the limitations of current computer vision
539   techniques. Our findings pave the way for future research opportunities to enhance object detection
      capabilities, ultimately contributing to improved gun detection accuracy.
