009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper investigates the one-epoch overfitting phenomenon in Click-Through
012            Rate (CTR) models, where performance notably declines at the start of the sec-
013            ond epoch. Despite extensive research, the efficacy of MEL over the conventional
014            one-epoch approach remains unclear. As a result, all potential rewards from MEL
015            can hardly be obtained. We identify the overfitting of the embedding layer in-
016
               stead of the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) layers, as the primary issue. To ad-
               dress this, we introduce a novel Multi-Epoch learning with Data Augmentation
017
               (MEDA) framework. We design algorithms for both non-incremental and incre-
018
               mental learning scenarios in the industry. MEDA minimizes overfitting by re-
019            ducing the dependency of the embedding layer on trained data, and achieves data
020            augmentation through training the MLP with varied embedding spaces. MEDA’s
021            effectiveness is established on our finding that pre-trained MLP layers can adapt
022            to new embedding spaces and enhance model performances. This adaptability
023            highlights the importance of the relative relationships among embeddings over
024            their absolute positions. We conduct extensive experiments on several public and
025            business datasets, and the effectiveness of data augmentation and superiority over
026            conventional SEL are consistently demonstrated for both non-incremental and in-
027
               cremental learning scenarios. To our knowledge, MEDA represents the first uni-
               versally reliable MEL strategy tailored for deep CTR prediction models. We pro-
028
               vide theoretical analyses of the reason behind the effectiveness of MEDA. Finally,
029
               MEDA has exhibited significant benefits in a real-world incremental-learning on-
030            line advertising system.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Click-through rate (CTR) prediction is crucial in online recommendation and advertising systems,
036   benefiting significantly from advancements in deep learning-based models (Cheng et al., 2016; Qu
037   et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2018; 2019; Pi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022).
038   Despite the progress and diverse approaches, including non-incremental learning for smaller datasets
039
      and incremental learning (Cai et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2022; Mi et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023) for
      larger or real-time datasets, a common challenge persists: “one-epoch overfitting (OEO)” (Zhou
040
      et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022). This phenomenon, where model performance drops sharply at the
041
      beginning of the second training epoch, contrasts with other deep learning domains like computer
042   vision (He et al., 2016; Russakovsky et al., 2015) and audio processing (Purwins et al., 2019),
043   where multi-epoch learning (MEL) enhances model convergence. The OEO issue has been under
044   investigation since 2018 (Zhou et al., 2018), yet a universally reliable solution remains elusive. As
045   a consequence, it is hard for us to obtain any potential benefits from MEL. This includes the further
046   convergence of models (especially for cold-start scenarios), the re-training necessary to rebuild MLP
047   or mitigate catastrophic forgetting (Katsileros et al., 2022), and the implementation of “rethinking”
048   training techniques (e.g., unsupervised domain adaptation (Wilson & Cook, 2020) or label-noise
049   correction (Song et al., 2022)). Moreover, the OEO problem also affects large language models
050
      (LLM) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Komatsuzaki, 2019).
051   Current straightforward solutions (including regularization, dropout, and model simplification) for
052   OEO can only mitigate the problem but can hardly solve it, except for simple datasets with very
053   limited high-dimensional categorical features (Zhang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2018). And the only
      conclusion from existing research is that the OEO issue is related to feature sparsity (Zhang et al.,

102   2   R ELATED W ORKS
103
104   One-Epoch Overfitting. OEO has been studied since the work of Zhou et al. (2018). They have
105   proposed a method named mini-batch aware regularization (MBA-reg) to approximate the ℓ2 -
106   regularization for computational efficiency to handle OEO. However, it only works on their sim-
107   ple dataset with very limited high-dimensional categorical features. The ℓ2 -regularization has been
      found ineffective by subsequent empirical research of Zhang et al. (2022), and unstable and hard


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to tune hyperparameters by our work. The research of Zhang et al. (2022) indicates that reduc-
109   ing feature sparsity can diminish the prevalence of OEO, yet the potential superiority of MEL over
110   traditional SEL remains uncertain. Specifically, they have performed extensive experiments with
111   straightforward approaches to reduce the sparsity, such as regularization, dropout, and model sim-
112   plification (including ID hashing, ID filtering, reducing the embedding dimension, reducing the
113   number of neurons or layers of the MLP), and changing batch sizes, activation functions, and opti-
114   mization algorithms. They concluded that none of these methods can solve the OEO problem: these
115   methods either still confront overfitting when the number of epochs is greater than one, or harm
116   the capacity of the model such that the results are lower than direct SEL. They have concluded that
117
      OEO is related to feature sparsity, but cannot further uncover its fundamental causes. Parallel ob-
      servations (Ouyang et al., 2022) in large language models undergoing supervised fine-tuning reveal
118
      a similar tendency towards OEO, albeit with a suggestion that a moderate level of overfitting might
119
      actually benefit downstream tasks. This concept of ”appropriate overfitting” presents an intriguing
120   avenue for future exploration within our proposed framework.
121
122   Pre-training. Recent approaches (Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Muhamed
123
      et al., 2021) have explored pre-training to enhance the representational capabilities of embedding
      and feature extraction layers within MLPs for various applications, yet these advancements fall
124
      short in demonstrating their efficacy in avoiding overfitting when CTR prediction is incorporated
125
      as an auxiliary training objective, nor do they facilitate MEL for such models. In contrast, graph
126   learning research has delved into fine-tuning pre-trained models for new graphs, facing challenges
127   related to either maintaining a consistent node ID space (Hu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
128   2021; Hu et al., 2020) or solely leveraging graph structure while neglecting node features (Qiu et al.,
129   2020; Zhu et al., 2021). This leaves an open question in the context of CTR models: the potential for
130   pre-trained MLP layers to contribute positively to a distinct embedding space remains unexplored
131   and warrants further investigation.

      3    BACKGROUND
134
135
      CTR (Click-Through Rate) prediction models are distinguished by their handling of high-
136
      dimensional sparse data, often involving billions of categorical features , e.g., User ID, Item ID,
137
      and user behaviors (lists of watched/clicked Item IDs of each user), with low occurrence rates (Jiang
138   et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2019; 2020). To handle these categorical features, deep CTR prediction
139   models typically adopt an embedding layer (Zhang et al., 2016) at the front, followed by various
140   types of MLP structures, with the embedding layer responsible for mapping the high-dimensional
141   categorical features to low-dimensional vectors. Given the concatenated dense representation vector,
142   an MLP is employed to capture the nonlinear interaction among features (Liu et al., 2020).

183   4.1       P ROBLEM I DENTIFICATION
184
      In Figure 2, we show that the primary causative factor of OEO is overfitting of the embedding
185
      instead of the MLP. We treat the embedding and MLP as two factors and design control strategies
186
      to compare their effects. First, we test the “Emb (MLP) fix” strategy: fixing the embedding (MLP)
187   and training the MLP (embedding). Figure 2 (a) shows that the MLP does not overfit when the
188   embedding is fixed, while the embedding overfits when the MLP is fixed. We further test “Emb
189   (MLP) 1 epoch fix”: after 1 epoch of joint training of the embedding and MLP, fixing the embedding
190   (MLP) and training the MLP (embedding). The same phenomenon happens after the second epoch,
191   although overfitting occurs at the second epoch. Then we test two reinitialization strategies: “Emb
192   (MLP) same init: for each epoch, using the same initialization result to reinitialize the embedding
193   (MLP)” and “Emb (MLP) Reinit: for each epoch, independently reinitialize the embedding (MLP)”.
194   It shows that, when the embedding is reinitialized, for the first time, the OEO is solved: the test
195
      AUC steadily improves across epochs. While reinitializing the MLP cannot stop the overfitting.
      These results show that only when the embedding is controlled, the overfitting can be controlled
196
      or avoided altogether, and thus reveal that OEO primarily stems from embedding (instead of MLP)
197
      overfitting. This discrepancy is likely due to the sparse nature of high-dimensional data, where
198   a vast number of categorical values exist but each appears infrequently. Consequently, embedding
199   vectors, representing these infrequent values, are prone to overfitting due to limited training samples.
200   Meanwhile, MLP parameters, engaging with the entire dataset, exhibit a lower risk of overfitting.

253
254   As introduced, for MEL, we propose MEDA to avoid the OEO.
255
      Non-incremental Problem Formulation. Based on the findings discussed, the initial embedding
256
      of each epoch is crucial for mitigating OEO and must be devoid of any exact information from
257
      trained samples. A straightforward solution is to randomize the initial parameters, ensuring their
258   independence from trained data. Therefore, we propose the novel strategy of randomly initializing
259   embedding parameters at the start of each epoch in our non-incremental MEDA framework. See
260   Algorithm 1 for details.
261
      One might think that reinitializing embedding parameters may cause information loss. While we
262
      should note that, compared with SEL, MEDA losses no information because both MEDA and SEL
263
      use the embedding layer trained once, and MEDA uses the MLP layer trained more epochs than
264   SEL does. Therefore, we can regard the multi-epoch of training as just pre-training the MLP for
265   a final regular SEL. And we indeed show that such pre-training is effective: the performances im-
266   prove steadily across epochs. The essential insight is that for CTR model MLP layers, the precise
267   values or absolute positions of embeddings are less critical than their interrelations. This under-
268   standing allows us to view the additional data samples with different embeddings while maintaining
269   crucial semantic relationships as augmented data samples. Furthermore, our results in Figure 4 (a)
      demonstrate that the MLP is indeed nearing convergence throughout the MEL.

      Incremental Problem Formulation. In an incremental learning framework, where datasets are pro-
301   cessed successively, we encounter a unique challenge: OEO also occurs upon the second training
302   of the tth dataset t > 1, involving both embedding and MLP layer optimization. This scenario
303   diverges from the non-incremental setting, as reinitializing embedding parameters at the start of the
304   tth dataset’s training will disregard the accumulated knowledge from datasets 1 to (t − 1), which is
305   undesirable. Based on our findings, to prevent OEO, the initial embedding parameters should not
306   contain exact information in any data sample in the tth dataset, but should contain information in
307   datasets 1 ∼ (t − 1). Therefore, one option involves adopting the final embedding parameters from
308   the t − 1th dataset’s training Et−1 . Nonetheless, our findings in Section 4.1 show that Et−1 is al-
309
      ready problematic because it overfits absolute positions of embeddings. Thus, drawing from insights
      in the non-incremental MEDA, we propose to leverage multiple Et−1 s with different positions to
310
      perform data augmentation. Specifically, we independently initialize multiple groups of embedding
311
      parameters to form distinct embedding spaces. These are then trained sequentially with the MLP
312   on each dataset. See Algorithm 2 for details. Our non-incremental MEDA can benefit more from
313   data augmentation than our incremental MEDA because the differences between final embeddings
314   are larger than those in our incremental MEDA due to training on more data in an epoch, while our
315   incremental MEDA can benefit more from embedding consistency.

      with N representing the number of IDs and D embedding vector dimension.

353   CTR Models and Metrics for Evaluation. We apply our method on the following CTR Models:
354   DNN is a base deep CTR model, consisting of an embedding layer and a feed-forward network with
355   ReLU activation. DIN (Zhou et al., 2018) proposes an attention mechanism to represent the user
356
      interests w.r.t. candidates. DIEN (Zhou et al., 2019) uses GRU to model user interest evolution.
      MIMN (Pi et al., 2019) proposes a memory network-based model to capture multiple channels
357
      of user interest drifting for long-term user behavior modeling. ADFM (Li et al., 2022) proposes
358
      an adversarial filtering model on long-term user behavior sequences. For the business datasets,
359   we adopt DIN as default. We denote our non-incremental and incremental MEDA as MEDA-NI
360   and MEDA-I, respectively. Our methods equal SEL when the number of epoch is 1. For binary
361   classification tasks, i.e., click or order prediction, we use Area under the curve (AUC) and binary
362   cross-entropy loss as evaluation metrics, while for the regression tasks, i.e., the LTV prediction,
363   we use AUC score between the LTV prediction scores and the binary order labels as the evaluation
364   metric, following the common business practice.
365   Implementation Details. All CTR Models adhere to the optimal hyperparameters reported in their
366   respective papers. For public datasets, we adopt Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) as the optimizer with
367   a learning rate of 0.001, and Glorot (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) as the initializer for embedding param-
368   eters. For business datasets, we adopt Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) as the optimizer with a learning
369   rate of 0.01, and uniform initializer with the range of 0.01. Other details are in Appendix A.1.

      5.3       O NLINE R ESULTS
516
517
518
      We conduct an online A/B test on a large industrial video advertising platform, focusing on retention
      prediction. This platform processes billions of user requests daily, with millions of item candidates.
519
      It incorporates a four-stage recommender system: candidate retrieval, pre-ranking, ranking, and
520
      reranking, each progressively narrowing down the item selection for users. We deploy MEDA in
521   the ranking module. The baseline method performs SEL, while we adopt our incremental MEDA to
522   conduct the online A/B experiment. The experiment spanned 9 days, with 10% of the total online
523   traffic allocated for both the baseline and our MEDA approach. Results in Table 4 demonstrate that
524   MEDA significantly enhances the test AUC, user retention, and overall platform rewards (as evalu-
525   ated by revenue and revenue expected by clients). This marks the first universally reliable solution
526   addressing overfitting in MEL of large-scale sparse models for advertising recommendations. In our
527   scenario, achieving satisfactory model performance typically necessitates training on one month’s
528   worth of data. Remarkably, with MEDA, comparable results can be obtained after just two weeks
529
      of training. Thus, implementing MEDA substantially reduces the required sample size and training
      costs while maintaining equivalent outcomes.

      demonstrate its substantial benefits in practical applications.

795   A.4   T HEORETICAL A NALYSES
796
797   The OEO results from the embedding layer excessively memorizing each single data sample, where
798   the overfitting starts exactly at the onset of the second epoch, when the embedding layer trains on the
799
      exactly same data samples that it has trained on in the first epoch. Therefore, the key to solving the
      excessive memorization problem is to control memorization. We provide a theoretical foundation
800
      of differential privacy (Chaudhuri et al., 2011; Dwork et al., 2014) for the memorization control.
801
      Because differential privacy can theoretically control the amount of information of any input data
802   sample obtained from the learned parameters.
803
804   Here we discuss this in detail. Let Ẽ1 be the learned embedding parameters at the end of the first
805   epoch. Then let E2 = Ẽ1 + b be the initial embedding parameters at the beginning of the second
806   epoch, where b is the noise matrix with the same size of Ẽ1 . And assume that the loss funcion of the
807   first epoch is added with the ℓ2 normalization of the embedding with the coefficient of Λ, and each
808   ith row vector of b, bi , is a random noise vector with the density of v(bi ) = α1i exp(− ni2Λϵ ||bi ||),
809   where αi is a normalizing constant, ni is the number of samples corresponding to the ith embedding
      vector. Then under some mild condition, the Theorem 6 of Chaudhuri et al [1] guarantees that


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      such a noise addition algorithm provides ϵ-differential privacy for each ith row of E2 , such that for
811   each i, for any two data sets D and D′ that differ in a single sample and for any set S, we have
812   exp(−ϵ)P(E2i ∈ S|D′ ) ≤ P(E2i ∈ S|D) ≤ exp(ϵ)P(E2i ∈ S|D′ ), where E2i is the ith row of
813   E2 . The theoretical result means that, for anyone, it is difficult to know whether the input data set
814   has changed any single data sample from E2i . In other words, E2i contains little information about
815   any specific data sample in the first epoch. The amount of information is precisely controlled by
816   the pre-set hyper-parameter of ϵ. Based on the above theoretical foundation, our methods of both
817   non-incremental MEDA and incremental MEDA can be regarded as letting E2 = Ẽ1 × 0 + b,
818   which for differential privacy equals letting E2 = Ẽ1 + b × (+∞), then it can be achieved by
819   setting ϵ = 0, then we can guarantee the perfect 0-differential privacy, such that for each i, for any
820   two data sets D and D′ that differ in a single sample and for any set S, we have exp(−0)P(E2i ∈
821   S|D′ ) ≤ P(E2i ∈ S|D) ≤ exp(0)P(E2i ∈ S|D′ ), where E2i is the ith row of E2 , which means that
822
      P(E2i ∈ S|D′ ) = P(E2i ∈ S|D), suggesting that E2i contains no information of any specific data
      sample in the first epoch, then the excessive memorization is perfectly solved.
823
824   On the other hand, the data augmentation property is another reason that our method can improve
825   prediction performances. One common type of data augmentation is changing input data samples
826   without changing the corresponding labels. Our method belongs to this type. Specifically, through
827   different initializations, our method changes the embedding space, i.e., the linear projection ma-
828
      trix, of categorical features, without changing the CTR labels and the categorical values (i.e., IDs).
      Therefore, for the MLP layers, the input data samples mapped by the linear projection matrix are
829
      changed, but the corresponding CTR labels are kept the same. Therefore, we call our method a data
830
      augmentation method. Furthermore, the above type of data augmentation usually requires that the
831   semantic meaning of the input data sample will not be changed. Such property is also assumed to be
832   held in our method, because the CTR labels and the categorical values (i.e., IDs) are kept the same.
833   Therefore, the similarity relationships between IDs — the semantic meaning of the input data sam-
834   ple — are still kept. The theoretical foundation of the data augmentation ability can be referenced to
835   dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), and the reason to improve prediction performances is also model
836   ensemble.
