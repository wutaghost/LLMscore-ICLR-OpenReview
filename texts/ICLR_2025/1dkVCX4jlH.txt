011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Analyzing the cardiovascular system condition via Electrocardiography (ECG) is
014            a common and highly effective approach, and it has been practiced and perfected
015
               over many decades. ECG sensing is non-invasive and relatively easy to acquire,
               and yet it is still cumbersome for holter monitoring tests that may span over hours
016
               and even days. A possible alternative in this context is Photoplethysmography
017
               (PPG): An optically-based signal that measures blood volume fluctuations, as typ-
018            ically sensed by conventional “wearable devices”. While PPG presents clear ad-
019            vantages in acquisition, convenience, and cost-effectiveness, ECG provides more
020            comprehensive information, allowing for a more precise detection of heart condi-
021            tions. This implies that a conversion from PPG to ECG, as recently discussed in
022            the literature, inherently involves an unavoidable level of uncertainty. In this pa-
023            per we introduce a novel methodology for addressing the PPG-2-ECG conversion,
024            and offer an enhanced classification of cardiovascular conditions using the given
025            PPG, all while taking into account the uncertainties arising from the conversion
026
               process. We provide a mathematical justification for our proposed computational
               approach, and present empirical studies demonstrating its superior performance
027
               compared to state-of-the-art baseline methods.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Cardiovascular diseases are a significant public health problem, affecting millions of people world-
033   wide and remaining a leading cause of mortality (Tsao et al., 2022). The analysis of cardiovascular
034   conditions using Electrocardiography (ECG) signals has emerged as one of the most widely utilized
035   diagnostic tool (Kligfield et al., 2007), and its practice and efficiency have been continuously refined
036   over many decades.
037   While ECG sensing is non-invasive and relatively easy to acquire, it is time-consuming and requires
038   the expertise of trained professionals with specialized skills in order to ensure accurate diagnosis.
039   Consequently, there has been a surge in the development of numerous contemporary wearable ECG
040   systems in recent decades for digital diagnosis. However, the materials used to deliver a high-quality
041   signal via electrodes can often lead to skin irritation and discomfort during extended usage, thereby
042   limiting the long-term viability of these devices (Zhu et al., 2019).
043   A possible alternative to ECG sensing and diagnosis is the use of Photoplethysmography (PPG),
044   which is an optically-based non-invasive signal associated with rhythmic changes in blood volume
045   within tissues (Reisner et al., 2008). Unlike ECG, PPG signals are easier to obtain, convenient, and
046   cost-effective, as they are widely available in clinics and hospitals and can be sensed through fin-
047   ger/toe clips. Moreover, the popularity of PPG is increasing with the emergence of wearable devices
048   like smart watches, enabling continuous long-term monitoring without causing skin irritations.
049   Naturally, the PPG and ECG signals are inter-related, as the timing, amplitude, and shape charac-
050   teristics of the PPG waveform contain information about the interaction between the heart and the
051   blood vessels. These features of the PPG have been leveraged to measure heart rate, heart rate vari-
052   ability, respiration rate (Karlen et al., 2013), blood oxygen saturation (Aoyagi & Miyasaka, 2002),
053   blood pressure (Payne et al., 2006), and to assess vascular function (Marston, 2002; Allen & Mur-
      ray, 1993). Despite these capabilities, using PPG-based monitoring during daily activities and light

070   As the use of wearable devices capturing PPG signals is becoming widespread, there is an emerging
071   interest in accurately converting PPG sources into ECG signals (Chiu et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Zhu
072   et al., 2021; Sarkar & Etemad, 2021; Vo et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Tian et al.,
073   2022; Vo et al., 2023; Lan, 2023; Abdelgaber et al., 2023; Hinatsu et al., 2023; Shome et al., 2023).
074   However, such a mapping can be regarded as an ill-posed inverse problem. This implies that multiple
075
      ECG solutions may correspond to the same input PPG signal. Nevertheless, previous work in this
      domain has tended to focus on providing a single ECG solution to the conversion task, ignoring the
076
      inherent uncertainty in the conversion and thus failing to capture the complete information contained
077
      in the PPG signal. Such studies suggest optimizing neural models either via regularized regression
078   with an L1 /L2 loss (Li et al., 2020; Chiu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Ho et al.,
079   2022; Tian et al., 2022; Lan, 2023; Abdelgaber et al., 2023; Hinatsu et al., 2023) or relying on a
080   generative approach that produces a single solution that approximates a sample from the posterior
081   distribution of ECG given the PPG signal (Sarkar & Etemad, 2021; Vo et al., 2021; 2023; Shome
082   et al., 2023). Here, we argue that both these strategies are lacking, and the conversion in question
083   calls for a different approach.
084   In this paper, we introduce Uncertainty-Aware PPG-2-ECG (UA-P2E), a novel PPG-2-ECG con-
085   version methodology, aimed at enhancing the classification of cardiovascular conditions using ECG
086   signals derived from PPG data. Unlike prior work, our approach aims to address both the uncer-
087   tainty arising from the conversion task, regarding the spread and variability of the possible ECG
088   solutions given the PPG (Angelopoulos et al., 2022; Belhasin et al., 2023), and also the uncertainty
089   in the classification task, enabling the selection of PPG samples with low classification risk and high
090   confidence (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017).
091   To achieve the above, we leverage a conditional diffusion-based methodology (Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
092   2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020). These generative models have recently emerged as the lead-
093   ing synthesis approach in various tasks (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Yang et al., 2023), and have been
094   shown to enable high-fidelity posterior modeling for inverse problems (Yang et al., 2023; Kawar
095   et al., 2021; 2022; Song et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022). We utilize this capability to produce a
096   multitude of posterior sampled ECG results that correspond to the input PPG, and provide classi-
097   fication decisions based on this cluster of outcomes. Additionally, we introduce several methods
098
      for displaying the resulting ECG candidates for better human interpretability. Figure 1 provides an
      illustration of our approach to the conversion-classification process.
099
100   Our computational methodology is grounded on a mathematical proof showcasing the optimality of
101   the expected classification score derived from our proposed approach for classifying cardiovascular
102   conditions from source PPG signals. This optimality relies on the assumption that we have access
103   to a perfect posterior sampler and ECG classification models. This stands in contrast to the single-
104
      solution approach used by prior work, as described above, which necessarily performs worse.
105   Our work presents a thorough empirical study demonstrating superior performance in classifica-
106   tion and uncertainty estimation compared to baseline methods. Our main results are demonstrated
107   on the “Computing in Cardiology” (CinC) dataset (Reyna et al., 2021), which provides the most
      challenging classification data in cardiology.

      In summary, our contributions are the following: (1) We introduce a state-of-the-art diffusion-based
109   methodology for PPG-2-ECG conversion-classification that accounts for the uncertainty in the con-
110   version task, thereby enhancing performance; (2) We provide a mathematical proof that justifies
111   our proposed computational process for the classification task; (3) We offer effective methods for
112   displaying the ECG solutions obtained, enhancing interpretability while taking into account the un-
113   certainty perspective; and (4) We present an empirical study that supports our mathematical proof
114   and demonstrates the superiority of our methodology over baseline strategies.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
117
118
      Naturally, our work is not the first one to consider the conversion of PPG signals to ECG ones. Below
119
      we outline the main trends in this line of work, and add a discussion on uncertainty quantification,
120   as we aim to practice in this paper.
121
122   Regression: Earlier approaches to the PPG-2-ECG conversion problem primarily focused on ap-
123
      proximating either the Minimum Mean Absolute Error (MMAE) estimator using L1 loss (Li et al.,
      2020; Chiu et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2022; Lan, 2023) or the Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE)
124
      estimator with L2 loss (Zhu et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2022; Abdelgaber et al.,
125
      2023; Hinatsu et al., 2023). These works commonly employed regularization techniques to enhance
126   perceptual outcomes. However, the work reported in Blau & Michaeli (2018) which discovered the
127   distortion-perception tradeoff has shown that when optimizing for distortion (of any kind), percep-
128   tual quality is necessarily compromised, thus pushing the solutions in the above methods to signals
129   that are likely to drift out of the valid ECG manifold, thus weakening their classification perfor-
130   mance.
131
      Generation: The first attempt at PPG-2-ECG conversion using generative models was introduced by
132   the authors of CardioGAN (Sarkar & Etemad, 2021). They employed an adversarial training scheme
133   with an encoder-decoder architecture. Similarly, P2E-WGAN (Vo et al., 2021) utilized conditional
134   Wasserstein GANs for this task. Vo et al. (2023) introduced a variational inference methodology
135   to approximate the posterior distribution with a Gaussian prior assumption. The authors of RDDM
136   (Shome et al., 2023) have first adapted a diffusion-based model that accelerates a DDPM (Ho et al.,
137   2020) diffusion process for this conversion task. However, despite the success of the generative
138   approach, all the above works have chosen to use a single (possibly random) ECG solution from the
139   posterior distribution, thus missing the complete picture of the possibilities of the different ECGs
140
      given the PPG signal.
141   Uncertainty Quantification: In the framework of the conversion-classification explored in this
142   paper, uncertainty quantification serves a dual purpose. Firstly, it seeks to characterize the range,
143   spread, and variability of potential solutions for a given input PPG signal (Angelopoulos et al., 2022;
144   Belhasin et al., 2023). Secondly, it also aims at assessing the confidence in the correctness of clas-
145   sification predictions, e.g. via a selective classification (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017) scheme, which
146
      we adopt in this paper. Building upon principles from these two threads of work, we characterize
      the diverse solutions that can potentially describe the input, leading to an improved methodology
147
      within the conversion-classification framework.

152   Let X ∈ X ⊆ Rd be a random vector representing an ECG signal that follows the prior distri-
153   bution π(X). We assume access only to its observation Y = h(X) ∈ Y ⊆ Rd , where h is an
154   unknown, possibly stochastic, and non-invertible function. The observation Y represents a PPG sig-
155   nal that follows the distribution π(Y |X). In this paper, our first and primary objective is to provide
156
      a comprehensive and interpretable estimation of the ECG signal(s) that corresponds to the given Y ,
      focusing on the approximation of the posterior distribution π(X|Y ). Prior work in this field (see
157
      Section 2) adopted a single-solution approach for the conversion task, focusing on estimating a sin-
158
      gle reconstructed ECG solution from π(X|Y ), typically achieved through regression or generation
159   models.
160
161   Here, we propose a generalization of this conversion approach using a conditional stochastic gen-
      erative model, denoted as g : Y × Z → X s.t. g(Y, Z) = X̂ ∼ π̂(X|Y ), where Z ∈ Z denotes

162
      a stochastic component that typically follows a normal distribution π(Z) := N (0, I), and X̂ rep-
163   resents a potential ECG solution derived as a sample from the approximate posterior distribution
164   π̂(X|Y ). In this context, a single ECG solution may be derived by fixing Z = z in g(Y, Z), or by
165   marginalizing over the random seed, i.e., Ez∼π(Z) [g(Y, Z)]. In contrast, allowing Z to be random
166   may provide a more comprehensive set of solutions to the inverse problem, exposing the inherent
167   uncertainty within this conversion task. Our prime goal in this work is therefore to train the stochas-
168   tic conversion model g using a training data consisting of many (ECG, PPG) signal pairs, denoted
169   by {(Xi , Yi )}ni=1 .
170   A second objective in this work is classification, enabling diagnosis of various cardiovascular con-
171   ditions via the incoming PPG signals. Consider a binary classification problem where the input is
172   an observation Y ∈ Y with a corresponding binary label C ∈ {0, 1} that follows a conditional
173   distribution π(C|Y ). In this classification setting, we aim to model a classifier fY : Y → R+ for
174   approximating the posterior distribution, denoted as π̂(C|Y ).
175   Addressing this classification task, one might be tempted to adopt a straightforward and naive strat-
176   egy, of training directly on data that consists of PPG signals and their associated labels. Nevertheless,
177   appropriate cardiovascular labels linked with PPG signals are relatively rare and hard to acquire. An
178   alternative is to use labels that were produced for ECG signals, X, and tie these to their matched
179   PPG, Y . However, as the mapping between PPG and ECG is not injective, this implies that other
180   ECG signals (possibly with a different label) could have led to the same PPG. As a consequence,
181   these labels are necessarily noisy, impairing the learning task.
182   A presumably better alternative is to operate in the reconstructed ECG domain, hoping for improved
183   performance. Indeed, the classification proposed in prior work operates on the single estimated ECG
184   X̂, approximating this way the posterior π(C|X̂). This is achieved using a classifier fX : X → R+ ,
185   trained over data of the form {(Xi , Ci )}ni=1 , where Xi represents a grounded ECG signal and Ci
186   denotes its associating label. As we rely now on labels that do match their corresponding signals,
187   this may seem like a better approach. However, the same flaw as above applies here – the single-
188   produced ECG, X̂, captures a partial truth about the given PPG, thus weakening the classification.
189   Here is a formal definition of this classification strategy, as practiced in prior work:
190   Definition 3.1 (Single Score Classifier). We define a stochastic classifier fY as a Single Score Clas-
191   sifier (SSC) if it is given by

195   ECG, and when π(Z ′ ) = δ(Z − W ) for W ∼ π(Z), we get a single sample from the approximate
196   posterior distribution, π̂(X|Y ).

231   4       UA-P2E: U NCERTAINTY-AWARE PPG-2-ECG
232
233   We now turn to introduce Uncertainty-Aware PPG-2-ECG (UA-P2E), our methodology for PPG-2-
234   ECG conversion, aiming to provide a comprehensive understanding of the various ECG solutions
235
      derived from a given PPG signal. UA-P2E may assist medical professionals in analyzing diverse
      ECG signal variations resulting from the conversion process. Additionally, our method can be lever-
236
      aged to enhance cardiovascular diagnostic accuracy and confidence assessments, facilitating more
237
      informed clinical decisions.
238
239   While our proposed approach is applicable using any conditional stochastic generative estimator,
240   denoted as g : Y × Z → X , we focus in this work on conditional diffusion-based models (Sohl-
241
      Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020), which have emerged as the leading syn-
      thesis approach in various domains (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Yang et al., 2023). Our diffusion
242
      process is trained and evaluated on raw signal data of fixed-length Y, X ∈ Rd PPG and ECG signal
243
      pairs, sampled at 125Hz, thus covering d/125 seconds.1 The central part of the diffusion process
244   involves a conditional denoiser, denoted as ϵ(Xt , Y, t). This denoiser is trained to predict the noise
245   in Xt , which is a combination of X0 (representing a clean instance) and white additive Gaussian
246   noise at a noise level denoted by t. In this conditional process, the denoiser leverages the PPG signal
247   Y and the noise level t as supplementary information. Training ϵ is based on pairs of (X, Y ) signals
248   - a clean ECG and its corresponding PPG. After training, we apply multiple diffusion operations on
249   a given PPG signal Y ∈ Y to draw potential ECG solutions {X̂i }K      i=1 , where each X̂i ∼ π̂(X|Y ) is
250   a sample from the approximated posterior distribution of the ECG given the PPG signal.
251   In Section 4.1 we describe our enhanced classification procedure that relies on the above posterior
252   sampler, denoted by g. Additionally, in Section 4.2 we discuss how to improve the quantification of
253   uncertainty in classification, aiming to enhance confidence in predictions. Lastly, in Section 4.3, we
254   offer methods to visualize the cloud of ECG solutions {X̂i }K
                                                                  i=1 . This visualization aims at enhancing
255   interpretability for medical professionals, allowing for a better understanding of the various ECG
256   signals that emerge from a given PPG signal.

284   4.2   UA-P2E: U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION
285
286   The proposed PPG-2-ECG conversion has two layers of uncertainty awareness, as described herein.
287   The first takes into account the inherent uncertainty that emerges from the ill-posed conversion it-
288
      self, quantifying the spread and variability of the possible ECG candidates that derive from the
      PPG inputs. In Appendix C, we present empirical evidence illustrating the uncertainty from this
289
      perspective. The second uncertainty layer leverages the above property for providing classification
290
      predictions with a better confidence in their correctness. This serves a selective classification (Geif-
291   man & El-Yaniv, 2017) scheme, in which a carefully chosen subset of the test PPG data is selected
292   by their calculated confidence, exhibiting improved accuracy.
293
294
      Selective classification (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017) enables handling cases in which classifying
      every PPG signal within a test set is challenging due to uncertainty or data corruption. This tech-
295
      nique allows for abstaining from making predictions for signals with low confidence, thus reducing
296
      classification errors on the remaining data. A confidence function, defined as κ : Y → R+ , serves
297   as the engine for this task by predicting the correctness of predictions. One common choice of this
298   confidence function κ is to use the maximal softmax score of a classifier. In a similar spirit, in this
299   work we set κ(Y ) to take the value κ(Y ) = max{1 − fY (Y ), fY (Y )}, where fY (Y ) approximates
300   the ESC (Definition 3.2), as summarized in Algorithm 1.

      6     C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
522
523
524   This paper presents Uncertainty-Aware PPG-2-ECG (UA-P2E), a novel approach that addresses the
525
      inherent uncertainty in the PPG-2-ECG task. By incorporating the potential spread and variabil-
      ity of derived ECG signals from PPG inputs through sampling from a diffusion-based model, we
526
      achieve state-of-the-art performance in the conversion task. In addition, we show that our approach
527
      enhances cardiovascular classification, through the approximation of our proposed Expected Score
528   Classifier (ESC), which averages classification scores of the ECG solution cloud for each input PPG
529   signal. This classification strategy is proven to be optimal, and demonstrated to perform better than
530   baseline methods in practice. Finally, we present ECG visualization methods, designed to enhance
531   interpretability for medical professionals while considering the multitude ECG solutions associated
532   with each PPG signal.
533
      Our work presents rich empirical results demonstrating the superior classification performance of
534   our approach compared to direct classification methods operating on given PPG signals. However,
535   these results are partially reliant on synthetic PPG signals, for which we provide empirical validation,
536   though further exploration is required to study additional cardiovascular conditions and the potential
537   risk of hallucinations in the signal domain. Additionally, although the motivation for using PPG-
538   2-ECG is based on the widespread use of wearable devices, no database containing signals from
539   wearable settings was utilized due to the lack of publicly available data; instead, data from hospital
      facilities were used. Future studies in these areas will require additional data collection.

       E.2   T RAINING D ETAILS FOR THE C ONVERSION M ODEL
1027
1028   Our study leverages a diffusion-based conversion methodology. Specifically, we adopt the DDIM
1029   framework (Song et al., 2020). All the training details for the reversed conversion model, designed
1030   for generating PPG signals given ECG ones, are similar to the training details for the ECG-2-PPG
1031   conversion, as described hereafter.
1032
       The denoising model employed in our experiments is based on a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) ar-
1033   chitecture with an attention mechanism, as proposed in Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021).
1034   Originally introduced in the field of image processing, this architecture comprises a series of resid-
1035   ual layers and downsampling convolutions, followed by a subsequent series of residual layers with
1036   upsampling convolutions. Additionally, a global attention layer with a single head is incorporated,
1037   along with a projection of the timestep embedding into each residual block. To adapt the architecture
1038   for our purposes, we replace the convolutions and attention layers with 1D counterparts, enabling
1039   training and evaluation directly in the raw 1D signal domain. In our study we use d = 1024 PPG
1040   and ECG signal length.
1041   Regarding hyperparameters for the U-Net architecture, we set 256 dimensions for time embedding.
1042   The downsampling and upsampling is applied by factors of [1, 1, 2, 2, 4, 4, 8], with each factor cor-
1043   responding to a stack of 2 residual blocks. Attention is applied after the downsampling/upsampling
1044   matching to the factor of 4. To address overfitting, we incorporate dropout with a ratio of 0.2.
1045   Concerning training, the model architecture is trained on MIMIC-III. We split the data into training
1046   and validation sets after preprocessing. Specifically, we randomly select 400 patients for the vali-
1047   dation set while the remaining patients use for training. For each patient in the training set we split
1048   the raw PPG/ECG signals into pairs of 10-seconds (length 1024) signals, resulting in 15,225,389
1049   PPG/ECG signals. For each patient in the validation set, we sample 100 distinct 10-seconds (length
1050   1024) PPG/ECG signals from the raw signals, resulting in 40,000 PPG/ECG signals.
1051   The model architecture is trained for 10,000 epochs, employing a batch size of 2,048 PPG and ECG
1052   recordings across 8×V100 GPUs. The noise schedule progresses linearly over 1,000 time steps,
1053   starting at 1e-6 and ending at 1e-2. For optimization, we employed the standard Adam optimizer
1054   with a fixed learning rate of 1e-4. An L2 loss is utilized to estimate the noise in the input instance.
1055
       For evaluation, we sample K = 100 ECG candidates for each PPG signal using the diffusion-
1056
       based framework, and repeat the generation process for 3 different seeds. We adopt a non-stochastic
1057   diffusion process as proposed in Song et al. (2020), with skip jumps of 10 iterations, resulting in a
1058   total of T = 1000/10 = 100 diffusion iterations.

1310   I   W HY THE O RIGINAL ECG P ERFORMANCE S ETS AN U PPER B OUND FOR
1311       PPG- BASED A PPROACH ?
1312
1313   The performance of the original ECG signal represents an upper bound for classification perfor-
1314   mance, including our own method. Intuitively, this discrepancy arises because cardiovascular labels
1315   are directly linked to heart conditions determined by ECG signals, while PPG signals only capture
1316   blood volume fluctuations, which provide partial information about the heart. As a result, PPG
1317   signals are a degraded version of ECG signals, leading to a natural decline in performance due
1318
       to the loss of information. Even when generating ECG candidates from PPG signals, some infor-
       mation loss is unavoidable, which further reduces performance. Our approach mitigates this issue
1319
       by accounting for conversion uncertainty, yielding superior results compared to other PPG-based
1320
       strategies.
1321
1322   From a more theoretical perspective, we adopt a Markovian framework: Y → X → C, where
1323   Y represents the PPG signal, X denotes the ECG signal, and C is the cardiovascular condition
1324
       label. Under this assumption, π(C|X, Y ) = π(C|X), meaning that, given the ECG signal X, the
       PPG signal Y does not provide any additional information about the cardiovascular condition C.
1325
       Consequently, when we degrade the class information from X to Y , performance naturally declines
1326
       due to the inherent loss of information.
