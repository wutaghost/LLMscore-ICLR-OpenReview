009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Object counting is a challenging task with broad applications in security surveil-
012            lance, traffic management, and disease diagnosis. Existing methods face three ma-
013            jor challenges: achieving superior performance, maintaining high generalizabil-
014            ity, and minimizing annotation costs. We introduce TFCounter, a novel training-
015            free, segmentation-based, class-agnostic object counter supporting both few-shot
016            and zero-shot counting. This approach employs an iterative counting frame-
017            work with a dual prompt system for broader recall and features a background-
018
               enhanced similarity module to improve accuracy by incorporating background
               context. To demonstrate cross-domain generalizability, we collected a new dataset
019
               named BIKE-1000, consisting of 1000 images of shared bicycles from Meituan.
020
               Extensive experiments on FSC-147, CARPK, and BIKE-1000 datasets show
021            that TFCounter outperforms existing leading training-free methods and delivers
022            competitive results compared to trained counterparts. Our code is available at
023            https://github.com/tfcounter/TFCounter

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Object counting, the task of estimating the number of specific objects within an image, plays a
029   crucial role in various domains, including crowd countingLiu et al. (2023a); Liang et al. (2023);
030   Abousamra et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2016); Peng et al.
031   (2018); Lian et al. (2019); Sindagi et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2015) for urban planning and security,
032   vehicle countingHsieh et al. (2017); Mundhenk et al. (2016) for traffic management, and cell count-
033
      ingTyagi et al. (2023); Wang (2023); Arteta et al. (2016); Xie et al. (2018) in medical applications.
034   Traditional object-counting approaches are class-specific, counting objects belonging to predefined
035   categories such as humans, cars, or cells. Typically grounded in CNN architectures, these methods
036   require extensively annotated datasets. While exhibiting remarkable accuracy in dealing with trained
037   categories, these methods fail to maintain their performance when counting novel classes during
038   testing. To address this limitation, recent researchesRanjan & Nguyen (2022); Shi et al. (2022);
039
      Yang et al. (2021); Ranjan et al. (2021); ukić et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2019); Huang et al. (2024);
      Kang et al. (2024); Pelhan et al. (2024) have shifted towards class-agnostic object counting. They
040
      usually extract features from chosen exemplars and the query image to create a similarity map,
041
      which generates a density map to infer object count. This methodology , exemplified in ukić et al.
042   (2023), allows for dynamic adaptation to arbitrary object classes, significantly broadening the scope
043   and utility of object counting in computer vision.
044
045
      Recent progress in class-agnostic object counting have been primarily channeled through three main
      axes: the training-based versus training-free axis, the density-based versus detection/segmentation-
046
      based axis, and the few-shot versus zero-shot axis (also referred to as the visual exemplar versus text
047
      exemplar axis). The former typically offers greater versatility and universality, while the latter tends
048   to be more accurate. Current research, based on these developmental directions, aims to achieve
049   comprehensive counting results that strike a balance between universality and precision. (i) Most
050   of the current approaches are training-based and relies on density maps, as exemplified by Ranjan
051   et al. (2021); Shi et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2021); You et al. (2023); ukić et al. (2023) . These
052   methods treat the counting problem as a simple regression task, focusing more on the count values
053   rather than precisely matching target objects, thereby reducing task difficulty. Meanwhile, end-to-
      end training methods often yield better precision. However, a downside is their dependence on

      In this work, focusing on greater practicality, we introduce TFCounter, as shown in Figure 2, a

090
      and zero-shot counting. This approach performs a multi-round counting strategy that utilizes poste-
091
      rior knowledge to broaden the recall scope. Subsequently, it introduces an innovative background-
092   enhanced similarity module incorporating background context to augment accuracy. Moreover, it
093   uses two types of points prompts, grid points prompts and residual points prompts, with the latter

099   competitive performance when compared with training-based models. In short, our contributions

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Zero-shot and training-free object counting. Minimizing labor annotations was a focal point in
111   the task of class-agnostic object counting. Existing methods frequently depended on annotations
112   such as points and boxes during training and testing. To improve flexibility, several approaches
113   aimed to eliminate bounding boxes during testing for zero-shot counting. Among these, EF-
114   CACRanjan & Nguyen (2022) counted all repeating objects through the region proposal network,
115   while ZSCXu et al. (2023), CounTXAmini-Naieni et al. (2023), CLIP-CountJiang et al. (2023),
116   VLCounterKang et al. (2023) and PseCoHuang et al. (2024) accepted an arbitrary object class de-
117   scription to predict the object number. Concurrently, other methods were designed for training-free
118
      object counting, capitalizing on the robustness and generalizability inherent in large-scale founda-
      tional models. SAMKirillov et al. (2023) could perform zero-shot segmentation and subsequently
119
      estimated the number of objects by tallying all the generated masks. Based on it, SAM-FreeShi
120
      et al. (2023) combined three distinct types of class-specific priors to improve efficiency and accu-
121   racy. GroundingDINOLiu et al. (2023b) excelled in open-set detection, counting objects by aggre-
122   gating detected bounding boxes. Nevertheless, zero-shot models often necessitated extensive point
123   annotations during the training phase. Training-free methods typically struggled in complex scenes
124   or exhibited constraints in their ability to generalize across multiple object categories.
125
      Improving the quality of similarity maps. Most of class-agnostic object counting methods strived
126
      to generate high-quality similarity maps between visual features of input and example images to
127   guide the object counting. FamNet+Ranjan et al. (2021) introduced a novel adaptation strategy
128   for few-shot regression counting, adapting the model to new visual categories at test time with a
129   few exemplars. BMNetShi et al. (2022) and its extension, BMNet+Shi et al. (2022), focused on
130   a similarity-aware framework with a learnable bilinear similarity metric. CFOCNet+Yang et al.
131   (2021) used a two-stream Resnet for different scales similarity calculation and aggregation. SAFE-
132   CountYou et al. (2023) proposed a learning block with a similarity comparison module and a fea-
133   ture enhancement module, while LOCAukić et al. (2023) developed an object prototype extraction
134   module for low-shot counting problems. However, these methods often overlooked background
135
      considerations in favor of foreground focus.

139   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION AND F RAMEWORK
140
141   In this paper, we study the challenging problem of how to enhance counting accuracy and the capa-
142
      bility for cross-dataset generalization while adhering to the constraint of remaining training-free.
143   As illustrated in Figure 2, let I ∈ RH×W ×3 be the input image, and let BE = {bi }i=1:k be a set of
144   k exemplar bounding boxes denoting object exemplars. TFCounter is required to report the masks
145   MO = {mi }i=1:NO of all segmented target objects along with their count.
146   Specifically, we introduce a novel framework named TFCounter, designed for generalized object
147   counting and segmentation. Initially, TFCounter generates an image embedding and a mask list,
148   followed by the production of foreground and background similarity maps via the background-
149   enhanced similarity module. Subsequently, the prompt-aware counting module generates two types
150   of point prompts, which are then fed into the mask decoder to generate a set of masks. An iterative
151   counting mechanism is employed to enhance recall. Both modules are built upon the image embed-
152   ding and the three key components from SAM. We detail their designs in the following sections.

      3.4   D ISCUSS
252
253   Based on SAM, SAM-Free integrates three types of priors to achieve training-free, segmentation-
254   based, class-agnostic object counting. However, because it generates the masks only from grid points
255   and the similarity map relies solely on input exemplars, SAM-Free performs poorly in counting
256   small objects and is prone to misidentifying the background.
257
      TFCounter draws inspiration from SAM-Free and adheres to a similar framework. To address these
258
      issues, we utilize background context to enhance the discriminative potency of the similarity map.
259   Additionally, we employ an iterative counting framework with a dual prompt system to achieve
260   broader recall. Detailed qualitative and quantitative comparisons are provided in Section 5.2.

263   4     BIKE-1000 DATASET
264
265   This paper utilizes exclusive data from Meituan, one of China’s leading shared bicycle enterprises.
266   In the bike-sharing and ebike-sharing industry, accurate bicycle counting is a central requirement
267   across multiple application scenarios, including orderly operations management, inventory audits,
268   and street silt removal. To support these scenarios and advance the research and development of
269   more precise and efficient counting technologies, we have established a novel object counting dataset
      named BIKE-1000. This dataset provides a large collection of bicycle images accompanied by their


                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      count annotations, which aids in improving bicycle management, enhancing operational efficiency,
271   and ultimately optimizing the user experience.
272
273   The BIKE-1000 dataset encompasses a collection of 1000 images, each featuring distinctly visible
274
      shared bicycles situated within various scenes. These images were primarily captured by opera-
      tors. A significant portion of the dataset is characterized by photographs taken from an oblique
275
      perspective, which presents the bicycles with considerable variations in shape, appearance, and size,
276
      as well as instances of partial occlusion. Such attributes pose typical challenges in the domain of
277   object counting in computer vision. The annotation protocol for the BIKE-1000 dataset adheres to
278   the methodology used in FSC147 Ranjan et al. (2021), comprising (1) point annotation, where each
279   countable bicycle seat is marked, and (2) bounding box annotation, with three instances per image
280   demarcated as examples. The dataset includes high-resolution imagery with bicycles ranging from
281   3 to 70 per image, averaging 13 objects. Note that shared bicycles consist of numerous compo-
282   nents, such as frames, handlebars, wheels, seats, etc., whose appearance can vary significantly when
283   viewed from different angles. Manually counting over 70 bicycle seats in a single image proved
284   difficult, especially in images with oblique perspective. Therefore, we have limited our image se-
285
      lection to those with fewer than 70 bicycle seats for the BIKE-1000 dataset. The visualizations are
      displayed in Figure 3, while the statistical data and comparisons with object count benchmarks are
286
      shown in Figure 4 and Table 1.

472
473   Component Analysis. To validate the effectiveness of each component, we conduct an ablation
474   study as presented in Table 5. Starting with SAM-Free (M0), we add Background Similarity, Multi-
475   round Counting, and Residual Points Prompts in M1, M2, and M3, respectively, and then combine
476   them pairwise in M4, M5, and M6. Among these experiments, the best performance is observed in
477   M7, followed by M5, while M6 performs the worst. Additionally, the performance of each individual
478   component such as M1, M2, and M3 is worse than M0. This occurs because each component has a
479   unique function: Background Similarity filters out irrelevant masks to boost accuracy, while Multi-
480
      round Counting and Residual Points Prompts expand the recall scope to include more target objects.
      The best performance comes from combining these components, as Background Similarity alone
481
      may exclude smaller objects, and Multi-round Counting or Residual Points Prompts alone may

      include non-target objects.

      6   L IMITATIONS
518
519
      The initial version of TFCounter presents several limitations. Segmentation Problem. As a
      segmentation-based approach, TFCounter struggles with high-density overlapping objects compared
520
      to density-based methods. It also faces double-counting issues, especially when segmenting objects
521
      with multiple parts, such as the red flesh and green calyx of a strawberry. Future research on more
522   fine-grained or improved semantic/instance segmentation models could help mitigate these limita-
523   tions. Train-free Problem. TFCounter’s train-free design avoids large annotated training datasets
524   and reduces computational overhead. However, relying on manually designed structures limits its
525   generalization. Future work will explore training components such as prompt selection and similar-
526   ity fusion, or using techniques like LoRA to improve performance.
527
528
      7   C ONCLUSIONS
529
530
      In this paper, we explore an intriguing question: how to adapt large-scale foundation models to var-
531   ious downstream tasks and domain data without training, while maintaining superior performance.
532   To this end, we introduce TFCounter, a novel training-free, segmentation-based, class-agnostic ob-
533   ject counter that supports both few-shot and zero-shot counting. The originality of TFCounter stems
534   from three core designs: a multi-round counting strategy, a dual prompt system, and a background-
535   enhanced similarity module. The first two broaden the recall scope, while the latter boosts accuracy
536   by incorporating background context. Experimental results show that TFCounter outperforms ex-
537   isting state-of-the-art training-free models on two standard counting benchmarks and the proposed
538   BIKE-1000, and displays competitive performance compared to training-based models. Future work
539
      will focus on improving the counting of high-density overlapping objects and developing lightweight
      training for better performance.
