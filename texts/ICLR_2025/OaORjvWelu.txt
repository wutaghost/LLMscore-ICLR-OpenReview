009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Alignment is a critical step in large language model (LLM) post-training. It typ-
013
               ically requires human annotations to align the model’s output to human prefer-
               ences, which is prohibitively expensive. This paper proposes a novel approach
014
               to reduce the alignment cost. Specifically, we consider multiple levels of align-
015
               ment with different qualities and response-generating costs, which we refer to as
016            multi-fidelity alignment. We develop a new approach to incorporating the varying
017            levels of response quality to train a language model, aiming to reduce the cost
018            of response collection for alignment while maintaining the performance of the
019            language model. We provide theoretical insights and empirical results to support
020            the effectiveness of the proposed multi-fidelity alignment approach. Lastly, we
021            conduct experiments to corroborate the effectiveness of the proposed approach by
022            comparing its performance with the vanilla alignment methods.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022) have illustrated their power in various tasks,
029
      such as text generation (Dathathri et al., 2019), question answering (Su et al., 2019), and image
030   captioning (Devlin et al., 2015). However, to harness its power, one often needs to align these mod-
031   els to human preference so that the generated content meetshuman’s expectation. More specially,
032   the alignment either asks the human to demonstrate the task, e.g., write out the answer of a given
033   question for a language model, or to evaluate the outputs of generative models, e.g., rank multiple
034   answers according to human preference. Although crucial in LLMs, alignment is a very expen-
035   sive task due to the requirement of extensive human participation, and the cost of alignment is a
036   significant challenge in training LLMs (OpenAI, 2023).
037   Recent progress in alignment shows that weaker supervision or alignment can make a “strong”
038   model (with a large number of model parameters) perform better than the “weak” one that provides
039   the supervision (Burns et al., 2023), a.k.a, “weak-to-strong”. Specifically, Burns et al. (2023) cor-
040   roborates that the output model’s performance of using GPT-2’s response to align a model with the
041   infrastructure of GPT-4 is similar to GPT-3.5 (exceeding GPT-2). This observation implies that the
042   alignment from a low level of expertise can help train a strong generative model.
043   With this “weak-to-strong” observation in mind, this paper proposes a novel approach to reducing
044   the alignment costs. Specifically, we consider multiple levels of alignment with different quali-
045   ties and response-generating costs, which we refer to as multi-fidelity alignment. For example, a
046   high-fidelity alignment can refer to collecting responses or preferences from human experts, while
047   a low-fidelity alignment may correspond to collecting responses or preferences from a language
048   model weaker than human experts. With these alignments of varying quality levels, we propose a
049   new approach to incorporating them to train a language model efficiently. We aim to reduce the
050
      cost of response collection for alignment while maintaining the performance of the language model.
      We note that this cost improvement is from the perspective of response collection, not the computa-
051
      tional cost of training the language model, which was the focus of some previous works, like direct
052
      preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024).

065   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
066
067   Alignment To align the powerful generative models to human preference, various alignment meth-
068   ods have been proposed (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Hong et al.,
069   2024). Reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) is one of the
070
      most popular method to align a language model. This method consists of three steps: (1) use col-
      lected response to fine-tune the language model, (2) use the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) model to
071
      generate responses, ask human experts to label the preference of these responses, and then uses the
072
      preference labels to train a reward model, and (3) use the estimated reward model to align the LLM
073   via proximal policy optimization (PPO). In the second step, one alternative is to ask another ad-
074   vanced large language model to label the preference, which is known as reinforcement learning with
075   artificial intelligence feedback (RLAIF) (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). However, both the RLHF
076   and RLAIF rely on an intermediate reward model, and the PPO training is computational expensive.
077   Motivated to remove the intermediate reward model, Rafailov et al. (2024) proposes a direct policy
078   optimization (DPO) method to align the language model to human performance without the need of
079   an intermediate reward model. DPO is a more efficient method to align the language model and can
080   be optimized via simple supervised learning methods instead of the PPO in reinforcement learning.
081
      Following the spirit of DPO, many very recent new alignment methods have been proposed, such
      as self-play fine-tuning (SPIN) (Chen et al., 2024) and monolithic odds ratio preference optimiza-
082
      tion (ORPO) (Hong et al., 2024). While these reward-model free methods are more efficient in the
083
      perspective of computational cost, they still require a large number of human responses to align the
084   language model, which is also expensive. This paper aims to further reduce the cost of the response
085   collection in alignment.
086
087   Multi-Fidelity Multi-fidelity optimization is a general optimization framework that aims to opti-
088   mize an expensive objective function by using multiple levels of fidelity approximations (Bonfiglio
089   et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2013; Forrester et al., 2007; Huang et al., 2006). Later on, the multi-
090   fidelity approach is extended to the literature of AutoML, e.g., HyperBand (Li et al., 2017) and
091   BOHB (Falkner et al., 2018). Meanwhile, the multi-fidelity approach is also studied in the sequen-
092   tial decision making literature, e.g., in multi-armed bandits (Kandasamy et al., 2016) and Bayesian
093   optimization (Kandasamy et al., 2017; 2019). Very recently, the multi-fidelity best arm identification
094   (MF-BAI) problem is studied by Poiani et al. (2022; 2024); Wang et al. (2023). This paper is the
095
      first to study the multi-fidelity approach in the context of alignment of large language models.

      This section presents a multi-fidelity alignment algorithm (Algorithm 2). Given a set of M fidelities
187   to query responses with different costs, the algorithm aims to train a large language model (LLM)
188   with small costs.
189
190
      We consider a step-by-step alignment process, where the algorithm starts with the lowest fidelity and
      gradually increases the fidelity until the stopping criterion is met. The stopping criterion consists
191
      of two parts (inverse of Line 2): either the W IN R ATE between the trained LLM f (·; θt ) and the
192
      fidelity expert g (m) exceeds a threshold γ or the LLM parameters θt converge. Especially, we use
193
      W IN F REQ(f, g) to empirical estimate the W IN R ATE between two models/experts f and g (Line 2).
194   After the algorithm finishes the training at the last fidelity M , it outputs the trained LLM and its
195   parameters f (·; θt ).
196
197   At each fidelity m, the algorithm maintains a dataset D(m) , updated in a batch manner. For each
198
      batch, the algorithm, by randomly picking B ∈ N+ prompts from a given set X , collects B response
199
      pairs from both the trained LLM f (·; θt ) and fidelity expert g (m) (Lines 5–8), where the subroutine
      Generate(x; f ) means to generate one response for prompt x from a LLM or expert f . Then,
200
201
      the algorithm concatenates these new responses pairs to the dataset D(m) (Line 9). Lastly, with
202
      this extended dataset D(m) , the algorithm updates the LLM parameters θt using directed preference
      optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) (Line 11).

      5       E XPERIMENTS
292
293
      This section empirically evaluates the effectiveness of multi-fidelity alignment to obtain a better
294   LLM under a given budget. First, we employ 3 fidelities with increased performance to train our
295   LLM step by step, and compare it with directly using DPO to train our LLM. We choose the best
296   model derived from two methods and evaluate the model’s performance on the test dataset. Our
297   results show that if we allocate part of the budget to lower fidelity responses, we can derive an LLM
298   with better performance, i.e., better use of the budget. Before presenting these results, we describe
299   the experiment setup.

301   5.1      S ETUP
302
303   Datasets. In this study, we adopt rm-static2 , a split of Anthropic’s Helpful Harmless dataset, as
304
      our dataset. In our experiment, we shuffle the dataset and reserve 200 samples for validation and
      500 samples for testing.
305
306   Hyper-parameter setting. During policy training, we set maximum length to 256, adopt top-
307   p sampling with p =0.9 and temperature set to 0.1. When testing response generation, we set
308   maximum length to 256, p =0.95 and restrict to top-k=50 in top-p sampling, and temperature to
309   0.1. For DPO training, we set its learning rate to 5e-6, using cosine learning rate schedule and leave
310
      10 percent of steps for warm up. For the cost setting of different fidelities, we set it to 1:4:16 for
      each response they generate. For upgrade criterion, we use the win rate metric: if our LLM’s win
311
      rate against fidelity exceeds 0.3, we proceed to the next fidelity. For the final fidelity, training is
312
      halted when the win rate declines compared to the previous iteration. This criterion is chosen for
313   two reasons. First, it minimizes unnecessary costs associated with not utilizing the newly trained
314   LLM. Second, since the LLM’s response is automatically designated as the rejected response in
315   DPO training, a lower win rate in the current LLM supports better performance in the subsequent
316   training process. For batch size, we conducted training using batch sizes B of 512 and 1024, with
317   each experiment repeated twice.

356
357   We present the training trajectories in Table 2 and 3, as well as the testing performance in Table
358   4. We observed that the experiment results accord with our expectations. In step 1 and step 2, we
359   switch to the next fidelity when our LLM’s W IN R ATE exceeds 0.3 and note our models outperform

361   W IN R ATE begins to decline, and the LLM with the highest W IN R ATE against Qwen is selected as
362   our best LLM. Our results indicate that our LLMs outperform vanilla (m = 3) in both validation
363
      and test datasets with a batch size of B = 1024, and our LLMs surpass vanilla (m = 3) in the

364
      to vanilla(m = 3) in test dataset next.
365
366   Discussion. In Table 4, we observe that the performance is constrained when the sample size is
367   limited to 512 per iteration. Based on this, we conclude the following suggestions for applying our
368   method. First, GPT evaluation and prompts have variances. When the increment of performance
369
      is small, GPT may mislead us to move to the next fidelity or stop training. Second, it’s difficult to
      align the LLM’s distribution to fidelity’s distribution when using small amount of samples. The dis-
370
      tribution may end up in an inferior position which leads to the drop of performance. Combining the
371
      first two points, we give two suggestions to get a satisfactory result. First, we can train on a larger
372   batch size to get greater improvement and give our LLM enough time to align its distribution with
373   fidelities. Second, we can enlarge our validation dataset for more accurate results. Third, automati-
374   cally labeling our LLM’s response as rejected response may not be effective when LLM’s W IN R ATE
375   arise. As the performance of the student model improves, it may generate responses that surpass the
376   current fidelity, potentially leading to incorrect preference labels for the DPO training. The more
