                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               An AI assessor is an external, ideally independent system that predicts an indica-
               tor, e.g., a loss value, of another AI system. Assessors can leverage information
011
               from the test results of many other AI systems and have the flexibility of being
012
               trained on any loss function: from squared error to toxicity metrics. Here we ad-
013            dress the question: is it always optimal to train the assessor for the target loss?
014            Or could it be better to train for a different loss and then map predictions back to
015            the target loss? Using ten regression problems with tabular data, we experimen-
016            tally explore this question for regression losses with monotonic and nonmonotonic
017            mappings and find that, contrary to intuition, optimising for more informative
018            losses is not generally better. Surprisingly though, some monotonic transforma-
019            tions, such as the logistic loss used to minimise the absolute or squared error, are
020            promising.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   AI models and systems are evaluated with very different metrics, depending on the purpose of ap-
026   plication. For instance, metrics as diverse as the BLEU score (Papineni et al., 2002) for trans-
027
      lation, ‘Bold’ toxicity score (Dhamala et al., 2021) for text generation, the area under the ROC
      curve (Fawcett, 2006) for classification, asymmetric loss (Elliott et al., 2005) for sales prediction
028
      (Gogolev & Ozhegov, 2023) or any reward function (Eschmann, 2021) for reinforcement learning,
029
      are commonly used. Models can be built or trained to minimise some loss, and then repurposed for a
030   situation where another metric matters more. The most characteristic example today of this process
031   is represented by ‘foundation models’ (Bommasani et al., 2021), such as language models. Even if
032   the model can produce uncertainty estimates about the next token, and these are well calibrated, the
033   metric of interest may be toxicity. Since the model does not estimate toxicity, we need some external
034   way to do this.
035
      One solution to this challenge is the development of assessor models (Hernández-Orallo et al.,
036   2022). An assessor is a predictive model designed to estimate how well another system, called
037   the base or subject system s, will perform on a given example or problem instance i for a specific
038   validity metric before it is actually deployed. An assessor can estimate the conditional distribution
039   p̂(v|s, i) or simply (pointwise) map ⟨s, i⟩ 7→ v. Assessors are related to verifiers (Li et al., 2023) but
040   are anticipatory: rather than simply checking outcomes post-execution, they predict the outcomes
041   in advance (i.e., given a new example i, they can predict the value v of the metric that s is expected
042   to achieve). For instance, consider s a self-driving car and i a specific journey. An assessor could
043   predict the safety outcome v of s for i.
044   Assessors are used to anticipate any metric of quality, safety, bias or, in general, validity for any
045   kind of subject system, from RL agents to language models. Assessors can be used to monitor or
046   forecast system performance (Schellaert et al., 2024), to optimise configurations (Zhao et al., 2024),
047   to do anticipatory reject (Zhou et al., 2022; da Costa et al., 2023), or to delegate by routing(Hu et al.,
048   2024; Lu et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024). Assessors are usually trained on test data, capitalising on
049   vast information from results of many systems and examples (Burnell et al., 2023).
050   It may seem natural that the assessor is trained to optimise for the metric we are interested in. For
051   instance, if the subject system s estimates daily energy consumption of households and the metric
052   value v is given by the squared error (L+2 ) between actual and estimated consumption values, then
053   one would expect that the assessor should be trained to predict the squared error that the system will
      incur for each household. However, in this paper we challenge the general assumption that training

082

083

084   our experimental analysis we observe some results that may be explained by the distribution of er-
085   rors (residuals) in the test data of the base subject systems. However, some other results are more
086   surprising, such as the logistic error being the best in all situations. This finding suggests that learn-
087   ing an assessor for one central metric might suffice to optimise a family of monotonically-related
088   metrics.

      2     BACKGROUND
091
092
      This work situates itself within a broad spectrum of research on error analysis and the exploration
093   of alternative loss functions for training predictive models. However, the use of assessors resituates
094   this question at the meta-level, as a second-order regression problem, an area that, to our knowledge,
095   has not been explored yet.
096
097   2.1   E RROR ANALYSIS IN REGRESSION
098
099   In regression problems, the choice and optimisation of loss functions is critical to model perfor-
100   mance. There is an extensive literature on traditional error measures (Hyndman & Koehler, 2006;
101   Botchkarev, 2018; 2019; Chicco et al., 2021) such as Mean Squared Error (MSE), Absolute Er-
102   ror, and more robust variants such as Huber Loss (Owen, 2007), which falls somewhat in between
103   squared and absolute error, or Tukey’s biweight loss (Beaton & Tukey, 1974; Belagiannis et al.,
104
      2015), which caps quadratic loss beyond a given point. Optimisation of these loss functions leads
      to different kinds of bias. For instance, quadratic error leads to estimators that are unbiased for the
105
      mean while absolute error leads to estimators that are unbiased for the median.
106
107   Beyond their use in performance evaluation, the analysis of errors and residuals also serves a di-
      agnostic purpose, helping to identify model inadequacies or violations of assumptions, providing a


                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      comprehensive understanding of the linear and non-linear relationships captured by regression mod-
109   els. For instance, Rousseeuw & Leroy (2005) use regression diagnostics, e.g., outlier diagnosis, to
110   identify problems in both the explanatory and response variable, further refining the understanding
111   of errors in predictive models.
112
      Some studies have also explored more complex loss functions and their impact on regression model
113
      performance. According to Gneiting & Raftery (2007), appropriate scoring rules incentivise truth-
114
      ful prediction by optimising prediction distributions. However, as models and tasks become more
115   complex, optimising a single loss function may not always align with the broader objectives of
116   the system. In this regard, research such as (Huber, 1992) experiment with alternative, often non-
117   convex, loss functions designed to improve model training under specific constraints or performance
118   benchmarks.
119
120
      2.2   A SSESSORS
121
122   The concept of assessors was first introduced in (Hernández-Orallo et al., 2022), and further ex-
123   plored specifically for large language models (LLM) by Zhou et al. (2022), who presented encour-
124   aging results in a limited setting involving a small domain focused on data wrangling. Kadavath
125   et al. (2022) extended this by examining LLM and their role as assessors, finding that larger models
126   tended to be more accurate and consistent in predicting outcomes across multiple tasks, although
127   they acknowledged a lack of generalisation in out-of-distribution scenarios. Other applications of
128
      assessors focus on forecasting system performance (scaling laws) (Schellaert et al., 2024), team
      configurations (Zhao et al., 2024), anticipatory reject (Zhou et al., 2022; da Costa et al., 2023) or
129
      delegation (routing) to the best language model depending on the prompt (Lu et al., 2023; Hu et al.,
130
      2024; Ding et al., 2024). However, an analysis of the chosen validity metric and its distribution has
131   not been done to date.
132
133   An assessor is an external, second-order system that predicts the scores of another, first-order sys-
134
      tem, the subject. It is populational, trained on test data spanning numerous instances and potentially
      multiple subjects. It operates as a standalone entity, independent of the subject. This attribute
135
      allows it to be anticipatory; it can predict the subject’s performance solely on the basis of the in-
136
      put and the subject’s characteristics, without needing access to the subject’s output or the ability
137   to execute it. Furthermore, the standalone nature of assessors offers advantages in terms of ac-
138   countability and verification, as they can be developed by external auditors or for datasets different
139   from those used to train the original subject. In addition, their use extends to increasing curriculum
140   complexity, as in Bronstein et al. (2022), or facilitating instance-level model selection, a concept
141   derived from algorithm selection (Kerschke et al., 2019). Finally, a perfect assessor (in an ideal
142   scenario) would completely capture the epistemic uncertainty (error) associated with the subject’s
143   performance (Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021), with the error of the assessor depending only on the
144   aleatoric error of the subject.
145   Assessors must learn from a very specific kind of distribution, given by the results of a loss function
146   applied to the predictions of the base model. For instance, if this loss function is based on residuals,
147   the dependent variable in the regression problem the assessors have to deal with will be affected
148   by the distribution of residuals. Depending on the base model, this distribution may be normal or
149   asymmetric, but the outliers tend to be of aleatoric character rather than epistemic. Figure 2 (top)
150   shows a scatter plot for the predicted and actual values of the Software Effort test set with 255
151
      regression models. We seem some outliers near 14000 for which models predict values between
      4000 and 10000, leading to high residuals. This suggests that giving lower proportional weight to
152
      these errors in the loss function, as the LL loss in the bottom image does, may be a particularly
153
      interesting route to explore for assessors. This hypothesis is behind the experimental methodology
154   in the following section.

245   Once we have defined the loss functions, we must describe how to properly train assessors. Consider
246   a class of subject systems M , which are represented by their size, number of parameters and other
247   features, making a subject feature vector m ∈ M . All these subject systems have previously been
248   evaluated using a loss metric L. In order to build an assessor a, we need the input feature space X
249
      and the subject space M as inputs, and the loss as output, namely: a : X × M 7→ R. The training
      set for the assessor is then composed of rows such as ⟨xi , ms , li,s ⟩, where i and s are the instance
250
      and system indexes respectively, li,s = L(ŷi,s , yi ) is the value to predict, with yi being the ground
251
      truth output for instance i, represented by xi and ŷi,s = ms (xi ).
252
253   In usual circumstances, L is the target loss we care about and the one that appears in the training
254   dataset for the assessor. However, in this paper we are going to distinguish between the target loss
255
      and the proxy loss. Consider that we build the training set Dtr for the assessor with a proxy loss L◦  ,
      and we train the assessor a for this loss. If the target loss, L ◦ , is different from the proxy loss, then
256
257
      we need to transform the output of the assessor ˆl back to the target loss by using a transformation
      function f . This gives us two possible routes given a target loss L ◦ : we can either train an assessor
258
      that directly optimises for L ◦ or train an assessor that optimises for a proxy error L◦  and then
259
      transform the assessor predictions via f . For instance, the transformation function f between the
260   unsigned simple error and the unsigned squared error is f (l) = l2 . Following the example of energy
261   consumption from Figure 1, we could train an assessor model to predict the target loss (squared
262   error) or train an assessor to predict a proxy loss (such as the unsigned logistic loss L+    L ) and then
263   transform the output to obtain L+ 2 .

304   The training process for the assessors is defined as follows: given a pair of target and proxy losses
305   (L ◦ and L◦  , respectively), we train two assessors independently:
306
            1. The target assessor: this assessor is trained to directly predict the target loss, using the
307
               tuple ⟨x, s, L ◦ (ŷ, y)⟩. No output post-processing is required.
308
309         2. The proxy assessor: this assessor is trained to predict the proxy loss L◦  , using the tuple
310
               ⟨x, s, L◦  (ŷ, y)⟩. The output is then transformed into the target loss, via the corresponding
               transformation function f .
311
312   The data for training the assessors is also partitioned using a 70/30 split, from the instance-level eval-
313   uation data set. This partitioning strategy is distinct from the initial split used for training the base
314   models. Specifically, an assessor should not encounter, when predicting the test set, an example from
315   the original problem x ∈ X that has been used to train said assessor, as this could produce contam-
316   ination. This relies on keeping track of the instance identifier xid . Several regression models were
317   used as assessors: namely, XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), linear regression (Galton, 1886),
318   feed-forward neural networks (McCulloch & Pitts, 1943) and Bayesian ridge regression (Tipping,
319   2001), to account for the different strategies these models use to solve tasks (Fabra-Boluda et al.,
320
      2020; 2024), testing whether our results hold independently of the choice of assessor model.

322   the Spearman’s correlation coefficient ρ. To assess the statistical significance of the differences in ρ,s

      6   D ISCUSSION
477
478
      AI assessors represent a second-order estimation problem whose goal is to predict a loss or utility
479
      function, for any new example and base subject model. This is much more flexible than uncertainty
480   self-estimation because we can choose the metric of the assessor to be different from the ones the
481   base models are optimised for or evaluated. Still, in this context it may seem natural to build an
482   assessor to optimise for the target loss. However, we see that some other proxy losses may be
483   more effective. Looking at the distribution of residuals, one explanation may be found in a double
484   penalisation of high residuals (e.g., for outliers). That indicates that for convex loss functions used
485   at the first-order level (base models) we may benefit for concave loss functions at the second level
      that compensate for the weight in the extremes of the distribution.

520   assessors and then integrate the prediction of the overall metric. Overall, this paper opens a wide
