011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Emotion reasoning is crucial for achieving human-like emotional understanding in
014            Emotion Recognition in Conversation (ERC). Current ERC datasets provide only
015
               emotion-labeled utterances, lacking the rich annotations necessary for emotion
               reasoning. Although Large Language Models (LLMs) show promise in generat-
016
               ing rich emotional knowledge, they still struggle to apply this knowledge effec-
017
               tively for emotion reasoning. To address these challenges, we propose a learn-
018            ing framework based on cognitive appraisal theory, utilizing an agent powered
019            by LLMs to learn emotion reasoning from a third-person perspective, which we
020            refer to as the third-person appraisal agent. This learning framework comprises
021            two phases: self-evaluation and meta-evaluation. In the self-evaluation phase, the
022            agent generates appraisals essential for inferring emotions, incorporating coun-
023            terfactual thinking to refine its appraisals. The meta-evaluation phase uses re-
024            flective actor-critic reinforcement learning to train the agent to generate accurate
025            appraisals during testing. The training samples are appraisals generated during the
026
               self-evaluation phase, which eliminates the need for human annotations. By fine-
               tuning a specialized LLM in this framework, we significantly outperform baseline
027
               LLMs across ERC tasks, demonstrating superior reasoning capabilities and better
028
               generalization across various dialogue datasets. Additionally, we provide inter-
029            pretable results that clarify the model’s reasoning process behind its predictions.
030            To the best of our knowledge, this research is the first to apply cognition-based
031            methods to enhance LLMs’ emotional reasoning capabilities, marking a signifi-
032            cant advancement toward achieving human-like emotional understanding in arti-
033            ficial intelligence. The code is available here.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Emotion reasoning is crucial for understanding the causes behind expressed emotions, as it involves
039
      analyzing the complex interplay of a speaker’s thoughts, feelings, and behaviors in the field of Emo-
      tion Recognition in Conversation (ERC)(Wondra & Ellsworth, 2015; Ong et al., 2019). Applications
040
      of ERC range from mental health support systems to empathetic conversational systems, where emo-
041
      tion reasoning is essential for advancing toward human-like conversations. Current ERC methods
042   (Wondra & Ellsworth, 2015; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Hazarika et al., 2018; Ong et al., 2019; Jiao et al.,
043   2020; Vellido, 2020; Gao et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2022; Sabour et al., 2022; Zhao
044   et al., 2022; Cortiñas-Lorenzo & Lacey, 2023; Hu et al., 2023) rely on identifying emotion triggers to
045   infer emotions. However, these triggers are surface-level stimuli that evoke emotional reactions and
046   fail to capture the deeper underlying reasons that explain why certain triggers lead to specific emo-
047   tional responses (Poria et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2024). This gap raises a critical research question:
048   How can we develop emotion reasoning approaches that more closely mimic human understanding
049   of emotions in conversations?
050   Currently, there are no datasets specifically designed for emotion reasoning tasks (Poria et al., 2019;
051   Gan et al., 2024). Existing ERC datasets, such as IEMOCAP (Busso et al., 2008) and DailyDialog
052   (Li et al., 2017), only provide emotion labels for individual utterances and lack the detailed annota-
053   tions needed for emotion reasoning. Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-3 and GPT-4 have
      shown potential in generating rich emotional content (Li et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Team, 2024).

054
      For instance, Zhang et al. (2023) utilizes LLMs to generate visual information, providing supple-
055   mentary knowledge for emotional context, while Lee et al. (2022) leverages GPT-3’s in-context
056   learning to generate empathetic responses. However, despite their ability to produce rich emotional
057   knowledge, LLMs still struggle to apply this knowledge effectively for emotion reasoning. Our
058   work addresses this gap by using LLMs to generate the detailed annotations necessary for emotion
059   reasoning in ERC tasks.
060
      The appraisal theory of emotion (Lagattuta et al., 1997; Wondra & Ellsworth, 2015; Ong et al.,
061   2019) explains that emotions emerge from individuals’ appraisals (i.e., cognitive evaluations) of
062   situations, particularly in relation to their goals, desires, intentions, and expectations. Inspired by
063   this theory, we develop a novel framework that integrates cognitive appraisal principles into emotion
064   reasoning tasks. At the core of this framework is the third-person appraisal agent, powered by
065   LLMs. This agent acts as an external observer, analyzing conversations to evaluate how contextual
066   utterances align with an interlocutor’s objectives and expectations, and subsequently inferring their
067   emotional reactions. For instance, as shown in Figure 1, Person A’s anger may result from Person
068   B’s indifferent attitude, which contradicts Person A’s expectations. By simulating the cognitive
069
      appraisal process, this approach offers a possible solution for emotion reasoning, enabling LLMs to
      better capture the emotional dynamics of conversational contexts.

084   We introduce a novel learning framework comprising two distinct phases: self-evaluation and meta-
085   evaluation, both enhanced by integrated reflection mechanisms. In the self-evaluation phase, the
086   agent engages in reflective assessment by generating and refining emotional appraisals through coun-
087
      terfactual reasoning (Roese, 1997). This process enables the agent to explore alternative emotional
      responses and evaluate their alignment with conversational context. To the best of our knowledge,
088
      this work is among the first to incorporate counterfactual thinking into a verbal reinforcement learn-
089
      ing (RL)-based method (Shinn et al., 2024) for emotion reasoning tasks. Building on this foundation,
090   the meta-evaluation phase employs a reflective actor-critic RL strategy (Flavell et al., 2001; Haarnoja
091   et al., 2018) to continuously refine the model’s reasoning strategies based on self-generated correct
092   and incorrect appraisals from the self-evaluation phase. This meta-evaluation phase iteratively en-
093   hances the agent’s emotional understanding and reasoning accuracy without requiring human anno-
094   tations.
095
      Meanwhile, the efficient and reproducible evaluation of emotion reasoning remains challenging due
096   to the reliance on manual annotations(Kazienko et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
097   2024), which are time-consuming, costly, and highly variable. This variability limits large-scale
098   model comparisons and hinders the reliable replication of results. We aim to simplify emotion
099   reasoning performance evaluation by enabling LLMs to automatically assess and score emotional
100   reasoning tasks. Specifically, we evaluate: (1) Emotional Comprehension, which assesses the ability
101   to recognize emotional causes and understand the speaker’s motivations;(2) Contextual Understand-
102   ing, which measures the understanding of context and how emotions evolve within a conversation;
103   and (3) Expressive Coherence and Performance, which evaluates whether the model communicates
104
      its emotional reasoning clearly and is easy to understand. In this way, we transform complex emo-
      tion reasoning evaluation into a multiple-choice format that capable LLMs can assess, enabling an
105
      efficient and reproducible method for emotion reasoning evaluation in the ERC field.
106
107   Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, surpassing LLM baselines in both
      accuracy and weighted F1 scores for ERC tasks. To further validate its generalization capabilities,

      2     R ELATED W ORK
129
      Current approaches to emotion reasoning with LLMs emphasize prompt tuning for tasks such as
130
      emotional cause extraction (Doe & Smith, 2023; Bhaumik & Strzalkowski, 2024; Belikova &
131   Kosenko, 2024). However, there is limited research exploring the integration of self-reflection or
132   feedback mechanisms specifically within emotion reasoning tasks. Currently, self-reflection or feed-
133   back mechanisms have been explored in other domains, such as mathematical reasoning, code gen-
134   eration, and so on (Welleck et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Paul et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2024;
135   Shinn et al., 2024). Shinn et al. (2024) introduces Reflexion, a self-reflection mechanism that en-
136   ables LLMs to improve their reasoning capabilities by learning from past mistakes. However, the
137   application of Reflexion to emotion reasoning tasks has yet to be thoroughly investigated. Although
138   Madaan et al. (2024) demonstrates self-reflection in sentiment style transfer, which involves modi-
139
      fying the sentiment of a text while preserving its meaning, this task is tangentially related to ERC
      tasks. Our work is unique in combining reflection-mechanism with a domain-principles-driven ap-
140
      proach based on cognitive appraisal theory. This framework allows LLMs to not only generate
141
      self-feedback and refine their outputs but also align with human-like emotion reasoning processes,
142   simulating how humans understand emotions.

      3     E XPERIMENTS & R ESULTS
337
338   In this section, we present five major experiments designed to evaluate the performance of our pro-
339
      posed model. The experiments are structured as follows: (1) a comparative analysis against LLM-
      based models; (2) an ablation study assessing the impact of appraisal knowledge dataset quality
340
      on the model’s reasoning performance; (3) a comparative analysis of two verbal RL-based strate-
341
      gies for evaluating the effectiveness of the self-evaluation phase; (4) an ablation study assessing the
342   meta-evaluation phase; and (5) a qualitative analysis of the model’s appraisal performance on the
343   DailyDialog dataset.
344
345
      Baselines: For comparison, we use the instruction-tuned LLaMA3.1-8B-Instruct, Gemma1.1-7B-
      Instruct, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 as baseline models.
346
347   Evaluation Metrics: We use accuracy (Acc.) and Weighted-F1 as our performance metrics for both
348   IEMOCAP and DailyDialog datasets.
349   Implementation Details: We set the fixed window length, l, to 5. We utilize GPT-4 for MX . For
350   fine-tuning the third-person appraisal MA , we utilize the LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct LLM. In the self-
351   evaluation phase, the reflective cycle is set to 2 iterations. During the meta-evaluation phase, each
352   of the double critic models is implemented as a 3-layer MLP, while the value model is implemented
353   as a 2-layer MLP, with their embeddings initialized using pre-trained RoBERTa (Liu, 2019). Both
354   the actor and critic models are trained using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with the
355   same learning rate of 1 × 10−5 . Training is conducted over 10 epochs. The constant coefficients α
356   and β are set to 0.9 and 0.45, respectively. The MA model is trained using 4-bit quantized low-rank
357
      adapters (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021b), with r = 16. During inference (the test mode), the model’s
      temperature is set to 0.8.

524   4   C ONCLUSION
525
526   We integrate cognitive appraisal theory with a novel learning framework to train an agent capable of
527   performing emotional reasoning from a third-person perspective. This approach allows the agent to
528   continually refine its emotion reasoning abilities, even with a limited amount of data. Our approach
529   advances the development of explainable AI by training the agent to perform emotion reasoning in
530   a way that more closely aligns with human emotional understanding.
531   A key limitation of our work is the inherent difficulty LLMs face in interpreting complex emotional
532   transitions. For example, understanding how an extremely positive emotion like ‘happiness’ can
533   shift into an extremely negative one like ‘sadness’ remains a major challenge. Addressing these
534   limitations will be a primary focus of our future research as we aim to further improve the agent’s
535   ability to comprehend and reason through complex emotion shifts.
