011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Extensive fine-tuning of the synthesis between multimodal encoders and Large
014            Language Models (LLMs) on modality-specific data can expand the modalities
015
               that LLM can handle, leading to the formation of Multimodal Large Language
               Models (MLLMs). However, this paradigm to expanding modalities heavily relies
016
               on initiating fine-tuning from scratch with new multimodal data, which is both
017
               resource-intensive and inflexible. In this paper, we propose MMER (Multi-modality
018            Expansion and Retention), a novel training-free approach that reuses and com-
019            poses existing MLLMs to facilitate effective multimodal expansion while retaining
020            the original performance of each MLLM. In particular, MMER maintains the
021            multimodal encoders of the MLLMs while merging their LLM parameters. By
022            comparing the original LLM parameters with the merged ones, MMER can create
023            binary masks that enable an approximate separation of the LLM parameters for each
024            modality. This process allows the decoupled parameters to independently process
025            modality-specific inputs, thereby reducing parameter conflicts and maintaining
026
               the fidelity of the original MLLMs. Additionally, MMER integrates strategies
               to prevent catastrophic forgetting by employing a similar approach to separately
027
               decouple the parameters fine-tuned on new tasks from the original parameters. Ex-
028
               periments on three multimodal tasks and fourteen dual-modal tasks show significant
029            improvements over recent baselines, demonstrating that MMER can effectively
030            expand multimodal capabilities of LLMs while retaining 99.6% of the original per-
031            formance. Further experiments in both single-task and cross modalities multi-task
032            scenarios reveal that MMER significantly mitigates catastrophic forgetting.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Touvron et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b)
038   have recently become a cornerstone in artificial intelligence due to their exceptional performance.
039
      Building upon the capabilities of LLMs, researchers (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
      2023) integrate encoders for additional modalities and utilize extensive modality-text data for align-
040
      ment. These integrated systems are then fine-tuned to develop Multimodal Large Language Models
041
      (MLLMs), which excel at processing multimodal information. This paradigm has led to the successful
042   creation of numerous MLLMs across various modalities (Wu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023).
043
044   Most MLLMs are specialized in dual modalities, with examples including vision-oriented LLMs
045
      like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024b), as well as video LLMs (Lin et al.,
      2023; Maaz et al., 2024) and audio LLMs (Chu et al., 2023; Deshmukh et al., 2023). Despite these
046
      advancements, there is a growing impetus to expand the number of modalities that MLLMs can
047
      handle in order to address diverse applications. A straightforward method involves adding multiple
048   new modality encoders or employing a unified multimodal encoder, followed by re-fine-tuning the
049   MLLMs with fresh modality-text data. For instance, X-LLMs (Chen et al., 2023a) and MACAW-
050   LLMs (Lyu et al., 2023) integrate encoders for images, videos, and audio with an LLM simultaneously,
051   while OneLLM (Han et al., 2024) connects a universal encoder that handles eight distinct modalities
052   with an LLM. These different architectures are then aligned and fine-tuned using new multimodal
053   instruction data (e.g., image-speech chat). However, this kind of method lacks flexibility because it
      requires fine-tuning from scratch on new modality-text data to expand the multimodal capabilities

      To overcome the aforementioned limitations, researchers have explored model merging or compo-
072
      sition (Chen et al., 2024; Shukor et al., 2023; Panagopoulou et al., 2023) to facilitate multimodal
073   expansion in MLLMs. For instance, Chen et al. (2024) proposed NaiveMC, a basic, training-free
074   framework that reuses and combines modality-specific encoders from multiple MLLMs into the
075   merged LLM, enabling it to handle multiple modalities. They further introduced the DAMC frame-
076   work (Chen et al., 2024), which retrains existing MLLMs by separating modality parameters from
077   language model parameters. This approach mitigates parameter interference in the merged MLLM,
078   thereby enhancing the performance of multimodal expansion. However, these two frameworks
079   encounter a trade-off: NaiveMC is train-free but delivers lower performance, whereas DAMC, though
080   requiring training, yields better results. Nevertheless, both frameworks share a common drawback:
081
      they struggle to retain the original modality performance of the MLLMs due to parameter interference.
082   In this paper, we propose a novel training-free approach named MMER (Multi-modality Expansion
083   and Retention), which achieves multimodal expansion while bypassing the trade-off of previous
084   methods and retains the performance of each original MLLM (See Figure 1). First, we merge the task
085   vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023), which represent the difference between the fine-tuned parameters and
086   the pre-train LLM parameters, into a single merged task vector. Next, by comparing the Directional
087
      Congruence and Dominant Significance of the merged task vector with the original task vectors, we
      construct modality-specific binary masks. These masks effectively identify the modality-specific
088
      information retained in the merged task vector and approximately decouple it back into the original
089
      modality parameters without requiring additional training. This decoupling strategy enables the
090   merged MLLM to independently process non-textual modality information using its corresponding
091   reconstructed parameters, thereby significantly reducing interference from other modalities.
092
093
      Additionally, by re-adding the decoupled modality task vectors back to the base LLM parameters and
      integrating the corresponding encoders, we are able to reconstruct the original MLLMs approximately.
094
      This strategy not only retains the performance of the original modalities but also saves storage space.
095
      Remarkably, our MMER approach exhibits strong resistance against catastrophic forgetting (Yang
096   et al., 2023; Goodfellow et al., 2013) when handling new tasks. It can enhance performance on new
097   tasks without diminishing effectiveness on previous tasks. The process begins with selecting the
098   corresponding original MLLM based on the modality of the new task for fine-tuning. Then, the task
099   vector of the newly fine-tuned MLLM is merged with the task vectors of all original MLLMs to create
100   a merged task vector, and a corresponding mask is generated. This strategy effectively separates the
101   parameters of the new task from the original parameters, thereby preventing damage to the original
102   parameters during fine-tuning and significantly mitigating catastrophic forgetting.
103   We demonstrated the effectiveness of our MMER approach by composing four MLLMs (i.e., image,
104   audio, video, and point cloud) and conducted extensive experiments across three scenarios. Firstly,
105   in benchmarks like MCUB (Chen et al., 2024), which involve more than two modalities, MMER
106   achieves a significant improvement compared to NaiveMC framework and various model merging
107   methods such as TA (Ilharco et al., 2023) and TIES (Yadav et al., 2023). This confirms that MMER
      realizes and enhances the multimodal expansion capabilities of LLMs without requiring additional

      training. Secondly, we examined the performance of MLLMs approximately reconstructed by MMER
109   on fourteen dual-modal benchmarks covering four modalities. The experiments reveal that these

      In summary, our work makes several significant contributions: (i) We propose a training-free MMER

118   across three scenarios, and the experimental results confirm the effectiveness of the MMER approach.

      2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
123
124   As text-based LLMs are insufficient to meet evolving demands, significant research (Dai et al., 2023;
125   Li et al., 2023a; Achiam et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) efforts are focused on developing LLMs that
126   can effectively handle multimodal inputs. Vision LLMs (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b;
127   Liu et al., 2023), as trailblazers in this field, have excelled across numerous vision-language tasks
128   by connecting pre-trained visual encoders to LLMs through various alignment layers. Similarly,
129
      other modalities like audio (Rubenstein et al., 2023; Deshmukh et al., 2023) and video (Lin et al.,
      2023; Maaz et al., 2024) have rapidly followed suit, resulting in a surge of dual-modality MLLMs.
130
      Meanwhile, researchers have explored unifying multiple modalities into a single LLM. Approaches
131
      like Pandagpt (Su et al., 2023) and ImageBind-llm (Han et al., 2023) connect a unified multimodal
132   encoder like ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) with an LLM but rely only on image-text data, leading
133   to suboptimal performance. OneLLM (Han et al., 2024) improves on this by introducing a versatile
134   encoder for eight modalities and aligning all of them with language. However, these methods cannot
135   expand modalities due to the encoders have fixed input modalities. Another approach connects
136   multiple modality-specific encoders to an LLM, as seen in X-LLM (Chen et al., 2023a) and MACAW-
137   LLM (Lyu et al., 2023), which integrate encoders for images, videos, and audio. AnyMAL (Moon
138   et al., 2023) further adds an IMU encoder. These methods typically require high-quality multimodal
139   data for joint training and still struggle with modality expansion. In contrast, our MMER approach
140
      provides an efficient, training-free solution for seamless multimodal expansion in LLMs.
141
142   2.2   M ODEL M ERGING AND M ODEL C OMPOSITION
143
      The proliferation of various model checkpoints has sparked concerns over data privacy and resource
144
      efficiency, shifting focus toward model merging and composition (Yang et al., 2024; Lu et al.,
145   2024). Model merging, which merges multiple models fine-tuned from the same initialization, can
146   improve single-task performance (Gupta et al., 2020; Wortsman et al., 2022), out-of-distribution
147   generalization (Arpit et al., 2022; Ramé et al., 2022), or combine their capabilities (Wan et al., 2024;
148   Ilharco et al., 2022). For example, TA (Ilharco et al., 2023) defines the concept of task vectors
149   and uses arithmetic operations like addition to merge models. TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) mitigates
150   interference during merging by pruning redundant parameters and resolving sign conflicts, while
151   DARE (Yu et al., 2024) proposes a preprocessing step that randomly drops and scales parameters to
152   achieve the same goal. TALL-masks (Wang et al., 2024) proposes an algorithm to identify original
153
      task information from the merged task vector and eliminate harmful parameters to enhance merging
      performance. Model merging has been further applied to multimodal models (Aiello et al., 2024;
154
      Wu et al., 2023a). Sung et al. (2023) and Sundar et al. (2024) investigated merging multimodal
155
      transformers to improve performance in specific tasks, such as speech recognition. Model Tailor (Zhu
156   et al., 2024c) merges the original model with a fine-tuned model to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in
157   MLLMs. However, they do not explore the merging of multiple MLLMs. The NaiveMC and DAMC
158   frameworks (Chen et al., 2024) address this by using model merging and composition to create a
159   unified MLLM that inherits the modality capabilities of multiple MLLMs, thus enabling seamless
160   expansion into new modalities. Nonetheless, each framework has its limitations: one necessitates
161   additional training, while the other delivers subpar performance. In contrast, MMER approach
      avoids both of these drawbacks. It enhances the multimodal expansion capabilities of MLLMs

      3      M ETHODOLOGY
197
198
199
      In our MMER approach, we first merge the LLM parameters {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θn } from multiple MLLMs,
      all fine-tuned from the same base LLM θpre , into a unified LLM. However, such a merged model
200
      is particularly susceptible to interference between parameters from different modalities, which can
201
      degrade the performance of modality-specific representations. To overcome this challenge, we adopt
202   a training-free parameter decoupling method to effectively enhance the multimodal performance of
203   the merged LLM while also ensuring that the performance of each original modality is retained. This
204   method approximately decouples modality-specific parameters within the merged parameter, ensuring
205   that the representation of each non-text modality is processed independently by their respective
206   parameters. A visual workflow illustrating our MMER approach is depicted in Figure 2.
207

313   3.2.3     M ITIGATING C ATASTROPHIC F ORGETTING
314
315
      Typically, fine-tuning MLLMs on new data enhances their performance on newly introduced tasks, but it often
      comes at the cost of diminished performance on the previous tasks (Goodfellow et al., 2013). This degradation
316   in performance, referred to as catastrophic forgetting, arises when fine-tuning for new tasks. Drawing on the
317   insight that most parameter information from individual task vectors is retained in the merged task vector, we can
318   leverage MMER to additionally mitigate catastrophic forgetting. Specifically, we first select the corresponding
319   original MLLM based on the modality of the new task and proceed to fine-tune its LLM using the new data, while
320
      keeping the modality-specific components frozen. Next, we apply the parameter merging and decoupling method
      to the fine-tuned MLLM, alongside all original MLLMs, generating a new merged task vector and modality-
321   or task-specific binary masks. For the new task, we apply the task-specific mask to recover the fine-tuned
322   MLLM for processing. For the previous tasks, we maintain the use of the previously proposed multi-modality
323   expansion or retention strategy. This method enables MMER to effectively adapt to new tasks while retaining its
      performance on previous tasks, thereby substantially mitigating the risk of catastrophic forgetting.

346   4.2    BASELINE M ETHODS
347
      In the multi-modality expansion and retention experiments, we first compared MMER with the training-free
348   modal composition framework NaiveMC (Chen et al., 2024). We excluded the comparison with DAMC
349   framework (Chen et al., 2024), as its reliance on additional training introduces variables that could compromise
350   the fairness of our experimental evaluation. Additionally, since model merging methods can substitute the
351   average merging strategy in NaiveMC framework to enhance multimodal performance, we also compared against
      training-free model merging baselines, including TA (Ilharco et al., 2023) and TIES (Yadav et al., 2023). Given
352
      that DARE (Yu et al., 2024) is complementary to other model merging methods, we combined it with TA,
353   TIES, and NaiveMC framework in our experiments. Additionally, we included the zero-shot performance of the
354   original MLLMs as a performance upper bound. We evaluated the performance based on both the evaluation
355   scores or accuracy and the performance retention, the latter of which is defined in Appendix B.1.

469
      Modality-Specific Masks Analysis. Figure 4 (a) illustrates the percentage of parameters selected by different
      modality masks and compares the performance retention of MLLMs reconstructed with MMER against
470   NaiveMC. It can be observed that MMER outperforms NaiveMC across all four modalities, with performance
471   that is close to or even exceeds the original levels. This indicates that all integrated knowledge is preserved
472   after merging, and the parameter subsets selected by the masks retain crucial modality-specific information.
473   Additionally, we find that the audio mask, despite retaining only 2.2% of the parameters, still contributes to
474
      performance improvement. This finding is consistent with previous research (Yu et al., 2024), which noted that
      "Supervised fine-tuned language models tend to acquire excessively redundant delta parameters (also known as
475   task vectors)." Our results further confirm that this observation holds true for MLLMs as well.
476
477
      Hyperparameters Analysis. Figure 4 (b) explores the impact of the hyperparameters Top K% and the scaling
      factor λ in multimodal parameter merging and decoupling. Firstly, Top K% controls the sparsity of the original
478   task vectors. When sparsity is excessively high (e.g., 20% or 40%), performance deteriorates markedly due to the
479   insufficient information retained in the sparse parameters. Conversely, in the absence of sparsity (e.g., 100%), the
480   method fails to mitigate parameter conflicts among the original parameters, thereby hindering the approximate
481   decoupling of modality parameters. The effect of the scaling factor λ is akin to that of Top K%. Specifically,
482
      the scaling factor λ regulates the extent of information that the mask extracts from the merged task vector. If
      λ is set too high, the decoupled modality parameters will contain insufficient effective information, leading to
483   performance collapse (e.g., 60% or 75%). Conversely, if λ is too low (e.g., 15%), irrelevant parameters remain,
484   resulting in poor performance. In summary, Top K% and the scaling factor λ work in tandem to regulate the
485   amount of effective information contained in the decoupled parameters. For instance, the performance with K
      set to 60% and λ to 30% closely mirrors the results when K is set to 80% and λ to 45%.

      7                               C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we propose a novel training-free approach MMER that reuses and composes existing MLLMs to
533   resolve the dilemma faced by MLLMs during multimodal expansion: costly retraining or suboptimal performance.
534   MMER retains multimodal encoders and merges LLM parameters, constructing binary masks to decouple
535   modality-specific parameters. This innovative mechanism enables independent handling of modality-specific
536
      inputs by their respective parameters, thereby significantly reducing parameter conflicts to enhance multimodal
      performance. Additionally, MMER can reconstruct the original MLLMs by integrating decoupled parameters
537   with the base LLM, effectively retaining the original performance. Finally, by incorporating fine-tuned MLLMs
538   for new tasks into MMER approach, it isolates the parameters for new tasks from the original ones, thus
539   mitigating catastrophic forgetting. Extensive experiments have validated the effectiveness and robustness of
      MMER. We hope this work inspires further exploration of training-free multimodal expansion for LLMs.

777   A     N OVELTY AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
778
779   Our research aims to achieve training-free multi-modality expansion and retention for LLMs through parameter
780   merging and decoupling. We conduct a comparative analysis with existing relevant methods to demonstrate the
781
      innovation of our MMER approach.
782   Comparison with NaiveMC and DAMC frameworks. Our MMER approach is based on the NaiveMC
783   framework (Chen et al., 2024) and employs a parameter dynamic decoupling strategy similar to that of the
784
      DAMC framework (Chen et al., 2024) to mitigate parameter conflicts in the merged MLLM. However, there are
      several key differences:
785
786         1. Compared to the NaiveMC framework, our MMER approach effectively enhances the multimodal
787            performance of the merged MLLM.
788         2. Compared to the DAMC framework, our MMER approach employs a training-free parameter decou-
789            pling strategy instead of separating parameters during the initialization training of the MLLMs and
790            achieves similar results. Additionally, MMER is additional compatible with full-parameter fine-tuned
               MLLMs, whereas DAMC is restricted to parameter-efficient fine-tuned MLLMs.
791
792         3. Compared to the NaiveMC and DAMC frameworks, our MMER approach retains the performance of
               the original MLLMs while also providing additional capabilities to mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
793
794   Our MMER approach integrates the strengths of the NaiveMC and DAMC frameworks, while additionally
795   providing original performance retention capabilities.
796   Comparison with training-free model merging methods. Training-free model merging methods, such as
797   TA (Ilharco et al., 2023), TIES (Yadav et al., 2023), and DARE (Yu et al., 2024), are primarily designed
798   for merging models with identical architectures. Consequently, they must be combined with the NaiveMC
799   framework to achieve multi-modality expansion for LLMs. These methods alleviate parameter conflicts in
800
      merged MLLMs to some extent, leading to performance enhancement. However, their overall effectiveness, both
      in terms of multimodal performance and retention of original performance, falls significantly short compared to
801   our MMER approach.
802
803
      Comparison with alignment and fine-tuning methods. Compared to methods (Chen et al., 2023a; Lyu et al.,
      2023; Han et al., 2024) that achieve multimodal expansion for LLMs by adding multiple new modality encoders
804   or employing a unified multimodal encoder followed by alignment and fine-tuning, the advantages of our MMER
805   approach are clear. MMER can effectively reuse a large number of MLLMs from the open-source community
806   and merge them enabling multimodal expansion without the need for extensive resources and time spent on
807   training models and constructing high-quality modality instruction data.
808   Comparison with TALL-masks. TALL-masks (Wang et al., 2024) is an information localization algorithm
809   that, similar to our approach, compresses original parameters and subsequently approximates their restoration.
      However, there are several key differences:

810
               1. From an algorithmic perspective, TALL-masks overlooks the Consistency of original and merged
811               parameter signs. In contrast, we have addressed this aspect and demonstrated its effectiveness in our
812               ablation experiments (See Table 5).
813            2. In terms of application scenarios, our MMER applies parameter merging and decoupling to the
814               multimodal expansion for LLMs, enhancing their multimodal capabilities. Additionally, we utilize

890
      This section details the calculation of storage costs for MMER approach and the relevant methods mentioned
891   above. Let N , P , P ′ , and P ∗ represent the number of original MLLMs, the total parameters of the LLMs,
892   the number of the modality-specific component parameters, and the number of additional trainable parameters
893   of parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, respectively. Assuming each float parameter occupies 32 bits, the
894   storage cost for these methods across N original MLLMs is calculated as follows:
895
              • Original fine-tuned models: 32N (P +P ′ ). 32(P +P ′ ) represents the number of parameters contained
896             in a single MLLM.
897
              • NaiveMC framework: 32P + 32N P ′ . Stores a merged LLM and N modality-specific components.
898
899           • DAMC framework: 32P + 32N P ′ + 2N (32P ∗ ). Stores a merged LLM and N modality-specific
900
                components. 2N (32P ∗ ) represents the need to store an additional 2N trainable parameters of
                parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods for parameter separation.
901
902           • NaiveMC wit TA / TIES / DARE: 32P + 32N P ′ . Same as the NaiveMC framework.
903           • MMER: 64P + 32N P ′ + N P . 64P is for storing the parameters of a base LLM and a merged
904             task vector, while 32N P ′ indicates N modality-specific components. Additionally, N P denotes the
905
                storage for N binary masks.
