                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Bilevel optimization, characterized by a two-level hierarchical optimization struc-
010
               ture, is prevalent in real-world problems but poses significant challenges, espe-
011            cially in noisy, constrained, and derivative-free settings. To tackle these chal-
012            lenges, we present a novel algorithm for BILevel Bayesian Optimization (BILBO)
013            that optimizes both upper- and lower-level problems jointly in a sample-efficient
014            manner by using confidence bounds to construct trusted sets of feasible and lower-
015            level optimal solutions. We show that sampling from our trusted sets guarantees
016            points with instantaneous regret bounds. Moreover, BILBO selects only one func-
017            tion query per iteration, facilitating its use in decoupled settings where upper-
018            and lower-level function evaluations may come from different simulators or ex-
019
               periments. We also show that this function query selection strategy leads to an
               instantaneous regret bound for the query point. The performance of BILBO is
020
               theoretically guaranteed with a sublinear regret bound and is empirically evalu-
021
               ated on several synthetic and real-world problems.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Many real-world problems have hierarchical decision making processes involving two levels of op-
027   timization. Decisions made at the upper level affect the optimization problem at the lower level and
028   vice versa. Bilevel optimization can model hierarchical structures well and enable analysis of such
029   problems. Applications of bilevel optimization range from machine learning (e.g., hyperparame-
030   ter optimization, meta-learning) to economic problems (e.g., pricing strategies, toll setting) (Beck
031   & Schmidt, 2021). In energy management, energy providers determine optimal pricing strategies
032   for electricity (upper level) while consumers optimize their electricity demands based on the pric-
033
      ing (lower level). Similarly, in investment, brokers or regulators set fees on different asset classes
      to maximize their revenues (upper level), while investors optimize their portfolios considering ex-
034
      pected returns and risk (lower level). Bilevel optimization has been applied in both cases (Shu et al.,
035
      2018; Leal et al., 2020), typically using a nested framework with linear solvers at the lower level.
036   This approach may limit practical effectiveness but it is due to the inherent complexity of bilevel
037   optimization. Even with only linear constraints and objective functions, the set of feasible solu-
038   tions can be non-convex and non-continuous (Kleinert et al., 2021). Lower-level solutions that are
039   ϵ-feasible w.r.t. non-linear constraints may also lead to a bilevel solution that is arbitrarily far from
040   the actual bilevel solution (Beck et al., 2023).
041
      Classical approaches (Bard & Falk, 1982; Bard & Moore, 1990) have relied on simplifying assump-
042   tions, such as linearity or convexity, while others, assuming the presence of gradients, use gradient
043   descent to solve the lower-level problem and approximate hypergradients for the upper level. On the
044   other hand, meta-modeling-based methods employ surrogate models for efficient optimization, like
045   BLEAQ (Sinha et al., 2013) which uses quadratic approximations to map upper-level points to op-
046   timal lower-level solutions. Meta-modeling methods have advantages over gradient-based methods
047   in the presence of noisy observations, derivative-free functions, constraints and discrete variables.
048   They can also enable global optimization, even when dealing with non-convex functions. However,
049   existing meta-modeling methods are limited by the fit of low capacity models and lack of theoretical
050
      analysis on the performance bounds.
051   Bayesian optimization (BO), a popular meta-modeling method, has been applied extensively, includ-
052   ing to constrained optimization (Gelbart et al., 2014). In particular, Xu et al. (2023) and Nguyen
053   et al. (2023) both introduced confidence-bounds based optimistic estimations of the feasible set,
      with the former providing an infeasibility declaration scheme and the latter including a function

054
      query strategy for decoupled settings. Compared to constrained optimization, bilevel optimization
055   has additional significant challenges from being constrained by unknown optimal lower-level solu-
056   tions, which requires optimization of a separate lower-level problem. These challenges also differ
057   from those in robust optimization (Bogunovic et al., 2018) and composite objectives optimization
058   (Li & Scarlett, 2022), as both are single level optimization, despite the additional random variable
059   and composite objective function respectively. Our work tackles challenges in bilevel optimization,
060   particularly where we consider the optimality of estimated lower-level solutions.
061   In bilevel optimization, BO has only been employed in a nested framework (Kieffer et al., 2017; Do-
062   gan & Prestwich, 2023). This approach involves optimizing the upper level via BO while separately
063   optimizing the lower level at each upper-level query point. The nested approach suffers from sample
064   inefficiency due to the lack of information flow between the upper- and lower-levels of the optimiza-
065   tion problem. To address this issue, Dogan & Prestwich (2023) introduced an acquisition function
066   that is conditional on the optimal points of the lower level during the upper-level optimization. It
067   still requires the lower-level problem to be optimized separately to convergence at each upper-level
068   point. On theoretical analysis, Fu et al. (2024) provided a theoretical guarantee for a bilevel frame-
069
      work with stochastic gradient descent at the lower-level and BO at the upper-level. However, the
      lower-level is not a blackbox optimization due to this gradient assumption.
070
071   Contributions. We propose BILevel Bayesian Optimization (BILBO), a Bayesian optimization
072   algorithm for general bilevel problems with blackbox functions, where both levels are optimized
073   simultaneously. BILBO introduces trusted sets to iteratively reduce the search space by removing
074   infeasible solutions and sub-optimal lower-level solutions. Points in the trusted sets have upper-
075
      bounded instantaneous regrets, ensuring that they are good candidates for sampling. Functions
      are modelled using Gaussian processes (GPs) over both upper- and lower-level variables, which
076
      improves sample efficiency by enhancing the flow of information between lower-level optimization
077
      and various upper-level points. The trusted sets are constructed from confidence bounds of GPs,
078   enabling the derivation of regret bounds. Intuitively, the trusted sets provide estimates of feasible
079   solutions for upper-level optimization without requiring the lower-level problem to be optimized to
080   convergence. We also address the decoupled setting, where only one function is chosen to be queried
081   per iteration instead of querying all the functions at both levels simultaneously. This approach allows
082   different functions of varying complexities or costs to have different number of evaluations for a
083   good approximation. We show that our function query selection strategy provides an instantaneous
084   regret bound for the query point, which leads to a sublinear regret bound. The code and data used in
085   the paper will be released upon publication. For clarity, notations used in this paper are summarized
086
      in a notation table provided in Appendix A.

148   3   B ILEVEL BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
149
150   Our method, called BILevel Bayesian Optimization (BILBO), maintains and samples from trusted
151   sets constructed from confidence bounds, where points in the trusted sets have upper-bounded in-
152
      stantaneous regrets on constraints and the lower-level objective. This ensures that points in the
      trusted sets are sufficiently good for use in the optimization of the upper-level problem, enhancing
153
      sample efficiency. We introduce a function query strategy based on estimated regrets, which pro-
154
      vides an instantaneous regret bound on the query point. This leads to a sublinear cumulative regret
155   bound for BILBO. The pseudocode is in Algorithm 1.
156

158
      regret bounds on points in the trusted sets. Definition 3.7 defines the function query selection and
      Lemma 3.8 presents a instantaneous regret bound on the query point. Finally, the cumulative regret
159
      bound of BILBO is stated in Theorem 3.9 and the simple regret bound in Lemma 3.10.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      We evaluate the performance of BILBO on 4 synthetic and 2 real-world problems. We compare
327   BILBO with 2 baselines we introduced: “TrustedRand” and “Nested”. TrustedRand involves ran-
328   domly sampling query points from trusted sets, which can provide valuable insights into how much
329   trusted sets contribute to the overall performance of BILBO. On the other hand, Nested optimizes
330   the upper- and lower-level problems separately, which serves as a baseline for nested BO approaches
331   such as those in Kieffer et al. (2017); Dogan & Prestwich (2023). More details on TrustedRand and
332   Nested are in Appendix D.1. Note that Nested cannot handle constraints and it is not compared in
333   experiments with constraints.
334   Algorithms are implemented using GpyTorch (Gardner et al., 2018). All experiments, except Nested,
335   are initialized with 3 observations on each function. Nested approaches require more initial ob-

509   5   F UTURE W ORK
510
511   We have shown theoretically and empirically that BILBO is a regret-bounded, sample efficient al-
512   gorithm for noisy, constrained, and derivative-free bilevel optimization. A key direction for future
513
      work is improving scalability to high-dimensional spaces, which is a common challenge in BO.
514   We currently model upper-level objective F (x, z) for (x, z) ∈ X × Z, but this can be memory
515   inefficient as many lower-level variables z are suboptimal and irrelevant. A more efficient approach
516   could involve directly modeling F (x, z∗ (x)) for x ∈ X , reducing the dimension of the surrogate
517   model from dX × dZ to dX . This poses another challenge: incorporating the uncertainty associated
518   with the optimality of the lower-level solution into the uncertainty of the upper-level objective value.
519   Adaptive discretization (Shekhar & Javidi, 2017) may also reduce computational complexity by
520   reducing the effective dimension of the explored space. Discretization strategies could be integrated
521   with trusted sets, for example concentrating the discretizations to within the trusted sets, while
522   taking into account constraint and objective estimates of both upper- and lower-level problems.
523   Approximate surrogate models (Calandriello et al., 2019) offer another possible direction for scaling
524   to high-dimensional functions while preserving confidence bound estimates. The theoretical work
525
      presented in this paper could be extended to selected approximate surrogate models.
526   The representation of trusted sets will need to scale effectively to higher dimensions as well. Possible
527   approaches could be via sampling strategies like Latin Hypercube Sampling (McKay et al., 2000)
528   for efficient point representation in high-dimensional spaces or using hyperrectangles to represent
529   the trusted set efficiently (Eriksson et al., 2019).
530
531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We introduced BILBO, a novel bilevel Bayesian optimization algorithm that optimizes functions on
534   the upper- and lower-levels simultaneously, using trusted sets to reduce the search space for query-
535   ing. The trusted sets are constructed using confidence bounds, from which we derived instantaneous
536   regret bounds for points in these sets, and lead to a sublinear cumulative regret bound in the de-
537   coupled setting. Our experiments also show that BILBO outperforms other bilevel optimization
538   baselines, especially in problems with many non-convex functions. BILBO is a significant step to-
539   wards a more general bilevel solver, which will enable applications to complex real-world bilevel
      problems involving blackbox functions.

      D.1      BASELINE DETAILS
894
      TrustedRand implements a vanilla variant of the trusted sets St+ and Pt+ , where mean µ is used
895   instead of upper confidence bound u. Query points are then randomly sampled from the trusted set
896   variants.
897
898
      Nested uses the sequential least squares programming (SLSQP) optimizer for lower-level optimiza-
      tion and BO with upper confidence bound acquisition function Srinivas et al. (2010) at the upper-
899
      level. The lower-level problem is solved to convergence at each upper level query point. Note that
900
      SLSQP can only work on continuous functions.

      D.2      I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
903
904   GP with Matérn 5/2 kernel was used, and the GP hyperparameters were automatically tuned at
905   each iteration using maximum likelihood estimation on the past observations. The hyperparameters
906   include length scale and prior mean. The prior mean initialized to 0 for all experiments since the
907   output is already normalized. The initial length scale and other parameters for each experiment are
908   set according to Table 1. For SMD2, energy, and chemical experiment, we sampled from P̄t ≜
909   {(x, z̄t (x)) ∀x ∈ X } instead of Pt+ as it was empirically found to be better.
