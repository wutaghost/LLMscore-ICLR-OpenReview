                                                       A BSTRACT
009
010
               Entity summarization aims to compute concise summaries for entities in knowledge graphs.
               However, current datasets and benchmarks are often limited to only a few hundred entities
011
               and overlook knowledge graph structure. This is particularly evident in the scarcity of
012
               ground-truth summaries, with few labeled entities available for evaluation and training. We
013            propose W IK ES (Wiki Entity Summarization Benchmark), a large benchmark comprising
014            of entities, their summaries, and their connections. Additionally, W IK ES features a
015            dataset generator to test entity summarization algorithms in different subgraphs of the
016            knowledge graph. Importantly, our approach combines graph algorithms and NLP models,
017            as well as different data sources such that W IK ES does not require human annotation,
018            rendering the approach cost-effective and generalizable to multiple domains. Finally,
019            W IK ES is scalable and capable of capturing the complexities of knowledge graphs in
020            terms of topology and semantics. W IK ES features existing datasets for comparison.
021
               Empirical studies of entity summarization methods confirm the usefulness of our benchmark.
               Data, code, and models are available at: https://anonymous.4open.science/
022
               r/Wikes-2DDA/README.md.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Knowledge Graphs (KGs) are a valuable information representation: interconnected networks of entities
028
      and their relationships that enable machine reasoning to empower question answering Hu et al. (2018); Lan
029
      et al. (2019), recommender systems Wang et al. (2018), information retrieval Raviv et al. (2016). KGs may
030   comprise millions of entities representing real-world objects, concepts, or events.
031
032   Yet, the size and complexity of these KGs progressively expand, rendering it increasingly challenging to
033
      convey the essential information about an entity in a concise and meaningful way Suchanek et al. (2007);
      Vrandečić & Krötzsch (2014). This is where entity summarization becomes relevant. Entity summarization
034
      (ES) Liu et al. (2021) is the process of generating a concise and informative summary that captures the most
035
      salient aspects of the entity, based on the information available in the KGs. In ES, the entity description refers
036   to all the triples involving such an entity. For instance, Figure 1 illustrates a set of relationships surrounding
037   the entity Ellen Johnson Sirleaf in a KG, along with a possible summary for this entity. Extensive
038   descriptions can overwhelm users and exceed the capacity of typical user interfaces, making it challenging
039   to identify the most relevant triples. Entity summarization addresses this issue by computing an optimal
040   compact summary for an entity, selecting a size-constrained subset of triples Liu et al. (2021).
041
      Despite advancements in entity summarization techniques Liu et al. (2021), their development and evaluation
042   face significant limitations in current benchmarks and datasets Liu et al. (2020); Cheng et al. (2023). First,
043   existing benchmarks comprise only a few hundred entities, limiting dataset size. Second, generating ground-
044   truth summaries primarily relies on costly and time-consuming manual annotation, which can introduce bias
045   based on the preferences and knowledge of a few annotators. Lastly, these benchmarks often overlook the
046   rich information contained within the knowledge graph structure.

076   • Automatic entity summarization dataset generator allows for the creation of arbitrarily large datasets,
077     encompassing various domains of knowledge.

156   3.1    E XTRACTING S UMMARIES FROM W IKIDATA USING W IKIPEDIA A BSTRACTS
157
      We extract summaries for each Wikidata item using Wikipedia abstracts and infoboxes. Each abstract is a
158
      joint effort of many users and experts, which ensures quality and accuracy. Leveraging Wikipedia, we avoid
159   time-consuming manual annotation and enable the automatic generation of large-scale datasets.
160
161   Wikidata is a free and collaborative knowledge base that collects structured data to support Wikipedia and
162
      other Wikimedia projects. It includes descriptions and labels for entities. The descriptions offer in-depth
      details, while the labels serve as concise identifiers, facilitating efficient data retrieval and integration in
163
      subsequent steps. We load all Wikidata items XML dump files published on 2023/05/011 as entities V
164
      alongside their properties as relationships R into a graph database2 . The result is a graph that connects all
165   Wikidata items and statements. We include items if they (1) are not marked as redirects, (2) belong to the
166   main Wikidata namespace, and (3) have an English label or description. Additionally, we load metadata for
167   each Wikidata item and property, including labels and descriptions, into a relational database3 .
168
      Wikipedia pages contain infoboxes, abstracts, page content, categories, references, and more. Links to other
169
      Wikipedia pages are referred to as mentions. We detect these mentions in the abstracts and infoboxes of
170
      Wikipedia pages to use them later for labeling the summaries in Wikidata. We extract and load all the content
171   from the XML dump files of Wikipedia pages, published on 2023/05/014 , into a relational database under the
172   same conditions as Wikidata: the pages must be in English and not redirected.
173
174
      Summary annotation. We annotate the summaries in Wikidata using the corresponding Wikipedia pages.
      For each Wikipedia page corresponding to a Wikidata entity, we iterate through all connected Wikidata items
175
      using Wikidata properties. If a connected Wikidata item is mentioned in the Wikipedia abstract and infobox,
176
      we annotate the Wikidata item with the corresponding Wikidata property as part of the summary.
177
178   Wikidata is a directed multigraph, which means that each entity (Wikidata item) can be connected to another
179   entity via multiple relations (Wikidata properties). Yet, links in Wikipedia are not labeled; as such, we need to
180
      select one of the relations for the summary. To annotate the correct Wikidata property as part of the summary,
      we employ the DistilBERT model Sanh et al. (2019). DistilBERT is a fast and lightweight model with a
181
      reduced number of parameters compared to the original BERT model. This way, we can efficiently process
182
      large amounts of data while maintaining high-quality embeddings for accurate relation selection.

194   3.2   C APTURING THE G RAPH S TRUCTURE
195
196   Here we introduce the W IK ES generator algorithm. The main idea is to sample a connected graph that
197   preserves the original graph structure. To this end, we employ random walks Pearson (1905). The random
198   walk model is a straightforward yet effective method for preserving graph structure. While more recent
199
      techniques may yield superior results, we choose to use this widely accepted and fundamentally sound
      approach that exhibits good results even with 1% sampled nodes (Figure 3).
200
201   A random walk is a stochastic process defined as a sequence of steps, where the direction and magnitude of
202   each step are determined by the random variable Xt+1 = Xt + St where Xt represents the position at time t,
203   and St is the step taken from position Xt .
204   The process is a Markov process, characterized by its memoryless property:
205
                      P (Xt+1 = x|Xt = xt , Xt−1 = xt−1 , . . . , X0 = x0 ) = P (Xt+1 = x|Xt = xt )                   (2)
206
207
       In adapting this concept to our work, we redefine the number of random walks assigned to nodes based on
      their degrees, ensuring the distribution remains proportional to real data. This is achieved through logarithmic
208
      transformation and normalization. The logarithmic transformation is applied to reduce the impact of high-
209
      degree nodes and also low-degree nodes, making it more manageable for the random walk. Given a graph
210   with node degrees {d1 , d2 , . . . , di }, the log-transformed degree for node i is Li = log(di ). These values are
211   then normalized:
212                                                           Li − min({L})
                                                   Ni =                                                               (3)
213                                                       max({L}) − min({L})
214   where Ni is the normalized logarithmic degree of node i. Finally, the number of random walks Ri assigned
215   to each node is:
216                                  Ri = round (minRW + Ni × (maxRW − minRW))                                        (4)
217    Here, minRW and maxRW are the user-defined minimum and maximum limits for random walks. This
218   adaptation ensures that the random walks are proportional to the normalized logarithmic degree of each node,
219   reflecting the true structure of the network. For a small dataset we set minRW = 100 and maxRW = 300; for a
220   medium dataset minRW = 150 and maxRW = 600; for a large dataset, minRW = 300 and maxRW = 1800.
221
      This ensures that the random walks are tailored to both the scale and the complexity of the dataset. Importantly,
      our approach can be used to extract further subgraphs at the scale needed for benchmarking in a given scenario.
222
223   Moreover, the random walk sampling process requires a set of seed nodes as a starting point. In our case,
224   the seed nodes represent the target entities we are interested in. The seed nodes can be any Wikidata Item
225   Identifier, Wikipedia title, or Wikipedia ID of the Wikipedia pages. We collect the seed nodes on the condition
226   that they have at least k (default k = 5) common entities with the abstract section and the infobox in the
227
      Wikipedia pages. Therefore, this model is flexible, allowing you to choose any seed nodes from any domain
      as an input. In the datasets that we generated, we collect seed nodes from Laouenan et al. (2022). This paper
228
      has published information about individuals from various domains. The authors collected data from multiple
229
      Wikipedia editions and Wikidata, using deduplication and cross-verification techniques to compile a database
230   of 1.6 million individuals with English Wikipedia pages. The seed nodes that we use include actor, athletic,
231   football, journalist, painter, player, politician, singer, sport, writer, lawyer, film, composer, novelist, poet, and
232   screenwriter. Using combinations of these seed nodes, we generate four sets of datasets, with each set having
233   small, medium, and large versions. In Table 8 in Section A in the supplementary material, we present the
234   seed nodes and their proportions for each dataset and their corresponding train-test-val splits.

      3.3    W IK ES G ENERATOR
236
237   We discuss how W IK ES is created, and how further benchmarks can be generated without the need for manual
238   annotators. Algorithm 1 details the generator, which consists of the following steps.
239
      Step1: Retrieve summaries of each seed node (explained in Section 3.1)
240
241   Step2: Expand the graph using the random walk method in Section 3.2. Set the random walk’s length n
242   (default n = 2), which means it explores up to the n-hop neighborhood of each seed node. We choose
243   n = 2 because extending beyond two hops risks introducing irrelevant entities, while our approach balances
244
      efficiency and accuracy. This ensures scalability and relevance for large datasets like Wikidata, complementing
      existing benchmarks Lissandrini et al. (2018).
245
246   Step3: Check if the graph is connected. If it is, done. If not, identify all disconnected components and sort
247   them by size, from largest to smallest. In each iteration, connect smaller components to the largest component
248   using h connections. Utilize the shortest path method, selecting paths that are equal to or less than a minimum
249   path length l. Continue connecting nodes from the smaller component to the larger one until h nodes are
250
      connected. After each iteration, check graph connectivity again. If all components are connected to the largest
      component, the algorithm ends. Otherwise, re-sort components and increase l by 1. Repeat until the graph is
251
      a single connected component.
252

      20:    end while

      entity, and relation frequency. For Map score, we are exactly observing the same behavior for ESBM. We
      believe that the bias comes from the fact that the datasets are small, their second-hop neighborhood is not
322
      considered, and the relations between their first-hop neighbors are not considered. On the other hand, Figure 3
323
      shows the F-score for top-5, top-10 and dynamic F-score on W IK ES. Since the length of summaries varies
324   with the abstract, we calculate the F-score for each seed node based on its summary length. Results show that
325   W IK ES F-score is close to random for different statistics, thus rejecting the hypothesis of obvious biases. We
326   observe a minor bias towards node frequency in small datasets. Yet, as we increase the size of the dataset,
327   this bias disappears. We observe a similar behavior with MAP in Figure 11 (in appendix). Furthermore, we
328   use the entire Wikidata to measure the F-score for our seed nodes. Thus, importantly, we observe that our

      dataset’s F-score trend is comparable to that of the entire data, especially our large dataset. We also extracted
330
      the first-hop neighborhood of all our seed nodes and observed a small bias in the F-score top-5 and dynamic

364   We evaluate the performance of different entity summarization methods on our benchmark, and provide all
365   implementations in the W IK ES GitHub repository.
366   • PageRank Ma et al. (2008) is an unsupervised method that ranks nodes in a graph based on the structure of
367     incoming links, with the idea that more important nodes are likely to receive more links from other nodes.
368   • RELIN Cheng et al. (2011) is another unsupervised approach, a weighted PageRank algorithm that
369     evaluates the relevance of triples within a graph structure. We have re-implemented this model according to
370     the specifications in the referenced paper. On our smaller dataset version, RELIN takes approximately 6
371     hours to compute all summaries.
372   • LinkSum Thalhammer et al. (2016) , also an unsupervised approach, is a two-step, relevance-centric method
373     that combines PageRank with an adaptation of the Backlink algorithm to identify relevant connected entities.
374     We have re-implemented it according to the paper. The LinkSum method initially takes 10 hours to compute
375     the backlinks for each node in the small version of our dataset. By parallelizing the implementation, we

                                                                                                   8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


376
          reduced this to one hour. Additionally, the Backlink algorithm itself initially takes 100 minutes, but with
377
          parallelization, this was reduced to 10 minutes for the small version of our dataset.
378
379   • GATES Firmansyah et al. (2021) is a recent supervised approach that integrates graph structure using
380     Graph Attention Networks with knowledge graphs and text embeddings. We run GATES using the best
381     performing hyperparameters of the original paper. GATES takes 20 minutes to run on the our small datasets.
382   We evaluate the methods on the smallest W IK ES dataset due to their inefficiency. Table 2 shows that LinkSum,
383   generally outperforms other models. Interestingly, GATES, despite being supervised, achieves lower accuracy
384   compared to LinkSum. The deficiency in GATES may be due to its reliance on the frequency of nodes and
385   relations, which are used as weights. As mentioned earlier, this frequency bias is present in ESBM but not in
386   W IK ES.These results highlight the significance of graph structure in summarizing entities within real-world
387   knowledge graphs like W IK ES, emphasizing the advantages of graph-based methods.
388   Efficiency concerns. The evaluation we conducted on various entity summarization models reveals significant
389   efficiency issues with many recent baselines. For example, BAFREC Kroll et al. (2018), a model highlighted
390   in a recent survey on unsupervised entity summarization, was unable to process a graph with 13 000 nodes —
391   which is 5× smaller than our smallest dataset — even after running for two days. Similarly, MPSUM Wei
392   et al. (2020) did not finish after 15 days on the same graph. Additionally, models like INFO Cheng et al.
393   (2023), which depend on unavailable external resources, were excluded from our evaluation. These results
394
      highlight the need for more scalable approaches that can efficiently handle large knowledge graphs without
      sacrificing performance or accuracy.

      We introduce W IK ES (Wiki Entity Summarization Benchmark), a benchmark for KG entity summarization
      which provides a scalable dataset generator that eschews the need for costly human annotation. W IK ES uses
417
      Wikipedia abstracts for automatic summary generation, ensuring contextually rich and unbiased summaries.
418
      It preserves the complexity and integrity of real-world KGs through a random walk sampling method that
419   captures the structure of entities down to their second-hop neighborhoods. Empirical evaluations demonstrate
420   that W IK ES provides high-quality large-scale datasets for entity summarization tasks, and that it captures
421   the complexities of knowledge graphs in terms of topology, making it a valuable resource for evaluating and
422   improving entity summarization algorithms.
