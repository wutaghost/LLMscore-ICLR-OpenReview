009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Although large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across
012            various domains, their considerable scale necessitates substantial computational
013            resources, posing significant challenges for deployment in resource-constrained
014            environments. Layer pruning, as a simple yet effective compression method, re-
015            moves layers of a model directly, reducing computational overhead. However,
016            what are the best practices for layer pruning in LLMs? Are sophisticated layer
017            selection metrics truly effective? Does the LoRA (Low-Rank Approximation)
018            family, widely regarded as a leading method for pruned model fine-tuning, truly
019
               meet expectations when applied to post-pruning fine-tuning? To answer these
               questions, we dedicate thousands of GPU hours to benchmarking layer pruning in
020
               LLMs and gaining insights across multiple dimensions. Our results demonstrate
021
               that a simple approach, i.e., pruning the final 25% of layers followed by fine-
022            tuning the lm head and the remaining last three layer, yields remarkably strong
023            performance. Following this guide, we prune Llama-3.1-8B-It and obtain a model
024            that outperforms many popular LLMs of similar size, such as ChatGLM2-6B,
025            Vicuna-7B-v1.5, Qwen1.5-7B and Baichuan2-7B. We release the optimal model
026            weights on Huggingface1 , and the code is available on GitHub2 .

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have achieved unprecedented success in many fields,
032   such as text generation (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), semantic analysis (Deng et al.,
033   2023; Zhang et al., 2023b) and machine translation (Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023). How-
034   ever, these achievements come with massive resource consumption, posing significant challenges
035   for deployment on resource-constrained devices. To address these challenges, numerous techniques
036   have been developed to create more efficient LLMs, including pruning (Ma et al., 2023a; Sun et al.,
037   2023), knowledge distillation (Xu et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024), quantization (Lin et al., 2024; Liu
038
      et al., 2023), low-rank factorization (Saha et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024a), and system-level infer-
      ence acceleration (Shah et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024).
039
040   Among these methods, pruning has emerged as a promising solution to mitigate the resource de-
041   mands of LLMs. By selectively removing redundant patterns—such as parameters (Sun et al.,
042   2023), attention heads (Ma et al., 2023a) and layers (Men et al., 2024)—pruning aims to slim down
043   the model while maintaining its original performance as much as possible. Among different types
044
      of pruning, layer pruning (Kim et al., 2024; Siddiqui et al., 2024) has garnered particular interest
      due to its direct impact on pruning the model’s depth, thereby decreasing both computational com-
045
      plexity and memory usage. Additionally, thanks to the nice structure of the existing LLMs such as
046
      Llama (Dubey et al., 2024), whose transformer blocks have the exactly same dimension of input
047   and output, layer pruning becomes a straightforward and simple solution. Therefore, in this paper,
048   we focus on layer pruning. Unlike existing studies (Men et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Chen
049   et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) that aim to propose various sophisticated pruning
050   methods, we take a step back and focus on the following questions:

      To answer the aforementioned questions, we spent thousands of GPU hours to benchmark layer
075
      pruning, conducting extensive experiments across 7 layer selection metrics, 4 state-of-the-art open-
076   source LLMs, 6 fine-tuning methods, 5 pruning strategies on 10 common datasets. From these
077   efforts, we have developed a practical list of key insights for LLM layer pruning in Figure 1:
078
079          1). Reverse-order pruning is simple yet effective, i.e., simply pruning the last several layers
080              performs better than many complex pruning metrics (Kim et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024) .
081
             2). LoRA performs worse than expected, i.e., LoRA, the most commonly used fine-tuning
082              methods in existing pruning approaches (Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023b; Kim et al.,
083              2024; Men et al., 2024), is not the best choice for post-pruning performance recovery.
084              In contrast, freezing the other layers and fine-tuning only the last few remaining layers
085              and lm head, also known as partial-layer fine-tuning, can achieve higher accuracy while
086              reducing the training time. The result is unique to layer pruning since LoRA and partial-
087              layer fine-tuning perform similarly as Table 3 in full-model fine-tuning.
088
             3). Iterative pruning offers no benefit, i.e., considering both training costs and performance
089              gains, iterative pruning, where layers are removed step-by-step, fails to beat the one-shot
090              pruning, where a single cut is made.
091
092   In addition to the above practices, we also conduct sensitivity analyses on the number of calibration
093   samples, the choice of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) datasets and various pruning rates for LLM
094   layer pruning. We find that the number of calibration samples affects the performance of data-
095   driven pruning methods, highlighting the importance of considering performance stability as a key
096   criterion when evaluating the quality of pruning metrics. Similarly, we discover that fine-tuning
097
      with different SFT datasets significantly impacts the performance of pruned models. This suggests
      the need for further exploration of the most suitable datasets for fine-tuning. Finally, we apply our
098
      insights and practices to prune Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), obtaining Llama-3.1-
099

102   et al., 2024a) and Baichuan2-7B (Baichuan, 2023). We hope our work will help guide future efforts
103   in LLM layer pruning and inform best practices for deploying LLMs in real-world applications. In
104   a nutshell, we make the following contributions:

118   2     R ELATED W ORK
119
120   LLM Layer Pruning. LLM layer pruning is a technique used to reduce the number of layers in
121   LLMs, aiming to lower computational costs without significantly degrading performance. Specif-
122   ically, it evaluates the contribution of each layer to the model’s overall performance, using criteria
123   such as gradients, activation values, parameter weights, or the layer’s influence on the loss function.
124
      Layers that contribute the least are then pruned to reduce complexity. For example, LaCo (Yang
      et al., 2024b) achieves rapid model size reduction by folding subsequent layers into the previous
125
      layer, effectively preserving the model structure. Similarly, MKA (Liu et al., 2024b) uses manifold
126
      learning and the Normalized Pairwise Information Bottleneck measure (Tishby et al., 2000) to iden-
127   tify the most similar layers for merging. ShortGPT (Men et al., 2024) uses Block Influence (BI) to
128   measure the importance of each layer in LLMs and remove layers with low BI scores. Kim et al.
129   (2024) utilize Magnitude, Taylor and Perplexity (PPL) to evaluate the significance of each layer.
130
      Differences from Traditional Layer Pruning. Unlike traditional Deep Neural Networks (Szegedy
131
      et al., 2014; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; He et al., 2015; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
132
      2021) (DNNs), typically trained for a single, specific task, LLMs are designed to handle a wide
133   range of tasks and are structured with billions of parameters. These differences in model scale and
134   task complexity fundamentally alter the challenges associated with layer pruning. For example, in
135   traditional DNN layer pruning (Chen & Zhao, 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2022; Tang et al.,
136   2023; Guenter & Sideris, 2024), assessing the importance of each layer is relatively straightforward,
137   as it is tied to a single task. In contrast, the parameters of LLMs are optimized across diverse tasks,
138   complicating the evaluation of layer importance. Furthermore, traditional DNN pruning commonly
139   involves full parameter fine-tuning after pruning, while LLMs often employ Parameter-Efficient
140   Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques (Hu et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024b; Dettmers
141
      et al., 2024) such as Low-Rank Approximation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) to accommodate their mas-
      sive parameter space. Consequently, traditional DNN pruning methods may not adequately address
142
      the unique challenges posed by LLMs, highlighting the need for specialized pruning strategies.
143
144   Exploration of LLM Pruning. Although recent research focuses on developing sophisticated prun-
145   ing methods (Kim et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023a; Men et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c;b; Yang et al.,
146   2024b; Zhong et al., 2024), few studies (Jaiswal et al., 2023; Williams & Aletras, 2024; Muralid-
147
      haran et al., 2024) take a step back and revisit existing LLM pruning techniques. For example,
      Jaiswal et al. (2023) re-evaluate the effectiveness of existing state-of-the-art pruning methods with
148
      PPL. Williams & Aletras (2024) systematically investigate how the calibration dataset impacts the
149
      effectiveness of model compression methods. Muralidharan et al. (2024) develop a set of prac-
150   tical practices for LLMs that combine layer, width, attention and MLP pruning with knowledge
151   distillation-based retraining. However, these methods either do not consider layer pruning or lack a
152   comprehensive comparison. In contrast, we systematically validate different layer selection metrics,
153   fine-tuning techniques, and pruning strategies to provide a thorough evaluation.

198
      To assess the performance of the model, we follow the evaluation of Ma et al. (2023a) to per-
199
      form zero-shot task classification on 8 common sense reasoning datasets using the lm-evaluation-

      harness (Gao et al., 2023) package: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), CMMLU (Li et al., 2023),

202   ARC-easy (Clark et al., 2018), ARC-challenge (Clark et al., 2018) and OpenbookQA (Mihaylov
203   et al., 2018). Additionally, we evaluate the model using perplexity on the WikiText2 (Merity et al.,
204   2016) and Penn Treebank (PTB) (Marcus et al., 1993) datasets. For the PPL metric, we follow (Ma
205   et al., 2023a; Muralidharan et al., 2024) and use WikiText2 for calculation. Following (Ma et al.,
206   2023a), we randomly select 10 samples from BookCorpus (Zhu et al., 2015) to compute Taylor and
207   BI, truncating each sample to a sequence length of 128. Unless otherwise specified, we utilize the
208   Alpaca-cleaned (Taori et al., 2023) with LoRA to recover the performance. Uniformly, we set the
209
      training epoch to 2 and batch size to 64. All experiments are conducted on 2 NVIDIA A100 GPUs
      with 40 GB of memory and 4 NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPUs with 24 GB of memory.

295   4.2          I S THE L O RA FAMILY THE BEST CHOICE FOR POST- PRUNING FINE - TUNING ?
296
      In previous studies (Kim et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024), LoRA is often used to restore the perfor-
297
      mance of pruned models. This raises a question: Is the LoRA family the best choice for post-pruning
298
      fine-tuning? To answer this question, we further use QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) and partial-layer
299   fine-tuning techniques to conduct experiments. We briefly introduce these methods as follows:
300
301   LoRA Fine-tuning. LoRA is one of the best-performed parameter-efficient fine-tuning paradigm
302
      that updates dense model layers using pluggable low-rank matrices (Mao et al., 2024). Specifically,
      for a pre-trained weight matrix W0 , LoRA constrains its update by representing the latter with a
303
      low-rank decomposition W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA. At the beginning of training, A is initialize with
304
      a random Gaussian initialization, while B is initialized to zero. During training, W0 is frozen and
305   does not receive gradient updates, while A and B contain trainable parameters. Then the forward
306   pass can be formalized as:
307                                     W0 x + ∆W x = W0 x + BAx.                                      (4)
308
309   QLoRA Fine-tuning. QLoRA builds on LoRA by incorporating quantization techniques to further
310   reduce memory usage while maintaining, or even enhancing the performance.
311
      Partial-layer Fine-tuning. Compared to LoRA and QLoRA, which inject trainable low-rank factor-
312   ization matrices into each layer, partial-layer fine-tuning simply freezes the weights of some layers
313   while updating only the specified layers to save computing resources and time (Shen et al., 2021;
314   Ngesthi et al., 2021; Peng & Wang, 2020). Following by the common practice of previous stud-
315   ies (Khan & Fang, 2023), we choose to fine-tune only the later layers that are closer to the output,
316   while keeping the earlier layers, which capture more general features, frozen. Specifically, we use
317   two different fine-tuning strategies: one is to finetune only the model head (lm head only), and the
318   other is to finetune the lm head plus the last layer (lm head + last layer), the last two layers (lm head
319   + last two layers), and the last three layers (lm head + last three layers).
320   In view of the superiority of the reverse-order metric in Section 4.1, we use it to prune here. For
321   the Vicuna-7B-v1.5, Qwen1.5-7B, and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct models, we prune 8 layers. For the
322   Gemma2-2B-Instruct model, we prune 6 layers. Subsequently, we utilize LoRA, QLoRA and
323   partial-layer fine-tuning methods to restore performance. We provide more results of fine-tuning
      with the taylor metric in Table B. In particular, because Gemma2-2B-Instruct employs weight ty-

416   ing requires more computational overhead than one-shot pruning, which is not cost-effective with
417   limited performance gains.

526   7         C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we revisit LLM layer pruning, focusing on pruning metrics, fine-tuning methods and
529   pruning strategies. From these efforts, we have developed a practical list of best practices for LLM
530   layer pruning. We use these practices and insights to guide the pruning of Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and
531   obtain Llama-3.1-6.3B-It-Alpaca and Llama-3.1-6.3B-It-Dolly. Our pruned models require fewer
532   training tokens compared to training from scratch, yet still performing favorably against various
533   popular community LLMs of similar size. We hope our work will help inform best practices for
534   deploying LLMs in real-world applications.
535   Limitations and Future Work. In Section 5, we find that SFT datasets do effect the performance
536   of pruned models. Therefore, we will explore which SFT datasets are more suitable for fine-tuning
537   pruned models in future work. Additionally, in this paper, we focus primarily on layer pruning due
538   to the straightforward nature of pruning layers in LLMs, where the input and output dimensions
539   are identical. However, we plan to further investigate weight pruning (Sun et al., 2023; Frantar &
      Alistarh, 2023) and width pruning (Xia et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023b) in future experiments.
