                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Shapley value-based data valuation methods, originating from cooperative game
010
               theory, quantify the usefulness of each individual sample by considering its con-
011            tribution to all possible training subsets. Despite their extensive applications, we
012            observe these methods encounter value inflation—while samples with negative
013            Shapley values are detrimental, some with positive values can also be harmful. This
014            challenge prompts two fundamental questions: the suitability of zero as a threshold
015            for distinguishing detrimental from beneficial samples and the determination of
016            an appropriate threshold. To address these questions, we focus on KNN-Shapley
017            and propose Calibrated KNN-Shapley (CKNN-Shapley), a semi-value method that
018            calibrates zero as the threshold to distinguish detrimental samples from beneficial
019
               ones by mitigating the negative effects of small-sized training subsets. Through
               extensive experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness of CKNN-Shapley in alle-
020
               viating data valuation inflation, detecting detrimental samples, and assessing data
021
               quality. We also extend our approach beyond conventional classification settings,
022            applying it to diverse and practical scenarios such as learning with mislabeled data,
023            online learning with stream data, and active learning for label annotation.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The significance of data as inputs in machine learning algorithms cannot be overstated for algorithmic
029   performance (Liang et al., 2022; Zha et al., 2023). Beyond the development of sophisticated
030   algorithms, there is a growing emphasis on the curation of high-quality training sets. Data-centric
031   learning has emerged to assess the valuation of data within the context of a learning task. This
032   encompasses a spectrum of tasks, ranging from outlier detection (Boukerche et al., 2020) to noisy
033
      label correction (Zheng et al., 2021), from best subset selection (Hazimeh and Mazumder, 2020) to
      sample reweighting (Li and Liu, 2022), and antidote data generation (Chhabra et al., 2022; Li et al.,
034
      2023) to active labeling (Tharwat and Schenck, 2023; Liu et al., 2021).
035
036   Understanding the value of an individual data sample is fundamental in data-centric learning. Leave-
037   one-out influence (Cook and Weisberg, 1982), a straightforward method, offers an initial assessment
038   of the relative influence of the specific sample compared to the rest of the training set. Shapley
039
      value-based methods (Shapley et al., 1953; Roth, 1988) quantify the usefulness of each individual
      sample towards the utility on a validation set by considering its contribution to all possible training
040
      subsets. Unlike the leave-one-out influence, Shapley value represents the weighted average utility
041
      change resulting from adding the point to different training subsets, showcasing greater robustness
042   in diverse contexts (Bordt and von Luxburg, 2023; Tsai et al., 2023; Sundararajan et al., 2020).
043   Despite the absence of assumptions on the learning model, Shapley value-based methods require
044   expensive and extensive model retraining, which are prohibitive for large-scale data analysis and deep
045   models (Hammoudeh and Lowd, 2022).
046
      With the advent of KNN-Shapley (Jia et al., 2019b; 2021), a pragmatic tool enabling the computation
047
      of Shapley values without the need for costly model retraining, Shapley-based approaches have
048   become feasible and widely applied. KNN-Shapley leverages the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)
049   classifier as a surrogate for the original learning model, recursively calculating the Shapley value
050   for each training sample. Despite their promise, KNN-Shapley and its variants grapple with the
051   issue of value inflation. We note that While samples with negative Shapley values are recognized
052   as detrimental, the dilemma arises as certain samples with positive values may also have harmful
053   effects. This challenge gives rise to two pivotal questions: the appropriateness of zero as a threshold
      for distinguishing detrimental from beneficial samples and the identification of a suitable threshold.

      Our Contributions. In this paper, we focus on addressing the value inflation issue observed in
055   KNN-Shapley. To tackle this, we propose Calibrated KNN-Shapley (CKNN-Shapley), a semi-value
056   method that calibrates zero as the threshold to distinguish detrimental samples from beneficial ones
057   by mitigating negative effects of small training subsets. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
058     • We unveil the value inflation issue of KNN-Shapley, which not only misidentifies a portion of
059       detrimental samples as beneficial, but also distorts the interpretation of data valuation. Beyond
060       the misidentified samples, value inflation further impacts the assessment of beneficial samples.
061     • We propose CKNN-Shapley, a simple but not incremental method to calibrate zero as the
062       threshold to distinguish detrimental samples from beneficial ones. Our hypothesis attributes
063       value inflation to improper subset selection in KNN-Shapley, and we address this issue through a
064       straightforward yet effective strategy by imposing a constraint on the size of training subsets.
065     • We perform comprehensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, comparing CKNN-
066       Shapley with KNN-Shapley-based methods. The results showcase the effectiveness of CKNN-
067       Shapley in mitigating value inflation and improving classification performance. We also extend
068       our approach beyond conventional classification settings, applying it to learning with mislabeled
069       data, online learning with stream data, and active learning for label annotation.
070   Related Work. We introduce Shapley-based methods with a major focus on data valuation and
071   KNN-Shapley. (i) Shapley-based Data Valuation. The Shapley value (Shapley et al., 1953; Roth,
072   1988) measures the weighted average utility change when adding a point to all possible training
073   subsets, making it a primary tool for assessing the valuation of individual samples (Jiang et al.,
074   2023). Shapley-value based methods have found extensive application in various domains, including
075   variable selection (Cohen et al., 2005; Zaeri-Amirani et al., 2018), feature importance (Lundberg
076   and Lee, 2017; Covert and Lee, 2020; Jethani et al., 2021), model valuation (Rozemberczki and
077   Sarkar, 2021), health care (Pandl et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021), federated learning (Han et al.,
078
      2021), collaborative learning (Sim et al., 2020), data debugging (Deutch et al., 2021), and distribution
      analysis (Schoch et al., 2022; Ghorbani et al., 2020). Building upon this concept, Beta Shapley (Kwon
079
      and Zou, 2022) and Banzhaf value (Wang and Jia, 2023a) have been developed by relaxing the
080
      efficiency axiom of the Shapley value. While Shapley-based data valuation approaches are model-
081   agnostic, exponential model retraining renders these methods computationally challenging even
082   for small datasets (Ghorbani and Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019b). Efforts to accelerate computation
083   include efficient sampling (Zhang et al.), utility learning (Wang et al., 2021), and the assumption of
084   independent utility (Luo et al., 2022). In addition to data valuation. (ii) KNN-Shapley Data Valuation.
085   KNN-Shapley (Jia et al., 2019a; Wang and Jia, 2023b) emerges as one of the most promising solutions
086   to mitigate the computational challenges associated with Shapley values. It leverages assumptions
087   about the learning model by employing a KNN classifier as a surrogate, recursively computing
088   the Shapley value for each training sample without the need for retraining. This high efficiency
089
      has spurred the development of numerous KNN-Shapley variants, such as weighted KNN (Wang
      et al., 2024), soft KNN (Wang and Jia, 2023b), and threshold KNN(Wang et al., 2023), tailored to
090
      enhance generalization, sample reuse, and privacy risks, respectively. In this paper, our focus is on
091
      the KNN-Shapley category, with the objective of addressing issues related to value inflation.

      As a variant of KNN-Shapley, our CKNN-Shapley inherently integrates several key axioms from
      KNN-Shapley, focusing on three fundamental principles: group rationality, fairness, and additivity, as
246
      emphasized in (Jia et al., 2019b). CKNN-Shapley prioritizes fairness, ensuring symmetry (where two
247
      identical samples receive identical Shapley values) and zero elements (no contribution, no payment).
248   Additionally, it upholds additivity, where values across multiple utilities sum up to the value under
249   a utility that is the aggregate of all these utilities. Enhancing group rationality, CKNN-Shapley
250   refines this axiom by disregarding subsets with fewer than T samples, thereby distributing the utility
251   difference between the entire dataset and the subset. Since the utility of a subset, according to
252   the KNN classifier, expects only the nearest samples to contribute, the utility of such a subset is
253   anticipated to be zero, a factor overlooked in KNN-Shapley. By addressing this, CKNN-Shapley
254   mitigates the adverse impact of overemphasizing the cooperative game with samples distant from the
255   target sample, rendering the axiom of group rationality more compatible with the KNN classifier.

444   Online Learning. In addition to static datasets, we further evaluate our approach in the context
445   of online stream data. Specifically, we use three text datasets—AGnews, SST-2, and News20—in
446   this experiment. We segment these datasets into 10 equal-sized batches and gradually feed them
447   into the KNN classifier to simulate stream data. KNN-Shapley-based data valuation approaches are
448   employed to remove detrimental samples with negative Shapley values. Figures 6A-C depict the
449
      online learning performance of three KNN-Shapley-based data valuation approaches on the three
      text datasets across different batches. CKNN-Shapley consistently outperforms other methods with
450
      large margins, resolving the value inflation issue and showing more effectiveness in identifying
451
      detrimental samples in the stream context. Figure 6D displays the remaining training samples in
452   each batch, combining samples from the last batch and new arrivals in the current batch by removing
453   detrimental samples. KNN-Shapley involves more training samples compared to others, indicating
454   severe inflation issues. In contrast, CKNN-Shapley removes a significant portion of detrimental
455   samples, enhancing learning performance with fewer training samples. Figure 6E provides details
456   of involved training samples for CKNN-Shapley on News20 across different batches. Additionally,
457   Figure 6F shows the Shapley value change for representative samples. The pink line represents a
458   sample included in all batches, while other lines represent samples initially associated with positive
459   Shapley values in the initial batches, which later become negative and are subsequently removed.
460   Active Learning. We further evaluate our approach in the context of active learning. Specifically,
461   to select the unlabeled data for labeling, we use a 2-layer fully connected neural network to fit the
462   relationship between features and Shapley values of labeled training data. Then we use that neural
463   network to predict Shapley values of unlabeled training data and choose the data with the highest
464   predicted Shapley values to label. In this experiment, we choose three chemical datasets —Pol, Wind,
465   and CPU. We segment these datasets into two parts, 20 percent of them are used as labeled data,
466
      other 80 percent are used as unlabeled data. The unlabeled data will be taken out 8 times through
      the prediction of neural network. Figure 7 shows the performance of three KNN-Shapley-based
467
      data valuation approaches and three baselines of active learning (i.e., random sampling, entropy
468
      sampling (Holub et al., 2008), margin sampling (Balcan et al., 2007), uncertainty sampling (Nguyen
469   et al., 2022)) on three chemical datasets. Random sampling involves the completely random selection
470   of unlabeled samples for labeling; entropy sampling chooses samples with high prediction uncertainty,
471   measured by entropy; margin sampling focuses on the gap between the two highest probabilities in
472   the model’s predictions; Uncertainty sampling prioritizes annotation of samples with high nearest
473   neighbor inconsistency. From Figure 7, CKNN-Shapley outperforms other methods and is more
474   effective in identifying useful samples in the unlabeled data, extending the application context of
475   Shapley value and providing a new way to select data for active learning.

477   6                         C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this paper, we revealed the value inflation of KNN-Shapley, which not only misidentifies a large
480   portion of detrimental samples as beneficial, but also overestimates the value for the majority of
481   samples. To address these issues, we proposed Calibrated KNN-Shapley to calibrate zero as the
482   threshold for distinguishing detrimental samples from beneficial ones, via mitigating the negative
483   effects of small training subsets when calculating data valuation. Through extensive experiments, we
484   demonstrated the effectiveness of CKNN-Shapley in alleviating data valuation inflation and detecting
485
      detrimental samples. Furthermore, we extended our approach beyond conventional classification
      settings to the context of learning with mislabeled samples, online learning, and active learning.

738   C.3     G ENERALIZATION
739
      Traditional Shapley values have no constraint on the base classifier, but have to require retraining the
740
      base classifier 2N times. Therefore, even for a small dataset, 2N times model training is inflexible
741   for the traditional Shapley values. KNN-Shapley and its variants including our CKNN-Shapley
742   work a series of pragmatic tools computing Shapley values efficiently in a recursive manner with
743   O(N log N ) or O(N ) time complexity. For practice, if the base classifier is not KNN, a routine
744   solution employs the KNN classifier as a surrogate.
745
      To test the generalization of our CKNN-Shapley on different models, we conduct extra experiments
746
      with non-KNN classifiers, including Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), Logistic Regression (LR), and
747   Support Vector Machine (SVM). Specifically, we train the non-KNN classifiers with the whole
748   training set, apply KNN-Shapley variants to identify detrimental samples, and finally retrain the
749   non-KNN classifiers with the identified detrimental samples removed. Table 7 reports the accuracy on
750   three datasets. We can see that our CKNN-Shapley can effectively boost the performance of non-KNN
751   classifiers in most cases, indicating a good generalization of CKNN-Shapley across different models.

801
      Our research focuses on overcoming challenges associated with value inflation in the application of
      KNN-Shapley for data valuation influence estimation. By introducing a method that enables accurate
802
      assessment of training samples’ impact on model performance, we provide a tool for practitioners
803
      to determine the positive or negative effects of these samples. Through comprehensive testing
804   across various problem scenarios, our Shapley value removal strategy has been proven superior to
805   existing methods, enhancing model efficiency by eliminating harmful data points. Consequently, our
806   contributions have the potential to drive substantial societal benefits, particularly as the use of more
807   complex and expansive neural networks, like Large Language Models, becomes more prevalent. For
808   the limitations, we do not provide a theoretical analysis on the selection of T , where we posit that the
809   choice of T is contingent upon the dataset characteristic. To tackle this limitation, we recommend
      T = N − 2K as the default setting for its satisfactory performance on the datasets in our experiments.
