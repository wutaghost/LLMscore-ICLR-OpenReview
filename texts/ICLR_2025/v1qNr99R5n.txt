009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Adversarial prompts that can jailbreak large language models (LLMs) and lead
012            to undesirable behaviours pose a significant challenge to the safe deployment of
013            LLMs. Existing defenses, such as input perturbation and adversarial training, de-
014            pend on activating LLMs’ defense mechanisms or fine-tuning LLMs individually,
015            resulting in inconsistent performance across different prompts and LLMs. To ad-
016
               dress this, we propose CurvaLID, an algorithm that classifies benign and adversar-
               ial prompts by leveraging two complementary geometric measures: Local Intrin-
017
               sic Dimensionality (LID) and curvature. LID provides an analysis of geometric
018
               differences at the prompt level, while curvature captures the degree of curvature
019            in the manifolds and the semantic shifts at the word level. Together, these tools
020            capture both prompt-level and word-level geometric properties, enhancing adver-
021            sarial prompt detection. Unlike traditional approaches that rely on token-level
022            LID, we introduce PromptLID, which calculates LID at the prompt level to better
023            capture the geometric properties of text prompts within the adversarial subspace.
024            Additionally, we propose TextCurv to further analyze the local geometric struc-
025            ture of prompt manifolds by calculating the curvature in text prompts. CurvaLID
026            achieves over 0.99 detection accuracy, effectively reducing the attack success rate
027
               of advanced adversarial prompts to zero or nearly zero. Importantly, CurvaLID
               provides a unified detection framework across different adversarial prompts and
028
               LLMs, as it achieves consistent performance regardless of the specific LLM tar-
029
               geted.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The safety of Large Language Models (LLMs) has gained significant attention due to their deploy-
035   ment in many real-world applications. Research into adversarial prompts that threaten LLM safety
036   has grown, along with the development of defenses. Adversarial training and prompt engineering
037   have been adapted to defend LLMs. Input perturbation techniques, such as IntentionAnalysis (Zhang
038   et al., 2024a) and SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2023), depend on how the prompt appears after pertur-
039
      bation and whether the modified prompt can successfully trigger the self-defense mechanisms of the
      targeted LLM. The effectiveness of these techniques varies depending on the extent of the perturba-
040
      tion and the LLM being targeted. Adversarial training methods require fine-tuning of each model.
041
      Moreover, they often face limitations when scaling to larger models. For example, Latent Adversar-
042   ial Training (LAT) is limited to LLMs with fewer than 10 billion parameters (Sheshadri et al., 2024).
043   Both adversarial training and prompt engineering defenses are dependent on the targeted LLMs. As
044   a result, they fail to guarantee robust protection across different adversarial prompts and models.
045
      An alternative defense method is adversarial prompt detection, which is both efficient and practical.
046
      In this approach, practitioners can reject queries that contain adversarial prompts. Adversarial input
047
      detection has been extensively studied in the context of image classification tasks (Dong et al., 2020;
048   Machado et al., 2021; Bajaj & Vishwakarma, 2024; Liu et al., 2024). For instance, Ma et al. (2018)
049   demonstrated that Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) can effectively characterize an adversarial
050   subspace and enable efficient adversarial input detection. They leveraged image-level representation
051   vectors to estimate LID and distinguish adversarial examples from clean inputs. However, a funda-
052   mental difference in LLMs lies in the fact that inputs are represented as sequences of words. To the
053   best of our knowledge, no prior work has investigated LID in the context of adversarial prompt de-
      tection for LLMs. Yin et al. (2024) explored using LID to assess the truthfulness of LLM responses


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      by estimating LID with token-level representations. We demonstrate that token-level LID is ineffec-
055   tive for adversarial prompt detection because LID values for benign and adversarial prompts often
056   overlap significantly, primarily due to shared nearest neighbors being dominated by stop words and
057   punctuation. Even after removing stop words and punctuation, which reduces the standard devia-
058   tions, the average LID values for benign and adversarial prompts remain similar, making token-level
059   LID still ineffective for distinguishing between them.
060
      To address these limitations, we introduce CurvaLID, an algorithm designed to classify benign and
061   adversarial prompts by leveraging two complementary geometric measures: LID and curvature. In-
062   stead of using token-level representations, CurvaLID utilizes PromptLID, a prompt-level LID analy-
063   sis that quantifies and characterizes the distinct local subspace where adversarial prompts reside. We
064   train a simple convolutional neural network (CNN) on clean prompts to extract these prompt-level
065   representations, enabling the distinct LID of adversarial prompts to be identified during testing.
066   Unlike previous work (Ma et al., 2018), which relies on the specific victim model for detection,
067   CurvaLID detects adversarial prompts across different LLMs using a separate CNN for prompt rep-
068   resentation extraction. This independence from specific LLMs ensures broader applicability and
069
      allows CurvaLID to provide consistent and universal defense across different LLMs. Furthermore,
      we introduce TextCurv, a novel mathematical framework that captures the geometric properties of
070
      text prompts through curvature analysis, focusing on the degree of curvature in the manifolds and the
071
      semantic shifts at the word level. Together, PromptLID and TextCurv provide complementary in-
072   formation—PromptLID captures prompt-level geometric differences, while TextCurv reveals local
073   structural differences at the word level. While PromptLID achieves great accuracy, when combined
074   with TextCurv, CurvaLID surpasses 0.99 classification accuracy, significantly outperforming state-
075   of-the-art (SOTA) defenses on various adversarial prompts, including PAIR (Chao et al., 2023),
076   RandomSearch (Andriushchenko et al., 2024), and AmpleGCG (Liao & Sun, 2024). CurvaLID
077   consistently performs across all LLMs by detecting adversarial prompts independently, without de-
078   pending on specific model architectures as many SOTA defenses do. CurvaLID is highly efficient,
079   requiring only 15 minutes of training on a single Nvidia H100 GPU, compared to SOTA adversarial
080
      training methods that can take up to 16 hours on an 8-node A100 setup (Mazeika et al., 2024) or
      nearly 12 hours on a single A100 or H100 GPU (Sheshadri et al., 2024).
081
082   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
083
084          • We devise PromptLID, a prompt-level LID estimation method that enhances adversarial
085            prompt detection, and effectively addresses the limitations of conventional token-level LID
086            estimation. By leveraging prompt-level representations learned by a simple CNN, we can
087
               effectively capture the distinct characteristics of clean and adversarial prompts in their local
               intrinsic dimension.
088
089          • We propose TextCurv, a novel method for calculating curvature in text prompts. TextCurv
090            captures local geometric properties by focusing on the semantic shift between consecutive
091            words and the magnitude of this change. It effectively distinguishes between harmless and
092            adversarial prompts, providing a robust classification capability.
093          • We introduce CurvaLID that utilizes both PromptLID and TextCurv to distinguish benign
094            and adversarial prompts. Its performance is independent of the underlying LLM and offers
095            consistent and efficient adversarial detection.

098   2   R ELATED W ORK
099
100   This section reviews prior research on adversarial attacks and defense mechanisms for LLMs, high-
101   lighting their objectives, underlying logic, and key characteristics.
102   Adversarial attacks on LLM. Adversarial attacks on LLMs involve crafted inputs designed to ma-
103   nipulate models into generating harmful content, like offensive language or dangerous instructions
104   (Zou et al., 2023). These attacks range from a single input (zero-shot) to more complex, continu-
105   ous dialogue scenarios (multi-shot) (Shen et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). This
106   research focuses on zero-shot text prompt attacks, including techniques like text perturbation that
107   adds gibberish or subtly alters input wording and social-engineered prompts that trick LLMs into
      harmful behavior (Zou et al., 2023; Schwinn et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Adversarial defenses for LLM. There are three primary defenses against adversarial attacks on
109   LLMs: input preprocessing, prompt engineering, and adversarial training. Input preprocessing per-
110   turbs inputs to disrupt adversarial prompts but may also affect benign ones, with its effectiveness
111   varying depending on the extent of the perturbation and the targeted LLM(Cao et al., 2023; Robey
112   et al., 2023; Yung et al., 2024). Prompt engineering enhances the LLM’s self-defensive capabilities
113   by adding prompts that help expose harmful intent, though its effectiveness varies with different
114   models (Zhang et al., 2024a; Phute et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). Finally, adversarial training
115   strengthens the LLM’s ability to reject harmful prompts by exposing it to adversarial scenarios dur-
116   ing training (Xu et al., 2024b; Jain et al., 2023). However, this approach requires fine-tuning the
117
      LLM, making its success dependent on the specific model being protected, with most adversarial
      training methods confined to white-box models. Moreover, these methods generally exhibit varying
118
      effectiveness across different adversarial prompts and LLMs (Sheshadri et al., 2024; Ziegler et al.,
119
      2022; Ganguli et al., 2022). Note that our paper belongs to the field of adversarial prompt detec-
120   tion. In contrast to our approach, existing methods, such as perplexity filtering, rely on LLMs for
121   next-token probability predictions (Hu et al., 2023).

217
218   To characterize the prompt-level geometric properties of benign and adversarial prompts, we propose
219   PromptLID, an LID estimation based on prompt representations obtained from a trained CNN. We
220   first train a model g (CNN) to perform prompt classification. Classification involves learning a
221   function g : B → Q that maps the input space to the label space Q. In our setup, Q consists of
222   four distinct labels {q1 , q2 , q3 , q4 }, each corresponding to one of the four benign datasets we use,
223   namely Orca, MMLU, AlpacaEval, and TQA. For a benign prompt dataset B = {(b, q)i }ni=1 , where
224
      b is the benign prompt and q is the corresponding dataset label, the model aims to identify which
      benign dataset each prompt belongs to. The objective function used is categorical cross-entropy,
225
      as the task involves multi-class classification. The representation z1 , derived from the penultimate
226
      dense layer, encodes the prompt as a single vector, which is then used to calculate the PromptLID.
227   The PromptLID is calculated based on the MoM estimation (Amsaleg et al., 2015) of LID on the
228   prompts’ representation in z1 .

241   4.2   T EXT C URV: C URVATURE AT THE WORD - LEVEL
242
      To characterize the word-level geometric properties of benign and adversarial prompts, we analyze
243   the curvature of word connections. The aim is to have curvature complement PromptLID by ana-
244   lyzing the word-level geometric properties of prompts, effectively identifying nearly all adversarial
245   prompts. Specifically, curvature captures the relationships between words, revealing subtle semantic
246   shifts based on word order, and uncovering local geometric differences between benign and adver-
247   sarial prompts.
248
      Previous research indicates that CNN activation creates a curved manifold, evidenced by the sig-
249   nificantly higher intrinsic dimension estimated by Principal Component Analysis (PCA) compared
250   to the GID estimated by TwoNN on activation data (Abdi & Williams, 2010; Facco et al., 2017;
251   Ansuini et al., 2019). However, there is a lack of findings on the curvature of benign versus adver-
252   sarial prompts. Therefore, we investigate the curvature differences between these prompts in CNN
253   convolution layers as potential classification features.
254
      Our goal is to establish a definition of text curvature based on existing mathematical definitions, with
255   the curvature capturing semantic shifts according to word sequence and the strength of these shifts.
256   Word sequence plays a crucial role in semantic analysis, helping to accurately capture the local
257   geometric properties of prompts. We focus on the representations of prompts in the convolutional
258   layers of the model g as mentioned in Section 4.1, where the prompt data remains unflattened and in
259   stacked lists of vectors at this stage, which can be viewed as word-level representation. Specifically,
260   we extract the representations z2 and z3 from these convolutional layers for further analysis. This
261   stage is critical, as it is where feature spaces are curved, according to prior research (Ansuini et al.,
262   2019).
263   To capture semantic shifts between consecutive words in a prompt, we draw on Whewell’s equation,
264   where the rate of directional change of a curve is represented by the tangential angular change.
265   In NLP, this angular change is connected to the dot-product formula and cosine similarity, which
266   indicate the semantic similarity or difference between two words (Mikolov, 2013; Levy et al., 2015).
267   We assume that this theory also applies to modern word embeddings like GPT-2 and RoBERTa, and
268   therefore, we define the rate of angular change in text curvature accordingly:

      4.3   C URVA LID: A DVERSARIAL P ROMPT C LASSIFICATION BY C URVATURE AND LID
317
318   CurvaLID classifies and filters adversarial prompts before they reach LLMs, thus ensuring their
319   security. Since CurvaLID operates independently of LLMs, it provides a unified defensive perfor-
320   mance across all LLMs. This differentiates it from existing SOTA defenses like input perturbation,
321   prompt engineering, and adversarial training, which show varying performance across different ad-
322   versarial prompts and LLMs. Moreover, CurvaLID’s evaluation is straightforward and standardized,
323   avoiding the need for subjective human assessments or reliance on LLM judgments, which can raise
      robustness concerns (Chen et al., 2024; Raina et al., 2024).


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      CurvaLID involves four steps (see Figure 1, pseudo code in Appendix A.1.1). In the first step,
325   we use our trained model g (defined in Section 4.1) to classify different types of benign prompts,
326   deriving the normal feature manifold. This is essential for amplifying the geometric and dimensional
327   distinctions between benign and adversarial prompts in subsequent steps. In the second step, we
328   extract the representations of both benign and adversarial prompts from z1 , z2 , and z3 . In the third
329   step, we compute PromptLID and TextCurv. Then in the final step, we train a Multilayer Perceptron
330   (MLP) to classify benign and adversarial prompts using the two mean TextCurv values, and the
331   PromptLID as inputs. The MLP performs binary classification and filters out adversarial prompts
332   before they reach to LLMs. Details on the MLP architecture are included in Appendix A.1.6.

      5.2     A NALYSIS OF LOCAL AND GLOBAL ID ON BENIGN AND ADVERSARIAL PROMPTS
425
      This subsection addresses the limitations associated with using token-level LID, for classifying be-
426   nign and adversarial prompts. Adversarial prompts manipulate rarely encountered areas of the fea-
427   ture space using complex words and irregular combinations (Wallace et al., 2019; Ilyas et al., 2019;
428   Ren et al., 2019). Existing work has shown that adversarial perturbation leads the representation
429   to a subspace with distinct local dimensional properties (high LID) (Ma et al., 2018). As a result,
430   we hypothesize that each word in adversarial prompts could lead to a representation with high LID.
431   By utilizing token-level representations and aggregating the LIDs for each prompt by taking the
      average, it is possible to detect adversarial prompts.

517
      We analyzed the average TextCurv of adversarial prompts in CurvaLID Step 2. To ensure TextCurv
518
      is independent of the embedding model, we conducted curvature analysis using various embeddings,
519   including GPT-2, BERT, XLNet, and DistilBERT (Radford et al., 2019; Devlin, 2018; Yang, 2019;
520   Sanh, 2019). As shown in Table 4, adversarial prompts consistently showed at least 30% higher
521   curvature than benign prompts across all embeddings. This result supports our hypothesis that words
522   in adversarial prompts tend to be more irregular and complex than those in benign prompts.
523
      We also demonstrated that CNN activation significantly amplifies the TextCurv differences between
524
      benign and adversarial prompts. Specifically, the mean TextCurv of adversarial prompts is at least
525   30% higher than that of benign prompts in the representations of both CNN layers. In contrast,
526   when considering only the word embedding, the TextCurv for benign prompts based solely on word
527   embedding is 4.91, compared to 5.42 for adversarial prompts. The difference in TextCurv is much
528   smaller—just 13%, which is less than half of the difference observed in the CNN layers.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce CurvaLID, a framework that uses LID and curvature to distinguish be-
533
      nign and adversarial prompts. CurvaLID achieves over 0.99 accuracy and reduces the ASR of the
534   tested adversarial prompts to near zero. Unlike conventional SOTA defenses, such as prompt en-
535   gineering and input perturbation, CurvaLID operates independently of LLMs, providing consistent
536   performance across all models. We also demonstrate the limitations of word-level LID due to stop
537   words and punctuation. We address this by introducing PromptLID and TextCurv, which respec-
538   tively explore the geometric properties of prompts at the prompt-level and word-level in text data.
539   Future directions for this work include expanding CurvaLID to spam detection, adding a human
      feedback system to CurvaLID for optimisation, and testing CuravLID with noisy prompts.

992

993

997    as:
998                                              ∆s = ∥⃗u − ⃗v ∥.

1000
       their semantic significance as encoded by the vector magnitudes.
1001
1002   2. Semantic Weight and Embedding Norms
1003   In NLP, the norm of a word embedding, ∥⃗u∥, encodes the semantic ”weight” or importance of a
1004   word within its context Schakel & Wilson (2015); Reif et al. (2019). Larger norms indicate that the
1005   embedding carries more semantic information, while smaller norms suggest less significance.
1006
       For two consecutive embeddings, ⃗u and ⃗v , their combined semantic importance is proportional to
1007
       their norms:
1008

1035   A.1.3   T IME AND SPACE COMPLEXITY OF C URVA LID
1036   The time complexity for PromptLID is O(np), where n is the number of prompts and p is the
1037   dimensionality of the prompt embeddings. In our implementation, we use the representation layer
1038   of the CNN to obtain the embeddings. For TextCurv, the time complexity is O(nmd), where m
1039   is the number of words in a prompt and d is the word embedding dimensionality. In our case,
1040   we use RoBERTa embeddings for word representations. Therefore, the overall time complexity is
1041   O(n(p + md)).
1042   The space complexity for PromptLID is O(np), as we store n prompts, each with p dimensions. For
1043   TextCurv, the space complexity is O(nz), where z represents the dimensionality of the trained CNN
1044   layers used in our computations. Consequently, the space complexity is O(n(p + z)).
