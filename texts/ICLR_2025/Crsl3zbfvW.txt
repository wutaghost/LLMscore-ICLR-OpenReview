011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning (RL) has enabled robots to develop complex skills, but
014            its success in image-based tasks often depends on effective representation learn-
015            ing. Prior works have primarily focused on 2D representations, often overlooking
016
               the inherent 3D geometric structure of the world, or have attempted to learn 3D
               representations that require extensive resources such as synchronized multi-view
017
               images even during deployment. To address these issues, we propose a novel
018
               RL framework that extracts 3D-aware representations from single-view RGB in-
019            put, without requiring camera pose or synchronized multi-view images during
020            the downstream RL. Our method employs an autoencoder architecture, using a
021            masked ViT as the encoder and a latent-conditioned NeRF as the decoder, trained
022            with cross-view completion to capture fine-grained, 3D geometry-aware represen-
023            tations. Additionally, we utilize a time contrastive loss that further regularizes the
024            learned representation for consistency across different viewpoints. Our method
025            significantly enhances the RL agent’s performance in complex tasks, demonstrat-
026            ing superior effectiveness compared to prior 3D-aware representation-based meth-
027
               ods, even when using only single-view RGB images during deployment.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reinforcement learning (RL) has empowered an embodied agent such as a robot to acquire com-
033
      plex skills. However, its capability heavily depends on the representation of the underlying systems,
      especially in the image domain. In other words, central to image-based RL is the challenge of repre-
034
      sentation learning, where the goal is to distill high-dimensional visual data into compact, informative
035
      features that capture the essence of the environment. Effective representation learning schemes for
036   image-based RL enable agents to interpret and act upon visual data more efficiently, facilitating
037   faster convergence and improving performance in tasks such as robotic manipulation.
038
039
      Many previous works have focused on learning efficient representation from visual inputs for down-
      stream RL tasks. These can be roughly categorized into several approaches: pre-training an image
040
      encoder via contrastive objectives (Laskin et al., 2020; Nair et al., 2022), employing data augmen-
041
      tations (Yarats et al., 2021a;c), using autoencoders for reconstruction (Seo et al., 2023a; Xiao et al.,
042   2022), and leveraging in-the-wild internet-scale datasets (Ma et al., 2022; Ghosh et al., 2023). While
043   these methods are effective and widely utilized, they typically treat visual inputs as 2D grids, over-
044   looking the structured 3D geometric information inherently present in the 3D world. Such a lack of
045   3D awareness forces the embodied agent to rely on view-specific features such as surface-level pixel
046   patterns or 2D shapes that are unique to the particular perspective, hindering its ability to adapt to
047   different viewpoints or address occlusion. Furthermore, this limitation compels the policy network
048   to implicitly infer 3D actions from 2D visual inputs (2D-to-3D mapping), rather than leveraging
049   3D-aware representations that are better suited for mapping directly to 3D actions (3D-to-3D map-
050
      ping). Therefore, learning 3D-aware representations from 2D image inputs is crucial for achieving
      superior task performance, particularly when precise 3D spatial information inference is critical.
051
052   Recent approaches have attempted to learn 3D representation (Ke et al., 2024; Gervet et al., 2023;
053   Goyal et al., 2024). However, these methods typically require not only expert demonstration data
      but also calibrated cameras for accurate depth projection during deployment. Other prior works have

      multi-view images into a single latent feature and providing it with a volume rendering network in

      neural radiance field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) to reconstruct the 3D world. Despite these
070
      advancements, they often rely on the object-level mask or still require synchronized multi-view
071   images along with camera pose information even during downstream RL. All of these constraints can
072   be a significant burden in real-world robotics applications, where calibration and multi-view setups
073   are impractical. To overcome these challenges, a single-view 3D-aware representation inference
074   framework that relies solely on RGB images is necessary. It is particularly beneficial in practical
075   situations where multiple calibrated cameras are available during pre-training, but the robot has to
076   rely on just single-view RGB images without a camera pose to perform the task during downstream
077   RL deployment.
078   To develop such an effective 3D-aware representation, we propose a Single-view 3D-aware repre-
079   sentation inference framework for RL by performing Cross-view completion via NeRF (SinCro).
080   Specifically, it adopts an autoencoder structure, trained only with RGB supervision, and consists of
081   two stages: (1) pre-training a masked ViT-based (Dosovitskiy, 2020) 3D scene encoder through a
082   latent-conditioned NeRF decoder, and (2) deploying only the pre-trained 3D scene encoder in down-
083   stream RL tasks. The encoder utilizes a pixel masking strategy (He et al., 2022) with cross-view
084   completion (Weinzaepfel et al., 2022) for 3D geometry-aware representation, and the NeRF decoder
085
      leverages multi-view reconstruction with a custom ray sampling strategy to capture inherent, essen-
      tial 3D information of the environment and fine-grained details crucial for downstream robotic tasks.
086
      Additionally, we further regularize the intermediate representation to ensure consistency across dif-
087
      ferent viewpoints by applying a time contrastive loss (Sermanet et al., 2018). Combining all of
088   the proposed components for enhanced 3D-aware representation, our method outperforms the prior
089   works in downstream RL. Further ablation studies and analyses validate that the proposed method
090   is crucial to perform the single-view inference successfully and enables us to obtain representations
091   that are view-invariant and robust to the viewpoint changes.
092
      In summary, this work has the following key contributions:
093
094          • We present SinCro, a 3D-aware representation-based RL framework. It can extract 3D-
095            aware representation only with single-view RGB images during the downstream RL.
096
             • The proposed method learns 3D geometry-aware and view-invariant representations of the
097            scene by leveraging a NeRF-based cross-view completion and contrastive learning.
098
             • The proposed method achieves superior downstream RL results, and we qualitatively
099
               demonstrate that the learned representation provides an implicit understanding of the 3D
100
               world.

107   Prior works have been proposed to develop an efficient, effective representation learning strategy
      for RL in the image domain. Some prior approaches formulate representation learning as encoder

      pre-training via auxiliary learning objectives (Yarats et al., 2021b; Liu & Abbeel, 2021a;b), self-
109   supervised reconstruction (Nair et al., 2018; Seo et al., 2023a; Xiao et al., 2022), masked image

119   2.2   3D S CENE R EPRESENTATION
120
      Building on recent progress in robot learning and computer vision, several approaches have emerged
121   that leverage multiple cameras to capture multi-view images for vision-based control (Sermanet
122   et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2022; Guhur et al., 2023; Jangir et al., 2022). While most
123   of them directly utilize multi-view images as inputs, they do not perform explicit reconstruction of
124   the 3D world, which is crucial for 3D understanding. Some prior works have attempted to explicitly
125   model the 3D space (Ke et al., 2024; Shridhar et al., 2023; Gervet et al., 2023; Goyal et al., 2024;
126   Qian et al., 2024; Ze et al., 2024), but they require calibrated cameras to get depth images and project
127   the queried pixel into a 3D space during deployment. Other prior works propose to learn implicit
128   3D-aware representation (Driess et al., 2022; Shim et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022) by reconstructing
129
      the 3D world via neural radiance field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021). However, multiple cameras
      are still required in these methods to infer the 3D-aware representation and downstream behavioral
130
      learning. Furthermore, some of them even require semantic masks for all pixels in the image for
131
      semantic (Shim et al., 2023) or object-level reconstruction (Driess et al., 2022). In this work, we
132   propose a NeRF-based cross-view completion for 3D scene representation learning, trained only
133   with RGB supervision. It allows the inference of 3D scene representation using just single-view
134   images, eliminating the need for calibrated cameras during the downstream RL.

175   NeRF model that learns 3D geometry-aware scene representations. In section 4.2, time contrastive

305   5     E XPERIMENT
306
307   Following the prior work (Shim et al., 2023), we pre-train and evaluate the proposed method for
308   each environment in the Meta-world (Yu et al., 2020), where the robot should perform manipulation
309   tasks with randomly initialized object-of-interest. However, we modified the environment to make
310   it look more realistic and rich in texture compared to the prior work. For the dataset, we recorded
311   episodic videos from six distinct viewpoints (N = 6). We used two reference images (K = 2)
312   during NeRF pre-training. However, with additional computational resources, both N and K could
313
      be expanded. More details about the environments and datasets are included in Appendix A.
314   To evaluate the learned representation’s effectiveness in downstream RL, we compare our method
315   with some prior 3D-aware representation-based RL methods, which can be summarized as follows:
316
      NeRF-RL (Driess et al., 2022) – it performs NeRF-based 3D reconstruction by leveraging object
317   masks for object-level reconstruction, which are required both in pre-training and deployment.
318
319   SNeRL (Shim et al., 2023) – it performs NeRF-based 3D reconstruction while distilling the feature
320
      field of DINO (Caron et al., 2021) and semantic labels of each pixel into the latent representation.
321   3D-NSR (Li et al., 2022) – it learns 3D Neural Scene Representations by performing self-
322   reconstruction of multi-view images via NeRF while enforcing time contrastive loss for view-
323   invariancy. SNeRL, NeRF-RL, and 3D-NSR require camera pose information and synchronized
      images from multiple viewpoints during the downstream RL process.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we considered a 3D-aware representation-based RL framework where the agent should
526   learn how to perform the given task from multiple viewpoints. We proposed SinCro, which can
527   extract 3D geometry-aware representation while enabling a single-view inference without synchro-
528   nized calibrated cameras during deployment. We have shown that the proposed method outperforms
529
      the baselines in qualitative and quantitative ways. However, SinCro still has some limitations. For
      example, we have experimented with a small number of camera viewpoints due to the huge require-
530
      ments of the computational resources, but access to more camera viewpoints will enable us to obtain
531
      more fine-grained details and perform more viewpoint-robust control. Also, the proposed method
532   still requires synchronized and calibrated cameras during the NeRF pre-training, which might hin-
533   der real-world application if we want it to be used with dozens of cameras. An interesting future
534   research direction could involve using multiple videos captured from moving cameras with varying
535   viewpoints in place of the current prerequisites.

721   A.2   S IN C RO I MPLEMENTATIONS
722
723   We used NVIDIA A6000 and AMD EPYC 9274F for encoder pre-training, and NVIDIA A5000
724   and AMD Ryzen Threadripper 3960X for RL training. Each encoder pre-training took 1 day and
725   each RL experiment took 2∼3 days for RL training.
726
727   Dataset for pre-training NeRF. Following the prior work (Shim et al., 2023), the dataset for
728   each environment consists of 14400 scenes (120 episodes x 120 timesteps). Half of the dataset is
729   collected by using the scripted policy (suboptimal) provided by Meta-world (Yu et al., 2020) and the
730
      other half is collected by random action. We record images from 6 different viewpoints located in
      the hemisphere with a radius of 0.6 m, centered around the environment. All cameras are set up to
731
      look at the center of the hemisphere. The size of observed images from each camera is 128 × 128.
732
733
      Encoder pre-training details in Section 4. The hyperparameters for encoder pre-training are
734
      presented in Table 2. We conducted the encoder pre-training for a total of 300K iterations with a
735
      batch size of 8. Each data in the batch includes a pair of images (e.g. one for the primary image,
736   two for the reference images, and four for the reconstruction) from a specific episode and timestep.
737   During the first 2000 training iterations, we sampled rays from center-cropped image regions with
738   size 64 × 64 to facilitate initial training by focusing on rich texture regions.
739
      For each training iteration, we randomly sample an episode, a timestep, and three non-duplicated
740
      camera viewpoints from a dataset, then concatenate three temporally consecutive images from each
741                                                                      i
      viewpoint. One of these images serves as the primary input (Ot−2:t     ) and the remaining two view-
742                                r1       r2                             i      r1       r2
      points serve as references (Ot−2:t , Ot−2:t ). The image patches (Pt−2:t , Pt−2:t , Pt−2:t ) are embedded
743
      into 256 dimensions before entering the image encoder. Each ViT block in the image encoder Eθ
744   consists of multi-head self-attention and an MLP layer, and then layer normalization is applied at
745   the end of the image encoder.
746
747
      Both the primary input and reference patches are processed by a fully connected layer, before con-
      catenating mask tokens to the primary input patches from the image encoder. Then, the concate-
748
      nated inputs and reference patches are fed into transformer blocks in the state encoder Sθ , followed
749
      by layer normalization for each view. To obtain the state features for the primary input and refer-
750   ences, vti , vtr1 , vtr2 , the latest timestep patches for each view are concatenated and embedded into
751   256 dimensions via a fully connected layer. The scene representation zt is subsequently generated
752   by averaging the state features, followed by processing through a two-layer MLP and L2 normaliza-
753   tion. Then, a latent-conditioned NeRF Fθ (x, d, zt ) utilizes this scene representation as an additional
754   input. Specifically, building upon the vanilla NeRF architecture (Mildenhall et al., 2021), the scene
755   representation zt is concatenated with the high-frequency embedded position x both in the first and
      fifth MLP layers.

      B.1     3D RECONSTRUCTION
883   This section presents additional reconstruction results across various environments. As shown in
884   Figure 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, SinCro shows consistent qualitative and qualitative results compared to
885   the baselines, particularly outperforming in the single-view inference.
886
      In the multi-view input case, SinCro clearly captures small objects, such as the window handle in the
887
      window environment, the green cube in the push environment, or the hammerhead in the hammer en-
888
      vironment, whereas all baselines struggle to render these parts. Despite using only three viewpoints
889   (one primary input and two references), SinCro achieves competitive quantitative results compared
890   to the baselines, which are provided with six viewpoints. Although the baselines show comparable
891   PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS to SinCro in the multi-view input case, this is essentially attributed to the
892   baselines prioritizing the accurate reconstruction of non-salient parts of the image, which are not
893   crucial for downstream tasks, and their RL performances are inferior to SinCro since capturing tiny
894   objects in the scene is crucial for downstream RL tasks. Since our method is designed to take a
895   handful of reference images rather than relying on multi-view images from densely located cam-
896   eras, it requires significantly fewer viewpoints compared to the baselines. Therefore, the efficiency
897
      becomes even more pronounced with access to datasets from more diverse viewpoints, making the
      proposed method highly advantageous for memory efficiency and practical application in robotics.
898
899   In the single-view input case, SinCro outperforms the baselines both qualitatively and quantita-
900   tively. Moreover, our approach demonstrates consistent qualitative and quantitative results similar
901   to those in the multi-view input case. In contrast, baselines experience significant rendering qual-
902   ity degradations in most environments, showing incomplete reconstruction, severe noise, jittering,
903
      teleportation, and confusion in distinguishing each different scene, leading to rapid degradations in
      PSNR and SSIM, and increases in LPIPS. These results are illustrated in the our supplementary
904
      video. Notably, 3D-NSR, which leverages time contrastive learning between image features, expe-
905
      riences less quantitative degradation than SNeRL and NeRF-RL. This suggests that time contrastive
906   loss plays a key role in promoting view-invariancy in scene representations, encouraging them to be
907   less sensitive to the number of viewpoints.

       B.3   ROBUSTNESS TO V IEWPOINT C HANGE
1135
1136   Computing optimal transport matrix. A comparison of the optimal transport (OT) matrix be-
1137   tween SinCro and baselines is presented in Figure 15. To compute the OT matrix, we utilize two
1138   observations of a task-solving trajectory from a default camera setting used in encoder pre-training
1139   and perturbed camera viewpoints. The perturbed camera continuously rotates around the default
1140   camera pose with maximum perturbations of five degrees in both azimuth and elevation. We collect
1141   all scene representations throughout the episode from each observation, then compare the two dis-
1142
       tributions using the equations provided in (Haldar et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023). Due to the definition
       of the optimal transport, if the two distributions are similar, a scene representation zt′ of the observa-
1143
       tion from the perturbed viewpoints will be transported to a representation zt of the observation with
1144
       the same timestep t obtained from the default camera pose. That is, bright colors (high values) will
1145   be aligned on the diagonal of the OT matrix, indicating strong correspondences between the two
1146   trajectories. Therefore, we can implicitly validate that the proposed method is robust to viewpoint
1147   perturbations as the scene representations are still aligned on the diagonal despite the perturbations.
1148   Compared to the baselines, SinCro demonstrates clear alignments of scene representations between
1149   the two observations. It indicates that the proposed method possesses 3D geometry-aware represen-
1150   tations, enabling the agent to be robust in camera perturbations.
