                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               We present ElastoGen, a knowledge-driven AI model that generates physically ac-
010
               curate 4D elastodynamics. Unlike deep models that learn from video- or image-
011            based observations, ElastoGen leverages the principles of physics and learns from
012            established mathematical and optimization procedures. The core idea of Elasto-
013            Gen is converting the differential equation, corresponding to the nonlinear force
014            equilibrium, into a series of iterative local convolution-like operations, which nat-
015            urally fit deep architectures. We carefully build our network module following
016            this overarching design philosophy. ElastoGen is much more lightweight in terms
017            of both training requirements and network scale than deep generative models. Be-
018            cause of its alignment with actual physical procedures, ElastoGen efficiently gen-
019
               erates accurate dynamics for a wide range of hyperelastic materials and can be
               easily integrated with upstream and downstream deep modules to enable end-to-
020
               end 4D generation.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Recent advancements in generative models have enhanced the ability to produce high-quality dig-
026   ital contents across diverse media formats (e.g. images, videos, 3D models, 4D data). In partic-
027   ular, the generation of 4D data, including both spatial and temporal dimensions, has seen notable
028   progress (Singer et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Ling et al., 2023; Bahmani et al.,
029   2024a; Yin et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2024b).
030
      On the other hand, learning physical dynamics that exhibit temporal consistency and adhere to phys-
031   ical laws from observable data remains a difficult problem. Data are in the wild and noisy. Their
032   underlying coherence is agnostic to the user. As a result, existing deep models have to assume some
033   distributions of the data, which may not be the case in reality. In theory, the network would extract
034   any knowledge provided sufficient data. In practice however, such data-based learning becomes
035   more and more cumbersome with increased dimensionality of generated contents – it is unintu-
036   itive to define the right network structure to guide a physically meaningful generation; it requires
037   terabyte- or petabyte-scale high-quality training data, and center-level computing resource to facili-
038   tate the training. Those theoretical and practical obstacles combined impose significant challenges.
039   We explore a new way to establish physics-in-the-loop generative models. Our argument is that
040   learning from knowledge instead of from raw data is more effective for generative models. Physical
041   laws and principles are often in the form of partial differential equations (PDEs) and numerically
042   solved with discretized differential operators. We note that those operators hold a similar struc-
043   ture as a convolution kernel on the problem domain, where the values of those convolution kernels
044   depend on the specific problem setting. Inspired by those observations, we propose ElastoGen, a
045
      knowledge-driven neural model that generates physically accurate and coherent 4D elastodynamics.
      ElasoGen can be easily coupled and integrated with upstream and downstream neural modules to
046
      enable end-to-end 4D generation. The core idea of ElastoGen is converting the global differential
047
      operator, corresponding to the nonlinear force equilibrium, to iterative local convolution-like proce-
048   dures. Such knowledge-level priors allow us to design dedicated network modules for ElastoGen,
049   where each network module has a well-defined purpose of relaxing locally concentrated strain rather
050   than being treated as a piece of a “black box”. Compared with other data-learning-based generative
051   models, ElasoGen is lightweight – in terms of both training requirements and the network scales.
052   Furthermore, due to its consistency with physics procedure, ElastoGen generates physically accurate
053   dynamics for a wide range of hyperelastic materials. Specifically, we summarize some features of
      ElastoGen as follows:

070   Generative models The primary objective of generative models is to produce new, high-quality sam-
071   ples from vast datasets. These models are designed to learn and understand the distribution of data,
072   thereby generating samples that meet specific criteria. Techniques such as Generative Adversarial
073   Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling,
074   2014), and flow-based methods (Dinh et al., 2015; 2017) have all demonstrated significant success.
075   However, each method has its limitations. For instance, GANs can generate high-quality images
076
      but are notoriously difficult to train and optimize (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Gulrajani et al., 2017;
      Mescheder, 2018). VAEs (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020; Child, 2021) and flow-based methods (Kingma &
077
      Dhariwal, 2018) offer efficient training processes but generally fall short in sample quality compared
078
      to GANs. Recently, diffusion models have emerged as another powerful technique, achieving state-
079   of-the-art results in generating high-fidelity images (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020;
080   Rombach et al., 2022), setting the stage for further explorations in more complex applications.
081
082
      4D generation based on diffusion models As research on diffusion models advances, these meth-
      ods could potentially be applied to the generation of 3D content (Jain et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023;
083
      Metzer et al., 2023; Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023; 2024), video con-
084
      tent (Blattmann et al., 2023; Harvey et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022b;a; Karras et al., 2023; Ni et al.,
085   2023), and more complex forms such as 3D videos or what might be termed 4D scenes (Singer
086   et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Ling et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2024a; Yin et al.,
087   2023; Bahmani et al., 2024b). These advanced applications demonstrate the versatility and expand-
088   ing potential of diffusion models across diverse media formats. However, existing video generation
089   techniques struggle to ensure temporal consistency and require substantial training data, underscor-
090   ing the challenges of capturing and replicating the dynamic and interconnected behaviors present in
091   real-world scenarios within a generative model framework.
092   Neural physical synamics Physical dynamics traditionally relies on numerical solutions such as
093   the finite element method (FEM) (Zienkiewicz & Morice, 1971; Zienkiewicz et al., 2005; Huebner
094   et al., 2001; Reddy, 1993), finite difference method (Zhu et al., 2010; Godunov & Bohachevsky,
095   1959), or mass-spring systems (Liu et al., 2013). Each approach offers distinct advantages and
096   limitations. For example, Position-Based Dynamics (PBD) (Müller et al., 2007) and Projective
097   Dynamics (PD) (Bouaziz et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2013) offer simplified implementation and faster
098   convergence but can struggle with complex material behaviors and do not always guarantee consis-
099
      tent convergence rates. Recently, neural physics solvers, which integrate neural networks with tradi-
      tional solvers, aim to accelerate and simplify the computation process. The pioneering works (Chang
100
      et al., 2017; Battaglia et al., 2016) directly utilized neural networks to predict dynamics, achieving
101
      promising results in simple particle systems. Subsequent studies (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2018;
102   Kipf et al., 2018; Ajay et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019c;a;b) adopted network architectures to the specific
103   features of the systems, thereby enhancing performance. The advent of Physics Informed Neural
104   Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019; Pakravan et al., 2021) marks a leap forward. These networks
105   incorporate extensive physical information to constrain and guide the learning process, ensuring that
106   predictions adhere more closely to physical laws and has succeeded in domains such as cloths (Geng
107   et al., 2020) and fluids (Um et al., 2020; Gibou et al., 2019; Chu et al., 2022). Some work (Yang
      et al., 2020) shifts away from end-to-end structures and use neural networks to optimize part of the

      4.1        M ETHOD OVERVIEW: PIECE - WISE LOCAL QUADRATIC APPROXIMATION
181
182
      Our elastodynamic generation mimics numerical optimization procedures that minimize the varia-
183   tional energy of equation 1. It is possible to tackle this problem at the global level, i.e., optimizing
184   all the degrees of freedom (DoFs) of the system at once e.g., using Newton’s method. Such a brute-
185   force scheme requires to learn dense inter-correlations among features at all DoFs, which inevitably
186   leads to complex and large-scale network architectures with numerous parameters to be learned.
187
      Alternatively, we opt for a divide-and-conquer way to approach equation 1. We consider        P the total
188
      potential energy U as the summation of multiple energies of quadratic form: U (q) ≈ i Ei (qi ),
189                                                   2
      where Ei (q) = minpi ∈Mi ω2i ∥Gi [qi ] − pi ∥ . Here, i indicates the i-th sub-volume of the object.
190
      For instance, one may discretize the object into a tetrahedral mesh, and Ei then represents the elastic
191
      potential stored at the i-th element. Gi denotes a discrete differential operator, which converts
192   positional features qi to strain-level features. To this end, we build Gi such that Gi [qi ] = vec(Fi ),
193   i.e., the vectorized deformation gradient (F ∈ R3×3 ) of the sub-volume, which gives the local first-
194   order approximation of the displacement field. The constraint manifold Mi denotes the zero level
195   set of Ei . In other words, we consider Ei as a quadratic energy based on how far local displacement
196   qi is from its closest energy-free configuration (pi ), given the local material stiffness ωi .
197                                                                                                                                                        2
      Provided the current deformed shape qi , we can find arg minpi ω2i ∥Gi [qi ] − pi ∥ , which suggests
198
      a locally optimal descent direction to reduce U . The global displacement can then be obtained by
199
      minimizing q over Ei at all the sub-volumes. While this is a global operation that we would like
200   to avoid, it is essentially a Laplacian-like smoothing operator, which can still be processed with
201   repeated local smoothing. This procedure share a similar nature of shape matching method (Müller
202   et al., 2005) and PD (Bouaziz et al., 2014) — it offers a piece-wise SQP way (Boggs & Tolle,
203   1995) to approximate U locally. ElastoGen functions like a neural version of the aforementioned
204   procedure with a nested RNN structure. It handles local solve, or strain relaxation in the form of a
205   volume convolution so that the overall network structure is compact and lightweight.
206   Unfortunately, real-world materials are more than a collection of quadratic forms. The appropriate
207   Mi nonlinearly vary under different deformation or material models, aka material nonlinearity. As
208   a result, shape matching or PD can only handle simplified material behavior unless we know how
209   Mi changes along the generation. To this end, we augment ElastoGen with a NeuralMTL module
210   to make sure each local SQP matches actual materials.

      could generate. By encoding the input rhs of equation 10 into a low-dimension latent space of
337   low-frequency deformations such as body-wise bending, twisting, or rotation, RNN-2 only needs to
338   handle the remaining residual strains, which are often condensed locally. Determining the subspace
339   encoding involves performing an SVD on the global matrix. Since our objects are rasterized, we use
340   a rasterization grid as a general-purpose subspace. Each latent mode is visually similar to a gentle
341   sine or cone wave e.g., see figure 2.

526   6        C ONCLUSION
527
528   ElastoGen is a knowledge-driven deep model that embeds physical principles and numerical proce-
529   dures into the network design. As a result, EasltoGen is surprisingly lightweight and compact. Each
530   module is tailored for a well-defined computational task for minimizing the total variational energy.
531   This design allows for decoupled training, eliminating the need for large-scale training datasets. The
532   accuracy of ElasoGen can be easily controlled by NeuralMTL which predicts the current strain from
533   observed numerical computations.
534   ElastoGen also has limitations. The current version of ElastoGen lacks the support for collisions.
535   It becomes less efficient for thin geometry as many convolution operations is applied on empty
536   voxels. ElastoGen may fail to converge with extremely stiff materials like a near-rigid object. In the
537   future, we plan to keep enhancing the scope of ElastoGen e.g., by integrating dynamics for more
538   physical phenomena such as fluids, granular materials and plasticity, adding collision support, and
539   automating the setting of physical parameters to ultimately achieve the goal of generating real-world
      dynamics with mouse clicks.

816   A.2    D IFFUSION NETWORK D
817
818   The goal is to train a diffusion network D to generate the weights W of a corresponding NeuralMTL
819   model N , given the material parameters {e, ν}. Here, W denotes the weights of N , and the process
820
      is formulated as a conditional diffusion problem guided by {e, ν}, such that W = D(e, ν).
821   To this end, we first construct a dataset consisting of 1000 paired samples of {e, ν} and W, as
822   described in § 4.3. Following the approach of Wang et al. (2024a), we utilize Latent Diffusion
823   Models (LDM, Rombach et al. (2022)) to generate W, as our preliminary experiments showed that
824   directly learning W led to suboptimal performance. To address this, we train an autoencoder to
825   map the network weights W to a 256-dimensional latent vector, in which the diffusion process is
826
      performed.
827   When training the diffusion model, the autoencoder remains fixed, serving solely to encode W
828   into its latent representation l. At each diffusion timestep t, we introduce noise ϵt to l, resulting
829   in lt = l + ϵt . The objective is to train a noise prediction model, ϵθ (lt , t; e, ν), to estimate the
830   noise ϵt at each timestep t, as described in § 3.2. During inference, we begin with random noise
831   and progressively remove noise from it using the noise prediction model ϵθ , guided by the material
832
      parameters e, ν. This iterative denoising process produces a 256-dimensional latent vector, which is
      subsequently passed through the decoder to generate the corresponding network weights W.
833
834   We train the autoencoder using a learning rate of 1 × 10−3 and the diffusion model with a learning
835   rate of 1 × 10−4 . Both models are trained for 1000 epochs with a batch size of 64. The architecture
836   of the autoencoder and diffusion model is detailed in table 3. Note that in diffusion process the
837   256-dimensional latent vector is viewed as a 1-channel 16 × 16 image.
