009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The efficiency of Bayesian optimization (BO) relies on careful selection of the sur-
012            rogate model to balance exploration and exploitation under limited budget. Tra-
013            ditional BO methods often struggle with sub-optimal kernel choices when using
014            Gaussian processes (GPs) as the surrogate model. When the kernel is inadequately
015            chosen, BO may converge slowly or even get stuck at an undesired local mini-
016
               mum. To address such drawback, we propose the novel Context-Aware Kernel
               Search (CAKES) to automate optimal kernel design in BO with large language
017
               models (LLMs). Concretely, CAKES exploits LLMs as crossover and mutation
018
               operators to adaptively generate and refine GP kernels based on the observed data.
019            CAKES works entirely in-context and can be easily integrated into existing sys-
020            tems without requiring any fine-tuning. We further present a theoretical analysis
021            demonstrating that our method achieves sub-linear regret relative to the budget for
022            any input dimension. Experimental results demonstrate that CAKES outperforms
023            various salient baseline methods in numerous synthetic and real-world optimiza-
024            tion tasks. Notably, CAKES improves the overall performance on benchmark
025            functions by roughly 36%. In hyperparameter tuning tasks, CAKES can effec-
026            tively leverage fewer data samples to quickly identify high-performing configura-
027
               tions and consistently ranks first across various datasets. As an encouraging real
               application, we successfully applied CAKES to design photonic chips, achieving
028
               significant improvements in key performance indicators while speeding up the de-
029
               sign cycle by a factor of ten compared to the baselines. Our code is accessible at
030            https://github.com/cakes4bo/cakes.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Many important scientific and engineering problems require optimizing objective functions that are
036   noisy and expensive to evaluate. These objective functions often lack closed-form expressions, let
037   alone gradient information, making optimization particularly difficult (Wang et al., 2023). Nonethe-
038   less, Bayesian optimization (BO) has shown remarkable success in optimizing such functions, due to
039
      its ability to operate on limited data and incorporate prior knowledge to guide the optimization pro-
      cess (Garnett, 2023). In the past couple of decades, BO has been used for diverse tasks ranging from
040
      tuning hyperparameters in machine learning (Snoek et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2024) to designing poli-
041
      cies in robotics (Calandra et al., 2016; Martinez-Cantin, 2017) and recommending new molecules
042   in drug discovery (Korovina et al., 2019; Tripp & Hernández-Lobato, 2024). The main idea behind
043   BO is to first construct a surrogate model, typically using a Gaussian process (GP, Rasmussen &
044   Williams, 2006), to represent the prior belief about the objective function. Then, by conditioning
045   on the observations and the prior, the posterior is calculated using Bayes’ rule to reflect the updated
046   belief about the objective function. Based on this posterior, an acquisition function is further used to
047   determine the next promising positions to query by balancing exploration (moving to regions with
048   high uncertainty) and exploitation (moving to regions with high expected value).
049   Although the past decades have witnessed rapid development of BO, much of the focus has been on
050   designing novel acquisition functions (Ament et al., 2023; Aglietti et al., 2024). In comparison, the
051   challenge of appropriately choosing the surrogate model has received comparatively less attention
052   (Shahriari et al., 2016). Many practitioners simply regard surrogate modeling as a “black art” and
053   perceive it as less critical to the optimization outcome compared to the acquisition function design
      (Malkomes & Garnett, 2018). In the context of GPs, most off-the-shelf BO methods simply use

080   2012). While this seems reasonable at the first glance, the so-called one-size-fits-all approach has
081   significant limitations. Since the kernel encodes our assumptions about the underlying objective
082   function, the GP will be a poor fit if these assumptions do not match the reality (Roman et al.,

085   et al., 2017). These considerations underscore the need for a more sophisticated kernel design in
086   BO.
087   While the pursuit for optimal kernel design is not new, existing approaches might not be straightfor-
088   ward in the setting of BO. In BO, the objective function is typically expensive to evaluate, resulting
089   in limited observations for tuning the kernel. The lack of known functional structure and gradi-

098   their capacity to process contextual information and improve search performance (Yang et al., 2024).
099   Moreover, LLMs are also pre-trained on massive internet data, which potentially contains transfer-
100   able domain knowledge applicable to various optimization tasks. Given these insights, we aim to
101   investigate the following question,
102
103            “Can LLMs, with their encoded knowledge and few-shot prompting, help to en-
104            hance the kernel design in BO?”

126   2.1   BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
127
128   Consider optimizing an objective function f : X ⊂ Rd → R,
129
                                                x∗ = arg max f (x).                                           (1)
130                                                        x∈X
131
      Here, f is treated as a “black-box” function, meaning we can only obtain noisy evaluations of the
132
      form y = f (x) + ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, σϵ2 ) represents the Gaussian noise. The evaluations of f are
133   also costly, limiting the number of queries we can make. In addition, the gradient of f is not directly
134   accessible and must be estimated from the noisy evaluations, further complicating the optimization
135   process. To address these challenges, Bayesian optimization (BO) employs a probabilistic surrogate
136   model g to approximate f on the fly (Garnett, 2023). A popular choice for g is the family of Gaussian
137   processes (GP, Rasmussen & Williams, 2006), where a GP can be fully defined by a mean function
138   m : X → R and a positive semi-definite covariance (or kernel) function k : X ×X → R. In practice,
139   the mean function is often set to zero, allowing the GP to be solely characterized by the choice of the
140   kernel function. At each iteration t, the calibration of the posterior distribution p(gt | Dt ) given the
141   previous observations Dt = {(xi , yi )}t−1
                                               i=1 informs where to explore and exploit in X . Then, based
142
      on p(gt | Dt ), an acquisition function α : X → R defines a policy to choose the next best point
      to evaluate. Common choices of α include expected improvement (EI, Jones et al., 1998), upper
143
      confidence bound (UCB, Srinivas et al., 2012), and Thompson sampling (TS, Thompson, 1933).
144
      After each evaluation, the surrogate model g is refined to reflect the updated belief about f . We refer
145   the readers to Appendix B.1 for a thorough treatment of BO with GPs.

      2.2   K ERNEL G RAMMAR
148
149   The kernel grammar introduced by Duvenaud et al. (2013) defines a comprehensive and flexible
150   space of kernels. Such space exploits the closure properties of kernel functions under addition and
151   multiplication, which ensures that: if k1 (x, x′ ) and k2 (x, x′ ) are valid kernels, then both k1 (x, x′ )+
152   k2 (x, x′ ) and k1 (x, x′ ) × k2 (x, x′ ) are also valid kernels (Smola & Schölkopf, 1998). Starting from
153   a set of base kernels, such as the squared exponential (SE), linear (LIN), periodic (PER), and rational
154   quadratic (RQ), one can construct more expressive kernels by combining such base kernels via the
155
      addition and multiplication operators. For instance, LIN + PER kernel can capture periodic structure
      with a linear trend and SE × PER kernel can capture locally periodic components. Let B denote a
156
      base kernel and S denote a subexpression, the entire kernel space is described by all kernels that can
157
      be constructed using the following grammar rules:
158
159         1. Add a base kernel to a subexpression: S → S + B;
160
            2. Multiply a subexpression with a base kernel: S → S × B;
161
            3. Replace a base kernel with another base kernel: B → B.

183   3    C ONTEXT-AWARE K ERNEL S EARCH
184
185   We propose Context-Aware Kernel Search (CAKES) to automate GP surrogate modeling in BO with
186   LLMs. CAKES uses LLMs as genetic operators to adaptively propose kernels based on the observed
187   data. The main motivation is to refine the kernel choice at each iteration before determining the next
188   query point. The complete procedure of CAKES is summarized in Algorithm 1 and the designed
189   prompts are shown in Appendix D.1.
190
      Conditioning the LLM. We begin by randomly sampling n points from the input space X to ini-
191   tialize the observations D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 . These observations are then used as few-shot samples
192   to prompt the LLM for analysis. The prompt is designed based on the concept of conditioning on
193   high performance, as suggested by Zhou et al. (2023). Specifically, we start the prompt with a state-
194   ment, “You are an expert in machine learning, specializing in Gaussian processes”, to simulate the
195   reasoning of a human expert in the field. It has also been shown that chain-of-thought reasoning, or
196   generating intermediate reasoning steps, can improve the performance of LLMs (Wei et al., 2022b;
197   Kojima et al., 2022). Motivated by this, we instruct the LLM to analyze the provided observations
198   and identify patterns that can be represented by kernel functions, before proposing the kernels at
199
      each iteration. To enhance the LLM’s understanding, we embed domain-specific knowledge by
      including examples of kernel functions along with their characteristics.
200
201   Initializing the population. We draw some inspiration from the genetic algorithm (Holland, 1992),
202   where we maintain a population of candidates (kernels) throughout the optimization process. Our
203   initial population consists of six base kernels: squared exponential (SE), periodic (PER), linear
204   (LIN), rational quadratic (RQ), Matérn-3/2 (M3), and Matérn-5/2 (M5). We denote this initial set
205
      as K = {SE, PER, LIN, RQ, M3, M5}. For each kernel k ∈ K, we measure its fitness using the
      Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC, Schwarz, 1978), which balances model fit and complexity.
206
      BIC is commonly used for model selection and can be seen as an approximation to the Laplace
207
      approximation (Murphy, 2022). More detailed discussions on BIC and model selection for GPs can
208   be found in Appendix B.3.
209
210   Proposing the kernels. We consider a generalized notion of the kernel grammar, which involves a
211
      set of base kernels {B1 , . . . , Br } and a set of operators {T1 , . . . , Tl }, where r, l ∈ N. Each operator
      Tj : K × K → K, for j = 1, . . . , l, is a closed operator (e.g., addition and multiplication) on the
212
      space of kernels K. The grammar rules can be summarized in the following way:
213
214         1. Apply operator Tj on a subexpression and a base kernel: S → Tj (S, B);
215
            2. Replace a base kernel with another base kernel: B → B.

216
      Based on these grammar rules, we can formally define the kernel grammar space as follows:
217
      Definition 1. Let {k1 , . . . , kr }, where r ∈ N, be a set of base kernels, and let {T1 , . . . , Tl }, where
218
      l ∈ N, be a set of operators defined by Tj : K × K → K for j = 1, . . . , l. For i ∈ N, we can define
219
      the kernel grammar space recursively as follows:
220
221                          K0 := {k1 , . . . , kr },
222                          Ki := {Tj (k1 , k2 ) | k1 , k2 ∈ Ki−1 , j = 1, . . . , l} ∪ Ki−1 .
223
224
      When we refer to a kernel k from the kernel grammar space, we actually refer to the associated
      kernel family over its hyperparameters {kθ | θ ∈ Θ}. Hence, if we apply an operator T on two
225
      kernels k1 and k2 , we obtain another kernel family {T (k1,θ1 , k2,θ2 ) | θ1 ∈ Θ1 , θ2 ∈ Θ2 }. By
226
      leveraging this kernel grammar space, we can use the LLM as genetic operators to propose kernels
227   through the following steps:
228
229          • Crossover: We perform nc crossover operations. For each crossover, we sample two parent
230            kernels k1 , k2 from K with probability proportional to their fitness. We then prompt the
231            LLM to propose a new kernel kc that has a lower BIC value, by applying an operator on
232            the parent kernels.
233          • Mutation: With probability pm , we perform a mutation operation. We select the fittest
234            kernel kf from K and prompt the LLM to suggest a new kernel km , by replacing one of the
235            base kernels in kf with another base kernel.
236
      In the prompts, we also ask the LLM to report a brief analysis of its reasoning behind the proposed
237
      kernels. This serves as a sanity check, enabling us to verify and interpret the choices made by the
238
      LLM. The proposed kernels from both steps are added to K, and their fitnesses are measured. Then,
239   we select the top np fittest kernels to form the next generation of K.
240
241   Choosing the next query point. In our experiments, we observed that some kernels may promise
242
      a good fit, but the actual improvement is not as substantial as expected when the proposed query
      points are evaluated. For this reason, we do not always rely on the fittest kernel to choose the next
243
      query point. Instead, we adopt the following weighting strategy,
244
245                                        k ∗ = arg max wk α(xt,k ; D, k),                                     (2)
                                                       k∈K
246                                P
      where wk = exp(−BICk )/ j∈K exp(−BICj ) and xt,k denotes the proposed query point for that
247
      kernel. This strategy allows us to balance the kernel’s ability to fit the data (as indicated by wk ) with
248
      the expected improvement at the proposed query point (as measured by α). After determining k ∗ ,
249
      we set the next query point at xt = xt,k∗ and update the observations as D ← D ∪ {(xt , yt )}. This
250   iterative process continues until a predefined budget T is exhausted.

305   Kernel design. Traditionally, kernel design relied on visually inspecting patterns in the data and
306   manually combining elementary kernels, such as squared exponential kernel and periodic kernel
307   (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006). However, this approach is highly subjective and requires expert
308   knowledge, making it impractical for complex data patterns. As a result, several methods have
309
      been developed to automate the kernel selection process. One such method involved multiple kernel
      learning techniques, which leverage data to identify the optimal kernel configuration by optimizing
310
      a linear or nonlinear combination of elementary kernels (Bach, 2008; Gönen & Alpaydin, 2011).
311
      Yet, these methods impose restrictions on the kernel space and require the hyperparameters to be
312   specified in advance. Another line of research focused on designing flexible kernel families that
313   can approximate arbitrary stationary kernels in the frequency domain (Lázaro-Gredilla et al., 2010;
314   Wilson & Adams, 2013; Yin et al., 2020). While these methods can capture a wide range of kernels,
315   they are limited by the stationarity assumption. Other works have also attempted to develop more
316   expressive kernels by integrating GPs with deep neural networks (Damianou & Lawrence, 2013;
317   Wilson et al., 2016). Unfortunately, these methods often require complex inference techniques that
318   are computationally more expensive than traditional kernels. In contrast to these approaches, our
319   method is based on the kernel grammar and in-context learning with pre-trained LLMs, which offers
320
      a flexible yet computationally feasible approach.
321   Surrogate modeling in BO. When using GPs as the surrogate model in BO, the kernel is typi-
322   cally selected a priori based on expert knowledge about the problem at hand. Unfortunately, when
323   there is no prior knowledge available, most BO methods simply use default kernels such as squared
      exponential kernel or Matérn-5/2 kernel (Snoek et al., 2012). While this seems reasonable, it has

358   adaptive kernel design.
359   LLMs as genetic operators. As the model size and amount of training data increase, LLMs exhibit
360   several emergent abilities that improve their performance across diverse tasks (Wei et al., 2022a).
361   One such ability is few-shot learning, where the model can generalize from a few samples without
362   adjusting its parameters (Brown et al., 2020). This generalization is achieved through in-context
363   learning, in which the model identifies the patterns in the samples and extrapolates them via next-
364   token prediction. Inspired by these abilities, recent works have explored the use of LLMs for op-
365   timization tasks, notably as genetic operators in evolutionary algorithms. For example, Meyerson
366
      et al. (2024) utilized LLMs to propose evolutionary crossovers on tasks such as image and code
      generation, while Lehman et al. (2023) trained an LLM on computer programs to act as a mutation
367
      operator for robot simulations. Chen et al. (2024) combined evolutionary search with soft prompt
368
      tuning to evolve neural network architectures using LLMs. To the best of our knowledge, the cur-
369   rent work is the first to use LLMs as genetic operators for GP kernel design in BO. Compared to the
370   state-of-the-art, our method can be applied entirely in-context and does not require any fine-tuning.

528   7            C ONCLUSION
529
530   We introduced CAKES, a novel kernel design method that integrates LLMs to automate surrogate
531
      modeling in BO. By leveraging LLMs as crossover and mutation operators, CAKES adaptively
      proposes kernels based on the observed data, bridging the gap between kernel selection and domain
532
      expertise. Our theoretical analysis demonstrates that CAKES achieves sub-linear regret relative to
533
      the budget for any input dimension. Experimental results strongly support our theoretical findings.
534   In benchmark function optimization, CAKES consistently ranked among the top two for all test
535   functions, achieving the best performance in 12 out of 15 cases. In hyperparameter tuning tasks,
536   CAKES obtained the lowest regret across all models, particularly excelling in the early stage of
537   optimization with fewer observations. In the photonic chip design problem, CAKES significantly
538   enhanced chip performance in fewer than 30 trials, resulting in a tenfold speedup of the design
539   cycle compared to baseline methods. We believe this work represents a solid step towards a fully
      automated BO system that can be used by non-experts on arbitrary objectives.

1694
       We consider tuning a controller for a lunar lander implemented in OpenAI Gym2 . The state space
1695
       of the lunar lander consists of its position, angle, time derivatives, and indicators of whether either
1696   leg is in contact with the ground. This results in an 8-dimensional state vector, which is passed
1697   to a handcrafted parameterized controller that determines the appropriate action to take. There
1698   are four possible actions for each frame: firing the main engine, fire the left orientation engine,
1699   fire the right orientation engine, or doing nothing. The handcrafted control policy is defined by
1700   d = 12 parameters, which parameterize linear score functions based on the state vector and establish
1701   thresholds that dictate which action to prioritize. The reward system is defined as follows:
1702
                            • Successful landing yields +100 points, while crashing incurs a penalty of −100 points.
1703
1704                        • Each leg in contact with the ground yields +10 points.
1705                        • Firing the main engine incurs a penalty of −0.3 points per frame, and firing side engines
1706                          incurs a penalty of −0.03 points per frame.
1707
1708
       The benchmark for winning the game is 200 points, so we use it as the target objective. We run
       the simulation for T = 250 iterations and average the results over 50 randomly generated terrains,
1709
       initial positions, and velocities. The results, illustrated in Figure 13, demonstrate that the controller
1710
       optimized by CAKES significantly outperforms other methods, achieving both the highest rewards
1711   and the lowest regret. Specifically, CAKES consistently reached or exceeded the target objective of
1712   200 points across various terrains, while the other methods struggled to maintain high scores and
1713   often incurred greater penalties due to crashes or inefficient landings. In our simulations, we also
1714   found that CAKES not only optimized the landing strategies but also minimized unnecessary engine
1715   usage, resulting in lower penalties over time.

1718
1719   Computational cost. While CAKES can be applied entirely in-context and does not require any
1720   fine-tuning, using LLMs for inference may result in a larger computational footprint compared to
1721   traditional BO methods. Despite this, our findings indicate that CAKES trades this off with improved
1722   sample efficiency, which is a particularly desirable property for black-box optimization tasks. This
1723   suggests the potential for integrating CAKES with more computationally efficient approaches, such
1724   as deploying it in the earlier stage of the optimization process.
1725   Choice of LLM. We have demonstrated the potential of integrating LLMs to enhance kernel design
1726   in BO using the gpt-4o-mini model. We chose gpt-4o-mini as it offers an excellent balance

1729   that the choice of the LLM may influence the results, the overall methodology and key insights
1730   presented in this work are generally applicable, and extend beyond the specifics of any particular
1731   LLM. A promising direction for future research would be to benchmark various LLMs to better

       Generalized kernel grammar. While we focus on addition and multiplication as initial proof-
1734

1735   of kernels. For example, by only using these operations, we can construct polynomial kernels to
1736   capture non-linear patterns as well as multi-dimensional kernels to model interactions among input
1737   features (Duvenaud et al., 2013). However, we would like that to note that the kernel grammar
1738   can be extended using other operators that preserve the closure properties of kernel functions, such
1739   as convolution, composition, and affine transformations (Smola & Schölkopf, 1998). We aim to
1740   explore these possibilities further in future work.
