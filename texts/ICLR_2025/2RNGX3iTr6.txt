009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011       While advances in large language models (LLMs) have greatly improved the quality of synthetic
012       text data in recent years, synthesizing tabular data has received far less attention. Many of the
013       top-performing approaches to this problem rely on techniques that adapt models originally devel-
014       oped for other modalities, potentially leaving generative performance on the table. We address
015       these disparities in attention and performance for tabular data by introducing Tabby, a simple but
016       powerful post-training modification to the standard Transformer-based language model architec-
017       ture that enables its use for tabular dataset synthesis. Tabby relies on Gated Mixture-of-Experts
018
          layers, allowing each data column to be modeled by a dedicated set of parameters within the
          transformer multi-layer perceptrons or language modeling heads. Applying Tabby to Distilled-
019
          GPT2 improves synthetic data quality up to 7% compared to previous tabular dataset synthesis
020
          methods, achieving performance near or equal to that of real data.

023   1      I NTRODUCTION
024
025   From spreadsheets to databases, much of our modern life is encoded in tables. Airplane black
026   boxes, website visitor logs and hospital patient records are just a few examples of this versatile
027   modality. Despite the widespread use of tabular data and many calls for improved tabular modeling
028   approaches, this type of data has received less attention in deep learning research than images and
029   text (Fang et al., 2024; Davila et al., 2024; van Breugel and van der Schaar, 2024).
030   Progress towards the synthesis of realistic tabular data has encountered several key challenges. First,
031   tabular columns often exhibit complex interdependencies. Second, many tabular datasets are in fact
032   a combination of various modalities, with text, numerical, and nested datatypes (such as a JSON,
033   dictionary, or other structured object) possible among the columns in one dataset. Third, although
034   the order of items within one column of one row is important, the order of columns with respect to
035   each other is usually not meaningful and is a potential source of spurious correlations when training
036   a model. How to best design and train models that can address these issues remains an open question.
037   There have been notable efforts to adapt several model architectures to tabular data, recently focusing
038   on generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Xu et al., 2019), LLMs (Borisov et al., 2022) and
039   diffusion models (Kotelnikov et al., 2022). However, because these architectures were each designed
040   with text or images in mind, significant preprocessing must be made to tabular datasets in order to
041   allow their usage, likely resulting in lower performance than would be possible for an architecture
042   designed specifically for tabular data.
043   For these reasons, works including van Breugel and van der Schaar (2024) have called for the devel-
044   opment of pretrained Large Tabular Models (LTMs) to fill a similar role to text and image foundation
045   models, such as GPT (OpenAI, 2023) or DALL-E (OpenAI, 2021). Unfortunately, the creation of
046   an LTM would require (1) large and diverse tabular pretraining sets which have not yet been curated,
047   (2) a specialized tabular model architecture which has yet to be designed, along with (3) a staggering
048   amount of compute resources for pretraining.
049   In this work, we take an initial step towards the development of a LTM by proposing
050   Tabby, a post-training modification to the transformer-based LLM architecture for enabling tab-
051   ular data synthesis. After training on text data—but before finetuning on tabular data—Tabby re-
052   places designated LLM blocks with Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) layers (Shazeer et al., 2017), which
053   allow each data column to be modeled by a dedicated set of parameters within the LLM. The greater
      model expressivity afforded by this modification results in higher-fidelity synthetic data.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Tabular data has played a central role in machine learning since the field’s early days. In particular,
097   decision trees (Song and Lu, 2015) and their relatives such as random forests (Biau and Scornet,
098
      2016) are well-adapted to classification or regression on tabular datasets.
099   Classical synthesis: Classical machine learning methods may be used to synthesize tabular data,
100   by modeling each column as a random variable and sampling from the resulting multivariate distri-
101   bution. This technique has been successfully applied to decision trees (Reiter, 2005) and Bayesian
102   networks (Aviñó et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2017) . Copulas (Frees and Valdez, 1998; Janke et al.,
103   2021; Benali et al., 2021) are another traditional approach, which rely on first modeling each column
104
      as a univariate random distribution, then fitting a probabilistic model to the multivariate distribution
      formed by all columns. However, these approaches are limited in the data types that may be repre-
105
      sented among the columns and the varieties of relationships that may be modeled across columns.
106
107   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): Many tabular synthesis methods rely on GANs
      (Goodfellow et al., 2020), but have encountered several inherent limitations. In particular, the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      distributions of ordinal columns are frequently rather imbalanced, leading GANs to undesirable
109   phenomena such as mode collapse. Continuous columns may possess multiple modes and complex
110   interactions with the other columns which GANs also struggle to capture. The top-performing CT-
111   GAN (Xu et al., 2019) employs conditional generation in an effort to address these shortcomings.
112   However, the fidelity of CTGAN’s synthetic data leaves further improvements to be desired, as we
113   will demonstrate in Section 4.
114
      LLMs: A small body of work has sought to apply LLMs’ demonstrated abilities of modeling com-
115   plex relationships to tabular data. The landmark work in this area, GReaT (Borisov et al., 2022),
116   details methods to convert tabular data into a sentence format which may be input to LLMs, then
117   proposes a training technique of “shuffling” the order in which columns occur for each row, which
118   is reported to improve the modeling of inter-column dependencies.
119
      Two notable works have built off of GReaT to achieve further improved tabular data fidelity: TapTap
120
      (Zhang et al., 2023) pretrains full or Distilled-GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019) on a variety of tabular
121   datasets before fine-tuning on a downstream tabular synthesis task, while Tabula (Zhao et al., 2023)
122   explores methods of preprocessing the training data to be more-easily modeled by LLMs. Other
123   LLM-based works have adapted these recent advances to relational tables (Solatorio and Dupriez,
124   2023), or used the emergent abilities of very large models such as GPT-4 to generate synthetic data
125   using In-Context Learning in place of fine-tuning (Seedat et al., 2024). Because many of these
126   prior LLM-based works are training techniques, they may be applied in concert with the Tabby
127   architecture modification. We demonstrate this using GReaT, TapTap and Tabula in Section 4.
128   MoE Architectures: The key innovation of Tabby is the application of Gated Mixture of Expert
129   (MoE) layers (Shazeer et al., 2017; Masoudnia and Ebrahimpour, 2014) for LLM synthesis of tab-
130   ular data. MoE layers have enjoyed utility in multitask (Ma et al., 2018; Gupta et al., 2022) and
131   multimodal learning (Zhao et al., 2024; Park et al., 2018), by creating sets of model parameters that
132   are dedicated to a specific task. We describe our use of MoE layers in Section 3.
133
134   3     M ETHOD
135
136   Tabby is an architecture modification that may be applied to any transformer-based language model
137   (LM) (Vaswani, 2017). In Section 3.1, we describe the variations of Tabby. In Section 3.2 , we
138   describe the process for training an arbitrary LM on tabular data, then compare the training process’s
139   forward pass and loss calculation for a Tabby model with a non-Tabby model in Section 3.3. Tabby
140   increases the expressivity of LMs, allowing for better modeling of individual columns’ distributions
141   and resulting in higher generative fidelity.
142
143   3.1   A RCHITECTURE OF TABBY M ODELS
144
145
      Suppose that a tabular dataset contains V columns and let the order of blocks within an arbitrary
      transformer-based LM be represented as [L1 , L2 , . . . , LH ]. We apply the MoE technique by replac-
146
      ing an LM block La with a vector ⇤a = [La,1 , La,2 , . . . , La,V ] of V blocks. As such, a Tabby model
147
      with one MoE block ⇤a is represented as
148
149                       [L1 , L2 , . . . , La 1 , [La,1 , La,2 , . . . , La,V ], La+1 , . . . , LH ].
150   The i-th column in the dataset is modeled by La,i within ⇤a .
151
      Tabular data comes in myriad forms, such as relational, multidimensional or sparse tables. The
152
      flexibility in placement of MoE layers enables Tabby’s adaptation to a variety of table setups. While
153
      this technique may be applied to any set of layers within the model, we focus on the transformer
154   blocks’ multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and the language modeling (LM) head. We refer to Tabby
155   models with MoE MLPs or LM heads as MMLP or MH models, respectively. Those with both MoE
156   MLPs and MoE LM Heads are MMLP-MH models. For a visual comparison of MMLP, MH and
157   non-Tabby models, refer to Figure 1.
158
159   3.2   F INE -T UNING LLM S ON TABULAR DATA
160
161   We now describe the general conventions applied when training or fine-tuning an LM on a tabular
      dataset. Suppose our training dataset contains N rows and the dataset’s column names are denoted


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      by v1 , v2 , . . . , vV , where the value of the j-th row in the i-th column is denoted as vij . For a given
163   row, the model will train on the columns in order `1 , `2 , . . . , `V (for general training we consider
164   this order to be simply [V ], while GReaT training allows this order to be arbitrary).
165
166
      To provide the LM with its expected text modality input, we convert the j-th row as follows, where
      <EOS> is the end-of-sequence token and <EOC> is a specialized end-of-column token which we
167
      introduce to divide the text between columns:
168
169       ‘‘<BOS> v`1 is v`j1 <EOC> v`2 is v`j2 <EOC> · · · v`V is v`jV <EOS>"
170
171   After converting the tabular dataset in this fashion, an LM is capable of fine-tuning on the dataset
172   in a normal sequence-to-sequence style. The prompt for each row during training is the beginning-
173   of-sequence token <BOS>. During generation, the LM will output text in a similar format to the
174   training data, which can then be parsed into tabular data as desired.
175
176   3.3    TABBY T RAINING
177
      Suppose that we construct a Tabby model from a base LM by replacing one of its blocks La with an
178
      MoE set ⇤a . At the beginning of fine-tuning the Tabby model, the weights for each block in ⇤a are
179
      initialized to equal the weights of La .
180
181   The Tabby training process requires only slight modifications as compared to the training of other
182   LMs on tabular data. Instead of representing each training row as one string, we convert each row
183
      into a length-V list of strings as follows:
184
          [‘‘v`1 is v`j1 <EOC>", ‘‘v`2 is v`j2 <EOC>", · · · , ‘‘v`V is v`jV <EOS>"]
185
186   Internally, the Tabby model begins by training on column `1 with prompt <BOS>, attending to
187   tokens 0 through k 1 when predicting the k-th token. After computing the loss on column `1 , this
188   column’s tokens are appended to the prompt used to train column `2 . As such, the prompt when
189   training on column `i is
190
191       ‘‘<BOS>v`1 is v`j1 <EOC> v`2 is v`j2 <EOC> · · · v`i 1 is v`ji 1 <EOS>"
192
193   A favorable side-effect of this training style is that we calculate the losses for each column sep-
194   arately, allowing the performances of each column to be monitored separately and compared, as
195
      demonstrated in Section 4.3.

200
      Claim 1: Tabby models generate higher-quality data than pre-existing tabular synthesis approaches.
201   Claim 2: The Tabby architecture modification allows smaller LLMs to achieve synthetic data fidelity
202   more similar to that of LLMs with higher parameter counts.
203   Claim 3: Tabby’s loss formulation allows for convenient tracking of per-column performance at
204   training time, leading to better understanding of model behavior.
205

206

233   4.0.1    BASELINES AND C OMPARISONS
234
235   LLM Approaches: As previously described, there are multiple approaches to training LLMs on
236
      tabular data, regardless of whether Tabby is applied. As a baseline training technique, we implement
      Plain training. While this method has not been described in prior LLM works, it represents a basic
237
      method of training the LLM on the columns in the same order as they are found in the training
238
      dataset. At sample time, we simply prompt with <BOS> and parse the resulting model output.
239
240   Next, we explore the GReaT technique (Borisov et al., 2022) as introduced in Section 2. At each
241   step, the order in which the columns are presented to the model are selected at random. During
242
      generation, GReaT enforces that the distribution of the label column matches that of the training
      distribution. Suppose that the label column is vt and let St be the set of all unique values taken by
243
      vt in the trainset (regardless of whether vt is a categorical or numerical column). GReaT prompts
244
      the LLM with <BOS>vt is st , where st is sampled from St with probability proportionate to the
245   frequency of st in column vt .
246
247   We additionally explore the use of two more training techniques in conjunction with GReaT. TapTap
248
      (Zhang et al., 2023) is a checkpoint of Distilled-GPT2, pretrained using GReaT on a large collection
      of tabular datasets. Meanwhile, Tabula (Zhao et al., 2023) aims to address the challenges encoun-
249
      tered by LLMs on categorical columns: Tabula converts each categorical column into an ordinal
250
      format by replacing each unique value of the column with a unique integer. In many cases, this tech-
251   nique reduces sequence length, decreasing training and generation time, and helps the LLM during
252   sampling to only generate values that occur within the categorical column’s training distribution.
253   We abbreviate the training system of using GReaT, TapTap and Tabula together as GTT.
254
      All LLM methods use Distilled-GPT2 as a base model, save for the GTT methods which use the
255
      TapTap Distilled-GPT2 that is pretrained on tabular data as a base model. We finetune Non-Tabby
256
      (NT) Distilled-GPT2 using Plain training as a baseline.
257
258   Other Approaches: To represent non-LLM tabular synthesis techniques, we include a diffusion
259   model, Tab-DDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2022), as well as CTGAN (Xu et al., 2019) and TVAE (Xu
260
      et al., 2019), the leading GAN and VAE approaches, respectively.

267   4.0.3    M ETRICS
268
269   We focus on the standard metrics of machine learning efficacy and detection accuracy to measure
      the fidelity and quality of synthetic datasets.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Machine learning efficacy (MLE) (Dankar et al., 2022) quantifies whether a synthetic dataset is
271   capable of replacing the original, real data used to train a generative model. MLE serves as our
272   primary metric for data quality. Given a real dataset, we form disjoint training and test sets, denoted
273   R and D respectively. A generative model is trained on R, then generates synthetic dataset S.
274
      To calculate MLE, a downstream classifier or regressor KR is trained using R to predict a predeter-
275
      mined label column, using all other columns as features. An additional classifier or regressor KS is
276
      similarly trained on S. Then, the performance of KS and KR on the real test dataset D is evaluated:
277   a high-fidelity synthetic dataset S will allow KS to exhibit similar performance to KR despite never
278   encountering real datapoints before test-time. We report both KR and KS in our results, considering
279   MLE to be the difference in performance between KR and KS .
280
      We use a random forest classifier or regressor as our downstream model K. For classification
281
      datasets, we compare the accuracy of KR and KS , while for regression datasets, we compare the
282
      coefficient of determination R2 . We define the coefficient of determination R2 as max(1 rt , 0),
283   where r and u are the residual sum of squares and total sum of squares, respectively. This formula-
284   tion means that if a model performs worse than random guessing, its R2 value will be represented
285   as 0. For both the accuracy and R2 coefficient metrics, a higher score indicates higher-quality data.
286   In Appendix B, we also report the mean squared error of KR and KS for the regression datasets,
287   where lower scores indicate better performance.
288
      Detection accuracy (Qian et al., 2023) quantifies the degree to which the generative model introduces
289   spurious correlations or other patterns that differentiate synthetic from real data. Given the real
290   training dataset R and a synthetic dataset S, we sample the same number of rows from each. Next,
291   we train a random forest classifier C to discriminate between real and synthetic examples. Highest-
292   quality synthetic data will result in 50% discrimination accuracy, indicating that C is unable to
293   distinguish between R and S. For this reason, our reported discrimination scores are calculated
294   as the absolute difference between 50% and the accuracy of discriminator C. Accordinly, lower
295   discrimination scores represent better performance.
296   Calculation of results: The reported result for each model and training setup is the average across
297   three training runs. For each of the three trained models, we sample 10, 000 datapoints, compute
298   all evaluation metrics separately for the three resulting synthetic datasets, then calculate the average
299   metric value across all runs. For LLM approaches, each model is trained for up to 50 epochs, using
300   early stopping when the validation loss (assessed every 5000 steps) fails to improve twice in a row.
301   We perform grid search to select the learning rate with lowest validation loss for each model and
302   training setup. For non-LLM works, we follow the procedures detailed in each of these works.

      4.4   D ISCUSSION
514
515   We find that Tabby models synthesize high-quality data in a variety of settings. In particular, Plain-
516   trained Tabby MH consistently outperforms all prior LLM-based approaches. The Tabby archi-
517   tecture modification allows LLMs to better model univariate column distributions, as well as the
518   multivariate relationships across columns. This impact is particularly evident in smaller models, as
519   demonstrated in Section 4.2. Additionally, Tabby’s loss formulation allows per-column performance
520   to be evaluated more easily than other deep learning-based tabular approaches, which may be useful
521   in selecting training techniques and strengthening trust in model performance.
522   Unusually, we find that the baseline Plain training technique with Distilled-GPT2 achieves near-
523   optimal MLE performance on several datasets. The exclusion of the Plain training technique from
524   prior LLM works for tabular data synthesis, which rely on more complex techniques, is surprising.
525
      As of this writing, the Adult, House and Diabetes datasets have become quite prevalent evaluation
526   tasks for tabular synthesis works: these three datasets are common to most works in this area.
527   It is our recommendation that new standard evaluation datasets be identified, which pose greater
528   challenges for baselines than these three datasets. A focus on more challenging datasets will allow
529   researchers to better-demonstrate the value of novel and more-complex synthesis techniques.
530
531
532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We introduce Tabby, a Mixture-of-Experts-based architecture modification that allows LLMs to
535   be better suited to tabular datasets. Tabby reaches parity with non-synthetic data in two out of
536   three evaluated datasets, according to machine learning efficacy with a Decision Tree Classifier.
537   Given the promising performance of Tabby, we hope to spur future work in this area and further
538   experimentation with architecture modifications that allow LLMs to better fit to tabular data. The
539   concepts behind Tabby may find utility in similar modalities as well, such as geospatial, nested-list,
      or other highly-structured data.
