009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models (LLMs) have achieved notable success across
012            various domains, while research in the medical field has largely focused on uni-
013            modal images. Meanwhile, current general-domain multimodal models for videos
014            still lack the capabilities to understand and engage in conversations about surgical
015            videos. One major contributing factor is the absence of datasets in the surgical
016
               field. In this paper, we create a new dataset, Surg-QA, consisting of 102,000
               surgical video-instruction pairs, the largest of its kind so far. To build such a
017
               dataset, we propose a novel two-stage question-answer generation pipeline with
018
               LLM to learn surgical knowledge in a structured manner from the publicly avail-
019            able surgical lecture videos. The pipeline breaks down the generation process
020            into two stages to significantly reduce the task complexity, allowing us to use a
021            more affordable, locally deployed open-source LLM than the premium paid LLM
022            services. It also mitigates the risk of LLM hallucinations during question-answer
023            generation, thereby enhancing the overall quality of the generated data. We further
024            train LLaVA-Surg, a novel vision-language conversational assistant capable of an-
025            swering open-ended questions about surgical videos, on this Surg-QA dataset, and
026            conduct comprehensive evaluations on zero-shot surgical video question-answering
027
               tasks. We show that LLaVA-Surg significantly outperforms all previous general-
               domain models, demonstrating exceptional multimodal conversational skills in
028
               answering open-ended questions about surgical videos. We will release our code,
029
               model, and the instruction-tuning dataset.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Surgery, as a discipline with rich multimodal information in the medical field, diverges significantly
035   from general medical diagnoses that often depend on static imagery, such as magnetic resonance
036   imaging and chest X-ray. The dynamic nature of surgical procedures with complex sequence of
037   actions and multi-stage processes, cannot be fully captured or understood through a single image.
038   The medical field has recently witnessed the significant impact of the Large Language Model (LLM),
039   especially in the arena of medical question answering. Domain-specific LLMs like LLaVA-Med (Li
040   et al., 2023) and Med-PaLM (Singhal et al., 2022), fused with publicly accessible medical question-
041   answer data such as PubMed (Zhang et al., 2023a), can assist with inquiries about a biomedical image
042   and meet the safety-critical demands of the medical domain. Moreover, general purpose LLMs such
043   as GPT (OpenAI, 2024), despite not being explicitly aligned to the medical field, have shown great
044
      potential and versatility when applied to some specific clinical knowledge areas. However, these
      models are still limited to processing single images, thus falling short of venturing into the surgical
045
      domain where the video modality plays a crucial role.
046
047   The availability of parallel video-text datasets has proven to be useful for pretraining generative
048   model in a self-supervised manner, as demonstrated by conversational multimodal LLMs such as
049   Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023) and Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023), and text-to-video generative
050
      models such as Sora (Brooks et al., 2024). However, obtaining surgical video-text pairs is more
      challenging than biomedical image-text pairs or general-domain video-text pairs due to the need of
051
      more expensive surgical expertise.
052
053   In this work, we introduce the Large Language and Vision Assistant for Surgery (LLaVA-Surg), the
      first attempt at a surgical multimodal conversational assistant. LLaVA-Surg leverages an adapted LLM

      that integrates the visual encoder of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) with Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) as
055   a language backbone, fine-tuned on generated instructional image-text pairs. Our approach further
056   adapts the design for spatiotemporal video modeling and finetunes the model on video-instruction
057   data to capture temporal dynamics and frame-to-frame consistency relationships available in video
058   data.
059
      A fundamental contribution of this work is the introduction of a novel two-stage question-answer
060
      generation pipeline. This pipeline extracts surgical knowledge from widely available surgical lecture
061   videos, resulting in the creation of Surg-QA, a dataset comprising over 102K surgical video-instruction
062   pairs. Each pair consists of a video and its corresponding instructional content in a question-answer
063   format. This extensive and diverse dataset enables LLaVA-Surg’s to understand surgical videos and
064   engage in comprehensive conversations about surgical videos.
065
      The major contributions of our paper are as follows:
066
067         1. Surg-QA. We introduce Surg-QA, to the best of our knowledge, the first large-scale surgical
068            video instruction-tuning dataset, featuring over 102K surgical video question-answer pairs
069            derived from more than 44K surgical video clips across 2,201 surgical procedures. We also
070            introduce the novel two-step question-answer generation pipeline behind Surg-QA. This
071            pipeline effectively mitigates the issue of LLM hallucination, providing a cost-effective
072            solution for large-scale question-answer generation.
073         2. LLaVA-Surg. We present LLaVA-Surg, to the best of our knowledge, the first video con-
074            versation model capable of expert-level understanding of surgical videos and answering
075            open-ended questions about surgical videos. LLaVA-Surg is trained in under 6 hours
076
               using eight A100 GPUs, by fine-tuning a general-domain vision-language model on Surg-
               QA. Comprehensive evaluations show that LLaVA-Surg excels in zero-shot surgical video
077
               question-answering tasks, outperforming previous models and demonstrating strong multi-
078
               modal conversational skills.
079
            3. Open-source. We will publicly release the surgical video instruction-tuning dataset, model,
080
               and code for data generation and training to advance research in the surgical domain.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Surgical Video Question Answering (Surgical VQA) models can answer questions based on
086   surgical videos and offer assistance to practicing surgeons and surgical trainees. Early surgical VQA
087
      methods were largely discriminative (Twinanda et al., 2016; Czempiel et al., 2020; Yengera et al.,
      2018), treating the task as a classification problem where answers were chosen from a predefined set.
088
      They excelled in identifying surgical steps, instruments, and organs, but were limited to closed-set
089
      predictions and struggled with open-ended questions and answers. Recent developments have shifted
090   towards generative methods (Seenivasan et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023; Seenivasan et al., 2023) that
091   produce free-form text sequences but are limited to single-turn conversations, preventing them from
092   engaging in a dialogue or answering follow-up questions. Unlike these models, our LLaVA-Surg
093   model can engage in meaningful multi-turn dialogues, answering surgical questions and providing
094   comprehensive surgical knowledge for an interactive learning experience.
095
      Multimodal LLM for Biomedical Image Conversations represents a significant advancement in the
096   field of medical artificial intelligence. These models combine text and image understanding to enable
097   more nuanced and contextually aware interactions between clinicians and AI systems. For instance,
098   the LLaVA-Med model demonstrates the potential of multimodal LLMs to interpret and generate
099   detailed medical image descriptions, thereby aiding both diagnostics and patient communication (Li
100   et al., 2023). The application of such models extends to various tasks including VQA, where they
101   provide accurate and relevant answers based on medical images and related queries (Zhang et al.,
102   2023b; Pal et al., 2023). This multimodal approach also enhances the ability to perform complex
103   reasoning and decision-making processes, which are critical in clinical settings (Liu et al., 2024a).
104
      Collectively, these developments underscore the transformative potential of multimodal LLMs in
      enhancing biomedical image conversations and ultimately improving patient care outcomes (He et al.,
105
      2020; Lau et al., 2018).
106
107   Multimodal LLM for Video Conversations has demonstrated great potential by integrating general-
      domain text, images, and video data. Early works like FrozenBiLM (Yang et al., 2022) demonstrates

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the promise of aligning vision and language models for multimodal understanding. Recent advance-
109   ments like Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023), Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023), and ChatUniVi (Jin
110   et al., 2024) illustrate practical applications in video contexts, delivering real-time, contextually aware
111   responses that improve user interactions. Specifically, Video-LLaVA integrates visual and language
112   data using the Language-Bind framework, enhancing video understanding and generating coherent,
113   contextually relevant responses. Video-ChatGPT excels in handling complex video data, providing
114   detailed analysis and responses. ChatUniVi pushes the boundaries further by integrating unified video
115   and language processing capabilities, facilitating more natural and interactive video conversations.
116   But their applicability to domain-specific videos like surgery videos have not yet been proven.

142   Figure 1: Surgical Knowledge Pyramid. Surgical video interpretation can be categorized into
143   four levels. The first two levels represent the observation capabilities, which can be captured by
144   traditional computer vision tasks such as object detection, segmentation, and labeling. But this only
145   conveys a superficial level of understanding. The next two levels represent the reasoning capabilities.
146   Interpretation at the reasoning levels provides the rationale behind the observations, further offering
147   deductions and plannings, conveying deep, surgical expert-level understanding.

149   However, existing datasets (Bai et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024) lack level 3 and 4 information. To
150   address this, we create Surg-QA, the first surgical instruction-tuning dataset that contains all four
151   levels of information. The proposed dataset consists of 100K video-text pairs from structured learning
152   of surgical lecture videos and 2K pairs focusing on the surgical visual concept alignment.

185   unlike previous work (Gilardi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023) that directly prompt
186   LLM to generate multi-round questions and answers based on the text information, we first prompt
187   LLM to extract the key information from the transcripts in a structured manner, focusing on four
188   main components: the observation Io and the corresponding reason Ir , plan Ip and deduction Id as
189   shown in Figure 1. This structured representation of videos ensures high-quality data by extracting
190   only surgery-related information, thus mitigating noise from non-surgical clips or non-informative
191   conversations. Additionally, it reduces the risk of LLM hallucination (Huang et al., 2023; Li et al.,
192   2023) by restricting the model to information extraction only. We also manually curate few-shot
193
      examples to teach how to extract high-quality information based on the transcript. See Appendix A.2
      for the prompt and few-shot examples.

320   4                   S URGICAL V ISUAL I NSTRUCTION T UNING
321
322   Architecture. LLaVA-Surg is a large vision-language model that aims to generate meaningful
323   conversation about surgical videos. It employs the architecture of Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023),
      a general-domain multimodal conversation model. Given a video, the model first samples N frames


                                                                                                                                                                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      uniformly, and calculate the frame-level features h ∈ RN ×h×w×D for each of the frames using CLIP
325   ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021), where D is the hidden dimension of CLIP features and h, w are the
326   video height and width respectively. The features h are fused through a temporal-fusion operation,
327   where the temporal features t ∈ RN ×D are derived through an average-pooling operation along the
328   temporal dimension, and spatial features s ∈ R(h×w)×D are derived using the same average-pooling
329   operation but along the spatial dimensions. By concatenating t and s, we derived the video-level
330   features f ∈ R(N +h×w)×D , then feed it through a linear projection layer that connects f to the
331   language model.
332
      End-to-End Instruction-Tuning. To balance the knowledge from levels 1 to 4, we combine the
333   structured surgical video learning data and concept alignment data as discussed in Section 3, this
334   results in 38K training video clips with 90K question-answer pairs. These pairs are converted to
335   instruction-following data as described in Equation 3, the data includes instructions that simply
336   present the task of describing the video, and tasks that answer various reasoning tasks. To train the
337   model to follow various instructions and complete tasks in a conversational manner, we finetune
338   LLaVA-Surg as a chatbot on the conversational data. During our training, we keep the weights of the
339   CLIP visual encoder only and finetune the rest of the parameters.

341   5     E XPERIMENTS
342
343   We conduct experiments to study two key components: the performance of LLaVA-Surg and the
344   quality of the produced multimodal surgical instruction-tuning data. Our experiments focus on two
345   evaluation settings: (1) How does LLaVA-Surg perform in surgical video question-answering, and
346   how does it compare to existing methods in the surgical domain? (2) How does the GPT evaluation
347   framework compare to the clinician evaluation?
348
349   5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
350
351
      Data. We collected 2,054 surgical procedures from WebSurg using the keyword "intervention" and
      an additional 97 procedures with the keyword "gallbladder" for future evaluation purposes, totaling
352
      2,151 procedures. These were randomly divided into a training set of 1,935 procedures and a test
353
      set of 216 procedures. In our instruction-tuning data generation pipeline, we use the ’large-v2’
354   version of WhisperX (Bain et al., 2023) to transcribe the surgical lectures. We use Llama-3-70B-
355   Instruct (AI, 2024) for information extraction and data generation as mentioned in Section 3. We use
356   ’gpt-3.5-turbo-0125’ to perform the following quantitative evaluation.
357
      Training. We use LLaVA-Med as our pre-trained language backbone and finetune the model on
358
      90K surgical video instruction following data. We use CLIP ViT-L/14 as the image encoder and
359
      use LLaVA-Med’s language backbone as the initial weight of LLaVA-Surg. We update the linear
360   layer projecting the video features to the LLM’s input space and the language backbone, while the
361   CLIP encoder is kept frozen. We finetune the model for 5 epochs using a learning rate of 2e-5 and an
362   overall batch size of 128. The training of our 7B model took around 6 hours on 8 A100 40GB GPUs.
363   For the rest of the hyperparameters, we follow the settings in (Maaz et al., 2023).

467   6      C ONCLUSION
468
469   In this paper, we introduced Surg-QA, a surgical video instruction-tuning dataset of 102K video-text
470   pairs. Surg-QA is generated primarily through a cost-efficient, two-stage question-answer generation
471   pipeline, which effectively reduces hallucinations during question-answer generation by LLM. We
472
      then trained LLaVA-Surg, a multimodal LLM in the surgical video domain, on Surg-QA. LLaVA-Surg
      shows great potential in understanding surgical videos and engaging in surgical video conversations,
473
      outperforming previous multimodal LLMs in our comprehensive evaluation. While LLaVA-Surg
474
      performs competitively compared to existing methods in the surgical video domain, we note that
475   LLaVA-Surg is limited by hallucinations. Future work is directed toward engaging experts to review
476   the generated samples in Surg-QA to improve the accuracy and reliability of LLaVA-Surg.
