009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the encoding mechanisms of hippocampal place cells remains a
012            significant challenge in neuroscience. Although sparse autoencoders have been
013            shown to exhibit place cell-like activity, the underlying processes are not fully
014            understood. In this study, we compare spatial representations learned by dense and
015            sparse autoencoders trained on images of 3D environments and find that only sparse
016
               autoencoders with orthonormal activity regularization in latent space produce place
               cells. We then show that this regularization promotes similar images to map
017
               onto the same neurons, acting as a locality-sensitive hash function. Notably, we
018
               demonstrate that these neurons are visually interpretable through activity clamping
019            and decoding, suggesting the formation of detailed episodic memories at the single-
020            neuron level. We then introduce a novel metric to quantify how neurons discretize
021            the image space into disjoint receptive fields, revealing that sparse autoencoders tile
022            input spaces with minimal overlap. Furthermore, we observe that whereas dense
023            autoencoders generate population codes resembling visual cortex activity near
024            criticality, sparse autoencoders produce higher-dimensional codes, thus suggesting
025            a similar coding strategy in the hippocampus. Extending our approach to the
026            auditory domain, we also replicate the emergence of ”frequency place cells” by
027
               training sparse autoencoders on audio snippets sampled from a frequency-varying
               signal, and show that population representations retain the statistical structure
028
               of the sample distribution. Lastly, we demonstrate that reinforcement learning
029
               agents can leverage these high-dimensional image representations to solve complex
030            spatial-cognitive tasks, despite their inherent brittleness. Overall, our findings
031            elucidate how sparse input compression in autoencoders can give rise to discrete,
032            interpretable memories, establishing an explicit link between episodic memory
033            formation and spatial representations in the hippocampus.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Early physiological experiments with rats revealed that certain neurons in the hippocampus exhibit in-
039
      creased activity when the animal occupies specific regions of the environment O’Keefe & Dostrovsky
      (1971). Ever since the discovery of such ”place cells”, decades of animal research have established the
040
      hippocampus as a neural system that learns a cognitive map of the environment and uses it for spatial
041
      navigation (Moser et al., 2008). Subsequent experimental studies also identified the hippocampus
042   as a key structure in episodic memory formation (Moser et al., 2015). Although several attempts
043   have been made to unify these observations under a coherent conceptual framework (Redish, 1999;
044   Eichenbaum, 2017), a clear mechanistically relationship episodic memory and spatial representations
045   remains elusive. Furthermore, numerous experiments reporting the instability of place cell activity
046   over time and their modulation by other non-spatial variables (Fenton & Muller, 1998; Jercog et al.,
047   2019) raise an open question: what are these cells truly encoding?
048   Efforts to answer this question have demonstrated that place cell-like activity can emerge under various
049   conditions: when artificial agents optimize a predictive coding objective (Recanatesi et al., 2021;
050   Uria et al., 2020; Ratzon et al., 2023; Gornet & Thomson, 2023; Levenstein et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
051   2022), when networks optimize temporal stability and pairwise decorrelation in processing visual
052   inputs (Wyss et al., 2006), or when building sparse, compressed representations of environmental
053   states (Santos-Pata et al., 2021a;b; Benna & Fusi, 2021; Ketz et al., 2013). Notably, the approach
      where sparse compression of information leads to spatial tuning aligns with the earlier hippocampal


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      autoencoder model (Gluck & Myers, 1993), and has been shown to replicate several distinct place
055   cell phenomena following environmental manipulations (Santos-Pata et al., 2021a;b).
056
057   In this work, we further investigate the mechanisms behind episodic memory formation and the
058
      emergence of place cells in the hippocampal autoencoder model. We demonstrate that sparse
      autoencoders equipped with orthonormal activity regularization can create discontinuities in the
059
      manifold of the latent space, discretizing arbitrary input spaces into disjoint receptive fields, whereby
060
      subsets of similar inputs converge onto distinct neurons. When applied to visual images, this clustering
061   process generates place cells operating on a very high-dimensional population code. In turn, these
062   neurons are shown to encode detailed visual memories. Moreover, we show that similar effects result
063   from applying the same principle in the auditory domain, recapitulating recently reported ”frequency
064   place cells”. Lastly, we show that reinforcement learning agents can make use of such sparse and
065   high-dimensional hippocampal-like representations to solve spatial-cognitive tasks.

      2.1   H IPPOCAMPAL - LIKE PLACE CELLS EMERGE IN SPARSE AUTOENCODERS
070
071   We studied the learning of spatial representations by training autoencoders (Figure 1a) with randomly
072   sampled images from four different tasks in the Animal-AI environment (Beyret et al., 2019): Double
073   T-maze, Cylinder, Object Permanence, and Thorndike. We trained two types of autoencoders. ”Dense”
074   autoencoders aimed solely to reconstruct the input images, thus preserving input information in
075   their latent space Z. In contrast, ”sparse” autoencoders had an additional objective beyond input
076   reconstruction: to develop sparse activity patterns in the latent space Z. This was achieved using the
077   following loss function:

080   where m denotes the batch size, n the number of neurons in Z, and the first term is the mean
081   squared error (MSE) between inputs X and their reconstructions X̂, encouraging Z to preserve input
082   information. The second term is an orthonormal activity regularization term, whose strength is
083   controlled by λ, pushing the Gramian ZT Z towards the identity matrix In . Since ZT Z captures
084

085

086
      regularization term typically used in sparse autoencoders, particularly in alleviating the dead ReLU
087   problem (Lu et al., 2019). For dense autoencoders, λ was set to zero, leaving only the reconstruction
088   error. We refer the reader to the Detailed methods section in the Appendix for a complete description
089   of the environments, dataset generation, and parameters used in this study.
090
      Training both types of autoencoders yielded significantly different internal representations of space
091
      in their latent space. Dense autoencoders developed many neurons that fired almost everywhere in
092
      space, with no defined place fields. In contrast, sparse autoencoders developed a majority of neurons
093   with one or two localized place fields, similar to place cells in the hippocampus (Figure 1b, c). The
094   spatial specificity of sparse autoencoder neurons was also reflected in significantly higher spatial
095   information scores compared to dense autoencoder neurons (Figure 1d). These results demonstrate
096   that single-unit spatial tuning emerges in sparse autoencoders but not in dense autoencoders, despite
097   both types of networks containing the same amount of positional information at the population level,
098   as shown by linear decoding analyses (Figure 1e).

      2.3   H IGH - DIMENSIONAL POPULATION STRUCTURE IN SPARSE AUTOENCODERS
210
211   Having linked the formation of episodic memories with the discretization of the image space in sparse
212   autoencoders, we explored the population structure of the latent space representations. Inspired by
213   Stringer et al. (2019) on the dimensionality of the population code in the mouse visual cortex, we
214   examined the dimensionality in our autoencoders. Dimensionality was estimated by performing
215   PCA on Z and computing the linear fit of the resulting eigenspectrum in log-log space, yielding a
      power-law exponent, α. High α values indicate low-dimensional codes, while low α values suggest


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      high-dimensional codes. An α ≈ 1 indicates a criticality regime with a high-dimensional but smooth
217   (i.e., no discontinuities) underlying manifold, as seen in neural responses in the visual cortex (Stringer
218   et al., 2019).
219
220
      We found that dense autoencoders had dimensionality scores close to 1, similar to visual cortex
      (Stringer et al., 2019), whereas sparse autoencoders exhibited higher-dimensional representations
221
      (Figure 3a), aligning more with the efficient coding hypothesis (Barlow et al., 1961). The almost-flat
222
      eigenspectrum suggests that sparse autoencoders’ population activity indeed encodes fine stimulus
223   features. Moreover, the orthonormal activity regularization also disrupted the input-output similarity
224   preservation typically seen in dense autoencoders (Figure 3b), further supporting the idea of a sharp
225   discretization of the image space by sparse autoencoders.

277   2.4   Z ERO - SHOT LEARNING OF PLACE CELLS IN SPARSE AUTOENCODERS
278
279   A typical observation in the hippocampus is that place cells can be identified within the first minutes
280
      of an animal being exposed to a new environment (Frank et al., 2004). Given that we have shown
      that sparse autoencoders exhibit very high dimensionality (Figure 3a) and single neurons tend to
281
      encode small clusters of samples (Figure 2), we investigated the extent to which neurons that learned
282
      to encode samples in one environment could generalize to encoding unseen environments and exhibit
283   zero-shot place cells. Therefore, we trained sparse autoencoders in one environment and tested them
284   across all others. Strikingly, neurons developed place fields with distributions very similar to those in
285   their training environment (Figure 4a). Furthermore, the average spatial information across neurons
286   and the mean decoding error of the rate maps were very similar, with no significant degradation
287   compared to the training environment (Figure 4b). These results suggest that the network’s circuitry
288   learned to cluster similar samples onto single neurons in a more generic manner, beyond the specific
289   details of the training data.

      2.5   S PARSE AUTOENCODERS DISCRETIZE AND TILE THE INPUT FREQUENCY SPACE IN AN
311
            EXPERIENCE - DEPENDENT MANNER
312
313   If the hippocampus functions as a generic, modality-independent episodic memory system, our
314   findings with the sparse autoencoder should generalize to other input modalities, such as sound.
315   Indeed, ”place cell”-like activity in the hippocampus has been reported for tasks involving ”navigating”
316   the sound frequency space, with neurons developing localized receptive fields around particular sound
317   frequencies (Aronov et al., 2017). To investigate whether a similar effect could be observed within
318   our framework, we trained autoencoders to compress and encode sound waves uniformly sampled
319   from a linearly-varying frequency signal (Figure 5a).
320   We observed the emergence of frequency-specific receptive fields in sparse autoencoders, but not
321   in dense autoencoders (Figure 5b), reproducing the main observations in Aronov et al. (2017).
322   These receptive fields tiled the entire frequency space in a linear manner. However, when sampling
323   was biased towards certain frequencies, the neurons’ receptive fields became denser and clustered
      around those frequencies, preserving the statistical structure of the sample distribution (Figure 5d).

325   individual neurons encoded particular frequencies so that these representations were readily decodable

414   3    D ISCUSSION
415
416   Optimization objectives underlying place cell emergence We have demonstrated the distinct
417   emergence of place cells in autoencoders with orthonormal activity regularization (Figure 1). Notably,
418   sparse compression alone was sufficient to develop spatial tuning, without the need for a predictive
419   objective (Recanatesi et al., 2021; Ratzon et al., 2023; Uria et al., 2020; Gornet & Thomson, 2023;
420   Levenstein et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022). While predictive coding may explain other features of
421   the hippocampus, such as place-cell theta sequences (Dragoi & Buzsáki, 2006), prediction does not
422   appear to be necessary for the emergence of realistic place cells. We speculate that models optimized
423
      for next-input predictions likely learn compressed representations of the environment implicitly as
      part of the predictive objective. Furthermore, by training the autoencoders with randomly sampled
424
      and shuffled images, we have shown that neither temporal contiguity of samples nor any temporal
425
      correlations are required to develop place cells. This finding suggests that while predictive learning
426   capturing temporal input correlations might correspond to experience-dependent theta sequences
427   in the hippocampus, the formation of compressed state representations might correspond to time-
428   independent learning processes at the gamma frequency scale (Lisman, 2005). Additionally, we
429   have demonstrated that sparse autoencoders can learn localized receptive fields of the input space
430   while breaking the relationship between input similarity and latent space similarity. This finding
431   contrasts with previous research that emphasized the preservation of input-output similarity matching
      for learning localized receptive fields (Sengupta et al., 2018; Qin et al., 2023). Overall, it appears that


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      sparse compression alone is sufficient to learn localized receptive fields, that in turn manifest as place
433   cells when applied to the visual domain.
434
435
      Sparse and very high-dimensional codes A slowly-decaying eigenspectrum, where fine details are
436
      over-weighted, represents codes that create discontinuities by disrupting the locality of the manifold
437   structure supporting the input space distribution (Nassar et al., 2020). We demonstrated that this
438   discontinuity can be induced by an orthonormal activity regularization objective, facilitating the
439   creation of event memories by discretizing the image input space (Figure 2). Our results suggest
440   that very high-dimensional codes underlie the formation of place cells (Figure 3), aligning with the
441   efficient coding hypothesis (Barlow et al., 1961), which posits that the brain maximizes information
442   by eliminating correlations in sensory inputs, leading to sparse coding (Olshausen & Field, 1996).
443   Indeed, it has been shown that hippocampal neurons in rodents become sparser with prolonged
444   exposure to the environment (Ratzon et al., 2023). Moreover, the storage of social memories in
445
      mice has been linked to high-dimensional representations in the hippocampus (Boyle et al., 2024).
      Importantly, sparsity has been shown to control a generalization-discrimination trade-off (Barak
446
      et al., 2013), which could explain why the hippocampus relies on sparse representations, well-suited
447
      for progressive discrimination of similar environments and events. Our study shows that smooth
448   place cell maps can coexist with and emerge from extremely high-dimensional sparse codes (Chettih
449   et al., 2023). We therefore predict that the dimensionality of the population code along the sensory
450   hierarchy should decrease to support the learning of invariant sensory representations (Froudarakis
451   et al., 2020), and then increase sharply at the apex, in the hippocampus, to enable the formation of
452   detailed memories based on the specific combination of such invariant representations. This role
453   of the hippocampus aligns with our observation of mixed selectivity, i.e., neurons learning similar
454   feature combinations (Figure 5c), which in turn has been proposed to enable high-dimensional
455   representations important for higher cognition areas (Fusi et al., 2016; Bernardi et al., 2020).
456
457   Circuit mechanisms underlying memory formation The surprising observation of zero-shot
458   learning of place cells (Figure 4) suggests that the sparse autoencoders learned to cluster similar
459
      samples onto single neurons in a generic manner. We hypothesize that the responsible circuits might
      correspond to known hippocampal processes, mainly pattern completion and pattern separation (Rolls,
460
      2013). On the one hand, neurons are pushed to collapse across-sample variability by clustering
461
      samples based on similarity, an effect akin to pattern completion. On the other hand, sparsity also
462   imposes sharp discontinuities between clusters in neuronal space, even when they might be close in
463   input space, a process akin to pattern separation. The combination of both processes is reminiscent
464   of the locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) algorithms used in the computer science field for fast image
465   search (Kulis & Grauman, 2009). Future work will shed light on the learned circuit mechanisms
466   behind such a LSH in sparse autoencoders, and their potential mapping to pattern separation and
467   completion.
468
469   Place cell over-representation near reward areas We have shown that the development of
470   localized receptive fields can be generalized to other input modalities, such as sound. Crucially, we
471   used this simplified framework to demonstrate that receptive field distribution tends to be modulated
472   by the input sampling distribution (Figure 5d). Importantly, it has been observed that the density of
473
      place fields increases near reward areas (Mamad et al., 2017). This has led researchers to seek external
      reward signals in the hippocampus that would modulate the place cell map (Kaufman et al., 2020).
474
      However, based on our results, we propose an alternative explanation based on oversampling: animals
475
      tend to spend more time within rewarded areas due to consummatory behaviors, hence biasing sensory
476   sampling and learning. Additionally, in line with this hypothesis, place cell trajectories leading to
477   rewards or goals tend to be replayed more often than unrewarded past trajectories (Ambrose et al.,
478   2016), which would further reinforce the sampling bias.
479
480   Biological plausibility Although it has been claimed that error backpropagation and gradient
481   descent are mechanisms that could be implemented in the brain (Lillicrap et al., 2020), particularly in
482   the hippocampus (Santos-Pata et al., 2021b), we believe that such strong assumptions are unnecessary
483   to map our model and observations to the real hippocampus. The orthonormal activity regularization
484   term used in our sparse autoencoders could be realized by combining strong lateral inhibition (pro-
485   moting pairwise decorrelation) and homeostatic plasticity (ensuring that neurons maintain equalized
      firing rates over time). Additionally, the orthonormal term can be thought of as sparse whitening


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      in ReLU-like neurons (i.e., pairwise decorrelation and variance normalization in the low-firing rate
487   regime), a mechanism proposed to be realized in the brain by an overcomplete basis of inhibitory
488   interneuron projections (Duong et al., 2023b;a; Lipshutz et al., 2022). Therefore, we contemplate
489   several alternative mechanisms whereby the main objective driving our sparse autoencoders could be
490   realized in brain circuits.
491
492   Limitations While our reinforcement learning experiments suggest that DQNs can make use of very
493   high-dimensional representations to solve complex tasks, the present study is limited in scope (Figure
494   6). We tested only a few tasks (four tasks within the Animal-AI testbed) and a single model (DQN).
495   To gain a more comprehensive understanding of the suitability of hippocampal-like representations
496   for behavioral and policy learning, further testing is required with a broader range of tasks and
497   models, especially in non-stationary environments where unseen samples are the norm. Additionally,
498
      future research should explore how these sparse autoencoders could enhance reinforcement learning
      algorithms that rely on discrete representations, potentially enabling algorithms based on, e.g., the
499
      successor representations (Dayan, 1993), to extend beyond simplified grid worlds.

788   Firing ratemaps To generate ratemaps from latent space activity, we first created a grid of 60 × 60
789   bins (or 30 × 30 for computing spatial information scores) for each neuron. For each bin in the grid,
790   we summed the neuron’s activity values for images sampled within that bin, generating an activity
791
      map in space. Then, an occupancy map was generated to account for the variability in the number
      of images sampled at each spatial bin (sampling density), which was used to normalize the values
792
      in the activity map. Finally, Gaussian smoothing was applied to each neuron’s normalized activity
793
      map, using a standard deviation of 3 bins. The resulting maps were normalized to their corresponding
794   maximum values, yielding smooth ratemaps representing spatially-distributed neural activity.
795
796
      Place field identification To identify and quantify place fields in each neuron’s ratemap, we first
797   binarized the ratemap by setting pixels with activity below 20% of the maximum activity to zero
798   (inactive bins) and those above to one (active bins). Clusters were identified by grouping adjacent
799   active bins, forming a cluster if a group of active bins was completely surrounded by inactive bins.
800   Clusters not meeting the size criteria for place cells (between 3% and 50% of the total number of
801   bins, 3600) were discarded. The remaining clusters were considered place fields.
802

      of the ratemap, denoted as p(r). Finally, we applied the formula introduced in Skaggs et al. (1992) to

861   performed PCA on Z and computed the linear fit of the resulting eigenspectrum in log-log space over
862   the range of the first 10 to 100 principal components. Since the exponent α provides an estimate of
863   how fast the population activity eigenspectrum decays as new dimensions are added, high α values
      are indicative of low-dimensional codes, whereas low α values indicate high-dimensional codes.

      A.1.4    R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING EXPERIMENTS
865
866   Animal-AI Testbed The Animal-AI testbed is a comprehensive platform designed for evaluating
867   the cognitive and learning capabilities of AI agents in a variety of tasks that simulate real-world
868   challenges (Beyret et al., 2019). This testbed provides diverse environments where agents must use
869   visual cues and navigate complex structures to achieve specific goals. The visual inputs from these
870
      environments are standardized to a resolution of 84 by 84 pixels, and agents can perform actions
      defined by a 2-dimensional vector of integers: the first component goes from 0 to 2 and corresponds
871
      to not moving, moving forward, or moving backwards, respectively; and the second component also
872
      goes from 0 to 2 and corresponds to not rotate, rotate left, or rotate right, respectively. To encourage
873   efficient behavior, a standard frameskip of 4 is applied, and the reward value decreases by 0.001 at
874   each step. Episodes terminate either when the agent obtains the reward or after 1000 frames.
875
876
      We evaluate our reinforcement learning agents using four distinct benchmarks within the Animal-AI
      testbed: the Double T-maze, Object Permanence, Cylinder, and Thorndike tasks. Each of these tasks
877
      presents unique challenges that require the agent to apply different strategies and cognitive abilities.
878
879           • Double T-maze. Each episode starts with the agent positioned randomly at one of the
880             corners of the maze, and the objective is to navigate to the center to obtain the reward. The
881             center contains the only positive reward (+3) available in the environment. Due to the high
882             and opaque maze walls, the agent cannot directly see the reward and must explore the maze
883             to find it.
884           • Object Permanence. At the beginning of each episode, the agent observes a large reward
885             (+3) falling behind a wall until it is completely occluded. The agent must then navigate to
886             the hidden reward, avoiding a small and visible reward (+1) along the way.
887           • Cylinder. This task involves an opaque cylinder with a medium-sized reward hidden inside.
888             The agent begins outside the cylinder and must navigate into the cylinder to obtain the
889             reward (+2).
890           • Thorndike. The task tests the agent’s ability to escape from a closed box to reach a reward
891             located outside the box. The box is semi-transparent, allowing the agent to see the reward
892             from inside. The only exit is blocked by a movable obstacle that the agent must push to
893             escape. A medium reward (+2) outside the box is the sole positive reward available.
894
895   Model To evaluate the performance of our sparse autoencoders in reinforcement learning scenarios,
896   we used a standard Deep Q-Network (DQN) architecture (Mnih et al., 2015) with modifications to
897   the input layer. Instead of feeding raw pixel data from the Animal-AI environments, we used the
898   compressed representations of 1000 units generated by the Visual Autoencoder (Vis-AE).
