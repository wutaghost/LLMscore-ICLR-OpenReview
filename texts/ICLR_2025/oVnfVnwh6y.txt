008                                              A BSTRACT
009
010
               Sequence parallelism (SP) serves as a prevalent strategy to handle long sequences
               that exceed the memory limit of a single device. However, for linear sequence
011
               modeling methods like linear attention, existing SP approaches do not take advan-
012
               tage of their right-product-first feature, resulting in sub-optimal communication
013            efficiency and usability. In this paper, we introduce Linear Attention Sequence
014            Parallelism (LASP), an efficient SP approach designed for linear attention-based
015            transformer models. Specifically, we design an efficient point-to-point ring-style
016            communication mechanism to leverage the right-product kernel trick of linear
017            attention, which sharply decreases the communication overhead, comparing with
018            existing SP methods. We enhance the computation efficiency of LASP by per-
019            forming kernel fusion and intermediate state caching, making the implementation
020            of LASP hardware-friendly on GPUs. Furthermore, we meticulously ensure the
021
               compatibility of sequence-level LASP with all types of batch-level data parallel
               methods, which is vital for distributed training on large clusters with very-long
022
               sequences. We also discuss the generalization of LASP on other linear sequence
023
               modeling methods. Extensive experiments on linear attention-based models are
024            conducted with varying sequence lengths from 2K to 4096K. LASP scales sequence
025            length up to 4096K on 128 GPUs, which is 8× longer than existing SP methods.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recently, linear-complexity sequence modeling methods (Qin et al., 2024a; 2022a; Choromanski
031   et al., 2022) are becoming increasingly popular due to their faster processing speed and comparable
032   modeling performance to vanilla Softmax attention-based transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017;
033
      Zeng et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a;b). As the size of large language models (LLMs) increases
      and sequence lengths extend, the capacity limitations of single GPU’s memory become a significant
034
      challenge, constraining the maximum sequence length manageable by a large model. To address
035
      this, Sequence Parallelism (SP) techniques (Li et al., 2022; Korthikanti et al., 2022) are employed,
036   which partition a long sequence into multiple sub-sequences to be processed on separate devices.
037   However, current implementations of SP methods do not fully exploit the right-product advantages
038   of linear-complexity attention mechanisms Qin et al. (2024b). This results in less than optimal
039   parallelism efficiency and reduced usability on linear sequence modeling methods.
040
      In this paper, we present Linear Attention Sequence Parallelism (LASP) approach for efficient SP
041
      on models with linear sequence modeling. Our approach takes linear attention (Katharopoulos
042   et al., 2020) as an instance to design a sophisticated point-to-point (P2P) ring-style communication
043   mechanism during both forward and backward among devices within a node or across multiple
044   nodes. This design maximizes the utilization of right-product kernel tricks in linear attention, by
045   only exchanging one single intermediate state instead of both of key and value states in other
046   counterparts. Notably, our approach is independent of attention heads partitioning, allowing it to be
047   applied to models with varying numbers or styles of attention heads, such as multi-head, multi-query,
048   and grouped-query attentions. This flexibility exceeds the capabilities of existing SP methods in
049   Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019; Korthikanti et al., 2022) or DeepSpeed (Jacobs et al., 2023).
050   Our implementation of LASP incorporates system engineering optimizations such as kernel fusion
051   and KV State caching, resulting in significantly enhanced execution efficiency. Furthermore, we
052   have taken great care in ensuring compatibility of LASP with various (sharded) distributed data-
053   parallel (DDP) (Li et al., 2020) training methods during the implementation, which we refer to as the
      data-sequence hybrid parallelism. Through extensive experiments with linear transformer models of


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      different parameter numbers, cluster sizes, and sequence lengths, we demonstrate the performance
055   and efficiency of LASP when used with different DDP instances. Specifically, LASP can extend
056   sequence length up to 4096K on 128 GPUs, which is 8× longer than existing SP methods.

058
      Our primary contributions can be summarized as follows:
059          • A new SP approach called LASP that is designed for linear sequence modeling methods.
060            LASP is able to perform sequence-level distributed training on 8× longer sequences than
061            existing SP methods while being significantly faster.
062          • Sequence length-independent communication overhead. Our proposed P2P ring-style com-
063            munication strategy leverages right-product kernel trick of linear attention to ensure that the
064
               exchanging of linear attention intermediate states is sequence length-independent.
             • GPU friendly implementation. We optimize the execution of LASP on GPU hardware
065
               through meticulous system engineering, including kernel fusion and KV State caching.
066
             • Data-parallel compatibility. LASP is compatible with all batch-level DDP methods, includ-
067            ing PyTorch/Legacy DDP, FSDP, and ZeRO-series optimizers.

071   2.1   P RELIMINARY
072
      Softmax Attention. Consider the standard attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) computation with causal
073
      masking in the transformer architecture, formulated as:
074                                                         √
075                                  O = Softmax(QK⊤ / d ⊙ M)V,                                     (1)
076
      where d denotes the hidden dimension. The matrices Q, K, V ∈ RN ×d represent query, key, and
077
      value matrices, respectively. These matrices are linear projections of the input X ∈ RN ×d , i.e.,
078
      Q = XWQ , K = XWK , V = XWV . The output matrix is denoted as O ∈ RN ×d , and
079
      M ∈ RN ×N represents the causal mask matrix. The Softmax(·) operation introduces quadratic time
080   complexity relative to the input sequence length N , limiting the scalability of vanilla transformers to
081   extended input sequences.
082
083
      Linear Attention. Linear attention is originally proposed in (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), with the
      elimination of Softmax operation (Vaswani et al., 2017). Qin et al. (2022a; 2024a) propose to replace
084
      the Softmax operation with a normalization operation Norm(·), which turns to
085
086                                       O = Norm((QK⊤ ⊙ M)V).                                           (2)
087
      When considering bidirectional tasks, the above formulation can be simplified as O =
088
      Norm((QK⊤ )V). Then by performing the associativity property of matrix products, it can be
089
      mathematically equivalently transformed into a right-product version:
090
091                                          O = Norm(Q(K⊤ V)).                                           (3)
092
      This linear attention formulation facilitates recurrent prediction with a computational complexity of
093
      O(N d2 ). And the recurrent update of K⊤ V without needing to compute the entire attention matrix
094   makes its inference efficient.
095
096   While linear complexity offers significant advantages in terms of computational efficiency and
097
      memory optimization for linear attention, it still incurs a proportional increase in computation and
      memory utilization on a single GPU as the sequence length N grows. This can lead to memory
098
      constraints on a single GPU, such as the 80GB limit in NVIDIA A100, for exceptionally long
099
      sequences. The challenge of achieving zero-redundancy (on sequence level) training for such
100   long sequences using linear attention-based LLMs across GPU clusters remains an open problem.
101   Furthermore, the complexity of addressing this issue in a casual setting further intensifies the
102   challenge. To address this, we propose LASP as a solution for parallelizing linear attention training
103   at the sequence level, even in a casual setting.

158   Data Distribution. LASP is designed for training long sequences on linear transformers in a
159   distributed environment, achieved by partitioning the input data along its sequence dimension. In
160   this situation, each GPU within the distributed environment undertakes the training of a subset of
161   sub-sequences, which serves to diminish the large memory footprint associated with activation during
      the training of long sequences. Communication operations are introduced between GPUs to transmit

      intermediate states. The final trained model assimilates the knowledge derived from the entirety of
163   the long sequences.

165   For an input sequence of length N , we establish its embedding space representation denoted as
166
      X ∈ RN ×d with a feature dimension of d. In the LASP framework, X is evenly partitioned into
      T chunks, where T is called the sequence parallel size, which must be divisible by the distributed
167
      world size W . These segmented data chunks are subsequently assigned to the respective GPUs. It is
168
      essential to note that different sequence parallel groups receive dissimilar data batches. However,
169   within the same group, all data chunks originate from an identical batch of data. A comprehensive
170   depiction of the data distribution process in LASP is provided in Algorithm 1.

      2.3   C OMPARISON
315
316   In LASP, it is important to note that the forward pass requires communication for the KV ∈ Rd×d
317   state in each linear attention module layer. The communication volume is determined by Bd2 /h,
318   where B is the batch size and h is the number of heads. In comparison, Ring Attention also adopts
319   P2P ring-style communication on states K, V ∈ RV ×d , which results a communication volume of
320   BN d/h. SP in Megatron-LM utilizes all-gather operations twice after two layer normalization layers
321   within each transformer layer, and a reduce-scatter operation after the attention and Feedforward
322   Neural Network (FFN) layers. This results in a communication volume of 2BN d + 4BN d/T .
323
      DeepSpeed uses all-to-all collective communication (Thakur et al., 2005) for input Q, K, V, and
      output O of each attention module layer, resulting in a communication volume of 4BN d/T .

335   It is worth to note that, although Ring Attention and LASP both use P2P ring-style communication,
336   they have differences lie in both communication and computation sides. Communication: In both
337   forward and backward, Ring Attention involves communicating two states K, V ∈ RV ×d . In contrast,
338   LASP only communicates one single state KV ∈ Rd×d , which does not depend on the sequence
339
      length. LASP has a lower theoretical communication complexity. This makes LASP more efficient,
      especially in environments with slower interconnects where the communication-computation overlap
340
      may not be optimal. Computation: Ring Attention is specifically designed for standard attention,
341
      utilizing a left-product manner, i.e., ((QK⊤ )V). On the other hand, LASP is specifically tailored
342   for linear attention-like sequence modeling methods, which leverages the right-product kernel trick
343   (Q(K⊤ V)) to achieve linear-time complexity.

      2.4   S YSTEM E NGINEERING O PTIMIZATION
346
347   Kernel Fusion. To improve the efficiency of LASP on GPU, we perform kernel fusion in both the
348   intra-chunk and inter-chunk computations, and also fused the updates of KV and dKV into the
349   intra-chunk and inter-chunk computations.
350
      KV State Caching. To avoid recomputing activation KV during the backward pass, we choose to
351   store it in the HBM of the GPU right after computing it in the forward pass. During the subsequent
352   backward pass, LASP directly accesses KV for use. It is important to note that the size of the KV
353   activation cached in HBM is d × d, which is not affected by the sequence length N . When the input
354   sequence length N is exceptionally large, the memory usage of KV becomes negligible.
355
356   2.5   H YBRID PARALLELISM
357
358   Data-Sequence Hybrid Parallelism. As illustrated in Fig. 2, LASP allows for the specification of
359   a smaller sequence parallel size that is divisible by the distributed world size. This configuration
360   results in the input data being split along both the batch and sequence dimensions, which is a type of
361   hybrid parallelism called data-sequence hybrid parallelism. The ZeRO-series optimizers (Rajbhandari
362   et al., 2020) in DeepSpeed and FSDP (Zhao et al., 2023) in PyTorch propose to distribute model
363
      states, which include optimizer states, gradients, and model parameters, across all GPUs within the
      distributed environment. As variants of data parallelism, these techniques seamlessly align with
364
      LASP. Furthermore, their focus on minimizing the memory of model states complements LASP’s
365
      objective of reducing activation memory on each GPU. By integrating these techniques, the training
366   of large models handling long sequence lengths is rendered more practical.
367
368   Compatibility with Tensor Parallelism and Pipeline Parallelism. LASP supports both tensor
369
      parallelism (TP) and pipeline parallelism (PP). In the context of PP, as exemplified by the GPipe (Kim
      et al., 2020) scheduling method, the model is initially partitioned across multiple devices, with each
370
      device holding a segment of the model. Data within a mini-batch is then divided into micro-batches,
371
      which are sequentially fed into the device containing the first segment. Each device processes its
372   micro-batch and forwards the output to the next device in the sequence, simultaneously preparing to
373   receive and process the subsequent micro-batch from the preceding device. This method of pipelining
374   inputs effectively minimizes device idle times. When LASP is integrated with PP, micro-batches are
375   substituted with sub-sequences derived from a mini-batch. Unlike standard PP, each device retains
376   the intermediate states (KV in the forward pass and dKV in the backward pass) locally, rather than
377   transmitting them to the next device as typically done in LASP alone. For TP, the integration with
      LASP is fluid. Linear attention layers utilize TP to segment matrix operations across both intra-chunk


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      and inter-chunk computations, whereas the handling of MLP layers under TP remains unchanged.
379   The experiment tests on hybrid of LASP, DP, TP and SP will be conducted in the future work.

      3     R ELATED W ORK
382
383   Linear Attention. Linear Transformer models bypass the use of Softmax attention by adopting
384   various approximation methods (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Choromanski et al., 2020; Peng et al.,
385   2021; Qin et al., 2022b;a) instead. The central concept involves using the "kernel trick" to speed up
386   the calculation of the attention matrix, specifically by multiplying keys and values before tackling
387   the computationally intensive n × n matrix multiplication. For instance, Katharopoulos et al.
388
      (2020) use 1 + elu activation function, Qin et al. (2022b) utilizes the cosine function to imitate
      Softmax characteristics, and Choromanski et al. (2020); Zheng et al. (2022; 2023) leverage sampling
389
      techniques to closely replicate the Softmax process are all strategies employed to achieve this.
390
391   Memory-Efficient Attention. Rabe & Staats (2021) first employs the online Softmax technique to
392   efficiently compute numerically stable attention scores sequentially, resulting in a linear memory
393   for attention, yet still needs quadratic time complexity. While FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022;
394
      Dao, 2023) employs tiling to minimize the number of memory reads/writes between GPU’s high
      bandwidth memory (HBM) and on-chip SRAM to reduce time and memory in the training process,
395
      PagedAttention (Kwon et al., 2023) optimizes the utilization of the KV cache memory by reducing
396
      waste and enabling adaptable sharing among batched requests during inference. Ring Attention (Liu
397   et al., 2023) reduces memory requirements for Transformer models when handling long sequences
398   by distributing sequences across multiple devices and overlapping the communication of key-value
399   blocks with blockwise attention computation.
400
      Sequence Parallelism. SP as a widely used method to train long sequences has been integrated
401
      into many large model training frameworks, including Megatron-LM, DeepSpeed, and Colossal-AI.
402
      Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019) implements SP along with model (tensor) parallelism (MP) to
403   perform large matrix multiplications on GPUs. However, MP partitions the attention heads, which
404   limits the maximum parallelism degree to be less than the number of attention heads. DeepSpeed-
405   Ulysses (Jacobs et al., 2023) uses an all-to-all communication primitive to reduce communication
406   volume, but also partitions attention heads and faces similar issues as Megatron-LM.

      5    D ISCUSSION
508   Linear-complexity sequence modeling methods are emerging as important alternatives to traditional
509   transformers (using Softmax attention) for next-generation foundational models due to their sig-
510   nificantly faster training and inference times, coupled with performance that rivals conventional
511   approaches. Recently, the AI community has seen a rapid development of novel linear-complexity
512   models, gaining considerable interest. Examples include linear attention models such as TransNormer-
513
      LLM, state space models (SSM) like Mamba and Jamba, and linear RNN models including RWKV,
      HGRN, and Griffin. We contend that the LASP design can be seamlessly integrated into most
514
      linear-complexity models. To underscore LASP’s generalization, we use a generalized form of linear
515
      attention in Appendix A.4 (Qin et al., 2024b), demonstrating that the majority of linear-complexity
516   models can be accommodated within this broad framework.
517
518   6    C ONCLUSION
519
520   We presented LASP to effectively address the limitations of existing SP methods on linear-complexity
521   sequence modeling methods by leveraging their right-product features, which significantly enhanced
522
      communication and parallelism efficiency. Through the design of an efficient P2P ring-style com-
      munication mechanism and elaborated engineering optimizations including kernel fusion and KV
523
      state caching, LASP achieved a notable reduction in communication traffic and improved hardware
524
      utilization on GPU clusters. Compatibility with all types of batch-level DDP methods ensured the
525   practicability of LASP for large-scale distributed training with very-long sequences. Our experiments
526   highlighted the advantages of LASP on scalability, speed, memory usage and convergence perfor-
527   mance. In specific experimental setup, LASP achieves significant faster sequence-level distributed
528   training speed on a maximum 8× longer sequence length than the out-of-the-box SP methods.

803
      The training configuration is set with specific hyperparameters: a learning rate of 0.0005 to control
804   the optimization step size, a cap of 50,000 updates to define the training duration, and a 2,000-update
805   warmup period to stabilize early training by gradually adjusting the learning rate. Additionally,
806   a weight decay rate of 0.01 is used for regularization to avoid over-fitting. The Adam optimizer,
