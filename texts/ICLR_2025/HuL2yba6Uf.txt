009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Disentanglement, or identifying salient statistically independent factors of the data,
012            is of interest in many areas of machine learning and statistics, with relevance
013            to synthetic data generation with controlled properties, robust classification of
014            features, parsimonious encoding, and a greater understanding of the generative pro-
015            cess underlying the data. Disentanglement arises in several generative paradigms,
016            including Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), Generative Adversarial Networks and
017            diffusion models. Particular progress has recently been made in understanding dis-
018            entanglement in VAEs, where the choice of diagonal posterior covariance matrices
019
               is proposed to promote mutual orthogonality between columns of the decoder’s
               Jacobian. We continue this thread to show how this linear independence translates
020
               to statistical independence, completing the chain in understanding how the VAE’s
021
               objective identifies independent components of, or disentangles, the data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Variational Autoencoders (VAEs, Kingma (2013); Rezende et al. (2014)) and a range of variants, e.g.
      β-VAE (e.g. Higgins et al., 2017) and Factor-VAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018), have been shown empirically
028
      to disentangle latent factors of variation in the data. For example, a trained VAE may generate face
029
      images that vary in distinct semantically meaningful ways, such as hair colour or facial expression, as
030   individual latent variables are adjusted. This is both of practical use, e.g. for controlled generation of
031   synthetic data with chosen properties, and intriguing as it is not knowingly designed into the training
032   algorithm. A related phenomenon is observed in samples from a Generative Adversarial Network
033   (GAN), which, in common with a VAE, applies a deterministic neural network function to samples of
034   independently distributed latent variables, producing a push-forward distribution.
035
      Understanding why disentanglement arises, seemingly “for free”, is of interest since identifying and
036   separating generative factors underlying the data goes to the heart of many aspects of machine learning,
037   from classification to generation, interpretability to identifiability, right down to a fundamental
038   understanding of the data itself. With a better appreciation of why disentanglement happens, we
039   might be able to induce it more reliably, particularly in domains where we cannot easily perceive
040   when features are disentangled, as we can for images and text.
041
      Research into the cause of disentanglement has gradually led to a refined understanding of what is
042   meant by “disentanglement”, which typically refers to the separation of semantically meaningful
043   generative factors (Bengio et al., 2013). Recent progress has been made towards understanding
044   why disentanglement occurs in VAEs, tracing the root cause to the common use of diagonal poste-
045   rior covariance matrices, a seemingly innocuous design choice made for computational efficiency
046   (Rolinek et al., 2019; Kumar & Poole, 2020). Diagonal covariances are shown to promote orthogo-
047   nality between columns of the Jacobian of the decoder, a property linked to disentangled features
048   (Ramesh et al., 2018) and independent causal mechanisms (Gresele et al., 2021). We extend this
049   line of work by providing a firmer basis for the covariance-orthogonality relationship together with
050
      theoretical analysis to show how orthogonality in the Jacobian translates to disentanglement in
      the push-forward distribution of a VAE, connecting linear independence of partial derivatives to
051
      statistical independence of components, or generative factors, of the data.
052
053   Interest in understanding how disentanglement arises in VAEs has increased as their generative quality
      has improved (e.g. Hazami et al., 2022) and they often a key component in state of the art diffusion

184   4    F ROM O RTHOGONALITY TO D ISENTANGLEMENT
185
186   Having clarified the connection between diagonal posterior covariance and column-orthogonality of
187
      the Jacobian, we now develop our main result to show why such orthogonality causes disentanglement.
      This relates two different notions of “independence”: orthogonality pertains to linear independence, a
188
      geometric property, while disentanglement relates to statistical independence of factors/components
189
      of a distribution. These concepts do not always go hand-in-hand and it is not immediately clear why
190   a column-orthogonal Jacobian implies that different latent dimensions correspond to statistically
191   independent, often semantically meaningful, factors of variation in the data.
192
193
      The generative model of a VAE can be decomposed into stochastic and deterministic steps: sample
      the prior z ∼ p(z); apply a deterministic function x̂ = µ ◦ d(z) (composing the decoder d : z 7→ θ and
194                              .
      mean function µ : θ 7→ x̂ = E[x|z]); and add element-wise noise, x ∼ p(x|x̂). For continuous data,
195
      e.g. images, element-wise noise often serves only as “blur” and is omitted when generating synthetic
196   data. Thus, samples come from the “push-forward” distribution of mean parameters p(x̂) over a
197   d-dimensional manifold defined by the decoder. Note that if data are truly generated under the VAE

      5     I NTERPRETING β OF β-VAE
408
409   We now consider the role of β parameter in the β-VAE objective (Eq. 1), which is empirically
410   observed to affect disentanglement (Higgins et al., 2017). Previous works interpret β as re-weighting
411   the KL and reconstruction components of the ELBO, or serving as a Lagrange multiplier for a KL
412   “constraint”. We provide an interpretation more in keeping with the original ELBO.
413
      To model data from a given domain, a (β-)VAE requires a suitable likelihood pθ (x|z), e.g. a Gaussian
414
      likelihood for coloured images, and a Bernoulli for black and white images where pixel values
415   xk ∈ [0, 1] are bounded (Higgins et al., 2017) . In the Gaussian case, dividing Eq. 1 by β shows that
416   training a β-VAE with encoder variance Var[x|z] = σ 2 is equivalent to a VAE with Var[x|z] = βσ 2
417   and adjusted learning rate (Lucas et al., 2019). We now interpret β for other likelihoods.
418
      In the Bernoulli example mentioned above, black and white image pixels are not strictly black or
419
      white (xk ∈ {0, 1}) and may lie between (xk ∈ [0, 1]), hence the Bernoulli distribution appears invalid
420
      as it does not sum to 1 over the domain of xk . That is, unless each sample is treated as the mean x̄ of
421   multiple (true) Bernoulli samples. Multiplying the likelihood by a factor κ > 1 is then tantamount to
422   scaling the number of observations as though each were made κ times, lowering the variance of the
423                                κ→∞
      “mean” observation, Var[x̄] −→ 0.7 Thus, multiplying the KL term by β in a β-VAE, or equivalently
424   dividing the likelihood by β, amounts to scaling the likelihood’s variance by β, just as in the Gaussian
425   case: higher β corresponds to lower κ (“fewer observations”) and so higher likelihood variance.
426   Since the argument holds for any exponential family likelihood, we have proved
427   Theorem 3 (β-VAEσ2 ≡VAEβσ2 ). If the likelihood pθ (x|z) is of exponential family form, a β-VAE
428   with Var[x|z] = σ 2 is equivalent to a VAE with Var[x|z] = βσ 2 .

462   model the data distribution, p(x|z) = p(x), leaving latent variables redundant (Bowman et al., 2015).
463   Corollary 3.1 (β < 1). Setting β < 1 is expected to mitigate posterior collapse.
464   Proof. From Theorem 3, β < 1 reduces Var[x|z], constraining the distributional family that pθ (x|z)
465   can describe. For some β, Var[x|z] < Var[x] and so p(x) ̸= pθ (x|z), ∀θ, making PC impossible.

468   6    R ELATED W ORK
469
470   Many works study aspects or variants of VAEs, or disentanglement in other modelling paradigms.
471
      Here, we review those that offer insight into understanding the underlying cause of disentanglement
      in VAEs. Higgins et al. (2017) first showed that disentanglement is enhanced by increasing β in
472
      Eq. 1, and Burgess et al. (2018) hypothesised that diagonal posterior covariances may be the cause,
473
      encouraging latent dimensions to align with generative factors of the data. Rolinek et al. (2019)
474   empirically showed and theoretically supported a link between diagonal posterior covariances and
475   orthogonality in the decoder Jacobian, deemed responsible for disentanglement. Kumar & Poole
476   (2020) simplified and generalised the argument. These works demonstrate that diagonal posteriors
477   provide an inductive bias that breaks the rotational symmetry of an isometric Gaussian prior, side-
478   stepping impossibility results related to independent component analysis (e.g. Locatello et al., 2019).
479
      Several works investigate analytically tractable linear VAEs (Lucas et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2020;
480   Koehler et al., 2022). Zietlow et al. (2021) show that disentanglement is sensitive to perturbations
481   to the data distribution. Reizinger et al. (2022) relate the VAE objective to independent mechanism
482   analysis (Gresele et al., 2021), which encourages column-orthogonality in the mixing function
483   of ICA, similarly to that induced implicitly in the decoder of a VAE. Ramesh et al. (2018) trace
484   independent factors by following leading left singular vectors of the Jacobian of a GAN generator. In
485   the opposite direction, Chadebec & Allassonnière (2022) trace manifolds in latent space by following
      a locally averaged metric derived from VAE posterior co-variances. Pan et al. (2023) claim that the


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      data manifold is identifiable from a geometric perspective assuming Jacobian-orthogonality, which
487   differs to our focus on statistical independence. More recently, Bhowal et al. (2024) consider the
488   encoder/decoder dissected into linear and non-linear aspects, loosely resembling our view of the
489   Jacobian in terms of its SVD. However, the decoder function is quite different to its Jacobian and
490   dissecting a function into linear and non-linear components is not well defined whereas an SVD is
491   unique.
492
      Recently, Buchholz et al. (2022) analysed several function classes identifiable by Independent
493   Component Analysis (ICA), including conformal maps. This relates closely to our analysis of a
494   decoder with column-orthogonal Jacobian (§4.2), which is a conformal map (see Def. 2, Buchholz
495   et al., 2022). Conformal maps are proved to be identifiable in abstract via Moebius transforms,
496   whereas we give a constructive proof for VAEs in terms of the SVD of the decoder’s Jacobian.
497   Combining these appears a promising direction to better understand the interplay between stochastic
498   and deterministic approaches to learning latent generative factors.

500   7    C ONCLUSION
501
502   Unsupervised disentanglement of independent factors of the data is a fundamental aim of machine
503   learning and significant recent progress has been made in the case of VAEs. We extend that work
504   by showing: (i) that the previously proposed approximate relationship can be defined precisely;
505   and (ii) that the choice of diagonal posterior covariances in a VAE causes statistically independent
506   components of the data to align with distinct latent variables of the model, i.e. disentanglement. In
507   the process, we provide a novel yet straightforward interpretation of β in a β-VAE, which plausibly
508   explains why increasing β promotes disentanglement but degrades generation quality; and why
509   decreasing β mitigates posterior collapse. We also supplement the proof of orthogonality by showing
510
      that the likelihood’s Hessian is necessarily encouraged to be diagonal and giving a detailed analysis
      of the Jacobian’s optimal singular values.
511
512   Neural networks are often considered too complex to explain, yet recent advances make their
513   deployment in everyday applications all but inevitable. Improved theoretical understanding is
514   therefore essential to be able to confidently take full advantage of machine learning progress in
515   non-trivial and potentially critical systems, and we believe that the body of work that we add to here
516
      is a useful step. Interestingly, our approach rests on the fact that, regardless of the model’s complexity,
      its Jacobian, which transforms the density of the prior, can be considered in relatively simple terms.
517
518   Not only is a better understanding of VAEs of interest in itself, VAEs are often part of the pipeline in
519   recent diffusion models that achieve state-of-the-art generative performance (e.g. Pandey et al., 2022;
520   Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022). Other recent works show that supervised learning (Dhuliawala
521   et al., 2023) and self-supervised learning (Bizeul et al., 2024) can be viewed from a latent variable
522
      perspective and trained under a suitable variant of the ELBO, connecting VAEs to other learning
      paradigms in a common mathematical language.
523
524   One limitation of our work and of current understanding more generally is that disentanglement
525   is observed in VAEs with non-Gaussian likelihoods (Higgins et al., 2017), whereas current work,
526   including ours, focus predominantly on the Gaussian case. We plan to address this in future work.
