009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            With the increasing use of GPS technology, path has become essential for appli-
012            cations such as navigation, urban planning, and traffic optimization. However,
013            obtaining real-world path presents challenges due to privacy concerns and the dif-
014            ficulty of collecting large datasets. Existing methods, including count-based and
015            deep learning approaches, struggle with two main challenges: handling complex
016
               distributions of road segments and ensuring global coherence in generated paths.
               To address these, we introduce DiffPath, a path generation model based on Latent
017
               Diffusion Models (LDMs). By embedding path into a continuous latent space and
018
               leveraging a transformer architecture, DiffPath captures both local transitions and
019            global dependencies, ensuring the generation of realistic paths. Experimental re-
020            sults demonstrate that our model outperforms existing approaches in generating
021            paths that adhere to real-world road network structures while maintaining privacy.

104
105   2.1      PATH G ENERATION FOR PATTERN M INING
106
107   Many methods have been developed to apply for path pattern mining. These approaches can broadly
      be categorized into two main categories: count-based and deep learning-based models.


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Early models primarily relied on count-based methods, such as Markov chain models, which gen-
109   erate paths by capturing local state dependencies (Sutton et al., 1999). These models assume that
110   the probability of transitioning to the next road segment depends solely on the current road segment,
111   effectively modeling short-term dependencies. However, in the large state spaces typical of urban
112   road networks, many state transitions are rare or absent from the training data, leading to sparsity
113   issues. To mitigate this, hierarchical hidden states were introduced to reduce state space complexity
114   and alleviate sparsity (Baratchi et al., 2014). Despite these improvements, count-based models still
115   predominantly rely on information from the current and neighboring states, making it difficult to
116   capture global dependencies and ensure long-range path consistency.
117   With advances in machine learning and reinforcement learning, deep learning-based models have
118   become increasingly prominent in path generation. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have
119   been used to enhance path generation by integrating complex reward functions, improving both the
120   diversity and quality of generated paths (Choi et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2017). However, GAN-based
121   models often require large amounts of data and can struggle with the stability of training. Re-
122   searchers have also increasingly turned to sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) models for path genera-
123
      tion (Wu et al., 2017). These models show promise for modeling long paths by encoding sequences
      of road segments and iteratively predicting subsequent road segments. However, adapting seq2seq
124
      models to respect the topological constraints of road networks presents a significant challenge. Ar-
125
      chitectures based on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Rao et al., 2020) have been explored to
126   capture sequential dependencies, but they often suffer from the vanishing gradient problem when
127   handling long sequences, limiting their ability to capture global path information.
128
129
      To address some of these limitations, discrete diffusion models have been introduced (Shi et al.,
      2024). These models represent road network topology using adjacency matrices during the diffusion
130
      process, which is particularly effective for conditional task generation. However, in unconditional
131
      generation tasks, maintaining symmetry in the adjacency matrix can inaccurately represent one-way
132   streets as bidirectional, introducing bias and inaccuracies in the generated paths.
133
134
135   2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS
136
137
      The diffusion model, initially introduced as a probabilistic generation framework (Sohl-Dickstein
138   et al., 2015), operates through two continuous processes: a forward process that gradually perturbs
139   the data distribution by adding multiscale noise and a reverse process that reconstructs the data
140   by learning its underlying distribution (Ho et al., 2020). Subsequent advances have significantly
141   improved the quality of the generated samples and accelerated the sampling process. Further in-
142   novations include the introduction of a non-Markovian diffusion process (Song et al., 2021; Nichol
143   et al., 2022), which reduces the number of sampling steps, and the approach of learning the vari-
144   ance in the reverse process to further streamline sampling. In addition, structural optimizations have
145   been performed in reverse denoising neural networks to enhance the quality of the generated sam-
146
      ples (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021). As a cutting-edge generative model, the diffusion model has out-
      performed other models in various tasks, including computer vision (Rombach et al., 2022), natural
147
      language processing (Li et al., 2022), multimodal learning (Liu et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2022),
148
      and traffic prediction (Wen et al., 2024). Recent research has used diffusion models to generate
149   synthetic data to improve datasets in specific domains (Zhu et al., 2024a;b), such as the generation
150   of spatio-temporal trajectory data. Despite their widespread application, diffusion models have not
151   been extensively explored for path generation. This is primarily due to the distinct nature of the
152   task: path generation requires adherence to road network topology and sequential constraints (Wang
153   et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024), which are fundamentally different from the data structures handled in
154   typical computer vision or natural language tasks.

517   6     C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we propose a path generation method based on a latent diffusion model (DiffPath).
520
      Our approach leverages the latent diffusion model’s strength in generating discrete data and the
      transformer’s ability to learn sequence features effectively. Specifically, real paths are first embedded
521
      into a latent space, where they undergo a forward noise trajectory process, gradually transforming
522
      them into random noise. The model then applies reverse trajectory denoising to reconstruct the paths
523   from noise and finally decodes the synthesized paths. The effectiveness of DiffPath is validated
524   through extensive experiments. Further experimental results show that the paths generated by the
525   model align well with the statistical characteristics of real-world paths.
