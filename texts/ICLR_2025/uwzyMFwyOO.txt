009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks use relational information as an inductive bias to enhance
012            prediction performance. Not rarely, task-relevant relations are unknown and graph
013            structure learning approaches have been proposed to learn them from data. Given
014            their latent nature, no graph observations are available to provide a direct training
015            signal to the learnable relations. Therefore, graph topologies are typically learned
016            on the prediction task alongside the other graph neural network parameters. In this
017            paper, we demonstrate that minimizing point-prediction losses does not guarantee
018
               proper learning of the latent relational information and its associated uncertainty.
               Conversely, we prove that suitable loss functions on the stochastic model outputs
019
               simultaneously grant solving two tasks: (i) learning the unknown distribution of
020
               the latent graph and (ii) achieving optimal predictions of the model output. Finally,
021            we propose a sampling-based method that solves this joint learning task. Empiri-
022            cal results validate our theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the
023            proposed approach.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Relational information processing has provided breakthroughs in the analysis of rich and complex
029   data coming from, e.g., social networks, natural language, and biology. This side information takes
030   various forms, from structuring the data into clusters to defining causal relations and hierarchies, and
031   enables machine learning models to condition their predictions on dependency-related observations.
032   In this context, predictive models take the form y = fψ (x, A), where the input-output relation
033
      x 7→ y – modeled by fψ and its parameters in ψ – is conditioned on the relational information
      encoded in variable A. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) [Scarselli et al., 2008] are one example
034
      of models of this kind that rely on a graph structure represented as an adjacency matrix A and
035
      have been demonstrated successful in a plethora of applications [Fout et al., 2017; Shlomi et al.,
036   2020]. Throughout this paper, we focus on predictors where A is an adjacency matrix, although the
037   theoretical results we develop are valid for A being any discrete latent random variable.
038
039
      Indeed, relational information is needed to implement such a relational inductive bias and, in some
      cases, it is provided at the application design phase. However, more frequently, such topological
040
      information is not rich enough to address the problem at hand, and – not seldom – it is completely
041
      unavailable. Therefore, Graph Structure Learning (GSL) emerges as an approach to learn the graph
042   topology [Kipf et al., 2018; Franceschi et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2021; Fatemi et al., 2021; Zhu et al.,
043   2021; Cini et al., 2023] alongside the predictive model fψ . This entails formulating a joint learning
044   process that learns the adjacency matrix A – or a parameterization of it – along with the predictor’s
045   parameters ψ. This can be achieved by optimizing a loss function, e.g., a point prediction measure
046   based on the square or the absolute prediction error.
047
      Different sources of uncertainty affect the graph structure learning process, including epistemic un-
048   certainty in the data and variability inherent in the data-generating process. Learning appropriate
049   models of the data-generating process can provide valuable insights into the modeled environment
050   with uncertainty quantification enhancing explainability and interpretability, ultimately enabling
051   more informed decision-making. Examples of applications are found in the study of infection and
052   information spreading, as well as biological systems [Gomez Rodriguez et al., 2013; Lokhov, 2016;
053   Deleu et al., 2022]. It follows that a probabilistic framework is appropriate to accurately capture the
      uncertainty in the learned relations whenever randomness affects the graph topology. Probabilistic

054
      approaches have been devised in recent years. For instance, research carried out in Franceschi et al.
055   [2019]; Zhang et al. [2019]; Elinas et al. [2020]; Cini et al. [2023] propose methods that learn a
056   parametric distribution PAθ over the latent graph structure A. However, none of them have studied
057   whether these approaches were able to learn a calibrated latent distribution PAθ , properly reflecting
058   the uncertainty associated with the learned topology.
059
      In this paper, we address the joint problem of learning a predictive model yielding optimal point-
060
      prediction performance of the output y and, contextually, a calibrated distribution for the latent
061
      adjacency matrix A. In particular, the novel contributions can be summarized as:
062
063         1. We demonstrate that models trained to achieve optimal point predictions do not guarantee
064            calibration of the adjacency matrix distribution [Section 4].
065
            2. We provide theoretical conditions on the predictive model and loss function that guarantee
066            both distribution calibration and optimal point-predictions [Section 5].
067
            3. We propose a theoretically grounded sampling-based learning method to address the joint
068
               learning problem [Section 5].
069
070         4. We empirically validate the theoretical developments and claims presented in this paper and
071            show that the proposed method is indeed able to solve the joint learning task [Section 6].

073   2   R ELATED WORK
074
075   Graph Structure Learning GSL is often employed end-to-end with a predictive model to better
076   solve a downstream task. Examples include applications within graph deep learning methods for
077   static [Jiang et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2021; Kazi et al., 2022] and temporal data [Wu et al., 2019;
078   2020; Cini et al., 2023; De Felice et al., 2024]; a recent review is provided by Zhu et al. [2021].
079   Some approaches from the literature model the latent graph structure as stochastic [Kipf et al., 2018;
080   Franceschi et al., 2019; Elinas et al., 2020; Shang et al., 2021; Cini et al., 2023], mainly as a way to
081
      enforce sparsity of the adjacency matrix. To operate on discrete latent random variables, Franceschi
      et al. [2019] utilize straight-through gradient estimations, Cini et al. [2023] rely on score-based
082
      gradient estimators, while Niepert et al. [2021] design an implicit maximum likelihood estimation
083
      strategy. A different line of research is rooted in graph signal processing, where the graph is es-
084   timated from a constrained optimization problem and the smoothness assumption of the signals
085   [Kalofolias, 2016; Dong et al., 2016; Mateos et al., 2019; Coutino et al., 2020; Pu et al., 2021]. A
086   few works from the Bayesian literature have tackled the task of estimating uncertainties associated
087   with graph edges. The model-based approaches by Lokhov [2016]; Gray et al. [2020] are two ex-
088   amples tackling relevant applications benefiting from uncertainty quantification. Within the deep
089   learning literature, Zhang et al. [2019] propose a Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) modeling the
090   random graph realizations. Differently, Wasserman & Mateos [2024] develop a BNN designed over
091   graph signal processing principles. While some results on the output calibration are exhibited, to
092
      the best of our knowledge, no guarantee or evidence of calibration of the latent variable is provided,
      which we study in this paper instead.
093
094   Calibration of the model’s output Research on model calibration has primarily focused on ob-
095   taining accurate and consistent predictions of the statistical properties of the target (random) vari-
096   ables y, from which uncertainty estimates on the model’s predictions are derived. For discrete
097   outputs, such as in classification tasks, Guo et al. [2017] investigated the calibration of modern deep
098   learning models and proposed temperature scaling as a solution. Other techniques in the same con-
099   text include Histogram Binning [Zadrozny & Elkan, 2001], Cross Entropy loss with label smoothing
100   [Müller et al., 2019], and Focal Loss [Mukhoti et al., 2020]. For continuous output distributions,
101
      Laves et al. [2020] proposed σ scaling, while Kuleshov et al. [2018] developed a technique inspired
      by Platt scaling. More recently, conformal prediction techniques [Shafer & Vovk, 2008] have gained
102
      popularity for providing confidence intervals in predictions. We stress that within this paper, we are
103
      mainly concerned with latent variable calibration, rather than output calibration, although the two
104   are related to each other.
105
106   Deep latent variable models Latent variables are extensively used in deep generative modeling
107   [Kingma & Welling, 2013; Rezende et al., 2014], both with continuous and discrete latent variables
      [Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Bartler et al., 2019]. In deep models, latent random variables often


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      lack direct physical meaning, with only the outputs being collected for training. Therefore, studies
109   mainly focused on maximizing the likelihood of the observed outputs in the training set, rather
110   than calibrating the latent distribution. A few works proposed regularization of the latent space to
111   improve stability and accuracy [Xu & Durrett, 2018; Joo et al., 2020], facilitate smoother transitions
112   in the output when the latent variable is slightly modified [Hadjeres et al., 2017], and apply other
113   techniques aimed at enhancing data generation or improving model performance in general [Connor
114   et al., 2021].
115   To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has studied the joint learning problem of calibrating the
116   latent graph distribution while achieving optimal point predictions.

508   7   C ONCLUSIONS
509
510   Graph structure learning has emerged as a research field focused on learning graph topologies in
511   support of solving downstream predictive tasks. Assuming stochastic latent graph structures, we
512   are led to a joint optimization objective: (i) learning the correct distribution of the latent topology
513   while (ii) achieving optimal predictions on the downstream task. In this paper, at first, we prove
514   both positive and negative theoretical results to demonstrate that appropriate loss functions must be
515   chosen to solve this joint learning problem. Second, we propose a sampling-based learning method
516   that does not require the computation of the predictive likelihood. Our empirical results demonstrate
517   that this approach achieves optimal point predictions on the considered downstream task while also
518
      yielding calibrated latent graph distributions.
519   Finally, we acknowledge that the proposed method requires sampling and processing multiple ad-
520   jacency matrices for each input and, although the model and prediction accuracy is enhanced, a
521   computation overhead is requested. We plan future research to explore the applicability of this
522   method to other classes of neural networks beyond GNNs.
