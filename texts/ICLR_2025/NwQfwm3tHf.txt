011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
               Large pre-trained models perform well on many Federated Learning (FL) tasks.
013
               Recent studies have revealed that fine-tuning only the final layer of large pre-trained
014
               models can reduce computational and communication costs while maintaining high
015            performance. We can model the final layer, which typically performs a linear
016            transformation, as a Generalized Linear Model (GLM). GLMs offer advantages in
017            statistical modeling, especially for anomaly detection. Leveraging these advantages,
018            GLM-based methods can be utilized to enhance the security of the fine-tuning
019            process for large pre-trained models. However, integrating GLMs with large pre-
020            trained models in FL presents challenges. GLMs rely on linear decision boundaries
021            and struggle with the complex feature representation spaces from pre-trained mod-
022            els. To address this, we introduce the Hierarchy Statistical Network (HStat-Net).
023
               HStat-Net refines the spaces to make them more discriminative, allowing GLMs
               to work effectively in FL. Based on HStat-Net, we further develop F ED RACE to
024
               detect poisoning attacks using deviance residuals from GLMs. We also provide a
025
               theorem to support F ED RACE’s detection. Extensive experiments conducted on
026            CIFAR-100, Food-101, and Tiny ImageNet demonstrate that F ED RACE signifi-
027            cantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art defense algorithms.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large pre-trained models, such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021), have played a significant role in
033
      computer vision by demonstrating strong adaptability across various tasks. These models are usually
      fine-tuned on centralized servers, where data from multiple clients is collected to build a powerful
034
      global model. However, this centralized approach raises privacy concerns, as clients are often
035
      reluctant to share their data. Federated Learning (FL) addresses these concerns by allowing clients to
036   train models locally without transferring raw data to a central server. Instead, only model updates are
037   shared and aggregated, ensuring a privacy-preserving training mechanism McMahan et al. (2017);
038   Nguyen et al. (2021). This method has proven particularly effective in privacy-sensitive fields such as
039   healthcare and activity monitoring Yu et al. (2020); Khan et al. (2021); Cui et al. (2021).
040
      Despite the advantages, integrating large pre-trained models into FL is challenging due to the
041
      limited computation and communication resources of mobile devices, making full model fine-tuning
042   impractical. An efficient solution is to adapt only the final layer or the last few layers of the pre-trained
043   model. In transfer learning, a common strategy is to replace the pre-trained model’s classification head
044   with a task-specific layer. For example, in Vision Transformer (ViT) models, the classification head
045   is typically replaced by a linear layer to adapt to new datasets Dosovitskiy et al. (2021). Similarly,
046   in BERT-based natural language processing tasks, both full model fine-tuning and feature-based
047   approaches, in which a simple classification layer is added while keeping the pre-trained parameters
048   frozen, have demonstrated effectiveness Devlin et al. (2018). In FL, adapting only the final layer
049   reduces both computational and communication overhead while still leveraging the knowledge
050
      embedded in the pre-trained model Kornblith et al. (2019). Recent research further suggests that only
      training the classifier can yield good performance in FL Legate et al. (2024).
051
052   Since the final layer is typically a fully connected layer that performs a linear transformation, we can
053   model this transformation using a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) McCullagh & Nelder (1989);
      Emami et al. (2020). GLMs offer several benefits in statistical modeling, particularly in anomaly

124   2.2   G ENERALIZED L INEAR M ODEL
125
      Generalized linear models extend traditional linear regression by allowing the response variable
126
      to follow distributions from the exponential family, such as binomial, Poisson, or multinomial
127   distributions. In GLMs, the expected value of the response variable is related to the linear predictors
128   through a link function McCullagh & Nelder (1989). In GLMs, the response variable Y is modeled as
129   a linear combination of explanatory variables R, with wh representing the task-specific parameters
130   of h. The link function g(·) relates the expected value of Y to the linear predictor:
131
                                                g(E[Y ]) = R⊤ wh .                                          (2)
132
133   Here, R denotes the representations extracted by the pre-trained model, and Y represents correspond-
134
      ing target variable, such as class probabilities. In multi-class classification, the inverse link function
      g −1 is typically the softmax function, which maps linear predictors to class probabilities.
135
136   Integrating GLMs with large pre-trained models in federated learning provides advantages like
137   flexible feature-output modeling, and enhanced anomaly detection. However, a key limitation is
138   that GLMs rely on linear decision boundaries, making them less effective in capturing complex,
139   non-linear relationships embedded in the data. When class separation in the feature representation
140
      space is unclear, GLMs may struggle with outlier detection, leading to biased model parameters and
      distortion towards extreme values Montgomery et al. (2021).

143   3     H IERARCHY S TATISTICAL N ETWORK
144
145   We introduce a Hierarchy Statistical Network (HStat-Net) to integrate large pre-trained models with
146   GLMs. This architecture enables efficient and secure fine-tuning for large pre-trained models, as
147   shown in Figure 1. HStat-Net consists of three key components:
148   Pre-trained Feature Extractor (ϕ) The first component, the pre-trained feature extractor ϕ, processes
149   raw input data x into an initial feature representation vector: z = ϕ(x). During federated learning,
150   the parameters of ϕ remain frozen to reduce computational costs.
151
      Statistical Net (s) The second component, the statistical net s, refines the representation vector z
152   into a more discriminative representation: r = s(z). Here, r serves as the explanatory variables R in
153   Equation 2.
154
155
      Task Net (h) The final component, the Task Net h, performs the downstream task, such as classifica-
      tion, using the representation r: ŷ = h(r). Thus, for client i, the complete HStat-Net is:
156
157                                      ŷi = hi (si (ϕi (x))) = ψi (ϕ(x)),                                (3)
158   where ψi denotes the combined operations of hi and si . To reduce model complexity and enhance
159   privacy, the statistical net s performs dimensionality reduction from z ∈ RD to r ∈ Rd , where
160   d ≪ D. During the training process, only h and s are trained locally. Clients send their trained
161   hi and si to the central server, where they are aggregated with equal weights to obtain the globally
      updated ψ = h ◦ s, following a process similar to FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017).

      4.1   ATTACKS IN F EDERATED L EARNING
237
238   Federated learning is vulnerable to various poisoning attacks, which can severely degrade the global
239   model’s performance. These attacks are typically classified based on the adversary’s intent:
240
             • Untargeted attacks Baruch et al. (2019); Fang et al. (2020); Xie et al. (2020); Shejwalkar &
241
               Houmansadr (2021): The adversary seeks to degrade the overall performance of the global
242
               model, reducing its accuracy (ACC) across all inputs. ACC is defined as:
243
244                                          ACC = E(x,y)∼D [P(G(x) = y)],                                (6)
245
               where x is the input, y is the true label, and G(x) is the global model’s prediction.
246
             • Targeted attacks Bhagoji et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2021): Here, the
247
               adversary focuses on specific samples or classes, aiming to reduce the model’s accuracy for
248            those targets. The effect of the attack is measured by the attack success rate (ASR):
249
250                                    ASR = E(x,y)∼D,y=yattack [P(G(x) = y target )].                    (7)
251
             • Backdoor attacks Bagdasaryan et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020a); Xie et al. (2019);
252            Nguyen et al. (2022); Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov (2021): The adversary introduces a hidden
253            malicious sub-task, causing the model to misclassify inputs containing specific triggers into
254            a designated class y target . The effectiveness is measured by backdoor accuracy (BA):
255
                                    BA = E(x,y)∼D, y=yattack [P(G(Tri (x)) = y target )],                 (8)
256
257            where Tri (·) is the trigger function that embeds the backdoor into the input x.
258
259
      Adversary’s capability The adversary can control up to M out of N clients, with M/N < 50%, as
      exceeding this threshold would undermine Byzantine-robust aggregation rules Fang et al. (2020);
260
      Shejwalkar & Houmansadr (2021). In each communication round, the central server randomly selects
261
      a subset of clients N (t) , and the number of malicious clients m in this subset can vary. The adversary
262
      has full access to the global model parameters and can execute attacks through the clients under their
263   control Xie et al. (2018); Bhagoji et al. (2019); Bagdasaryan et al. (2020); He et al. (2020).
264
265   Adversary’s knowledge The adversary operates with limited knowledge, unaware of the aggregation
266
      rule or gradient updates from benign clients. They only have access to information about the malicious
      clients under their control, making the scenario more realistic.
267
268   Despite the robustness of pre-trained large models, traditional poisoning attacks can still be highly
269   effective, even when only task-specific layers are fine-tuned Kurita et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021).
      When the parameters of the pre-trained model ϕ are frozen and only h is updated, attackers can exploit

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      this to manipulate model behavior without substantially affecting the overall model. Our experiments
271   on CIFAR-100 show that an untargeted attack Xie et al. (2020) reduces the model’s accuracy from
272   75.99% to 64.24%. To counter these attacks in federated learning, we propose F ED RACE (Federated
273   Representation-based Adaptive Client Evaluation), which leverages the properties of GLMs in
274   HStat-Net to detect and remove them during global aggregation of ψ = h ◦ s on the central server.

480
481   We propose HStat-Net, an architecture trained using a novel two-step method, which bridges large
482   pre-trained models with GLMs. Building on HStat-Net, we develop F ED RACE, which leverages
483   GLM-based statistical methods to detect poisoning attacks in federated learning. However, HStat-Net
484   is currently tailored for classification tasks in FL, posing challenges for extending to other tasks, such
485   as text generation. Additionally, the refined representation space is not ideally linearly separable,
      resulting in higher false positive rates that require further improvement.

      Here, we present the baseline attacks and defense algorithms used to evaluate our proposed method.
781
782
      A.3.1    BASELINE ATTACKS
783
784   Min-Max Attack Shejwalkar & Houmansadr (2021): This model poisoning attack targets FL by
785   crafting malicious gradients that maximize damage to the global model. The attack minimizes the
786   maximum distance between benign and malicious gradients, ensuring that malicious updates remain
787   close to benign ones to evade detection by robust aggregation algorithms while still degrading the
788   model’s accuracy.
789   Inner Product Manipulation Attack Xie et al. (2020): This attack compromises Byzantine-tolerant
790   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms by manipulating the inner product between the true
791   gradient and the aggregated gradient. Adversaries design Byzantine gradients so that the inner
792   product becomes negative, disrupting the descent direction of SGD and leading to a decline in model
793   performance.
794   Targeted Label Flipping Attack Tolpegin et al. (2020): This attack aims to misclassify samples
795   from a specific source class to a target class. Malicious clients flip the labels of samples from the
796   source class to the target class in their local datasets and train their models accordingly, causing the
797   global model to misclassify these samples.
798
      Edge-Case Backdoor Attack Wang et al. (2020a): Edge-case backdoors involve altering label
799   data points that are typically correctly classified by the model but are rare or unlikely to appear in
800   regular training or testing data. These backdoors activate only under specific, uncommon conditions,
801   making them hard to detect during standard evaluations. This stealthy approach allows the attacker to
802   maintain the model’s performance on typical data while embedding a functional backdoor.
803
      Distributed Backdoor Attack Xie et al. (2019): In this attack, the adversary divides the trigger
804
      pattern into multiple parts, with each client injecting a partial trigger into a subset of their training
805   samples.
806
807   A.3.2    BASELINE D EFENSES
808
809   FLShield Kabir et al. (2024): FLShield is a validation-based defense framework for FL that protects
      against poisoning attacks. It addresses the validation subject dilemma and the validation integrity

      dilemma by generating representative models from local updates. FLShield uses a new metric called
811   Loss Impact Per Class (LIPC) to validate these models and filter out malicious updates, ensuring
812   robust defense against various attacks, including untargeted poisoning, targeted label flipping, and
813   backdoor attacks.
814
      FedRoLA Yan et al. (2024): FedRoLA is a robust FL defense algorithm designed to protect against
815
      model poisoning attacks through layer-based aggregation. It analyzes the similarity of updates at each
816
      layer of a deep neural network using cosine similarity metrics to detect malicious updates. FedRoLA
817   employs a dynamic layer selection and aggregation process that improves threat detection while
818   maintaining model performance.
819
820
      FLAIR Sharma et al. (2023): FLAIR is a defense mechanism for FL systems that protects against
      model poisoning attacks by detecting abnormal patterns in client gradient updates, focusing on
821
      changes in gradient direction (flip-score). It assigns reputation scores to each client based on these
822
      patterns and adjusts their contributions to the global model accordingly. FLAIR is effective against
823   advanced untargeted model poisoning attacks, such as directed deviation attacks, and can defend
824   against both white-box and adaptive attacks. However, it relies on a predefined reputation decay
825   parameter, which controls the weight given to past versus current client behavior.
826
      Trimmed-mean Yin et al. (2018); Xie et al. (2018): Trimmed-Mean is a robust aggregation algorithm
827
      used in FL to defend against malicious or outlier updates. It processes each dimension of the input
828
      gradients separately by removing the largest and smallest β values from the set of local model updates
829   for each dimension. After removing these extremes, it computes the mean of the remaining n − 2β
830   values for that dimension, which becomes the corresponding dimension in the global model update.
831   This method provides resilience against outliers, but its effectiveness depends on choosing the correct
832   β, which may vary with the proportion of malicious clients.
833
      Multi-krum Blanchard et al. (2017): Multi-Krum is a robust aggregation algorithm designed to
834
      reduce the impact of malicious updates in FL. Building on the original Krum algorithm, Multi-Krum
835   selects gradients that are closest to their n − m − 2 neighbors based on squared Euclidean distance,
836   where n is the number of participating clients and m is an upper bound on the number of malicious
837   clients. Selected gradients are added to a selection set and removed from the pool of gradients. This
838   process continues until a specified number of gradients are selected, which are then averaged to form
839   the global model update.
