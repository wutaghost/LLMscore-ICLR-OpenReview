009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series data is ubiquitous and appears in all fields of study. In multivariate
012            time series, observations are interconnected both temporally and across compo-
013            nents. For instance, in traffic flow analysis, traffic speeds at different intersections
014            exhibit complex spatiotemporal correlations. Modelling this dual structure poses
015            significant challenges. Most existing forecasting methods tackle these challenges
016            by separately learning spatial and temporal dependencies. In this work, we intro-
017            duce Temporal Graphormer (T-Graphormer), a Transformer-based approach de-
018
               signed to model spatiotemporal correlations directly. Extending the Graphormer
               architecture to incorporate temporal dynamics, our method updates each node rep-
019
               resentation by selectively attending to all other nodes within a graph sequence.
020
               This design enables the model to capture rich spatiotemporal patterns with mini-
021            mal reliance on predefined spacetime inductive biases. We validate the effective-
022            ness of T-Graphormer on real-world traffic prediction benchmark datasets, achiev-
023            ing up to 10% reductions in both root mean squared error (RMSE) and mean ab-
024            solute percentage error (MAPE) compared to state-of-the-art methods.

062   processing (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al., 2016; Radford et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2018), has
063   shifted from traditional machine learning methods to non-linear deep learning models. With growing

065   capable of learning representations that capture the complex dynamics of multivariate time series,
066   leading to more accurate predictions. Many researchers have employed convolutional modules to
067   address spatial and temporal dependencies separately (Li et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019;
068   2020). By interleaving temporal learning modules with spatial learning modules, these approaches
069
      facilitate the exchange of features between the time domain and the graph domain, enabling the
      decoupled modelling of spatiotemporal dependencies.
070
071   In this paper, we investigate whether directly modelling spatiotemporal data within a single unified
072   framework leads to superior representation learning compared to approaches that use separate spa-
073   tial and temporal learning modules. To do so, we explore variants of the Transformer architecture.
074   Although originally designed for sequential data such as text and speech, Transformers have shown
075
      great promise in handling more complex data structures through additional encoding techniques. For
      instance, architectures like Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021) and Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovit-
076
      skiy et al., 2020; Feichtenhofer et al., 2022) have successfully adapted these techniques to recover
077
      and utilize structural information when data is flattened and treated sequentially.
078
079   Building on this foundation, we introduce T-Graphormer (Temporal-Graphormer), which extends
080   the encoding techniques of Graphormer into the temporal dimension. This approach allows
081
      T-Graphormer to leverage the global attention mechanism inherent in Transformers to capture spatial
      and temporal relationships simultaneously. Our extensive experiments on real-world traffic fore-
082
      casting datasets demonstrate that T-Graphormer achieves state-of-the-art performance, surpassing
083
      existing methods by a significant margin. Furthermore, through ablation studies, we identify which
084   encoding methods in T-Graphormer are responsible for its predictive abilities. These results high-
085   light the potential of using Transformers as a unified framework for spatiotemporal modelling.

      In this section, we first review recent advancements in spatiotemporal forecasting. Then, we examine
      the relevant literature on the adoption of Transformers for various applications.

093   2.1   T RAFFIC P REDICTION
094
      Traditional methods for time series modelling have long been foundational, with Autoregressive
095
      Moving-Average (ARMA) being a cornerstone linear model for stationary processes. To accom-
096   modate non-stationary time series, Autoregressive Integrated Moving-Average (ARIMA) was intro-
097   duced (Box et al., 2015; Brockwell & Davis, 2002). Many have extended these models for multi-
098   variate time series. For instance, Lippi et al. (2013) and Williams & Hoel (2003) evaluated several
099   statistical algorithms on traffic forecasting, including ARIMA with Kalman filters to estimate unob-
100   served variables (the moving average components) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) models.
101
      As deep learning showed tremendous success in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and computer
102   vision, many borrowed ideas and architectures from these fields for traffic forecasting. Yu et al.
103   (2018) proposed Spatio-Temporal Graph Convolutional Networks (STGCN), which uses Convolu-
104   tional Neural Network (CNN) architectures to extract graphical and temporal features in traffic data.
105   Each layer in STGCN contains a “sandwich” structure with two gated sequential convolution layers
106   and a spatial graph convolution layer in between. Around the same time, Li et al. (2018) developed
107   Diffusion Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network (DCRNN). Inspired by the work by Atwood &
      Towsley (2016), it captures spatial dependencies using diffusion convolution, which models traffic


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      flow as a diffusion process characterized by random walks. DCRNN then captures temporal de-
109   pendencies with Recurrent Neural Network (RNN). Subsequently, Wu et al. (2019) created Graph
110   WaveNet, which combines graph convolutional networks with the WaveNet architecture (Oord et al.,
111   2016). In WaveNet, dilated causal convolutions (Yu & Koltun, 2016) are used to expand the recep-
112   tive field for capturing historical temporal context. These convolutions are efficient, requiring fewer
113   layers by skipping inputs based on a dilation factor that typically grows exponentially with each
114   layer. Dilated convolutions are also utilized in Multivariate Time Series Forecasting with Graph
115   Neural Networks (MTGNN) by Wu et al. (2020).
116   With the introduction of the attention mechanism in Transformers, many models began integrating
117   it into their spatial and temporal learning modules. For instance, Attention-based Spatial-Temporal
118   Graph Convolutional Network (ASTGCN) (Guo et al., 2019) integrates convolutional networks with
119   temporal and spatial attention modules to selectively focus on critical information. Similarly, Zheng
120   et al. (2020) proposed GMAN, which uses an autoencoder architecture where each block integrates
121   spatial and temporal attention mechanisms.
122   More recently, Jiang et al. (2023) and Liu et al. (2023) introduced PDFormer and STAEformer, re-
123   spectively. Both models utilize Transformer-based encoders for representation learning and fully
124   connected layers for prediction. PDFormer incorporates meticulously designed self-attention mod-
125   ules, whereas STAEformer employs learnable spatiotemporal embeddings. Our proposed model,
126   T-Graphormer, aligns with STAEformer’s minimal reliance on architectural changes. However, T-
127   Graphormer fundamentally differs in its approach to information processing. Unlike models with
128   separate temporal and spatial learning modules, T-Graphormer enables all nodes to attend to one
129
      another and leverages the Transformer’s inherent global attention capacity to learn coupled spa-
      tiotemporal dependencies simultaneously.
130
131   Most of the models we have discussed so far interleave temporal learning modules with spatial
132   learning modules, transferring features between the time domain and the graph domain. In another
133   direction, Spatial-Temporal Synchronous Graph Convolutional Network (STSGCN) simultaneously
134   captures spatial and temporal dependencies using a novel synchronous graph convolution operation.
135
      This is done by adding positional embeddings and connecting the nodes across time steps, improving
      the model’s ability to handle complex and evolving traffic conditions (Song et al., 2020). While
136
      this approach aligns with our goal of avoiding separate spatial and temporal learning modules, it
137
      is important to note that STSGCN treats the input as a graph and uses GCN for message-passing.
138   In comparison, T-Graphormer processes the input as a sequence. This distinction underscores a
139   fundamental difference in how the two models integrate spatial and temporal information.

      2.2   T RANSFORMERS
143
144
145   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) were originally designed for NLP applications, addressing limi-
146   tations of earlier models like RNNs (Rumelhart et al., 1986) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)s
147
      (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), which struggled with long-range dependencies and training is-
      sues such as vanishing and exploding gradients (Pascanu et al., 2013). In RNN-based models, infor-
148
      mation from previous time steps is stored in a hidden state, requiring computational effort propor-
149
      tional to the distance between signals to relate them effectively. Transformers mitigate this challenge
150   with an attention mechanism that processes the entire sequence as input, enabling tokens to interact
151   directly in a constant number of operations. The multi-head attention mechanism empowers Trans-
152   formers to model long-range dependencies and extract meaningful features, making them versatile
153   for various data types, including text (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022),
154   images (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), and graphs (Ying et al., 2021; Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kreuzer
155   et al., 2021).
156   In NLP, Devlin et al. (2018) demonstrated that masking random tokens in sequences enables Bidirec-
157   tional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) to capture contextual information from
158   both preceding and succeeding text. Since Transformers apply the attention mechanism indepen-
159   dently to each token, they can fully leverage parallel computing. This capability allows pre-training
160   on massive datasets, scaling to billions of parameters. As a result, pre-trained Transformers general-
161   ize effectively to downstream tasks like sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, and question
      answering (Radford et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020).


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The success of Transformers in NLP inspired researchers to adapt the architecture to other data
163   modalities. However, because Transformers are inherently designed for linear sequences, modifi-
164   cations are needed to accommodate non-text inputs. Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) proposed the Vision
165   Transformer (ViT), which extends Transformers to images. ViT transforms images into a sequence
166   of patches (or uses CNN patches directly), maps these patches to latent vectors via a trainable linear
167   projection (patch embedding), and incorporates positional embeddings to preserve spatial structure.
168   Unlike CNNs, ViTs have minimal image-specific inductive biases, such as convolutional kernels,
169   while leveraging global attention mechanisms to scale efficiently.

315   4.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
316
317   To project the learned node representations from the encoder to the output space, we explore two
318   architectures. In the first, a vanilla setup, we use two linear layers to project the hidden representation
319   at the last layer Hk ∈ Rl×d from d dimensions to d2 and then to C. In the second setup, we introduce
320
      additional dilated causal convolutional layers (Yu & Koltun, 2016). This imposes a time bias that
      ensures future predictions are based solely on representations from previous time steps. The output
321
      from these convolutional layers is then projected to C dimensions using the same linear layers.
322

404   5.2     S ETTINGS
405
406   We conduct extensive experiments to investigate how different hyperparameters influence the per-
407   formance of T-Graphormer. Specifically, we evaluate 2 different Transformer configurations with
408   d = {128, 384}, k = {6, 10}, and number of heads = {4, 8}. These configurations are chosen to
409
      maximize the available hardware memory. As detailed in section 4.3, we test 2 distinct prediction
      layer architectures. For dilated causal convolutions, we select a dilation factor of 2 and use 3 layers.
410
411   Training of T-Graphormer is guided by minimizing the mean squared error (MSE) between predicted
412   values Ŷ and ground truth Y for T = 12 only. When evaluating the prediction performance on
413   horizons 3 and 6, we simply remove the redundant token values. We employ the AdamW optimizer
414   (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with an effective batch size of 128, using a cosine decay learning rate
415   schedule with warmup (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). After training, model configurations with the
416   lowest mean absolute error (MAE) on the validation dataset are selected for testing. T-Graphormer
417
      is implemented in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and utilizes distributed data parallelism to speed
      up training and increase batch size.
418
419   T-Graphormer is compared against the following baseline methods: 1. Vector Autoregressive (VAR)
420   (Zivot & Wang, 2006), 2. Support Vector Regression (SVR) (Smola & Schölkopf, 2004), 3. FC-L-
421   STM (Sutskever et al., 2014), 4. Graph Multi-Attention Network (GMAN) (Zheng et al., 2020),
422   5. STGNN is Enhanced by scalable time series Pre-training model (STEP) (Shao et al., 2022),
423
      6. PDFormer (Jiang et al., 2023), 7. STAEformer (Liu et al., 2023). Additional baselines, including
      DCRNN, STGCN, Graph WaveNet, MTGNN, ASTGCN, and STSGCN, are discussed in section
424
      2.1. It is worth noting that STEP leverages masked pre-training on long-term historical time series
425
      data, enabling models to capture long-term dependencies.
426
427   For more information on experimental settings, see Appendix at section A.

430
431   T-Graphormer demonstrates exceptional performance in long-range traffic prediction. As shown
      in Table 2, T-Graphormer outperforms all other models across every metric when predicting the

      next 12 time steps (1-hour window). Notably, with minimal spacetime inductive bias, it achieves a
433   10.00% reduction in RMSE on the PEMS-BAY dataset and a 10.40% reduction in MAPE on the
434   METR-LA dataset compared to the state-of-the-art model STEP. We attribute this success to the
435   global receptive field provided by the self-attention mechanism, a key feature of Transformers.
436
      However, T-Graphormer underperforms in short-range traffic prediction. When forecasting the next
437
      15 minutes, it consistently lags behind STEP across all metrics. One possible explanation is that
438
      T-Graphormer’s reliance on structural encoding methods is limiting its ability to capture local space-
439   time patterns which are critical for short-term predictions. Another factor could be overfitting, as
440   the model is optimized by backpropagation on the prediction error for the next hour (Horizon 12),
441   and short-range predictions (Horizon 3 and 6) are evaluated by masking the extra tokens.
442
      We observe that T-Graphormer performed differently across the two datasets (see Table 6) as the
443
      prediction task on METR-LA is harder. In Figure 5, we see that despite overfitting, the training loss
444
      of the best model is much lower in PEMS-BAY (9.27) than in METR-LA (55.28). The difficulty
445   of the task is further illustrated in Figure 4 where the validation MAPE is around 4 for PEMS-
446   BAY but increased to around 9 for METR-LA. This is unsurprising since the traffic speed standard
447   deviation (19.49) of METR-LA is much higher than that of PEMS-BAY (9.44), meaning there is
448   more variability in the road conditions of the Los Angeles traffic grid.
449
      Additionally, when comparing the training and validation loss over epochs in Figure 5, the 2 loss
450
      curves follow much more closely in PEMS-BAY than in METR-LA, a sign that the model is sig-
451   nificantly overfitting on METR-LA. From the dataset side, this can be explained by the fact that
452   PEMS-BAY has 50% more samples than METR-LA (52116 vs. 34727), which allows the model to
453   fully learn the spatiotemporal relationships between flattened tokens on the PEMS-BAY dataset.
454
      This overfitting behaviour is inspected from another perspective in Figure 6. Between the 3 model
455
      sizes, although the initial loss differences appear random, a trend emerges towards the end of train-
456
      ing, where the bigger the model, the bigger the loss difference between training and validation. This
457   overfitting behaviour is consistent with the recent findings on the empirical scaling laws for training
458   LLM (Kaplan et al., 2020). Using equation (6.6) from this work: D ∝ N 0.74 , we find that the traffic
459   prediction dataset sizes are only optimal for training the mini models, but too small for the larger
460   models and cause overfitting.

508   6   C ONCLUSION
509
510
511   We introduce a novel framework for modelling multivariate time series. By leveraging structural
512   encoding methods and extending them along the temporal dimension, we show that the Transformer
513
      architecture can be directly applied to spatiotemporal data without separate temporal and spatial
      learning modules. This integration allows T-Graphormer to capture spatiotemporal dependencies
514
      simultaneously with minimal spacetime inductive bias. Building on this foundation, future work can
515
      readily incorporate domain knowledge into the architecture, such as using adaptive spatiotemporal
516   embeddings from STAEformer and custom spatial attention mechanisms from PDFormer.
517
518   However, our study has certain limitations that merit discussion. While T-Graphormer exhibits
519
      strength in modelling spatiotemporal data, it incurs high memory demands due to its O(n2 ) com-
      plexity. Flattening the entire time series into a sequence significantly increases context length as
520
      more historical data is included. For example, in the PEMS-BAY dataset, adding one additional
521
      time step increases the context length by 325. This dramatically impacts memory usage, constrain-
522   ing T-Graphormer’s applicability to large networks or longer time windows. For instance, training
523   T-Graphormer on the large-scale traffic forecasting dataset LargeST (Liu et al., 2024), which in-
524   cludes up to 8600 nodes, poses significant challenges. However, incorporating techniques such as
525   sparse attention (Beltagy et al., 2020) could mitigate this issue.
526
      Additionally, this work focuses on applying T-Graphormer to static graphs, where the graph structure
527
      remains constant over time. Dynamic graphs, where the graph structure evolves, present a promising
528   area for extension. For instance, Shang et al. (2021) demonstrated that graph learning techniques
529   can effectively re-parameterize dynamic graphs for forecasting tasks. Adapting T-Graphormer to
530   dynamic graphs would require strategies such as introducing a maximum graph size and applying
531   padding to handle structural changes over time. These enhancements can expand T-Graphormer’s
532   applicability to a broader range of spatiotemporal forecasting tasks and provide deeper insights into
533   its graph representation learning capabilities. Extending T-Graphormer to other data modalities with
534   grid-like structures presents another exciting avenue. Potential applications include video data (Han
535   et al., 2022) and geographical measurements (Bi et al., 2023).
536   Transformer’s success in NLP can be attributed to its self-supervised pre-training technique (Devlin
537   et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2018; 2019). Following the approaches of Feichtenhofer et al. (2022) and
538   Shao et al. (2022), T-Graphormer can be readily utilized as a masked autoencoder on spatiotemporal
539   data. Our preliminary results indicate that masked pre-training can improve T-Graphormer training
      stability and reduce training time (see Figure 7).

724   We also use the training dataset to Z-score normalize the entire dataset. In cases where the time of

726   enrich the input.
727
      Besides the details mentioned in the main text, we also use gradient accumulation to increase batch
728   size. This results in an effective batch size that is calculated by multiplying the original batch size by
729   the number of GPUs (graphics processing units) used in distributed data parallelism and the number
730   of accumulated gradient iterations. We also utilize layer-wise learning rate decay following (Raffel
731   et al., 2020), where the learning rate decays exponentially in earlier layers. This is typically used to
732   stabilize training when fine-tuning Transformer models. We find using gradient clipping and dropout
733   improves T-Graphormer training as well (Srivastava et al., 2014).
734   We summarize our training configurations in Tables 4 and 5. All training has been done on SLURM
735   workload manager environments. For T-Graphormer mini and small models (including causal mini
736   and causal small), training was done on 4 compute nodes. Each compute node has 187 gigabytes of
737   memory, 2 Intel Silver 4216 Cascade CPUs (central processing units), and 4 NVIDIA V100 Volta
738   GPUs with 32 gigabytes of memory. For training the medium-sized models, we used 4 compute
739   nodes with 498 gigabytes of memory, 2 AMD Milan 7413 CPUs, and 4 NVIDIA A100SXM4 GPUs

      Table 6. We confirm that on both datasets, models with added causal dilated convolution in their
834   final prediction layers scale poorly. Specifically, the “causal mini” models consistently outperform
835   the “causal small” models which outperform the “causal medium” model. This might be due to
836   the added spatiotemporal bias in dilated causal convolution, which leads to easier overfitting as
837   the model size grows. Another possible explanation is that since we fix the number of dilated
838   convolution layers (3 layers with a dilation factor of 2) across model sizes, the larger embedding
839   sizes in these models may require more dilated convolution layers to fully capture the context of the
840   learned representations. In other words, the number of dilated convolution layers should grow with
841   the embedding size.
842   Conversely, models with a simple linear final prediction layer scale better in the PEMS-BAY dataset
843   where the “medium” models perform the second best, and the “small” models perform the third
844   best. However, this comparison is not entirely fair since there are inconsistencies in our training
845   configurations for medium-sized models. Specifically, we found that early-stopping and training the
846   medium-sized models for 30 epochs instead of 50 works better on the PEMS-BAY dataset.
847   Finally, T-Graphormer prediction layer variants perform differently on the two datasets. The best-
848   performing model on METR-LA utilizes causal dilated convolutions in its prediction layer. While
849   this increases the number of parameters, we theorize that the additional temporal bias introduced
850   by causal convolutions improves the model’s prediction accuracy under limited training samples.
851   However, it remains unclear why linear prediction layers perform poorly on the METR-LA dataset
852   and why casual prediction layers perform poorly on the PEMS-BAY dataset.
853   The difficulty of predicting traffic conditions in the METR-LA dataset is also compounded by the
854   presence of missing values. This is evident in Figures 3a and 3b. In Figure 3b, the traffic speed
855   across all sensors abruptly drops to zero at approximately hours 8, 30, and 34. Such abrupt drops
856   are absent in the PEMS-BAY dataset. Although we did not directly download the dataset, the simul-
857   taneous changes across all sensors in the METR-LA dataset strongly indicate missing values rather
858   than traffic jams or road blockages.
