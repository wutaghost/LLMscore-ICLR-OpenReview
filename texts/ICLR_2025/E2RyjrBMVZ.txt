009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Evaluation benchmarks are the cornerstone of measuring capabilities of large
012            language models (LLMs), as well as driving progress in said capabilities. Originally
013            designed to make claims about capabilities (or lack thereof) in fully pretrained
014            models, evaluation benchmarks are now also extensively used to decide between
015            various training choices. Despite this widespread usage, we rarely quantify the
016            variance in our evaluation benchmarks, which dictates whether differences in
017            performance are meaningful. Here, we define and measure a range of metrics
018            geared towards measuring variance in evaluation benchmarks, including seed
019
               variance across initialisations, and monotonicity during training. By studying a
               large number of models – both openly available and pretrained from scratch – we
020
               provide empirical estimates for a variety of variance metrics, with considerations
021
               and recommendations for practitioners. We also evaluate the utility and tradeoffs
022            of continuous versus discrete performance measures and explore options for better
023            understanding and reducing this variance. We find that simple changes, such as
024            framing choice tasks (like MMLU) as completion tasks, can often reduce variance
025            for smaller scale (∼7B) models, while more involved methods inspired from
026            human testing literature (such as item analysis and item response theory) struggle
027            to meaningfully reduce variance. Overall, our work provides insights into variance
028            in evaluation benchmarks, suggests LM-specific techniques to reduce variance,
029            and more generally encourages practitioners to carefully factor in variance when
030
               comparing models.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Evaluation benchmarks are the cornerstone of establishing and defining progress with large language
036   models (LLMs). Virtually any new model release is accompanied by a range of scores on common
037   evaluation benchmarks, illustrating how the model tallies up against previous releases (Mesnard et al.,
038   2024; AI@Meta, 2024; Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024). As such, evaluation benchmarks
039
      play an important role in claiming progress and the title of state-of-the-art. Consequently, choices
      in model development are often based on how they impact performance on benchmarks considered
040
      important by the field, giving benchmarks a prominent role in model iteration as well. Yet, despite
041
      their importance, benchmark scores are often regarded as a point estimate, and it is rare that they are
042   given a more detailed consideration. While it is well known that benchmarks scores can be heavily
043   influenced by the choice of prompt (Sclar et al., 2023), the distributions of labels in the provided
044   few-shots (Weber et al., 2023) or even the symbols that are used for the different options in a multiple
045   choice setup (Zheng et al., 2023; Alzahrani et al., 2024), papers rarely report more than a single
046   number per benchmark, or specifics on how each number was computed. Furthermore, statistical
047   significance values are scarcely reported on major release papers or leaderboards, or even in papers
048   that study how scores vary across various dimensions. These issues muddy the power of evaluation
049   benchmarks, both during development and evaluation: if we cannot ‘trust’ our evaluation results or do
050
      not understand what improvements are statistically significant, we cannot make sound comparisons,
      thus making it more challenging to reliably use benchmarks during model development.
051
052   To address this, we present a deep dive into variance in benchmark scores, at much larger scale than
053   any previous work. Across all our experiments, we consider 13 different popular benchmarks and
      compute their performance over 280 different models, including fully trained public models as well


                                                         1
054
      as a set of 7B models and their intermediate checkpoints that we trained from scratch, differing only
055   in their initialisation random seed.
056
057   With this, our contributions are three-fold:
058         1. We provide a comprehensive reference guide for what magnitudes of variance are expected
059            for what benchmarks across various circumstances.
060         2. We make suggestions of how variance can be reduced for smaller scale models on choice
061            tasks of important value (MMLU).
062         3. We caution against the use of efficient benchmarking methods like item analysis and item
063            response theory as a means of reducing cost when doing pre-training ablations, as the
064            methods often lead to increased variance (and thus less power in comparisons as compared
065            to using the full benchmark).
066
      Our work brings to light the often overlooked problem of variance in evaluation benchmarks, quanti-
067   fies its effects, and provides a set of positive and negative results on how to mitigate it.

      3    H OW MUCH VARIANCE DO WE OBSERVE ?
104
      We first investigate how much variance there is across different models and datasets. We define a
105
      range of metrics for quantifying different kinds of variance.
106
107   First, using the 7B models we trained ourselves, we consider variance due to changes in seed, across
      otherwise identical setups. This seed variance gives us a metric useful for performing data ablations


                                                        2
108
      – to conclude that pretraining dataset or hyperparameter set B is better than pretraining dataset or
109   hyperparameter set A, we would want the benchmark performance increase to be larger than that due
110   to random seed variance across different models trained in setup A. To this end, we also compute a
111   benchmark’s monotonicity, quantifying how stably performance on it develops during training.
112
      To ground the seed variance numbers, we compare them with bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals
113
      on individual models, as well as observed variance across different setups. In all our experiments, we
114
      consider both the (discrete) metric preferred for the benchmark and a more continuous representation
115   for the same task.

253   3.3   T HE CURIOUS CASE OF MMLU
254
255   Motivated by prior work considering the inconsistency of multiple choice benchmarks (Wang et al.,
256   2024; Alzahrani et al., 2024), we examined two formulations of MMLU: (Standard) MMLU and
257   MMLU-Cloze.
258   Standard MMLU refers to the prompting format where the choices along with the choice texts are
259   present for the few-shot examples as well as the question in the prompt text. To evaluate the sample,
260   we append the choice letters (“A”, “B”, “C”, or “D”) at the end of the prompt text, and pick the choice
261   that has the lowest negative log-likelihood (NLL). For MMLU-Cloze, just the correct choice’s text
262   is present for the few-shots, and we pick the choice that gives the lowest NLL after appending the
263   choice texts at the end of the prompt. The prompts used for the two cases are detailed in Appendix B.
264   In Figure 2, we plot performance over training and see that standard MMLU is at chance performance
265   even after training on 210B tokens. The cloze formulation performs better, and importantly has lower
266   seed variance and much higher monotonicity (0.95 instead of 0.09, see Table 1). This result seems
267   surprising, given that the cloze format is not standard. Further investigation yields that fully-trained
268   large models tend to have better performance on standard MMLU compared to MMLU-cloze (e.g.
269   78.7% on standard MMLU vs. 60.6% for MMLU-Cloze for LLaMa 3 70B). Despite this difference
      in absolute performance, we find the performance on standard and cloze formats is highly correlated

      4       U NDERSTANDING VARIANCE THROUGH THE LENS OF ITEM ANALYSIS
300
      In the previous section, we computed the empirically occurring variances for commonly used evalua-
301
      tion benchmarks, considering benchmark-level scores, and we showed how looking at continuous
302
      metrics or cloze formulations of tasks can boost SNR.
303
304   As another avenue of possibly reducing variance, and to better understand it, we take inspiration
305   from item analysis, a common method used to assess the usefulness of individual test questions on
306
      standardised tests administered to humans (Livingston, 2011; University of Washington, 2024). Item
      analysis focuses on metrics of individual samples (e.g. difficulty) to understand the types of questions
307
      on tests in terms of how individuals (in our case, models) perform on them.
308
309
310
      4.1       M ETHOD
311
      In applying item analysis to benchmarks, we consider two metrics. Item difficulty refers to the
312
      average score on an item across models; Item discrimination refers to the correlation between models’
313   performances on a single data point and models’ overall performances. Intuitively, items with either
314   high or low difficulty will have low discrimination (as all models will be wrong or right, respectively).
315
316
      As we wish to make recommendations about evaluation datasets that extend to future models, we split
      our 70 models into train and test sets. We consider two splits: “random” and “difficulty”. As the name
317
      suggests, in the random split, we split models randomly; In the difficulty split, we hold out the best
318
      performing 14 models. The full lists of models in each split can be found in Appendix D.1. We then
319   calculate item analysis metrics on individual data points for the train and test sets. As is often done
320   with human testing, we also consider the use of removing data points with low item discrimination,
321   and observe the effects this has on evaluation metrics such as mean, standard error of the mean (std.
322   err.),3 and monotonicity.

348   4.2                   R ESULTS
349
350   In Figure 3, we show results for two illustrative benchmarks: ARC-C and GSM8k. Full results
351   across other benchmarks can be found in Appendix D.2. Overall, we find that item discrimination
352   scores may not provide much useful signal for the field of language model evaluations (unlike their
353   widespread usage in human standardised testing). This is especially true given that state-of-the-
354   art models perform better and better, and we would like tests to stay informative when models
355   improve. To illustrate this, we show how high discrimination on train (weaker) models often does
356   not correspond to high discrimination on test (stronger) models (Figure 3, second column). Striping
357
      around x = 0 corresponds to items that train set models always get wrong (yielding 0 discrimination)
      but are informative on test set models. Similarly, striping around y = 0 corresponds to items that test
358
      models always get right (yielding 0 discrimination) but are informative on the train set. If we instead
359
      consider item discriminations on a random split of models (Figure 3, third column), we see stronger
360   correlations, indicating that the low correlation is in fact due to the difference in item discrimination
361   on weaker and stronger models.
362
363
      In Appendix D.3, we qualitatively inspect examples with negative item discrimination (which are
      thus anti-correlated with overall model performance), but are not able to discern any clear patterns
364
      for most benchmarks (a notable exception being Hellaswag, see Figure 12). While these negative
365
      results suggest item discriminations may not be the most informative means of understanding (or
366   reducing) variance on stronger models, we consider further application to explore the causal effect.
367
368   Specifically, we consider pruning data points with low item discrimination, with the hopes that this
369
      will reduce variance or improve monotonicity. More precisely, we prune data points with low item
      discrimination on the train set of models from the difficulty split and we visualise metrics calculated
370
      using the pruned subset on the test set of models from the difficulty split. Results are presented
371
      in the three rightmost columns of Figure 3. Overall, while we find modest improvements in both
372   standard error (a decrease) and monotonicity (an increase), the drift in the estimated accuracy is

400   5   P OSSIBLE PITFALLS OF USING EFFICIENT BENCHMARKING
401
402   In a similar category to item analysis, item response theory (Cai et al., 2016; van der Linden, 2018;
403   Brzezińska, 2020; Lord & Novick, 1968) describes a set of statistical models used to analyse human
404   abilities on standardised test data. In the recent past, the method has become popular as a means of
405
      understanding model performance on a set of evaluation samples (Lalor et al., 2016; Vania et al.,
      2021; Rodriguez et al., 2021). Most recently, Polo et al. (2024) used IRT to cluster evaluation points
406
      with the aims of reducing eval benchmark size (and thus, the cost of running).
407
408   Following our mixed findings applying item analysis, we apply the IRT method from Polo et al.
409   (2024) to our models and the overlapping set of evaluation benchmarks. For a brief summary of the
410   IRT method, we refer to Appendix E.1. Specifically, we go beyond the comparisons drawn in prior
411
      work and consider how our defined variance metrics (§ 3) change under this model. We believe the
      application to evaluating intermediate checkpoints during pretraining is especially relevant, as that’s
412
      the application where smaller evaluation datasets could have the most efficiency gains (as opposed to
413
      one-time evaluations of larger models).
414
415   In Tables 3 and 4, we report various metrics on the discrete performance measure for GSM8k,
416   Hellaswag, and ARC-C. We find that simply using the performance on the 100 datapoints selected by
417
      Polo et al. (2024) for each benchmark can lead to quite large deviations in the mean (an overestimation
      by 7% for ARC-C). The full IRT++ method obtains less deviation, replicating prior findings (Polo
418
      et al., 2024). However, both methods suffer from greatly increased seed variance (final two columns,
419
      Table 3), indicating that the tiny-benchmarks method may have limited use during pretraining
420   ablations as it makes model comparisons more likely to be confounded by randomness from the
421   initialisation and data ordering seed. This increased variance is also reflected in the monotonicity
422   metrics – we see a decrease in monotonicity in Table 4, indicating that performance oscillates
423   more during training (see Figure 5). This clearly shows that we cannot use efficient benchmarking
424   techniques for building scaling laws and doing pre-training/datamix ablations.
425
      Beyond the smaller scale models, we also considered the use of tiny-benchmarks for evaluating larger
426   models, like the ones used for item analysis in Section 4. In Figure 4, we find that IRT-based methods
427   generalise relatively well when it comes to the average performance metric (with the IRT++ estimator
428   performing better), but have much larger standard error of the mean. This increased error cautions
429   against the use of IRT-based subsets for model evaluations that will be used to compare different
430   models. To quantify how this increased standard error of the mean may affect model rankings, we
431   also compute the Kendall rank correlation on our 70 models using the performance estimate obtained
      from using the full dataset, as well as the IRT and IRT++ methods. In Table 7, we find that the

457   6   R ELATED WORK
458
459   While a significant body of work exists proposing natural language processing (NLP) benchmarks
460   to evaluate the capabilities of models, there is comparatively less work studying the benchmarks
461   themselves. Before the era of chat large language models, Marie et al. (2021) conducted a large
462   scale meta-analysis of 769 research papers published from 2010 to 2020 and identified troubling
463   trends, including one that partially motivates our work: models are frequently declared superior to
464   competitors based on small differences in performance scores, without proper hypothesis testing that
465
      takes into account natural fluctuations in benchmark scores. Spiritually similar claims were made
      by Dehghani et al. (2021) in their provocatively titled paper “The Benchmark Lottery”. Kocmi et al.
466
      (2021) further leveraged large-scale human experiments to evaluate benchmarks with automated
467
      metrics and concluded that commonly used metrics such as BLEU score had led to poor deployment
468   decisions. Their conclusion was echoed by a meta-analysis of 3500 NLP benchmark scores published
469   on Papers with Code (Blagec et al., 2022).
470
471
      More recently, with accelerating progress in NLP, researchers have begun to study benchmarks in
      earnest to understand their properties and limitations (Gehrmann et al., 2023). Von Werra et al.
472
      (2022) proposed a framework to evaluate benchmarks themselves and provided a mechanism for
473
      researchers to share their benchmarking analyses. Certain papers have studied specific aspects of
474   benchmarks, focusing on the sensitivity of language models to various factors. Sclar et al. (2023)
475   tested how sensitive language models are to differently formatted prompts, while Wang et al. (2024)
476   and Alzahrani et al. (2024) find that models are inconsistent across changes in the format of MCQA
477   benchmarks. Our work builds on these works by focusing on the inherent variance in benchmarks
478   (e.g. due to model seed) that pracitioners should consider when making decisions, and suggesting
479   minor modifications (e.g. in how a task is scored or formulated) that can reduce this variance.
480   With the aims of improving efficiency in model development cycles, recent work proposes reducing
481   the size of evaluation benchmarks by picking representative samples (Vivek et al., 2023; Polo et al.,
482   2024). Polo et al. (2024) show that methods from human standardised testing (specifically, item
483   response theory; Lord & Novick, 1968) can be combined with clustering to subselect evaluation
484   benchmarks without incurring too much deviation from the mean. However, they do not consider
485   the increased variance from their method nor how small deviations in means can compound when
      comparing multiple models. We go beyond their work by considering the use of additional methods

520   7   C ONCLUSION
521
522
      As language models become more and more prevalent, it has become increasingly important to get
523   a sense of their capabilities. One of the primary ways to assess these capabilities is through the
524   use of evaluation benchmarks, where a model is scored on a series of examples. These scores are
525   often directly compared, without consideration of the variance. This obscures the interpretation of
526   evaluation results, in assessing final models as well as making decisions during model development.
527   In this work, we aimed to quantify evaluation benchmark variance across a range of settings (from
528   pretraining intermediate checkpoints, to the largest frontier LLMs) using a diverse set of metrics
529   (seed variance, confidence intervals, and monotonicity). Beyond quantifying variance, we also
530   experimented with various techniques used in human standardised testing (item analysis; University
531
      of Washington (2024), item response theory; Cai et al. (2016)), but generally found these methods to
      be ineffective on the models and benchmarks we considered, in terms of reducing variance. Future
532
      work could explore such avenues further, and it is possible that as models reach closer and closer
533
      to human-level performance these methods will provide more useful insights. On the other hand,
534   in line with recent work advocating for a teleological approach to measuring capabilities (McCoy
535   et al., 2023), we demonstrated LLM-specific techniques (e.g. the use of continuous metrics or cloze-
536   formatted tasks) can improve the signal-to-noise ratio in our evals. Such techniques are not available
537   when assessing humans, but provide a unique opporutnity for LLM evaluations, especially when
538   performing pretraining ablations. We hope our work spurs future work in this direction of reducing
539   variance, in addition to serving as an empirical guide for model practitioners to use when comparing
      models and assessing performance.

      For pre-training the 7B Llama-2 like checkpoints, we use a pre-training mix of publicly available
813   data. We apply filtering to remove documents containing a high amount of personal information. We
814   use a learning rate of 3.0 × 104 , sequence length of 4096, and a batch size of 4.1M tokens to train
815   the 7B models for 50000 steps. We use 256 80GiB A100 GPUs for a single pre-training run for 50k
816   steps on our internal cluster. We do 10 such runs with different seeds. Each step takes 4.3 seconds.
817
      For running the evaluations, we use 8 GPUs for each evaluation job comprising multiple evaluation
818
      datasets in a single job. A single evaluation job takes on average takes 3.5 hours for 13 benchmarks.

821   of samples for each of the benchmarks we consider. We can choose any continuous metric like
822
      character NLL, raw NLL, probability mass, log of probabilities, etc. for the benchmarks, but to
      maintain consistency, we choose probability mass of the predicted answer for choice-based tasks and
823
      negative log likelihood (NLL) of the target answer for generation-based benchmarks. Choice-based
824
      benchmarks are evaluated by appending the possible option choice letters or choice texts and then

1270   E.1                   A BRIEF PRIMER ON IRT
1271
1272   While IRT can refer to a variety of methods, here we focus on the two-parameter multidimensional
1273   IRT model used by Polo et al. (2024) to make tiny-benchmarks. Specifically, we define a matrix of
1274   model scores on a set of evaluation examples, Y , such that Yms is the score of model m on evaluation
1275   example s. As this model is mostly applied to discrete metrics in our cases (e.g., accuracy), we
1276
       focus our exposition on the case where Yms ∈ [0, 1] (see Polo et al. (2024) for details on extending
       to continuous metrics). The IRT model then learns vector embeddings for each model, θm , vector
1277
       embeddings for each example αs as well as a scalar bias for each example βs to maximize the
1278
       likelihood of the observations:
1279
1280
1281                                                                              1
                                               P (Yms = 1|θm , αs , βs ) =
1282                                                                         −αs⊤ θm + βs
1283
1284   Polo et al. (2024) then learn values of θm , αs , βs for a set of train models across a range of benchmarks.
1285   Then, they perform clustering on the evaluation samples where the embedding of each sample is
1286
       given by (αs , βs ). Finally, they subselect 100 data points that are the most representative and assign
       weights equal to the size of their clusters.
1287
1288   For a new model, they propose two methods for evaluation. In the first, which is termed “IRT” (to
1289   match their paper), we simply use the weighted performance of a model on the 100 data points they
1290   identify. In the second, which is termed “IRT++”, we consider a weighted combination of “IRT” and
1291   an adjusted estimate (which is achieved by 1. learning a θm for the new model on the 100 evaluated
1292
       data points, using fixed αs , βs , then 2. using the learned θm with the fixed αs , βs for all data points to
       estimate model performance). Polo et al. (2024) find IRT++ to outperform the IRT estimator, which
1293
       we reproduce (see Figure 4).
1294
1295   For a full description of the method, we refer the reader to Polo et al. (2024)—we include this primer
       here for completeness.
