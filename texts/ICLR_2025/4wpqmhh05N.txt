009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Representation learning is a crucial task of deep learning, which aims to project
012            texts and other symbolic inputs into mathematical embedding. Traditional represen-
013            tation learning encodes symbolic data into an Euclidean space. However, the high
014            dimensionality of the Euclidean space used for embedding words presents consid-
015            erable computational and storage challenges. Hyperbolic space has emerged as a
016            promising alternative for word embedding, which demonstrates strong representa-
017            tion and generalization capacities, particularly for latent hierarchies of language
018
               data. In this paper, we analyze the Skip-Gram Negative-sampling representa-
               tion learning method in hyperbolic spaces, and explore the potential relationship
019
               between the mutual information and hyperbolic embedding. Furthermore, we
020
               establish generalization error bounds for hyperbolic embedding. These bounds
021            demonstrate the dimensional parsimony of hyperbolic space and its relationship
022            between the generalization error and the sample size. Finally, we conduct two
023            experiments on the Wordnet dataset and the THUNews dataset, whose results
024            further validate our theoretical properties.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Representation learning has gained widespread attention in the past decade as a significant task of
030   natural language processing (NLP). Various methods have been proposed to embed words into vector
031   spaces to facilitate further inference. The most straightforward approach is one-hot embedding,
032   which converts each word into a binary vector corresponding to its position in the vocabulary. Latent
033
      Semantic Indexing (LSI) (Deerwester et al., 1990) generates low dimensional word embedding by
      applying singular value decomposition of the word-context matrix. Then a model called Latent
034
      Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) (Blei et al., 2001) was introduced based on the bag of words hypothesis.
035
      The neural language model further advanced word embeddings Bengio et al. (2000), followed by
036   Mnih and Hinton’s development of Neural Probabilistic Language Models (NPLMs) Mnih and Hinton
037   (2008). Collobert and Weston Collobert et al. (2008) proposed pre-trained word embeddings through
038   multitask learning. Mikolov’s Word2Vec Mikolov et al. (2013) remains a powerful toolkit for training
039   word embeddings, alongside widely used methods like GloVe Pennington et al. (2014) and FastText
040   Bojanowski et al. (2017), which play crucial roles in various NLP tasks.
041
      Although projecting words into an Euclidean space has achieved remarkable success in various
042   applications, these word embedding methods need high dimensional spaces as the representing ability
043   is positively proportional to the space dimension. Due to the immense computational and storage
044   burden brought by high dimensions, we hope to reduce the dimensionality of the representation
045   space. To address this issue, Nickel and Kiela applied word embedding in Poincare disk (Nickel and
046   Kiela, 2017), benefiting from the stochastic Riemannian optimization (RSGD) (Bonnabel, 2013).
047   This approach alleviates the dimension constraints by substituting Euclidean space with hyperbolic
048   space. Following their work, numerous studies based on Hyperbolic embedding have been researched
049   such as embedding graphs in Poincare ball (Sala et al., 2018). Beyond the Poincare disk, Poincare
050
      hyperplane (Ganea et al., 2018b), hyperbolic cone (Ganea et al., 2018a), and hyperbolic disks (Suzuki
      et al., 2019) are been considered as embedding spaces.
051
052   In this article, we aim to quantitatively assess the effectiveness of hyperbolic embedding. However, it
053   is challenging to directly characterize the embedding error of words, because the true embedding
      of words in a certain space is often hard to specify accurately. To address this, we first establish a

054
      relationship between mutual information and the hyperbolic embedding method. We then analyze the
055   error between the embedding distance matrix and mutual information matrix, and provide theoretical
056   properties for error bounds in hyperbolic embedding. We divide the error bounds into two components:
057   the spatial error, which reflects the influence of the dimensions and the structure of the hyperbolic
058   space on the embedding error; and the generalization error, which describes the relationship between
059   the error and the sample size across different spaces. Additionally, we verify the theoretical results of
060   hyperbolic embedding on the Wordnet and THUNews datasets.
061   This paper is organized as follows: Section 2 provides a brief introduction to the Lorentz model,
062   Poincare ball, and key details of hyperbolic embedding. In section 3, we analyze the relationship

064   further theoretical analysis for the generalization error bound of hyperbolic embedding. Section
065   4 outlines the experiment setups, presents and discusses the experiment results. Finally, section 5
066   provides a short summary of this paper.

      3.2   PARSIMONY EMBEDDING BY USING P OINCARE DISK
259
260   In this section, we will introduce the parsimony property of hyperbolic embedding, which indicates
261   that hyperbolic space can store information using a much smaller embedding space dimension
262   compared to Euclidean space. However, our research also finds that the parsimony property of
263   hyperbolic space requires training with more samples and has greater computational complexity when
264   calculating distances for the encoding.
265   Following the results from the previous section, the distance matrix of word embedding obtained
266   by the SGNS method in the hyperbolic embedding is a characterization of the original mutual
267   information matrix between words. By analyzing the error between the distance matrix obtained from
268   the embedding in hyperbolic space and the original PMI matrix, we connect the problem of word
269   embedding to graph embedding, thereby enabling theoretical analysis of the errors in the hyperbolic
      embedding. We divide the obtained error bound into two parts. The first part characterizes the error

369   hyperbolic space, this bound can also be further refined. Based on what we currently understand, the
370   results indicate that the performance of the Theorem 2 matches our experimental results.

530   In this work, we give a brief analysis of the Hyperbolic embedding from the mutual information
531   matrix. We reveal the relationship between the point-wise mutual information matrix and the distance
532   matrix of embedding. Then following this result, we give an analysis of the errors of the hyperbolic
533   embedding including the embedding errors and the generalization errors. Our findings indicate that
534   low-dimensional hyperbolic spaces can accommodate more linear structures of mutual information,
535   highlighting the equivalence between the Gramian matrix in hyperbolic embedding and the dimension
536
      of the space. Furthermore, we demonstrate that hyperbolic embedding is more unstable during
      training than its Euclidean counterpart, necessitating more samples for effective training. This
