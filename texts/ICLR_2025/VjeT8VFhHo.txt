009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               A World Model is a compressed spatial and temporal representation of a real
012
               world environment that allows one to train an agent or execute planning meth-
013
               ods. However, world models are typically trained on observations from the real
014            world environment, and they usually do not enable learning policies for other real
015            environments. We propose One-Shot World Model (OSWM), a transformer world
016            model that is learned in an in-context learning fashion from purely synthetic data
017            sampled from a prior distribution. Our prior is composed of multiple randomly ini-
018            tialized neural networks, where each network models the dynamics of each state
019            and reward dimension of a desired target environment. We adopt the supervised
020            learning procedure of Prior-Fitted Networks by masking next-state and reward
021            at random context positions and query OSWM to make probabilistic predictions
022
               based on the remaining transition context. During inference time, OSWM is able
               to quickly adapt to the dynamics of a simple grid world, as well as the CartPole
023
               gym and a custom control environment by providing 1k transition steps as context
024
               and is then able to successfully train environment-solving agent policies. How-
025            ever, transferring to more complex environments remains a challenge, currently.
026            Despite these limitations, we see this work as an important stepping-stone in the
027            pursuit of learning world models purely from synthetic data.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   World models have emerged as a powerful approach for creating compressed spatial and temporal
034   representations of real-world environments, enabling efficient agent training and planning in rein-
035   forcement learning (RL) tasks (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Kaiser et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2023;
036   Wu et al., 2022). These models have shown significant promise in improving sample efficiency
037   and performance across various RL domains. For instance, SimPLe (Kaiser et al., 2019) demon-
038   strated strong results on Atari games by using a learned dynamics model to generate simulated data.
039
      More recently, transformer-based world models have pushed the boundaries of sample efficiency
      and performance. TWM (Robine et al., 2023) utilized a Transformer-XL architecture to surpass
040
      other methods on the Atari 100k benchmark Kaiser et al. (2019), while IRIS (Micheli et al., 2023)
041
      and STORM (Zhang et al., 2023) achieved human-level performance using GPT-style transformers.
042   However, these approaches typically require training on observations from the target environment,
043   which can be time-consuming and impractical in many real-world scenarios. Moreover, traditional
044   world models often lack the ability to generalize across different environments, limiting their ap-
045   plicability in diverse RL tasks. The challenge of transferring learned dynamics efficiently to new
046   environments remains a significant hurdle in the field of model-based RL.
047
      To address these challenges, we explore the potential of training world models with in-context learn-
048   ing using purely synthetic data. We propose the One-Shot World Model (OSWM), a transformer-
049   based approach that learns a world model from a synthetic prior distribution. Our method draws in-
050   spiration from Prior-Fitted Networks (Müller et al., 2022) and leverages in-context learning to adapt
051   to new environments with minimal real-world interactions. By training on a diverse synthetic prior,
052   OSWM aims to capture a wide range of environment dynamics, potentially enabling rapid adapta-
053   tion to various RL tasks. We release our code under https://anonymous.4open.science/
      r/PFN-SE-7ABB/ and our contributions can be summarized as follows:

084   World Models and Model-Based Reinforcement Learning Classical RL often suffers from sam-
085   ple inefficiency, as it requires many interactions with the environment. Model-Based Reinforcement
086   Learning (MBRL) mitigates this by learning environment dynamics, allowing agents to train using
087   simulated data. For example, Ha & Schmidhuber (2018) proposed World Models, which use gener-
088   ative neural networks to encode compact spatial-temporal representations, optimizing RL efficiency.
089   MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2020) advanced MBRL by learning both environment dynamics and
090   reward functions, which proved highly effective across board games. Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2020;
091
      2021; 2023) applied learned world models across diverse domains, including real-world robotics
      (Wu et al., 2022). More recently, TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al., 2024) demonstrated scalability and ro-
092
      bustness in continuous control tasks. Transformer-based models have also become prominent, with
093
      TransDreamer (Chen et al., 2022) or TWM (Robine et al., 2023) that excelled in sample efficiency
094   on Atari 100k. Other Transformer-based approaches such as IRIS (Micheli et al., 2023) or STORM
095   (Zhang et al., 2023) achieved over 100% human performance on Atari 100k with GPT-style train-
096   ing. However, Most if not all methods are trained on the target environment and utilize the attention
097   mechanism to attend to previous parts of the roll-out.
098
      Despite these successes, transferring learned dynamics across environments remains a significant
099
      hurdle in the field of MBRL. Augmented World Models (Ball et al., 2021) tackle environmental
100   dynamics changes by learning a world model from offline data. During training, they provide pre-
101   dicted dynamics and possible changes as latent context, helping agents generalize to new variations.
102   Similarly, Evans et al. (2022) uses transformers or RNNs to encode environment parameterization
103   into a latent space, enabling a world model robust to variations like friction or object mass changes.
104
105   Synthetic Data and Priors and RL Synthetic data plays a crucial role in RL, particularly in
106   methods that transfer knowledge from simulation to real environments, known as sim2real. Domain
107   randomization (Tobin et al., 2017), which varies simulation settings like lighting and object shapes,
      enhances generalization and improves the transfer from simulation to the real world. Pretraining


                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      with synthetic data has also gained prominence. For example, Wang et al. (2024) pretrains the
109   Decision Transformer using synthetic Markov chain data, outperforming pretraining with natural
110   text (e.g., DPT trained on Wikipedia (Lee et al., 2023)) in both performance and sample efficiency.
111   Other techniques include training on synthetic reward distributions to allow zero-shot transfer to
112   new tasks (Frans et al., 2024), while TDM (Schubert et al., 2023) demonstrates strong few-shot and
113   zero-shot generalization across procedural control environments. UniSim (Yang et al., 2023) uses
114   internet-scale data to train realistic robotic control models, enabling more efficient RL training. A
115   meta-learning approach trains Synthetic Environments (Ferreira et al., 2021) for RL that serve as
116   proxies for a target environment, providing only synthetic environment dynamics. These synthetic
117
      dynamics allow RL agents to significantly reduce the number of interactions needed during training.
      Lastly, Prior Fitted Networks (PFNs) (Müller et al., 2022) utilize synthetic priors for supervised
118
      learning, with its adaptation to tabular data, TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023), achieving state-of-the-
119
      art results while significantly speeding up inference.
120
121   Unlike previous approaches that depend on real-world observations or extensive training in target
122   environments, we introduce a new approach that trains a transformer world model entirely on syn-
123
      thetic data sampled from a prior distribution which is based much further away from reality as it
      based on randomly initiliazed neural networks. Using the Prior-Fitted Networks paradigm, OSWM
124
      employs in-context learning to adapt to new environments with just a simple context sequence.

183   3.2   P RIOR FOR T RAINING OSWM
184
185   One of the core contributions of this method is the design of a prior that aims to mimic the properties
186   of RL environments while incorporating stochasticity for diverse dynamics. The prior consists of
187   two components: a neural network-based (NN) prior and a physics-based momentum prior. These
188   two priors are combined, with the states produced by both the NN and momentum priors concate-
189
      nated as input to the NN prior for further updates. This split allows the model to capture both
      complex, neural network-generated behaviors and simple, physics-driven interactions, like pendu-
190
      lum motion or velocity-position relations. In Figure 1 (left), we illustrate the mechanics of the NN
191
      prior, and below we describe both priors in more detail.
192
193
194   Neural Network Prior The NN prior generates dynamics using randomly initialized neural net-
195
      works. Each state dimension sit is produced by a separate neural network fθii , which is randomly-
196   initialized and untrained and takes as input the entire previous state st−1 = [s1t−1 , ..., sdt−1s
                                                                                                          ] and
                       1        da                                  i     i
197   action at = [at−1 , ..., at ]. The next state is computed as st = fθi (st−1 , at−1 ). The networks con-
198   sist of three linear layers, with random activations (ReLU, tanh, or sigmoid) after the first two layers,
199   and a residual connection that aggregates the outputs of the first and second layers. This structure
200
      allows for complex dependencies between state dimensions and actions. To introduce variability,
      each NN-based state dimension is initialized with a random scale and offset. When the individual
201
      NN prior networks are reset, which occurs periodically after a pre-defined fixed interval, their initial
202
      state values s0 are sampled from U(0, 1), and then scaled and offset according to the prior configura-
203   tion (see Table 6 for the prior hyperparameters in the appendix), ensuring stochastic behavior across
204   environments. This method allows the model to capture rich and diverse dynamics by introducing
205   different dependencies between states and actions across dimensions.
206
207
      Momentum Prior The momentum prior models physical interactions through two components:
208
      velocity and positional updates. Velocity is updated based on the action and gravity (vt+1 = vt +
209
      at · ∆t − g · ∆t), while position is updated using the current velocity (pt+1 = pt + vt+1 · ∆t).
210   In this model, velocity vt and position pt are influenced by factors such as gravity and the current
211   action, and the position updates rely on velocity. The initial position is sampled from [0, 2π], and
212   the initial velocity is sampled from U(−3, 3). This setup enables the model to simulate both linear
213   and angular motion. Angular dynamics can incorporate gravity, and they are represented internally
214   in radians, though the output can be sine, cosine, or radian values. The momentum prior values
215   are concatenated with the NN prior values and fed into the NN prior networks for the subsequent
      transitions.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Rewards and Invariance The reward function follows a similar structure to the NN prior used
217   for state dynamics but with different inputs, including the new state, action, and the previous state.
218   This reflects how rewards in real RL environments are based on state transitions and action costs,
219   such as penalizing high action magnitudes. The reward at time step t can be expressed as: rt+1 =
220   g(st+1 , at , st ) where g represents the reward model that takes the new state st+1 , the action at ,
221   and, optionally, the previous state st as inputs. To maintain flexibility, the reward is replaced by a
222   constant reward of 1 with a probability of 0.5, a common approach in tasks like CartPole, where
223   extending the episode is rewarded, or MountainCar, where faster completion is incentivized. To
224   prevent the model from overfitting to the order of state-action dimensions, we shuffle both states and
225
      actions and apply identical permutations to X1 and Y1 .

233   4.1     R ESULTS FOR AGENT T RAINING
234
      We evaluate the performance of OSWM by training an RL agent using the PPO algorithm (Schul-
235
      man et al., 2017), as implemented in stable-baselines 3 (Raffin et al., 2021). We chose PPO because
236   it can handle both discrete and continuous action spaces, making it well suited for the variety of
237   environments in this study. We selected environments that provide a mix of discrete and continu-
238   ous state and action spaces, allowing us to assess OSWM’s performance across different types of
239   RL challenges. The selected environments include two custom environments, GridWorld and Sim-
240   pleEnv (see Appendix D for details), as well as CartPole-v0, MountainCar-v0, Pendulum-v1, and
241   Reacher-v4 from the classic control gym suite.
242   In GridWorld, the agent navigates a discrete, 8x8 grid to reach a target location, receiving a positive
243   reward for reaching the target and small penalties for each step, and the environment is considered
244   solved when the agent consistently reaches the target efficiently. SimpleEnv involves moving a point
245   along a 1D continuous line toward the center, with rewards negatively proportional to the distance
246   from the center. CartPole-v0 is solved with an average reward of 195, MountainCar-v0 with an
247   average reward of -110, Pendulum-v1 maximizes the reward when balancing the pendulum upright,
248   and Reacher-v4 is solved with an average reward of around -3.75.
249   We trained agents for 50k steps in all environments, except MountainCar-v0, where training was
250   extended to 200k steps with actions repeated five times to enhance exploration. All PPO hyperpa-
251   rameters were kept in their default settings. In all experiments, unless stated otherwise, OSWM was
252   provided with 1k context steps collected from the real environment using random actions.

369   4.3   S TUDYING THE C ONTEXT S AMPLING
370
371   Context is crucial for the predictive performance of OSWM. This section explores how different
372   context sampling methods affect the model’s predictions. Assessing the role of sampling strategies
373   requires multiple agent trainings in OSWM and evaluations across multiple test episodes and envi-
374
      ronments. Since this is computationally expensive, we make use of a proxy dataset to evaluate the
      effectiveness of various sampling strategies more efficiently. The details of the generation of the
375
      proxy set are provided in Appendix B, but a high-level overview is given here.

410   5   C ONCLUSION
411
412   We introduced One-Shot World Model (OSWM), a world model trained purely on synthetic data
413   sampled from a prior distribution based on randomly initialized, untrained neural networks by
414   leveraging In-context Learning. Despite the simplicity of the prior, OSWM achieved promising
415   results as it is able to train RL agents to solve tasks like GridWorld and control tasks such as
416   CartPole-v0, demonstrating the potential of synthetic pretraining in Model-Based Reinforcement
417   Learning. Although the model still struggles with more complex environments like Pendulum-v1
418
      and MountainCar-v0, our empirical analysis suggests that improving the priors and refining context
      sampling are key to enhancing performance. Our results highlight the potential of synthetic pre-
419
      training in RL, suggesting that with further optimization, this approach could be a key step towards
420
      foundation world models, capable of tackling increasingly complex tasks. With further optimization
421   of the prior, synthetic pretraining could enable the development of more generalizable foundation
422   world models, offering a scalable solution for RL training, especially when evaluating real-world
423   environments is costly and challenging.

581

591   as those where the pole is not upright or the cart velocity is high—allow the model to observe
592   critical states not typically encountered by an expert agent alone. By including random actions in
593   the proxy set, we aim to capture these middle-ground dynamics, such as a scenario in MountainCar
      where a fast-moving car decelerates, a behavior not covered by either purely expert or random
