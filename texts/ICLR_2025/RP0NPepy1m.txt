009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has achieved remarkable training results by freez-
012            ing the original weights and training only low-rank matrices, establishing itself
013            as the predominant fine-tuning method for LLMs. Many LoRA variants have
014            emerged, yet they lack a design tailored to the characteristics of LLM weights and
015            fail to leverage the original weights effectively. To address the sparsity of LLM
016            weights, and drawing inspiration from GQA and MQA, we propose Block-Affine
017            Adaptation (Bone), a novel PEFT technique distinct from LoRA. By dividing the
018            original weights into multiple subspaces that share a single matrix for weight up-
019
               dates, Bone simplifies the process by requiring the trainable matrix to be initialized
               to zero, eliminating the need for complex initialization as in some LoRA variants.
020
               Compared to LoRA, Bone significantly reduces memory usage and achieves faster
021
               computation. Evaluation of both NLU and NLG tasks demonstrates that Bone
022            substantially outperforms LoRA and its variants. Inspired by Pissa, we propose a
023            new theory called “Weight Guide” to better utilize the information embedded in
024            the original weights. This approach extracts valuable information through a linear
025            transformation of the original weight matrix using a trainable matrix. To val-
026            idate the effectiveness of “Weight Guide” we combined it with Bone to create a
027            new structure called Block-Affine Transformation (Bat), and ablation experiments
028            confirmed the effectiveness of “Weight Guide”.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large models have been integrated into various industries, revolutionizing many traditional tech-
034   nologies Radford et al. (2019); Raffel et al. (2020). However, general-purpose large models often
035   struggle to meet the needs of all downstream tasks, making it necessary to fine-tune base models
036   for specific scenarios. Full-scale fine-tuning of large models is computationally costly; for exam-
037   ple, finetuning the LLaMA2-7B Touvron et al. (2023) model with bfloat16 Wang & Kanwar (2019)
038   precision requires around 60GB of VRAM. In contrast, PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning)Xu
039
      et al. (2023) techniques could reduce the VRAM requirement to fit into a 24GB VRAM GPU. As
      a result, numerous PEFT techniques and quantization methods have emerged to reduce the training
040
      costs of large models. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) Hu et al. (2021) has become one of the most
041
      popular PEFT methods due to its small tunable parameter size, its effectiveness, and the possibility
042   of zero inference overhead after finetuning.
043
044   LoRA significantly reduces memory usage by freezing the original weights W and updating two
045
      low-rank matrices A and B. Typically, either A or B is initialized to zero, ensuring that the initial
      state of LoRA is consistent with the pre-trained model, The figure 2 illustrates the structure visu-
046
      alization. However, extensive experiments Ding et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024b); Biderman et al.
047
      (2024) have shown that LoRA’s convergence is significantly slower compared to full fine-tuning.
048   This slow convergence is likely due to the small gradients caused by the zero initialization of either A
049   or B. To address this issue, researchers have proposed several LoRA variants, such as LoRA+ Hayou
050   et al. (2024), PISSA Meng et al. (2024), and LoRA-GAWang et al. (2024). Despite their excellent
051   performance, these LoRA variants inevitably introduce complexity into the fine-tuning process and
052   reduce structural flexibility. For example, LoRA+ requires manual adjustment of different learning
053   rates for A and B; PISSA necessitates performing SVD decomposition on W at the beginning of
      training, which can be time-consuming when the model parameters are large. As research on LoRA


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      becomes increasingly saturated, it is essential to explore new directions for PEFT techniques and
055   effectively leverage the unique characteristics of LLMs.
056
057   In this work, we first designed Block-Affine Adaptation (Bone), which divides the original weights
058
      into multiple subspaces that share a single trainable matrix for updates, making it more efficient
      than LoRA’s two low-rank matrices. Secondly, to address the limitations of LoRA and fully utilize
059
      the information in the original weights, we propose a new theory, “Weight Guide”. “Weight Guide”
060
      enables feature extraction by applying a simple linear transformation to the original weights. Finally,
061   to validate the effectiveness of “Weight Guide”, we combined Bone with “Weight Guide” to develop
062   Block-Affine Transformation (Bat).
063
064
      Our extensive evaluation shows that Bone not only excels in both Natural Language Understand-
      ing (NLU) and Natural Language Generation (NLG) tasks but also significantly outperforms LoRA
065
      and its variants. Additionally, Bone retains the advantages of LoRA, such as ease of use and no
066
      additional computational overhead for the model. In NLG tasks, Bone demonstrates superior per-
067   formance, with evaluation metrics surpassing even the strong LoRA variant Pissa across the board.
068   To verify the feasibility of Bone, we conducted experiments on two different LLM architectures
069   (LLaMA2 Xu et al. (2023), RWKV6 Peng et al. (2024)). As shown in Figure 1, Bone achieves the
070   fastest convergence, and the results on the test set demonstrate its superior data fitting and general-
071   ization capabilities ( Table 2). As a completely new structure distinct from LoRA, Bone offers clear
072   improvements in computational efficiency and memory savings, as detailed in table 7.
073   Our contributions can be summarized as follows
074
075        1. We propose a novel PEFT technique called Block-Affine Adaptation (Bone). Bone out-
076
           performs LoRA and its variants across various fine-tuning tasks. Additionally, Bone is more
           memory-efficient and computationally faster compared to LoRA.
077
078        2. To effectively leverage the implicit information in the original weights, we propose the
079        “Weight Guide” theory. By integrating this theory with Bone, we design Block-Affine Trans-
080        formation (Bat), demonstrating the effectiveness of “Weight Guide”.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      The PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning) techniques are diverse and include approaches like
111
      adapter tuningHoulsby et al. (2019); He et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Pfeiffer et al. (2020),
112   prefix tuningLiu et al. (2023); Li & Liang (2021), prompt tuningBrown (2020); Liu et al. (2023);
113   Lester et al. (2021); Razdaibiedina et al. (2023); Li & Liang (2021), LoRAHu et al. (2021); Meng
114   et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024); Si et al. (2024), and layer-freezing methods such as LISA.
115
      The adapter method does not require fine-tuning all the parameters of the pre-trained model. Instead,
116
      it introduces a small number of task-specific parameters to store knowledge related to that task,
117
      thereby reducing the computational demands of model fine-tuning. Prefix tuning is a lightweight
118   fine-tuning method for generative tasks. It adds a continuous, task-specific vector sequence, called
119   a prefix, to the input. Unlike prompts, prefixes are entirely composed of free parameters and do not
120   correspond to actual tokens. Compared to traditional fine-tuning, prefix tuning only optimizes a set
121   of prefixes related to specific tasks, without any change to the original model.
122
      Prompt tuning defines a unique prompt for each task, prepending it to the input data while freezing
123
      the pre-trained model during training. LoRA allows us to indirectly train certain dense layers in a
124   neural network by optimizing low-rank decomposition matrices that adapt these layers while keep-
125   ing the pre-trained weights unchanged. This approach significantly addresses the inference latency
126   introduced by adapters and the fitting challenges of prefix tuning.
127
      In LoRA, the adapter matrices A and B are updated with the same learning rate, but using the same
128
      rate for both may not effectively learn the features. LoRA+ extended this method by introducing in-
129
      dependent learning rates for matrices A and B with a fixed ratio, improving the method’s efficiency.
130   The DoRA Liu et al. (2024b) method combines weight decomposition to achieve learning capabili-
131   ties similar to full fine-tuning without sacrificing LoRA’s inference efficiency. PiSSA optimizes the
132   compact parameter space by representing the matrices in the model as the product of two trainable
133   matrices, augmented with a residual matrix for error correction. Using Singular Value Decompo-
134   sition (SVD), PiSSA initializes the dominant singular values and vectors to train these matrices,
135   while keeping the residual matrix static during fine-tuning. OLoRA Büyükakyüz (2024) leverages
136   QR decomposition to initialize the adaptation matrices during the fine-tuning process, ensuring that
137   these matrices are orthogonal. This orthogonal initialization helps maintain the stability of the pa-
138
      rameter space during optimization. LoRA-GA and PiSSA are similar in form, but they differ in that
      LoRA-GA initializes A and B by computing the initial gradient, thereby closely approximating full
139
      fine-tuning.

144   3.1   M OTIVATION
145
146   The primary motivations are as follows:
147
      Firstly, current research on LoRA has reached a saturation point, underscoring the need for a new
148
      direction in Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques.
149
150   Secondly, existing PEFT methods lack a thorough exploration and effective utilization of the original
151   model weights.
152
153   3.2   B ONE : B LOCK -A FFINE UPDATE MATRICES
154
155   To design a novel PEFT structure that rivals LoRA, we conducted an in-depth study of dense ma-
156   trices. Current theories suggest that LLM weights exhibit sparse properties. For example, LoRA
157   utilizes low-rank matrices to update dense matrices, while techniques like GQA Ainslie et al. (2023)
158
      and MQA Shazeer (2019) employ shared mechanisms to reduce parameter overhead with remark-
      able effectiveness.
159
160   Inspired by these approaches, we hypothesize that dividing LLM weights into multiple subspaces
161   allows different subspaces to share a single low-rank matrix for updates. As illustrated in the figure
      2, we name this structure Block-Affine Adaptation (Bone).

      It is evident that the ways to partition LLM weights are diverse, as illustrated in the figure. Different
163   partitioning methods influence the shape of the Bone matrix, the computation logic, and ultimately,
164   the processing speed. In this work, we propose the Best Bone structure. For a pre-trained weight
165   matrix W ∈ Rd×k , we update it using a single matrix: W + ∆W = W + expand(B), where B ∈
166   Rr×d , and the rank r ≪ k. This design not only retains the simplicity and ease of use characteristic
167   of LoRA but also achieves higher computational efficiency. The calculation is expressed as follows:

      3.3   “W EIGHT G UIDE ”
188
189   It is well-known that LLMs have extremely large parameters, with complex interactions among
190   their internal weights, but most LoRA variants utilize original weight information minimally, with
191   only a few leveraging it during initialization to enhance performance. To increase the utilization
192   of this information and promote internal interactions among weights, we propose a novel concept
193   called “Weight Guide” This approach ensures that the trainable matrices are consistently guided and
194   constrained by the original weights throughout every step of the training process. As illustrated in
195   Figure 1b, LoRA variants interact with the original weights only once during initialization to extract
196
      essential components. In contrast, the Bone structure is guided (constrained) by the original weights
      after each update, significantly increasing the new weights’ utilization of the original weights. As
197
      the training steps increase, the influence of the original weights becomes more pronounced. This
198
      is evident from the loss curves, which show that the differences between Bone and other methods
199   grow larger in the later stages of training.
200
201   So, how can we extract the critical information from the original weights? A straightforward ap-
202
      proach is to apply a learnable matrix directly to the original weights using the Hadamard prod-
      uct. This method essentially assigns a weight to each element, allowing the learnable matrix to
203
      autonomously determine the importance of individual elements. Although this method is simple
204
      and straightforward, the elements within the weights remain isolated, making it impossible to learn
205   interactions between them. Therefore, we replace the Hadamard product with matrix multiplication,
206   essentially applying a simple linear transformation. This approach links the entire weight matrix
207   space, providing a better chance to discover the optimal solution. The corresponding formula is
208   shown below:
209                                       ∆Wn,n = Wn,n ⊙ bonen,n
                                                                                                      (2)
210                                       ∆Wn,n = Wn,n ⊗ bonen,n

447   throughput and reduce memory usage.

462   6   C ONCLUSION
463
464   This work, inspired by GQA and MQA, leverages the sparsity of LLM weights to design the Block-
465   Affine Adaptation (Bone) structure. In Bone, the original weights are divided into multiple sub-
466   spaces, all of which share a single low-rank matrix initialized to zero for updates. Extensive exper-
467   iments demonstrate that Bone consistently outperforms LoRA and its variants across various tasks,
468   while also offering superior computational efficiency. To break the limitations of LoRA and ef-
469   fectively utilize the information from the original weights, we propose the “Weight Guide” theory,
470   which enables significant improvements through simple linear transformations.
471   By integrating these innovations, we introduce a new structure called Block-Affine Transformation
472   (Bat). Bat not only validates the effectiveness of “Weight Guide” but also addresses the limitation
473   of Bone, where identical updates are applied to all subspaces. Experimental results show that Bat
474   surpasses Bone on multiple tasks, although with a trade-off in computational efficiency.
475   Bone brings new possibilities to existing LLM PEFT techniques. Instead of focusing solely on
476   optimizing LoRA, we should shift our attention to innovative PEFT methods that are better suited
477   to the architecture of LLMs.
