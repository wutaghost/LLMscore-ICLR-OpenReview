009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Bias in model estimation can lead to wider prediction intervals, diminishing the
012            utility of predictive inference. Existing methods have attempted to address this
013            issue, but they often rely on nontrivial assumptions such as specific error distri-
014            butions or model sparsity, and fail to guarantee coverage in finite samples, which
015            makes their predictions unreliable in practice. To overcome these limitations, we
016            propose a model-aware conformal prediction method that utilizes known model
017            information to achieve debiasing while leaving the unknown aspects, such as
018            data distribution, to the conformal prediction framework. This approach requires
019            only the assumption of exchangeability, making it broadly applicable across var-
020
               ious models. Importantly, it retains the finite-sample coverage property and pro-
               duces shorter prediction intervals compared to existing methods. When applied
021
               to threshold ridge regression, we theoretically demonstrate that the model-aware
022
               conformal prediction maintains finite-sample marginal coverage and, under cer-
023            tain assumptions, converges to the oracle prediction band, achieving asymptotic
024            conditional validity. Numerical experiments further show that our method out-
025            performs existing methods, providing more efficient prediction intervals across
026            diverse regression datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032
      Uncertainty quantification is crucial in developing machine learning models, particularly in contexts
      involving high-stakes decision-making in fields such as medicine and finance. However, a key chal-
033
      lenge in constructing effective prediction bands is the bias in model estimation. Whether introduced
034
      by model assumptions, data sparsity, or other factors, bias often leads to overly conservative predic-
035   tion bands that unnecessarily widen to account for the model’s systematic errors. This results in less
036   informative prediction bands and reduces their utility in practice. Many existing methods seek to ad-
037   dress this issue by constructing prediction bands that account for model bias(Zhang & Zhang (2014);
038   Javanmard & Montanari (2014); Van de Geer et al. (2014); Zhang & Politis (2022; 2023)). However,
039   these methods all rely on nontrivial assumptions such as the error distribution, the homoscedasticity
040   of errors, the quality of the estimator, sparsity, and low intrinsic dimensionality, which are often not
041   true in practice. In addition, most of them obtain asymptotic but not finite-sample validity.
042   To overcome these limitations, we propose the model-aware conformal prediction, a novel approach
043   integrating the underlying mechanisms of the model and parameters to alleviate the influence of bias
044   on prediction inference, and leaving the unknown aspects—such as the distribution of the data—to
045   the conformal prediction framework to maintain the finite-sample coverage property. Specifically,
046   We account for the bias when constructing the nonconformity score function. Then we take the
047   threshold ridge regression as an example to illustrate its better performance, since it is computa-
048   tionally simple and may be preferable in some settings(Zhang & Politis (2022) and Shao & Deng
049
      (2012)).
050   In summary, our contribution are as follows:
051
052          • We propose a model-aware conformal prediction framework, which provides shorter and
053            more efficient prediction intervals while retaining the finite-sample coverage property
               across a wide range of applications.

             • We apply our method to threshold ridge regression and theoretically demonstrate that, un-
055            der certain conditions, it converges to the oracle prediction band, and achieves asymptotical
056            conditional validity.
057
058          • Through experiments on real-world datasets, we show that model-aware conformal pre-
059            diction produces shorter prediction intervals while maintaining the required coverage than
060            existing methods.

063   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
064
065   In recent years, the inference problem in high-dimensional models has garnered significant atten-
066   tion, though much of the focus has been on regression coefficients. For instance, Zhang & Zhang
067   (2014) assumed that the linear model is correct and constructed confidence intervals for individual
068   coefficients βj using debiased estimators obtained by inverting the KKT conditions of ℓ1 -penalized
069   regression problems. Similar methods are discussed in Javanmard & Montanari (2014) and Van de
070
      Geer et al. (2014). Zhang & Politis (2022) proposed the debiasing method for the estimator in
      threshold ridge regression and used Bootstrap to construct prediction intervals. Zhang & Politis
071
      (2023) extended it to linear models with heteroskedastic and correlated errors. Another prominent
072
      approach in high-dimensional inference is post-selection inference(Liu & Yu (2013); Berk et al.
073   (2013); Lee et al. (2016); Tibshirani et al. (2018); Zrnic & Jordan (2023)), which first applies vari-
074   able selection techniques to identify influential covariates, followed by fitting ordinary least-squares
075   regression on the selected covariates. These methods focus on providing coverage for coefficients in
076   the best linear approximation given the selected covariates. However, these approaches often rely on
077   nontrivial assumptions, such as specific error distributions, homoscedasticity of errors, the quality
078   of the estimator, sparsity, and low intrinsic dimensionality, which are frequently violated in prac-
079   tice. When these assumptions do not hold, the inference tools become invalid, especially in cases of
080   model misspecification.
081   In contrast, conformal inference does not depend on such stringent conditions, making it particularly
082   well-suited for high-dimensional settings where traditional assumptions are often unrealistic. There
083   have been several attempts to conformalize the high-dimensional models. Hebiri (2010) proposed
084   a partial conformalization of LASSO; however, this approach did not provide coverage guarantees.
085   Lei (2019) introduced a piecewise linear homotopy method for LASSO to construct prediction bands
086   efficiently and extended this technique to the elastic net framework. Izbicki et al. (2022) considered
087
      the conditional density function as a nonconformity score function and utilized a data-driven parti-
      tion that scales to high dimensions. However, these methods do not adequately address the impact
088
      of estimation bias in the models, which can significantly affect their efficiency.
089
090   Our work is related to a recent work by Zhang & Politis (2022), which used a bias correction method
091   in threshold ridge regression to improve the performance of prediction inference. They proposed the
092   hybrid bootstrap to construct prediction intervals and established the consistency property of the
093
      prediction region, but in an asymptotic sense, which is often insufficient in practice. This paper
      uses the same debiasing technique as Zhang & Politis (2022) but extends it into the conformal pre-
094
      diction framework, offering several advantages. First, our approach ensures finite-sample marginal
095
      coverage without depending on strong assumptions, making it more robust and practical in real-
096   world applications. Second, we theoretically prove that the prediction intervals constructed by our
097   method converge to the oracle prediction band in Lei et al. (2018) under certain conditions and have
098   asymptotic conditional validity.

      5   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this paper, we propose conformal prediction with model-aware debiasing as a novel approach that
524   leverages known information from the data to formulate the debiasing nonconformity score function
525   and leaves the unknown aspects to the conformal prediction framework. Our method enhances
526   the efficiency of prediction intervals while preserving finite-sample coverage. Notably, it achieves
527   stronger validity, including asymptotic conditional coverage under some conditions.
528
      We plan to extend this concept to more general settings. Such an extension of conformal prediction
529
      holds promise not only for regression problems but also for classification and other unsupervised
530   learning tasks, such as clustering. Specifically, we aim to enhance our method to estimate a pre-
531   dictive probability distribution (see more details in Izbicki et al. (2022)) using a bias correction ap-
532   proach, rather than merely providing interval estimates. This is particularly important since oracle
533   prediction intervals can often be quite large, especially in the context of mixed models. Therefore,
534   we propose considering the highest predictive density set as the oracle prediction band. This pre-
535   dictive set requires minimal assumptions and represents the smallest Lebesgue measure with local
536   validity and asymptotic conditional validity, thereby facilitating improved performance in a broader
537   range of scenarios.

      Proof of Theorem 4. We follow the methodology outlined in Theorem 3.4 of Lei et al. (2018). The
865   proof is divided into two main parts: first, we demonstrate that the center of the prediction interval
866   derived from our method is asymptotically close to the center of the oracle prediction interval;
867   second, we show that the lengths of the two intervals are also asymptotically equivalent.
868
869   Indeed, we establish that the center of the prediction interval, Xn+1 θ̃, is close to the oracle center,
870
      Xn+1 θ. This claim can be rigorously verified using Equation (26). Next, we analyze the length of
      the prediction interval. This part directly follows from Theorem 3, which provides the necessary
871
      bounds and asymptotic equivalence for interval lengths.
872
873   By combining these two results, we conclude that the prediction interval constructed by our method
874   asymptotically matches the oracle prediction interval in both center and length, completing the proof.
