009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011                Building on studies documenting gender and racial biases in vision-language
012                models, recent works show that such models often fail to generate geographically-
013                representative images that accurately reflect different regions around the world. A
014                common concern is that the data used to train these models is not representative,
015                prompting the question: which parts of the world do these training examples
016
                   come from? To answer this question, we develop a system, GeoProfiler, which
                   geographically profiles multimodal datasets by mapping image-caption pairs to
017
                   countries. Using location information from captions, GeoProfiler maps examples to
018
                   countries with a high precision (0.86). We then apply GeoProfiler to geographically
019                profile the English captions of the LAION dataset for 10 common entities (e.g.,
020                house, flag, etc.). We observe the geographical distribution of 8 entities to obey
021                the power law distribution. The United States, the United Kingdom, and India are
022                most represented, appearing in 53.7% of samples. Problematically, African and
023                South American countries are severely under-represented with only 2.0% and 4.3%
024                of images respectively. We also observe a high correlation between a country’s
025                GDP and frequency (ρ = 0.79). Lastly, we analyze the diversity of images from
026                individual countries, and find that more images does not imply higher diversity.1

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Vision-language models (VLMs) (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023) suffer from gender and
031   racial biases (Hall et al., 2023a; Fraser & Kiritchenko, 2024; Agarwal et al., 2021), making their
032   deployment in real-world applications harmful. Expanding on these findings, recent studies (Basu
033   et al., 2023; Hall et al., 2023b; 2024) highlight that text-to-image models fail to generate images
034   that accurately reflect the surroundings of different geographical regions around the world. These
035   works share concern over the contents of the data used to train such models, raising an important
036   question: which parts of the world do the training image-text pairs come from? Answering this
037
      question can serve multiple purposes. Data curators can measure and improve the geographical
      representativeness of datasets, while practitioners can decide among various datasets before training
038
      their models. Additionally, auditors can probe relationships between the geographical distribution in
039
      the training data and model behavior, similar to previous studies (Razeghi et al., 2022).
040
041   Determining image sources can be challenging in practice. Given a multimodal dataset, one may con-
042   sider predicting which country an image-caption pair belongs to based on the content of the image, its
043
      metadata, URL, or the associated caption. However, image-geolocalizers are still imprecise (Vivanco
      et al., 2023; Astruc et al., 2024) and image metadata often lacks location details; similarly, such
044
      information may either be absent or inaccurate in image URLs. Captions on the other hand, describe
045
      images and may mention locations. This calls for a system that can map captions to countries, and
046   also determine the fraction of underspecified captions (Hutchinson et al., 2022), (i.e., captions without
047   location mentions). Inferring location from a given caption is in itself a difficult task. First, it requires
048   detecting any location mentions within the text, which is highly context dependent (e.g., “Buffalo”
049   can refer to either a place or an animal). The next step is to correctly assign the detected location to
050   its country name, which can be ambiguous (e.g., “Cambridge” is a city in both the US and England).
051
      In this paper, we develop GeoProfiler, a system that maps data points in multimodal datasets to
052   their respective countries. We apply this system to analyze common entities that may have diverse

      Existing Geoparsing Algorithms. Extracting location mentions from text and mapping them to
115   countries or geographic coordinates is a well-studied challenge (Middleton et al., 2018; Martínez

118   OpenStreetMap (OSM)(OpenStreetMap) gazetteer, can be used for recognizing diverse place names,
119   including multilingual or abbreviated ones. Similarly, GazPNE2(Hu et al., 2022) employs deep
120   learning techniques and gazetteers like OSM and GeoNames (GeoNames database) for location
121   extraction. Despite their high precision, these systems often suffer from low recall (Hu et al., 2023),
122   depend heavily on gazetteers, and perform best with formal text, limiting their coverage. Our approach
123
      leverages large language models to extract and disambiguate location mentions into country names
      automatically, eliminating the need for gazetteers and improving coverage.

125   Geographical Profiling of Existing Datasets. Previous studies (De Vries et al., 2019; Shankar
126   et al., 2017; Naggita et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Faisal et al., 2022) assess the geo-diversity

129
      and European countries (De Vries et al., 2019; Shankar et al., 2017). Furthermore, web-scraped

130
      perspectives (Naggita et al., 2023). Most of these works obtain geographical annotations from the
131
      Flickr API, or utilize external services. Another recent study (Faisal et al., 2022) finds that English
132   speaking and wealthier countries are heavily represented in datasets like SQUAD (Rajpurkar et al.,
133   2016) and MLQA (Lewis et al., 2019). Their work examines geographical representation in language
134   datasets, while ours focuses on vision-language datasets.

      In this section, we describe the proposed system, GeoProfiler. We first discuss the necessary notation
139
      and then provide details about the various components of our system.
140
141   Preliminaries. Let D = {(xi , yi )}Ni=1 be a vision-language dataset where yi is the caption accompa-
142   nying image xi . To analyze the geographical distributions for various entities, we select each entity n
143
      to be diverse in appearance, yet well-known and globally relevant (e.g., house).3 We then randomly
      sample Dn ∈ D such that for each (xi , yi ) ∈ Dn , n is a word in caption yi . GeoProfiler maps each
144
      (xi , yi ) ∈ Dn to a tag ci ∈ C (the set of all possible countries including a “no country” tag). For
145
      simplicity, we choose countries as our denomination of analysis since finer distinctions like states
146   or cities would significantly increase the complexity of the study. In the following subsections, we
147   describe the the method we use for geo-profiling the captions of Dn (§3.1). To ensure that we only
148   consider images that contain the entity n, we train and use an entity-presence classifier (§3.2).
149
150   3.1       G EO - PROFILING C APTIONS
151
152   The caption-based geo-profiling involves two steps: (1) detecting locations in captions (e.g., Cam-
153   bridge), and (2) mapping locations to their countries (e.g. England). We first geographically annotate
154   a dataset of 1000 randomly sampled captions from Dn (inter-annotator agreement is 90%), and
155   explore several approaches for geo-profiling using this dataset. We observe that string-matching,
156
      which matches substrings in captions with locations from a geodatabase (e.g., GeoNames (GeoNames
      database)), leads to many false positives. Since this approach does not account for context, common
157
      entities like “Stock” and “Century” (places in Canada and the US) are tagged as places, resulting in
158
      a very low precision of 0.12 and recall of 0.79 on our annotated data. These false positives can be
159   reduced by NER taggers (Spacy) that mark places in a text with a location tag (precision is 0.71).
160   However, NER taggers tend to miss location mentions, increasing false negatives (recall is 0.59).

186   These challenges in geo-profiling lead us to the realization that the system must be context-aware and

197   this task to future work. Further details on geo-profiling captions are available in the Appendix A.4.
198   A Note on Geo-profiling Images. We also assess the feasibility of inferring countries directly from
199   images. For each entity n, we sample 100 images per country, based on silver labels from the Mixtral
200   model (we only consider entity and country pairs whose frequency exceeds 100). We evaluate two
201   state-of-the-art geo-localization models: GeoCLIP (Vivanco et al., 2023) and OSV-5M (Astruc et al.,
202   2024). GeoCLIP achieves an accuracy of 35.5% at the country level and 69% at the continent level.
203   In contrast, the OSV-5M model performs less accurately, with country and continent accuracies
204   of 22% and 56%, respectively. These results highlight the challenges and limitations of existing
205
      geo-localization methods in predicting specific countries or even broader continent-level locations
      from visual data alone. We share the accuracies for individual entities in Appendix A.6. Further
206
      research is needed to accurately infer locations from images. Comparatively, LLM-based methods
207
      are more effective in inferring locations from captions whenever they contain geographical cues.

212   However, images in Dn may not always depict the entity n. This calls for a filtering step to remove
213   these irrelevant images. We find zero-shot foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to

217   classifier, a binary classifier that predicts whether a given entity n is present in an image.
218
219   To train this classifier, we sample a set of approximately 550-650 images from Dn . To ensure these
220
      images are geographically diverse, we use the predictions obtained from geo-profiling the captions
      to sample images from various regions (Economic Regions) and economic stratas (income, None),
221
      categorizing countries into 4 income groups and 17 geographical regions (details in the Appendix
222
      A.3). We select 15 images from every possible income-region combination, creating a geographically
223   and economically diverse set. We then recruit crowd workers from Prolific (prolific, None) to mark
224   images in which the given entity is visible. Each image is shown to 3 annotators, and the final
225   annotation is determined by majority voting. For a 15 minute survey, annotators are paid $2.45. The
226   inter-annotator agreements are presented in Appendix A.3.3. Of the annotated images, 100 form
227   the test set, and the rest are used for training. We train an SVM classifier with CLIP image features
228   and image annotations as labels, which achieves a high precision of 0.88. Finally, using this model,
229   we filter out irrelevant image-caption pairs from Dn . An overview of our geo-profiling approach is

236

      4.4       F REQUENCY VS H UMAN R ATINGS
408
409   A recent study inspects the geographical representativeness of Stable Diffusion, where humans
410   rate how well generated images reflect the surroundings in their respective countries (Basu et al.,
411   2023). This study is conducted across 27 countries with 10 commonly-occurring entities, 5 of which
412   overlap with our chosen ones. The study also discusses how representativeness differs for generations
413   with underspecified prompts (e.g., High definition image of a {entity}) and country-
414   specific prompts (e.g., High definition image of a {entity} in {country}). We
415
      follow up on this work and study the relationship between frequencies of countries in the training
      dataset and human ratings of geographical representativeness of generated images. For generations
416
      with unspecified prompts, we observe a positive correlation with frequency for flag, kitchen, and beach
417
      (ρ > 0.4), indicating that human ratings of geographical representativeness for different countries
418   are proportional to the frequency of occurrence in the training data (see Table 4). However, the
419   correlation is weak for house and road. A similar pattern is seen for the country-specific generations,
420   although the correlation reduces for all entities except house. We feel this happens as the explicit
421   mention of the country name in the prompts increases the scores for each country, not necessarily
422   proportional to their frequencies.

      6    C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this work, we proposed GeoProfiler, a system that maps image-caption pairs of vision-language
477
      datasets to corresponding countries. We investigated the geographical distribution of the LAION2B-
      en dataset (the subset of the LAION-5B dataset with English captions) with respect to 10 entities, and
478
      found that 46.1% of the total captions were underspecified. For the remaining samples, we observed
479
      that the geographical distributions for 8 out of 10 entities obey the power law distribution. When
480   comparing the geographical distributions of each entity with ground truth reference distributions,
481   we found that for 5 entities, more than 50% of the countries exhibit underrepresentation. Across
482   all entities, the US, UK, and India are the most frequent countries, while many countries in South
483   America, Oceania, and Africa appear infrequently. We also explored the country-wise diversity of
484   images for each entity, and discovered that the frequency of a country does not correlate with its
485   diversity. We hope that GeoProfiler encourages and enables greater transparency into the geographical
      representation of large-scale multimodal datasets.

      Hall et al. (Hall et al., 2023b) find that popular text-to-image generative models exhibit lower diversity
731
      and realism when generating images from African and Western Asian countries as compared to
732
      European images. Moreover, another work (Hall et al., 2024) discovers that the perception of
733   geographical representation varies region to region over the globe, making evaluation of such models
734   more difficult. Some works also highlight how countries having large English-speaking populations
735   are underpredicted by language models (Schwöbel et al., 2023). Not only do these biases affect
736   generative models, but also the state-of-the-art object recognition models. For example, Gustafon
737   et al. (Gustafson et al., 2023) show that performances of popular models like CLIP (Radford et al.,
738   2021) degrade with decreasing income level. Similar performance drops are seen in other works
739   as well (Ramaswamy et al., 2024). These papers point out the disparities of models trained on
740   different modalities with respect to various parts of the world – thus, highlighting a big drawback of
741
      current machine learning models. Analysing the geographical composition of the datasets used to
      train these models is crucial to assess their behavior. As discussed in Section 2 (main paper), there
742
      are several works (De Vries et al., 2019; Shankar et al., 2017; Faisal et al., 2022) on exploring the
743
      geographical distributions of popular image and text datasets that uncover the overrepresentation of
744   North American and European countries in existing image and text datasets. REVISE (Wang et al.,
745   2022) measures biases in image datasets with respect to objects, people and also geographies, however
746   it relies on external availability of geographical data. Our proposed tool focuses on multimodal
747   datasets, and maps countries from location mentions in the relevant image-captions. We are hopeful
748   that the analysis we perform in this paper will guide future dataset curators and model auditors to
749   evaluate other datasets as well as understand model predictions and generations.
750   Existing Geo-diverse Datasets. Existing datasets overrepresent Western, English-speaking coun-
751   tries (De Vries et al., 2019; Shankar et al., 2017). Thus, several geo-diverse datasets have been recently
752   proposed. For example, DollarStreet (Rojas et al., 2022) and GeoDE (Ramaswamy et al., 2024) are
753   geo-diverse image datasets collected through manual and crowdsourcing efforts. Other multi-cultural
754   geo-diverse image and language datasets include GeoYFCC (Dubey et al., 2021), MaRVL (Liu et al.,
755   2021), GD-VCR (Yin et al., 2021), CultureAtlas (Fung et al., 2024), GeoNet (Kalluri et al., 2023),
      among others.

1374   provided by UN World Tourism (Hotel reference data). We approximate the number of beaches in a
1375   country through its length of coastline available (Beach reference data).
1376
       Additional Findings. In the main paper, we determine geographical representation of a country by
1377   computing the ratio of its probability in the dataset (for a given entity) with its probability in the
1378   ground truth distribution. Intuitively, a country can be called overrepresented if this ratio is greater
1379   than a threshold r, and similarly it is called underrepresented if this ratio is lower than 1r . We show our
1380   findings with respect to r = 3 in the main paper subsection 4.2. Here we investigate for a few other
1381   values of r (specifically r = 2 and r = 5), showing that with higher values of r, the percentages of
1382   over and underrepresentation increases (Table 14). The choice of the exact value of r is dependent on
1383   the perception of the practitioner who is evaluating the geographical representativeness of a dataset.
1384   We select the value to be 3, following related previous works (Schwöbel et al., 2023).
