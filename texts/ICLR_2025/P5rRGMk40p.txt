                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We address a novel task for monocular explicit surface reconstruction that ex-
               tends traditional surface normal integration over measurements on a regular grid
011
               to direct continuous surface depth estimation. Our solution accepts coordinates
012
               as queries and predicts both the normal and depth of an arbitrary query point by
013            its relative locations and orientations to the points distributed in its vicinity. In
014            general, all points are regarded by our model as random samples drawn from an
015            underlying continuous gradient field of a surface which we parameterize using
016            a field of polynomials to establish its topology. We establish a mapping from
017            coordinates to a sequence of learnable polynomial coefficients to model a con-
018            tinuous surface and train a neural network to approximate it. We decompose a
019            continuous surface representation into two components: (1) a set of grid points of
020            unknown orientations whose locations are picked by a quadtree and (2) a set of
021
               sample points whose orientations are directly observable. Our training workflow
               estimates the normal of grid points and the locations of depth discontinuities iter-
022
               atively. During each iteration, we generate a normal map of grid points for it to
023
               be processed by a standard bilateral normal integrator to identify the locations of
024            depth discontinuities, which we use to refine the estimation for grid-based normal
025            map in the subsequent iteration. As a result, the learned model generates both
026            normal and depth for arbitrary coordinates accurately in a continuous field. We
027            provide both theoretical formulation for our design and extensive empirical evi-
028            dence to demonstrate that our proposed method not only delivers a performance as
029            effective as its grid-based counterpart approaches but also flexibly and accurately
030            addresses the continuous cases that existing methods are unable to handle.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Normal integration establishes an inverse mapping from a surface’s normal map to its depth. It com-
036   pletes the production cycle of multiple important 3d computer vision tasks including photometric
037   stereo, shape from shading, etc., which settles on surface normal as their output. Most existing so-
038   lutions to normal integration formulate the problem as an inverse problem of recovering a discrete
039
      scalar field from its corresponding gradient map by a numerical solver to the corresponding partial
      differential equation (PDE) in a 2D space subject to various boundary conditions. This PDE is of-
040
      ten solved by a large linear system involving spatial numerical differentiation and recent research
041
      effort have been put into modeling and identifying the locations of depth discontinuities properly.
042   In the discrete domain, PDE solvers consume input stored over a regular grid Quéau et al. (2018a),
043   where the spacing between adjacent measurements is uniform so that numerical differentiation is
044   properly defined consistently over the entire integration domain. For two reasons we believe it is
045   necessary to develop a tool for explicit surface reconstruction that directly interoperates with con-
046   tinuous representations: on the device end, present vision data acquisition techniques may provide
047   unstructured representations Peers et al. (2006) including surface normal which is directly made
048   available for a wide spectrum of downstream tasks Xiu et al. (2023); on the design end, as dense
049   representations for surface normal becomes an inalienable product of data-driven inverse rendering
050
      pipelines Bae & Davison (2024); He et al. (2024), but a model that explicitly and flexibly abridges
      dense representations involving surface normal and surface structure still remains elusive.
051
052   We study a new type of task for monocular explicit surface reconstruction and introduce a design that
053   directly accepts coordinate-based queries and produces estimates of both depth and normal for query
      points of arbitrary coordinates. We regard the observed surface points as a set of random samples

098   there are two types of pair-wise constraints formed by two connected points in terms of how these
099   two points located geometrically: (1) grid-grid connection, whose bonding is directly determined by

101   between a grid point and its neighboring sample points whose weighting is derived from the nearby
102   grid-grid connection.

107   neural network that accurately approximates the mapping from field coordinates to the polynomial
      coefficients. This implies that the training has to carry out an iterative procedure that alternatively

201
202   Per-point normal of a scattered point cloud can be estimated as a weighted average of neighbor-
203   ing surface normals Ben-Shabat & Gould (2020). The surface is parameterized using polynomials
204   Cazals & Pouget (2005), based on which polynomial coefficients are fitted by least-square solver.
205   The weight is per-point and learned as a product of supervised learning. This learning process can
206
      be improved by making the order and scale of the polynomial location-adaptive Zhu et al. (2021).
      Moreover, the implicit 0-level set can be integrated to further improve the global orientation consis-
207
      tency of the normal estimation for a compact surface Li et al. (2024). We extend these formulation to
208
      the inverse domain where the shape structure is not provided. This formulation can also be applied
209   to shading analysis Xiong et al. (2014).

250   3.2   S URFACE M ODEL WITH D EPTH D ISCONTINUITIES
251
252   Correctly identifying the location of depth discontinuities (Figure 2c) and properly utilizing this
253   information for depth estimation is crucial for normal integration. The parameterization scheme
254   proposed by bilateral normal integrator Cao et al. (2022) has demonstrated excellent performance
255
      on estimating depth map recovery for regular-grid measurements. We propose a decomposed sur-
      face parameterization to extend the benefit of this scheme to continuous domain. In particular, we
256
      categorize surface points into two groups: sample points xs whose surface orientations are directly
257
      observable and grid points xg whose locations are aligned with their neighbors along either x-axis
258   or y-axis.
259
260   Accordingly, this decomposed parameterization leads to two types of connections between two
261
      points: (1) grid-grid connection that links two adjacent grid points, which we denote as xg ; (2)
      grid-sample connection that associates a grid point with one of its child sample points, denoted as
262
      xs , designated by the quadtree. As examplified in Figure 3a, bilateral normal integrator weighs two
263
      axis-aligned edges joining the same grid point by a binomial random variable whose outcomes are
264   either (0, 1) or (0.5, 0.5), with 0 indicating that the edge is completely cut off by a depth disconti-
265   nuity and 1 for complete connection, whereas (0.5, 0.5) means the grid point is connected from the
266   both sides with even balance. In addition, if we are also able to accurately estimate the normal map
267   Ng (x, y) consisting of all xg , the corresponding depth map Zg (x, y) can be obtained by a traditional
268   normal integrator. Since a quadtree creates a rectangular “cell” that quarantines a sample point xs , it
269   can be readily seen that the depth of a sample point xs inside a cell relative to the cell’s four vertices
      of xg , and the decomposed parameterization achieves a complete model for continuous surface.

      As discussed in Section 3.1, our model interoperates with a network that establishes a mapping
273   Φ : R2 → RK that associates a coordinate (x, y) with a sequence of K polynomials coefficients
274   {βj }, leading to a continuous vector field β (x, y) encoding an point-wise n-jet surface whose local
275   system of coordinates takes (x, y) as its origin. Our model approximates this mapping Φ using
276   a coordinate-driven neural network, which, through training, is expected to produce an accurate
277   estimate normal map estimate, Ng (x, y) on grid points.
278
      In addition to learning Ng (x, y), correctly identifying the locations of the depth discontinuities not
279   only improves the accuracy of depth map for grid points xg but also leads to a better formulation of
280   the loss function which in turn improves the accuracy of Ng (x, y). Since depth discontinuities are
281   formulated on the premises that the structure of the surface is known, the training of the model essen-
282   tially follows an iterative optimization process that alternatively refines β (x, y) and the likelihood
283   estimate of the locations of depth discontinuities.

354   Continuous surface depth estimation also utilizes grid-sample connections over which relative
355   heights of xs to a set of nearby grid points xg are integrated to produce a single depth value.
356   This requires rearranging grid-centered grid-grid connections as four orthogonal boundaries for a
357   sample-centered rectangular cell. Specifically, each sample point, xs , is circumscribed by these
358   four boundaries, and we establish four grid-sample connections between the sample point and its
359   four vertices made of grid points xg .
360   Because the grid-grid connections in the same cell are drawn from different binomial variables,
361   normalization is required to produce a consistent estimate. Our solution is to formulate a binary
362   clustering problem for each connection independently. In particular, a sample point xs in a cell
363   is to be evaluated against each of the four boundaries, where the connection over each boundary
364   encodes one or two clusters with their centers located at its two ends. Here the weight of grid-grid
365   connection serves as the prior distribution of the clusters. For instance, a grid-grid connection with
366   0 weight indicates that its two ends belong to two separated clusters; on the other hand, there exists
367   a single cluster if the weight is greater than 0.5. Quantitatively, we use cosine distance between the
368
      surface normal of the two ends of a grid-sample connection to measure likelihood of the sample

409   5     E XPERIMENT
410
411   We prepare two types of input data to our model to test its effectiveness. We first conduct experiment
412   using input data stored on a standard regular grid to test verify its “backward compatibility”. Namely,
413   we do not assume the structure of data is known as a priori and the locations of grid points are
414   determined by a quadtree independently. In this case, we compare our results against results obtained
415
      from existing counterpart normal integration approaches and let them process grid input directly.
      Throughout our experiment, we fix the order of per-point polynomials to be 3, meaning the length
416
      of coefficient sequence is constant 9 (e.g. Equation 3 contains 9 additive terms).
417
418   In addition, we also prepare a set of scattered measurements in the form of 5-tuple, (x, y, nx , ny , nz ),
419   as exemplified in Figure 1, to which existing designs do not apply. To achieve fair comparison, we
420   follow a similar routine to our design that applies a quadtree to prepare a best-effort grid based setting
421
      that is suitable for counterpart method to deliver meaningful results. In particular, we establish the
      grid-based normal map through nearest neighbor value mapping from the unstructured input data.
422
      We normalize measurement coordinates to be inside a [−1, 1] × [−1, 1] square. Our solution is
423
      implemented using PyTorch Paszke et al. (2017), and experiments are conducted on a single Nvidia
424   RTX 4090 GPU with 24G RAM. K-Nearest-Neighbor search is performed by Pytorch3D Ravi et al.
425   (2020). Estimation results are evaluated using Mean Absolute Depth Error (MADE).

524   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   This paper introduces a novel computational framework that, by taking coordinate-based depth
527   queries, allows for normal integration to be performed over a continuous domain. We propose
528
      to represent continuous surface using a continuous field of learnable polynomial coefficients, and
      we integrate a depth-discontinuity-aware edge weighting scheme for pair-wise point connections
529
      into our training pipeline to obtain these parameters. Our experiment on various settings and various
530
      datasets shows that our method not only performs as effectively as existing approaches on traditional
531   grid-based input, but also successfully delivers continuous surface normal integration that existing
532   methods cannot handle. Furthermore, because continuous monocular surface representations en-
533   ables flexible across-view alignment in a multi-view setting, extending this monocular design to
534   multi-view explicit dense surface reconstructions is the goal of our future work.
