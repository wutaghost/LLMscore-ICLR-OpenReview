009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce Diffusion Compose, a zero-shot approach for depth-aware scene
012            editing using Text-to-Image diffusion models. While existing methods for 3D-
013            aware editing focus on object-centric control, they do not support compositional
014            depth-aware edits, such as placing objects at specific depths or combining multi-
015            ple scenes realistically. We address this by incorporating depth-based multiplane
016            scene representation in diffusion models. These planes, placed at fixed depths,
017            can be individually edited or composed to enable 3D-aware scene modifications.
018
               However, direct manipulation of multiplane representation of diffusion latents of-
               ten leads to identity loss or unrealistic blending. To overcome this, we propose a
019
               novel multiplane feature guidance technique that gradually aligns source latents
020
               with the target edit at each denoising step. We validate Diffusion Compose on two
021            challenging tasks: a) scene composition, blending scenes with consistent depth
022            order and scene illumination, and b) depth-aware object insertion, inserting novel
023            objects at specified depths in a scene while preserving occlusions and scene struc-
024            ture and illumination. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Diffusion Com-
025            pose significantly outperforms task-specific baselines for object placement and
026            harmonization. A user study further confirms that it produces realistic, identity-
027            preserving, and accurate depth-aware scene edits.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Text-to-Image (T2I) diffusion models Rombach et al. (2022); Saharia et al. (2022); Esser et al.
033
      (2024) can generate highly realistic images from text prompts. Various conditioning mechanisms
      have been proposed Zhang et al. (2023a); Epstein et al. (2023) for complex image editing, such
034
      as altering scene appearance Brooks et al. (2023) or teleporting objects within scenes Chen et al.
035
      (2024). However, these approaches lack the ability to edit scenes with 3D control, such as placing
036   a new vase at a specific 3D location in an image (Fig. 1). Achieving this requires addressing the
037   following challenges: i) Geometric consistency: the placed object should fit naturally in the scene
038   ii) Occlusion handling: for realistic placement, the placed object should be naturally occluded by
039   the existing objects without any artifacts iii) Illumination and lighting: the placed object should
040   respect the lighting in the scene to create realistic shading.
041
      Existing methods for 3D control in T2I models primarily focus on editing geometric object proper-
042   ties such as rotating or translating the existing objects in the scene. This is achieved by applying the
043   required geometric transformation to the diffusion features of the individual objects during denois-
044   ing Wang et al. (2024); Pandey et al. (2024a); Sajnani et al. (2024); Kumari et al. (2024). Others
045   rely on large-scale training with synthetic datasets conditioning on explicit 3D pose or geometric
046   information with text Michel et al. (2024a); Wu et al. (2024), but struggle with generalization to
047   real-world scenes. Although effective for object-centric 3D editing, these methods lack the ability
048   to perform compositional 3D scene editing, such as depth-aware object insertion or scene compo-
049   sition. Our framework addresses this gap, enabling designers and artists to achieve precise object
050
      placement and seamless scene blending with depth-aware control. This enables workflows in areas
      such as advertising, game design, and visual effects, where depth-based layering is crucial.
051
052   We propose DiffusionCompose to enable depth-aware scene editing from a single image without the
053   need to explicitly model a complete scene geometry. The key idea is to use a multiplane representa-
      tion, where planes are placed at discrete depth levels, allowing for 3D-aware editing by manipulating

094
095
096   Editing in Generative models. Text-to-Image diffusion models have enabled several image editing
097   tasks previously difficult to achieve Hertz et al. (2022); Epstein et al. (2023); Pandey et al. (2024a);
098   Brooks et al. (2023). An effective approach for editing with diffusion models involves manipu-
099
      lating the intermediate cross-attention and self-attention maps Hertz et al. (2022); Patashnik et al.
      (2023); Cao et al. (2023) as they provide control in defining the layout, structure, and color in an
100
      image. This operation can be performed during inference time, eliminating the need for training.
101
      Some methods swap the attention maps Hertz et al. (2022); Cao et al. (2023), and while concatenate
102   both features and take attention across the batch Zhou et al. (2024); Tewel et al. (2024). Control-
103   Net Zhang et al. (2023a) introduced spatial conditioning modalities such as depth maps or edge maps
104   for finer control. Another set of works explores the diffusion framework and condition at different
105   timestepPatashnik et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b) at U-Net layersVoynov et al. (2023b); Alaluf
106   et al. (2023). Others aim to find semantic direction in latent space or the text space Kwon et al.
107   (2022); Brack et al. (2023); Baumann et al. (2024) for editing. However, these approaches do not
      allow for 3D control in the generated scene.

      3D editing with Generative Models. Diffusion models though excellent at generating realistic im-
109   ages often fail to generate consistent 3D effects Sarkar et al. (2024); Upadhyay (2024). To achieve
110   some 3D control in the generation a common approach is to use depth conditioned diffusion model
111   and edit the depth map. One effective approach is to provide guidance Mou et al. (2024); Pandey

113   Sajnani et al. (2024) using depth and edit it, and use diffusion models to refine the rendered image

115   ing with 3D conditioned dataset Bhat et al. (2024); Michel et al. (2024b). In Wang et al. (2024),
116   multiple iterations of 3D edits are performed in image space followed by image refinement using
117
      diffusion prior. Similarly, in Pandey et al. (2024b), edited depth maps are used for conditioning and
      performing appearance guidance to preserve object and scene identity. On the other hand, Bhat et al.
118
      (2024); Michel et al. (2024a); Wu et al. (2024) perform large-scale training to achieve object-centric
119
      3D geometric control, however struggle to handle complex real scenes with multiple objects.
120
121   Object Insertion. Given a 3D representation one can insert an object while following scene geom-
122   etry Shahbazi et al. (2024) and perform 3D aware edits Haque et al. (2023). However, obtaining a
123
      good 3D representation of a scene from a single image is difficult. In Ge et al. (2024), they find an
      approximate floor plane and scene lighting to generate and place synthetic 3D assets in the given
124
      scene with relighting which is challenging to obtain for real-world objects. When dealing with
125
      only a single scene image and object image, the most common methods for object placement are
126   reference-based inpainting methods like IP-Adapter, PaintbyExample, and Anydoor Ye et al. (2023);
127   Yang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024). However, these methods do not provide control to place ob-
128   jects at a particular depth and always generate full objects without occlusion. Another recent work
129   Winter et al. (2024) performs realistic object insertion with accurate lighting and shading as it is
130   trained on high-quality datasets curated for the task. In this work, we propose a zero-shot approach
131   to generate realistic depth-aware object placement given a single object and background image with
132   consistent shadings and blending effects.

136   3.1   OVERVIEW
137
138   Our goal is to perform realistic depth-aware editing with a single image using the generative priors
139   of Text-to-Image models without retraining. To this end, we utilize the multiplane scene represen-
140   tation, where a scene is decomposed into a set of frontoparallel planes at fixed depths, enabling 3D
141   scene editing. Directly applied in the image space the multiplane representation does not respect the
142   scene semantics during editing and leads to ‘cut-paste’ appearance (Fig. 3). Instead, we integrate
143
      the multiplane representation into the latent space of T2I diffusion models, capitalizing on their rich
      image generation priors. We accomplish this through multiplane feature guidance at each denoising
144
      step, updating the intermediate source latents to enable consistent depth-aware editing. In the fol-
145
      lowing sections, we discuss the preliminaries of our work and present our approach for multiplane
146   feature guidance, along with its applications in depth-aware editing.
147

      3.2   P RELIMINARIES
149
150   Diffusion models learn to transform random noise into an image with iterative denoising. In the
151   forward diffusion process, image x0 is corrupted by sequentially adding standard Gaussian noise
152   ϵ. A denoiser network ϵθ is trained to estimate the added noise, conditioned on the timestep and
153   optional conditioning such as text. For generating images, the reverse diffusion process denoises
154   the random noise xT , with several passes through denoising network ϵθ . To accelerate the diffusion
155   models, Latent Diffusion Models Rombach et al. (2022) take a two-stage approach where the input
156   image is first encoded into a lower dimensional latent space, and the diffusion process is applied in
157   the compressed latent space, significantly reducing the computational requirements.
158   Guidance. There are two main approaches for conditioning diffusion models on additional modal-
159   ities: classifier guidance and classifier-free guidance. In classifier-free guidance Ho & Salimans
160   (2022), conditional ϵθ (xt , y, t) predictions with y conditioning are combined with unconditional
161   predictions ϵθ (xt , t) using a scalar weight w(t). Classifier guidance, on the other hand, provides
      inference time conditioning by guiding the reverse diffusion process using a predefined energy func-

291   4     E XPERIMENTS
292
293   We perform extensive experiments to evaluate Diffusion Compose for the task of depth-aware edit-
294   ing. In this section, we first discuss the implementation and dataset details, followed by experiments
295   on object placement scene composition, and ablations.
296
      Implementation Details. We use Stable Diffusion v2-depth Rombach et al. (2022) which has depth
297
      conditioning as the base T2I model for scene composition and Anydoor Chen et al. (2024) for depth-
298   aware object placement. For multiplane feature guidance, we use features from the last and the
299   penultimate layers of the diffusion UNet which results in accurate edits as discussed in ablations.
300   We give guidance from 0 to 38 timesteps for scene composition and update the latent ztedit for 5
301   iterations at each timestep, and for object placement, we give guidance from 30 to 50 timestep and
302   update the latent ztedit for 3 iterations at each timestep. More details are in the appendix.
303
      Dataset. As a zero-shot approach, we curated two datasets for a thorough evaluation of depth-
304   aware editing tasks. For object placement, we compiled 490 image-object pairs annotated with 2D
305   bounding boxes, object depth, and scene depth maps. The dataset includes diverse objects from
306   indoor and outdoor environments, ensuring occlusion by other objects to effectively assess depth-
307   aware placement. For scene composition, we curated a dataset of 2, 844 image pairs with diverse
308   foreground and background scenes, combining the SSHarmonization dataset Jiang et al. (2021) and
309   web-sourced images. The dataset spans a wide range of indoor and outdoor scenes, varying in
310   lighting, composition, and appearance.
311
312   4.1   O BJECT P LACEMENT
313
314
      To our knowledge, we are the first to perform depth-aware object placement using only a single
      object and background image. For the evaluation of Diffusion Compose, we define the following
315
      baselines for Diffusion Compose.
316
317   Image MPI + Harmonization (Image MPI+H). We perform MPI decomposition using the depth
318   image of the background and paste the object in the next plane to the given object depth (Sec. 3.4).
319   We preprocess the object image by segmenting the object of interest using SAM Kirillov et al. (2023)
320
      and resizing it according to the bounding box. Then, we recompose the MPI representation using
      Eq.3 to obtain the edited image. Additionally, to blend the object well in the scene, we apply a recent
321
      image harmonization technique Ke et al. (2022) on the placed object.

519   5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
520
521   Conclusion. In this work, we propose Diffusion Compose an efficient zero-shot framework for
522   depth-aware scene editing with Text-to-Image diffusion models. We leverage multiple scene repre-
523
      sentations and incorporate them in the generation process of diffusion models. Precisely, we propose
      a novel multiplane feature guidance approach to guide the generating diffusion latents towards the
524
      target edit while preserving the scene structure. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our depth-
525
      aware editing framework with the task of realistic scene composition and 3D-aware object insertion.
526   Diffusion Compose generates highly realistic scene editing results without a need for retraining.
527   We believe our work provides a new perspective on augmenting the capabilities of Text-to-Image
528   diffusion models for 3D-aware editing.
529
      Limitations. Our framework is based on pretrained Text-to-Image diffusion models and inherits the
530
      limitations and biases of the base model, such as geometrically inconsistent shadows and perspec-
531
      tives in some cases. Further, as we are applying guidance at each step of denoising, the proposed
532   method is slower than generation from the base Text-to-Image diffusion model.

      Object Placement In the depth-aware object placement task, since inpainting distorts the foreground
811   region, we use our guidance to preserve the foreground region based on the MPI masks. In this, our
812   guidance tries to preserve the foreground, and Anydoor’s additional conditioning tries to erase the
813   foreground and paint the whole object in the foreground. As we can see, both of these go against
814   each other, and since the guidance is weak compared to edge conditioning in Anydoor, initially, we
815   do latent space MPI rendering as mentioned in Sec. 3.4 and then give guidance to foreground region.
816   And there is a choice to do this latent MPI rendering at which timestep. We saw that doing this at
817   an earlier timestep gives more freedom to Anydoor, and thus, it doesn’t put the object with proper
818   occlusion as we can see in Fig. 11 and doing this at a later timestep can cause artifact at the mpi
819
      mask foreground edge.

821   D    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
822
823   Our guidance method is based on Pandey et al. (2024b). As we can see from 10, using any layer
824   other than the last feature layer causes major artifacts in generated images. Using only the last layer
825   features for guidance, doesn’t cause any artifacts, but image appearance is lost for background and
826   foreground. So, we give high weightage to the last layer feature and very low weightage to the last
827   before layer for better identity preservation. For scene composition, we start from the inverted latent
828
      of the background image and give guidance to the foreground and background layers according to
      their corresponding features. Starting from background layer latents causes the scene lighting to be
829
      inherited from the background scene. We give guidance from 0th timstep to 38 similar to diffusion
830
      handles. We also show in 10 that giving guidance till 50th timestep causes it to look similar to cut
831   paste without any lighting change.
832
833   The same layered guidance is used for object placement, and we use the same parameter used for
834
      scene composition except the number of optimization per step. For object placement, we only
      optimize for 3 steps per iteration since the inpainting model already does well in preserving the
835
      appearance of the features outside of the bounding box. At T th timestep we perform a cut paste
836
      of latents according to the MPI layer mask, then for the rest of the generation, we give guidance to
837   preserve the foreground. If the T value is early, then Anydoor edgemap condition becomes strong
838   and puts the object in the foreground, and if T is late, then image looks similar to latent cut paste
839   with artifacts at the border. We qualitatively found T=30 timstep to be working best for most cases.
840
      The time taken to generate a single image for object placement is 60 seconds, and for scene compo-
841
      sition, it takes around 86 seconds.

844   E    U SER S TUDY
845
846   We conduct a qualitative comparison of our Object Placement method against four baselines: Image
847   MPI + H, IP Adapter, Paint by Example, and Anydoor. The evaluation focuses on three key goals:
848   scene realism, identity replication of the placed object and background, and accurate placement at
849
      the intended depth. To assess these goals, we carried out a user study on 15 edits across 15 images
      from our Object Placement dataset. Each goal was evaluated separately by presenting users with
850
      pairs of images and asking them to select the one that better met the specific goal. A total of 60
851
      randomized image pairs were generated, with each pair comparing a result from our method to a
852   corresponding result from a randomly chosen baseline. These pairs were divided into three groups
853   of 20 pairs each, corresponding to the three goals. The study involved 40 participants with varied
854   experience in image editing, who evaluated all 20 pairs for each goal, resulting in 800 data points
855   per goal and 2400 data points in total. To mitigate bias, the order of image pairs and the methods
856   within each pair were randomized.
857
      For Scene Composition, we compare our approach against Image MPI + H, SDEdit, and Pair Dif-
858   fusion, focusing on two goals: realism and depth consistency. Using a subset of 15 edits across 15
859   images from our Scene Composition dataset, we generated 40 image pairs, split evenly across the
860   two goals. The same 40 users participated in this evaluation, generating 800 data points per goal,
861   for a total of 1600 data points.
