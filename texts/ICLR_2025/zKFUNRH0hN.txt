009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Video retrieval requires aligning visual content with corresponding natural lan-
012            guage descriptions. In this paper, we introduce Modality Auxiliary Concepts for
013            Video Retrieval (MAC-VR), a novel approach that leverages modality-specific
014
               tags – automatically extracted from foundation models – to enhance video re-
               trieval. Previous works have proposed to emulate human reasoning by introduc-
015
               ing latent concepts derived from the features of a video and its corresponding
016
               caption. Building on these efforts to align latent concepts across both modalities,
017            we propose learning auxiliary concepts from modality-specific tags. We introduce
018            these auxiliary concepts to improve the alignment of visual and textual latent con-
019            cepts, and so are able to distinguish concepts from one other. To strengthen the
020            alignment between visual and textual latent concepts – where a set of visual con-
021            cepts matches a corresponding set of textual concepts – we introduce an Alignment
022            Loss. This loss aligns the proposed auxiliary concepts with the modalities’ latent
023            concepts, enhancing the model’s ability to accurately match videos with their ap-
024            propriate captions. We conduct extensive experiments on three diverse datasets:
025
               MSR-VTT, DiDeMo, and ActivityNet Captions. The experimental results consis-
               tently demonstrate that modality-specific tags significantly improve cross-modal
026
               alignment, achieving performance comparable to current state-of-the-art methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      The emergence of prominent video-sharing platforms like YouTube and TikTok has supported up-
031   loading of millions of videos daily. The demand for better video retrieval methods, which align
032   textual queries with relevant video content, has subsequently increased. Most existing works use
033   two main approaches. The first Fang et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2023b) exclusively
034   uses word and frame features without leveraging the multi-modal information of videos. On the con-
035   trary, the second approach Dzabraev et al. (2021); Gabeur et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Gabeur
036   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021a); Croitoru et al. (2021) introduces additional multi-modal informa-
037   tion from videos, such as audio, speech, objects, that are encoded and used for feature aggregation.
038   In real-world scenarios, online videos often come with related textual information, such as tags –
039
      keywords associated with a video that describe its content and make it easier to search/filter. Few
      works Chen et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022a;c) extract and exploit tags in video retrieval to better
040
      align the video and textual modalities. Inspired by these previous works, we develop a novel method
041
      called MAC-VR that integrates multi-modal information by independently extracting relevant tags
042   for both videos and texts, utilizing the extensive knowledge from pre-trained Vision-Language
043   Models (VLM) and Large Language Models (LLM) as shown in Fig.1. The example in Fig.1 shows
044   the query “a girl doing gymnastics in the front yard”, the extracted visual (VT) and textual (TT)
045   tags include sports, physical, outdoors, and outside can help align this video to the corresponding
046   caption. We extend the recent work DiCoSA Jin et al. (2023b), where the visual and textual coarse
047   features are split into compact latent factors which explicitly encode visual and textual concepts.
048   Our MAC-VR introduces visual and textual tags whose coarse features are used to learn auxiliary
049   modality-specific latent concepts. These are aligned to latent concepts directly extracted from video
050
      and text, through an introduced Alignment Loss.
051   Recently, many works Liu et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2023b;c; 2022); Ibrahimi et al.; Chen et al. (2023);
052   Wang et al. (2024a) use different inference strategies to improve the final video retrieval performance
053   such as Querybank Normalisation (QB) Bogolin et al. (2022) and Dual Softmax (DSL) Cheng et al.
      (2021). In this paper, we analyse the impact of such strategies on our MAC-VR architecture to ensure

      Video Retrieval. Video retrieval aims to learn an embedding for video and text to establish ef-
085   fective connections between pertinent video content and natural language descriptions. Early ap-
086   proaches Dzabraev et al. (2021); Gabeur et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Gabeur et al. (2022); Liu
087   et al. (2021a); Croitoru et al. (2021); Fragomeni et al. (2022); Zolfaghari et al. (2021); Kunitsyn et al.
088   (2022); Dong et al. (2022) relied on pre-trained features and/or multi-modal information inherent in
089   videos, such as audio or speech, specialized to bridge the gap between video and text data. Notably,
090   MMT Gabeur et al. (2020) explores multi-modal data extracted by seven pre-trained experts but
091   integrates them without explicit guidance, employing a brute-force method. Input modalities have
092   also been masked, e.g. in Gabeur et al. (2022), where the method can learn robust representations
093
      that enhance cross-modal matching. On the contrary, MAC-VR uses only video and text modalities
      without considering any additional modalities, such as audio or speech.
094
095   Recent advancements in video retrieval have followed two main methodologies. The first involves
096   extensive pre-training of models on large-scale video-text datasets, Ge et al. (2022); Bain et al.
097   (2021). The second focuses on transferring knowledge from image-based CLIP models Radford
098   et al. (2021a) trained on extensive image-text pairs Kunitsyn et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2021); Gorti
099
      et al. (2022); Luo et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2023c); Huang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024a); Dong
      et al. (2023); Guan et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2024); Jin et al. (2024; 2023b); Xue et al. (2023); Fang
100
      et al. (2023). Some works Dong et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2024) use a distillation approach where a
101
      large network is first trained as a teacher network and then a smaller network is trained as a student
102   network. In contrast, MAC-VR does not use any distillation approach and is trained directly by
103   introducing auxiliary modality-specific tags. Similar to Jin et al. (2023b), where learnable queries
104   and latent concepts are learnt during training, we learn latent auxiliary concepts from our modality-
105   specific tags in addition to visual and textual latent concepts and use them as additional features to
106   align the visual and textual concepts.

      3.1 C ROSS -M ODAL T EXT- TO -V IDEO R ETRIEVAL
138   Given a pair (vi , ti ), where vi represents a video and ti denotes its corresponding caption, the objec-
139   tive of Cross-Modal Video Retrieval is to retrieve the video vi given the caption ti as query or vice
140   versa. Typically, models use two projection functions: fv : vi −→ Ω ∈ Rd and ft : ti −→ Ω ∈ Rd .
141
      These functions map the video and text modalities, respectively, into a shared d-dimensional latent
      embedding space, denoted as Ω. Previous approaches aim to align the representations in this space
142
      so that the representation of a video is close to that of its corresponding caption. Following training,
143
      standard inference strategies embed a gallery of test videos and ranks these in order of their dis-
144   tance from each query caption. Recent approaches utilise additional inference strategies to improve
145   performance. Two popular inference strategies are: Querybank Normalisation (QB) Bogolin et al.
146   (2022) and Dual Softmax (DSL) Cheng et al. (2021). We introduce these here and later showcase
147   their impact on fair comparison of the current SOTA methods.
148
      The QB strategy was introduced to mitigate the hubness problem of high-dimensional embedding
149
      spaces Radovanovic et al. (2010), where a small subset of samples tends to appear far more fre-
150   quently among the k-nearest neighbours of all embeddings. This phenomenon can have harmful
151   effects on retrieval methods that rely on nearest-neighbour searches to identify the best matches for
152   a given query. To mitigate this phenomenon, the similarities between embeddings are altered to
153   minimise the influence of hubs. To do this a querybank of a set of samples is constructed from the
154   query modality and is used as a probe to measure the hubness of the gallery. In other words, for each
155   query, given its vector of unnormalised similarities, S(vj , ti ), over all the elements in the gallery G
156   and a probe matrix P , whose each row is a probe vector of similarities between the querybank and
157   each element in the gallery, we can define a querybank normalisation function, QB, and get a vector
158
      of normalised similarities, ηq = QB(S(vj , ti ), P ), where the querybank normalisation function is
      the Dynamic Inverted Softmax (DIS), introduced in Bogolin et al. (2022).
159
160   The DSL strategy is proposed to avoid one-way optimum-match in contrastive methods. DSL in-
161   troduces an intrinsic prior of each pair in a batch to correct the similarity matrix and achieves the
      dual optimal match. In practice, we modify the original S(vj , ti ) by multiplying it with a prior ri,j .

180   3.2 TAG E XTRACTION
181
      We propose to estimate tags from either the video vi , using a VLM, or the text in the caption ti ,
182
      using an LLM. These tags are word-level representations of common objects, actions, or general
183
      ideas present in the caption or the video. They can add additional useful information to retrieve the
184   correct video given a text query as shown in Fig. 2. For instance, given the caption: a commercial
185   for the Mazda 3 the car sliding around a corner, the general tags estimated from this caption are:
186   product showcase, style, brand differentiation, advertising technique, automotive marketing which
187   reflect the commercial. These words are abstract terms that go beyond the exact caption but can help
188   the retrieval model to better understand the specific characteristics of this caption.
189
      In contrast, leveraging the video modality to create tags enables us to both capture a broader array
190   of visual elements that characterise the video content and also have a representation of the video
191   in words, facilitating matching the video content to the captions within the text modality. E.g.,
192   given the video associated with the previous caption, extracted visual tags include road, vehicle, car,
193   transportation, engine, reflecting important objects in the video, and racing, driving reflecting the
194   action in the video. These tags directly correspond to pertinent visual components of the video.
195
      For extracting visual and textual tags, we use a custom prompt (see Appx. C for more details) to
196   query the most relevant general tags for the input video vi and caption ti . We extract tags individually
197   from both modalities so they can be used for both training and inference. As the tags are extracted
198   from a single modality, we refer to these as modality-specific tags. We detail how these tags are
199   used within MAC-VR next.

529   same importance. It is possible that some generated words can be more common than others and
530   therefore less discriminative. We leave this for future work. See Appx. I for more details on the
531   reported limitations.
532   6 C ONCLUSION
533
      In this work, we introduce the notion of visual and textual tags extracted by foundation models from
534   a video and its caption respectively and use them to boost the video retrieval performance. We pro-
535   pose MAC-VR (Modality Auxiliary Concepts for Video Retrieval), where we incorporate modality-
536   specific auxiliary tags, projected into disentangled auxiliary concepts. We use a new Alignment Loss
537   to better align each modality with its auxiliary concepts. We ablate our method to further show the
538   benefit of using auxiliary modality-specific tags in video retrieval. Our results indicate, both qualita-
539   tively and by comparing to other approaches, that modality-specific tags help to decrease ambiguity
      in video retrieval on three video datasets.

1126   the extracted law, testimony, marriage, couple court, and visual tags such as musical performance,
