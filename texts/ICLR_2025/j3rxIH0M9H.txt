009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Repurposing pre-trained diffusion models has been proven to be effective for
012            novel view synthesis (NVS). However, these methods are mostly limited to a
013            single object; directly applying such methods to compositional multi-object scenar-
014            ios yields inferior results, especially incorrect object placement and inconsistent
015            shape and appearance under novel views. How to enhance and systematically
016            evaluate the cross-view consistency of such models remains under-explored. To
017            address this issue, we propose MOVIS to enhance the structural awareness of the
018
               view-conditioned diffusion model for multi-object NVS in terms of model inputs,
               auxiliary tasks, and training strategy. First, we inject structure-aware features,
019
               including depth and object mask, into the denoising U-Net to enhance the model’s
020
               comprehension of object instances and their spatial relationships. Second, we
021            introduce an auxiliary task requiring the model to simultaneously predict novel
022            view object masks, further improving the model’s capability in differentiating
023            and placing objects. Finally, we conduct an in-depth analysis of the diffusion
024            sampling process and carefully devise a structure-guided timestep sampling sched-
025            uler during training, which balances the learning of global object placement and
026            fine-grained detail recovery. To systematically evaluate the plausibility of synthe-
027            sized images, we propose to assess cross-view consistency and novel view object
028            placement alongside existing image-level NVS metrics. Extensive experiments on
029
               challenging synthetic and realistic datasets demonstrate that our method exhibits
               strong generalization capabilities and produces consistent novel view synthesis,
030
               highlighting its potential to guide future 3D-aware multi-object NVS tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Novel view synthesis (NVS) from a single image is imperative for various applications, including
036   AR/VR, interior designs, robotics, etc. This is highly challenging as it requires understanding complex
037   spatial structures from a single 2D perspective observation while being able to extrapolate consistent
038   and plausible content for unobserved areas. The substantial demands for comprehensive knowledge
039   of the 3D world render it a difficult task, even for humans with rich priors of the 3D environments.
040
      Recently, significant progress has been made in the realm of single-object image-to-3D genera-
041
      tion (Tang et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023a; Shi et al., 2023b; Long
042   et al., 2024) empowered by the advances in 2D diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al.,
043   2020). Among them, one prominent line of research (Liu et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023a; Qian et al.,
044   2023; Tang et al., 2023a; Weng et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023d; Huang et al., 2023;
045   Chen et al., 2023) has achieved compelling results by building on insights from Zero-1-to-3 (Liu
046   et al., 2023c): repurposing a pre-trained diffusion model as a novel view synthesizer by fine-tuning
047   on large 3D object datasets can provide promising 3D-aware prior for image-to-3D tasks.
048   However, these methods are mostly restricted to the single-object level. It remains unclear if this
049   paradigm can be effectively extended to the multi-object level to facilitate more complex tasks
050   like reconstructing an indoor scene. In Fig. 1, we visualize cross-view matching results of directly
051   applying the aforementioned novel view synthesizers (Liu et al., 2023c) in multi-object scenarios,
052   which showcases weak consistency with input views. Specifically, we believe that the lack of
053   structural awareness is the primary reason for the disappearance, distortion, incorrect position, and
      orientation of objects under novel views. While several works (Sargent et al., 2023; Tung et al., 2024)

083   objects, such as spatial placement, per-instance geometry and appearance, and occlusion relationship,
084   makes incorporating such awareness non-trivial.
085   Inspired by the discussion above, our paper seeks to address the question: How to enhance the
086   structural awareness of current diffusion-based novel view synthesizers? We begin by identifying the
087   key challenges in extending single-object methods for multi-object NVS tasks. A multi-object image
088   possesses more complicated structural information than a single-object one. The model must first
089   grasp the hierarchical structure within, which includes both high-level global object placement, e.g.,
090   position and orientation, and low-level ones like per-object geometry and appearance. High-level
091   structural information significantly reduces the ambiguity in object composition while low-level
092   details are essential for accurately capturing the characteristics of each object instance. Subsequently,
093
      the model needs to retain this hierarchical information captured from the input view while synthesizing
      novel-view images to ensure cross-view structural consistency. These capabilities are less critical in
094
      single-object level NVS tasks due to the reduced ambiguity in one-to-one mapping but are crucial for
095
      effective multi-object NVS models.
096
097   Building on these insights, our technical designs are threefold. We first propose injecting structure-
098   aware features, i.e., depth and object mask, from the input view as additional inputs to provide
099
      information on both high-level global placement and fine-grained local details. Secondly, we utilize
      the prediction of novel view object masks as an auxiliary task during training for the model to
100
      differentiate object instances, laying a solid foundation for fine-grained geometry and appearance
101
      recovery. Finally, through an in-depth analysis of the model’s inference process, we highlight the
102   importance of revising the noise timestep sampling schedule, which influences the learning focus
103   in the training process. To be specific, larger timesteps emphasize global placement learning, while
104   smaller timesteps focus on local fine-grained object geometry and appearance recovery. To endow
105   the view-conditioned diffusion model with both capabilities, we propose a structure-guided timestep
106   sampling scheduler that prioritizes larger timesteps in the initial stage, gradually decreasing over time
107   to balance these two conflicting inductive biases. This design is fundamental to our proposed model’s
      effectiveness in addressing the complexity of multi-object level NVS tasks.

      To systematically assess the plausibility of synthesized novel view images, we additionally evaluate
109   novel-view object mask and cross-view structural consistency apart from the existing NVS metrics.
110   Specifically, we employ image-matching techniques (Wang et al., 2024; Leroy et al., 2024) to compare
111   the input-view image with both the ground-truth and synthesized novel-view images. Cross-view
112   structural consistency evaluates how closely the matching results align, providing a measure of the
113   accuracy in recovering object placement, shape, and appearance. On the other hand, the object
114   mask, as measured by Intersection over Union (IoU), assesses the precision of object placement.
115   Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method excels at multi-object level NVS in indoor
116   scenes, achieving consistent object placement, shape, and appearance. Notably, it exhibits strong
117
      generalization capabilities for generating novel views on unseen datasets, including both synthetic
      ones 3D-FRONT (Fu et al., 2021a), Room-Texture (Luo et al., 2024) and Objaverse (Deitke et al.,
118
      2023b), as well as the real-world SUNRGB-D (Song et al., 2015).
119
120   In summary, our paper focuses on enhancing structural awareness of view-conditioned diffusion
121   models, improving the quality and consistency of synthesized images. Our main contributions are:
122   1. We introduce structure-aware features as model inputs and incorporate novel view mask prediction
123      as an auxiliary task during training. This enhances the model’s understanding of hierarchical
124      structures in multi-object scenarios, leading to improved NVS performance.
125   2. We present a novel noise timestep sampling scheduler designed to balance the learning of global
126      object placement and fine-grained detail recovery, which is critical for addressing the increased
127      complexity in multi-object scenarios.
128   3. We introduce additional metrics to systematically evaluate the novel view structural consistency.
129
         Through extensive experiments, our model demonstrates superiority in consistent object placement,
         geometry, and appearance recovery, showcasing strong generalization capability to unseen datasets.

134   2.1   S INGLE OBJECT NVS WITH GENERATIVE MODELS
135
136   Synthesizing novel view images for single objects given a single-view image is an extremely ill-posed
137   problem that requires strong priors. With great advances achieved in diffusion models (Ho et al.,
138   2020; Rombach et al., 2022), research efforts (Xu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023b; Melas-Kyriazi
139
      et al., 2023) seek to distill priors (Jain et al., 2022; Poole et al., 2022) learned from Text-to-Image
      (T2I) diffusion models via image captioning like Li et al. (2023). However, this presents a huge gap
140
      between the image and semantics due to the ambiguity of the text, hindering the 3D consistency of
141
      these methods. On the other hand, view-conditioned diffusion models like Zero-1-to-3 (Liu et al.,
142   2023c) explore an Image-to-Image (I2I) generation paradigm that “teaches” the diffusion model to
143   control viewpoints to synthesize plausible images under novel views, providing a more consistent
144   3D-aware prior. Subsequent work focuses on accelerating the generation speed (Liu et al., 2023b;
145   Tang et al., 2023a), enhancing the view consistency (Chen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b; Weng et al.,
146   2023; Liu et al., 2023d; Huang et al., 2023), or accelerating the training process (Jiang et al., 2023).
147   However, all these methods deal with single and complete object novel view synthesis tasks since
148   they usually fine-tune their model on Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023b;a), an extensive single-object
149   level dataset, contrary to real images which normally consist of multiple or incomplete objects. The
150
      lack of specific model designs for compositional scenes also leads to significant inconsistencies when
      directly applying them to the multi-object scenarios, as can be seen from Fig. 1.

      2.3     S CENE - LEVEL NVS WITH SPARSE VIEW INPUT
200
201   Early efforts (Jain et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021) attempted to directly perform scene-
202   level NVS tasks by extracting image features from input-view images and inferring the underlying
203   3D representation (Mildenhall et al., 2020). With the development of Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al.,
204   2023), recent works (Charatan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c) attempt to switch the underlying
205   representation to Gaussian Splatting for efficiency. However, they mainly deal with synthesizing
206   views near input ones with limited generative capabilities to the unseen region. Inspired by the
207
      great success of diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) and the object-level 3D-aware novel-view
      synthesizer (Liu et al., 2023c; Wang & Shi, 2023), several recent works have also attempted to
208
      perform scene-level NVS tasks by directly conditioning the generative models on a single-view scene
209
      image or a monocular dynamic scene video (Van Hoorick et al., 2024). ZeroNVS (Sargent et al.,
210   2023) proposes to train a view-conditioned diffusion model on a mixture of real-world datasets,
211   MegaScenes (Tung et al., 2024) further scales up the training dataset with Internet-level data pairs for
212   stronger generalization capabilities. However, all these works mainly deal with small view-change and
213   simple scenarios in terms of object number, with few adaptations to tackle the multi-object complexity.
214   In this work, we systematically examine the cross-view consistency of NVS by proposing new metrics,
215   and explore the critical designs required to enhance the structural consistency of the view-conditioned
      diffusion models in the multi-object scenarios.

      3     M ETHOD
217
218   In this section, we address the challenge of enhancing the structural awareness of diffusion-based
219   novel view synthesizers for better cross-view consistency in multi-object scenarios. We begin with
220   a brief introduction to diffusion models and view-conditioned diffusion models (Sec. 3.1). Next,
221   we detail the key architectural designs of MOVIS, including how we incorporate structural-aware
222   features as input to improve the model’s understanding of hierarchical structure information (Sec. 3.2)
223   and how we introduce novel view mask prediction as an auxiliary task, instructing the model to
224   differentiate the object instances with correct object placement (Sec. 3.2). Finally, we provide an
225   in-depth analysis of the inference process and adopt a structure-guided timestep sampling scheduler
226
      (Sec. 3.3) to balance the learning of global object placement and local fine-grained object geometry
      and appearance recovery. We provide an overview of our view-conditioned diffusion model in Fig. 2.
227

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
229
230   Diffusion Models Diffusion models learn to generate images by gradually adding noise to an image
231   (forward process) and recovering the original image from a noisy image (backward process) (Ho
232   et al., 2020). Specifically, in the forward process, Gaussian noise is progressively introduced to the
233   image via q(xt |xt−1 ). Due to the additivity of Gaussian distributions, this iterative process can be
234   written as q(xt |x0 ) = N (αt x0 , σt2 I), where αt and σt are designed to converge to N (0, I) at the
235   end of the forward process (Kingma et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020b). In the backward process, the
236   model learns to progressively denoise from a noisy image pθ (xt−1 |xt ). This learning is formulated
237   as learning the noise estimator ϵθ (xt , t) following Ho et al. (2020):
238                                         E[||ϵθ (αt x0 + σt ϵ, t) − ϵ||22 ],                           (1)
239   where ϵ is drawn from N (0, I) and the timestep t is uniformly sampled from U(1, 1000). In the
240   inference stage, one can either apply a stochastic (Ho et al., 2020) or a deterministic (Song et al.,
241   2020a) sampler to generate high-quality images via iterative refinement.
242
243   View-conditioned Diffusion Models Diffusion models have been recently repurposed as a novel
244
      view synthesizer. By training on posed image pairs {(x0 , x̂0 )} where x̂0 ∈ RH×W ×3 denotes
      the input view image and x0 ∈ RH×W ×3 denotes the target view, view-conditioned diffusion
245
      models (Watson et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c) use the input image x̂0 and camera pose transformation
246
      as conditions to predict the target view image x0 from a different viewpoint. Specifically, the learning
247   objective of view-conditioned diffusion models is:
248
                                     E[||ϵθ (αt x0 + σt ϵ, t, C(x̂0 , R, T )) − ϵ||22 ],                    (2)
249
      where R, T represent the relative camera pose transformation between the target view x0 and the
250
      input view x̂0 . C(x̂0 , R, T ) is the view-conditioned feature, combining the relative camera pose
251
      transformation with encoded image features to form a new ‘pose-aware’ feature map, taking the place
252   of the origin CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) feature embedding. Moreover, input view image x0 will
253   be concatenated with the noisy image as the input of the denoising U-Net. As discussed in Sec. 2.1,
254   single-image-based NVS is extremely challenging, current methods inherit natural image priors from
255   large-scale pre-training (Rombach et al., 2022) and fine-tune diffusion models on large-scale 3D
256   object datasets like Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023b) to learn the transformation between objects
257   in the input and novel views given the relative camera pose. Despite their ability to generalize to
258   in-the-wild objects, these view-conditioned diffusion models struggle with multi-object scenarios
259   like multi-furniture indoor scenes due to the scarcity of similar data and increased complexity arising
260
      from intricate object compositions. Our method builds on the insight of repurposing the diffusion
      model as a novel view synthesizer while emphasizing the inherent properties of multi-object scenarios
261
      in both model design and training strategy to facilitate multi-object NVS.

      3.2   MOVIS
264
265   Our proposed method extends view-conditioned diffusion models to multi-object level, as illustrated
266   in Fig. 2. The model leverages a pre-trained Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and concatenates
267   the 2D structural information from the input view with a noisy target image as input. Additionally, it
268   integrates a pre-trained image encoder (Oquab et al., 2023) to capture semantic information, which is
269   injected into the network through cross-attention alongside the relative camera pose. Moreover, it
      predicts novel view mask simultaneously as an auxiliary task to aid global object placement learning.

      Structure-Aware Feature Amalgamation To synthesize plausible images under novel viewpoints,
271   the model must first grasp the compositional structural information from the input view, laying a
272   solid foundation for generation. To address the innate complexity in multi-object scenarios due
273   to the intricate object relationship, we propose to leverage structure-aware features to facilitate
274   model’s comprehension. Specifically, we use depth maps and object masks as proxies for image-level
275   structural information. Object masks provide a rough concept of object placement and shape as
276   well as distinguishing distinct object instances, while depth maps encode the rough relative position
277   and shape of the visible objects. Together with input-view images, these conditions provide both
278   global structural information like object placement and local fine-grained details like object shape.
279

      Auxiliary Novel View Mask Prediction Task Input-view depth maps and mask images are intended
291

295   approach draws inspiration from classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), where a classifier
296   pϕ (y|xt , t) guides the denoising process of image xt to meet the criterion y via incorporating the

466

467
         accurately capture the compositional structure under novel views. For example, in the first row, the
468
         red bed is incorrectly oriented in Zero-1-to-3 and is either missing or distorted in other baselines.
469   2. From the visualized cross-view matching results and the metrics in Tab. 1, it is evident that our
470      method significantly outperforms the baseline approaches in Cross-view Consistency. It achieves
471      a much higher IoU and Hit Rate while exhibiting a considerably lower matching distance. The
472      visualized results are consistent with the metrics, further validating our method’s accuracy in
473      capturing cross-view structural consistency, which cannot be reflected by existing NVS metrics.
474   3. Our model exhibits strong generalization capabilities on unseen datasets, e.g., Room-Texture and
475      Objaverse. We demonstrate more qualitative results, including on 3D-FRONT and SUNRGB-D,
476      in Appx. B.4. We showcase potential applications, including object removal and reconstruction in

514   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
515
516   To verify the efficacy of each component, we perform an ablation study on our key technical designs,
517   including the depth input (w/o depth), mask prediction auxiliary task (w/o mask), and the scheduler
518   (w/o sch. learns with a uniform sampler t ∼ U(1, 1000) ). Results in Tab. 2 show that the auxiliary
519
      mask prediction task and the timestep sampler are the most critical components, significantly affecting
      all the metrics and the realistic object recovery as demonstrated by the misoriented brown cabinet
520
      in the second example from Fig. 5. Without the scheduler, the model produces less accurate object
521
      positions, evident both qualitatively and quantitatively. Furthermore, removing depth or mask predic-
522   tions weakens the model’s understanding of spatial relationships and object existence, exemplified by
523   the completely missing white cabinet in the first example. This also shows incorporating structure-
524   aware features as inputs, though seemingly intuitive, offers the most straightforward approach to
525   enhancing the model’s structural awareness, particularly given recent advancements in monocular
526   predictors (Kirillov et al., 2023; Ke et al., 2024). We present a more comprehensive discussion on the
527   scheduler strategy in Appx. A.4 and ablations on more datasets are in Appx. B.4.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We extend diffusion-based novel view synthesizers to handle multi-object compositions in indoor
533
      scenes. Our proposed model generalize well across diverse datasets with more accurate object
534   placement, shape, and appearance, showing a stronger cross-view consistency with input view. The
535   core of our approach lies in integrating structure-aware features as additional inputs, an auxiliary mask
536   prediction task, and a structure-guided timestep sampling scheduler. These components enhance the
537   model’s awareness of compositional structure while balancing the learning of global object placement
538   and fine-grained local shape and appearance. Given the prevalence of multi-object compositions
539   in real-world scenes, we believe that our model designs and comprehensive evaluations can offer
      valuable insights for advancing scene-level NVS models in more complex environments.

      A     M ODEL DETAILS
811
812
      A.1    DINO PATCH FEATURE AND CAMERA VIEW EMBEDDING
813
814   The original image encoder of Stable Diffusion is CLIP, which excels at aligning images with
815   text. Other image encoders like DINO-v2 (Oquab et al., 2023) or ConvNeXtv2 (Woo et al., 2023)
816   may provide denser image features that may benefit generation tasks as mentioned by previous
817   works (Jiang et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024). Therefore, we opt to use the DINO feature instead of
818   the original CLIP feature in our network following Jiang et al. (2023). To inject the DINO patch
819
      feature into our network, we encode the input view image using DINO-v2 (Oquab et al., 2023) “norm
      patchtokens”, whose shape dimension is (b, 16, 16, 1024). We will simply flatten it into (b, 256, 1024)
820
      to apply cross-attention, and b means batch size here.
821
822   As for the camera view embedding, we choose to embed it using a 6 degrees of freedom (6DoF)
823   representation. To be specific, let Ei be the extrinsic matrix under the input view and Ej be the
824   extrinsic matrix under the output view, we represent relative camera pose change as Ei−1 Ej . We will
825   also flatten it into 16 dimensions to concatenate it to the image feature. Afterwards, we will replicate
826   the 16-dimension embedding 256 times to concatenate the embedding to every channel of the DINO
827
      feature map. A projection layer will later be employed to project the feature map into (b, 256, 768)
      to match the dimension of the CLIP encoder, which was originally used by Stable Diffusion so
828
      that we can fine-tune the pre-trained checkpoint. It is worth noting that we also tried other novel
829
      view synthesizer’s camera embedding like Zero-1-to-3 (Liu et al., 2023c) using a 3DoF spherical
830   coordinates in early experiments, but we found that it does not make much of a difference.
831
832
      A.2    D EPTH AND MASK CONDITION
833
834   In this section, we will explain how input view depth and mask are incorporated as additional
835   conditioning inputs. For depth maps, regions with infinite depth values are assigned a value equal
836   to twice the maximum finite depth value in the rest of the image. After this adjustment, we apply
837   a normalization technique to scale the depth values to the range of [−1, 1], enabling the use of the
838   same VAE architecture as for images.
839   For mask images, we assign unique values to different object instances in the input view. For
840   instance, if there are four objects in the multi-object composite, they will be labeled as 1, 2, 3, and 4,
841   respectively, while the background will be assigned a value of 0. The same normalization technique
842   used for depth maps is applied to these mask images. These mask images, like all other inputs, are
843   processed by the VAE, with all images set to a resolution of 256 × 256.
844
845   A.3    S UPERVISION FOR AUXILIARY MASK PREDICTION TASK
846
847
      To implement the auxiliary mask prediction task, we encode the output view mask images into the
      same latent space as the input view mask images. Object instances viewed from different angles
848
      will be assigned the same value, which is ensured during the curation of our compositional dataset.
849
      Supervision is directly applied to the latent mask features extracted from the final layer of the
850   denoising U-Net. Only the input view mask images are required during inference, simplifying the
851   process while preserving consistency across views.

922   B.2   DATASETS
923
924   C3DF We use the furniture models from the 3D-FUTURE dataset (Fu et al., 2021b) to create
925
      our synthetic multi-object compositional data. The 3D-FUTURE dataset contains 9,992 detailed
      3D furniture models with high-resolution textures and labels. Following previous work Chen et al.
926
      (2024a), we categorize the furniture into seven groups: bed, bookshelf, cabinet, chair, nightstand,
927
      sofa, and table. To ensure unbiased evaluation, we further split the furniture into distinct training and
928   test sets, ensuring that none of the test set items are seen during training.
929
930   After filtering the furniture, we first determine the number of pieces to include in each composite,
931
      which is randomly selected to be between 3 and 6. Next, we establish a probability distribution based
      on the different types of furniture items and sample each piece according to this distribution. To
932
      prevent collisions and penetration between furniture items, we employ a heuristic strategy. Specifi-
933
      cally, for each furniture item to be added, we apply a random scale adjustment within the range of
934   [0.95, 1.05], as the inherent scale of the furniture models accurately reflects real-world sizes. We also
935   rotate each model by a random angle to introduce additional variability. Once these adjustments are
936   complete, we begin placing the furniture items in the scene. The first item is positioned at the center
937   of the scene at coordinates (0, 0, 0). Subsequent objects are added one by one, initially placed at the
938   same central location. Since this results in inevitable collisions, we randomly sample a direction
939   and gradually move the newly added item along this vector until there is no intersection between
940   the bounding boxes of the objects. By following these steps, we generate a substantial number of
941   multiple furniture items composites, ultimately creating a training set of 100,000 composites and a
942
      test set of 5,000 to evaluate the capabilities of our network.
943   After placing all the furniture items, we render multi-view images to facilitate training, using
944   Blender (Community, 2018) as our renderer due to its high-quality output. We first normalize each
945   composite along its longest axis. To simulate real-world camera poses and capture meaningful
946   multi-object compositions, we employ the following method for sampling camera views.
947   Cameras are randomly sampled using spherical coordinates, with a radius range of [1.3, 1.7] and
948   an elevation angle range of [2◦ , 40◦ ]. There are no constraints on the azimuth angle, allowing the
949   camera to rotate freely around multiple objects. The chosen ranges for the radius and elevation angles
950   are empirical. In addition to determining the camera positions, we establish a "look-at" point to
951   compute the camera pose. This point is randomly selected on a spherical shell with a radius range of
952   [0.01, 0.2].
953   To enhance the model’s compositional structural awareness, we also render depth maps and instance
954   masks (both occluded and unoccluded) from 12 different viewpoints. The unoccluded instance mask
955   ensures that if one object is blocked by another, the complete amodal mask of the occluded object
956   is still provided, regardless of any obstructions. Although these unoccluded instance masks are not
957   currently necessary for our network, we render them for potential future use.
958
959   Objaverse To evaluate our network’s generalization capability, we create a small dataset comprising
960   300 composites sourced from Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023b). Specifically, we utilize the provided
961   LVIS annotations to select categories that are commonly found in indoor environments, such as beds,
962   chairs, sofas, dressers, tables, and others. Since the meshes from Objaverse vary in scale, we rescale
963   each object based on reference object scales from the 3D-FUTURE dataset (Fu et al., 2021b). The
964   composition and rendering processes follow the same strategy employed in C3DF.

981
       Intersection over Union (IoU) Since all baseline methods do not possess the concept of every
       object instance, we compute a foreground-background IoU for comparison. This metric can provide
982
       a rough concept of the overall placement alignment with ground truth images. We extract the
983
       foreground object mask by converting the generated image to grayscale (IL ). Given that the generated
984    image has a white background, we compute the foreground mask M as M = IL < βth , where βth is
985    a threshold that is fixed as 250.

987
988    Cross-view Matching As outlined in the main paper, we introduce two metrics to systematically
989    evaluate cross-view consistency with the input view: Hit Rate and Nearest Matching Distance.
990
       Since direct assessment of cross-view consistency is not feasible by merely evaluating the success
       matches between each method’s predicted novel view images and the input view image, we opt to
991
       how far the predicted matches deviate from the ground-truth matches.
992
993    We first compute ground-truth matching points and every model’s matching points using
994    MASt3R (Leroy et al., 2024) upon the input view image and the output view image (ground truth

1269   Limitations We identify two limitations of our work. Firstly, though we achieve stronger cross-view
1270   consistency with the input view image, our model does not guarantee the multi-view consistency
1271   between our synthesized images. It is plausible to synthesize any results in areas with ambiguity,
1272   leading to potential multi-view inconsistency in our model. We believe incorporating multi-view
1273   awareness techniques Shi et al. (2023b); Wang & Shi (2023); Shi et al. (2023a); Kong et al. (2024); Liu
1274   et al. (2023d); Yang et al. (2024a) can mitigate this problem. Secondly, we do not model background
1275   texture in our framework due to difficulty of realistically mimicking real-world background texture,
1276
       making it less convenient to directly apply our method to in-the-wild images. We believe training on
       more realistic data with background in the future can make our model more convenient to use.
