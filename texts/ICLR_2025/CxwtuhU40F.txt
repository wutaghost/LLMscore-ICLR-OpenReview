011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Nonlinear dimensionality reduction often lacks interpretability due to the ab-
014            sence of source features in low-dimensional embedding space. We propose Fea-
015
               tureMAP, an interpretable method that preserves source features by tangent space
               embedding. The core of FeatureMAP is to use local principal component anal-
016
               ysis (PCA) to approximate tangent spaces. By leveraging these tangent spaces,
017
               FeatureMAP computes gradients to locally reveal feature directions and impor-
018            tance. Additionally, FeatureMAP embeds the tangent spaces into low-dimensional
019            space while preserving alignment between them, providing local gauges for pro-
020            jecting the high-dimensional data points. Unlike UMAP, FeatureMAP employs
021            anisotropic projection to preserve both the manifold structure and the original data
022            density. We apply FeatureMAP to interpreting digit classification, object detec-
023            tion and MNIST adversarial examples, where it effectively distinguishes digits and
024            objects using feature importance and provides explanations for misclassifications
025            in adversarial attacks. We also compare FeatureMAP with other state-of-the-art
026
               methods using both local and global metrics.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Nonlinear dimensionality reduction methods are widely used for visualising and preprocessing high-
031
      dimensional data in machine learning (Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Roweis & Saul, 2000; Zhang &
032   Wang, 2006; Donoho & Grimes, 2003; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Zhang & Zha, 2004; Van der Maaten
033   & Hinton, 2008; McInnes et al., 2018). These methods assume that the intrinsic dimensionality of
034   the underlying manifold is significantly lower than the ambient dimensionality of real-world data
035   (Levina & Bickel, 2004; Pope et al., 2021; Wright & Ma, 2022). Nonlinear dimensionality reduction
036   techniques approximate this manifold using a discrete graph, such as a k-nearest neighbor (kNN)
037   graph. These methods project data from high-dimensional to low-dimensional space to preserve the
038   topological structure of the original data.
039   While nonlinear dimensionality reduction is effective for visualizing high-dimensional data,
040   one major limitation is the lack of interpretability in the reduced-dimension results (McInnes
041   et al., 2018). Unlike linear methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), where
042   the dimensions in the embedding space correspond to the directions of the greatest vari-
043   ance of the original data, nonlinear methods do not provide such clear interpretations.
044    Specifically, nonlinear dimensionality reduction, such as t-SNE and UMAP, prioritises preserving
045    distances between data points, which often results in the loss of source feature information in the
046    embedding space. Consequently, it fails to illustrate feature contributions, or loadings, as effectively
047    as linear methods like PCA, making it challenging to explain the significance of individual features
048
       in the reduced-dimensional space.
049   In this paper, we aim to enhance the interpretability of nonlinear dimensionality reduction. Beyond
050   preserving the topological structure of data points in the embedding space, we focus on incorporating
051   source features to create a more interpretable approach. Feature information is encoded within the
052   column space of the data, and we use the tangent space to locally represent this column space
053   (Singer & Wu, 2012; Lim et al., 2021). The concept of employing tangent space arises from the
      observation of anisotropic density on a manifold, where some curves passing through a point are

086    We propose Feature-preserving manifold approximation and projection (FeatureMAP), to address
087
       these gaps by integrating feature preservation directly into the embedding process. Unlike exist-
       ing methods, it embeds tangent spaces alongside neighborhood graphs, enabling a more faithful
088
       representation of local geometry and feature relationships. This distinct approach ensures that the
089
       embedding not only maintains global and local accuracy but also supports interpretability by visu-
090    alizing feature contributions.
091
      We approximate the manifold’s topological structure using a kNN graph and compute the tangent
092   space by local PCA at the local nearest neighbours. Based on the tangent space, FeatureMAP
093   computes the gradient for each feature, enabling the computation of both feature importance and
094   the direction at each point. Feature importance at a data point serves as a saliency map, highlighting
095   the significant features after embedding, while feature direction illustrates how features change
096   locally in the low-dimensional space ( Fig. 1). Additionally, FeatureMAP embeds the tangent
097   spaces to construct local gauges for projecting data points in the low-dimensional space. The gauge
098   at each point is associated with a hyperellipsoid, with its radii determined by the singular values,
099   reflecting anisotropic density. Thus, FeatureMAP preserves this anisotropic density by maximising
100
      the correlation between the local anisotropic radii in the high- and low-dimensional spaces.
101   To summarize, we make the following contributions:
102
103
             • We propose FeatureMAP, an interpretable nonlinear dimensionality reduction method that
               preserves source features and local density.
104
105          • We evaluate FeatureMAP on digit and object data. FeatureMAP uses feature gradients to
106
               successfully explain the digit classification and object detection.
107          • We apply FeatureMAP to MNIST adversarial examples to explicitly show that the adver-
               sarial attack changes the feature importance, which fools the LeNet classifier.

      2       R ELATED W ORK
114
115
      Nonlinear dimensionality reduction is often considered superior for preserving distance and neigh-
116   borhood information (Borg & Groenen, 2005; Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008; McInnes et al.,
117   2018). Methods like t-SNE, UMAP, and LargeVis rely on kNN graphs to approximate the
118   manifold (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008; McInnes et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2016). Recent
119   improvements, such as TriMAP(Amid & Warmuth, 2019), PaCMAP (Wang et al., 2021), and
120   DensMAP (Narayan et al., 2021), aim to better preserve global and local properties. Moreover,
121   h-NNE (Sarfraz et al., 2022) applied a hierarchical nearest neighbour graph to preserving multi-
122   level grouping properties of original data. SpaceMAP (Zu & Tao, 2022) used space expansion to
123   match the high and low dimensional space, while CO-SNE (Guo et al., 2022) extended t-SNE from
124
      Euclidean space to hyperbolic space. However, these methods lack interpretability as they do not
      incorporate source features into the embeddings.
125
126   The interpretability of nonlinear dimensionality reduction has been largely overlooked in the design
127   and evaluation of embedding methods (Liu et al., 2016; Vellido et al., 2012; Frénay & Dumas,
128   2016; Dumas et al., 2018). While linear methods like PCA naturally provide interpretability by
129   revealing source features (Gabriel, 1971), few approaches have addressed this issue in nonlin-
130
      ear methods. Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2016) examined the trade-off between interpretability and
      embedding structure, and Bibai et al. (Bibal & Frénay, 2019; Bibal et al., 2018; Marion et al.,
131
      2019; Sips et al., 2009) proposed explaining low-dimensional axes and scatter plot positions.
132
      Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2019) introduced subspace projection for kernel dimensionality reduction,
133   and Bibal et al. (Bibal et al., 2020) adapted local interpretable model-agnostic explanations
134   (LIME) to explain t-SNE. Bardos et al. (Bardos et al., 2022) introduced a model-agnostic expla-
135   nation technique for dimensionality reduction. More recently, Corbugy et al. (2024) proposed
136   gradient-based explanations for nonlinear methods, relying on loss function’s derivatives. In
137   contrast, our method, FeatureMAP, directly computes gradients over the manifold using local PCA,
138   enhancing the exploration of topological structures and enabling a more interpretable embedding.
139    Additionally, the debate on UMAP and t-SNE revolves around interpretability, with critiques
140    like Chari & Pachter (2023) arguing their embeddings can be arbitrary, while others Lause et al.
141    (2024) demonstrate their potential for biologically meaningful insights despite not preserving high-
142    dimensional distances. FeatureMAP builds on manifold learning by not only preserving the topolog-
143
       ical structure but also retaining source features in the tangent space, enhancing the interpretability
       through local feature gradient analysis within the embedded tangent space.

146   3       T HE F EATURE MAP METHOD
147
148
      We present FeatureMAP to augment manifold approximation by preserving feature information
149
      through gradient calculation. We begin by computing the tangent spaces by local PCA, which en-
150   ables computing manifold gradients. Next, we embed the tangent spaces while preserving their
151   alignment, and then project the data points along the embedded gauges into a low-dimensional
152   space. In the following sections, we elaborate on each step and validate the proposed methods.
153   Fig. 2 illustrates the framework of our method.

266

267

352   The tangent space embedding V e = {V1e , ..., Vme } provides local gauges for visualizing feature
353   gradients (Fig. 1e, 2d). These local gauges V e represent the principal directions along which the
354   data points are locally distributed in the low-dimensional embedding space. The directions of the

358   Note that both local linear embedding (LLE) (Roweis & Saul, 2000) and local tangent space align-
359   ment (LTSA) (Zhang & Zha, 2004) also depict the local manifold structure using tangent space.
360   LLE ensures that each embedding data point is represented as the same linear combination of its
361   neighbors in high-dimensional space, while LTSA implicitly aligns the tangent space to learn the
362   embedding. Our method differs from these approaches by explicitly embedding the tangent spaces

524   5   D ISCUSSION
525
      We propose FeatureMAP, an interpretable nonlinear dimensionality reduction method that em-
526
      ploys manifold gradients to explain the low-dimensional embedding space. Specifically, we em-
527
      bed tangent spaces to locally construct gauges that display feature gradients, thereby explaining the
528   reduced-dimensional results through feature directions and importance. FeatureMAP also maintains
529   anisotropic density in two-dimensional plots, which further enhances the interpretability of the vi-
530   sualization. Our method of preserving features through tangent space embedding provides a plug-in
531   module for manifold learning.
532
      Experiments on digit classification, object detection, and MNIST adversarial examples demonstrate
533
      that FeatureMAP produces interpretable dimensionality reduction results, which facilitate the ex-
534   planation of classification outcomes and feature detection. In future work, we plan to extend our
535   feature-preserving paradigm by applying tangent space embedding to other nonlinear dimension-
536   ality reduction methods to enhance interpretability. Additionally, we will apply FeatureMAP to
537   real-world image datasets such as CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, as well as to biological gene expres-
538   sion data, to strengthen interpretation in classification and feature detection tasks. Furthermore,
539   we intend to explore complex data by learning sparse, high-order features through sparse manifold
      transforms (Chen et al., 2018; 2022).

      A.1.5                          H YPERPARAMETER TUNING
865
866    In this section, we discuss how to set the hyperparameters including n neighbors, min dist and λ,
867    and their impact on FeatureMAP’s performance.
868    The n neighbors parameter determines how FeatureMAP balances local and global structures by
869    defining the neighborhood size for learning the manifold. Smaller values capture local details but
870
       may miss the overall structure, while larger values prioritize the broader structure at the expense of
       fine details. Following UMAP and t-SNE conventions, where n neighbors is typically 15 or 30, we
871
       set n neighbors = 15 for FeatureMAP.
872
       The min dist parameter controls point spacing in FeatureMAP embeddings. Lower values create
873    compact clusters, useful for clustering and fine details. Higher values preserve broader topological
874    structures by spreading points further apart.
875

       A.2.4    C OMPARING F EATURE MAP WITH OTHER STATE - OF - THE - ART
1243
1244   We employ state-of-the-art nonlinear dimensionality reduction methods on benchmark datasets to
1245   generate the visualizations shown in Fig. 13. FeatureMAP demonstrates comparable density preser-
1246   vation to densMAP.
1247   We also perform a quantitative comparison of FeatureMAP with other methods in terms of local
1248   and global accuracy preservation, as shown in Tab. 1. Overall, FeatureMAP illustrates performance
1249   comparable to other methods. On the MNIST and Fashion MNIST datasets, FeatureMAP outper-
1250   forms the others in local metrics such as continuity and kNN accuracy, notably surpassing densMAP,
1251   spaceMAP, and PHATE. For global metrics, FeatureMAP consistently ranks either first or second
1252   across measures such as Shepard goodness, normalized stress, and centroid triplet accuracy.
1253   Furthermore, we assess the efficiency of FeatureMAP in comparison with other methods Fig. 14.
1254
       The running time of FeatureMAP is comparable to that of densMAP and TriMAP, as all three re-
       quire additional computations beyond kNN graph embedding. Specifically, densMAP involves local
1255
       density calculations, while TriMAP calculates distances within triplets. While FeatureMAP requires
1256
       more time than UMAP (e.g., 30s for UMAP vs. 150s for FeatureMAP on 50, 000 samples), this is
1257   reasonable given its extended functionality. Unlike UMAP, which focuses solely on kNN graph
1258   embedding, FeatureMAP also embeds tangent spaces and facilitates feature visualization, justifying
1259   the additional computational cost. Moreover, PHATE performs the worst when the data size ex-
1260   ceeds 50, 000. This is attributed to its reliance on the embedding technique of Multi-dimensional
1261   Scaling (MDS), which requires computing all pairwise distances, resulting in significantly increased
1262   computational overhead for larger datasets.
1263
1264   Optimization Strategies to Improve Efficiency
1265
       To enhance efficiency for large datasets, we consider applying the following strategies in our future
       work:
1266
1267           • Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search: Utilize efficient libraries like Annoy or FAISS to
1268             accelerate kNN graph construction without significant accuracy loss.
1269           • Dimensionality Reduction Before Embedding: Apply initial dimensionality reduction (e.g.,
1270             PCA) to reduce the feature space size, lowering the computational cost for tangent space
1271             embedding.
1272           • Parallel Computing: Implement parallelization for distance calculations and graph-based
1273             operations to leverage multi-core CPUs or GPUs.
1274           • Batch Processing: Divide the dataset into batches to reduce memory usage during embed-
1275             ding, especially when dealing with extremely large datasets.
1276
               • Adaptive Neighborhood Size: Dynamically adjust the n neighbors parameter based on
1277             the dataset size and structure to balance runtime and embedding quality.

1405   A.2.5    F EATURE MAP ENABLES VISUALIZING FEATURE DIRECTIONS AND IMPORTANCE BY
1406            FEATURE GRADIENTS
1407   In this section, we demonstrate FeatureMAP’s ability to preserve and visualize important features
1408   of the source space, which methods like UMAP and t-SNE fail to achieve in Fig. 15.
1409   Unlike non-linear methods, the linear PCA can illustrate feature contributions through a biplot,
1410   where feature contributions explain how source features influence the data distribution. In Fig. 15a,
1411
       we visualize feature contributions in a PCA plot. Specifically, the selected feature, pixel127, is
       highlighted with its feature contribution and feature count. The feature contribution and feature
1412
       count are consistently correlated, as the arrow of the feature contribution points toward the region
1413
       with a higher feature count.
1414   In contrast, the nonlinear methods UMAP and t-SNE are devised such that no source features are
1415   retained in the embedding. Their embedding, especially the two-dimensional visualization, are co-
1416   ordinates to maintains the relative positions in high-dimensional space. Since these coordinates are
1417   the only available information in the embedding, we mimic PCA’s biplot to associate source features
1418   with UMAP and t-SNE’s embedding by computing the correlation of features with the embedding
1419   coordinates. For each feature f in high-dimensional space, its correlations with the embedding coor-
                                        <f,y1 >                  <f,y2 >
1420   dinates y1 and y2 are corr1 = ||f  ||||y1 || and corr2 = ||f ||||y2 || , respectively. These two correlations
1421   form an arrow for the feature in the embedding, illustrating how the feature interprets the embedding
1422   (Fig. 15b, c).
1423   Our FeatureMAP enhances nonlinear dimensionality reduction by introducing feature-preserving
1424   functionality. Based on the manifold’s assumption that data points are locally distributed over
1425   Euclidean space, we compute PCA locally to derive feature gradients, representing local feature
1426
       contributions in terms of both direction and magnitude (Fig. 15d). To ensure consistency between
       local feature contributions and the data embedding, we first align the local gauges by projecting
1427
       the tangent space into the low-dimensional space (section 3.3). This alignment allows local feature
1428
       contributions to be effectively revealed through the local gauges.
1429   To further benchmark FeatureMAP’s feature-preserving functionality with PCA, UMAP and t-SNE,
1430   we show the 10 most important features identified by each method in terms of feature contribution,
1431   feature importance and feature count Figs. 16 to 19. FeatureMAP outperforms the others on visu-
1432   alizing features. Therefore, FeatureMAP combines the strengths of manifold learning and principal
1433   component analysis, augmenting UMAP with feature-preserving properties.
