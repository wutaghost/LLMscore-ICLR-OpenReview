009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider coverless steganography where a Large Language Model (LLM)
012            drives an arithmetic coding decoder to generate stego-texts. An efficient method
013            should embed secret message bits in as few language tokens as possible, while
014            still keeping the stego-text natural and fluent. We show that on the individual to-
015            ken level, this problem is mathematically equivalent to maximizing the entropy
016            of a replacement probability distribution of the next token generation, subject to a
017
               constraint on the KL divergence between the chosen probability distribution and
               the original distribution given by the LLM. A closed-form solution is provided for
018
               the optimization problem, which can be computed efficiently. Several important
019
               practical issues are also tackled: 1) An often-overlooked tokenization mismatch
020            issue is resolved with a simple prompt selection approach, 2) The combination of
021            the optimized distribution and the vocabulary truncation technique is considered,
022            and 3) The combination of the optimized distribution with other sequence-level
023            selection heuristics to further enhance the efficiency and reliability is studied.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027   In a steganography system, Alice, the sender, aims to convey a secret message to Bob, the receiver.
028   The carrier signal can take the form of text, image, audio, or video (Anderson & Petitcolas, 1998;
029   Cox et al., 2007; Provos & Honeyman, 2003). In this work, we focus on natural language text
030   messages as the type of carrier signals, and in this case, the resultant signal with the secret message
031   embedded is referred to as the stego-text. Alice transmits the stego-text to Bob via a public channel,
032   which is being monitored by an eavesdropper Eve. Eve wishes to determine whether there is a
033
      hidden message in the stego-text. Alice must ensure that the stego-text can be decoded correctly by
      Bob, and at the same time, guarantee with a high probability that Eve cannot detect whether a secret
034
      message exists or not. A good analogy is that Bob is a prisoner, Alice is the family member outside
035
      the prison who has a letter for Bob, and Eve is the prison guard who may confiscate the letter if
036   something unusual is detected about the letter (Simmons, 1984).
037
038   Conventionally, steganography relies on an existing cover signal (cover text), and achieves steganog-
039
      raphy by making subtle changes imperceptible to Eve on the cover text. For example, Alice can re-
      place certain words by their synonyms following pre-agreed patterns (Topkara et al., 2006; Chang &
040
      Clark, 2010; Safaka et al., 2016). Recently, as generative models, particularly large language mod-
041
      els, become more and more powerful, coverless steganography has shown significant performance
042   advantages. With this approach, the stego-text appears indistinguishable from natural languages,
043   and more importantly, a large amount of the secret information can be hidden in shorter stego-texts
044   than the traditional cover-text-based approaches (Fang et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2018; Ziegler et al.,
045   2019; Xiang et al., 2017; Dai & Cai, 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2020; Kaptchuk et al.,
046   2021; Ding et al., 2023; de Witt et al., 2024).
047
      The underlying driver for LLM-based steganography is usually the arithmetic coding (AC) algorithm
048   (Witten et al., 1987), which is an efficient data compression algorithm based on the idea that any
049   finite-length finite-alphabet data sequence (e.g., text) can be mapped to a small interval in the range
050   of [0, 1) based on the cumulative probability distribution function. Therefore, a binary representation
051   that accurately specifies this interval is a compressed representation of the sequence. The decom-
052   pression process can reverse this encoding process and recover the original sequence. In LLM-based
053   steganography, Alice utilizes the arithmetic coding decoder, together with the probability distribu-
      tion produced by LLM, to map the secret binary sequence to a stego-text. Bob can then recover the

054
      secret message by performing the arithmetic encoding, which is assumed to have access to the same
055   LLM. Intuitively, the arithmetic coding decoder essentially performs token-wise sampling following
056   the conditional probability distribution given by the LLM, using the secret message bits as the start-
057   ing randomness (from a uniform distribution to a non-uniform one). The stego-text would appear
058   natural and fluent if the LLM captures accurately the true distribution of natural languages.
059
      In many use scenarios, the security requirement in steganography can in fact be relaxed: 1) when
060
      Eve is computation-bounded (e.g., in a mobile device), 2) when Eve is delay-constrained (e.g., in
061   streaming processing or time-sensitive applications), or 3) under societal constraint (e.g., censor-
062   ship under constitutional right protection). In such cases, Eve can be modeled as a weak detec-
063   tor, and correspondingly the steganography security requirement can be relaxed to take advantage
064   of the situation. This consideration is in fact already implicit in several previous works invoking
065   “near-imperceptibility” (Dai & Cai, 2019; Shen et al., 2020) where the LLM next token probability
066   distributions were truncated or positions were skipped to either reduce computation or avoid exces-
067   sive distribution mismatch. Clearly, the “perceptibility” of a casual user is different from that of an
068   expert, and the authors there used the KL divergence to quantity the security loss, and studied its re-
069
      lation with the embedding capability when more truncation is taken or more positioned are skipped.
      Further generalizing this idea, we can replace the conditional probability distribution produced with
070
      another distribution, as long as the replacement mechanism is deterministic and causal, such that
071
      Alice and Bob remain synchronized, when the steganography security requirement is less stringent.
072
073   Taking this generalized view, our work is based on the following observation. There appears to
074   be a fundamental tradeoff between the amount of secret bits one can hide in the stego-text and the
075
      detectability of steganography; the former consideration is usually measured by the embedding ca-
      pability or embedding utilization in the literature (Dai & Cai, 2019; Shen et al., 2020; Kaptchuk
076
      et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023). Improving the utilization is particularly important for LLM-based
077
      steganography, since the generative process in LLMs can become almost deterministic, and it be-
078   comes difficult to embed secret bits unless a very long stego-text is used. In the context of LLM-
079   based steganography and the underlying arithmetic coding decoder, this requirement at the token
080   level essentially translates to maximizing the entropy of a replacement probability distribution, sub-
081   ject to a constraint on the distance between this new token generation distribution and the original
082   one produced by the LLM. We formalize this problem under the KL divergence constraint, and show
083   that it has a closed-form solution that can be computed efficiently. We refer to this steganography
084   approach via an optimized distribution simply as OD-Stega.
085   Our formulation formalizes the general tradeoff between steganography security and embedding
086   utilization, and our approaches can be specialized to previous methods. For example, in the perfectly
087   secure extreme, our approach essentially reduces to the approach given in Kaptchuk et al. (2021).
088   Moreover, given the fundamental nature of the mathematical formulation, our approach can also be
089   straightforwardly incorporated into other methods such as Ding et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2021).
090   In addition to the principled formulation outlined above, our work also tackles several practical
091   issues. Firstly, previous works using LLM for steganography assumed that the tokenizer is one-to-
092   one, such that Bob can decode correctly every time. However, modern tokenizers in LLM are often
093   not one-to-one, and therefore, these approaches often encounter decoding errors. We propose a
094   simple strategy to remedy this issue through LLM prompting selection. Secondly, we combine OD-
095   Stega with the existing technique of vocabulary truncation to reduce the computation complexity,
096   and analyze the overall KL divergence of this strategy. Lastly, we combine the proposed single-token
097   probability adjustment technique with other heuristics on the sequence level, and adaptively select
098
      optimization parameters based on the conditional entropy for each token. We conduct extensive
      experiments and demonstrate that the proposed approach can indeed embed significantly more secret
099
      message bits into the stego-text, and the generated stego-text remains perpetually indistinguishable.
100
101   The contribution of this work can be summarized as follows: 1) We provide a principled formulation
102   to optimize the generative conditional distribution in order to embed more bits in shorter stego-texts;
103   2) We design an efficient algorithm to compute the optimized distribution for each token; and 3) We
104
      tackle several practical issues and provide strategies to combine OD-Stega with other methods to
      improve the efficiency and reliability at the sequence level.
105
106   We defer a detailed discussion on related works to the appendix.

110   2.1         LLM- BASED S TEGANOGRAPHY
111
112   A Large Language Model (LLM) can provide an estimate for the conditional probability distribution
113   for the next token, given the sequence of tokens preceding it (Vaswani, 2017; Brown, 2020; Tou-
114   vron et al., 2023). To generate a natural language sequence, one can sample the tokens from these
115
      distributions in an autoregressive manner. We next provide some notation for the rest of the paper.
116   Following the work of Shen et al. (2020) for LLM-based steganography, we assume that the secret
117   message bit sequence S is already encrypted, before Alice starts to embed it in the stego-text. Before
118   encoding, Alice selects an initial prompt text Tp , independent of S, which typically determines
119   the nature or semantic of the resulting stego-text. To encode S, Alice uses an encoding function
120   f (Tp , S) to produce a sequence of tokens xi>0 = (x1 , x2 , x3 , . . .), which is then converted to the
121
      corresponding stego-text Ts via detokenizing. The prompt and the stego-text (Tp , Ts ) are sent on
      the public channel. Bob first converts Ts into the token form xi>0 , then uses a decoding function
122
      g(·) such that g(Tp , xi>0 ) = S.
123
124   In LLM-based steganography, both f and g rely on the same LLM. At time i, an LLM
125   takes the tokenized input xi−1 = (x−np −1 , x−np −2 , · · · , xi−1 ) as the prompt, where x0 =
126   (x−np −1 , x−np −2 , · · · , x0 ) represents the tokenized sequence of Tp and np is the number of to-
127
      kens in Tp . This produces the probability distribution PLLM for the next token xi . We shall write
128
      it as PLLM (Y = xi | xi−1 ), or simply P i , which is the conditional probability for the next token,
      given the proceeding tokens (in the context window).

131   2.2         A RITHMETIC C ODING
132
133   Several authors have shown that Arithmetic Coding, or AC for short, can be used together with
134   language models to perform steganography (Ziegler et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2020; Ivasenko et al.,
135
      2021). AC is a method for data compression that encodes a whole sequence of symbols as a single
      value, based on the probability distribution. Typically, AC compresses the character in the sequence
136
      sequentially into a sequence of bits at the transmitter, and converts the sequence back to text during
137
      decompressing. The main idea of using AC for steganography is that an AC decoder can be viewed
138   as a sampler in the set of natural language paragraphs using the secret message as a random seed,
139   and since the secret message is uniformly distributed on the message set, the sampled text would
140   look like natural language. Note that the AC encoding procedure is the steganography decoding
141   procedure, and the AC decoding procedure is the steganography encoding procedure.

170   3     P ROPOSED M ETHODOLOGY
171
172   A well-known fact in data compression is that the expected minimum number of bits to represent a
173   message symbol following a probability P is H(P ), i.e., the entropy of symbol (Cover & Thomas,
174
      1991), and AC is one algorithm that can compress at a rate close to the minimum value. The same
      relation holds for LLM-based steganography using AC, in the sense that the expected number of
175
      secret message bits that can be embedded for a given token position-i is the entropy of the conditional
176
      distribution H(P i ). For example, if a token has a conditional distribution of { 14 , 14 , 14 , 14 } on four
177   possible token values, then 2 bits of secret message can be embedded in the stego-text.
178
179   It is obvious that a slight modification to the probability distribution from the true natural language
180
      distribution is nearly imperceptible to a human reader (weak detector), or even to a computer pro-
      gram for that matter. We can take advantage of such an opportunity to make the conditional distri-
181
      bution P more amicable for embedding secret message bits, i.e., choose a different distribution Q
182
      such that the entropy H(Q) is larger. As long as Q is kept close to P under certain measure, we
183   expect the generated stego-text to be nearly imperceptible, which leads us to the formulation given
184   next.
185

274   LLM-based steganography relies on several assumptions. Firstly, the underlying LLM and the pa-
275   rameters given to both Alice and Bob must be identical. Second, it is essential that Bob’s tok-
276   enization process matches that intended by Alice. The second assumption is in fact quite sub-
277   tle, and is complicated by the sub-word tokenizer used in modern pre-trained LLMs. These tok-
278   enizers can guarantee that after detokenizing, the original text can be recovered; however, it does
279   not guarantee the tokenizer can always reproduce the same sequence of tokens from the detok-
280
      enized text. For example, a token sequence Alice generated during the stego-text encoding process

281
      high”, which Bob might incorrectly tokenize to {“This”, “mountain”, “is”, “high”}. In order words,
282
      the tokenizer merged “mountain” into a single token rather than the two that the stego-text encoder
283   intended. This issue exists in most of the previous LLM-based steganography approaches (Ziegler

408   In our experiment, we chose the LLAMA2-7B pretrained model (Touvron et al. (2023)) as our main

410
411   We performed experiments using a range of starting prompts on different topics of interest. Exam-
412   ples topics include the Olympics, news, technology, and blogs, among others. The prompts usually
413   have 10 to 20 words. Despite their brevity, we demonstrate that OD-Stega can still generate stego-
414   texts that remains relevant to the initial prompt with the assistance of contemporary LLMs.
415   In our two-stage optimization framework, we typically select a cutoff value ϵ within the range
416   (0, 0.05], and adjust the constant C ∈ [0, 0.2) in (12) to control the δi values. Additionally, the
417   threshold α is adjusted within the interval [0, 2] to enhance the optimization procedure. Setting the
418   cutoff ϵ at its maximum of 0.05 results in the effective elimination of roughly 2000 variables. More-
419   over, by adjusting the range of δi and α values, we can assess how these values influence both the
420   performance of the generated stego-text and the number of embedded bits.
421   The primary evaluation metric used in this study is the number of embedded secret bits per token,
422   or equivalent the number of embedded bytes for a fixed number of generated stego-text tokens, for
423   which a higher value indicates more efficient embedding. The quality of the generated stego-text is
424   measured by two metrics: 1) The KL Divergence where a lower value implies better imperceptibil-
425   ity; 2) A perception evaluation using GPT-4 as a human perception surrogate, where we simply ask
426   GPT to determine whether the stego-text is written by human or not. We refer to the approach of
427
      Shen et al. (2020) as the truncation-based method, and use it as the main reference to compare with
      our proposed OD-Stega method.

531
532
      To embed more secret messages in stego-texts while reducing computational complexity and main-
533
      taining near-imperceptibility, we propose the OD-Stega method. This approach optimizes the prob-
534   ability distribution towards a more uniform structure under a perception constraint. Additionally,
535   we address the tokenization errors that often arise in LLM-based steganography due to the use of
536   sub-word tokenizers in modern LLMs. Together with the vocabulary truncation technique, our two-
537   stage embedding process significantly increases the embedding efficiency under the KL divergence
538   constraint, and demonstrates strong imperceptibility performance. We conducted extensive tests and
539   evaluate the outputs both using the KL divergence value and the GPT evaluation. OD-Stega provides
      a robust solution, enhancing both efficiency and security in LLM-based steganographic embedding.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
649
650
651
652
      Linguistic Steganography (LS) can be divided into two main areas: modification-based (cover-
653
      based) and generation-based (coverless). The modification-based approach conceals secret mes-
654
      sages by altering the cover text through synonyms, syntactic changes, and word substitutions (Top-
655   kara et al., 2006; Chang & Clark, 2010; Qi et al., 2013; Chang & Clark, 2014). In contrast, the
656   generation-based approach creates stego-texts using methods like Markov chains (Dai et al., 2009;
657   2010; Moraldo, 2012) and deep learning techniques. With the advancement of generative language
658   models, an increasing number of steganography research efforts now leverage neural networks to
659   produce steganographic texts (Fang et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2018; Ziegler et al., 2019; Xiang et al.,
660   2017; Dai & Cai, 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2020; Kaptchuk et al., 2021; Ding et al.,
661   2023; de Witt et al., 2024)
662   Fang et al. (2017), for instance, explored a block-based methodology in which they designed a text
663   generation model that first partitions the dictionary and allocates a specific code for each word.
664   During the output stage, modified word-level LSTM neural network is utilized to choose words
665   according to the encoded secret information. Their method organizes the vocabulary into subsets,
666   the best word is chosen from a candidate pool based on the encoded bitstream at every generation
667   step. Yang et al. (2018) presented a model that enhances text fluency and security in steganography
668   by encoding each word dynamically based on its conditional probability distribution, employing
669
      both fixed-length coding (FLC) and variable-length coding (VLC). Through the use of structures
      like full binary trees or Huffman trees, this method enhances the naturalness and quality of generated
670
      texts while embedding hidden information more effectively.
671
672   Ziegler et al. (2019) also utilized GPT-2 to create stego-texts, by proposing a linguistic steganog-
673   raphy method that uses arithmetic coding with a pretrained neural language model. This method
674   encodes secret messages by truncating the token distribution to the top K most probable tokens
675
      at each generation step, thus minimizing the difference between the conditional probability distri-
      butions of steganographic and normal text, achieving close to optimal statistical security. Human
676
      evaluations were conducted to confirm that the generated text successfully deceived readers.
677
678   Building on Ziegler et al.’s arithmetic coding and truncating probability method, Shen et al. (2020)
679   modified K for each iteration, adjusting the conditional probability threshold with each new token.
680   They claimed to select the smallest K that still ensured near-imperceptibility. Additionally, they
681
      employed human evaluations to confirm their findings, demonstrating their method’s effectiveness
      in deceiving eavesdroppers.
682
683   Dai & Cai (2019) employed GPT-2 for generating steganographic texts, crafting a novel stegano-
684   graphic mapping to embed secret messages and showcasing that effective mapping increases text
685   security. They also proposed the patient-Huffman algorithm in such setting, which dynamically ad-
686   justs the embedding rate through the application of Kullback-Leibler divergence, enhancing both the
687
      quality and imperceptibility of steganographic texts. Their approach achieved near-imperceptibility,
      validated using total variation distance.
688
689   Recognizing the informal nature in the treatment of the security aspect of the methods in the studies
690   from natural language processing community Ziegler et al. (2019); Dai & Cai (2019); Shen et al.
691   (2020), the security research community further refined these methods to obtain provably secure
692   protocols (Kaptchuk et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023; de Witt et al., 2024). Zhang
693
      et al. (2021) attempted to use grouping to match the granularity of probability to that of the secret
      message distribution granularity, however, their method is only perfectly secure when the natural
694
      language distribution allows such a grouping. Moreover, the grouping operation itelf also leads to
695
      a loss of embedding utilization. Kaptchuk et al. (2021) replaced the repeated secret key in Ziegler
696   et al. (2019) with pseudo-random generators, and showed that the resulting protocol is provably
697   secure. However, the arithmetic coding component in Kaptchuk et al. (2021) is a reduced version
698   from the full version, resulting in a slight loss in the embedding utilization. Instead of encrypting
699   the original message and then using the generative model for steganography encoding, Ding et al.
700   (2023) combined the encryption step and the steganography encoding, resulting in another provably
701   secure protocol. The work de Witt et al. (2024) proposed a different approach to couple the message

      In this paper, we present our encoding-decoding framework, drawing inspiration from Ziegler et al.
703   (2019) and Shen et al. (2020). We observed that truncating a significant portion of the conditional
704   probability from below leads to a reduction in bits embedded, which improves computational effi-
705   ciency but reduces capacity. In fact, their approach for embedding long secret messages requires
706   more computation in order to generate long stego-texts. To resolve this issue, we propose a novel
707   method for adjusting the conditional probability to maximize the information embedded while main-
708   taining near imperceptibility. Our results demonstrate that we can embed nearly 1.5 times the amount
709   of bits compared to the previous work.
