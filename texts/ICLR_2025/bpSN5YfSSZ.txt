009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A key challenge in artificial intelligence is inferring underlying factors that are
012            not directly observable but are crucial for understanding and predicting complex
013            behaviors. In this paper, we introduce a novel neural-symbolic framework that
014            advances beyond traditional rule induction by integrating latent predicate discov-
015            ery with rule learning. Our approach utilizes a recurrent unit to iteratively refine
016            and learn rules from observed data, employing dynamic programming techniques
017            to identify fixed points and solve complex problems. This framework enables the
018            discovery of hidden predicates—such as user engagement or underlying motiva-
019
               tions—that influence observable outcomes but are not directly grounded in the
               data. By encoding both explicit and latent predicates into a unified rule embed-
020
               ding, our method facilitates a deeper understanding of complex phenomena and
021
               enhances predictive accuracy. This joint learning process captures explicit rela-
022            tionships and invents new predicates essential for comprehensive inference. We
023            validate our method across various tasks, demonstrating its capability to reveal
024            hidden structures and enhance symbolic reasoning with deeper, more accurate in-
025            sights.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Neural-symbolic reasoning integrates neural networks’ ability to learn complex patterns from data
031   with symbolic reasoning’s precision in applying logical rules Hitzler & Sarker (2022). This hybrid
032   approach creates AI systems capable of not only detecting intricate patterns in unstructured data but
033   also reasoning about them in a structured, explainable manner Yang et al. (2024).
034
      Traditional rule induction methods focus on extracting explicit patterns from data but often fail to un-
035
      cover latent predicates—hidden variables or relationships that are not directly observable Campero
036   et al. (2018)Claire Glanois (2022). These methods are effective for learning surface-level rules
037   but struggle with identifying underlying factors that drive complex phenomena. For example, in
038   healthcare diagnostics, critical latent conditions must be inferred from incomplete or noisy data, and
039   predicate invention is crucial for discovering these hidden factors and generating new, abstract con-
040   cepts. Probabilistic models like Markov Logic Networks (MLNs) Richardson & Domingos (2006)
041   can infer latent predicates but typically assume that the logical rules are predefined, which can limit
042   adaptability and lead to computational challenges Oltramari et al. (2020).
043   Our framework tackles this gap by jointly learn the logical rules and infer (and discover) the hidden
044   predicates. We employ a specialized recurrent unit, which we call the Recurrent Neuro-Symbolic
045   Forward Chaining Network (RNS-FCN). Our RNS-FCN operates in two distinct phases: forward in-
046   ference, where the current logical rules are applied recursively to make the inference, and backprop-
047   agation, where the model refines and updates its logical rules based on error signals. The forward
048   inference is achieved by running the recurrent unit through several steps until the states converge
049   to a fixed point, making latent predicate inference straightforward. Rule learning is achieved in a
050
      differentiable manner, allowing for adaptive updates based on error signals and ensuring both trans-
      parency and flexibility in the model.
051
052   Our RNS-FCN shares some similarities with traditional Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) Gross-
053   berg (2013). Like RNNs, we utilize recurrent units where each layer reuses the same set of model
      parameters across steps. However, the depth of our model refers to more than just the number of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      neural layers—it represents deeper reasoning akin to human thought processes. Much like how hu-
055   mans engage in iterative reasoning, refining their understanding as new evidence emerges. Think of
056   it as a process of deeper thinking: as our model progresses through multiple layers, it is reasoning
057   more deeply about the logical connections in the data, adjusting its beliefs until it reaches a stable
058   conclusion Kuang et al. (2024). This contrasts with traditional deep learning, where increased depth
059   typically refers to larger networks, but without a logical reasoning process guiding the layers. In this
060   way, our model simulates recursive reasoning, pushing beyond surface-level pattern recognition and
061   engaging in multi-step inferencing akin to human thought, offering a novel depth of interpretability
062   and precision.
063   Each recurrent layer in the RNS-FCN performs a one-step inference guided by the current set of
064   logical rules. The rules themselves, represented by the parameters of the recurrent units, are updated
065   not during the inference phase but through backpropagation. In the forward computation, these rules
066   act as constraints, shaping how the model updates its beliefs or hypotheses about the data at each
067   step. The iterative process continues until the model reaches a fixed point where its inferences stabi-
068   lize and no further updates are required. In the backward computation, backpropagation via gradient
069
      descent adjusts the model’s parameters based on the discrepancy between predicted and actual out-
      comes, ensuring that the learned logical rules improve with each iteration. The backpropagation
070
      mechanism allows us to optimize the rule set in an end-to-end differentiable way.
071
072   In summary, our framework bridges the gap between traditional rule induction and the modeling of
073   latent variables. The RNS-FCN framework provides a robust tool for discovering hidden structures
074   and generating new concepts, offering a comprehensive solution for tasks that require deep under-
075
      standing and adaptability. This end-to-end approach enhances our ability to uncover latent factors,
      making it invaluable for applications ranging from user behavior analysis to advanced decision-
076
      making systems in AI.

081   Traditional ILP Methods Inductive Logic Programming (ILP) focuses on learning logical rules
082   from relational data. Some papers rely on heuristic methods for efficiency. Cohen (1995) pro-
083   posed RIPPER, a fast rule induction algorithm that builds rules iteratively using a separate-and-
084   conquer strategy. Similarly, Quinlan (1990) developed FOIL, which operates by iteratively gen-
085
      erating clauses that define target relations based on a training set of examples. It uses a greedy
      algorithm to add literals (conditions) to the rules, aiming to generalize the target relation effectively.
086
      The method presented in Dash et al. (2018) learns Boolean decision rules in disjunctive normal
087
      form (DNF) or conjunctive normal form (CNF) for binary classification. The method uses column
088   generation (CG) to efficiently search through the exponentially large space of possible clauses. Wei
089   et al. (2019) propose Generalized Linear Rule Models (GLRM), which integrate decision rules into
090   generalized linear models (GLM) to balance interpretability and accuracy. The method formulates
091   the rule learning process as an optimization problem, trading off rule complexity and predictive per-
092   formance. Cropper & Morel (2023) propose an ILP approach called Learning from Failures (LFF),
093   which consists of three stages: generate, test, and constrain. In the generate stage, a hypothesis is
094   created based on syntactic constraints. In the test stage, the hypothesis is evaluated against training
095   examples. The method is implemented in Popper, which integrates Answer Set Programming (ASP)
096
      and Prolog to learn optimal and recursive logic programs efficiently. These approaches demonstrate
      the reliance of ILP and related methods on heuristics to manage complexity. However, they gener-
097
      ally may not guarantee globally optimal solutions. Pellegrina & Vandin (2024) propose SamRuLe,
098
      a scalable algorithm for learning nearly optimal rule lists via sampling. This method leverages VC-
099   dimension bounds to ensure that the learned rule lists are close to the optimal solution on the full
100   dataset. However, due to its reliance on sampling, SamRuLe may not handle noisy data effectively.
101
102   ILP Methods with Differentiable Model Traditional Inductive Logic Programming (ILP) models
103   struggle with noisy data and scalability. Differentiable approaches address these issues by integrat-
104   ing continuous relaxation, which allows gradient descent for optimization. Evans & Grefenstette
105   (2018) proposed ∂ILP, which represents logic rules in a differentiable form and combines neural
106   networks with symbolic logic. Manhaeve et al. (2018) introduced DeepProbLog, which extends
107   ProbLog by integrating neural predicates for reasoning over both symbolic and subsymbolic data.
      While these differentiable ILP methods improve robustness to noise and enable joint optimization,

      they face challenges in scalability and computational cost. Neural Logic Machines (NLMs) (Dong
109   et al., 2019) combine MLPs with logic programming to perform inductive learning and logical rea-
110   soning. However, the implicit representation of rules in the network weights reduces interpretability.

112
      ILP Methods with Neural Embedding Embedding-based models are widely used for Knowl-
113
      edge Base (KB) completion. These models typically represent entities as low-dimensional vectors,
114
      while relations are modeled as linear or bilinear operators applied to these entities. Early models like
115   TransE (Bordes et al., 2013) interpret relations as translations in the vector space but struggle with
116   complex relations. TransH (Wang et al., 2014) and TransR (Lin et al., 2015) address this by project-
117   ing entities onto hyperplanes or distinct relation-specific spaces, improving the handling of diverse
118   relations. More expressive models, such as Neural Tensor Networks (NTN) (Socher et al., 2013) and
119   RESCAL (Nickel et al., 2011), capture higher-order interactions at the cost of increased complexity.
120   ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016) introduces complex-valued embeddings for asymmetric relations,
121   while multi-hop reasoning methods like Guu et al. (2015) (Guu et al., 2015) leverage path-based
122   embeddings for traversing knowledge graphs. However, these approaches often face limitations in
123
      reasoning power, particularly in multi-hop scenarios.
124   Recent advances in inductive logic programming (ILP) are significantly influenced by Rocktäschel
125   & Riedel (2017), who propose Neural Theorem Proving (NTP). NTP integrates symbolic logic with
126   neural networks through differentiable backward-chaining reasoning. Building on this, Campero
127   et al. (2018) introduces a neural forward-chaining differentiable rule induction network. However,
128   both approaches still rely on carefully hand-designed templates for each ILP task, which limits
129
      scalability. Claire Glanois (2022) advance these models by incorporating a hierarchical structure
      with an expressive set of meta-rules, enabling more flexible rule induction without the need for
130
      manual template design. Nevertheless, the model struggles to discover latent variables or invent
131
      new predicates, limiting its ability to uncover hidden patterns in complex reasoning tasks.

134   3   BACKGROUND
135
136
      Predicate In the context of logic-based AI systems, a predicate is a fundamental Boolean logic
137   variable used to describe properties of or relationships between entities. Predicate variables are
138   grounded by data, being True or False, and are served as the basic building blocks for logical expres-
139   sions. For instance, a predicate like Has F ever(P atient) denotes whether a patient has a fever,
140   while U se Drug(P atient) specifies whether a drug treats a particular patient. These predicates
141   capture essential aspects of the system’s state and relationships.
142
143   Logic rules are formal constructs used to infer new knowledge based on the relationships among
144   predicates. A prevalent form of logic rule is the Horn clause, which is expressed as:
145
146                                      f:     Q ← P 1 ∧ P 2 ∧ · · · ∧ Ph                                 (1)
147
148   where P1 , P2 , . . . , Ph are predicates constituting the body, representing conditions that must be
149   satisfied, while Q is a predicate in the head, representing the conclusion. The rule indicates that if
150   all predicates in the body hold true, the predicate Q can be inferred. Horn rules are integral to logical
151
      reasoning systems, enabling the derivation of new facts from existing knowledge.
152
153   Latent Predicates and Rule Learning In many complex domains, such as healthcare, finance,
154   or social networks, not all predicates are known a priori. Latent predicates are variables represent-
155   ing hidden or unknown aspects that influence observed phenomena but are not directly observable.
156   Identifying these latent predicates is crucial for comprehensive understanding and accurate decision-
157   making. In our approach, the model learns rules with the template as Eq. (1) that include latent pred-
158
      icates, treating them as placeholders for unknown relationships or factors. As the model identifies
      and integrates these latent predicates into the rules, it uncovers hidden structures within the data.
159
160   Once the rules are established, we perform post-hoc labeling of the latent predicates by interpreting
161   their roles in the rule structure. This process allows us to assign meaningful labels to previously
      unknown predicates based on their discovered relationships and functions.

189   As a preparation for our RNS-FCN model, we will introduce predicate embeddings for X, Y , and
190   latent variables. Note that these predicate embeddings will be frozen during the training.
191
192
      Predicates Embedding for X Denote X = {X1 , . . . , Xn } as a set of predicate variables. Each
193   predicate Xi ∈ {1, 0} is a Boolean random variable with a prespecified meaning. We associate each
194   of the True and False states of the predicate with two paired predicate embeddings, denoted as KXi
195   and K¬Xi , with KXi , K¬Xi ∈ Rd and KXi = −K¬Xi , ∀Xi . In this way, for each states of the pred-
196   icate, we can find their embedding from KX = [KX1 , . . . , KXn ] and K¬X = [K¬X1 , . . . , K¬Xn ].
197   These embeddings can be pretrained to capture the meaning and relationships between predicates.
198   They act like a lookup dictionary, providing a fixed representation for each state of the predicate.
199
200   Predicate Embeddings for Latent Variables Our model is designed to discover new predicates, a
201   crucial capability in scenarios where not all predicates are known a priori. Let U = {U1 , . . . , Um }
202   represent a set of latent or yet-to-be-invented predicates with initially undefined meanings. The
203   number of these unlabeled predicates, m, is a hyperparameter that can be adjusted based on the
204   specific application or data complexity. Each latent predicate Ui ∈ {1, 0}, and its True and False
205   states are also linked to a paired embedding vectors, denoted as KUi , K¬Ui ∈ Rd with KUi =
206
      −K¬Ui , ∀Ui . These embeddings act as placeholders for these unlabeled predicates. We aim to
      interpret these latent predicates in a posthoc way as our model uncovers logical rules.
207
208
209   Predicate Embeddings for Y In the classification problem, each label y ∈ Y can be represented
210   as an embedding vector (for example, a one-hot vector).
211   We freeze the predicate embeddings during rule learning, and each rule is represented as a rule em-
212   bedding. All the rule embeddings are treated as model parameters and are constructed as conjunctive
213   combinations of predicate embeddings. Our model learn to select the best matching predicates to
214   fill the rule’s structure. The rule embeddings are optimized via gradient descent in a differentiable
215   manner, allowing efficient learning. This separation—fixed predicate embeddings and learnable rule
      embeddings—enables the model to focus on discovering relevant logical rules

235
236   Our proposed RNS-FCN models rule embeddings as trainable parameters within a recurrent neural
237   network architecture. Unlike traditional RNNs that process sequences of data, each recurrent unit
238   in RNS-FCN is responsible for performing logical reasoning over predicates (symbolic variables).
239   At each step, the recurrent unit applies the learned rules to update the model’s understanding of the
240   logical relationships in the data. This process continues iteratively, refining inferences until a stable
241   state is reached. All rule embeddings are optimized end-to-end through backpropagation across the
242   recurrent units, enabling the model to learn complex logical patterns.

      5.2.5   S ENSITIVE A NALYSIS FOR H YPER -PARAMETERS
510   The most critical hyper-parameters in our proposed framework are the number of iterations, the
511   number of rules for inference y and the number of latent variables. We conducted experiments on
512   the dataset Heloc to test our model’s sensitivity to hyperparameters and select the most suitable
513   ones. The model’s performance under different hyperparameter settings was also evaluated using
514   prediction accuracy and F1 score as the evaluation metrics. The result in 4(a) shows that our method
515   achieve its best performance when Iterate six times. The result in 4(b) shows that our method
516   performs best when there are four rules. Moreover, the result in 4(c) demonstrates that our model
517   achieves the peak performance when setting four latent variables. This is because when there are
518
      too few latent variables, the model fails to capture enough of the missing information, leading to
      underfitting. Conversely, when there are too many latent variables, they introduce noise that can
519
      disrupt the prediction, resulting in overfitting. Furthermore, Consequently, and the default literations
520
      is six times, the default number of rules to infer y is four, and the default number of latent variables
521   is also four.

523   5.2.6   V ISUALIZATION FOR I TERATIONS
524
525   In this setting, we set the number of iterations to 10 and the number of latent variables to 4 5. We
526   examined how the values corresponding to the latent variables change with the number of iterations.
527   We visualize the changes in the v-values of two sample points across the iterations. The experimental
528
      results show that the v values of different samples tend to converge around the 6th or 7th iteration,
      but as the iterations approach 10, they start to diverge. This indicates that the v-values are in an
529
      optimal state around the 6th-7th iteration, and further iterations disrupt this balance. This aligns
530
      with our experimental findings, where the model performs best after 6 iterations.
