009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal graph recovery traditionally relies on statistical estimation of observable
012            variables or individual knowledge, which suffer from data collection biases and
013            knowledge limitations of individuals. Leveraging the broad knowledge in scien-
014            tific corpus, we propose a novel method for causal graph recovery to deduce causal
015            relationships with the large language models (LLMs) as a knowledge extractor.
016
               Our method extracts associational relationships among variables and further elim-
               inates the inconsistent relationship to recover a causal graph using the constraint-
017
               based causal discovery methods. Comparing to other LLM-based methods that
018
               directly instruct LLMs to do highly complex causal reasoning, our method shows
019            advantages on causal graph quality on benchmark datasets. More importantly, as
020            causal graphs may evolve when new research results emerge, our method shows
021            sensitivity to new evidence in the literature and can provide useful information to
022            update causal graphs accordingly.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Estimating causal effect between variables from observational data is a fundamental problem to
      many domains including medical science (Höfler, 2005), social science (Angrist et al., 1996), and
028
      economics (Imbens & Rubin, 2015; Yao et al., 2021). It enables reliable decision-making from
029
      complex data with entangled associations.
030
031   While it is usually expensive and infeasible to investigate causal effects using randomized experi-
032   ments, researchers employ causal inference (Pearl, 2010) to estimate causal effects from observa-
033
      tional data. There are two main frameworks for causal inference: the potential outcome frame-
      work (Rubin, 1974) and the structural causal model (SCM) (Pearl, 1995). Priori causal structures,
034
      usually represented as Directed Graphical Causal Models (DGCMs) (Pearl, 2000; Spirtes et al.,
035
      2001), are often used to represent and analyze the causal relationships. These causal graphs help
036   disentangle the complex interdependencies and facilitate the analysis of causal effects. Recov-
037   ering causal graphs often relies on experts’ knowledge or statistical estimation on observational
038   data (Spirtes & Glymour, 1991). Causal Discovery (CD) algorithms (Spirtes & Glymour, 1991)
039   are the main statistical estimation-based methods that use conditional independence tests to assess
040   conditional associational relationships (called associational reasoning) for inferring causal connec-
041   tions (Spirtes et al., 2001; Chickering, 2002; Shimizu et al., 2006; Sanchez-Romero et al., 2018).
042   Consequently, the reliability of these algorithms is affected by the quality of data, which can be
043   compromised by issues such as data collection bias (Zhang et al., 2017; Bareinboim et al., 2014;
044   Bhattacharya et al., 2021) (See Example 1 in Appendix A.1). Additionally, CD algorithms often
045   assume certain distribution, such as Gaussian about data, which may fail to accurately reflect the
046   complexity of real-world scenarios.
047
      To overcome these limitations, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023) have been em-
048   ployed for causal graph recovery. While few studies have explored hybrid solutions that use LLMs
049   to refine the results of statistical estimation-based methods (Vashishtha et al., 2023; Ban et al., 2023),
050   most work relies solely on LLMs to output causal graphs. Among these, one way is to directly ask
051   LLMs to infer the conditional associational relationships (CARs) between each pair of factors in
052   the graph to build the causal graph (Choi et al., 2022; Long et al., 2022; Kıcıman et al., 2023),
053   relying only on the LLM’s background knowledge. However, it is questionable whether LLMs
      possess sufficient domain-specific knowledge or causal reasoning capabilities to perform this task


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      effectively (Kandpal et al., 2023; Zečević et al., 2023). An alternative methodology is to inject the
055   causal graph knowledge into LLMs (Kandpal et al., 2023; Zečević et al., 2023). However, studies
056   such as (Cohrs et al., 2023) have reported low LLM performance in recognizing CARs. Experiments
057   conducted by (Jin et al., 2023b) show that fine-tuning LLMs on synthetic CAR datasets can improve
058   performance on trained tasks. However, they also demonstrate that the LLM is merely rote learning
059   the CARs from the synthetic data rather than learning how to reason about CARs, as evidenced by
060   significant performance drops when variable names are changed.
061   We propose the LLM Assisted Causal Recovery (LACR) method to address the challenges faced by
062   current causal graph recovery approaches. Instead of relying on LLMs’ ability to perform complex
063   causal reasoning, LACR capitalizes on their strength in understanding and extracting information
064   from vast amounts of scientific literature. By doing so, we leverage the LLMs’ ability to interpret
065   complex associational and causal insights hidden in a large scientific corpus, rather than relying
066   solely on their reasoning capabilities.
067   LACR retrieves relevant knowledge from a comprehensive scientific corpus that contains valuable
068   information about the relationships between variables. The LLM is used to infer how each document
069   supports or refutes the conditional associational relationship (CAR) between two factors, extracting
070   CAR estimations based on the evidence provided by the retrieved literature. This retrieval-based
071   strategy allows us to build a rich dataset of CAR estimations that are grounded in scientific knowl-
072   edge and experimental data, which helps us to overcome the data collection bias problem.
073   By aggregating the CAR estimations returned by the LLMs, LACR recovers the causal graph
074   through a constraint-based causal discovery algorithm. The aggregation process is not arbitrary;
075   instead, it is formalized as a collective decision-making problem, ensuring that the most consistent
076   CAR estimations are retained while maintaining an acyclic structure for the graph. We demonstrate
077   that this problem is NP-hard and provide approximation algorithms to address it effectively.
078
      We validate the effectiveness of LACR through extensive experiments on two well-known real-world
079   causal graphs. Our results show that LACR not only recovers accurate causal graphs but also identi-
080   fies biases in validation datasets commonly used in the causal discovery community. This highlights
081   the potential of LACR to recover causal graphs that are better aligned with the latest domain knowl-
082   edge, suggesting avenues for improving current validation practices in causal discovery.

089   2.1   D IRECTED G RAPHICAL C AUSAL M ODELS (DGCM S )
090
091   A DGCM is a tuple M = ⟨G, P ⟩, in which, G = ⟨V, E⟩ is a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), also
092   known as a causal graph. The set of nodes V = {v1 , · · · , vn } represents random variables (with
093   |V | = n), and E ⊆ {(vi , vj ) | vi , vj ∈ V, vi ̸= vj } are directed edges, also called causal edges, that
094   encode causal relationships. Let Ḡ = ⟨V, Ē⟩ be the skeleton of DAG G, where each (vi , vj ) ∈ Ē
095   is an undirected edge, and it indicates that one of (vi , vj ) and (vj , vi ) is in E. Given a variable set
096   V , we denote the set of all DAGs and all skeletons by G and Ḡ, respectively. In G, let a sequence
097   of distinct nodes ℓ = (vj1 , vj2 , · · · , vjm ) denote a path, such that for each i ∈ {1, 2, · · · , m − 1},
098
      either (vji+1 , vji ) or (vji , vji+1 ) ∈ E. A path is a causal path from vj1 to vjm if for each i ∈
      {1, 2, · · · , m − 1}, (vji , vji+1 ) ∈ E. P is a joint probability distribution of all variables in V . Note
099
      that in our method, we allow the existence of exogenous variables, i.e., variables not contained in V
100
      may mediate the causal relationships between variables in V .

      2.2   C ONSTRAINTS OF C AUSAL G RAPHS
103
104   A causal graph is subject to a series of constraints on variables’ conditional associational rela-
105   tionships (CARs). Especially, the causal edges specify the causal relationships between variables.
106   (vi , vj ) represents that vi is a direct cause of vj , i.e., when holding the other variables constant, vary-
107   ing the value of vi triggers a corresponding change in the value of vj , but not vice versa. This causal
      relationship thus entails the associational relationship between the variables, i.e., their marginal

      probability distributions P (vi ) and P (vj ) are associated (or correlated), which does not have the
109   direction attribute. Note that two variables can be associated even though they do not have a di-
110   rect causal relationship. Typical examples are that two variables linked by a causal path, and two
111   variables pointed to by two causal paths that have the same starting node (which is usually called a
112   covariate). The precise constraints follow the well known Causal Markov Assumption.
113

114   its non-descendants conditioned on its parents in the causal graph, i.e., v ⊥ non desc(v) | parent(v).

117

118

119
      encodes that the two variables are independent conditioned on V ′ . We say the association between
120   vi and vj is blocked by V ′ , and if vi and vj cannot be d-separated, their association is unblockable.

122   Assumption 1 is a necessary condition for the encoding of the associaitonal relationship constraints
123
      in P . On the other hand, the following faithfulness assumption is a sufficient condition that P
      encodes such constraints.

126   conditional associational relationships other than those consistent with G’s d-separation informa-
127   tion. We call such P is faithful to G.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
142
143
      In this section, we introduce our large language model assisted causality recovery (LACR) method,
144
      which runs in two phases: the causal edge existence verification, and the edge orientation. LACR
145   employs LLMs to extract the CARs from relevant scientific literature, and recovers the causal graph
146   using the constraint-based causal discovery principles. Specifically, LACR extracts CARs of vari-
147   ables from previous data analysis in relevant scientific literature, to investigate whether each variable
148   pair can be d-separated. Then, it uses such extracted CARs to recover the causal graph based on the
149   constraints of the causal graph (Definition 1).
150
      Notably, in constraint-based causal discovery algorithms, if the dataset satisfies the faithfulness
151   assumption, the obtained CARs do not conflict against each other. However, this may fail extracted
152   CAR estimations from scientific literature, as CAR conflicts may arise due to the analysis noise
153   introduced by previous scientific research and the noise introduced in the extraction process.
154
155
      3.1   I NCONSISTENT A SSOCIATIONS
156
157   Two types of CAR inconsistency may occur in our setting, namely the causal existence inconsistency
158   and the d-separation inconsistency.
159   Causal existence inconsistency specifies the situations where for a specific pair of variables vi and
160   vj , part of the extracted CARs indicate that ∄V ′ ⊆ V \ {vi , vj } s.t. α(ij | V ′ ) = 0, however, the
161   other part indicate that ∃V ′ ⊆ V \ {vi , vj } s.t. α(ij | V ′ ) = 0. Note that it is possible that V ′ = ∅.
      On the other hand, d-separation inconsistency denotes the following conflict. For a variable pair

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      vi , vj ∈ V , we call V ′ ∈ V \ {vi , vj } a minimal d-separation set if α(ij) = 1, α(ij | V ′ ) = 0, and
163   ∄V ′′ ⊂ V ′ such that α(ij | V ′′ ) = 0. Then, we first have the following lemma.
164
      Lemma 1. Let V ′ be a minimal d-separation set of vi and vj . Then, for each variable v ′ ∈ V ′ , v ′
165
      and vi are associated, and so do v ′ and vj .
166
167   Note that all full proofs can be found in Appendix C. If a dataset is faithful to the underlying causal
168   graph, Lemma 1 is satisfied. However, this cannot be guaranteed in our setting.
169
      Apparently, if d-separation inconsistency can be avoided, it is straightforward to deal with the causal
170
      existence inconsistency problem by checking the extracted CARs for each variable pair separately.
171   However, the process tackling the d-separation inconsistency needs involving the CARs of other
172   variable pairs, which considerably enhance the computational complexity.

174   3.2     LACR 1: C AUSAL E DGE E XISTENCE V ERIFICATION
175
176   Now, we are ready to introduce the first phase of LACR. In LACR 1, given a set of variables V ,
177   for each pair of variables vi , vj ∈ V , we first retrieve a fixed number of the most relevant scien-
178   tific papers. Then, for each paper, we use the LLMs to extract the corresponding estimated CAR
179   information, which we call a CAR estimation piece, as follows:
180   1. are vi and vj associated, i.e., the value of α̂(ij)?
181   2. If α̂(ij) = 1, does the paper indicate vi and vj can be d-separated by a variable set? That is, does
182   it hold that ∃V ′ ⊆ V \ {vi , vj } s.t. α̂(ij | V ′ ) = 0?
183   3. If ∃V ′ ⊆ V \ {vi , vj } s.t. α̂(ij | V ′ ) = 0, find a minimal d-separation set.
184   Note that both causal existence inconsistency and d-separation inconsistency potentially occur in the
185   above extracted CARs. We therefore define and solve an optimization problem where we delete the
186   least number of CAR estimation pieces to eliminate both types of inconsistency. Finally, we recover
187   the skeleton of the causal graph following Definition 1.
188

      estimation piece extraction workflow with the least task specific prompt, so to maintain the general-
192
      ization ability of the workflow. In the workflow, we first retrieve a fixed number of the most relevant
193
      scientific papers from scientific literature databases, and we query the LLMs to extract desirable
194   CAR estimation from each paper, and to respond in a structured format.
195
196   Scientific document retrieval Given the variable set V , for each variable pair vi , vj ∈ V , we
197   retrieve relevant scientific papers, called scientific documents, from databases. We rank the retrieved
198   scientific documents based on a matching function, e.g., a key word matching function or a semantic
199   matching function, between each document and the paper searching query “vi and vj ”, for each
200   variable pair, and store the first k documents in set DOCij . Let DOC = {DOCij }vi ,vj ∈V .

203   CAR estimation pieces from retrieved scientific documents, including a task background reminding
204   prompt, and two CAR context prompts. We first prepare the extraction process by making the
205

206

207

335   individually, there may be inconsistencies in the orientation collection D, such as directional in-
336   consistency or cyclic inconsistency. A straightforward approach is to order all edges by weight and
337   process each edge from the highest to the lowest weight, orienting it based on the majority of ori-
338   entation estimations. If this creates a cycle, we reverse the direction, and if a cycle still forms, the
339   edge is removed. However, this method risks cascade failures, where an early misorientation of a
340
      high-weight edge could negatively affect the remaining orientations.
341   This problem is NP-hard, as it can be reduced from the Feedback Arc Set (FAS) problem, which
342   aims to minimize the number of edges removed to make a directed graph acyclic. To address this, we
343   propose Algorithm 3 (detailed in the Appendix), an approximation solution that selects a directed
344   acyclic graph (DAG) with the maximal subset of consistent orientation estimations from D, while
345   eliminating both directional and cyclic inconsistencies. Due to page limitations, we only provide an
346
      outline here. For the problem definition, algorithm, and all proofs, please refer to the Appendix D.2.

498
499   We show the inconsistency issue in realistic scenarios by showing the number of extracted CAR esti-
500   mation pieces used in LACR 1 (Figure 1: “extracted”: total CAR estimations extracted by the LLM;
501   (2) “consistency 1”: CAR pieces remaining after removing associational inconsistencies; and (3)
502
      “consistency 2”: CAR pieces remaining after removing both associational and d-separation incon-
      sistencies. Blue and orange bars stand for the Asia and Sachs datasets, respectively.). In the ASIA
503
      dataset, out of 147 extracted CAR estimation pieces, 123 (83.7%) passed the associational consis-
504
      tency check (consistency 1), and 114 (77.6%) passed both associational and d-separation consis-
505   tency checks (consistency 2). For the SACHS dataset, 237 pieces were extracted, with 199 (83.9%)
506   passing consistency 1, but only 147 (62.0%) passing consistency 2. This indicates that SACHS ex-
507   periences a more significant reduction in adopted CAR estimations after applying the d-separation
508   consistency check compared to ASIA.
509
      Theoretically, applying consistency 1 involves removing minority opinions among the extracted
510
      CAR estimation pieces for each pair of factors, thereby reducing causal existence inconsistency.
511   This process ensures that only the majority-supported associations are considered, enhancing the
512   associations’ reliability. Consistency 2 checks the validity of indirect associations by examining
513   d-separation sets mentioned in the literature. If inconsistencies are found, such as factors in the
514   d-separation set not being connected to the two factors under investigation, the support for an in-
515   direct association is weakened. Consequently, relationships previously considered indirect may be
516   reclassified as direct associations. This shift can lead to an increase in AR, as more associations are
517   identified, but may cause a decrease in AP due to the potential inclusion of false positives.
518   This theoretical impact is reflected in the performance results shown in Table 1. In the ASIA dataset,
519   both consistency levels are relatively high, with minimal reductions after applying the consistency
520   checks. In contrast, the SACHS dataset exhibits a significant decrease in the number of adopted CAR
521   estimations after applying consistency 2, with 38% of the literature removed due to d-separation
522   inconsistencies. This substantial reduction increases the likelihood of voting for direct associations,
523   as fewer indirect associations are supported by the remaining literature. The increased emphasis on
524   direct associations leads to a rise in FP and a decrease in FN. As a result, the AP decreases from
525
      1.0000 in LACR 1 (DOC) to 0.6429 in LACR 1 (CON), while the AR slightly increases from 0.5000
      to 0.5625, as shown in Table 1. This shift reflects the trade-off between precision and recall when
526
      inconsistency removal disproportionately affects one type of association over another.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we proposed a novel LLM-based causal graph construction method called LACR
533
      which uses the constraint-based causal prompt strategy designed according to the constraint-based
534   causal graph construction (CCGC) method. Comparing to most existing LLM-based causal graph
535   construction methods, that use the direct causal prompt to query LLMs to do highly complex causal
536   reasoning, LACR mainly relies on LLMs to do low-complexity associational reasoning, and follows
537   the process of CCGC to determine the causal relationships. For accurate associational reasoning,
538   we retrieve information from external scientific corpus as the context of LLM queries. We evaluate
539   LACR’s efficacy on benchmark datasets, particularly,we show LACR is sensitive to the new evidence
      in the latest literature, which indicates its usefulness for scientific research.

      The theory of Wisdom of the Crowd (Grofman et al., 1983) states that if (1) each individual voter
766
      can make the correct decision better than random decision (e.g., by a toss), and (2) voters make
767
      their decision independently, then, the accuracy of the collective decision made by simple majority
768   monotonically increases with the number of voters. In LACR, each CAR estimation can be seen as a
769   voter. Generally the above conditions tend to be guaranteed because (1) both BG and DOC have high
770   quality and the delivered information is better than random information, and (2) different research
771   papers deliver their results in a relatively independent way because of scientific integrity. Therefore,
772   LACR’s decision tends to be more accurate than querying single knowledge base, and it can be
773   improved by adding more relevant documents.

779   Proof. Let V ′ be a minimal d-separation set of vi and vj in a causal graph G. Without loss of
780   generality, we reason that an arbitrary variable v ∈ V ′ is associated with vi .
781
      Assume that v and vi are not associated. Since at least a path between v and vi exists due to the
782
      definition of d-separation, a collider must exist on all paths between vi and v. That is, between vi
783   abd vj , a collider exists on each path that goes through v. Then, if we remove v from V ′ , these
784   paths are still blocked, which contradicts against the assumption that V ′ is a minimal d-separation
785   set. This completes the proof.

851   Based on the skeleton recovered, we query the LLMs to extract the direction of each undirected
852   edge in Ḡ from the same set of scientific documents for each variable pair. Then, we select a subset
853   of LLMs’ extractions to shape a cycle-free directed graph, coinciding with our causal background

      D.1   O RIENTATION K NOWLEDGE E XTRACTION
857
858   For each variable pair vi , vj ∈ V such that (vi , vj ) ∈ Ē, we first use the same background reminder
859   prompt to make the LLMs clarify the meaning of the variables in V with inputting the domain
860   names. Then, we input each retrieved document in DOCij as the knowledge context, as well as
861   input a causal direction context that instructs the intuition of causal direction, into the LLMs, and
862   query a simple question “Is vi a cause of vj , or vj a cause of vi ?” We discard all unusable orientation
863   estimations with an answer of “unknown”, and record each usable orientation estimation, i.e., either
      “vi → vj ” or “vi ← vj ”, in an orientation collection Dij ∈ D.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      D.2       O RIENTATION
865
866   Apparently, we may also encounter inconsistency issues in the orientation collection D, i.e., the
867   directional inconsistency and the cyclic inconsistency.
868
      For each variable pair vi , vj ∈ V , a directional inconsistency occurs if there are two orientation
869
      estimations d, d′ ∈ Dij such that d specifies that vi → vj and d′ specifies that vi ← vj . Let
870
      Di←j and Di→j be the subsets of Dij such that each orientation estimation is vi ← vj and vi →
871   vj , respectively, and let max(Dij ) be the direction between vi and vj that has more number of
872   orientation estimations (ties are broken randomly). A directional inconsistency typically points
873   to the orientation estimations that specify two conflicting directions for a causal edge. A cyclic
874   inconsistency happens if for a set of variables V ′ = {v1 , · · · , vk } ⊆ V such that for all 1 ≤ i ≤ k,
875   (vi , vi+1 ) ∈ Ē, and an orientation estimation is returned specifying that vi → vi+1 , where vk+1 =
876   v1 . That is, a set of orientation estimations shape a directed cycle in the causal graph, which is not
877   permitted under our DAG setting1 .
878   To avoid directional inconsistency, a straightforward and efficient approach is first to order all edges
879   by weight, then process each edge from the highest to the lowest weight, attempting to orient it based
880   on the orientation estimation. If adding the edge creates a cycle, we reverse its direction, and if a
881   cycle still forms, we remove the edge. This method continues until all edges have been processed.
882   However, it may lead to cascade failures, as incorrectly orienting a high-weight edge early on could
883   impact the orientation of the remaining edges. Clearly, this problem is NP-hard, which can be
884   reduced from the Feedback Arc Set (FAS) problem that aims to find the minimal set of edges whose
885
      removal makes a directed graph acyclic, which is analogous to ensuring that the oriented edges in our
      graph do not form cycles. Therefore, we propose Algorithm 3, an approximation solution, towards
886
      selecting a DAG with the maximal orientation estimation subset of D under the constraints of the
887
      directional and cyclic inconsistency. It aims to discard the fewest number of orientation estimations
888   to eliminate both types of inconsistency and outputting a DAG.
889
890   The algorithm is initiated by setting the graph G as a complete undirected graph Ḡc , an orientation
891
      estimation collection D, a d-separation collection S, and setting a weight vector w as an empty list.
      Then, from Lines 2-6, we remove each undirected edge such that the corresponding d-separation
892
      collection suggests the end node variables can be d-separated by a variable set (including the case
893
      s = all). For each remained edge (vi , vj ), we record its weight as the number of d-separation
894   sets in the d-separation collection Sij . By Lines 7-12, we orient the undirected edges in G in the
895   order decided by the edges weight (high to low), and the direction of each edge is decided by the
896   dominant orientation estimation in Dij , i.e., max(Dij ). If orienting an edge results in a directed
897   cycle, we un-orient the edge, otherwise we decide the edge’s direction in G and remove its weight
898   from w. From Line 13 to 18, we recheck the remained undirected edges, i.e., those form a directed
899   cycle. From the edge with the highest weight, we first try if we orient it by the reverse direction
900   of max(Dij ) still forms a directed cycle. If the orientation does not result in a directed cycle, we
901   decide the edge’s direction, otherwise we remove the edge from G and finally return a DAG.

1273

1274   pair, and the number is limited by a predefined parameter as described in Section 4.2. However,
1275   not all documents can provide useful information to support the CAR decision-making between the
1276   variable pair, and LACR returns “Unknown” if the document does not contain relevant contents.
1277   Assume that LACR retrieves k documents for a variable pair, and LACR returns “Unknown” for m
1278   (1 ≤ m ≤ k) documents. Then, the UR for the variable is m/k. The lower is the UR, the more
1279   informative documents are retrieved for a variable pair.

1283   variable pair denotes that there is a causal edge in the ground truth DAG but LACR fails to recover
1284   it, or there is no causal edge in the ground truth DAG but LACR recovers one by mistake. Note that
1285   the UR for All variable pairs is not the weighted average of the URs of TP variable pairs and False
1286   variable pairs, since we do not report the average UR for true negative variable pairs, i.e., LACR

       Interpretation The performance of LACR on the Alzheimer dataset reveals an interesting trend:
1297   while AR remains consistent at 0.8750 for both the BG approach and the DOC approach, the AP
1298   decreases from 0.5000 (BG) to 0.4375 (DOC). This decline in AP leads to a corresponding drop
1299   in the F1 score from 0.6364 (BG) to 0.5833 (DOC), highlighting a negative impact on the overall
1300   balance between precision and recall when using retrieved documents. These results indicate that
1301   the additional edges introduced in the DOC setting are largely false positives, degrading the quality
1302   of the recovered causal graph. Notably, this trend aligns with our earlier observations in other
1303   datasets, such as Asia and Sachs, where involving documents initially led to performance drops
1304   under outdated ground truth causal graphs.
1305   The Alzheimer dataset’s performance behavior supports our hypothesis: when the ground truth
1306   graph is outdated and does not reflect the latest scientific consensus, incorporating new knowledge
1307   from retrieved documents tends to result in a performance dropping under the old ground truth. As
1308   aforementioned, based on trends observed in Asia and Sachs (as discussed in Section 4.5), we notice
1309   that, upon updating the ground truth graph to align with the current consensus, the performance in
1310   the DOC setting will surpass that of the BG setting. This is because the additional edges introduced
1311
       by DOC, while penalized under outdated ground truth, are more likely to align with modern causal
       understandings.
1312
1313   Table 5 offers further evidence for this assumption. While the UR for TP edges is relatively high in
1314   Alzheimer’s, indicating that BG knowledge dominates the decision to recover most correct causal
1315   relationships, the UR for false edges is relatively low, highlighting that retrieved documents are
1316   introducing new edges perceived as relevant. This trend is similar to the findings in Asia and Sachs,
1317
       where incorporating documents initially caused performance drops but, upon aligning the evaluation
       with updated ground truth, demonstrated the advantage of DOC in leveraging contemporary insights.
1318
1319   Therefore, the observed performance drop for Alzheimer’s in the DOC shows our method is sen-
1320   sitive to documents used.This highlights the importance of updating ground truth causal graphs to
1321   align with evolving scientific understanding, ensuring a fair and accurate assessment of the added
1322   value provided by document-enhanced methods. As seen in other datasets, incorporating up-to-date
1323
       consensus into the ground truth improves LACR’s performance.
1324   It is also worth noting that the overall URs on Ice and Alzheimer datasets are 0.8765 and 0.9069,
1325   compared to 0.5058 and 0.4171 for Asia and Sachs datasets. This indicates that the performance
1326   drop is the lack of supporting documents. The limited useful documents returned from paper search
1327   generate additional noises for the LLM when deciding the causal edges.
