                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance
012            across various natural language processing tasks. Recently, several LLMs-based
013            pipelines have been developed to enhance learning on graphs with text attributes,
014            showcasing promising performance. However, graphs are well-known to be suscep-
015            tible to adversarial attacks and it remains unclear whether LLMs exhibit robustness
016            in learning on graphs. To address this gap, our work aims to explore the potential of
017            LLMs in the context of adversarial attacks on graphs. Specifically, we investigate
018            the robustness against graph structural and textual perturbations in terms of two
019            dimensions: LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. Through extensive
020
               experiments, we find that, compared to shallow models, both LLMs-as-Enhancers
               and LLMs-as-Predictors offer superior robustness against structural and textual
021
               attacks. Based on these findings, we carried out additional analyses to investi-
022
               gate the underlying causes. Furthermore, we have made our benchmark library
023            openly available to facilitate quick and fair evaluations, and to encourage ongoing
024            innovative research in this field.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, significant progress has been made in the development of Large Language Models
030   (LLMs) like Sentence-BERT Reimers & Gurevych (2019), GPT Radford et al. (2018), LLaMA Tou-
031   vron et al. (2023), etc. These variants showcase exceptional performance across a range of natural
032   language processing tasks, such as sentiment analysis Sun et al. (2023b); Rønningstad et al. (2024),
033
      machine translation Feng et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023), and text classification Sun et al. (2023a);
      Zhang et al. (2024). While LLMs are widely employed for handling plain text, there is an in-
034
      creasing trend of applications where text data is linked with structured information represented as
035
      text-attributed graphs (TAGs) Chen et al. (2024); He et al. (2023). Recently, solely utilizing LLMs
036   for graph data has proven effective in various downstream graph-related tasks, and integrating LLMs
037   with Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) Kipf & Welling (2016); Veličković et al. (2017) can further
038   enhance graph learning capabilities Chen et al. (2024).
039
      Although graph machine learning methods with LLMs (Graph-LLMs) have reported promising
040
      performance Qin et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2023); Zhao et al.
041
      (2024); Wang et al. (2024); Cao et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Chien et al.
042   (2022); Duan et al. (2023), their robustness to adversarial attacks remains unknown. Robustness has
043   always been a crucial aspect of model performance, especially in high-risk tasks like medical diagno-
044   sis Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al. (2021), autonomous driving Xiao et al. (2023), epidemic modeling Liu
045   et al. (2024b), where failures can have severe consequences. It is well-known that graph learning
046   models such as GNNs are vulnerable to adversarial attacks, where adversaries manipulate the graph
047   structure to produce inaccurate predictions Zügner et al. (2018); Jin et al. (2021). Moreover, text
048   attributes in TAGs are also vulnerable to manipulation, raising concerns about the reliability of graph
049   learning algorithms in safety-critical applications. In the era of LLMs embracing graphs, a critical
050
      question arises: Are Graph-LLMs robust against graph adversarial attacks?
051   To address this question, we identify several research gaps in existing evaluations that hinder our
052   understanding of current methods. These include: (1) Limited Structural Attacks for Graph-
053   LLMs: Existing structural attacks are tailored for GNNs and have not been tested on Graph-LLMs,
      leaving it uncertain whether Graph-LLMs are sensitive to subtle structural changes within graphs.

081   In response to these challenges, we aim to conduct a fair and reproducible evaluation of both structural
082
      and textual attacks under the representative pipelines of Graph-LLMs for node classification. Our
      contributions can be summarized as follows:
083
084   • New evaluation perspective. Different from existing works focusing on the predictive power
085     of Graph-LLMs, we stress test the robustness of Graph-LLMs against graph adversarial attacks.
086     Specifically, our work introduces a dual-focus evaluation of robustness against both structural
087     and textual attacks, specifically targeting Graph-LLMs. Unlike traditional LLM studies Wu et al.
088
        (2024); Chao et al. (2024); Shuyuan et al.; Wang & Zhao (2024), we consider the unique challenges
        posed by interconnected graph structures and multi-dimensional perturbations.
089
      • Reproducible and comprehensive comparison. We conduct a comprehensive comparison of
090
        various Graph-LLM pipelines across multiple datasets. Specifically, we systematically evaluate
091     two distinct groups of Graph-LLMs: LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. To guarantee
092     reproducibility and fairness in our comparison, we fine-tune all models using the same set of hyper-
093     parameters. We employ two types of evaluation metrics: performance degradation percentage and
094     attack success rate.
095   • Insights into the robustness of Graph-LLMs: This study reveals several interesting observations
096     about the robustness of Graph-LLMs:
097     (a) LLMs-as-Enhancers exhibit greater robustness against adversarial structural attacks compared
098          to shallow embeddings like Bag of Words (BOW) and TF-IDF, particularly at high attack rates.
099
            Additionally, the better the distinguishability of the encoded features, the better the robustness.
        (b) LLMs-as-Predictors show better robustness in resisting structural attacks than Vanilla GNN in
100
             both zero-shot and full-shot settings.
101
        (c) LLMs-as-Predictors show better robustness in resisting textual attacks than MLP and the
102          fine-tuned predictor holds even better robustness than vanilla GNN.
103     (d) LLMs-as-Enhancers demonstrate excellent robustness against textual attacks, with GCN being
104          significantly more robust than MLP as the victim model.
105     (e) Text entropy, text length, and node centrality show certain negative correlations with the attack
106          success rate of the textual attack for Graph-LLMs.
107   • Open-source benchmark library: To support and advance future research, we have developed an
        easy-to-use open-source benchmark library, now publicly available on https://anonymous.

      2      F ORMULATIONS AND BACKGROUND
129
130   We begin by providing preliminaries on graph neural networks, and then formalize the graph adver-
131   sarial attacks. Finally, we briefly introduce the developments in large language models on graphs.
132   More details are shown in Appendix A.
133   Notations. We define a graph as G = (V, E), where V denotes the node set and E represents the
134   edge set. We employ X ∈ RN ×d to denote the node feature matrix, where N is the number of nodes
135   and d is the dimension of the node features. Furthermore, we use the matrix A ∈ RN ×N to signify
136   the adjacency matrix of G. Finally, the graph data can be denoted as G = (A, X).
137
      Graph Adversarial Attacks. While graph adversarial attacks can perturb node features or graph
138
      structures, most existing attacks focus on modifying the graph structure due to its complexity and
139   effectiveness. These modifications often involve adding, deleting, or rewiring edges Jin et al. (2020);
140   Madry et al. (2017); Geisler et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2019); Chang et al. (2020); Ma et al. (2019);
141   Entezari et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021), exemplified by the PGD Madry et al.
142   (2017) and PRBCDGeisler et al. (2021) attacks.
143
      Textual Attack. Textual attacks can be performed on different levels like character level or sentence
144
      level according to the target to be perturbed. In this work, we focus on word-level attacks, applying
145   substitutions to fool the classifier with minimal text perturbation. For example, SemAttack Wang
146   et al. (2022a) generates adversarial text by employing various semantic perturbation functions.
147
148
      Large Language Models (LLMs) on Graphs. Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs)
      like BERT Devlin et al. (2018), Sentence-BERT (SBert) Reimers & Gurevych (2019) E5 Wang
149
      et al. (2022b), GPT Radford et al. (2018), LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) and their variants have
150
      significantly impacted graph-related tasks. Two main paradigms are LLMs-as-Enhancers, improving
151   node features (e.g., TAPE He et al. (2023), KEA Chen et al. (2024), GLEM Zhao et al. (2022)), and
152   LLMs-as-Predictors, leveraging LLMs for graph predictions (e.g., InstructGLM Ye et al. (2023),
153   GraphLLM Chai et al. (2023), GraphGPT Tang et al. (2023)).

156   3      B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
157
158   To deepen our understanding of the potential of Graph-LLMs in the context of robustness on
159   graph learning, we need to design diverse pipelines to systematically assess the robustness of LLM
160   approaches against adversarial attacks on graphs. Our benchmark evaluation encompasses two pivotal
161   dimensions: LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. In this section, we will introduce the
      benchmark design. Details about the benchmark datasets are provided in Appendix B.

      3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
163
164   We describe the characteristics of the graph adversarial attacks we developed, including both structural
165   and textual attacks, from the following aspects. (1) Adversary’s Goal: The primary objective is
166   focused on evasion attacks, where the adversary seeks to manipulate the input graph data at inference
167   time to cause the model to make incorrect predictions. In this scenario, the adversaries do not have
168
      the authority to change the classifier or its parameters. (2) Victim Models: The targets of these
      attacks include LLMs-as-Predictors and LLMs-as-Enhancers, of which the details will be thoroughly
169
      elaborated in Sections 3.2 and 3.3. (3) Adversary’s Knowledge: The attacks are designed under
170
      white-box and grey-box frameworks, meaning that the adversary either possesses complete knowledge
171   of the model architecture and parameters or not respectively. White-box attacks are employed during
172   the LLM-as-Enhancers experiments to evaluate robustness in the worst-case scenarios. However, for
173   LLM-as-Predictors, it becomes impractical to perform white-box attacks due to the significant time
174   costs associated with these large, complex models, and thus we adopt a grey-box setting.
175
176
177   3.2   LLM S - AS -E NHANCERS
178   For LLMs-as-Enhancers, our benchmark provides a fair and comprehensive comparison of existing
179   representative methods from two perspectives: structural attack and textual attack.
180
181
      Structural attack: We have discussed two commonly used methods for structural attacks, PGD Xu
      et al. (2019) and PRBCD Geisler et al. (2021), in Section 2. Currently, existing frameworks rely on
182
      shallow features such as Bag of Words (BOW) and TF-IDF. In the era of LLMs, it’s imperative to
183
      examine the impact of LLM features on structural attacks. Therefore, we designed a pipeline for
184   structural attacks using LLMs-as-Enhancers. Specifically, we first generate diverse feature types
185   derived from various LLMs, including SBert, E5, LLaMA, and Angle-LLaMA Li & Li (2023), as
186   well as LLaMA fine-tuned (LLaMA-FT) with LoRa Hu et al. (2021), etc. We then utilize these
187   features to evaluate the performance of structural attacks. The pipeline is visualized in Figure 2(a).
188
      Textual attack: We conduct an evaluation on text attacks to verify whether LLMs-as-Enhancers
189
      can withstand textual attacks compared to traditional text preprocessing techniques. Specifically, in
190   the white-box setting, we first conduct text attacks by using SemAttack Wang et al. (2022a) on the
191   texts of text-attributed graphs. Then, we encode the texts using different methods such as traditional
192   techniques and LLMs. Finally, we assess their performance on GCN Kipf & Welling (2016) and
193   MLP. Incidentally, to enhance efficiency on LLMs, we modified SemAttack for batch-wise operation
194   instead of word-level processing. The pipeline is illustrated in Figure 2(b).
195
196
197   3.3   LLM S - AS -P REDICTORS
198   For LLMs-as-Predictors, we also perform structural and textual attacks on pre-trained and fine-
199   tuned LLMs, respectively. Different from the attacks on LLMs-as-Enhancers, white-box attacks
200   on the predictors can bring enormous computational costs due to the complexity of the models.
201   Therefore, this study adopts the grey-box setting, choosing LLMs-as-Enhancers as the victim model
202   and transferring the attacked texts or graphs to the LLMs-as-Predictors. For pre-trained models, we
203   utilize the same pipeline in Chen et al. (2024), which describes the graph structure in text and inputs
204   it along with text features directly into GPT-3.5 for prediction. For fine-tuned models, we perform
205   attacks on InstructGLM Ye et al. (2023), which uses LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) as the backbone
206
      and is fine-tuned on different benchmark datasets.
207   Structural attack: Although the LLMs-as-Predictors in this study flatten graph structure into
208   texts and only incorporate a small number of neighbors when predicting a node, it is possible that
209   introducing irrelevant or false neighbors can influence the prediction results. During the structure
210   attack, we use PRBCD as the attack algorithm and choose SBert and GCN as the surrogate model.
211   The perturbed graph then serves as the input of GPT-3.5 or InstructGLM. The pipeline is depicted in
212
      Figure 2(c).
213   Textual attack: The LLMs-as-Predictors directly utilize texts as inputs. Thus, perturbing the input
214   texts is also likely to have impacts to the prediction results. In this study, we first perform SemAttack
215   on the selected nodes with SBert and GCN as the surrogate model. After that, the perturbed texts are
      used to evaluate GPT-3.5 and InstructGLM. The pipeline is shown in Figure 2(d).

235   4       E XPERIMENTS
236
237   In this section, we assess the robustness of LLMs against graph adversarial attacks in their two roles:
238
      LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. Specifically, we aim to answer the following questions:
      Q1: How effective are the LLMs-as-Enhancers on structural attack? Q2: What is the effectiveness of
239
      LLMs-as-Enhancers on textual attacks? Q3: How effective are the LLMs-as-Predictors on structural
240
      attack? Q4: How do LLMs-as-Predictors perform on textual attacks? The experimental settings and
241   usage instructions are reported in Appendix C.
242

      4.1      S TRUCTURAL ATTACK FOR LLM S - AS -E NHANCERS
244
245
      Experiment Design. To tackle the research question Q1, we enhance text attributes using LLMs
246   and generate new features. These enriched features are then used to train a GCN as the victim
247   model. Specifically, we use PGD to conduct white-box evasion attacks on the structures of small
248   graphs such as Cora, Citeseer, Pubmed, and Wikics, while we employ PRBCD to conduct white-box
249   evasion attacks on the structures of large graphs Arxiv and History. We vary the perturbation rates
250   at 0% (clean graphs), 5%, and 25%, which represent the ratio of perturbed edges to original edges.
251   Subsequently, we feed the perturbed graphs into different LLMs-as-Enhancers architectures and
252   compare the LLM features with shallow features. To quantify model robustness, we report the test
253   accuracy (ACC) and the percentage accuracy degradation after attacks compared to the original
254
      accuracy (GAP).
255   Results. The results are reported in Table 1. Further details, including standard deviations, can be
256   found in Appendix D. In addition, we incorporate additional GNN architectures, such as GraphSAGE,
257   in our experiments to evaluate the robustness against adversarial attacks. The time costs are provided
258   in Appendix L. The results of Deepseek Liu et al. (2024a) are shown in Table 20. Lastly, we conducted
259   PGD and PRBCD attacks on the Cora, Citeseer and Pubmed and found that the performance of large
260
      language models remained consistent against both attacks. The detailed data and conclusions are
      provided in Appendix J. From these results, we have the following observations.

268   by 3.9%. Moreover, we observe that fine-tuning enhances the robustness of LLMs, as demonstrated by

453   4.5    A NALYSIS FOR S TRUCTURAL ATTACK
454
455
      Since Sections 4.1 and 4.3 have shown the robustness of Graph-LLMs against the structure attack,
      we conduct analysis from the following perspectives to explore the reasons behind such robustness.
456
457   t-SNE visualization. First, we examine t-SNE visualizations of various initial features and find that
458   initial features generated by language models are more distinguishable in categories compared to
459   traditional features as shown in Fig. 4.
460   DBI. To further evaluate the separability of input features, we use the Davies-Bouldin Index
461   (DBI) Davies & Bouldin (1979), as shown in Table 7. The DBI score represents the average
462   similarity measure between each cluster and its most similar cluster, with lower scores indicating
463   better clustering quality. A score of zero is ideal, signifying optimal clustering. We find that initial
464   LLM features have lower initial DBI scores. We further examine the DBI of embeddings both before
465   and after the attack, and the differences therein. Notably, embeddings from larger models exhibit a
466   smaller decrease in DBI scores post-attack, as indicated by the DBI Diff in the Table 7.
467   Homophily. Additionally, we analyze the homophily of the Cora dataset before and after attacks, as
468   shown in Table 7. It discovers that the pre-attack Cora dataset has a homophily of 0.81, and after the
469   attack, Cora with shallow features exhibits lower homophily.
470
      Based on the above results, we find that robustness is strongly positively correlated with the quality
471   of features, indicating that higher distinguishability of features leads to stronger robustness and
472   higher homophily after attacks. This could be attributed to the richer information present in features
473   generated by pre-trained language models, resulting in higher distinguishability in clustering. The
474   higher the quality of the features, the less the model depends on the structure. Therefore, high-quality
475   features of LLMs can be more robust against structural attacks.

518   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
519
520   This work introduces a comprehensive benchmark for exploring the potential of LLMs in context of
521   adversarial attacks on graphs. Specifically, we investigate the robustness against graph structural and
522   textual attacks in two dimensions: LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. Through extensive
523   experiments, we find that, compared to shallow models, both LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-
524
      Predictors offer superior robustness against structural and textual attacks. Despite these promising
      results, several critical challenges and research directions remain worthy of future investigation.
525
526   Rethinking Textual Attack. Based on the observations above, we realize that textual attacks can
527   significantly affect the prediction of individual samples. However, when GCN serves as the victim
528   model, the incorporation of neighbor information helps mitigate these perturbations, significantly
529   reducing the attack’s effectiveness. From an attack perspective, weakening the resistance of GCN-
530
      based victim models is crucial, particularly when targeting stronger Graph-LLMs.
531   Combining Textual and Structural Attack on Graphs. To enhance attack capabilities, a combined
532   framework that perturbs both text attributes and graph structure is needed. However, challenges such
533   as integrating textual and structural attacks to improve attack efficiency remain unsolved. In this study,
534   we provide preliminary results in the Appendix G. Our experiment shows that adding additional
535   textual perturbations on top of structural perturbations can further degrade model performance.
536   Rethinking Graph-LLMs. From the results in Table 1, we can conclude that the angle-optimized
537   Angle-LLaMA, which is more suitable for text encoding, exhibits better robustness against adversarial
538   attacks compared to LLaMA. This phenomenon may inspire us to design better Graph-LLMs for text
539   attribute encoding. Finally, we can use LLMs to perform attacks on graphs by generating harmful
      structures and text attributes.

      A     F ORMULATIONS AND BACKGROUND
757
758
      We begin by providing preliminaries on graph neural networks, and then formalize the graph adver-
759   sarial attacks. Finally, we introduce the developments in large language models on graphs.
760
761   Notations. We define a graph as G = (V, E), where V denotes the node set and E represents the
762
      edge set. We employ X ∈ RN ×d to denote the node feature matrix, where N is the number of nodes
      and d is the dimension of the node features. Furthermore, we use the matrix A ∈ RN ×N to signify
763
      the adjacency matrix of G. Finally, the graph data can be denoted as G = (A, X).
764
765   Graph Neural Networks. GCN Kipf & Welling (2016) is one of the most representative models of
766   GNNs, utilizing aggregation and transformation operations to model graph data. Unlike GCN, which
767   treats all neighbors equally, GAT Veličković et al. (2017) assigns different weights to different nodes
768
      within a neighborhood during aggregation.
769   Graph Adversarial Attacks. In the context of G = (A, X) and a subset Vm ⊆ V comprising victim
770   nodes, where yi signifies the label for node i, the attacker’s objective is to discern a perturbed graph
771   denoted as G̃ = (Ã, X̃). The primary goal is to minimize the attack objective Lattack .
772                           X
773
        min Lattack fθ (G̃) =              ℓattack fθ∗ (G̃)i , yi s.t., θ∗ = arg minθ Ltrain fθ Ĝ , (1)
                                      i∈Vm
774   where fθ indicates the model function of GNN, Lattack represents the loss function for attacks, one
775
      option is to set Lattack = −L, and Ĝ can be either G or G̃. Here, G̃ is chosen from a constrained
776   domain Ψ(G). Given a fixed perturbation budget D, a typical constraint for Ψ(G) can be expressed
777
      as ∥Ã − A∥0 + ∥X̃ − X∥0 ≤ D. This constraint implies that the perturbations introduced in the
778
      adjacency matrix Ã and node feature matrix X̃ should be limited, and their combined L0 should not
779   exceed the specified budget D.
780
781   While graph adversarial attacks can perturb either node features or graph structures, the complexity
782
      of structural information has led the majority of existing adversarial attacks on graph data to focus on
      modifying graph structure, particularly through actions such as adding, deleting, or rewiring edges Jin
783
      et al. (2020); Madry et al. (2017); Geisler et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2019); Chang et al. (2020); Ma
784
      et al. (2019); Entezari et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021). For example, the PGD
785   attack Madry et al. (2017) uses edge perturbation to overcome the challenge of attacking discrete
786   graph structures via first-order optimization. In contrast, the PRBCD attack Geisler et al. (2021)
787   addresses the high cost of adversarial attacks on large graphs with a sparsity-aware optimization
788   approach.
789
      On one hand, we explore the robustness against structural attacks. On the other hand, instead of
790
      targeting continuous features as in existing feature attack works, we adopt a direct approach by
791   employing textual attacks to evaluate robustness, which remains a relatively unexplored direction.
792
793   Textual Attack. For tasks on TAGs, the raw inputs are in text format and it can be hard for attackers
794
      to manipulate the encoded features directly, which makes the traditional feature attacking on graphs
      less practical. Therefore, the textual attack is used in this study to evaluate the robustness of LLMs
795
      enhanced graph features. Textual attacks can be performed on different levels like character level or
796
      sentence level according to the target to be perturbed. In this work, we focus on word-level attacks,
797   which can be defined as follows.
798
799   Word-level attacks. Given a classifier f that predicts labels y ∈ Y , the input X is defined in a
800
      categorical space and each input is a sequence of n words x1 , x2 , · · · , xn . Each word xi has a limited
      amount of substitution candidates, denoted as S(x). To keep the perturbation as unnoticeable as
801
      possible, a constraint, usually L1 or L2 distance is applied. Finally, to fool classifier f to the largest
802
      extent, the following objective is maximized:
803
804                                          arg maxx′ ∈S(x) L(f (x′ ), y)
                                                                                                              (2)
805                                           s.t., ||x′ − x||2 < ϵ,
806   where L is the loss function between original prediction y and prediction f (x′ ) after perturbation.
807   ϵ is the budget of the perturbation, which keeps the original and the perturbed sample as close as
808   possible. As the loss function above is maximized, we will get a perturbed sample x′ that makes the
809   classifier f generate a prediction far from the original output. For example, SemAttack Wang et al.
      (2022a) generates natural adversarial text by employing various semantic perturbation functions.

810
      Large Language Models on Graphs. In recent years, remarkable progress has been achieved
811   in the field of Large Language Models (LLMs), with notable contributions from transformative
812   architectures such as Transformers Reimers & Gurevych (2019), BERT Devlin et al. (2018), Sentence-
813   BERT (SBert) Reimers & Gurevych (2019) E5 Wang et al. (2022b), GPT Radford et al. (2018),
814   LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) and their variants. These LLMs can be applied to graph-related tasks.
815   The collaboration between Large Language Models and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) can be
816   mutually advantageous, leading to improved graph learning. The two most popular paradigms for
817   applying LLMs to graphs are LLMs-as-Enhancers and LLMs-as-Predictors. LLM-as-Enhancers
818   aim to enhance the quality of node features through the assistance of LLMs. TAPE He et al. (2023)
819
      is a groundbreaking example of explanation-based enhancement, encouraging LLms to produce
      explanations and pseudo-labels for the augmentation of textual attributes. KEA Chen et al. (2024)
820
      instructs LLMs to produce a compilation of knowledge entities, complete with text descriptions,
821
      and encodes them using fine-tuned pre-trained language models (PLMs). GLEM Zhao et al. (2022)
822   considers pseudo labels generated by both PLMs and GNNs and incorporates them into a variational
823   EM framework. The fundamental concept of the LLMs-as-Predictors are to leverage LLMs for
824   making predictions in graph-related tasks. InstructGLM Ye et al. (2023) formulates a set of scalable
825   prompts grounded in the maximum hop level and fine-tunes LLMs to output predicted labels directly.
826   GraphLLM Chai et al. (2023) derives the graph-enhanced prefix from the graph representation. This
827   method boosts the LLM’s capability in conducting graph reasoning tasks by graph-enhanced prefix
828   tuning. The GraphGPT Tang et al. (2023) framework aligns LLMs with graph structural knowledge
829   using a paradigm of graph instruction tuning.
