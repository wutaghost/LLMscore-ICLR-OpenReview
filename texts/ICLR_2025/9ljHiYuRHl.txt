009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this work, we investigate the causal reasoning abilities of large language mod-
012            els (LLMs) through the representative problem of inferring causal relationships
013            from narratives. We find that even state of the art language models rely heavily on
014            unreliable shortcuts, both in terms of the narrative presentation and their paramet-
015            ric knowledge. For example, LLMs tend to determine causal relationships based
016            on the topological ordering of events (i.e., earlier events cause later ones), result-
017            ing in lower performance whenever events are not narrated in their exact causal
018            order. Similarly, we demonstrate that LLMs struggle with long-term causal rea-
019            soning — they often fail when the narratives are longer and contain many events.
020
               As an additional failure mode, we show LLMs appear to heavily rely on their
               parametric knowledge at the expense of reasoning over the provided narrative.
021
               This degrades their abilities whenever the narrative opposes parametric knowl-
022
               edge. We extensively validate these failure modes through carefully controlled
023            synthetic experiments, as well as evaluations on real-world narratives. Finally, we
024            observe that explicitly generating a causal graph generally improves performance
025            while naive chain-of-thought is ineffective. Collectively, our results distill precise
026            failure modes of current state-of-the art models and can pave the way for future
027            techniques to enhance causal reasoning in LLMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Causal reasoning is a core component of intelligence and decision-making, enabling an agent to
      move beyond associations (i.e., events that are observed to occur together) to determine the con-
034
      sequences of their actions (i.e., interventions) and answering what-if questions (counterfactuals).
035
      Causal models have been widely studied in machine learning and artificial intelligence (Peters et al.,
036   2017; Pearl, 2009) as well as in the context of human cognition (Waldmann, 2017). The advent of
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) has led to opportunities for leveraging large-scale textual data for
038   improving causal inference (Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023).
039
      While many works have shown that LLMs are capable of memorizing and recalling causal knowl-
040
      edge, they can fail at reliably leveraging that knowledge for reasoning (Jin et al., 2023; Zečević et al.,
041
      2023). For example, when reading a news article or a story, understanding why a particular event
042   occurred requires reasoning over the causal structure of the events implied by the given narrative.
043   Similarly, determining which events in the narrative will be affected by certain actions requires rea-
044   soning about which events are causally downstream of that action. In such cases, simply recalling
045   parametric causal knowledge (that some event typically causes another event) may not suffice as the
046   agent must leverage the causal structure inherent to the specific narrative at hand.
047
      In this work, we aim to understand the causal reasoning abilities of LLMs from textual narra-
048   tives. We consider settings where there is an (unknown) underlying causal chain graph of the form
049   V1 → V2 → . . . → VN , where each node Vi has some semantic meaning (e.g., smoking), that is
050   verbalized in the form of a (realistic) narrative S. For a given narrative S, we consider two kinds
051   of causal reasoning tasks: (1) Does Vi have a causal effect on Vj ? and (2) Given the node iden-
052   tities (V1 , . . . VN ), construct a causal chain graph faithful to the narrative. While these tasks do
053   not encompass all aspects of causal reasoning (e.g., counterfactual reasoning), they are important
      primitives for successful causal reasoning.

054
      Our primary contribution is to shed light on the limitations and unreliable shortcuts that LLMs use
055   for these two causal reasoning tasks. We focus on the following failure modes. Firstly, we show that
056   LLMs heavily rely on the order in which the causal relationships are verbalized in the narrative. We
057   observe that when the narrative is constructed in the reverse topological order of the causal chain
058   (i.e., edge Vi → Vi+1 is narrated before Vi−1 → Vi ), the performance of the LLM suffers as it
059   often assigns the cause to an earlier event and the effect to a later event in the narrative. Secondly,
060   we show that LLMs use their parametric causal knowledge (i.e., if an event typically causes another
061   event) as a shortcut to answer causal questions. Thus, when the cause-and-effect pairs implied
062   by the narrative conflict with the parametric knowledge, the LLM often ignores the specifics of
063
      the narrative and defaults to its parametric knowledge. This occurs even when the narratives are
      written to explicitly disagree with parametric knowledge and the LLM is prompted to ignore outside
064
      information. Thirdly, we find that LLMs fail more often as the narratives become longer.
065
066   Perhaps surprisingly, we also find that the LLM’s answers to cause-and-effect queries, including
067   with Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) and In-Context Learning, do not have a high degree
068   of consistency with the causal graph that the LLM extracts from the narrative. On the other hand,
069
      we observe that just using the extracted graph to make decisions often seems to alleviate the above
      failure modes. This finding is notable as it suggests that the specific formatting of the causal graph
070
      extraction task can more reliably elicit the language model’s causal reasoning capabilities relative to
071
      the baseline of CoT prompting.
072
073   We first validate our findings through a series of carefully controlled experiments with synthetic
074   causal events and narratives (Sec. 3). We use the LLM to generate a causal chain of events that form
075
      the ground truth causal graph. We then prompt the LLM to generate a narrative-based on that causal
      graph. Next, we conduct experiments on narratives generated from real-world causal chain graphs
076
      (Sec. 4) extracted from CauseNet (Heindorf et al., 2020), a dataset of (claimed) causal relationships
077
      between real-world concepts. We generate narratives for these chain graphs using the LLM as well
078   as from sentences in CauseNet, ensuring that the narrative as a whole remains coherent.
079
080   Leveraging the LLM to verbalize causal relationships leads to more natural-sounding narratives
081
      (which is difficult with formulaic templates). Depending on the specific causal relationship, causal-
      ity might be indicated using a phrase like ‘causes’, ‘leads to’, ‘resulted in’, etc. We focus on simple
082
      chain graphs to isolate the simplest cases of the failure modes, demonstrating that the LLMs take
083
      unreliable shortcuts on these simple graphs, both when the narratives are generated by the LLM
084   itself as well as narratives with real-world sentences.
085
086   Taken together, our work makes the following contributions:
087
            1. We demonstrate that even state-of-the-art large language models are unreliable at extracting
088
               causal relationships from realistic narratives expressing simple chain graphs.
089
090         2. Focusing on two key aspects of causal reasoning, we distill concrete failure modes to ac-
091            count for this unreliability: reliance on positional shortcuts, parametric knowledge, and
092
               narrative length.
093         3. We investigate the impact of various prompting strategies on reasoning ability, finding that
094            incorporating the estimated causal graph structure is most helpful.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      Reasoning in Large Language Models Recently, there has been significant interest in evaluating
      and improving the reasoning capabilities of large language models. Prior works have examined
100
      reasoning in diverse settings including mathematics (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021),
101
      social interactions (Sap et al., 2019),and common-sense tasks (Zellers et al., 2019). Through this
102   extensive investigation, large language models have demonstrated unreliable reasoning capabilities,
103   often failing unexpectedly on relatively straightforward queries (Wan et al., 2024; Nezhurina et al.,
104   2024). Extensive prior work has also studied methods for improving the reasoning capabilities of
105   large language models such as by eliciting step-by-step explanations (Wei et al., 2023), finetuning
106   on reasoning traces (Zelikman et al., 2022), and training models to find and correct errors (Kumar
107   et al., 2024). In this work, we perform a comprehensive study of the causal reasoning capabilities of
      large-language models, identifying the existence of key failure modes in state-of-the-art models.

      Causal Reasoning in Large Language Models Prior works have also examined the causal ca-
      pabilities of large-language models. Jin et al. (2023) develops a benchmark for testing the causal
121
      reasoning in LLMs given causal graphs, finding that language models can struggle with the task.
122
      However, the settings examined in Jin et al. (2023) require computations based on probabilities
123   which is known to be challenging for LLMs. Zečević et al. (2023) hypothesizes that large language
124   model may only be “imitating” causal reasoning abilities from their pretraining corpus. Their work
125   additionally investigates what they call “natural word chains,” or how well the LLM is able to accu-
126   rately identify causation of events when they are linked together in chain graphs. Our paper builds
127   on this by performing various tests on narratives that correspond to graphs. Kıcıman et al. (2024)
128   shows that LLMs have strong abilities to generate causal texts. We make use of this generation for
129   narrative creation in our synthetic experiments. Tan et al. (2022) shows the capability of a neural
130   network trained on news data to label causal structures in individual sentences.
131
132   Parametric Knowledge Conflicts Large language models have been shown to memorize factual
133   knowledge present in their pretraining corpora, resulting in parametric knowledge (Petroni et al.,
134
      2019; Jiang et al., 2020). Prior works, however, have observed that language models can often
      fail to use information provided in their context as a result of such memorized knowledge (Krishna
135
      et al., 2021; Longpre et al., 2022). This has been identified as a significant hurdle in the reliability of
136
      summarization and retrieval augmented-based systems (Rehman et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024). Xie
137   et al. (2024) finds that the coherence of provided context can control the extent to which parametric
138   knowledge is over-utilized. In this work, we examine the distinct setting of causal reasoning and
139   demonstrate that it can also be harmed by over-reliance on parametric knowledge.

      3.1   DATA G ENERATION P ROCESS
145
      In our synthetic experiments, we use two leading LLMs: OpenAI’s GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) and
146   Anthropic’s Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024). The purpose of our synthetic setup is to carefully
147   control the conditions under which the LLMs are tested. In terms of the general setup of our fully
148   synthetic experiments, as we summarize in Figure 1, we first use the LLM to generate events (which
149   are real world phenomena like rain or plants growing). Then these events are linked together into
150   a chain graph G that acts as the causal ground truth. The LLM is given G and asked to create a
151   narrative that stays faithful to the causal relationships in G.
152   Providing only the narrative as input (and not G), we then ask the LLM to find G′ , the prediction
153   of the underlying causal structure expressed by the narrative. Next, a series of causal questions is
154   created by randomly sampling 10 tuples of events from G and asking the LLM whether an event
155   in the tuple causes the other based on the narrative and/or G′ . We also explore many mediums of
156   prompting including Chain of Thought (COT) ((Wei et al., 2023)) and In-Context Learning ((Dong
157   et al., 2024)) (prompts are in Appendix A and the supplement).
158

253   3.3   PARAMETRIC K NOWLEDGE C ONFLICT
254
255
      We also find that LLMs tend to rely on parametric knowledge when it is present, and can fail when
      narratives run counter to their parametric knowledge. To test this, instead of picking random events,
256
      we have to draw out the LLM’s pre-existing parametric knowledge. To do so, we prompt the LLM
257
      to pick a series of events such that each event has some relation to the subsequent event–either the
258   event is Causal to the next event (e.g., disease causes shorter lives) or the event is Anti-Causal (e.g.,
259   disease causes longer lives). Let’s call this graph of parametric knowledge P . We then take the
260   odd indexed events (1st, 3rd etc) from P and place them in the first half of the causal ground truth
261   graph G and the even indexed events (2nd, 4th etc) from P in the second half of G. As we can see
262   in the example illustration in Figure 3, each node in the first half of the causal graph G now has a
263   parametric relation to a specific node in the second half of G. For example we might know that node
264   1 is Anti-Causal to node 2 from parametric knowledge. Thus when we make the causal ground truth
265   graph (1 → 3 → 2), create a narrative from it, and then ask the LLM if node 1 causes 2 based on
266
      the narrative, it should say yes even though that disagrees with its parametric knowledge. After the
      ground truth graph is created, we generate the narrative in the Forward topological orientation to
267
      avoid confounding failure modes.
268
269   We find that, in synthetic experiments, this kind of occurrence is generally rare and the LLM finds
      the correct causal relation generally only when that relation agrees with its parametric knowledge.

314   3.4   FAILURE OF L ONG -T ERM C AUSAL R EASONING
315
316
      The performance of LLMs tends to decay as the size of the narrative and the number of events in
317
      the narrative increases. As we can see in Figures 2 and 3, generally all prompts that solely rely on
318   the narrative without the extracted graph to answer causal questions have a performance that decays
319   with the number of events in the narrative (chain size). The exception to this occurs in the Reverse
320   topological orientation in 2 as the accuracy is psuedo-random at all chain sizes. This finding is
321   supported by the well known phenomenon that LLMs often fail to reason about longer form contexts
322   especially as they “get lost in the middle.” (Liu et al., 2023). What perhaps is a surprising result that
323   is the extracted graph tends to maintain a consistent level of accuracy for causal answering across
      narrative sizes even when only using the narrative in the prompt leads to severe dips in accuracy.

339

353   the following is the generated narrative for the causal chain fatigue → accidents → injury:
354

      5     D ISCUSSION
487
488
489   Our work takes some initial strides towards examining the reasons behind the success and failure
490   of LLMs to reason causally on narratives that express a chain of causal events. We focus on two
491
      questions of key importance in causality: (1) Does one event cause another? and (2) Can the LLM
      extract the causal graph from the narrative.
492
493   We find three significant failure modes of LLM reasoning by conducting experiments in carefully
494   controlled synthetic, semi-synthetic and real-world settings:
495
496         1. Topological Ordering: LLMs tend to perform well when the ordering of events in the nar-
497            ratives matches that of the ordering of the underlying graph. For example if B is mentioned
498            first in the narrative and then A, then the LLM would would expect B → A even if A → B
499            is the truly underlying causal graph. As such, performance breaks down when the ordering
500
               of events in the narratives does not match that of the underlying graph.
501
            2. Parametric Knowledge: LLMs rely on their parametric pre-training knowledge as a shortcut
502
               to infer causal relations. When the narrative suggests one causal relation (e.g., A → B) and
503
               the parametric knowledge suggests another (B → A), the LLM will often answer using its
504            parametric knowledge, ignoring the narrative.
505
506         3. Narrative Size: LLM accuracy degrades as the chain length increasing, resulting in poor
507            reasoning on longer narratives.
508
509   We also find that asking the LLM to extract what it predicts to be the underlying graph from the
510   narrative, and using just that extracted graph or the extracted graph in concert with the narrative to
511   answer can lead to mitigation of these failure modes.
512
      Our work builds upon past work that examines causal reasoning in LLMs. Various works includ-
513
      ing Jin et al. (2023) suggest that LLMs may have difficulty performing causal reasoning in tasks
514   involving numerical probabilistic computations. Previous work by Zečević et al. (2023) conjectured
515   that LLMs simply imitate causal relations from their training data. Our work aims to assess the
516   more colloquial kind of causal reasoning from textual narratives (not just on individual sentences)
517   that is required to understand why certain events occurred and how they would impacted by certain
518   interventions. Like previous works, we find a strong reliance on pre-training knowledge. However,
519   we also suggest a way to mitigate this by using the LLM to extract the entire causal graph from the
520   narrative at once. When doing this, the graph remains generally accurate even in the presence of
521   conflicts with parametric pre-training knowledge.
522
523
      5.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
524
525
      One limitation of our work is that there are other forms of causal reasoning that we did not test for
526
      in the narratives. This motivates many potential directions for future work. For example, it could be
527
      interesting to ask the LLM to reason about counterfactual cases. Another limitation of our work is
528   that we only deal with chain graphs. An interesting direction for future work would be to generate
529   narratives from complex graph structures. This could potentially yield novel failure modes and
530   insights into how LLMs reason. Another limitation of our work is the amount of insight we provide
531   behind the phenomenon we observe. When we notice the graphs doing better than the prompts that
532   only use the narrative itself, one conjecture is that this is because the LLM is looking at the entire
533   narrative and reasoning about it to make the extracted graph–however we don’t formally prove this.
534   Future work could theoretically or empirically examine explanations for such LLM behavior and
535   others noted in the paper.
536   Our analysis also has implications for algorithmic interventions to improve causal reasoning. The
537   failure modes we identify in this paper could inform the design of targeted synthetic tasks to use in
538   finetuning for improved causal reasoning. Additionally, our findings on the benefits of extracting a
539   causal graph can inform prompt engineering efforts to elicit reliable causal reasoning from language
      models. We believe investigating both directions represents an exciting direction for future works.

      B.1     P ROMPT TEMPLATES FOR NARRATIVE GENERATION
774
      Recall that we have a ground truth causal chain graph of the form V1 → V2 → . . . → VN from
775   CauseNet that we need to verbalize into a coherent narrative. For the semi-synthetic narratives,
776   we use the LLM (GPT-4o) to do so one edge at a time, while ensuring that the newly verbalized
777   edge logically follows the previous one. The following is the prompt template for generating the
778   narratives in the topological order of the graph:
779
780            Output a short narrative (use one or two sentences) that expresses the causal link
781            [Vi → Vi+1 ] and logically follows this narrative:
782            { Narrative for the previous edge Vi−1 → Vi }.
783            Ensure that the combined sentences convey the causal chain [Vi−1 → Vi → Vi+1 ]
784            and that the words [Vi , Vi+1 ] are present. Only output the newly generated narra-
785            tive.
786
      Similarly, we generate narratives in the reverse topological order of the graph by verbalizing edges
787
      in the reverse direction with the following prompt template:
788
789            Output a short narrative (use one or two sentences) that expresses the causal link
790            [Vi → Vi+1 ] and logically follows this narrative:
791            { Narrative for the previous edge Vi+1 → Vi+2 }.
792            Ensure that the combined sentences convey the causal chain [Vi → Vi+1 → Vi+2 ]
793            and that the words [Vi , Vi+1 ] are present. Only output the newly generated narra-
794            tive.
795
796   For generating real-world narratives, for each edge Vi → Vj , we use the set of sentences from
797   CauseNet. Note that each edge in CauseNet is linked to multiple sentences from various sources.
798
      Picking a sentence for each edge at random and concatenating them does not always lead to sen-
      sible narratives. To improve the quality of narratives, we use the following prompt to concatenate
799
      sentences for adjacent edges:
800
801            Consider the following sentences.
802            { Sentence for edge Vi → Vi+1 }. { Sentence for edge Vi+1 → Vi+2 }.
803
               Do the sentences logically follow each other and express the causal chain [Vi →
804
               Vi+1 → Vi+2 ]? Answer with Yes or No.
805
806   For verbalizing narratives in the topological order, for a given graph V1 → V2 → . . . → VN , we only
807   use sentences such that the above prompt returns Yes for every pair of adjacent edges Vi → Vi+1 →
808   Vi+2 . This ensures that the narrative as a whole remains coherent and conveys the entire causal
809   chain graph. We use a similar prompting strategy to verbalize narratives in the reverse topological
      order.
