                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               Dataset distillation generates a small synthetic dataset on which a model is trained
               to achieve performance comparable to that obtained on a complete dataset. Current
016
               state-of-the-art methods primarily focus on Trajectory Matching (TM), which
017
               optimizes the synthetic dataset by matching its training trajectory with that from
018            the real dataset. Due to convergence issues and numerical stability, it is impractical
019            to match the entire trajectory in one go; typically, a segment is sampled for matching
020            at each iteration. However, previous TM-based methods overlook the potential
021            interactions between matching different segments, particularly the presence of
022            negative correlations. To study this problem, we conduct a quantitative analysis
023            of the correlation between matching different segments and discover varying
024            degrees of negative correlation depending on the image per class (IPC). Such
025            negative correlation could lead to an increase in accumulated trajectory error
026
               and transform trajectory matching into a continual learning paradigm, potentially
               causing catastrophic forgetting. To tackle this issue, we propose a concurrent
027
               learning-based trajectory matching that simultaneously matches multiple segments.
028
               Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method consistently surpasses previous
029            TM-based methods on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny ImageNet, and ImageNet-1K.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The increasing scale of data has significantly enhanced the performance of neural networks (Brown
035
      et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). However, it remains an unresolved question
036   whether networks trained on much smaller datasets can achieve similar success. To address this
037   question, Dataset Distillation (DD) (Wang et al., 2018) has emerged as a prominent research area
038   due to its straightforward concept of distilling large datasets into smaller synthetic ones, while still
039   maintaining comparable model performance. (Zhao et al., 2021; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Wang
040   et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). Among various data distillation methods (Zhao
041   et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhao & Bilen, 2023), Trajectory Matching (TM)-
042   based methods (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023) achieve excellent
043   and even lossless results (Guo et al., 2023) by ensuring that the training trajectories on synthetic
044
      dataset closely match those of the full dataset. During the matching process, the complete training
      trajectory is divided into several segments for individual matching to ensure training stability and
045
      convergence (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023).
046
047   However, this segmented matching scheme overlooks a critical issue: Matching different segments
048   may be negatively correlated. This issue may bring an obstacle in the optimization because matching
049   one segment can significantly increase the matching loss of other segments.
050   In this paper, we conduct an in-depth study on the correlation between different segments of trajectory
051   matching. Specifically, we theoretically analyze how negative correlation affects the accumulated
052   trajectory matching error (Du et al., 2023), and then we conduct a series of experiments to verify
053   that negative correlations do exist. We monitor the matching loss of other epochs when one epoch
      is selected for matching. By calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient between the loss of the

      To understand this observation, we formalize the correlation and sampling strategy in the trajectory
079
      matching into a continual learning problem (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Chen & Liu, 2018; Kudithipudi
080
      et al., 2022), where matching different segments of the complete trajectory without strict correlation
081   can lead to catastrophic forgetting (McClelland et al., 1995; McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). This makes
082   the Existing TM-based methods unlikely to achieve a training trajectory on synthetic data that closely
083   resembles the real expert trajectory.
084
      To overcome this issue, we develop a Concurrent Training-based Trajectory Matching(ConTra)
085
      method. In the continual learning community, it is commonly believed that simultaneous multi-
086
      task learning (MTL) achieves optimal results when dealing with multiple negatively correlated
087   tasks, representing an upper bound. Conversely, naive sequential learning (SL) is considered as a
088   lower bound (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Shin et al., 2017; Schwarz et al., 2018). Therefore, instead
089   of sampling a specific part from the complete trajectory to match each time as naive sequential
090   learning (SL), we concurrently match those negatively correlated parts with multi-task learning
091   (MTL). Furthermore, considering that different IPCs have varying information capacities, we employ
092   a curriculum learning approach (Bengio et al., 2009a; Zhang et al., 2024) to generate the expert
093   trajectory. Our experiments demonstrate that ConTra can consistently outperform other trajectory
094   matching methods on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny ImageNet, and ImageNet-1K.
095
096   Contributions. (1) We theoretically analyze how the negative correlations affect the accumulated
097   trajectory error and systematically quantify the correlation between matching different parts of a
098   complete trajectory under various IPCs. (2) We explicitly highlight the inherent continual learning
099   nature and the issue of catastrophic forgetting and based on this perspective, propose a new matching
100   strategy—concurrent training—from the upper bound of continual learning, MTL.(3) We validate the
101   effectiveness of our approach through extensive experiments.

106   (Wang et al., 2018) firstly formalized the concept of Dataset Distillation as a bi-level optimization
107   problem, with the goal of distilling large-scale datasets into smaller synthetic ones while preserving
      comparable test performance. Dataset distillation can primarily be divided into following categories:

      Gradient matching. Zhao et al. (2021) pioneered the gradient matching approach to Dataset
109   Condensation (DC), which optimizes the synthetic data by minimizing difference between model
110   gradients trained with a large training set and with the synthetic dataset. Kim et al. (2022) and Zhang
111   et al. (2023) improved gradient matching by focusing on data regularity characteristics and model
112   augmentation. MTT (Cazenavette et al., 2022) introduced a long-range matching strategy. FTD (Du
113   et al., 2023) leveraged a flatter expert trajectory, and DATM (Guo et al., 2023) firstly achieved lossless
114   condensation and conducted coarse-grained studies on matching early and late parts. PDD (Chen
115   et al., 2023) generates several subsets to capture the entire training dynamics. However, all of the
116   previous works used a segmented matching strategy and there is no detailed analysis of whether the
117
      matching of different segments is correlated.
118
119   Distribution matching. Another line of DD is feature or distribution matching, aiming at
120   synthesizing data that can accurately approximate the distribution of the real training data(Wang
121   et al., 2022; Zhao & Bilen, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). They can only continually approximate lossless
122
      test accuracy and cannot achieve it with relatively small IPCs due to their spirit akin to coreset
      selection (Sener & Savarese, 2018; Welling, 2009)
123
124
125   Kernel-based methods. KIP (Nguyen et al., 2020), the first Kernel-based method, simplified
126   dataset distillation into a single-level optimization problem through kernel ridge-regression with
127
      NTK (Lee et al., 2019). The computation cost of KIP scales quadratically in the number of pixels
      for convolutional kernels. Although subsequent studies (Zhou et al., 2022; Loo et al., 2023) have
128
      significantly reduced training costs, they still struggle to scale up to larger datasets and IPCs.

      4     I NCONSISTENT C ORRELATIONS B ETWEEN S EGMENTS M ATCHING
163
164
      Previous TM-based methods calculate the matching loss L by sampling a segment from the expert
165   trajectory in each iteration. This paradigm assumes that if each segment of the trajectory is well-
166   matched, the complete trajectory will also be matched accurately. However, this assumption is
167   questionable. We find that if negative correlation exists between different segments, reducing the
168   matching loss of a single segment may cause the complete trajectory to deviate from the real trajectory.
169   In this section, we begin by demonstrating this issue from the perspective of accumulated trajectory
170   error as introduced in (Du et al., 2023). We then empirically verify that negative correlations do exist
171   prevalently in commonly used datasets.

256   This pattern highlights a significant issue of matching later segments while earlier segments are

260   previously well-matched early part from the real trajectory.
261
      As the IPC increases, the negatively correlated parts gradually shift from the lower triangular area to
262   the upper triangular area. When IPC reaches 1000, matching the early part causes an increase in the
263   matching loss of the well-matched late part. Experiments in (Guo et al., 2023) demonstrate that at an
264   IPC of 1000, matching only the late part yields satisfactory outcomes. From a correlation perspective,
265   this is because, at this IPC level, matching the late part is positively correlated with matching the
266   entire trajectory.
267
268   Roles of Training dynamics in the correlation variation. Although neural networks can nearly
269   memorize the entire training set (Zhang et al., 2021), the fitting of samples is a dynamic process.
      In the early epochs of training, those easy patterns (Carlini et al., 2022) dominate the matching


                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      gradient (Arpit et al., 2017). That is to say, conducting gradient matching in the early epochs causes
271   the synthetic data to primarily fit the easy patterns, such as lines and curves. In the late stages of
272   model training, the situation becomes more complex. In particular, the training process does not
273   exclusively focus on fitting hard patterns in later stages; rather, it dynamically adjusts to also refit
274   simpler ones as needed (Arpit et al., 2017; Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018). This dynamic process is
275   controlled by both easy and hard patterns, and it thus contains the information of the entire dataset.
276
      Regarding the variation in correlation with changes in IPC, we speculate that with a small IPC, the
277   synthetic dataset’s limited capacity suffices only to fit simple patterns. Easy patterns learned through
278   matching early segments are likely forgotten when matching later segments, so matching late segments
279   are negatively correlated with early ones. In contrast, a high IPC enables the synthetic dataset to
280   simultaneously fit both simple and complex patterns through complex training dynamics, facilitating
281   lossless distillation as reported in (Guo et al., 2023). With this increased IPC, matching early epochs
282   may result in the loss of information regarding complex patterns learned in later segments, thereby
283   exhibiting a negative correlation with these later epochs.

285   5    C ONCURRENT T RAINING - BASED T RAJECTORY M ATCHING
286
287   Trajectory matching as a continual learning problem. As discussed in Section 4, it is evident
288   that various correlations exist between different segments’ matching, resembling the scenario in
289   continual learning where different tasks exhibit high diversity. In continual learning, when tasks
290   are either uncorrelated or negatively correlated, sequential learning—where tasks are optimized one
291   by one—often leads to the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting. This phenomenon occurs when
292   adaptation to a new task significantly diminishes the ability to perform previous tasks(Wang et al.,
293
      2023). This parallel is precisely what we have observed in trajectory matching, where we regard the
      matching of different segments as separate tasks. According to Eq. 6, our objective is to minimize
294
      cumulative matching errors across different segments, representing the aggregated performance
295
      across all tasks. Previous strategies failed to consider the potential for catastrophic forgetting by
296   employing a naive sequential learning (SL), which minimizes the performance of each task, i.e., δ,
297   sequentially. However, SL is considered the least effective learning paradigm, thereby serving as a
298   lower bound in continual learning (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Shin et al., 2017; Schwarz et al., 2018).
299
300   Concurrent training. For multiple negatively correlated tasks, compared to naive SL, a simple
301   yet effective method to significantly enhance the aggregated performance of these tasks is to learn
302   them simultaneously. This approach, known as multi-task learning (MTL) or concurrent training, is
303   considered the upper bound in continual learning (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Shin et al., 2017; Schwarz
304   et al., 2018).
305   Specifically, suppose a complete expert trajectory τ ∗ comprises T segments. Previous methods
306   typically set an upper bound T + and a lower bound T − , and only one segment within this range
307   {Θ∗T − , · · ·, Θ∗T + } is sampled to match (Guo et al., 2023; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du et al.,
308   2023). Compared to them, in addition to sampling, we match multiple segments within this range
309   simultaneously, and the objective is defined as:

      speculate that this is because, as K increases, the distance R between segments decreases, and Figure

      6.5   C OST ANALYSIS
501
502   We report the training time for various values of K on the NVIDIA H800 GPU (IPC=10) in Table
503   4 right, measured in hours per 1000 iterations. The time cost is approximately proportional to the
504   value of K (number of tasks), but we find that larger K values lead to faster convergence. For
505   example, on CIFAR-10, DATM converges at 4000 iterations, whereas ConTra with K = 5 requires
506   only about 1500 iterations. ConTra does not incur additional GPU memory costs, as we can compute
507   the gradient of different tasks and backpropagate them, separately. Despite ConTra is slower, this
508
      does not affect our core contribution: identifying the negative correlation in trajectory matching when
      matching different segments. Concurrent Training, as a straightforward solution, offers significant
509
      improvements.

      Stability and visualizations. Another disadvantage of negative correlation is that it can cause
514   training to be highly unstable and convergence to be poor. We demonstrate the superior stability of
515   our method in Appendix E.1. In Appendix E.5, we provide visualizations of parts of the synthetic
516   dataset.
517
518   Scalability We can scale up ConTra to ImageNet-1K using TESLA (Cui et al., 2023). TESLA is a
519   plug-in trick that can compute the unrolled gradient in trajectory matching with constant memory
520   complexity. The result is provided in Appendix E.2, which demonstrate that concurrent learning can
521   also yield improvements on large-scale datasets.
522
523   Generalization across other architectures. To the best of our knowledge, cross-architecture
524   generalization from CNNs to Transformer-based models remains an unexplored problem. We study
525   the generalization VIT in Appendix E.4, and we find that trajectory matching is structurally bound;
526   therefore, synthetic datasets distilled from CNNs struggle to achieve good generalization performance
527   on ViTs.
528
529   Downstream task. We also perform experiments on neural architecture search, detailed in Appendix
530   E.3. We implement NAS on CIFAR10 with the search space of 720 ConvNets varying in network
531
      depth, width, activation, normalization, and pooling. The result demonstrates that the synthetic
      datasets distilled by ConTra can perform well in downstream task.

534   7     C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this work, we systematically study the interactions between matching different segments in
537   trajectory matching. We further analyze the potential effect of the negative correlation from the
538   perspectives of accumulated trajectory error and catastrophic forgetting and argue that such correlation
539   cannot be ignored. Based on these analyses, we propose a simple yet effective method, ConTra, and
      validate its effectiveness through extensive experiments.

      B     C URRICULUM L EARNING
782
783
      Zhang et al. (2024) tries to incorporate curriculum learning into the generation of expert trajectories in
      graph condensation task. Inspired by them, we prepare curriculum-based trajectory expert trajectory
784
      on image datasets. The core idea of curriculum learning is to arrange samples from simple to
785
      complex, allowing the model to mimic the human learning process by starting with simple samples
786   and gradually progressing to more complex ones (Bengio et al., 2009b; Krueger & Dayan, 2009).
787
788   In TM-based distillation methods, the size of the IPC indicates the information capacity of the
789
      synthetic dataset. Therefore, when the IPC is small, it is crucial to focus more on the simple samples
      that constitute the majority of the real dataset. We define the learning difficulty of samples based on
790
      the order in which they are correctly classified during the model’s training process on the real dataset.
791
      Samples that are classified correctly earlier are considered easy samples, while those classified
792   correctly later are deemed more complex. After assigning sample difficulty, we sort the entire training
793   set according to sample difficulty. Initially, the training set includes only simple samples; as training
794   progresses, complex samples are incrementally introduced using a linear function. To manage this
795   progression, we use a pacing function h(e) that maps each training epoch e to the proportion of
796   samples selected from the ordered training set. The pacing function h(e) is defined as follows:
797                                                                 e
798                                        h(t) = min(1, λ + (1 − λ) ,                                        (12)
                                                                    γ
799
800   where λ is the initial proportion of the training set, and γ is the threshold of epoch when the full
801   dataset is used. The expert trajectory obtained in this way ensures that early epochs mainly contains
802
      easy patterns. We only use this trick for low IPC experiments, as we find it doesn’t work for IPC
      larger than 10.
