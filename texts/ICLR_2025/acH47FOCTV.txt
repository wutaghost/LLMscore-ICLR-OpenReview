009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Direct Advantage Estimation (DAE) was recently shown to improve sample-
012            efficiency of deep reinforcement learning algorithms. However, DAE assumes full
013            observability of the environment, which may be restrictive in realistic settings. In
014            the present work, we first show that DAE can be extended to partially observable
015            domains with minor modifications. Secondly, we address the increased compu-
016
               tational cost due to the need to approximate the transition probabilities through
               the use of discrete latent dynamics models. Finally, we empirically evaluate the
017
               proposed method using the Arcade Learning Environments, and show that it is
018
               scalable and sample-efficient.

021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Real-world decision-making problems often involve incomplete information, where observations
024   received by the agents are not enough to fully determine the underlying state of the system. For
025   example, a robot navigating a building may only have a local view of its surroundings; a doctor has
026
      to decide the course of treatment for a patient based on a limited set of test results. The Partially
      Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) framework (Kaelbling et al., 1998) provides a
027
      generalization of the fully observable MDP framework (Puterman, 2014) to tackle these problems.
028
029   While reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton and Barto, 2018) paired with deep neural networks (deep
030   RL) has achieved unprecedented results in various domains (Mnih et al., 2015; Berner et al., 2019;
031   Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Wurman et al., 2022), it is known to be challenging to
032
      train and often requires millions or billions of samples (Henderson et al., 2018). Approximating the
      state(-action) value functions (Qπ or V π ) is a crucial part of training deep RL agents. However, these
033
      functions often depend strongly on the policy, making them highly non-stationary and difficult to
034
      learn. Recently, Pan et al. (2022) demonstrated that the advantage function is more stable under policy
035   variations, proposing Direct Advantage Estimation (DAE) to learn the advantage function directly for
036   on-policy settings. DAE demonstrated strong empirical performance, but is restricted to on-policy
037   settings. Later, Pan and Schölkopf (2024) observed that the return of a trajectory can be decomposed
038   into two different advantage functions, which enabled a natural generalization of DAE to off-policy
039   settings (Off-policy DAE). Off-policy DAE was reported to further improve the sample efficiency of
040   DAE; however, the method suffers from significantly increased computational complexity due to the
041   need to learn a high dimensional generative model to approximate the transition probabilities.
042   The present work explores the feasibility of DAE in partially observable domains, and ways to reduce
043   its computational complexity. More specifically, the contributions are:
044
045          • We show that Off-policy DAE can be applied to POMDPs with minor modifications to the
046
               constraints of the objective.
047          • We address the problem of increased computational cost of Off-policy DAE by modeling
048            transitions in a low dimensional embedding space, which circumvents the need to model
049
               high dimensional observations.
050          • We show that truncating trajectories, a common technique for reducing the computational
051            cost of training POMDP agents, can lead to confounding and degrade performance.
052          • We evaluate our method empirically using the Arcade Learning Environment (Bellemare
053            et al., 2013), and perform an extensive ablation study to show the contributions of various
               corrections and hyperparameters.

      In the present work, we consider a discounted POMDP defined by the tuple (S, A, T , Ω, O, r,
057   γ) (Kaelbling et al., 1998), where S is the state space, A is the action space, T (s, a, s′ ) denotes
058   the transition probability from state s into state s′ after taking action a, Ω is the observation space,
059   O(s, o) denotes the probability of observing o ∈ Ω in state s, r(s, a) denotes the reward received by
060   the agent after taking action a in state s, and γ ∈ [0, 1) denotes the discount factor. When the context
061   is clear, we shall simply denote T (s, a, s′ ) by p(s′ |s, a), and O(s, o) by p(o|s). In this work, we shall
062   consider the case where S, A, and Ω are finite. An agent in a POMDP cannot directly observe the
063   states, but only the observations emitted from the state through O. We consider the infinite-horizon
064   discounted setting, where the goal ofPan agent is to find a policy π, which maximizes the expected
                                                 ∞
065
      cumulative reward, i.e., J(π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t r(st , at )].

      3.2   P RACTICAL C ONSIDERATIONS — T RUNCATING S EQUENCES & C ONFOUNDING
245
246   As states are now replaced by histories, we have to process sequences of observations instead of
247   singular states. In modern deep RL, this is typically achieved by using recurrent neural networks
248   (RNNs), such as LSTMs or GRUs (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997; Hausknecht and Stone, 2015;
249   Mnih et al., 2016; Kapturowski et al., 2018; Gruslys et al., 2018; Cho et al., 2014; Hafner et al.,
250   2023). This can be computationally heavy during training when trajectories extend to thousands of
251   steps. Instead, it is common to truncate histories by sampling random segments of trajectories from
252   the replay buffer, and initialize the hidden recurrent state with the first few steps (burn-in) before
253   updating the values (Kapturowski et al., 2018), as demonstrated below:
254
255                 (o0 , a0 , r0 , · · · , ot−k−1 , at−k−1 , rt−k−1 , ot−k , · · ·, ot , at , rt , · · · , ot+n )
256
                     |             {z            } |             {z              } |              {z           }
                               truncated               burn in (initialize RNN state)        value updates

258   Here, we highlight a problem that is commonly overlooked due to confounding (Pearl, 2009).
259   Confounding can bias our estimates when the unobserved variables (the truncated part of the trajectory
260   h0:t−k−1 ) simultaneously influence both the input variables (the remaining part of the trajectory
261   ht−k:t ) and the output variables (the future rewards rt′ for t′ ≥ t). We illustrate this problem with
262   a toy example (see Figure 2). In this environment, the optimal policy is π ∗ (up|o0 ) = p ∈ [0, 1]
263   (arbitrary), and π ∗ (a|o0 , a0 =a, o1 ) = 1 (repeat previous actions). Now, let us consider the case where
264   the behavior policy is the optimal policy with π ∗ (up|o0 ) = 0.5, but the truncation length is 0 (i.e.,
265   memoryless) for the target policy. In this case, we will incorrectly infer that V π (o1 ) = Qπ (o1 , ·) = 1
266
      for any target policy π, since all the collected trajectories receive a reward 1 irrespective of the
      action a1 . This is a classic example of confounding, where the unobserved variable a0 behaves as a
267
      confounder that biases our estimates.

      and the target policy on the same set of variables (i.e., same memory capacity). This then breaks the

      4    E XPERIMENTS
295
296
297   We examine the performance of the proposed method using the Arcade Learning Environment
298   (ALE) (Bellemare et al., 2013), which includes environments with diverse dynamics and various
299
      degrees of partial observability. More specifically, we use the five environments subset (Battle Zone,
      Double Dunk, Name This Game, Phoenix, Qbert) suggested by Aitchison et al. (2023) because it was
300
      found that the learning performance in this subset strongly correlates with the overall performance of
301
      an algorithm. We use the same environment setting as the Dopamine baselines (Castro et al., 2018),
302   which largely follows the protocols proposed by Machado et al. (2018), including the use of sticky
303   actions (repeat previous action with a certain probability) and discarding end-of-life signals. Note
304   that while sticky actions were originally proposed to inject stochasticity into the environments, they
305   also introduce additional partial observability due to its dependency on previous actions.
306
      We evaluate our method using a DQN-like (Mnih et al., 2015) agent with some modifications,
307
      which we briefly summarize below. (1) Recurrent Architecture: We do not use frame-stacking,
308   but simply use an LSTM after the convolutional encoder to process sequences of observations.
309   Aside from image inputs, we also feed previous actions and rewards into the LSTM. (2) DAE
310   objective: We replace the 1-step Q-learning objective with our multi-step DAE objective (Equation 9),
311   and use three separate MLPs on top of the LSTM to model Â, B̂, and V̂ . (3) Discrete Latent
312   Dynamics Model: We use three additional MLPs on top of the LSTM to estimate the next observation
313   embedding x̂t+1 (ht , at , zt ), the immediate reward p(rt |ht , at ), and the prior probability p(zt |ht , at ).
314   (4) Exponential Moving Average: Similar to SPR, we use an exponential moving average of the
315   online network as the target network to generate the next observation embeddings for the dynamics
316   model. This target network is also used to construct smoothly changing target policies and value
317   bootstrapping targets for the DAE objective. (5) Deeper Network: We use the deep residual network
318
      proposed by Espeholt et al. (2018) instead of the shallow three-layer convolutional network, which
      we found to enjoy better scalability and improved sample efficiency. In our implementation, we use
319
320
      a CNN-LSTM backbone with MLP heads on top to model the value functions (Â, B̂, V̂ ) and the
      dynamics (x̂, p(·|h, a)). For more details, we refer the reader to Appendix C.
321
322   In the following experiments, we train the agents for 20 million frames (5 million environment steps
323   due to frame-skipping), and evaluate the agent every 1 million frames by averaging the cumulative
      scores of 50 episodes.

460   Advantage Estimation Estimating the advantage function is an important part of policy optimiza-
461   tion (Kakade and Langford, 2002). Schulman et al. (2015) proposed Generalized Advantage Es-
462   timation (GAE), which utilizes TD(λ) (Sutton, 1988) to perform on-policy multi-step estimates
463   of the advantage function. Wang et al. (2016) proposed dueling network to parametrize Qθ into
464   Vθ + Aθ and showed that it can improve the performance of the original DQN. Tang et al. (2023)
465   proposed VA learning to estimate V and A separately, and showed that it can outperform the dueling
466   architecture. Pan et al. (2022) proposed DAE to perform on-policy multi-step estimation of the
467   advantage function. This was later generalized to the off-policy setting by Pan and Schölkopf (2024).
468
      The present work extends off-policy DAE to partially observable environments and improves its
      computational efficiency.
469
470   POMDP POMDPs provide a general framework for studying decision making with incomplete
471   states (Åström, 1965). In RL, POMDPs are usually solved by first converting them into MDPs either
472   using belief states (Kaelbling et al., 1998) or information vectors (Bertsekas, 2012). In deep RL,
473   partial observability is usually addressed using frame-stacking (Mnih et al., 2015), or by modeling
474   the histories directly (Kapturowski et al., 2018; Gruslys et al., 2018; Hafner et al., 2023; Hausknecht
475   and Stone, 2015; Mnih et al., 2016).
476   Latent Dynamics Model Learning dynamics models in the latent space is a promising approach to
477   model-based RL (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018; Han et al., 2019; Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Hafner et al.,
478   2023; Antonoglou et al., 2021). It is, however, still common to rely on reconstructing observations
479   to learn meaningful latent representations (Anand et al., 2021). In the present work, we combine
480   ideas from self-supervised learning methods (Schwarzer et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020) and the WTA
481   loss (Makansi et al., 2019; Rupprecht et al., 2017) to estimate the transition probabilities purely in
482
      the latent space, and found it to be beneficial.
483   Causality The problem of inferring the effect of an action under partial observability dates at least
484   back to Splawa-Neyman et al. (1990); Rubin (1974), and is a central topic in the study of causal
485   inference (Pearl, 2009; Peters et al., 2017). In RL, these problems have been studied in the bandit
      setting (Bareinboim et al., 2015; Tennenholtz et al., 2021) and the sequential setting (Tennenholtz


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      et al., 2020; Pace et al., 2023). We showed that the confounding problem can also have negative
487   impacts when training with recurrent policies.

490   6   D ISCUSSION
491
492   In the present work, we showed how to extend DAE for POMDPs and addressed the computational
493
      cost issue by using discrete latent dynamics models. Through experiments in the ALE, we demon-
      strated that DAE is sample efficient and scalable, and that the proposed corrections are effective.
494
495   One limitation of our method is the need to approximate the transition probabilities through the use
496   of latent dynamics. This introduces additional hyperparameters (e.g., network architectures of the
497   dynamics model), and renders our method closer to model-based than model-free, although we do
498   not explicitly use the model for rollouts. One direction for future work is to explore model-free
499
      approaches to approximate the constraints. Another limitation is that, while we can partially mitigate
      the problem of confounding caused by using truncated trajectories, our approach is only applicable in
500
      the online setting, where we can control the behavior policy. An important direction is to develop
501
      computationally efficient methods for eliminating the confounding bias for broader settings.

732
      lead to confounding. In the confounding experiment in section 4, the two sampling strategies differ in

733
734   Modern recurrent agents (e.g., R2D2 (Kapturowski et al., 2018), Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2023)) often
735   store recurrent states in the replay buffer during sampling and initialize the RNN states from the
736   replay buffer during training. While, in theory, this can mitigate the confounding bias if the stored
737   recurrent states contain enough information to predict future observables (i.e., the recurrent states are
738
      sufficient statistics), these methods are harder to analyze, as it is difficult to quantify (or measure) the
      quality of the recurrent states. Consequently, we only consider the simplest case (no recurrent states),
739
      and leave it for future work to explore other directions.
