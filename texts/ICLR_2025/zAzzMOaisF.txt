011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            To develop autonomous agents capable of executing complex, multi-step decision-
014            making tasks as specified by humans in natural language, existing reinforce-
015            ment learning approaches typically require expensive labeled datasets or access
016            to real-time experimentation. Moreover, conventional methods often face diffi-
017            culties in generalizing to unseen goals and states, thereby limiting their practical
018            applicability. This paper presents TEDUO, a novel training pipeline for offline
019            language-conditioned policy learning. TEDUO operates on easy-to-obtain, unla-
020
               beled datasets and is suited for the so-called in-the-wild evaluation, wherein the
               agent encounters previously unseen goals and states. To address the challenges
021
               posed by such data and evaluation settings, our method leverages the prior knowl-
022
               edge and instruction-following capabilities of large language models (LLMs) to
023            enhance the fidelity of pre-collected offline data and enable flexible generalization
024            to new goals and states. Empirical results demonstrate that the dual role of LLMs
025            in our framework—as data enhancers and generalizers—facilitates both effective
026            and data-efficient learning of generalizable language-conditioned policies.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Motivation. A central aim of AI research is to develop autonomous agents capable of solving com-
032   plex, multi-step decision-making tasks based on human-provided instructions articulated in natural
033   language. Current approaches, which rely on traditional reinforcement learning (RL) methods, re-
034   quire either vast amounts of data in the form of offline expert demonstrations or data collected from
035   real-time interactions with the environment. Such data is expensive to gather and online experi-
036   mentation may be impractical in many real-world scenarios. Moreover, even with substantial data,
037   RL agents are often constrained to a limited set of previously attained goals, or their performance
038   deteriorates significantly when tested on new goal-reaching tasks (Yang et al., 2023).
039   Problem setting. Given the above, this paper approaches the problem of learning generalizable
040   language-conditioned policies under minimal data requirements. Specifically, we consider a fully
041   offline setup with access to a pre-collected dataset of state-action transitions, D, alongside an un-
042   paired set of natural language commands, G tr . The dataset D consists of triplets (x, a, x′ ), where x
043   and x′ belong to a high-dimensional state space X , and a represents actions within an action space
044   A. The natural language goals g ∈ G tr describe a subset of tasks achievable within the environ-
045
      ment. We posit that such data is often easy to obtain by simply recording agents interact with the
      environment and creating a list of natural language commands corresponding to the tasks typically
046
      performed within that environment. For instance, in household robotics, D may be derived from
047
      random exploration of the environment, while G tr may describe tasks such as “Go and open the
048   window” or “Fetch me a cup of tea.” In the case of personal assistants, D might be collected by
049   recording the daily activities of a human interacting with their mobile device, while G tr would de-
050   scribe goals like “Book a restaurant for 7 PM” or “Send an email to John.” With no assumptions
051   regarding how the offline data has been collected, nor access to the ground-truth state-transition dy-
052   namics or rewards, our aim is to learn a language-conditioned policy, π ∗ , that can determine optimal
053   actions for previously unseen goals g ∈/ G tr and states x ∈
                                                                 / D. For a formal definition of the problem
      setup, please refer to section 2.

070   Challenges. The task of learning π ∗ from D and G tr alone might seem impossible without resorting
071   to human supervision. We can immediately identify the following challenges: C1) Unlabeled data.
072   The dataset D lacks explicit labels linking states x ∈ X to the goals g ∈ G tr . Nor does it include
073   any rewards indicating the optimality of actions in relation to these goals. C2) Limited exploration.
074   We are in an offline setup with our knowledge of the environment dynamics being constrained to
075   the state-action transitions observed in D. C3) Unknown data collection policy. We make no
076   assumptions regarding the optimality of the data collection policy concerning the training or testing
077   goals. The actions in D could be entirely random or generated by policies aimed at solving goals
078
      with an unknown relationship to those in G tr . C4) Generalization to new goals and states. Beyond
      solving goals from G tr and taking optimal actions in previously observed states x ∈ D, we want our
079
      agent to generalize to new states and language commands corresponding to novel goal states.
080
081   Proposed Solution: LLMs to elevate conventional RL. Recent advances in LLMs offer a promis-
082   ing solution to these challenges. LLMs, pre-trained on vast amounts of Internet data, possess the
083   requisite prior knowledge to understand natural language commands and follow simple instructions.
084
      However, while LLMs excel at general language comprehension, their ungrounded knowledge is in-
      sufficient for executing complex, multi-step decision-making tasks in dynamic environments (Finn,
085
      2024; Szot et al., 2024). In this paper, we propose a novel training pipeline for offline language-
086
      conditioned policy learning—TEDUO: Teaching the Environment Dynamics from Unlabeled Ob-
087   servations. TEDUO distills knowledge of the environment dynamics into a pre-trained LLM through
088   supervised fine-tuning. This knowledge is obtained by learning optimal policies with traditional RL,
089   based on the offline dataset augmented with LLM-generated state abstractions and labels. Thus,
090   within TEDUO, LLMs fulfill the dual role of cheap data enhancers and flexible generalizers, ele-
091   vating conventional RL to address challenges C1-C4.
092
      Contributions. 1) We introduce TEDUO—a novel LLM fine-tuning pipeline, which, to the best
093   of our knowledge, is the first to enable the learning of generalizable language-conditioned policies
094   based solely on an unlabeled dataset of state-action transitions and an unpaired set of natural lan-
095   guage commands. 2) We demonstrate that LLMs can be effectively employed both as cost-effective
096   data enhancers—automating critical tasks typically handled by humans—and as versatile general-
097   izers—acting as a general language-conditioned policy capable of solving new tasks in previously
098   unseen scenarios. 3) We empirically show that TEDUO enhances both the data efficiency and gener-
099   alization capacity of offline training, outperforming competing approaches by a significant margin.
100   We analyze the scalability of our method and provide insights into the learning process, showing
101
      that fine-tuned LLMs acquire core skills, rather than simply memorize optimal actions.

134   3       T HE M ETHOD : TEDUO
135
136   To address the problem of learning language-conditioned policies solely based on the inputs D and
137   G tr , we must overcome the challenges C1-C4 outlined in the introduction. While conventional RL
138   methods are successful at learning optimal policies within well-explored environments, they typi-
139   cally require additional data labeling and are limited in generalization to new, previously unseen lan-
140   guage commands and states. In contrast, although LLMs can understand the meaning of sentences
141   in natural language describing each goal, their skills lack grounding in relation to the environment’s
142
      dynamics. Our pipeline, TEDUO, employs LLMs to enhance conventional RL, effectively address-
      ing challenges C1-C4. TEDUO consists of three main steps:
143
144
       Step 1.      Construction of abstract MDPs. For each goal, g ∈ G tr , we construct an ab-
145    LLMs as data stract MDP by employing LLM-automated hindsight labeling and state abstraction,
146    enhancers    addressing C1 and C2, respectively.
147
       Step 2.         Goal-conditioned policy learning with offline RL After obtaining a labeled dataset
148
                       for each goal in G tr , we solve the set of abstract MDPs using an out-of-the-box
149
                       offline RL algorithm. As a result, we obtain a set of learned policies {πg : g ∈ G tr }.
150                    The learned policies improve on naive imitation learning, addressing C3.
151
152
       Step 3.         LLM supervised fine-tuning. We distill the knowledge about the environment dy-
       LLMs as         namics and optimal actions into a pre-trained LLM with supervised instruction fine-
153
       generalizers    tuning (SFT). This step grounds the prior knowledge of the base LLM in the en-
154
                       vironment dynamics, thus enabling generalization to new, previously unseen states
155                    and goals, addressing both challenges C2 and C4.

      3.1     S TEP 1. C ONSTRUCTION OF ABSTRACT MDP S
163
164   Starting with the dataset of unlabeled observations D and the training goals G tr , we first construct
165   a set of abstract MDPs {Mg : g ∈ G tr }, where Mg := (S g , A, P g , Rg , ρ, γ), with S g being the
166   abstract state space for the goal g, P g the induced transition operator, and Rg the reward function
167   with respect to the goal g. To define our abstract MDPs, we employ hindsight labeling and state
168   abstraction with LLM-based operators.
169
170   3.1.1     S TATE ABSTRACTION
171
      The goal of state abstraction is to reduce the size of the environmental state space by grouping
172
      together similar states in a way that reduces the complexity of the underlying problem being solved
173   (Li et al., 2006). With a well-designed state abstraction, RL algorithms can learn more efficiently,
174   requiring fewer samples of data, which is particularly relevant in our offline setup. Formally, let
175   F : X × G → S g be an abstraction operator that given a goal g maps a single observation x ∈ X to
176   an abstract state sg = F (x; g). The abstraction map, F , should be such that |X | ≫ |S g |.

      In this paper, we use natural language to guide state abstraction, so that the resulting abstract states
193
      contain only the goal-relevant information. Namely, we consider environments that can be repre-
194
      sented in a d-dimensional feature space, X = X 1 × X 2 × . . . × X d . We assume that only a relatively
195   small subset of these variables is relevant for solving a specific goal g and an even smaller subset is
196   required to identify the goal states ϕ(g).
197
198
      The state abstraction operator is implemented as a collection of Python functions built on top of
199
      the feature selection made by a prompted language model, F ( · ; g) = LLMabstrct (g)(·). Using
      LLM powered Python functions instead of directly applying the LLM to create an abstraction of
200
      each state reduces the number of LLM calls from |X ||Gtr | to |Gtr | and ensures that the abstraction
201
      is consistent across all states. The prompt for generating the code includes contextual information
202   about the environment, a list of features, and a description of the goal, instructing the LLM to
203   create a function that removes features of a state that are irrelevant to achieving the specified goal.
204   Additionally, state abstraction functions separate the set of relevant features into two subsets: sgϕ
205   and sgϕ̄ , so that sgϕ ∪ sgϕ̄ . The features in sgϕ are the ones which are necessary for identifying if
206
      the underlying low-level state achieves g, i.e. if x ∈ ϕ(g). The remaining features, which are
207   relevant for solving the task specified by g but not strictly necessary for identifying if this goal is
208   achieved are found in sgϕ̄ . We introduce this separation of abstracted features to even further reduce
209   the dimensionality of the state space for hindsight labeling (see next section). Figure 2 shows an
210   example effect of applying our LLM state abstraction on a grid-world from the BabyAI environment.

212   3.1.2     G OAL - CONDITIONED H INDSIGHT L ABELING .
213
214   Following recent works (Kwon et al., 2023), we hypothesize that the existing abilities of LLMs in
215   natural language understanding are sufficient to perform the simple task of identifying whether a
      particular state belongs to the set of goal states ϕ(g) associated with a given goal description g.

      In order to perform hindsight labeling of our dataset D, we wish to approximate ϕ, and thus the
      reward Rϕ (·; g), with a prompted language model LLMrwrd ≈ 1(g is achieved in sg ) . Note, we assign
217
218
      the rewards in the abstracted spaces S g and not the original state space X . Given the large number
219   of goals and states, to reduce the number of LLM calls needed, instead of using language models
220   directly, we train proxy reward models–lightweight neural networks trained to predict the labels
221   generated by the prompted language model, LLMrwrd . For each goal g, we build a supervised
222   dataset, {(sg , rg ) : rg = LLMrwrd (sg ; g), sg ∈ S̃ g }, where S̃ g is a small, diverse subset of the
223   abstract space S g and rg ∈ {0, 1}. We train a neural network Rθ ( · ; g) : S g → {0, 1} to predict
224   binary rewards for the entire abstract state space S g . The subset S̃ g is chosen so that for any two
225   abstract states, the set of features relevant for goal-identifications is distinct, i.e. ∀sg1 ̸= sg2 ∈
226   S̃ g , sg1,ϕ ̸= sg2,ϕ . This maximizes the chances of including goal-states in S̃ g , mitigating the potential
227   issue of generating a highly-imbalanced dataset for training our proxy neural networks. These proxy
228   reward functions provide a much more cost-effective way to perform hindsight labeling compared
229   to labeling all states from D for all goals from G tr directly by LLM prompting or with human
230   annotators. Appendix D shows that for the BabyAI environment, depending on the goal, proxy
231   rewards reach near 100% accuracy in comparison to the ground truth rewards of the environment.

233   3.2   S TEP 2. S OLVING THE A BSTRACT MDP S
234
235   After applying state abstractions and hindsight labeling to our offline dataset D, for each goal g ∈
236
      G tr , we obtain an offline dataset Dg := {(sg , a, sg , rg )}. Given these data, we can apply any offline
      reinforcement learning method to learn optimal policies π g , for each goal g ∈ G tr . In practice,
237
      however, to learn the goal-conditioned policies, the chosen RL method should be scalable, as we
238
      must solve multiple MDPs, one for each goal in G tr . Therefore, in our instantiation, we discard
239   computationally intensive methods. Furthermore, as the generalization to new states is tackled by
240   the next step, we do not require at this stage that the learned policies generalize to unseen states.
241   Given these considerations, we simply choose tabular Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992) to solve
242   the set of abstract MDPs. At the end of this stage, we obtain a set of learned policies {π g : g ∈ G tr }.
243   These policies are limited to the set of training goals and the set of states observed in D. The final
244   step of our pipeline addresses these limitations.

265   4     R ELATED W ORK
266
267   LLMs for decision making. There is growing interest in using general-purpose LLMs directly as
268   decision-making agents (Yao et al., 2023). Various prompting techniques, such as chain of thought
269   (Wei et al., 2023) and self-reflection (Ji et al., 2023), have been developed to enhance LLMs’ abilities
      in long-term planning tasks. However, as demonstrated in previous works (Szot et al., 2024; Finn,


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      2024), prompting techniques alone are insufficient for solving complex decision-making tasks in
271   dynamic environments. To effectively utilize the knowledge embedded in LLMs for RL problems,
272   these models must be grounded in the dynamics of the environment. This grounding can be achieved
273   either through in-context learning (Wang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) or fine-tuning (Carta et al.,
274   2023; Tan et al., 2024; Brohan et al., 2023a). A key limitation of in-context learning is its restricted
275   window size. In this work, we focus on fine-tuning; however, unlike prior studies, we significantly
276   reduce the requirements on input data for fine-tuning the decision-making agent.
277   LLMs as data enhancers. To apply conventional RL methods in search of optimal goal-conditioned
278   policies, we must augment our dataset of state-action transitions with goal-dependent rewards. This
279   process, known as hindsight labeling, has traditionally been performed manually by human anno-
280   tators or through learning the reward function from expert demonstrations (Ziebart et al., 2008; Fu
281   et al., 2018; Bahdanau et al., 2018). Recent studies, however, have demonstrated that task-specific
282   rewards can be effectively generated using pre-trained LLMs (Yu et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2023; Xie
283   et al., 2023). While successful, most LLM-based approaches rely on iterative prompting strategies,
284   which are costly in terms of LLM calls. Our approach to hindsight labeling reduces this cost by ap-
285
      proximating the LLM-induced reward function with a lightweight neural network. Furthermore, we
      assign rewards in abstracted state spaces, significantly reducing the number of states to be labeled.
286
      Similar to the work of Peng et al. (2023), our state abstraction function uses the language command
287
      to guide the elimination of irrelevant state features.
288
289   Language-conditioned RL. Numerous previous studies have explored learning language-
290   conditioned policies by assuming access to ground-truth environment rewards (Jiang et al., 2019;
291
      Co-Reyes et al., 2018), real-time experimentation (Fu et al., 2018; Bahdanau et al., 2018; Mirchan-
      dani et al., 2021), or expert demonstrations paired with language annotations (Stepputtis et al., 2020;
292
      Lynch & Sermanet, 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023b;a). In contrast, our approach aims
293
      to learn language-conditioned policies from entirely offline datasets, which may be highly subopti-
294   mal and which are unlabeled, with no environment- or human-provided reward signals. Regarding
295   policy evaluation, much of the prior work in language-conditioned RL and IL tests agents on new
296   language commands synonymous with those seen during training (Lynch & Sermanet, 2021; Nair
297   et al., 2022). Similar to the works of (Xiao et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023a; Shridhar et al., 2021a;
298   Jang et al., 2022), our focus lies on novel instructions corresponding to previously unsolved goals.
299
      Refer to Appendix A for an extended discussion of the related work.

303
304
      Questions. In our experiments we aim to answer the following questions: (Q1) Does the use of a
305
      pre-trained language model enable generalization to new language commands and new states? (Q2)
306   How does our method compare to simpler prompting-based methods and alternative approaches to
307   language-conditioned RL? (Q3) As a result of SFT, does the language model memorize the optimal
308   actions or does it learn generalizable and compositional skills? (Q4) How does our method scale
309   with computer power and what is the effect of language abstractions on data efficiency?
310
      Experimental Setup. For the experiments we require a controlled environment where a wide variety
311
      of distinct goals can be specified, and where the semantics of the states and actions are easily in-
312   terpretable by an LLM. Thus, we choose the BabyAI environment (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018),
313   a grid world platform for instruction following where an agent receives natural language goal in-
314   structions such as: “Go to the tile (3,2)”, “Pick up the blue key” or “Look behind the green locked
315   door”. A detailed discussion on the environment choice can be found in Appendix A. The grids
316   can consist of multiple rooms connected by open or locked doors and different distractor objects
317   that the agent can interact with (boxes, keys, balls, etc.). The action space A consists of several
318   navigation primitives (forward, pickup, etc.). In our setup, we assume full observability of the
319   original state space. Each state can be represented as a long list of features and their coordinates
320
      (see Appendix B.2 for example state representations in a text format).
321   Metrics. We rely on the following metrics to evaluate our learned policies: success rate: proportion
322   of attempts in which the agent achieves the goal within the time limit (500 steps); episode Length:
323   the average number of steps taken to reach the goal or the time limit; invalid actions: ratio of invalid
      actions (e.g., moving into a wall) to total actions.

403   5.3     Q3: L EARNING AND EXPLOITING CORE SKILLS
404
405   The aim of the first part of our experiments is to investigate the generalization abilities of the LLM
406   fine-tuned with our pipeline. We wish to investigate if by learning the optimal policies for diverse
407   goals and environments, the LLM can integrate the core skills required to achieve these goals and
408   how such skills can be transferred across tasks. We also investigate the aspect of skill composition-
409
      ality. Does the prior knowledge of the LLM, now grounded in the environment dynamics, suffice to
      compose together learned skills to solve novel tasks?

522   6      D ISCUSSION
523
      Limitations. Leveraging LLMs’ prior knowledge enables efficient policy generation with minimal
524
      data. However, some applications may benefit more than others. First, certain scenarios may be
525
      out of distribution even for LLMs trained on extensive Internet data. Second, we assume that the
526   environment state can be represented textually, which, although feasible for many applications due
527   to language’s expressiveness, may not be ideal in all cases. Third, due to the discrete nature of LLM
528   tokenization, using fine-tuned LLMs to directly output actions requires discretization of the action
529   space, which can hinder performance in continuous control tasks. Lastly, while data requirements
530   are minimal, they still assume some practitioner knowledge of the environment and the data D to
531   propose training goals G tr likely achievable in D (see Appendix C.1 for details).
532   Conclusions. TEDUO introduces a novel framework for developing natural language instruction-
533   following agents capable of generalizing to new states and instructions in a zero-shot setting, using a
534   collection of unlabeled state-action transitions. This is the first RL pipeline to create natural language
535   goal-conditioned policies in an offline setting using unlabeled data. It surpasses the current state-of-
536   the-art in zero-shot generalization for both goal and domain adaptation. To achieve this, we propose
537   a data-driven approach to teach environment dynamics to a large language model, enabling it to
538   interact effectively with the environment—a task where LLMs typically underperform. This result
539   could potentially extend the applicability of LLMs to new domains requiring multi-step reasoning
      and dynamic interaction with the environment.

      A     E XTENDED R ELATED W ORK
865
866   A.1    G ENERALIZATION IN OFFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
867
868   Following the work of Mediratta et al. (2024), we separate the generalization abilities of offline
869   reinforcement learning algorithms into two categories: new instruction following and adaptation to
870   new states or environments.
871   Goal-conditioned RL. Goal-conditioned Reinforcement Learning (GCRL) is a subfield of RL ded-
872   icated to developing policies capable of achieving multiple goals within the same environment dy-
873   namics. These policies are conditioned on an additional input, g, indicating the goal that the next
874   action should aim to achieve. While most recent research has focused on online settings (Islam et al.,
875   2022; Han et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2021), only a few methods have addressed the
876   offline GCRL problem (Yang et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Chebotar et al., 2021). (Yang et al., 2023)
877   offers a comparison of these methods and highlights the key challenges in offline GCRL. Addition-
878   ally, these approaches typically restrict goal representations to those expressible as a single state in
879
      the state space (Chebotar et al., 2021), a scalar parameter (Ma et al., 2022), or a fixed set of known
      goals (Yang et al., 2022).
880
881   Language-conditioned RL. Our work addresses the problem of goal-conditioned RL, where goals
882   are expressed in natural language. While using language to specify goals is natural and broadens
883   the range of possible goals it comes with the challenge of grounding the semantics of language
884   in the environment state space and dynamics. Such language-instruction following agents have
885
      been widely studied in both reinforcement learning and imitation learning contexts. However, most
      existing methods either rely on access to an online environment for interaction (Fu et al., 2018;
886
      Bahdanau et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2019; Mirchandani et al., 2021) or require costly, goal-annotated
887
      expert datasets of offline demonstrations (Stepputtis et al., 2020; Lynch & Sermanet, 2021; Xiao
888   et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023b;a). In contrast, our approach does not assume any environment-
889   provided reward signal or access to real-time exploration. Furthermore, in terms of generalization
890   to new natural language instructions, we distinguish between evaluation on instructions that simply
891   paraphrase the training goals (Nair et al., 2022; Lynch & Sermanet, 2021) from those that represent
892   semantically novel goals. Similar to the works of Xiao et al. (2023); Brohan et al. (2023a); Stepputtis
893   et al. (2020); Shridhar et al. (2021a); Jang et al. (2022), our focus is on the latter, more challenging
894   scenario.
895   Domain Generalization. While the previous section addressed generalization to new goals, this sec-
896   tion focuses on generalization to novel state-action transitions. This type of generalization extends
897   beyond goal-conditioned RL, as it is essential even for single-goal RL. It has been widely studied
898   and observed that Offline RL methods often overfit to the training distribution of state-action tran-
899   sitions, resulting in poor performance when the test distribution differs. Various approaches have
900   been proposed to address this distribution shift, including regularization techniques Kostrikov et al.
901   (2021); Kumar et al. (2020), model-based RL Yu et al. (2020); Kidambi et al. (2021), and enhanced
902
      representation learning Mazoure et al. (2021); Fan & Li (2022). In TEDUO, we intentionally avoid
      domain generalization when solving the abstract MDPs in step 2 to prevent providing incorrect ex-
903
      amples to the LLM in step 3. However, our method achieves domain generalization by leveraging
904
      the zero-shot capabilities of the fine-tuned LLM. Future work could enhance TEDUO by replacing
905   tabular Q-learning in step 2 with a method that generalizes to new state-action transitions.
906
907   A.2    O FFLINE POLICY LEARNING WITH MINIMAL DATA REQUIREMENTS .
908
909   This paper focuses on realistic requirements regarding the training inputs. We work under offline
910   setting, with a limited number of unlabeled environment transitions (i.e., (xt , at , xt+1 ) triplets) and
911   without any assumptions about the policy that generated the actions. To address this scenario, we
912   employ LLMs to label the data, enabling the use of RL methods, and as an abstraction function to
913   enhance sample efficiency. Below we discuss the related work regarding these two steps.
914   Hindsight labeling. Labeling data for goal-conditioned RL requires the design of reward functions
915   for each goal. The most common approach for designing the rewards relies on handcrafted meth-
916   ods that are often require multiple refinements through trial and error (Knox et al., 2022). With a
917   large number of goals, manual reward design becomes infeasible. Inverse Reinforcement Learning
      (Ziebart et al., 2008; Fu et al., 2018) attempts to generate reward functions directly from data, but it


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      requires a large amount of expert demonstrations. Recent studies have explored the use of LLMs and
919   VLMs as reward functions. These methods typically involve creating a preference dataset (Klissarov
920   et al., 2023), comparing the cosine similarity between natural language goals and state representa-
921   tions, or leveraging the coding abilities of LLMs (Yu et al., 2023a), especially in an online iterative
922   fashion (Ma et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024). These approaches are however aimed at densifying the
923   reward signal. In contrast, our method requires generating reward labels for a large number of goals
924   (approx. 100-1000), making the scalability of the process crucial. Therefore, we focus on gener-
925   ating rewards with a limited number of LLM calls. Our approach relies on LLM-based detection
926   of task completion, which has been proven effective by Kwon et al. (2023). We further reduce the
927
      number of LLM calls by approximating the LLM-generated rewards with lightweight proxy neural
      networks.
928
929   State abstraction. State abstraction aims to reduce the complexity of the state space by eliminat-
930   ing irrelevant features, thereby improving the efficiency of learning algorithms. Early work in this
931   area focused on state aggregation, where similar states are grouped together to form more compact
932   representations, with state similarity defined through the transition dynamics, value- or Q-functions
933
      (Andre & Russell, 2002; Li et al., 2006; Givan et al., 2003; Abel et al., 2018). Recent advance-
      ments have explored more sophisticated methods, such as deep learning-based state abstractions,
934
      employing neural networks to learn abstract representations of states (Allen et al., 2021). In this
935
      work, we explore the use of LLMs to accomplish the task of state abstraction. Our approach relies
936   on prompting a pre-trained LLM to remove the features of a state that are irrelevant in solving the
937   given goal. Such LLM-based state abstraction has been previously shown effective in the context of
938   robotics by Peng et al. (2023) who employ LLMs to translate the language command into a binary
939   mask highlighting the location of the goal-object.
940
941
      A.3    L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR D ECISION M AKING
942
943   Decision Transformers. Pre-trained models based on the Transformer architecture have been
944   widely used to address decision-making problems. However, this paper does not focus on Decision
945   Transformer (DT) models (Chen et al., 2021). Although DTs have been applied in goal-conditioned
946   RL and IL (Xu et al., 2022; Raparthy et al., 2023; Putterman et al., 2022), the joint modelling of
947   goal, state, and action representations remains challenging and requires large labeled datasets. In-
948   stead of training a decision transformer, this papers leverages the prior knowledge accumulated in
949   LLMs trained on Internet data to a) enable effective use of the limited offline, unlabeled data, b)
950   enable generalization to previously unseen goals and states.
951   General-purpose LLMs for decision making. Utilizing off-the-shelf LLMs has gained significant
952   attention due to its simplicity. In decision-making, LLMs have been used to create assistance func-
953   tions within training pipelines to enrich data (Klissarov et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; Ma et al., 2023;
954   Xie et al., 2023; Laskin et al., 2022), and as high-level planners during inference to guide traditional
955   RL policies (Shah et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2022). Additionally, there has been growing interest in
956   using general-purpose LLMs directly as decision-making agents (Yao et al., 2023). Improving the
957
      reasoning capabilities of LLM agents is now an active research area, focusing on methods that are
      independent of traditional RL. These include iterative prompting techniques such as self-reflection
958
      (Ji et al., 2023), chain of thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2023), and integration with planning al-
959
      gorithms like Monte Carlo Tree Search (Pouplin et al., 2024). Nevertheless, such methods have
960   been shown inefficient in completing complex, multi-step decision-making tasks in dynamic envi-
961   ronments (Finn, 2024; Szot et al., 2024). To effectively use the knowledge embedded in LLMs for
962   solving RL problems, these models need to be grounded in the dynamics of the environment.
963
      Grounding LLMs with the environment dynamics. An LLM agent grounded in an environment
964
      can link the semantics of both observations and possible actions to its internal representation system,
965
      enabling appropriate decision-making (Carta et al., 2023; Harnad, 1990). One approach to achieve
966   such grounding is through in-context learning. For instance, Voyager (Wang et al., 2023) pushes the
967   concept of an LLM agent to its limits by developing an automatic curriculum for GPT-4, supported
968   by a library of executable programs, to play Minecraft. Another method involves providing the LLM
969   with a game manual (Wu et al., 2023). However, these approaches either rely on extensive expert
970   knowledge, such as carefully designed prompts, or on game manuals, which may not always be
971   available. Additionally, in-context learning has limitations in data-driven scenarios, partly due to the
      restricted context window size, which is insufficient for incorporating entire datasets. An alternative


                                                         18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       approach involves fine-tuning LLMs to achieve grounding. Studies such as (Tan et al., 2024) and
973    (Carta et al., 2023) use Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to propose
974    online fine-tuning of LLMs. In the robotics domain, RT2 (Brohan et al., 2023a) demonstrates that
975    co-fine-tuning on both web-scale data and expert robot demonstrations improves performance of
976    VLMs for decision making in the context of robotics. Our method differs from previous work by
977    significantly lowering the requirements on input data, as we do not need online interaction or labeled
978    expert demonstrations. Furthermore, while RT2 implements co-fine-tuning, our method utilizes an
979    off-the-shelf pre-trained LLM, which is then fine-tuned.
980
981    A.4   T ESTING E NVIRONMENTS
982
983    This paper uses the Minigrid-BabyAI environment to benchmark its method. This choice was mo-
984    tivated by several factors. Most importantly, we require a sandbox environment in which a wide
985    range of goal reaching tasks can be expressed in natural language. Robotic environments (Todorov
986
       et al. (2012); James et al. (2019)) were excluded due to precise control of robotic components be-
       ing beyond LLM’s prior knowledge and the need to discretize continuous actions to match LLM’s
987
       tokenized output. Additionally, 3D environments (Fan et al. (2022); Puig et al. (2018)) were not
988
       considered due to computational constraints. Text-based games (Côté et al. (2018); Shridhar et al.
989    (2021b)) were also excluded as they involve high-level text interactions, contrary to this paper’s
990    focus on low-level control task for language models.
991
992
       Given the significant computational resources and time required to perform all three steps of our
       pipeline, in particular fine-tuning an LLM agent, our current scope is necessarily limited to BabyAI.
993
       Nonetheless, the insights derived from this controlled setting are broadly applicable and provide a
994
       foundation for future work in environments with similar tabular structures, such as NetHack (Küttler
995    et al. (2020)) and Overcooked (Carroll et al. (2020)), which differ mainly in thematic focus (video
996    game dungeon crawling and collaborative cooking, respectively).

1135
1136   As detailed in Section 3.1.2, the reward shaping process involves two stages.
1137
1138
       In the first stage, a large language model LLM, Llama-3-70B-Instruct, is utilized to generate a

1139
       1, max tokens: 8000}, using the prompt template shown in Figure B.4. For each goal g, up to 5000
1140
       states are randomly sampled from S̃ g and labeled.
1141

1143   tions are transformed from text to a grid format. The network architecture consists of a small con-
1144   volutional neural network with one convolutional layer (output dimension: 32, kernel size: (2,2)),
1145
       followed by two linear layers (hidden dimension: 32, output dimension: 1). A Sigmoid activation
       function is applied after the final linear layer, and ReLU is used after all other layers. Dropout lay-
1146
       ers are added before each linear layer. The network is trained with the following hyperparameters:
1147
       learning rate of 1e-5, maximum of 3000 epochs, and dropout rate of 0.1. The dataset is split into
1148   training and validation sets (90%/10%), and the model weights with the lowest validation loss are

       C.1          DATA COLLECTION POLICY
1353
1354   We examine the effect of the data collection policy on our pipeline’s performance. Specifically, we
1355   demonstrate that our pipeline remains effective irrespective of the optimality of the data collection
1356   policy with respect to the set G tr .
1357
       Given the observational dataset D collected under a policy π β , let G D represent the set of goals
1358   corresponding to goal states that have been visited in D. This set is defined as:
1359
1360
1361                                       G D = {g ∈ G : ∃(x, a, x′ ) ∈ D s.t. Rϕ (x, a, x′ ; g) = 1}.                                                                               (3)
1362
       We can measure the alignment between the dataset D and the training goals G tr by the size of
1363
       G D ∩ G tr , i.e. the set of goals from G tr that have been visited in D. A key point is that, in step 2 of
1364
       TEDUO, we cannot generate a policy π g for any goal g not present in G D . As discussed in section
1365   5.4, the performance of the fine-tuned LLM depends on the size of the synthetically generated dataset
1366   DSF T , making |G D ∩ G tr | an import ant metric for evaluating the fidelity of our training inputs: D
1367   and G tr .
1368
       To empirically analyze this, we consider two randomized policies:
1369
1370   • A) Goal-oriented policy: This is the policy used for data collection in the main experimental
1371     section. For each trajectory, a random goal from a set of goals G π is drawn and the agents acts
1372     according to the goal-oriented policy provided in the BabyAI environment in order to achieve
1373     it. This policy simulates agents attempting to accomplish multiple task within the environment.
1374
         Examples of real-world unlabeled data that could be generated from such policy include CCTV
         footage of employees at work, logs of medical procedures performed on a patient, or YouTube
1375
         videos.
1376
1377   • B) Random policy: Actions are drawn uniformly at random from the action space. This policy
1378     represents an agent that explores the environment without a specific goal. Although this scenario is
1379
         less common in real-world settings—where agents typically pursue objectives—it remains appli-
         cable forSo batch RL, particularly when learning from untrained agents with no prior knowledge.
