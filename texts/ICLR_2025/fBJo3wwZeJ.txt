011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are a class of generative models that
014            sample objects in proportion to a specified reward function through a learned policy.
015            They can be trained either on-policy or off-policy, needing a balance between
016
               exploration and exploitation for fast convergence to a target distribution. While
               exploration strategies for discrete GFlowNets have been studied, exploration in
017
               the continuous case remains to be investigated, despite the potential for novel
018
               exploration algorithms due to the local connectedness of continuous domains.
019            Here, we introduce Adapted Metadynamics, a variant of metadynamics that can
020            be applied to arbitrary black-box reward functions on continuous domains. We
021            use Adapted Metadynamics as an exploration strategy for continuous GFlowNets.
022            We show several continuous domains where the resulting algorithm, MetaGFN,
023            accelerates convergence to the target distribution and discovers more distant reward
024            modes than previous off-policy exploration strategies used for GFlowNets.
025

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are a type of generative model that samples from a discrete
030   space X by sequentially constructing objects via actions taken from a learned policy PF (Bengio
031   et al., 2021a). The policy PF (s, s′ ) specifies the probability of transitioning from some state s to
032   some other state s′ . The policy is parameterised and trained so that, at convergence, the probability
033
      of sampling an object x ∈ X is proportional to a specified reward function R(x). GFlowNets offer
      advantages over more traditional sampling methods, such as Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC),
034
      by learning an amortised sampler, capable of single-shot generation of samples from the desired
035
      distribution. Since GFlowNets learn a parametric policy, they can generalise across states, resulting in
036   higher performance across various tasks (Bengio et al., 2021a; Malkin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022;
037   Jain et al., 2022; Deleu et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Shen et al.,
038   2023b) and applications to conditioned molecule generation (Shen et al., 2023b), maximum likelihood
039   estimation in discrete latent variable models (Hu et al., 2023), structure learning of Bayesian networks
040   (Deleu et al., 2022), scheduling computational operations (Zhang et al., 2023), and discovering
041   reticular materials for carbon capture (Cipcigan et al., 2023).
042   Although originally conceived for discrete state spaces, GFlowNets have been extended to more
043   general state spaces, such as entirely continuous spaces, or spaces that are hybrid discrete-continuous
044   (Lahlou et al., 2023). In the continuous setting, given the current state, the policy specifies a continuous
045   probability distribution over subsequent states, and the probability density over states x ∈ X sampled
046   with the policy is proportional to a reward density function r(x). The continuous domain unlocks
047   more applications for GFlowNets, such as molecular conformation sampling (Volokhova et al., 2023)
048   and continuous control problems (Luo et al., 2024).
049   GFlowNets are trained like reinforcement learning agents. Trajectories of states are generated either
050   on-policy or off-policy, with the terminating state x ∈ X providing a reward signal for informing a
051   gradient step on the policy parameters. GFlowNets therefore suffer from the same training pitfalls
052   as reinforcement learning. One such issue is slow temporal credit assignment, which has thus far
053   been addressed by designing more effective loss functions, such as detailed balance (Bengio et al.,
      2021b), trajectory balance (Malkin et al., 2022) and sub-trajectory balance (Madan et al., 2022). This


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      latter approach has recently been extended by providing an energy function inductive bias at the early
055   stages of training (Pan et al., 2023).
056
057   Besides loss functions, another aspect of GFlowNet training is the exploration strategy for acquiring
058
      training samples. Exclusively on-policy learning is generally inadequate as it leads to inefficient
      exploration of new modes. More successful strategies therefore rely on off-policy exploration. For
059
      the discrete setting, numerous exploration strategies have been proposed including ϵ-noisy with
060
      a uniform random policy, tempering, Generative Augmented Flow Networks (GAFN) (Pan et al.,
061   2022), Thompson sampling (Rector-Brooks et al., 2023) and Local Search GFlowNets (Kim et al.,
062   2024). While these approaches can be generalised to the continuous domain, there is limited literature
063   benchmarking their effectiveness in this setting.
064
      Sampling in the continuous setting is a common occurrence in various domains such as molecular
065
      modelling (Hawkins, 2017; Yang et al., 2019) and Bayesian inference (Shahriari et al., 2016).
066
      The local connectedness of a continuous domain allows for novel exploration strategies that are
067   not directly applicable in the discrete setting. Sendera et al. (2024) compared several exploration
068   strategies in the context of diffusion samplers, and proposed alternating between on-policy sampling
069   and backward sampling from a fixed set of MCMC samples when training continuous GFlowNets.
070   These samples were selected prior to training, thereby inducing a fixed pre-training cost but keeping
071   the cost of each training trajectory constant. The authors showed effective exploration, even in
072   high-dimensional problems. However, their MCMC approach requires access to cheap gradients
073   of the reward landscape, which might not always be readily available (Rengarajan et al., 2022).
074   In addition, there are some settings, such as in molecular conformation sampling, where MCMC
075
      approaches are known to require significantly longer timescales to overcome energy barriers than
      methods based on molecular dynamics (MD) simulations (Abrams and Bussi, 2014), thereby making
076
      the pre-training cost prohibitively expensive.
077
078   In this work, we propose MetaGFN, a novel exploration algorithm for continuous GFlowNets inspired
079   by metadynamics, an enhanced sampling method widely used for molecular modelling (Laio and
080   Parrinello, 2002). Unlike MCMC-based sampling methods, MetaGFN operates in a general black-box
081
      setting, relying solely on an oracle for reward values, without requiring access to reward gradients. In
      common with standard metadynamics, MetaGFN converges quickly when a reduced-dimensional
082
      representation of the reward measure, specified by a so-called collective variable (CV) basis, is known.
083
      While our method is domain-agnostic, this feature makes MetaGFN particularly well-suited for small
084   molecular systems, where such representations are known and often low-dimensional (Fiorin et al.,
085   2013). MetaGFN requires no pre-training and only introduces a small, constant cost for each training
086   trajectory, which becomes negligible as the cost of reward evaluation increases.
087
      The main contributions of this work are:
088
089          • Introducing MetaGFN, an algorithm that adapts metadynamics to black box rewards and
090            continuous GFlowNets;
091
             • Proving that the Adapted Metadynamics formulation underlying MetaGFN is consistent and
092            reduces to standard metadynamics in the appropriate limit;
093
             • Showing empirically that MetaGFN outperforms existing GFlowNets exploration strategies
094
               in various continuous environments, including alanine dipeptide conformation sampling.
095
096   The rest of the paper is as follows. In Section 2 we review the theory of discrete and continuous
097   GFlowNets as well as metadynamics and collective variables. In Section 3, we present the Adapted
098   Metadynamics and MetaGFN algorithms. In Section 4, we evaluate MetaGFN against other ap-
099   proaches, showing that MetaGFN generally outperforms existing exploration strategies in various
100   continuous environments. We finish with limitations and conclusions in Sections 5 and 6. Code for
101   MetaGFN is available at [link in camera-ready].

105   2.1   D ISCRETE GF LOW N ETS
106
107   In a GFlowNet, the network refers to a directed acyclic graph (DAG), denoted as G = (S, A). Nodes
      represent states s ∈ S, and edges represent actions s → s′ ∈ A denoting one-way transitions between


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      states. The DAG has two distinguishable states: a unique source state s0 , that has no incoming edges,
109   and a unique sink state ⊥, that has no outgoing edges.
110
111   The set of states, X ⊂ S, that are directly connected to the sink state are known as terminating
112
      states. GFlowNets learn forward transition probabilities, known as a forward policy PF (s′ |s), along
      the edges of the DAG so that the resulting marginal distribution over the terminal states complete
113
      trajectories (the terminal distribution) , denoted as P ⊥ (x), is proportional to a given reward function
114
      R : X → R. GFlowNets also introduce additional learnable objects, such as a backward policy
115   PB (s|s′ ), which is a distribution over the parents of any state of the DAG, to create losses that
116   train the forward policy. Objective functions for GFlowNets include flow matching (FM), detailed
117   balance (DB), trajectory balance (TB) and subtrajectory balance (STB) (Bengio et al., 2021a;b;
118   Malkin et al., 2022; Madan et al., 2022). During training, the parameters of the flow objects are
119   updated with stochastic gradients of the objective function applied to batches of trajectories. These
120   trajectory batches can be obtained either directly from the current forward policy or from an alternative
121   algorithm that encourages exploration. These approaches are known as on-policy and off-policy
122   training respectively.

124   2.2    C ONTINUOUS GF LOW N ETS
125
      Continuous GFlowNets extend the generative problem to continuous spaces (Lahlou et al., 2023),
126
      where the analogous quantity to the DAG is a measurable pointed graph (MPG) (Nummelin, 1984).
127
      MPGs can model continuous spaces (e.g., Euclidean space, spheres, tori), as well as hybrid spaces,
128   with a mix of discrete and continuous components, as often encountered in robotics, finance, and
129   biology (Bortolussi and Policriti, 2008; Swiler et al., 2012; Neunert et al., 2020).
130
      Definition 2.1 (Measurable pointed graph (MPG)). Let (S̄, T ) be a topological space, where S̄ is
131
      the state space, T is the set of open subsets of S̄, and Σ is the Borel σ-algebra associated with the
132   topology of S̄. Within this space, we identify the source state s0 ∈ S̄ and sink state ⊥∈ S̄, both
133   distinct and isolated from the rest of the space. On this space we define a reference transition kernel
134   κ : S̄ × Σ → [0, +∞) and a backward reference transition kernel κb : S̄ × Σ → [0, +∞). The
135   support of κ(s, ·) are all open sets accessible from s. The support of κb (s, ·) are all open sets where s
136   is accessible from. Additionally, these objects must be well-behaved in the following sense:
137
138          (i) Continuity: For all B ∈ Σ, the mapping s 7→ κ(s, B) is continuous.
139         (ii) No way back from the source: The backward reference kernel has zero support at the source
140              state, i.e. for all B ∈ Σ, κb (s0 , B) = 0.
141
142         (iii) No way forward from the sink: When at the sink, applying the forward kernel keeps you
143               there, i.e. κ(⊥, ·) = δ⊥ (·), where δ⊥ is the Dirac measure of the sink state.
144         (iv) A fully-explorable space: The number of steps required to possibly reach any measurable
145              B ∈ Σ from the source state, and to guarantee to reach the sink state, with the forward
146              reference kernel is bounded.
147
148   The set of objects (S̄, T , Σ, s0 , ⊥, κ, κb ) then defines an MPG.
149
150

      DAG. Similarly, a discrete GFlowNet’s DAG satisfies discrete versions of (ii), (iii), and (iv).

      2.3      E XPLORATION STRATEGIES FOR GF LOW N ETS
178
179   GFlowNets can reliably learn using off-policy trajectories, a key advantage over hierarchical varia-
180   tional models (Malkin et al., 2023). For optimal training, it is common to use a replay buffer and
181   alternate between on-policy and off-policy (exploration) batches (Shen et al., 2023a). In the discrete
182   case, proposed techniques to encourage exploration include ϵ-noisy exploration, tempering and the
183   incorporation of intermediate rewards (Bengio et al., 2021a; Pan et al., 2022). Exploration strategies
184   for continuous GFlowNets are less well studied in the literature but several methods designed for
185   discrete GFlowNets can be adapted (Sendera et al., 2024). In this work, we consider:
186   Local Search GFlowNets (Kim et al., 2024): Explores by backtracking and resampling on-policy
187   trajectories. Reconstructed trajectories with a higher reward than the original are used for training,
188   thus encouraging the learning of high-reward modes.
189
      Thompson sampling. (Rector-Brooks et al., 2023): Explores high-uncertainty regions by using an
190
      ensemble of policy heads with a shared torso. A random head generates the on-policy trajectory,
191   and the loss is computed by averaging contributions over heads, where each head is independently
192   included with probability p.
193
194
      Noisy exploration: Explores by increasing policy uncertainty. A small constant is added to the policy
      variance which is gradually reduced to zero over the course of training.
195
196   Nested sampling (Lemos et al., 2023): A Markov-Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm is used
197   to sample from the reward distribution. Backward sampling from these terminal states are used to
198   generate off-policy trajectories.

200   2.4      M ETADYNAMICS AND COLLECTIVE VARIABLES
201
202
      Molecular dynamics (MD) simulates that dynamics of molecules using Langevin dynamics (LD)
      (Pavliotis, 2014), a stochastic differential equation that models particle motion under friction and
203
      random noise. LD trajectories ergodically sample the molecule’s Gibbs measure, ρβ (x) ∝ e−βV (x) ,
204
      where x ∈ X represents atomic positions, V (x) is the molecular potential, and β is the thermo-
205
      dynamic beta.1 However, when V (x) contains multiple, deep local minima, as is common in
206   biomolecules, then LD becomes inefficient, as it tends to get trapped in these minima, slowing
207   exploration of the full state space.
208
209
      Metadynamics overcomes this by progressively modifying the potential landscape to discourage
      visits to already-explored regions (Laio and Parrinello, 2002). It achieves this by regularly depositing
210
      repulsive Gaussian biases, centered at the current state of the evolving LD trajectory. This modifies
211
      the potential to Vtotal (x, t) = V (x) + Vbias (x, t), where Vbias (x, t) is the cumulative bias at time t.
212   Intuitively, metadynamics thus progressively transforms the landscape into more level surface, in
213   which the system can diffuse more freely, thereby accelerating exploration (Figure 1). In the limit
214   t → ∞, the dynamics approximates free diffusion and uniformly samples the domain of V (x).

231

232

233   from the original (high-dimension) state space X to a lower-dimensional space Z. For potential V (x)
234   and collective variables z, metadynamics     is guaranteed to eventually uniformly sample the domain of
      the marginal potential V (z ′ ) := X δ(z ′ − z(x))V (x)dx. In molecular contexts, ideal choices of
                                            R
235
236   CVs correspond to the physical reaction coordinates that underly rare-event transitions (e.g. backbone
237   dihedrals, interatomic distances) (Laio and Gervasio, 2008; De Vivo et al., 2016). In more general
238   settings, ideal CVs should resolve rare events dynamics on V (x), simplfy the landscape (retaining
239   its essential features e.g. barriers and basins), and minimise the dimensionality of this representation.
240   For simple systems, CVs can be derived from known symmetries or macroscopic order parameters
241
      that describe state changes. However, if the choice of CVs is not obvious, then they can learnt through
      data-driven methods such as Time-Lagged Independent Component Analysis (TICA) (Molgedey and
242
      Schuster, 1994), manifold learning algorithms, neural network autoencoders or variational methods
243
      (Mardt et al., 2018; Bonati et al., 2021; Ramaswamy et al., 2021). For an in-depth review on these
244   CV-learning approaches, see Sidky et al. (2020). Metadynamics has seen numerous extensions (Bussi
245   and Laio, 2020). In the next section, we adapt the original metadynamics algorithm to the continuous
246   black box setting. We will assume suitable CVs are given, but the theory we present is agnostic to
247   their form–be it analytical expressions, neural networks, or non-parametric tabular mappings.

      3       M ETAGFN: A DAPTED M ETADYNAMICS FOR GF LOW N ETS
251
      Metadynamics has two properties that make it well-suited as an exploration strategy. Firstly, it
252
      eventually uniformly samples the domain, ensuring exploration of all local minima. In contrast,
253
      techniques such as Local Search GFlowNets, Thompson sampling, and noisy exploration rely on
254   localised strategies that are prone to mode locking (Section 4). Secondly, its gradual diffusion from
255   the starting configuration allows incremental exploration, resulting in more stable training than global
256   approaches like nested sampling. However, metadynamics requires a potential and its gradients,
257   which are not always available in a black-box GFlowNet setting. We adapt metadynamics to this
258   setting by interpreting the reward landscape as a potential energy surface, resulting in the exploration
259   algorithm Adapted Metadynamics (AM) (Section 3.1) and training algorithm MetaGFN (Section 3.2).
260   Interpreting reward density as a potential: We assume that X is a manifold and that the reward
261   density r(x) is bounded and L1-integrable over X with Rat most finitely many discontinuities. Thus,
262   the target density over terminal states,R ρ(x) := r(x)/ X r(x′ ) dx′ , can be expressed as a Gibbs
263   distribution: ρ(x) = exp(−β ′ V (x))/ X r(x′ ) dx′ , where we identify V (x) = − β1′ ln r(x) as the
264
      corresponding potential, for some constant scalar β ′ > 0. Reward maxima thus correspond to
265   potential energy minima. From simplicity, we set β ′ equal to the thermodynamic beta (β). Making
266   this choice means that the single parameter β uniquely controls the (unbiased) transition rates
267   between minima of the potential2 . Our goal is to use metadynamics to explore V (x), generating
268   high-reward terminal states that guide the GFlowNet to sample all reward density modes.

         3.2   M ETAGFN
337
        Each Adapted Metadynamics sample xi ∈ X is an off-policy terminal state sample. To train a
338     GFlowNet, complete trajectories are required. We generate these by backward sampling from the
339     terminal state, giving a trajectory τ = (s0 , s1 , . . . , sn = xi ), where each state si−1 is sampled from
340     the current backward policy distribution p̂B (si−1 |si ; θ), for i from n to 1. This approach means that
341     the generated trajectory τ has reasonable credit according to the loss function, thereby providing a
342     useful learning signal. However, since this requires a backward policy, this is compatible with DB,
343     TB, and STB losses, but not FM loss. Given the superior credit assignment of the former losses, this
344     is not a limitation (Madan et al., 2022).
345     Additionally, we use a replay buffer. Due to the theoretical guarantee that Adapted Metadynamics
346     will eventually sample all collective variable space (Theorem 3.1), AM samples are ideal candidates
347     for storing in a replay buffer. When storing these trajectories in the replay buffer, there are two
348     obvious choices:
349

478

      5   L IMITATIONS
516
517
      For metadynamics to be an effective sampler, the CVs must be low-dimensional and bounded. Either
518   these CVs should be known analytically (as in our experiments) or learnt from data (Sidky et al.,
519   2020). Alternatively, CVs could be learnt adaptively by parameterising z(x; θ) by a neural network
520   and updating its parameters by back-propagating through the GFlowNet loss during training. The
521   metadynamics algorithm could also be replaced with a variant with smoother convergence properties,
522   such as well-tempered metadynamics (Barducci et al., 2008) or on-the-fly probability enhanced
523   sampling (OPES) (Invernizzi, 2021). We leave these as extensions for future work.
524
525   6   C ONCLUSIONS
526
527   While exploration strategies for discrete Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) have received
528   extensive attention, the methodologies for continuous GFlowNets remain relatively underexplored.
529   To address this gap, we illustrated how metadynamics, a widely used enhanced sampling technique in
530   molecular dynamics, can be adapted as an effective exploration strategy for continuous GFlowNets.
531
      In molecular dynamics, atomic forces can be computed as the gradient of the potential, whereas
532
      continuous GFlowNets tackle problems where the reward function is a black box and gradients
533   are inaccessible. We demonstrated how the method could be adapted by updating a kernel density
534   estimate of the reward function on-the-fly, and proved that this is guaranteed to explore the space
535   in an appropriate limit. Our empirical investigations show that MetaGFN offers a computationally
536   efficient means to explore new modes in environments where prior knowledge of collective variables
537   exists. Importantly, this work advocates an approach wherein techniques derived from molecular
538   modelling can be adapted for machine learning tasks. Looking ahead, we anticipate that this could
539   be a fruitful area of cross-disciplinary research, where existing ideas from the enhanced sampling
      literature can find further applications in generative modelling and reinforcement learning.

       D.2    L INE ENVIRONMENT DETAILS
988
989    Parameterisation: The forward and backward policies are a mixture of three Gaussians. We
990    parameterise p̂F , p̂B and the flow fˆ through an MLP with 3 hidden layers, 256 hidden units per
991    layer. We use the GELU activation function and dropout probability 0.2 after each layer. This
992    defines the torso of the MLP. Connecting from this common torso, the MLP has three single-layer,
993    fully-connected heads. The first two heads have output dimension 9 and parameterise the 3 means (µ),
994    standard deviations (σ) and weights (w) of the mixture of Gaussians for the forward and backward
995    policies respectively. The third head has output dimension 1 and parameterises the flow function fˆ.
996    The mean and standard deviation outputs are passed through a sigmoid function and transformed so
997    that they map to the ranges µ ∈ (−14, 14) and σ ∈ (0.1, 1). The mixture weights are normalised with
998    the softmax function. The exception to this parameterisation is the backward transition to the source
999
       state which is fixed to be the Dirac delta distribution centred on the source, i.e. p̂B (s0 |s1 ; θ) = δs0 .
