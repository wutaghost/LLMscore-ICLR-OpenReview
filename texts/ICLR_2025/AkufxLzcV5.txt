025                                           A BSTRACT
026
027
               Dynamic novel-view synthesis and point tracking have emerged as promising
               tasks. However, existing methods often struggle with efficiency and accu-
028
               rately capturing deformations. In this paper, we propose LayeredGS, a novel
029
               Deformation-based Dynamic Gaussian Splatting method that excels in both 3D
030            tracking of dense scene elements and real-time dynamic scene rendering. By
031            learning Gaussian deformations between frames, LayeredGS preserves their
032            point-like characteristics while capturing motion. Unlike previous methods,
033            our approach optimizes efficiency by grouping Gaussians with similar deforma-
034            tions using a coarse-to-fine clustering structure. Experimental results show the
035            rapid convergence within 100 iterations per time frame on fast-moving dynamic
036            datasets, maintaining rendering quality and tracking accuracy comparable to state-
037            of-the-art methods using only 1/20 training iterations. Additionally, we introduce
038
               the application of self-supervised articulated object segmentation, highlighting the
               utility of deformation information for the first time.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Dynamic novel-view synthesis offers a groundbreaking approach to modeling the 3D world, with
043   promising applications in fields such as AR/VR, robotics, and self-driving cars. The learned dynamic
044   rendering can enable immersive and interactive experiences. In recent years, various attempts (Chen
045   et al., 2022; 2023a; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
046   2023b; Garbin et al., 2021; Hedman et al., 2021; Reiser et al., 2023; Wizadwongsa et al., 2021;
047   Chen et al., 2021; Niemeyer et al., 2022; Wynn & Turmukhambetov, 2023; Yu et al., 2021) have
048   been made to solve this task. These works are inspired by the success of Neural Radiance Field
049   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and use radiance field to model the 3D scenes. However, the time-
050
      consuming network querying and volume rendering procedure make real-time rendering difficult.
      Moreover, the implicit representation of NeRF limits the possibility of some downstream applica-
051
      tions like tracking.
052
053   Recently, the emergence of 3D Gaussian Spatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) significantly improves
      the efficiency of static scene rendering. By modeling a 3D scene as a set of 3D Gaussians and


                                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      utilizing efficient rasterization, 3DGS can achieve real-time rendering speed and fast training speed.
055   Such insight also inspires research in dynamic scenes (Wu et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024; Sun
056   et al., 2024; Luiten et al., 2023). In contrast to implicit approaches that utilize a single module
057   to encode entire dynamic scenes, some explicit online methods (Luiten et al., 2023; Abou-Chakra
058   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) that try to directly learn the change of position, rotation, and other
059   parameters of each Gaussian between adjacent frames have shown to be more effective in capturing
060   the deformation across time.
061   These methods (Luiten et al., 2023) can realistically capture the deformation of the scene over time
062   rather than merely accomplishing the rendering task. They preserve the point-like structural char-
063   acteristics of “3D” Gaussian Splatting, enabling effortless tracking of the evolution of any Gaus-
064   sian within the dynamic process without the need for any further processing. In particular, Dy-
065   namic3DGS (Luiten et al., 2023) uniquely enables 3D tracking across all frames for every scene
066   element, offering the capability to track all Gaussians throughout the entire sequence. This makes
067   it an effective method for tasks requiring fine-grained tracking, but its optimization process is slow.
068   Per-frame optimization often requires a large number of iterations (e.g., 2000 per frame), signifi-
069
      cantly hindering its use in real-time applications. In addition, simply modeling the translation and
      rotation of each Gaussian cannot yield good enough results for longer videos, making it difficult to
070
      fully utilize the motion information.
071
072   These observations motivate us to study for a better deformation strategy. In real-world dynamic
073   scenes, objects are often composed of multiple parts, with Gaussians within each part exhibiting
074   similar deformations. It is thus natural to leverage this structural information and reduce the need
075
      for training each 3D Gaussian individually. To achieve this, we organize the Gaussians into clusters
      and optimize the deformation of the entire cluster rather than individual Gaussians. Moreover, the
076
      covariance matrix and centroid position of each Gaussian are coupled during deformation. This
077
      means we do not need to learn the changes in the covariance matrix and centroid positions separately.
078   By making these changes, we can significantly reduce the number of parameters that need to be
079   optimized, thereby improving the efficiency of the optimization process.
080
081
      However, the size of the clusters is a trade-off. Training efficiency cannot significantly improve if
      a cluster contains too few Gaussians. On the other hand, if a cluster contains too many Gaussians,
082
      the large cluster tends to move as a whole, making it difficult to model the deformation among
083
      the Gaussians within the cluster. This can reduce the modeling capability for objects with detailed
084   movements. Therefore, we adopt a coarse-to-fine multi-layer cluster structure. In our experiments,
085   we demonstrate that this approach greatly enhances optimization efficiency, ensuring high rendering
086   quality and 3D tracking performance in complex dynamic scenes. Notably, our method achieves
087   20⇥ speed up compared with Dynamic3DGS (Luiten et al., 2023).
088
      A remaining challenge lies in learning the deformation information for each cluster. We address
089
      this issue by explicitly constructing a trainable deformation function with parameters that represent
090   a cluster’s rotation, translation, and fine-grained scaling. This enables us to learn the deformation
091   information efficiently via backpropagation of a 2D image loss.
092
093
      Following the acquisition of deformation information, we introduce a straightforward yet important
      application that is enabled by our method: Self-supervised Articulated Object Segmentation. Specif-
094
      ically, we propose clustering the object’s parts by our deformation, achieving superior segmentation
095
      to real scenes and objects, as shown in Figure 1.
096
097   In summary, the contributions of our paper are four-fold:
098         • We present LayeredGS, a method for online dynamic rendering that achieves both real-
099           time rendering and rapid convergence. LayeredGS delivers rendering quality comparable
100           to state-of-the-art methods, requiring only 1/20 of the iterations and consuming just 1 to 3
101
              seconds per frame for training. Meanwhile, LayeredGS enables 3D point tracking across
              all frames, providing accurate motion tracking for dynamic scenes.
102
103         • We introduce a multi-layer, coarse-to-fine, cluster-based optimization strategy that signifi-
104
              cantly improves the efficiency of the optimization process.
105         • We propose a trainable deformation function for clusters, enabling efficient learning of
106           deformation information via backpropagation of a 2D image loss.
107         • We demonstrate one novel application, Self-supervised Articulated Object Segmentation,
              showcasing the utility of deformation information for the first time.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
111   Static Novel-View Synthesis has become popular in 3D vision in recent years. Specifically, given
112
      a set of images from different camera poses, high-fidelity rendered images on novel views are ex-
      pected. The potential of achieving photorealistic results on this task is revealed by Neural Radiance
113
      Field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020), which encodes the scene as a fully connected deep network.
114
      Following this, a series of works are proposed to improve the efficiency, rendering quality, storage
115   consumption, and other aspects of NeRF (Chen et al., 2022; 2023a; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022;
116   Hu et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Garbin et al., 2021; Hedman et al., 2021;
117   Reiser et al., 2023; Wizadwongsa et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Niemeyer et al., 2022; Wynn &
118   Turmukhambetov, 2023; Yu et al., 2021). However, the design of costly volume rendering and neu-
119   ral networks makes the improvements very challenging, especially in balancing the time efficiency
120   and rendering quality. Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) is proposed to elegantly
121   solve this problem by explicit 3D Gaussian representation and differentiable rasterization.
122   Our work is highly inspired by this but extends from static scenes to dynamic scenes. In particular,
123   we start from static 3D Gaussians and optimize towards the dynamic scene. The natural represen-
124   tation of 3D Gaussians allows for explicit modeling of deformation and high efficiency for both
125   training and inference.
126
      Dynamic Novel-View Synthesis is a more challenging task in dynamic scenes. Inspired by the
127   success of NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020), various attempts have been made to model the dynam-
128   ics (Attal et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023; Fang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022b;d; 2021; 2023; Park
129   et al., 2021a;b; Pumarola et al., 2021; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Weng et al.,
130   2022). These works solve the dynamic problem by different routes. Specifically, some works (Li
131   et al., 2022b; Weng et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022) focus on certain scenarios
132   like human motion and leverage prior knowledge, such as human skeletons, to facilitate the synthe-
133   sis. While achieving impressive results, the modeling strategy cannot be applied to general cases.
134   Deformation-based methods (Attal et al., 2023; Park et al., 2021a;b; Pumarola et al., 2021) build
135
      a canonical stage and warp the other frames to this stage. This approach can be applied to more
      general scenes but can’t work well on complex scenes with high variations. Impressed by the high
136
      rendering speed of 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), many recent works focus on dynamic scenes with the
137
      idea of 3DGS (Wu et al., 2023; Luiten et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Duan et al., 2024; Sun et al.,
138   2024). Dynamic3DGS (Luiten et al., 2023) optimize the attributes of existing Gaussians to deal with
139   new frames and perform tracking. 4DGS (Wu et al., 2023) build a multi-resolution voxel planet to
140   compute voxel feature with timesteps. Realtime4DGS (Yang et al., 2024) build a 4D Gaussian struc-
141   ture and condition it to 3D Gaussian with a given timestep. 3DGStream (Sun et al., 2024) focuses
142   on online training and builds a transformation cache for optimization. However, despite being an
143   online method, 3DGStream continuously prunes Gaussians during training, making it impossible to
144   perform 3D point tracking across all time frames. While all these methods benefit from the effi-
145   ciency of differentiable rasterization, they fail to leverage the internal structural information of the
146
      real world and still suffer from notable training time.
147   Our method is mainly inspired by Dynamic3DGS (Luiten et al., 2023) and focuses on the online
148   dynamic scenes (Sun et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023), where
149   the method must continually deal with new incoming frames. To make online training much more
150   efficient, we propose a multi-level structure for 3D Gaussians with a new deformation optimiza-
151   tion strategy. In addition, our explicit deformation format allows for broad applications like object
152
      insertion and part segmentation.
153   Recent advances in dynamic Gaussian splatting, such as SC-GS (Huang et al., 2024), utilize control
154   points to compress the motion information of Gaussians, transitioning from per-Gaussian training
155   to per-control point training. While SC-GS (Huang et al., 2024) also seeks to optimize Gaussian
156   representations, unlike our method, it focuses on using a single-layer Gaussian structure to improve
157   rendering quality and conducting tasks like scene editing. In contrast, our approach leverages a
158
      multi-layer, coarse-to-fine structure to significantly enhance training efficiency. Furthermore, our
      method is designed for online tasks, while SC-GS is tailored for offline tasks.
159
160   Dynamic Novel-View Synthesis Datasets for online methods must provide multi-view inputs for
161   each frame. As opposed to offline methods, online methods can only reconstruct one timestep of
      the scene at a time, with each timestep being initialized using the previous timestep’s representation.

190   3     M ETHOD
191
192   Overview Our method utilizes online dynamic scene reconstruction, meaning that for each new
193   time frame, we only need the reconstruction result from the previous frame and perform a single
194   deformation prediction. Firstly, we need to perform static scene reconstruction for the initial frame
195   (frame 0) of the entire video. This process follows a standard 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al.,
196
      2023) procedure. Given multi-view observations of a static scene (I0,1 , I0,2 , . . . , I0,N ) and their cor-
      responding camera poses (C1 , C2 , . . . , CN ), we need to train a module ⇥0 that contains parameters
197
      of all the Gaussians. This enables us to generate a predicted image Iˆ for any input camera pose C,
198
199
      such that Iˆ = ⇥0 (C).
200   Based on this, we can proceed with subsequent online dynamic scene reconstruction. To be more
201   specific, we use S0 , S1 , . . . , ST to represent the dynamic scene from time frame 0 to time frame T .
202   For each time frame t, we have a sequence of images It,1 , It,2 , . . . , It,N from the cameras. Our goal
203   is to train a representation ⇥ that can fit the scenes S0 , S1 , . . . , ST . Given an arbitrary camera C at
204   time frame t, we can predict the image as Iˆ = ⇥t (C).

257   3.2   C OARSE - TO -F INE C LUSTERING S TRUCTURE
258
      The deformation function t can be a complicated non-linear one for the entire scene, making it
259
      hard for us to directly learn it. An intuitive idea is that if we can cluster points that are close in space
260
      and make an approximation that all the Gaussians within one cluster follow the same deformation
261   function, then the difficulty of learning the deformation function as a whole will be reduced. Also,
262   with this clustering structure, we can make the learning process more efficient than learning it for
263   each Gaussian independently. Which will be revealed in the experiment results. Furthermore, the
264   deformation function within one cluster can be constructed using deformations such as rotation,
265   translation, and scaling, making it possible to parameterize t in an explicit form.
266
      The intuition is that one small chunk of the object is nearly rigid, thus its movement can be repre-
267   sented by a transformation and a rotation around its centroid. Also, to increase the flexibility, we
268   can add a scaling factor. The deformation function t within one cluster j can be represented as

      At the coarsest level, clusters are expected to make broad approximations of the scene’s deforma-

      rotations, and scalings. These delta values are applied to the Gaussians after they have been de-
297
      formed by the deformation function.

299   3.3   L EARNING THE D EFORMATION
300
301
      Based on the previously mentioned deformation process, we present our method for learning defor-
      mation. Specifically, our method for learning deformations consists of two key stages.
302
303   Initialization Stage: We begin by training Gaussians on a static scene using observations from the
304   first frame. Following this, we perform a coarse-to-fine multilevel clustering of the Gaussian cen-
305   troids, which typically needs to be done only once during initialization. However, if there are sig-
306   nificant changes in the scene, this clustering can be recomputed mid-training to adapt to the new
307
      conditions.
308   Training Stage: Once initialization is complete, we proceed to the training phase, where we optimize
309   deformation parameters frame by frame. For each input frame, we combine the current input images
310   with the Gaussians from the previous frame to predict the deformation. Through backpropagation
311   of 2D loss, we iteratively refine the deformation function. The deformation parameters for each
312   frame are initialized by inheriting those from the previous frame, based on the assumption that the
313
      deformations between consecutive frames will be similar.

504   5   A PPLICATIONS : S EGMENT A RTICULATED O BJECTS
505
506
      Next, we demonstrate another application of the learned deformation information: performing seg-
507   mentation on articulated objects without any semantic knowledge. Many objects in daily life, al-
508   though not rigid as a whole, are composed of many rigid parts. As humans, we can distinguish
509   these parts by watching a dynamic video and observing their motions. In this section, we show
510   how to perform segmentation of different parts of an object in a zero-shot manner by only utilizing
511   deformation information.
512
      After training, we can obtain the centroid positions and rotations of Gaussians at each time frame.
513   We then use KMeans clustering to group Gaussians into different clusters based on this information.
514   Specifically, for a given Gaussian i, we use the notations pi,t and Ri,t to represent its position and
515   rotation matrix at time frame t, respectively. The KMeans feature for each Gaussian is a tensor of
516   shape [T, 15], where T is the total number of time frames. This tensor is the concatenation of pi,t ,
517   flattened Ri,t , and pi,0 repeated T times. Additionally, we multiply three hyperparameters: p , R ,
518   and p0 to these three parts before concatenation, respectively, to balance their importance.
519   The intuition behind the KMeans design is that, (1) Gaussians belong to the same part of the object
520   should be close to each other at all times, and (2) the rotations of Gaussians within the same rigid
521   part should be the same.
522
      As illustrated in Fig. 7, we present our segmentation results on the Panoptic and FastParticle datasets.
523
      To enhance visualization, we assign different colors to Gaussians belonging to distinct categories
524
      before rendering the final outcomes. Notably, our simple K-means algorithm yields highly intuitive
525   segmentation results, regardless of whether the scene comprises synthetic objects (left) or intricate
526   real-world environments (right). This observation serves as indirect evidence that the deformation
527   information captured by our learned Gaussians closely aligns with the actual motion of objects in
528   dynamic scenes.

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we show that a natural multi-layer structured 3D Gaussian can greatly improve the
534   training speed in dynamic scene rendering. Based on this, a trainable deformation function for
535   multi-level clusters is proposed to achieve high-fidelity rendering results. With these strategies, our
536   LayeredGS can perform very efficient per-frame training with only 1/20 iterations of the state-of-the-
537   art (Luiten et al., 2023), maintaining comparable rendering performance. Compared with previous
538   methods, our LayeredGS explicitly models the deformation and allows applications like articulated
539   object segmentation. As we use the standard 3D Gaussian format, experiments with other 3DGS
      variants can be future directions.

      A    FAST PARTICLE AND PANOPTIC DATASETS
025
026   In this section, we introduce the FastParticle and Panoptic datasets used in our experiments in details.
027   The real-world Panoptic dataset includes six scenes: Basketball, Boxes, Football, Juggle, Softball,
028
      and Tennis. Each frame in these scenes comes with segmentation provided by the original authors.
      Following Luiten et al. (2023), we distinguish between foreground and background in these scenes
029
      and utilize background loss and floor loss accordingly. Each scene in this dataset contains 150
030
      frames captured by a total of 31 cameras, with 27 cameras used for training and 4 for testing.
031
032   The synthetic FastParticle dataset, which we have accelerated, contains six dynamic scenes: Robot,
033   Spring, Wheel, Pendulums, Robot-Task, and Cloth. After acceleration, these scenes respectively
034
      have 35, 18, 38, 24, 35, and 35 frames. As illustrated in fig. I, we show the dynamic evolution of
      some scenes, highlighting the significant changes between frames. This dataset includes 40 cameras
035
      in total, from which we randomly select 4 as testing cameras and the remaining 36 as training
036
      cameras.
037
038   For all experiments, we provide the same static checkpoints to all baselines. For the 12 scenes
039   across the two datasets, we train for 20,000 iterations to obtain the checkpoints. Due to the varying
040
      complexity of the static scenes, 3,000 iterations are sufficient for most FastParticle scenes.

046

047
      provide more explanations about the second point. As shown in fig. II, suppose we have a rigid
048   body with its centroid denoted as C0 . This rigid body can be considered as a combination of two
049   smaller rigid bodies, with their centroids denoted as C1 and C2 , respectively. After rotation, C1 and
050   C2 move to C10 and C20 . Taking C0 as the origin of the coordinate system, the movement of the rigid
051   body can only be a rotation R around C0 , and the two smaller rigid bodies move accordingly. When
052   considering the left smaller rigid body alone, its motion should consist of a translation of its centroid
053   C1 and a rotation R1 around C1 . We aim to prove that R1 = R. Therefore, consider a point P on
      the left rigid body, which moves to point P 0 after the movement. From the perspective of C0 , we
