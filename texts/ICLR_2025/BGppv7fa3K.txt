009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing deployment of AI is shaping the future landscape of the internet,
012            which is set to become an integrated ecosystem of AI agents. Orchestrating the
013            interaction among AI agents necessitates decentralized, self-sustaining mechanisms
014            that harmonize the tension between individual interests and social welfare. In this
015            paper we tackle this challenge by synergizing reinforcement learning with principal-
016            agent theory from economics. Taken separately, the former allows unrealistic
017            freedom of intervention, while the latter struggles to scale in sequential settings.
018
               Combining them achieves the best of both worlds. We propose a framework where
               a principal guides an agent in a Markov Decision Process (MDP) using a series of
019
               contracts, which specify payments by the principal based on observable outcomes
020
               of the agent’s actions. We present and analyze a meta-algorithm that iteratively
021            optimizes the policies of the principal and agent, showing its equivalence to a
022            contraction operator on the principal’s Q-function, and its convergence to subgame-
023            perfect equilibrium. We then scale our algorithm with deep Q-learning and analyze
024            its convergence in the presence of approximation error, both theoretically and
025            through experiments with randomly generated binary game-trees. Extending our
026            framework to multiple agents, we apply our methodology to the combinatorial
027            Coin Game. Addressing this multi-agent sequential social dilemma is a promising
028            first step toward scaling our approach to more complex, real-world instances.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The deployment of AI agents is becoming increasingly prevalent across every facet of society (see,
      e.g., the survey of Wang et al., 2024a). Recent advancements have enabled large language models
034
      to leverage external tools via function-calling capabilities (e.g. Srinivasan et al., 2023; Li et al.,
035
      2024). For example, models like GPT-4 can now interface with databases, APIs, and other AI
036   agents, significantly broadening their range of applications. Looking ahead, we anticipate the future
037   internet to connect not only traditional computing devices but also everyday objects powered by AI
038   agents. These developments mark a shift toward more integrated agent ecosystems, and necessitate
039   scalable mechanisms for orchestrating efficient interactions among AI agents in a decentralized and
040   heterogeneous environment.
041
      However, learning efficient interactions is inherently complex. AI agents are typically hosted by
042   different entities, and the parties involved in these interactions have individual interests, leading to a
043   natural tension between their incentives and long-term overall welfare. Sequential social dilemmas
044   (SSDs) (Leibo et al., 2017) capture this tension, highlighting how optimization of each agent’s
045   own reward in a Markovian environment may result in suboptimal global outcomes. Multi-agent
046   reinforcement learning (MARL) attempts to address this challenge using methods like reward shaping,
047   which adjusts the agents’ reward functions to align their incentives. While effective at optimizing
048   global objectives, these methods typically assume centralized control over each agent’s training
049   procedure, providing unrealistic freedom of intervention to their designer, as well as overlooking the
050
      costs of said interventions.
051   This discrepancy prompts us to look to the economics literature, where complex incentive structures
052   between independent self-interested parties are focal. One of the primary directions in this line of
053   work is the celebrated principal-agent theory (e.g., Holmström (1979); Grossman & Hart (1983);
      Rogerson (1985); Spear & Srivastava (1987)). It distinguishes between agents, who directly take


                                                          1
054
      costly actions to complete tasks, and the principal, who receives rewards from the execution of those
055   tasks. The main tool of this theory are contracts, which define payments from the principal to the
056   agent, based on observable outcomes of the agents’ actions. Conceptually, this theory fits well with
057   the requirements of modeling interaction among AI agents: Some applications may explicitly involve
058   a principal – for example, a navigation app benefiting from drivers exploring new routes (Ben-Porat
059   et al., 2024). In other applications, including general frameworks like SSDs, a principal can serve as
060   a helpful abstraction that represents social objectives and long-term global welfare.
061   Contract theory was recognized with the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2016, and has recently garnered
062   interest from the computer science community (e.g., Ho et al. (2016); Dütting et al. (2019); Dütting
063   et al. (2023)). However, the learning of contracts has largely been confined to single-round settings or
064   relies on strict assumptions about agent capabilities (Zhu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Guruganesh
065   et al., 2024; Scheid et al., 2024). These limitations leave contract theory inadequately equipped to
066   handle the complexities of future AI agent ecosystems. Motivated by this gap, we aim to synergize
067   reinforcement learning and contract theory—we propose learning contracts as a scalable mechanism
068   to align agents’ incentives, and employ (multi-agent) reinforcement learning to extend contract
069
      learning to general, sequential settings.
070   Our Contribution. We begin by formulating a general framework for principal-agent reinforcement
071   learning with a single agent (Section 2). In this framework, the agent learns a policy for an MDP, and
072   the principal learns to guide the agent using a series of contracts. Importantly, we explicitly model
073   the (usually misaligned) preferences of the principal and the agent.
074   We first examine this problem from a purely economic perspective (Section 3) with full access to the
075   MDP, thus alleviating the need for learning. We focus on the standard solution concept of subgame-
076   perfect equilibrium (SPE, Section 3.1). We formulate a meta-algorithm (Algorithm 1) that iteratively
077   optimizes the principal’s and agent’s policies in their respective MDPs with the other player’s policy
078   being fixed. We show that the meta-algorithm finds an SPE in a finite-horizon game in at most T + 1
079   iterations, where T is the horizon (Theorem 3.3). We also show that the meta-algorithm can be
080   viewed as iteratively applying a contraction operator to the principal’s Q-function (Theorem 3.4),
081
      ensuring its monotonic improvement with each iteration.
082   Next, in Section 4, we turn to the standard model-free RL setting where the MDP is unknown, and
083   the policies are learned by sampling stochastic transitions and rewards through interacting with the
084   MDP. We instantiate the meta-algorithm (see Algorithm 2) by solving both principal’s and agent’s
085   MDPs with (deep) Q-learning and apply it in a two-phase setup. First, we train the principal assuming
086   centralized access to the agent’s optimization problem (of maximizing its Q-function estimate given
087
      a contract in a state). Then, we lift the assumption and validate the learned principal’s policy against
      a black-box oracle agent, mimicking its execution in the real world. We analyze this learning
088
      setup theoretically in the presence of approximation errors, including a tool for addressing potential
089
      deviations of the oracle agent through extra payments, which we call nudging. We present a host of
090   experiments in a controlled environment with randomly generated binary game-trees, and verify that
091   our algorithm approximates SPE well despite approximation errors and even without nudging.
092
093
      Finally, in Section 5, we extend our model to multiple agents and identify sequential social dilemmas
      (SSDs, Leibo et al. (2017)) as a natural testbed for principal-agent RL. Complementing the prior
094
      work that has approached SSDs through multi-agent RL and reward shaping, we adopt a more
095
      economic perspective by framing interventions into reward functions as payments made by an
096   external, benevolent principal. The principal’s objective is to maximize social welfare through
097   minimal intervention. We present extensive experiments with a prominent SSD, the Coin Game
098   (Foerster et al., 2018), where two agents (blue and a red) collect blue and red coins in a grid world.
099   Collecting a coin of the matching color is five times as rewarding to an agent as collecting one of the
100   opposite color, suggesting cooperation by collecting only coins of agents’ respective colors. This
101   seemingly innocent grid world is highly combinatorial in terms of states (all possible agent and coin
102   positions on a 7x7 grid), actions (all possible sequences across 50 timesteps), and agents (all possible
103   opponent policies). The complexity is further elevated when adding the principal to the mix. Through
104
      our two-phase experimental setup – centralized training followed by black-box validation – we verify
      the approximate convergence of our method to SPE in the Coin Game. We also compare to a simpler,
105
      hand-coded payment scheme inspired by Christoffersen et al. (2023), and observe that with the same
106
      amount of subsidy, this less-targeted intervention achieves a significantly lower welfare level.

      1.1       R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Our work fits into the wider literature on automated mechanism design (Conitzer & Sandholm, 2002),
111   in particular approaches based on deep learning (Dütting et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023) also known
112   as differentiable economics (Dütting et al., 2024). Most closely related from this line of work, Wang
113   et al. (2023) consider stateless one-shot contracting problems and provide a network architecture for
114   capturing the discontinuities in the principal’s utility function. We differ from this work in our focus
115   on sequential contracting problems and the entailing unique challenges.
116   There is a number of algorithmic works that focus on repeated principal-agent interactions on MDPs,
117   including work on environment design and policy teaching (Zhang & Parkes, 2008; Zhang et al.,
118   2009b; Yu & Ho, 2022; Ben-Porat et al., 2024). Our approach differs from these earlier works in
119   several ways, including that we actively search for the best (unconstrained) equilibrium in the game
120   between the principal and the agent through reinforcement learning. A closely related line of work,
121   including Gerstgrasser & Parkes (2023), is concerned with learning Stackelberg equilibria in general
122
      leader-follower games, including games on MDPs. Our work differs in its focus on SPE, which is the
      more standard equilibrium concept in dynamic contract design problems. Several works have studied
123
      repeated contract design problems from a no-regret online-learning perspective (Ho et al., 2016;
124
      Zhu et al., 2023a; Guruganesh et al., 2024; Scheid et al., 2024). However, these works are typically
125   limited to stateless and/or non-sequential interactions. A prominent exception is a contemporaneous
126   study by Wu et al. (2024) that introduces a model of principal-agent MDPs nearly identical to ours,
127   barring an important notational distinction of encoding outcomes as next states.1 However, their and
128   our studies pursue orthogonal algorithmic developments: whereas they treat contract policies as arms
129   of a bandit and minimize regret, we rely on deep RL to scale to large MDPs and multiple agents.
130
      Starting with the work of Leibo et al. (2017), there is a huge literature on SSDs. Most closely related
131   in this direction is the work by Christoffersen et al. (2023) on multi-agent RL and applications to
132   SSDs. This work pursues an approach in which one of the agents (rather than the principal) proposes
133   a contract (an outcome-contingent, zero-sum reward redistribution scheme), and the other agents
134   can either accept or veto. They consider several SSDs and show how hand-crafted contract spaces
135   strike a balance between generality and tractability, and can be an effective tool in mitigating social
136   dilemmas. An important distinction of our work is that we distinguish between principal and agent(s),
137   and insist on the standard limited liability requirement from economics. Furthermore, in our approach
138   the principal learns the conditions for payments, allowing it to utilize contracts in their full generality.
139
      Also, our method has an interpretation as learning k-implementation (Monderer & Tennenholtz,
      2003). We provide additional details on the related literature in Appendix A.

148   2.1       T HE S TATIC H IDDEN -ACTION P RINCIPAL -AGENT P ROBLEM

      In a classic principal-agent problem, the principal wants the agent to invest effort in a task. The agent
150
      has a choice between several actions a ∈ A with different costs c(a), interpreted as effort levels. Each
151
      action stochastically maps to outcomes o ∈ O according to a distribution O(a), with higher effort
152   levels more likely to result in rewarding outcomes, as measured by the principal’s reward function
153   rp (o). By default, a rational agent would choose the cost-minimizing action. To incentivize the agent
154   to invest effort, the principal offers a contract b prior to the action choice. Crucially, the principal
155   is unable to directly view the agent’s hidden action, so the contractual payments b(o) are defined
156   per outcome o ∈ O. Payments are always non-negative – this is referred to as limited liability. The
157   principal seeks an optimal contract: a payment scheme b that maximizes the principal’s E[utility],
158   Eo∼O(a) [rp (o)−b(o)], given that the agent best-responds with a = arg maxa′ Eo∼O(a′ ) [b(o)]−c(a′ ).
159   If costs and outcome distributions are known, the optimal contract can be precisely found using
160   Linear Programming (LP): for each action a ∈ A, find the contract that implements it (makes the

189   In order to extend contract design to MDPs, we assume a principal-agent problem in each state of the

191   a hidden-action principal-agent MDP is a tuple M = (S, s0 , A, B, O, O, R, Rp , T , γ). As usual
192   in MDPs, S is a set of states, s0 ∈ S is the initial state, and A is a set of n agent’s actions a ∈ A.
193
      Additionally, O is a set of m outcomes o ∈ O, B ⊂ Rm         ≥0 is a set of principal’s actions (contracts)
194   b ∈ B, and b(o) is the o-th coordinate of a contract (payment). In each state, the outcome is sampled
195

196

197

      3.1   S UBGAME -P ERFECT E QUILIBRIUM (SPE)
233
      In what follows let G be a principal-agent stochastic game. Let a subgame of G in state s ∈ S be a
234   game G ′ defined by replacing s0 with s, and S with a subset of states that can be reached from s.
235
236
      Observation 3.1. Fixing one player’s policy in G defines a (standard) MDP for another player. In
      particular, a principal’s policy defines the agent’s MDP by modifying the reward function through
237
      contracts; likewise, an agent’s policy defines the principal’s MDP by modifying the transition
238
      and reward functions through the agent’s responses to contracts. For exact formulations of these
239   MDPs, see Appendix B.2. The optimal policies in both MDPs can be assumed w.l.o.g. to be
240   deterministic (in single-agent setting), and can be found with any suitable dynamic programming or
241   RL algorithm (Sutton & Barto, 2018).
242
243   Agent’s perspective. In the agent’s MDP defined by ρ, refer to the optimal policy as best-responding
244   to ρ (where ties are broken in favor of the principal, as is standard in contract design). Define
245   a function π ∗ that maps a principal’s policy ρ to the best-responding policy π ∗ (ρ). Denote the
246
      action prescribed by π ∗ (ρ) in state s given contract b by π ∗ (s, b | ρ) ≡ π ∗ (ρ)(s, b). Note that the
      best-responding policy is defined in s for any b ∈ B and is not limited to the principal’s action ρ(s).
247
248   Principal’s perspective. Similarly, in the principal’s MDP defined by π, refer to the optimal policy as
249   subgame-perfect against π. Define a function ρ∗ that maps an agent’s policy π to the subgame-perfect
250   policy ρ∗ (π). Denote the contract prescribed by ρ∗ (π) in state s by ρ∗ (s | π) ≡ ρ∗ (π)(s). In all
                                                                                                  ∗
251   states, this policy satisfies: ρ∗ (s | π) ∈ arg maxb Q∗ (s, b | π), where Q∗ (s, b | π) ≡ Qρ (π) (s, b | π)
252   – that is, in each subgame, the principal takes the optimal action.
253   Definition 3.2. A subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) of G is a pair of policies (ρ, π), where the
254   principal’s policy ρ ≡ ρ∗ (π) is subgame-perfect against an agent that best-responds with π ≡ π ∗ (ρ).
255
256   SPE is the standard solution concept for extensive-form games (Osborne, 2004). It always exists and is
257   essentially unique (see Lemma B.12 for completeness). Compared to non-subgame-perfect solutions
258
      like the well–studied Stackelberg equilibrium, SPE can lose utility for the principal. However, it is
      considered more realistic since it disallows non-credible threats, i.e., commitments by the principal
259
      to play a suboptimal contract if one of the subgames is reached. Moreover, Gerstgrasser & Parkes
260
      (2023) show that learning a Stackelberg equilibrium necessitates the principal to go through long
261   episodes of observing the learning dynamic of the agent, with only sparse rewards (see also Brero
262   et al. (2022)). SPE, in contrast, naturally fits RL, as both players’ policies solve their respective
263   MDPs. We demonstrate the difference on our running example in Appendix B.1.

313   outer optimization tasks of the meta-algorithm with Q-learning. These tasks correspond to finding
314   optimal policies in the respective agent’s and principal’s MDPs defined in Observation 3.1. We
315   operate in a standard model-free RL setting, where learning is performed through interactions with a
316   black-box MDP. For both principal and agent, we introduce modified Q-functions, which we formally
317   derive as fixed points of contraction operators in Appendix B.3. We detail deep implementations in
318   Appendix D.

      5.1   P ROBLEM S ETUP
389
390   Both our principal-agent model and our theory for the meta-algorithm can be extended to multi-agent
391   MDPs. First, we formulate an analogous principal-multi-agent MDP, where a principal offers a
392   contract to each agent, and payments are determined by the joint action of all agents. We treat joint
393   actions as outcomes and omit hidden actions. Then, the theory extends by viewing all agents as a
394   centralized super-agent that selects an equilibrium joint policy (in the multi-agent MDP defined by the
395   principal). Finally, we address the issue of multiple equilibria by imposing an additional constraint
396
      on the incentive-compatibility of contracts, making our approach more robust to deviations. See also
      Appendix C, where we illustrate the multi-agent model on Prisoner’s Dilemma.
397
398   A principal-multi-agent MDP is a tuple MN = (S, s0 , N, (Ai )i∈N , B, T , (Ri )i∈N , Rp , γ). The
399   notation is as before, with the introduction of a set of k agents, N , and the corresponding

402   agent; bi denotes a contract offered to agent i, and bi (a) denotes a payment to i determined by joint
403   action a ∈ AN ; T : S ×AN → ∆(S) is the transition function; Ri : S ×AN ×B → R is the reward
                                                                                         p               k

405

408   Additionally, ρ : S → B k is the principal’s policy, and πi : S × B → ∆(Ai ) is an agent’s policy.
409   Because B grows exponentially with the number of agents k, in our implementation, the principal
410   gives an action recommendation to each agent, and the payments are determined after agents act.
411
      Analogously to Observation 3.1, a fixed principal’s policy ρ defines a multi-agent MDP by changing
412
      the agents’ reward functions. Importantly, this MDP can itself be analyzed as a Markov game between
413   the agents (Littman, 1994). In this game, we use a basic solution concept called Markov Perfect
414   Equilibrium (MPE, Maskin & Tirole (2001)), defined as a tuple of agents’ policies π ∗ (ρ) such that
415   the following holds:
416                                       π ∗ (ρ)
417
                        ∀s, bi , i, πi : Vi i       (s, bi | π ∗−i (ρ), ρ) ≥ Viπi (s, bi | π ∗−i (ρ), ρ).    (4)
418   Here, π ∗−i (ρ) denotes equilibrium policies of agents other than i, and Viπi (· | π −i , ρ) is the value
419   function of agent i playing πi given that other agents play π −i and principal plays ρ. In MPE, no
420   agent has a beneficial, unilateral deviation in any state.
421   Call MPE π ∗ (ρ) a best-responding joint policy; in case there are multiple, assume that agents break
422   ties in favor of the principal. This assumption allows agents to freely coordinate the joint action,
423   similarly to the equilibrium oracle of Gerstgrasser & Parkes (2023). The principal’s subgame-perfect
424   policy is defined by ρ∗ (s | π) ∈ arg maxb Q∗ (s, b | π), and an SPE is defined as a pair of policies
425   (ρ, π ∗ (ρ)) that are respectively subgame-perfect and best-responding against each other.
426   With this, our theory in Section 3 can be extended to the multi-agent model. Particularly, convergence
427   proofs of Algorithm 1 apply with the swap of notation and the new definition of best-responding policy
428   π ∗ (ρ). However, implementing this theory is problematic because of how strong the tie-breaking
429   assumption is. In practice, there is no reason to assume that decentralized learning agents will
430   converge to any specific equilibrium. For example, even in simple games such as Iterated Prisoner’s
431   Dilemma, RL agents typically fail to converge to cooperative Tit-for-Tat equilibrium (Foerster et al.,
      2018; Willis et al., 2023).

437   This way, an agent prefers πi∗ (ρ) regardless of other players’ policies. We refer to contracts that
438   make a strategy profile dominant as Incentive-Compatible (IC), and to the IC contracts that minimize

441

443   5.2      A S EQUENTIAL S OCIAL D ILEMMA : T HE C OIN G AME
444
445   In this section, we augment a multi-agent MDP known as the Coin Game (Foerster et al., 2018) with a
446
      principal and conduct a series of experiments. These experiments complement our theoretical results
      by empirically demonstrating the convergence of our algorithm to SPE in a complex multi-agent
447
      setting. In addition, we find a minimal implementation of a strategy profile that maximizes social
448
      welfare in a complex SSD, which is a novel result of independent interest.
449
450   Environment. The Coin Game is a standard benchmark in Multi-Agent RL that models an SSD
451   with two self-interested players that, if trained independently, fail to engage in mutually beneficial
452
      cooperation. This environment is highly combinatorial and complex due to a large state space and
      the inherent non-stationarity of simultaneously acting and learning agents. Each player is assigned a
453
      color, red or blue, and collects coins that spawn randomly on a grid. Players earn +1 for collecting a
454
      coin of their color and +0.2 for other coins. 2 Our experiments are carried out on a 7 × 7 grid with
455   each episode lasting for 50 time steps; results on a smaller grid are provided in Appendix D.2.
456
457   Experimental procedure. We implement the two-phase approach described in Section 4. We
458
      parameterize principal’s and agent’s Q-functions as the respective deep Q-networks, θ and ϕ. The
      principal is trained to maximize social welfare (sum of rewards) of the agents using VDN (Sunehag
459
      et al., 2018), and the agents’ networks share parameters. Given the complexity of the Coin Game,
460
      validating the resulting policy against oracle agents is infeasible. Instead, at validation phase we train
461   black-box agents from scratch as independent DQNs. For details and pseudocode, see Appendix D.2.
462
463   For baselines, we compare against a heuristic that distributes a constant proportion of social welfare.
464
      For a fair comparison, this proportion is set to be exactly equal to the proportion that our method ends
      up paying after the validation phase. This heuristic is at the core of approaches that improve social
465
      welfare through contractual agreements between agents (Christoffersen et al. (2023), see Appendix A).
466
      We also include a selfish baseline with self-interested agents in the absence of contracts, and an
467   optimal baseline where agents are fully cooperative and directly maximize social welfare. These are
468   instances of the constant proportion baseline with the proportion set to 0 and 1, respectively.
469
470
      5.3      E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
471
472   The results are presented in Figure 2. The social welfare metric (Fig. 2a) shows a gap between the
473   performances of selfish and optimal baselines, confirming the presence of a conflict of interests.
474   During training, our algorithm finds a joint policy that matches the optimal performance, and is
475   implemented with an average payment of just above 30% of social welfare, substantially reducing
476   the intervention into the agents’ rewards compared to the optimal baseline (Fig. 2b).
477
      After the validation phase, the social welfare and the proportion paid to agents closely match the
478   corresponding metrics in training. Furthermore, the agents follow the principal’s recommendations in
479   around 80% to 90% of states in an average episode (Fig. 2c). These results suggest that the principal
480   closely approximated the SPE, as agents deviate only rarely and in states where it does not hurt
481   social welfare. Given the challenges of convergence of independent RL agents to mutually beneficial
482   equilibria, we find this success quite surprising, and attribute it to the IC property of the principal.
483   From the perspective of an agent, there could be other optimal policies against different opponents,
484   but following the principal’s recommendations is robust against any opponent.

526   6    C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we develop a scalable framework that combines contract theory and reinforcement
530   learning to address decentralized single- and multi-agent RL problems. Our framework offers formal
531
      definitions, a simple algorithmic blueprint, and a proof of convergence to subgame-perfect equilibrium.
      We implement our approach with deep RL, validate its performance empirically, and introduce a
532
      theoretical analysis in the presence of approximation errors, including a nudging mechanism that
533
      uses small extra payments to discourage potential deviations by the agent. We demonstrate the
534   framework’s effectiveness for solving sequential social dilemmas, showing how it can maximize
535   social welfare with minimal intervention to agents’ rewards. Our findings suggest several promising
536   directions for future research, including scaling to even more complex environments, exploring
537   partially-observable settings where different agents have different observations, and incorporating
538   randomized contracts for enhanced agent coordination. We believe our work will inspire further
539   exploration at the intersection of principal-agent theory and RL, potentially yielding novel solutions
      to complex AI agent interactions.

898   A.1    AUTOMATED M ECHANISM D ESIGN
899
900   Our study concerns finding an optimal way to influence the behavior of one or multiple agents
901   in an environment through optimization, it can thus be attributed to the automated mechanism
902   design literature. The field was pioneered by Conitzer & Sandholm (2002; 2003; 2004); the early
903
      approaches mostly concerned optimal auctions and relied on classic optimization and machine
      learning algorithms (Likhodedov et al., 2005; Lahaie, 2011; Sandholm & Likhodedov, 2015; Dütting
904
      et al., 2015; Narasimhan et al., 2016). The field has received a surge of interest with the introduction
905
      of RegretNet (Dütting et al., 2019) – a deep learning based approach to approximately incentive-
906   compatible optimal auction design. This inspired multiple other algorithms for auction design (Rahme
907   et al., 2021; Duan et al., 2022; Ivanov et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b), as well as
908   applications of deep learning to other economic areas such as multi-facility location (Golowich et al.,
909   2018), two-sided matching markets (Ravindranath et al., 2021), E-commerce advertising (Liu et al.,
910   2021), and data markets (Ravindranath et al., 2023).
911
      Notably, a deep learning approach to contract design has been recently proposed by Wang et al.
912   (2023) as a viable alternative to linear programming in problems with a high number of actions and
913   outcomes. Since our investigation focuses on scenarios where the primary source of complexity
914   comes from large state spaces rather than action or outcome spaces, we do not use their approximation
915   technique. At the heart of their approach is a novel neural network architecture specifically designed
916   to approximate discontinuous functions. Given that the principal’s Q-function, Qρ (s, b | π), in our
917   setting is discontinuous with respect to b, this architecture holds potential to bring further scalability
      and practicality to our approach; we leave this direction as future work.

      A.2    A LGORITHMIC C ONTRACT D ESIGN
919
920   A body of work studies repeated principal-agent interactions in games either stateless or with states
921   unobserved by the principal (such as agent types). Depending on the model, learning an optimal
922   payment scheme can be formalized as a bandit problem with arms representing discretized contracts
923   (Conitzer & Garera, 2006; Ho et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 2023b) or as a constrained dynamic programming
924   problem (Renner & Schmedders, 2020). Scheid et al. (2024) extend the bandit formulation to a
925   linear contextual setting and propose a learning algorithm that is near-optimal in terms of principal’s
926   regret. Zhang et al. (2023) formulate a so-called steering problem where a mediator can pay no-regret
927   learners throughout repeated interactions and wishes to incentivize some desirable predetermined
928
      equilibrium while satisfying budget constraints. Similarly, Guruganesh et al. (2024) study a repeated
      principal-agent interaction in a canonical contract setting, with the agent applying no-regret learning
929
      over the course of interactions. Li et al. (2021) study contracts for afforestation with an underlying
930
      Markov chain; they do not extend to MDPs and do not apply learning.
931
932   There is also work on policy teaching, which can be seen as the earliest examples of contract
933   design in MDPs. Zhang et al. (2009b) study a problem of implementing a specific policy through
934
      contracts and solve it with linear programming. Zhang & Parkes (2008) additionally aim to find
      the policy itself, which they show to be NP-hard and solve through mixed integer programming.
935
      Contemperaneously with the present work, Ben-Porat et al. (2024) extend these results by offering
936
      polynomial approximation algorithms for two special instances of MDPs. These, as well as our
937   work, can be seen as instances of a more general environment design problem (Zhang et al., 2009a).
938   Crucially, these works focus on MDPs of up to a hundred states. By employing deep RL, we extend
939   to much larger MDPs. Our approach also generalizes to hidden-action and multi-agent MDPs.
940
      Monderer & Tennenholtz (2003) propose k-implementation, which can be seen as contract design
941
      applied to normal-form games. Specifically, the principal wants to implement (incentivize) some desir-
942
      able outcome and can pay for the joint actions of the agents. The goal is to find the k-implementation
943   (we call it a minimal implementation), i.e., such payment scheme that the desirable outcome is
944   dominant-strategy incentive compatible for all agents, while the realized payment k for this outcome
945   is minimal. Our multi-agent problem setup can be seen as learning a minimal implementation of a
946   social welfare maximizing strategy profile in a Markov game.
947
      Related to algorithmic contract design is a problem of delegating learning tasks in the context of
948
      incentive-aware machine learning (Ananthakrishnan et al., 2024; Saig et al., 2023). These studies
949   concern a principal properly incentivizing agent(s) through contracts to collect data or train an ML
950   model in a one-shot interaction.
951
952
953   A.3    M ULTI -AGENT RL (MARL)
954
955   In our applications, we focus on general-sum Markov games where naively trained agents fail to
956   engage in mutually beneficial cooperation – colloquially known as “Sequential Social Dilemmas”
957
      or SSDs (Leibo et al., 2017). The solution concepts can be divided into two broad categories. The
      majority of studies take a purely computational perspective, arbitrarily modifying the agents’ reward
958
      functions (Peysakhovich & Lerer, 2018a;b; Hughes et al., 2018; Jaques et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
959
      2019; Eccles et al., 2019; Jiang & Lu, 2019; Durugkar et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020; Ivanov et al.,
960   2021; Zimmer et al., 2021; Phan et al., 2022) or training procedures (Gupta et al., 2017; Foerster et al.,
961   2018; Willi et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022) in order to maximize the aggregate reward. Alternative
962   solutions view the problem as aligning the players’ incentives by modifying the rules of the game
963   to induce better equilibria. Examples include enabling agents to delegate their decision making to
964   a mediator (Ivanov et al., 2023), allowing agents to review each others’ policies prior to decision
965   making (Oesterheld et al., 2023), and adding a cheap-talk communication channel between agents
966   (Lin et al., 2024). Our work should be attributed to the second category as we model the principal-
967   agents interaction as a game. While the principal effectively modifies agents’ reward functions,
968
      the payments are costly. The question is then how to maximize social welfare through minimal
      intervention, which is an open research question.
969
970   The works on adaptive mechanism design (Baumann et al., 2020; Guresti et al., 2023) can be seen as
971   precursors of contract design for SSDs. These consider augmenting the game with a principal-like
      planning agent that learns to distribute additional rewards and penalties, the magnitude of which is

1035   First, consider the SPE notion: At the left subgame, the principal incentivizes the agent to take
1036
       noisy-left by choosing a contract that pays 1 for outcome L and 0 otherwise. This way, both actions
       yield the same value for the agent, 0.9 · 1 + 0.1 · 0 − 0.8 = 0.1 · 1 + 0.9 · 0 = 0.1, and the
1037
       agent chooses aL by tie-breaking in favour of the principal. So the principal’s value in state sL is
1038
       0.9(rp (sL , L) − 1) = 0.9( 14
                                    9 − 1) = 0.5. By the same logic, the principal offers the same contract
1039   in sR and its value equals 0.5 (and the agent’s value equals 0.1). Then, in s0 , the agent is indifferent
1040   between transitioning to sL and sR , so the principal has to offer the same contract again. The
1041   principal’s value in s0 (given that the agent chooses aL ) is 0.5 + 0.9 · 0.5 + 0.1 · 0.5 = 1, and the
1042   agent’s value is 0.1 + 0.9 · 0.1 + 0.1 · 0.1 = 0.2. These are estimated by considering utilities in sL
1043   and sR without discounting (using γ = 1). Note that in this analysis, we found SPE using backward
1044   induction: we first analyzed the terminal states, then the root state.
1045   Compare this with Stackelberg: If non-credible threats were allowed, the principal could threaten to
1046   act suboptimally in the right subgame by always paying the agent 0. Both principal and agent then
1047   value sR at 0. In sL , the contract is the same as in SPE. Knowing this, the agent values sL over sR
1048   (0.1 > 0), which would drive it to choose the noisy-left action at the root state even if the principal
1049   pays only 1 − 0.1 = 0.9 for outcome L. By paying less, the principal’s utility (value in s0 ) would be
1050   higher compared to SPE, ( 14 9 − 0.9 + 0.5) · 0.9 = 1.04 > 1.
1051   This illustrates how Stackelberg equilibrium may produce more utility for the principal, but the
1052   surplus comes at the cost of the inability to engage in mutually beneficial contractual agreements
1053   in certain subgames, even if these subgames are reached by pure chance and despite the agent’s
1054   best efforts. On the one hand, Stackelberg requires more commitment power from the principal.
1055   Whereas in SPE the agent can be certain that the principal sticks to its policy in future states because
1056   it is optimal in any subgame, in Stackelberg, the principal has to preemptively commit to inefficient
1057   contracts and ignore potential beneficial deviations. On the other hand, Stackelberg is not robust to
1058   mistakes: if the agent mistakenly chooses the wrong action, it might be punished by the principal,
1059
       losing utility for both players. This is especially concerning in the context of learning, where mistakes
       can happen due to approximation errors, or even due to the agent exploring (in online setups). SPE is
1060
       hence a more practical solution concept.

1065   A (standard) MDP is a tuple (S, S0 , A, R, T , γ), where S is a set of states, S0 ∈ S is a set of
1066   possible initial states, A is a set of actions, T : S × A → ∆(S) is a stochastic transition function,
1067   R : S × A × S → P(R) is a stochastic reward function (R(s, a, s′ ) is a distribution and may depend
1068   on the next state s′ ), γ is an (optional) discounting factor. Assume finite horizon (as in Section 2).
1069   Consider a hidden-action principal-agent MDP M = (S, s0 , A, B, O, O, R, Rp , T , γ) as defined in
1070   Section 2. First, consider the agent’s perspective. Let the principal’s policy be some ρ. This defines
1071   a standard MDP (S a , S0a , A, Ra , T a , γ) that we call the agent’s MDP, where: The set of states is
1072   S a = S × B (infinite unless B is discretized). The set of initial states is S0 = {s0 } × B. The set of
1073   actions is A.
1074   The transition function T a : S a × A → ∆(S a ) defines distributions over next state-contract pairs
1075   (s′ , ρ(s′ )), where s′ ∼ T (s, o ∼ O(s, a)). Note that the next state s′ is sampled from a distribution
1076   that is a function of state-action pairs (s, a) marginalized over outcomes, and the next contract ρ(s′ )
1077   is given by the principal’s policy. Informally, the agent expects the principal to stick to its policy
1078   in any future state. At the same time, since the state space S a is defined over the set of contracts
1079   B, the agent may adapt its policy to any immediate contract in the current state, and thus its policy
       π : S a → ∆(A) is defined by π(s, b) for any b ∈ B.

       Let St ⊆ S denote the set of all possible states at time step t. For example, S0 = {s0 }. States in ST
1277   are terminal by definition. We assume that sets St are disjoint. This is without loss of generality, as
1278   we can always redefine the state space of a finite-horizon MDP such that the assumption holds; e.g.,
1279   by concatenating the time step to a state as snew
                                                     t     = (st , t). In other words, any finite-horizon MDP
1280   can be represented as a directed acyclic graph.
1281
       The next lemma is a precursor to proving the convergence of Algorithm 1 and concerns its single
1282
       iteration. Given a pair of policies that form an SPE in all subgames but the original game, it states
1283   that performing one additional iteration of the algorithm (update the agent, then the principal) yields
1284   an SPE in the game. This is because the agent observes the contract offered in a state and adapts
1285   its action, in accordance with our definition of the agent’s MDP in Appendix B.2. In particular,
1286   the agent’s best-responding policy is defined as π ∗ (s, b | ρ) for any pair (s, b), so in s0 , the agent
1287   best-responds with π ∗ (s0 , b | ρ) for any b regardless of the principal’s policy ρ(s0 ).
1288   Lemma B.8. Given a finite-horizon principal-agent stochastic game G and a principal’s policy
1289   ρ, if (ρ, π ∗ (ρ)) is an SPE in all subgames with a possible exception of G (i.e., all subgames in
1290   s ∈ S \ {s0 }), then (ρ∗ (π ∗ (ρ)), π ∗ (ρ)) is an SPE in G.
1291
1292   Proof. In s0 , the agent observes the principal’s action. Consequently and because the agent best-
1293   responds to ρ, it also best-responds to any {ρ′ | ∀s ∈ S \ {s0 } : ρ′ (s) = ρ(s)} regardless of the
1294   offered contract ρ′ (s0 ), including the subgame-perfect ρ∗ (π ∗ (ρ)) (that only differs from ρ in s0 , as
1295   subgames in other states are in SPE already). Thus, (ρ∗ (π ∗ (ρ)), π ∗ (ρ)) is an SPE in G, as well as in
       all subgames of G (by Observation B.7).

1328   pendix B.3, solving this task can be interpreted as finding the fixed point of a contraction operator Bρ

1331   Observation B.11. H∗ is a composition of linear operators and thus is a linear operator.

1333   For a linear operator H∗ to be a contraction, its fixed point has to be unique. Since its iterative
1334   application (the meta-algorithm, Algorithm 1) converges to an SPE, we next prove the uniqueness of
1335   Q-functions in SPE.
1336   Lemma B.12. Given a finite-horizon principal-agent stochastic game G, the principal’s Q-function
1337   is equal in all SPE for any state-action; the same holds for the agent’s Q-function.
1338
1339   Proof. Consider a principal’s policy ρ that forms SPE with any best-responding agent’s policy π ∗ (ρ).
1340   Any π ∗ (ρ) solves the agent’s MDP and thus all such policies define a unique agent’s Q-function
1341   (which is the fixed point of the Bellman optimality operator). Furthermore, by the assumption that
1342   the agent breaks ties in favor of the principal, all best-responding policies also uniquely define the
1343   principal’s Q-function (in other words, any policy that solves the agent’s MDP but does not maximize
1344   the principal’s Q-function when breaking ties in some state is not a best-responding policy by the
1345   assumed tie-breaking). Thus, for any pair of SPE with non-equal Q-functions, the principal’s policies
1346
       must also differ.
1347   Consider two principal’s policies, ρx and ρy , that form SPE with any respective best-responding
1348   agents’ policies, π ∗ (ρx ) and π ∗ (ρy ). By the above argument, the choice of π ∗ (ρx ) and π ∗ (ρy ) is
1349   inconsequential, so we can assume those to be unique (e.g. by adding lexicographic tie-breaking if
       the principal-favored tie-breaking does not break all ties).

1759
       Consider a standard one-step Prisoner’s Dilemma with the payoff matrix as in Table 3a. Here, the
       only equilibrium is mutual defection (DD), despite cooperation (CC) being mutually beneficial. How
1760
       should a benevolent principal change the matrix through payments to incentivize cooperation?
1761
1762   One answer is that CC should become an equilibrium through minimal payments in CC. In one of
1763   the payment schemes that achieve this, the principal pays a unit of utility to both players for the
1764   CC outcome, resulting in the payoff matrix as in Table 3b.4 However, note that DD remains an
1765
       equilibrium for the agents. In fact, the new payoff matrix is also a social dilemma known as the Stag
       Hunt. In the context of (decentralized) learning, there is no reason to expect the agents to converge
1766
       to CC instead of DD, and convergence to suboptimal equilibria has been observed empirically in
1767
       generalized Stag Hunt games (Peysakhovich & Lerer, 2018b).
1768
1769   As a more robust approach, the principal can make action C dominant. This is stronger than just
1770   making CC an equilibrium because each agent will prefer action C regardless of the actions of others.
1771
       To achieve this, in addition to paying a unit of utility to both agents in CC, the principal pays two units
       of utility to the cooperating agent in CD and DC, resulting in the payoff matrix as in Table 3c. This is
1772
       the kind of solution we implement in our application to SSDs, where we formulate the principal’s
1773
       objective as learning a social welfare maximizing strategy profile and its minimal implementation.
1774
1775   Importantly, in the context of our principal-agent model, the minimal implementation is consistent
1776   with SPE in that the principal minimizes payments when agents best-respond by following recom-
1777
       mendations (CC in our example). So the IC property is an additional constraint, which specifies
       an otherwise ambiguous objective of finding the principal’s policy in SPE, and which only requires
1778
       additional payments in equilibrium.

1932   (following the principal’s recommendations) or rational deviation (default optimal policy disregarding
1933   contracts, which gives the same utility). We encapsulate this behavior in a binary variable fi ∈ {0, 1},
1934   indicating whether agent i follows the recommendation ap . Then, Qϕi is conditioned on the joint f−i
1935   variable of the other agents, indicating both the immediate outcome and the agents’ future behavior.
1936   The efficacy of this simplification is validated through our experimental results.

       Hyperparameters. The neural networks consist of 1 hidden convolutional layer followed by 2
       hidden fully connected layers and an output layer. The convolutions have 4 × 4 kernels and transform
1979
       the initial 4-channel states into 32 channels. The fully connected layers consist of 64 neurons. All

1981   each iteration including a single environment interaction and a single gradient update on a mini-batch

1983   updated by copying the parameters of the online networks. The learning rate is initialized at 0.0005
1984   and is exponentially annealed to 0.0001 throughout the training. Similarly, the exploration rate ϵ is
1985   initialized at 0.4 and is linearly annealed to 0 throughout the training. The discounting factor is set at
1986   γ = 0.99. For more efficient training, we use prioritized replay buffers (Schaul et al., 2015) with a
1987   maximum size of 100000, α = 0.4, β = 0, and ϵ = 10−7 .

1991   state space and the horizon are smaller.
1992   During training, our algorithm finds an approximately optimal joint policy (Fig. 4a) and estimates
1993   that about 30% of social welfare is sufficient for an IC implementation (Fig. 4c). Interestingly
1994   and contrary to our previous results, the validation phase does not confirm this: we observe that
1995   the validation agents fall short of the optimal performance, as well as that our algorithm does not
1996   outperform the constant proportion baseline (Fig. 4a). On the one hand, we do not find evidence that
1997   it does not converge to SPE, as in Figure 4e, the utility ratio hovers around 1. On the other hand, a
       test on incentive compatibility (Fig. 4f) reveals that the validation agents consistently find policies

2002
       As a remedy, we implement a form of nudging intended to counteract the approximation errors, as
2003
       discussed in Appendix B.9. Specifically, we increase the offered payments in each state by 10% of
2004
       the social welfare; we increase the proportion of the constant baseline accordingly. The effect of this
2005   modification is illustrated in Figure 4b: the performance of our algorithm reaches that of the optimal
2006   baseline, whereas the constant baseline still falls short. Furthermore, the IC property appears fixed
2007   as the utility ratio decreases to around 1 (Fig. 4f). The accuracy metric also improves, raising the
2008   frequency of agents following the principal’s recommendation from around 80% to around 90%.
2009
       Overall, we believe these results to be positive: our algorithm falls short somewhat predictably given
2010
       the practical approximation of IC (and the tie-breaking assumption), and still manages to outperform
2011   the constant baseline while paying the same. As a side note, these results also showcase the usefulness
2012   of our two-step experimental procedure, as opposed to the usual performance comparison during the
2013   training phase, which does not reveal the hidden issues.
