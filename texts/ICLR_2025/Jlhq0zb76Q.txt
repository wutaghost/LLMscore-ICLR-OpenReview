009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have drawn attention for their ability to gen-
012            erate high-quality images with precise text alignment. However, these models can
013            also be misused to produce inappropriate content. Existing safety measures, which
014            typically rely on text classifiers or ControlNet-like approaches, are often insuffi-
015            cient. Traditional text classifiers rely on large-scale labeled datasets and can be
016            easily bypassed by rephrasing. As diffusion models continue to scale, fine-tuning
017
               these safeguards becomes increasingly challenging and lacks flexibility. Recent
               red-teaming attack researches further underscore the need for a new paradigm
018
               to prevent the generation of inappropriate content. In this paper, we introduce
019
               SteerDiff, a lightweight adaptor module designed to act as an intermediary be-
020            tween user input and the diffusion model, ensuring that generated images adhere
021            to ethical and safety standards with little to no impact on usability. SteerDiff iden-
022            tifies and manipulates inappropriate concepts within the text embedding space
023            to guide the model away from harmful outputs. We conduct extensive experi-
024            ments across various concept unlearning tasks to evaluate the effectiveness of our
025            approach. Furthermore, we benchmark SteerDiff against multiple red-teaming
026            strategies to assess its robustness. Finally, we explore the potential of SteerDiff
027            for concept forgetting tasks, demonstrating its versatility in text-conditioned im-
028
               age generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have attracted attention for their out-of-the-box functionality
034
      and the superior quality of their generated images. Using models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach
035
      et al., 2022; 2021) or DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2022), users can use simple natural language de-
036   scriptions as input to generate high-quality images with precise text alignment. This capability is
037   largely contributed by pre-trained language models (Hammoud et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2020; Doso-
038   vitskiy, 2020) that learn and reflect the underlying syntax and semantics, as well as by extensive
039   multimodal training datasets that encompass a wide range of text-to-image aligned content. How-
040   ever, these training methods also introduce risks of generating inappropriate images to the models.
041   Thus, preventing the generation of inappropriate images is both critical and urgent.
042   To this end, existing T2I diffusion models have integrated several safety strategies to prevent in-
043   appropriate content generation. Stable diffusion incorporates a Not Safe For Work (NSFW) post-
044   generation filter and ad-hoc filter during training to avoid generating unsafe images (Schuhmann
045   et al., 2022; Schramowski et al., 2023; Rando et al., 2022). However, existing text-based attacks (Li
046   et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2020; Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020; Maus et al., 2023) can mislead the classi-
047   fication mechanisms in the filter by rephrasing “A photo of a billboard showing a naked man.” into
048   “A photo of a billboard showing an LGBT man in an explicit position”. Additionally, users report
049   that removing explicit images and other subjects from training data may have had a negative impact
050
      on the output quality, thus harming the utility of the models (Rombach et al., 2022).
051   The prevention of inappropriate content generation in AI models has primarily been approached
052   through concept removal, which aims to control the generation process itself. Two primary strate-
053   gies have been explored: (1) using ControlNet-like (Zhang et al., 2023a) structures to guide the
      diffusion process (Schramowski et al., 2023; Rando et al., 2022), and (2) identifying and pruning


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      weights within the diffusion model (Gandikota et al., 2023). However, these methods face signifi-
055   cant challenges as generative models grow in scale and complexity. The increasing size of models
056   makes these approaches computationally expensive and impractical (Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach
057   et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022). Additionally, simply guiding the generative process introduces
058   vulnerabilities, leaving models open to jailbreaking attempts (Zhang et al., 2023b; Qu et al., 2023).
059   These limitations highlight the need for more efficient and robust solutions.
060
      Although the aforementioned safety mechanisms have shown effectiveness according to their re-
061   spective evaluation schemes, recent red-teaming studies demonstrate their potential flaws (Zhang
062   et al., 2023b; Qu et al., 2023). Chin et al. (2023) shows that approximately half of the prompts,
063   which were originally mitigated by existing safety mechanisms, can be manipulated by their Prompt-
064   ing4Debugging (P4D) to become problematic. Similarly, Unsafe Diffusion (Qu et al., 2023)
065   found that 14.56% of generated images across four state-of-the-art T2I models and their four
066   prompt datasets were unsafe, underscoring the vulnerability of these models to generating harm-
067   ful content. Moreover, black-box jail-breaking approaches Jailbreak Prompt Attack (JPA) and
068   SneakyPrompt (Ma et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) successfully attack both online services and
069
      offline T2I models with the current safety mechanisms.
070   In this work, we propose SteerDiff, a two-stage lightweight adaptor model for text-conditioned dif-
071   fusion models that focuses on guiding text prompt embeddings rather than controlling the generative
072   process. Our method constructs a semantic boundary that maximally distinguishes between safe and
073   unsafe content. We then project potentially unsafe embeddings toward the safe region while pre-
074   serving the original semantics and maintaining the diffusion model’s generative capabilities. This
075
      approach offers three key advantages: efficiency, effectiveness, and versatility. By operating at the
      prompt embedding level, our method eliminates the need for computationally intensive model re-
076
      training while preserving the original semantics. Moreover, by preventing unsafe content at the
077
      earliest stage of generation, we can block the formation of unsafe latent representations more di-
078   rectly and reliably. Lastly, our lightweight approach can be easily trained and applied to various
079   concept removal tasks.
080
081
      We benchmark our prototype SteerDiff against state-of-the-art concept removal techniques, in-
      cluding Erased Stable Diffusion (ESD) (Gandikota et al., 2023) and Safe Latent Diffusion
082
      (SLD) (Schramowski et al., 2023; Rando et al., 2022), for removing inappropriate content. Ex-
083
      perimental results demonstrate that SteerDiff significantly reduces inappropriate content generation
084   while preserving image quality and semantic fidelity. Furthermore, we evaluate its robustness by de-
085   fending against red-teaming methods such as P4D and SneakyPrompt, demonstrating the effective-
086   ness of our approach in mitigating various forms of adversarial attacks on text-to-image generation
087   systems.

      2.1   T EXT- TO -I MAGE D IFFUSION M ODELS
092
093   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) are a type of
094   probabilistic generative model that learns a data distribution by gradually transforming a simple dis-
095   tribution into a complex target distribution. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho
096   et al., 2020) model the data generation process as a sequence of denoising steps. Given an image,
097   DDPMs progressively add noise sampled from Gaussian distribution to generate an intermediate
098   noisy image xt at each time step t called forward diffusion steps. The noisy image xt can be ex-
099   pressed in closed form as a function of the original image x0 , the time step t, and noise ϵ sampled
100   from a Gaussian distribution N (0, I). The model is then trained using the reverse process, where
101
      the objective is to learn a model parameterized by θ that predicts ϵ. This objective is defined as:
102
                                        L = Et,x0 ,ϵ ∥ϵ − ϵθ(xt , t)∥22 .

105   2.2   T OWARDS S AFE I MAGE G ENERATION
106
107   Current approaches to prevent undesirable images from generation generally follow two main strate-
      gies. The first involves removing undesired images from the training set, such as excluding all hu-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      man figures (Nichol et al., 2021) or selectively omitting specific undesirable image categories in the
109   dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2022; DAL, 2022; Rombach & Esser, 2022). However, this approach is
110   costly as it necessitates retraining the model, and removing certain data classes often degrades the
111   overall quality of the generated images (O’Connor, 2022). The second strategy is post-hoc, includes
112   using blacklists for blocking unsafe concepts (DAL, 2022; mid, 2024; Leo, 2023; Markov et al.,
113   2023), editing images after generation, or fine-tuning diffusion models to guide the inference pro-
114   cess (Schramowski et al., 2023). Although blacklists are straightforward to implement, they can be
115   easily bypassed. Image editing and diffusion process manipulation methods are effective but still re-
116   quire image synthesis, adding computational overhead. Other approaches (Park et al., 2024; Zhang
117
      et al., 2024) use inpainting to mask unsafe content or attempt to unlearn inappropriate concepts either
      within the diffusion model (Zheng & Yeh, 2023) or the textual encoder (Poppi et al., 2023). These
118
      methods involve expensive fine-tuning, while SteerDiff offers an intermediary solution that oper-
119
      ates between the input prompt and the diffusion model without additional training. Similarly, Latent
120   Guard (Liu et al., 2024) identifies inappropriate concepts prior to diffusion by learning a latent space
121   on top of the T2I model’s text encoder. However, while Latent Guard is capable of detecting inap-
122   propriate concepts, it lacks the ability to remove them. In this work, we compare the naive blacklist
123   method commonly used in commercial platforms, Erased Stable Diffusion (Gandikota et al., 2023),
124   the state-of-the-art model-editing approach, and Safe Latent Diffusion (Schramowski et al., 2023),
125   a guidance-based model. Our focus is to introduce a new approach that identifies and manipulates
126   inappropriate concepts in the text embedding space to ensure safer image generation.
127
128   3   M ETHODOLOGY
129
130   Determining what constitutes inappropriate imagery is highly subjective and varies based on context,
131   setting, cultural and social predispositions, and individual factors. Additionally, Qu et al. (2023)
132   observes that unsafe content can be shared through memes. In the context of T2I models, these
133   new concepts are embedded within the words of a prompt. Consequently, adding new concepts
134   to block T2I generation without retraining the diffusion model is impractical. To overcome these
135   limitations, we formalize the problem by identifying unsafe concepts in text embedding space and
136
      then projecting potentially unsafe embeddings toward safe regions. This projection preserves the
      original semantics while maintaining the diffusion model’s generative capabilities. Our approach
137
      allows us to define blacklisted concepts at test time, enabling greater flexibility.

166   3.1     T RAINING DATA C OLLECTION
167
168   Directly classifying safe/unsafe prompts requires large-scale annotated datasets (Markov et al.,
169   2023). Following Safe Latent Diffusion, we base our definition of inappropriate content on the
170   work of Gebru et al.: “[data that] if viewed directly, might be offensive, insulting, threatening, or
171   might otherwise cause anxiety” (Gebru et al., 2021; Schramowski et al., 2023). Specifically, we
172
      consider an image as inappropriate if it contains any concept c ∈ Cunsafe , where
173                     Cunsafe ={hate, harassment, violence, self-harm, sexual content,
                                                                                                          (1)
174                              shocking images, illegal activity}.
175
176   It is important to note that the definition of inappropriateness is not restricted to these seven cate-
177   gories, as the boundaries of appropriateness vary across cultures and evolve over time. In this study,
178   however, we limit our scope to images that display clear and tangible acts of inappropriate behavior.
179   Although we limit our scope to the current Cunsafe , our framework can be extended beyond these
180
      categories, as discussed in section 5.

      3.1.1    I DENTIFIER DATASET
182
183   As mentioned previously, we aim to detect inappropriate concepts in prompts to avoid inappropri-
184   ate content generation. The first step in our pipeline is to construct the dataset with unsafe terms
185   capturing the concepts from Cunsafe . To achieve this, we start by collecting open-sourced blacklisted
186   phrases. Midjourney (mid, 2024) employs a blacklist of words and phrases that includes phrases
187   associated with violence, hate speech, explicit sexual content, illegal activities, and other categories
188   considered inappropriate by the platform. Additionally, Latent Guard (Liu et al., 2024) introduces
189   the CoPro dataset, which includes safe and unsafe prompts centered around blacklisted concepts.
190
      We build our blacklist based on Midjourney and CoPro dataset.
191   Although we can collect numerous NSFW terms from open-source datasets, these datasets may be
192   imbalanced and could lack some categories defined in Cunsafe . For example, the number of shocking
193   images or illegal activity is lower compared to images in other categories. To tackle this, we leverage
194   an LLM to generate related terms tc centered around one sampled concept c in the blacklist of Cunsafe
195   as illustrated in Figure 1, similarly to Hammoud et al. (2024) and Liu et al. (2024). This allows us
196
      to create a set Tunsafe = {tc |c ∈ Cunsafe }. The unsafe terms in Tunsafe simulate typical unsafe terms
      that a malicious user may input. In addition to the unsafe terms, we combine randomly selected 500
197
      prompts (Blue block on the left) from the COCO 30k dataset (Lin et al., 2014) and the following
198
      mentioned paired safe phrases dataset (Green block on the left) to serve as our safe prompt dataset.
199   All phrases within these prompts are considered safe.

201   3.1.2    S TEER M ODEL DATASET
202
203   In our subsequent steering transformation training procedure, we synthesize additional safe terms to
204   steer unsafe embeddings toward safe ones. The core idea is to associate each unsafe term tc ∈ Tunsafe
205
      with a corresponding safe term tc ′ of similar meanings, allowing us to convert unsafe concepts into
      safe alternatives while preserving the original semantic intent of the prompt. For example, consider
206
      the term “killed” in the prompt “A man got killed.” which represents a violent visual scene linked
207
      to the concept of “violence”. We use an LLM to eliminate unsafe concepts in the input term tc .
208   In this case, a possible safe term tc ′ would be “saved”, transforming the meaning of the prompt to
209   something like “A man got saved”. By processing all elements in Tunsafe , we generate paired safe
210   phrases dataset Tsafe , comprising M safe terms tc ′ ∈ Tsafe . For more details of the synthesized
211   dataset, please refer to subsection A.2.

215   To ensure the generation of safe and appropriate content, we detect and mitigate undesirable con-

217   enabling precise identification of inappropriate content.
218
219   This task can be framed as classifying phrases as either appropriate or inappropriate. We employ
220

221

223   nor “you” is related to the concept “hate”. To address such cases, we utilize a sliding window
224   technique to identify single terms and phrases that may collectively express inappropriate concepts.

      3.3   S TEERING T OWARD S AFE C ONTENT
263
      To steer the inappropriate prompts toward generating safe images, we propose to learn a linear
264   transformation to the embedding of identified unsafe prompts. This transformation shifts unsafe
265   concepts toward safe ones while preserving original semantic meaning. The intuition behind this
266   approach is that linear transformations of word embeddings can effectively steer the generation
267   style of language models (Han et al., 2023). Since SteerDiff operates in word embedding space,
268   applying these transformations enables us to adjust unsafe embeddings and steer the diffusion model
269   toward producing safe outputs. Specifically, let E denote the embedding of a word, we define the
      transformation as follows:


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
271                                    Esteered = ϵW Eunsafe + (1 − ϵ)Eunsafe                                (3)
272
273   In this equation, Esteered represents the adjusted embedding after steering, Eunsafe refers to the original
274   embedding of the unsafe phrases in Tunsafe , ϵ is a steering parameter that controls the intensity of the
275   transformation, and W is a linear transformation matrix learned during training.
276   The key concept is to map the embedding of inappropriate content to safe content through a linear
277   transformation. By applying Equation 3, we shift the embedding within the semantic space, guiding
278   it toward regions associated with safe imagery. The steering parameter ϵ offers fine-grained control
279   over the degree of transformation, allowing flexibility in balancing semantic preservation and safety.
280   To learn the transformation matrix W , we employ a supervised learning method using a paired
281   dataset of unsafe phrases and their corresponding safe phrases, as described in subsection 3.1. The
282   training process minimizes the following loss function:

284                                          L = |Esafe − W · Eunsafe |                                      (4)

286   where Esafe represents the embedding of the safe phrases in Tsafe .

312   Consider a T2I model equipped with a text encoder. As illustrated in Figure 3, a user provides
313   a prompt p, which can be either safe or unsafe. The input prompt is first embedded by a text
314   encoder. We define a concept blacklist Cunsafe that contains potentially inappropriate concepts. Then
315   the identifier detects whether prompt p contains any inappropriate concepts c′ ∈ Cunsafe . If such a
316   concept is detected, a linear transformation is applied to the prompt’s embedding to steer it toward
317   safer content. Specifically, the transformation described in Equation 3 is applied to the embedding of
318   p, altering the latent representation to avoid generating inappropriate content. This transformation
319   adjusts the embedding space without compromising the semantics of the original prompt. Once
320
      the transformation is applied, the modified embedding is passed through the diffusion model to
      generate a safe image. The transformed embedding ensures that the resulting image adheres to SFW
321
      guidelines while preserving the user’s original intent.
322
323   This approach allows SteerDiff serving as an intermediary between the user’s input and the diffu-
      sion model, ensuring that the generated images align with ethical and safety standards. Additionally,

      this steering process is efficient, eliminating the need for model retraining or introducing significant
325   delays in the image generation pipeline. The transformation can also be dynamically adapted as
326   blacklists or safety criteria evolve, making the system robust and scalable for real-world applica-

342   inappropriate, while allowing otherwise provocative images (Schramowski et al., 2023; Wu et al.,

      2024; Ma et al., 2024). Unless otherwise specified, an image was classified as inappropriate if at

346   generating inappropriate images with state-of-the-art defense model Safe Latent Diffusion (SLD)

      from P4D and Sneakyprompt, we conducted our evaluation on the I2P dataset using P4D testbed

530
531
      The evolution of diffusion models in generating intricate images highlights both their potential and
      associated risks. Although recent unlearning methods for diffusion models have made notable
532
      progress in mitigating inappropriate content generation, red-teaming studies reveal that these de-
533
      fenses can still be bypassed. Moreover, many defense strategies rely on fine-tuning the model to
534   avoid generating inappropriate content, which becomes increasingly challenging as diffusion mod-
535   els grow larger. In this paper, we present SteerDiff, a lightweight method that acts as an intermediary
536   between the user’s input and the diffusion model, ensuring that generated images comply with ethical
537   and safety standards. We conduct comprehensive experiments across various unlearning concepts
538   to evaluate their effectiveness. Additionally, we benchmark SteerDiff using multiple red-teaming
539   approaches to assess the robustness of our method. Lastly, we explore the potential of SteerDiff in
      concept removal tasks.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   In this work, we introduce a paradigm to identify inappropriate concepts within input prompts and
543   steer their embeddings to mitigate the generation of unsafe content. Unlike previous methods that
544   primarily focus on post-hoc prevention or concept removal, SteerDiff operates directly on the em-
545   beddings of the input prompts, prior to the diffusion process. By intervening earlier in the generation
546   pipeline, we aim to more effectively prevent the propagation of unsafe content.
547
      However, the real-world application of SteerDiff requires carefully defining what constitutes inap-
548   propriate concepts, which may vary depending on the application domain. Defining these concepts is
549   a non-trivial task and is likely to require input from human experts, potentially leading to subjective
550   biases. These biases may stem from the social and cultural context in which the system is deployed,
551   as the definition of inappropriateness is highly subjective and dependent on societal norms, which
552   differ across regions and communities. Moreover, since the notion of inappropriateness is largely
553   defined by social norms, the system’s performance may vary depending on whose norms are re-
554   flected in the training data. This introduces the risk of reinforcing the biases present in the data,
555   as SteerDiff may disproportionately represent the values of the social groups most prominent in the
556
      training set.
557   Our testbed for evaluating inappropriateness is limited to specific, predefined concepts, which may
558   not fully capture the diversity of opinions and sentiments regarding what is considered inappropri-
559   ate. As societal norms evolve, so too must the definitions of inappropriateness used by SteerDiff.
560   This necessitates regular updates to the training data and model parameters to ensure the continued
561   relevance and fairness of the system.
562   Beyond the identification and mitigation of inappropriate content, we believe that SteerDiff can be
563   applied to other areas, such as concept or artistic style removal. As discussed in section 5, SteerDiff
564   has the potential to be extended to various applications, including the removal of specific artistic
565   styles or other undesired concepts in generative models. However, such applications must also
566   carefully consider the ethical implications of content modification, as indiscriminate use of these
567   techniques could lead to censorship or the suppression of artistic expression.
568   In summary, while SteerDiff offers a promising approach to mitigating unsafe content generation,
569   its reliance on subjective definitions of inappropriateness and the potential for reinforcing societal
570   biases limit its scope and fairness.

759   A.1     BACKGROUND
760
761   A.1.1    T EXT- TO - IMAGE DIFFUSION MODEL
762
763   Text-to-image (T2I) generation has recently seen significant advancements with the advent of dif-
764   fusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; 2021; DAL, 2022), which have shown remarkable ability to
765
      synthesize high-quality images from textual descriptions. Diffusion models operate by gradually
      denoising a noisy image, starting from pure Gaussian noise, until a coherent image is formed. The
766
      integration of textual information into the diffusion process differentiates T2I diffusion models from
767
      traditional diffusion models (Ho & Salimans, 2022), enabling the generation of images that align
768   closely with a given textual input.
769
770   In T2I diffusion models, a pre-trained language model, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), is com-
771
      monly employed to convert text prompts into embeddings. These embeddings are then incorporated
      into the noise prediction model at various stages of the denoising process. By conditioning the
772
      image generation process on these embeddings, the model learns to generate images that not only
773
      match the content described in the prompt but also capture finer details of the semantics conveyed
774   by the text. The use of large-scale pre-trained models allows for generalization across a wide range
775   of prompts, enabling the synthesis of highly detailed and contextually appropriate images.
776
777
      Moreover, recent advances have introduced techniques such as classifier guidance (Ho & Salimans,
      2022) and score-based models, which further improve the control over the generated images by
778
      adjusting the noise gradients based on the provided text. These methods have made T2I diffusion
779
      models not only capable of generating realistic images but also flexible in terms of creative control,
780   making them highly valuable for various generative applications.
781
782
783   A.1.2    R ED - TEAMING TEXT- TO - IMAGE DIFFUSION MODELS
784
785   While T2I diffusion models have demonstrated impressive capabilities, they also pose potential risks
786   related to the generation of harmful or inappropriate content. Red-teaming (Ganguli et al., 2022;
787   Perez et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024), the practice of stress-testing AI systems to uncover vulnerabilities,
788   has become a crucial area of research in ensuring the safety and ethical use of these models. In the
789   context of T2I diffusion models, red-teaming (Chin et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024;
790
      Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhuang et al., 2023; Tsai et al., 2023) focuses on detecting and mitigating
      the generation of undesirable or unsafe content, such as offensive imagery or representations of
791
      inappropriate concepts.
792
793   Given that T2I diffusion models rely heavily on textual prompts to guide image generation, vul-
794   nerabilities often arise from the misalignment between the text embeddings and the visual content
795   produced. Adversaries could potentially exploit these models by crafting malicious prompts that
796
      subtly bypass content filters. As a result, recent work has explored methods to steer these models
      away from generating unsafe outputs, such as by applying transformations to the prompt embed-
797
      dings (Han et al., 2023), or by integrating ethical constraints into the generative process. The chal-
798
      lenge lies in maintaining the model’s ability to generate diverse and creative images while ensuring
799   that the output adheres to safety guidelines. Addressing this issue is essential for deploying these
800   models in real-world applications, particularly in sensitive domains such as media, art, and content
801   moderation.

890   A.5   L IMITATION
891
892   In addition to the ethical concerns discussed earlier, SteerDiff has certain technical limitations. One
893   key limitation is the use of a hard-coded steering degree during the experiments, which may lead to
894   over-steering in some cases. This is particularly problematic for prompts containing mildly inappro-
895   priate content, where excessive steering could result in unnecessary modifications, thereby altering
896   the intended meaning of the prompt. A more dynamic and context-aware approach could mitigate
897   this issue by adjusting the steering degree based on the severity of the unsafe content, ensuring more
898
      precise control without compromising the prompt’s original intent.

901   A.6   E XAMPLES OF G ENERATED I MAGES
902
903   In our evaluation of different models—SD v1.4, SteerDiff, ESD, SLD STRONG, and SLD MAX.
904   We observe clear trade-offs between the ability to mitigate inappropriate content and the quality of
905   generated images.
906   SteerDiff consistently demonstrates superior mitigation of inappropriate content while preserving
907   the generative capabilities of the diffusion model. The images produced by SteerDiff maintain
908   higher fidelity, with details and clarity closely resembling those from the baseline SD v1.4, making
909   it the most balanced approach in terms of both safety and image quality. On the other hand, ESD and
910   SLD STRONG offer competitive image quality but occasionally fail to fully suppress inappropriate
911   content generation. These models generate visually appealing images with relatively high quality,
912   especially in complex textures and objects, but their inconsistency in filtering undesirable elements
913   presents a notable shortcoming in safety-critical applications.
914   SLD MAX achieves a mitigation performance comparable to SteerDiff in terms of defense against
915   inappropriate content generation. However, this comes at the cost of image quality, particularly in
916   depictions of human faces. Images generated with SLD MAX tend to blur facial features, reduc-
917   ing the overall aesthetic quality. This blurring effect is less pronounced in non-human objects but
      remains a significant limitation in scenarios requiring fine detail preservation.

      Overall, SteerDiff stands out as the most effective model for generating high-quality, safe content,
919   whereas SLD MAX provides strong mitigation at the expense of visual detail, particularly in more
920   nuanced areas like facial generation.
