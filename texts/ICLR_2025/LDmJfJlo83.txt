011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce Generative-Semantic Entropy Estimation (GSEE), a model-
014            agnostic algorithm that efficiently estimates the generative uncertainty associated
015            with foundation models, while requiring no additional auxiliary model inference
016
               steps. In principle, for any foundation model input data, GSEE numerically es-
               timates the uncertainty encapsulated in the internal, semantic manifold of the
017
               LLM generated responses to the input data. In this way, high uncertainty is in-
018
               dicative of hallucinations and low generative confidence. Through experiments,
019            we demonstrate the superior performance of GSEE for uncertainty quantifica-
020            tion (UQ) amongst state-of-the-art methods across a variety of models, datasets
021            and problem settings, including: unbounded language prompting, constrained
022            language prompting, variable generative stochasticity, acute semantic diversity
023            prompting and as a barometer for hallucinations and predictive accuracy.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, powerful foundation models, including Large Language Models (LLMs) (Naveed
029   et al., 2023; Minaee et al., 2024) and Large Multi-Modal Models (LMMs) (Cui et al., 2024; Liu
030   et al., 2023a) have ushered in a new epoch of multi-faceted, intelligent conversational agents. De-
031   spite their significant early successes and widespread use, foundation models nevertheless currently
032   suffer from several critical shortcomings, including their lack of transparency and predilection for
033
      “hallucinations.” (Huang et al., 2023) These deficiencies severely limit the trustworthiness and po-
      tential deployment of LLMs for real-world applications, particular in safety-critical domains. The
034
      widespread adoption and future success of LLMs and related models is critically dependent upon
035
      efforts to improve their transparency and explainability.
036
037   While the problem of uncertainty quantification (UQ) has enjoyed a rich and extensive research
038   focus across many classical machine learning and deep learning (Gawlikowski et al., 2023) applica-
039
      tions, LLM and related natural language generation (NLG) problem settings pose unique challenges
      to UQ. This difficulty primarily stems from: (i) the unbounded character of NLG and (ii) complexi-
040
      ties surrounding efforts to effectively and reliably quantify semantic structure (Kuhn et al., 2023). In
041
      the former case, reliable uncertainty quantification for variable-length NLG tasks is complicated by
042   a variety of issues, including ambiguity of model confidence probing, e.g., which output token(s) are
043   most representative of model confidence, (Chen et al., 2024) and sensitivity to generative text length
044   (Bengio et al., 2003) – to name but two critical issues. In addition, precise methods to capture model
045   uncertainty are also complicated by the problem of how to ascertain semantic self-consistency for
046   NLG (Malinin & Gales, 2021).
047
      With these challenges in mind, we introduce Generative-Semantic Entropy Estimation (GSEE) a
048   lightweight, model-agnostic algorithm to estimate UQ for LLM and other generative models. Our
049   method renders predictive uncertainty in a mathematically-principled fashion, by numerically esti-
050   mating the uncertainty encapsulated in the internal, semantic manifold of the LLM generated re-
051   sponses to the input data. GSEE follows a series of straightforward steps: (1) Given an input datum
052   (text/text + image), we render multiple text outputs with the generative model through a single for-
053   ward pass; (2) we then extract latent embeddings for each of these generated outputs; (3) next, we
      calculate the covariance matrix with respect to these mean-centered semantic embeddings; (4) lastly,

054
      we define the model predictive uncertainty as the spectral-entropy of this covariance matrix, viz., the
055   entropy of the distribution of eigenvlues of the principal components of the generated outputs of
056   the model. This measure is known to approximate the effective dimension of the semantic manifold
057   spanned by the generated outputs (Zhang et al., 2024). Hence, here, larger spectral-entropy corre-
058   sponds with larger semantic diversity in the generated outputs, and thus higher model uncertainty.
059
      The current work provides the following contributions:
060
061          • We define a novel, mathematically-principled algorithm, GSEE, to approximate uncertainty
062            quantification for LLM and related foundation models. GSEE operates on the internal states
063            of an LLM, yielding a holistic measure of semantic self-consistency in model responses.
064            Concretely, we leverage spectral-entropy to estimate the information content in the seman-
065            tic manifold of generated responses.
066          • We test our algorithm against established UQ methods on a variety of essential experimen-
067            tal settings, including across LLM and LMM model types.
068
             • We introduce several nuanced experimental conditions to benefit UQ understanding and
069
               hallucination detection for LLMs, including what we term: constrained language prompt-
070            ing and high stochaticity conditioning.
071
072   At a high level, improving uncertainty quantification for LLMs and related models can greatly ex-
073   pand the usefulness and applicability of these nascent models Zhao et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023b).
074   In particular, reliable UQ can help facilitate better LLM performance, enhance LLM-related hu-
075   man interaction and help foster trust in foundational model dependent systems. In terms of core
076   algorithm advantages, GSEE does not require additional natural language inference (NLI) (Welleck
077
      et al., 2019) or related auxiliary model processing steps – boosting its relevance for real-time and
      computationally-constrained environments; GSEE is moreover model-agnostic and outperforms
078
      other baseline techniques on many challenging UQ tasks, as we show in our experimental results.

081   2   BACKGROUND AND P RIOR W ORK
082
083   Much of the prior art for uncertainty quantification and hallucination detection with LLMs and re-
084   lated foundation models relies on the thesis that when a model is uncertain about its generated out-
085   put, this answer distribution tends to exhibit high variability. In the domain of language generation
086   specifically, this variation can be codified with respect to the notion of semantic consistency (Raj
087
      et al., 2023), predictive uncertainty, or entropy. As such, for example, a semantically differentiated
      answer distribution is more likely to be erroneous (or hallucinatory) than a semantically consistent
088
      distribution.
089
090   UQ methods for LLMs generally fall into two broad categories of black-box and white-box tech-
091   niques for measuring semantic consistency, predictive uncertainty and variability in the generated
092   answer distribution. Black-box techniques rely on analyzing these quantities with respect to the
093
      model outputs – this is to say, in the textual domain. Many such techniques exist (Lin et al., 2022;
      Wang et al., 2022), including, notably, Lexical Similarity (Liu et al., 2023a) which leverages a sim-
094
      ilarity measure using a bespoke natural language inference (NLI) model (e.g., BERT (Devlin et al.,
095
      2018)) to assess semantic consistency across generated outputs by averaging similarity over gener-
096   ated output pairs. In this vein, much previous related work leverages NLI models to compare the
097   semantic similarity of LLM-generated responses or some other NLI-related measure, including en-
098   tailment, equivalence and consistency (Bowman et al., 2015). Two basic drawbacks of these prior
099   methods are that they (i) require additional model compute/inference to approximate semantic simi-
100   larity and (ii) the semantic similarity measure relies on extrinsic uncertainty estimates, independent
101   of the LLM itself, thus adding undesirable noise to the UQ estimation process.
102   White-box models by contrast directly leverage internal representations of the generative process
103   (e.g., hidden layer activations) to estimate predictive uncertainty. Perplexity (Ren et al., 2023) de-
104   fines predictive uncertainty as the joint probability of output tokens for a single generated output.
105   While Perplexity is an effective and widely-used measure of semantic consistency in literature, it is
106   nevertheless known to suffer from brittleness due to the instability of token-level likelihoods (Wang
107   et al., 2024). To mitigate these deficiencies, other techniques rely on multiple output NLG, where
      semantic diversity and related metrics account for larger sample outputs. The works closest to the

      current work include (Chen et al., 2024) and (Wei et al., 2024). The former differs from the current
109   work in two fundamental ways: (1) we include length normalization directly into our UQ metric in
110   order to reduce semantic consistency sensitivity to output length; (2) because GSEE is calculated
111   from spectral-entropy, our measure of uncertainty accounts for the (comparatively richer) holistic

114   model latent space (whereas ours reflects entropy in generated model outputs); moreover they lever-
115   age this UQ metric to assess the information content in training datasets for LLMs, and not UQ for
116   NLG prediction.

118   3   G ENERATIVE -S EMANTIC E NTROPY E STIMATION
119
120   Formally, suppose we wish to approximate the predictive uncertainty (for hallucination detection,
121   improved generative performance, etc.) of a large language – or multi-modal – foundational model,
122   denoted f (·) with respect to an input language (or multi-modal) datum x. To estimate predictive
123   UQ, we generate multiple language outputs for the input data and then approximate the semantic
124   diversity of the low-dimensional manifold encapsulating these generated outputs. A large semantic
125   diversity indicates high model uncertainty. GSEE proceeds in four total steps:
126   (1) Generate a set of M language responses for the input data (modulated via the temperature pa-
127   rameter of the model):
128
129                                                            i=M
130                                            f (x) → {ŷ(i) }i=1                                        (1)
131
132   (2) Next, we extract latent embeddings, denoted z ∈ Rl , where l denotes the latent embedding
133   dimension, from f for each of these generated outputs:
134
135                                                i=M            i=M
                                            {ŷ(i) }i=1 → {z(i) }i=1                                      (2)
136
137
      GSEE is agnostic to the locus of latent embedding extraction for f . However, following current best
138   practices, we extract rich semantic embeddings by averaging penultimate layer representations of
139   generated output tokens.
140
141
      (3) Next, we center these embeddings by mean-subtracting; we then calculate the covariance of
      these centered embeddings, where Z ∈ RM ×l represents the corresponding centered data matrix:
142

143
                                        Cov {z(i) }i=1
                                                   i=M
                                                          = ZT Z                                  (3)
144
145
146   We denote the covariance matrix above as Σ ∈ RM ×M .
147
      (4) Finally, we define the uncertainty quantification of f with respect to the input x as the length-
148   normalized spectral-entropy of Σ, which we define as the entropy of the normalized spectrum of
149   Σ, denoted H(ΣN ):
150
151
152                                         U Q(f (x)) = H(ΣN )/µy                                        (4)
153
154   Above, µy is defined as the mean length of the generated output set {ŷ(i) }i=M
                                                                                  i=1 . We include length-
155   normalization in 4 to reduce our UQ formulation sensitivity to textual length (Raj et al., 2023).
156   To gain a better intuition as to why the calculation above helps quantify model uncertainty, recall that
157   resolving the eigendecomposition of a centered covariance matrix is equivalent to solving the for-
158   mal principal components analysis (PCA) problem for dimensionality reduction (Abdi & Williams,
159   2010). Thus in 4, we compute the entropy of the spectrum of Σ, which is to say we are approxi-
160   mating the “information content”, viz., the effective subspace rank of the semantic manifold (Roy &
161   Vetterli, 2007) encapsulated by the set: {z(i) }i=M
                                                        i=1 }. Simply put, the more “semantically diverse”
      the set of outputs for the generative model, the higher the predictive uncertainty.

      It is helpful to visualize an idealization of the preceding ideas; we provide this visualization in
179
      Figure 1; experimental evidence supporting this postulate is given in Figure 2. When an LLM is
180
      more certain of its response to input data, it tends to produce a set of semantically unified responses
181   (this result is well-known in research literature (Lin et al., 2022)). Conversely, much like their
182   human counterparts, LLMs tend to “flub” when they are uncertain. Thus, when a generative model
183   is confident in its reply, a small number of differentiated, principal axes emerge in the semantic
184   feature space, giving rise to a non-isotropic semantic manifold (yielding small spectral-entropy),
185   whereas the absence of major semantic axes tends to give rise to isotropic manifolds exhibiting
186   large spectral-entropy.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
191
192   To validate the effectiveness of GSEE, we ran a variey of UQ-related experiments, including: con-
193   ventional experiments aimed to evaluate UQ performance against baseline LLM UQ methods (Sec-
194   tion 4.2), novel, supplemental UQ experiments (Section 4.3) and ablation studies (Section 4.4).
195
      Models. Our experiments comprise four different open-source LLM and LMM model types, includ-
196
      ing: GPT2-xl (Radford et al., 2019) and GPT3 (Brown et al., 2020) LLMs, and TinyLLaVA-3.1B
197
      (Jia et al., 2024) (with Phi-2 LLM backbone), TinyLLaVA-2.0B (with Stable-LM2 LLM backbone)
198   LMMs. In each case, we employ pre-trained Hugging Face instances of these models, with no
199   fine-tuning applied.
200
201
      Datasets. We use four standard datasets for our experimental evaluations: Stanford Question An-
      swering Dataset (SQuAD) (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), a reading comprehension dataset consisting of
202
      question-answer pairs culled from Wikipedia articles, Conversational Question Answering Dataset
203
      (CoQA) (Reddy et al., 2019), consisting of question-answer pairs generated from conversations, as
204   well as the MM-Vet benchmark (Yu et al., 2023) and LAVA-Bench (in-the-wild) datasets (Li et al.,
205   2024) which evaluate LMMs in terms of their integrated vision-language capabilities.
206
207
      Evaluation Metrics. We use standard evaluation metrics from uncertainty estimation literature
      (Lin, 2004). In particular, we calculate the semantic agreement between the NLG output of the
208
      aforementioned LLM and LMMs with the ground-truth answer from the preceding dataset question-
209
      answer and question+image-answer pairs as a measure of correct NLG prediction. Concretely, we
210   use both Rouge-L (Lin, 2004) and Semantic Similarity (SS) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) metrics to
211   quantify agreement between NLG prediction (ŷ) and ground-truth answers (y), i.e., Rouge-L(y, ŷ)
212   and SS(y, ŷ). Next, we report the absolute magnitude of the Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC)
213   between these Rouge-L and Semantic Similarity scores and the model uncertainty score, respec-
214   tively. A large magnitude PCC value indicates strong correlation between the UQ method and the
215   (in-)correctness of the NLG prediction; thus, large correlation implies that the UQ method is a good
      predictor of hallucinations and generative accuracy.

281   4.3   C ONSTRAINED L ANGUAGE P ROMPTING AND H IGH S TOCHASTICITY C ONDITIONING
282
283   For constrained language prompting, we alter the prompting strategy so that for each ground-truth
284   prompt-answer (or prompt+image-answer) pair in a dataset, we augment the prompt set with both
285   “good” and “bad” prompts. More precisely, we stipulate:
286
            • Good prompt: ground-truth prompt from dataset
287
288         • Bad prompt: “(You should ignore the input image). Say something random and incoherent
289           in 1-2 sentences.”
290
      The purpose of introducing this constrained language prompting paradigm is to more directly control
291   against noisy hallucination detection and errors in generative prediction correctness that otherwise
292   degrade UQ evaluation processes. We assert that using this dichotomized prompting strategy sup-
293   ports this aim, as it enables a clearer delineation between correct and incorrect NLG responses.
294   Tables 2 and 3 summarize GSEE performance for constrained language prompting experiments.
295   Overall, GSEE demonstrated best performance compared to baseline models across the test datasets,
296   with particularly strong results on the MM-Vet and CoQA datasets. On the Lava-Bench (in-the-wild)
297   dataset, however, GSEE results were somewhat degraded. We believe that this phenomenon can be
298   attributed, in part, to the relatively small size of this dataset.
299   To further assess the ability of GSEE to differentiate correct vs. incorrect NLG, we record summary
300   statistics of the UQ score across the preceding experiments. These results are shown in Table 4. Our
301   experiments demonstrate the ability of GSEE to capture semantic self-consistency (and its absence)
302   in a generalized setting, across all four of our diverse experimental datasets.
303   We also tested the effectiveness of GSEE in a large semantic diversity setting, which we term high
304   stochasticity conditioning. Concretely, we use the following prompt strategy:
305
306         • Good prompt: “In 1-2 sentences: describe in fine detail what you see in the image.” (for
307           single input image)
308         • Bad prompt: “In 1-2 sentences: describe in fine detail what you see in the image.” (for M
309           randomly sampled image from the dataset)
310
311   The results of these experiments are shown in Table 5; GSEE provides a strong signal for testing
312   semantic self-consistency with high stochasticity conditioning. For “bounded” NLG length experi-
313
      ments, we constrain both good and bad prompts to 1-2 sentences, whereas with “unbounded” NLG
      length experiments, we do not include this stipulation. These experiments are intended to evalu-
314
      ate GSEE under conditions of acute semantic diversity and to furthermore test robustness to NLG
315
      length.
