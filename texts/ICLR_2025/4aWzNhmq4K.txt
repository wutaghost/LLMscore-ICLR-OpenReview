011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large-scale conditional diffusion models (DMs) have demonstrated exceptional
014            ability in generating high-quality images from textual descriptions, gaining
015            widespread use across various domains. However, these models also carry the
016
               risk of producing harmful, sensitive, or copyrighted content, creating a pressing
               need to remove such information from their generation capabilities. While retrain-
017
               ing from scratch is prohibitively expensive, machine unlearning provides a more
018
               efficient solution by selectively removing undesirable knowledge while preserving
019            utility. In this paper, we introduce COncept REconditioning (CORE), a simple
020            yet effective approach for unlearning diffusion models. Similar to some existing
021            approaches, CORE guides the noise predictor conditioned on forget concepts to-
022            wards an anchor generated from alternative concepts. However, CORE introduces
023            key differences in the choice of anchor and retain loss, which contribute to its
024            enhanced performance. We evaluate the unlearning effectiveness and retainabil-
025            ity of CORE on UnlearnCanvas. Extensive experiments demonstrate that CORE
026            surpasses state-of-the-art methods including its close variants and achieves near-
027
               perfect performance, especially when we aim to forget multiple concepts. More
               ablation studies show that CORE’s careful selection of the anchor and retain loss
028
               is critical to its superior performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, large-scale text-to-image generative models, especially Diffusion Models (DM),
034
      have made remarkable advancements in artificial intelligence by exhibiting an unprecedented ability
035
      to create high-resolution, high-quality images from text descriptions (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
036   Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022). The versatility and accessibility of diffusion models have led
037   to their widespread adoption across various industries (Croitoru et al., 2023; Kazerouni et al., 2023;
038   Yang & Hong, 2022; Xu et al., 2022).
039
      Despite their broad utility, diffusion models come with inherent risks due to their extensive training
040
      on diverse datasets. These models have the potential to generate inappropriate, harmful, or legally
041
      sensitive content. For example, Stable Diffusion can produce images that involve pornography, ma-
042   lign stereotypes, and gender and race biases based on the embedded prejudices in their training
043   data, even conditional on non-harmful prompts (Birhane et al., 2021; Schramowski et al., 2023;
044   Larrazabal et al., 2020). They can memorize and reproduce realistic yet inappropriate depictions
045   of individuals without their consent, posing huge privacy risks (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b; Carlini
046   et al., 2023). They can also create misleading or harmful media involving real individuals, such as
047   deepfakes (Mirsky & Lee, 2021). Moreover, they can mimic potentially copyrighted content and
048   replicate styles of real artists, raising legal concerns related to copyright infringement and intellec-
049   tual property rights, as well as undermining artistic originality (Shan et al., 2023; Roose, 2022; Liu,
050
      2022; Popli, 2022; Scenario, 2022; Brittan, 2023).
051   To address these concerns, legislative frameworks such as the European Union’s General Data Pro-
052   tection Regulation (GDPR) (Mantelero, 2013; Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017) and the US’s Cal-
053   ifornia Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) (CCPA, 2018) have established the Right to be Forgotten.
      These laws mandate that applications must support the deletion of personal information contained

      A straightforward method is to retrain the model from scratch using a filtered dataset devoid of in-
      appropriate content. However, this approach is computationally intensive and often impractical due
077
      to the enormous resources required. For instance, training Stable Diffusion 2.0 on a filtered image
078
      set (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Rombach & Esser, 2022) demands approximately 150,000 GPU hours
079   on 256 A100 GPUs. Early attempts to unlearn large-scale generative models include decoding-time
080   guidance and post-generation filtering (Rando et al., 2022; Schramowski et al., 2023); however,
081   these methods do not modify the model weights and can be easily bypassed during deployment. Re-
082   cent research has pivoted towards more robust fine-tuning-based unlearning approaches that modify
083   a model’s weights to effectively forget specific undesirable elements (Gandikota et al., 2023; Fan
084   et al., 2023; Heng & Soh, 2024; Kumari et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wu &
085   Harandi, 2024; Li et al., 2024b). These methods aim to steer the noise predictor in diffusion models
086   away from the target concepts intended to be forgotten by efficiently fine-tuning a small fraction of
087
      parameters.
088   In this work, we propose COncept REconditioning (CORE), a novel, simple, but effective un-
089   learning method for diffusion model. This method leverages a fixed, non-trainable noise to guide
090   the unlearning process, circumventing the need for dual noise predictors or the use of Gaussian noise
091   as a target. CORE specifically alters the noise prediction mechanism for the target images condi-
092   tioned on concepts in the forget set (i.e., forget concepts), aligning them closer to concepts in the
093
      retain set (i.e., retain concepts), thereby blurring the distinction between correctly generated images
      from forget concepts and incorrectly generated ones from retain concepts. We position CORE within
094
      a more general framework of Concept Erasing, and compare our method with other baselines that fit
095
      into this framework. Despite its simplicity, we demonstrate its superiority over existing methodolo-
096   gies through rigorous testing on the UnlearnCanvas framework, and show CORE excels in overall
097   performance including unlearning ability, retainability, and generalization ability, especially when
098   we aim to forget multiple concepts.

112   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
113
114   Malicious Behavior of Diffusion Models. Diffusion models have demonstrated impressive ca-
115   pabilities in generating high-quality, efficient text-to-image outputs (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
116   2020; Rombach et al., 2022). However, these large-scale trained models can pose significant pri-
117   vacy and ethical risks. They are capable of memorizing private images and reproducing objection-
118   able content, such as pornography, private personal photos, malign stereotypes, gender and race
119
      biases (Schramowski et al., 2023; Larrazabal et al., 2020; Carlini et al., 2023; Somepalli et al.,
      2023a; Rando et al., 2022). This mainly stems from the contaminated data sources which involves
120
      problematic image-text pairs (Birhane et al., 2021). Furthermore, diffusion models can cause po-
121
      tential issues about copyright infringement by mimicking, or even replicatiing the styles of some
122   specific artistic and their copyrighted work (Shan et al., 2023). Reports showed that AI-generated
123   arts can sometimes be published commercially (Liu, 2022; Popli, 2022; Scenario, 2022) and even
124   awarded prizes (Roose, 2022), raising more serious social concerns about intellectual property vio-
125   lations (Brittan, 2023; Somepalli et al., 2023b; Shan et al., 2023).
126
127   Diffusion Model Unlearning. The goal of unlearning diffusion models is to eliminate unwanted
128   concepts and their influence on model outputs. Directly retraining a model to remove such concepts
129   is highly resource-intensive and thus inefficient for large diffusion models (Nichol et al., 2021; Rom-
130   bach et al., 2022; Schuhmann et al., 2022). Recent research has explored more efficient unlearning
131
      techniques. One approach focuses on inference-time methods, which attempt to filter or steer the
      model away from undesirable outputs during generation (Rando et al., 2022; Schramowski et al.,
132
      2023). However, these methods are often limited in effectiveness and can be bypassed, particu-
133
      larly in open-source models (SmithMano, 2023). A more robust alternative involves fine-tuning the
134   model’s parameters to actively remove undesirable concepts from its learned representations (Zhang
135   et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b; Lyu et al., 2024; Heng & Soh, 2023; Vyas et al., 2023; Gandikota
136   et al., 2024). Some methods are similar to ours: Gandikota et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024) match the
137   denoising network of correct images given a target concept to another distribution. Fan et al. (2023)
138   additionally adds a saliency map to fine-tune only a small fraction of parameters. Heng & Soh
139   (2024) does gradient ascent on the training loss of diffusion models. Kumari et al. (2023) minimizes
140   the distribution mismatch between the target concept and another anchor concept. We will discuss
141   the difference between our algorithm and theirs in more detail in Section 3.2. Effective though,
142
      achieving robust unlearning on complex tasks still remains challenging (Zhang et al., 2024c;d). For
      a comprehensive review of unlearning techniques in generative models, see Liu et al. (2024a).
143
144
      Machine Unlearning. Machine unlearning has been extensively explored within classification
145
      tasks (Cao & Yang, 2015; Bourtoule et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021; Izzo et al., 2021; Thudi
146
      et al., 2022) and is now being applied to large generative models. One popular class of unlearn-
147   ing methods stems from Gradient Ascent(GA) (Jang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023; Chen & Yang,
148   2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). More methods include preference optimization (Zhang et al., 2024b;
149   Maini et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024), model-editing (Meng et al., 2022; Mitchell et al., 2022; El-
150   dan & Russinovich, 2023), knowledge negation (Liu et al., 2024b), representation control (Li et al.,
151   2024a), logits difference method (Ji et al., 2024), random labeling, saliency map (Dou et al., 2024;
152   Tian et al., 2024), and in-context unlearning approaches (Pawelczyk et al., 2023), etc. Some other
153   methods are developed for adversarial unlearning or sequential unlearning tasks (Zhang et al., 2024e;
154   Yuan et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). These unlearning methods for language models are orthogonal
155
      to our proposed method for unlearning diffusion models.

157   2     P RELIMINARIES
158
159   Machine Unlearning. Machine Unlearning (MU) refers to the process of systematically removing
160   the influence of specific data points from a trained machine learning model, ensuring that the model
161   forgets information as if the data points were never included in its training set. In this context, let
      D represent the training dataset, and let Df ⊂ D denote the forget set, the subset of data that needs


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to be unlearned. The retain set, denoted as Dr ⊂ D, is the complement of the forget set. The
163   goal of machine unlearning is to produce a new model that closely approximates the performance
164   of retraining from scratch on Dr while also ensuring that the model does not retain any knowledge
165   of Df . Unlearning has traditionally been explored in the context of classification models, where
166   the model aims to either forget the influence of specific classes of data or forget some random
167   samples (Cao & Yang, 2015; Bourtoule et al., 2021). In recent developments, machine unlearning
168   has been extended to large generative models, where the model must unlearn specific objectives to
169   ensure that certain generated outputs, such as sensitive, private, copyrighted, or harmful content,
170   will not be generated. Traditionally, the most straightforward method of unlearning is to retrain the
171
      model from scratch solely with the retain set. However, for large generative models, this approach
      is practically infeasible due to the huge computational costs involved. Alternative methods, such as
172
      prompt-based methods or post-generation filtering, have been explored. Yet, these techniques are
173
      easily circumvented since they do not modify the internal model weights. As a result, they fail to
174   truly “forget” the data. In this paper, we focus on efficient fine-tuning techniques, which allow for
175   effective unlearning while maintaining computational feasibility by directly updating only a subset
176   of the model’s parameters.
177
178   Unlearning Diffusion Models. Diffusion models are a class of generative models that have gained
179   significant attention for their ability to generate high-quality images. They work by transforming
180   data distributions through T forward and reverse steps, gradually adding noise to the data and then
181   learning to reverse this process to generate new samples. Mathematically, this can be described by a
182   series of noisy images x0 , x1 , ..., xT ∈ Rd , where x0 is the original image, and xT is the Gaussian
183   noise. Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) first compresses high-dimensional
184
      pixel-based data into a low-dimensional latent space using an encoder E. It then simulates the diffu-
      sion process on the space of latent variables z = E(x) and reconstructs the image through a decoder
185
      D. For notational simplicity, we do not differentiate between latent variables and pixel-based data,
186
      denoting both as x. In this context, let εθ (xt , p) represent the noise estimator parameterized by θ,
187   where xt is the noisy observation at step t, and p is a conditioning variable such as a class label or
188   text description. The training objective of latent diffusion models is the mean squared error (MSE)
189   between the predicted noise and the true noise across all diffusion steps, expressed as:
190                                                           h                   i
                                                                                2
191                               LMSE (θ) = Ep,t,ε∼N (0,I) ∥ε − εθ (xt , p)∥2 ,                         (1)
192
193   where p is sampled from a distribution over all prompts and t is sampled uniformly from [0, T ].
194   Given a pre-trained latent diffusion model, the objective of unlearning this diffusion model is to
195
      ensure that harmful or sensitive content, such as depictions of nudity or violence, can no longer be
      produced by the model when prompted with the corresponding text descriptions. The challenge lies
196
      in balancing the removal of unwanted generations while preserving the model’s ability to generate
197
      high-quality, appropriate content for normal prompts. The most common unlearning process in
198   diffusion models involves updating the noise estimator to ensure that harmful concepts associated
199   with Df are no longer learned or reinforced during the reverse diffusion process. This form of
200   unlearning, often referred to as “concept erasure”, is critical for ensuring the safe deployment of
201   generative models in real-world applications. More details are included in Section 3.2.

      3     C ONCEPT R ECONDITIONING
205
      In this section, we propose COncept REconditionng (CORE), a simple yet effective algorithm
206
      for unlearning in diffusion models. Our approach focuses on reconditioning the model’s learned
207   representations by substituting concepts from the forget set with selected alternative concepts from
208   the retain set. First, we introduce the objective function and key designs within. Then, we position
209   it within the broader framework of Concept Erasing and compare it with similar algorithms in prior
210   works to showcase its advantage.
211
212   3.1   P ROPOSED M ETHOD
213
214   Unlearn objective. In the context of unlearning in diffusion models, we denote the noise predictor
215   in Latent Diffusion Models by εθ (xt , p), where xt is the noisy version of the input image x0 at time
      step t generated during the forward diffusion process, p is the prompt associated with the image


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      (e.g., “A cat in the style of Van Gogh”), and θ represents the model parameters. We use εθ∗ (xt , p)
217   and θ∗ to denote the pre-trained diffusion model and its parameter. In CORE, we aim to recondition
218   images from the forget set onto alternative concepts. This is achieved by aligning the noise estimator
219   for images in the forget set, conditioned on their original concepts pf ∈ Df , toward the ground truth
220   noise estimator for the same image but conditioned on an alternative concept pa . Mathematically,
221   the unlearn objective function is formulated as
222                      Lf (θ) := E(pf ,x0 )∼Df ,pa ̸=pf ,t ∥εθ (xt , pf ) − εθ∗ (xt , pa ) ∥22 ,

                                                                                                         (2)
223
224   where the expectation is taken over the concept-image pairs (pf , x0 ) from the forget set, alternative
225   concepts pa different from pf , and time steps t uniformly sampled from [0, T ]. Intuitively, this
226
      process effectively weakens the association between the images and their original concepts in the
      model, steering it away from the initial pre-trained associations.
227
228
      Alternative concepts. A key design choice in CORE is the selection of alternative concepts pa in
229
      equation (2). In the unlearning objective, pa acts as an anchor concept, and we recondition images
230   from the forget set onto the anchor concepts. Previous works typically use an empty string or a single
231   base concept for pa consistently across all concepts to be unlearned (Zhang et al., 2024c; Gandikota
232   et al., 2023). In contrast, CORE adopts a different approach by pairing each forget concept pf with
233   a specific alternative concept pa . Our pairing scheme imposes minimal restrictions: the alternative
234   concept pa does not necessarily have to come from the retain set; it can even be another forget
235   concept different from pf . In our implementation, when the number of concepts to forget is smaller
236   than the number of retain concepts, we map each forget concept to a unique concept in the retain set,
237   rather than using a single base concept for all forget concepts. Meanwhile, when the retain concepts
238
      are limited and there are more concepts to forget, we create a one-to-one mapping among the forget
      concepts themselves. This means that each forget concept pf is paired with another forget concept
239
      pa (where pa ̸= pf ) to serve as its alternative concept during unlearning. Empirically, we show
240
      that this one-to-one mapping strategy significantly outperforms methods that consistently use a base
241   concept or randomly sample alternative concepts at each step.
242
243   Retain objective and the full loss function. To ensure the model continues generating high-
244   quality images for the retain concepts, we introduce a retain loss to regularize the unlearning process.
245   Traditionally, the retain loss is defined as the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the noise predic-
246   tion for the retain set and the Gaussian noise vector used to generate the noisy images, similar to the
247   objective used in training a diffusion model (see equation 1). However, in CORE, rather than fine-
248   tuning the noise predictions to match a Gaussian random vector, we instead align them with those
249   generated by the pre-trained diffusion model itself. Mathematically, the retain objective is defined
250
      as
                              Lr (θ) := E(pr ,x0 )∼Dr ,t ∥εθ (xt , pr ) − εθ∗ (xt , pr ) ∥22 ,

                                                                                                            (3)
251
252   where t is uniformly sampled in [0, T ] and (pr , x0 ) are concept-image pairs sampled from the retain
253   set. Using εθ∗ (xt , pr ) as the target helps ensure the model does not deviate too far from its original
254
      capabilities, as it leverages the pre-trained model’s learned knowledge. Empirical results (see Sec-
      tion 4) demonstrate that aligning the noise predictions with εθ∗ (xt , pr ), rather than the Gaussian
255
      noise, yields better performance. This improvement arises potentially because using the estimated
256
      noise from the pre-trained model reduces variance in the unlearned model and stabilize the training
257   process. Interestingly, this phenomenon, where using estimated signals outperforms true signals,
258   has also been observed in other domains in statistics (Robins et al., 1992; Henmi & Eguchi, 2004;
259   Hitomi et al., 2008; Su et al., 2023).
260
      Finally, the complete loss function in CORE combines both the unlearn and retain objectives:
261
262                                        L(θ) := Lf (θ) + α · Lr (θ),                                    (4)
263   where α > 0 controls the regularization strength. Intuitively, CORE ensures that the model is
264   steered away from generating images associated with forget concepts while preserving its overall
265   performance on other concepts.

267   3.2   R ETHINKING C ONCEPT E RASING AND R ECONDITIONING
268
269   At first glance, our proposed objective might seem similar to existing methods for unlearning in
      diffusion models, as it also involves steering the error predictor on the forget set while keeping it


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      unchanged on the retain set. However, under closer scrutiny, Concept Reconditioning introduces
271   several key distinctions that set it apart and enable it to outperform previous approaches. Take a
272   broader view of the framework of unlearning diffusion models: unlearning methods for diffusion
273   models that are based on fine-tuning the error predictor εθ (x, p) can generally be categorized into
274   two classes:
275
276          • Concept Erasing (CE): This method works by shifting the noise prediction network for
277            images corresponding to the forget concepts towards an alternative noise distribution. In-
278            tuitively, by doing so, it directly acts on εθ (xft , pf ), where xft is the noisy observation for
279            images in the forget set, and misleads them away.
280          • Image Relabeling (IR): In this approach, alternative images that do not match the forget
281            concepts are selected, and the model is fine-tuned on the forget concepts paired with these
282            mismatched images. The model directly acts on εθ (xrt , pf ) where xrt is the noisy images
283            constructed from the retain set, and effectively overwrites the old knowledge with new
284            associations, forcing it to forget by learning new, incorrect pairings.
285
      Mathematically, these two classes can be formulated as
286
                           LCE (θ) := λ · E(pf ,x0 )∼Df ,t ∥εθ (xt , pf ) − yCE ∥22 ,

287                                                                                                            (5)
288
                            LIR (θ) := λ · Epf ∼Df ,x0 ∼Dr ,t ∥εθ (xt , pf ) − yIR ∥22 .

                                                                                                               (6)
289
290   Here, λ ∈ {±1} controls the direction of the objective function. In the CE method, images are
291   drawn from the forget set, while in IR, images come from the retain set. The target noises yCE
292   and yIR can be either random vectors (e.g., Gaussian or Uniform) or derived from a trainable noise
293   predictor.
294   Many existing unlearning methods fit within this framework. For example, Heng & Soh (2024)
295   suggests λ = −1 and yCE ∼ N (0, Id ) in equation (5) in the unlearning objective, while proposing
296   a surrogate objective with λ = 1 and yIR ∼ N (0, Id ) in equation (6). The former corresponds to
297   a gradient ascent loss applied to the pre-training objective on forget concepts, while the surrogate
298   objective simply mirrors the standard training loss applied to the forget concepts with retain images.
299   Fan et al. (2023) takes yCE in equation (5) as a trainable noise predictor εθ (xt , pa ) where pa ̸= pf
300
      is an alternative concept coming from the retain set. Wu et al. (2024) also proposes this target noise,
      as well as suggesting an alternative with yCE as a uniformly distributed random vector. Kumari et al.
301
      (2023) takes yIR in equation (6) to be either a standard Gaussian random vector or the error predictor
302
      at the last iterate, evaluated at retain images paired with corresponding retain concepts. Even when
303   the objective function appears divergent from this framework, as seen in Gandikota et al. (2023),
304   it can still be decomposed into a linear combination of objective functions in the framework above
305   (see Appendix B).
306
      Although these prior works often include additional techniques such as weight decay (Heng &
307
      Soh, 2024), saliency map (Fan et al., 2023), or even applying a monotonic function to the squared
308
      loss (Park et al., 2024), the backbone of their unlearning objectives can be positioned into this simple
309   framework or its simple variants. Our method distinguishes itself from prior approaches by its sim-
310   plicity. Unlike previous methods, CORE requires no auxiliary techniques, and simply optimizing
311   the objective L(θ) in equation (4) achieves state-of-the-art results.
312
      Another key distinction is that CORE uses a fixed, non-trainable noise predictor from the pre-trained
313
      diffusion model as the target noise. This fixed anchor provides a clearer target noise compared to a
314
      trainable network or a random vector with a fixed distribution (e.g., a uniformly distributed random
315   vector). Let us compare the three types of target noises. With a random vector from a fixed distri-
316   bution (Kumari et al., 2023; Heng & Soh, 2024), there is no guarantee that this manually designed
317   random vector will effectively disrupt the noise predictor conditioned on the forget concepts. A
318   trainable, non-fixed noise (Fan et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) is unstable during
319   the unlearning process, particularly when aiming to forget many concepts over a long training pe-
320   riod, since this target may drift towards an undesired direction. While methods using trainable target
321   noises include a retain term in their loss function, this retain objective directly influences εθ (xrt , pr )
322   but not εθ (xft , pr ), where xrt and xft are noisy observations from the retain and forget sets, respec-
323   tively. In contrast, CORE’s use of a non-trainable target noise ensures that the noise predictor always
      learns from a reference “incorrect” noise estimator derived from the pre-trained model.

353   4.1    E XPERIMENT S ETUP
354
      Dataset and Tasks. UnlearnCanvas is a high-resolution stylized image dataset designed to evalu-
355
      ate diffusion model unlearning methods (Zhang et al., 2024c). The dataset consists of images across
356   50 unique styles and 20 distinct objects, with 20 images for each style-object combination. Each
357   image is labeled with both a style and an object, making it particularly well-suited for measuring the
358   unlearning effectiveness and the retainability both within a single domain and across domains. In
359   this paper, we mainly focus on style unlearning within the UnlearnCanvas dataset. We define three
360   unlearning tasks, each progressively forgetting more styles: Forget01 (forgetting 1 style), Forget06
361   (forgetting 6 styles), and Forget25 (forgetting 25 styles).
362
363   Models and Baselines. We use a Stable Diffusion v1.5 model (Rombach et al., 2022) to perform
364   the fine-tuning and unlearning, and we also use a vision Transformer (ViT-Large) (Dosovitskiy,
365   2020) on UnlearnCanvas for style and object classification. Before unlearning the model, the base

504   Anchor Selection: How do we approach it? Another key distinction between CORE and other
505   baseline methods lies in how anchors pa are selected in the unlearning objective (as defined in equa-
506   tion 2). In CORE, each forget concept pf is paired with a distinct alternative concept. This contrasts
507   with other methods that recondition all forget concepts to a single base concept or the empty string.
508   To demonstrate the effectiveness of CORE’s one-to-one pairing, we compare different selection
509
      schemes: One approach involves pairing each forget concept with a set of alternative concepts (or
      even the entire retain set) and randomly sampling one at each gradient step to recondition the tar-
510
      get images. Another approach reconditions images from multiple or even all forget concepts onto
511
      a single base concept. As shown in Table 4, CORE’s one-to-one reconditioning scheme signifi-
512   cantly outperforms these strategies. Specifically, unlearning accuracy declines sharply when forget
513   concepts are paired with multiple alternatives (one-to-all or one-to-five) and a random alternative is
514   sampled at each step. Conversely, the model’s stylistic retainability suffers when all forget concepts
515   are reconditioned to just one or a few base concepts.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
518
519
      In this paper, we introduce COncept REconditioning (CORE), a novel and effective method for
520   unlearning in diffusion models. CORE leverages a non-trainable target noise from the pre-trained
521   diffusion model to guide both the unlearning and retain objectives, thereby avoiding the pitfalls of
522   using trainable noise predictors or random Gaussian noise targets. Through extensive experiments
523   on the UnlearnCanvas dataset, we demonstrate that CORE consistently outperforms state-of-the-
524   art baseline methods in terms of unlearning effectiveness, retainability, and generalization ability,
525   particularly in challenging tasks involving multiple forget concepts. Moreover, we highlight the
526   importance of a one-to-one concept reconditioning scheme, which proves superior to other anchor
527   selection strategies.
528   There are several promising directions for future research. One key area is improving the efficiency
529   of unlearning, particularly when dealing with a large number of forget concepts. Current methods
530   can still be time-consuming when unlearning many concepts simultaneously. Exploring accelerated
531   unlearning methods while maintaining performance is an exciting avenue. Additionally, future work
532   could investigate the robustness of unlearning methods in dynamic environments, where new con-
533   cepts might continuously be added to the model, requiring continuous updates without retraining
534
      from scratch.
