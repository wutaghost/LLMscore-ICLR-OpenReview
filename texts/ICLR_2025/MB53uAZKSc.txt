009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) are trained on data crawled over many years from
012            the web. We investigate how quickly LLMs become outdated as the world evolves
013            with time and how to best update them with newer data. Specifically, we simulate
014            a world where the latest dump of Common Crawl (CC), the most prominent public
015            source of pre-training data, is used every month to continually train an LLM. We
016            design various dynamic evaluations from the CC data, Wikipedia, StackExchange,
017            and code documentations to measure continual learning metrics such as forgetting
018
               and forward transfer. Notably, our T I C-CC training data is more than 100× larger
               compared with prior continual learning benchmarks for language modeling. We
019
               discover that recent DataComp-LM (Li et al., 2024a) models trained on data before
020
               2023 have already become outdated, incurring up to 45% larger noun-perplexity on
021            2024 Wikipedia articles compared to pre-2023 articles. Further, we use our setup to
022            evaluate the effectiveness of several large-scale continual learning methods and find
023            that replaying older data is most effective for combating forgetting: for previously
024            seen CC dumps, it can reduce the regret on held-out loss by 60% compared to
025            other optimizer and loss-based interventions. However, some domains evolve more
026            quickly than others, favoring different trade-offs between mixing old and new data.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) rely on massive amounts of data, a major portion of which comes
032
      from large-scale web-crawls that have been running over the past 10–20 years. Common Crawl (CC),
      the most well-known source of such data, has been active since 2007 and continues to release monthly
033
      dumps of data. While typically many (or all) previous dumps are combined together to train LLMs
034
      from scratch (Penedo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a), the vast costs and inherent knowledge cutoffs of
035   LLMs raise natural questions about how they can be most effectively updated as future dumps are
036   released. In this work, we introduce a benchmark for Time-Continual Learning of Language Models
037   (TiC-LM) and investigate how to continually train LLMs over many months and years. Taking
038   inspiration from the recent TiC-CLIP (Garg et al., 2024) work, our goal is to find efficient alternatives
039   to training LLMs from scratch by reusing and updating prior pre-trained models. Overall, we seek to
040   answer the following:
041   • How quickly does a pre-trained LLM become outdated? We observe that Gemini, Gemini-2, and
042     DCLM models are outdated on 2024 data by 34%, 28%, and 45%, respectively (Fig. 2).
043   • Can continual pretraining reach the performance of training from scratch when fixing the number
044     of tokens? We demonstrate a variety of methods that shrink the gap, but this proves to be an open
045     and challenging problem to be studied in future using our benchmark.
046   • Do forgetting and forward transfer vary across domains such as Wikipedia, News, etc? Yes.
047     For example, although data replay methods generally avoid forgetting, they hurt performance on
048     domains that change rapidly (Fig. 5).
049   Our contributions in TiC-LM are: (1) introducing a large-scale continual pretraining benchmark
050   for language modeling, and (2) evaluating continual learning strategies. TiC-LM centers around
051   TiC-CommonCrawl (T I C-CC), a massive time-stratified set of training and evaluation data created
052   using 114 CC dumps spanning 2013–2024 including evaluation subsets T I C-CC-W IKI and T I C-
053   CC-N EWS. T I C-CC contains 2.9T tokens, more than 100× larger than prior continual continual
      learning benchmark for LLMs. TiC-LM also contains domain-specific evaluations sourced from

093   Learning new capabilities from multiple, sequentially observed, distributions has long been an active
094   area of ML research (Wang et al., 2024). More recently, several works have studied this setting
095   for LLMs (Wu et al., 2024), targeting improvements on: (1) general capabilities (via updating
096   on improved datasets) (Parmar et al., 2024; Ibrahim et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2023); (2) specific
097   domains (Jin et al., 2022; Gururangan et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024); (3) newer data as the world
098   evolves (Jin et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2022b;a; Lazaridou et al., 2021; Nylund et al., 2024; Loureiro
099
      et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2022; Liška et al., 2022). Works in this third category have demonstrated
      that in many domains, the performance of LLMs decay as training and evaluation sets grow farther
100
      apart in time, motivating the need for methods to efficiently and non-distruptively adapt to temporal
101
      distribution shifts. Our work scales up these previous efforts to more closely match current LLM
102   training practices. While older works typically focus on continual training runs involving individual
103   sources (e.g., news, Wikipedia, and social media) and <10 timesteps, we consider training on a
104   generic web-crawl (i.e., Common Crawl) spanning 114 different months. In turn, the generality
105   of our training data allows us to go beyond single-domain evaluations. We provide an extended
106   discussion of related works in Appx. E. Table 1 summarizes our proposed datasets compared with the
107   most related time-continual benchmarks. With 2.9T tokens, T I C-CC is the largest and most diverse
      continual learning benchmark for language modeling.

134   3       T I C-C OMMON C RAWL (T I C-CC): M ORE THAN A D ECADE OF W EB DATA
135
136   We create a large time-stratified dataset of 2.9T tokens based upon Common Crawl, a free and open
137   corpus of web-crawled data that has been online since 2007. CC releases new snapshots of the web
138   roughly every month. Each dump creates a representative snapshot of the web by sampling a limited
139   number of pages from each domain. Sampled URLs change from month to month independent
140   of whether they appeared in the previous dumps. We collect all dumps between May-2013 and
141
      July-2024, resulting in 114 corresponding splits that we refer to by the month of their release date.
      For each split, we then apply a pre-processing pipeline based on that of DataComp-LM (Li et al.,
142
      2024a). Notably, we do not perform any operations on a particular month that depend on future
143
      months to retain causality and temporal order.
144
145   Data processing. We build upon the existing pipeline from DataComp-LM (Li et al., 2024a),
146   starting with DCLM-Pool (Li et al., 2024a), which contains all CC dumps between May-2013
147
      and Dec-2022 and parsed to extract plain text from webpages via the open-source resiliparse
      library (Bevendorff et al., 2018; 2021). We split this data by month and reuse the same download
148
      and processing scripts to extend DCLM-Pool until July-2024. Next, we follow DCLM-Baseline’s
149
      pipeline by applying heuristic filters from RefinedWeb (Penedo et al., 2023) and a fuzzy-deduplication
150   step which we modify to run only within each month rather than non-causal global deduplication.
151   Alternatively, similar to TiC-CLIP, one could deduplicate data globally but keep the earliest occurrence
152   of each document. We avoid this deduplication for two reasons: (1) fuzzy deduplication across
153   months may not always be helpful, potentially removing near-duplicates such as Wikipedia pages
154   where a few key facts have changed but most of the text is the same, (2) it allows for exploring the
155   benefits/pitfalls of such data-centric interventions as part of method design. Also, we do not use the
156   final classifier-based filter in DCLM-Baseline, as this classifier was trained on data from all months.
157   Finally, we leverage the fact that DCLM-Pool was randomly partitioned into ten equally-sized chunks
158   to construct held-out sets for loss-based evaluations (Sec. 4.1). In Fig. 3, we show the number of
159   tokens we have for each month of the dataset. In total, the dataset spans 29T tokens, with individual
160   months ranging between 100B to 500B tokens. We use smaller subset of 220B tokens from a single
161   global shard with 2.9T for our training while future work can expand to the full 2.9T/29T tokens. For
      more details about the data pipeline see Appx. A.

164   In this section, we will discuss various time-continual evaluations that are designed both with
165   and independent of CC data. As our focus is on continual pretraining, we focus on evaluations
166   without instruction-tuning. We introduce three sets of novel evaluations: T I C-CC, T I C-CC-W IKI,
167   T I C-CC-N EWS, T I C-W IKI, T I C-S TACK E, and T I C-C ODE D OCS.
168
      Static downstream evaluations. We focus on pre-trained base models without any instruction
169
      fine-tuning and evaluate our models on a variety of suitable downstream zero-shot and few-shot
170   tasks. Specifically, we use the C ORE evaluations from the DCLM benchmark (Li et al., 2024a)
171   which includes 22 zero-shot and few-shot in-context learning tasks. These evaluations, which include
172   benchmarks such as ARC-Easy (Clark et al., 2018) and Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019), assess general

174   tasks. While these evaluations are not designed to be time-dependent, we use them to assess (1)
175   whether continually trained models match the general capabilities of models trained on all dumps; (2)
176   if they benefit from different months of Common Crawl.

192   4.1   T I C-C OMMON C RAWL (T I C-CC) EVALUATIONS
193
194   CC data is a consistent, albeit partial, snapshot of the web over years that does not require special
195
      processing of the history per website. We compute token-perplexity (ppltoken ) on three monthly
      subsets of our CC data which were held out from training:
196
197   • T I C-CC: Held-out documents coming from the full distribution for each month of T I C-CC.
198   • T I C-CC-W IKI: Pages in T I C-CC from English Wikipedia (i.e., whose URLs contain either the
199     domain en.wikipedia.org or simple.wikipedia.org).
200   • T I C-CC-N EWS: Pages in T I C-CC from a set of news sites based on WMT competitions (Barrault
201     et al., 2020).
202
      4.2   T I C-W IKIPEDIA (T I C-W IKI )
203
204   Our T I C-CC-W IKI evaluation in Sec. 4.1 is based on sampled Wikipedia pages existing in each
205   CC dump which is a representative set of Wikipedia but not all of it. We create T I C-W IKI, a more
206   comprehensive evaluation from full dumps of Wikipedia while utilizing the knowledge graph from
207   Wikidata. T I C-W IKI allows us to construct question/answer and factual evaluations as well as split
208   the performance over changed/unchanged knowledge. We build upon TemporalWiki (Jang et al.,
209   2022a), which generates evaluations from four consecutive monthly snapshots of English Wikipedia
210   and Wikidata. Our T I C-W IKI evaluation captures a broader spectrum of knowledge evolution, we
211   extend the evaluation timespan to a full decade (2014–2024) and improve upon the matching of
212
      Wikipedia/Wikidata (see Appx. B.1 for more details).
213   To evaluate performance on T I C-W IKI diffsets, we adopt the approach of Lazaridou et al. (2021)
214   and Jang et al. (2022a), calculating the average perplexity of proper nouns (pplnoun ) identified by a
215   Part-of-Speech tagger (Honnibal & Montani, 2017). This method serves as a proxy for assessing
      factual knowledge changes, as proper nouns often contain key factual information.

      4.3   T I C-S TACK E XCHANGE (T I C-S TACK E)
217
218   We design another question/answering evaluation based on the historical data from StackEx-
219   change.StackExchange has 182 communities that share knowledge by posting questions and answers.
220   We measure answer-perplexity (pplanswer ) on high-quality answers from selected sites by collecting
221   answers that have been accepted by the question author (using the accepted answer timestamp to
222   bin examples by month). The resulting evaluation contains examples from 2008–2024. We provide
223   details of T I C-S TACK E data processing in Appx. B.2.

225   4.4   T I C-C ODE D OCUMENTATIONS (T I C-C ODE D OCS )
226
      Our T I C-C ODE D OCS evaluation is based on code documentations from popular open-source Python
227
      libraries: NumPy (Harris et al., 2020) and PyTorch (Ansel et al., 2024). For NumPy, we use
228   documentations from 16 major releases, ranging from version 1.13.0 (June 2017) to version 2.1.0
229   (August 2024). For PyTorch, we use documentations of major releases ranging from version 1.8.0
230   (March 2021) to version 2.4.0 (July 2024).
231
      We build the documentation directly from each library’s git repository. The process involves the
232
      following steps: (1) Identify the commit tagged for the major release, (2) revert to that specific
233
      commit, (3) install necessary dependencies, (4) build the project from source, (5) generate HTML
234   documentation from the source. We then convert all HTML pages to raw text by extracting the
235   main body of the documentation pages. This approach ensures that template-related elements of the
236   pages such as the index and footer are not included in the final text, focusing solely on the relevant
237   documentation content. We evaluate the model’s code understanding using perplexity (ppltoken ),
238   calculated across entire snapshots of code docs.

      5     C ONTINUAL L EARNING BASELINE M ETHODS
241
242
      The goal for TiC-LM methods is to match the performance of the Oracle which trains on all data (114
243   months) starting from random initialization for the full token budget. We consider methods from three
244   categories: optimization-based, data replay, and regularization. Aside from average metrics across all
245   timesteps, methods should balance forgetting and forward transfer metrics (defined in Sec. 6).
246
      Optimization-based methods. In non-continual settings, LLMs are often trained with a cosine-
247
      decayed learning rate schedule which requires knowledge of total training steps ahead of time. In
248
      a continual setup, however, the number of total tokens grows over time and we care about the
249   performance after each month. We benchmark the following optimization approaches in our work:
250
      • Cyclic Cosine is the simplest alternative which applies cosine decay within each training month
251
        using the same maximum learning rate and warmup for each round. This was found to be most
252
        effective in TiC-CLIP (Garg et al., 2024).
253
      • Cyclic Cosine + AR (autoregressive) is similar to cyclic cosine decay except the maximum learning
254
        rate in each cycle decays across months, regressed from a single-cycle cosine decay and shown to
255     offer improvements by Roth et al. (2024).
256                        √
      • Rsqrt (reciprocal- ) are infinite schedules that decay the learning rate slowly in a global training
257
        run and branch off of this trajectory with linear cooldowns (Zhai et al., 2022). To keep training
258
        steps fixed compared to other methods, we follow Roth et al. (2024) and implement a version that
259     maintains only a single trajectory by re-warming up from the previous cooldown.
260   • Schedule-Free is an optimizer proposed by Defazio et al. (2024) which aims to circumvent the need
261     for defining a learning rate schedule by using iterate averaging and has achieved promising results
262     in i.i.d. non-continual settings.
263
264   Data replay methods. Data replay is a classical continual learning strategy to prevent forgetting,
265   whereby in each training round, the model is fed a mixture of data from both older and the current
266   timesteps (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Rebuffi et al., 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2018). Defining a replay
267
      method therefore boils down to how the mixture ratios are specified. We consider the following replay
      strategies based on the best-performing strategies in TiC-CLIP (Garg et al., 2024):
268
269   • For the current timestep t, we allocate a ratio 0 ≤ αt ≤ 1 of the monthly token budget Bt to data
        from the current month, seeing αt Bt tokens from that month.


                                                        5
270
      • For previous months, we redistribute the remaining (1 − αt )Bt tokens equally, i.e., each month
271
        contributing 1−α
                      t−1 Bt tokens to this round’s training set.
                         t

272
273   In particular, when αt = 1/t, we see an equal number of tokens from all observed months. We also
274   consider setting αt = 1/2 which always allocates half the token budget to the current month. The
275
      general downside of replay-based methods is the cost of retaining old data. This can be particularly
      challenging if old data expires and needs to be removed. Methods with larger values of αt are less
276
      affected by such limitations.
277
278   Regularization-based methods. These methods alter the training objective instead of the data,
279   generally by adding a regularization term which encourages newer model updates to stay close to the
280   model weights learned after the previous month. Following TiC-CLIP, we try two notable methods:
281
      LwF (Li & Hoiem, 2018) and EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017).
282   • LwF adds an additional loss term based on KL divergence which penalizes differences in model
283     outputs between the previous checkpoint and the current model.
284   • EWC attempts to slow down updates to particular model parameters which are highly influential
285     for performing well on older months as measured by the (approximate) Fisher information matrix.
286
      Because both LwF and EWC involve extra loss terms and model copies, it is important to note that
287   they induce larger GPU memory footprints and run-times compared to optimizer and replay-based
288   methods. That being said, we do not try to adjust the token counts to account for this given that our
289   re-implementations may not be optimally efficient.

      Training details. For all runs, we train 3B parameter language models using OpenLM. Unless
294
      otherwise indicated, each method observes a fixed total number of 220B tokens, equivalent to 4x
295   the Chinchilla optimal amount. 1 We further assume that current practitioners are (a) likely to have
296   access to more than enough data to train initial models; (b) unlikely to wait to obtain non-trivial
297   performance. Hence, we front-load the total token budgets such that half is allocated to training on
298   the first month, May-2013. Then, the remaining 110B tokens are split equally among the other 113
299   continual timesteps. For more realistic hyperparameter selection in our continual setup (Cha & Cho,
300   2024), we only use the first 10 of these timesteps for tuning (see Appx. C for more details).
301   Evaluation metrics. Each continual run actually produces a Tt × Te matrix of evaluations E where
302   Tt , Te are the total number of training/evaluation timesteps, Ei,j is the performance of the model
303   trained after training on data up to month i and evaluated on the month j. To control for inherent
304   difficulty differences across evaluation months, we measure the regret Ri,j = Ei,j − Ej∗ where Ej∗ is
305   the performance of the Oracle trained on all months (May-2013–July-2024) on month j. We subtract
306   Ej∗ instead of Ej,j since if Ej,j is bad it may lead to misleadingly good forward/backward metrics.
307   Following Garg et al. (2024), we consider the following summary metrics (assuming Tt = Te = T ).
308
                                                                                   PT
309   • In-distribution (ID) performance: averages along the matrix diagonal, i.e., i=1 = Ri,i /T .
310
                                                                    PT P           Ri,j
      • Backward transfer: averages the lower triangular of R, i.e., i=1 j<i T (T −1)/2  .
311
      • Forward transfer: averages the upper triangular of R analogously to backward transfer.
312
313   For some downstream evaluations, the train/evaluation periods do not exactly align (Tt ̸= Te ), making
314   the definition of ID more nuanced. For such evaluations, we define ai as the index of the nearest
315   evaluation timestep that comes before the training timestep i. We then count Ri,ai towards the ID
316   average only if no other training timestep is closer to ai (i.e., ai ̸= ai−1 ). Otherwise, we count Ri,j
317   towards backward and forward transfer when j < ai and j ≥ ai respectively.

406   Tab. 3 presents results for several of our downstream evaluations, while Fig. 5 and Appx. D show
407   corresponding evaluation matrices. Here, we observe broadly that methods exhibit trade-offs across
408   the different evaluations, showcasing the inherent challenges of performing well on a variety of
409   domains while training on a sequence of generic web-data dumps (in which the coverages of said
410   domains may also vary over time). We summarize the key findings below:
411   EWC is the best method for adapting to new knowledge in T I C-W IKI-Diff. This differs from
412   the results of Garg et al. (2024) and Jin et al. (2022) which found EWC to have little positive impact
413   in their settings. It also differs from T I C-CC-Wiki, where EWC could not match replay in terms
414   of Backward performance. One explanation for this is that by isolating new/changed segments of
415   Wikipedia, T I C-W IKI-Diff places strong pressure on seeing newer data. Indeed, we see in Tab. 8
416   in Appx. D that measuring performance on the unchanged segments returns replay’s superiority on
417   Backward, though the improvements are much smaller. To further shed light on this discrepancy, we
418
      also see from Fig. 5 (left) that unlike for T I C-CC-W IKI, peak performance on each T I C-W IKI
      month is often years after that month is seen, even when no replay is used in Cyclic Cosine. This
419
      suggests that the knowledge T I C-W IKI captures each month is also successfully learned from CC
420
      dumps that were crawled quite a bit later, which could be due to: (1) delayed alignment between
421   CC’s crawls of Wikipedia and T I C-W IKI’s more comprehensive coverage of Wikipedia edits; (2)
422   measuring T I C-CC loss on all tokens versus focusing on specific segments and proper nouns in
423   T I C-W IKI to capture only factual knowledge rather than irrelevant nuances (e.g., page formatting).
424

531
      We introduce a benchmark for continual LLM pretraining spanning more than a decade of times-
532   tamped data. TiC-CommonCrawl (T I C-CC) consists of training and evaluation data spanning
533   more than 100 months. We also introduce new TiC evaluations, TiC-Wikipedia (T I C-W IKI), TiC-
534   StackExchange (T I C-S TACK E), and T I C-C ODE D OCS. Using these assets, we clearly observe models
535   need to be continually trained to stay up to date but that the ideal update frequency varies based
536   upon the domain, motivating the need for methods that prevent forgetting. To this end, we compared
537   various baseline strategies for continual pretraining, finding that simple cyclic learning rate schedules
538   and data-replay shrink the gap to an Oracle that trains on all data. However, completely closing the
539   gap remains an open and challenging problem to be studied by future work on our benchmark.

      A    DATASET C ONSTRUCTION

866
      We build upon the existing pipeline and assets from DataComp-LM (Li et al., 2024a) to build our
867
      dataset, only altering steps that rely on global operations across months.
868
869   Initial pool and temporal splitting. We start with DCLM-Pool (Li et al., 2024a) which contains all
870   CC dumps between May-2013 and December-2022. The only pre-processing that has been done on
871
      this pool is to parse the HTML (contained in WARC files of CC) into plaintext for each webpage
      via the open-source resiliparse library (Bevendorff et al., 2018; 2021) 2 . In DCLM-Pool,
872
      documents are naturally grouped together into files based upon the CC dump, which is indicated by
873
      the file prefix 3 . To split the data by month, we simply group files that share the same prefix. Since
874   DCLM-Pool contains data up to December-2022, we also follow their exact download and extraction

      B.1     T I C-W IKI
921
922   We construct T I C-W IKI from Wikipedia and Wikidata which are sister projects from the non-profit
923   Wikimedia Foundation. Wikidata is a structured knowledge graph that stores the structured data of
924   Wikipedia and other sister projects. Data on Wikidata is represented in the form of statements in
925   the form of property-value about an item in the simplest form. For example, “Mount Everest is the
926   highest mountain in the world” is represented as Earth (Q2) (item) → highest point (P610) (property)
927
      → Mount Everest (Q513) (value) 4 . The triplet (item, property, value) can also be referred to as
      (subject, relation, object).
928
929   TemporalWiki dataset generation. TemporalWiki constructs evaluations from monthly snapshots
930   of English Wikipedia and Wikidata through the following steps:
931
932
              1. Generate TWiki-Diffsets by identifying changes and additions between consecutive snap-
                 shots of Wikipedia. For new articles, the entire article is added to the Diffset while for
933
                 existing articles, only the changed or new paragraphs are added.
934
935           2. Construct TWiki-Probes by processing two consecutive snapshots of Wikidata. Statements
936
                 are categorized into changed if the property/value has changed or categorized into unchanged
                 otherwise.
937
938           3. Align TWiki-Diffsets with Wikidata by ensuring changed statements exist in TWiki-Diffsets
939              and unchanged statements exist in Wikipedia.
940           4. Heuristic filtering by removing statements where the subject or object is a substring of the
941              other or the object is more than 5 words. Moreover, a single subject is limited to maximum
942              1% and relation/object is limited to maximum 5% of the total statements.
943
      T I C-W IKI extends TemporalWiki in various ways:
944
945           1. Expanding the timespan from four months to a decade (2014-2024), thus capturing a broader
946              spectrum of knowledge evolution.
947
              2. We improve the matching process of Wikipedia and Wikidata dumps, and enhance the
948              robustness of data parser to format changes over time.
949
950   B.1.1     DATA PREPROCESSING
951
952   Wikidata and Wikipedia dumps. Wikimedia releases regular dumps 5,6 , but only retains data for
953   the most recent 4 months. To access historical data, we utilized the Internet Archive 7 . The earliest
954
      available dump dates back to November 2014. It is important to note that the archived dumps do not
      cover every month, with several months missing from the record. In our study, we made use of all
955
      available monthly dumps. The filenames of the dumps include the specific date of month that has
956
      been collected on, which is typically the 1st or 20th of the month, though this can vary. We include
957   only one dump per month if multiple dumps are available. We check for the first date if not available
958   look for 20th and if neither we start from begining the monthh and check for the first availble date in
959   that month.
960
      Data cleanup. We utilize WikiExtractor 8 to clean up the Wikipedia data. This step extracts the main
961
      content and removes extraneous and non-essential characters.
962
963   Wikipedia diffsets. To construct consecutive diffs of Wikipedia, we developed a method comparing
964   snapshots of articles from consecutive dumps. For comparing two snapshots of an article, we first
965   remove extraneous whitespace and standardize formatting by preprocessing the text. This involves
966
      removing empty lines, stripping newline characters, and creating a normalized version of each line
      where punctuation is removed and text is converted to lowercase.

977

978

979    as entirely changed content. This approach allows us to focus on meaningful content changes while
980    maintaining the context of unchanged information, providing a comprehensive view of how Wikipedia
981    articles evolve over time. Algorithms 1 and 2 describe the process of constructing Wikipedia diffs

       Wikidata diffsets. Next, we extract changed and unchanged Wikidata statements of the form
984
       (subject, relation, object) from each consecutive dump. Identical triplets in both dumps are marked
985    as unchanged. Triplets in the new dump not present in the old are categorized as new, with the
986    exception that if a subject entity has more than 10 triplets, the algorithm randomly samples 10 to
987    represent it. When a triplet has the same subject and relation as one in the old dump but a different
988    object and the old and new objects differ only in case (upper/lowercase), the triplet is classified as
989    unchanged; otherwise, it is categorized as new. Triplets from the old dump not found in the new
990    one are implicitly considered removed, but importantly, these are not included in the output sets of
991    changed or unchanged triplets. Throughout this process, the algorithm filters out triplets with overly
992    long object values (more than 5 words) and ensures no duplicates are added. This approach efficiently
993
       tracks Wikidata evolution, capturing nuanced changes while managing the volume of data for new
       entities. Algorithm 3 describes the process of triplet extraction.

1106      To construct our dataset, we first build the graph of question-answer relationships based on the

1110      We construct binary classification tasks from StackExchange content. For each question, we extract
1111      two responses: the solution accepted by the original author and an alternative option. Our goal is to
1112      create clear distinctions in answer quality, so we implement rigorous selection criteria. Specifically,
1113      we requir the accepted solution to have received at least four times the number of upvotes as the
1114      alternative. For the alternative, we choose the response with the lowest upvote count that was posted
1115      before the accepted answer. This strict filtering, while effective in creating distinct quality differentials,
1116      significantly reduced our sample size across most categories. To maintain robust evaluation metrics
1117      while preserving data volume, we introduce an additional metric: the average perplexity of accepted
1118
          answers, calculated without applying the strict upvote ratio filter. This approach allows us to include
          more samples in our analysis while still capturing meaningful performance trends

1244
       In general we follow the configurations used in DataComp-LM (Li et al., 2024a) unless further speci-
1245
       fied. For our Oracle and initialization trained on May-2013, we exactly follow their hyperparameters
1246
       given that these were also standard pre-training runs from scratch.
1247
1248   For our various continual methods, we do perform additional hyperparameter tuning using the first 10
1249   TiC-CC training sets and held-out validation sets. Following Cha & Cho (2024), we limit the tuning
1250
       to an early set of months given that it would be impossible for a practitioner to be able to tune based
       upon data they have not seen far in the future. We discuss the tuning and hyperparamter choices for
1251
       specific methods in more detail below.
1252
1253   Cyclic Cosine. We mainly tuned the maximum learning rate in each cycle, trying values between
1254   1e-3 and 3e-5, as shown in Tab. 5. On our tuning set, the best setting across the board was 1e-4.
1255   When carrying out these tuning runs to completion on all 113 timesteps, we do observe an important
1256
       difference in behavior. While 1e-4 continues to offer the best ID performance and strictly dominates
       all higher settings, lowering it further can be used to trade-off Backward and ID performance. The
1257
       smallest fixed max learning rate, 3e-5 results in a similar yet overall worse performance profile to
1258
       using an an AR meta-schedule. This makes sense given the AR schedule roughly can be considered
1259   to decrease the maximum learning rate at a 1/t rate; since our setup involves over 100 months, AR
1260   schedules set the maximum learning rate very close to the minimum of 3e-5 in most rounds. Overall,
1261   we find that learning rates do need to be lowered by at least 30x compared to the the May-2013
1262   initialization (which used 3e-3). This is in contrast to Ibrahim et al. (2024); Gupta et al. (2023) which
1263   both suggest re-warming up to a similar learning rate as the initial pre-training or Parmar et al. (2024)
1264   who start from the minimum learning rate of the pre-trained model. We suspect this is due to the
1265   difference in setup (i.e., these works use only 2 or 3 training rounds of comparable sizes and face
1266   distribution shifts related to data quality and language rather than temporal evolution).

       LwF. Following the original paper (Li & Hoiem, 2018), we used a temperature parameter of T = 2.

1785   Temporal knowledge evaluations. Language models are expected to have an understanding of
1786   time to answer questions about specific time periods and generally reason about time. Various
1787
       benchmarks have been proposed to evaluate temporal knowledge of LLMs. TemporalWiki (Jang
       et al., 2022a) evaluates the capability of models to update factual knowledge. TemporalWiki is
1788
       constructed from the difference between four consecutive snapshots of Wikipedia and Wikidata. Our
1789
       T I C-W IKI evaluation expands and improves on TemporalWiki in various ways (see Appx. B.1).
1790   StreamingQA (Liška et al., 2022) consists of human written and generated questions from 14 years
1791   of news articles. The evaluation is either open-book where a model receives a collection of news
1792   articles that contain the answer, or closed-book where the model is first fine-tuned on the training
1793   set containing the documents and then tested. We evaluate our TiC checkpoints on StreamingQA
1794   both in open/closed-book setups and find that there is high ambiguity in the questions that evaluates
1795   reasoning more than temporal knowledge understanding. TempEL (Zaporojets et al., 2022) evaluates
1796   entity linking performance across 10 yearly snapshots of Wikipedia. Entity linking is the task of
1797   mapping anchor mentions to target entities that describe them in a knowledge base. In comparison,
1798
       our T I C-W IKI evaluates general language and knowledge understanding. TempLAMA (Dhingra
       et al., 2022) constructs an evaluation for factual queries from Wikidata. They focus on temporally
1799
       sensitive knowledge with known start and end dates in a specific Wikidata snapshot. Notably, they
1800
       propose TempoT5 to jointly model text and timestamp which allows a language model to answer
1801   temporal questions that change over time such “Who is the president”. EvolvingQA (Kim et al., 2024)
1802   is also a benchmark for training and evaluating on Wikipedia over time where a LLM automatically
1803   generates question-answers from 6 months of articles in 2023. We avoid using any LLMs for
1804   generating our evaluations to prevent transfer of bias. TIQ (Jia et al., 2024) benchmark consists of
1805   10k questions-answers based on significant events for the years 1801–2025.
1806
       Temporal generalization. Beyond understanding the past, LLMs need to be prepared for the future.
1807   Li et al. (2024b) observes performance deterioration of public LLMs on Wikipedia, news, code, and
1808   arXiv papers after their training data cutoff date. They particularly use compression rate achieved by
1809   treating an LLM as a general input compressor using arithmetic coding (Delétang et al., 2024). Our
1810   comprehensive evaluations on CommonCrawl, Wikipedia, news articles, StackExchange, and code
1811   evaluations verifies their results and more comprehensively shows that the rate of deterioration is
1812   domain-specific. DyKnow (Mousavi et al., 2024) evaluations also reaffirm that LLMs private and
1813   open-source LLMs have outdated knowledge by asking them questions constructed using Wikidata.
1814   They also observe LLMs output inconsistent answers in response to prompt variations and current
1815
       knowledge editing methods do not reduce outdatedness. TAQA (Zhao et al., 2024) further demonstrate
       that pretrained LLMs mostly answer questions using knowledge from years before their pretraining
1816
       cutoff. They construct question/answers from Wikipedia for years 2000–2023 and propose three
1817
       methods to improve the temporal alignment of models. Similar observations have been made in
1818   RealTimeQA (Kasai et al., 2024) and TempUN (Beniwal et al., 2024). These works further solidify
1819   the need for continuously updating models with continual pretraining.
1820
1821
       Temporal understanding. General temporal understanding involves reasoning based on the relation
       between existing knowledge. Test of Time (Fatemi et al., 2024) benchmark evaluates temporal
1822
       reasoning, logic, and arithmetics by constructing a synthetic dataset. Their goal is to reduce the
1823
       chance of factual inconsistency in the evaluation using synthetic data. Our benchmark is designed
1824   to be fully realistic based on real data and timestamps to understand the challenges of large-scale
1825   continual pretraining in practice. Gurnee & Tegmark (2024) find that LLMs learn a representation of
1826   space and time with individual neurons that encode spatial and temporal coordinates. They construct
1827   datasets of named entities and find that linear probing LLMs performs well on predicting spatial
1828   and temporal coordinates. Nylund et al. (2024) proposed time vectors that specify a direction in the
1829   model’s weight space that improve performance on text from a specific time period.
1830   Temporal domain-specific evaluations. We can further analyze the temporal understanding of
1831   a model based on the performance on specific domains with varying rates of change. Luu et al.
1832   (2022) studied temporal misalignment such as quantifying temporal degradation of domain-specific
1833   finetuning in four domains: social media, science, news, and food reviews. They observed significant
1834   temporal degradation in domains such as news, social media, and science but less in food reviews.
1835   Gururangan et al. (2020) studied domain-adaptive pretraining and task-adaptive pretraining on
       unlabeled data for four domains in science, news, and reviews. They observe domain/task-adaptive


                                                        34
1836
       pretraining improves performance on the new domain but do not evaluate forgetting on previous
1837   domains. Agarwal & Nenkova (2022) studies the temporal model deterioration on future evaluations.
1838   They find that the deterioration is task-dependent and domain-adaptive pretraining does not help
1839   hypothesizing that limited pretraining data is detrimental in continual pretraining. Jin et al. (2022)
1840   domain-incremental pretraining for four scientific domains as well as temporal pretraining on social
1841   media over 6 years. They focus on the impact on downstream performance after fine-tuning. They
1842   observe distillation-based approaches are the most effective in retaining dowstream performance
1843   for tasks related to earlier domains. Overall, the gap between different continual learning methods
1844   remained small that can be due to the small scale of pretraining. In comparison, our T I C-CC training
1845
       is simulating large-scale pretraining.
1846   Domain/task-continual learning for LLMs. In domain/task continual learning, the model is
1847   presented with a sequence of tasks with predefined labels (Hsu et al., 2018; Van de Ven & Tolias,
1848   2019; Zhou et al., 2023). Each task comes with its training and test sets. In contrast with continual
1849   pretraining, the model needs to support a growing set of labels while compared with temporal
1850   continual learning, the order of tasks are often arbitrary (e.g., Split-CIFAR, Perm-MNIST). Prominent
1851
       methods in this domain are regularization-based methods (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Mirzadeh et al.,
       2020a;b; Farajtabar et al., 2020), replay-based methods that often perform superior (Lomonaco et al.,
1852
       2022; Balaji et al., 2020; Prabhu et al., 2020), and architecture-based methods that adapt the model
1853
       over time (Schwarz et al., 2018; Rusu et al., 2016). Continual learning for language models has
1854   also been dominated by domain/task continual works. Jin et al. (2022) proposed benchmarks for
1855   continually training models on a sequence of research paper domains as well as chronologically-
1856   ordered tweet streams. Razdaibiedina et al. (2023) proposed learning a new soft prompt for each task
1857   and pass soft prompts for all seen tasks to the model which provides adaptability while preventing
1858   catastrophic forgetting. Luo et al. (2023) studied continual learning for instruction tuning and
1859   observed catastrophic forgetting, especially for larger models. Mehta et al. (2023) showed that
1860   generic pretraining implicitly reduces catastrophic forgetting during task incremental finetuning.
1861   Continual pretraining of LLMs. Recent work have studied continual pretraining of foundation
1862   models at large-scale. TiC-CLIP (Garg et al., 2024) proposed a benchmark of training and evaluation
1863   of image-text foundation models and demonstrated the deterioration of existing foundation models
1864   on new data. Lazaridou et al. (2021) studied time-stratified language pretraining on WMT, news, and
1865   arXiv up to 2019 and observed the models become outdated quickly on news data that holds even
1866   for models of various sizes. They study dynamic evaluation as a continual pretraining method that
1867   trains on a stream of chronologically ordered documents and observed that models can be updated.
1868
       However, they did not explore the impact on forgetting and scalability of the method to more generic
       pretraining data over years. Jang et al. (2022b) proposed continual knowledge learning as a new
1869
       problem and suggested that parameter expansion is necessary to retain and learn knowledge. They
1870
       focus on one-step continual pretraining where models are pretrained on C4/Wikipedia data up to
1871   2020 and then trained once more on recent news articles. They find adapter methods perform better
1872   than regularization and replay methods. Adapter methods are not directly applicable in our multi-
1873   year continual pretraining setup where we train in more than 100 steps on large-scale data. Gupta
1874   et al. (2023) proposed simple recipes for continual pretraining of LLMs such as utilizing cyclical
1875   learning rate schedules with warmup and ablated on hyperparameters such as warmup duration when
1876   continuing the pretraining on a fixed pair of pretraining datasets.
1877   Time-aware training. Orthogonal to continual pretraining, one can modify the training or fine-tuning
1878   of a model to include explicit information about time. TempLAMA (Dhingra et al., 2022) proposed
1879   prepending a time prefix to each example during training which gives the model the flexibility to
1880   respond to time-sensitive questions. They train models on news articles where the time can be reliably
1881   extracted. Drinkall et al. (2024) proposed training a series of models with sequential data cutoffs
1882   dates to avoid data contamination with benchmark and private data. The observe no difference across
1883   time on static downstream evaluations when training models on news and Wikipedia
1884   Factual editing and retrieval augmented generation (RAG). Another set of works aims to ad-
1885   dress the staleness of pretrained LLMs without further standard pretraining. One approach is to
1886   surgically edit the facts a model “knows” by identifying and updating the relevant weights within a
1887   model (Mitchell et al., 2022a). Another is to store edits in an explicit memory and learn to reason
1888   over them (Mitchell et al., 2022b). Retrieval augmented generation (RAG) pairs an LLM with new
1889   data sources to retrieve the most relevant document for a query. Generally, continual pretraining
       and RAG are orthogonal approaches to generate up to date responses. RAG methods increase the
