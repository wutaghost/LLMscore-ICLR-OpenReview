009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            DNA is a promising storage medium due to its high information density and
012            longevity. However, the storage process introduces errors, thus algorithms and
013            codes are required for reliable storage. A common important step in the recovery
014            of the information from DNA is trace reconstruction. In the trace reconstruction
015            problem, the goal is to construct a sequence from noisy copies corrupted by dele-
016            tion, insertion, and substitution errors. In this paper, we propose to use language
017
               models trained with next-token prediction for trace reconstruction. A simple chan-
               nel model for the DNA data storage pipeline allows for self-supervised pretrain-
018
               ing on large amounts of synthetic data. Additional finetuning on real data enables
019
               us to adapt to technology-dependent error statistics. The proposed method (TRe-
020            conLM) outperforms state-of-the-art trace reconstruction algorithms for DNA data
021            storage, often recovering significantly more sequences.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION AND M OTIVATION
025
026   An important problem in DNA data storage and biological data analysis is trace reconstruction:
027   Given multiple versions of a string (traces) corrupted by deletions, insertions, and substitutions, the
028   goal of trace reconstruction is to reconstruct the original string from as few traces as possible.
029   Within DNA data storage, the string to be reconstructed is typically a DNA sequence consisting
030   of 50-200 bases adenine (A), cytosine (C), guanine (G), and thymine (T). The traces are corrupted
031   by deletions, insertions, and substitutions through the writing, storage, and reading processes. Trace
032   reconstruction is often used as an important information reconstruction step (Antkowiak et al., 2020;
033   Organick et al., 2018; Bar-Lev et al., 2024).
034   The current trace reconstruction algorithms used for DNA data storage, including general trace re-
035   construction algorithms like MUSCLE (Edgar, 2004) and ITR (Sabary et al., 2020), and algorithms
036   specialized for DNA data storage like RobuSeqNet (Qin et al., 2024) do not perform well for high
037   error rates and a small number of traces. Therefore, it is of interest to develop trace reconstruc-
038   tion methods that can operate in the medium to high noise regime for a small number of traces,
039   typically two to ten traces. Being able to perform trace reconstruction from few traces can further
040   improve the reliability of current DNA data storage systems. Furthermore, some classical algorithms
041
      (Viswanathan & Swaminathan, 2008; Srinivasavaradhan et al., 2021) utilize estimated values for the
      error probabilities but do not consider the distribution of these over the sequence length, others oper-
042
      ate solely on the observed sequences (Sabary et al., 2020; Edgar, 2004; Gopalan et al., 2018). This
043
      motivates the use of data-driven approaches.
044
045   In this work, we treat trace reconstruction for DNA data storage as a next-word prediction problem.
046   We train language models to predict sequence estimates from noisy observations.
047   Our contributions are as follows:
048
049          • We develop a language model-based trace reconstruction method called TReconLM,
050            standing for Trace Reconstruction with a Language Model. The method outperforms state-
051            of-the-art trace reconstruction methods for reconstructing DNA sequences from few traces.
052
               Our method is trained on synthetic data, overcoming the lack of available real data.
053          • We demonstrate that additional finetuning on real datasets can overcome the distribution
               shift to real systems, and can benefit data reconstruction in DNA storage systems.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
055
056
      Theoretical works on trace reconstruction typically ask for the minimum number of traces (obtained
057
      through a deletion channel) such that a binary string can be reconstructed with high probability
058   (Batu et al., 2004; Holenstein et al., 2008; De et al., 2017; Holden & Lyons, 2020; Chase, 2021).
059   However, perfect reconstruction using only a few traces is usually not possible.
060
061
      Several trace reconstruction algorithms suitable for DNA data storage applications have been de-
      veloped. For traces with deletions, Batu et al. (2004) introduced the bitwise majority alignment
062
      (BMA) algorithm, which is based on symbol-wise majority voting. Viswanathan & Swaminathan
063
      (2008) extended the algorithm to traces with deletions, insertions, and substitutions. Gopalan et al.
064   (2018) provide another BMA-based method.
065
066   In the work of Antkowiak et al. (2020), the trace reconstruction was achieved by first performing
067
      a multiple sequence alignment (MSA) using the MUSCLE algorithm (Edgar, 2004) followed by a
      majority vote of each column of the alignment.
068
069   Sabary et al. (2020) proposed several reconstruction methods for DNA data storage based on dy-
070   namic programming, namely shortest common supersequence and longest common subsequence
071   algorithms. The proposed iterative algorithm (ITR) gives state-of-the-art performance. Srini-
072   vasavaradhan et al. (2021) introduced the TrellisBMA algorithm, which combines the BCJR al-
073
      gorithm (Bahl et al., 1974) and BMA-based algorithms.
074    Qin et al. (2024) proposed a neural network-based method (RobuSeqNet) for trace reconstruction
075   using a combination of an attention module, conformer-encoder, and LSTM-decoder. The attention
076   module assigns a score to each sequence within a cluster which allows to reduce the influence of
077   erroneously clustered sequences. Sequences are one-hot encoded and padded to a fixed length to
078   obtain a matrix representation. These matrix representations are then added for different sequences.
079
      For perfectly clustered data, the performance is slightly worse than that of the iterative algorithm
      proposed in Sabary et al. (2020).
080
081   Bar-Lev et al. (2024) proposed an end-to-end solution for DNA data storage (DNAformer), including
082   a coding scheme. This involves using transformers for trace reconstruction. The neural network
083   architecture differs from that of this work in several key aspects. First, like in Qin et al. (2024),
084   sequences are one-hot encoded and padded to a fixed predetermined length. Second, the network
085
      consists of two branches with shared weights, where one branch operates on the reversed data.
      Third, the network uses an alignment module to learn the required alignment of all reads. The
086
      alignment module is followed by a transformer block without positional embeddings and causal
087
      attention masks. The method uses dynamic programming methods to postprocess the neural network
088   outputs. The DNAformer performs similarly to ITR (Sabary et al., 2020).
089
090   In Nahum et al. (2021), a method for single-read trace reconstruction utilizing the transformer ar-
091
      chitecture was proposed where noisy sequences are grouped based on their length, and a separate
      transformer network is applied to each group. The method operates on a set of 256 codewords.
092
093   Finally, Dotan et al. (2023) introduced BetaAlign, an encoder-decoder-based transformer network
094   for multiple sequence alignment of biological sequences.

097
098
      DNA data storage is an interesting storage technology because of its high information density and
099
      longevity. For technological constraints, it is currently not possible to write long sequences of DNA,
100
      and therefore data in DNA is stored on many relatively short sequences, i.e., sequences of length
101   L = 50 to L = 200 bases.

105
106   The set of sequences D is then written in DNA (synthesized) and stored. After reading (sequenc-
107   ing), we obtain many unordered erroneous reads. The first step in recovering the information often
      involves a clustering step to group sequences that are potentially perturbed versions of the same orig-

140   4     M ETHOD
141
142   We formulate trace reconstruction as a next-word prediction problem and train a sequence-to-
143   sequence model (we    consider transformers) on this next-word prediction problem. Given a set of
144
      N sequences C = y1 , . . . , yN , our goal is to train a model fθ with parameters θ so that if we
      prompt it with the concatenation of the observations
145
146                                     p = y1 | y2 | . . . | yN −1 | yN : ,                             (1)
147
148
      then the model provides an estimate x̂ of the original sequence x as a completion for this prompt.
      Here, we introduced the “|” token to concatenate the noisy reads and the “:” token to mark the end of
149
      the noisy observations. Thus, the vocabulary of our model is given by V = {A, C, T, G, “|”, “:” }.
150
151   The model, prompted with p, generates the sequence estimate x̂ by predicting the L tokens following
152   the prompt in an autoregressive manner via multiple forward passes. The generation is performed
153   via greedy sampling, where we choose the most likely token in each prediction step. See Figure 1,
154
      right panel, for an illustration of the method.

156   4.1   P RETRAINING AND PRETRAINING DATA GENERATION
157
158
      In DNA data storage, each sequence is corrupted by deletions, insertions, and substitutions (Heckel
      et al., 2019). As stated before, the original sequence x can be assumed to consist of bases chosen
159
      uniformly at random.
160
161   Therefore, we generate training data as follows. We first generate an original sequence x ∈
      {A, C, G, T}L of length L uniformly at random, and then obtain associated noisy observations

      y1 , . . . , yN by independently corrupting the sequence x with deletions, insertions, and substitu-
163   tions.
164
165   Specifically, to generate the noisy observation yj we go through the original sequence x element-
166
      wise and introduce independent deletions, insertions, substitutions, or transmissions (i.e., no change)
      with probabilities pI , pD , pS , and pT respectively. Note that pI +pD +pS +pT = 1. Figure 2 depicts
167
      the transition from xℓ to xℓ+1 . We then concatenate the noisy observations y1 , . . . , yN together with

171   We generate large numbers of such training examples (see the experiments Section 5.2 for details),
172   and vary the number of traces N in the range from two to ten, since this is a practically relevant
173
      and challenging regime. With regards to the error probabilities, we uniformly sample the error
      probabilities pI , pD , and pS from the interval [0.01; 0.1] for each training instance.

175   On the so-generated training data, we train the transformer model by minimizing the cross entropy

205   struction method, TReconLM, on synthetic data and on real DNA storage data. We find that our
206
      proposed approach based on next-word prediction outperforms state-of-the-art trace reconstruction

208

210            one, x̂, which is the number of positions where the sequences x and x̂ differ, normalized by

212            of sequence estimates shorter than L and cutting sequence estimates longer than length L
213            to length L.
214          • The Levenshtein distance dL (x, x̂), which is the minimum number of single-character edit-
215            events (deletions, insertion, and substitutions) to transform the sequence estimate x̂ into the
               ground-truth vector x, again normalized by the sequence length L.

      5.3.1                     R EAL DATA EXPERIMENT 1: N OISY-DNA DATASET
354   The Noisy-DNA dataset contains M = 16383 ground-truth sequences, each of length L = 60
355   bases, along with unclustered noisy reads. We first discard reads outside the interval of [55, 70]
356   which yields 1.4e7 unclustered noisy observations. Each sequence contains a unique index of length
357   twelve, which can be used to order the sequences for data reconstruction. The error probabilities
358   were estimated in Antkowiak et al. (2020) with pI = 0.057, pD = 0.06, and pS = 0.026, which is
359   high. The error probabilities depend on the position within the sequence (Antkowiak et al., 2020).
360   The insertion probability is up to pI = 0.3 towards the sequence end.
361   We consider two pretrained models consisting of 20M parameters. The first model is trained to
362   reconstruct two to five sequences, and the second model to reconstruct six to ten. Both are trained
363   on 32M instances of form shown in Equation 2. Since we have two pretrained models that we
364   want to finetune, we generate a separate training/validation and testing set for each model. In order
365   to obtain training/validation and test sets, we first cluster all reads by the sequence index, which
366   yields M index-clusters that are separated into train/validation and test index-clusters. For the first
367   model (two to five sequences), we generate a test set out of 500 index-clusters and use the remaining
368
      index-clusters for the generation of a training and validation set. For the second model (six to ten
      sequences), we use 1000 index-clusters for test set generation.
369
370   The training/validation index-clusters are processed as follows. To remove erroneously clustered
371   sequences, we filter each of the index-clusters for training and validation by calculating the Leven-
372   shtein distance to the corresponding ground-truth sequence. Note that a sequence length of L = 60
373   gives around 60 ∗ 0.057 + 60 ∗ 0.06 + 60 ∗ 0.026 = 8.58 edit operations per noisy observation. Given
374
      this value, we discard noisy reads with Levenshtein distance smaller than five and larger than 13 to
      match the error probabilities stated above.
375
376   The test index-clusters are processed as follows to evaluate close to the pipeline of clustering fol-
377   lowed by trace reconstruction used in practice. We join all reads from the test index-clusters into one
      set of reads. This set of reads is clustered using the algorithm proposed in Zorita et al. (2015), which
