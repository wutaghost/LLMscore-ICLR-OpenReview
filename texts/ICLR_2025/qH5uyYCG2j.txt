009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Controllable text-to-speech (TTS) systems aim to manipulate various stylistic at-
012            tributes of generated speech. Despite considerable research in this area, existing
013            models that use natural language prompts as an interface often lack the ability
014            for fine-grained control and face a scarcity of high-quality data. To address these
015            challenges, we propose a two-stage style-controllable TTS system with language
016            models, utilizing a masked-autoencoded style representation as an intermediary.
017            In our approach, we employ a masked autoencoder to learn a content-disentangled
018            style feature of speech, which is then discretized using a residual vector quantizer.
019            In the first stage, an autoregressive transformer is used for the conditional gen-
020
               eration of these style tokens from text and control signals. In the second stage,
               we generate codec tokens from both text and sampled style tokens. Experiments
021
               demonstrate that training the first-stage model on extensive datasets enhances the
022
               robustness of the two-stage model in terms of quality and content accuracy. Ad-
023            ditionally, our model achieves superior control over attributes such as pitch and
024            emotion. By selectively combining discrete labels and speaker embeddings, we
025            can fully control the speaker’s timbre and other stylistic information, or adjust at-
026            tributes like pitch and emotion for a specified speaker. Audio samples are available
027            at https://style-ar-tts.github.io.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Controllable text-to-speech (TTS) systems aim to generate high-fidelity speech while allowing con-
      trol over various style attributes of the synthesized speech, such as speaker timbre, pitch level and
034
      variation, emotion, acoustic environment, etc. Due to its promising applications in digital media
035
      production and human-computer interaction, controllable TTS has been attracting growing interest
036   in the machine learning community with a substantial amount of research working on it (Guo et al.,
037   2023; Leng et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
038
039
      Despite the extensive research on this topic, controllable TTS still faces some unsolved challenges:
      (1) Control Interface Issue. Most existing works use natural language prompts as a medium of
040
      style control, which is friendly for non-professional users. However, style descriptions with natu-
041
      ral language tend to be broad and coarse-grained, making it difficult to precisely control specific
042   attributes. Moreover, the rich diversity of natural language brings more challenges to modeling the
043   relationship between style attributes and prompts. It is also difficult to fully encompass the user in-
044   structions in real-world scenarios, restricting the application of these methods. (2) Data Issue. The
045   training of well-performed TTS systems relies on high-quality speech corpora, which are often lim-
046   ited in both data volume and stylistic diversity. When using natural language as the control interface,
047   the additional cost of generating prompt sentences further restricts the data size. Present controllable
048   TTS datasets (Guo et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024) are often limited to hundreds of hours. This constraint
049   puts challenges on learning precise control abilities and improving generation diversity.
050   In this paper, we propose a fine-grained controllable TTS system. In contrast to natural language
051   prompts, We divide the value ranges of various stylistic attributes of speech into multiple intervals,
052   each represented by a label, and use these labels as conditional inputs to achieve fine-grained control.
053   By selectively combining these labels with speaker embeddings, we can generate new speaker timbre
      while controlling other attributes, or adjust certain attributes such as emotion for a given speaker.

054
      Our controllable TTS system adopts a two-stage generation paradigm using two language models
055   (LMs), with a style representation as an intermediate output. We adopt a masked autoencoder (MAE)
056   which learns to capture content-disentangled style information by reconstructing mel filterbank from
057   the encoded content input and masked fbank. The features extracted by the style encoder of the
058   trained MAE are then discretized and used as an intermediary of the TTS pipeline. Each of the
059   two stages relies on a decoder-only transformer. The first stage generates style tokens conditioned
060   on content and control signals, while the second stage generates codec tokens from the content
061   input and the predicted style tokens. Due to low dependence on high-quality corpora, the style
062   token generation phase can scale up to a large amount of data, boosting control capability and
063
      generation diversity; while in the codec token generation stage, a relatively small amount of data
      is sufficient to learn how to reconstruct codec tokens from content and style units, addressing the
064
      issue of high-quality data scarcity. To enhance the control accuracy of fine-grained attributes, we
065
      introduce classifier-free guidance to the style token generation stage. Moreover, considering the
066   correlations between control signals, we discuss methods to determine the range of low-level label
067   intervals from high-level labels to reduce information conflicts. Experiments indicate that our model
068   achieves good style control ability while keeping decent audio quality and content accuracy.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
072
      2.1   C ONTROLLABLE TEXT- TO - SPEECH
073
074   Controllable TTS aims to enable control over stylistic attributes of the speech during synthesis. The
075   earliest exploration, PromptTTS (Guo et al., 2023), extracts textual features from prompts with a
076   fine-tuned BERT and incorporates them in a TTS backbone with attention. InstructTTS (Yang et al.,
077   2024) achieves a text-controlled expressive TTS system with cross-modal representation learning.
078   PromptTTS 2 (Leng et al., 2023) employs a variational network to generate reference acoustic fea-
079   tures conditioned on text features. Audiobox (Vyas et al., 2023) builds a unified natural-language-
080
      instructed flow-matching model integrating speech, music, and audio generation. TextrolSpeech (Ji
      et al., 2024) integrates natural language style prompt into the condition of VALL-E (Wang et al.,
081
      2023a) for controllable TTS. VoxInstruct (Zhou et al., 2024) merges the content input and style
082
      prompt of TTS into a single composite textual instruction and utilizes a multimodal codec language
083   model as the backbone for TTS. Unlike these methods using natural language as the control inter-
084   face, we adopt a two-stage controllable TTS system with attribute labels for fine-grained control.
085
086   2.2   S PEECH ATTRIBUTE DECOUPLING
087
088   Some works attempt to obtain speech representations that disentangle content and stylistic attributes
089   through self-supervised learning or other methods, facilitating applications like voice conversion,
090   acoustic attribute editing, and controllable TTS. NANSY (Choi et al., 2021) deconstructs input
091   speech into multiple information flows explicitly, and then reconstructs speech from these flows,
092
      obtaining a model capable of voice conversion, pitch shift, and other applications. DSVAE Lian
      et al. (2022b;a; 2023) presented a self-supervised method to disentangle content information and
093
      global speaker information, in an end-to-end manner. Prosody-TTS (Huang et al., 2023) utilizes
094
      an MAE to learn a prosody representation disentangled from content and speaker timbre, boosting
095   expressive TTS. SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al., 2023) decouples different aspects of speech infor-
096   mation hierarchically across different quantizer layers by distillation, thereby unifying semantic and
097   acoustic representations; NaturalSpeech 3 (Ju et al., 2024) proposes a codec that factorizes speech
098   into individual subspaces representing different attributes like content, prosody, timbre, and acoustic
099   details, facilitating modeling of intricate speech. In this paper, we adopt a masked autoencoder to
100   extract content-disentangled style features of the speech, which are then used as an intermediate
101   representation to facilitate controllable TTS.

      3.2   S TYLE MASKED AUTOENCODER AND STYLE TOKENIZATION
144
145   The style masked autoencoder aims to learn to extract style information other than content, like
146   speaker timbre, prosody, and acoustic environment by reconstructing mel filterbanks from masked
147   ones and an additional content input with reconstruction and several auxiliary losses. Its architecture
148   is illustrated in figure 1 (a). The two branches of input, which are masked fbanks and a temporal-
149   aligned phoneme sequence where each phoneme is duplicated by its duration, are processed by
150   two encoders separately. Both the style encoder and content encoder are multi-layer transformer
151   encoders. The output of the two encoders together with sinusoidal positional embedding are added
152   and fed to the transformer decoder.
153   Following Huang et al. (2023), we append four different linear heads at the end of the decoder for
154   output projection used for different optimization objectives. The four objectives are (1) reconstruc-
155   tion loss Lr : mean square loss between the masked fbank patches and the output of the reconstruc-
156   tion head; (2) contrastive loss Lc : InfoNCE loss to maximize the similarity between the head output
157   and the corresponding fbank patch, while minimizing its similarity with non-corresponding fbank
158   patches; (3) pitch classification loss Lp and (4) energy classification loss Le , which are cross-entropy
159
      losses calculated on log-scale fundamental frequency (f0) and the L2-norm of the amplitude spec-
      trogram from short-time Fourier transform, respectively, both of which are frame-level and binned
160
      to 256 scales. The final loss is a linear combination of the four losses:
161
                                          L = λr Lr + λc Lc + λp Lp + λe Le                                          (1)


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      where λr = 10, and λc , λp , λe are all 1. Intuitively, this design enables the MAE to extract content
163   information from the encoded feature of the aligned phonemes, while extracting style information
164   from the encoded feature of the masked fbank for reconstruction. Once the MAE finishes training,
165   its style encoder should be capable of extracting style information from speech.
166
      To reduce the sequence length for language modeling and eliminate redundant information in the
167
      style features, we conduct phone-level merge by averaging the frame-level features in the range of
168
      each phoneme. After that, we train an RVQ with 3 codebooks over the phone-level style features
169   for discretizing the style representation for LM-style modeling. The RVQ module is trained inde-
170   pendently from the MAE. Subsequent codec generation results using phonemes and ground truth
171   style tokens demonstrate that this style representation encapsulates rich style information with little
172   content leakage.

175
176   We use a decoder-only transformer for autoregressive generation for both of the two stages. Specifi-
177   cally, we adopt the multi-scale transformer as the backbone model (Yang et al.; Huang et al., 2024),
178
      which utilizes a stacked global-local transformer architecture to handle detailed acoustic token mod-
      eling and has exhibited remarkable capabilities in audio synthesis and modeling intrinsic relation-
179
      ships among different modalities, while remaining high efficiency in generating long sequences
180
      based on sub-quadratic self-attention. During training, the conditional inputs and target outputs are
181   concatenated into a single sequence and fed to the transformer, with each part having a modality-
182   specific start and end token at both ends. The LMs model the conditional distribution using next-
183   token prediction with cross-entropy loss calculated on the target output part.
184

257
258   The datasets used for different stages are illustrated in table 1. We adopt large-scale corpora for
259   training the style MAE, where we combine Gigaspeech-xl (Chen et al., 2021) and Librispeech
260   (Panayotov et al., 2015). We use Gigaspeech-xl solely for training the style LM, and use high-

262   evaluation, we randomly pick approximately 200 samples respectively from LibriTTS, GigaSpeech,
263   and a dialogue dataset, DailyTalk (Lee et al., 2023), to evaluate the models’ performance across
264   different data domains. We note the specific sizes of each test set after the dataset it is sampled from
265   in the table.
266   To train the style LM, we need to label the different attributes of the data. We utilize multiple
267   annotation tools to extract continuous values or classification probabilities for different speech at-
268   tributes, and split them into different bins by performing equidistant division within an upper and
269   lower boundary that covers most of the data to obtain the discrete control labels. Details of labeling
      tools and splitting strategies are provided in appendix B.

282   4.2     M ETRICS
283
284   Our evaluation of model performance primarily consists of speech naturalness, content accuracy,
285   speaker similarity, speech reconstruction quality, and control accuracy. We use objective metrics for
286   evaluation. For speech naturalness, we adopt UTMOS (Saeki et al., 2022) to predict the MOS score
287   of each sample and report mean values and 95% confidence intervals for each test set. For content
288
      accuracy, we use Whisper large-v3 (Radford et al., 2022) to transcribe the speech and calculate the
      word error rate (WER) against the ground truth text. For speaker similarity, we compute cosine
289
      similarity on speaker embedding extracted by wavlm-base-plus-sv 1 . For reconstruction quality, we
290
      calculate MCD between generated and ground truth speech with tools provided in fairseq 2 . For
291   control accuracy, we use the annotation tools to extract attribute labels and compute percentage
292   accuracy with ground truth labels. Considering the challenges of achieving precise control with
293   fine-grained labels, we make some relaxation that results differing from the ground truth attribute
294   label by one bin are also considered correct.

298   4.3.1    R ECONSTRUCT SPEECH STYLE FROM STYLE TOKENS
299
300   To validate that our style tokens encapsulate rich voice style information, we reconstruct speech
301   from phonemes and ground truth (GT) style tokens, and compare them with original speech, com-
302   pressed speech from the codec, and zero-shot TTS results. We use YourTTS (Casanova et al., 2022)
303
      and XTTS-V2 (Casanova et al., 2024) as representative zero-shot TTS systems for comparison. The
      results on LibriTTS and Gigaspeech are shown in table 2. For results on both test sets, our model
304
      achieves comparable UTMOS to recent zero-shot TTS systems. This demonstrates the reliability of
305
      our model in terms of speech naturalness. Besides, our model achieves comparable speaker similar-
306   ity with zero-shot TTS systems, indicating that the style tokens contain rich speaker information for
307   speech synthesis. Moreover, the reconstruction results have significantly lower MCD than zero-shot
308   TTS, proving that it is closer to the original audio in terms of prosody and other style information
309   like acoustic environment, which further validates the effectiveness of our style MAE.
310
      Figure 2 illustrates the spectrogram of some sample results on DailyTalk. It can be observed that
311
      despite some over-smoothing in certain details, the acoustic LM is able to leverage the style infor-
312   mation contained in the style tokens to achieve accurate reconstruction on out-of-domain samples,
313   indicating the effectiveness of our style representation. In contrast, zero-shot TTS that only leverages
314   speaker information cannot achieve prosody reconstruction. On the other hand, when we swap the
315   phonemes and style tokens from different samples, the model fails to generate meaningful speech.
316   This demonstrates that our style representation does not result in significant content information
317   leakage, and contains more complex stylistic information beyond simple speaker, pitch, and energy
318   attributes.

      5   C ONCLUSION
510
      In this paper, we propose an LM-based fine-grained controllable TTS system. We adopt a two-stage
511
      generation pipeline, with an autoregressive transformer as the backbone for each stage. We design
512   a masked autoencoder for extracting content-disentangled style features from the speech and use
513   the discretized feature as the intermediate output of the TTS pipeline. By selectively combining
514   discrete control labels with speaker embeddings, our model supports both generating new speaker
515   timbre while controlling other attributes, and controlling emotion and pitch variation for a specified
516   speaker. Experiments indicate the effectiveness of our model.
517
      In the future, we may explore more diverse control signals and employ techniques such as prompt
518   engineering to integrate large language models with controllable TTS, enabling support for both
519   natural language prompts and fine-grained control signals.
520
521
522
      6   L IMITATIONS
523
      Despite that our approach achieves fine-grained control over multiple style attributes, our method
524
      and evaluation protocols still suffer from several limitations: 1) Due to the performance limitations
525   of labeling tools, there may be errors in the attribute annotations of the training data, which could
526   lead to a decline in the model’s control capabilities. 2) Evaluation with label combinations from real
527   data may present issues of uneven distribution, particularly for attributes with significant distribution
528   bias, such as SNR and C50. Therefore, the evaluation may not fully accurately reflect the model’s
529   control capabilities. 3) Due to their small proportion in the training data, some marginal label
530   combinations may lead to degraded generated audio and diminished control performance. We will
531   explore solutions to these issues in future work.
