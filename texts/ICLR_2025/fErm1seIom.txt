009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Unsupervised anomaly detection in time series presents significant challenges,
012            especially due to the lack of labeled data and the prevalence of highly imbal-
013            anced datasets. Traditional statistical and machine learning methods often suffer
014            from low recall and computational inefficiency. While deep learning techniques
015            can automatically extract features, they still struggle with data imbalance. This
016
               paper introduces a novel anomaly detection model, Feature map Matrix Profile
               with an AutoEncoder (FMP-AE), which integrates matrix profile techniques with
017
               deep learning. The model uses a 1D-CNN to extract features and compute the
018
               matrix profile. A new Matrix Profile loss function is introduced and combined
019            with the Autoencoder’s reconstruction loss to enhance anomaly detection. The
020            approach also incorporates a sliding window technique to improve sensitivity to
021            sparse anomalies and increase efficiency. Experimental results on the UCR250
022            benchmark datasets demonstrate the model’s superior performance across mul-
023            tiple metrics, including accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC. These
024            results highlight the FMP-AE model’s ability to efficiently process large-scale
025            datasets and generalize well across diverse time series domains, offering signifi-
026            cant improvements in both detection accuracy and computational efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      As society and industrial processes continue to digitize, numerous sensor-equipped devices generate
031   vast amounts of time series data, including data from financial markets, meteorological data, web
032   traffic monitoring, and manufacturing sensors. Anomaly detection (AD), often referred to as outlier
033   detection (Kieu et al., 2019), is the technique used to identify data points that exhibit substantial
034   deviations from the norm. This field of research has gained widespread attention since the 1960s
035   (Grubbs, 1969), with increasing importance due to growing demand and applications (Pang et al.,
036   2021). The primary goal is to detect these abnormal behaviors to enable corrective or preventive
037   measures. Anomaly detection plays a crucial role across numerous fields, including identifying
038   fraudulent or manipulative trading behaviors in finance (Hilal et al., 2022), monitoring equipment
039
      performance to prevent breakdowns in industrial manufacturing (Hsieh et al., 2019), assessing pa-
      tient vital signs for early detection of potential health problems in healthcare (Chauhan & Vig, 2015),
040
      and recognizing unusual network traffic to guard against cyber attacks and data breaches in network
041
      security (Hwang et al., 2020). Generally, Anomalies are categorized into three primary categories:
042   point anomalies, contextual anomalies, and collective anomalies, which are common across different
043   datasets.
044
045
      Time-Series Anomaly Detection (TSAD) is critical in applications like fault detection in industrial
      systems, health monitoring, and fraud detection in finance. However, TSAD faces unique challenges
046
      due to the sequential nature of time-series data. One major issue is the scarcity of labeled anoma-
047
      lies, as they are rare and often require domain expertise to identify, making unsupervised meth-
048   ods particularly important. Additionally, time-series data often varies significantly across different
049   domains, and existing methods struggle to generalize due to the dynamic temporal dependencies,
050   making generalizability a key requirement. Another challenge is efficiency. Real-time monitor-
051   ing systems must process vast amounts of time-series data at scale, yet many current methods lack
052   the computational efficiency needed for large-scale deployment. Despite the development of vari-
053   ous approaches, many still depend heavily on labeled data, lack cross-domain adaptability, or are
      computationally expensive, limiting their applicability in real-world TSAD scenarios.

054
      To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel model, the Feature map Matrix Profile combined
055   with an Autoencoder (FMP-AE), which addresses the key issues of label scarcity, generalizability,
056   and computational efficiency. Our approach introduces a new Matrix Profile (MP) loss function
057   that complements the Autoencoder’s reconstruction loss, enabling unsupervised anomaly detection
058   without the need for labeled data. This effectively addresses the challenge of label scarcity by lever-
059   aging the power of unsupervised learning, allowing the model to detect anomalies in a wide range
060   of time-series data. Additionally, we incorporate a sliding window technique that further mitigates
061   label scarcity and data imbalance by treating smaller subsequences as individual units, making it
062   easier to detect local anomalies and reducing the impact of rare events. To further enhance the gen-
063
      eralizability of our model, we conduct experiments on univariate time-series datasets from multiple
      domains, demonstrating that the FMP-AE model exhibits strong cross-domain adaptability and can
064
      generalize well across varied time-series patterns. Additionally, we incorporate 1D Convolutional
065
      Neural Networks (CNN) within the FMP-AE model, which not only speeds up Matrix Profile com-
066   putation but also allows for parallel processing, making the model highly scalable and suitable for
067   large-scale, real-time applications. This design addresses the challenge of efficiency by reducing
068   computational overhead and improving processing time without sacrificing accuracy.
069
      The key contributions of this paper are the following:

               verse domains.
078          • We employ a sliding window technique to address the issue of label scarcity and data

087   2   R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Unsupervised anomaly detection in time series is an essential real-world problem that has been ex-
090   tensively studied. Approaches for detecting anomalies can be categorized into statistical, machine
091   learning, and deep learning methods. Statistical methods detect anomalies by calculating statistical
092   characteristics of time series data. While these methods are generally simple and computation-
093
      ally efficient, they tend to be less effective in capturing complex time series patterns and struggle
      with non-linear or high-dimensional data. Machine learning methods model time series data using
094
      supervised or unsupervised learning algorithms to detect anomalies, such as Local Outlier Factor
095
      (Breunig et al., 2000), One-Class SVM (Erfani et al., 2016), and Support Vector Data Description
096   (Zhou et al., 2021). These classic methods do not take into account temporal information, making
097   them challenging to generalize to unknown real-world scenarios.
098
099
      Deep learning methods have demonstrated considerable advantages in the field of time series
      anomaly detection in recent years. It aims to learn feature representations and calculate anomaly
100
      scores through neural networks for detecting anomalies. Deep anomaly detection methods include
101
      Autoencoder (AE) (Sakurada & Yairi, 2014), Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (An & Cho, 2015),
102   Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), and Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTM) (Malhotra
103   et al., 2015). In addition, many hybrid approaches have been proposed. The LSTM-VAE method
104   (Lin et al., 2020) uses a VAE to extract robust local features from short windows of the sequence,
105   then pass these features to an LSTM to estimate the long-term dependencies in the sequence, thereby
106   achieving anomaly detection. The Beat-GAN (Zhou et al., 2019) uses the Generative Adversarial
107   Networks to generate samples similar to real data through adversarial training between the genera-
      tor and discriminator models, detecting anomalies by comparing generated samples with real data.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Anomaly Transformer method (Xu et al., 2022) introduces the Association Discrepancy of time
109   series with a new Anomaly-Attention mechanism to extract information for anomaly detection.
110
111   One-dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks (1D-CNN) have been applied to time series data
112
      (Yin et al., 2020), utilizing convolutional operations to effectively extract local patterns and fea-
      tures. This approach has proven to be well-suited for detecting local anomalies in time series data,
113
      as shown in previous studies. In 2016, Eamonn Keogh’s team at the University of California, River-
114
      side, introduced the matrix profile data structure (Yeh et al., 2016), specifically designed to analyze
115   internal associations within subsequences of time series data by computing a similarity matrix. It
116   is primarily used for pattern (i.e., Motif) discovery and anomaly detection in both univariate and
117   multivariate time series data. There are several algorithms for computing Matrix Profile, and effi-
118   ciency is crucial for large time series. Notable methods include STAMP (Yeh et al., 2016), STOMP
119   (Zhu et al., 2016), and SCRIMP (Zhu et al., 2018). Among them, SCRIMP and anomaly detec-
120   tion techniques like MERLIN++ (Nakamura et al., 2023) achieve the highest accuracy. However,
121   MP methods can be computationally demanding due to pairwise distance calculations. In scenarios
122   where rapid anomaly detection is critical, we aim to optimize the MP computation to reduce costs.
123   Despite the development of numerous anomaly detection methods for various datasets, a recent
124   survey (Goswami et al., 2023) found that no single method consistently outperforms others across
125   all datasets. They performed extensive experiments on various real-world datasets and introduced
126   an innovative robust rank aggregation approach to merge several surrogate metrics into a cohesive
127   model selection standard.
128   This study presents an accurate, efficient anomaly detection method with strong generalization abil-
129   ity, demonstrating its effectiveness on the UCR datasets. This approach leverages Autoencoder to
130   capture normal patterns and employs matrix profiles to assess local similarities, significantly en-
131   hancing the performance of anomaly detection models.

      3     M ETHOD
134
135
      A time series T = ⟨t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ⟩ represents a sequence of vectors arranged in chronological order.
136   A subsequence of length k that begins at position i is represented as xi = ⟨ti , ti+1 , . . . , ti+k−1 ⟩,
137   with the condition 1 ≤ i ≤ n − m + 1. An anomaly within a time series is characterized by a notable
138   divergence in behavior or patterns compared to the series’ typical behavior.
139
      Anomaly Score (AS) quantifies how much a data point or vector deviates from the expected normal
140
      behavior. For a time series T = ⟨t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ⟩, the anomaly score AS(ti ) for each ti indicates
141
      the likelihood of ti being an outlier. Generally, a higher value of AS(ti ) indicates an increased
142   probability that the vector is an anomaly.
143
144   Problem Description. For a given time series T , the goal is to develop a model that generates an
145
      anomaly score AS(ti ) for every data point ti . A higher score indicates a greater deviation from the
      normal behavior, thus increasing the probability that ti is an anomaly.

      3.1   C ALCULATE OPTIMIZED -MP BY 1D-CNN
149
      One-Dimension Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN) is usually used for processing sequence
150   and time series data. Our model framework is shown in Figure 1. First, We preprocess the time
151   series data using a sliding window technique, where the window moves step-by-step across the
152   series, generating overlapping segments. This approach helps handle long sequences and addresses
153   data imbalance and label scarcity, as each segment can be independently analyzed for anomalies.
154   Additionally, we introduce a rule that if any point within a window is flagged as anomalous, the
155   entire window is considered anomalous. This increases the likelihood of detecting larger anomalous
156   regions and ensures that sparse anomalies or larger abnormal events are captured as a whole, rather
157   than fragmented.
158   Each segment is input into the 1D-CNN for feature extraction. The CNN consists of three con-
159   volutional layers with batch normalization and ReLU activation, followed by Max Pooling. The
160   convolutional layers capture local patterns, while pooling reduces dimensionality and highlights key
161   features for anomaly detection. After feature extraction, we compute the Matrix Profile by calcu-
      lating Euclidean distances between feature maps of different segments. These distances form an


                                                          3

162
      optimized matrix profile, which identifies the most similar segments. The segments with unusually
163   large distances from their most similar counterparts are flagged as anomalies.

165   The Matrix Profile (MP) serves as a data structure specifically designed for analyzing time series. It
166
      consists of a vector that holds similarity scores among the subsequences within the time series. Tra-
      ditional Matrix Profile is computed directly on raw time series data, but it can be sensitive to noise,
167
      trends, and seasonality. In our approach, we first extract high-dimensional feature representations
168
      from the time series using a 1D-CNN. These features are more robust and better suited for capturing
169   complex patterns, which allows for a more reliable matrix profile calculation. Given a time series T
170   and a predetermined subsequence length k, the set of all subsequences of length k can be defined as:

      5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
501
502   In this paper, we propose a hybrid method, FMP-AE, which combines traditional matrix profile
503   techniques with deep learning for univariate time series anomaly detection. We employ a 1D-CNN
504   to extract features and compute the matrix profile, while introducing a novel Matrix Profile loss
505   function that integrates with the reconstruction loss during model training. The MP loss captures
506   internal similarities between sequences, and the reconstruction loss accounts for discrepancies be-
507   tween the original and reconstructed sequences. We conducted comparative experiments and five
508   ablation studies on the UCR250 datasets. The results show that FMP-AE significantly outperforms
509   traditional methods in key metrics such as Precision, Recall, F1-score, and AUC-ROC. Our ap-
510
      proach not only improves anomaly detection accuracy but also enhances computational efficiency
      and generalization across diverse time series domains, demonstrating the advantages of combining
511
      matrix profile techniques with deep learning for robust and scalable anomaly detection. Future work
512
      could focus on dynamic or multi-scale windowing techniques, adaptive thresholding methods, and
513   testing in real-world applications like industrial control and financial monitoring to further enhance
514   anomaly detection.

      A.1   T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
651
652   Our method FMP-AE uses 1D-CNN to compute the Matrix Profile, which can significantly reduce
653   the computational complexity compared to traditional methods. It can also leverage parallelism to
654   accelerate execution and shorten runtime.
655   (1) Traditional Matrix Profile Algorithms
656
657   The traditional Matrix Profile computation method uses a sliding window mechanism on the original
658
      time series to calculate the Euclidean distance or other similarity metrics between each window.
      Here we introduce two commonly used traditional algorithms. The first is STAMP (Scalable Time
659
      series Anytime Matrix Profile), a brute-force global computation method that uses Fast Fourier
660
      Transform (FFT) to calculate the similarity between sliding windows, with a time complexity of
661   O(n2 log n). The other is STOMP (Scalable Time series Ordered Matrix Profile), an incremental
662   update method that optimizes STAMP, reducing time complexity to O(n2 ). However, it still operates
663   at a quadratic complexity.
664
      (2) Our Method: FMP-AE
665
666   The 1D-CNN extracts local features through convolution operations. While convolution is still a
667   sliding window operation, it reduces the computational load by sharing weights. The time complex-
668   ity of CNN is generally O(nf k), where n is the sequence length, f is the number of filters, and k is
669   the kernel size. Comparatively, 1D-CNN can effectively lower the computational complexity, espe-
670
      cially for long time series. Additionally, 1D-CNN operations are highly parallelizable, especially on
      GPUs or other hardware accelerators, which can significantly improve computational speed. Many
671
      Deep Learning Frameworks like PyTorch offer efficient GPU support for CNNs. By moving the
672
      model and data to the GPU, convolution operations and backpropagation are automatically paral-
673   lelized. we use ’torch.cuda()’ to ensure both the model and data run on the GPU. Convolution
674   operations also are highly parallel because the computation for each output feature map is nearly
675   independent, allowing for parallel processing of different windows and channels. On the other hand,
676   we can process multiple time series segments at once, called Batch Processing. By increasing the
677   batch size, data processing parallelism can be significantly improved, especially on multi-GPU se-
678   tups. Specifically, each sequence segment in a batch can be independently processed, enabling par-
679   allel computation. Finally, for the computation of similarity matrices in the matrix profile, parallel
680   matrix operation libraries (such as GPU tensor operations in PyTorch) can be utilized to accelerate
681
      the process. This enables the distribution of pairwise computations across multiple threads or GPU
      cores. Additionally, CNNs reduce feature redundancy between windows, further speeding up the
682
      computation. For instance, by selecting appropriate filter numbers and kernel sizes, the computa-
683
      tional load of CNNs can be significantly reduced without substantially affecting performance.
684
685   Consequently, compared to traditional matrix profile algorithms, using 1D-CNN for feature extrac-
686   tion and MP computation reduces the complexity from O(n2 ) to O(nf k), making it more efficient,
687
      particularly for long time series. 1D-CNN is highly parallelizable, especially on GPUs, where batch
      processing, convolution operations, and matrix computations can greatly accelerate both training
688
      and inference. Increasing the batch size, enabling GPU computation, parallelizing matrix opera-
689
      tions, and leveraging multi-core CPUs are effective strategies to optimize parallelism. Our method
690   substantially reduces the time required for matrix profile computation while effectively leveraging
691   parallelism to accelerate the runtime. This advancement enables real-time or near-real-time anomaly
692   detection in large-scale datasets, expanding its applicability to time series anomaly detection appli-
693   cations.

      how effectively the model can reconstruct the input time series, L̃MP is the difference in the matrix

      measured using Mean Squared Error (MSE):

715   The second derivative of the loss function measures its curvature, which reflects the convergence

718   Euclidean distance between the original and reconstructed time series, assessing whether the model

726   feature representations F , the similarity matrix is computed as S(i, j) = similarity(Fi , Fj ). This
727   similarity matrix reflects the similarity between different parts of the time series. After computing

733   CNN feature extraction layers are designed appropriately, this loss function is also convex. Even if
734   the non-linear structure of the CNN may introduce local non-convexity, the dynamically adjusted λ
735   helps to gradually overcome local minima traps.
736
      Batch Normalization In our loss function design, batch normalization is applied to ensure con-
737
      sistency in loss calculations across different batches, addressing potential variations in data distri-
738   bution. We normalize using the mean reconstruction error within each batch for the reconstruction
739   loss, which effectively adjusts the scale of the loss. This normalization is based on the average
740   reconstruction error across all batches, ensuring that reconstruction errors are comparable across
741   batches. The Matrix Profile loss is normalized using the global mean of the Matrix Profile values
742   across all batches. Batch normalization is necessary because batch-level data characteristics may
743   vary significantly. Without normalization, it could lead to instability in the training process, such
744   as gradient explosion or premature convergence. By normalizing based on actual reconstruction
745   errors and Matrix Profile values, we ensure that each batch contributes more evenly to the total
746
      loss function, facilitating more effective model learning and improving overall training stability and
      robustness.
747
748   Dynamically Adjusted Coefficient λ During model training, λ is incrementally adjusted to increase
749   the emphasis on matrix profile error over time. This approach offers several benefits. At the early
750   stages of training, the model primarily focuses on reconstructing input data using the Autoencoder,
751   which allows it to learn the overall structure of the data. As training advances, λ is gradually in-
752
      creased, shifting the model’s attention toward minimizing the matrix profile error. This encourages
      the model to focus more on local feature similarities or emerging anomaly patterns, which is crucial
753
      for detecting subtle irregularities in the time series.
754
755   This gradual increase in λ is a strategic choice. If the model were to emphasize anomaly detection
      too early, it might converge prematurely to suboptimal solutions or fail to grasp the global structure


                                                           14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      of the data. By initially prioritizing global feature learning and progressively incorporating the
757   matrix profile error, the model can effectively capture both broad patterns and local anomalies.
758
759   Gradient Clipping In addition, during the optimization of the loss function, gradient clipping is
760
      employed to mitigate the risk of gradient explosion, a problem that becomes particularly critical in
      the later stages of training when λ increases. As the matrix profile error term grows in influence,
761
      it can lead to larger gradient updates during backpropagation, potentially destabilizing the training
762
      process. Without proper control, these large gradients could cause abrupt changes in model weights,
763   resulting in convergence issues or even complete divergence.
764
765   By applying gradient clipping, we limit the magnitude of the gradients to a predefined threshold,
766
      ensuring that updates remain within a stable range. This not only preserves the model’s ability
      to learn from significant error signals but also prevents excessive weight shifts that could otherwise
767
      cause the loss function to oscillate or explode. Consequently, gradient clipping serves as an essential
768
      procedure, promoting stable convergence while allowing the model to effectively learn both global
769   structures and local anomaly as λ continues to increase during training.
770
771   In essence, gradient clipping strikes a balance between preventing overly aggressive updates and
772
      maintaining the flexibility needed for the model to adapt to the increasing emphasis on the matrix
      profile error, ensuring smoother and more reliable optimization.

955   Feature Map & Matrix Profile When calculating the Matrix Profile, larger values in the feature
956   maps indicate that certain segments of the time series exhibit pronounced characteristics, suggesting

958   relatively ordinary and do not stand out in the model. To compute the similarity between different
959   time series windows, the features extracted by the 1D-CNN (i.e.,the feature maps) serve as the
960   primary input.
961   The process begins with calculating the Euclidean distance between feature maps to construct a
962   similarity matrix. This matrix captures the similarity between various time series windows by mea-
963   suring how closely related they are in terms of their extracted features. Following this, the Matrix
964   Profile is computed based on the minimum distances found in the similarity matrix. Smaller values
965   in the Matrix Profile denote higher similarity between windows, suggesting that the segments are
966   closely related. Conversely, larger values indicate significant differences between windows, which
967   may help in identifying unusual or anomalous time segments.
968   Therefore, the Matrix Profile reflects both large and small values from the feature maps. Specifically,
969   anomalies detected in the feature maps tend to result in higher values in the Matrix Profile, signaling
970   potential anomalies or deviations from normal patterns. On the other hand, normal values in the
971   feature maps lead to lower values in the Matrix Profile, indicating that the time series segment is
      similar to other segments and is more likely to represent typical, non-anomalous behavior. This
