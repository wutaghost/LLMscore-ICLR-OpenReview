011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            While Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting approaches have significantly consoli-
014            dated the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs), they still face
015
               limitations that require extensive human effort or have performance needs to be
               improved. Existing endeavors have focused on bridging these gaps; however, these
016
               approaches either hinge on external data and cannot completely eliminate manual
017
               effort, or they fall short in effectively directing LLMs to generate high-quality ex-
018            emplary prompts. To address the said pitfalls, we propose a novel prompt approach
019            for automatic reasoning named LBS3, inspired by curriculum learning which better
020            reflects human learning habits. Specifically, LBS3 initially steers LLMs to recall
021            easy-to-hard proxy queries that are pertinent to the target query. Following this,
022            it invokes a progressive strategy that utilizes exemplary prompts stemmed from
023            easy-proxy queries to direct LLMs in solving hard-proxy queries, enabling the
024            high-quality of the proxy solutions. Finally, our extensive experiments in various
025            reasoning-intensive tasks with varying open- and closed-source LLMs show that
026
               LBS3 achieves strongly competitive performance compared to the SOTA baselines.
               Our code is here: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LBS3-B926.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With super-sized training corpora and computational cluster resources, Large Language Mod-
032   els (LLMs) have demonstrated emergent capabilities, thus enabling state-of-the-art performance in a
033   wide range of natural language tasks (Wei et al., 2022a; Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023;
034   Liang et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). However, directly applying
035   LLMs to complex reasoning tasks (e.g., mathematical reasoning, commonsense reasoning, etc.) in a
036   naive manner presents significant challenges (Yin et al., 2023b; Wei et al., 2022b; Kojima et al., 2022).
037   For instance, the performance may be inadequate when simply feeding queries or using few-shot
038   query-answer pairs in in-context learning (ICL) approaches for these kinds of tasks. Recent studies
039
      have shed light on that prompting LLMs to generate multiple reasoning steps (i.e., rationale) can
      markedly enhance their ability to reason, resulting in the development of the chain-of-thought (CoT)
040
      prompting (Wei et al., 2022b; Kojima et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2022b; Aggarwal
041
      et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024).
042   Current CoT prompting approaches fall broadly into two categories, namely Few-Shot CoT (FS-
043   CoT) (Wei et al., 2022b) and Zero-Shot CoT(ZS-CoT) (Kojima et al., 2022). Among them, FS-CoT
044   involves providing LLMs with few task-specific context exemplars of query-rationale-answer triplets
045   tied to the target query to prompt the generation of reasoning steps; ZS-CoT instead stimulates LLMs’
046   reasoning capabilities by furnishing general trigger instructions (such as "Let’s think step
047   by step") appended to the target query.
048   Despite their considerable success, obstacles persist in the field of prompt engineering research that
049   plague real-world applications. FS-CoT, for example, delivers well-crafted exemplary prompts but
050   at the cost of labor-intensive manual annotations. To mitigate this, some efforts have been made
051   to enhance the quality of exemplary prompts by retrieving the most relevant, complex and diverse
052   existing queries or exemplars for the target task, which is achieved by tapping into external sources
053   related to the task at hand, such as datasets or corpora, and employing various pre-defined similarity
      metrics (Liu et al., 2021; Rubin et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Wu


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2022; Ye & Durrett, 2023; Diao et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2023). Nevertheless, the required
055   external sources these approaches rely on may not always be available in practice, and they may
056   not completely obviate the need for manual labeling. Moreover, while ZS-CoT offers versatility, its
057   performance often lags behind FS-CoT in a variety of complex reasoning tasks.
058
      To overcome the aforementioned issues, recent initiatives (e.g., Self-ICL (Chen et al., 2023), Auto-
059
      ICL (Yang et al., 2023) and Analogical Prompting (Yasunaga et al., 2023)) work on bootstrapping
060
      LLMs to self-generate few-shot new proxy queries that are relevant and diverse to the target query,
061   along with constructing corresponding exemplary prompts of triplets, thereby augmenting their
062   capabilities to tackle reasoning tasks. Essentially, these methods draw parallels with the concept of
063   analogical reasoning in psychology, where individuals rely on past related experiences to approach
064   new problems (Vosniadou & Ortony, 1989). The underlying insight behind them is that pre-trained
065   LLMs (such as Llama3, GPT-3.5 and GPT-4.0, etc.) have amassed a wealth of knowledge that
066   equips them to fulfill various reasoning tasks. However, we observed that merely prompting LLMs
067   to recall experiences related to the target queries may lead to the generation of proxy queries that
068   are as difficult as the target queries themselves, along with corresponding incorrect proxy solutions,
069
      misguiding the resolution of the target queries, as exemplified in Fig. 1. See the related works in

098   The issues mentioned above motivate us to propose a novel automatic reasoning prompt approach,
099
      coined LBS3, which is inspired by curriculum learning that mirrors the progressive nature of hu-
      man learning styles. The idea of curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009; Cornacchia & Mossel,
100
      2023) has been widely applied in the field of artificial intelligence, emulating the human learn-
101
      ing process from easy to hard tasks (Campos, 2021; Maharana & Bansal, 2022; Huang et al.,
102   2020; Kong et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; Soviany et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2020).
103   Thus, LBS3 critically aims to 1) guide LLMs to generate easy- (or hard-) proxy queries re-
104   lated to the target query and 2) enhance the effectiveness of the solutions for these proxy
105   queries to benefit that for the target query. For the former, diverging from existing approaches
106   (e.g., Self-ICL and Auto-ICL) that generate proxy queries in one pass, we develop a two-stage
107   framework of generation for proxy queries. Specifically, we first prompt LLMs with instruc-
      tions like "Generate n1 different new relevant problems that are easier

108
      to solve than the example problem below." to generate simpler proxy queries
109   than the given query, that is, easy-proxy queries. Then, we instruct LLMs to formulate ana-
110   logical proxy queries for the given query, which are the hard-proxy queries, by using in-
111   structions like "Generate n2 different new problems that are analogous to
112   the example problem below." Note that n1 and n2 denote the number of proxy queries
113   generated. For the latter, we initially leverage LLMs to solve each easy-proxy query independently
114   in the ZS-CoT manner, creating corresponding triplet exemplary prompt. Subsequently, we combine
115   these prompts with each hard-proxy query and generate solutions in the FS-CoT fashion. Ultimately,

143   One of the advantages for the proposed approach is that LBS3 explicitly distinguishes between easy-
144   and hard-proxy queries, and ensures that the difficulty of solving proxy queries does not exceed that
145   of the given query. Additionally, in contrast to existing approaches that tackle each proxy query
146   from scratch, we adopt a progressive strategy to harness exemplary prompts derived from easy-proxy
147   queries to guide the generation of solution for hard ones, thereby alleviating the accumulation of
148   errors arises from reasoning ab initio (Yu et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, our work is the
149   pioneering attempt to emulate the idea of curriculum learning, aiming to investigate how LLMs can
150
      self-generate few-shot exemplary prompts to facilitate the reasoning process.
151   Our main contributions of this work are summarized as follows. First, we put forward a new automatic
152   reasoning prompt approach (LBS3), which is inspired by the idea of curriculum learning to assist
153   LLMs in recalling easy and hard proxy queries related to the target query. Second, we adopt a
154   progressive strategy that utilizes exemplary prompts derived from easy-proxy queries to direct LLMs
155   in solving hard-proxy queries, improving the quality of the proxy solutions. At last, we conducted
156
      extensive experiments focused on reasoning-intensive tasks. These tasks included mathematical
      problem-solving (GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and SVAMP (Patel
157
      et al., 2021)), commonsense reasoning (StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021) and CommonsenseQA (Talmor
158
      et al., 2018)), as well as reasoning tasks within BBH (Srivastava et al., 2022). Moreover, LLMs
159   used for these reasoning tasks encompass open-source models (Qwen1.5-14B (Bai et al., 2023),
160   Qwen1.5-72B (Bai et al., 2023), and Llama3-70B (AI@Meta, 2024)) and closed-source models1

      2     P RELIMINARIES
169
170
      In this paper, we work on scenarios wherein we address a given query (e.g., a math problem, multiple-
      choice question, or true/false assessment, etc.) without any additional information. Given a query
171
      [q], the objective is to produce a solution consisting of the rationale (i.e., multiple reasoning steps)
172
      and the final answer, denoted by [r, a]. A prompt template, represented by T , is designed for solving
173   [q]. Note that multiple sub-prompt templates are assembled to form pipeline templates in certain
174   specific prompt approaches. A prevalent prompting approach aims to integrate T with [q], resulting in
175   h = inte(T, [q]), which is then fed to an LLM to elicit the corresponding solution [r, a] = LLM (h).
176   Listed below are the existing prompting approaches that are most pertinent to our work.
177
178          • In ZS-CoT (Kojima et al., 2022), T and [q] are integrated as h ="[q] Let’s think
179            step by step."
180          • In FS-CoT (Wei et al., 2022b), n-shot manually crafted exemplary prompts are used to form
181            T , which, when combined with [q], results in h ="[q1 ][r1 , a1 ] · · · [qn ][rn , an ][q]".
182          • In Analogical Prompting (Yasunaga et al., 2023), the integration of T with [q] yields h,
183            which prompts an LLM to self-generate n-shot distinct proxy exemplars relevant to [q]
184            and proceed to solve [q], i.e., [q1 ][r1 , a1 ] · · · [qn ][rn , an ][q][r, a] = LLM (h). Of note, the
185            one-pass generation mode employed in this approach necessitates that the LLM possesses
186            robust capabilities for both following instructions and generating responses.
187          • In Self-ICL (Chen et al., 2023)/Auto-ICL (Yang et al., 2023), a two-step policy is used to
188            steer an LLM to self-generate n-shot exemplars for solving [q]. Initially, the construction of
189            h1 , as illustrated in detail in Fig. 1, directs the LLM to generate n-shot proxy queries, i.e.,
190            [q1 ] · · · [qn ] = LLM (h1 ). Subsequently, the LLM is deployed to solve each proxy query one
191            by one, i.e., [ri , ai ] = LLM (hi2 ) (i ∈ [n]) where hi2 ="[qi ] Let’s think step by
192            step." The process culminates with the assembly of h ="[q1 ][r1 , a1 ] · · · [qn ][rn , an ][q]".
193            Notably, compared to Analogical Prompting, Self-ICL/Auto-ICL, despite incurring ad-
194
               ditional computation and costs due to multiple interactions with the LLM, offer greater
               flexibility and more closely mirror human cognitive processes. Also, they place more modest
195
               demands on the LLM’s ability to follow instructions and generate responses.
196
197
      We aim to tailor a prompt approach that enables an LLM to self-generate high-quality proxy exemplars,
198   improving the accuracy of the solution it produces for a given query [q].

201   3     A PPROACH
202
203   In this section, we elaborate on our approach, LBS3, which draws inspiration from the concept of
204
      curriculum learning. LBS3 empowers an LLM to self-generate few-shot exemplars that are pertinent
      to the target query (a.k.a. problem), ranging from simple to complex. Figure 2 illustrates the reasoning
205
      pipeline of LBS3 in a modular fashion, which contains three key modules: the Simple Problem
206
      Generation (SPG) module, the Analogous Problem Generation (APG) module and the Rationale and
207   Answer Generation (RAG) module. Remarkably, the RAG consists of two sub-modules: one that
208   solves the given query using the ZS-CoT manner and the other utilizing the FS-CoT manner, denoted
209   as RAG-Z and RAG-F, respectively. Thereafter, we introduce LBS3 from two perspectives: firstly,
210   how it bootstraps an LLM to generate proxy queries related to the given query in increasing order of
211   difficulty, and secondly, how it effectively addresses the more challenging proxy queries.
212

399   4.3.1                    C OMPARISON OF THE NUMBER FOR EASY AND HARD PROXY EXEMPLARS
400   We look into the impacts of different hyperparameter combinations (n1 , n2 ) within the two-stage
401   framework for self-generating proxy queries of LBS3 across various benchmarks, including GSM8K,
402   SQA, CQA, BBH-idfo, BBH-raco, and BBH-ts. For clarity, assume that the number of proxy
403   exemplars n is 3, with both n1 and n2 taking values from {0, 1, 2, 3}. Note that since n = n1 + n2 ,
404   we opt to only consider n1 , then n2 = n − n1 . When n1 = 1 and n2 = 2, this indicates that LBS3 is
405   tasked with generating one easy-proxy exemplar and two hard-proxy ones. We report the performance
406   of LBS3 corresponding to varying n1 on different models and benchmarks in Fig. 3.
407   It can be observed from Fig.3 that the accuracy of LBS3 improves with the increase of n1 ∈ {0, 1, 2}
408   in most cases, and that the performance of LBS3 at n1 = 3 consistently underperforms its performance
409   at other n1 values across all cases. To be specific, the top accuracy is achieved for LBS3 in 13 out
410   of 18 cases when n1 = 2 and is improved by an average of 1.25% compared to n1 = 1 which
411   achieves sub-optimal accuracy. This suggests that compared to the case where only easy- or hard-
412   proxy exemplars are generated, LBS3 has superior performance when they are both present. To
413   put it differently, LBS3, drawing on the principle of curriculum learning, effectively enhances the
414
      abilities of LLMs to handle complex reasoning tasks. Also, we see from Fig. 3 and Table 1 that
      LBS3’s accuracy at n1 = 0 (i.e., generating only hard-proxy exemplars) consistently exceeds that
415
      at n1 = 3 (i.e., generating only easy-proxy exemplars) with an average improvement of 2.75%.
416
      Additionally, LBS3’s accuracy at n1 = 3 consistently surpasses that of ZS-CoT by an average of
417   2.91%. This suggests that easy-proxy exemplars provide a weaker performance boost to LLMs than
418   hard-proxy exemplars. We posit that this is because, although hard-proxy exemplars may come with
419   lower-quality solutions, they help LLMs to recall more useful information, whereas the opposite
420   is true for easy-proxy exemplars. Therefore, the primary utility of the easy-proxy exemplars is to
421   augment the quality of solutions in the hard-proxy exemplars.

500   4.3.4                U TILITY OF P ROGRESSIVE S TRATEGY
501
502
      As previously mentioned, the progressive strategy in LBS3 (labeled as Strategy1) is designed to
      enhance the quality of solutions for hard-proxy queries. In particular, LBS3 utilizes easy-proxy
503
      exemplars solved via the ZS-CoT manner as prompts for each hard-proxy query. Also, it employs

533
      In this paper, we introduce a novel automatic reasoning prompt approach, dubbed as LBS3, drawing
534   inspiration from the concept of curriculum learning. Concretely, LBS3 initially ushers LLMs to
535   recall easy-to-hard proxy queries that are pertinent to the target query. Following this, it implements
536   a progressive strategy that utilizes exemplary prompts stemmed from easy-proxy queries to direct
537   LLMs in solving hard-proxy queries, enabling the high-quality of the proxy solutions. At last, we
538   validate the effectiveness of LBS3 with extensive experiments on several state-of-the-art open- and
539   closed-source LLMs and reasoning benchmarks. Due to space constraints, we discuss in detail the
      limitations and broader impacts of our work in Appendixes E and F, respectively.

867   A    R ELATED W ORKS
868
869   Curriculum Learning. The underlying insight of curriculum learning is to emulate the learning
870   paradigms of humans and animals, that is, by following the sequence and content of standardized
871   educational materials, they leverage previously learned concepts to aid in the acquisition of new and
872   more challenging ones (Krueger & Dayan, 2009; Pavlov, 2010; Skinner, 1958). Inspired by cognitive
873   science research (Rohde & Plaut, 1999), curriculum-based machine learning algorithm was first
874
      proposed by (Bengio et al., 2009) with the core idea of initially training models with simple samples
      and gradually increasing the complexity during the training process. Over the subsequent decade,
875
      the concept of curriculum learning has been widely applied in the field of artificial intelligence,
876
      including computer vision (Gong et al., 2016; Xiangli et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
877   2022c), machine translation (Zhang et al., 2018; Platanios et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Mohiuddin
878   et al., 2022),pre-training (Campos, 2021; Li et al., 2021; Nagatsuka et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022a),
879   fine-tuning (Chen et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2024; Yigit & Amasyali, 2023; Zhang et al., 2022), natural
880   language understanding (Wang et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020; Christopoulou et al.,
881   2022), knowledge distillation (Li et al., 2023b;a; Matiisen et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021; Maharana
882   & Bansal, 2022), and more. However, the utilize of curriculum learning strategies to enhance the
883   reasoning capabilities of language models remains unexplored. To the best of our knowledge, our
884   work is a pioneering attempt to mimic the idea of curriculum learning, aiming to investigate how
885
      LLMs can self-generate few-shot exemplary prompts to facilitate the reasoning process.
886   Chain of thought (CoT) Prompting Approaches. Few-Shot CoT (FS-CoT), initially proposed
887   by Wei et al. (Wei et al., 2022b), has shown that providing intermediate reasoning steps (termed
888   "thoughts") in manually crafted few-shot exemplary prompts can ignite the step-by-step reasoning
889   capabilities of LLMs, thereby significantly enhancing their accuracy in solving complex reasoning
890   tasks. This approach is bolstered by the self-consistency approach (Wang et al., 2022b; Aggarwal
891
      et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024). Despite its achievements, Few-Shot CoT confronts challenges such
      as the accumulation of errors, the limited quality of exemplary prompts, and the time-consuming
892
      labor-intensive task of manual annotation.
893
894   In order to alleviate the performance degradation caused by accumulated errors, a plethora of variants
895   for Few-shot CoT have been proposed. For instance, there are more complex CoT approaches (Lee &
896   Kim, 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024;
897
      Zhou et al., 2022b; Sun et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023) as well as
      those with feedback and verification mechanisms (Zhang et al., 2023; Ling et al., 2024; Poesia et al.,
898
      2023; Paul et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2022; Madaan et al., 2024), etc. The mentioned methods are
899
      committed to constructing frameworks that guide the language model to generate correct intermediate
900   steps, thereby reducing accumulated errors in the intermediate reasoning process and improving the
901   accuracy of the final answer. However, such meticulously designed frameworks inevitably come with
902   a steep computational cost.
903
      Research indicates that existing LLMs are sensitive to the quality and sequence of exemplary
904
      prompts, making the construction of high-quality prompts crucial (Liu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021).
905
      Consequently, a series of efforts have been dedicated to enhancing the quality of these exemplary
906   prompts (Rubin et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Ye
907   & Durrett, 2023; Diao et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2023). The above-mentioned approaches rest on a
908   fundamental assumption that there is an accessible external resource related to the current task, such
909   as a dataset or corpus. They employ various predefined similarity metrics to retrieve the most relevant,
910   complex and diverse existing queries or exemplars from the external resource to improve the quality
911   of exemplary prompts. Nevertheless, the requisite external resources these approaches rely on are not
912   always available in practice, and they may not entirely circumvent the need for manual annotation.
913   Moreover, to leverage pre-trained knowledge and eliminate manual annotation, Zero-Shot CoT (ZS-
914   CoT) (Kojima et al., 2022) induces language models to arrive at solutions through multi-step reasoning
915   with the generic prompt "Let’s think step by step." While ZS-CoT boasts versatility, its
916   performance often lags behind Few-Shot CoT (FS-CoT) across various complex reasoning tasks. As
917   such, our work is devoted to guiding LLMs to self-construct high-quality exemplary prompts without
      the introduction of human labor, thereby increasing the accuracy of solutions for given queries (or


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      problems). Prior to our efforts, there has already been work striving towards this goal. For example,
919   Self-ICL (Chen et al., 2023) begins by prompting the LLM to generate few-shot new, diverse, and
920   creative proxy queries tailored to the target task, and then solves each of that independently using the
921   ZS-CoT manner, which in turn yields proxy exemplars for prompting LLMs to engage in reasoning.
922   Auto-ICL (Yang et al., 2023) operates similarly to Self-ICL, but it differs in that Auto-ICL instructs
923   the LLM to produce proxy queries that have the same structure as the given query. Analogical
924   Prompting (Yasunaga et al., 2023) draws on the cognitive process of solving new problems from
925   relevant past experiences, i.e., inspired by analogical reasoning, which prompts the language model
926   to self-generate relevant examples in context before embarking on the solution of a given query.
927
      Notably, the one-pass generation mode employed in Analogical Prompting necessitates that the LLM
      possesses robust capabilities for both following instructions and generating responses. We revisit
928
      the aforementioned approaches and discern that their efficacy hinges on guiding the LLM to recall
929
      experiences relevant to the given query. However, solely considering such experiences may lead to
930   the generation of proxy queries that are as challenging as the given query, along with corresponding
931   erroneous proxy solutions, potentially misleading the solution of the original given query.

       E      L IMITATIONS
1005
1006
       Here, we discuss the shortcomings of the LBS3 method as follows:
1007
1008   1) In the field of prompt engineering for reasoning tasks, there are many trade-offs to consider,
1009   including computational efficiency, cost, and utility. It is notoriously challenging to try to develop
1010   a general prompting approach that satisfies all of the above trade-offs. In this work, we primarily
1011
       focus on tailoring a prompting approach that enables LLMs to autonomously generate high-quality
       proxy exemplars, thereby enhancing the accuracy of the solutions they produce for a given query [q].
1012
       However, we acknowledge that compared to existing approaches like Self-ICL and Auto-ICL, our
1013
       LBS3 approach feeds more content (i.e., exemple prompts) to the language model when solving hard
1014   proxy queries, incurring additional computational and monetary costs. In our experiments, LBS3 take
1015   roughly 1 to 1.1 times the reasoning time per query than that of Self-ICL and Auto-ICL. Moreover,
1016   while the ZS-shot CoT and Ana-Pro approaches have advantages in terms of computational efficiency
1017   and cost, they are significantly weaker than LBS3 in terms of utility.
1018
       2) The proposed LBS3 suggests that LLMs generate both easy and hard queries, but it does not
1019
       delve into a clear definition of whether the generated queries are genuinely easy or hard. Existing
1020   works (Yasunaga et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) similarly lack research in this
1021   area. We believe that the aforementioned analysis is necessary to ascertain whether the reported
1022   improvements are truly because the queries have become easier (for humans or models) and more
1023   hard, or simply due to the prompts. To this end, in Appendix C, we provide examples of simple and
1024   complex proxy queries generated by five LLMs, as shown in Tables 4 to 52. It can be intuitively
1025   observed that they can generate simple and difficult queries based on prompts, thereby intuitively
       confirming the main claim of this paper related to curriculum learning. However, the black-box


                                                           19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       nature of Large Language Models (LLMs) precludes us from conducting a comprehensive qualitative
1027   analysis, even though we are keen to do so. In summary, the effectiveness of our method—and
1028   related approaches, including Analogical Prompting, Self-ICL, and Auto-ICL—is predicated on the
1029   LLM’s capacity to follow instructions and its a wealth of knowledge that enable them to fulfill various
1030   reasoning tasks. We will continue to address the aforementioned shortcomings in future work.
