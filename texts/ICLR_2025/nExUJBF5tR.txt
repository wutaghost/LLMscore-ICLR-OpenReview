011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion neural networks have become the go-to solution for tasks involving
014            automatic image generation, but the generation process is expensive in terms of
015            memory, energy, and computational cost. Several works have aimed to reduce
016
               the cost via quantization to 8 bits or less. Despite that, half-precision (FP16)
               is still the default mode for large text-to-image generation models — where re-
017
               ducing numerical precision becomes more challenging. In this work, we show
018
               that the reduction of quantization bias can be more important than the reduction
019            in quantization (mean square) error. We propose a data-free method for model
020            quantization, with the goal of producing images that are indistinguishable from
021            those generated by large, full-precision diffusion models. We show that simple
022            methods like stochastic rounding can decrease the quantization bias and improve
023            image generation quality with little to no cost. To close the remaining gap between
024            full-precision and quantized models, we suggest a feasible method for partial
025            stochastic-rounding of weights. When using the MS-COCO dataset as the baseline,
026            we show our quantization methods achieve as good FID scores as the full-precision
027
               model. Moreover, our methods decrease the quantization-induced distortion of the
               images generated by the full-precision model, with the distortion decreasing with
028
               the number of diffusion steps.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      At this time, the best automatic method to generate high-quality images is using diffusion models
034
      (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) with a deep neural network (DNN) architecture. While the quality of
035
      the images generated by diffusion models is currently unmatched (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), these
036   models have a high computational cost. Diffusion processes are inherently iterative and require
037   multiple steps for the generation of each output image. When studying the energy cost of different AI
038   models, Luccioni et al. (2023) claimed that generating a single image with the smallest text-to-image
039   model tested has the same energy cost as fully charging a smartphone, and the process emits 10
040   times more CO2 than a text-to-text process performed by the largest language model in that study.
041   Furthermore, diffusion models have a high memory cost, which scales with the image resolution.
042   This is a major limiting factor when using a memory-bounded accelerator to process these models.
043   While the energy requirements are higher than ever before, the challenge of increasing efficiency in
044   DNNs, by itself, is not new. The most common solution, that spans most DNN applications, is DNN
045   quantization (e.g., Gupta et al. (2015); Hubara et al. (2017)). DNNs that use datatypes with lower
046   numerical precision require less memory and can be computed much more efficiently on dedicated
047   hardware (van Baalen et al., 2023). The downside of using quantization is that lower precision
048   datatypes (with fewer bits) result in less accurate models. Reducing the quantization inaccuracies
049   while minimizing quantization data size is the focus of most research on quantization in DNNs.
050   Several prior works (Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023) have tackled
051   the issue of quantization in diffusion models, and have suggested distinctive methods to quantize
052   these models post-training, without harming the model’s output to a significant degree. The apparent
053   success of quantizing latent diffusion models, did not, however, extend to standard practice. Despite
      the high demand for lighter, faster, SOTA text-to-image models, the standard workflow for latent


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      diffusion models still relies heavily on half-precision datatypes (16-bit), or naive FP8 quantization,
055   which is publicly distributed for Flux. As we explain next, in Sec. 2, existing methods struggle to
056   scale to larger models (with more than a billion parameters), such as SDXL and Flux, and offer
057   limited functionality that hampers their wide-scale adoption.
058
      In this work, we aim to bridge the gap and suggest 8-bit diffusion models that are more relevant
059
      to practitioners. Our work will focus on the popular open-sourced Stable Diffusion XL and Flux
060
      models (Podell et al., 2023; Labs, 2024), and will aim to generate images that look just like the
061   images produced by the full-precision, unquantized models. We will do so without relying on any
062   external data, as it is not, in general, broadly available in the context of SOTA image generation
063   applications. To accomplish this goal, we will leverage the fact that the diffusion process includes
064   multiple iterative steps. Consequently, the effect of quantization noise is dominated by its bias. As
065   we will show, reducing this bias in data-free settings is possible and beneficial for all large models:
066   from the smaller SD1.4 to Stable-Diffusion XL and Flux.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
069
070
      Several existing works proposed varying schemes for the quantization of diffusion models. In Li et al.
071
      (2023), the authors suggest that by accounting for the changes made to the statistics of the quantized
072   neural network during the diffusion process, it is possible to quantize diffusion models (post-training)
073   to INT4/INT8 data types, which reduces the computational cost and memory substantially. The paper
074   shows that despite the aggressive quantization, the unconditional models manage to maintain the high
075   quality of generated images. Conditional Text-To-Image models were also examined and were shown
076   to be of high quality, despite some degradation when compared with the non-quantized models.
077
      In Yang et al. (2023), the authors propose integer quantization of latent-diffusion models as well,
078   which they achieve by analyzing the effects of different components in the U-Net architecture. With a
079   single-calibration step, intended to optimize the layer-level signal-to-quantization-noise-ratio (SQNR),
080   the authors use quantization corrections to transform full-precision, pre-trained models into partially
081   quantized models. They show that their quantized model produces high-quality images. Specifically,
082   when applied over high-quality stable diffusion models (SD 1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) or SDXL
083   (Podell et al., 2023)), the quality was assessed to be on par with non-quantized diffusion models.
084   Diffusion models were also quantized to integers in He et al. (2024) (PTDQ). There, the authors
085   suggest a correction to the DDPM scheduling regime, which they denote as Variance Schedule
086   Calibration (VSC). When used in combination with step-aware mixed precision, their method
087   achieves good metrics for images generated over LSUN datasets. In addition to the previous methods,
088   the best results in the paper rely on bias correction on the models’ output, which is adjusted per
089   timestep. For conditional diffusion models, the bias correction is also calibrated per class.
090   Post-training quantization methods for FP8 datatypes were reviewed in Shen et al. (2023). Unlike the
091   previous papers that targeted diffusion models, Shen et al. (2023) covered a vast amount of tasks,
092   studying the effect of different data types on the quality of different neural networks. When applied
093   to stable diffusion models, the paper shows that the FP8-M4E3 format (4 Mantissa bits) is superior to
094   other formats (FP8 or INT8). The results were further improved by implementing dynamic range
095   calibration, where values were scaled based on the minimum and maximum over training samples.
096   The most noteworthy practical implementation of quantized diffusion models is offered by Nvidia’s
097   TensorRT (Cheng et al., 2024), which is associated with Li et al. (2023). In a blog post, the authors
098   report results for either FP8 or INT8 quantization, arguing that the performance of FP8 is preferable
099   since INT8 quantization can not be extended to all the operations in the attention block. While Cheng
100   et al. (2024) did not include any quality metrics to numerically assess the generated images, it did
101   establish the similarity between images generated by the full-precision and the quantized models to
102   be a key metric, and was the only work that offered official implementation for the quantization of
103   Stable-Diffusion XL.
104   The primary criterion for assessing image quality in each of the academic works mentioned in this
105   section was the Frechet Inception Distance (FID). In the case of unconditional image generation,
106   the images generated by all methods appear to be very similar to the baseline images, even when
107   using a very low quantization format (4 bits). However, scaling these methods to large, text-to-image
      models is more challenging: Even when using mixed precision (excluding the Softmax layers), the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      images generated when using these methods (Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) diverge from the
109   full-precision image. Furthermore, as we show in Appendix A, the existing methods have multiple

111   scaling TensorRT to SDXL results in lower image quality (higher FID) and that the solutions are not

      In Appendix D, we provide a breakdown of all the primary methods suggested by prior works,

119   3.1   N ETWORK Q UANTIZATION
120
      In DNN quantization, values such as weight and activations (W/A) in the network are modified
121
      using a quantization function Q(x) : R → R, so that Q(x) can be represented using fewer bits.
122   The benefits of quantization are two-fold: First, by reducing the amount of bits required to store
123   weights and activations, quantization effectively compresses the model. This is especially important
124   when working with deep-learning accelerators, since the memory requirement of the device is a
125   common limiting factor for using large models. Second, quantization enables the utilization of
126   cheaper General-Matrix-Multiplication (GeMM) engines in hardware. Therefore, the computation
127   of quantized neural networks will require less energy and time, provided the hardware supports
128   multiplication with the quantized format. There are many types of quantization methods (Guo, 2018),
129   which depend on the specific goals, with different works focusing on different phases of training,
130
      different sources of information, or different formats.
131   In this work, we are interested in Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) of the diffusion’s denoiser model.
132   Most prior works (Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; He et al., 2024) have aimed to do this using
133   integer (fixed-point) quantization:

167   Stochastic Rounding One particular quantization method that is important to this work is stochastic
168   rounding (Gupta et al., 2015). For integers, stochastic rounding (SR) replaces the round operation in
169   Eq. (1) with the stochastic function:
170
                                   SR (x) = ⌊x + n⌋ ,       n ∼ Uniform (0, 1) .                         (3)
171
172   Stochastic rounding can be used for integer or floating-point quantization (Wang et al., 2018)). For
173   floating point, we simply use SR for integer quantization (QINTM ) when implementing Eq. (2). As
174   shown in Chmiel et al. (2022), when applied on a linear layer, SR increases the mean-square-error
175
      (MSE) when compared with round-to-nearest (R2N) rounding. Importantly, since E [SR (x) |x] = x,
      SR also reduces the quantization bias. Therefore, stochastic rounding was used in DNN training to
176
      reduce bias in gradients, which helps optimization (e.g., Chen et al. (2020); Chmiel et al. (2022)).
177
      As a direct consequence, stochastic rounding has been implemented in the hardware of modern
178   accelerators (Alben et al., 2020; Mikaitis, 2021), albeit it is not used by default in training and
179   was never recommended for inference, to the best of our knowledge. Instead, modern quantization
180   methods often aim for direct minimization of the quantization error (usually, by relying on training
181   data), as is the case with methods such as AdaRound (Nagel et al., 2020), AdaQuant (Hubara et al.,
182   2021) and BREC-Q (Li et al., 2021).

215   One of the main challenges when working with generative algorithms is the evaluation of the generated
      outputs. To avoid the expensive measurement of Mean-Opinion-Scores (MOS), most works utilize

217   the model output, despite the well-known limitations of these measurements for evaluating high-end
218   models (it was shown to have a weak correlation with MOS with SDXL (Podell et al., 2023)). The
219   mismatch between FID measurements and MOS may explain, to some degree, the limited impact
220   that previous works had on the practical usage of quantized diffusion models thus far. To address this,
221   we add one additional goal for our quantized diffusion model: That is, our goal in this work will be
222   to produce the same image, as produced by the full-precision (FP) model. We believe that we have
223   no better judge than the original model itself, when trying to evaluate the distribution of generated
224   images of a quantized model.

226   between the generated images, with either full precision or quantized model. For any image (Y ) and

238   Our main interest in this work will be the quantization of the denoiser neural networks (U-Net or

240   the first and last layers. In accordance with previous works (Shen et al., 2023), we will use varying

352   4.3   S TOCHASTIC W EIGHTS
353
354   While the bias of activation quantization was reduced substantially with SR, our fully quantized
355   network is still expected to have a large bias as a result of the weight quantization. Most advanced
356   approaches for weight quantization (Li et al., 2021; Hubara et al., 2021) rely on stable statistics and
357   data, and are thus not relevant for our case. Meanwhile, using stochastic rounding as we did for
358   activations is not practical for the weights: Unlike activations, weights are only quantized once (per
359
      model), and keeping the full-precision weight in device memory just to quantize it again in every step
      would defeat some of the purposes of weight quantization (e.g., reducing peak memory).
360
361   Therefore, our solution will take the middle ground, between proper stochastic rounding and hard-
362   ware efficiency.

      5.1    F LUX : Q UANTIZING D IFFUSION T RANSFORMERS
500
501   The latest significant development for open-sourced text-to-image diffusion models was the release
502   of Flux (Labs, 2024). Contrary to most diffusion models that rely on U-Nets, the denoising model
503   in the Flux pipeline is a diffusion Transformer. While integer quantization of transformer models
504   is exceptionally challenging (Xiao et al., 2023), Flux models that were naively quantized to FP8
505   datatypes have already been publicly distributed. Fortunately, the methods suggested in this work
506   are agnostic to the specific architecture of the denoising neural network, allowing straightforward
507   integration in any model. In Tab. 2, we test our methods over Flux, measuring the average PSNR
508   score of 100 images generated with stable-diffusion-prompts.
509   In accordance with the results of the experiments over SDXL, using the M4E3 format with flex-bias
510   achieved the best results, which were further improved by using Stochastic Rounding during the
511   quantization of activations and weights in the model. By itself, the effect of SR for activations was
512   less significant when implemented over the diffusion transformer, when compared with the more
513   complicated, weight-SR, which resulted in a clear and consistent improvement. One immediate
514   benefit of the Flux model is that naive M3E4 quantization is much more reliable, as opposed to its
515
      SDXL counterpart. As a result, our methods can be used to improve the output of the Flux model
      with M4E3 quantization implemented over existing, commercial hardware.

518   6     C ONCLUSION
519
520
      In this work, we have shown that it is possible to take advantage of the iterative nature of the diffusion
521
      process, in order to reduce quantization bias and enable faster, cheaper models. Our first novel
522   recommendation — using SR during activation quantization, allows for almost cost-free improvement
523   of the diffusion model’s output. Our second recommendation, stochastic weights, can be used in
524   addition to SR, trading small computation costs and small memory footprint for higher quality images.
525   While our method works better for longer inference processes, we find that even shorter processes
526   (20 steps) benefit from using it.
527
      Image similarity evaluations, which we used to compare quantized and full-precision models, have
528   proven themselves to be powerful tools for assessing the quality of post-training quantization. Despite
529   their relatively small cost (only a few generated images), PSNR measurements were robust and highly
530   predictive of FID scores that required 30K images to measure. We recommend using either PSNR
531   or SSIM in all works that aim to make existing diffusion models more efficient. Research that is
532   aimed at improving existing hardware, and therefore relies on slower software simulations, is likely
533   to benefit greatly from using these tools.
534   Although some of the improvements suggested by this paper are tangible with existing hardware,
535   the best results we have observed hinges on the availability of efficient, M4E3 GeMM computation
536   in hardware. While none of the common FP8 formats have yet to receive an official IEEE standard
537   definition, it appears that most hardware vendors prioritize the implementation of M3E4 and M2E5
538   formats. We strongly recommend prioritizing M4E3 implementation: as we have shown, the format
539   not only performs better under some reasonable conditions but also opens the door for further
      improvements, which were not observed for the other FP8 formats.

      A.4               D OMAIN L IMITATION
744
745   All integer quantization methods require a calibration of the full-precision models, for the model
746   values to be scaled, in preparation for operations with limited range, and larger numerical errors. Via
747   calibration, the algorithms capture the statistics of the model, which allows better compression than
748   what can be achieved by data-free models. However, reliance on calibration means that the internal
749   statistics of the model must remain consistent. This is especially challenging for diffusion models,
750   which are traditionally expected to work over different timestamps, different prompts, different
751   schedulers (that include varying input normalization schemes), and different hyperparameters, without
752
      being re-calibrated.
753   Previous works (Li et al., 2023) have focused on the changes in statistics resulting from changing
754   timestamps, albeit the rest of the diffusion model’s parameters remained mostly unexplored in
755   academic research. For example, several works have opted to use MS-COCO captions for calibration,
      while also using the same captions during the evaluation of the model. To check whether calibration


                                                               14

      results in domain gaps, we opt to reproduce the results of the default TensorRT setting (Appendix A.2),
757   but use different schedulers and key hyperparameters during the model calibration. Our results, as
758   seen in Tab. 3, show that quantized diffusion models produce images of lower quality (Up to 0.75
759   increase in FID score) when the calibration hyperparameters do not fit the hyperparameters used in
760   inference. This type of degradation will not occur in data-free settings that do not rely on calibration.

      B    C HOOSING THE Q UANTIZATION F ORMAT
763
764
      The first important decision for any quantized neural network before any adjustment or correction, is
765   the choice of quantization format. First, we need to choose an optimal partition between mantissa
766   and exponent bits for the 8-bit floating point format. Second, we know from previous works (Shen
767   et al., 2023) that scaling factors can be very helpful, especially in cases where the range is limited.
768   However, scaling factors are traditionally calibrated using training samples, which are not available
769   in data-free settings.
770
      Instead, our scaling method will rely on online operations, that will be performed during forward
771   propagation. While scalar scaling operations are not, in general, as expensive as GeMM operations
772   (They have linear complexity, compared with the square complexity of GeMM), they may still induce
773   significant computational cost. To mitigate this additional cost, we suggest the following, lightweight
774   flex-bias (Kuzmin et al., 2022) scaling operation, which is performed once per quantized tensor:
775                                         b = 2E − max (xexp ) − 1 ,                                     (10)
776
      where the max operation is performed on all the exponent components (xexp ) of the values in the
777
      quantized tensor. The main benefit of this approach, and what distinguishes it from implementations
778   seen in previous works, is that we ensure that all scaling factors (2−b ) are power-of-two scalars,
779   since b is integer and shared per tensor. This will simplify any multiplication and addition operations
780   between quantized tensors that have different scaling factors. The limited range of the values of b
781   can potentially be utilized in hardware to also reduce the cost of non-linearities. We note that the
782   main benefit of using offline scaling factors is the ability to merge the factors in the network to avoid
783   additional operations during forward propagation, but this method is not applicable when the factors
784   have to be adjusted from step to step (as is the case for diffusion models (Li et al., 2023)). A more
785   detailed analysis of the cost of this method with either software or hardware implementations is
786
      included in Appendix C.
787   Another key decision during model quantization is the choice of which elements we should quantize
788   and which we should keep at full precision. Previous works have studied the effect of different
789   components in the U-Net (Yang et al., 2023), and highlighted several components, with some (He
790   et al., 2024) even going as far as changing the level of quantization per diffusion timestep. While
791   these approaches can be very effective at reducing the computation time, one of the main goals in
792
      our work is the reduction in peak on-device memory, which necessitates maintaining as constant
      memory as possible (The peak memory will be determined exclusively by the steps using the larger
793
      data-types). As a result, we will follow the example of Shen et al. (2023): We quantize all Linear,
794
      convolutions, and Matmul operations, with the exception of the first/last convolutions in the U-Net,
795   which are kept in full precision.

      C    F LEX -B IAS C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
799
800
      One of the main features that allows our conditional, text-to-image models to operate in data-free
      settings, is the implementation of lightweight flex bias. In this section, we break down the cost of
801
      each operation as a result of the use of the flex-bias. For ideal efficiency, each computation element
802
      will be implemented in hardware, but for practical reasons, we also estimate the cost of the software
803   implementation of each component, using common, available hardware. We note that most of the
804   analysis is not unique to our method of scaling, but it may still be helpful to be reminded of the
805   effects scaling factors have over the network.
806
      For all matrix operations, we take D to be the number of elements in the tensor, E to be the number
807
      of exponent bits, and M to be the number of mantissa bits, with B = M + E + 1.
808
809   For our software analysis, we will treat all operations excluding reads (fetch) and multiplication (mul)
      as having the same cost as addition, ADDB (1). For our hardware analysis, we assume that operations


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      are integrated within existing hardware components, meaning that the cost of fetching the data is
811   considered to be zero, and it is possible to operate on specific bits. All estimates in this analysis
812   will be rough, as the goal is to prove the feasibility of our approach and highlight implementation
813   priorities, and not to suggest a specific design. Despite using similar terminology, we think of the
814   software cost in terms of operations, while the hardware cost is considered in terms of gates.
815
      As a point of reference, remember that the majority of computation in every  neural network occurs
816
      during general matrix multiplications, and has a complexity of MULM,E D2 . An estimation of the
817   cost of MULM,E for GeMM operations can be found in van Baalen et al. (2023)– as a general rule
818   the values for all 8-bit formats are similar, but it is cheaper to have more bits allocated to the exponent.
819   In any case, the cost of multiplication is greater than the cost of integer addition, and D2 ≫ D.
820
821   C.1    O NLINE EXPONENT- BIAS DISCOVERY
822
823   As we saw in Eq. (10), the formula we used for finding the flex bias during forward operation is
824   b = 2E−1 − max (xexp ).
825   In a software implementation, it is not possible to distinguish the sign, exponent, and mantissa. The
826   straightforward approach would be to find the maximal value in the tensor by comparing all absolute
827   values and extract the mantissa value from the scalar we get. The approximate cost would be therefore
828   be ADDB (D).
829
      With dedicated hardware, we have the option to only look at the bias exponent when calculating the
830   exponent bias. Consequently, the cost in this case would be estimated to be around ADDE (D).
831
832   Of course, it is also possible to forgo the online gathering of statistics, calibrate the model offline,
833
      and use values from memory. With the cost of increased complexity of this approach from the user
      view (Forces calibration with data that may not be available) and the risk of bad results due to the
834
      difficulty of calibration (Different prompts may result in different statistics, and it is always possible
835
      that out-of-distribution prompts may affect the results), using an offline method will reduce the cost
836   of this operation to a single scalar fetch operation. The cost of the fetch operation will depend on
837   the accelerator architecture, but may not be trivial, since the scaling factor is expected to change in
838   every timestamp for every tensor (Li et al., 2023), and existing accelerators don’t necessarily have the
839   capacity to fetch a single scalar with low latency.
840
841   C.2    Q UANTIZATION
842
843   The value of flex-bias b works as a scaling factor for the quantized value.
844   Of course, there is no practical reason to work with quantized neural networks, without implementa-
845   tion of the quantization in hardware. During software simulation, we multiply each value in 2b before
846   simulating quantization using the qtorch (Zhang et al., 2019) library and multiply the number by 2−b
847   afterward.
848
      For hardware implementation, b is used to clip the values of the mantissa, to the range [−b, 2E −b−1].
849   This would mean first shifting the value of the unquantized exponent (8 − bit with full precision) by
850   b (ADD8 (D)), and performing the quantization with the updated values.
851
852   C.3    T ENSOR M ULTIPLICATION
853
854   In either software or hardware implementation, multiplying two tensors with a power-of-two scale
855   factor has a negligible cost.
856
857   C.4    T ENSOR A DDITION
858
859   Addition operations are often overlooked in the context of quantization, despite being very common in
860
      U-Nets, and being generally more problematic when performed in combination with scaling factors.
861   When adding two tensors in either software or hardware, we must first rescale the factors of one of the
862   tensors to match the factors of the other, before performing the addition. With a power-of-two scaler,
863   the scaling itself does not require multiplication and will have roughly the same cost as addition,
      ADDB (D). The B factor is relevant for hardware as well, because despite only operating over the


                                                          16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      exponent, we may still need to set the mantissa in the cases where the operation over the exponent
865   cause overflow (xexp > 2E − b − 1) or underflow (xexp < −b). In hardware, it is also possible to
866   leverage the lower range of the tensors by having a dedicated addition operation for tensors with
867   different scales.
868
869   C.5    N ON - LINEARITY
870
871   For activation functions implemented in software, the input values must be in full/half-precision
872   format and will have the same complexity of full-precision operation. In this case, there is no benefit
873   to quantizing the number before the activation. If we do so for simulation purposes, we must rescale
874
      the value before the activation by multiplying it by 2−b (ADD16 (D)), perform the activation, and
      quantize the output of the activation operation once again.
875
876   For hardware, the implementation is straightforward for homogenous functions like ReLU
877   (ADDB (D)). Polynomial functions and lookup table implementation are expected to benefit as well,
878   but their actual gain will depend on the activation function itself.
879
880   C.6    N ORMALIZATION
881
882
      Normalization is straightforward: we can simply terminate the scaling factor and proceed to the
      normalization as is.

885   D     B RIEF S URVEY OF M ETHODS P ROPOSED BY P RIOR W ORKS
886
887   In this section, we detail all methods suggested by prior works. For all components, we provide a
888   brief summary of costs and benefits. For each component not included in this work, we explain our
889
      reason for not including it.
890
891   D.1    I NTEGER Q UANTIZATION
892
      Integer quantization was used in most prior works (Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
893   2023). The immediate benefit of integer quantization is that it is more efficient from a hardware
894   perspective, and is already included by default in most hardware accelerators. Integer quantization
895   also exhibits smaller errors while the values are in the range of representation. The downside of
896   integer quantization is that the range itself is limited, and once values are out of range, the error rises
897   drastically. This tends to lead to lower accuracy (Fig. 1a and Fig. 1b). Consequently, we consider
898   integer quantization to be less robust and preferred using FP8 formats that have an extended range.
899   Due to the larger range, FP8 quantization is less prone to large errors caused by out-of-distribution
900   values. Our choice to use FP8 was also influenced by the better, zero-shot accuracies we observed for
901
      FP8 formats.
902
903   D.2    T IME S TEP -AWARE C ALIBRATION (L I ET AL ., 2023)
904
      Time step-aware calibration directly addresses the problem of changing statistics as a result of changes
905
      in t, which was observed in diffusion models. As the name implies, in time-step aware calibration
906   we re-calibrate the quantization (e.g. finding s from Eq. (1)) for all layers/components, for different
907   values of t. In Li et al. (2023), the authors used a small set of samples to re-calibrate the model. It
908   should be noted that re-calibration has a hidden cost on performance: we can no longer “merge” the
909   scaling factors into the network in any natural way, meaning that you must either reload weight values
910   (not recommended) or perform the scaling operation explicitly, paying the cost of extra, scalar-vector
911   multiplications.
912   In our work, we insisted on a data-free approach and were generally careful of relying on statistics
913   that depend on prompts (It’s not clear how one can sample the prompt space to begin with). In general,
914   FP8 quantized networks are more robust to changes in scaling, enabling the use of our power-of-two,
915   flex bias implementation instead of time-step aware calibration. Our flex-bias implementation is
916   also time-step-aware (it’s performed online), and has the additional cost of finding the min/max of
917   each tensor, but it is more software/hardware friendly in other aspects (e.g. rescaling tensors during
      addition is cheaper).


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      D.3    S HORTCUT-S PLITTING Q UANTIZATION (L I ET AL ., 2023)
919
920   In shortcut splitting quantization, specific tensors in the U-Net are broken down into two tensors,
921   each of them using a different scaling factor (Scaling factors are usually selected per output channel,
922   for easy multiplication). While the authors give a convincing argument for why the added scaling
923   factors only have a small effect on the following matrix multiplication operation, we note that in the
924   case of SDXL model, the values of all candidates for shortcut-splitting quantization were also passed
925   along through residual connections. This adds additional difficulties, since two scaling factors will
926   now be needed for all tensors during the upsampling process–otherwise, we will lose the information
927   we tried to preserve after a single addition operation.
928   As a result, we decided not to use shortcut-splitting quantization, and rely on the extended range of
929   the M4E3 format instead.
930
931
932   D.4    C ORRELATED N OISE C ORRECTION (CNC) (H E ET AL ., 2024)
933
934
      He et al. (2024) makes the observation that the quantization error in ϵ-predicting quantized U-Net
      is highly correlated with the output of the full precision U-Net, possibly as a result of the multiple
935
      normalization layers in the network, that entangle the error with the clean output. This makes the
936
      attempt to remedy the quantization noise and integrate it into the diffusion process more difficult since
937   we can not regard it as white noise (which we need for DDPM/DDIM). Correlated Noise Correction
938   is designed with this in mind. First, we collect statistics to estimate the correlation in every timestep,
939   and then, apply it to better “reconstruct” the clean output from the quantized network’s output.
940
      Despite CNC being relevant in combination with VSC, and despite observing the same correlation as
941
      reported by He et al. (2024) in our setting, we forgo implementing CNC when designing our adjusted
942
      scheduler (Sec. 4.4), for three reasons. First, since the network output is much less biased with SR
943   quantization, this decreases these correlations, as we explain in Appendix L. Second, based on the
944   values of correlation we measured, CNC would mean an amplification of the quantization noise,
945   which can result in a diffusion process that is too far away from the full-precision diffusion process
946   (and thus would go against our SSIM target). Third, we observed that the correlation statistics were
947   very unstable, and were affected greatly by changing seeds and prompts. Therefore, we preferred not
948   to rely on these statistics and so did not use this method.
949
950
      D.5    VARIANCE S CHEDULE C ALIBRATION (VSC) (H E ET AL ., 2024)
951
952
      In Variance Schedule Calibration, the scheduler is adjusted to address the addition of quantization
953
      noise to the process (DDPM). This adjustment eventually translated to changes in the noise schedule
954   (σt ). In Sec. 4.4, we suggest different adjustments to the Discrete Euler scheduler. While the
955   surface goal is similar in both cases (to address quantization noise), the difference in the schedulers
956   is substantial. As a result, our adjusted scheduler maintains the original noise-schedule but has a
957   modified step size (∆t ).
958
      In the end, we decided not to recommend an adjusted scheduler. The adjusted scheduler was reliant
959
      on statistics, which while stable, still went against our data-free approach. Furthermore, the absolute
960   effect on step size was negligible and we could not show a significant advantage (with the SSIM
961   metric) when using it.

963
964   D.6    B IAS C ORRECTION (BC) (H E ET AL ., 2024)
965
966   Despite its simplicity, Bias Correction was shown to be one of the more effective methods (Table
967   1,He et al. (2024)) for network quantization. In bias correction, we first calibrate the network using a
968
      subset of training samples, measuring the error in the network’s output for every time step (And in
      the case of the conditional model, for every class). The statistics are then used during inference to
969
      “correct” the output of the network in each step.
970
971   Of course, this makes Bias-Correction unfeasible in data-free settings, especially for text-to-image
      models, where the number of classes is effectively infinite.

       D.7    S TEP - AWARE M IXED P RECISION (H E ET AL ., 2024)
973
974
       Changing the quantization precision for different values of t during inference is no doubt effective at
975
       reducing the quantization noise in the vital steps in which the image’s final form is shaped. However,
976    changing the precision during inference also means that the peak memory is determined solely by
977    the memory in the steps where the highest precision was used. We avoid using step-aware mixed
978    precision for that reason.

981    D.8    B LOCK S ENSITIVITY I DENTIFICATION AND C ALIBRATION (YANG ET AL ., 2023)
982
983    To mitigate the wide, dynamic range of activations, Yang et al. (2023) suggested studying the
984    quantization noise (via SQNR), and enhancing precision on demand on bottleneck components. In
985    our case, we also used hybrid quantization, since the first and last convolution layers in our setting
986    are kept in half precision. However, changing the precision of internal operations may increase the
987    peak memory (the component with higher precision may become the bottleneck). In a network with
988    as many residual connections as U-Net, bottlenecks are abundant, so special care is required for the
989    selective quantization of layers. Lastly, SQNR relies on training samples, and so is not data-free. For
990    these reasons, we avoid using this method in our work.

       E.1   SSIM
996
997
998
       Out of the many different methods to measure similarity between images, we chose to use PSNR
       as the key measurement. PSNR is commonly used to measure differences between outputs, as it
999
       benefits from being simple (Both in terms of being easy to understand, and being easy to compute).
1000
       Nevertheless, it is also important to acknowledge that, by virtue of PSNR’s simplicity, it may
1001   overlook subtle details in images that are not captured through a basic pixel-to-pixel comparison. To
1002   address this, this section will include additional results for image similarity, that are based on the
1003   Structure-Similarity-Index Measure (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) score.
1004
       SSIM evaluates the perceived change in the structural information between two images. Its scores
1005
       range from −1 to 1, with 1 being the ideal score, indicating a complete match between the image and
1006
       its reference. For baseline numbers, we observe that the FP16 variant of SDXL generates images
1007   with SSIM of 0.973 ± 0.002 when compared with the FP32 model (For BF16 quantization, the SSIM
1008   are in the range 0.948 ± 0.005). In the context of this work, we note that even with an SSIM value
1009   of 0.8, some effort is required to distinguish the image from its reference, while an SSIM of 0.6
1010   indicates that the images are substantially different. (See Appendix O for illustrations).
1011
1012
1013   E.2   FID TO FP32
1014

1016   (2025) in the context of the quantization of diffusion models. Like FID, FID-FP32 measure the
1017   Fidelity Inception Distance, but uses the outputs of the full-precision model as the baseline the
1018   distance measurement, rather than using the ground-truth (such as the images of the MS-COCO
1019   dataset). Consequently, FID-FP32 measures similarity between the images of the quantized and
1020   non-quantized models, with emphasis on the features of the Inception neural network.
1021   In figure Fig. 5, we present the PSNR and FID-32 measurements corresponding to the experiments
1022   presented in Figs. 1a, 1b and 3 and Tab. 1. All similarity metrics (SSIM, PSNR and FID-32) are in
1023   strong agreement regarding the similarity in all experiments. The main exception is that with PSNR
1024   evaluation, we can see a small benefit for using SR with all floating-point formats when the number
1025   of steps exceeds 80. The small gap between SR and R2N rounding was not observed for M2E5 and
       M3E4 when we used SSIM as a metric, as we saw in Fig. 1b.
