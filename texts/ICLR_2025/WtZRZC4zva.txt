009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphs offer unique insights into relationships and interactions between entities,
012            complementing data modalities like text, images, and videos. By incorporating
013            relational information from graph data, AI models can extend their capabilities
014            beyond traditional tasks. However, relational data in sensitive domains such as
015            finance and healthcare often contain private information, making privacy preser-
016
               vation crucial. Existing privacy-preserving methods, such as DP-SGD, which rely
               on gradient decoupling assumptions, are not well-suited for relational learning
017
               due to the inherent dependencies between coupled training samples. To address
018
               this challenge, we propose a privacy-preserving relational learning pipeline that
019            decouples dependencies in sampled relations during training, ensuring differen-
020            tial privacy through a tailored application of DP-SGD. We apply this method to
021            fine-tune large language models (LLMs) on sensitive graph data, and tackle the
022            associated computational complexities. Our approach is evaluated on LLMs of
023            varying sizes (e.g., BERT, Llama2) using real-world relational data from four text-
024            attributed graphs. The results demonstrate significant improvements in relational
025            learning tasks, all while maintaining robust privacy guarantees during training.
026            Additionally, we explore the trade-offs between privacy, utility, and computational
027
               efficiency, offering insights into the practical deployment of our approach.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph data, commonly used to represent relationships between entities, are widely employed to
032   model complex systems in the real world (Leskovec et al., 2007; Kwak et al., 2010; Shamsi et al.,
033   2022; Madani et al., 2022). In AI applications, the relationships captured by graph structures provide
034   complementary information to foundation models pretrained on other modalities, such as text and
035   images, enabling these models to more effectively handle tasks involving multiple entities (Brown
036   et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024; Madan et al., 2024). For instance, models
037   trained on product descriptions or pictures may not fully capture the relationships revealed by user
038   behaviors, such as co-purchases or co-viewings. Incorporating such relational information allows
039
      AI models to better meet users’ needs, e.g., in product recommendations. Models pretrained on text
      or images and subsequently fine-tuned with relational information from graphs have recently found
040
      applications in various domains (Ling et al., 2023), including healthcare (Wu et al., 2021; Zhang
041
      et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023), finance (Ouyang et al., 2024), and computer vision (Li et al., 2023a).
042   However, the relationships involved in these applications often contain sensitive personal informa-
043   tion, such as social connections for recommendations (Zheng et al., 2022), patient-hospital visits for
044   clinical diagnosis (Lu & Uddin, 2023), and financial transactions for fraud detection (Kurshan &
045   Shen, 2020). This raises critical concerns about how to protect the privacy of relational data when
046   exposed to AI models, motivating the research in this work.
047
      Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006; Dwork et al., 2014) is widely considered the gold standard
048   for measuring the privacy guarantees of data-processing algorithms (Xu et al., 2021; Pan et al.,
049   2024). Current DP methods for model training, such as DP-SGD (Song et al., 2013; Abadi et al.,
050   2016; Ponomareva et al., 2023), are primarily designed for tasks other than relational learning. DP-
051   SGD, in particular, operates under the assumption that the gradient in each training step can be
052   decoupled with respect to individual training samples that require privacy protection. Under this
053   assumption, DP-SGD controls the norm of the gradient induced by each sample, obfuscates it by
      adding Gaussian noise, and thus ensures a privacy guarantee. However, relational learning on graphs

085   In relational learning, each loss term typically involves an observed relation (represented by edges
086   in the graph), paired with one or more missing relations for contrast. The traditional coupled sam-
087

088

089

091   theoretically compatible with the privacy accounting of the DP-SGD framework.

094
      entities with rich textual attributes. While modern privacy libraries like Opacus (Yousefpour et al.,
095

098   tokens (denoted by M ). Naively computing per-sample gradients results in keeping O(KM ) gra-
099   dient copies in memory per loss term. Even with parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques

497
498   LLMs with Relational Data Extensive work has focused on using relational data to enhance foun-
499
      dation models, especially for fine-tuning LLMs on graphs. These methods can be classified into
      two types. Objective-based: Yasunaga et al. (2022b); Duan et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023) pro-
500
      posed to associate entity representations from LLMs with relational information by optimizing the
501
      objective based on specific graph tasks. E.g., relation prediction is a typical task in unsupervised
502   graph learning, as adopted in this work. Graph-encoder-based: Chien et al. (2021); Yasunaga et al.
503   (2022a); Zhu et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2023b) pair LLMs with a graph encoder
504   (e.g., GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2017)) to incorporate relational information in an end-to-end manner,
505   where LLMs act as feature extractors for textual attributes, and their output with associated relations
506   is fed into GNNs for aggregation and prediction. These models may be privatized by combining the
507   approach proposed in this work with the privatized method for GNNs (Sajadmanesh et al., 2023;
508   Chien et al., 2024), though the entire pipeline could be complex and beyond the scope of this work.
509   Privacy-preserving Graph Learning Significant research has focused on privacy-preserving graph
510   embedding and learning algorithms with DP guarantees (Li et al., 2023b). Daigavane et al. (2021)
511   proposed a privacy-preserving approach for training GNNs via extensions of DP-SGD. Olatunji
512   et al. (2021) adopted teacher-student models to enable the DP release of GNNs. Sajadmanesh et al.
513   (2023) improved utility-privacy trade-offs by decoupling feature propagation and network training,
514   and their work further got extended in subsequent studies (Sajadmanesh & Gatica-Perez, 2024;
515   Chien et al., 2024). These methods specialize in generating private node representations, which do
516
      not mitigate privacy risks when relations that involve multiple entities are used for supervision.
517   Privacy-preserving for LLMs Data privacy in LLMs focuses on safeguarding sensitive information
518   that could be exposed during operations (Yao et al., 2024). Recent efforts have utilized DP-SGD for
519   both pretraining and fine-tuning LLMs. For instance, Anil et al. (2021) trained a privacy-preserving
520   BERT-Large model from scratch. However, due to the resource-intensive nature of LLMs, the focus
521   has shifted towards private fine-tuning of publicly pretrained models. Hoory et al. (2021) explored
522
      private full fine-tuning of BERT models with domain-specific data, while further advancements in
      this field include the works of Basu et al. (2021); Kerrigan et al. (2020); Senge et al. (2021); Li
523
      et al. (2021). There is also growing interest in efficient fine-tuning techniques. Yu et al. (2021)
524
      applied parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods for private fine-tuning of LLMs, and Li
525   et al. (2021) introduced ghost clipping to accelerate gradient clipping in DP-SGD. However, these
526   methods primarily address privacy concerns for standard text data. In contrast, our work extends
527   these privacy-preserving approaches to relational data, filling an important gap in this research area.
528

      6                               C ONCLUSION
531
      Leveraging relational data to enhance AI models holds great promise. This work proposes a novel
532
      privacy-preserving training pipeline that addresses the unique privacy and computational challenges
533
      in relational learning by decoupling the dependencies in sampled relations for training and exploit-
534   ing the structure of individual gradients for efficient clipping. We consider scenarios frequently
535   encountered in applying relational learning to fine-tune pretrained models and enforce privacy guar-
536   antees on the relationships used for training. Our study on private relational learning shows that
537   fine-tuning pretrained language models with our approach can significantly improve their perfor-
538   mance on new test domains while keeping the relational data used for training private. We further
539   explore the privacy, utility, and computational efficiency trade-offs and conduct an extensive study
      on hyperparameter selection for private learning on relational data.

877
878   Private Graph Embedding Methods Graph embedding encodes nodes into low-dimensional vec-
879   tors, preserving topological information (Hamilton et al., 2017). Xu et al. (2018) proposed a pri-
880   vate network embedding method using objective perturbation in DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014)
881   but faced scalability issues for complex sensitivity calculations. Zhang & Ni (2019) addressed
882   these issues by applying a Lipschitz condition (Raskhodnikova & Smith, 2016) and gradient clip-
883   ping. Epasto et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2024) studied DP PageRank methods, which can be leveraged
884   to generate DP graph embedding as well. These methods specialize in preventing privacy leakage
885
      during generating node embeddings but do not mitigate privacy risks when relations that involve
      multiple entities are used for supervision.
886
887   Contrastive Learning with Differential Privacy Existing studies on private contrastive learning
888   aim to eliminate the risk of sample correlation in contrastive loss and thus protect the privacy of
889   individual training samples. Li et al. (2022) proposed to add privacy noise to the similarity ma-
890   trix between pairs of inputs to reduce the sensitivity of gradients w.r.t. the contrastive loss. Kong
891
      et al. (2023) extended it to similarity-based loss functions by bounding the pairwise similarity gra-
      dients. Bao et al. (2024) proposed to train vision models with the mixup technique under DP by
892
      leveraging augmentation multiplicity. These methods focus on privately learning representations of
893
      non-relational samples by contrastive views but cannot be used to address the privacy challenge of
894   relation coupling in training models on relational data.
895

897   B       S TANDARD DP L EARNING P IPELINES
898
899   DP-SGD (see Alg. 2) (Song et al., 2015; Abadi et al., 2016) is proposed for training deep learning
900   models on (non-relational) samples with a privacy guarantee. DP-Adam (see Alg. 3) works simi-
901
      larly as regular Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) but performs updates and moment accumulation with
      privatized gradients. The gradient privatization part is the same as that performed in DP-SGD, where
902
      the privacy analysis and guarantees for DP-SGD still hold for DP-Adam due to the post-processing
903
      property of DP (Dwork et al., 2014).

       Inference Setting Once the model is privately fine-tuned on relational data, it is deployed for
       inference under two settings for relation prediction and entity classification on the corresponding
1050
       test domains (see Table 1):
1051
1052   • Zero-shot, where the model is directly used without further training on samples from the test
1053     domain. This setting is only applied for relation prediction: the dot product between entity em-
1054     beddings is used as the scoring function for inference, which contains no additional parameters.
1055   • Few-shot, where limited labels from the test domain are provided to further fine-tune the target
1056     models obtained after private relational learning. This setting is used for both relation prediction
1057     and entity classification (the classifier requires some labels for initializing parameters), corre-
1058     sponding to the data scarcity scenario from the test domain and limited resources to perform full
1059     domain-specific fine-tuning.

1099   F                                        S COPE AND L IMITATION
1100
1101   Our proposed pipeline aims at private relational learning, with applications in fine-tuning pretrained
1102   large models when relational information indicated by graph edges is used for supervision in a
1103   privacy-preserving manner. Due to resource constraints and the intensity of privacy computing for
1104   large pretrained models, we choose parameter-efficient fine-tuning over full parameter fine-tuning.
1105   Our privacy setting only targets protecting the relationships between entities used for training and
1106   assumes the pretrained model weights are risk-free since they are publicly accessible. The two
1107
       relational datasets of e-commerce and academic networks used in the experiment are open source
       and widely adopted in the community. The textual attributes associated with them are the titles
1108
       and descriptions of shopping items and the titles and abstracts of research papers, respectively, and
1109
       neither of them contains harmful, offensive, or biased language.
