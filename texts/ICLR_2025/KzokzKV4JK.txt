009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion-based purification (DBP) methods aim to remove adversarial noise from
012            the input sample by first injecting Gaussian noise through a forward diffusion pro-
013            cess, and then recovering the clean example through a reverse generative process.
014            In the above process, how much Gaussian noise is injected to the input sample
015            is key to the success of DBP methods, which is controlled by a constant noise
016            level t∗ for all samples in existing methods. In this paper, we discover that an
017            optimal t∗ for each sample indeed could be different. Intuitively, the cleaner a
018
               sample is, the less the noise it should be injected, and vice versa. Motivated by
               this finding, we propose a new framework, called Sample-specific Score-aware
019
               Noise Injection (SSNI). Specifically, SSNI uses a pre-trained score network to
020
               estimate how much a data point deviates from the clean data distribution (i.e.,
021            score norms). Then, based on the magnitude of score norms, SSNI applies a
022            reweighting function to adaptively adjust t∗ for each sample, achieving sample-
023            specific noise injections. Empirically, incorporating our framework with exist-
024            ing DBP methods results in a notable improvement in both accuracy and robust-
025            ness on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-1K, highlighting the necessity to allocate dis-
026            tinct noise levels to different samples in DBP methods. Our code is available at:
027            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SSNI-F746.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep neural networks (DNNs) are vulnerable to adversarial examples, which is a longstanding prob-
033
      lem in deep learning (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2015). Adversarial examples aim
      to mislead DNNs into making erroneous predictions by adding imperceptible adversarial noise to
034
      clean examples, which pose a significant security threat in critical applications (Dong et al., 2019;
035
      Finlayson et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2021; Jing et al., 2021). To defend against adversarial examples,
036   adversarial purification (AP) stands out as a representative defensive mechanism, by leveraging
037   pre-trained generative models to purify adversarial examples back towards their natural counterparts
038   before feeding into a pre-trained classifier (Yoon et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2022). Notably, AP meth-
039   ods benefit from their modularity, as the purifier operates independently of the downstream classifier,
040   which facilitates seamless integration into existing systems and positions AP as a practical approach
041   to improve the adversarial robustness of DNN-based classifiers.
042   Recently, diffusion-based purification (DBP) methods have gained much attention as a promising
043   framework in AP, which leverage the denoising nature of diffusion models to mitigate adversarial
044   noise (Nie et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lee & Kim, 2023). Generally, diffusion models train a
045   forward process that maps from data distributions to simple distributions, e.g., Gaussian, and reverse
046   this mapping via a reverse generative process (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b). When applied
047   in DBP methods, the forward process gradually injects Gaussian noise into the input sample, while
048   the reverse process gradually purify noisy sample to recover the clean sample. The quality of the
049   purified sample heavily depends on the amount of Gaussian noise added to the input during the
050
      forward process, which can be controlled by a noise level parameter t∗ . Existing DBP methods (Nie
      et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lee & Kim, 2023) manually select a constant t∗ for all samples.
051
052   However, we find that using a sample-shared t∗ may overlook the fact that an optimal t∗ indeed
053   could be different at sample-level, as demonstrated in Figure 1. For example, in Figure 1a, t∗ = 100
      is too small, resulting in the adversarial noise not being sufficiently removed by the diffusion models.

074   This is because diffusion models are good at denoising samples that have been sufficiently corrupted
075
      by Gaussian noise through the forward process (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b). With a small
      t∗ , the sample remains insufficiently corrupted, which limits the denoising capability in the reverse
076

079   in the reverse process. In this case, both robustness and clean accuracy are compromised, as the
080   purified samples struggle to preserve the semantic consistency of clean samples. These observations
081   motivate us to make the first attempt to adjust the noise level on a sample-specific basis.
082
      In this paper, we propose Sample-specific Score-aware Noise Injection (SSNI), a new framework
083
      that leverages the distance of a sample from the clean data distribution to adaptively adjust t∗ on a
084   sample-specific basis. SSNI aims to inject less noise to cleaner samples, and vice versa.
085
086   To implement SSNI, inspired by the fact that scores (i.e., ∇x log pt (x)) reflect the directional mo-
087
      mentum of samples toward the high-density areas of clean data distribution (Song & Ermon, 2019),
      we use score norms (i.e., ∥∇x log pt (x)∥) as a natural metric to measure the deviation of a data point
088
      from the clean data distribution. In Section 3, we establish the relationship between the score norm
089
      and the noise level required for different samples. Specifically, samples with different score norms
090   tend to have accumulated different noise levels. Furthermore, we empirically show that the cleaner
091   samples – those closer to the clean data distribution – exhibit lower score norm, justifying the ra-
092   tionale of using score norms for reweighting t∗ . Concretely, we use a pre-trained score network
093   to estimate the score norm for each sample. Based on this, we propose two reweighting functions
094   that adaptively adjust t∗ according to its score norm, achieving sample-specific noise injections (see
095   Section 4.3). Notably, this reweighting process is lightweight, ensuring that SSNI is computationally
096   feasible and can be applied in practice with minimal overhead (see Section 5.5).
097   Through extensive evaluations on benchmark image datasets such as CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
098   2009) and ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009), we demonstrate the effectiveness of SSNI in Section
099   5. Specifically, combined with different DBP methods (Nie et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lee &
100   Kim, 2023), SSNI can boost clean accuracy and robust accuracy simultaneously by a notable margin
101   against the well-designed adaptive white-box attack (see Section 5.2 and Algorithm 2).
102   The success of SSNI takes root in the following aspects: (1) an optimal noise level t∗ for each sample
103   indeed could be different, making SSNI a more effective approach to unleash the intrinsic strength of
104   DBP methods; (2) existing DBP methods often inject excessive noise into clean samples, resulting
105   in a degradation in clean accuracy. By contrast, SSNI injects less noise to clean samples, and thereby
106   notably improving the clean accuracy. Meanwhile, SSNI can effectively handle adversarial samples
107   by injecting sufficient noise on each sample; (3) SSNI is designed as general framework instead of
      a specific method, allowing it to be seamlessly integrated with a variety of existing DBP methods.

136   Score and score norm. In this paper, a score refers to the gradient of the log-probability density, i.e.,
137   ∇x log pt (x), which represents the direction of maximum increase in the log-density across a vector
138   field (Song & Ermon, 2019). The score norm is the magnitude (or length) of this gradient, denoted as
139   ∥∇x log p(x)∥. It reflects how much a data point deviates from the clean data distribution. A larger
140   score norm suggests the data point is situated in a low-probability region, while a smaller score norm
141   indicates it is closer to regions where clean data occurs. Yoon et al. (2021) discover that score norms
142   can effectively differentiate adversarial examples from clean examples. Building on this, Zhang et al.
143   (2023) propose a more robust score called the expected perturbation score (EPS), which computes
144
      the expected scores of perturbed samples over a range of noise levels. EPS improves robustness
      against noise variations, providing a more reliable metric for detecting adversarial samples.
145
146   Diffusion-based purification. Adversarial purification (AP) leverages generative models as an add-
147   on module to purify adversarial examples before classification. With in this context, diffusion-
148   based purification (DBP) methods have emerged as a promising framework, exploiting the inherent
149   denoising nature of diffusion models to filter out adversarial noise (Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
150
      2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lee & Kim, 2023). Specifically, Nie et al. (2022) and Xiao et al. (2023)
      integrate diffusion models to purify adversarial inputs. Wang et al. (2022) introduce input guidance
151
      during the reverse diffusion process to ensure the purified outputs stay close to the inputs. Lee &
152
      Kim (2023) further establish a reliable evaluation framework for DBP methods and propose a fine-
153   tuned gradual noise scheduling for multi-step purifications. More recently, Bai et al. (2024) propose
154   to guide diffusion models with contrastive loss through the reverse process.

158
      In this section, we elaborate on the motivation of our method by connecting the impact of different
159
      perturbation budgets ϵ to the required noise level t∗ of each sample through score norm.
160
161   Sample-shared noise-level t∗ fails to address diverse perturbation budgets. We empirically ob-
      serve that an optimal noise level t∗ for each sample indeed could be different. Figure 1 illustrates

      that, while a constant noise level t∗ = 100, as suggested by Nie et al. (2022), yields strong perfor-
163   mances for some samples, it leads to suboptimal results for others. Since DBP relies on adequate
164   t∗ for forward noise injection to remove adversarial perturbation, a shared t∗ cannot adapt to the
165   distinct perturbation budgets of individual examples, leading to a suboptimal accuracy-robustness
166   trade-off. Specifically, Figure 1a shows that t∗ = 100 is insufficient remove the adversarial noise,
167   leaving residual perturbations that compromise robustness. On the contrary, Figure 1c and 1b reveal
168   that t∗ = 100 overly suppresses the other sample’s semantic information during the forward process,
169   making it difficult to recover the original semantics via the reverse process. These findings highlight
170   the need for sample-specific noise injection levels tailored to individual perturbation budgets.
171   Score norms vary across perturbation budgets. Adversarial and clean examples are from distinct
172   distributions (Gao et al., 2021). Motivated by this, we further investigate how different perturbation
173   budgets ϵ affect score norms under adversarial attacks (see Appendix B). Specifically, we compute
174   the score norm of different samples undergoing PGD+EOT ℓ∞ (ϵ = 8/255) with perturbation bud-
175   gets varying between 0 and 8/255 on CIFAR-10. Our finding reveals a consistent pattern: The
176   results consistently reveal that samples subjected to stronger perturbations (higher ϵ) exhibit larger
177
      score norms, whereas cleaner samples (lower ϵ) show smaller score norms. These results imply the
      utility of score norms can be extended from detecting adversarial samples from clean ones, as shown
178
      by (Yoon et al., 2021), to differentiate adversarial examples based on their perturbation strength.

182   with the noise level t∗ , hypothesizing that score norms can inform noise strengths t∗ tailored to

      individual samples. The proofs of the following Lemma 1 and Proposition 1 are in Appendix A.

300   4.2   U NIFYING S AMPLE - SHARED AND S AMPLE - SPECIFIC DBP
301
302   We define a generalized purification operator encompassing both sample-shared and sample-specific
303   noise based DBP methods as Φ(x) = R(xT (x) ), where R denotes the reverse process, xT (x) is the
304   noisy version of x after T (x) steps of diffusion, and T : X → T is a function that determines the
305   noise level for each input, with T = [0, Tmax ] being the range of possible noise levels. With this
306
      operator, we have the following understandings (detailed justifications are in Appendix C).
307   Sample-shared DBP is a special case of SSNI. For a sample-shared DBP, the noise level, denoted
308   as TSH (x) = t∗ , is a constant for ∀x ∈ X , while for SSNI: TSI (x) = t(x) = f (∥sθ (x)∥ , t∗ ).
309   Clearly, any TSH (x) can be expressed by TSI (x), implying that any sample-shared noise level t∗ is
310   equivalently represented by SSNI with a constant reweighting function.
311   SSNI has higher purification flexibility. For a given  DBP strategy, we further define the purifica-
312   tion range Ω for an input x ∈ X , as the set Ω(x) = Φ(x) | Φ(x) = R(xτ (x) ), τ : X → T , which
313   characterizes all possible purified outputs of the input x. This concept captures the flexibility of a
314   DBP strategy. We find that ΩSH ⊆ ΩSI holds for any x ∈ X , and there exists at least one x ∈ X for
315   which the inclusion is strict, i.e., ΩSH ⊊ ΩSI . These results show that SSNI achieves a broader pu-
316   rification range than sample-shared DBP, thus enabling greater flexibility in the purification process.

321   Realization of the score. In Section 4.1, The samples {xi }ni=1 in sθ (xi ) are associated with a cor-
322   responding set of noise levels {ti }ni=1 . However, a direct realization of this idea presents two chal-
323   lenges: (1) If each sample xi corresponds to a single noise level ti , the resulting score calculation
      becomes highly sensitive to the choice of ti (Zhang et al., 2023). (2) It is difficult to pre-determine


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      an optimal set of noise levels {ti }ni=1 when calculating sθ (xi ), which still remains as an open ques-
325   tion. To address the above-mentioned limitations, following Zhang et al. (2023), we use expected
326   perturbation score (EPS) to measure how much a data point deviates from the clean distribution,
327   which is defined as:
328                                    EPS(x) = Et∼U (0,T ′ ) ∇x log pt (x),                               (7)
329
      where pt (x) is the marginal probability density and T ′ is the maximum noise level for EPS.
330   EPS computes the expectation of the scores of perturbed images across different noise levels
331   t ∼ U (0, T ′ ), making it more invariant to the changes in noise levels. Notably, this T ′ is differ-
332   ent from the optimal noise level t∗ in this paper. Following Zhang et al. (2023), we set T ′ = 20. In
333   practice, a score ∇x logpt (x) can be approximated by sθ (x), where sθ is a score network. In this
334   paper, we use a pre-trained score network that has a training objective of score matching (Song &

527   7     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we find that an optimal t∗ indeed could be different at sample-level. Motivated by this
530   finding, we propose a new framework called Sample-specific Score-aware Noise Injection (SSNI).
531   SSNI sample-wisely reweight t∗ for each sample based on its score norm, which generally injects
532   less noise to clean samples and sufficient noise to adversarial samples, leading to a notable improve-
533
      ment in accuracy-robustness trade-off. We hope this simple yet effective framework could open up
      a new perspective in DBP methods and lay the groundwork for sample specific noise injections.

984
       For all chosen DBP methods, we utilize surrogate process to obtain gradients of the defense system
985
       during white-box adaptive attack, but we directly compute the full gradients during defense evalu-
986
       ation. Furthermore, we consistently apply DDPM sampling method to the selected DBP methods,
987    which means we replace the numeric SDE solver (sdeint) with DDPM sampling method in Diff-
988    Pure (Nie et al., 2022) and GDMP (Wang et al., 2022). The reason is that the SDE solver does not
989    support sample-specific timestep input. For DDPM sampling, we can easily manipulate sample-
990    specific timestep input by using matrix operation.
991
992    D.1   D IFF P URE
993
994    Existing DBP methods generally follow the algorithm of DiffPure (Nie et al., 2022). DiffPure con-
995    ducts evaluation on AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020) and BPDA+EOT adaptive attack (Athalye
996    et al., 2018) to measure model robustness. DiffPure chooses optimal t∗ = 100 and t∗ = 75 on
997
       CIFAR-10 against threat models ℓ∞ (ϵ = 8/255) and ℓ2 (ϵ = 0.5), respectively. It also tests on high-
       resolution dataset like ImageNet-1K with t∗ = 150 against threat models ℓ∞ (ϵ = 4/255). Calcu-
998
       lating exact full gradients of the defense system of DiffPure is impossible since one attack iteration
999
       requires 100 function calls (with t∗ = 100 and a step size of 1). DiffPure originally uses numerical
1000   SDE solver for calculating gradients. However, the adjoint method is insufficient to measure the
1001   model robustness since it relies on the performance of the underlying SDE solver (Zhuang et al.,
1002   2020; Lee & Kim, 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Therefore, we apply surrogate process to efficiently
1003   compute gradients of direct back-propagation in our evaluation. To overcome memory constraint is-
1004   sue, we align the step size settings of denoising process in adversarial attack to 5 with the evaluation
1005   settings in (Lee & Kim, 2023) and keep the timestep t∗ consistent with DiffPure. For ImageNet-1K
1006   evaluation, we can only afford a maximum of 10 function calls for one attack iteration.
1007
1008   D.2   GDMP
1009
1010
       GDMP basically follows the purification algorithm proposed in DiffPure (Nie et al., 2022; Wang
       et al., 2022), but their method further introduces a novel guidance and use multiple purification
1011
       steps sequentially. GDMP proposes to use gradients of a distance between an initial input and a
1012
       target being processed to preserve semantic information, shown in Eq. (12).

1016   xtadv is the adversarial example at t. Also, GDMP empirically finds that multiple purification steps
1017   can improve the robustness. In the original evaluation of GDMP, the defense against the PGD attack
1018   consists of four purification steps, with each step including 36 forward steps and 36 denoising steps.
1019   For BPDA+EOT adaptive attack, GDMP uses two purification steps, each consisting of 50 forward
1020   steps and 50 denoising steps.
1021   Lee & Kim (2023) evaluated GDMP with three types of guidance and concluded that No-Guidance
1022   provides the best robustness performance when using the surrogate process to compute the full
1023   gradient through direct backpropagation. In our evaluation, we incorporate the surrogate process
1024   with No-Guidance to evaluate GDMP. Since it is impossible to calculate the gradients of the full
1025   defense system, we use a surrogate process consisting of same number of purification steps but with
       larger step size in the attack (with 6 denoising steps and 10 denoising steps for PGD+EOT and

       BPDA+EOT attack, respectively). Notably, GDMP only uses one purification run with 45 forward
1027   steps to evaluate on ImageNet-1K, which we keep consistent with this setting.

       D.3    GNS
1030
1031   Lee & Kim (2023) emphasizes the importance of selecting optimal hyperparameters in DBP meth-
1032   ods for achieving better robust performance. Hence, Lee & Kim (2023) proposed Gradual Noise-
1033   Scheduling (GNS) for multi-step purification, which is based on the idea of choosing the best hy-
1034   perparameters for multiple purification steps. It is basically the same architecture as GDMP (no
1035   guidance), but with different purification steps, forward steps and denoising steps. Specifically,
1036   GNS sets different forward and reverse diffusion steps and gradually increases the noise level at
1037
       each subsequent purification step. We just keep the same hyperparameter settings and also use an
       ensemble of 10 runs to evaluate the method.

1040   E     S URROGATE P ROCESS OF G RADIENT C OMPUTATION
1041
1042   The surrogate process is an efficient approach for computing approximate gradients through back-
1043   propagation, as proposed by (Lee & Kim, 2023). White-box adaptive attacks, such as PGD+EOT,
1044   involve an iterative optimization process that requires computing the exact full gradients of the entire
1045   system, result in high memory usage and increased computational time. DBP methods often include
1046
       a diffusion model as an add-on purifier, which the model requires extensive function calls during
       reverse generative process. Hence, it is hard to compute the exact full gradient of DBP systems ef-
1047
       ficiently. The surrogate process takes advantage of the fact that, given a fixed total amount of noise,
1048
       we can denoise it using fewer denoising steps (Song et al., 2021a), but the gradients obtained from
1049   the surrogate process slightly differ from the exact gradients. Instead of using the full denoising
1050   steps, we approximate the original denoising process with fewer function calls, which allows us to
1051   compute gradients by directly back-propagating through the forward and reverse processes.
1052
       There are other gradient computation methods such as adjoint method in DiffPure (Li et al., 2020;
1053
       Nie et al., 2022). It leverages an underlying numerical SDE solver to solve the reverse-time SDE.
1054
       The adjoint method can theoretically compute exact full gradient, but in practice, it relies on the
1055   performance of the numerical solver, which is insufficient to measure the model robustness (Zhuang
1056   et al., 2020; Lee & Kim, 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Lee & Kim (2023) conducted a comprehensive
1057   evaluation of both gradient computation methods and concluded that utilizing the surrogate pro-
1058   cess for gradient computation poses a greater threat to model robustness. Hence, we use gradients
1059   obtained from a surrogate process in all our experiments.
