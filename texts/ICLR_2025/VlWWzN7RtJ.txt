009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce iMotion-LLM, a Multimodal Large Language Model (LLM) in-
012            tegrated with trajectory prediction, designed to guide interactive motion predic-
013            tion scenarios. Unlike conventional multimodal trajectory prediction approaches,
014            iMotion-LLM generates diverse and feasible future trajectories conditioned on tex-
015            tual instructions as a guidance signal. By augmenting real-world driving scenarios
016            in the Waymo Open Motion Dataset (WOMD) with textual motion instructions,
017            we propose InstructWaymo data augmentation. Leveraging this data augmentation,
018
               iMotion-LLM integrates a pretrained LLM, fine-tuned with LoRA, to map scene
               features into the LLM input space. Key results demonstrate that making the trajec-
019
               tory prediction model conditional improves its instruction-following capabilities.
020
               Specifically, the integration of the LLM enables a 11.07x ratio of actual-scenario
021            feasible to infeasible recall instruction following, compared to 5.92x when us-
022            ing the Conditional GameFormer alone. These findings highlight the ability of
023            iMotion-LLM to generate trajectories that not only align with feasible instructions
024            but also reject infeasible ones, enhancing overall safety. Despite its improvements
025            in instruction following, iMotion-LLM inherits the strong trajectory prediction per-
026            formance of the baseline model, making it versatile across different driving modes.
027            This combination of skills positions iMotion-LLM as a powerful augmentation
028            technique for trajectory prediction models, empowering autonomous navigation
029
               systems to better interpret the motion prediction. This work lays the groundwork
               for future advancements in instruction-based motion prediction.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In autonomous driving, accurate trajectory prediction is critical for ensuring safe and efficient
035
      navigation in dynamic environments. Given a window of observed history, the task is to predict
036   multi-modal trajectories of multiple agents surrounding the ego vehicle in addition to the ego vehicle.
037   A significant challenge in this field is the inherently diverse nature of driving behaviors in real-world
038   scenarios, where an agent’s future trajectory is not deterministic but can follow multiple feasible
039   paths due to various factors such as traffic rules, interactions with other agents, and environmental
040   conditions. Hence, developing models that can effectively predict diverse trajectories is crucial for
041   autonomous systems to anticipate and adapt to potential hazards, make informed decisions, and
042   ultimately achieve reliable and safe operation.
043   Recent challenges, e.g., Waymo Open Motion Dataset (WOMD) challenges (Ettinger et al., 2021b),
044   introduce a track specifically designed to concentrate on motion prediction where 1.1 seconds of the
045   past motion is observed, and 8 seconds to be predicted into the future. Various methodologies (Huang
046   et al., 2023a; Shi et al., 2022a; Seff et al., 2023) have been developed to tackle this challenge.
047   Although previous models can predict multi-modality trajectories, the predicted paths are not diverse
048   enough and mainly focus on one driving behavior (e.g., the trajectory of only one feasible direction.).
049   The reason is that previous prediction models are trained to imitate real-driving scenarios, fitting
050
      the driving behavior recorded future ground truth trajectory. Therefore, they lack comprehension of
      different driving behaviors in a given scenario.
051
052   To address the aforementioned challenge, we introduce a novel task called Text-Guided Intention
053   Trajectory Prediction that aims to generate trajectories conditioning on a driving instruction for a
      selected vehicle. Additionally, the task provides a textual description predicting the feasibility of

083   The proposed Text-Guided Intention Trajectory Prediction task offers several key advantages over
084   traditional trajectory prediction models. First, it allows examining trajectory prediction modeling
085   capability in covering different feasible driving behaviors for a given scenario. Second, by generating
086   text-guided driving scenarios that are safety-critical or more challenging, the task can be used to
087
      train or test trajectory prediction and planning frameworks under diverse and demanding conditions,
      improving the robustness of autonomous systems. Furthermore, this approach enhances the inter-
088
      pretability of prediction models by providing explicit descriptions of driving intentions and how they
089
      translate into vehicle movements, making it easier to understand and trust the model’s decisions.
090
091   To instantiate a dataset and model for this task, we augment WOMD (Ettinger et al., 2021a) with
092   vehicle direction instructions. Additionally, we did evaluation experiments to show the generalizability
093
      on the NuPlan dataset (H. Caesar, 2021). The instruction details and statistics are explained in
      Section 3. For the evaluation, we propose two novel metrics. The Instruction Following Recall
094
      (IFR) measures how well the predicted trajectories adhere to the specified driving instruction, while
095
      the Direction Variety Score (DVS) captures the diversity of predicted directions. Subsequently, we
096   introduce the iMotion-LLM: an instructable motion prediction model based on Large Language
097   Models (LLMs). iMotion-LLM, harnesses pretrained models’ multi-modal trajectory prediction
098   capabilities through integrating their encoder to map scene vector features and their decoder to decode
099   trajectories. As shown in Figure 3, it employs an LLM Projection to project encoded scene context
100   embeddings from the Scene Encoder into the LLM input space. The LLM generates an instruction
101   token [I] and N [S] tokens representing the scene context embeddings. The instruction token is
102   mapped to represent an additional intention query used by the decoder, while the scene tokens are
103   used as keys and values. Our design of the encoder-decoder for the trajectory prediction model
104
      introduces an additional instruction query, alongside the learnable queries present in the original
      model design, which act as decoding seeds.
105
106   Our experiments, using GameFormer (Huang et al., 2023a) as a backbone, show that iMotion-LLM
107   empowers autonomous navigation systems to interpret and predict the dynamics of agents, while
      almost matching the performance of the base model.

      Our contributions can be summarized as:
109
110          • We augmented WOMD with instruction categories, enabling the motion prediction task to
111            be instructed. This augmentation, named InstructWaymo, is easily expandable to include
112            more detailed driving scenarios and will benefit future research in this direction.
113          • We enable traditional trajectory prediction modeling through the design integration of
114            iMotion-LLM to generate text-guided instructable trajectory predictions, allowing the model
115            to cover diverse feasible driving behaviors in a given scenario.
116          • We integrate LLMs with traditional trajectory prediction models to reason about predicted
117            trajectories and determine how they should be executed in steps, while also training iMotion-
118            LLM to accept or reject instructions based on feasibility.
119          • We introduced two evaluation metrics: Instruction Following Recall (IFR) and Direction
120            Variety Score (DVS), to measure the model’s ability to follow instructions and the diversity
121            of predicted modalities across different directional categories, which cannot be captured by
122
               conventional metrics used in motion prediction.

124   2    R ELATED W ORK
125
126   Multimodal Large Langauge Models. Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly advanced
127   in recent years (Radford et al., 2019; Devlin et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023b;a;
128   Achiam et al., 2023), with models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) demonstrating remarkable
129   abilities in generating coherent, contextually relevant text across numerous domains. With the strong
130
      performance of LLMs, there is an emergence of multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022),
      which extend the LLMs with reasoning abilities across diverse modalities. Notable works includes
131
      Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023b), MiniGPT-4 (Chen et al., 2023; Zhu
132
      et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024; 2023), and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023). These works used
133   visual instruction tuning to align with human intentions. There are some extensions that focus on
134   detection and segmentation (Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023),
135   videos (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Maaz et al., 2023), and 3D (Hong et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
136   2023; Guo et al., 2023). Our work focuses on MLLMs for motion prediction tasks.
137
      Trajectory Prediction Models for Driving Scenarios. The task of trajectory prediction involves
138
      analyzing the historical tracks of agents on a corresponding map to predict their joint future positions
139   several seconds into the future. LSTMs (Alahi et al., 2016; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) have
140   been used to encode the historical states of agents, while CNNs (Cui et al., 2019; Gilles et al., 2021;
141   Salzmann et al., 2020) have been employed to encode the rasterized images of the scene. Recently,
142   GNNs (Chen et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b; Mo et al., 2022) have been employed to depict agent
143   interactions effectively. The advent of Transformer-based models, like SceneTransformer (Ngiam
144   et al., 2021) and WayFormer (Nayakanti et al., 2023), has further enhanced prediction through
145   their efficient structure, though they primarily focus on the encoding process of driving scenarios
146   vectorized representation. Motion Transformer (Shi et al., 2022b; 2024) and GameFormer (Huang
147
      et al., 2023b) innovates by improving the decoding stage, leading to better accuracy. MotionLM (Seff
      et al., 2023) used similar structures of LLM for the modeling, but still did not introduce the language
148
      reasoning ability to motion prediction task.
149
150   Multimodal Large Language Models for autonomous driving. With the emergence of Large
151   Language Models (LLMs), there is a growing trend to adapt LLMs for autonomous driving scenar-
152   ios (Chen et al., 2024; Dewangan et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022a). Innovations like
153
      GPT-Driver (Mao et al., 2023) and SurrealDriver (Jin et al., 2023) exemplify the transformative impact
      of LLMs on motion planning and driving maneuver generation, marking significant advancements in
154
      autonomous vehicle technology. However, most existing methods primarily focus on text or image
155
      inputs, overlooking the benefits of vector representation in motion prediction. Vector representation
156   offers an abstraction of driving scenarios, directly capturing the necessary information for motion
157   prediction. Similar to Driving with LLMs (Chen et al., 2024), we integrate LLMs with vector-based
158   data for motion prediction. While (Chen et al., 2024) introduced a benchmark focused mainly on QA
159   tasks for driving scenarios, with motion only represented as a single quantized action (acceleration,
160   braking, and steering), our work differs by focusing on motion represented as multi-modal multi-agent
161   trajectories. This approach aligns more closely with existing trajectory prediction modules, making it
      more suitable for safe and reliable motion prediction.

246   4.1   R EVISITING EXISTING MODELS
247
248
      Recent successful transformer-based interactive trajectory prediction models (Huang et al., 2023a;
      Shi et al., 2022a) commonly employ a schema comprising two main blocks. Initially, a scene
249
      encoder encodes the observed map and agent information into embeddings representing scene context
250
      information S ∈ RR×dscene , where dscene is the embedding dimension. This context information is
251   crucial for understanding the dynamics of the environment. The second component is a multimodal
252   trajectory prediction decoder. This decoder employs cross-attention, using the scene context S as the
253   keys and values (denoted as K&V ). The decoder also utilizes K learnable queries qmotion ∈ RK×dscene
254   to predict a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) of the potential future multi-modal trajectory of multiple
255   agents. Based on this, the GameFormer model (Huang et al., 2023a) consists of two core blocks, the
256   Scene Encoder and Trajectory Decoder, which are visually represented in purple in Figure 3. In the
257   scene encoder,
258
              • Vectorized motion data is encoded using a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network.
              • Map features are processed via Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for continuous data, such
259             as the geometric layout of center lanes, or through embedding layers for categorical data
260             like the state of traffic lights.
261           • Once encoded, the scene encoder serves as a feature fusion layer, combining all the processed
262             features to form scene representation.
263
      After feature fusion, each token retains a specific correspondence to its map components. For example,
264
      in GameFormer’s two-agent joint prediction model:
265
             • The ego agent state is represented by two tokens, with one token being self-referential (when
266             the ego agent is the focal agent), and the other token corresponding to the interaction with
267             another agent (when the other agent becomes the focal agent).
268          • This pattern extends across all map features, where each map feature has two versions:
269             one normalized with respect to the ego agent and another normalized with respect to the
                interacting agent.

356   6.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
357
      Implementation Details. We started by reproducing the checkpoint of GameFormer (Huang et al.,
358
      2023a) using 4,228,499 training samples (using the same training setup and hyperparameters as
359
      suggested). Similarly, we trained the conditional GameFormer model with 2,011,265 training
360   samples augmented with categorical instructions. We use this as our pretrained trajectory forecasting
361   backbone. For iMotion-LLM, which integrates the pretrained conditional GameFormer with Llama-
362   2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023b) and extends the vocabulary to include 3 additional tokens ([I], [S1], and
363   [S2] tokens), the LLM mapping modules are fully fine-tuned, and LoRA weights are fine-tuned with
364   LoRA parameters of r = 8 and α = 16 for 3,510 training steps. The training involved 39 iterations
365   per inner epoch, a batch size of 64 per GPU, using 4x A100-80GB GPUs, effectively covering
366   900,000 training samples over 90 epochs. We utilized the Adam optimizer with an initial learning
367   rate (LR) of 1e-4, incorporating a linear warmup for the first 100 steps starting from a warmup LR
368
      of 1e-6, followed by a cosine LR scheduler. Training takes approximately 8 hours to complete 90
      epochs.
369
370   Training Scenarios. The model was trained with Actual-Scenario (AS) instructions and Infeasible
371   (INF) instructions. During training, the selection of a driving scenario (AS or INF) sample was
372   random. For AS samples, the loss is calculated using both the LLM output text cross-entropy loss
373   (feasibility detection text, and transcription of how the action is performed) and the trajectory negative
374
      log-likelihood loss (the same training objective as GameFormer). For INF instructions, since there
      are no reference ground-truth trajectories, the loss solely consists of the cross-entropy of the LLM
375
      output text for feasibility detection.
376

525   8      C ONCLUSION
526
527
528   In conclusion, we introduce iMotion-LLM, a Large Multimodal Model powered by LLMs, tailored for
529   trajectory prediction in interactive multi-agent scenarios within autonomous navigation. By leveraging
530   textual instructions as key inputs, our model not only generates contextually relevant trajectory
531
      predictions but also showcases an enhanced ability to interpret and act upon these instructions.
      Through integration with a pretrained LLM fine-tuned with LoRA, iMotion-LLM effectively translates
532
      scene features into the LLM input space, enabling accurate multimodal trajectory forecasts. Notably,
533
      our model’s ability to generate trajectories aligned with provided instructions inherits the performance
534   of the underlying backbone model, marking a significant advancement in empowering autonomous
535   navigation systems to anticipate the dynamics of multi-agent environments. iMotion-LLM, combined
536   with InstructWaymo instructions and captions, provides the capability to align trajectories with
537   feasible instructions and reject infeasible ones, thereby enhancing operational safety. This work not
538   only advances the field of autonomous navigation by enabling systems to better anticipate and react
539   within multi-agent environments but also sets a solid foundation for further innovations in interactive
      autonomous systems.

      Following the illustration shown in Figure 6, motion direction is measured based on the relative
      heading angle between a time step and a future target step. We calculate direction solely based
802
      on trajectory information; the heading angle is calculated using two consecutive trajectory discrete
803
      samples. If the maximum future speed is within a threshold of vstationary = 2m/s, and the vehicle
804   traveled a distance within dstationary = 5m, the vehicle is considered stationary. Otherwise, the
805   vehicle is moving straight if the relative heading is within θs = 30 degrees. But if the longitudinal
806   displacement is greater than dv = 5m, it is categorized as straight veering right/left. If the relative
807   heading exceeds θs , and the latitudinal shift is less than du = 5m in the opposite direction, it is
808   considered as turning right/left. Otherwise, it is a U-turn. Right and left directions are distinguished
809   based on the sign of the relative heading. Figure 6 illustrates the different classes. Table 1 provides
      detailed statistics on these eight categories.
