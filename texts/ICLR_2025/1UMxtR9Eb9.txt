009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing disentangled representation learning methods rely on inductive biases
012            tailored for specific factors of variation (e.g., attributes or objects). However, these
013            biases are incompatible with other classes of factors, limiting their applicability
014            for disentangling general factors of variation. In this paper, we propose a unified
015            framework for disentangled representation learning, accommodating both attribute
016            and object disentanglement. To this end, we reformulate disentangled representa-
017            tion learning as maximizing the compositionality of the latents. Specifically, we
018
               randomly mix two latent representations from distinct images and maximize the
               likelihood of the resulting composite image. Under this general framework, we
019
               demonstrate that adjusting the strategy for mixing between two latent representa-
020
               tions allows us to capture either attributes or objects within a single framework. To
021            derive appropriate mixing strategies, we analyze the compositional structures of
022            both attributes and objects, then incorporate these structures into their respective
023            mixing strategies. Our evaluations show that our method surpasses or is comparable
024            to state-of-the-art baselines such as DisDiff (Yang et al., 2023) in attribute disen-
025            tanglement (DCI, FactorVAE scores), and LSD (Jiang et al., 2023) and L2C (Jung
026            et al., 2024) in object property prediction tasks for object disentanglement.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Understanding the underlying structure of data is crucial for building robust and interpretable machine
032   learning models. In particular, by perceiving the world through compositional concepts, unseen
033
      data can be decomposed into simpler, more interpretable components. This approach dramatically
      improves data efficiency of learning, as unseen data can be explained as combinations of already
034
      learned concepts (Lake et al., 2017; Kuo et al., 2021). In this context, disentangled representation
035
      learning (Higgins et al., 2018; Bengio & LeCun, 2007) aims to decompose the data into its underlying
036   factors of variation. As Locatello et al. (2019) theoretically prove that disentangled representation
037   learning cannot be achieved without inductive biases or direct supervision, the field has focused on
038   designing appropriate inductive biases to disentangle desirable factors in an unsupervised manner.
039
      Attribute and object disentanglement are two of the most common tasks in disentangled representation
040
      learning. We commonly refer to attributes as properties shared globally across the entire scene (e.g.,
041
      color, lighting, or style), while objects are distinct spatial components within a scene (e.g., individual
042   entities). Attribute disentanglement (Burgess et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2016; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen
043   et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2022) aims to isolate various features or properties of the data. Disentanglement
044   between latent variables is often encouraged by additional regularization terms, such as minimizing
045   Total Correlation in VAEs (Burgess et al., 2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018), maximizing
046   mutual information between latents and images Chen et al. (2016); Lin et al. (2020b), or minimizing
047   mutual information between vector-wise latents (Yang et al., 2023). On the other hand, object-centric
048   learning focuses on decomposing scenes into individual objects (Burgess et al., 2019; Greff et al.,
049   2019; Engelcke et al., 2020; Locatello et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023). These methods rely on a spatial
050
      exclusiveness bias, where each pixel in an image must correspond to a unique object, implemented
      within model architectures such as spatial-attention masks (Burgess et al., 2019; Engelcke et al.,
051
      2020), pixel-mixture decoders (Greff et al., 2019), or slot attention (Locatello et al., 2020).
052
053   While both attribute and object disentanglement share the common goal of identifying underlying
      factors of variation, the aforementioned inductive biases are crafted specific to their respective target

054
      factors and are incompatible with each other. Moreover, relying on these inductive biases may limit
055   their extension to disentangling general factors of variation or scenarios that involve both attributes and
056   objects in a scene. This challenge motivates us to develop a unified inductive bias capable of capturing
057   a broader range of factors of variation. In this paper, we present a unified framework for disentangled
058   representation learning that supports both attribute and object disentanglement. Inspired by the fact
059   that the goal of disentangled representation learning is to achieve combinatorial generalization, we
060   formulate disentangled representation learning as the process of maximizing compositionality and
061   carefully design the composition rule that ensures valid combinations of latents. Specifically, given
062   two sets of latent representations from different images, we construct a composite representation
063
      by exchanging random subsets of latents and maximize the likelihood of the resulting composite
      image. By analyzing the compositional structures of attributes and objects, we derive specific mixing
064
      strategies that enable valid combinations for effective attribute and object disentanglement. Unlike
065
      previous methods, which introduce inductive biases specifically tailored to either attribute or object
066   disentanglement and are not compatible with both, our framework can handle both types of factors.
067   Our experiments demonstrate that our framework effectively disentangles both attributes and objects
068   by simply adjusting the mixing strategy, without altering model architectures or objective functions.
069
      Our contributions are as follows: (1) We present a unified framework for disentangled representation
070
      learning that effectively addresses both attribute and object disentanglement. (2) We derive simple
071
      yet effective mixing strategies for disentangling attributes or objects, drawing from their underlying
072   compositional structures. (3) We compare our methods with strong baselines specifically designed
073   for disentangled representation learning and object-centric learning and verify that our method can
074   achieve comparable or even superior performance to the baselines.

077   2    BACKGROUND : D ISENTANGLED R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
078
079   In this section, we briefly review the two main streams of disentangled representation learning: at-
080   tribute and object disentanglement. We discuss how previous methods have achieved disentanglement
081
      and why they are incompatible with each other. More in-depth discussions on related works are
      presented in Appendix A.1.
082
083
084
      Attribute disentanglement In attribute disentanglement, scenes are assumed to consist of a fixed
      number of random variables (Kim & Mnih, 2018). Typical approaches aim to discover independent
085
      latent variables by designing objective functions that promote their statistical independence. For
086
      instance, (Burgess et al., 2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018) use Total Correlation (Watanabe,
087   1960) within the VAE framework to assess independence between latent dimensions. Alternatively,
088   (Lin et al., 2020b; Ren et al., 2022) introduce contrastive regularization, encouraging variations in
089   each latent variable to produce distinct changes in the output space of GANs. Recently, Yang et al.
090   (2023) proposed minimizing the upper bound of mutual information between latent variables. These
091   information-theoretic objectives are suited for scenarios where each data is composed of a fixed set of
092   factors, with each latent variable corresponding to a specific factor. However, when this assumption is
093   violated, defining and directly measuring independence between latent variables becomes non-trivial.
094   For example, in object-centric scenes, the same objects can appear in different spatial locations,
095
      complicating the definition of independence metrics for object representations.
096
097   Object disentanglement In object-centric learning, random variables are assumed to be indepen-
098   dent but share a generative mechanism, such that different orderings of the latents still produce
099
      identical images (Greff et al., 2019). Since measuring independence between object representations
      is challenging, object-centric approaches use architectural biases to promote independence indirectly.
100
      Early methods implemented spatial exclusiveness through decoders that render each latent into pairs
101
      of a RGB image and a mask, blending them to form the final output (Burgess et al., 2019; Greff
102   et al., 2019; Engelcke et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2020a). Each mask corresponds to a distinct region,
103   inducing spatial exclusivity among the latents. Slot attention (Locatello et al., 2020) adopts a spatially
104   exclusive mechanism within the encoder, where each latent (slot) exclusively binds to spatial locations
105   in the input images. These spatial exclusive biases constrain each latent to bind to non-overlapping
106   spatial regions, and the auto-encoding objective encourages the encoder to cluster spatially correlated
107   pixels. While these biases facilitate the disentangling of spatial factors, they restrict the ability to
      disentangle non-spatial factors like attributes.

      3.1    U NIFIED F RAMEWORK FOR LEARNING DISENTANGLED REPRESENTATION
145
      Disentangled representation learning aims to represent an image x ∈ RH×W ×C into a set of K
146   latent representations z = {zi }K                              d
                                       i=1 , where each latent zi ∈ R is expected to capture independent
147   factors of variation. Previous approaches achieve this goal by utilizing specific assumptions about
148   the latent representations, such as statistical independence (Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018)
149   or spatial exclusiveness (Greff et al., 2019; Locatello et al., 2020) between the latent variables.
150   Such assumptions are specific to the type of factors of variation (e.g., attributes or objects) and
151   imposed by tailored architecture or regularization, making them incompatible with different types of
152   disentanglement.
153   Instead, we propose to employ the maximization of compositionality in the representation as a general
154   objective for disentanglement learning while instantiating various disentanglement structures by
155   controlling only the composition operator. To this end, we follow (Jung et al., 2024) by randomly
156   composing latent representations from two images and maximizing the likelihood of the resulting
157   composite image. Specifically, given two images x1 , x2 ∼ p(x) and their representations z1 , z2 ∈
158   RK×d , respectively, we produce their composite representation zc by some composition operator.
159   Then, we decode zc into a composite image xc and maximize its likelihood p(xc ) to ensure the

      production of realistic images by:

173   3.2   M IXING S TRATEGY FOR REFLECTING THE COMPOSITIONAL STRUCTURE
174
175
      The mixing strategy is defined to produce a random composition between two sequences of latent
      representations: z1 , z2 . It is important to note that not all random compositions result in valid
176
      outcomes. For instance, when we mix the ground-truth factors of face attributes, the composition
177
      having two noses becomes an invalid composition. This is because ground-truth factors follow a
178   certain structure to be composed into a complete image, which we refer to as the compositional
179   structure of factors of variation. Therefore, we characterize the compositional structure of each
180   factor of variation, and derive a corresponding mixing strategy. We start by defining disentangled
181   representation, following (Roth et al., 2023).
182
183   Definition 1 (Disentanglement with a factorized support) Let us denote the support of p(x) as
184   S(p(x)) = {x|p(x) > 0}. Given a sequence of random vectors z = {zi }K
                                                                          i=1 , z is disentangled with
                                                                                          def
185   a factorized support if S(p(z)) = S(p(z1 )) × S(p(z2 )) × · · · × S(p(zK )) = S × (p(z)), where ×
186   denotes Cartesian product.
187
188   Note the factorized support implies that for any composition of zi independently encoded from
189   multiple images, there must exist some real image x represented by z, aligning with our formulation.
190
      To achieve the disentangled representation, following the definition, we design mixing strategies
      that achieve qθ (z|x) that the aggregate distribution q̄θ (z) = Ex [qθ (z|x)] has factorized support:
191
      S(q̄θ (z)) = S × (q̄θ (z)). While the factorized support in the definition implies independent sampling
192
      of zi , we theoretically and empirically show that mixing between two images and K images is equiv-
193   alent (see Appendix A.2). We now illustrate how we derive a mixing strategy between two images
194   based on the specific compositional structure of factors for attribute and object disentanglement.
195
196   Mixing Strategy for Attribute Disentanglement In attribute disentanglement, it is typically
197   assumed that each scene is composed of K unique factors. For example, a human face consists of
198   fixed set of features such as eyes, a nose, a mouth, and ears, with each factor being distinctive and
199   included only once. It indicates that our mixing strategy should guarantee mutual exclusiveness
200   in mixing z1 , z2 to ensure the resulting zc always contains K distinct factors. From definition 1,
201   this condition translates into the mutual exclusiveness on support between latent variables, i.e.,
202
      S(p(zi )) ̸= S(p(zj )), ∀i ̸= j. Based on this compositional structure for attribute disentanglement,
203
      we design a corresponding mixing strategy between two images x1 , x2 by randomly selecting each
      latent zi exclusively from either z1i or z2i (see Figure 1 (a) above), i.e., each latent zi is drawn from
204
      one of the two, but never from both.
205
206   Specifically, let I S be a randomly sampled subset of the index set I = {1, . . . , K}. The mixing
207   strategy πattr for attribute disentanglement is defined as:
208
                               πattr (z1 , z2 ) = {z1j |j ∈ I S } ∪ {z2j |j ∈ I − I S }                      (2)

213   independence in the latent space and minimizes the KL divergence between the factorized posterior
214   (the distribution of the randomly mixed samples) and the aggregated posterior (the distribution of the
215   original samples). While effective for disentangling statistically independent factors of variation, this
      objective is inherently limited to such attribute factors, making it non-trivial to extend to disentangling

      Compositional Consistency Loss In addition to maximizing the likelihood p(xc ), we encourage
271   computational consistency between zc and ẑc = Eθ (Dϕ (zc )) to avoid generating realistic images
272   regardless of the given zc . A straightforward way to promote compositional consistency is to minimize
273   the cosine distance between zc and the inverted latent ẑc = Eθ (Dϕ (zc )). However, our empirical
274   observations reveal that this direct minimization alone is insufficient to prevent misalignment between
275   xc and zc . In practice, we find that z from all of the images tend to cluster closely together in the
276   latent space, when directly minimizing cosine distance between zc and ẑc . This clustering causes
277   the distance between ẑc and zc to remain small, even when the generated composite image xc does

279

      Implementation Details We use the same encoder and decoder architectures as the baselines (Yang
305   et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2024) for a fair comparison. Following the state-of-the-art methods in
306   attribute (Yang et al., 2023) and object (Jiang et al., 2023) disentanglements, we employ a pre-
307   trained VAE (Rombach et al., 2022) to represent an image as a latent feature and a latent diffusion
308   model (Rombach et al., 2022) for the decoder Dϕ . Since the diffusion decoder operates on VAE
309   features, we design image encoder to take VAE features as an input. When generating the image xc
310   from zc , we iteratively decode images using only a few steps (1 to 4 steps) following DDIM (Song
311   et al., 2020) to efficiently reduce the costly iterative decoding process. As back-propagating the
312   gradients through all of the denoising step is often computationally prohibitive, we follow recent
313
      work in diffusion-based optimization (Clark et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023) and truncate the
      gradient at the last iteration of decoding. Also, to ensure reliable image generation via few-step
314
      decoding, we use a v-prediction objective when training the diffusion model (Salimans & Ho, 2022).
315
      For the generative prior Gψ , we train an unconditional diffusion model on each training dataset from
316   scratch. More implementation details can be found in the Appendix A.4.

      4.1   ATTRIBUTE D ISENTANGLEMENT
319
320   Datasets We evaluate our method on three standard datasets in disentangled representation learning.
321   Shapes3D (Kim & Mnih, 2018) consists of 3D shapes with 6 ground truth factors. Cars3D (Reed

      Evaluation Metrics We use two evaluation metrics: the FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018) score
355
      and the DCI (Eastwood & Williams, 2018) metric. The FactorVAE score measures disentanglement
356
      using majority vote classifiers trained to predict the changing ground-truth factor. The DCI metric
357   quantifies disentanglement by assessing each dimension’s dominance in predicting each attribute.
358   Since our method induces a vector-wise disentanglement, we perform PCA as post-processing on the
359   representation before evaluation, following (Du et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023).
360
361   Baselines We compare our method with state-of-the-art baselines: (1) VAE-based methods, includ-
362   ing FactorVAE Kim & Mnih (2018) and β-TCVAE Chen et al. (2018), (2) GAN-based methods,
363   including InfoGAN-CR Lin et al. (2020b), GANspace (GS) Härkönen et al. (2020), LatentDiscovery
364   (LD) Voynov & Babenko (2020), and DisCo Ren et al. (2022), and (3) the diffusion-based model
365   DisDiff Yang et al. (2023). We mostly follow the experimental settings in DisDiff and use the same
366   encoder and diffusion decoder architecture as DisDiff.

368   Main Results We first report the comparison results of our method with baselines for attribute
369   disentanglement in Table 1. Our method outperforms all baselines on the Shapes3D and MPI3D
370   datasets by a clear margin, achieving 8% higher FactorVAE scores and 15.7% to 21.4% higher DCI
371   metrics compared to the second-best baselines. For the Cars3D dataset, our method achieves the best
372   DCI metric. Notably, on Shapes3D and MPI3D datasets, our method outperforms the state-of-the-art
373   baseline DisDiff (Yang et al., 2023) with substantial margin. This indicates the effectiveness of our
374
      objective in directly enforcing the support factorization between latent representations via our mixing
      strategy for disentangling factors, compared to using an approximate measure such as the upper
375
      bound of mutual information between latents.

      utilizes random mixing of representations. We hypothesize that our method benefits from flexible

396   Datasets We evaluate our method for object disentanglement on three multi-object datasets.
397
      CLEVR-Easy (Singh et al., 2022b) contains images with 2-3 objects in different colors, shapes,
      and positions. CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017) consists of images containing 3-10 objects, further
398

402
      Evaluation Protocol We evaluate the quality of object representations through an object property
403   prediction task, following (Jiang et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2024). For each property, we train a
404   network to predict the property based on frozen object representations. Correspondences between the
405   each representation and GT objects are determined through Hungarian matching using masks. For
406   baselines, slot-attention masks are used for matching. In contrast, as our method does not produce
407   masks, we identify the corresponding region of the object representation by averaging the differences
408   in output images when we compose each representation with other representations. For the classifier,
409   we employ a 2-layer MLP with a hidden dimension of 256. We report accuracy for categorical
410   properties and mean squared error (MSE) for continuous properties.
411
412   Baselines We compare our method with object-centric learning methods leveraging slot-attention:
413   SA (Locatello et al., 2020) and SLASH (Kim et al., 2023). Also, we compare our method against
414
      state-of-the-art methods using the diffusion decoders: LSD (Jiang et al., 2023) and L2C (Jung et al.,
      2024). It’s worth noting that ours does not employ slot attention or any kinds of spatial-exclusiveness
415
      biases. For a fair comparison, we employ the same encoder architecture across all baselines including
416
      ours, and all diffusion-based methods share the same decoder.

      p(xc |zc ), it can be achieved by local encoding and decoding instead of maximizing p(x). Overall,

524   5    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   While our method aims to identify underlying factors of variations by compositionality within the
527   representation, discovered factor may not exactly aligned to ground-truth factors. As data may not be
528   decomposed in a unique way, it’s challenging to discover the exact decomposition of data using our
529   method. In this work, our framework demonstrates how to uncover the general factors of variation,
530   focusing on the representative examples in the field (e.g., attributes and objects.). For future work, we
531   will further explore nuanced and intricate factors of variation within the data.
532
533   6    C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we introduced a unified framework for disentangled representation learning that is
536   compatible with both attribute and object disentanglement. We formulate disentangled representation
537   learning as the process of maximizing compositionality within the representation, enabling both
538   attribute and object disentanglement by controlling only the composition operator. Although compati-
539   ble with both attribute and object disentanglement, our method achieved competitive performance
      against strong baselines in each domain.

709   models (Burgess et al., 2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018) demonstrates that controlling
710   the importance of total correlation between latent dimensions hidden in the ELBO bounds encour-
711
      ages to disentangle independent factors. Empowered with enhanced generative models such as
      GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), (Chen et al., 2016; Lin et al.,
712
      2020b) optimizes the mutual information between latents and generated images by GANs, and (Ren
713
      et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023) proposed to optimize contrastive loss (Oord et al., 2018) or mutual
714   information between the latents using pretrained GANs and diffusion model, respectively. Such
715   information-theoretic approaches have shown promising disentangling capabilities, but it becomes
716   challenging when a scene does not consist of fixed combination of factors, especially when there
717   exists repeated appearances of the same factors, as seen in object-centric scenes.
718
719   Object-Centric Learning Built on the observation that each pixel in a scene must correspond
720   exclusively to an unique object, the spatial-exclusive mechanism has been recognized as a key
721   inductive bias in object-centric learning (Burgess et al., 2019; Greff et al., 2019; Engelcke et al., 2020;
722   Locatello et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2022a). Early attempts in object-centric learning
723   employed spatial masks to compose independently decoded RGB images from each latent (Burgess
724
      et al., 2019; Greff et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2020a; Engelcke et al., 2020). In addition to the spatial-
      exclusive bias, iterative refinement of each latent representation gradually improves the initially
725
      inaccurate spatial association between each latent and the pixels of the image (Greff et al., 2019). In
726
      slot attention (Locatello et al., 2020), each latent (slot), is randomly initialized and iteratively refined
727   by a dot-product attention mechanism normalized over the slots. This mechanism induces competition
728   between the slots to bind to spatial locations in the scene. Empowered by strong generative models
729   combined with Slot Attention, recent studies (Singh et al., 2022a; Jiang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023;
730   Jung et al., 2024) have demonstrated remarkable performance in unsupervised object discovery on
731   complex real-world datasets. While these architectural biases excel at object disentanglement, strong
732   assumption on spatial exclusiveness limits their applicability to disentangling non-spatial exclusive
733   factors, such as attributes.

      A.5     M ATCHING T ECHNIQUE
798
799   We have developed a technique to identify the specific region corresponding to an object’s represen-
800   tation based on composed images of that representation. For a given target object representation,
801   we first random sample multiple images and encode them into object representations. For each
802   image, we then replace one object representation with the target object representation and decode
803   the mixed representations. The images generated from this composed representation may include
804   the target object if it is appropriately encoded. To determine the object region, we measure the RGB
805   variance between the generated images. Additionally, we use the original image containing the target
806
      object representation and select the region that closely matches the original. Finally, we combine two
      metrics—the variance and the distance to the original image—to accurately specify the region.

      A.7     U NSUPERVISED S EGMENTATION
883
884   In this secntion, we additionally measured unsupervised segmentation performance of pretrained
885   encoders. Unlike slot-attention-based methods, our method does not have a built-in mechanism
886   to directly express group memberships between pixels. Therefore, we trained a Spatial Broadcast
887   Decoder (Watters et al., 2019) on top of the frozen latent representations to predict explicit object
888   masks for each latent representation. We train Spatial Broadcast Decoder with a reconstruction
889   loss to recover the original image from frozen latents in an unsupervised manner, and it requires
890   minimal training costs as the encoder remains frozen and the decoder is shallow (See details in
891
      Table 17). We trained the decoder for 30k iterations with learning rate of 1e-3. After training Spatial
      Broadcast Decoder, we extract explicit object masks for each latent and evaluate our method against
892
      two strongest baselines in slot-attention-based works, LSD and L2C, on CLEVR and CLEVRTex
893
      datasets. For a fair comparison, we evaluate the baselines using both slot-attention mask and object
894   masks obtained by training a Spatial Broadcast Decoder on their frozen slot representations. The
895   results are reported in Table 15, Table 16 and Figure 5.
896
897
      On the CLEVR dataset, our method achieved the best mIoU and mBO scores, along with comparable
      FG-ARI. A high FG-ARI indicates that each mask captures complete objects, confirming the effec-
898
      tiveness of our method in object disentanglement. However, we observed that the background is often
899
      split across multiple latents. This occurs because the constant backgrounds in CLEVR do not affect
900   compositional generation and therefore avoid penalties from the compositional loss. As constant
901   backgrounds carry null information, this does not impact the quality of object representations. On the
902   CLEVRTex dataset, our method outperformed both LSD and L2C across all three metrics. As shown
903   in Figure 5, our method consistently encodes complete objects into distinct latents, whereas LSD and
904   L2C frequently split objects across multiple latents. This explains the high FG-ARI achieved by our
905   method and verifies its superior object disentanglement. Additionally, unlike CLEVR, as CLEVRTex
906   has various background colors, our model successfully encodes all of the background information
907   into a single latent.
908   Together, the experiments on unsupervised segmentation also confirm that our method achieves
909   robust object-wise disentanglement. It is notable that our method outperforms baselines in object
910   segmentation without relying on spatial clustering architectures such as slot attention. This highlights
911   the strength of our approach.

       A.8     A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTS ON C OMPLEX DATASET
973
974    To explore scalability of our method, we additionally conduct experiments on CelebA-HQ for attribute
975    disentanglement and MultiShapeNet (MSN) (Stelzner et al., 2021) for object disentanglement,
976    respectively.
977
978    Attribute Disentanglement in CelebA-HQ For the CelebA-HQ dataset, we use the attribute-
979    mixing strategy to disentangle attribute factors. As CelebA-HQ has much more visual complexity
980    compares to synthetic datasets, we replace a shallow encoder used in main experiment with Resnet-18
981
       encoders. For generative prior, we leverage a off-the-shelf unconditional diffusion model 3 . We
       trained our model for 150k iterations with learning rate of 1e-4.
982
983    To verify the disentanglement of the learned representations, we swap each latent vector one by one
984    between two images and present the qualitative results in Figures 6 and Figure 7. In the third columns
985    of each figure, we observe that while the source images lack bangs, the swapped images successfully
986    generate bangs while preserving other attributes. Similarly, in the fourth and fifth columns, the facial
987
       expressions and skin tones of the target images are effectively transferred to the source images. These
       qualitative results demonstrate that our attribute-mixing strategy is capable of disentangling attribute
988
       factors, even in complex datasets like CelebA-HQ.
989
990
       Object Disentanglement in MultiShapeNet We validate our method on MSN dataset with object-
991
       wise manipulation and unsupervised segmentation. The model architecture and hyper-parameters
992    were kept the same as in the previous object disentanglement experiments. For the object-wise
993    manipulation task, we encode pairs of images into N = 5 object representations and exchange
994    random object latents between the pairs to construct composite images. As shown in Figure 8, our
995    method successfully performed object-level insertion and removal, demonstrating that each latent
996    representation distinctly captures individual objects. This confirms that our approach effectively
997    disentangles object representations within the latent space.
998    For the unsupervised segmentation task, we measure FG-ARI, mIoU, mBO on object masks following
999    common practices in object-centric literature. As our method does not have a built-in mechanism
1000   to directly express group memberships between pixels, we additionally train Spatial Broadcast
1001   Decoder (Watters et al., 2019) on the frozen latent representations to predict explicit object masks
1002   for each latent representation (please refer to Appendix A.7 for details). The results are reported in
1003   Table 18. Among the competitive slot-attention based baselines, our method achieves second-best
1004   performances across all of three metrics. The high segmentation scores of L2C are mainly due
1005   to its slot-attention-based regularization term (see Equation 8 in the L2C paper), which explicitly
1006
       encourages the slot masks to align with object shapes. Except for L2C, our method outperforms
       rests of the baselines (LSD, SLATE) across all metrics, even though ours does not employ any of a
1007
       spatial clustering mechanism like slot attention. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of our
1008
       framework in disentangling object representations in a complex dataset.
