009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the growing interest in designing truly interactive hyperparameter opti-
012            mization (HPO) methods, to date, only a few allow to include human feedback.
013            However, these methods add friction to the interactive process, rigidly requiring
014            to fully specify the user input as prior distribution ex ante and often imposing
015            additional constraints on the optimization framework. This hinders the flexible
016
               incorporation of expertise and valuable knowledge of domain experts, which might
               provide partial feedback at any time during optimization. To overcome these limi-
017
               tations, we introduce a novel Bayesian optimization approach leveraging tractable
018
               probabilistic models named probabilistic circuits (PCs) as surrogate model. PCs
019            encode a tractable joint distribution over the hybrid hyperparameter space and
020            evaluation scores, and enable exact conditional inference and sampling, allowing
021            users to provide valuable insights interactively and generate configurations adher-
022            ing to their feedback. We demonstrate the benefits of the resulting interactive HPO
023            through an extensive empirical evaluation of diverse benchmarks, including the
024            challenging setting of neural architecture search.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
      Hyperparameters crucially influence the performance of machine learning (ML) algorithms and must
029
      be set carefully to fully unleash the algorithm’s potential (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Hutter et al., 2013;
030
      Probst et al., 2019). Manually finding good hyperparameters is a tedious and costly task. Thus, various
031   approaches have been proposed to automatize this process which is referred to as hyperparameter
032   optimization (HPO) (Bischl et al., 2023). Generally, HPO is framed as optimizing an expensive black-
033   box function since the true functional form of the objective is commonly unknown, and the evaluation
034   of hyperparameter configurations is costly, as it requires training ML models several times. Given the
035   rise of deep learning, there is also a growing interest in optimizing the hyperparameters defining the
036   architecture of neural models, commonly referred to as neural architecture search (NAS). The main
037   goal in NAS and HPO is to efficiently traverse the search space to find good configurations quickly
038   and avoid unpromising regions. Bayesian optimization (BO) methods have proven to be sample
039
      efficient and converge on good configurations quickly. They exploit knowledge about previously
      evaluated configurations by learning a surrogate model at each iteration to approximate the optimized
040
      unknown objective function (Hutter et al., 2011; Falkner et al., 2018). A selection policy employs the
041
      learned surrogate to determine the next configuration to be evaluated (Garnett, 2023; Wang et al.,
042   2022). Selection policies aim to balance the exploitation of promising regions of the search space
043   with the exploration of undiscovered ones. Prominent policies optimize an acquisition function
044   assessing the utility of each configuration in terms of the mentioned trade-off (Hutter et al., 2011;
045   Hvarfner et al., 2022) or employ sampling strategies that frame the selection of the next configuration
046   as a multi-armed bandit problem and maximize a reward provided by the surrogate (Shahriari et al.,
047   2016; Wang et al., 2022).
048   Although the recent advancements in HPO and NAS could facilitate the design and optimization of
049   ML models for non-experts, in most of the cases, hyperparameters are still tuned manually (Bouthillier
050   & Varoquaux, 2020) and the majority of cutting-edge neural architectures, e.g., transformers (Vaswani
051   et al., 2017), are derived by hand. Given that many ML practitioners perform hyperparameter tuning
052   purely based on their knowledge, experience, and intuition, integrating this valuable knowledge
053   to guide HPO algorithms during optimization is of high value since it can substantially foster
      the search and mitigate its cost. For example, in Fig. 1 (Left), three hyperparameters of the

      approaches are valid and principled ways to guide an HPO task, their weighting schemes limit the set

092   To overcome the above limitations and to integrate user feedback in HPO and NAS more flexibly,
093   making the optimization truly interactive, we introduce I NTERACTIVE BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
094   VIA H YPERPARAMETER P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS (IBO-HPC).1 This novel BO method provides
095   an elegant and flexible mechanism to incorporate user knowledge at any time during optimization
096   without relying on weighting schemes reshaping an acquisition function. Instead, we derive a selection
097   policy that accurately reflects user beliefs by allowing users to directly condition the surrogate on
098   those beliefs. Conditions can be specific values or distributions representing users’ uncertainties over
099
      values of a subset of hyperparameters. Besides this natural incorporation of user knowledge, we
      introduce a novel, purely data-driven selection policy which suggests new configuration candidates
100
      leveraging conditional sampling and avoiding an additional inner loop optimization of an acquisition
101
      function. We achieve these benefits by employing as a surrogate a fairly recent tractable probabilistic
102   model called probabilistic circuits (PCs) (Choi et al., 2020). In contrast with other common choices
103   in HPO and NAS such as Gaussian processes (GPs) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) and random
104   forests (RFs) (Breiman, 2001), PCs compactly model a joint distribution. PCs allow the tractable

      enables direct incorporation of user knowledge into the selection policy at any time s.t. the user

      2   R ELATED W ORK
123
      Bayesian optimization (BO) is an approach to optimize an unknown and expensive black-box
124
      function and thus is a natural choice for tackling HPO tasks. BO methods sequentially update,
125
      based on observations, a surrogate model that approximates the unknown objective function and
126   captures dependencies between configurations from the search space and the evaluation function’s
127   values. In a selection policy, the surrogate is used to choose only promising configurations evaluated
128   next (Hutter et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; Mockus, 1975; Shahriari et al., 2016). Common choices
129   for surrogate models are Gaussian processes (GPs) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) or random forests
130   (RFs) (Breiman, 2001).
131
      Due to the rise of deep learning, neural architecture search (NAS) has become increasingly relevant.
132   Besides BO, different approaches based on local search (Den Ottelander et al., 2021), reinforcement
133   learning (Pham et al., 2018; Zoph & Le, 2017), and gradient descent (Liu et al., 2019) have been
134   proposed. See White et al. (2023) for a comprehensive survey on NAS. To foster the development
135   and fair comparison of HPO and NAS algorithms, different benchmarks have been proposed. These
136   benchmarks define a search space over architectures and hyperparameters and train all candidates
137   to provide several quantities such as validation and test accuracy. Ying et al. (2019) and Dong &
138   Yang (2020) introduce such benchmarks for NAS (NAS-Bench-101/201) while Bansal et al. (2022)
139   introduces a benchmark for joint optimization of hyperparameters and neural architectures (JAHS).
140   Leveraging previous HPO runs to increase the efficiency of HPO on related problems has been
141   considered in several works under the umbrella of hyperparameter transfer learning (HTL). Prominent
142   approaches perform HTL by projecting objective responses of all runs to a common response surface,
143   via learning a common representation of HPO tasks or by pruning the search space based on previous
144   tasks (Yogatama & Mann, 2014; Wistuba et al., 2015; Perrone et al., 2018; Vanschoren, 2018; Salinas
145   et al., 2020; Horváth et al., 2021).
146   With the goal of making HPO more trustworthy, several works have introduced methods to provide
147   explanations in HPO (Hutter et al., 2014; Moosbauer et al., 2021; Watanabe et al., 2023; Segel et al.,
148   2023), while involving feedback during search has received little attention. Hvarfner et al. (2022)
149   allow users to provide prior beliefs via prior distributions over the search space. The provided user
150   prior is used to reshape the acquisition function when selecting new configurations, thus, favoring
151   configurations that received a high likelihood in the prior. Mallik et al. (2023) propose a similar
152
      mechanism to incorporate user knowledge in multi-fidelity optimization. However, such approaches
      assume an ex ante full specification of the priors, often with additional constraints such as requiring
153
      invertible priors (Ramachandran et al., 2020) or a specific acquisition function (Souza et al., 2021).
154
      As illustrated in Sec. 1, the mentioned approaches have several drawbacks in reflecting given user
155   knowledge well when selecting new configurations. We believe that truly interactive HPO methods
156   should not assume ex ante specification of user knowledge and should reflect user knowledge properly.

161   We now present IBO-HPC, which provides a flexible way to interact with the optimization process
      via probabilistic queries. This way, user knowledge is decoupled from shaping acquisition functions

162
      while reflecting given knowledge in the selection policy as specified by the user. In this section, we
163   briefly revise BO as a general framework for HPO before introducing our definition of a feedback
164   adhering interactive policy. Next, we introduce HPCs and present the concrete instantiation of our
165   interactive optimization method. For completeness, we start with a general formal definition of the
166   HPO problem (Kohavi & John, 1995; Hutter et al., 2019).
167   Definition 1 (Hyperparameter optimization). Given hyperparameters H = {H1 , . . . , Hn } with
168   associated domains H1 , . . . , Hn , and a set of problem instances X , we define a search space
169   Θ = H1 × · · · × Hn . For a given problem instance x ∈ X and evaluation function f : Θ × X → R,
170   hyperparameter optimization aims to solve θ ∗ = arg minθ∈Θ f (θ; x).
171
172   3.1   I NTERACTIVITY IN BAYESIAN HPO
173
174   Bayesian Optimization. BO aims to optimize a black-box objective function f : Θ → R which is
175   costly to evaluate, i.e., to find the input θ ∗ ∈ arg minθ∈Θ f (θ) (Shahriari et al., 2016). BO typically
176   tackles such problem in sequential steps, leveraging two key ingredients: a probabilistic surrogate
177
      model and a selection policy determining the next θ ′ to be evaluated. Given a set D n of observations
      that correspond to the configurations with associated evaluations (θj , f (θj ))j=1...n , the surrogate
178
      s ∈ S aims to induce a distribution over functions p(f |D n ). The selection policy uses s to select the
179
      next θ ′ ∈ Θ s.t. it achieves a good exploration-exploitation trade-off. Prominent selection policies
180   optimize an acquisition function a : Θ × S → R, such as expected improvement (Jones et al., 1998),
181   that estimates the utility of an evaluation at an arbitrary point θ ∈ Θ under a surrogate s ∈ S. The
182   configuration θ ′ , evaluated next, is selected by optimizing a. A popular alternative to obtain the next
183   configuration θ ′ is Thompson sampling (Wang et al., 2022). The obtained tuple (θ ′ , f (θ ′ )) is added
184   to D n and used to update the surrogate model for the next iteration. This process is repeated until
185   convergence.
186   An interactive BO method should be capable of incorporating, at any time, the knowledge provided
187   by users; also, the selection policy should reflect the provided knowledge as specified by the user.
188   Consequently, we formalize the concept of an interactive selection policy, or interactive policy for
189   short, that adheres to these requirements.
190   Definition 2 (Interactive Policy). An interactive policy is a function p : S × Θ × K → P(Θ)
191   mapping from the set of surrogates S and search space Θ to the set of all distributions P(Θ) over
192   the search space Θ while accepting user knowledge K ∈ K.
193
194   Note that the collection of user knowledge K and the set of surrogates S are left unspecified to keep
195   Def. 2 rather general. Since Def. 2 can be trivially achieved, e.g. by ignoring user knowledge, we
196
      introduce feedback adhering interactive policies that guarantee that (i) user knowledge affects the
      policy’s outcome and (ii) user knowledge is reflected in the interactive policy as specified.
197
198   Definition 3 (Feedback Adhering Interactive Policy). Given user knowledge K ∈ K and surrogate
199   st ∈ S at iteration t, an interactive policy p is called efficacious if p(Θ, st , K) ̸= p(Θ, st , ∅) where
200
      ∅ indicates that p is applied without user knowledge. If further K is Rprovided as a distribution
201
      q(Ĥ) over Ĥ ⊂ H, we call p feedback adhering if it is efficacious and H\Ĥ p(Θ, st , K) = q(Ĥ)
202   holds, i.e., the distribution over Ĥ induced by the selection policy equals the prior q(Ĥ) in the next
203   iteration.
204
205
      In Def. 3, the first condition ensures that the user knowledge has an effect on the sampling policy.
      The second condition ensures that in the first iteration, after that a user provides a distribution over a
206
      subset of hyperparameters, the values sampled for the specified hyperparameters follow exactly the
207
      distribution q given by the user. Note that user knowledge could also be misleading; thus, Def. 3
208   does not require user knowledge to have exclusively positive effects. Equipped with Def. 2 and 3, we
209   now introduce IBO-HPC that adheres to both definitions.

374   We now provide an extensive empirical evaluation of IBO-HPC and aim to answer the following
375   research questions: (Q1) Can IBO-PC compete with prominent baseline HPO algorithms? (Q2)

      Experimental Setup. We compare IBO-HPC against seven diverse competitors: local search
379   (LS) (White et al., 2020), BO with random forest (RF) surrogate (Head et al.), and SMAC (Hutter
380   et al., 2011) as standard HPO methods. Furthermore, we used random search (RS) (Bergstra &
381   Bengio, 2012) with user priors, BOPrO (Souza et al., 2021), πBO (Hvarfner et al., 2022) and
382   Priorband (Mallik et al., 2023) which allow ex ante incorporation of user knowledge. Since Priorband
383   is a multi-fidelity method, we reserved the number of epochs as the fidelity in each benchmark. For
384   all non-multi-fidelity methods, set it to the highest possible value. For our evaluation, we employ
385   four real-world benchmarks, i.e., NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019) and NAS-Bench-201 (Dong &
386   Yang, 2020) as tabular NAS benchmarks, JAHS (Bansal et al., 2022) as a surrogate benchmark for
387
      joint architecture and hyperparameter search, as well as HPO-B (Arango et al., 2021) containing a
      large collection of challenging OpenML tasks. We evaluate IBO-HPC on seven diverse tasks and five
388
      different search spaces covering continuous, discrete, and mixed spaces. Our evaluation considers the
389
      optimization of neural architectures and tree-based models on image and tabular data. For NAS (and
390   NAS+HPO), we consider CIFAR-10 (JAHS, NAS-Bench-101, NAS-Bench-201), Fashion-MNIST
391   (JAHS), and Colorectal Histology (JAHS). To make the optimization problem more challenging, we
392   extended the search space definition of JAHS (see App. D). This is possible since JAHS is a surrogate
393   benchmark. Additionally, we consider optimizing an XGBoost and random forest classifier on the
394   credit-g dataset, taken from the HPO-B benchmark. As usual, we optimize the validation accuracy as
395   our objective. All algorithms were repeated on 500 different seeds for a maximum of 2000 iterations
396   (100 iterations for HPO-B). We report the mean test error against computational cost and provide
397   standard error to quantify uncertainty. The computational costs are reported as the accumulated
398
      wall-clock time of training and evaluation of each sampled configuration, where the benchmarks
      provide wall-clock times for each configuration. All experiments were run on DGX-A100 machines.
399
400   User Interactions. For the experiments, beneficial and misleading user interactions have been defined
401   as user priors for each benchmark. To define priors, we randomly sampled 10k configurations and
402   kept the best/worst performing ones, denoted as h+ and h− , respectively. To demonstrate that user
403   priors over a few hyperparameters are enough to improve the performance of IBO-HPC significantly,
404   we defined beneficial interactions by selecting a small subset of hyperparameters Ĥ ⊂ H. Then,
405   we defined a prior over each H ∈ Ĥ s.t. the probability of sampling the value of H given in h+ ,
406   denoted by h+ [H], is 1000 times higher than sampling a different value than h+ [H]. For misleading
407   interaction, Ĥ was chosen to be large to demonstrate that IBO-HPC recovers even if a large amount
408   of misleading information is provided. We then defined priors over Ĥ as for beneficial interactions;
409   however, this time the probability to sample h− [H] is 1000 times higher than for other values for
410   each H ∈ Ĥ. The priors were chosen to be rather strong since, as emphasized in Sec. 1, the stronger
411   the prior, the better πBO and BOPrO reflect user knowledge in their selection policy. Striving for a
412   fair comparison, we opted for such strong priors. Further, we aimed to show that IBO-HPC reliably
413
      recovers from receiving large amounts of strongly misleading knowledge. Sometimes, it is easier for
      users to specify a concrete value for certain hyperparameters instead of defining a distribution. Thus,
414
      we also conducted experiments with priors defined as a point mass. See App. D for further details.

418   4.1   (Q1) IBO-HPC IS C OMPETITIVE IN HPO & NAS
419
420   To demonstrate the effectiveness of IBO-HPC, we ran IBO-HPC on all tasks without user interaction.
421   We compared its performance against two strong BO baselines and LS. Fig. 2 shows that the
422   performance of IBO-HPC without user interaction is competitive to or outperforms BO baselines in
423
      4/5 tasks across the NAS-101/201 and JAHS benchmarks. We obtained similar results on HPO-B
      (see App. D.3). These results show that IBO-HPC performs well in complex and realistic settings.
424
      Also, it underlines that HPCs accurately capture characteristics of the objective function and that our
425
      sampling-based selection policy reliably identifies good configurations. Besides the quality of the
426   final solution, we also observe that IBO-HPC converges at rates similar to those of the baselines. The
427   only exception, where SMAC and LS achieve better results than IBO-HPC, is NAS-101. While LS is
428   known to be a strong baseline for NAS (White et al., 2020; Den Ottelander et al., 2021), we posit that
429   SMAC’s more complex selection policy is particularly effective in handling sparse search spaces like
430   NAS-101, which comes with a much sparser representation than NAS-201 and JAHS. To summarize,
431   we state that IBO-HPC is competitive with existing strong BO baselines when no interaction takes
      place, and answer (Q1) affirmatively.

460
461   We now demonstrate that IBO-HPC successfully handles various kinds of user knowledge (point
462   values and distributions), analyze the benefits of user knowledge w.r.t. convergence speed, and
463   demonstrate IBO-HPC’s recovery from misleading beliefs. Therefore, different beneficial and
464   misleading user beliefs about hyperparameters for all benchmarks were defined (details in App. D).
465   Beneficial Interactions. Fig. 2 shows a clear positive effect of providing beneficial user beliefs
466   (i.e. distribution or fixing hyperparameters) to IBO-HPC across all tasks. This holds for very early
467   interactions (after 5 iterations;  and ) and later interactions (after 15 iterations; ). Remarkably,
468   we observed a clear benefit in terms of convergence speed and improvement in solution quality,
469   especially for more complex search spaces. Besides outperforming strong HPO baselines incapable
470   of incorporating user knowledge, IBO-HPC is competitive to or outperforms πBO, Priorband and
471
      BOPrO, which assume user priors to given ex ante. These results show that IBO-HPC’s policy reflects
      user beliefs accurately and leverages provided knowledge effectively by sampling configurations
472
      that are promising according to both, user belief and the surrogate HPC. App. D provides additional
473
      experiments on HPO-B showing similar results.
474

476   process; thus, an interactive HPO algorithm should recover from such misleading interactions and
477
      allow users to correct their initial beliefs. We demonstrate that IBO-HPC recovers from misleading
      user knowledge by deliberately providing IBO-HPC with known sub-optimal values for a large number
478
      of hyperparameters to ensure a significant negative effect on the optimization process (see App. D for
479

484   their beliefs when no improvement is obtained, we demonstrate that IBO-HPC successfully handles

            5                              C ONCLUSION
528
529
530       We introduced a novel definition of interactive BO policies and an interactive BO method named
531       IBO-HPC that leverages the flexible inference of probabilistic circuits to flexibly incorporate user
532       beliefs. With no user knowledge, IBO-HPC is competitive with strong baselines and it outperforms
533       competitors when knowledge is available. Also, it reliably recovers from misleading user beliefs and
534       converges significantly faster when provided with valuable user knowledge, thus, saving resources.
535        Limitations & Future Work. Whereas IBO-HPC enables flexible interactive BO, the necessity to
536        retrain the surrogate model at each iteration remains. Thus, prospective directions could explore
537        methods of continual learning (Mundt et al., 2023) to increase the overall efficiency and knowledge
538        reuse over different HPO and NAS settings. Moreover, to model hybrid domains, we relied on
539        HPCs employing piecewise polynomials that might not be sufficient to model complex distributions.
           Therefore, more sophisticated alternatives could further improve performance.

      B.4       ACCURACY OF IBO-HPC’ S S ELECTION P OLICY
942
943   Here, we briefly discuss the accuracy of IBO-HPC’s policy in selecting new configurations for
944   evaluation based on the obtained data (see Eq. 2). Note that the sampling from the distribution
945   provided in Eq. 2 is accurate if (1) the s represents the data D accurately and (2) sampling from s and
946   the prior q is unbiased (i.e., samples are drawn according to the underlying distribution). Let us start
947   with (1). Since we employ LearnSPN (Gens & Domingos, 2013) to obtain s (a PC in form of SPN), s
948   will locally maximize the log-likelihood of the training data (i.e., the configuration-evaluation pairs
949   obtained). This means that there is no other SPN in the space of the learnable SPNs via LearnSPN
950   that achieves a better log-likelihood given the data.2 Hence, as long as the ground truth distribution p
951
      (or a good approximation of it) is representable by an SPN, we can recover p with arbitrarily small
      error with iterations T → ∞.
952
953   Looking at (2), we sample from two distributions when selecting a new configuration. First, we
954   sample from the prior q, then from the conditional s(H \ Ĥ|Ĥ = h, F = f ∗ ) where h ∼ q.
955   Assuming q is a tractable distribution (e.g., a parametric one such as an isotropic Gaussian), sampling
956   is immediate and not biased (i.e., performed via simple transformations such as the Box-Muller
957   transform). Note that the assumption on q being a tractable (and relatively simple) distribution can
958
      be made safely since providing highly complex distributions as user knowledge is hard to do for
959   most users. When considering sampling from the conditional s(H \ Ĥ|Ĥ = h, F = f ∗ ), it should
960
      be noted that this conditional is a valid PC again (specifically, a PC in the form of an SPN when
      obtained with LearnSPN). The model is unchanged and only evaluated differently, i.e., by providing
961
      the partial evidence at leaves and evaluating the model bottom-up first (see Choi et al. (2020)). Then,
962
      PC sampling is performed top-down by sampling from the simple categorical variables represented
963   by the sum nodes and then from the selected univariate leaves. Thus, the process is tractable (linear
964   in the circuit size) and not biased by further operations or assumptions (Choi et al., 2020). Thus, we
965   conclude that the approximation in Eq. 2 is accurate in the limit N, T → ∞.

      Since probabilistic circuits (PCs) are a key component of our method, we provide more details on
970   these models in the following. Let us first start with a rigorous definition of PCs.
971

       Definition 5. A probabilistic cricuit (PC) is a computational graph encoding a distribution over a
973    set of random variables X. It is defined as a tuple (G, ϕ) where G = (V, E) is a rooted, directed
974    acyclic graph and ϕ : V → 2X is the scope function assigning a subset of random variables to each
975    node in G. For each internal node N of G, the scope is defined as the union of scopes of its children,
976    i.e. ϕ(N) = ∪N′ ∈ch(N) . Each leaf node L computes a distribution/density over its scope ϕ(L). All
977    internal nodes of G are either a sum node SP or a product node P where each sum node computes a
978    convex combination of its children, i.e., S = N∈ch(S) wS,N N, and each product computes a product
                                 Q
979    of its children, i.e., P = N∈ch(P) N.
980
981
       With this definition at hand, we describe the tractable key operations of PCs relevant to our method in
982
       more detail.
983
984    Inference. Inference in PCs is a bottom-up procedure. To compute the probability of given evidence
985    X = x, the densities of the leaf nodes are evaluated first. This yields a density value for each leaf.
986    The leaf densities are then propagated bottom-up by computing all product/sum nodes. Eventually,
987
       the root node holds the probability/density of x. Note that typically, multiple leaf nodes correspond to
       the same random variable. Thus, if the children of a sum node have the same scope, we can interpret
988
       sum nodes as mixture models. Conversely, if the children of a product node have non-overlapping
989
       scopes, a product node can be interpreted as a product distribution of two (independent) random
990    variables. We call these two properties smoothness and decomposability. More formally, smoothness
991    means that for each sum node S ∈ V it holds that ϕ(N) = ϕ(N′ ) for N, N′ ∈ ch(S). Decomposability
992    means that for each product node P ∈ V it holds that ϕ(N) ∩ ϕ(N′ ) = ∅ for N, N′ ∈ ch(P), N ̸= N′ .
993    Hence, PCs can be interpreted as hierarchical mixture models.
994
       Marginalization. Decomposability implies that marginalization is tractable in PCs and can be
995
       done in linear time of the circuit size. This is because integrals that can be rewritten by nesting
996    single-dimensional integrals can be computed only in terms of leaf integrals, which are assumed to
997    be tractable as they follow certain distributions (e.g., Gaussian). Computing such nested integrals
998    only in terms of leaf integrals is possible because single-dimensional integrals commute with the
999    sum operation and affect only a single child of product nodes. For more details on the computational
1000   implications of decomposability, refer to (Peharz et al., 2015).
1001
       Practically, there are two ways to marginalize certain variables from the scope of a PC. One approach
1002   is structure-preserving, and marginalization is achieved by setting all leaves corresponding to the set
1003   of random variables that are supposed to be marginalized to 1. The second approach constructs a
1004   new PC representing the marginal distribution, i.e. the structure of the PC is changed. The second
1005   approach is beneficial if samples should be drawn from the marginalized PC because the sampling
1006   procedure remains the same, i.e. the PC is adopted to obtain the marginal distribution, not vice versa.
1007

       Conditioning. Computing a conditional distribution p(X1 |X2 ) = R p(X)

                                                                               X2

1009   and X1 ∩ X2 = ∅ is achieved by combining marginalization (denominator) and inference (numerator).
1010   Since inference is tractable for PCs in general and marginalization is tractable for decomposable PCs,
1011   conditioning is also tractable.
1012   Sampling. Sampling in PCs is a top-down procedure and recursively samples a sub-tree, starting at
1013                                                               P| ch(S)|
       the root. Each sum node S holds a parameter vector w s.t. i=0 wi = 1. Based on the distribution
1014   induced by w, one of the children of S is sampled as a sub-tree. By decomposability, the scope of the
1015   children of a product node is non-overlapping; thus, sampling from a product node corresponds to
1016   sampling from all its child nodes. If a leaf node is reached, a sample is obtained from the distribution
1017   at that leaf.
1018
       Learning. Learning PCs consists of two steps: Identify the structure of the PC and learn the parame-
1019
       ters of the PC. A common approach to learning both the structure and parameters is LearnSPN (Gens
1020   & Domingos, 2013). We employ LearnSPN to learn the PC after obtaining new data. The basic
1021   idea of LearnSPN is to split the data by alternating clustering (i.e., split the data along the sample
1022   dimension) and independence tests (i.e., split the data along the features dimension). In other words,
1023   the data matrix is split by rows (samples) and columns (features). Usually, rows are clustered when
1024   the independence test fails in splitting the features. Clusters correspond to sum nodes in the learned
1025   PC, while product nodes correspond to successfully passed independence tests (assessing that two
       subsets of features are statistically independent). The parameters (i.e., weights of sum nodes) are

       set proportional to the cluster sizes of clusters represented by the child nodes of a sum node. Leaf
1027   parameters are commonly defined via maximum likelihood estimation.

1206   In this section, we provide further results and ablations. Fig. 6 provides additional results on two
1207   challenging tasks of the HPO-B benchmark (search space IDs 6767, 6794; dataset ID 31). Both search
1208   spaces have more than 10 hyperparameters and the goal is to solve a classification task. IBO-HPC
1209
       outperforms the baselines or is competitive with the baselines in both cases, i.e., where feedback
       is given, and no user feedback is given. Fig 7 shows results of IBO-HPC on JAHS, NAS201, and
1210
       NAS101 where the given user feedback was either a fixed value or a distribution over configurations.
1211
       Both cases are handled well by IBO-HPC, demonstrating its flexibility. Fig. 8 provides a more detailed
1212   view of the effectiveness of IBO-HPC and its recovery mechanism. It can be seen that IBO-HPC
1213   successfully recovers from harmful user feedback in JAHS and NAS-201 ( ). Also, it can be seen
1214   that IBO-HPC handles alternating and contradictory user feedback well by leveraging information
1215   from beneficial feedback and ignoring harmful feedback ( ). In NAS-101, however, IBO-HPC is
1216   less effective in general, which can be explained by the extreme sparsity of the NAS-101 benchmark.
1217   While NAS-101 and NAS-201 are highly similar, NAS-101 uses a binary encoding of architectures,
1218   while NAS-201 uses a much denser dictionary-like representation. Although both benchmarks are
1219   highly similar, IBO-HPC performs well on NAS-201 but is not as effective on NAS-101, underlining
1220
       our explanation.
1221   Fig. 9 shows the CDF of test accuracy across the baselines and IBO-HPC. It can be seen that IBO-
1222   HPC invests more computational resources in good-performing configurations than other methods
1223   while achieving state-of-the-art or better results. In other words, IBO-HPC avoids exploration in
1224   unpromising regions of the search space. This is because IBO-HPC samples configurations from a
1225   conditional distribution where the condition is the best evaluation score obtained. Thus, exploration
1226
       is purely data-driven and focuses on regions that perform similarly to the incumbent at a particular
       iteration.
1227
1228   Fig. 10 shows the influence of the decay parameter γ in cases where harmful or misleading user
1229   knowledge was provided to IBO-HPC at an early iteration (10 in this case). It can be seen that
1230   for higher γ, IBO-HPC requires more time to recover than for smaller γ. This aligns with our
1231   expectations since a larger γ corresponds to a high likelihood of the user knowledge being used for
1232
       many iterations. In contrast, if γ is small, likely, the user knowledge is only considered for a certain
       number of iterations with high likelihood. Thus, for smaller γ IBO-HPC can recover faster.
1233
1234   Fig. 11 shows the effect of conditioning on the {0.25, 0.5, 0.75}-quantile of the obtained evaluation
1235   scores instead of the maximum evaluation score. As expected, the higher the quantile, the better
1236   the performance of IBO-HPC as we aim to maximize the objective function. Thus, conditioning on
1237   higher values guides the optimization algorithm to configurations that yield better evaluation scores.
1238   Lastly, Fig. 12 depicts the effect of changing L, i.e. the number of samples drawn from the
1239   surrogate before the surrogate is updated. We found that the sample size has no effect on the overall
1240   performance of IBO-HPC. However, for some tasks (JAHS CIFAR-10 and CO), a significant variation
1241   of convergence speed in early iterations – depending on the choice of L – was obtained. Choosing
       L = 20 seems to lead to fast and stable convergence behavior.

1460         We now provide details on the computational costs of IBO-HPC. Therefore, we analyzed the composi-

1466
             in 4/5 cases in terms of runtime. Here, we considered the time spent in updating the surrogate and

1467

       D.6    H YPERPARAMETERS OF IBO-HPC
1567
1568   IBO-HPC comes with a few hyperparameters itself, which have to be set. For our experiments, we
1569   set the number of iterations the surrogate is kept fixed L = 20, the decay value γ = 0.9. We let all
1570   methods optimize for 2000 iterations for fair comparison. Our surrogate models, i.e., PCs and the
1571   associated learning algorithm, have some hyperparameters as well. The structure learning algorithm
1572   splits use the RDC independence test and K-means clustering. The threshold to detect independencies
1573   is set to 0.3, and the minimum number of instances per leaf is adapted dynamically based on the
1574
       number of configurations tested during an optimization run.
1575
1576   D.7    H ARDWARE
1577
       We ran all our experiments on DGX-A100 machines and used 10 CPUs for each run, thus parallelizing
1578   some sub-routines (e.g. learning of PCs). We did not use any GPUs as we queried the benchmarks
1579   employed to provide the performance of configurations. The JAHS benchmark requires a relatively
1580   large RAM (> 16GB) to run smoothly as it loads large ensemble models.
