                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Vector-Quantized Generative Models (VQGMs) have emerged as powerful tools
013
               for image generation. However, the key component of VQGMs—the codebook
               of discrete tokens—is still not well understood, e.g., which tokens are critical to
014
               generate an image of a certain concept? This paper introduces Concept-Oriented
015
               Token Explanation (CORTEX), a novel approach for interpreting VQGMs by
016            identifying concept-specific token combinations. Our framework employs two
017            methods: (1) a saliency-based method that analyzes token saliency value in indi-
018            vidual images, and (2) an optimization-based method that explores the entire code-
019            book to find globally relevant tokens. Experimental results demonstrate COR-
020            TEX’s efficacy in providing clear explanations of token usage in the generative
021            process, outperforming baselines across multiple pretrained VQGMs. CORTEX
022            not only improves VQGM transparency but also enables tasks such as targeted
023            image editing, offering valuable insights into the model’s internal representations.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Vector-Quantized Image Generative Models (VQGMs) (Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021; Yu
029
      et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2023) have become powerful tools for high-quality image generation using
030   discrete latent space representations. A critical component of these models is the codebook (Esser
031   et al., 2021), which acts as a learned dictionary of visual elements. This codebook stores a finite
032   set of discrete tokens, each representing various patterns or features within an image, such as tex-
033   tures, shapes, or object parts. During the generation process, the model selects tokens from this
034   codebook to compose the final image. However, these high-dimensional tokens are difficult to in-
035   terpret, making it challenging to understand how specific concepts are represented in the generative
036   process. Moreover, not all tokens contribute equally to the generation of a particular concept (e.g.,
037   object categories or visual attributes), leading to the need for methods that can distinguish between
038
      concept-relevant and background tokens. Improving the interpretability of these tokens can provide
      deeper insights into how VQGMs represent semantic concepts, enabling more precise control over
039
      the generation process and facilitating tasks such as targeted image editing.
040
041   A straightforward approach to token interpretation may involve selecting tokens frequently appear-
042   ing in images generated for a specific concept/object (Blei et al., 2003). However, this naive method
043   often selects tokens that represent contextual or background elements, resulting in explanations clut-
044
      tered with irrelevant information. This inability to differentiate between essential and non-essential
      tokens hinders clear understanding of how the model represents specific concepts.
045
046   To address this issue, we draw on the Information Bottleneck principle (Tishby et al., 2000), which
047   focuses on compressing input data while retaining the most relevant information for a given task.
048   In the context of VQGMs, we apply this principle to identify and preserve the tokens that are most
049   informative for a specific concept, while filtering out those that contribute little to its representation.
050   In this paper, we propose CORTEX (Concept-Oriented Token Explanation), a novel framework that
051   interprets VQGMs by identifying concept-specific token combinations. CORTEX comprises two
052   methods: a saliency-based method that analyzes individual token importance for a given image and
053   an optimization-based method that explores the entire codebook to find globally relevant tokens for
      a concept. By focusing on the most critical tokens and filtering out non-essential information, COR-

054
      TEX provides clearer, more interpretable explanations of how VQGMs generate specific concepts.
055   This, in turn, enables precise image manipulation and editing based on token-level representations.
056
057   Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of CORTEX according to the pretrained VQGM,
058
      including ResNet and Vision Transformer architectures. Our saliency-based method consistently
      identifies the most relevant tokens for concept-specific image generation, while the optimization-
059
      based method allows targeted image editing by manipulating these tokens. Together, these methods
060
      significantly enhance the transparency and controllability of VQGMs, providing valuable insights
061   into the model’s internal representations and offering practical tools for downstream generative
062   tasks. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
063
064          • We propose a saliency-based method for input-dependent token explanation, which iden-
065            tifies concept-relevant tokens in generated images by leveraging an information extractor
066            based on the Information Bottleneck principle.
067          • We introduce an optimization-based method that explores the entire codebook to find
068            globally relevant tokens for concept explanation, utilizing the same information extractor
069            to optimize token combinations independent of specific inputs.
070          • Our experiments validate both token-based explanation methods, demonstrating their ef-
071            fectiveness in enhancing VQGM interpretability and enabling applications such as precise
072            image editing.

076   2.1   V ECTOR Q UANTIZED I MAGE G ENERATIVE M ODEL
077
078   Vector Quantized Image Generative Models (VQGM) (Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021; Yu
079   et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2023) generate images via decoding from discrete tokens. These models are
080   typically designed for conditional generation and are capable of creating images based on given
081
      concepts, such as text descriptions. During the image generation process, these models have three
      key parts: a codebook that stores token information, a transformer that predicts tokens based on the
082
      codebook and the concepts, and a decoder that turns tokens into images. It is worth noting that the
083
      term “concept” refers to the “input condition” in conditional generation, which guides the image
084   generation process. The codebook plays a crucial role in VQGM by quantizing continuous high-
085   dimensional visual features into discrete tokens. It is obtained through training on a large volume of
086   image data to encode a diverse set of visual elements. However, tokens in the codebook are difficult
087   to interpret directly because they represent high-dimensional features extracted from image pixels,
088   capturing complex visual patterns that are not intuitively comprehensible to humans.
089
      Let G be a VQGM model with a codebook C ∈ RK×d = [t0 , . . . , tK−1 ]⊤ , where K is the total
090   number of unique tokens, and ti ∈ Rd is a d-dimensional vector representing the token i. The code-
091   book C can be viewed as a look-up table. To generate an image, the model first uses its transformer
092   to predict a sequence of m2 tokens according to the input concept. It then looks up each token’s
093   corresponding vector in the codebook. These vectors are arranged into a 3D tensor E ∈ Rd×m×m ,
094   which we call the token-based embedding. Finally, the decoder transforms this embedding E into
095   an H × H image, where H is typically larger than m. This E is central to our analysis, as it directly
096   shows how the model uses tokens to create images.

141   3.1         OVERVIEW
142
143   We propose a novel framework for concept-specific token explanation in VQGMs, aiming to identify
144   the most relevant tokens within the discrete token space for a user-specified concept as the token-
145   based explanation. Here, a concept can refer to a certain aspect of image content, such as object
146   categories (e.g., “cat” or “dog”), visual attributes (e.g., “red” or “shiny”), or more abstract concepts
147
      (e.g., “rural scene” or “urban scene”). Our approach is inspired by the Information Bottleneck prin-
      ciple, focusing on extracting the most pertinent tokens to the concept while filtering out background
148
      information. The core of our framework is an information extractor introduced in Section 3.2 that
149
      predicts the concept of interest from token-based embedding E. It is important to note that since
150   our goal is to explain the behavior of VQGMs, all token-based embeddings E used in this study are
151   generated by the VQGM itself, rather than derived from real images.
152
153
      Recognizing that users are often interested in explanations for images generated during inference, we
      first introduce an input-dependent method based on saliency analysis in Section 3.3 to identify to-
154
      kens that are most relevant to the concept in a specific image. Furthermore, we extend this approach
155
      to a concept-level analysis by aggregating identified tokens across a diverse set of images associ-
156   ated with the concept Y . This aggregation yields a more comprehensive, yet still input-dependent,
157   view of how the model represents the concept across various instances. To complement the input-
158   dependent method, we also present an input-independent method in Section 3.4. This approach
159   identifies globally relevant token combinations for the concept by exploring the entire codebook,
160   rather than relying on sampled images. Through an optimization process, we select tokens that
161   best explain the user-specified concept Y , providing a comprehensive token-based explanation that
      considers all possible combinations within the codebook.

165   visual or semantic features that are associated with the concept to be explained.

      3.2   I NFORMATION E XTRACTOR
168
169   The Information Extractor serves as the cornerstone of our framework, designed to identify the most
170   relevant tokens for a given concept. This model operates on token-based embeddings E ∈ Rd×m×m ,
171   generating predictions related to the concept of interest Y :
172
                                                   y = fY (E),                                           (2)
173
174   where fY is a classification model specifically trained for predicting concept Y . It determines
175   whether the token-based embedding E contains the concept Y . For instance, if Y denotes a par-
176   ticular image label, then fY is a classifier capable of identifying the presence of that label in the
177   decoded image based on E. The output y represents the model’s prediction, indicating the likeli-
178   hood that E contains concept Y . The model’s architecture and training objective align closely with
179   the Information Bottleneck principle (Saxe et al., 2019; Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby, 2017). To accu-
180
      rately predict or characterize the concept Y , the model must inherently focus on tokens and token
      combinations that are most representative and relevant to Y , while minimizing their reliance on irrel-
181
      evant input tokens. This behavior mirrors the core tenets of the Information Bottleneck framework:
182
      maximizing information about the target variable while compressing the input.
183
184   Given an information extractor fY and a target concept Y , we aim to find the optimal token combi-
185   nation T ∗ that servers as the explanation of concept Y :
186                                             T ∗ = ϕ(fY , [E]),                                       (3)
187
188   where the function ϕ selects the most relevant tokens based on the trained information extractor fY ,
189   the concept Y that needs to be explained, and optionally the input embeddings E. Specifically, ϕ
190   encompasses both our saliency-based and optimization-based approaches for token selection.

230   3.4   O PTIMIZATION - BASED T OKEN E XPLANATION
231
232

      explore the entire token space. To address this limitation, we propose an optimization-based, input-
233

234

235   codebook, relying solely on the information extractor fY rather than any specific input. Since we

280   range of concepts. Consistent with prior research (Chefer et al., 2021; Binder et al., 2016; Si-

283   ify that the token combinations selected by CORTEX are indeed the most relevant to the concept
284   (label) being explained. In this experiment, the information extractor fY is trained as an image clas-
285   sifier with 1,000 ImageNet categories. To ensure the robustness of our evaluation, we employ four
286
      well-established pretrained classification models as benchmarks. The selected pretrained models in-
      clude variants of ResNet (He et al., 2016) (ResNet18 and ResNet50) and Vision Transformer (ViT)
287
      (Dosovitskiy, 2020) (ViT-B/16 and ViT-B/32). These models represent state-of-the-art approaches

      in image recognition.

      4.1.1    S YNTHETIC DATASET FOR T OKEN - BASED A NALYSIS
291
292   To elucidate the selected T ∗ from our proposed explanation method ϕ of VQGMs, we use a syn-
293   thetic data set generated by VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021), which encompasses the same categories
294   as ImageNet. This symthetic dataset allows us to directly examine how the generative model uti-
295   lizes tokens to encode concept-specific information, specifically class labels, rather than analyzing
296   real-world images. Our synthetic dataset consists of 1,000,000 training images, 300,000 validation
297   images, and 50,000 test images, evenly distributed across all ImageNet categories. This balanced
298   distribution provides 1,000, 300, and 50 images per category in the training, validation, and test sets,
299   respectively. Each generated image has 256×256 pixels, with 16×16 tokens. During the generation
300
      process, we can simultaneously obtain both the image and its token-based embedding E.

503   Vector quantization in computer vision. Vector quantization has been instrumental in advanc-
504   ing image generative models (Gray, 1984; Nasrabadi & Feng, 1988). VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord
505   et al., 2017) pioneered the use of discrete latent codes for efficient image reconstruction, overcom-
506   ing “posterior collapse” issues in VAEs. DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) extended this to text-to-
507
      image generation, while VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) and ViT-VQGAN (Yu et al., 2021) enhanced
      image quality through perceptual and adversarial objectives. In video generation, MAGVIT (Yu
508
      et al., 2023), VideoPoet (Kondratyuk et al., 2023), and LaVIT (Jin et al., 2024; 2023) applied vector
509
      quantization for spatial-temporal modeling and multimodal learning. Our work builds upon these
510   VQGMs, offering a novel approach to interpreting discrete tokens and providing insights into visual
511   information encoding and utilization.
512
513
      Vision model explainability. Traditional approaches to explaining vision models primarily fall
514   into two categories: heatmap-based methods (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Selvaraju et al., 2020;
515   Binder et al., 2016; Gandelsman et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2021), which highlight influential image
516   regions, and optimization-based methods (Nguyen et al., 2016; Erhan et al., 2009; Yosinski et al.,
517   2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Simonyan, 2013), which generate synthetic inputs to maximize specific
518   activations. While insightful, these pixel-level approaches are limited in explaining complex gener-
519   ative models like VQGMs. Our CORTEX approach extends these ideas to the token level, providing
520   concept-specific explanations of how VQGMs utilize discrete latent representations. This novel per-
521   spective offers deeper insights into the internal generative processes of VQGMs, bridging the gap
522
      between traditional explainability methods and the complexities of modern generative models.

524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we introduce CORTEX, a novel framework for interpreting VQGMs through concept-
527
      specific token analysis. Guided by the Information Bottleneck principle, CORTEX combines
528   saliency-based and optimization-based methods to extract critical token combinations while filtering
529   out non-essential tokens. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrated CORTEX’s effectiveness
530   in providing clear explanations of token combination in the generative process, significantly out-
531   performing baselines. The saliency-based method accurately identified tokens critical for specific
532   concepts, while the optimization-based method enabled targeted manipulation of these tokens to
533   achieve controlled, concept-driven modifications in generated images.
534   This elucidation of the relationship between codebook tokens and encoded concepts enhances our
535   understanding of VQGMs’ internal representations and decision-making processes. CORTEX not
536   only improves VQGM transparency but also enables precise image manipulation and editing based
537   on token-level representations. This work opens up new possibilities for fine-grained control in im-
538   age generation and editing, paving the way for more transparent and controllable generative models.
539   Future work could explore extending CORTEX to other generative architectures and investigating
      its potential in multi-modal settings.

655   A.1.1    CE: CNN- BASED E XTRACTOR
656
      The CNN-based Extractor (CE) is a convolutional neural network designed for image classifica-
657
      tion. The model comprises two main blocks, each containing four convolutional layers (conv1 1 to
658
      conv1 4 and conv2 1 to conv2 4). Each convolutional layer utilizes 512 filters with a 3 × 3 kernel
659   size, stride of 1, and padding of 1, followed by batch normalization and ReLU activation. Max
660   pooling (2 × 2 kernel, stride 2) is applied after each block. The network concludes with three fully
661   connected layers: the first transforms 512 × 4 × 4 input features to 4096 output features, the sec-
662   ond maintains 4096 features, and the final layer maps to the number of classes. Additional features
663   include batch normalization and ReLU activation after the first two fully connected layers, with
664   dropout (0.5) applied after the first fully connected layer.
665
666   A.1.2    RE: R ES N ET- BASED E XTRACTOR
667
      The ResNet-based Extractor (RE) is an advanced model incorporating residual connections and
668
      Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks. The network consists of two main layers, each containing 3
669   residual blocks. Each residual block comprises two convolutional layers (3 × 3 kernel, maintaining
670   channel size) with batch normalization and ReLU activation, a shortcut connection, and an SE block
671   for channel-wise attention. The SE block employs global average pooling followed by two fully
672   connected layers with reduction and expansion, using sigmoid activation for generating attention
673   weights. The model concludes with global average pooling to reduce spatial dimensions, followed
674   by two fully connected layers: 512 to 2048 features, and 2048 to the number of classes. Batch
675   normalization and ReLU activation are applied after the first fully connected layer, with dropout
676   (0.5) implemented.
677   Both CE and RE are designed to process input tensors with 256 channels and spatial dimensions of
678   16 × 16 tokens.

694

812   Figure 6 illustrates the pairing strategy employed in our optimization experiments. We selected 10
813   bird categories and organized them into 5 pairs, as shown in the figure. Each pair consists of two

      For each category, we utilized 50 images from the test set. In the experimental process, when
816   images from a category in the first row serve as the original images, the corresponding category
817   in the second row becomes the target label, and vice versa. For instance, in the pair (Goldfinch,
818   Water Ouzel), when Goldfinch images are being optimized, Water Ouzel serves as the target label.
819   Conversely, when Water Ouzel images are used as original images, Goldfinch becomes the target
820   label.
821
      This reciprocal design is applied consistently across all five pairs depicted in Figure 6. Every image
822   in our dataset undergoes optimization as an original image, with its paired category serving as the
