011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Referring expression comprehension (REC) involves localizing a target instance
014            based on a textual description. Recent advancements in REC have been driven by
015            large multimodal models (LMMs) like CogVLM, which achieved 92.44% accu-
016            racy on RefCOCO. However, this study questions whether existing benchmarks
017            such as RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and RefCOCOg, capture LMMs’ comprehensive
018
               capabilities. We begin with a manual examination of these benchmarks, revealing
               high labeling error rates: 14% in RefCOCO, 24% in RefCOCO+, and 5% in Re-
019
               fCOCOg, which undermines the authenticity of evaluations. We address this by
020
               excluding problematic instances and reevaluating several LMMs capable of han-
021            dling the REC task, showing significant accuracy improvements, thus highlighting
022            the impact of benchmark noise. In response, we introduce Ref-L4, a comprehen-
023            sive REC benchmark, specifically designed to evaluate modern REC models. Ref-
024            L4 is distinguished by four key features: 1) a substantial sample size with 45,341
025            annotations; 2) a diverse range of object categories with 365 distinct types and
026            varying instance scales from 30 to 3,767; 3) lengthy referring expressions averag-
027            ing 24.2 words; and 4) an extensive vocabulary comprising 22,813 unique words.
028            We evaluate a total of 24 large models on Ref-L4 and provide valuable insights.
029
               The cleaned versions of RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and RefCOCOg, as well as our
               Ref-L4 benchmark and evaluation code, will be made available to the community.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Referring expression comprehension (REC) (Nagaraja et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2024d) involves the
036   task of localizing a specific target instance based on a given textual description. The advancement
037   of REC has been significantly propelled by the superior language processing capabilities of large
038   language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Meta, 2024). This progress is particularly ev-
039
      ident in the exceptional performance of large multimodal models (LMMs) (He et al., 2024; Wang
      et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024) on well-known benchmarks such as RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016),
040
      RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016), and RefCOCOg (Mao et al., 2016). These models have demonstrated
041
      remarkable accuracy, with CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023b), for instance, achieving an impressive
042   accuracy rate of 92.44% on the RefCOCO benchmark.
043
044   This paper begins with a critical question: do existing REC benchmarks truly capture the compre-
045
      hensive capabilities of LMMs? The foundational benchmarks, RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and Ref-
      COCOg, were introduced sequentially in 2015, 2016, and 2016, respectively. In RefCOCO, the
046
      referring expressions are notably succinct, ranging from single words like “lady” and “yellow” to
047
      brief descriptions such as “far left person” and “white shirt”. RefCOCO+ intentionally excludes
048   locational prepositions commonly found in RefCOCO, favoring short yet semantically rich expres-
049   sions like “plastic cup with just ice” and “man on screen”. Conversely, RefCOCOg provides more
050   elaborate annotations, including examples such as “a table of food, with plates, a pizza, pitchers,
051   and glasses” and “a red and white checkered table with two wooden chairs”. These variations high-
052   light the evolution and complexity of referring expressions across different benchmarks, raising the
053   question of whether they can effectively assess the nuanced capabilities of modern LMMs in under-
      standing diverse linguistic inputs and associating languages with visual elements.

      Labeling Error Rates of Existing Benchmarks. To begin, we manually assess the labeling er-
090   ror rates of the validation and test sets in RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and RefCOCOg, discovering a
091   high error rate across these benchmarks. The labeling errors include, typos, misalignment between
092   referring expressions and target instances, as well as inaccurate bounding box annotations, as de-
093   picted in Section A. As illustrated in Table 1, the labeling error rates for RefCOCO, RefCOCO+,
094   and RefCOCOg are 14%, 24%, and 5%, respectively, indicating that evaluations performed on these
095   benchmarks may lack authenticity.
096   Reevaluation on RefCOCO, RefCOCO+ and RefCOCOg. In response, we manually exclude
097   the problematic instances from the validation and test sets of RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and Re-
098   fCOCOg. Subsequently, we reevaluate four LMMs capable of handling the REC task—namely
099   ONE-PEACE (Wang et al., 2023a), OFA-L (Wang et al., 2022), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), and
100   CogVLM-Grounding (Wang et al., 2023b)—on both the cleaned and original versions of these
101   datasets, as shown in Table 2. Across all models and cleaned benchmarks, we observe a significant
102   accuracy improvement, ranging from 1.57 to 3.08, compared to their performance on the original
103   versions. This demonstrates that noise in the benchmarks has impacted the models’ true capabili-
104
      ties. To support further research in the REC field, we will release the cleaned versions of RefCOCO,
      RefCOCO+, and RefCOCOg.

161   REC and Its Benchmarks. Referring Expression Comprehension (REC) (Qiao et al., 2020; Na-
      garaja et al., 2016; Zheng et al., 2022; Kazemzadeh et al., 2014; Pi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2019)

                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is a task that involves identifying a specific object within an image based on a given referring ex-
163   pression. Unlike object detection (Lin et al., 2014; Krishna et al., 2017; Shao et al., 2019; Red-
164   mon et al., 2016; Carion et al., 2020), which operates within fixed categories and a single visual
165   modality, REC necessitates understanding free-form text to locate objects of any category. Phrase
166   Grounding (Plummer et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al.,
167   2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020b) is similar but typically involves shorter phrases and
168   identifies multiple regions, whereas REC requires parsing longer expressions to pinpoint a single
169   unique region. This complexity makes REC an ideal task for evaluating emerging large multimodal
170   models. Current REC benchmarks such as RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016), RefCOCO+(Yu et al., 2016),
171
      and RefCOCOg(Mao et al., 2016) include tens of thousands of annotations but are limited by their
      short expression lengths—averaging 3.6, 3.6, and 8.4 words, respectively. Additionally, they en-
172
      compass fewer than 80 categories, lacking real-world diversity. Other REC benchmarks (Liu et al.,
173
      2019; Chen et al., 2023c; Qiu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024c; Kurita et al., 2023;
174   Wang et al., 2020a; Cirik et al., 2020; Bu et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023; De Vries et al., 2017; Jia
175   et al., 2024) are often designed for specific scenarios. For example, CLEVR-Ref+(Liu et al., 2019)
176   focuses on simple objects like boxes, spheres, and cylinders. SK-VG(Chen et al., 2023c) integrates
177   prior scene knowledge as additional input, while RefCrowd (Qiu et al., 2022) targets identifying a
178   person within a crowd. By contrast, we introduce Ref-L4, a more general and comprehensive bench-
179   mark encompassing 365 categories and 45,341 annotations. Ref-L4 features expressions averaging
180   24.2 words and a vocabulary of 22,813 words, facilitating the accurate evaluation of REC models
181   on complex expressions and diverse objects.
182   REC Models. The evolution of REC models has transitioned from specialized models (Kamath
183   et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2017; Su et al., 2019; Zheng et al., 2019; Yan et al., 2023;
184   Zou et al., 2024) to generalist models or large multimodal models (LMMs)(Wang et al., 2023b; Lin
185   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Wei et al.,
186   2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Zhan et al., 2023; 2024; Pramanick et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023;
187   Wang et al., 2024b; Shen et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024; KOSAREVA, 2024). No-
188
      table examples of these LMMs include CogVLM-Grounding(Wang et al., 2023b), SPHINX (Lin
      et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024), ONE-PEACE (Wang et al., 2023a), Qwen-VL-Chat (Bai et al., 2023),
189
      MiniGPTv2 (Chen et al., 2023a), and Lenna (Wei et al., 2023). These models, benefiting from
190
      larger model sizes and extensive training on diverse datasets, exhibit remarkable performance on
191   conventional REC datasets. For example, CogVLM-Grounding achieves an accuracy of 94.58%
192   on RefCOCO (cleaned). Additionally, the performance gap among models is shrinking, with many
193   LMMs surpassing 90% accuracy. This performance saturation raises concerns about the adequacy
194   of current REC benchmarks for making meaningful comparisons. In response, we propose Ref-L4,
195   a more comprehensive and challenging benchmark. We have also conducted rigorous evaluations of
196   24 LMM models, offering holistic comparisons that highlight their weaknesses and suggest direc-
197   tions for improvement.

202   Data Sources. Our benchmark is derived from two sources: 1) our cleaned validation and test
203   sets of the RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016), RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016), and RefCOCOg (Mao et al.,
204   2016) datasets; and 2) the test set from the large-scale object detection dataset Objects365 (Shao
205   et al., 2019). The Objects365 dataset provides a broader range of categories, varying instance sizes,
206   higher image resolutions, and more intricate scenes. In the RefCOCO series, each instance includes
207   a bounding box, a category name, and an extremely brief expression like “right teddy bea”. In con-
208   trast, the Objects365 benchmark labels each instance with mainly a bounding box and the relevant
209   category.
210   For the RefCOCO (cleaned) series, we begin by consolidating duplicate images and instances,
211   resulting in a subset of 6, 502 images containing 14, 186 unique instances. For Objects365, we
212   select samples from its testing set based on several criteria: 1) each image has both height
213   and width greater than 800 pixels; 2) each image is sufficiently complex, containing

215   greater than 0.05, where (h, w) represents the instance size and (H, W ) denotes the image size;
      4) we randomly sample N instances for each of the 365 classes defined in Objects365, with

245   Referring Expression Generation. Given a target instance and its corresponding image, we lever-
246   age GPT-4V with human reviewers in the loop to generate its precise and detailed referring expres-
247   sions. Figure 2 illustrates the three-step generation process:
248   Step-1: Each instance in the Objects365 dataset is linked to a bounding box and a category name. We
249   begin by cropping these instances from the original images. Next, we input each cropped area along
250   with the prompt detailed in Section B.1 into GPT-4V to produce a context-independent description.
251   For instances from the RefCOCO series, this step is omitted as each instance already has a brief
252   expression.
253   Step-2: Drawing inspiration from recent studies on GPT-4V (Yang et al., 2023), where GPT-4V is
254   able to pay more attention to instances highlighted by a red circle within an image, we similarly
255   encircle the target instance in red to facilitate GPT-4V in generating a context-aware referring ex-
256   pression. Following this, as depicted in Figure 2, we process the image and use the prompt outlined
257   in Section B.2 to generate a context-aware referring expression for each instance. We instruct GPT-
258   4V to describe various features such as color, size, position, and context. Additionally, we provide
259   a hint (the context-independent description from Step-1) in the prompt to mitigate hallucination
260
      issues, resulting in more accurate descriptions.
261   Step-3: We manually review all generated referring expressions to correct any hallucination issues.
262   We ensure that each expression uniquely describes the instance and is factual, accurate, and harm-
263   less.
264   Annotation Expansion. To date, we have compiled 18,653 unique referring expressions, each de-
265   scribing a distinct instance. To assess the robustness of REC models to diverse language inputs,
266   we employ a two-stage rephrasing process to expand our benchmark: 1) utilizing GPT-4 with the
267   prompt detailed in Section B.3, to generate rephrased versions of each expression; 2) conducting
268   a manual review to ensure that the rephrased expressions are unique, factual, relevant, and harm-
269   less. Consequently, our final Ref-L4 benchmark encompasses 9,735 images with 45,341 referring
      expressions, each accurately describing one of the 18,653 unique instances.

300   datasets: RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, RefCOCOg, and our Ref-L4. Due the the high overlap of data

303   peaking at approximately 8.4 words. In contrast, our Ref-L4 displays a significantly different dis-
304   tribution, with expressions ranging much longer, peaking at around 24.2 words and having a long
305   tail extending up to 117 words. This suggests that our Ref-L4 benchmark is designed to push the
306
      boundaries of current REC models, requiring them to process and comprehend more intricate and
      detailed descriptions.

417   Benchmark Division. Modern large multimodal models (LMMs) that are able to handle the REC
418
      task typically use unrestricted and extensive data for training. Our Ref-L4 benchmark is designed
      to assess the capabilities of these advanced models without imposing any limitations on the training
419
      data sources. The benchmark is divided into two subsets: a validation set, comprising 30% of
420
      the data with 7, 231 images, 10, 311 instances, and 13, 420 referring expressions; and a test set,
421   comprising 70% of the data with 9, 467 images, 17, 242 instances, and 31, 921 referring expressions.
422   Given that our benchmark includes instances from 365 categories, we ensure that each category has
423   at least one sample in both the validation and test sets. While we provide these two splits, we
424   encourage the combined use of both sets for model evaluation, especially in the current LMM era,
425   where the use of unrestricted training data is prevalent.

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
      In this work, we first point out several limitations of the current REC benchmarks, such as substan-
534   tial labeling inaccuracies and very brief referring expressions. To better assess the capabilities of
535   models, particularly those LMMs that can perform the REC task, we present Ref-L4, which features
536   four key characteristics: 1) a large-scale dataset with 45,341 annotations; 2) a wide range of object
537   categories and varying instance scales; 3) detailed referring expressions; and 4) an extensive vocab-
538   ulary comprising 22,813 unique words. We evaluate a total of 24 models using various evaluation
539   protocols. We wish that Ref-L4 could serve as a valuable resource for researchers and developers,
      fostering the development of more robust and versatile REC models in the LMM era.

964
965
      Ref-L4 provides a more comprehensive and detailed evaluation of REC capabilities, helping to
966   better understand and improve the performance of large multimodal models capable of handling the
967   REC task. The public availability of Ref-L4 and its evaluation code encourages further research and
968   collaboration, driving innovation and advancements in the field of REC and beyond. While Ref-L4
969   aims to cover a wide range of scenarios, it may still miss out on specific edge cases or unique contexts
970   that could be encountered in real-world applications. The detailed and lengthy referring expressions
971   might pose a challenge for current models, requiring significant advancements in natural language
      processing and comprehension capabilities.
