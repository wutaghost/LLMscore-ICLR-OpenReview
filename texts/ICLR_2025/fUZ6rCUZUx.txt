009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in outlier detection have been primarily driven by deep learning
012            models, which, while powerful, have substantial drawbacks in terms of explain-
013            ability. This is particularly relevant in fields that demand detailed reasoning and
014            understanding of why observations are classified as outliers. To close the gap be-
015            tween state-of-the-art performance and enhanced explainability, we propose Vine
016
               Copula-Based Outlier Detection (VC-BOD). We utilize Sklar’s theorem in con-
               junction with vine copulas and univariate kernel density estimators to decouple
017
               marginal distributions and their dependency structure for outlier detection. Our
018
               model uses a closed-form equation for the outlier score, which allows for detailed
019            explainability and feature attribution. VC-BOD employs a traceable criterion to
020            determine whether a new observation is an outlier, while also identifying the spe-
021            cific features responsible for this classification. The proposed model further dis-
022            tinguishes whether these features deviate from their own distributions or from
023            interactions with other features. Our empirical evaluations demonstrate that VC-
024            BOD outperforms most benchmarked classical models and several deep learning
025            approaches in terms of average rank performance while proving competitive with
026            the best-performing models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Outlier detection (OD), or anomaly detection (AD), has experienced substantial advances, primar-
031   ily fueled by the emergence of modern deep learning architectures and the increased availability
032   of computational resources. These advancements have been particularly notable in the fields of
033   computer vision and natural language processing (Kim et al., 2020; Liznerski et al., 2021). More re-
034   cently, deep models for tabular data have also gained popularity (Thimonier et al., 2024; Shenkar &
035   Wolf, 2022). While these developments have significantly pushed the boundaries of OD research in
036   tabular data, they concurrently introduce challenges, concerning interpretability, explainability, and
037   reliability—attributes critical in many practical applications (Hilal et al., 2022; Malaiya et al., 2018).
038   In contrast, traditional OD methods, such as KNN (Ramaswamy et al., 2000), Isolation-Forest (IFor-
039
      est) (Hariri et al., 2021) and COPOD (Li et al., 2020), which do not rely on Multi-Layer Perceptrons
      (MLPs), can offer notable advantages regarding interpretability, computational efficiency, and ex-
040
      plainability. However, this field has not been the main focus of recent research, with potential
041
      benefits left unexplored.
042
043   The most straightforward approach to detect outliers is to estimate the complete distribution of the
044   data and then determine the density of a new observation. If, for a new observation, the density is
045
      strikingly small, it is deemed anomalous. However, this becomes problematic with high-dimensional
      data (Zimek et al., 2012). To circumvent this problem, Li et al. (2020; 2023) have demonstrated that
046
      univariate empirical cumulative distribution functions can effectively model marginal distributions
047
      for outlier detection. Yet, these models make several restrictive distributional assumptions and put
048   little emphasis on the interactions between features, which can be crucial for understanding more
049   complex anomaly patterns (Ahirwar et al., 2012). Horváth et al. (2020) aim to estimate the complete
050   distribution by explicitly modeling the dependency structure between certain features, utilizing a
051   composition of bivariate copulas. Yet, neither of these approaches utilize Sklar’s Theorem (Sklar,
052   1959) to its full potential, which states that any multivariate distribution can be decomposed into its
053   marginal distributions and a copula function that captures the dependency structure. Horváth et al.
      (2020) approximate the dependency structure to a limited degree, they only considers two features


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      at a time and do not separate the marginals from their dependency structure. The former limitation
055   hinders the detection of more complex dependencies, while the latter reduces explainability.
056
057   Building on this foundation, we introduce Vine Copula-Based Outlier Detection (VC-BOD), to
058
      bridge the gap between recent state-of-the-art models’ high performance and a theoretically mo-
      tivated understanding of outlier scores. To improve on the shortcomings detailed in the previous
059
      paragraph, we fully utilize Sklar’s theorem in conjunction with vine copulas (Joe, 1997; Bedford &
060
      Cooke, 2002; Aas et al., 2009) and univariate kernel methods (Marron & Wand, 1992; Sheather &
061   Jones, 1991). Vine copulas are multivariate distributions, constructed out of several bivariate cop-
062   ulas which are arranged across successive tree structures. A vine copula is a highly flexible model
063   capable of capturing diverse dependence patterns. We construct an outlier detector by aggregating
064   separately calculated scores for the marginal distributions and each feature interaction, as captured
065   by the vine copula, in a closed-form equation. The copulas appearing in the higher trees of the vine
066   copula enable us to consider multiple features together, thus capturing more complex dependencies.
067   We leverage the individual scores to determine those features which significantly contribute to an
068   observation being classified as an outlier. Furthermore, for each influential feature, we can determine
069
      whether it was flagged due to a deviation from its marginal distribution or because of its interactions
      with other features. This degree of feature attribution establishes a new benchmark in the field.
070
071   To clarify the process behind our approach, we provide a theoretical example based on a syn-
072   thetic dataset, that showcases the workings of VC-BOD. To evaluate the performance we follow
073   the well-established experimental design from works such as Thimonier et al. (2024); Shenkar &
074   Wolf (2022), and conduct a comprehensive analysis across a set of 31 tabular datasets to bench-
075
      mark VC-BOD against existing OD methodologies. The empirical findings confirm that our model
      consistently demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, achieving the highest average rank among
076
      non-MLP-based models and the third-highest rank across all models. A statistical test further in-
077
      dicates that there is no significant gap between VC-BOD and the best-performing baseline models.
078   VC-BOD is thus to be positioned at the forefront of OD methodologies in terms of performance,
079   while simultaneously offering one of the most advanced explainability frameworks for outlier de-
080   tection.
081
      To summarize, our work offers the following contributions:
082
083         1. A new OD-method, theoretically motivated and defined via a closed-form equation.
084
            2. A new framework for enhanced explainability and detailed feature attribution.
085
086         3. State-of-the-art outlier detection capabilities.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   For the contexts of our work, we categorize OD methodologies into two main categories: classical,
091   which encompass models that do not utilize MLPs, and MLP-based. This distinction is important
092   because MLP-based approaches often suffer from a unique set of limitations that are not present
093   with classical approaches, as we discuss in the subsequent paragraphs. VC-BOD is to be classified
094   under the distribution-based models of the classical category.
095
096   Classical. OD approaches without MLPs can be broadly classified into four main categories:
097   distribution-based, proximity-based, reconstruction-based, and one-class classification methods.
098   Distribution-based methods aim to learn the underlying distribution of the data and infer the like-
099   lihood of a sample belonging to this distribution. The early works in this category, such as Parzen
100   (1962) and Roberts & Tarassenko (1994), laid the foundation by modeling data distributions directly.
101   However, challenges arise when dealing with high-dimensional or multimodal data, as real-world
102   data rarely conforms neatly to standard probability distributions. More recent methods, such as Li
103   et al. (2020; 2023) and Horváth et al. (2020), utilize copula analysis to capture the data distribution.
104   Proximity-based methods utilize distance information in the representation space to identify anoma-
105   lies. A straightforward yet effective approach was introduced by Ramaswamy et al. (2000), where
106   outliers are classified based on their distance to their nearest neighbors. More refined methods, such
107   as Local Outlier Factor (Breunig et al., 2000), incorporate local density information, identifying a
      data point as an outlier if it has a substantially lower local density compared to its neighbors. While


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      they have been applied with great success, Zimek et al. (2012) have shown that these methods also
109   suffer from the curse of dimensionality.
110
111   Reconstruction-based methods detect outliers by training models to reconstruct samples from the
112
      normal distribution, with high reconstruction errors indicating potential anomalies. Techniques in
      this category include Hawkins (1974); Hoffmann (2007), which utilize PCA to detect outliers. These
113
      methods may fail in situations where the true dependence is more complex than a multivariate nor-
114
      mal distribution.
115
116   One-Class-Classification techniques use discriminatory models to establish a decision boundary
117   that separates normal data from potential anomalies. Successful applications include kernel-based
118
      methods, such as Schölkopf et al. (1999); Tax & Duin (2004); Ben-Hur et al. (2001) or tree-based
      models, such as Liu et al. (2008); Hariri et al. (2021); Guha et al. (2016); Gopalan et al. (2019).
119
      Yet these models rely on hyper-parameter tuning and are often constrained in their ability to capture
120
      complex or non-linear data distributions effectively (Perera et al., 2021; Seliya et al., 2021).
121
122
      MLP. More recently, MLPs, have gained popularity due to advancements in self-supervised learn-
123   ing and diverse neural architectures. These methods leverage strategies like masking (Thimonier
124   et al., 2024), transformation techniques (Bergman & Hoshen, 2020), and contrastive learning (Qiu
125   et al., 2021; Shenkar & Wolf, 2022), often incorporating attention mechanisms, CNNs, and GANs.
126   Reconstruction loss is another common approach, where autoencoders (Principi et al., 2017; Chen
127   & Konukoglu, 2018; Kim et al., 2020) and GANs (Schlegl et al., 2019) are employed to reconstruct
128   input data and detect anomalies. Additionally, MLPs have been used in one-class classification to
129   isolate inliers from outliers by minimizing the volume of a data-enclosing hypersphere (Ruff et al.,
130   2018), which has been extended in Ruff et al. (2021).
131   Despite their significant potential, these models face notable limitations. One primary concern is
132   their high-dimensional parameter space, which often exceeds the cardinality of the training set,
133   potentially impeding generalization. Another significant limitation, which can render these models
134   unsuitable for certain applications, is the inherent complexity in interpreting their results. Although
135   methods like those described in Thimonier et al. (2024) provide mechanisms for attribution, these
136   often rely on indirect methods, which may not provide clear insights into model decisions.

      Influence of truncation. An important hyper-parameter of our model is the degree of precision to
300   which the dependency structure is to be approximated. This is controlled via the truncation level T
301   of the vine copula in equation 4. With each additional tree more feature interactions are captured. If
302   the vine copula fit is close to the ground truth, then adding more trees increases accuracy by reducing
303   the number of false negatives, as more aspects of the total dependence are considered, which could
304   potentially be violated. Yet in practice a low level of truncation is advisable: Often, the fit is not
305   perfect and accumulating errors in higher trees can result in the algorithm becoming less accurate.
306   We illustrate this aspect with a theoretical example in Section 6.1. We also provide a statistical
307   analysis concerning this trade-off in the last paragraph of Section A.1 in the Appendix.

312   around this problem, we take advantage of the helpful property that the outlier score in equation 5
313   is calculated as the average of the feature outlier scores. We therefore calculate the score, as in
314   equation 5, solely for the continuous features. For the non-continuous features, we only calculate
315   a marginal outlier score in a manner analogous to that of continuous features, with further details
316   provided in Appendix A.4. The final outlier score is determined as the average of all feature scores.

      5     E XPLAINABILITY AND FEATURE ATTRIBUTION
325
326
      One of the main advantages of our approach is its high level of explainability. VC-BOD offers the
327   following procedure to analyse a new observation:
328
329         1. Decision if a new observation is to be classified as an outlier, based on a comprehensible
330            criterion.
331         2. If an observation is classified as an outlier: Identification of those features that are decisive
332            for this classification.
333
            3. For each decisive feature: Distinction whether it was flagged because it does not fit with the
334
               learned marginal distribution or because of the way it interacts with other features. (Only
335
               for truncation level T ≥ 1)
336
337   VC-BOD is trained based on a sample of ’normal’ observations and a confidence level τ ∈ (0; 1),
338   e.g. τ = 0.95, is set for the analysis.
339
      For each new observation obs, the outlier score s(obs) is calculated according to equation 5. Next,
340
      determine the quantile q(obs) of this score compared to those of the training sample, i.e. determine
341   the fraction of the training scores smaller than s(obs). If q(obs) exceeds τ , then obs is classified as
342   an outlier.
343
344
      If the observation is identified as an outlier, it can be assessed for each feature i ∈ {1, . . . , d} whether
      it plays a significant role in the classification. To do so, determine the quantile qi (obs) of the feature
345
      score si (obs) from equation 5 amongst the i-th feature scores of the sample. If qi (obs) > τ , feature
346
      i contributes significantly to the observation being classified as an outlier. If no feature crosses
347   the quantile threshold, then the observation is anomalous because of the aggregated influence of all
348   features.
349
350
      For each of the significant features it can be decided why it was flagged. Determine the quantile
      qmi (obs) of the i-th marginal outlier score mi amongst the corresponding values of the training
351
      sample. If qmi (obs) > τ , then the i-th feature of obs does not fit with the learned marginal distri-
352
      butions. Else, if qmi (obs) ≤ τ , the feature is considered anomalous as it violates both the marginal
353   distribution and the established dependence with other features. However, since the marginal vio-
354   lation alone did not trigger the quantile threshold, the violation of the learned dependence structure
355   can be regarded as significant.
356
      This level of feature attribution redefines the current standard in outlier detection, offering valuable
357

360   B.3, we demonstrate the attribution framework using the ’wine’ dataset, which is also part of the
361   empirical study presented in the second part of Section 6.

      7   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      The main limitations of VC-BOD are due to its usage of vine copulas to capture dependence. In
489   situations with high feature dimension and simultaneously high sample size, fitting the vine copula
490   leads to long run-times for model training. Inference remains fast, and the margin-only approach
491   does not suffer from these complexity issues. Notably, all vine copula computations currently use
492   multi-threaded C++ on CPU cores, and training time could likely be reduced with GPU utilization.
493
      By laying the foundation for highly explainable outlier detection, our work invites future research
494
      to adapt our methodology to unsupervised outlier detection. This requires adjustments since VC-
495   BOD relies on kernel methods which reflect the training data very accurately. While effective in
496   semi-supervised settings, where the model is trained on uncontaminated data, this can become more
497   problematic in unsupervised settings, as the proportion of outliers in the training data increases.
498
      In this work, we introduced Vine Copula-Based Outlier Detection (VC-BOD), significantly advanc-
499
      ing the field of outlier detection by providing enhanced explainability. Our empirical evaluations
500
      demonstrate that VC-BOD achieves state-of-the-art detection capabilities. By incorporating both
501   marginal and dependence-related information into a closed-form equation, VC-BOD offers inter-
502   pretability in complex, potentially high-dimensional settings - a crucial advantage in domains re-
503   quiring transparent decision-making. In conclusion, VC-BOD demonstrates the continuing value of
504   probabilistic approaches in outlier detection, providing a powerful and highly interpretable alterna-
505   tive to deep-learning based models.

774   A.1    S TATISTICAL VIEW OF VC-BOD
775
776   In this section, we analyze VC-BOD from a statistical standpoint. First, we explicitly outline the
777   assumptions underlying the method. Next, we conceptualize VC-BOD as a statistical test, establish-
778   ing its theoretical framework. Finally, we explore the trade-off between accuracy and the increasing
779   complexity introduced by a growing truncation level T .
780
781   Assumptions. In the context of VC-BOD, outlier detection is approached as an out-of-distribution
782   detection task, a common strategy in distribution-based outlier detection. This method aims to
783   estimate the true underlying distribution based on a given sample and then determine whether a new
784
      data point is unlikely under the inferred distribution. The following assumptions are made:
785
          (A1) The training sample consists of i.i.d. realizations from an unknown distribution.
786
787       (A2) The unknown distribution allows for a vine copula specification, which can be determined
788            using Dissmann’s algorithm with Gaussian kernels for the marginals and Gaussian kernel
789
               copulas for the pair-copulas, where:
790         (A2-1) The marginal distributions are correctly specified.
791         (A2-2) The pair-copulas in the trees Ti with i = 2, . . . , T are independent of the conditioning
792                variables.
793         (A2-3) The pair-copulas in the trees Ti with i = 1, . . . , T are correctly specified.
794
795   Assumption (A1) ensures that the underlying distribution, representing the data-generating process,
796   can be effectively learned from the sample. The additional assumptions, grouped under (A2), per-
797   tain to the distributional properties of the underlying distribution. Since VC-BOD employs kernel
798
      estimators for both the marginal distributions and the pair copulas of the vine copula, explicitly
      detailing all assumptions becomes cumbersome. Specifically, (A2-1) assumes that each marginal
799
      distribution can be expressed as a mixture of (truncated) Gaussian kernels. Meanwhile, (A2-2) rep-

      resents the simplifying assumption that all pair copulas from tree T2 onward are independent of the

867   Trade-off between accuracy and growing set of assumptions. If it is established that a new
868   observation violates an aspect of the overall distribution, then this is a sufficient condition that the
869   observation is out-of-distribution also in the context of the whole distribution. An observation may
870   violate an aspect captured in a later tree of the vine copula while matching every aspect captured in
871   the lower trees. An example of this is provided in Section 6.1 of the main text.
872
      Therefore, adding more trees, i.e., working with a larger truncation level T , can improve accuracy
873   by reducing the number of false negatives, i.e., anomalous observations that are not identified as
874   outliers. This is because more sufficient conditions for the observation to be an outlier are checked.
875
876
      However, by increasing the truncation level T , the assumptions made in (A2) become more restric-
      tive. Especially the simplifying assumption (A2-2) is often inappropriate in practice (Acar et al.,
877
      2012; Spanhel & Kurz, 2019). Note that the variants VC-BOD T0 and VC-BOD T1, where the
878
      truncation level is set to T = 0 and T = 1 respectively, do not require (A2-2). Another problem that
879   comes into play when considering more aspects of the total distribution is that the combined test
880   statistic loses power; see Shaffer (1995).
881
882
      To achieve a good balance, we recommend using a relatively low level of truncation. The vine
      copula is fitted iteratively, identifying the (conditional) bivariate distributions with the strongest de-
883
      pendencies at each step, indicating that they are well-suited for out-of-distribution detection. For
884
      this reason, the truncated vine copula serves as a useful tool for identifying those feature mappings
885   that are most appropriate for outlier detection. In the context of VC-BOD, the truncated vine copula
886   should therefore be regarded primarily as a tool for feature extraction, rather than as an approxima-
887   tion to the entire distribution.

889   A.2    A LGORITHMIC IMPLEMENTATION OF VC-BOD
890
891   Here we provide an algorithmic description of the training, see Algorithm 1, and inference phase,
892   see Algorithm 2, of VC-BOD.
893   Algorithm 1: In the first stage, we address the feature distributions by distinguishing between contin-
894   uous and non-continuous features. In both cases, we store information about the feature distributions
895   to compute outlier scores later. In the second stage, we fit a vine copula to the transformed continu-
896   ous features. For each of its pair-copulas, we calculate the factor maxij and, if the pair-copula does
897   not exhibit significant dependence, we exclude it from further considerations. Finally, we calculate
898   the scores for the training sample.
899   References to detailed explanations in the main text correspond to the first three subsections of
900   Section 4. The calculations of the density grids (lines 6 and 25) and individual outlier scores (lines 7
901   and 26), as well as the final outlier scores (line 32), are thoroughly explained there. The handling of
902   non-continuous features (lines 10–14) is discussed in Appendix A.4. The definition of the strength-
903   of-dependence parameter sodij (line 22) is provided in Appendix A.3.
904   Algorithm 2: The outputs from the first algorithmic stage are used as input alongside the new ob-
905   servations. The algorithm iterates over the test data, computing the marginal scores and dependence
906   scores for each test instance. Based on these individual scores, the final outlier score for each in-
907   stance is calculated according to equation 5 in the main text. By comparing these scores against the
908   τ -quantile of the training scores, we assign a label to each test instance, where ’1’ indicates that the
909   observation is declared an outlier. The calculations of the individual outlier scores (lines 5 and 12)
910   and the final outlier score (line 14) are explained in detail in the main text.
911   We make the following choices for the involved hyper-parameters. The number of total grid-points,
912   both for the marginal scores and the dependence scores, is chosen as k = 10 000. The discount
913   factor, which appears in the calculation of the dependence scores, is chosen as η = 0.75.

1140
       example, the 1st and 2nd features of the point are respectively chosen to be in the 10th percentile and
1141   90th percentile of the standard normal distribution. As this distribution is uni-modal and symmetric
1142   around its mean, this means that 20% of all possible realisations are less likely than the observed
1143   features. This circumstance is reflected in the quantiles of the marginal outlier scores being 0.8 in
1144   both cases.
1145
       Further, we can infer from Figure 4 that the attribution of the significant features is correct: Fea-
1146
       tures 1 and 2 of the distribution are related such that simultaneous small realisations are likely, see
1147   Figure 4(a), but the 1st feature of the indicated point is small, while its 2nd feature is big, thereby
1148   breaking with the true dependence. The marginal distributions are not violated, as the points are
1149   in the 10th and 90th percentile, respectively, which is not too extreme. With feature 4, on the other
1150   hand, the situation is reversed, and it is anomalous because it does not fit with the true marginal
1151   distribution. The dependence is not violated since it is only related to feature 3. This dependence is
1152   such that it is likely that features 3 and 4 simultaneously have larger realisations, which is the case
1153   with the indicated point. It can be seen in Figure 4(b) that the indicated point is out-of-distribution
1154   simply because its 4th feature is too large.

       D.1     S TATISTICAL E VALUATION OF P ERFORMANCE
1569
1570   To evaluate if there is a significant difference in the performance of VC-BOD, as indicated by av-
1571   erage rank across the datasets, against benchmark models, we conduct a statistical analysis using
1572   the Friedman test followed by the Nemenyi post-hoc test, as described in Liu et al. (2019); Campos
1573   et al. (2016). The null hypothesis of the Friedman test assumes no significant difference between
1574   algorithms; it is rejected if the p-value is below the chosen significance level, indicating at least two
1575   algorithms differ significantly. The Nemenyi test is used to identify specific pairs with significant
1576   differences.
1577   Our analysis yields several noteworthy observations. The Friedman-Nemenyi test does not confirm
1578   that our method significantly outperforms the KNN algorithm. However, it also reveal that none
1579   of the current state-of-the-art deep learning methods demonstrate statistically significant superiority
1580   over our approach in terms of rank performance. This finding suggests that our method achieves
1581   competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art techniques in outlier detection, in-
1582   cluding those based on deep learning.

1584   the table, an upward arrow (↑) indicates that the model in the row significantly outperforms the
1585   model in the column at a confidence level of 0.1, while a downward arrow (↓) indicates the opposite.
