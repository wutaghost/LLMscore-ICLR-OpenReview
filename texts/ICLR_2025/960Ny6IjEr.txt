009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Approaches for large-language model compression using low-rank decomposition
012            have made strides, particularly with the introduction of activation and loss-aware
013            Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) that improve the trade-off between decom-
014            position rank and downstream task performance. Despite these advancements, a
015            persistent challenge remains—selecting the optimal ranks for each layer to jointly
016            optimize compression rate and downstream task accuracy. Current methods ei-
017            ther rely on heuristics that can yield sub-optimal results due to their limited dis-
018
               crete search space or are gradient-based but are not as performant as heuristic
               approaches without post-compression fine-tuning. To address these issues, we
019
               propose Learning to Low-Rank Compress (LLRC), a gradient-based approach
020
               which directly learns the weights of masks that select singular values in a fine-
021            tuning-free setting. Using a calibration dataset of just 3,000 documents, this
022            training architecture teaches the model to select fewer and fewer singular val-
023            ues while minimizing the divergence of intermediate activations from the original
024            model. Our approach outperforms competing fine-tuning-free rank selection ap-
025            proaches, such as Sensitivity-based Truncation Rank Searching (STRS), Adaptive
026            Rank Selection (ARS), and LLM-Pruner on Llama-2-7B, Llama-3-8B, Gemma-
027            7B, and Llama-2-13B across various compression rates on common-sense reason-
028            ing and open-domain question-answering tasks; For instance, with a compression
029
               rate of 20%, our approach outperforms the competitive STRS on MMLU, BoolQ,
               and OpenbookQA by 12%, 3.5%, and 4.4%, respectively, using Llama-2-13B.
030
               More remarkably, our fine-tuning-free approach consistently outperforms LLM-
031
               Pruner, even after fine-tuning, on NQ-Open, MMLU, BoolQ and OpenbookQA
032            with Llama-2-7B.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
038   2023) are showing remarkable results in natural language understanding and generation tasks. These
039   models are not just pivotal in zero-shot language modelling but also extend their utility to applica-
040   tions such as code generation (Chen et al., 2021), conversational agents (Kumar et al., 2023), and
041   personalised education (Kasneci et al., 2023). Despite the success of these models in solving a wide
042   range of tasks, their use is limited by high computing and memory requirements. For example,
043   LLaMA-2 comes in 7 billion and 40 billion parameter variants (Touvron et al., 2023), requiring
044
      25.79 GB and 153.87 GB of memory, respectively. Yet, these models are small compared to others,
      such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022), which have 175 billion and
045
      500 billion parameters, respectively.
046
047   As these models grow, various compression techniques have been developed to reduce their size.
048   Quantization, which reduces the number of bits required to represent each parameter, is widely used
049   for compressing language models (Dettmers et al., 2022; Frantar et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). For
050
      instance, LLM.int8() is a procedure for using 8-bit precision in matrix multiplication in trans-
      former layers that had cut inference memory requirements by half without significant performance
051
      degradation (Dettmers et al., 2022).
052
053   As an alternative to quantisation, other works explored the structural pruning of LLMs (Ma et al.,
      2023; Xia et al., 2024). These works follow two stages for compression: first, pruning for model

054
      compression and then a continued training step to recover performance. LLM Pruner (Ma et al.,
055   2023) utilizes parameter-efficient fine-tuning after compression to recover performance whereas
056   Sheared Llama (Xia et al., 2024) performs extensive training on 50 billion tokens after compression.
057   Other recent works show that applying low-rank matrix decomposition techniques can compress
058   and reduce memory requirements of language models (Hsu et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2023; Yuan et al.,
059   2024). Moreover, the application of these methods has seen improvements through weighted singu-
060   lar value decomposition, a variant that makes the decomposition loss aware or activation aware (Hsu
061   et al., 2022a; Yuan et al., 2024), thus improving the trade-off of compression and performance. Yet,
062   one key challenge that both approaches face is the selection of optimal ranks for each layer, which
063
      determines the amount of compression. Given that research has shown that different layers of a lan-
      guage model may have different optimal compression rates Yuan et al. (2024); Nawrot et al. (2024),
064
      using a constant rank across layers may not be an effective solution. To address this problem, Yuan
065
      et al. (2024) propose a heuristic named Sensitivity-based Truncation Rank Searching (STRS), which
066   iteratively searches for optimal ranks per layer by evaluating model perplexity on a small calibration
067   set. Despite proving better than a naive selection of constant compression ratios across layers, this
068   approach has two core problems that can lead to suboptimal solutions. First, the search space of
069   ranks is a discrete set of only 10 elements, significantly restricting the number of options available
070   for the ranks. Second, the optimal decomposition of each layer is identified independently, without
071   taking into account the decomposition of the other layers. On the other hand, Gao et al. (2024)

105   2   R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Model compression is a crucial field in deep learning that focuses on reducing the computational
      costs associated with deploying models while maintaining their performance. There are several


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      approaches to neural network compression, including pruning (Han et al., 2015; Zhu & Gupta,
109   2017; Ma et al., 2023), quantisation (Bai et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023), and low-rank factorisation,
110   the focus of this work. Specifically in natural language processing (NLP), there have been various
111   efforts along these lines. Early work aimed to reduce the number of parameters in LSTMs. For
112   instance, Winata et al. (2019) applied low-rank decomposition techniques such as Semi-NMF and
113   SVD for LSTM compression and compared the results to pruning. In contrast to performing low-
114   rank factorisation on a trained model, other works applied tensor decomposition to re-parameterize
115   the model architecture for training (Yang et al., 2017; Pan et al., 2019; Zangrando et al., 2023).
116   For instance, TR-LSTM utilised the low-rank tensor ring decomposition (TRD) to reformulate the
117
      input-to-hidden transformation (Pan et al., 2019). While Tensor Train (Yang et al., 2017) and Tensor
      Ring (Pan et al., 2019) are effective model compression techniques, their application to compress an
118
      already trained model is not straightforward.
119
120   More recent works focused on applying low-rank factorisation to compress language models with-
121   out full re-training. For example, Hsu et al. (2022a) develop a weighted singular value decomposi-
122   tion approach called Fisher-Weighted SVD (FWSVD) that tries to preserve the model performance
123
      on a given downstream task. The authors compare approaches using SVD, SVD with fine-tuning,
      FWSVD, and FWSVD with fine-tuning and show that FWSVD with fine-tuning yields more accu-
124
      rate results than naive SVD with fine-tuning (Hsu et al., 2022a). Activation-Aware Singular Value
125
      Decomposition (ASVD) uses the intermediate activations in the weighted singular value decomposi-
126   tion (Yuan et al., 2024), minimising the reconstruction error of the output of the linear transformation
127   rather than minimising the error of the weight itself (Yuan et al., 2024). This method yields more
128   accurate results than Fisher SVD and allows for increased compression at a better trade-off with
129   performance (Yuan et al., 2024).
130
      In both these approaches, while performing low-rank decomposition for compression, the singular
131
      value rank for each layer has to be determined. Besides a naive approach that selects an equal
132   rank across all layers, recent works explored approaches to find the optimal rank for each layer.
133   A relevant approach is Sensitivity-based Truncation Rank Searching (STRS; Yuan et al., 2024),
134   which iteratively searches for optimal ranks per layer by evaluating model perplexity on a small
135   calibration set. STRS performs a binary search by using a discrete set of pre-defined 10 compression
136   rates and iteratively applying low-rank decomposition to one layer, leaving the rest of the network
137   unchanged (Yuan et al., 2024). Whereas, ARS Gao et al. (2024) tackle rank selection using gradient-
138   based optimization, employing a recurrent neural network and linear layers to predict binary masks
139   over singular values.

142
143   We now describe how Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) can be used to compress linear layers.
144   Moreover, we also briefly cover details of ASVD (Yuan et al., 2024), a weighted SVD technique
145   which yields higher model compression rates at lower performance loss.

      3.1   C OMPRESSING T RANSFORMERS WITH SVD
148
149   SVD is a fundamental matrix factorization technique used in various fields, including image process-
150   ing and machine learning (Chen, 2018; Yang, 2021; Schotthöfer et al., 2022). The SVD decomposes
151   a matrix W into the product of three other matrices: Ur , Σr , and VrT as shown in Eq. (1):
152
153
                                                 W = Ur Σr VrT                                            (1)
154   Here, W ∈ Rm×n is a rank-r matrix, Ur ∈ Rm×r and Vr ∈ Rn×r are orthogonal, Σr ∈ Rr×r is
155   the diagonal matrix of the singular values, and r is the rank of the decomposition. Decreasing r,
156   thus discarding some of the singular values, reduces the dimension of Ur and Vr but worsens the
157   approximation of W . In particular, for any k ≤ r, it holds:
158
159                                Uk Σk VkT = arg       min       ∥Wk − W ∥2 .
                                                     rank(Wk )=k
160
161   If we approximate W using a low-rank SVD, then we only need to store the highest r singular values
      and their corresponding singular vectors. This would result in the storage of three smaller matrices,

163   can be quantified by the parameter ratio in Eq. (2), where r denotes the rank of decomposition:

170   3.2   ACTIVATION -AWARE SVD
171
172   Activation-Aware SVD is a form of weighted SVD that aims to minimise the reconstruction error
173   of the output of a linear transformation rather than minimising the error of the reconstructed weight
174
      matrix (Yuan et al., 2024). This approach can be formulated as optimising the following quantity:
175                                     arg      min        ∥Wk X − W X∥2                                 (3)
                                              rank(Wk )=k
176
177   where Wk is the reconstructed weight using k singular values and X is an input into the linear
178   transformation. This is achieved by performing SVD on W S rather than W and then scaling the
179   reconstructed weight by S −1 , where S is a matrix calculated from a set of inputs X designed to
180   capture the influence of the input channels on the weights (Yuan et al., 2024).

      4     L EARNING TO L OW-R ANK C OMPRESS
183
184
      In this work, we propose Learning to Low-Rank Compress (LLRC), an approach that learns optimal
185   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) ranks for each layer from data to achieve a desired compres-
186   sion rate. The approach centres on applying SVD to each weight matrix targeted for compression,
187   followed by a learnable masking layer in the weight reconstruction that selectively masks singular
188   values, as shown in Fig. 1. The training process begins with an uncompressed model, and through
189   training, the model increases the compression until the desired overall compression rate is reached.
190   During training, these masking layers can adaptively learn highly different compression rates for
191   each layer.
192   The advantage of LLRC is that it jointly optimises compression and model performance, where the
193   model is trained by optimising a multi-task training objective composed of compression, distillation,
194   and mask smoothing loss. During training, the only learnable parameters are the weights that control
195   the masking while the rest of the model is frozen. The first part of the objective is a compression loss
196   that minimizes the average value of the weights responsible for masking, generating more sparsity in
197   the mask. The second part of the objective is a distillation objective, which minimizes the divergence
198   between two intermediate activations between the compressed model and the uncompressed model.
199
      The third part of the objective is a Total Variation loss that is applied to the learnable masking layer
      to guide the learning of smooth masks. The following sub-sections outline further provide details of
200
      the training procedure.
201
202
      4.1   A PPLYING SVD
203
204   We perform SVD on all linear projection layers in the model except for the logits layer. Given
205   the ability of weighted SVD approaches to retain performance at higher compression rates (Hsu
206   et al., 2022b; Yuan et al., 2024), we utilize ASVD as a drop-in replacement for SVD in most of our
207   experiments. For consistency with Yuan et al. (2024), we use α = 0.5.

      4.4   M ODEL P OST-P ROCESSING
276
      After training, we use the learned singular value masks to select the required singular values for
277
      compression. During the evaluation, we applied a 0.5 threshold to the sigmoid of the logits to
278   generate the binary mask without needing the differentiable masking operator.
279
280   If a layer is not compressed more than 1%, we reconstruct its entire weight matrix using its full
281
      rank, and the layer remains uncompressed. The early stopping criteria while training accounts for
      this and only terminates training when the precise compression rate is achieved. This design choice
282
      of avoiding using the mask is crucial; even if the learned singular value mask retains 90% of the
283
      singular values, compression might not be achieved as per Eq. (2), and the full rank can be used to
284   reconstruct the weight.

288
289   5.1   DATASET AND EVALUATION
290
291   We used a calibration dataset of 3000 documents from WikiText-2 (Merity et al., 2016) for training.
292   We evaluate the compressed model on zero-shot PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019),
293   and OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and 5-shot evaluations
294   on NQ-Open (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019). Datasets cover common-sense reasoning datasets and
295
      open-domain QA.
296
297   5.2   T RAINING AND DATA C ONFIGURATION
298
299   The training dataset for LLRC consisted of 3,000 unique documents from WikiText-2. To have a
300   greater number of token activations present per batch, all documents selected had more than 150
301
      words. Experiments were conducted using a batch size of 4, a maximum token length of 256, and
      optimized with the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). We employed an initial learning
302
      rate of 0.01, which was halved upon reaching the target parameter ratio, to improve training stability.
303
      To avoid unnecessary training, we use early stopping, terminating the training 750 steps after the
304   target parameter ratio was achieved.
305
306
307   5.3   M ASKING L AYER I NITIALIZATION
308
      At the start of the training, the learnable weight matrix Wlearnable is initialized such that the model
309
      starts in an uncompressed state, selecting all singular values. Given that higher singular values are
310
      theoretically more significant, we introduce an inductive bias into the weight initialization of the
311   mask. To this end, Wlearnable is initialized with linearly distributed values ranging in [3, 6], aligned
312   with the magnitude of the singular values. Following Nawrot et al. (2024), we use a temperature of
313   0.1 for the Gumbel function, which controls the hardness of the mask.
314
315
      5.4   O BJECTIVE F UNCTION C ONFIGURATION
316
317
      The objective function in Eq. (5) represents a weighted sum of the compression, distillation, and
318   total variation loss. The weight on the compression loss, denoted by β, is set to a constant value of 1
319   until the target compression ratio is achieved, after which it is set to 0 to prevent further unnecessary
320   compression and focus on the model loss. Instead of using a constant value for α, which is more
321   susceptible to the initial choice, inspired by Fu et al. (2019), we oscillate α between two bounds
322   between 1 and 0 using the cosine function. This also enables the training to oscillate focus between
323   optimizing for compression and performance. More details on the scaling function of α can be found
      in Appendix A.1.

        8             C ONCLUSION
522
523     In summary, our study introduces Learning to Low-Rank Compress (LLRC), an approach that can
524     learn optimal Singular Value Decomposition ranks for each layer from data to perform low-rank
525     compression of language models. The rank selection layer is a learnable parameter vector, making
526     the training computationally more efficient than the related gradient-based rank selection approach
527     called ARS that uses RNNs and linear up-projection layers(Gao et al., 2024). Unique from other
528     rank selection approaches such as Yuan et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024), which select optimal ranks
529     (top-k singular values), our method selects any-k singular values to achieve compression. Among
530     rank selection methods, we showed our method applied on Llama-2-7b, Llama-3-8b, Llama-2-13b,
531
        and Gemma-7b significantly outperforms all other rank selection approaches on NQ-Open, PIQA,
        and OpenbookQA. For instance, for Llama-3-8b our method achieved over 6% higher accuracy than
532
        the second highest on NQ-Open, PIQA, and OpenBookQA at a parameter ratio of 0.80. On Llama-
533
        2-13B, our approach outperforms the competitive STRS on MMLU, BoolQ, and OpenbookQA by
534     12%, 3.5%, and 4.4%, respectively. Compared to existing compression techniques, our fine-tuning
535     free method outperforms LLM Pruner (without fine-tuning) on various compression rates. More-
536     over, our fine-tuning-free approach also is competitive with LLM-Pruner (with fine-tuning), out-
537     performing it on NQ-Open, MMLU, BoolQ, and OpenbookQA at compression rate of 20% with
538     Llama-2-7B. However, on higher compression rates of 15% and 20% on Llama-3-8B, LLM Pruner
539     (with fine-tuning) performs stronger, indicating the necessity of exploring efficient fine-tuning on
        low-rank compressed models.

712   This scaling α follows a cosine distribution that completes 1 cycle at 10% of total training steps,
713   and the min value is capped at b and max c, which are decided based on the rate of compression
714   required. The lower the value of b and c, the faster the model learns the required compression rate.
715   For llama-2-7b, we use b=0.3 and c=1.0, for llama-3-8b we use b=0.25 and c=0.5, for Gemma-7b

      we use b=0.5 and c=1.0. As a warmup, for the first 250 steps, we avoid any scaling and use α = 1.0.

      The learn compression rates in Llama-3-8B, as shown in Figure 7, differ from Llama-2-7B’s, in

814   projects from a dimension of 4096 to 14,336, whereas llama-2-7B projects 4096 to 11,008. Figure 6
815   showed us that the up projection and down projection layers are the least compressible in Llama-2-
816   7B. Hence, with the increased size of the up projection and down projection layers in Llama-3-8B,

      F   I MPLEMENTATION C AVEATS OF ARS
865
866
      Our benchmarking process of ARS Gao et al. (2024) involved a slight variation from the original
867   method, where we included the entire hypernetwork, including the GRU network, for every layer
868   required to be compressed. Based on feedback, this approach was later adjusted to align with the
869   original paper, where only a single GRU hypernetwork was used for the entire model but separate
870   linear projection layers for every layer required to be compressed. Despite this adjustment, results
871   did not change significantly to affect the competitiveness of the approach.
