011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made signifi-
014            cant strides by training on vast high-quality image-text datasets, enabling them to
015            generally understand images well. However, the inherent difficulty in explicitly
016
               conveying fine-grained or spatially dense information (e.g., object masks) in the
               text format poses a challenge for MLLMs, limiting their ability to answer ques-
017
               tions requiring an understanding of detailed or localized visual elements. Drawing
018
               inspiration from the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) concept, this paper
019            proposes a new visual prompt approach to integrate fine-grained external knowl-
020            edge, obtained from specialized vision models (e.g., instance segmentation/OCR
021            models), into MLLMs. This is a promising yet underexplored direction for enhanc-
022            ing MLLMs’ performance. Our approach diverges from concurrent works, which
023            transform external knowledge into additional text prompts, necessitating the model
024            to indirectly learn the correspondence between visual content and text coordinates.
025            Instead, we propose embedding fine-grained object knowledge directly into a spa-
026            tial embedding map as a visual prompt. This design can be easily incorporated into
027
               various MLLMs, such as LLaVA and Mipha, considerably improving their visual
               understanding performance. Through rigorous experiments, we demonstrate that
028
               our method can enhance MLLM performance across 11 benchmarks, improving
029
               their fine-grained context-aware capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The advancement of large language models (LLMs) [1, 2, 3, 4] has revolutionized how machines
035
      process and generate human-like text, demonstrating remarkable abilities in reasoning, translation,
036   and contextual understanding. The integration of language and vision into unified models, such as
037   GPT-4V [5], represents a significant leap forward in enabling machines to understand and interact
038   with the world in a manner akin to human cognition.
039
      Despite their remarkable capabilities, most of the MLLMs (shown in Figure 1 (a)) trained with image-
040
      text pairs still often struggle in fine-grained multimodal comprehension capacities, e.g., correctly
041
      counting objects or precisely locating a specific object. This is partially because of the lack of
042   high-quality data with fine-grained text descriptions. Moreover, text itself has inherent limitations
043   in accurately conveying fine-grained spatial information. As a result, current MLLMs often fail
044   to accurately interpret pixel-level visual content of localized regions within an image, which in
045   return impacts the overall comprehension capacity and thereby causing the notorious “hallucination”
046   problem [6].
047
      To tackle this challenge, one line of work [7, 8, 9] explicitly integrates region coordinates information
048   into the text prompt and trains on specialized region-level chat data. However this still demands
049   that the model implicitly learns to understand coordinates and establish connections with visual
050   content, thereby increasing the learning complexity. Another line of work [10, 11, 12] proposes
051   incorporating Region of Interest (ROI) features directly into model learning, necessitating bespoke
052   model architectures. In contrast to these approaches, rather than learning region information from
053   scratch, this paper explores leveraging finely-grained recognition predictions from existing vision
      models as external knowledge for MLLMs, inspired by the RAG concept. Concurrent with our

080   In this paper, we propose a new visual prompt paradigm to inject external knowledge, such as
081
      localized information, into MLLMs, addressing the challenge of precisely aligning detailed content
      across multiple modalities. As illustrated in Figure 1 (c), the core idea is, rather than treating local
082
      context information as a part of text prompts, we embed them directly within the visual prompts.
083
      Specifically, we start by leveraging panoptic segmentation [14] and OCR detection [15] models, and
084   a pre-trained text encoder to generate pixel-wise text embeddings, which are served as the local
085   context information for MLLMs. Subsequently, we extend the original visual prompts by adding
086   the newly generated context information in a spatial-wise manner. This integrated prompt is then
087   assimilated into MLLMs, improving fine-grained visual content comprehension. Consequently, our
088   approach is capable of enabling MLLMs to discern contexts in the pixel-level space and improve
089   their performance.
090   With the proposed visual prompt paradigm, we train a set of MLLMs on the LLaVA-1.5 datasets [16].
091   The experimental results show that, even with 3 billion parameters, our method improves upon the
092   leading open-source MLLMs such as LLaVA-1.5 [17, 16] and Qwen-VL [18], without collecting
093   additional chat data for training. Remarkably, our models show superior performance across a
094   wide array of benchmarks when compared to the 7-billion MLLM variants, including LLaVA-1.5,
095   Qwen-VL, and InstructBLIP [19], and in some instances, even outperform their 13-billion MLLM
096   counterparts. Our experimental results confirm the significance of integrating our proposed prompt
097   approach with MLLMs to enhance their capabilities.
098   The contributions can be summarized as follows:
099
100           • We systematically investigate integrating localized information into MLLMs. Empirical
101             findings suggest that our proposed visual prompt significantly outperforms the previous
102
                prompt paradigm relying solely on textual prompts containing coordinates.
103           • We propose to integrate contextual embeddings within local contours (e.g., object masks)
104             as the visual prompt, which facilitates the establishment of correlations between image
105             pixels and contexts, thereby enhancing the fine-grained understanding capabilities of various
106
                MLLMs across a spectrum of benchmarks.
107           • Based on our proposed approach, our model with 3B parameters surpasses or achieves
                comparable performances with both existing 7B and 13B models across 11 benchmarks.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Large Language Models. The initial potential of large language models (LLMs) was showcased
111
      by foundational works like BERT [20] and GPT [21]. They sparked a wave of scaling efforts, leading
112   to a range of influential projects, such as T5 [22], GPT-3 [23], Flan-T5 [24], and PaLM [25]. As
113   the volume of training data expanded and the dimensions of model parameters grew, these scaling
114   endeavors led to the creation of ChatGPT [26, 27]. Models like LLaMA [1] and GPT-4 [3] have been
115   trained on extensive corpora and demonstrated remarkable capabilities in diverse cognitive tasks.
116   Additionally, lightweight LLMs with fewer than 3B parameters, i.e., Phi [28, 29] and StableLM-
117   2 [30] have shown performance comparable to larger models [31]. In our work, we adopt Phi-2 [29],
118   Vicuna-7B [31] and Vicuna-13B [31] as our language backbone.
119
120   Multimodal Large Language Models.             Influenced by the success of instruction tuning from
121   LLM, LLaVA [17] and MiniGPT-4 [32] have adopted visual instruction tuning to improve LLMs’
122   interaction with visual data, yielding impressive outcomes. Kosmos-2 [33] and Shikra [34] have
123   advanced MLLMs by enhancing visual comprehension capabilities. Works like LLaVA-Phi [35],
124   MobileVLM [36] and Bunny [37] mainly focus on optimizing training recipes and architecture design
125
      for lightweight MLLMs. V∗ [38] searches visual targets using LLMs’ contextual cues to enhance
      MLLM’s performance. To solve the challenge of understanding fine-grained information in images,
126
      existing approaches propose to learn coordinate representations [7, 34, 8] and Region of Interest
127
      (ROI) features [33, 11], which use inflexible visual referral formats or necessitate the collection of
128   region-level training data. On the contrary, we focus on utilizing external knowledge to improve the
129   fine-grained vision-language alignment for MLLMs without collecting extra chatting data.
130
131
      Prompting Multimodal Large Language Models. Inspired by the ability of GPT-4V [5] to
132   process diverse inputs, ViP-LLaVA [9] collects a visual prompt instruction dataset containing various
133   visual prompts, e.g., scribbles and arrows, for MLLMs fine-tuning. [39] proposes to incorporate
134   the cropped regions to enhance the performance of MLLMs. Contemporary to our work, [13]
135   has offered advanced insights in prompting MLLMs through external knowledge, which introduces
136   bounding box and OCR coordinates into text prompt, however, it’s still challenging to interpret the
137   pixel-level contexts. In this paper, we investigate how to efficiently utilize external knowledge to
138   enhance multimodal fine-grained alignment of MLLMs and introduce a novel visual prompt paradigm
139   incorporating pixel-level contextual information.

253   guidance is crucial in helping the model disambiguate complex scenes, highlight salient features, and
254   distinguish finer objects, thereby improving its overall performance.

522   5      L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
523
524   Our method relies on pre-trained panoptic segmentation and OCR detection models in a zero-shot
525   fashion, making their performance critical to our approach—especially when substantial domain gaps
526
      exist between the benchmark images and their training data.
527   While our method promises to significantly enhance the cognitive capabilities of multimodal models
528   and inspire new methodologies, users should be aware of potential societal impacts, such as biases
529   arising from training data in MLLMs, segmentation, or OCR models, which may lead to biased
530   responses. However, typical textual prompting methods [13] that incorporate captions, object names,
531   and OCR information for MLLMs can also contain biases or errors.

533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   We propose a method to enhance multimodal language models (MLLMs) by leveraging external
536   knowledge such as localized contextual information. By extracting pixel-wise contextual information
537   using panoptic segmentation and OCR models and integrating it with visual features, our model better
538   understands fine-grained objects and global image context. Experimental results and comparisons
539   with state-of-the-art methods demonstrate our approach’s effectiveness. We hope this work highlights
      the importance of external knowledge for MLLMs and offers an effective way to leverage it.
