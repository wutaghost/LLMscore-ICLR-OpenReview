009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph contrastive learning has recently gained substantial attention, leading to
012            the development of various methodologies. In this work, we reveal that a simple
013            training-free propagation method PROP achieves competitive results over dedicat-
               edly designed GCL methods across a diverse set of node classification benchmarks.
014
               We elucidate the underlying rationale for PROP’s effectiveness by drawing con-
015            nections between the propagation operator and established unsupervised learning
016            algorithms. To investigate the reasons for the suboptimal performance of GCL, we
017            decouple the propagation and transformation phases of graph neural networks. Our
018            findings indicate that existing GCL methods inadequately learns effective transfor-
019            mation weights while exhibiting potential for solid propagation learning. In light of
020            these insights, we enhance PROP with learnable propagation, introducing a novel
               GCL method termed PROPGCL. The effectiveness of PROPGCL is demonstrated
021
               through comprehensive evaluations on node classification tasks.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Graph contrastive learning (GCL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for learning graph represen-
027   tations in the unsupervised manner. By leveraging the inherent structural information in graphs, GCL
028   has achieved state-of-the-art performance on graph learning tasks (Velickovic et al., 2019; Zhang &
029   Chen, 2018; You et al., 2020). However, the increasing complexity of these methods, often involving
030
      intricate transformation layers, augmentation strategies, and large-scale parameter tuning, has raised
      questions about the necessity of such complexity for effective learning.
031
032   In this work, we challenge the conventional wisdom that highly parameterized models are essential
033   for achieving strong performance in GCL. Instead, we explore a simple yet powerful alternative:
034
      uniform propagation, abbreviated as PROP, which involves no trainable layers. Remarkably, PROP
      demonstrates competitive performance on various node classification benchmarks, often matching or
035
      surpassing more sophisticated GCLs. This raises an important question:
036
037            How can the simple approach perform so well compared to complex GCL methods?
038
039   To address this, we provide a theoretical analysis of PROP, positioning it as a non-parametric method
040
      aligned with traditional unsupervised learning algorithms through iterative optimization. Additionally,
      we demonstrate that propagation inherently performs contrastive learning by aligning neighboring
041
      node representations, which elucidates the core strengths of PROP in enhancing feature clustering.
042   This analysis not only demystifies the success of PROP but also highlights the potential of simpler
043   models in graph self-supervised learning.
044
      On the other hand, we seek to explore why GCL occasionally exhibits suboptimal performance. By
045
      adopting a decoupling perspective, we isolate and independently analyze the transformation and
046   propagation phases within the GCL encoder. Our extensive analysis reveals a significant limitation in
047   the transformation phase: existing GCL methods often struggle to learn meaningful transformation
048   weights, which perform no better than random counterparts. However, the propagation phase tells a
049   different story. We demonstrate that GCL can consistently learn informative propagation coefficients,
050   effectively capturing structural information. This highlights the potential for developing more efficient
051
      GCL methods by prioritizing propagation over transformation.
052   Building on these insights, we propose a novel method, PROPGCL, which leverages the strengths of
053   PROP while addressing its limitations of uniform propagation. Specifically, PROPGCL enhances
      PROP by learning propagation coefficients through GCL. To validate the effectiveness of PROPGCL,

      we conduct experiments across a wide range of node classification benchmarks, including both ho-
055   mophilic and heterophilic datasets. Our results demonstrate that PROPGCL consistently outperforms
056   existing GCL methods, and requires far fewer computational resources.

      GCL Designing Principles. Popular GCL design approaches predominantly focus on three aspects:
      augmentation generation, view selection, and contrastive objectives. Augmentation strategies have
076
      been explored to enhance representation learning, such as topology-based, label-invariant, and spectral
077   augmentations (Zhu et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2022b; Trivedi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022). For view
078   selection, Guo et al. (2023b) question the necessity of positive pairs, while others focus on hard
079   negative mining (Robinson et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024). Meanwhile, contrastive
080   objectives are often grounded in the mutual information maximization principle (Velickovic et al.,
081   2019) or the information bottleneck principle (Xu et al., 2021). However, a critical aspect of GCL,
082
      the encoder design, has been largely overlooked, with most approaches defaulting to GCNs without
      thorough evaluation. In this work, we challenge this convention by decoupling the transformation
083
      and propagation phases, demonstrating that propagation alone is sufficient for effective GCL.
084
085   Simplifying GCL Architectures. Recent efforts in simplifying GCL have introduced various
086
      strategies aimed at reducing the complexity of existing methods. Some approaches remove the
      traditional augmentation process by employing K-means clustering, adding noise to the embedding
087
      space, or introducing invariant-discriminative losses (Yu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a).
088   Zheng et al. (2022) simplify similarity computations by directly discriminating between two groups of
089   summarized node instances, rather than comparing all nodes. Additionally, Li et al. (2023b) observe
090   lower layers in deep networks suffer from degradation and propose an efficient blockwise training
091   strategy. Other works explore using simpler models like MLPs or linear layers as the backbone for
092   GCL (Liu et al., 2023; Salha et al., 2019). However, these methods continue to rely on transformation
      layers that introduce additional parameters. In contrast, our method eliminates transformation layers
093
      entirely, relying solely on a minimal-parameter propagation layer. This design reduces complexity
094   while maintaining plug-and-play adaptability across various GCL frameworks.

100   3.1   G RAPH C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING P IPELINES
101
102
      GCL pipelines often include two stages, pretraining and evaluation. In the pretraining stage, aug-
      mented views are generated through learnable or artificial approaches and then embedded into
103
      representations via an encoder. GCL learns the encoder weights by maximizing the representation
104   consistency between different views. The purpose of pre-training is to learn high-quality node or
105   graph-level representations without relying on labeled data. In the evaluation stage, linear probing
106   is commonly adopted, where a simple linear classifier is trained in a supervised manner to map the
107   pretrained representations to the downstream label space. This enables a fair comparison of the
      quality of representations learned by different GCL methods.

      3.2   G RAPH C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
109
110   Graph convolutional neural Networks (GCNNs) are neural networks based on graph convolution.
111   One of the foundational works is GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017) which propagates information from
112   local neighborhoods and then transforms the aggregated representation in each layer by H(l+1) =
113   σ(ÃH(l) W(l) ), where H(0) = X denotes node features, Ã is the normalized adjacency matrix,
114   W(l) is transformation weights in the l-th layer, and σ is the activation function.
115
      Decoupled GNNs. In GCN, propagating information and transforming representation are inherently
116   intertwined in each layer. However, this tight coupling of operations can lead to limitations including
117   oversmoothing and scalability issues (Wu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2021). Therefore,
118   simpler yet effective models are proposed by decoupling the two operations (Wu et al., 2019; Gasteiger
119   et al., 2019a; He et al., 2020). For instance, SGC (Wu et al., 2019) composes two decoupled stages
120   of 1) propagation which uniformly aggregates information from K-hops neighboring nodes by
121   H′ = AK X, and 2) transformation which transforms features by H = σ(H′ W).
122   Polynomial GNNs. Despite the simplicity of SGC and its follow-ups, they are proven to perform
123   as a low-pass filter (Balcilar et al., 2021; Nt & Maehara, 2019; Zhu et al., 2021a) and show limited
124   expressiveness for solving various graph structures. To solve this, polynomial GNNs replace the
125   uniform propagation by learnable combinations of polynomial basis functions to approximate arbitrary
126
      spectral filters (Chien et al., 2021; He et al., 2021; 2022). Similarly, polynomial GNNs can be
      expressed in a unified propagation and transformation framework,

249   The preceding experiments reveal that existing GCL methods perform worse than the simple PROP. In
250   this section, we seek to understand the rationale behind this. For this aim, we analyze the decoupling
251   of the propagation and transformation phases, a widely adopted perspective in GNNs designing
252
      (Gasteiger et al., 2019a;b; Li et al., 2022a) and scalability considerations (Yu et al., 2024; Liao et al.,
      2024). Through this analytical framework, we aim to identify which phase is inadequately learned
253
      within the context of GCL.

      5.1         F EATURE TRANSFORMATION IS INEFFECTIVE IN GCL
256
257   To determine whether GCL effectively learns transformation weights, we consider a decoupled
258   encoder, i.e., HPROP = ÂK X followed by two transformation layers H = σ(HPROP W1 )W2
259   where W1 and W2 are the transformation weights. The unweighted propagation enables only
260   focusing on the transformation weights.
261
      The core idea is comparing the transformation weights learned in GCL with random matrices. In
262   practice, we first train the transformation weights through GCL methods. Then we replace the
263   learned transformation weights with a random matrix whose element is independently sampled from a
264   Gaussian distribution N (µ, σ), where µ is the mean and σ is the standard derivation. Representations
265   generated by the randomized model are then fed into the downstream task for evaluation.
266   As shown in Table 2, the transformation weights learned by GCL are no better than random.
267   The model with random weights W1 and W2 attains a performance of 71.42%, remarkably close
268   to the 71.76% reached by the transformation weights learned through GCL. Notably, while random
269   projection (Bingham & Mannila, 2001) is well-established in the literature and proven effective in
      various works (Bauw et al., 2021; Li et al., 2006; Freund et al., 2007), GCL should aim to learn

      weights tailored on data, rather than relying on a random matrix. Therefore, the results indicate that

307   5.2                L EARNING P ROPAGATION IS P ROMISING IN GCL
308
309   Now, we take a comprehensive view of both the transformation and propagation phases. While
310   polynomial GNNs incorporate learnable parameters in both, GCLs utilizing polynomial GNNs, as
311
      shown in Section 4.2, tend to underperform. This issue has been recognized in prior work, often
      attributed to the mismatch between the strong fitting capacity of polynomial filters and the lack of
312
      supervision signals in self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2022; 2024). However, through the
313   following experiments, we demonstrate that GCLs are capable of learning effective filters.
314
315
      From the decoupling perspective, there are three conjectures as to why polynomial GNNs perform
      poorly in GCL: 1) GCL learns suboptimal transformation weights, 2) GCL learns ineffective propa-
316
      gation coefficients, or 3) a combination of both. To investigate the cause, we separately replace the
317   propagation coefficients θ and the transformation weights W with well-trained parameters from a
318   supervised setting. Specifically, we first train polynomial GNNs via supervised learning and save the
319   optimized parameters as WSL and θSL . We then proceed with the following experiments:
320
      Experiment 1 (Fix-propagation). Corresponding to the first conjecture, we initialize and freeze θ
321   with the well-trained θSL , and only learn W through GCL. Representations are generated by the
322   fixed propagation coefficients and learned transformation weights.

      8     C ONCLUSION
458
459
      In this work, we suggest a training-free method PROP as a strong baseline in GCL. From the
460   decoupling perspective, we observe that transformation weights learned through GCL present a
461   quite smooth and uninformative characteristic. We further propose to only learn the propagation
462   coefficients in the encoder of GCL, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on diverse no
463   classification benchmarks. We believe that this work opens new avenues for exploring lightweight
464   and effective graph contrastive learning methods, with broad implications for both research and
465
      practical applications in the field of graph learning.
466
467   9     L IMITATIONS
468
469   Our study highlights the strong performance of the propagation-only PROP method on diverse node
470   classification benchmarks, showcasing its simplicity and effectiveness. For graph classification tasks,
471   which may involve low-quality or absent node features, PROP offers an initial approach that can be
472
      further adapted (see Appendix A). Building on PROP, PROPGCL introduces learnable propagation
      through spectral filters, making it particularly effective for single-graph tasks. Future research can
473
      explore extending PROPGCL to multi-graph settings and enhancing its applicability across diverse
474   graph structures.

      B    G RAPH STRUCTURE AS SUPERVISED SIGNAL
811
812
      The taxonomy of homophily and heterophily is widely used to tell whether the graph structure is
813
      informative for training GCN-like models. Beyond the discussion on homophily and heterophily,
814   recent metrics characterizing graphs are proposed and show closer relationships with the GNN
815   performance (Mao et al., 2023; Luan et al., 2023; Platonov et al., 2023a). For example, Ma et al.
816   (2021) claim that the inter-class similarity on Squirrel is slightly higher than the intra-class similarity
817   for most classes, which substantiates the middling performance of GCN.
818   However, the performance of GCN-like models is an interplay between graph structure and node
819   features. Therefore, a bad GCN performance can not indicate the helplessness of graph structure,
820   or vice versa. For verification, we design experiments based on the mutual information of labels
821   and different graph elements. To escape from the entanglement of structure and node features, we
822   use MLP instead of GCN as the trainable model with node features X, adjacency matrix A, and the
823
      concatenation of the two as inputs, respectively. The correspondence is as follows:
824
             • I(Y; X): MLP with X as inputs.
825
826          • I(Y; A): MLP with A as inputs.
827
             • I(Y; X; A): MLP with [X, A] as inputs, where [] denotes concatenation.
828
829   The results are shown in Table 10. It is surprising that for some heterophily datasets, MLP with
830   the graph structure as inputs gets satisfying performance. For example, for the Squirrel dataset
831   with a low homophily ratio of 0.22, MLP based on the graph structure achieves 73.58% accuracy.
832   Therefore, even presenting a low homophily ratio, the graph structure can still serve as a highly
833   qualified supervision signal for predicting labels.

      In Section 5, we observe that GCL has the potential to learn good propagation coefficients. It inspires
      methods in the few-shot scenario, where a model is tasked with achieving effective generalization
851
      from a minimal number of labeled examples per class.
852
853   In this study, we examine the N -shot case where N support examples are used for training. As
854
      baselines, we evaluate the ChebNetII model trained with both supervised learning (SL) and contrastive
      learning (CL). As shown in Table 11, SL exhibits low accuracy due to sparse labeling, while CL
855
      performs relatively better, given access to all provided samples.
856
857   Based on our findings, we first train the ChebNetII model using contrastive learning. We then fix the
858
      propagation coefficients learned in GCL and focus on optimizing the transformation weights through
      a supervised objective. We term the method as Fix-prop SL. As illustrated in Table 11, this approach
859

861   results demonstrate the potential of integrating SL and CL from a decoupling perspective in few-shot
862   learning. However, the Fix-prop SL approach has minimal impact on the Squirrel and Chameleon
863   datasets. It is important to note that we keep hyperparameters consistent across all training methods
      and benchmarks, leaving ample room for further exploration beyond this initial investigation.

1366   We categorize baselines for the node classification task into 1) traditional graph embedding al-
1367   gorithms DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014) and Node2Vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016); 2) graph
1368   autoencoders GAE (Kipf & Welling, 2016), VGAE (Kipf & Welling, 2016); 3) graph contrastive
1369   methods GRACE (Zhu et al., 2020b), DGI (Velickovic et al., 2019), GCA (Zhu et al., 2021c), MV-
1370

       CCA-SSG (Zhang et al., 2021) and BGRL (Thakoor et al., 2022), 5) heterophily baselines compared
1371
       in Section 6.2, PolyGCL (Chen et al., 2024), HGRL (Chen et al., 2022), GraphACL (Xiao et al.,
1372   2024), SP-GCL (Wang et al., 2023), DSSL (Xiao et al., 2022).The design details are as follows.
1373
1374
       1) Traditional graph embeddings.
1375
               • DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014). DeepWalk leverages truncated random walks to capture
1376             local network structures. The algorithm treats the random walks as sequences of nodes, akin
1377             to sentences in language models. It learns latent representations by applying skip-gram to
1378             maximize the co-occurrence probabilities of nodes appearing in these random walks.
1379           • Node2Vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016). Node2Vec is built on DeepWalk by introducing a
1380             flexible biased random walk strategy to explore network neighborhoods. The key innovation
1381             is balancing breadth-first sampling (BFS) and depth-first sampling (DFS). This allows
1382             Node2Vec to capture both homophily and structural equivalence, making the learned node
1383             embeddings more expressive.
1384
       2) Graph autoencoders.
1385
1386           • GAE (Kipf & Welling, 2016). GAE involves an encoder-decoder architecture, where the
1387             encoder is a GCN that transforms node features into latent embeddings by aggregating
1388             information from neighboring nodes. The embeddings are then used by the decoder, which
1389             typically applies a simple inner product operation to reconstruct the graph structure, such as
1390             predicting edges between nodes.
1391           • VGAE (Kipf & Welling, 2016). VGAE extends GAE by introducing a probabilistic frame-
1392             work using a variational autoencoder (VAE) setup. It models latent variables with Gaussian
1393             distributions, enabling the generation of node embeddings that capture uncertainty. This
1394
                 design improves the model’s ability to capture complex structures in graphs, especially in
                 tasks like link prediction.
1395
1396   3) Graph contrastive methods.
1397
1398
       The mode of GCL has three mainstreams: local-to-local, global-to-global, and global-to-local (Zhu
       et al., 2021b). A classic example of local-to-local is GRACE (Zhu et al., 2020b), which generates
1399
       two graph views by augmentations and the same nodes in augmented views are positive while all
1400   the other node pairs are negative. Global-to-global mode is often used with multiple graphs in
1401   the graph classification task, with GraphCL (You et al., 2020) as an early but influential trial. For
1402   the global-to-local perspective, positive pairs are taken as the global representation and nodes of
1403   augmented views, and negative pairs are the global representation and nodes of corrupted views. DGI
       (Velickovic et al., 2019) is a typical example.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
             • GRACE (Zhu et al., 2020b). GRACE generates two graph views by corruption and learns
1405           node representations by maximizing the agreement of node representations in these two
1406           views. To provide diverse node contexts for the contrastive objective, GRACE proposes a
1407           hybrid scheme for generating graph views on both structure and attribute levels.
1408         • GCA (Zhu et al., 2021c). GCA proposes adaptive augmentation that incorporates various
1409           priors for topological and semantic aspects of the graph. On the topology level, GCA designs
1410           augmentation schemes based on node centrality measures, while on the node attribute level,
1411           GCA corrupts node features by adding more noise to unimportant node features.
1412         • DGI (Velickovic et al., 2019). DGI relies on maximizing mutual information between
1413           patch representations and corresponding high-level summaries of graphs—both derived
1414           using established graph convolutional network architectures. The learned patch representa-
1415           tions summarize subgraphs centered around nodes of interest, and can thus be reused for
1416
               downstream node-wise learning tasks.
1417         • MVGRL (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020). MVGRL introduces a self-supervised approach
1418           for learning node and graph-level representations by contrasting structural views of graphs.
1419
               MVGRL shows that contrasting multi-scale encodings does not improve performance, and
               the best performance is achieved by contrasting encodings from first-order neighbors and
1420
               graph diffusion.
1421
1422         • ProGCL (Xia et al., 2022). ProGCL observes limited benefits when adopting existing
               hard negative mining techniques of other domains in graph contrastive learning. ProGCL
1423
               proposes an effective method to estimate the probability of a negative being true and devises
1424           two schemes to boost the performance of GCL.
1425
1426   4) Non-contrastive methods.
1427
1428         • CCA-SSG (Zhang et al., 2021). CCA-SSG optimizes a novel feature-level objective
1429           that aligns features across different graph augmentations. It uses decorrelation to prevent
1430
               degenerate solutions, allowing the model to learn invariant node representations. The model
               avoids a mutual information estimator or negative samples, which simplifies training and
1431
               reduces computational complexity.
1432
1433
             • BGRL (Thakoor et al., 2022). BGRL avoids the use of negative samples by predicting
               different augmentations of the input graph. BGRL relies on a bootstrapping mechanism,
1434
               where one branch predicts the output of another branch that is not updated by gradient de-
1435           scent. This method eliminates the complexity of contrastive learning and negative sampling,
1436           making it more scalable.
1437
1438   5) Heterophily baselines.
1439
1440
             • PolyGCL (Chen et al., 2024). PolyGCL integrates spectral polynomial filters into graph
               contrastive learning, enabling it to handle both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. The
1441
               method generates different spectral views using polynomials and incorporates high-pass
1442           information into the contrastive objective.
1443
             • HGRL (Chen et al., 2022). HGRL introduces self-supervised learning for heterophilic
1444
               graphs by capturing distant neighbors and preserving original node features. It achieves
1445           this through carefully designed pretext tasks optimized via high-order mutual information,
1446           avoiding reliance on labels.
1447
             • GraphACL (Xiao et al., 2024). GraphACL focuses on an asymmetric view of neighboring
1448           nodes. The algorithm captures both one-hop local neighborhood information and two-hop
1449           monophily similarity, crucial for modeling heterophilic structures.
1450
             • SP-GCL (Wang et al., 2023). SP-GCL introduces a single-pass graph contrastive learning
1451           method without augmentations. It theoretically guarantees performance across both ho-
1452           mophilic and heterophilic graphs by studying the concentration property of features obtained
1453           through neighborhood propagation.
1454         • DSSL (Xiao et al., 2022). DSSL decouples neighborhood semantics in self-supervised
1455           learning for node representation. It introduces a latent variable model that decouples node
1456           and link generation, making it flexible to different graph structures. The method utilizes
1457           variational inference for scalable optimization, improving downstream performance without
               relying on homophily assumptions.

       We categorize the baselines in the graph classification task into 1) graph kernel methods including
1459   GL (Shervashidze et al., 2009), WL (Shervashidze et al., 2011), and DGK (Yanardag & Vishwanathan,
1460   2015), 2) traditional graph embedding methods including node2vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016),
1461   sub2vec (Adhikari et al., 2018), and graph2vec (Narayanan et al., 2017), 3) contrastive learning
1462   methods including InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2020), GraphCL (You et al., 2020), MVGRL (Hassani &
1463   Khasahmadi, 2020), JOAOv2 (You et al., 2021), ADGCL (Suresh et al., 2021) as introduced in recent
1464
       works. The design details are as follows.
1465   1) Graph kernel methods.
1466
1467          • Graphlet Kernel (GL) (Shervashidze et al., 2009). GL works by counting the number
                of small subgraphs (known as graphlets) of a fixed size that appear in each graph. The
1468
                comparison of these counts across graphs allows the kernel to capture the local topological
1469            structures of the graphs, making it useful for tasks such as graph classification.
1470
              • Weisfeiler-Lehman Sub-tree Kernel (WL) (Shervashidze et al., 2011). WL extends the
1471
                concept of graph kernels by applying the Weisfeiler-Lehman test of isomorphism on graphs.
1472            It involves iteratively relabeling the nodes of the graphs based on the labels of their neighbors
1473            and then using these relabelings to define a kernel, typically counting matching sub-trees.
1474          • Deep Graph Kernel (DGK) (Yanardag & Vishwanathan, 2015). DGK combines deep
1475            learning techniques with graph kernels. It first learns a low-dimensional representation
1476            of the graphs through unsupervised learning (often using a form of graph embedding or
1477            autoencoders), then applies traditional kernel methods to these representations.
1478
1479
       2) Traditional graph embeddings.
1480          • Node2Vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016). Node2Vec is built on DeepWalk by introducing a
1481            flexible biased random walk strategy to explore network neighborhoods. The key innovation
1482            is balancing BFS and DFS. This allows Node2Vec to capture both homophily and structural
1483            equivalence, making the learned node embeddings more expressive.
1484          • Sub2Vec (Adhikari et al., 2018). Inspired by the word2vec model, sub2vec learns vector
1485            representations for subgraphs in a graph. It treats each subgraph as a "word" and the
1486            entire graph as a "document" to learn embeddings that capture the structural and contextual
1487            properties of subgraphs.
1488          • Graph2Vec (Narayanan et al., 2017). Similar to sub2vec, graph2vec is designed to learn
1489            embeddings for entire graphs. By treating each graph as a "document" and graph sub-
1490            structures as "words," graph2vec employs a document embedding approach to learn a
1491
                fixed-size vector representation for each graph.
1492   3) Graph contrastive methods.
1493
1494          • GraphCL (You et al., 2020). GraphCL designs four types of graph augmentations to
1495            incorporate various priors and learns graph-level representations by maximizing the global
1496            representations of two views for a graph.
1497          • InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2020). InfoGraph maximizes the mutual information between the
1498            graph-level representation and the representations of substructures of different scales (e.g.,
1499
                nodes, edges, triangles). By doing so, the graph-level representations encode aspects of the
                data that are shared across different scales of substructures.
1500
1501          • ADGCL (Suresh et al., 2021). ADGCL proposes a novel principle, adversarial GCL, which
1502
                enables GNNs to avoid capturing redundant information during training by optimizing
                adversarial graph augmentation strategies used in GCL.
1503
1504          • JOAO (You et al., 2021). JOAO proposes a unified bi-level optimization framework to
                automatically, adaptively, and dynamically select data augmentations when performing
1505
                GraphCL on specific graph data. JOAO is instantiated as min-max optimization.
