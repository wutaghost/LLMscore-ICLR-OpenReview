009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            State-of-the-art link prediction (LP) models demonstrate impressive benchmark
012            results. However, popular benchmark datasets often assume that training, valida-
013            tion, and testing samples are representative of the overall dataset distribution. In
014            real-world situations, this assumption is often incorrect; since uncontrolled factors
015            lead to the problem where new dataset samples come from different distributions
016            than training samples. The vast majority of recent work focuses on dataset shift
017            affecting node- and graph-level tasks, largely ignoring link-level tasks. To bridge
018
               this gap, we introduce a novel splitting strategy, known as LPShift, which utilizes
               structural properties to induce a controlled distribution shift. We verify the ef-
019
               fect of LPShift through empirical evaluation of SOTA LP methods on 16 LPShift
020
               generated splits of Open Graph Benchmark (OGB) datasets. When benchmarked
021            with LPShift datasets, GNN4LP methods frequently generalize worse than heuris-
022            tics or basic GNNs. Furthermore, LP-specific generalization techniques do little
023            to improve performance under LPShift. Finally, further analysis provides insight
024            on why LP models lose much of their architectural advantages under LPShift.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Link Prediction (LP) is concerned with predicting unseen links (i.e., edges) between two nodes in
030   a graph (Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003). The task has a wide variety of applications including:
031   recommender systems, (Fan et al., 2019), knowledge graph completion (Lin et al., 2015), protein-
032   interaction (Kovács et al., 2019), and drug discovery (Abbas et al., 2021). Traditionally, LP was
033
      performed using heuristics that model the pairwise interaction between two nodes (Newman, 2001;
      Zhou et al., 2009; Adamic & Adar, 2003). The success of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf
034
      & Welling, 2017) has prompted their usage in LP (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Zhang & Chen, 2018).
035
      However, GNNs are unable to fully-capture representations for node pairs (Zhang et al., 2021; Srini-
036   vasan & Ribeiro, 2019). To combat this problem, recent methods (i.e., GNN4LP) empower GNNs
037   with additional information to capture pairwise interactions between nodes (Shomer et al., 2024;
038   Chamberlain et al., 2022; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Wang et al., 2023a) and demonstrate tremendous
039   ability to model LP on real-world datasets (Hu et al., 2020).
040
      While recent methods have shown promise, current benchmarks (Hu et al., 2020) assume that the
041
      training and evaluation data are drawn from the same structural distribution, with the exception
042   of datasets like ogbl-collab. This assumption often collapses in real-world scenarios, where the
043   structural feature (i.e., covariate) distribution may shift from training to evaluation. Therefore, it’s
044   often necessary for models to generalize to samples whose newly-introduced feature distribution
045   differs from the the training dataset (Wiles et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022a;b; Bevilacqua et al., 2021).
046

      Furthermore, while numerous methods work to account for distribution shifts within graph machine
076
      learning (Li et al., 2022b), there remains little work doing so for LP. Specifically, we observe that
077
      (1) No LP Benchmark Datasets: Current graph benchmark datasets designed with a quantifiable
078   distribution shift are focused solely on the node and graph tasks (Zhou et al., 2022b; Ding et al.,
079   2021), with no datasets available for LP. (2) Absence of Foundational Work: There is limited ex-
080   isting work for distribution shifts relevant to LP (Zhang et al., 2022). Current methods are primarily
081   focused on detecting and alleviating anomalies within node- and graph-level tasks (Jin et al., 2022;
082   Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Sui
083   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022b). Additionally, few methods are designed for aiding LP generalization
084   in any setting (Dong et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b). Also,
085   other LP generalization methods which are theorized to improve performance in shifted scenarios
086
      remain crucially untested (Singh et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b).
087   To tackle these problems, this work proposes the following contributions:
088
089          • Creating Datasets with Meaningful Distribution Shifts. LP requires pairwise struc-
090            tural considerations (Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003; Mao et al., 2024). Additionally,
091            when considering realistic settings (Li et al., 2024) or distribution shift (Zhu et al., 2024),
092            GNN4LP models perform poorly relative to models used in graph (Wei et al., 2021; Yuan
093            et al., 2021) and node classification (Shi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). To better un-
094            derstand distribution shifts, we use key structural LP heuristics to split the links into
095            train/validation/test splits via LPShift. By applying LPShift to generate dataset splits, we
096
               induce shifts in the underlying feature distribution of the links which are relevant to the
               link’s formation (Mao et al., 2024). Further justification is provided in Appendix Section L.
097
098
099
             • Benchmarking Current LP Methods. To our surprise, GNN4LP models struggle more
               than simpler methods when generalizing to data split by LPShift. Despite the existence
100
               of LP generalization methods, such as FakeEdge (Dong et al., 2022) and Edge Proposal
101
               Sets (Singh et al., 2021), there remains little work benchmarking link-prediction models
102            under distribution shifts (Ding et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024). This lack
103            of benchmarking contributes to a gap in understanding, impeding the capabilities of LP
104            models to generalize. This work quantifies the performance of current SOTA LP models
105            under 16 unique LPShift scenarios and provides analysis as a foundation for improving
106            LP model generalization. We further quantify the effects of LP and graph-specific gen-
107            eralization methods, finding that they also struggle to generalize with differing structural
               shifts.

139   GNNs for Link Prediction (GNN4LP): LP’s current SOTA methods rely on GNNs for a given
140   model’s backbone. The most common choice is the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf &
141   Welling, 2017), integrating a simplified convolution operator to consider a node’s multi-hop neigh-
142   borhood. The final score (i.e., probability) of a link existing considers the representation between
143   both nodes of interest. However, (Zhang et al., 2021) show that such methods aren’t suitably expres-
144   sive for LP, as they ignore vital pairwise information that exists between both nodes. To account for
145   this, SEAL (Zhang & Chen, 2018) conditions the message passing on both nodes in the target link
146   by applying a node-labelling trick to the enclosed k-hop neighborhood. They demonstrate that this
147
      can result in a suitably expressive GNN for LP. NBFNet (Zhu et al., 2021) conditions the message
      passing on a single node in the target link by parameterizing the generalized Bellman-Ford algo-
148
      rithm. In practice, it’s been shown that conditional message passing is prohibitively expensive to
149
      run on many LP datasets (Chamberlain et al., 2022). Instead, recent methods pass both the standard
150   GNN representations and an additional pairwise encoding into the scoring function for prediction.
151   For the pairwise encoding, Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021) considers the higher-order overlap between
152   neighborhoods. BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022) estimates subgraph counts via sketching to infer
153   information surrounding a target link. Neural Common-Neighbors with Completion (NCNC) (Wang
154   et al., 2023a) encodes the enclosed 1-hop neighborhood of both nodes. Lastly, LPFormer (Shomer
155   et al., 2024) adapts a transformer to learn the pairwise information between two nodes.

188   In this section, we explain how LPShift induces a shift in each dataset’s structure; clarifying the
189   importance of each structural measure and their application to splitting graph data.

191
      3.1   T YPES OF D ISTRIBUTION S HIFTS
192
193   We induce distribution shifts by splitting the links based on key structural properties affecting link
194   formation and thereby LP. We consider three type of metrics: Local structural information, Global
195   structural information, and Preferential Attachment. Recent work by (Mao et al., 2023) has shown
196   the importance of local and global structural information for LP. Furthermore, due to the scale-
197   free nature of many real-world graphs relates to link formation (Barabási & Albert, 1999), we also
198   consider Preferential Attachment. A representative metric is then chosen for each of the three types,
199   shown as follows:
200   (1) Common Neighbors (CNs): CNs measure local structural information by considering only
201   those nodes connected to the target and source nodes. A real-world case for CNs is whether you
202   share mutual friends with a random person, thus determining if they are your “friend-of-a-friend”
203   (Adamic & Adar, 2003). CNs plays a large role in GNN4LP, given that NCNC (Wang et al., 2023a)
204   and EPS (Singh et al., 2021) integrate CNs into their framework and achieve SOTA performance.
205   Furthermore even on complex real-world datasets, CNs achieves competitive performance against
206
      more advanced neural models (Hu et al., 2020). To control for the effect of CNs on shifted perfor-
      mance, the relevant splits will consider thresholds which include more CNs.
207
208   (2) Shortest Path (SP): SP captures a graph’s global structural information, thanks to the shortest-
209   path between a given target and source node representing the most efficient path for reaching the
210   target (Russell & Norvig, 2009). The shift in global structure caused by splitting data with SP
211   can induce a scenario where a model must learn how two dissimilar nodes form a link with one
212
      another (Evtushenko & Kleinberg, 2021), which is comparable to the real-world scenario where two
      opponents choose to co-operate with one another (Schelling, 1978; Granovetter, 1978).
213
214   (3) Preferential Attachment (PA): PA captures the scale-free property of larger graphs by mul-
215   tiplying the degrees between two given nodes (Barabási & Albert, 1999). When applied to graph
      generation, PA produces synthetic Barabasi-Albert (BA) graphs which retain the scale-free property

216
      to effectively simulate the formation of new links in real-world graphs, such as the World Wide Web
217   (Barabási & Albert, 1999; Albert & Barabási, 2002). Similar to CNs, the relevant PA splits will
218   consider thresholds that integrate higher PA values.

      3.2   DATASET S PLITTING S TRATEGY
221
222   In the last subsection we described the different types of metrics to induce distribution shifts for LP.
223   The metrics cover fundamental structural properties that influence the formation of new links. We
224   now describe how we use these measures to split the dataset into train/validation/test splits to induce
225   such shifts.
226
      In order to build datasets with structural shift, we apply a given neighborhood heuristic to score
227
      each link. This score is then compared to a threshold (itrain , ivalid ) to categorize a link as a dif-
228   ferent sample. As denoted in Alg. 1, the heuristic score of the link (u, v) is h(u, v). The link falls
229   into: training when h(u, v) < itrain , validation when itrain < h(u, v) ≤ ivalid , and testing when
230   h(u, v) > ivalid . The new training graph is constructed from the original OGB dataset (Hu et al.,
231   2020). Validation and testing samples are removed from the new training graph to prevent test-
232   leakage and limited to 100-thousand edges maximum. The full algorithm is detailed in Algorithm 1
233   with additional details in Appendix C.
234   With Figure 2, we provide a small example of how splits are produced by our proposed splitting
235   strategy. Specifically, Figure 2(a) demonstrates an outcome of the CN split labelled “CN - 1,2”
236   where sampled edges pulled from the: black-dotted line = training (no CNs), red-dotted line =
237   validation, (1 CN), and blue-dotted line = testing (≥2 CNs). See Appendix C for information on
238   Figure 2(b) and Figure 2(c).

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
274
275   Datasets: We consider 16 “Forward” and “Backward” LPShift splits of the ogbl-collab and ogbl-ppa
276   datasets (Hu et al., 2020), for a total of 32 tested splits. The resulting datasets represent tasks in two
277   separate domains and three shifted scenarios, allowing a comprehensive study of LP generalization
278   under distribution shift. For both datasets, we create multiple splits corresponding to each structural
279   property detailed in Section 3.2. For the “Forward” split, denoted as (X, Y, Z), an increase in Y and
280   Z indicates more structural information available to the training adjacency matrix. The “Backward”
281
      split swaps the training and testing splits from their counterpart in the “Forward” split, resulting
      in the training adjacency matrix losing access to structural information as X and Y increase. See
282
      Appendix K for more details.
283
284   GNN4LP Methods: We test multiple SOTA GNN4LP methods including: NCNC (Wang et al.,
285   2023a), BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022), LPFormer (Shomer et al., 2024), SEAL (Zhang et al.,
286   2021) and Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021). We further consider GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017) as a
287
      simpler GNN baseline, along with the Resource Allocation (RA) (Zhou et al., 2009) heuristic. All
      models were selected based on their benchmark performance with the original OGB datsets (Hu
288
      et al., 2020) and their architectural differences detailed in Section 2.
289
290   Generalization Methods: We also test the performance of different LP models with multiple gen-
291   eralization techniques. This includes DropEdge (Rong et al., 2020), which randomly removes a
292   portion of edges from the training adjacency matrix. Edge Proposal Sets (EPS) (Singh et al., 2021),
293
      which utilizes one LP model to filter edges based on common neighbors and another method to rank
      the top-k filtered edges in the training adjacency matrix. Lastly, we consider Topological Concen-
294
      tration (TC) (Wang et al., 2023b), which re-weights the edges within the training adjacency matrix
295
      based on the structural information captured by the TC metric.
296
297   Evaluation Setting: We consider the standard evaluation procedure in LP, in which every posi-
298   tive validation/test sample is compared against M negative samples. The goal is that the model
299
      should output a higher score (i.e., probability) for positive sample than the negatives. To create the
      negatives, we make use of the HeaRT evaluation setting (Li et al., 2024) which generates M nega-
300
      tives samples per positive sample according to a set of common LP heuristics. In our study, we set
301
      M = 250 and use CNs as the heuristic in HeaRT.
302
303   Evaluation Metrics: We evaluate all methods using multiple ranking metrics as a standard practice
304   in LP literature (Li et al., 2024). This includes the mean reciprocal rank (MRR) and Hits@20.
305   Hyperparameters: All methods were tuned on permutations of learning rates in {1e−2 , 1e−3 } and
306   dropout in {0.1, 0.3}. Each model was trained and tested over five seeds to obtain the mean and
307   standard deviations of their results. Given the significant time complexity of training and testing
308   on the customized ogbl-ppa datasets, NCNC and LPFormer were tuned on a single seed, followed
309   by an evaluation of the tuned model on five separate seeds. We include additional hyperparameter
310   details within Appendix Section K

450
451   How effective is LPShift at inducing distribution shifts? The following section will explore the
452   capability of LPShift to induce a measurably-significant distribution shift in the structure of the ogbl-
453
      collab dataset. We apply the 2-sample Kolmgorov-Smirnov (KS) test (Hodges Jr, 1958) to compare
      if training, validation, and testing distributions can be sampled from one another, both before and
454
      after applying LPShift. As a controlled baseline to test LPShift, we generate a Holme-Kim (HK)
455
      graph (Holme & Kim, 2002) with a 40% chance to close a triangle, allowing the HK graph to contain
456   numerous Common Neighbors without becoming fully-connected. HK graph generation parameters
457   are included in Appendix D.
458
459
      The first subplot in Figure 5 extends the reasoning introduced with NCNC (Wang et al., 2023a). As
      such, this subplot indicates there is a natural shift for CNs within the original ogbl-collab dataset (Hu
460
      et al., 2020). The p-value of 0, measured across both split permutations, indicates that the training
461
      distribution of CNs is shifted from the validation and testing distributions. The second subplot
462   depicts a randomly-split HK graph (Holme & Kim, 2002), where CN distributions for each split
463   match one another, further indicated by the p-values of 1. The third subplot depicts the HK graph
464   from the second subplot split with LPShift’s CN - 5,3 strategy, resulting in a distinct shift between
465   all dataset splits, as confirmed by the 0 p-values. As such, LPShift induces structural shift that is as
466   measurably dissimilar as the structural shift present in the original ogbl-collab dataset, even when
467   the initial dataset splits are measurably identical. Additionally, the CN - 5,3 split causes the shape
468   of the HK graph’s CN distributions to become more similar to the shift observed within the original
469   ogbl-collab dataset, indicating that the ”Backward” LPShift strategy can function like a real-world
470
      distribution shift.
471   Does GNN4LP generalize and do generalization methods work? As detailed in Section 4, basic
472   GNN and Heuristic methods perform competitively compared to GNN4LP models. This observation
473   coupled with the limited success of LP generalization methods, indicates the challenging problem
474   LPShift poses. As shown in Appendix Section J, the ”Backward” CN and SP splits on ogbl-collab
475   and all ogbl-ppa splits result in a 30−90% performance decrease from the HeaRT standard (Li et al.,
476
      2024). This is especially notable given the difficulty HeaRT imposes on the original benchmark
      setting (Hu et al., 2020). We also note that the majority of PA and forward splits for ogbl-collab result
477
      in a 2-3 times performance increase. This result quantifies the prevailing assumption that GNN4LP
478
      generalize well in scenarios with increasing structural information (Mao et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
479   2023a). However, LPShift’s ogbl-ppa suffers from the inverse scenario, where the majority of splits
480   result in a 60 − 90% decrease in performance. Therefore indicating that LPShift has stronger effects
481   across dataset domains, particularly when the dataset is larger. DropEdge is the only method to
482   (Rong et al., 2020) consistently improve LP performance when handling LPShift for the ogbl-collab
483   dataset. Traditional generalization methods (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Sagawa et al., 2019; Ganin et al.,
484   2016; Sun & Saenko, 2016), such as VREx (Krueger et al., 2021), achieve some promising results.
485   However, these gains are typically marginal, especially on the ”Backward” ogbl-collab split; as
      indicated in Appendix Section H. Given LPShift’s impact on GNN4LP performance as well it’s

526   and 6, then augmenting the original graph structure with additional structural information, may
527   require a finer-scale focus than top-k CNs (Singh et al., 2021) to “repair” the missing structural
528   components in the adjacency matrix and enhance generalization under LPShift. As such, it would
529
      be pertinent for LP models to extrapolate pairwise information from more varieties of structural
      information in order to improve performance in shifted scenarios.

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   This work proposes LPShift, a simple dataset splitting strategy for inducing structural shift relevant
535   for link prediction. The effect of this structural shift was then benchmarked on 16 shifted versions
536   of ogbl-collab and ogbl-ppa, posing a unique challenge for SOTA GNN4LP models and general-
537   ization methods. Further analysis with EMD calculations and CN distributions indicate that current
538   generalization methods do not improve performance under structural shift. As such, LPShift pro-
539   vides a challenging problem requiring new considerations about how structure functions within link
      prediction architectures.

760          • Adversarial Recommender Systems = Shortest-Path: A company may want to under-
761            stand which products to avoid showing to a potential customer without need to hear the
762            user’s preferences directly. In this scenario, global information, as captured by Shortest-
763            Path, becomes the most valuable for the specific use-case.
764
             • Social Recommender Systems = Preferential-Attachment: A video-streaming platform
765            working with independent content creators may wish to understand what drives users to en-
766            gage with the platform’s content creators, so that engagement can increase for less-popular
767            creators. As a starting point, the content creators with the most followers may have differ-
768            ent characteristics that increase engagement versus less-popular creators. So, the streaming
769            platform may wish to tune their dataset to determine if their recommendation system can
770            generalize from more to less-popular creators.
771          • Movie Recommender Systems = Common-Neighbors: A movie-streaming platform
772            wants to provide suggestions to users that are the most relevant to the user’s current in-
773            terests. So, the movie platform sorts possible movie recommendations by how much over-
774            lap the movies share with one another. In order to enhance exposure to new movies that
775            overlap with a user’s interests, the streaming platform can apply the Common Neighbor
776            LPShift to their dataset to force the algorithm to generalize in a scenario where movies
777            may not fully-overlap.

789   This section provides additional details about the way data was formatted before being used as
790   input for Algorithm 1 of our proposed splitting strategy and the intuition behind how Preferential-
791   Attachment and Shortest-Path work within the splitting strategy. The details on the algorithm in-
792   cludes:
793
794          • Validation and Testing Edges are limited to 100k edges total.
795          • PPA Training Edges are limited to 3 million edges total.
796
             • Negative Testing and Validation edges are produced via HeaRT (Li et al., 2024).
797
798          • Validation and testing edges that are duplicated with training edges are removed from the
799            edge index.
800          • In order to provide overlap within a given dataset, validation and testing edges that do not
801            connect to training nodes are removed from the edge index.
802
             • After sampling the necessary training edges, the adjacency matrix is extracted from the
803
               edge index, converted to an undirected graph and has any edge weights standardized to 1.
804
805   Common Neighbors, Preferential-Attachment and Shortest-Path, as shown in Figure 2(a), 2(b), and
806   2(c) respectively, are interchangeable within the dataset splitting strategy. Details about how Com-
807   mon Neighbors functions within the strategy are included in Section 3.2. Figure 2(b) and Figure 2(c)
808   serve as toy examples and do not correspond directly to any dataset splits tested within our study.
809   However, the examples illustrated within Figure 2(b) and Figure 2(c) do correspond to how their
      given heuristic functions within our splitting strategy.

      For Figure 2(b) or Preferential-Attachment, it determines the degrees between a given source and
811   target node and then multiples the two to produce the score, based on that score, the sample is then
812   sorted into a new dataset split.
813
814
      For Figure 2(c) or Shortest-Path, the heuristic determines the score by determining the minimum
      number of nodes necessary to reach the target node from the source node. If there is a link between
815

816
      lation. The final Shortest-Path score applies the calculated shortest-path length, SP (u, v) as the

       L   W HY USE LPS HIFT ? P ERSPECTIVES ON D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT.
1317
1318         • Data Perspective: Link prediction is a task focused on understanding the dynamics when
1319           edges form between nodes; requiring models to effectively understand these dynamics to
1320           determine whether a link will form (or not) (Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003). As such,
1321
               link prediction is more interested in pairwise dynamics than what is necessary for graph
               and node classification. Due to this distinction, there is limited overlap between graph/node
1322
               classification and link prediction. As such, distribution shifts that are relevant in graph and
1323
               node classification do not mean as much in link prediction. For example, we consider
1324           the scaffold shift imposed on molecule datasets within the DrugOOD benchmark (Ji et al.,
1325           2022). This special type of structural shift groups molecules with similar subgraphs to
1326           induce an out-of-distribution scenario. Given that the scaffold shift in DrugOOD is not
1327           associated with the formation of links, then the shift loses it’s relevancy when applied to
1328           models that learn pairwise dynamics necessary for link prediction.
1329
1330         • Model Perspective: Structural heuristics that consider pairwise information are important
1331
               for SOTA GNN4LP models. For example, BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022) integrates
               RA directly into it’s architecture and NCNC (Wang et al., 2023a) elevates CN into a neural
1332
               architecture. The integration of these structural heuristics allow the link prediction models
1333
               to achieve SOTA performance. However, as demonstrated in Tables 23 and 24, GNN4LP’s
1334           reliance on structural heuristics leads to degraded performance when LPShift induces struc-
1335           tural shift within the graph dataset. The performance of models less reliant on structure,
1336           such as GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), do not degrade as significantly. This indicates that
1337           future models must balance between learning on graph structure and understanding shifts
1338           in link formation to improve performance under LPShift; especially in scenarios analogous
1339           to real-life (i.e. Figure 1).
1340
1341
             • Application Perspective: There is a practical difference between graph or node classifica-
               tion and link prediction as downstream tasks for graph representation learning (Hu et al.,
1342
               2020). Even though a graph or node classification model could consider pairwise dynam-
1343
               ics; it is limiting for a link prediction model to only consider node labels when predicting if
1344           edges will form (Yun et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Pairwise dynamics are distinct in that
1345           they determine how a graph forms on a finer scale, whereas node information constitutes
1346           what a graph is made of (Adamic & Adar, 2003; Barabási & Albert, 1999). The distinct-
1347           ness of these pairwise dynamics then necessitates dedicated evaluation to edge structures
1348           within the graphs. As such, LPShift serves as a measurable means to define a shift in
1349           these pairwise dynamics, allowing evaluation in a controlled setting that is relevant for link
               prediction.

       2022b) shift). Additionally, since the neighborhood heuristics compute discrete scores produced
1361
       from an input graph’s structural information and effectively training GNN4LP models requires no
1362   leakage with validation/testing, it may be difficult to determine the correct thresholds to extract a
1363   meaningful number of samples. For Common Neighbors and Preferential-Attachment, this is espe-
1364   cially relevant with smaller training graphs, given that larger and/or denser graphs have inherently
1365   more edges. Therefore, larger and denser graphs have inherently more possible Common Neighbors
1366   and Preferential-Attachment scores. For Shortest-Path, splitting can be exceptionally difficult for
1367   denser graphs, as demonstrated with the tiny split sizes for ogbl-ppa in Table 6.
