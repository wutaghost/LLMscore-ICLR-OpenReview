011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013             Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) integrate the linguistic capabili-
014             ties of LLMs with the ability to process multimodal data, enabling them to address
015
                a wider array of tasks. However, a comprehensive and standardized benchmark for
                evaluating MLLMs’ complex visual reasoning performance in multimodal tasks
016
                has yet to be established. We introduce GridAgent, a versatile 2D grid-based
017
                framework that serves as a benchmark for assessing five essential capabilities of
018             MLLMs: execution, perception reasoning, memory, learning, and planning.
019             The framework includes twelve unique game tasks specifically designed to avoid
020             overlap with the model’s pre-training corpus. Each task targets at least one core
021             competency and is enriched with diverse semantic information. Additionally, the
022             game layouts are randomly generated, ensuring a more rigorous and authentic
023             assessment of the MLLMs’ capabilities. Experimental results indicate that al-
024             though certain MLLMs excel in specific capabilities, none exhibit a comprehen-
025             sive skill set comparable to the human baseline. Our work can be seen at: https:
026
                //iclr2025gridagent.github.io/GridAgent-website/.

051   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant success across language-based
052   tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Sharan et al., 2023), laying a strong foundation for advancements in
053   artificial intelligence. Following this success, multimodal large language models (MLLMs), which
      integrate multiple data modalities such as images (Wang et al., 2024e), videos (Cai et al., 2024; Wang


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2024b) and speech (Çoban et al., 2024; Cappellazzo et al., 2024), are also experiencing rapid
055   growth and development. By fusing diverse information sources, MLLMs enable AI to learn and
056   reason (Gao et al., 2024) across different modalities, bringing it closer to human-like cognition (Du
057   et al., 2024). This cross-modal learning capability (Li et al., 2022) enhances the flexibility and gen-
058   eralization of AI systems, potentially leading to more comprehensive decision-making (Chen et al.,
059   2024a) and transfer learning (Wu et al., 2024b), which are viewed as important steps toward the
060   realization of artificial general intelligence (AGI) (Morris et al., 2024).
061   While research on large LLMs has increasingly concentrated on evaluating and improving their
062   advanced capabilities, such as reasoning (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Shinn et al., 2023), problem-
063   solving (Zhong et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023), and long-tern planning (Hong et al., 2023; Ahn
064   et al.), much of the MLLM research still primarily focuses on the effectiveness of these models in
065   understanding images and other non-text modalities. There is currently no comprehensive and uni-
066   fied benchmark to evaluate whether MLLMs, after acquiring such multimodal understanding, can
067   demonstrate the same remarkable capabilities across multimodal tasks as LLMs do in natural lan-
068   guage processing (Radford et al., 2019; Nasution & Onan, 2024). The absence of such a benchmark
069
      hampers the ability to systematically assess and compare the performance of different MLLMs,
      making it challenging to identify their strengths and weaknesses. This lack of standardization may
070
      also slow down progress in the field, as researchers and practitioners lack clear metrics to guide their
071
      work, ultimately hindering the development of more capable and versatile AI systems.
072
073   In this work, we introduce GridAgent, a benchmark specifically designed for evaluating MLLM
074   agents. To assess the gap between MLLMs and human-level capabilities (Lake et al., 2016; Xie
075
      et al., 2024; Koh et al., 2024), we draw inspiration from the widely recognized human intelligence
      test (Smith & Gasser, 2005), Wechsler Intelligence Scales (Guertin et al., 1966; Zhu et al., 2004; Park
076
      & Demakis, 2017), and propose five essential abilities that MLLMs require: execution, perception
077
      reasoning, memory, learning and planning. We design a series of tasks to evaluate the individual
078   capabilities of MLLMs, alongside composite tasks that assess more complex abilities, such as the
079   integration of memory and planning (Xu et al., 2024b). Our tasks also feature varying difficulty
080   levels to test the performance of more advanced MLLMs. Additionally, we pre-configure a variety
081   of background images containing semantic information and incorporate different types of game
082   items to construct specific semantic scenes. To ensure variability, each game is initialized with a
083   randomized layout. This multi-dimensional evaluation framework aims to assess the generalization
084   and robustness of MLLMs, offering a more comprehensive measure of their performance beyond
085   reliance on pre-training data (Zhang et al., 2024b).
086   Experimental results show that GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) outperforms other models, achieving the
087   highest success rate across most tasks and demonstrating a significant advantage in planning-related
088   tasks. Other models exhibit strengths in specific areas; for instance, Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024) excels
089   in abstract perception reasoning tasks. However, no MLLM currently possesses a comprehensive
090   capability comparable to that of humans, with some models even performing worse than random
091   baselines, falling well below human benchmarks.
092   Our contributions are as follows:
093
094          • We propose five key abilities, inspired by the Wechsler Intelligence Test, to evaluate both
095            the individual and composite capabilities of MLLMs.
096          • We introduce GridAgent, the first unified game benchmark specifically designed for
097            MLLMs, featuring diverse semantic environments, randomized layouts, and varying dif-
098            ficulty levels to ensure a generalizable and robust assessment.
099          • We conducted standard tests on seven MLLMs, and our empirical results highlighted the
100            current capability deficiencies in these models.

      expanded the applicability of MLLMs in areas such as image description (Liu et al., 2016; Tan et al.,

      2024), image reasoning (Ilievski & Feng, 2017; Wang et al., 2024d; Xiao et al., 2024), and visual
109   question answering (VQA) (Gaur et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a), bringing us closer to the ultimate
110   goal of AI research: general artificial intelligence (AGI) (Zhong et al., 2024), which aims to develop

114   2.2   MLLM B ENCHMARK
115
116   Many benchmarks have been introduced to assess the capabilities and performance of MLLMs.
117   However, most of them primarily focus on evaluating MLLMs’ ability to process and understand
118   multi-modal data, such as image comprehension and analysis (Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Yin
119   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024). In addition to these, some benchmarks specifically
120   evaluate MLLMs’ capacity for human-level planning (Chen et al., 2024b), while others focus on
121
      improving the models’ ability to make embodied decisions (Chen et al., 2023). While these assess-
      ments offer valuable insights into MLLMs’ performance on individual tasks, there is currently no
122
      comprehensive benchmark, akin to GLUE (Wang et al., 2019; Sarlin et al., 2020) for LLMs, that
123
      evaluates MLLMs across a diverse range of tasks. Furthermore, existing benchmarks lack a system-
124   atic definition of MLLMs’ capabilities and do not offer a well-defined classification of assessment
125   tasks, as seen in HELM (Liang et al., 2023) and BIG-Bench (Srivastava et al., 2023).
126
127
      Using games (Bellemare et al., 2018; Juliani et al., 2019; Samvelyan et al., 2021; Gan et al., 2021;
      Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) as benchmarking tools offers a unique approach to assessing LLM
128
      capabilities. For example, SmartPlay (Wu et al., 2024d) integrates six classic games, including
129
      Minecraft (Johnson et al., 2016) and Crafter (Hafner, 2021). It converts game scenarios into text-
130   based descriptions to evaluate core LLM abilities, such as instruction following and error correction.
131   GameBench (Costarelli et al., 2024) utilizes tabletop games to assess reasoning skills. Additionally,
132   concurrent studies have explored the use of board games (Topsakal et al., 2024) and open-ended
133   wargames (Hogan & Brennen, 2024) for benchmarking LLMs. While these benchmarks have sig-
134   nificantly advanced LLM development, they primarily focus on text-based evaluations and are not
135   well-suited for MLLMs.
136   Given that MLLMs are still in the early stages of development, game benchmarks based on existing
137   entertainment-focused games often overwhelm these models with excessive “redundant” details (Li
138   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). While complex visual elements such as intricate character de-
139   signs, background scenery, and narrative dialogue enhance the experience for human players, they
140   do not meaningfully contribute to the models’ reasoning and problem-solving processes. Further-
141   more, many existing games have been documented extensively, which can give an unfair advantage
142   to MLLMs that have been pre-trained on data containing information about these games. As a result,
143   a benchmark that presents well-designed tasks with limited redundant visual complexity, avoids re-
144
      liance on pre-documented games, and focuses on testing MLLMs’ reasoning, problem-solving, and
      generalization abilities across novel, unseen scenarios would offer a more accurate and meaningful
145
      evaluation of their capabilities (Wu et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024).
146
147   To address this gap, we develop an original benchmark with well-defined objectives and metrics
148   (see Section 4) specifically designed for evaluating MLLMs. Our framework adopts the Gym in-
149   terface (Brockman et al., 2016) and introduces a set of game environments that strike an appropri-
150
      ate balance between complexity and semantic richness. These environments are structured around
      twelve pre-defined tasks (see Section 5) that are carefully designed to target and evaluate MLLMs’
151
      core abilities. By incorporating five key abilities derived from human intelligence tests (see Sec-
152
      tion 3), our benchmark allows for a precise assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of MLLMs,
153   offering a more focused and effective evaluation of their capabilities.

      3     C APABILITIES
158
159   The cognitive development of human provides essential insights for creating truly flexible and in-
160   telligent agents (Wu et al., 2024c; Sumers et al., 2024). By borrowing concepts from Wechsler
161   Intelligence Scales, a well-established framework for assessing children’s intelligence, we identify
      five key abilities crucial for MLLMs.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Exuction: All human actions originate from intentions (Searle, 1983). Similarly, MLLMs also
163   needs to transform their understanding of goals into actions to achieve meaningful outcomes. We
164   define execution as the ability of MLLMs to carry out tasks based on their understanding of goals
165   and requirements. Whether navigating a virtual environment, manipulating objects, or interacting
166   with other agents, strong execution is crucial for MLLMs to turn abstract goals into real-world or
167   simulated behaviors, ensuring that their understanding of tasks translates into successful outcomes
168   by effectively carrying out the intended actions.
169
170   Perception Reasoning: Human cognition relies heavily on the ability to perceive and interpret
171   sensory information, forming the basis for reasoning and decision-making. In traditional reasoning,
172   particularly as it relates to LLMs, the focus is primarily on language, where reasoning processes
173   involve constructing thoughts and inferences articulated through linguistic constructs. In contrast,
174
      we define perception reasoning as the ability of MLLMs to derive inferences and make decisions
      directly from visual information, such as images. This process goes beyond merely understanding
175
      images; it involves analyzing and reasoning about visual data to form logical conclusions, predict
176
      outcomes, and guide actions. Perception reasoning is crucial because many future AGI challenges
177   will require handling multi-modal information (Guan et al., 2024), where reasoning directly from
178   visual inputs and integrating them with other sensory data is key to making comprehensive and
179   accurate decisions.
180
181   Memory: Humans utilize memory systems to store information and knowledge from past expe-
182   riences and apply them to current situations (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968). In MLLMs, memory
183   serves a similar role by allowing them to retain information (Wang et al., 2024c), build context, and
184   improve decision-making over time.” Memory allows MLLMs to integrate past observations and
185   learned knowledge into their reasoning process, which is essential for tasks that require an under-
186   standing of historical data or sequential patterns.
187
188   Learning: Learning is a fundamental aspect of human cognition, enabling individuals to acquire
189   new information, rules, and knowledge, and apply them to solve novel problems. Similarly, learning
190   in MLLMs refers to their capacity to absorb new information or rules and effectively utilize this
191
      knowledge in decision-making and problem-solving scenarios. A key challenge arises when the
      new information contradicts or contrasts with previously learned knowledge. For MLLMs, this is
192
      particularly difficult when the model has not been fine-tuned on data containing these new rules, as
193
      they need to reconcile conflicting knowledge without explicit retraining. Despite these challenges,
194   the ability to adapt to new information is crucial for AGI. Complex real-world problems often evolve
195   over time, requiring MLLMs to learn, adjust, and apply new knowledge dynamically, without re-
196   lying solely on pre-existing data or frequent fine-tuning, which can be expensive and slow. This
197   adaptability is essential for ensuring that MLLMs remain flexible and robust in diverse, changing
198   environments.
199
200   Planning: In human intelligence, planning plays a crucial role by allowing individuals to predict
201   future outcomes, devise strategies, and arrange actions in a structured sequence to achieve specific
202   objectives. In the context of MLLMs, planning refers to the capability to organize and prioritize
203   tasks, anticipate the consequences of actions, and execute multi-step strategies to address complex
204
      problems. This ability extends beyond reactive decision-making, as it requires foresight, where
      the model must account for long-term goals and carefully weigh the trade-offs between immediate
205
      actions and future outcomes.

304
305
      We design a series of tasks to evaluate the individual and composite abilities of MLLMs. Tasks
306
      vary in difficulty to challenge both basic and advanced models, while randomized layouts and di-
307   verse semantic scenes further enhance the assessment of generalization and robustness. Detailed
308   information specifics for each task can be found in Appendix G.

      5.1    S INGLE C APACITY TASK
311
312   Classification (CL): In this task, the agent is required to place each item into its designated container
313   based on specific instructions, such as “placing the cherry in the yellow basket” and “placing the
314   peach in the blue basket” (see Figure 1a). It is designed to evaluate the MLLM’s Execution ability,
315   which involves translating an understanding of goals into effective actions. The agent’s performance
316   in this task measures its accuracy in following instructions within a structured environment. The task
317   is designed with varying difficulty levels, where more difficult tasks involve an increased number of
318   items and placement operations. By increasing the complexity of the task, we further assess how
319   well the MLLM can adapt to more demanding scenarios that require precise execution.
320   Filling (FI): During the filling task, the agent will be presented with an image in which a quarter
321   section has been removed, such as “a goldfish with a missing head” (see Figure 1i). Then it needs
322   to restore the image by selecting the correct missing piece from a set of distractors in the backpack.
323   This task primarily evaluates the MLLM’s Perception Reasoning ability, as it requires the agent to
      develop a holistic understanding of the image and infer the missing part.

324
      Puzzle (PU): A target image composed of four puzzle pieces is displayed in the hint bar (see Fig-
325   ure 1j), and the agent needs to assemble the scattered puzzle pieces from its backpack to reconstruct
326   the target image. This task primarily evaluates the MLLM’s Perception Reasoning ability in ab-
327   stract visual mode, as it requires the agent to grasp the image’s overall structure, which cannot be
328   easily conveyed through language.
329
      Selection (SE): In Selection, before the game start, some random items will appear in the left hint
330
      bar (see Figure 1e). Once the game starts, these items will be hidden from players. The agent need to
331   select the items appeared in the hint bar before. This task evaluates the MLLM’s Memory capability
332   by requiring it to remember and recall the items previously shown. As the difficulty increases, the
333   number of items the agent is required to remember also rises.
334
      Decode (DE): The agent is provided with a code table, which contains a certain number of associ-
335
      ation rules between different items (see Figure 1j). The agent needs to first learn these correspon-
336
      dences. When a target item appears in the top left corner of the frame, then the agent is required
337   to select the item that corresponds to the target based on the learned associations. This task pri-
338   marily assesses the MLLM’s Learning capability, as it requires the agent to understand the new
339   relationships presented in the code table and apply that knowledge to make an informed decision.
340   As the task difficulty increases, the complexity of the correspondences the agent must remember
341   also increases.
342
      Maze (MA): This game is inspired by Procegon (Cobbe et al., 2019), where agent must obtain the
343   diamond in a maze with several locked doors. The agent needs to collect and use the corresponding
344   colored keys to unlock these doors (see Figure 1f). This task primarily evaluates the MLLM’s
345   Planning ability. Not every door needs to be opened, so the agent should carefully devise a strategy
346   to reach the diamond in as few steps as possible. As the difficulty increases, the number of necessary
347   doors to unlock also rises, and each action taken can significantly influence the agent’s subsequent
348   decisions.

      5.2   C OMPOSITE C APACITY TASK
352
353
354   Maze* (MA*): This task follows the same rules as the “Maze” described in Section 5.1, with an
355   added challenge. Before the game begins, the agent is shown the location of the diamond, but
356
      once the game starts, the diamond is hidden among several treasure chests. To succeed, the agent
      must correctly open the chest containing the diamond (see Figure 1g). This task primarily assesses
357
      the MLLM’s Memory and Planning abilities, as the agent must recall the diamond’s location and
358
      devise an effective strategy to retrieve it.
359
360   Decode* (DE*): This task follows the same rules as “Decode” in Section 5.1. The agent must addi-
361   tionally remember the relationships indicated in the code table, which will disappear once the game
362
      starts. This task primarily evaluates the LLM’s abilities in Memory and Learning, as it requires the
      agent to retain and utilize the information from the code table to make accurate selections.
363
364   Sorting (SO): In the sorting task, the agent is presented with a rule that may contradict real-world
365   knowledge. For instance, the agent might be instructed that “the faster the animal, the heavier it is”.
366   The agent is then expected to correctly rank the animals based on this given rule. This task evaluates
367   the MLLM’s Learning and Planning abilities, as it requires the agent to not only comprehend and
368
      integrate novel logic that may conflict with its prior knowledge but also apply it accurately in the
      sorting process.
369
370   Filling* (FI*): This task follows the same rule as “Filling” in Section 5.1. The agent must addi-
371   tionally remember the target image, which will disappear once the game starts. This task primarily
372   evaluates the LLM’s abilities in Perception Reasoning and Memory, as it necessitates recognizing
373   the overall image structure and recalling specific details to identify the correct piece.
374   Placement (PL): The agent is required to place the item in the opposite position based on the given
375   goal. For instance, if the rule states “place the toy car on the north side of the toy train” (see
376   Figure 1h), the agent actually need to place it on the “south” side. This task primarily evaluates the
377   MLLM’s abilities in Perception Reasoning and Learning, as it necessitates an understanding of
      placement rules and the awareness of spatial orientation.

378
      Counting (CO): The scene contains several piles of items, with quantities ranging from 1 to 3
379   (see Figure 1b). At the start of the game, the agent is given a target number and must collect
380   exactly that number of items. As the difficulty increases, more piles of items are introduced and
381   the target number grows, requiring the agent to gather more items from multiple piles. This task
382   primarily evaluates the MLLM’s Perception Reasoning and Planning abilities, focusing on the
383   agent’s awareness of item quantities and its strategic decision-making regarding how many items to
384   collect at once.

386   6     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULT
387
388   We selected seven prominent MLLMs for a comprehensive evaluation, including GPT-4o (OpenAI,
389   2024), Gemini-1.5-flash (Team, 2024b), Qwen2-VL-7b (Yang et al., 2024), LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-
390   7b (Liu et al., 2024), Deepseek-v1-7b (Team, 2024a), InternLM-XComposer2-7b (Zhang et al.), Phi-
391   v3.5-Vision (Abdin et al., 2024), and InternVL-Chat-v1.5 (Chen et al., 2024c). To ensure a robust
392   assessment, we first created 500 rounds of games for each task and tested all the above models on
393   the same set. We selected the option with the highest probability from the models’ outputs. Aligned
394   with the mainstream MLLM benchmarks (Chen et al., 2024b), we evaluate MLLM’s capabilities
395   through multiple-choice questions. This format facilitates the convenient calculation of accuracy as
396
      an objective metric. Moreover, it enables us to thoughtfully design incorrect options to control the
      quality and difficulty of our benchmark. We have included a detailed explanation of the evaluation
397
      procedure in Appendix C of the appendix.

      6.1   Q UANTITATIVE A NALYSIS
400
401   We observed that most MLLMs performed reliably only at Level 1, with performance dropping sig-
402   nificantly, often approaching the random baseline at Levels 2 and 3 (see Appendix B.4). Therefore,
403   we focused on Level 1 results in this section to highlight scenarios where the models demonstrated

453   MLLMs have error correction capability and can learn from interactions. For tasks requiring
454   multiple steps, as shown in Table 3, the agent was initially provided with more steps than necessary
455
      to complete the task, but this allowance was removed in the secondary test. Judging from the results,
      when the temperature of all models was set to 0 and no indication was given that the MLLM had
456
      made a wrong choice, the test with the maximum number of steps showed a significantly higher pass
457
      rate. This demonstrates that even when an error occurs during interaction with the environment, the
458   MLLM can adjust its subsequent choices based on environmental feedback, rather than repeatedly
459   selecting an invalid action.
460
461
462   Some MLLMs perform better in the memory counterpart of certain tasks. GPT-4o and
463   Qwen2-VL exhibit better performance in DE* and FI* tasks compared to their non-memory counter-
464   parts, which appears counter-intuitive. However, in our evaluation, most MLLMs acquire memory
465
      capabilities by treating previous information as history and concatenating it with the current state as
      input for inference. Consequently, both the image to be remembered and the image representing the
466
      problem state are provided in the prompt. This effectively allows MLLMs to “re-read” the problem,
467
      potentially providing an advantage in inference, as observed in Xu et al. (2024a), where repeating a
468   question improves performance. A similar pattern is not seen in SE* and its counterpart, likely due
469   to the confusing information present in SE*’s problem state (e.g., extra fake treasure chests), which
470   undermines the effectiveness of the re-reading strategy.
471
472
473   6.2   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
474
475   MLLMs’ training data lacks image-only perception training. The PU task showed the low-
476   est success rate among all single-ability tasks, primarily because it relies solely on abstract image
477   perception. The task description only instructs participants to restore the target image without pro-
478
      viding clear guidance on the restoration process. This separation between abstract visual content
      and textual instructions makes the task inherently more difficult.
479
480
481   It is difficult for MLLMs to learn knowledge contradict ones from training data. Most
482   MLLMs perform poorly in the Sorting and Placement tasks, where they are required to learn new
483   knowledge that may contradict prior knowledge from their training data. For instance, in the PL
484   task, the best-performing MLLM achieved only 0.27 score, a result that falls within the fluctuation
485   range of random selection. This suggests that MLLMs struggle to adapt to novel or conflicting
      information, particularly when it requires overriding existing knowledge embedded during training.

522
523   In this work, we introduce GridAgent, a 2D grid-based game environment framework and a unified
524   benchmark for evaluating LLMs as agents. Leveraging the current strengths and weaknesses of
525   MLLMs, our environment provides a rich semantic context, random layouts, high-level actions, and
526   multiple-choice questions. It also offers user-friendly interfaces for developers to easily create game
527   environments tailored to MLLM training.
528   Building on criteria referenced in human intelligence tests, we propose five novel dimensions to
529   evaluate an MLLM’s ability to solve tasks: execution, perception reasoning, memory, learning, and
530   planning. Additionally, our initial release of GridAgent includes twelve goal-oriented tasks designed
531   to assess these capabilities.
532
      We believe GridAgent will offer new datasets and tasks for the MLLM research community, con-
533   tributing to the continued development and enhancement of AGI.
