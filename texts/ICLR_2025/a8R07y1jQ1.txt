009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                The rapid advances of multi-modal agents built on large foundation models have
012                largely overlooked their potential for language-based communication between
013                agents in collaborative tasks. This oversight presents a critical gap in under-
014                standing their effectiveness in real-world deployments, particularly when com-
015                municating with humans. Existing agentic benchmarks fail to address key aspects
016                of inter-agent communication and collaboration, particularly in scenarios where
017
                   agents have unequal access to information and must work together to achieve
                   tasks beyond the scope of individual capabilities. To fill this gap, we introduce
018
                   a novel benchmark designed to evaluate the collaborative performance of mul-
019
                   timodal multi-agent systems through language communication. Our benchmark
020                features a variety of scenarios, providing a comprehensive evaluation across four
021                key categories of agentic capability in a communicative collaboration setting. By
022                testing both agent-agent and agent-human collaborations using open-source and
023                closed-source models, our findings reveal surprising weaknesses in state-of-the-
024                art models, including proprietary models like GPT-4o. These models struggle to
025                outperform even a simple random agent baseline in agent-agent collaboration and
026                only surpass the random baseline when a human is involved.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      The field of multimodal agents is experiencing rapid growth (Xu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Cao
      et al., 2024), with research efforts expanding at an unprecedented pace. However, amidst this growth,
032
      a critical gap in research has emerged: the lack of focus on collaborative work (Gurcan, 2024; Park
033
      et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) among multiple multimodal agents. Synergistic
034   operation of such agents is a highly promising but largely unexplored domain. Language agents can
035   collaboratively finish complex tasks such as software development by assuming functional roles such
036   as system designer, function generator, etc (Qian et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024)). Current research on
037   multimodal agents (Xu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024) has predominantly focused on
038   individual agent capabilities, neglecting the potential for inter-agent collaboration. This limitation is
039   further compounded by existing benchmarks such as VisualWebArena (Koh et al., 2024) and MME-
040   RealWorld (Zhang et al., 2024), which fail to adequately assess collaborative performance between
041   agents. As a result, our ability to evaluate and improve multi-agent systems remains constrained,
042
      hindering progress in this crucial area.
043   Several critical questions emerge in the context of multimodal agent collaboration. How can dif-
044   ferent agents effectively communicate multimodal information through language when they have
045   varying levels of access to information? In scenarios where different agents possess diverse task-
046   specific capabilities, how can they collaborate to accomplish objectives that are beyond the scope
047   of any individual agent? These research settings remain largely uncharted and present significant
048
      challenges. Furthermore, the ability of agents to handle incomplete information is of paramount
      importance, particularly when working with sensitive data (Li et al., 2024) (i.e. Agent application
049
      in healthcare where privacy concerns are critical (Tang et al., 2024)). The exploration of these
050
      questions is crucial for advancing the field of multimodal agent collaboration. By addressing these
051   challenges, we can expand the applicability of multimodal agents in real-world scenarios (Zhang
052   et al., 2024), particularly those involving sensitive or restricted information.

      Motivated by these aforementioned issues, we propose a novel benchmark for evaluating collabo-
083   rative multi-modal multi-agent frameworks to address critical gaps in current approaches (see Fig-
084   ure 1). Our evaluation setting simulates a scenario where an in-house agent with direct access to
085   sensitive data (i.e., the AI solver) collaborates with external expert agents (i.e., the AI expert) to
086   analyze information without compromising privacy. This evaluation setting could revolutionize how
087   we handle and extract insights from sensitive datasets across various domains.
088
      We assess multi-modal multi-agent systems using a series of carefully designed collaborative puzzle
089   games. These scenarios typically involve two-player setups where agents have access to different,
090   complementary information. (i.e., in a bomb defusal game, one agent possesses details about the
091   bomb, while the other has access to a disarming manual). By employing such diverse and interactive
092   scenarios, we aim to provide a thorough assessment of multi-modal multi-agent performance.
093
      Our benchmark includes 10 distinct, easily customizable puzzles with thousands of unique solutions.
094
      We tested two different settings (AI-AI and AI-Human) and evaluated several popular multimodal
095   models, including closed-source models (GPT-4V, GPT-4O, and GPT-4o1) and open-source models
096   (Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), and InternVL(Chen et al., 2024)). Surprisingly, the GPT series does
097   not outperform even a simple random baseline in the AI-AI setting, highlighting a potential growth
098   area for future model development. Our contributions are as follows:
099
100
101
       • We propose an evaluation framework called COMMA, a multimodal agent benchmark focusing
         on language communication between multiple agents (Section 3).
102
103
104    • Using COMMA, we carefully record conversations and performance metrics between state-of-
105
         the-art multimodal models such as QWenVL, InternVL, GPT-4o, GPT-4o1, etc (Section 4).
106
107    • We categorize the agent capabilities tested in our model and common failure modes, providing
         insight into future research directions for improving inter-agent communication (Section 5).

      Multi-agent Frameworks: There are many emergent agent collaboration works (Gurcan, 2024;
111   Park et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Ghafarollahi & Buehler, 2024; Li et al., 2023;
112   Wu et al., 2023) among multiple language agents. Multi-agent systems arise mainly in two different
113   scenarios: (1) role-playing different task executors (e.g., software development requiring different
114   roles of agents, such as program manager, software architect, programmer Du et al. (2024); Qian
115   et al. (2024); Hong et al. (2024), scientific discovery simulation Wu et al. (2023), and social simu-
116   lation Park et al. (2023); Gurcan (2024)); (2) communicating between agents with different pieces
117   of information Wu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023) (e.g., consulting experts without sharing some
118   sensitive or confidential data. In our case, the AI solver has some private multimodal data, and the
119
      AI expert has domain-specific knowledge or instructions).
120
121
      Instruction-based Agent Benchmarks: Instruction-based agent benchmarks evaluate an agent’s
      capability of following a human instructions to finish a task (e.g., navigating on a website, interacting
122
      with an operating system Xu et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024); Cao et al. (2024)). However, our
123
      benchmark focuses more on a communication-based evaluation where two clients engage in multi-
124   turn conversations to solve a task collaboratively.

129   3.1   D ESIGN P RINCIPLES OF THE B ENCHMARK
130
      Our benchmark is inspired by the cooperative gameplay scenario in Keep Talking and Nobody Ex-
131   plodes Games (2015). In this game, two players work together to defuse a bomb under time pressure.
132   One player, the defuser, can see the bomb but lacks the instructions to disarm it. The other player,
133   the expert, has access to the bomb’s manual but cannot see the bomb itself. The players must rely
134   on effective communication to exchange information, navigate challenges, and defuse the bomb.
135
      We generalize this dynamic for our benchmark, shifting the focus to solving vision-language puz-
136
      zles in a communication-based agent framework. As multimodal agent systems gain momentum,
137   our goal is to create a benchmark that rigorously evaluates their reasoning, communication, and
138   collaborative abilities. The design of our benchmark revolves around the following core principles:
139
140   Cognitive Science: Our benchmark draws inspiration from foundational principles of intelligence,
141   often defined as the ability to learn from experience, adapt to the environment, and solve prob-
142   lems using cognitive skills Kempf-Leonard (2005). Cognitive Science research has demonstrated
143   that even simple tests can effectively measure cognitive ability Davidson et al. (2006); St Clair-
144   Thompson & Gathercole (2006). Standardized intelligence tests, such as MENSA MENSA Interna-
145   tional (n.d.) and the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children Wechsler (1949), frequently employ
146   simple puzzles to evaluate these skills. Building on this approach, our benchmark aims to assess the
147
      core cognitive capabilities of multimodal agents by creating simple vision-language puzzles tailored
      to test these abilities.
148
149
      Language communication: A critical aspect of our benchmark is evaluating natural language
150
      communication between agents. Similar to how players in the original game exchange information
151
      verbally, agents in our framework must use language to share observations, clarify ambiguities, and
152   reason about tasks. In order for the agents to succeed, they must display clarity, efficiency, and depth
153   of communication, making it an essential factor in task completion.
154
155   Multi-agent collaboration: In our benchmark, agents must work together, much like the two-
156   player dynamic of Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes. The collaboration element ensures that
157   tasks require mutual dependency, where each agent contributes unique information or capabilities
158   that are critical to solving the puzzle. This principle highlights how well agents can cooperate and
159   leverage each other’s strengths.
160
161   Multimodality: Our benchmark emphasizes the integration of multiple sensory inputs and out-
      puts, such as vision, language, and audio. The puzzles involve visual elements that agents must

162
      perceive, describe, and interpret, alongside linguistic interactions. This principle assesses an agent’s
163   ability to handle and synthesize multimodal information, a skill crucial to real-world applications.

      3.2   C ATEGORIES OF AGENT C APABILITY
166
167   We benchmark agents working under different roles to solve various tasks in multiple settings, each
168   requiring different capabilities. Specifically, the Solver agent must demonstrate strong instruction-
169   following and multimodal reasoning, while the Expert agent is expected to excel in long text summa-
170   rization and information retrieval. Both agents must possess visual comprehension and descriptive
171   skills to succeed. Below, we outline the core capabilities tested in our benchmark.
172   Memory Recall ( MR ) In many puzzles, agents must remember their previous actions to progress.
173   This ability is also implicitly tested when agents make mistakes. A competent agent should recall
174   instances where past actions led to errors and adapt to avoid repeating them. The capacity to learn
175   from mistakes and leverage memory is crucial for effective problem-solving in real-world situations.
176
      Multimodal Grounding ( MG ) Since the solver agent can only communicate with the expert with
177   text, it must be able to ground relevant spans of the expert’s instructions to the image it currently sees.
178   This grounding of language in visual context is essential for interpreting and following guidance
179   from the expert agent effectively.
180
      Multi-Step Reasoning (MSR) Certain puzzles require agents to follow a sequence of actions based
181
      on step-by-step reasoning. Much like real-world tasks, such as following a recipe or placing an
182
      online order, each action must be deliberate and contribute toward the overall goal. Our benchmark
183   enables fine-grained evaluation of progress within these multi-step reasoning tasks, allowing for a
184   precise assessment of models’ reasoning capabilities.
185
186
      Real-time Reaction ( RT) Some puzzles challenge agents to process information rapidly and act
      with precise timing. This is a critical skill for embodied agents operating in dynamic, real-world
187
      environments where precise timing and quick reactions are vital.

      3.3   TASKS
190
191   We create 10 puzzles across 4 different categories briefly summarized below. A more comprehensive
192   description along with example images and instruction manuals can be found in Appendix A.
193
194    • ButtonPuzzle ( RT ): The solver must hold a colored button for a specific number of seconds
195      based on the button’s color, the strip’s color when pressed, and the time remaining on a timer.
196    • ColorPuzzle ( MR , MSR ): The solver presses squares of the least common color in a 4x4 grid,
197      then follows a sequence based on a table, aiming to turn all squares white.
198    • KeypadPuzzle (MG , MSR ): The solver must describe the symbol of each button in a 2x2
199      grid. The expert must then identify a column in the manual containing these four unique symbols
200      and tell the solver to press the symbols in the correct order from top to bottom.
201    • LedPuzzle (MR , MSR ): The solver presses a button if the value of its letter, when multiplied
202      by a stage’s LED color multiplier and taken modulo 26, matches the value of the letter diagonally
203      opposite it. At each stage, the letters on the buttons change.
204
       • MazePuzzle (MG , MSR ): The solver navigates a mouse through a maze to a colored sphere,
205      pressing the correct button to disarm the module based on the maze’s layout.
206
       • MemoryPuzzle (MR , MSR ): The solver presses buttons according to specific positional and
207
         label-based rules over five stages, with incorrect presses resetting progress.
208
209    • PasswordPuzzle ( MG , MSR ): The solver cycles through letters to form a valid word from a
210
         predefined list, submitting the correct word to complete the puzzle.
211    • DogPuzzle (RT ): The solver is presented with an image containing 0-4 dogs. Based on the
212      number of dogs in the image, the solver must press the submit button when the last digit of the
213      timer matches the number of dogs in the image.
214    • WhoPuzzle (MG ): The solver must tell the value on a display to the expert, who will identify
215      a button label. The solver must then tell this label to the expert, and then press the correct button
         based on a detailed list of instructions.

217      wires, and the correct wire to cut changes depending on the number and order of colors.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
223   In this section, we describe the experimental settings of our multi-agent interaction environment
224   where two distinct agents, namely the Solver agent and the Expert agent, engage in iterative dialogue
225   sessions. The primary aim of this setup is to assess the collaborative problem-solving capabilities
226   between different agents or multimodal large language models (MLLMs). During our experiments,
227   we limit the number of conversation turns to 20, allowing for a unified and systematic assessment of
228   interactions. We use greedy decoding when available to maintain consistent agent output across runs
229   and run inference on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80GB RAM. We parse the solver’s chosen
230   actions at each conversation turn using exact string matching, and use PyAutoGUI (Sweigart, 2023)
231
      to directly perform the action on the interface if the solver outputs a valid action. Our exact prompts
      for both the solver and expert agent can be found in Appendix D.
232
233
234
      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
235
      We meticulously recorded several key performance metrics through multiple iterations of the exper-
236   iments described below:
237
238    • Success Rate (SR): The solver agent is assigned a 0 or 1 value for each puzzle depending on if
239      it completed it. These values are averaged across all puzzles to obtain the success rate.
240    • Partial Success Rate (PSR): Because our benchmark includes puzzles with multi-step reason-
241      ing, some puzzles can have a more precise success rate evaluation. For these multi-step puzzles,
242      we assign the solver a number between 0 and 100 to indicate its progress towards the solution,
243      and average this number across puzzles to obtain partial success rate. For single-step puzzles,
244      partial success rate is identical to success rate.
245    • Average Mistakes (AM): After an action is chosen by the solver, the environment checks if
246      the action was a mistake. We tally up the mistakes made during each puzzle and take a global
247      average across puzzles to obtain average mistakes.
248    • Average Conversation Length (ACL): We count the number of conversation turns the Solver
249      took to arrive at the solution, or default to the maximum of 20 if the solver failed. This count is
250      averaged across all puzzles to get Average Conversation Length.

307   5.1      OVERALL P ERFORMANCE
308
309
      Table 2 illustrates the performances of all combinations of solver and expert pairs we evaluated.
      We evaluate some combinations of different open-source models because they are free, and leave
310
      pairings of separate closed-source models for future work. Intrerestingly, increasing model size
311
      does not always improve performance, as QWenVL 2b (38% PSR) outperforms QWenVL 7b (32%
312   PSR). We observe that the random agent performs well on simple puzzles which only require one
313   correct action (e.g. 100% on WirePuzzle, 100% on WhoPuzzle) and even outperforms all of the
314   AI-AI setting performances. However, a random agent struggles with puzzles which require several
315   correct actions in a row, as evidenced by its 0 percent success rate and low partial success rate in
316   Memory, Maze, Password, Keypad, and Color puzzles.
317
      Our results show that GPT-4o1 is the most powerful agent to use, although we only run a single
318   iteration of each puzzle with a Human-AI setting due to rate limit issues. It makes the most progress
319   in the color puzzle, achieving a partial success rate of 44%, followed by GPT-4o at 35%. In this
320   puzzle, we observed that GPT-4o1’s “reasoning process” made correct connections with different
321   parts of the puzzle’s manual, though sometimes it made incorrect conclusions at the end of the
322   process. GPT-4V is the second best agent, with an average partial score of 53% in the AI-AI setting,
323   although the performance is still far worse than human performance. For example, switching from
      a gpt-4o solver to a human solver increases overall partial success rate from 53% to 74%.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we address a critical gap in the field of multi-modal agents by introducing a novel
489
      benchmark specifically designed to evaluate communication in a multi-modal, multi-agent system.
490   While substantial progress has been made in developing individual multi-modal agents, collaborative
491   frameworks remain under-explored, particularly in scenarios requiring secure communication and
492   the handling of sensitive data. Our benchmark aims to bridge this gap by simulating real-world
493   conditions where agents possess complementary information and must work together to achieve
494   complex goals. We comprehensively evaluate metrics such as partial success rate, mistake rate,
495   and document common failure modes for both AI-AI and AI-Human interactions. Our findings
496   show that multimodal agents struggle to communicate with each other, often falling short of even
497   a simple random baseline due to poor communication and frequently repeated bad actions. These
498
      findings emphasize the need for deeper investigation into enhancing inter-agent collaboration. We
      hope the insights from our benchmark lay the foundation for future research on multi-modal agent
499
      collaboration and inspires the community to explore innovative approaches to improve multimodal
500
      agent capabilities this emerging field of communicative multimodal systems.
501
502
503   7   L IMITATIONS
504
505   While we aim to construct a holistic framework for multimodal agent communication, our experi-
506   ments may not represent all possible scenarios in our puzzles. We conduct a preliminary study by
507   sampling puzzle configurations and conversations between agents, and we leave more comprehen-
508
      sive evaluation and expansion of puzzle categories to future work. Additionally, there will inevitably
      be a simulation-to-reality gap from our benchmark to real-world situations, thus a high score on our
509
      benchmark may not perfectly generalize to real-world communication scenarios. Lastly, we ac-
510
      knowledge that there is inherent risk to using multimodal agents when handling private data. Given
511   that LLMs have been shown to be prone to jailbreaking, it is critical to take additional safety mea-
512   sures before deploying an agent in practice, even if it achieves a high score on our benchmark.

             • ButtonPuzzle: The solver is presented with an empty strip, a colored button and a timer
1056           counting down from 10 minutes. When the button is pressed and held, the strip turns a
1057           certain color. Based off of a combination of the color of the button, the color of the strip
1058           and the time on the clock, the solver has to keep the button pressed for a certain number of
1059           seconds.
1060         • ColorPuzzle: The solver is presented with a 4×4 grid of colored tiles. The solver must
1061           first identify the color group with the fewest squares on a 4x4 grid and press all the squares
1062           of that color to start the module. The solver then needs to refer to a table to determine
1063           the next group to press based on the current configuration. Pressing any incorrect square
1064           results in a strike and resets the module. Non-white squares may change color after each
1065           stage. The goal is to make all squares on the grid white by following the correct sequence
1066           of groups.
1067         • KeypadPuzzle: The solver has to examine a 2x2 grid of unique symbols and identify
1068           which of the four columns below the grid contains all four symbols from the grid. Once
1069           the correct column is found, the solver must press the buttons in that column in the order
1070           the symbols appear from top to bottom.
1071         • LedPuzzle: The solver progresses through 2 to 5 stages, each indicated by an LED color
1072           that specifies a multiplier (Red: 2, Green: 3, Blue: 4, Yellow: 5, Purple: 6, Orange: 7).
1073           Four buttons with changing letters are shown at each stage. The solver must assign values
1074           to letters (A = 0, B = 1, etc.) and press a button if its letter value, when multiplied by
1075           the stage’s multiplier and taken modulo 26, equals the value of the letter on its diagonally
1076           opposite button. Each stage requires pressing a correct button, and there may be multiple
1077
               valid choices.
