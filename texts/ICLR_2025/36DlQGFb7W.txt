011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           The increase in Arctic marine activity due to rapid warming and significant sea ice
014           loss necessitates highly reliable, short-term sea ice forecasts to ensure maritime
015           safety and operational efficiency. In this work, we present a novel data-driven ap-
016
              proach for sea ice condition forecasting in the Gulf of Ob, leveraging sequences
              of radar images from Sentinel-1, weather observations, and GLORYS forecasts.
017
              Our approach integrates advanced video prediction models, originally developed
018
              for vision tasks, with domain-specific data preprocessing and augmentation tech-
019           niques tailored to the unique challenges of Arctic sea ice dynamics. Central to
020           our methodology is the use of uncertainty quantification to assess the reliabil-
021           ity of predictions, ensuring robust decision-making in safety-critical applications.
022           Furthermore, we propose a uncertainty-aware model switching mechanism that
023           enhances forecast accuracy and model robustness, crucial for safe operations in
024           volatile Arctic environments. Our results demonstrate substantial improvements
025           over baseline approaches, underscoring the importance of uncertainty quantifica-
026           tion and specialized data handling for effective and reliable sea ice forecasting.

      I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      The Arctic region is experiencing an unprecedented rate of warming, leading to a significant reduc-
057
      tion in sea ice area by more than 30% over the last four decades, and a simultaneous decrease in
058   sea ice thickness (Kwok, 2018). Alongside this, the last century has seen active development of ice-
059   breaker construction technologies, including nuclear-powered ones. These changes have opened up
060   new sea routes, such as the Northern Sea Route, which provide faster and more economical trans-
061   port. However, increased navigation is accompanied by increased risks due to ice jams, posing a
062   serious threat to maritime safety.
063
      Traditional sea ice models, based on elastic-visco-plastic rheological properties, often fail to accu-
064   rately reflect all the nuances of ice deformation, rendering them unreliable for forecasting in some
065   cases (Nummelin et al., 2016; Eastwood et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Overland & Pease, 1988). Addi-
066   tionally, these models require significant computational resources to adequately simulate the interac-
067   tions between the ocean and ice. Consequently, there is a need to explore alternative methodologies
068   that leverage statistical methods such as machine learning techniques, known for their flexibility and
069   lower computational demands.
070   Our research is aimed at improving the forecasting of ice conditions in the Gulf of Ob, a region
071   significantly influenced by the interaction of the saline waters of the Kara Sea and the fresh water
072   of the big northern rivers, leading to complex ice formation dynamics (Weatherly & Walsh, 1996;
073   Osadchiev et al., 2021).
074
      We utilize radar images obtained in the Sentinel-1 mission (Sentinel-1), weather observation data
075
      (Weather & Climate), and operational forecasts and reanalysis from the GLORYS project (GLO) to
076   predict future sea ice conditions. From a machine learning perspective, the series of satellite radar
077   images can be treated as a continuous video sequence, therefore the problem can be formulated as a
078   conditioned video prediction task — the widely investigated problem in common-life domain (Ming
079   et al., 2024). Our research employs following video prediction models:
080
081          • Implicit Stacked Autoregressive Model for Video Prediction (IAM4VP) (Seo et al., 2023)
082            uses a fully convolutional neural network with an implicit multi-input-single-output work-
083
               flow, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy of weather predictions in datasets such as SEVIR;
084          • Dynamic Multi-Scale Voxel Flow Network (DMVFN) (Hu et al., 2023) utilizes voxel flow
085            for video prediction, addressing efficiency and adaptability in handling diverse motion
086            scales;
087          • MotionRNN (Wu et al., 2021) models spacetime-varying motions using the Motion Gat-
088            ing Recurrent Unit and Motion Highway mechanisms, enhancing prediction accuracy in
089            dynamic scenarios;
090
             • Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODE) and Vid-ODE (Park et al., 2021)
091            treat consecutive frames as solutions to systems of ordinary differential equations, offering
092            control over visual attributes and smooth transitions between frames.
093
094   As the primary loss for training models and metric for evaluating their performance, we use Mean
095   Squared Error (MSE) between predicted and target Synthetic-Aperture Radar (SAR) images. In ad-
096   dition to this, we utilize the Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) and its extension,
097   the Multi-Scale Structural Similarity Index (MS-SSIM) (Wang et al., 2003), to assess the perceived
098   quality of digital images and videos. Finally, the Integrated Ice Edge Error at level c (IIEE@c)
099
      (Goessling et al., 2016) is utilized to measure the similarity between forecasted and observed ice
      sheets. These indicators allow us to meticulously compare the accuracy of predicted ice conditions
100
      against observed data, ensuring that our models reflect not only general trends but also detailed
101
      spatial structures necessary for accurate ice mapping.
102
103   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
104
             • we explore the potential of modern deep learning video-prediction models in short-term
105
               regional sea ice forecasting;
106
107          • we address the problem of data irregularity and missing values within the Arctic area by
               exploring filtration, normalization, and augmentations techniques;

             • we show the ensemble of ML models provides sufficient uncertainty estimation, and we
109            propose novel uncertainty-aware model switching scheme that stabilizes the forecast and
110            enhances its quality;
111
             • finally, we assess a gap filling performance for satellite radar imagery and demonstrate the
112
               superiority of our method in comparison with a general approach for interpolating video
113            sequences.

      R ELATED WORKS
116
117   Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of machine learning in forecasting sea ice extent
118   and sea ice concentration. Chi and Kim (Chi & Kim, 2017) pioneered in the use of deep learning
119   for sea ice prediction. Their model employs multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) and long- and short-
120   term memory networks (LSTMs) to capture complex relationships in sea ice data. By training the
121   MLP- and LSTM-based models on historical data, they identify patterns for one-month predictions,
122   outperforming traditional statistical models. This work highlights the advantages of deep learning
123
      in sea ice forecasting.
124   Recent research has extended the application of UNet-based models to sea ice forecasting, high-
125   lighting their versatility beyond original medical imaging applications. Fernandez et al. (Fernández
126   et al., 2022) investigated coastal sea elements forecasting using various UNet-based architectures,
127   including 3DDR-UNet and its enhanced versions. Their study demonstrated the effectiveness of
128   these models in forecasting coastal sea conditions when using satellite imagery. Grigoryev et al.
129
      (Grigoryev et al., 2022) presented a recurrent UNet with a specialized training scheme that con-
      siderably outperformed persistence and linear trend baseline forecasts of sea-ice conditions in the
130
      regions of the Barents, Labrador, and Laptev seas for lead times up to 10 days. Kvanum et al.
131
      (Kvanum et al., 2024) showed that the similar approach in the Barents sea can overcome traditional
132   numerical models at the forecasting of sea ice concentration at one kilometer resolution and 3-day
133   lead time. Keller et al. (Keller et al., 2023) explored various UNet-based architectures for prediction
134   sea ice extent at kilometer resolution for lead time up to 7 days. These studies revealed the poten-
135   tial of machine learning methods over traditional approaches for high-resolution sea ice conditions
136   forecasting.
137
      Several studies showcase the prospects of uncertainty-aware data-driven sea ice forecasting. Horvath
138   et al. (Horvath et al., 2020) suggested using Bayesian logistic regression to forecast September
139   minimum ice cover from 1-month up to 7-month lead times. In this paper Bayesian uncertainty
140   quantification helps to assess the reliabillity of the forecasts. Andersson et al. (Andersson et al.,
141   2021) introduced a probabilistic deep learning sea ice forecasting system called IceNet with 6-month
142   lead time. Their system predicts monthly averaged sea ice concentration maps at 25-km resolution,
143   outperforming traditional models by effectively bounding the ice edge. Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2022)
144   suggested VAE-Based Non-Autoregressive Transformer as an uncertainty-aware model for long-
145   term sea ice concentration forecast along Northern Sea Route. Also uncertainty quantification is
146
      the key motive of the conjugate problems like sea ice data assimilation (Nazanin, 2019) or sea ice
      concentration retrieval (Chen et al., 2023b).
147
148   One challenge in sea ice forecasting and analysis based on satellite imagery data is the presence of
149   noise and gaps, which can occur due to limitations of satellite trajectories, instrumental errors, data
150   losses, and environmental factors. To address this, researchers have proposed various gap-filling
151   methods (Desai & Ganatra, 2012). They incorporate chained data fusion, multivariate interpola-
152
      tion, and empirical orthogonal functions to effectively fill missing data. Weiss et al. (Weiss et al.,
      2014) proposed an effective approach for continental-scale gaps inpainting based on nearest neigh-
153
      bors method and taking in account seasonality of the data. Their approach, additionally, quantified
154
      uncertainty of the filled values. Appel (Appel, 2024) introduced a deep learning approach based
155   on partial convolutions for filling gaps in consecutive data, highlighting the promise of deep learn-
156   ing for satellite imagery-related tasks. These methods enhance the quality and reliability of remote
157   observations, having potentially a wider range of applications than just sea ice analysis.

224   DATA PREPROCESSING AND FILTRATION
225
      The origin of the noise in SAR imagery is thermal interference within radar systems, influenced by
226
      the technology utilized for surface scanning, resulting in presence of speckles and scalloping patterns
227   (Singh et al., 2021). Thermal artifacts have significant magnitude relative to useful information,
228   which leads to huge biases and corrupts optimization convergence of neural networks. Therefore,
229   we preprocess data to filter out imagery artifacts. The results of the final filtering are presented in
230   Figure 1c.
231
      Our custom filtering algorithm treats images as vectors from RH×W space with standard scalar
232
      product, where H and W stand for sizes of the input frames. The core assumption is the orthogo-
233   nality of artifacts A to the subspace of clear images C ⊥ A. Therefore, the filtering process is an
234   orthogonal projection: P : RH×W → C, P 2 = P = P T .
235
236
      However, the construction of such operator requires the retrieval of aforementioned subspaces. The
      key thought is that all the ice-free frames IF must have the same projection: ∃c0 ∀c ∈ IF : P (c) =
237
      c0 . To achieve this, the frame c0 with neither ice nor noise should be chosen by hand. We obtained
238
      several candidates for such a frame through visual assessment and peaked the pixel-wise minimum
239   of all of them. Then artifact subspace A is constructed from IF to match orthogonality condition
240   to c0 at least, and the filtering operator P is constructed after choosing a basis in the subspace
241   containing all the artifacts A:

249
250   To determine the relative quality of our models performance, we compare them against two base-
251   lines: persistence forecast and linear one. To obtain the parameters of the linear transformation we
252   utilize the same techniques as for deep learning models.
253   IAM4VP (Seo et al., 2023) is fully convolutional neural network that leverages the trade-off between
254   temporal-consistency of autoregressive methods and error-independence of non-autoregressive ones
255   via implicit Multi-Input-Single-Output workflow. Like non-autoregressive methods, stacked autore-
256   gressive approach uses the same observed sequence to estimate future frames. However, the model
257   uses its own predictions as input, similar to autoregressive methods. As the number of time steps
258   increases, predictions are sequentially stacked in the queue. After the iterative process is finished,
259   all generated frames are refined by the last few layers to raise the temporal correlations.
260   DMVFN (Hu et al., 2023) is a video prediction model leveraging voxel flow estimation to focus on
261   movement and to handle the occlusion effect. DMVFN also incorporates a dynamic routing module
262   that adaptively selects sub-networks based on the input frames, allowing it to handle diverse motion
263   scales efficiently. The model’s architecture includes Multi-scale Voxel Flow Blocks (MVFBs) that
264   capture large motions and iteratively refine voxel flow estimates. DMVFN demonstrates improved
265   efficiency and adaptability, particularly for videos with complex motion patterns and is considered
266
      a state-of-the-art deep learning solution for video prediction.
267   MotionRNN (Wu et al., 2021) is a model designed for video prediction, specifically addressing the
268   challenge of predicting continuous spatio-temporal dynamics. MotionRNN is a successor of the
269   LSTM-based architectures that also incorporates warp transformation and introduces the concept
      of breaking down physical motions into transient variation and motion trend. Transient variation

      Neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018) offers a powerful framework for modeling dynamic systems by
290
      means of machine learning. The core idea is to model the transformation of data through a contin-
291
      uous dynamic equation in a Cauchy formulation instead of discrete layers used in traditional neural
292   networks. The forward pass is a numerical solution of a parametrized ODE. To train Neural ODEs
293   through backpropagation the gradients can be computed either directly through the dynamic equa-
294   tion using automatic differentiation or more memory-efficiently through the adjoint method. The
295   adjoint method treats gradients as solutions of a reverse-time differential equations, integrating it
296   backwards in time.
297
      Vid-ODE (Park et al., 2021) represents Neural ODE in a latent space on motion-vector dynamics
298   with a warp-correction mechanism. The main idea of Vid-ODE lies in the parameterizing visual
299   attributes such as pixels position by utilizing differential equations. The encoded input frames are
300   assimilated into an evolving state by the GRU cell. Neural ODE models latent dynamics. The
301   predicted state is decoded into pixels relative shifts and correction features that are used to correct
302   warping impurities and model color and brightness variation. This iterative process ensures smooth
303   transitions between generated frames.
304   UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), is an convolutional neural network which includes an encoder
305   for capturing context and a decoder for precise localization for the output. The decoder path in-
306   volves upsampling of the feature maps and concatenates them with the corresponding feature maps
307   from the encoder path. Originally designed for the biomedical image segmentation, UNet has been
308   adapted for different geophysical fields forecasts such as: coastal sea elements (Fernández et al.,
309   2022), precipitation (Kaparakis & Mehrkanoon, 2023; Trebing et al., 2021), and sea ice concen-
310   tration (Grigoryev et al., 2022; Kvanum et al., 2024). We use it within both autoregressive and
311   non-autoregressive approaches. The former one we will mention as rUNet, where ’r’ stands for
312
      recurrent.

      U NCERTAINTY- AWARENESS
366
367   Estimating uncertainty in data-driven weather forecasting models is crucial for better model inter-
368   pretation and decision-making. If the uncertainty estimation is well-calibrated, the reliable predic-
369   tions are characterized by high confidence. On the other hand, low confidence means the prediction
370   can not be trusted. In such cases one could replace it with a simple baseline or a more robust
371   model. Following this principle, automatized pipelines of uncertainty-aware model mixture can be
372   designed (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2023a; Zeng et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023).
373   The mechanism is as follows: the expert model makes a prediction, its uncertainty is estimated; if
374
      the uncertainty exceeds the preset threshold, the prediction is replaced by more stable baseline. In
      our work the threshold is selected on the validation subset. This helps to exclude unreliable forecasts
375
      and enhances the overall performance of the forecasting system.
376
377   Traditional weather and climate models estimate uncertainty as the spread of an ensemble, con-
      structed by the model inputs perturbations (Grimit & Mass, 2007). The ensemble spread is defined


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      as a standard deviation of predictions. Previous research (Scher & Messori, 2021) showed that,
379   when using neural networks, ensembles of models with similar architectures (homogeneous) pro-
380   vide similar results. Models weights in the ensemble have to be perturbed with retraining, dropout,
381   etc. Moreover, there are premises that an ensemble of diverse architectures (heterogeneous) might
382   provide better uncertainty estimation (Zaidi et al., 2022).
383
      In our research we construct both homogeneous and heterogeneous ensembles and compare their
384
      spread as a predictor for the uncertainty estimation for the model selection mechanism. The sug-
385   gested pipeline does not impose additional costs as all the models do not need to be modified or
386   retrained.

      D ISCUSSION
487
488
489   This research addresses the critical challenge of short-term regional sea ice forecasting, explor-
490
      ing a variety of approaches to improve prediction accuracy and reliability. Among the methods
      investigated, modern deep learning models for video prediction were tested for their potential in
491
      forecasting sea ice dynamics. However, the performance of these models is constrained by several
492
      factors, including the scarcity of high-resolution data, the complex physical processes governing sea
493   ice behavior, the stochastic nature of daily ice dynamics, and the discontinuities present in ice sheet
494   structures. We argue that these domain specialties mostly affect motion-related elements of video
495   prediction models like flow estimation and prediction, see appendix C for further details.
496
      UNet-based models deliver the best individual results, whereas state-of-the-art video prediction
497
      models struggle to surpass baseline performance, though they do offer varying levels of stability.
498
      It could be argued that the DMVFN model fails to accurately reproduce sea ice thermodynamics
499   due to its architectural limitations, which, paradoxically, contribute to more stable forecasts. On the
500   other hand, IAM4VP, while efficient at modeling different dynamics with minimal computational
501   cost, produces the most unstable predictions, likely due to the lack of sufficient training data.
502
      Advanced use of uncertainty-aware model switching scheme can further enhance the metrics. The
503
      ensemble spread of heterogeneous architectures provides accurate uncertainty estimation for the
504
      forecasted fields. Although the model-selection mechanism reduces the final spread-error correla-
505   tion, the total variance in model error can still be explained up to 87% by accounting for the sea ice
506   concentration and its rate of change (see Figure 14).
507
508
509   C ONCLUSIONS
510
511
      In this research article, we address the challenge of predicting ice conditions in the Gulf of Ob,
512
      a region characterized by complex ice formation dynamics influenced by the interaction of saline
513   water and freshwater. We explore the potential of machine learning methods as an alternative to
514   traditional numerical sea ice models, aiming to improve forecasting accuracy and efficiency.
515
516
      Our key findings reveal that even modern state-of-the-art machine learning models can not achieve
      sufficient forecasting performance solely. Furthermore, domain-aware data preprocessing and aug-
517
      mentations are essential to train deep learning models for this task. All models struggle due to lack
518
      of training data, long gaps in it and complex sea ice dynamics, leading to tricky fidelity-stability
519   trade-off. Although usage of ensembles cannot significantly improve average models performance,
520   it helps to eliminate high errors due to outliers in data, especially in spring season, thus increasing
521   overall system reliability. We consider also an interesting finding that the different ML models cap-
522   ture different aspects of the ice dynamics in such a way that their ensemble gives a reliable forecast
523   uncertainty quantification, as the spread-error correlation coefficient reaches 87%. To overcome
524   the aforementioned trade-off we construct the uncertainty-aware model switching scheme, that pro-
525   vides both stable and explainable forecasts while improving general performance. The mixture of
526   the rUNet and DMVFN architectures provides the best computer vision and geophysical metrics and
527
      beats baselines by a wide margin.
528   Future research directions include developing models that can effectively capture the dynamics of
529   ice formation and melting is crucial. Additionally, addressing the limitations of current approaches
530   through more advanced architectures and techniques can also be beneficial. Further advancements
531   in sea ice forecasting will not only improve maritime navigation safety but also deepen our under-
532   standing of complex sea ice dynamics.

770   2011). It is relatively shallow, with depths averaging 10 to 12 meters, limiting heavy sea transport.
771   The Taz Estuary, formed by the Taz River, spans about 330 km from Tazovsky to the Gulf of Ob,
772   with an average width of 25 km. It flows north to south and then bends westward before merging
773   with the Gulf of Ob, contributing to one of the largest estuarine systems in the world.
774
      This region is important for sea ice forecasting and research due to its highly variable ice conditions
775
      influenced by seasonal changes and river discharge (Osadchiev et al., 2021). It’s a sensitive indica-
776   tor of climate change and has significant economic and strategic value due to its location near major
777   shipping routes and natural resources (Tretiakov & Shiklomanov, 2022). The unique interaction be-
778   tween river outflows and the sea creates distinctive ice patterns, making it a key area for studying sea
779   ice dynamics and improving predictive models(Tikhonov et al., 2022). Additionally, sea ice in this
780   region affects local ecosystems and communities, highlighting the broader impacts of environmental
781   changes on ecology and society.

896   C    O PTICAL FLOW ESTIMATION FOR SEA ICE
897
898   We argue that a fundamental challenge with modern machine learning models is their inability to
899   replicate the complex mechanics of sea ice in coastal regions. The poor performance in capturing
900
      fine-scale ice mechanics is not unique to any one method but is a common issue across various
      approaches at the target resolution. For instance, neither modern sea ice motion vectors (GLO;
901
      Noriaki et al., 2013) nor optical flow estimation methods (Farnebäck, 2003; Weinzaepfel et al.,
902
      2023; Sun et al., 2018) are well-suited for high-resolution ice velocity estimation. This degradation
903   in quality when transitioning to higher resolutions is illustrated in Figure 8. Moreover, deep learning
904   methods for optical flow estimation may be overfitted on common images and lack the generalization
905   needed for sea ice SAR imagery. Consequently, motion information is scarcely useful for predictions
906   in the region of interest.
907
      Low quality of optical flows might be caused by high homogenity of ice-sheet surface and stochastic
908
      local dynamics on 1-day scale. For similar reasons one can expect state-of-the-art models on video-
909   prediction task to fail on ice-dynamics forecasting, as their architectures sometimes are based on
910   optical flow estimation and prediction, and they assume the simple mechanical and deterministic
911   dynamics.
