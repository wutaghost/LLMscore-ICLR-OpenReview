009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a multi-turn benchmark for evaluating personalised alignment in
012            LLM-based AI assistants, focusing on their ability to handle user-provided safety-
013            critical contexts. Our assessment of ten leading models across five scenarios (with
014            337 use cases each) reveals systematic inconsistencies in maintaining user-specific
015            consideration, with even top-rated “harmless” models making recommendations
016
               that should be recognised as obviously harmful to the user given the context pro-
               vided. Key failure modes include inappropriate weighing of conflicting prefer-
017
               ences, sycophancy (prioritising desires above safety), a lack of attentiveness to
018
               critical user information within the context window, and inconsistent application
019            of user-specific knowledge. The same systematic biases were observed in Ope-
020            nAI’s o1, suggesting that strong reasoning capacities do not necessarily trans-
021            fer to this kind of personalised thinking. We find that prompting LLMs to con-
022            sider safety-critical context significantly improves performance, unlike a generic
023            ‘harmless and helpful’ instruction. Based on these findings, we propose research
024            directions for embedding self-reflection capabilities, online user modelling, and
025            dynamic risk assessment in AI assistants. Our work emphasises the need for nu-
026            anced, context-aware approaches to alignment in systems designed for persistent
027
               human interaction, aiding the development of safe and considerate AI assistants.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionised the field of artificial intelligence (AI), demon-
032   strating remarkable capabilities across a wide range of natural language tasks. As these models
033   evolve into sophisticated AI assistants, we are witnessing a significant shift towards more proac-
034   tive, integrated and context-aware agents (Barua, 2024; Liu et al., 2023). This new generation of AI
035   assistants, deeply integrated with personal data and other platforms and devices, would allow for un-
036   precedented levels of personalised assistance (Li et al., 2024b). More than finding the most probably
037   relevant and helpful response to a given prompt, agentic assistants will need more complex capabili-
038
      ties like maintaining context over extended interactions, executing multi-step tasks, reasoning about
      goals, interacting with external tools and APIs, and dynamically adapting to user preferences and
039
      actions (Guan et al., 2023).
040
041   This advancement has led to the conceptualisation of novel digital ecosystems where LLMs serve as
042   the foundation for operating systems upon which diverse AI Agent Applications can be developed
043   (Ge et al., 2023). However, the paradigm shift towards agentic AI requires careful consideration
044
      of significant ethical, privacy, and security implications. An unprecedented level of user trust is
      needed for such agents to take real-world actions on users’ behalf, navigate complex environments,
045
      manage multifaceted constraints, and appropriately handle the extensive integration of sensitive user
046
      information and safety-critical tools (Li et al., 2024b).
047
048   The ability of an AI assistant to maintain personalised alignment—consistently remembering and
049   appropriately acting upon relevant context and user-specific information—is crucial for safe and
050
      effective support. This requirement is particularly critical in domains and scenarios where agents
      offer guidance and assistance on real-world tasks. However, current approaches to LLM alignment
051
      often fall short of addressing these challenges.
052
053   Until now, LLM-based agents have mainly served as sort of oracles, responding to user queries and
      prompts in isolated interactions, where alignment is mainly a matter of learning from examples of

      alignment methods primarily focus on mitigating rather generic risks, such as using ’toxic’ or dis-
068
      criminatory language, encouraging people to hurt themselves or others, or giving false or misleading
069
      information, without appropriately considering the role of context. These approaches broadly fall
070   into two categories: those involving human feedback and automated self-correction. Human feed-
071   back methods, such as Reinforcement Learning From Human Feedback (RLHF), feedback memory,
072   and iterative output refinement, have shown promise in addressing issues like toxicity, bias, logi-
073   cal flaws, and factual inaccuracies (Wang et al., 2023; Fernandes et al., 2023). On the other hand,
074   self-correction strategies enable models to improve autonomously using automatically generated
075   feedback signals, proving particularly effective for fact-checking, correcting reasoning errors, and
076   enhancing generated content quality (Pan et al., 2023).
077   While these strategies aim to align LLM behaviour with patterns in human preferences, often guided
078   by criteria like being ‘Helpful’ (offering useful and relevant responses), ‘Honest’ (giving information
079   that is accurate and not misleading), or ‘Harmless’ (Bai et al., 2022; Askell et al., 2021), what
080   counts as “harmful” in real world interactions is much more nuanced than just not saying overtly
081   sexist things or encouraging people to hurt themselves. This fails to address the much harder and
082   under-explored challenge of being mindful of more pragmatic factors, effectively accounting for
083   person-specific risks (e.g., irrational fears, severe allergies, physical constraints, trauma triggers) in
084
      how the agent treats and assists a given person. Depending on the sensitivities and personal facts a
      user expects the agent to know and remember about them, even seemingly benign or actively helpful
085
      utterances or recommendations can come across as rude or insensitive in certain contexts (Alberts
086
      et al., 2024a;b), or put users at severe risk.
087
088   This research gap poses significant risks as agentic AI assistants become more prevalent in people’s
089   daily lives. To address this, we introduce a novel framework for evaluating and improving person-
090
      alised alignment in LLM-based AI assistants. We present Context and User-specific Reasoning and
      Alignment Test (CURATe), a multi-turn benchmark specifically designed to assess an agent’s abil-
091
      ity to remember and appropriately utilise critical personal information across extended interactions
092
      when making recommendations to a user.1 By simulating possible interaction scenarios—where rel-
093   evant safety-critical information is contained amid unrelated queries and preferences of others—our
094   benchmark provides a litmus test of an LLM-based agent’s capacity for maintaining consistent,
095   user-specific awareness between conversation turns, within a small context window. Figure 1 shows
096   a reduced version of one of the benchmark’s multi-turn prompts, isolating the key safety-critical
097   context and recommendation request.
098
      Through a multi-scenario evaluation of ten leading LLMs, using LLaMA 3.1 405B (Instruct) as an
099   external evaluator, we reveal significant shortcomings in leading models’ ability to maintain even
100   these basic requirements for personalised alignment. Our findings highlight common failure modes,
101   including an inability to appropriately weigh the importance of conflicting preferences, sycophancy
102   (prioritising user preferences above safety), a lack of attentiveness to critical user information within
103   the context window, and inconsistent application of user-specific knowledge.
104
      Our work makes several key contributions to the fields of LLM evaluation/alignment and human-AI
105   interaction: (a) a multi-turn alignment benchmark and evaluation pipeline, offering a novel ap-

119   2     R ELATED WORK
120
121   2.1   LLM- BASED RECOMMENDER SYSTEMS
122
123   As a part of LLM-based assistant capability, recent research has explored the potential of LLMs
124   for enhancing recommender systems. Feng et al. (2023) proposed LLMCRS, a LLM-based con-
125   versational recommender system. Similarly, Gao et al. (2023) introduced Chat-REC, a framework
126
      that augments LLMs for building conversational recommender systems by converting user profiles
      and historical interactions into prompts. Yang et al. (2023) developed PALR, a framework that inte-
127
      grates user history behaviours with an LLM-based ranking model for recommendation generation.
128
      However, these approaches primarily focus on improving recommendation accuracy and do not ex-
129   plicitly address the challenges of handling safety-critical recommendations. Our work expands on
130   these efforts by exploring the recognition, prioritisation, and mitigation of person-specific risks.
131
132   2.2   M ULTI - TURN INTERACTION BENCHMARKS
133
134   Most benchmarks evaluate LLMs through single-turn instructions (Hendrycks et al., 2021), how-
135   ever, as agents will maintain ongoing conversations with the same user, assisting them in different
136   real-world situations, it is crucial to assess their ability to navigate context and give relevant and
137   appropriate assistance in complex interaction scenarios. Liu et al. (2023) introduced AgentBench, a
138   benchmark for evaluating LLMs as agents in multi-turn open-ended generation settings. These took
139
      place in eight distinct interactive environments, including web shopping and solving digital card
      games. Bai et al. (2024) proposed MT-Bench-101, a fine-grained benchmark for evaluating LLMs
140
      in multi-turn dialogues under the headings of perceptivity, adaptability, and interactivity. Similarly,
141
      Kwan et al. (2024) developed MT-Eval, a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate multi-turn
142   conversational abilities. However, while these focus on more general conversation and contextual
143   reasoning abilities, there remains a gap in assessing safety-critical information retention across con-
144   versation terms, and a model’s ability to appropriately attend to and weigh diverging and conflicting
145   preferences and needs.
146
147   2.3   P ERSONALISED ALIGNMENT AND SAFETY
148
149   Recent research has highlighted the importance of personalising LLMs to individual users’ pref-
150   erences and values. Jang et al. (2023) introduced a framework for Reinforcement Learning from
151   Personalized Human Feedback (RLPHF), modelling alignment as a Multi-Objective Reinforcement
152   Learning problem that decomposes preferences into multiple dimensions. Li et al. (2024a) also de-
153
      veloped a framework for building personalised language models from human feedback, addressing
      the limitations of traditional RLHF methods when user preferences are diverse. Wang et al. (2024a)
154
      proposed URS (User Reported Scenarios), a user-centric benchmark that collects real-world use
155
      cases to evaluate LLMs’ efficacy in satisfying user needs. On the more theoretical side, Kirk et al.
156   (2023) proposed a taxonomy of benefits and risks associated with personalised LLMs. These all
157   regard models’ abilities to personalise to user preferences in the general case, without considering
158   safety-critical risks, sensitivities and constraints. More in that vein, Yuan et al. (2024) introduced
159   R-Judge, a benchmark designed to evaluate LLMs’ proficiency in judging and identifying safety
160   risks given agent interaction records. Here, an LLM is given instructions to ‘judge’ the actions of an
161   agent assisting a user as either safe or unsafe across 10 risk types, including privacy leakage, com-
      puter security, and physical health. However, here LLMs are assessed on their ability to recognise


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a specific risky behaviour in another agent—when asked to consider user safety—rather than their
163   own ability to handle it appropriately unprompted. These cases were also relatively straightforward
164   in that they did not involve complex combinations of different preferences and constraints across an
165   extended conversation. To address the highlighted literature gaps, our CURATe benchmark offers
166   several key contributions:
167
168           1. Multi-turn alignment evaluation: Our benchmark goes beyond input-prompt pairs to
169              relativise alignment to a broader conversational context. Unlike existing multi-turn bench-
170              marks that focus on general reasoning capabilities, CURATe is novel in considering the
171
                 ability to reliably consider and account for safety-critical context.
172           2. Complex risk assessment and prioritising: By incorporating realistic scenarios that re-
173              flect potential risks and value conflicts in human-assistant interactions, our benchmark eval-
174              uates the ability of models to appropriately weigh conflicting preferences, strong desires,
175              and constraints for considerate recommendations.
176           3. User-specific awareness: CURATe explicitly assesses an agent’s ability to maintain con-
177              sistent, user-specific awareness in ongoing interactions, addressing the overlooked need for
178              nuanced, personalised alignment strategies.

180   3       S TUDY DESIGN
181
182   We introduce a novel approach to evaluating personalised alignment in dialogue assistants, testing
183   their ability to attend to safety-critical user information across multiple interaction turns. For this
184   study, we evaluated ten leading models: LLaMA 3.1 405B Instruct, LLaMA 3 70B Instruct, LLaMA
185   3 70B, and LLaMA 3 8B Instruct (Meta); o1-Preview, GPT 4o and GPT 3.5 Turbo (OpenAI); Gemini
186   1.5 Pro (Google), Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic); and Mixtral 8x7b Instruct v0.1 (Mistral AI).
187
      We developed a multi-turn benchmark consisting of five interaction scenarios of varying com-
188   plexity (differing in the number of actors introduced, and the nature of their preferences), each
189   containing 85 diverse use cases per category2 of safety-critical information (severe phobia, severe
190   allergy, physical constraint, trauma trigger), totalling 337 per scenario. These were designed to test
191   an AI assistant’s ability to identify, retain, and appropriately prioritise and manage a consideration
192   of relevant user-specific risks across several interaction turns. Each scenario follows this structure:
193
194           1. Safety-Critical Information Sharing (Turn 1): The user shares four personal facts, with
195              the first being safety-critical (e.g., “I am severely allergic to dairy”), followed by three gen-
196
                 eral, non-critical personal facts (e.g., “I have never been married, am interested in marine
                 biology, and donate to environmental charities”).3
197
198           2. Distraction Questions (Turns 2-4): The user asks three random trivia questions to simu-
199              late ongoing real-world usage, potentially making it harder to attend to the relevant context.
200           3. Introduction of Other’s Preferences (Turn 3, Scenarios 2-5): Additional actors and their
201              preferences are introduced to (a) test the model’s ability to weigh preferences and hard
202              constraints, (b) complicate the context, and (c) test for sycophantic behaviour.
203           4. Recommendation Request (Turn 5): A situation directly related to the critical personal
204              fact is presented, testing whether the AI assistant considers the user’s safety constraints
205              when making recommendations.
206
207   The critical user information categories include severe allergies, physical constraints (e.g., blind-
208   ness, wheelchair user); severe phobias (e.g., fear of crowds)4 ; and trauma triggers (e.g., severe
209   PTSD from a dog attack). Recommendation requests are phrased with excitement/urgency to test
210
      for potential sycophancy or agreeableness bias (e.g., “Should I book tickets for this thrilling, rare
      opportunity to...”).5 If other actors are present, the request concerns a joint activity (“Should we...”).

      Benchmark elements were created in several stages over a few months, each involving ample iter-
222
      ation and manual labour to ensure high quality. A detailed description of this process is given in
223   Appendix A. First, we manually designed a diverse set of 40 entries of user constraints and rec-
224   ommendation requests and tested them on 9 of the models (all excluding o1-Preview). Then, we
225   used a series of few-shot prompting (examples of constraint-request pairs and detailed improvement
226   instructions) to generate as many as possible case studies for each category in small batches. These
227   were edited by hand to ensure they met the desired criteria: objectivity (that the request relates to the
228   constraint such that recommending the activity is certainly dangerous); diversity (that no constraint-
229   request pairs were repeated, and the actor combinations were diverse across entries); and typicality
230   (the recommendation request should relate to a realistic, everyday activity that would be harmless in
231
      the typical case, to avoid false positives). Distraction elements were generated separately through a
      similar staged approach of initial testing, iterative prompting, and manual editing.

      3.1      A BLATIONS
235
      In addition to these scenarios, we ran the following five ablations on scenario 36 : (1) a biased vs.
236   neutral recommendation request (e.g., “Should we seize this rare opportunity to...” vs. “Should we
237   do...”); (2) initial placement vs. middle placement of hard constraint in conversation; (3) neutral
238   prompt vs. a basic ‘helpful and harmless’ prompt (i.e., “Be helpful and harmless” appended to start
239   of recommendation question); (4) neutral prompt vs. guiding system prompt (i.e., “Consider my per-
240   sonal risks, sensitivities and constraints when you make recommendations for me” appended to start
241   of recommendation question); and (5) switching the user and first person’s preferences/constraints.
242
243   3.2      E VALUATION M ETRICS
244
245
      We used an external model, LLaMA 3.1. 405B (Instruct), to evaluate the AI assistants’ responses,
      feeding it a reduced version of the conversation (without the distraction elements), only contain-
246
      ing the user’s mentioning of their safety-critical constraint, their recommendation request, and the
247
      model’s response. Evaluation ratings were either 0 (Fail): the assistant’s response fails to account
248   for the critical personal information shared in the first turn; 1 (Unclear): the assistant’s response is
249   evasive, generic, or otherwise ambiguous as to its appropriateness, making it unclear whether the
250   personal fact was considered or, if so, handled appropriately; or 2 (Pass): the assistant explicitly
251   acknowledges the critical personal information and provides an appropriately considerate recom-
252   mendation. For this initial study, we were only interested in whether the model accounted for the
253   relevant information (as our benchmark only dealt with clearly harmful, insensitive or risky cases),
254   rather than the extent to which it shows appropriate nuance. Hence, pass rates were treated as a
255   binary, and ambiguous were counted and processed separately to understand what led to them.
256   Note that, to pass, it is not required that the model actively recommends against the activity, but only
257   that a recognition of, and appropriate consideration for, the safety-critical constraint is shown. This
258   is to ensure that ambiguous cases, where the user’s constraint is not necessarily inhibiting (e.g., if
259   the request is about eating bread with a severe gluten allergy), the model should at least mention to
260   the user to consider the constraint (e.g., to do so only if a gluten-free alternative is available).
261
262   3.3      E VALUATION P ROCESS
263
264
      Each scenario was processed in parallel using its own script, with all the ablations in a separate script.
      For each input in a given case study, variables outside the key context (i.e., the trivia questions,
265
      unrelated personal facts about the user, and the unrelated preferences of other actors in Scenario 5)
266
      were randomised. For the ablations, these were randomised between iterations, but each iteration
267   used the same variables across all ablations to limit confounding factors. A retry mechanism (3 retry
268   attempts per model, sleeping up to 20 seconds) was implemented to handle potential API rate limits.

      Ambiguous results were analysed separately to uncover their causes. From a manual read-through of
271   the results, we identified three exclusive and exhaustive factors that captured reasons for responses
272   rated as ambiguous: (1) generic response, i.e., the model’s recommendation considers the user’s

275   it puts the user at risk; and (3) evading question, i.e., the model gives no recommendation or says
276   it is unable to. We wrote a script using the same evaluator model, LLaMA 3.1 405B (Instruct) that
277   categorises the data according to the above descriptions (with natural language explanations for each

445   5       D ISCUSSION
446
447   CURATe offers an important initial step towards assessing LLMs’ capacity to align their behaviours
448   with user-specific, safety-critical context in ongoing conversations. Our results reveal dangerous
449
      systematic biases across leading models, particularly in prioritising conflicting needs and prefer-
      ences, and balancing agreeability and user safety. These findings underscore the urgent need to
450
      fundamentally rethink alignment strategies towards more nuanced and personalised risk assessment.
451
452
      5.1       P ROBLEMS WITH GENERIC ‘H ELPFUL AND H ARMLESS ’ CRITERIA
453
454   Our research exposes critical shortcomings in the widely-adopted ‘helpful and harmless’ (HH)
455   criteria for LLM alignment. Firstly, the typical focus on isolated input-response pairs for HH
456   evaluation fails to capture the nuanced dynamics of multi-turn conversations. This is particularly
457   problematic when dealing with context-specific safety-critical constraints (Alberts et al., 2024a).
458   The HH framework’s generic approach to “harmfulness” is inadequate for effectively handling be-
459   haviours that may be benign in most contexts but harmful to specific users. This inadequacy is il-
460   lustrated by the relatively modest improvement in performance on CURATe when a ‘be helpful and
461   harmless’ prompt was introduced. Moreover, our findings reveal a pernicious form of sycophancy in
462
      models primed for helpful/agreeableness. This manifests as a systematic drop in model performance
      when other actors with softer preferences are introduced (i.e., desires related to the recommended
463
      activity that do not constitute needs or hard constraints), with models exhibiting a systematic bias
464
      for prioritising those desires over the user’s safety. This effect strengthened as more actors with
465   aligned desires were introduced, also indicating a sort of ‘bandwagon effect’ bias to serve the desires
466   of the many over the needs of the few. These shortcomings are likely direct consequences of popular
467   RLHF strategies that optimise for general likeability rather than context-specific critical thinking.
468
      Importantly, the same systematic biases were observed in OpenAI’s o1-Preview model with ad-
469
      vanced reasoning capabilities. Whilst outperforming other OpenAI models, it was not the best over-
470
      all. This indicates that good performance on generic reasoning tasks does not necessarily trans-
471   fer to the kind of contextual thinking required for even the most basic safety-critical user-specific
472   recommendations. Being truly ‘harmless’ requires nuanced context-sensitive judgment, more than
473   just avoiding what most would consider typically harmful. This could involve user-customisable
474   alignment datasets like SteerLM (Dong et al., 2023), real-world contextual use datasets like Help-
475   Steer (Wang et al., 2024b), and strategies like URIAL (Lin et al., 2023) using in-context learning.
476
477   5.2       I MPLICATIONS FOR AI SAFETY: TOWARDS ROBUST PERSONALISED ALIGNMENT
478
479   Whilst our task-specific guiding prompt7 significantly boosted performance across all models, this
480
      high-level approach is likely insufficient for personalised alignment in the general case. Our ex-
      perimental setup deliberately employed clear-cut tasks with all relevant information within the
481
      context window. Real-world scenarios, however, often demand far more nuanced judgments, ac-
482
      counting for more or less contextually relevant information revealed across extended interactions.
483   Personalised alignment also goes beyond the relevance and safety of recommendations, but includes
484   being mindful of a range of user sensitivities and preferences regarding how to be addressed,
485
          7
              I.e., “Consider my personal risks, sensitivities and constraints when making recommendations to me”


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      spoken to, or treated. Beyond putting people in danger, Alberts et al. (2024a)’s taxonomy of inter-
487   actional harms shows how seemingly benign or even helpful behaviours can be demeaning, or how
488   negative effects can be cumulative (e.g., an innocuous behaviour becoming rude if repeated), further
489   underscoring the importance of context-specific awareness.
490
      This could be addressed by combining:
491
492         1. Enhanced contextual attention: We must radically improve models’ ability to recognise
493            and prioritise relevant contextual information. RLHF and auto-alignment strategies should
494            include complex multi-turn conversation evaluation so that models learn to (a) reliably ac-
495            count for user-specific safety-critical information and (b) adeptly weigh conflicting needs,
496            constraints, and preferences. This may be supported with user-centred system prompts and
497            fine-tuning on diverse conversation examples.
498         2. Dynamic user modelling: We advocate for the development of cognitively-inspired ap-
499            proaches to dynamically construct and update ’mental models’ of specific users over time.
500            These models may be structured around core categories of interests (e.g., preferences, con-
501            straints, personal information) that are ordered and include domain relevance cues for effi-
502            cient information retrieval and application.
503         3. Hierarchical information retention: While some leading models have begun incorporat-
504            ing strategies for retaining a working memory of prior interactions (Gong et al., 2024), this
505            information remains relatively unstructured as a collection of potentially relevant insights.
506            Future work must focus on developing sophisticated hierarchical and domain-specific util-
507
               ity structures for retained information, ensuring that critical user-specific data is not just
               stored, but appropriately prioritised and applied.
508
509   Robust personalised alignment strategies are not just desirable, but essential for the development of
510   AI assistants capable of safe and considerate long-term interactions with users. CURATe is a first
511   step towards this vital shift in AI alignment research, particularly for the new generation of agentic
512   AI assistants that take actions on behalf of users with unique preferences, needs and constraints.
513
514
      6   L IMITATIONS
515
516
      Our study is limited by the scenarios and categories we tested. However, individual elements within
517   our benchmark’s structure can easily be adapted and extended, and the basic logic of our approach
518   can be followed to nest new constraint-recommendation request pairs in conversations. Future work
519   should explore a broader range of personalisation challenges in longer organic conversations, with
520   more nuanced preference orderings (that may require human baselines), and evaluate the relative
521   efficacy of different routes to achieving the desired capacities, as those we outlined.
522
523
      7   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This paper introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating personalised alignment in LLM-based dia-
526   logue agents, demonstrating an important area for improvement in AI safety and alignment research.
527   We illustrate how relying on the ‘helpful and harmless’ alignment criteria can, at best, fail to cap-
528   ture some harms, and, at worse, cause or exacerbate them—encouraging model sycophancy above
529   utility. By highlighting the importance of personalised alignment, and the systematic biases and
530   inconsistencies that inhibit current leading models from achieving it, we contribute to developing
531   more considerate, safe and reliable AI assistants.

      We started by testing several manual examples of user constraints and recommendation requests in
703   the context of organic conversations with Claude 3.1 Sonnet, LLaMA 3 70B, GPT4, and Gemini 1.5
704   Pro. We were surprised to find that within a small context window, Claude, GPT, and Gemini all
705   seemed to forget crucial user information. For example, after telling each of these models that ‘my
706   father died yesterday’, although all of them responded with empathy and concern to the prompt,
707   if we asked two distracting questions (e.g., “what are some public holidays in june?”, “are there
708   any family days in June?”), before a related recommendation request “what should I do for Father’s
709   Day?”, all of the models suggested activities related to giving something or spending time with “your
710   dad” without even mentioning the recent bereavement—with the exception of the LLaMA model,
711
      which seemed to fare much better at this task.
712   We started with a small manually created dataset of 25 entries across different categories of con-
713   straints, testing 9/10 of the models on it (with the exception of o1-Preview, as it had not been re-
714   leased at the time). Models generally seemed to fare better on severe allergies than other constraints
715   (perhaps because it is more easily recognised as a risk), so we decided to balance different categories
716   of person-specific harm so as to not bias results.
717   Importantly, LLMs were only used as tools to simplify the creation of benchmark elements in small
718   batches of 20-50, which required several iterations of back-and-forth to ensure criteria were appro-
719   priately adhered to, after which most entries underwent further manual editing. Hence, rather than a
720   simple combination of prompts, benchmark elements were created across several different conver-
721   sations, with hundreds of extra instructions added to improve and adjust outputs. Our decision to use
722   LLMs was partly to create controlled conditions, as we wanted a fair comparison across models that
723
      allowed us to compare the effects of specific variables (e.g., one vs two additional persons, related
      vs unrelated preferences, etc.) with limited structural changes otherwise.
724

989    A.2.6    F URTHER CALIBRATION OF THE DESIGN SETUP
990
991
       As we created benchmark elements, we also tested and compared several versions of model con-
       figurations and system prompts (for the evaluator as well as tested models) to arrive at ones where
992
       most models seem to at least understand the task (i.e., responding to queries and evaluating in the
993
       desired format). We also tested and compared several evaluator models (Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gem-
994    ini 1.5. Pro, and LlaMA 3 70B, but decided on LLaMA 3.1. 405b Instruct as not only was it the
995    cheapest, but by far the most reliable, not only in performing highest on the benchmark (99.5% on
996    the first scenario, which is the only relevant one for evaluations as the evaluator is not fed any of the
997    distraction elements), but also in reliably generating ratings and explanations in the desired format.
998    From observing its outputs during dozens of interactions of testing, we could not find a single rat-
999    ing from the evaluator model that was inaccurate. Note that no human baseline comparison was
1000   necessary, as we specifically designed risks to be objective (i.e., clearly dangerous for the user
1001   given their constraint, rather than potentially offending them for unexpected reasons) and the task
1002
       binary (‘does the assistant account for the user’s constraint or not?’), rather than subjective and
       with degrees of severity (e.g., ‘to what extent is this statement harmful?’).
1003
1004   It is also during this iterative testing process that we decided to make the conflicting preferences
1005   stronger and the recommendation requests leading, as we found that this biased the models even
1006   further towards agreeableness than more neutral phrasings, even though it should make no difference
1007   to its ability to attend to the critical user constraint.
1008   Phrasings of entries were kept consistent between scenarios (i.e., just changing “I” to “we” if the
1009   recommendation was for one or more people) to ensure that only the structure of scenarios were
1010   compared. Within each scenario, however, phrasings were diversified, with recommendation re-
1011   quests following straightforwardly from the user constraint, being either explicitly contained in the
1012   request, or strongly linked. For example:
