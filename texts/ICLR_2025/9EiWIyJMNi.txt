011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series prediction, a crucial task across various domains, faces signifi-
014            cant challenges due to the inherent complexities of time series data, includ-
015            ing non-stationarity, multi-scale periodicity, and transient dynamics, particularly
016            when tackling long-term predictions. While Transformer-based architectures have
017            shown promise, their quadratic complexity with sequence length hinders their ef-
018            ficiency for long-term predictions. Recent advancements in State-Space Mod-
019            els, such as Mamba, offer a more efficient alternative for long-term modeling,
020
               but they lack the capability to capture multi-scale periodicity and transient dy-
               namics effectively. Meanwhile, they are susceptible to the data noise issue in
021
               time series. This paper proposes a novel framework, FLDmamba (Fourier and
022
               Laplace Transform Decomposition Mamba), addressing these limitations. FLD-
023            mamba leverages the strengths of both Fourier and Laplace transforms to effec-
024            tively capture both multi-scale periodicity, transient dynamics within time series
025            data, and improve the robustness of the model to the data noise issue. Our ex-
026            tensive experiments demonstrate that FLDmamba achieves superior performance
027            on time series prediction benchmarks, outperforming both Transformer-based and
028            other Mamba-based architectures. This work offers a computationally efficient
029            and effective solution for long-term time series prediction, paving the way for
030            its application in real-world scenarios. To promote the reproducibility of our
031
               method, we have made both the code and data accessible via the following URL:
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FLDmamba.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Time series prediction, which forecasts the future values of a (multivariate) variable based on its
039
      historical values, finds its application across a wide range of fields. Examples include weather
      prediction (Lorenc, 1986; Bauer et al., 2015), power grid management (Tang, 2011), traffic predic-
040
      tion (Yu et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2020), and stock market (Fama, 1970), to name just a few. Despite
041
      significant advancements in this domain, the inherent complexities of time series data, such as non-
042   stationarity, multi-scale periodicity, intrinsic stochasticity, and noise, pose substantial challenges to
043   existing predictive models in long-term prediction.
044
045
      Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017), successful in NLP and computer vision,
      have been explored extensively in time series prediction. Although they demonstrate impressive
046
      performance, they face degraded accuracy and efficiency in long-term time series prediction due to
047
      their quadratic complexity w.r.t. sequence length. iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023) addresses inter-
048   series dependencies by inverting attention layers, but its tokenization approach, which uses a simple
049   MLP layer, fails to capture intricate evolutionary patterns in the data, as shown in Figure 1. Thus,
050   Transformer-based models face challenges in computational efficiency and predictive performance.
051   This can be explained by that the computational cost of self-attention mechanism, which is at the
052   heart of Transformer-based model, is O(L2 ), where L is the sequence length. Meanwhile, the self-
053   attention mechanism leads to point-wise treatment independently and failure to capture intricate
      evolutionary patterns in time series data.

054
      Recently, architectures based on State-Space Models (Gu et al., 2021a; Smith et al., 2022) have
055   emerged as a promising alternative due to the computational efficiency inherent in linear models to
056   address the long-term prediction challenge. A notable example is Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), which
057   employs a linear state space with input-dependent selection. The linear state space allows efficient
058   and parallelized long-sequence modeling, while the input-dependent selection allows propagating
059   and forgetting information in long sequences, facilitating in-context learning. Mamba’s design is
060   a good start. However, there are three challenges that Mamba-based methods for time-series pre-
061   diction cannot address. (1) Multi-scale periodicity. Time series data typically consists of patterns
062   that occur periodically, such as in traffic, electricity, and weather. In addition, the periodic patterns
063
      typically exist in multiple time scales and are superimposed together. For example, in weather data,
      the temperature can fluctuate both in the time scale of a day and a year. Mamba lacks frequency
064
      modeling to capture such multi-scale periodicity. (2) Transient dynamics. In addition to peri-
065
      odicity, time series data often shows complex transient dynamics, which can be characterized as
066   time-varying patterns, short-term fluctuations, or event-driven variations. These transient dynam-
067   ics pose significant challenges for Mamba, as Mamba exhibits a tendency to prioritize point-wise
068   temporal dynamics over neighboring transient dynamics. Figure 1 presents a comparative analysis
069   of the time series predicted by S-Mamba against the ground truth values on the real-world datasets
070   ETTm1 and ETTm2 (Zhou et al., 2021). A visual inspection reveals a distinct disparity in the dis-
071   tribution of the predicted time series compared to the ground truth. This discrepancy is due to that
072   S-Mamba fails to effectively capture multi-scale periodicity and transient dynamics inherent within
073   the time series data. (3) Data noise. Noise in time series data introduces random fluctuations into
074
      the data, increasing the uncertainty in predictions. Models trained on noisy data may produce less
      reliable forecasts with wider prediction intervals, making it harder to make accurate predictions.
075
076   To address the limitations of existing methods, this paper proposes two key technical advancements
077   to enhance Mamba in time series prediction: (1) Incorporating Fourier analysis into Mamba:
078   Mamba primarily focuses on capturing temporal dynamics in the temporal domain, lacking the abil-
079   ity to model long-term dynamics in the frequency domain, such as multi-scale patterns overlooked
080
      in the temporal domain. To address this, we propose integrating Fourier analysis into Mamba, en-
      abling it to capture long-term properties, such as multi-scale patterns, in the frequency domain.
081
      In addition, the Fourier Transform can help in separating the underlying patterns or trends from
082
      noise in the time series data by highlighting dominant frequency components. By focusing on these
083   dominant frequencies, the model can reduce the impact of noise that might otherwise affect the
084   accuracy of predictions, thereby enhancing the model’s robustness to noisy data. (2) Integrating
085   Laplace analysis into Mamba: To improve Mamba’s ability to capture transient dynamics, such
086   as short-term fluctuations, we introduce Laplace analysis into Mamba. This integration allows the
087   model to better understand the relationships between neighboring data points and capture transient
088   changes. Based on these two advancements, we propose a novel framework, FLDmamba (Fourier
089   and Laplace Transform Decomposition Mamba), specifically designed for long-term time series
090   prediction. FLDmamba leverages the strengths of both Fourier and Laplace analysis, enabling it to
091
      effectively capture both multi-scale periodicity and transient dynamics within time series data.
092   The core innovation of FLDmamba lies in its strategic integration of frequency analysis and Laplace
093   analysis within the Mamba framework. By representing time series data in the frequency domain
094   through Fourier analysis, FLDmamba effectively captures multi-scale periodicity, improving the
095   ability to conduct long-term prediction. Simultaneously, the incorporation of Laplace analysis im-
096   proves the model’s capacity to capture local correlations between neighboring data points, leading
097
      to a more accurate representation of transient dynamics. As shown in Figure 1, FLDmamba sig-
      nificantly outperforms S-Mamba. In addition, as the backbone of our framework FLDmamba is
098
      Mamba, it is highly efficient and well-suited for deployment in large-scale real-world applications.
099

866   While several Mamba-based methods exist for time series prediction, such as those by Wang et al.         W6

878   these transforms, we also tackle the issue of data noise in time series, setting our approach apart
879   from existing methods that primarily focus on long-term prediction efficiency.

      6.4     M ODEL C OMPLEXITY
883
      This section presents a complexity analysis of our proposed model, FLDmamba. The computational
884
      complexity of the base Mamba model is O(BLV N ), where B represents the batch size, L de-
885
      notes the sequence length, V signifies the number of variables, and N indicates the state expansion
886   factor. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) in FLDmamba has time complexity of O(BLN log L),
887   and the inverse Laplace transform has time complexity of O(BLN ), both significantly smaller than
888   O(BLV N ). Therefore, the total time complexity is still O(BLV N ). In other words, FLDmamba
889   maintains a comparable computational time complexity to the base Mamba model, making it a
890   promising framework for large-scale real-world applications in time series prediction. This compu-
891   tational efficiency allows FLDmamba to handle extensive datasets and complex time series scenarios
892   without significant performance degradation.
