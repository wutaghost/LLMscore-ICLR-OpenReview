009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Efficiently and accurately searching large-scale single-cell RNA-seq databases has
012                been a long standing computational challenge. There is an increasing number
013                of single-cell retrieval methods, particularly those based on single-cell founda-
014                tion models, proposed in the literature. However, this field lacks a comprehensive
015                benchmark among these methods. This gap exists due to the lack of standard eval-
016                uation metrics and comprehensive benchmark datasets. Addressing these chal-
017                lenges, we propose a comprehensive evaluation benchmark to assess the capabil-
018                ities of 12 existing single-cell retrieval methods from three classes: non-machine
019
                   learning method, VAE-based methods and single-cell foundation model (scFM)
                   based methods. We propose a series of label-dependent and label-free evaluation
020
                   metrics to assess the performance of single-cell retrieval methods. Through bench-
021
                   marking across diverse settings (cross-platform, cross-species and cross-omics),
022                our notable findings include: top scFMs such as UCE, scFoundation and SCimi-
023                larity show substantial overall advantage compared with other methods; traditional
024                non-machine learning method perform well in cell retrieval thus should not be ne-
025                glected; common cells retrieved by top methods share distinct gene expression
026                patterns; label-free metrics have consistent evaluation outcome compared with
027                label-based methods thus can be employed in a broader scenario. Our rigorous
028                and comprehensive evaluation identifies the challenges and limitations of current
029                retrieval methods and serves as foundation for further development of single-cell
030
                   retrieval methods.1

033   1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The technological advancements in single-cell sequencing has led to increasingly available scRNA-
036   seq datasets. To date, the transcriptome of trillions of cells spanning diverse tissues and species
037   have been profiled using various sequencing technologies (Biology et al., 2023). The main objective
038
      of vast scRNA sequencing is to create comprehensive single-cell reference for discovering new
      biological insights and serve as foundation for clinical usage. For example, for biologists, they
039
      could utilize existing single-cell references to identify cell-type specific marker genes or disease cell
040
      states (Anders & Huber, 2010). For clinicians, they could compare the sequencing outcomes from
041   patients with the single-cell reference atlas to determine patient specific transcriptome change thus
042   conduct precision diagnosis (Dann et al., 2023).
043
044
      Both the clinical and biological applications with respect to single-cell reference require easy and
      fast access to these datasets. However, searching the large scale single-cell reference spanning mul-
045
      tiple tissues, species or sequencing platforms is a challenging problem. First, scRNA-seq datasets
046
      are usually high dimensional and sparse (Kharchenko, 2021), thus searching with cosine similar-
047   ity or L2-distance between count vectors would be inefficient and inaccurate. Second, scRNA-seq
048   datasets are largely affected by batch effects (Haghverdi et al., 2018), which means that datasets
049   from different experiment batches or platforms may have substantial distribution shift. Therefore,
050   retrieving from massive single-cell datasets from different experiment platforms is considerably dif-
051   ficult. Furthermore, scRNA-seq datasets are affected by multiple types of technical noise during
052   sequencing (Mereu et al., 2020), thus the retrieval method must be robust.

071
072   Multiple methods have been proposed to resolve this challenging problem. These methods can be
073   categorized into three classes, non-machine learning methods, VAE (Variational Autoencoder)
074   based methods and single-cell foundation model (scFM) based methods. Initial attempts (Sato
075   et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2021) utilized classical approaches such as local sensitive hashing to ef-
076
      ficiently search large-scale scRNA-seq databases. With the development of advanced dimension
      reduction approach such as VAE (Kingma, 2013), there are growing interests in learning low dimen-
077
      sional representation from high dimensional sparse scRNA-seq count matrix (Lopez et al., 2018;
078
      Svensson et al., 2020). Therefore, several single-cell retrieval methods (Cao et al., 2020) have also
079   been developed to search for similar cells using learned low dimensional embedding. Recently, in-
080   spired by the advances of large-scale pre-trained foundation models in a wide range of biological
081   domains (Chen et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024c), there are also several single-cell
082   foundation models (scFMs) (Theodoris et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024) developed. These powerful
083   foundation models generate meaningful dense embeddings in a zero-shot manner and have been
084   applied to a wide range of downstream applications, including searching large databases (Heimberg
085   et al., 2023).
086   Despite numerous efforts in developing powerful methods for searching across large scale single-
087   cell databases, there lacks unified and comprehensive evaluation and benchmark on the effectiveness
088   of these methods. First, there is no direct comparison between scFMs and other methods in
089   existing works. SCimilarity (Heimberg et al., 2023) is the only scFM that explicitly performs cell
090   retrieval in the downstream applications, but the performance against other retrieval methods has
091   not been explicitly evaluated. Meanwhile, the evaluation metrics of cell retrieval are still limited.
092   Unlike query-passage retrieval in text-mining which has explicit ground-truth annotation on retrieval
093
      pairs, there are no ground-truth annotation on cell pairs. Thus, the cell retrieval methods are usually
      evaluated on whether the retrieved cells have similar cell types as the ground-truth annotation, which
094
      can be quite limited as the cell type annotations only provide coarse-grained information and can
095
      be biased or incorrect. Furthermore, existing cell retrieval methods have only been evaluated in
096   limited species or platforms, which limits their applicability and generalizability. For example,
097   SCimilarity is solely evaluated on scRNA-seq datasets generated with 10x platform for humans and
098   Cellfishing.jl (Sato et al., 2019) is only evaluated on single-platform datasets.
099
      In this paper, we systematically benchmarked the performance of cell retrieval of various single-
100
      cell FMs against traditional non-machine learning cell retrieval methods and VAE based retrieval
101
      methods with our proposed metrics (Figure. 1). We included 2 non-machine learning methods, 3
102   VAE-based methods and 7 scFM-based methods, which to the best of our knowledge covers all the
103   major methods for cell retrieval. Our evaluation datasets span multiple-platforms, multiple-species
104   and multiple-omics for unbiased and fair evaluation of cell retrieval. Meanwhile, We designed a
105   comprehensive evaluation pipeline for cell retrieval, including 1) label-dependent evaluation includ-
106   ing cell type matching, batch mixing and recall 2) label-free evaluation including the consistency
107   between retrieved cells and the consistency between the DE genes identified from the retrieved cells
      across methods.

      The key takeaways from the comprehensive benchmarking of cell retrieval methods can be summa-
109   rized as follows.
110
111          • Top scFMs such as SCimilarity, UCE and scFoundation show significant advantage over
112            other baseline methods in a zero-shot manner in the majority of settings, but still perform
113            poorly when used on datasets distant from the pre-training database (e.g. on tissues, species
114            or omics unseen or rare during pre-training).
115          • Traditional non-machine learning methods perform surprisingly well in most settings, high-
116            lighting their unique advantage in retrieving cells with similar cell states thus should not be
117            ignored in future benchmarking studies.
118          • Common cells retrieved by top single-cell retrieval methods share distinct differential gene
119            expression patterns, showing that top methods can identify similar cell states from the
120            reference cells.
121          • Label-free metrics are consistent with label-dependent metrics, which can be a comple-
122            mentary evaluation method when the cell annotations are missing or inconsistent across
123            different reference datasets.

      2     C ELL S EARCH AND R ETRIEVAL M ETHODS
127
128
      2.1   N ON - MACHINE LEARNING METHOD
129
      Even before the popularity of machine learning in biological data analysis, there are already a num-
130   ber of methods proposed to resolve the cell search and retrieval challenge. CellFishing.jl (Sato et al.,
131   2019) converts the gene expression count matrix to bit vectors with local sensitive hashing (LSH) to
132   perform retrieval. scFind (Lee et al., 2021) searches large scale scRNA-seq database to identify the
133   set of cells that express a set of genes specified by the user.
134
135   2.2   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER BASED M ETHOD
136
137   Projecting high dimensional and sparse gene expression count vector to low-dimensional dense
138   cell embedding is a central task in machine learning for single-cell analysis. Learning meaning-
139   ful cell embeddings serves as foundation for a wide range of downstream tasks, such as cell type
140
      annotation (Xu et al., 2021) and trajectory inference (Qiu et al., 2017). Therefore, a number of
      machine learning methods mainly based on variational autoencoder have been developed to learn
141
      low-dimensional cell embedding for cell search and retrieval, including CellBlast (Cao et al., 2020),
142
      scmap (Kiselev et al., 2018), scVI (Lopez et al., 2018) and LDVAE (Svensson et al., 2020).
143
144
      2.3   F OUNDATION M ODEL BASED M ETHOD
145
146   single-cell Foundation Models (FMs) have much higher model capacity than traditional machine
147   learning methods and are pre-trained on massive scRNA-seq datasets. Therefore, single-cell FMs
148   can generate meaningful low-dimensional embedding of cells, even in a zero-shot manner (He-
149   imberg et al., 2023). There are several single-cell FMs have been proposed, including Gene-
150   former (Theodoris et al., 2023), scGPT (Cui et al., 2024), UCE (Rosen et al., 2023), scFounda-
151   tion (Hao et al., 2024), scMulan (Bian et al., 2024) and SCimilarity (Heimberg et al., 2023). Among
152   all these methods, SCimilarity is the only FM specifically highlighting cell retrieval, while other
153
      scFMs are also capable of retrieving cells and the performance of different scFMs has not been
      explicitly evaluated and benchmarked.

205   3.2.2    L ABEL - FREE E VALUATION
206
207
      A critical challenge in evaluating cell retrieval is the lack of ground-truth cell-to-cell relationship an-
      notations. Unlike traditional information retrieval setting with ground-truth query-target pairs, there
208
      are no ground-truth cell pair annotations. Even though single-cell datasets are usually annotated
209
      with different cell types, using the cell type annotation accuracy as the sole evaluation metric can be
210   biased. In many cases, the cell types are annotated based on cell clustering with low-dimensional
211   representation (t-SNE, UMAP) of cells, therefore cannot be considered as fully accurate (Heimberg
212   et al., 2023). Inspired by the voting theory (O’Connor & Robertson, 2003) that performs a system-
213   atic aggregation of results in order to achieve consensus, we hypothesis that reference cells that are
214   commonly retrieved by different single-cell retrieval methods are likely to be more biologically rele-
215   vant with the query cells. We measure the similarity between different levels of consistency between
      the results from different single-cell retrieval methods.

      Consistency between the retrieved cells Following Fan et al. (2024a), we propose to compute
217   the average overlap between the retrieved cells from each method for the same query cells. Given
218                                                                  a          b
      query cell xi , retrieval methods a and b may retrieve cells {yik } and {yik } respectively. We com-
219                                           a K           b K
      pute the Jaccard Similarity between {yik }k=1 and {yik }k=1 and average over all query cells as the
220   AvgOverlap score.
221
222   Consistency between the DE genes from the retrieved cells Simply comparing the overlap of
223   retrieved cells ignores the gene expression similarity between retrieved cells. Therefore, we analyze
224   the consistency of gene expression features and patterns of the retrieved cells from different methods
225   by comparing their DE (Differentially Expressed) genes. For each query cell and its retrieved cells,
226   we computed their DE genes compared with all other cells. The DE genes are defined as genes with
227   statistically higher expression compared with background (Wilcoxon rank-sum test, p-value < 0.02
228   and log-fold changes > 0.5). Then, we computed the Jaccard similarity between the DE genes
229
      across all query cells. Therefore, this method better captures the consistency between retrieved cells
      by considering their DE gene patterns.

247   3.3.2    E VALUATION DATASETS
248
249   We utilized multiple commonly used scRNA-seq datasets to evaluate the effectiveness of cell re-
250   trieval. These datasets span multiple species, tissues and experiment platforms.
251
252   Multi-platform evaluation Multiple scRNA-seq technologies have been developed with differ-
253   ent tagging methods and sequencing libraries, thus different sequencing technologies may exhibit
254   significant technical variation. With the increasing number of sequencing technologies, it is vital to
255
      evaluate whether single-cell retrieval methods could retrieve cells from different platforms with high
      accuracy. Therefore, we adopted two scRNA-seq datasets including multiple platforms, the PBMC
256
      dataset spanning 9 different sequencing platforms (Ding et al., 2020) and the human pancreas dataset
257
      spanning 4 different sequencing platforms (Luecken et al., 2022).
258
259
      Multi-species evaluation Single-cell sequencing has been carried out extensively in different
260   species. Analysis of single-cell datasets from diverse organisms is vital for understanding evolution-
261   ary processes of conservation and diversification of cell types. Therefore, we evaluated the cross-
262   species retrieval capabilities of single-cell retrieval methods using two large scale human (Consor-
263   tium* et al., 2022) and mouse (Consortium, 2020) single-cell atlas spanning more than 10 tissues.
264
265   Multi-omics evaluation Single-cell multi-omics profiling allows for measurements of transcrip-
266   tome (scRNA-seq) and chromatin accessibility (scATAC-seq) for the same cells, i.e. {xi }N    i=1
267   (scRNA-seq) and {yi }Ni=1 (scATAC-seq) datasets where xj and yj measures the same cell j. We
268   collected three widely used single-cell multiomics profiling datasets, namely 10X PBMC (pbm,
269   2020), Chen-2019 (Chen et al., 2019) and Ma-2020 (Ma et al., 2020). These datasets contain 9631,
      9190 and 32231 cells respectively.

291   3.3.3    DATASET P RE - PROCESSING
292
293   General preprocessing Different single-cell retrieval methods exhibit different pre-processing
294   steps. For example, scFoundation does not select highly variable genes while scGPT selects the
295   top 4000 highly variable genes. To avoid bias, we adopt the default pre-processing method of each
296
      method respectively.
297   Cross-species mapping To perform cross-species retrieval, gene alignment is crucial as human
298   and mouse genes are different. Mouse and human have well annotated one-to-one gene homolog
299   mapping as they have close evolutionary distance and have been well studied. We download human
300   and mouse gene homology from existing database2 and align the mouse and human scRNA-seq
301   datasets.
302   Cross-omic mapping As scFMs are pre-trained on scRNA-seq datasets, directly applying them to
303   other omics such as scATAC-seq is not trivial. For each dataset, We retained 80000 highly variable
304   peaks for scATAC-seq and 8000 highly variable genes for scRNA-seq. We followed the standard
305   implementation from DeepMAPS (Ma et al., 2023a) to align scATAC-seq peaks to the same gene
306   space as scRNA-seq based on gene regulatory potential.

      Cell type annotations only provide coarse grained information as cells in the same cell type may
      belong to different regions or different stages. Meanwhile, cell type annotations can be highly in-
412
      consistent across different studies and can even sometimes be wrong. Motivated by this, besides
413
      computing the agreement between cell type annotations and retrieval results, comparing the con-
414   sistency of the retrieval cells between different methods may serve as another signal of correctness
415   for cell retrieval. As mentioned in Section 3.2, we proposed the label-free evaluation of single-cell
416   retrieval methods, considering both the consistency between the retrieved cells and the consistency
417   between the DE patterns from the retrieved cells.
418
      First, we evaluated the overlap between the retrieved cells between different single-cell retrieval
419
      methods. As shown in Fig. 2a, we found that Cellfishing.jl, scFoundation and scGPT have high
420   overlap on the retrieved cells. Meanwhile, UCE and SCimilarity also have high overlap with these
421   methods. In Fig. 2b, we visualized the AvgOverlap score of single-cell retrieval methods and
422   found that scFoundation, CellFishing.jl and scGPT rank the highest among all methods. It is vi-
423   tal to validate that whether the AvgOverlap score correlates with the Vote-Acc metric so that
424   AvgOverlap can be a signal of correctness of cell retrieval when no high-quality cell type anno-
425   tations are available. As shown in Fig. 2c, the label-free metric AvgOverlap and label-dependent
426   metric Vote-Acc are strongly correlated across four benchmarking datasets. Therefore, we could
427   use the label-free metric AvgOverlap to evaluate single-cell retrieval methods even when the cell
428
      type annotations on the target dataset are not available.

430   trieved cells of different methods. Simply relying on the overlap between cell indexes can be biased

521   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
522
523
524   In this paper, we comprehensively benchmarked and evaluated 12 existing single-cell retrieval meth-
525
      ods. We proposed two types of evaluation metrics: label-free evaluation and label-dependent eval-
      uation to assess the capabilities of single-cell retrieval methods. The key recommendations are
526
      summarized as follows: scFMs show promising performance in retrieving similar cell states given
527
      query cells, but are not reliable when the target species and omics are distant from the pre-training
528   dataset; Traditional non-machine learning cell retrieval methods still yield promising results in ma-
529   jor settings, which should be used as strong baselines in further method development; In cases where
530   the target species and omics are distant from the scFM pre-training datasets, VAE-based methods
531   such as scVI and LDVAE are good alternatives; Label-free metrics yield consistent results as label-
532   dependent results, thus can be adapted especially when the cell type labels are inconsistent across
533   studies. We envision the development of large-scale scFMs pre-trained on more species, tissues,
534   omics that enables the development of a general cell ”search engine”.
535   Limitations: Quantitatively benchmarking of single-cell retrieval methods still heavily relies on the
536   cell type annotations. However, these annotations may be inconsistent across studies or incorrect,
537   therefore causing bias in the evaluation outcome. In this paper, we aim to alleviate this issue by
538   proposing a label-free evaluation methodology. We believe that more label-free validation methods
539   of cell retrieval performance are key to evaluating the single-cell retrieval methods at scale across
      more diverse datasets.
