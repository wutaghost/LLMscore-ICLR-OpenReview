009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Misinformation remains a critical issue in today’s information landscape, sig-
012            nificantly impacting public perception and behavior. Among its various forms,
013            out-of-context (OOC) misinformation is particularly pervasive, misrepresenting
014            information by repurposing authentic images with false text. Traditional OOC
015            detection methods often rely on coarse-grained similarity measures between image-
016
               text pairs, which fall short of providing interpretability and nuanced understanding.
               Conversely, whereas multimodal large language models (MLLMs) exhibit vast
017
               knowledge and an inherent ability for visual reasoning and explanation generation,
018
               they remain deficient in the complexity required to understand and discern nuanced
019            cross-modal distinctions thoroughly. To address these challenges, we propose
020            MACAW, a retrieval-based approach that indexes external knowledge, focusing
021            on multiple granularities by extracting and cataloging relevant events and entities.
022            Our framework first extracts multi-granularity information to assess the contextual
023            integrity of news items, followed by a multi-agent reasoning process for accurate
024            detection. Extensive experiments demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of
025            our proposed framework in identifying out-of-context fake news, outperforming
026            the state-of-the-art solutions by 4.3%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The exponential growth of social media platforms has dramatically increased the accessibility, cost-
031
      efficiency, and speed of news dissemination through multimodal channels (Akhtar et al., 2023).
032   However, this has also led to an increase in the spread of misleading or fabricated information. One
033   particularly effective method of misinformation is out-of-context (OOC) news (Qi et al., 2024;
034   Papadopoulos et al., 2024), which manipulates authentic images by presenting them in incorrect or
035   misleading contexts. For example, during the recent U.S. presidential election, malicious actors could
036   have misled voters by swapping current authentic election images with unrelated textual context,
037   thereby creating a false narrative. To address these challenges, two methods have been widely
038   adopted: traditional similarity-based methods and more recent efforts utilizing MLLMs.
039
040   OOC Detection with News Content (Image-Text) Comparison. Traditional research (Zhou et al.,
041
      2020; Abdelnabi et al., 2022; Jaiswal et al., 2017; 2019) on OOC primarily relies on computing
      similarity scores between image-text pairs or learning unified latent representation spaces. As shown
042
      in Figure 1(a), these methods may yield high similarity scores, falsely suggesting a match between
043
      the image and text, while failing to detect nuanced discrepancies. The absence of interpretability
044   further limits their utility in tasks requiring detailed verification or reasoning.
045
046   OOC Detection with MLLMs. Recent efforts (Mu et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; Liu
047   et al.; Wang et al., 2024) have shifted towards using Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
048   for OOC misinformation detection, aiming to generate predictions with explanations. Despite
049   advancements, even state-of-the-art (SOTA) models like GPT-4o exhibit significant limitations when
050   facing ambiguous or up-to-date information. As shown in Figure 1(b), GPT-4o, when provided with
051   insufficient context in the image, cannot confirm whether an out-of-context mismatch exists. This
052   reveals a fundamental issue: constrained by the explicit data they receive (Hu et al., 2022), MLLMs
053   lack the deep reasoning necessary for cross-referencing temporal, contextual, or entity details, leading
      them to default to non-committal conclusions when faced with subtle inconsistencies. Consequently,

      Rethinking OOC Detection: How Human Specialists Handle It? As mentioned before, existing
079
      methods fail to capture the full complexity of OOC detection, particularly the need for deep, multi-
080   granular reasoning and interpretable, fine-grained explanations. In practice, fact-checking
081   experts follow a systematic verification process: they retrieve information from multiple sources and
082   modalities, validate details, and reason about timelines, contexts, and inconsistencies (Holan, 2018;
083   Center, 2020). While multiple experts may independently analyze content before reaching consensus,
084   each expert performs this complete sequence of analytical steps.
085
086   Our Proposal: A Multi-Agent Approach with Multi-Grained Retrieval. Drawing inspiration
087   from the systematic verification steps that experts perform, we propose MACAW (Multi-Agent
088   Cross-Modal Misinformation Analysis Workflow), a novel multi-agent system for OOC detection
089
      and explanation. As shown in Figure 1(c), our system is supported by a self-constructed, multi-
      granularity database, which integrates information from various levels to offer a robust foundation for
090
      detecting and explaining OOC misinformation. MACAW decomposes the sophisticated verification
091
      process into optimized subtasks, implementing three specialized agents: the Retrieval Agent for
092   gathering relevant information, the Detective Agent for examining potential inconsistencies, and the
093   Analyst Agent for synthesizing findings and generating final conclusions. Through this structured
094   division of responsibilities, our architecture ensures comprehensive verification while maintaining
095   both efficiency and interpretability in identifying subtle contextual discrepancies.
096
097   Contributions. Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
098
099                       • We construct a self-constructed multi-granularity database for OOC detection, which
100                         encapsulates both entity and event-level information of existing news and knowledge.
101                       • We propose a multi-agent OOC detection framework MACAW, which cross-validates multi-
102                         granularity information with input news. It can not only perform sophisticated reasoning
103                         and OOC detection, but also give interpretation that the news is OOC based on which
104                         information source.
105                       • Extensive experiments validate the robustness and effectiveness of our framework across
106                         various types of OOC misinformation. Our framework achieves a 4.3% improvement
107                         in accuracy compared to the SOTA methods, demonstrating its superior performance in
                            detecting OOC misinformation.

111   2.1   K NOWLEDGE - ENHANCED M ISINFORMATION D ETECTION
112
      Early misinformation detection research focused on semantic feature extraction from news content, but
113
      as fake and real news became semantically indistinguishable, researchers shifted towards leveraging
114
      external knowledge (Zhou & Zafarani, 2020). This transition led to various knowledge-enhanced
115   approaches, such as CompareNet (Hu et al., 2021), which constructs directed heterogeneous document
116   graphs to compare news content with knowledge bases through entity extraction. Building on this
117   foundation, recent work has emphasized knowledge retrieval for more precise fact-checking. Notable
118   advances include a retrieval-augmented generation framework for evidence-grounded outputs (Yue
119   et al., 2024), a unified inference framework integrating multiple evidence sources (Wu et al., 2024),
120   and document-level claim extraction methods (Deng et al., 2024). While these approaches have
121   demonstrated the value of external knowledge in improving detection accuracy (Dun et al., 2021; Hu
122   et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021), they have yet to fully address the utilization and interaction between
123
      information at different granularities.
124
125   2.2   V ISION -L ANGUAGE -M ODELS A SSISTED M ISINFORMATION D ETECTION
126
127   While traditional approaches to misinformation detection have predominantly focused on unimodal
128   data, recent advances in vision-language models have significantly enhanced the ability to detect
129   multimodal inconsistencies. For instance, Abdelnabi et al. (2022) extended the NewsCLIPpings
130   dataset (Luo et al., 2021) by incorporating external evidence and introduced the Consistency Checking
131   Network (CCN), which evaluates both image-to-image and text-to-text consistency. Similarly, the
132
      Stance Extraction Network (SEN) (Yuan et al., 2023) builds on the same encoders but improves
      performance by clustering external evidence semantically to infer its stance towards the claim. SEN
133
      also enhances consistency detection by capturing the co-occurrence of named entities across textual
134
      and external evidence.
135
136   Further advancing the field, the Explainable and Context-Enhanced Network (ECENet) combines
137   coarse- and fine-grained attention mechanisms to model multimodal feature interactions (Zhang et al.,
138
      2024). ECENet utilizes different encoders to jointly process textual and visual entities, offering more
      nuanced detection of inconsistencies. In addition, SNIFFER (Qi et al., 2024) addresses both “internal
139
      consistency” in image-text pairs and “external consistency” with external evidence. A parallel line
140
      of research has focused on developing interpretable multimodal architectures for misinformation
141   detection. These approaches emphasize transparent decision-making processes while maintaining
142   high detection accuracy. Notable works include Liu et al. (2023b); Ma et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
143   (2023b)

146   3     M ETHODOLOGY
147
148   We propose MACAW (Multi-Agent Cross-Modal Misinformation Analysis Workflow), a multi-
149   grained framework for OOC detection, integrating both fine-grained entity-level and coarse-grained
150   event-level information. As shown in Figure 2, our approach consists of three core components: 1)
151   Evidence Storage, where visual and textual entities are extracted and aligned using a lightweight
152   MLLM, alongside the storage of event-level information extracted from news captions; 2) Evidence
153   Retrieval, which retrieves both entity-level and event-level data through a unified encoding mecha-
154   nism; and 3) Multi-Agent Detection, leveraging specialized agents to analyze the consistency of the
155
      retrieved evidence and generate explainable OOC detection results.
156

160   well as event-level information from news items for efficient retrieval using similarity search of
161   Faiss (Douze et al., 2024). Both visual and textual inputs are processed through specialized models,
      and only aligned entities are stored for rapid querying.

185   3.1.1             M ULTIMODAL E NTITY E XTRACTION
186
      Given a news item N = (I, T ), where I represents the news image and T is the news caption, the
187   system first extracts visual and textual entities. A multimodal entity is defined as a pair consisting of
188   a visual entity and its corresponding textual entity, where both refer to the same real-world object
189   or concept. Specifically, a multimodal entity is represented as (vi , ti ), where vi is a visual entity
190   extracted from I, and ti is a textual entity extracted from T . Visual entity v is extracted from I using
191   the YOLO v8 Instance segmentation model MYOLO (Jocher et al., 2023), producing a set of detected
192   visual entities V . Textual entity t is extracted from T using the spaCy NER model MNER (Honnibal
193   & Montani, 2017), resulting in a set of textual entities T . Thus, the sets of textual entities and textual
194   entities make up the entity set E = {(v1 , t1 ), ...., (vk , tk )}, where k presents the number of entities.
195
196   3.1.2             M ULTI - MODAL A LIGNMENT
197
198   Before encoding, the system performs multimodal alignment using a lightweight MLLM. Considering
199
      factors such as computational cost, accuracy, and cross-modal understanding capabilities, we selected
      GPT-4o mini as the model. This model strikes a balance between efficiency and performance, offering
200
      robust cross-modal alignment while maintaining low cost. The alignment model Malign gives the
201
      similarity between extracted visual entity vi and textual entity ti , establishing potential mappings
202   between them:
203
204
205                                                                                           S(vi , ti ) = Malign (vi , ti )
206
207   A mapping between a visual entity vi and a textual entity ti is considered valid if the similarity score
208   S(vi , ti ) exceeds a predefined threshold τ . Only entities with valid mappings are retained for further
209   encoding and storage. Entities without sufficient cross-modal similarity are discarded:
210
211
212                                                                          Ei = (vi , ti ) ∈ E                    if        S(vi , ti ) ≥ τ
213
214   This alignment ensures that only meaningful and relevant visual-textual entity pairs are processed
215   further, reducing storage overhead and improving retrieval precision. The mapping information,
      along with the aligned entities, is saved for future retrieval and analysis.

      3.1.3    E NCODING AND S TORAGE
217
218   After establishing a valid visual-textual entity Ej = (vj , tj ), the system proceeds to encode these
219   aligned entities. The visual entities are encoded into high-dimensional feature vectors using the
220   Swin Transformer model Mswin (Liu et al., 2021b), while both the textual entities and the event-level
221   information are encoded using the RoBERTa model MRoBERTa (Liu, 2019):
222                     ZV = Mswin (vj ),     ZT = MRoBERTa (tj ) Zevent = MRoBERTa (T )
223
      The encoded representations of the aligned visual entities ZV , textual entities ZN , and event-level
224   information Zevent are stored in separate Faiss indices, referred to as IndexV , IndexT , Indexevent ,
225   to enable efficient retrieval.
226
227   3.2     E VIDENCE R ETRIEVAL , AGGREGATION , AND V ERIFICATION
228
229   The Evidence Retrieval module is responsible for retrieving relevant entities, and event-level informa-
230   tion from the pre-constructed Faiss index files. This module ensures efficient multimodal retrieval to
231   support the OOC detection process. The retrieval process consists of two main components: data
232   encoding and retrieval, followed by evidence aggregation and verification.
233
234
      3.2.1    E VIDENCE R ETRIEVAL
235   Given an input news item Ninput = (Iinput , Tinput ), where Iinput represents the image and Tinput is
236   the accompanying caption, the system first performs entity extraction and encoding following the
237   methods described in Sections 3.1.1 and 3.1.2. Specifically, this process results in the generation of
238   encoded query vectors: vquery for the visual component, tquery for the textual component, and equery
239   for the event-level information.
240   Subsequently, the system retrieves the most relevant entities from the respective Meta Faiss indices
241   by calculating the Euclidean distance between the encoded query vectors and the indexed entities.
242   For each modality, the top two nearest entities (in terms of Euclidean distance) are retrieved. This
243   process, referred to as top-k retrieval, is implemented as follows:
244                                  Vr = top-k(vquery , IndexV , k = 2)
245
                                      Tr = top-k(tquery , IndexT , k = 2)
246
247
                                      Er = top-k(Tinput , Indexevent , k = 2)
248   Here, top-k refers to the process of retrieving the top k entities from the corresponding index Index,
249   ranked by their similarity to the query vector. In this case, we set k = 2 to retrieve the two most
250   relevant entities. The choice of k = 2 is motivated by the need to provide diverse yet concise entity
251   representations for downstream tasks.
252
253
      3.2.2    E VIDENCE AGGREGATION AND V ERIFICATION
254   After retrieving the relevant visual entities Vr , textual entities Tr , and event information Er , these are
255   combined by the Evidence Aggregator into a unified evidence set:
256
257
                                                 Eagg = {Vr , Tr , Er }
258
259   The aggregated evidence Eagg is then passed to the Evidence Verifier, which assesses its consistency
260   and relevance for the OOC detection task. The verifier ensures that there are no duplicates in the
261   retrieved evidence and that the evidence is correctly formatted. After the verification process, Eagg
262   is cleaned and validated, ensuring it contains only unique and properly formatted items, ready for
263   further processing.
264   In summary, this process efficiently encodes and retrieves multimodal information through Faiss
265   indices, enabling fine-grained entity-level retrieval and broader event-level context for OOC detection.

296   3.3.1     R ETRIEVAL AGENT
297
298   The Retrieval Agent initiates the CoT-inspired process by cross-referencing input news Ninput with
299   retrieved evidence Ea . It performs the first consistency check, ensuring alignment between visual and
300   textual entities at both the entity and event levels. Leveraging MLLM’s pre-trained knowledge, the
301
      agent identifies significant misalignments, passing flagged inconsistencies as input to the next agent
      for deeper analysis.
302
303
      3.3.2     D ETECTIVE AGENT
304
305   Building on the Retrieval Agent’s flags, the Detective Agent conducts a more detailed investigation.
306   It systematically evaluates key elements—time, place, person, event, and object—to detect contradic-
307   tions between the retrieved evidence and the input news. For example, it checks if clothing matches
308   the season described or if objects align with the event. This agent’s refined analysis, aligned with
309   CoT reasoning, narrows the scope of potential inconsistencies. The resulting findings are passed to
310   the final agent.
311

      their findings into a coherent OOC detection report. Acting as an expert reviewer, it provides a

      4.1.1    DATASETS
333
334   We leverage the NewsCLIPpings benchmark (Luo et al., 2021), the largest dataset for detecting
335   out-of-context misinformation. This dataset is sourced from the VisualNews dataset (Liu et al.,
336   2021a), which was initially created for news image captioning. NewsCLIPpings contains news
337   articles from four major outlets: The Guardian, BBC, Washington Post, and USA Today. The dataset
338   is evenly balanced with respect to labels.
339
      Following prior work (Qi et al., 2024), we report results on the Merged/Balance subset, which
340   ensures an equal distribution of retrieval strategies and positive/negative samples. Specifically, the
341   retrieval strategies are categorized into four types: Text-Image, Text-Text, Person Matching, and
342   Scene Matching. This subset includes 71,072 samples for training, 7,024 for validation, and 7,264
343   for testing. Consistent with (Luo et al., 2021), we evaluate performance using accuracy across all
344   samples (All) and separately for the Falsified (Out-of-Context) and Pristine (Not Out-of-Context)
345   samples as evaluation metrics.
346
347
      4.1.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
348
349   MACAW relies on a proprietary multi-granularity database, constructed specifically from the training
350   subset of the NewsCLIPpings dataset. This database is built offline and comprises 18,305 unique
351   entities and 71,072 event instances, ensuring comprehensive coverage of the training data. By
352   pre-computing and indexing this data, we enable more efficient retrieval during inference.
353   To optimize retrieval efficiency, we employ a Faiss index, enabling rapid and scalable access to
354   the multi-granularity data during the reasoning process. Each agent in the multi-agent system is
355   instantiated using a GPT-4o-Latest architecture, with prompt engineering tailored to the specific task
356   of each agent. This allows us to dynamically generate specialized outputs for entity recognition, event
357   verification, and cross-modal consistency checking.
358
359
      4.1.3    BASELINES
360
361   To thoroughly evaluate MACAW’s performance, we compare it to a broad range of SOTA multimodal
362   models. EANN (Wang et al., 2018) uses adversarial training to learn event-invariant features, making
363   it robust across various detection scenarios. VisualBERT (Li et al., 2019) processes image-text pairs
364   through a unified transformer, optimizing key tasks such as image-text alignment. SAFE (Zhou et al.,
365   2020) enhances prediction accuracy by transforming images into descriptive sentences and applying
366   sentence similarity as an auxiliary loss. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) employs separate encoders for
367   images and text, aligned through contrastive learning to ensure semantically related pairs are closely
368   represented. CCN (Abdelnabi et al., 2022) builds on CLIP by incorporating cross-modal consistency
369
      checks and external evidence retrieval for improved decision-making. DT-Transformer (Papadopou-
      los et al., 2023) further extends CLIP by introducing additional transformer layers to refine multimodal
370
      interactions, capturing more complex relationships. Neu-Sym Detector (Zhang et al., 2023a) com-
371
      bines neural-symbolic reasoning by decomposing text into fact queries and aggregating outputs
372   through a pre-trained multimodal model. To demonstrate that MACAW’s performance is not solely
373   attributed to the underlying GPT-4o capabilities, we include GPT-4o-Latest in both zero-shot and
374   few-shot settings as strong baselines. These variants represent the direct application of GPT-4o’s
375   multimodal capabilities without the specialized framework components present in MACAW. Finally,
376   SNIFFER (Qi et al., 2024) selects the InstructBLIP as the base MLLM and enhances OOC detection
377   with a two-stage instruction tuning process based on , integrating GPT-4-generated OOC-specific
      data and external evidence retrieval to improve consistency checks and overall explainability.

466

467

470   is then mismatched with the original text. Person Matching focuses on cases where the correct
471   individual is depicted, but the person is placed in a misleading or unrelated context. Finally, Scene
472   Matching mislabels the broader setting or event, ensuring the environment looks similar but describes
473   a different situation, excluding any references to individuals. For our evaluation, we maintained an
474   equal distribution of 1,000 samples, with 250 examples from each category, to ensure a balanced and
475   comprehensive assessment of model performance across these different misinformation scenarios.

510   and the average ranking across all samples was calculated for both logic and explanation.
511

513
      metrics, achieving the lowest average rankings in both human and GPT-4o assessments. Specifi-

514

515
      respectively. These consistent results across different evaluation methods underscore the framework’s
516   robust capability in generating logically sound and clear explanations.

519
      few-shot LLaVA-1.5, suggesting the inherent strength of GPT-4o in handling OOC news detection.

520

      In this section, we provide a detailed comparison between our proposed MACAW framework and the
906   SNIFFER model Qi et al. (2024), a prominent approach in the field of OOC misinformation detection.
907   Both models leverage the power of MLLMs to tackle the challenges of OOC misinformation, yet they
908   differ significantly in methodology, performance, explainability, and adaptability to various datasets,
909   leading to distinct advantages and limitations.
910
      From a methodological perspective, SNIFFER employs a two-stage instruction tuning approach,
911
      adapted from InstructBLIP, to refine its ability to align generic objects with news-domain entities and
912   subsequently fine-tune its discriminatory powers for OOC misinformation detection. This process
913   involves the integration of external knowledge through retrieval mechanisms, enabling SNIFFER to
914   perform both internal checks (image-text consistency) and external checks (claim-evidence relevance),
915   with the final decision produced through composed reasoning. While this is an effective approach, it
916   introduces a reliance on external retrieval systems, which can introduce noise and latency in real-time
917   applications. In contrast, MACAW adopts a multi-agent architecture that decomposes the complex
      reasoning task into specialized subtasks, handled by agents responsible for retrieval, detection, and

      analysis. This modular structure not only enhances the interpretability of the system but also allows
919
      for more fine-grained verification through multi-granularity retrieval of both entity- and event-level
920   information. By structuring its framework around a self-constructed multi-granularity database,
921   MACAW reduces dependency on external sources, offering a more efficient and unified approach to
922   misinformation detection.
923
      In terms of performance, both models demonstrate state-of-the-art capabilities, but MACAW consis-
924
      tently outperforms SNIFFER across several benchmarks. SNIFFER reports an accuracy of 88.4% on
925
      the NewsCLIPpings dataset, leveraging its external retrieval mechanisms to detect inconsistencies
926   in OOC samples. However, MACAW achieves an accuracy of 92.7%, a significant improvement
927   attributed to its multi-agent collaboration and multi-granularity retrieval system. This structured
928   approach allows MACAW to handle more subtle and complex OOC cases by cross-validating in-
929   formation across different granularities, thus providing a more robust detection mechanism. While
930   SNIFFER’s retrieval-based methodology strengthens its performance, particularly in cases where
931   external evidence is readily available, MACAW’s internal verification process ensures that it remains
932   highly effective even in scenarios where such evidence may be limited or noisy.
933   Explainability is another critical dimension where the two models diverge. SNIFFER integrates its
934   internal and external verification results to generate explanations, often relying on external evidence
935   to justify its decisions. By incorporating web-based evidence, SNIFFER can provide detailed explana-

951   across various contexts. By cross-referencing data at both the entity and event levels, MACAW
952   ensures that it can consistently maintain high performance across diverse datasets without being overly
953   dependent on the availability of external evidence. This adaptability makes MACAW particularly
954   well-suited for real-world applications where external sources may not always provide reliable or
955   timely information.
956   Finally, with respect to efficiency, MACAW’s multi-agent system provides a significant advantage.
957   SNIFFER’s reliance on external tools and web-based retrieval can introduce latency, particularly in
958   real-time or large-scale applications where the availability and quality of external data are critical.
959   In contrast, MACAW’s internal multi-agent collaboration and self-constructed database allow it
960   to operate more efficiently. The modular design of MACAW’s agents ensures that each step of
961   the verification process is optimized for speed and accuracy, making it more suitable for real-time
962   OOC misinformation detection. By reducing dependency on external retrieval, MACAW minimizes
