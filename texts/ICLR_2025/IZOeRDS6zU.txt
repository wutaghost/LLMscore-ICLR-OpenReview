009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we reconstruct viewed images from EEG recordings with state-of-
012            the-art quantitative reconstruction performance using a linear decoder that maps
013            the EEG to image latents. We choose latent diffusion guided by CLIP embedding
014            as the primary method of image reconstruction as it is currently the most effective
015            at capturing visual semantics. We also explore reconstruction results from a latent
016
               space of PCA and ICA components, which capture luminance- and hue-related
               information from the EEG. The linear model provides interpretable EEG features
017
               relevant for differentiating general semantic categories of the images. We create
018
               spatiotemporal semantic maps that reflect the temporal evolution of class-relevant
019            semantic information over time.1

      neuroimaging modalities including functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), magnetoen-
055   cephalography (MEG), and electroencephelography (EEG) have been used for studying the brain’s
056   response to visual scenes, each with its own trade-off of spatial and temporal resolution.
057
058
      fMRI has millimeter-level spatial resolution, and the voxel activations extend into the brain with
      precise localization. At such meso-scale recording, fMRI is able to inform theories of how infor-
059
      mation is spatially organized in the brain. Basic scientific studies about visual (Kay et al., 2008)
060
      and language representations (Mitchell et al., 2008) in the brain with fMRI laid the groundwork
061   for impressive visual reconstructions seen recently (Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023). There is even evi-
062   dence suggesting that the brain representations generalize robustly from visual perception to visual
063   imagery with fMRI (Kneeland et al., 2024). However, while spatially rich, fMRI measures Blood-
064   Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signals, which have a slow effective time resolution of a few
065   seconds preventing visualization of dynamic responses.
066
      EEG on the other hand, has spatial resolution limited by volume conduction which has resulted in
067   its underuse in visual representation and reconstruction. Despite this, EEG responses to low-level
068   visual features have been observed, including visual field (Halliday & Michael, 1970; Jeffreys &
069   Axford, 1972), color (Paulus et al., 1988) and contrast (Schechter et al., 2005). In addition, specific
070   event-related potentials (ERPs) such as the N170 are sensitive to semantic visual categories such
071   as faces (Taylor, 2002). Importantly also, EEG has much better (millisecond) temporal resolution,
072   allowing for tracking the temporal dynamics of brain representations.
073   Taken together, the high temporal resolution and promising prior work on visual feature mapping,
074   along with its low cost and portability, make EEG an appealing neuroimaging modality for studying
075   the dynamics of visual reconstruction.

      2.1      DATASET
080
081   We used the preprocessed version of THINGS-EEG2 dataset2 from Gifford et al. (2022), which has
082   17 posterior EEG channels compared to the 63 total channels in the raw dataset. The EEG was
083   initially sampled at 1000Hz and down-sampled to 100Hz during the preprocessing. The only major
084   filtering method applied during the preprocessing is Multi-Variate Noise Normalization (MVNN),
085   which computes the covariance matrices of the EEG data (calculated for each time-point), and then
086   averages them across image conditions and data partitions. The inverse of the resulting averaged
087   covariance matrix is used to whiten the EEG data (independently for each session) (Gifford et al.,
088
      2022).
089   In the experiment, each image is presented for 100ms followed by a blank screen for 100ms before
090   the next image. The image presentation order is pseudo-randomized across the entire image set. All
091   10 subjects view the same 16740 images, of which the same 200 images are test images.
092   Trials are extracted from −0.2s to 0.8s relative to the onset of the stimulus. Each training image is
093   shown 4 times, and each test image is shown 80 times. We averaged all trials for the same image
094   (within subject) to form the final dataset. At 100Hz sampling rate, −0.2s to 0.8 seconds corresponds
095   to 100 samples. We discarded the first 20 samples which correspond to −0.2s to 0s, leaving 80
096   samples times 17 channels or 1360 dimensions per image per subject. The final dimensions of the
097   training data for each subject are (16540 images, 1360 features) for the training set, and (200 images,
098   1360 features) for the test set. Responses to random subsequent images will affect each trial because
099   images are presented every 200ms, but their effects should be reduced during the averaging step.

131   The reconstruction pipeline consists of three stages: training, testing, and evaluation (refer to Fig.
132   2). In the training stage, images are processed through a VDVAE encoder, generating VDVAE em-
133   beddings (91168 dimensions), and through a CLIP-Vision encoder, producing CLIP-Vision embed-
134
      dings (257 tokens × 768 dimensions). Corresponding captions are encoded by a CLIP-Text encoder
      to form CLIP-Text embeddings (77 tokens × 768 dimensions). Because CLIP-Vision and CLIP-Text
135
      embeddings have multiple tokens, separate regressors are trained to project the EEG data to each of
136
      the corresponding token space. Dashed red arrows indicate the fitting of these regressors from EEG
137   to the embeddings, and only one arrow is shown for clarity since all regressors use the same EEG
138   data. Before fitting, the EEG data is whitened to normalize each of the 1360 EEG dimensions across
139   the 16540 training classes (refer to Appendix Section B for the mathematical definitions).
140
      In the test stage, the predicted embeddings are shifted and rescaled to align their distributions with
141
      the training embeddings (refer to Appendix Section B for the mathematical definitions). Predicted
142
      VDVAE embeddings are fed into a VDVAE decoder to generate stage-1 reconstructions, capturing
143   basic visual features such as shape and color. While the stage-1 VDVAE reconstructions primarily
144   represent low-level visual features, the rich semantic details are preserved and guided by the pre-
145   dicted CLIP-Vision and CLIP-Text embeddings. These reconstructions serve as inputs to the Versa-
146   tile Diffusion model, which incorporates guidance from the predicted CLIP-Vision and CLIP-Text
147   embeddings to produce the final reconstructions.
148
      In the evaluation stage, the final reconstructions are compared with the ground-truth images to
149   assess performance.

151   For the THINGS-EEG2 dataset, the images come with their corresponding category names rather
152
      than a full-sentence description, so those category names were used to encode the training CLIP-
      Text embeddings. For VDVAE and CLIP-Vision, the original stimulus images are used to encode

      the training embeddings.

172   2.3   P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION M ETRICS
173
174   We used the same performance metrics (see Fig. 3) as in Ozcelik & VanRullen (2023), which has
175   been used in other followup studies such as MindEye Scotti et al. (2023). The 8 metrics we used
176   are Pixel Correlation (PixCorr), Structural Similarity (SSIM), AlexNet layer 2 and 5 outputs pair-
177   wise correlations, InceptionNet output pairwise correlation, CLIP ViT output pairwise correlation,
178   EfficientNet output distance, and SwAV output distance.
179
      PixCorr and SSIM involve comparing the reconstructed image with the ground-truth (GT) test im-
180   age. PixCorr is a low-level (pixel) measure that involves vectorizing the reconstructed and GT
181   images and computing the correlation coefficient between the resulting vectors. SSIM is a measure
182   developed by Wang et al. 2004 that computes a match value between GT and reconstructed images
183   as a function of overall luminance match, overall contrast match, and a “structural” match which
184   is defined by normalizing each image by its mean and standard deviation. We should note that this
185   measure was designed for comparing images with minor distortions and does not seem as reliable
186   for images that are not close matches as currently obtained with image reconstruction methods. One
187   way to see this is to observe (in Fig. 3b) that the SSIM measure does not seem much affected by
188
      the duration of EEG window over 50ms, unlike the other measures that show performance improve-
      ments from 100ms through 400ms.
189
190   Fig. 3a is computed by reconstructing with Versatile Diffusion using 7 different random seeds. For
191   each subject, the final performance is the average across the 7 runs. The standard deviation across
192   the 7 runs for each subject is represented by the error bars.
193   Fig. 3b is computed by models trained on each subject’s 4-trial-averaged training data, and tested
194   on their corresponding 80-trial-averaged test data. For Fig. 3b, the “first 200ms”, “first 400ms”,
195   “first 600ms” and “first 800ms” models use those corresponding time ranges after the onset of the
196   stimulus. The 0ms performance, which should correspond to chance level, is computed by passing
197   the 200ms before the onset of the stimulus onto the trained “first 200ms” model. The bars heights
198   and the error bars represent the mean and standard deviation across the 4 subjects.
199   Fig. 3c is computed by gradually increasing the the number of training images and the number of
200   averages in the test samples. In Fig. 3c, the y-axis shows gradual increase of the number of training
201   images, and the x-axis shows gradual increase of the number of trial averages for each of the test
202   images. Performance varies smoothly as a function of both training images and test trials.
203
      The Versatile Diffusion Pipeline maps each trial onto 3 latent embeddings used for the reconstruc-
204   tion: VDVAE, CLIP-Vision, and CLIP-Text. To assess the relative contribution of the 3 kinds of
205   latent embeddings toward the model performance we tested 6 kinds of ablated models (Fig. 3d):
206   “VDVAE only” used the low-resolution reconstruction outputs of the VDVAE; “CLIP-Text only”
207   uses only the CLIP-Text embedding; “no CLIP-Vision” uses VDVAE and “CLIP-Text”; “CLIP-
208   Vision only” uses only the CLIP-Vision embedding; “no CLIP-Text” uses VDVAE and CLIP-Vision;
209   “no AutoKL (VDVAE)” uses CLIP-Vision and CLIP-Text.
210   The ablation of the VDVAE is done by feeding a blank gray image (127, 127, 127 in RGB) instead
211   of the results from VDVAE as the input image into Versatile Diffusion, and increasing the diffusion
212   strength from 0.75 to 0.99. The ablation of the CLIP-Vision is done by encoding a blank black
213   image as CLIP-Vision instead of using the predicted CLIP-Vision from EEG data and setting the
214   mixing ratio from 0.4 to 0.99. (a blank black image is defined as the unconditioned image for this
215   part). The ablation of the CLIP-Text is done by encoding an empty string as CLIP-Text instead of
      using the predicted CLIP-Text from EEG data and setting the mixing ratio from 0.4 to 0.

403   4.3   S PATIOTEMPORAL S EMANTIC M AP
404
405
406   While the reconstructions reflect semantic alignment, we would like to delve further into the relevant
407   EEG features underlying the semantic classes. We did show that by mirroring the electrodes at
408   test time the semantic alignment degrades significantly indicating that, for individuals, the spatial
409   features underlying semantic category tend to be spatially asymmetric across the mid-line. In order
410   to see how different electrodes signal different general semantic categories over time, we created
411
      a visual semantic map of viewing images adapted to the temporal resolution of EEG. Note this is
      inspired by the fMRI semantic map by Huth et al. (2016), but, critically, it is not a static map as in
412
      the fMRI case; it is a map that unfolds in time – a spatiotemporal map (see Fig. 9).
413
414   To create the map, we use a more efficient CLIP embedding and diffusion model unCLIP (Ramesh
415   et al., 2022), which has only 1024 dimensions in the embedding space. (Reconstructions with un-
416   CLIP are similar to those with our original Versatile Diffusion Pipeline, see Fig. A.8). We trained
417
      a linear encoder model to re-encode the predicted CLIP-Vision embeddings back into EEG. The
      re-encoded EEG – here called “EEG patterns” (in analogy to common spatial patterns Blankertz
418
      et al. (2008) used in brain-computer interfaces) – accentuates features that are relevant for the visual
419
      semantics that CLIP-Vision embeddings capture (see Fig. A.9a for a subset of test images), com-
420   pared to the real EEG (see Fig. A.12a for the full pattern and compared to real EEG Fig. A.12b).
421   For better visualization, the 200 classes on the y-axis are already organized neatly into 3 general
422   semantic groups (others, animals, food) derived by hierarchical clustering on the CLIP-Vision em-
423   bedding vectors of the images. Within the “others” group (it can be further subdivided into “tools”,
424   “vehicles” and “clothing”, but here for simplicity we simply group them as “others”).
425
      We averaged the rows in “food” and “animals” and “others” to form the averaged EEG pattern for
426   these 3 general semantic groups, and plotted them on a 2-D topological scalp map (topomap) where
427   dots on the map represents the actual locations of the electrodes on the scalp (see Fig. 8a). Figure 9
428   shows the result across time for the average of these maps over all subjects for the “food”, “animals”,
429   and “others” groups. The spatiotemporal semantic map for Subject 1 is shown in Figure A.10 and
430   representations for maps of all individuals in Figure A.11. There are clear patterns across people,
431   but some individual differences in asymmetry and lateralization strength. The individual assymetries
      likely underlie the performance degradation we observed earlier from mirroring the electrodes.

      5     D ISCUSSION
487
488
      We have demonstrated the surprising power of learned linear mappings to different latent spaces.
489
      When mapped to the CLIP and VDVAE latent spaces and reconstructed with Versatile Diffusion,
490   realistic reconstructions are obtained that numerically outperform previous EEG reconstruction at-
491   tempts.
492
493   T ECHNICAL N OVELTY
494
495   We also demonstrated that even the principal components of images can be used as the latent em-
496   beddings for EEG mapping to reconstruct images; these are admittedly very blurry, but do contain
497   surprising color and shape information. While PCA and EEG have existed for decades, no attempt
498
      to reconstruct EEG with image PCA has been made. This underscores the novelty of these findings
      for EEG.
499
500
501
      C ONTRIBUTION TO C OGNITIVE S CIENCE
502   Finally we created a spatiotemporal map of the dynamic EEG representation for animal, food, and
503   other objects. There is a large amount of consistency between people, but some individual differ-
504   ences especially in relative lateralization.
505
506   5.1   L IMITATIONS
507
508   The dataset used in this work used fully randomized RSVP-style (rapid) presentation with stimuli
509   lasting 100ms and new stimuli appearing every 200ms. In all the reconstruction work in the literature
510   multiple repetitions of test data are required for high performance. (Note this is not dissimilar from
511   standard cognitive neuroscience studies which also average responses to several repetitions).
512   Even though the linear model itself is fast and computationally efficient, the diffusion models for
513   generating the images such as Versatile Diffusion and unCLIP still require dedicated desktop-level
514   GPUs to compute. Using distillation based image generation models while maintaining the CLIP
515   guidance aspect could reduce the amount of computation.
516
517   5.2   F UTURE D IRECTIONS
518
519   The EEG patterns for individual subjects here can serve as canonical patterns. Future research could
520   try modifying the experimental paradigm to see if or how the newly acquired patterns from the
521   modified experiment differ from the canonical patterns.
522   Future research may also work towards video reconstruction from EEG as AI generated video is
523   approaching a similar level of sophistication as AI generated images. Video stimuli may be more
524   representative of natural visual experiences. Motion-related features in videos may also contain de-
525   codable dynamics that would take advantage of the temporal resolution of EEG and MEG. Decoding
526   a sub-section of a video clip could provide insight into mechanisms of ongoing visual processing,
527   which, compared to evoked transient visual responses, might be more similar to internal visual rep-
528
      resentations such as visual imagery and visual dreams.
