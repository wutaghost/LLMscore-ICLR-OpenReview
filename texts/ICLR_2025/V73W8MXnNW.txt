                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               As an important component of visual scene, visual relationship has received ex-
010
               tensive attention in recent years. Most existing works directly utilize the rough vi-
011            sual appearance to represent visual relationships. Although they have been made
012            tremendous progress, the study of visual relationship may be still far from perfect.
013            This common idea may have three problems. 1) The similarity of space aggravates
014            the ambiguity of predicate representation. 2) The differences between many visual
015            relationships are subtle. 3) It lacks interpretability. To address these problems,
016            we propose a novel method - Progressive Visual Relationship Inference(PVRI)
017            - which considers both rough visual appearance and fine-grained visual cues to
018            gradually infer visual relationships. It includes the following three steps. 1)
019
               Known Cues Collection: firstly, we utilize Large Language Model(LLM) to col-
               lect the cues that may help infer visual relationships; 2) Unknown Cues Extrac-
020
               tion: secondly, we design UCE strategy to extract the cues that are not defined by
021
               the text. 3) Progressive Inference: thirdly, we utilize the obtained cues to infer vi-
022            sual relationships. We demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of our method
023            for the Visual Genome, Open Image V6 datasets.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   With the development of deep learning, people’s interest in visual scene understanding has increased
029   significantly in recent years(Ye & Xu (2024); Xin et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b)). As an im-
030   portant component of visual scene, visual relationship has also received extensive attention such as:
031   visual question answering(Qian et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024)), visual relation-
032   ship detection(Li et al. (2024b); Lu et al. (2016); Liang et al. (2018)) and scene graph generation(Li
033
      et al. (2024a); Zhao et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024)). Most of them(Krishna et al. (2017); Tang et al.
      (2019); Chen et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2019b)) directly utilize the rough visual appearance to rep-
034
      resent visual relationship. Although they have been made tremendous progress, the study of visual
035
      relationship may be still far from perfect.
036
037   This common idea may have three problems. 1) The similarity of space aggravates the ambi-
038   guity of predicate representation. For example, “playing” may be similar to “on”(boy-playing-
039
      skateboard) or “near”(boy-playing-ball). We can always find a similar geometric relationship for
      any kind of visual relationship(as shown in fig. 1.a). The rough appearance representation is diffi-
040
      cult to take into account their similarities and differences. 2) The differences between some similar
041
      visual relationships are subtle. For example, the difference between “stand on” and “walk on” is
042   in the legs. The rough visual appearance is not enough to capture the nuances of these similar visual
043   relationships. 3) It lacks interpretability. Visual relationship serves visual scene understanding,
044   and its interpretability is directly related to the user’s trust in the model. This idea is difficult to
045   explain the differences between different predicates.
046
      To address these problems, a primary challenge is how to explain the similarities and differences
047
      of visual relationships? A simple idea is to utilize Large Language Models(LLM, such as: GPT-4)
048   to explain visual relationships through some fine-grained cues(as shown in fig. 1.b). Through these
049   descriptions, we can well capture the subtle differences in similar visual relationships. For example,
050   the difference between “stand on” and “walk on” on leg state. Followed by, a natural question that
051   arises is: how to calculate the similarity of visual relationships? Some existing works(Zhang et al.
052   (2024a); Zhou et al. (2020)) utilize the semantic knowledge to construct a hierarchy of predicates to
053   reflect the similarity of predicates. Extensive experiments have proved that the semantic knowledge
      can effectively reflect the similarity between visual relationships. However, we recognize that it is

105   and fine-grained visual cues to gradually infer visual relationships. 2) We utilize LLM to provide the

107   but also does not limit the flexibility of the model. 3) We conduct experiments on four benchmark

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Visual relationship, which describes the interaction between subject and object, plays an important
111
      role in visual scene understanding. In recent years, there are many related researches on visual rela-
112
      tionship(Xu et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017b); Zellers et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2018); Li et al. (2018)),
113   which can be divided into two types: visual relationship detection and scene graph generation.
114
115   Visual relationship detection. Visual relationship detection(VRD) aims to detect objects in a given
116
      image and identify the interaction between them. The early works(Atzmon et al. (2016); Div-
      vala et al. (2014); Ramanathan et al. (2015); Sadeghi & Farhadi (2011)) regard the whole subject-
117
      predicate-object triplet as a unique category for classification. Due to the long-tailed data distribu-
118
      tion, most relationship categories suffer from the lack of sufficient training examples(Zheng et al.
119   (2019); Li et al. (2021)). To address this problem, later works are proposed to learn the subject,
120   object and predicate separately(Lu et al. (2016); Zhang et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017a); Yu et al.
121   (2017)). Most of them directly extract appearance features from bounding boxes of the subject and
122   the object of their union boxes. Although great progress has been made, the rough visual appearance
123   is not enough to capture complex visual relationships.
124
      Scene graph generation. VRD independently predicts each pair of relationships, while scene graph
125   generation(SGG) considers that there is a correlation between all objects in the scene. In recent
126   years, to enhance the discriminability of relationship representation, SGG attempts to design var-
127   ious message passing strategies(Chang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2017b); Cong et al. (2018); Zellers
128   et al. (2018)). A popular idea is to model the context based on a sequential model(Zellers et al.
129   (2018); Tang et al. (2019))(e.g.LSTM) or a fully-connected graph(Chang et al. (2021); Li et al.
130   (2017b)). They utilize context information to optimize the representation of objects and predicates,
131   and extensive research has proved the effectiveness of this idea.
132   Hierarchy of visual relationships. In recent years, many studies try to utilize a hierarchy to show
133   the similarity and difference of predicates. Zhou et al. (2020) utilizes clustering to construct hi-
134   erarchical structure of the predicates. Yang et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2024a) distinguishes the
135   hierarchical structure of the predicates based on their semantic meaning. Yu et al. (2020) construct
136   the hierarchical tree structure for predicate based on the cognition. Extensive experiments have
137   proved the effectiveness of their works. However, we recognize that it is difficult to explain the
138   spatial similarity between predicates. At the semantic level, many predicates do not show obvious
139
      spatial information. For example, when “boy-watching-crafts” is known, we can not accurately infer
      their geometric relationship. But we can always find a similar geometric relationship for any kind
140
      of visual relationship. Thus in this paper, we deal with geometric relationships independently and
141
      only build the hierarchy for non-geometric predicates.

148   In this section, we first introduce the formulation of the SGG task and then briefly introduce our
149   method. Some detailed descriptions can be found in our appendix A.1.
150   Formulation. Our main research task is scene graph generation. SGG is a task of generating a
151   scene graph G = {V, E} from an image, while V denotes the node set consisting of objects and E
152   denotes the edge set that represents the predicates between objects. Each object entity vi ∈ V has an
153   object category label vic from a set of object categories Cv and box coordinates vib . Each predicate
154   ej ∈ E represents the j-th triplet (sj , pj , oj ), where subject sj and object oj indicate related to
155   object entities and predicate pj has a predicate category label pcj from a set of predicate categories
156   Cp . Generating V and E correspond to object detection and relation extraction, respectively. We
157   give a comprehensive definition of the symbols used in this article in our appendix A.1, please
158
      consult yourself.

161   - which considers both rough visual appearance and fine-grained visual cues to gradually infer visual
      relationships. It includes the following three steps. 1) Known Cues Collection(KCC): firstly, we

220   3.3   K NOWN C UES C OLLECTION
221
222   A major problem is how to extract the fine-grained visual cues that can help infer the visual
223   relationships? A simple idea is to manually provide some fine-grained annotations. Due to the
224   large numbers of visual relationship categories, it is obviously unwise to manually annotate the cues
225   for each image. It is not only has a huge workload, but also limits the flexibility of the model.
226
      To address this problem, we propose Known Cues Collection(KCC) strategy to utilize the Large
      Language Model(LLM) to collect relevant cues. For us, these cues are already described in text, so
227
      we call them as known cues.
228
229   Inspired by Li et al. (2024b), we utilize LLMs to generate the textual description of the visual
230   cues. Specifically, we design the prompts about subject and object to generate the accurate and rich
231   descriptions of visual cues. The prompts we used can be found in appendix A.3. And then, we group
232
      them according to the category of objects. For the i-th object category, we denote the set of visual
      cues related to its categories as T Ci ∈ {T Ci1 , ..., T Cimi }, where mi represents that there are mi
233
      cues related to object i.
234
235   Extensive research has shown that LLM contains important world knowledge. Thus, it can provide
236   us with excellent guidance. However, we realize that these textual descriptions can not be directly
237   used to infer visual relationships. In a visual scene, an object may interact with multiple other
238
      objects. These text descriptions are not fully applicable to the current object pair. Meanwhile, due
      to the factors such as occlusion, image contains the changes that are difficult to summarize in text.
239
      Thus, we need to combine the image content to filter the text.
240
241   A natural question that arises is: how to effectively align text embedding and visual representa-
242   tion? In this paper, we utilize large Vision Language Model(VLM) to address this problem. VLMs
243   like CLIP(Radford et al. (2021)) are pretrained on web-scale datasets consisting of image-text pairs,
244
      resulting in a high degree of alignment between visual text. Many previous works have also proved
      its ability to capture fine-grained visual cues.
245

      3.4   U NKNOWN C UES E XTRACTION
284
285   Visual perception is a rich signal for modeling a vastness of experiences in the world that cannot be
286   documented by text alone. In fact, it is difficult for us to fully summarize the cues on which a visual
287   relationship depends. For example, although the “upright leg” is the key factor of our inference
288   of “stand on”, the “upright body” may also be used as a basis for our judgment when the leg is
289   occluded. Thus, text description can be used as an excellent guidance, but should not be used as the
290   only criterion. We call these cues that are not/are difficult to describe in text as unknown cues. Our
291   model must have the ability to extract unknown cues from images.
292   Since the visual relationship depends on the object, we believe that the information that helps to
293   infer the visual relationship should be at least related to the object. Therefore, we perform a simple
294   decoupling of the object. As shown in fig.2, we generates P convolutional filters independently
295   based on class semantic knowledge. Concretely, for the i-th object proposal, we get its class seman-
296   tic knowledge cki of its category through clip text encoder. And then, we design P different MLPs
297   to decouple it. Every MLP independently maps the class semantic vector from semantic space to a
298   1 × 1 convolutional filter in visual space.
299   Followed by, for the relationship proposal from object i to j, we can get 2 × P latent parts. We
300   treat them as queries to perform local cross attention similar to KCC to get the set of unknown cues
301   ucij ∈ {uc1ij , ..., uc2×P
                             ij  }. It is worth mentioning that because it is based on the decoupling of the
302   object, it is not necessary to judge whether it exists in the image. In other words, there is no global
303   similarity calculation. We will utilize them in the next step. The details of our UCE strategy can
304   be found in our appendix A.4.
305   Through the above operations, we captured the visual cues that may help to infer the visual relation-
306   ship of the current object pair through KCC and UCE strategies. For the relationship proposal from
307   object i to j, the visual cues we get include two parts: kcij (known cues) and ucij (unknown cues).
308   And then, we will utilize these visual cues to progressively optimize predicate representation.

390   4.1   E XPERIMENTS C ONFIGURATIONS
391
392   Dataset Details. To verify the effectiveness of our method, we conduct experiments on a variety of
393
      datasets, including Visual Genome dataset(Krishna et al. (2017)), Open Image V6(Kuznetsova et al.
      (2020)).
394
395   Visual Genome(VG). VG is the most commonly used dataset in SGG task. It consists of 108,073
396   images, including tens of thousands of unique object and predicate categories. However, most cate-
397   gories have a very limited number of instances. In our experiments, we follow the most commonly
398   used data splits proposed by (Xu et al. (2017); Zellers et al. (2018)). The 150 most frequent object
399
      categories and the 50 most frequent predicate types are adopted for evaluation.
400   OpenImages V6(OI). OI dataset is a large scale dataset commonly used for SGG tasks. It contains
401   a diverse collection of over 133k images with 126368 training, 1813 validation, and 5322 testing
402   images. This dataset covers a wide range of real-world scenarios. The OI provides object-level
403   annotations for each image, including bounding-boxes and 301 object categories. In addition, it
404   includes 31 relationship annotations that describe the interactions and connections between pairs of
405
      objects within a scene.
406   Evaluation Protocol. For VG dataset, we evaluate the model on three sub-tasks as Xu et al. (2017);
407   Zellers et al. (2018): 1) predicate classification (PredCls): Given the ground-truth bounding boxes
408   and class labels of objects, we need to predict the visual relationship classes among the object pairs.
409   2) scene graph classification (SGCls): Given the ground-truth bounding boxes of objects, we need
410   to predict both the object and predicate classes. 3) scene graph generation (SGGen, also denote
411
      as SGDet): Given an image, we need to detect the objects and predict their pairwise relationship
      classes. In each task, following the previous works Zellers et al. (2018); Li et al. (2021); Lin et al.
412
      (2020), we takes recall@K(R@K) and mean recall@K(mR@K) as evaluation metrics.
413
414   And for OpenImages V6 dataset, in addition to R@K and mR@K, we employed the following three
415   additional metrics to provide a more comprehensive assessment of SGG methods: 1) Weighted
416   Mean Average Precision for Relationships(wmAPrel ) evaluates the performance of the model in
417
      predicting the relationships between object pairs. It calculates the mean AP for each relationship cat-
      egory, weighted by the number of ground-truth instances of that relationship in a dataset. It provides
418
      a more balanced evaluation by considering the varying frequencies of different relationship types in
419
      scene graphs. 2) Weighted Mean Average Precision for Phrases (wmAPphr ) assess the ability of
420   the model to predict relationship phrases involving both object categories and their corresponding
421   relationships. 3) Weighted Score(scorewtd ) is a comprehensive evaluation metric that combines
422   the performance of the model with both the relationship and phrase predictions, considering their
423   relative importance in scene graphs. This is the weighted sum of wmAPrel and wmAPphr , where
424   the weights are determined based on the significance of the relationships and phrases in a dataset.
425   scorewtd was calculated as: scorewtd = 0.2R@50 + 0.4wmAPrel + 0.4wmAPphr .
426   Implementation details. Our model is similar to the existing two-stage model. In the first stage,
427   we utilize the Faster-RCNN as the object detector, which is based on ResNeXt-101-FPN backbone
428   provided by Xie et al. (2017). And in the second stage, we utilize VIT-B/16 of CLIP model as
429   the backbone to extract the visual cues. The size of patch embedding is 14×14×512. All of our
430   experiments were performed on three 3090 GPUs. The batch size and initial learning rate are set to
431   9 and 0.024, respectively. Our model is optimized by the Adam algorithm with the momentums of
      0.9 and 0.999. The division of datasets follows the most common strategy in the field.

      In general, in order to solve the shortcomings of the existing method to represent predicates, we
534   propose a novel method - Progressive Visual Relationship Inference(PVRI) - which considers both
535   rough visual appearance and fine-grained visual cues to gradually infer visual relationships. In order
536   to prove the effectiveness of our method, we conducted a large number of experiments on Visual
537   Genome, OpenImages datasets. Various experimental results prove the effectiveness and versatility

539   visual relationship are very important for us to understand the scene. We also look forward to more
      research attempts to better collect visual cues.

732   object pairs. In general, each node has a category label from a set of object classes, while each edge
733   has a predicate class.

      Related works. Visual relationship, which describes the interaction between subject and object,
      plays an important role in visual scene understanding. In recent years, there are many related re-
736
      searches on visual relationship(Xu et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017b); Zellers et al. (2018); Yang et al.
737
      (2018); Li et al. (2018)), which can be divided into two types: visual relationship detection and
738   scene graph generation.
739
740   Visual relationship detection. Visual relationship detection(VRD) aims to detect objects in a given
741
      image and identify the interaction between them. The early works(Atzmon et al. (2016); Div-
      vala et al. (2014); Ramanathan et al. (2015); Sadeghi & Farhadi (2011)) regard the whole subject-
742
      predicate-object triplet as a unique category for classification. Due to the long-tailed data distribu-
743
      tion, most relationship categories suffer from the lack of sufficient training examples(Zheng et al.
744   (2019); Li et al. (2021)). To address this problem, later works are proposed to learn the subject,
745   object and predicate separately(Lu et al. (2016); Zhang et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017a); Yu et al.
746   (2017)). Most of them directly extract appearance features from bounding boxes of the subject and
747   the object of their union boxes. Although great progress has been made, the rough visual appearance
748   is not enough to capture complex visual relationships.
749
      Scene graph generation. VRD independently predicts each pair of relationships, while scene graph
750   generation(SGG) considers that there is a correlation between all objects in the scene. In recent
751   years, to enhance the discriminability of relationship representation, SGG attempts to design var-
752   ious message passing strategies(Chang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2017b); Cong et al. (2018); Zellers
753   et al. (2018)). A popular idea is to model the context based on a sequential model(Zellers et al.
754   (2018); Tang et al. (2019))(e.g.LSTM) or a fully-connected graph(Chang et al. (2021); Li et al.
755   (2017b)). They utilize context information to optimize the representation of objects and predicates,
      and extensive research has proved the effectiveness of this idea.

787   Method Overview. Guided by our hierarchy, we propose a novel method - Progressive Visual
788   Relationship Inference (PVRI) - which utilize the visual cues to explain the similarities and dif-
789   ferences of visual relationships at different levels. An overview of our method shown in fig.3. It
790   includes the following three steps. 1) Object and relationship proposals generation(section 3.2):
791   we first generate object and relationship proposals; 2) Known Cues Collection(KCC)(section 3.3):
792   and then, we utilize Large Language Model(LLM) to collect the cues that may help infer visual
793   relationships; 3) Unknown Cues Extraction(UCE)(section 3.4): followed by, we design UCE strat-
794
      egy to extract the cues that are not defined by the text. 4) Progressive Inference(PI)(section 3.5):
      finally, we utilize the obtained cues to infer visual relationships. In summary, our PVRI strategy
795
      is based on the geometric relationships, considering the rough visual appearance and the detailed
796
      visual cues, and explains the similarities and differences of predicates in the hierarchical structure.

842

848
      For example, “sitting next to” and “standing next to” are two different predicate labels but express

      method.

851   Existing problems. It is no doubt that the semantic embedding of predicate category is effective.
852   In previous studies, Zhou et al. (2020) summarized the phrase-format predicate labels into three
853   categories: verb-prep(e.g. “walking by”), prep-prep(e.g.“in between”) and stereotyped expres-
854
      sion(e.g. “inside of”). It can be seen that these predicates are more or less similar to the parts that
      constitute them. Thus, the clustering method based on semantic embedding can well reflect the
855
      similarity between predicates to a certain extent.
856
857   However, we recognize that this method is difficult to explain the spatial similarity between pred-
858   icates. At the semantic level, many predicates do not show obvious spatial features. For example,
859   when “boy-watching-crafts” is known, we can not accurately infer their geometric relationship. But
860
      we can always find a similar geometric relationship for any kind of visual relationship.
861   As shown in fig. 3, in a visual scene, no matter what visual relationship a pair of objects has, we can
862   always find a similar geometric relationship for them. It can be said that geometric relationship is the
863   most basic visual relationship. Thus, to effectively explain the spatial similarity between predicates,
      it is necessary to deal with geometric relationships independently.
