009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Domain-specific adaptation is critical to maximizing the performance of pre-trained
012            language models (PLMs) on one or multiple targeted tasks, especially under
013            resource-constrained use cases, such as edge devices. However, existing methods
014            often struggle to balance domain-specific performance, retention of general knowl-
015            edge, and efficiency for training and inference. To address these challenges, we
016
               propose Modular Domain Experts (MoDE). MoDE is a mixture-of-experts archi-
               tecture that augments a general PLMs with modular, domain-specialized experts.
017
               These experts are trained independently and composed together via a lightweight
018
               training process. In contrast to standard low-rank adaptation methods, each MoDE
019            expert consists of several transformer layers which scale better with more training
020            examples and larger parameter counts. Our evaluation demonstrates that MoDE
021            achieves comparable target performances to full parameter fine-tuning while achiev-
022            ing 1.65% better retention performance. Moreover, MoDE’s architecture enables
023            flexible sharding configurations and improves training speeds by up to 38% over
024            state-of-the-art distributed training configurations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent advances in large-scale Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) have showcased impressive
029
      generalization capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Team et al.,
030   2023). However, when applied to specialized domains such as medical, legal, or financial sectors,
031   these general-purpose models often require further fine-tuning to maximize performance on target
032   domains (Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Singhal et al., 2023).
033
      A straightforward approach to domain adaptation is full-parameter fine-tuning, where the entire
034
      model is further trained on domain-specific data (Houlsby et al., 2019; Bapna et al., 2019). While
035
      this method provides strong performance on target domains, full-parameter fine-tuning may lead
036   to catastrophic forgetting where the model loses previously learned capabilities by overfitting to
037   the target domain (Goodfellow et al., 2013; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Additionally, this method is
038   memory-intensive to serve in multi-domain settings as each domain has a unique set of parameters,
039   incurring a significant parameter loading overhead when switching between domains (Hu et al., 2021).
040   In such cases, the cost of frequent “context switches” significantly impacts performance, making
041   full-parameter fine-tuning impractical for scalable, efficient deployment (Dhar et al., 2024).
042   To address the issues of forgetting and memory-efficiency, parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods
043   have been proposed, such as adapter modules (Houlsby et al., 2019), LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), and
044   CoDA (Lei et al., 2023). These methods introduce a small number of trainable parameters and keep
045   the original model frozen during training. Through targeted parameter updates, these approaches are
046   both computationally efficient and effective at retaining prior knowledge (Biderman et al., 2024).
047   Despite these benefits, parameter-efficient methods are limited in their expressive potential and
048   struggle to scale effectively across large domains and datasets (Niederfahrenhorst et al., 2023).
049   In this work, we propose Modular Domain Experts (MoDE), a scalable method for multi-domain
050   adaptation. MoDE is inspired by the Mixture of Experts (MoE) approach (detailed in Section 2),
051   and extends PLMs by introducing modular, composable domain-specialized experts. In MoDE,
052   each expert consists of several transformer layers that operate in parallel to the “backbone” PLM.
053   During training, the backbone remains frozen, and each expert is independently trained for its
      respective domain. For multi-domain deployment, MoDE combines multiple experts with different

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Domain-Adaptive Pre-training. MoDE is designed for scenarios where large domain-specific
097   unlabeled datasets are available, a process often referred to as “domain-adaptive pre-training”,
098   “continual learning”, “continued pre-training”, or “further pre-training” (Shi et al., 2024; Azerbayev
099   et al., 2023; Colombo et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024). Beyond single-domain adaptation, recent
100   research has expanded into multi-domain adaptation, which presents additional challenges (Saunders,
101   2022; Wu et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, the most relevant works are parameter-efficient
102   fine-tuning methods, which are described in detail in the next paragraph.
103   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning. Significant progress has been made in parameter-efficient fine-
104   tuning methods, which update only a small subset of model parameters (Houlsby et al., 2019; He
105   et al., 2021). Techniques such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) enhance
106   language model performance by introducing trainable low-rank decomposition matrices. However,
107   these low-rank approaches often fall short when adapting to domains that require high expressiveness,
      such as mathematical reasoning or coding (Biderman et al., 2024; Niederfahrenhorst et al., 2023).

108
      Mixtures of Experts (MoE) architectures are promising for expanding the capacity of language mod-
109   els while keeping the computational cost of training constant. MoE models learn a routing function
110   that selectively activates a subset of experts, enabling sparse computation (Du et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
111   2022). Unlike MoDE, MoE models are primarily used to enhance pre-training performance (Dai
112   et al., 2024; Team, 2024; xAI, 2024). Recent approaches extend MoE concepts to improve adaptation
113   to new domains, such as life-long learning with distribution-specialized experts (Chen et al., 2023),
114   conditional adapters (Lei et al., 2023), AdaMix (Wang et al., 2022), and MixPHM (Jiang & Zheng,
115   2023). These approaches modify PLMs at a fine granularity (e.g., by modifying the feed-forward
116   network in the transformer layers). In contrast, MoDE applies MoE at the transformer-level, offering
117
      a scalable and expressive solution for multi-domain adaptation.
118   Furthermore, MoE-inspired approaches have shown potential in selecting domain-specific adapters by
119   routing input sequences, which enhances model performance across diverse domains while alleviating
120   catastrophic forgetting (Feng et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on token-level routing, with
121   sequence-level routing as a natural extension for future research.
122   Distributed Training. Scaling PLMs to billions of parameters requires partitioning parameters
123   and models and training data across multiple processors (Dean et al., 2012; Li et al., 2014; Barham
124   et al., 2022). Common sharding strategies, including model parallelism and data parallelism, evenly
125   divide inputs and parameters across accelerators (Rajbhandari et al., 2020; Lepikhin et al., 2020;
126   Alabed et al., 2024). To address memory overheads and communication bottlenecks, researchers
127   have developed specialized tools to optimize the sharding configuration of a model for the available
128   processors (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Alabed et al., 2024).
129   Typically, distributed training and serving frameworks implement the Single Program, Multiple Data
130   (SPMD) model of computation which enables building models and training programs as if developing
131   for a single processor with a large pool of memory. However, SPMD has two key limitations: (i)
132   all operations (i.e., layers of a model) execute sequentially, precluding the execution of different
133   operations in parallel on different processors, and (ii) all operations must use all devices, which
134   may be inefficient (e.g., by incurring significant communication overhead). In contrast, the Multiple
135
      Program, Multiple Data (MPMD) computational model has the ability to run multiple programs in
      parallel on different devices, enabling fine-grained control over parallelism which has the potential
136
      for more efficient execution compared to SPMD (Zheng et al., 2022). We exploit parallelism in
137
      MoDE’s structure to implement an MPMD-enabled sharding strategy that accelerates training by up
138   to 38% over SPMD model parallelism.

144   Our proposed model architecture augments PLMs with modular, domain-specialized experts and
145   aims to achieve the following design goals:
146
             • Domain specialization. Experts must provide strong performance and high expressiveness
147            on their target domains. Moreover, composing multiple experts with a PLM should result in
148            strong performance on each of the target domains.
149          • Capability retention. The model must preserve the generalist capabilities of the PLM and
150            avoid catastrophic forgetting which may cause performance regressions on other tasks.
151          • Efficient training and inference. The model architecture must be efficient to train on large
152            clusters, and provide advantages for inference on edge devices.

      3.2   T RAINING P ROCEDURE
208
      MoDE has a two stage training procedure (Figure 1b): (i) train a single expert independently on each
209   domain, and (ii) compose different experts into a single model to improve multi-domain performance.
210
211   Single Modular Expert Training. For each domain, we independently train an MoDE model with
212
      a single expert. During training, only the expert layers and gating layers are updated, while the
      backbone layers remain frozen. Freezing the backbone not only reduces the computational cost of
213
      backpropagation but also helps mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
214
215   Composing Experts. After training the modular experts on each domain, we can compose them
      into a single MoDE model for multi-domain adaptation. While the experts contain domain-specific

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      knowledge, their outputs can interfere when combined, as they are pre-trained individually. To
217   address this, we apply a lightweight fine-tuning step using data from all domains to learn the optimal
218   weights for the gating function. Although freezing the experts requires fewer training examples
219   during composition Appendix C, we have demonstrated that unfreezing the experts during this step
220   further enhances multi-domain adaptation performance Table 1.

222   3.3   PARALLELIZING B LOCK E XECUTION
223
224   We exploit parallelism in MoDE’s structure to enable flexible sharding configurations supported by
225   the MPMD model of computation. These sharding configurations improve training efficiency due to:
226         1. The ability to execute the backbone and experts on distinct, smaller sets of devices which
227            reduces communication overheads (Figure 2).
228         2. The ability to configure the backbone and experts independently to maximize overall
229            efficiency, when their number of parameters are different.
230
231   While MPMD has the potential to reduce communication overheads by running blocks on fewer
232   devices, the merges in the MoDE model architecture are points of synchronization that require
233
      communication across all devices. To determine whether MPMD is beneficial for training, we evaluate
      whether MPMD configurations can afford the cost of resharding between meshes in Section 4.4.
234
235   While MPMD has the potential to reduce communication overheads by executing blocks on smaller
236   sets of devices, the linear combination of the outputs from the backbone and expert blocks requires are
237   points of synchronization that require communication across all devices. To assess the effectiveness of
238   MPMD in training, we evaluate if the benefits of MPMD configurations outweigh the costs associated
239
      with resharding between device meshes, as discussed in Section 4.4.
240   Moreover, MoDE’s architecture enables several benefits during inference for privacy and performance.
241   By using MPMD to run MoDE with the backbone and expert parameters on different devices, expert
242   parameters for sensitive domains (e.g., personal information, medical data, etc.) can remain private
243   (e.g., by executing on a user’s phone). To switch to another MoDE model adapted to different task,
244   only the new expert weights need to be loaded into memory because the backbone PLM’s weights are
245
      shared. This reduces the overhead of loading model weights and works well with existing techniques
      developer to serve many adapters concurrently, such as SLoRA (Sheng et al., 2024).

360
361   We evaluate the scalability of MoDE along two dimensions: scaling with additional parameters and
362   scaling with increased training examples. We use LoRA as the baseline in both experiments and
363   conduct experiments on a single domain using the Code dataset for training and evaluation.
364

365

366   of the decomposition matrices, ranging from 8 to 2,048. For MoDE, we increase the number of
367   transformer layers per expert while keeping the number of MoDE blocks constant. As shown in
368   Figure 3, while LoRA is parameter-efficient, it struggles to convert additional parameters into higher

377   MoDE continues to improve as the number of training examples increases, demonstrating superior

      Through our experiments, we find several MPMD-enabled sharding configurations which increase
396
      training speeds by up to 38% over SPMD model-parallelism. The results confirm that the reduction
397
      in communication costs by running backbones and experts on fewer accelerators outweighs the added
398   costs of resharding across meshes when merging the intermediate outputs of models.
399
400   All experiments use 8 TPUv5e accelerators which are managed by an orchestration layer that supports
401
      MPMD (Anonymized, 2024). We implement MPMD training by extending a distributed machine
      learning framework (Anonymized, 2024) implemented in Jax (Bradbury et al., 2018). While we
402
      use TPUs and a Jax-based framework as our training environment, we underscore that the key
403
      idea of using MPMD to increase training speed in distributed settings extends to other accelerators
404   (e.g., GPUs) and training systems such as PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). We measure the training
405   performance, but note that many of the performance improvements enabled by MPMD sharding
406   strategies may also benefit model serving. In details, we compare the following configurations:
407
408                    1. MPMD divides the TPUs into different meshes to which backbone or experts are assigned.
409
                       2. SPMD creates a single mesh which consists of all 8 TPUs, backbone and experts.
                       3. SPMD-pjit uses our training library’s to parallelize model training.
410
411   Cost of Resharding. We compare the following configurations, which train MoDE using 4 experts
412   where each consists of 6 transformer layers divided evenly into two blocks:
413
414
                       1. 1 mesh uses model parallelism to shard the model across a single mesh.
                       2. 3 mesh assigns the backbone to 4 TPUs and each expert shares 2 TPUs with another expert.
415
                          The backbone and the experts are sharded on their meshes using model parallelism.
416
                       3. 5 mesh assigns the backbone to 4 TPUs, and one expert to each of the remaining TPUs. The
417                       backbone is sharded with model parallelism.
418
419   We find that the cost of resharding between meshes is lower than the cost of model parallelism
420   (Figure 4a). The reduction in training step latency from 329 ms for 1 mesh to 204 ms, a 38% reduction,
421   results due to the reduction in communication caused by the MPMD sharding configuration. Because
422
      the latency of the 3 mesh and 5 mesh configurations is similar, we conclude that the cost to reshard
      does not increase with the number of meshes.
423
424   Configuring the Expert Size. We configure a mixture of 2 expert divided into two blocks and
425   change the total number of transformer layers added. For example, setting the expert block size to 2
426   transformer layers adds 8 total transformer layers to the model. The backbone is assigned to 6 TPUs
427   with a model parallel sharding, and each expert is assigned to one unoccupied TPU.
428   We find that SPMD performs better than MPMD when the expert size is small or large (Figure 4b)
429   due to under-utilization of the TPUs. When the expert size is small (e.g., 8 added layers total) and we
430   train using MPMD, the expert blocks finish executing before the backbone block, so the expert TPUs
431   remain idle until the backbone block completes and the merge begins. Similarly, when the expert
      size is large (e.g., 20 added layers total), the backbone blocks finish before the expert blocks so the


                                                                      8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      backbone’s TPUs idle until the expert complete. In contrast, SPMD executes the backbone and expert
433   blocks sequentially on all TPUs, ensuring that each TPU is always utilized.
434
435   When the runtime of a backbone block is similar to the runtime of the expert blocks, MPMD training
436
      results in high utilization of the accelerator. Several settings impact the TPU utilization using MPMD
      which can be configured to ensure high utilization, such as the number of accelerators allocated to
437
      each mesh, the size of the expert blocks, and the sharding strategy on each mesh.
438
439   Cost of Merges. We train a mixture of 4 experts with 6 transformer layers each. The backbone is
440   assigned to a mesh of 4 TPUs, and each expert is assigned to 1 unoccupied TPU. We change the
441   number of merges which determines the number of blocks (e.g., 3 merges divide the backbone and
442
      each expert into three blocks). We find that MPMD training speed decreases as the number of merges
      increases due to the added communication costs incurred by each merge (Figure 4c).

445   5   C ONCLUSIONS
446
447   Adapting pre-trained language models (PLMs) to complex, multi-domain settings is increasingly
448   important as these models are deployed in specialized environments requiring diverse capabilities.
449   To address this challenge, we propose Modular Domain Experts (MoDE), a scalable technique for
450   adapting PLMs to multi-domain tasks. MoDE introduces modular, domain-specialized experts while
451
      preserving the general knowledge of the PLM by keeping its weights frozen. MoDE allows experts
      to scale with both the number of parameters and the amount of training data, outperforming standard
452
      parameter-efficient methods like LoRA by 1.4% on a challenging multi-domain dataset. Additionally,
453
      MoDE delivers competitive performance compared to full-parameter fine-tuning, achieving 1.5%
454   better retention of general capabilities and 0.6% higher accuracy across all subdomains. To further
455   optimize training efficiency, MoDE ’s architecture supports flexible sharding strategies through
456   MPMD, resulting in up to 38% faster training compared to standard distributed training methods.
457   The ability to compose and reuse modular experts represents a significant advancement in adapting
458   PLMs to complex domains. We hope that our work inspires further research into building modular,
459   expert-based language models.
