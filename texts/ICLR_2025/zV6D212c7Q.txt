009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning meaningful representations is a core topic of deep learning. Through-
012            out the last decade, many strategies for learning image representations have been
013            proposed involving supervision and self-supervision and various data sources. In
014            most current work, evaluation is focused on classification tasks while neglecting
015            dense prediction tasks, possibly because linear probing is more challenging in the
016            latter case. Furthermore, dense prediction heads are often large and come with
017            specific inductive biases that distort performance measurement further. In this
018            work we propose masked cross-attention adapters (MAXA), a minimal adapter
019
               method that is capable of dense prediction independent of the size and resolution
               of the encoder output. This allows us to make dense predictions using a small
020
               number of additional parameters (< 0.3%) while allowing for fast training using
021
               frozen backbones. Using this adapter, we run a comprehensive evaluation assess-
022            ing instance awareness, local semantics and spatial representation of a diverse set
023            of backbones. We find that DINOv2 outperforms all other backbones tested –
024            including those supervised with masks and language – across all three task cate-
025            gories.
026            Code is available at https://to.be.released.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Computer vision builds to a large extent on a transfer learning-based paradigm. Instead of training
032   specific tasks from scratch, large-scale models (foundation models) are pre-trained on a large dataset
033   once. These models are often called backbone or feature extractor as they are used to address
034   multiple specific tasks, for example through in-context learning, adapters or (often costly) fine-
035   tuning. For pre-training the backbone, different variants exist, including classic supervised, self-
036   supervised, and vision-language training. The latter two variants tend to scale better as they are
037   not constrained by availability of labels and can use the internet as a data source. Many factors
038   influence the quality of the resulting backbone: the pre-training paradigm, model architecture, the
039
      training data. For both, computer vision scientists and practitioners, it is crucial to work out strengths
      and weaknesses of individual backbones through systematic benchmarks. Established approaches
040
      are linear and attentive probing. For (whole-image) classification performance such benchmarks are
041
      readily available (ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2014), VTAB (Zhai et al., 2019)). However, for
042   dense tasks, where the model output has spatial dimensions (for example semantic segmentation or
043   monocular depth estimation) such an evaluation is more challenging: Linear and attentive probing
044   can be applied but the prediction has the same resolution as the feature volume which is usually low
045   and varies across backbones. On the other hand, using standard task heads for dense prediction adds
046   a large number of parameters and introduces its own inductive biases. The measure performance
047   would depend to a large extent on the chosen task head and not on the underlying backbone.
048   Here we address the problem of measuring performance of feature backbones as directly as pos-
049   sible by introducing a dense equivalent to attentive probing that requires only a small number of
050   parameters. Our goal is to obtain a holistic characterization of strengths and weaknesses of common
051   feature extractors. To this end, we propose a novel method that uses masked cross-attention (Fig. 1)
052   to extract relevant features from the backbone activations. By using cross-attention, we decouple
053   the size and resolution of the input image and encoder output from that of the dense output, i.e.
      generate outputs at any resolution. We introduce a learnable masking radius in the cross-attention

088   2   R ELATED WORK
089
090
      Representation learning Self-supervised representation learning was a popular research topic
091   with multiple approaches that can roughly be categorized into joint-embedding (Chen et al., 2020;
092   2021; Caron et al., 2021) and reconstruction-based (He et al., 2022). DINOv2 is based on the
093   iBot (Zhou et al., 2021) method which uses a joint-embedding architecture in combination with
094   self-distillation and reconstruction. VicRegL (Bardes et al., 2021) and many other recent methods
095   explicitly addresses local features by modeling losses at the token level. There has been a discussion
096   about which techniques leads to better and more efficient features for perception tasks (Balestriero &
097   LeCun, 2024). While the approaches discussed above mainly address classification, another stream
098   of research focused on representation learning that disentangles objects, called object-centric learn-
099
      ing. While early methods only worked on synthetic data (Burgess et al., 2019; Locatello et al., 2020)
      more recent approaches succeed on natural images (Zadaianchuk et al., 2024; Aydemir et al., 2023).
100
      Recently, a new method for evaluating such object-centric representations was proposed: Didolkar
101
      et al. (2024). The main difference to object-centric methods is that we assume features encode ob-
102   ject instances whereas object-centric methods explicitly represent objects in their architecture (e.g.
103   in slots). The seminal CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) introduced another stream of research
104   called vision-language models, where the model is trained on aligning text-image pairs. Later, this
105   training paradigm was simplified to use a sigmoid-based loss function (Zhai et al., 2023) instead of
106   a softmax-basde loss, making the method less dependent on the batch size. Recently, the role of data
107   is investigated more closely in the context of vision-language models (Gadre et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
      2024; Fang et al., 2024).

108
      Feature evaluation Evaluations on features predate the deep learning era in computer vision. Re-
109   cently, there have been numerous attempts at characterizing and comparing common feature back-
110   bones but with different objectives. The works by Bonnen et al. and El Banani et al. focus on 3d
111   shape understanding. Chen et al. design a zero-shot benchmark for image encoders in contrastive
112   vision-language pre-training setting and propose the ViTamin architecture. Goldblum et al. evaluate
113   classification, instance segmentation, object detection and retrieval. Our work differs in focusing
114   on dense prediction tasks without large heads enabling a more direct measurement of the feature
115   quality. Further efforts to characterize vision backbones include the timm leaderboard (Wightman,
116   2019) for image classification, CLIP benchmark (LAION-AI, 2022) for vision-language models and
117
      CV-Bench for multimodal large language models (MLLMs) (Tong et al., 2024).
118
      Adapters and parameter-efficient fine-tuning Adapters are (often small) sub-networks that are
119
      trained to take generic features and use them to solve a specific task. For computer vision problems,
120
      many adapters were proposed that address whole-image classification (Chen et al., 2022; Steitz &
121   Roth, 2024). Also, the attentive probing (or attentional pooling) used in the CoCa (Yu et al., 2022)
122   and V-JEPA evaluation (Bardes et al., 2023) can be considered a minimal adapter for whole image
123   classification. These methods are not straightforward applicable for dense prediction. Based on the
124   upsampling method FeatUp (Fu et al., 2024), linear evaluation can be applied in higher resolutions.
125   To our knowledge this has not been done before but we compare to a baseline that uses this approach.
126   The methods by Bhattacharjee et al. and Yang et al. adapt to dense images but address multiple tasks
127   at once. In both methods, the backbones are not entirely frozen.
128   In another research stream, learnable parameters are added inside the frozen backbone network, for
129   instance in Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019), low-rank adaptation (Hu et al., 2021) and scaling-and-
130   shifting (Lian et al., 2022). ViT-Adapter applies this paradigm for dense prediction tasks but builds
131   on established task heads for segmentation (UperNet) and detection (Mask RCNN and HTC++).
132   Furthermore, the number of parameters introduced by the adapter depends on the backbone, ranging
133   from 2.5M to 23.7M parameters. For full review on adapters we refer to the survey of Yu et al..

137   In this section, we introduce MAXA. It is designed to be parameter and compute efficient adapter

139   arbitrary (frozen) image backbone ϕ receives an image x of size (Hs, W s, 3) and generates features

141   fixed positional encoding P . The resulting activations are projected to the internal dimension D and
142   flattened along the spatial dimensions (both by ψ), yielding F(x) of size (H, W, D):

      4      E XPERIMENTS
189
      Choice of models and training datasets We select a broad range of feature backbones that en-
190
      compass different training paradigms and were trained on different datasets (Tab. 1). This enables
191   us to conduct controlled comparisons along several axes, for instance, datasets and pre-training task.
192   In general, we differentiate between three broad classes of methods: supervised (Dosovitskiy et al.,
193   2021; Ravi et al., 2024), self-supervised (Caron et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Ryali et al., 2023),
194   and vision-language (Radford et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2024; Fini et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Liu
195   et al., 2022). The latter involves training on image-caption pairs often obtained from the Internet,
196   while self-supervised training operates on images only. Most models are trained on the ImageNet
197   dataset (Russakovsky et al., 2014), but there are several exceptions: All SigLIP models are trained
198   on the Webli dataset, a Google-internal dataset of 10 billion images with 12 billion multi-lingual
199
      text-image pairs. MetaCLIP uses a selection of the open LAION dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2021),
      CLIP is trained on the unpublished CLIP dataset by OpenAI. DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) is trained
200
      on the LVD-142M, a Meta-internal dataset of 142M images which were deduplicated and curated
201
      to be similar to ImageNet-22k images. The data mix of Aim2 Fini et al. (2024) contains DFN-2B,
202   COYO, the proprietary HQITP dataset and synthetic data.
203
204   We decide to mainly focus on vision transformers as many approaches share the same architecture
205
      and checkpoints are available for a large number of training paradigms. To ensure comparability
      with other work and control for model architecture, we primarily use ViT-B/16 and similarly sized
206
      models in our experiments. We also include larger models in some cases to obtain an estimate of
207
      how much performance can be improved simply by scaling-up model size. Pretrained-weights are
208   obtained from timm (Wightman, 2019).
209
210   Experiment design We provide images in the native resolution of the respective backbones to
211   prevent out-of-distribution input. For generating predictions, we make use of the capability of our
212   model to decouple input and output resolution (see Sec. 3): The output size is fixed to 224 × 224 for
213   all models, ensuring a fair comparison across models and limiting the advantage of large input sizes
214   for the backbone. In the masked cross attention we use a dimension D = 32 and eight attention
215   heads (i.e. four dimensions per head). Although the adapter could attend to backbone tokens at
      different levels we opt for using only the last layer, motivation by the simple pyramid from Li et al..

      We pursue a straightforward approach to comparison: We freeze the backbone features, train the
217   small readout adapter in a supervised way and evaluate on a hold-out test set. The rationale is that

223   4.1   E VALUATION TASKS
224
225   To characterize a broad spectrum of traits of the features we implement the following tasks:
226
227   Instance awareness In this task we evaluate how well the features are able to disentangle indi-
228
      vidual instances. In the field of object-centric machine learning (Burgess et al., 2019) models are
      designed to disentangle instances, here we ask to which degree this is already achieved in different
229
      backbones. Individual instances can be encoded in various ways in the features. We consider the
230
      following three notions of how instances are encoded (Fig. 2):
231
232          • Instance boundaries: The objective is to outline individual objects in the image. We frame
233            this problem as a binary segmentation and consequently use an output dimension Dout = 1
234            as well as the binary cross-entropy loss function on the adapter output.
235          • Distance Transform: This is similar to boundaries but computes a single dense map (Dout =
236            1) of normalized distances to the instance boundaries. Here we apply the mean squared
237            error as loss and metric.
238          • Instance discrimination: Another way to encode instances is to generate a latent space
239            where features within instances are the same (or similar) while being different to all other
240            instances. If this works perfectly, clustering the latent vectors of all pixels would yield
241
               instances. This task is sometimes also called coloring (Novotny et al., 2018). We train
               on only 8,000 sample images and treat every instance as an individual class (resulting
242
               in a around 60,000 classes). The dense adapter maps the features to a latent space (in
243
               our case 32, Dout = 32). Then, a linear layer maps each local 32-dimensional feature
244            to a probability over all instances in the dataset. Thus, the problem is essentially framed
245            as semantic segmentation with 60,000 classes. This way, the latent features before the
246            classification head learn to discriminate instances. For testing, we cluster these features
247            obtained from unseen images. For clustering we use k-means and provide the ground-
248            truth number of instances as well as a foreground mask. Then we compare the predicted
249            foreground instances with the ground truth instance segmentation based on the adjusted
250            rand index.

303   4.2   C OMPARISON ON BACKBONES
304
305   Results (Tab. 2) indicate that DINOv2 has the best instance awareness. The backbone of SAM2
306   shows a fairly low performance despite being trained for instance discrimination. This sug-
307
      gest that objects are not disentangled before SAM2’s mask decoder. Despite following the same
      reconstruction-based training, MAE performs better than Hiera which could potentially be due to
308
      the more intensive spatial compression in Hiera. Among the vision-language models CLIP, Meta-
309
      CLIP and SigLIP we did not find meaningful differences. The evaluation on spatial understanding
310   shows mixed results. Larger backbones tend to perform better, with exception of Hiera-B+. Again,
311   DINOv2 performs best, in this case by a large margin. Vison-language tend to show stronger local
312   semantics (Tab. 3). While all VLM 224px models show similar performance, the larger versions
313   of SigLIP (i.e. 384 and SO) perform better but at a higher cost. Also in this evaluation, DINOv2
314   achieves the best scores. All things considered, possibly the most striking finding is the dominance
315   of DINOv2. While one might argue that this is due to large image sizes and feature volumes, the
316   mediocre performance of SigLIP-512 and Hiera-B+ show that cannot be the only factor.

318   4.3   A DAPTER DESIGN
319
320   We next explore design choices of MAXA by varying relevant hyperparameters of our readout
321   adapter (Tab. 4). The two-layer cross-attention and the introduction of the σ parameter are cru-
322   cial for good performance. The latter suggests that information is organized locally in the feature
323   volume. Reducing the dimensionality impacts instance discrimination, possibly as space is scarce
      for embedding 60,000 instances. Thus, an even smaller adapter could be used for the other tasks.

      4.4   A DAPTER RESULT CORRELATE WITH DOWNSTREAM TASKS
407
408
      To assess the reliability of our findings, we consider previous work in object-centric representation
409
      learning and a classification-based evaluation (Fig. 5 left and middle). Object-centric learning shares
410   the goal of disentangling instances but tries to achieve this through specific model architectures
411   whereas we evaluate model-agnostic features for instance-specific signals. Relating to the instance
412   clustering performance by Aydemir et al. we find an almost linear relationship between their and
413   our scores. Comparing with DINOSAUR (Seitzer et al., 2023), we find the ordering of the scores
414   to be consistent. Note, no statement regarding better performance can be made since the evaluation
415   protocols do not match. The evaluation of Goldblum et al. shares the goal of characterizing current
416   backbones with our work but put more emphasis on out-of-distribution and backbone architecture.
417   We found our results (Fig. 5, right) to be consistent with their COCO fine-tuning scores, except
418
      for DINO. Note, to obtain their scores, a resource-intensive object detection training is required.
      Summed up, our method can be used to obtain similar insights on relative backbone performance to
419
      more complex evaluations, but much faster.

455   5     D ISCUSSION
456
457
      In this work we proposed the masked cross-attention adapter, a fast and parameter-efficient method
458
      for evaluating backbones. For example, our standard training for an adapter on a ViT-B/16 224 pixel
459
      backbone on Pascal VOC adds less than 200,000 parameters and takes less than 16 minutes (us-
460   ing a single Nvidia RXT2080 GPU). We use this method to systematically analyze common vision
461   backbones with respect to the three complementary aspects: instance awareness, depth and local se-
462   mantics. Our results suggest that DINOv2 is a highly capable backbone, it is the best ViT-B model
463   across all experiments. Using DINOv2 with a ViT-L backbone performs improves performance
464   further but at a three times longer runtime. Classic supervised pre-training on ImageNet results in
465   fairly poor performance. Based on our results, a promising direction would be to use DINOv2-like
466   objective function for pre-training in object detection, where ImageNet is currently the standard.
467   Also VLMs and MLLMs could benefit from adopting the DINOv2 loss into their training algo-
468
      rithms. We identified a trend that local semantics is better captured by language-vision models
      while reconstruction-based self-supervised learning appears to have better instance awareness. We
469
      also found the input image resolution to play a significant role, despite decoupling input and output
470
      resolution.
471
472   For practitioners, DINOv2 is a natural choice if enough compute is available. For compute-
473   constrained cases the decision is more complex. Vision-language models generally perform well
474
      on tasks that require local semantics, while for instance discrimination reconstruction-based self-
      supervised learning methods excel. We plan to retain an online leaderboard where new backbones
475
      can easily be incorporated to help tracking future progress of dense prediction performance.
476
477
478
      Limitations While we use a fairly small decoder (in terms of parameters), even this decoder can
479
      have inductive biases and favor certain backbones such that results might get distorted. Using more
480   complex task heads would enable more complex feature processing. In fact, this could be the reason
481   why SAM performs comparably poorly in instance awareness in our hands. A more direct com-
482   parison to object-centric approaches would be interesting, but is challenging as these approaches
483   explicitly encode objects (e.g. in attention slots) which can be compared to ground truth. The cur-
484   rent selection of tasks we evaluated is limited to three broad categories and a few instances of those.
485   Adding additional task categories (e.g. as in Taskonomy (Zamir et al., 2018)) would be desirable for
      a more detailed characterization of backbones.
