009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The multi-objective alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) is essential
012
               for ensuring foundational models conform to diverse human preferences. Cur-
013
               rent research in this field typically involves either multiple policies or multi-
014            ple reward models customized for various preferences, or the need to train a
015            preference-specific supervised fine-tuning (SFT) model. In this work, we intro-
016            duce a novel multi-objective alignment method, MOSLIM, which utilizes a single
017            reward model and policy model to address diverse objectives. MOSLIM pro-
018            vides a flexible way to control these objectives through prompting and does not
019            require preference training during SFT phase, allowing thousands of off-the-shelf
020            models to be directly utilized within this training framework. MOSLIM lever-
021            ages a multi-head reward model that classifies question-answer pairs instead of
022
               scoring them and then optimize policy model with a scalar reward derived from
               a mapping function that converts classification results from reward model into re-
023
               ward scores. We demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed method across several
024
               multi-objective benchmarks and conduct ablation studies on various reward model
025            sizes and policy optimization methods. The MOSLIM method outperforms cur-
026            rent multi-objective approaches in most results while requiring significantly fewer
027            GPU computing resources compared with existing policy optimization methods.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   While large language models (LLMs) have been widely adopted across various domains, generat-
034   ing text that aligns with human preferences has become a prominent area of research. Stiennon
035   et al. (2020) introduced the concept of learning from human feedback to better align model be-
036   havior with human preferences, specifically aiming to produce summaries that are more preferred
037   by human annotators. Ouyang et al. (2022) proposed the Reinforcement Learning from Human
038   Feedback (RLHF) approach within InstructGPT framework, employing a combination of super-
039
      vised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning to align models with human-defined preferences, such
      as instruction-following and safety. Later that year, Bai et al. (2022) also proposed an alignment
040
      method based on human feedback, focusing on enhancing helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty.
041
042   However, it has become evident that models aligned with mixed general preferences often struggle
043   to address diverse needs of different application scenarios (Zhang 2023; Lee et al. 2024; Kirk et al.
044   2024). The preferences required for varying tasks and users can differ significantly. For example, in
045
      certain cases, it may be necessary to prioritize helpfulness over honesty, thereby accepting a degree
      of hallucination. Consequently, multi-objective preference alignment has emerged as a significant
046
      area of research. Researchers have begun exploring methods to apply different combinations of
047
      preferences to generate content that better suits specific contextual requirements (Ramé et al. 2023;
048   Zhou et al. 2023; Jang et al. 2023; Yang et al. 2024; Guo et al. 2024b; Wang et al. 2024a; Li et al.
049   2024d). Methods such as MORLHF, Rewarded Soups(Ramé et al. 2023), and MODPO(Zhou et al.
050   2023) have been proposed to address this challenge by training models to align with distinct prefer-
051   ence combinations. However, techniques like MORLHF and MODPO can only align with a specific
052   combination of preferences once training is completed, whereas reward soups allows for more flexi-
053   bility by combining preferences at the inference stage but at the cost of increased computational and
      training overheads due to the need to train multiple policies.

054
      To address these limitations, researchers have explored methods for dynamically adjusting content
055   generation preferences through the use of prompts. Yang et al. (2024) proposed the RiC method,
056   which leverages supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to enable models to respond according to different tags
057   embedded in the prompts. Guo et al. (2024b) introduced the CDPO approach, asserting that most
058   real-world applications require balancing only a limited set of preferences simultaneously. These
059   methods rely primarily on SFT phase, with some incorporating additional RLHF training. However,
060   under the current LLM training paradigm, the SFT stage is mainly responsible for enhancing core
061   capabilities such as mathematical reasoning, coding, and specialized domain knowledge, while the
062   RLHF stage is used to integrate human-aligned responseset al (2024); Lu et al. (2024). As a result,
063
      directly incorporating complex preference combinations and intensities during SFT stage is imprac-
      tical for real application scenarios. For instance, defining what constitutes a helpful, harmless, or
064
      honest response in a mathematical context is inherently challenging.
065
066   In this paper, we propose the MOSLIM method, which enables content generation aligned with
067   varying combinations of preferences using prompt-based control while significantly improving train-
068   ing efficiency. MOSLIM eliminates the need to incorporate preference-specific generation capabil-
069
      ities during SFT phase, achieving dynamic preference alignment solely through the RLHF stage.
      This allows off-the-shelf models to be directly integrated with MOSLIM without requiring substan-
070
      tial modifications or retraining. MOSLIM employs a multi-head classification reward model and
071
      a policy gradient approach based on a reward mapping function to align with any combination of
072   preference objectives and intensities using a single reward model and policy model during training.
073   During inference, a prompt-driven mechanism enables flexible adaptation to varying preference in-
074   tensity combinations.
075
      We validate our method through experiments using three distinct preference evaluation benchmarks,
076
      demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving controllable preference alignment. Furthermore, we
077
      assess performance across different intensity levels, highlighting significant behavioral variations
078   as the intensity of preferences changes. To the best of our knowledge, our approach is the first to
079   achieve dynamic preference alignment using a single reward model and policy while with the ability
080   of leveraging off-the-shelf models, representing a novel contribution to the field of multi-objective
081   preference alignment.

      2.3   P OLICY O PTIMIZATION
226
      In this section, we are aiming to train policy models using the reward model constructed in 2.2.
227   The required data format in this stage consists of prompt data with specific preference dimensions
228   and intensities, which should be consistent with the objectives defined in reward model training.
229   An example of training samples used in this phase is shown in Appendix A. The entire policy
230   optimization method involves two key components: prompt alignment and reward mapping ,
231   with a complete policy optimization process depicted in Appendix A.
232
      Prompt Alignment: Since prompts did not include preference labels during reward model training,
233   we need to remove these labels when obtaining reward scores in the policy optimization stage. This
234   adjustment ensures that inference data received by reward model remains consistent with the data
235   format used during its training. The data flow for both the reward model and policy model inputs is
236   illustrated in Appendix A.
237
      Reward Mapping: Most current policy optimization methods receive a scalar reward signal. There-
238
      fore, we need to apply a reward mapping strategy for multi-head reward model to aggregate prefer-
239   ence classification results into a unified scalar. The reward mapping process addresses the following
240   two main challenges:
241
242         1. Dimensional Variability: The number of preference dimensions may vary depending on
243            the specific business scenario, requiring compatibility with any number of preference di-
244            mensions.
245         2. Intensity Scaling: Each preference dimension may have different levels of controllability.
246            For example, the helpfulness dimension might have 5 levels, while the harmless dimension
247            might only have 2. Consequently, scores across different dimensions need to be scaled to a
248
               consistent metric for comparability.
249   To address these challenges, we propose a reward mapping function, which can scale both dimen-
250   sions and intensities of preferences. During the training phase, we record the moving average and
251   standard deviation of each preference intensity within every preference head. During inference
252   phase, each preference dimension value is transformed into a sample value from a Gaussian dis-
253   tribution with zero mean and unit variance, making the intensity scores from different preference
254   dimensions additive. The specific reward mapping formula is defined as follows:
255

      4   R ELATED W ORKS
494
495
      Multi-objective Alignment: Multi-objective alignment approaches aim to extend RLHF (Ouyang
496   et al., 2022) by accommodating multiple preference objectives simultaneously. One foundational
497   method is MORLHF, which trains separate reward models for each preference dimension. Re-
498   warded Soups (Ramé et al., 2023) expand this framework by training multiple policies and merging
499   their parameters, enabling flexible preference combinations at inference time. Similarly, Wang et al.
500   (2024b) leverage parameter merging within a multi-task paradigm to train a conditioned LLM capa-
501   ble of handling diverse preferences. Park et al. (2024) propose a clustered Direct Policy Optimization
502   (DPO, Rafailov et al. 2023) approach to model diverse user preferences more effectively. MOLMA
503   (Zhang, 2023) explores how to balance conflicting multi-objective preferences in language model
504
      alignment through reinforcement learning. Both Jang et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2024c) focus on
      personalizing language models to generate content tailored to individual users, extending alignment
505
      objectives from general preferences to personal objectives. These works aim to push the bound-
506
      aries of multi-objective alignment by exploring strategies to align LLMs with the unique needs of
507   different users.
508
509   Prompt-driven Preference Generation: Due to the complexity and overhead associated with train-
510
      ing multiple reward models or policies for multi-objective alignment, there is growing interest in
      developing methods that enable preference control using a single model. Yang et al. (2024) pro-
511
      pose Rewards in Context (RiC), a supervised training approach that incorporates preference tags
512
      into prompts, demonstrating superior multi-objective control compared to non-prompt-based meth-
513   ods like MORLHF and Rewarded Soups. Guo et al. (2024b) introduce a flexible prompt-based
514   paradigm, arguing that it is unnecessary to seek the Pareto front every time during generation, as
515   real-world applications rarely require satisfying all preferences simultaneously. Their method priori-
516   tizes a single preference while disregarding others and further extends preference control to intensity
517   levels, allowing for more granular customization. Lee et al. (2024) present Janus, a framework that
518   aligns with various preferences through system prompts, offering a comprehensive set of preference
519   categories supported by the Janus model.
520
521   5   C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we propose MOSLIM, a novel framework for multi-objective alignment in Large Lan-
524   guage Models (LLMs) that leverages a single reward model and policy model to efficiently satis-
525   fies diverse human preferences. MOSLIM significantly reduces training complexity and resource
526   requirements and eliminats the need for preference-specific supervised fine-tuning (SFT), which en-
527   ables the use of off-the-shelf models. Experimental results on various multi-objective benchmarks
528   demonstrate that MOSLIM outperforms existing approaches in terms of preference controllability,
529   robustness, and computational efficiency. Moreover, our method provides a flexible and scalable so-
530
      lution for aligning LLMs with complex preference combinations, offering fine-grained control over
      both dimensions and intensities. This work advances the state-of-the-art in preference-based LLM
531
      alignment and opens new avenues for research into personalized and dynamic preference optimiza-
532
      tion in large language models.
