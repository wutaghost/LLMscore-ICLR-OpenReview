009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Pruning is a critical strategy for compressing trained large language models (LLMs),
012            aiming at substantial memory conservation and computational acceleration without
013            compromising performance. However, existing pruning methods typically necessi-
014            tate inefficient retraining for billion-scale LLMs or rely on heuristically designed
015            metrics to determine pruning masks, leading to performance degradation. This
016            paper presents, for the first time, a LASSO-like convex optimization model crafted
017
               to induce sparsity in LLMs. By leveraging the FISTA, we introduce FISTAPruner,
               a novel method that includes a cumulative error elimination mechanism within
018
               decoder layers and supports parallel pruning for unstructured pruning. Addition-
019
               ally, we extend this method to 2:4 semi-structured pruning. We comprehensively
020            evaluate FISTAPruner on models such as OPT and LLaMA variants with 125M to
021            70B parameters under unstructured and 2:4 semi-structured sparsity, showcasing
022            superior performance over existing methods across various language benchmarks.
023            Notably, it can remove 50% of the model parameters for LLaMA-3-70B while
024            retaining 98.6% and 95.6% of the zero-shot task performance under these two
025            sparsity patterns, respectively.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing
      fields, achieving impressive results in tasks such as machine translation, sentiment analysis, question
031
      answering, and text generation (Lyu et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a;b; Wang et al.,
032
      2023; Arefeen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). Advanced LLMs such as OpenAI’s GPT-4 (OpenAI,
033   2023), Meta’s LLaMA-3 (Meta AI, 2023), and Google’s Gemini (Gemini Team et al., 2023) excel in
034   generating coherent text with extensive parameters. However, the growth in model sizes outpaces
035   hardware improvements, posing significant deployment and inference challenges (Steiner et al., 2023).
036   For example, operating OPT-175B (Zhang et al., 2022) requires over 320GB of memory and at least
037   five 80GB A100 GPUs for loading its parameters in FP16 precision. This challenge becomes more
038   pronounced in environments with limited resources, such as mobile devices, edge computing systems,
039   and real-time applications. Consequently, there has been considerable interest in compressing LLMs
040   to enhance their efficiency and practicality for deployment across various applications.
041   Pruning is a key method for compressing LLMs, aiming to eliminate redundant weights to reduce
042   model size and computational demands while striving to maintain performance. Methods such
043   as those in (Huang et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c) require a retraining phase
044   post-pruning, which is inefficient for billion-scale LLMs. PERP (Zimmer et al., 2023) introduces
045   an efficient retraining approach after pruning to recover the performance of pruned model. Recent
046   developments, including SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), employ
047
      post-training pruning techniques for LLMs without retraining. These methods, however, rely on
      the heuristic-based optimal brain surgeon (OBS) framework (Hassibi & Stork, 1992) or utilize
048
      heuristic-based pruning metrics to determine pruning masks, potentially compromising performance.
049
      DSnoT (Zhang et al., 2023d) introduces a training-free fine-tuning approach that updates the results
050   of other pruning methods, such as SparseGPT and Wanda, which also depend on heuristic-based
051   adjustment metrics.

053   unstructured pruning of LLMs. Figure 1 provides an overview of our method, which is applied to
      each linear operator. We employ the Frobenius norm of the difference between the outputs obtained

091   exploration into understanding sparsity in LLMs with the tools of convex optimization.

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
094
095
      Pruning of LLMs. Pruning is a widely used strategy to compress LLMs by generating sparse
096   weight matrices under unstructured, semi-structured, and structured sparsity based on calibration data.
097   Unstructured sparsity of rate s%, eliminates s% of the entries in a weight matrix. Semi-structured
098   sparsity with proportion n : m maintains a fixed overall sparsity level n/m, and allows at most
099   n non-zero entries in every group of m consecutive entries. Pruning weights into semi-structured
100   sparsity, especially with proportion 2:4, could yield up to 2× inference speedup using NVIDIA GPUs
101   with the Ampere architecture (Mishra et al., 2021) and hence is of particular interest. Structured
102   sparsity, which zeroes entire rows or columns, offers significant computational and memory benefits
103   but can lead to greater performance losses.
104   Pruning with Retraining. Traditional pruning pipelines often include a retraining step to offset
105   performance losses (Huang et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c). However, the sheer
106   scale of LLMs makes this additional retraining costly in terms of both time and computational
107   resources. Dinh et al. (2020); Holmes et al. (2021); Xie et al. (2023) integrate retraining directly into
      the pruning process by targeting the minimization of the highly non-convex loss function related


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to the calibration dataset, using the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to derive
109   pruned weights. Nonetheless, this approach imposes significant computational demands and the use
110   of ADMM in non-convex optimization often results in unstable performance (He & Yuan, 2012).
111
112
      Pruning without Retraining. Pruning without retraining offers a straightforward alternative, elimi-
      nating the need for post-pruning retraining. These methods prune LLMs in a single step, simplifying
113
      implementation and reducing both time and computational demands. Consequently, various methods
114
      have been developed under different sparsity frameworks. For structured pruning, SliceGPT (Ashk-
115   boos et al., 2024) and Eigenpruning (Vergara-Browne et al., 2024) utilize singular value decompo-
116   sitions to prune singular values of weight matrices and reduce model dimensions. ZipLM (Kurtić
117   et al., 2024) adopts an OBS-based approach for structured pruning and updates remaining weights
118   to maintain performance. Our proposed FISTAPruner focuses on unstructured and semi-structured
119   pruning, and thus is orthogonal to these structured pruning methods, enabling further model com-
120   pression. For unstructured and semi-structured pruning, SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and
121   ISC (Shao et al., 2024) leverage the OBS framework to calculate saliency for each entry using the
122   inverse Hessian of the loss metric, based on which pruning masks are generated and weights updated.
123
      Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) implements a heuristic approach, removing weights based on the product of
      their magnitudes and activations without compensation. DSnoT (Zhang et al., 2023d) updates the
124
      results of other pruning methods, such as SparseGPT and Wanda, which also relies on heuristic-based
125
      adjustment metrics. Boža (2024) employs ADMM to optimize weight updates under iteratively
126   refined pruning masks chosen through heuristic methods based on Wanda. These strategies adopt a
127   layer-wise pruning strategy, where errors between the pruned output and the original output of each
128   operator accumulates. Moreover, due to their heuristic nature, the performances of the pruned models
129   are unstable and compromised.
130
      Error Corrections. Error correction techniques are increasingly used to mitigate error accumulations
131
      from layer-wise pruning by minimizing reconstruction errors between the pruned network and
132   the original one (Park et al., 2024; El Halabi et al., 2022). However, their implementations and
133   applications to pruning LLMs vary widely. Prominent methods like SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh,
134   2023) focus on pruning without explicit error correction, while approaches like K-prune (Park
135   et al., 2024) minimize global reconstruction error, facing scalability challenges as globally correcting
136   pruning errors will require global sequential pruning. Our work introduces intra-layer error corrections
137   for better accuracy and computational efficiency. By focusing on intra-layer adjustments, our method
138   provides a scalable and effective solution for pruning LLMs.

140   3     M ETHODOLOGY
141
142   In this section, we introduce our post-training pruning method, FISTAPruner, which comprises three
143   main components. First, we address the error accumulation issue in layer-wise pruning with an
144   intra-layer error correction mechanism and develop a novel convex optimization model tailored
145   for this purpose. We then detail the process for unstructured pruning using FISTA and adapt the
146   framework for n : m semi-structured pruning. Finally, we present an adaptive method that finely
147   tunes the hyperparameter λ in our model to minimize the output discrepancies between dense and
148
      pruned operators while achieving the desired sparsity level.
149

237   3.3   E XTENSION TO 2:4 S EMI - STRUCTURED P RUNING
238
      While our convex optimization framework effectively addresses unstructured pruning, practical de-
239
      ployment often necessitates structured or semi-structured sparsity patterns to fully leverage hardware
240
      acceleration capabilities. One notable pattern is the 2:4 semi-structured sparsity, which is supported
241   by NVIDIA’s Ampere architecture (Mishra et al., 2021), enabling significant speedups in inference.
242
243
      The inclusion of the n : m sparsity constraint render the optimization problem non-convex due to
      the combinatorial nature of selecting which elements to prune within each group. To tackle this
244   challenge, we adopt FISTA updates, incorporating a hard thresholding step as follows:
245

      We acknowledge that the non-convex nature of this extension introduces complexities in theoretical

      3.4   A DAPTIVE H YPERPARAMETER T UNING
255
256   In Equation 3, the regularization parameter λ plays a pivotal role in balancing the trade-off between
257   the output error and the sparsity of the pruned weights W ∗ . A larger λ emphasizes sparsity, potentially
258   increasing the output error, while a smaller λ focuses on minimizing the output error, resulting in less
259   sparsity. To attain a specific desired sparsity level, it is essential to select an appropriate value of λ
260   that guides the optimization toward the target sparsity.
261   To automate the selection of λ, we propose employing an adaptive hyperparameter tuning mechanism
262   based on the bisection method. This method iteratively adjusts λ within a predefined interval [0, M ],
263   where M is a sufficiently large upper bound, to find the optimal value that yields the target sparsity
264   upon solving the optimization problem using FISTA. We establish theoretical guarantees for the
265   convergence of this method in the context of unstructured pruning, as stated in the following theorem:
266   Theorem 1. Define s(λ) as the function that maps the regularization parameter λ to the sparsity
267   level obtained after resolving the optimization problem. Let s denote the desired sparsity level.
268   The adaptive hyperparameter tuning mechanism leveraging the bisection method is guaranteed to
269   converge to a λ∗ such that the resultant sparsity level s(λ∗ ) satisfies the inequality |s(λ∗ ) − s| ≤ ϵ,

337   (Sakaguchi et al., 2021), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), RTE (Wang et al., 2018), QNLI (Wang et al.,
338
      2018), and WNLI (Wang et al., 2018) tasks, facilitated by the LM Harness library (Gao et al., 2021).
339   Baselines. We compare FISTAPruner against two state-of-the-art pruning methods: SparseGPT
340   (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2023). Additionally, we evaluate against the latest
341   training-free approach, DSnoT (Zhang et al., 2023d), which updates the results of other pruning
342   methods, and the recent efficient prune-retrain approach, PERP (Zimmer et al., 2023). We evaluate
343   two types of sparsity configurations: unstructured and 2:4 semi-structured sparsity.
344   Setup. We implement FISTAPruner using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and leverage the HuggingFace
345   Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2019) for model and dataset management. All pruning experiments
346   are conducted on NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each equipped with 80GB of memory. We observe that
347   FISTAPruner efficiently prunes all LLMs using a single GPU and no more than 40GB of memory.
348   For calibration data, we adhere to the approach outlined in previous works (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023;
349   Sun et al., 2023), utilizing 128 sequences. Each sequence is composed of tokens sampled from the
350
      first shard of the C4 dataset, with the number of tokens equal to the maximum embedding length of
      the LLMs. For parameters of FISTAPruner, we set the initial value of λ to 1 × 10−5 , K to 20, T to 3,
351
      M to 106 , and ξ to 0.3. For the OPT model family, we use the result of SparseGPT as a warm start
352
      for the FISTA iteration and set ϵ to 1 × 10−6 . For the LLaMA model family, we use the result of
353   Wanda as a warm start and set ϵ to 1 × 10−3 .

462   4.4                               A BLATION S TUDY ON I NTRA - LAYER E RROR C ORRECTIONS
463
464   We perform ablation studies on the OPT-125M model with 50% unstructured sparsity to evaluate
465   the intra-layer error correction mechanism. We compare the performance of FISTAPruner with
466   and without the intra-layer error correction mechanism, with results on the WikiText dataset dis-
467   played in Figure 4(a) (see results on PTB and C4 datasets in Appendix D.3). We observe that the
468
      perplexity of the pruned model incorporating this mechanism consistently outperforms the version
      without it, thereby confirming its effectiveness. Moreover, FISTAPruner, even without the intra-layer
469
      error correction mechanism, outperforms existing methods such as SparseGPT and Wanda. This
470
      underscores the effectiveness of applying convex optimization theory and algorithms to pruning
471   problems. Additionally, we treat each decoder layer as an independent pruning unit with intra-layer
472   error correction, rather than using both intra- and inter-layer error correction for a global mechanism,
473   for the following reasons: (1) Intra-layer error correction allows independent pruning of each decoder
474   layer, enabling distribution of the task across multiple devices and improving overall efficiency. (2)
475   While combining intra- and inter-layer error correction can reduce error accumulation, it is effective
476   only at low sparsity levels. At higher sparsity, global error correction dominates layer-specific pruning,
477   leading to worse performance. A detailed analysis of this is provided in Appendix E.

490
      Warm Start. Warm start is a widely recognized technique in optimization that leverages starting at a
      point near the optimal solution to significantly reduce the total convergence time. In our framework,
491
      we evaluate the efficiency of warm start mechanism as follows: Dense Weights < Magnitude Pruning
492
      ≈ Wanda < SparseGPT. Dense weights, though readily obtainable, slow down the convergence due
493   to their significant deviation from the target sparsity level. Magnitude pruning, involving absolute
494   value computations and comparisons, meets sparsity requirements but generally yields lower-quality
495   solutions. Wanda, requiring absolute value computations, ℓ2 -norm calculations of activation columns,
496   and element-wise multiplication, is nearly as efficient as magnitude pruning. This near parity in
497   efficiency is due to our model’s reliance on activation data from calibration, allowing ℓ2 -norm
498   computations to occur incidentally during the process. Despite their similar efficiencies, Wanda’s
499   solutions markedly outperform those from magnitude pruning. SparseGPT is less efficient compared
500   with magnitude pruning and Wanda but may provide a stronger initial point.
501   To further illustrate the impact of warm start on FISTAPruner, we conduct additional tests using both
502   dense weights and magnitude pruning results as starting points. The results are presented in Table 6,
503   which indicates that FISTAPruner still can achieve comparable results.

516   5    D ISCUSSION
517
518   Despite the rigorous theoretical foundation and impressive pruning performance of FISTAPruner, the
519
      time required for pruning remains a limitation of our method compared to SparseGPT and Wanda.
      This is primarily due to the iterative nature of FISTA and the process of tuning λ. Pruning time varies
520
      with model size; for instance, it takes about 10 minutes for OPT-125M, while LLaMA-3-70B requires
521
      approximately 12 hours on a single Nvidia A100 GPU with 40GB of memory. However, the parallel-
522   pruning capability of FISTAPruner, which allows for simultaneous pruning of multiple decoder layers
523   across various devices, can mitigate this issue to some extent. Furthermore, as post-training pruning is
524   typically an offline process, time sensitivity may not be a critical factor in real-world applications. In
525   addition, FISTAPruner represents an attempt to integrate convex optimization theory and algorithms
526   into LLM applications, potentially inspiring further advancements in this area.

      6    C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this paper, we introduce FISTAPruner, a layer-wise post-training pruning method for LLMs.
531   Initially, we develop a convex optimization model that employs the ℓ1 -norm to induce unstructured
532   sparsity in the weights, complemented by an intra-layer error correction mechanism to eliminate
533   cumulative errors across operators in the traditional pruning process. Subsequently, we utilize FISTA
534   to efficiently solve the proposed model. Additionally, we extend FISTAPruner to accommodate
535   n : m semi-structured pruning. FISTAPruner supports parallel pruning, which can reduce the
536   total pruning time by utilizing various devices simultaneously. Extensive experiments on the OPT,
537   LLaMA, LLaMA-2, and LLaMA-3 model families demonstrate FISTAPruner’s superior performance
538   compared to existing methods. We hope this work enhances understanding of sparsity in LLMs and
539
      inspires further integration of convex optimization within LLM applications.

      The function s(λ) is continuous because small changes in λ lead to small changes in the sparsity level
      due to the continuity of the optimization problem’s solution with respect to λ. Besides, s(λ) is non-
814
      decreasing because increasing λ enhances the sparsity-promoting effect of the ℓ1 -norm, potentially
815
      resulting in more zero entries in W ∗ . Therefore, s(λ) is a continuous and non-decreasing function of
816   λ over the interval [0, M ] for the bisection method. At λ = 0, the problem reduces to minimizing the
817   reconstruction error without regularization, yielding the least sparse solution (s(0) = 0% sparsity).
818   At a sufficiently large λ = M , the regularization term dominates, driving most weights to zero
819   (s(M ) = 100% sparsity). Since s(λ) is continuous and s(0) ≤ s ≤ s(M ), the Intermediate Value
820   Theorem guarantees the existence of λ∗ ∈ [0, M ] such that s(λ∗ ) = s. The bisection method
821   halves the interval [λmin , λmax ] in each iteration, ensuring convergence to λ∗ within a tolerance δ
822   after k ≥ log2 ( λmax −λ
                            δ
                              min
                                  ) iterations. Since s(λ) is continuous and non-decreasing, the sparsity level
823   sk achieved at each iteration converges to s. The bisection methods terminates when |sk − s| ≤ ϵ,
824   ensuring that the final λ∗ = λk yields a sparsity level within the desired tolerance.

936   We apply only the intra-layer error correction mechanism for two reasons:
937
938                           1. Parallelization: Intra-layer error correction enables independent pruning of each decoder

942                              tuitively reduce error accumulation across the network, we found that this approach is

944                              sparsity), global error correction tends to overshadow the pruning process of individual
945                              layers, ultimately leading to worse performance.

994    First, the results confirm the effectiveness of our intra-layer error correction mechanism, as it
995
       consistently outperforms the no-error-correction approach.
996    Second, the results confirm the effectiveness of using both intra- and inter-layer error correction at
997    low sparsity levels, as it consistently outperforms the intra-layer error correction alone at 5% and
998    10% sparsity.
999    Third, the results show that using both intra- and inter-layer error correction is sensitive to sparsity
1000   levels and tends to perform worse at higher sparsity. Specifically, at 20% sparsity, it underperforms
1001   compared to intra-layer error correction alone, and at 50% sparsity, it even performs worse than the
1002   no-error-correction approach.
1003
       To explain why the use of both intra- and inter-layer error correction is sensitive to sparsity levels, we
1004
       believe this occurs because higher sparsity levels make the pruning task more difficult, leading to
1005   greater error accumulation across layers. When both intra- and inter-layer error correction are applied,
1006   mitigating the accumulated error from previous layers may dominate the optimization objective in
1007   deeper layers, causing the pruning performance of the current layer to suffer.
