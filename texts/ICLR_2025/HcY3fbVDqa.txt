                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Non-parametric models are flexible and can leverage a context set to express rich
012
               mappings from inputs to outputs. However, these methods often scale super-linearly
013
               in context size, e.g., attention-based methods scale quadratically in the number
014            of data points, which in turn limits model expressivity. In this work, we lever-
015            age advances in state-space modeling and introduce Non-Parametric State Space
016            Models (NPSSM). We find that NPSSMs attain similar performance to existing
017            non-parametric attention-based models while scaling linearly in the number of
018            datapoints. We apply NPSSMs to the task of genotype imputation, where the linear
019            scaling enables larger context sets resulting in competitive performance relative to
020            other methods and widely used industry-standard tools. We also demonstrate the
021            effectiveness of NPSSMs in the context of meta-learning where the ability to effi-
022
               ciently scale to larger training sets provides more favorable compute-to-accuracy
               tradeoffs.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine learning (ML) models often benefit from having access external datasets at inference time.
029   In meta-learning, we often seek to condition the model on a previously unseen training set. In biology,
030   data can be aligned to known sequences which can be used to improve predictions. More generally,
031   non-parametric ML uses the training set at test time and includes both classical algorithms (e.g., k-
032
      nearest neighbors, support vector machines), as well as modern methods such as retrieval-augmented
      language models and non-parametric transformers.
033
034   However, non-parametric ML algorithms can often have high computal and memory overhead.
035   For example, while modern transformer-based methods can be used to attend to representations of
036   sequences or datapoints, their computational complexity grows quadratically in the size of their
037   context. This mirrors classical kernel methods that also have quadratic complexity. Addressing these
038
      computational requirements typically requires approximations that compress the context size, such as
      inducing point methods.
039
040   In sequence modeling, the quadratic computational complexity of transformers has motivated the
041   development of alternative architecture such as state-space models (SSMs). They have received
042   significant attention because of their ability to capture long context without the quadratic compute
043   cost of attention-based architectures.
044   This paper applies state-space models to non-parametric ML and leverages their long context to model
045   interactions between large sets of datapoints. Specifically, we introduce NP-SSMs, a supervised
046   learning architecture that takes as input a collection of labeled and unlabeled datapoints and produces
047   accurate predictions by querying the labeled data. Key to this approach are novel non-parametric
048   layer blocks that compute representations over datapoints using bidirectional SSMs. These blocks are
049   analogous to existing architectures that apply attention to datapoints or alignments.
050   We use the NP-SSM architecture to parameterize a probabilistic model p(x∥D) of data x conditioned
051   on a dataset D, which we call the SSM neural process. Like other types neural processes (NPs), these
052   can be trained using forms of maximum likelihood, as well as forms of meta-learning where datasets
053   are sampled from a meta-training set. Leveraging SSMs enables these NPs to handle much larger
      context sizes, which in turn can significantly improve their performance what more data is available.

      We apply our novel models and architectures across benchmarks in the neural process literature,
055   where we observe significant reductions in memory usage, as well as problems in biology, where
056   they outperform classical transformer-based approaches. Overall, we find that NP-SSMs can serve
057   as a drop-in replacement for many applications that are currently the domain of non-parametric
058   transformers or neural processes.
059
      Our main application area is genotype imputation in statistical genetics, where inputs consist of
060
      aligned sequences of genetic variants and our goal is to fill in missing positions obtained using an
061   inexpensive assay to match the performance of an accurate sequencing instrument. We find that
062   our method outperforms highly engineered state- of-the-art software packages widely used within
063   commercial genomics pipelines (Browning et al., 2018), indicating that our technique has the potential
064   to impact real-world systems.
065
      In summary, our contributions are:
066
067          • We develop an architecture for non-parametric machine learning called the non-parametric
068            state-space model, made possible by SSM blocks applied to tokens that represent datapoints.
069
             • We combine this architecture with training objectives that maximize expected log-likelihood
070
               across datasets, and that are naturally suited for meta-learning.
071
072          • We apply our model to genotype imputation, an important problem in statistical genetics, and
073
               we show that by leveraging large sets of alignments, our approach obtains state-of-the-art
               performance and outperforms specialized software packages.

078   2.1   N ON -PARAMETRIC G ENERATIVE M ODELS
079
080   Most supervised models are parametric: given a dataset Dtrain = {x(i) , y (i) }ni=1 , with input features
081   x ∈ X and labels y ∈ Y, the goal is to learn a set of parameters θ ∈ Θ that yield an accurate
082
      predictive model f (x; θ). However, modern parametric models can have up to hundreds of billions
      of parameters, making training and prediction computationally expensive. Non-parametric or semi-
083
      parametric models of the form y = f (x∥Dtrain ; θ) leverage the training dataset Dtrain at inference
084
      time to reduce costs by retrieving information from Dtrain instead of having to compress Dtrain into
085   parameters θ. Multiple deep learning architectures fall under the non-parametric/semi-parametric
086   archetype, including memory-augmented architectures (Graves et al., 2014; Santoro et al., 2016),
087   retrieval based language models (Grave et al., 2016; Guu et al., 2020; Rae et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023),
088   models utilizing Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), and non-parametric
089   transformers (Kossen et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2021; Notin et al., 2023b).
090
      However, the flexibility of non-parametric models comes with a different computational cost, as most
091   models either scale superlinearly in the size of the context or require heuristics to make handling the
092   context set tractable.
093

106
107   Most existing deep non-parametric architectures use utilize some form of attention (Vaswani et al.,
      2017), causing those architectures to scale quadratically in the length of the context set. In practice,

      this quadratic scaling often limits the usability of these architectures and necessitates some sort of
109   approximate/sparse attention or some other heuristic to mitigate this scaling (Zaheer et al., 2021;
110   Wang et al., 2020; Jaegle et al., 2021; Rastogi et al., 2023) .
111
112
      State Space Models (SSM)s are a class of sequence models that have recently begun gaining popularity
      due to their performance on sequence modeling and their favorable performance characteristics (Gu

114

115   that maintains most of the performance of traditional self attention for most tasks, but with linear
116   scaling in the length of the sequence (Gu & Dao, 2024).

      2.4   G ENOTYPE I MPUTATION
119
120   Our specific motivating example is genotype imputation. Human genetic sequences can be thought
121   of as a sequence y ∈ {A, C, T, G}n . While there exist methods to read the entire sequence y,
122   these methods can be costly. Instead, we can consider reading some smaller sub-sequence x ∈
123   {A, C, T, G}t where t << n. If we leverage some statistical properties of the genome, we can
124   construct x so that we can use it to reconstruct the full sequence y while only reading t positions.
125   This problem of reconstructing the full y given some subset x is called genotype imputation. We use a
126
      relatively cheap device called a microarray to obtain a set x for some individual, and then using some
127
      statistical methods we reconstruct the full y given x and some context set Href = {(x(i) , y (i) )}ki=1
      that contains the full sequences for k individuals (Li et al., 2009). Existing methods for genotype
128
      imputation are all based on an HMM from Li & Stephens (2003), they leverage the fact that due to
129
      recombination, the full sequence y can be reconstructed as a mosaic of the y (i) s in Href . Therefore
130
      the problem of imputing y becomes finding the most likely path through Href given the observed sub
131   sequence x. Given how y is represented as a mix between the k sequences in Href , increasing the
132   number of individuals k in Href tends to subsequently increase performance. Existing methods like
133   Beagle and Impute (Browning et al., 2018; Rubinacci et al., 2020) scale quadratically in k, limiting
134   performance and necessitating workarounds.
135
      Non-parametric approaches provide a compelling case for use in genotype imputation. They admit
136
      a meta learning approach that fits some function of the form y = f (x; Href ) that can be trained on
137   multiple different (x, y, Href ) tuples. By included (x, y) pairs trained on different microarrays with
138   different typed positions, and with different compositions of Href , we can define a model that can be
139   applied to any arbitrary region of the genome for any microarray. However, existing non-parametric
140   models often utilize an attention operation that is also quadratic in k, running into the same bottleneck
141   that the existing HMM methods encounter. In this work, we propose to utilize State Space Models in
142   lieu of attention operations, giving us the capability to scale linearly in k while retaining performance,
143   allowing for increased performance due to the ability to scale to larger values of k.

145   3     N ON -PARAMETRIC S TATE S PACE M ODELS
146
147   This work applies advances in long-context state space models to non-parametric machine learning.
148   Specifically, we propose an architecture and associated training objectives that take as input a large
149   dataset and output a dataset representation that is useful for tasks such as prediction and imputation.
150
151   Notation and Inputs Given a training set Dtrain = {x(i) , y (i) }ni=1 , we seek to make an accurate
152   prediction at a new datapoint xquery using a model f (xquery , Dtrain ) that has access to Dtrain at inference
153   time. The dataset is composed of n datapoints x(i) ∈ Ra , each with a attributes and l labels y (i) ∈ Rl .
154   Our proposed models operate over an embedding X ∈ Rn×(a+l)×d of the training set, obtained by
155   projecting each label and attribute into a dense d-dimensional embedding.
156
157   High-Level Overview We introduce the Non-Parametric State Space Model (NPSSM), an architec-
158   ture that produces a distributed representation of the training data that is useful for downstream tasks.
159   Our method starts with an initial embedding X of a dataset and iteratively updates this representation
160   by applying a sequence of alternating SSM layers across the datapoints and across the attributes.
161   These SSM layers are analogous to attention mechanisms over datapoints and attributes used by the
      Axial Transformer and subsequent work (Ho et al., 2019; Kossen et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2021), but


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      are more computationally efficient and support longer contexts. These alternating layers allow the
163   model to capture relationships between the attributes for each datapoint, and relationships between
164   the datapoints along a given attribute. For notational simplicity, we will assume that xquery has been
165   concatenated to X and we will omit the batch dimension.
166
      The model is trained by masking a percentage of X and then reconstructing the masked elements by
167
      optimizing the negative log-liklihood. This effectively defines a probabilistic model p(x∥Dtrain ).
168

171
      Each NPSSM layer takes as input an embedding X of Dtrain and returns X̂ ∈ Rn×(a+l)×d . A layer
172   has two components: an SSM along attributes (SSMattr ) and a SSM along datapoints (SSMdata ).
173

176   Attribute-Level State-Space Layers To model the interactions between individual attributes and
177   labels, we apply a state-space model along the attributes and labels of each individual datapoint within
178   each dataset, SSMattr : Rn×(a+l)×d 7→ Rn×(l+a)×d . The SSMattr layer can be instantiated with any
179   arbitrary SSM; in this work we used a bidirectional version of Mamba (BiMamba) from Schiff et al.
180   (2024). To achieve bidirectionality we apply a Mamba operator along the length dimension of X, and
181   we then apply a Mamba operator on X reversed along the length dimension. Let reverse(M, dim)
182
      reverse matrix M along dimension dim; we then write:
183                     SSMattr (X) = BiMamba(X)

185
186   As in Schiff et al. (2024), the ”forward” and ”reverse” Mamba operators share the parameters of the
187   input and output projection matrices to reduce the total number of trainable parameters.
188
189   Datapoint-Level State-Space Layers To model the interactions between individual datapoints,
190   we apply a SSM along the attribute dimension to model the interactions between each datapoint at
191   every attribute. Note that this process is equivalent to applying an SSMattr layer, but permuting the
192   datapoint (n) and attribute (a + l) dimensions of X to form matrix X′ ∈ R(a+l)×n×d .

197   Non-Parametric State Space Models A single layer of a NPSSM is composed of a Attribute Level
198
      State Space Layer (SSMattr ) and a Datapoint Level State Space (SSMdata ) layer applied consecutively
      as described in Algorithm 1. A full NPSSM model is composed of multiple of these layers chained
199
      together, trained on a MLM objective LMLM to reconstruct the input X with an optional auxilliary
200
      supervised loss (Laux ) computed on a subset of labeled datapoints in the context set.

212   Supervised Learning Architecture For supervised learning problems, we have an input xquery
213   where the l labels are masked out but the a attributes remain unmasked, in addition to the masked
214   embedded matrix X. The input xquery is concatenated to X and an additional loss Laux is calculated
215   on the l labels, for example a BCE loss, L2 loss, etc. The full loss is therefore Ltotal = (1 − λ)LMLM +
      λLaux for some weight λ (see below).

      Multiple Sequence Alignment State Space Models NPSSMs lend themselves naturally to Multiple
217   Sequence Alignments (MSA)s. Each sequence is an individual datapoint and each attribute/token is
218   already aligned. Each sequence may have any labels or additional attributes appended at the end of

      3.2   T RAINING
222
223   Supervised Learning The NPSSM model is trained optimizing the weighted sum of an MLM
224   and supervised objective: Ltotal = (1 − λ)LMLM + λLaux for some weight λ. To compute the
225   reconstruction loss LMLM , we mask a portion of all the elements of X (10% for imputation). For
226   example, for discrete data, we compute the cross-entropy loss of the predicted tokens with the true
227   token in X. More generally, we can append to the NPSSM a final layer that predicts the parameters
228   of a probability distribution over X or over xquery ; this defines a probabilistic model p(xquery |X) that
229   is naturally amenable to maximum-likelihood training.
230   The auxiliary loss Laux is computed based on the masked labels of xquery and a subset of datapoints
231   in X that have the labels masked. For our imputation experiments, the auxiliary loss is a weighted
232   version of the cross entropy loss.
233
234   Meta-Learning For meta-learning, we want to learn some function yquery = f (xquery ; Dtrain ) that
235   generates yquery given an input xquery and a context dataset Dtrain . Models like NPSSM lend themselves
236                                                                                                    (i)
      to this application. To train a model for this setting, we first construct a meta-learning set {Dtrain }H
                                                                                                              i=1 .
237   In our motivating example of genotype imputation, each individual dataset corresponds to a different
238   region of the genome, and the model is trained to be able to impute arbitrary regions of the genome,
239   even for samples regions that were not seen during training.

278   4.2     G ENOTYPE I MPUTATION
279
      Genotype imputation is an important component of many genomic analysis and a natural application
280
      of meta-learning. Given some binary observed variants xquery = (x1 , . . . , xt ) and unobserved variants
281   yquery = (y1 , . . . , yu ), the goal is to predict all u unobserved variants using the t observed variants.
282   Generally, the observed variants x are obtained by genotypying an individual using a relatively cheap
283   DNA microarray at relatively few positions, that is t << u. The current methods for genotype
284   imputation all follow the same general setup, we assume that at inference time we have access to some
285   reference set Href ∈ 1k×(u+t) that contains k fully sequenced individuals and some new individual
286   xquery that is only genotyped at some subset of variants of size t.
287
      To solve this problem, we want some function yquery = f (xquery ; Href ). The current approaches are
288   fully non-parametric, they assume that xquery is a mosaic of the samples in Href , so the problem can
289   be solved by finding the most probable path through the samples in Href that result in xquery using an
290   HMM from Li & Stephens (2003). This setup lends itself naturally to meta-learning, in practice Href
291   contains an arbitrary number of haplotypes and Href covers an arbitrary section of the genome. By
292   training over a meta training set consisting of a variety of Href ’s, we can train a model that should
293   work for any arbitrary (xquery , Href ) pair like existing methods do.
294

296   dataset (The 1000 Genomes Project Consortium, 2015) into a training set with 4388 sequences
297
      (haplotypes), a validation set with 516 haplotypes, and a test set on 104 haplotypes. We consider
      chromosome 20, and use the IlluminaOmiExpress-24 microarry to define the typed and untyped
298
      variants. We subset chr20 to a region of 9159 contiguous untyped variants (∼ 1.3% of chr20) into
299
      92 blocks with 100 untyped variants and the closest 200 typed variants per block. Each datapoint
300   (xquery , yquery ) is a single block for one of the samples in the 4388 training sequences, leading to
301   92 × 4388 = 403696 (xquery , yquery ) data points. Each xquery has an associated context set Href
302   composed of k ≤ 4387 datapoints (xquery , yquery ) from the training set on that same block. We
303   subset each Href to the k closest haplotypes by L1 distance on xquery for each block. This results in a
304                                                                                        (0)    (0)
      meta-dataset of 403696 datasets where each individual dataset is composed of (xquery , yquery , Href =
305       (i)     (i))                                                                                   (0)
      {(xquery , yquery }ki=1 ) for the k datapoints on the same block with the smallest L1 distance to xquery .

307   no haplotypes from the validation or test set ever appear in Href . We compare to two industry

517   5   D ISCUSSION AND R ELATED W ORK
518
519   Comparisons to NPTs NPSSMs are generally a drop in replacement for models like MSA-
520   Transformer or Non Parametric Transformer (Rao et al., 2021; Kossen et al., 2021; Notin et al.,
521   2023b). Each of these methods make similar assumptions on the structure of the input data, therefore
522   they can be applied to similar problems. However there are still some capabilities of attention
523   based models that do not have an analogue in SSMs, for example Rao et al. (2021) used attention
524
      maps for contact predictions, and they had success with forcing each datapoint to share the same
      attention matrix (tied row attention). While NPSSMs have shown competitive results with much
525
      better scaling on the genotype imputation task, there are certain scenarios like these where SSM
526
      based non-parametric models are not equivalent out of the box.
527
528
      Comparisons to HMMs Models like Beagle and Impute (Browning et al., 2018; Rubinacci et al.,
529
      2020) that are based off of the Li & Stephens (2003) HMM have been the standard for genotype
530   imputation. However, Non-parametric models like NPSSM are now capable of matching or exceeding
531   their performance, even on a whole chromosome (Table 3) due to their favorable scaling properties.
532   In addition, existing HMM imputation methods are restrictive. Adding in additional auxiliary for
533   imputation like Notin et al. (2023b) did for proteins is difficult for HMMs, but when using a non-
534   parametric model it could be as simple as adding some additional columns to X and adjusting
535   Laux .
536
537   Conclusion We present NPSSMs, a drop in replacement for existing attention based non-parametric
538   architectures that scale linearly in the size of the context set. We show that on genotype imputation, we
539   are able to outperform existing models (Table 2), and that a large contributing factor to performance
      is the size of the context set we are able to accommodate (Figure 2).
