009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             We investigate the relationship between the geometry of token embeddings and
012             their role in next token prediction within transformer models. Toward this goal,
013             previous studies have utilized metrics such as intrinsic dimension and neighbor-
014             hood overlap to probe the geometry of internal representations, where prompts are
015             summarized as a single point in representation space. We expand single points to
016             point clouds by investigating how models geometrically distribute tokens in their
017             internal representations. We measure the intrinsic dimension, neighborhood over-
018
                lap, and cosine similarity on these point clouds for a large number of prompts.
                To validate our approach, we compare these metrics to a dataset where the tokens
019
                are shuffled, which disrupts the syntactic and semantic structure. Our analysis re-
020
                veals a correlation between the geometric properties of token embeddings and the
021             cross-entropy loss of next token predictions, implying that prompts with higher
022             loss values have tokens represented in higher-dimensional spaces.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In the context of interpretability of transformer models, a set of analytic approaches have been
028   developed with the goal of modeling transformer architectures as dynamical systems of particles
029   (Vuckovic et al., 2020; Geshkovski et al., 2024b; Cowsik et al., 2024). In this perspective, the
030   transformers are viewed as evolving a mean-field interacting particle system where the evolution of
031   tokens across layers is controlled by their empirical measure1 (Agrachev & Letrouit, 2024). Under a
032   set of assumptions such as time-independent weights, this interpretation is used to show that tokens
033   tend to cluster in the later layers (Geshkovski et al., 2023). This clustering behavior can be associated
034   with the empirically observed rank collapse phenomenon in transformer models (Anagnostidis et al.,
035
      2022; Shi et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
036   An important insight from Geshkovski et al. (2024b) in the context of next token prediction is that
037   the output measure of tokens encodes the probability distribution of the next token, and its clustering
038   indicates a small number of possible outcomes. A complementary perspective to the evolution of
039   token representations across layers can be gained by studying the latent predictions of transformer
040   models (Belrose et al., 2023) from the perspective of iterative inference Jastrzebski et al. (2018)
041
      which indicates that the probabilities of the next tokens are incrementally updated layer by layer.
      The work by nostalgebraist (2020) suggests that causal LLMs appear to develop a reasonably accu-
042
      rate prediction regarding the next token in the middle layers, with subsequent layers refining these
043
      predictions. This means we should expect the empirical measures of the internal layers to reflect this
044   trend, i.e. a rapid change of the empirical measure in the early layers and a more refined change to-
045   wards the later layers. Since the latent predictions are obtained by unembedding the residual stream
046   (Elhage et al., 2021), and our methods understand the geometric properties of the residual stream, we
047   can expect the statistical properties (eg. entropy) of the latent prediction probabilities to be encoded
048   in the geometry of the internal representations of the tokens.
049
      In this work, we combine these viewpoints to examine the empirical measure of the internal layers
050   from a geometric perspective. To observationally probe the empirical measure, we draw inspiration
051   from previous works using intrinsic dimension and neighbourhood overlap to study the geometry
052

060
      To test how the geometric properties of token representations change as a function of the model’s
061   internal dynamics, we probe it in a regime where the syntactic and semantic structures of the prompts
062   are disrupted through systematic token shuffling. Our analysis achieves these main results:
063
064          • Token-Level Intrinsic Dimension and Cosine Similarity: We observe that the intrinsic
065            dimension (ID) of token representations generally exhibits a peak, whose height increases
066            with the degree of token shuffling. This peak is located at early to middle layers of the mod-
067
               els. On the other hand, cosine similarity among tokens increases with shuffling, indicating
               increased alignment of token vectors.
068
069          • Neighborhood Overlap Consistency: The neighborhood overlap (NO) metric shows that
070
               token relationships around the ID peak become less consistent as the amount of shuffling
               increases. This highlights that structured data retains more coherent token neighborhoods
071
               through the model layers compared to shuffled data.
072
073          • Correlation with Model Loss: We find a statistical relation between the geometry of to-
074
               kens and the probability distribution of the next token: the intrinsic dimension of the token
               representations across hidden layers is correlated to the average cross-entropy loss of the
075
               next token probability distribution for a given prompt. This suggests that prompts with a
076
               higher cross-entropy loss have token representations lying in higher dimensional manifolds.

079   2   R ELATED WORK
080
081   Broader context of mechanistic interpretability in transformers. Mechanistic interpretability in
082
      transformers explores how transformer models encode and utilize information, focusing on seman-
      tic and algorithmic interpretations. Semantic interpretation investigates what latent properties are
083
      learned by models and how individual neurons may code for specific concepts (Hamrick & Mo-
084
      hamed, 2020). Structural probing (Rogers et al., 2020; Belinkov, 2022; Belinkov et al., 2020) and
085   dictionary learning (Lewicki & Sejnowski, 2000; Lee et al., 2006; Faruqui et al., 2015) offer in-
086   sights into how features are represented and reconstructed in transformer architectures. Relevant
087   to this work, is the approach of the logit lens (nostalgebraist, 2020). This method offers insight
088   into a model’s predictive process by applying the final classification layer, which converts the resid-
089   ual stream activation into logits/vocabulary space, to intermediate residual stream activations. This
090   reveals how prediction confidence evolves throughout the computational stages. This is feasible be-
091   cause transformers typically construct their predictions iteratively across layers (Geva et al., 2022).
092   Building on this concept, the tuned lens (Belrose et al., 2023) employs affine probes to translate in-
093
      ternal representations into probability distributions over the vocabulary. Similarly, the Future Lens
      (Pal et al., 2023) examines how individual representations encode information about forthcoming
094
      tokens.
095
096   Analytic Approaches to Transformer Models Recent analytical works (Geshkovski et al., 2023;
097   Cowsik et al., 2024) indicate that analyzing geometric properties of token representations and their
098   dynamics can offer meaningful insights into how transformers function. Geshkovski et al. (2023)
099
      introduced the novel perspective of viewing the evolution of tokens in the transformer layers as
      particles in a dynamical system. They predict clustering behavior in transformer models in a simpli-
100
      fied setting which was later extended to include causally masked attention (Karagodin et al., 2024).
101
      (Cowsik et al., 2024) adopts the above perspective and examines particle geometry in the presence
102   of MLP layers. This perspective not only offers insights into the geometric dynamics of tokens but
103   also addresses the trainability of transformers based on initialization hyperparameters, including the
104   strength of attentional and MLP residual connections. Further studies (Geshkovski et al., 2024c;a)
105   theoretically investigate the expressive power of transformers as maps from arbitrary input measures
106   to output measures and prove the appearance of dynamic metastability, i.e. the particles cluster in
107   the infinite time limit but they resemble a configuration of several clusters for a long period of time.
      This behavior aligns more closely with practical observations than the clustering dynamics. This

      analytical framework highlights the significance of studying the distribution of the internal repre-
109   sentations of the tokens (referred to as the empirical measure) by i) suggesting a relation between

111   the role of the empirical measure in governing the token dynamics (Agrachev & Letrouit, 2024).

      Geometric Approaches to Transformer Models. The manifold hypothesis posits that real-
113
      world high-dimensional data often lie on or near a lower-dimensional manifold within the high-
114
      dimensional space (Goodfellow et al., 2016). The dimension of this approximating manifold is usu-
115   ally named the intrinsic dimension of the data. Several studies have demonstrated that the intrinsic
116   dimension of data representations in deep networks shows a remarkable dynamic range, character-
117   ized by distinct phases of expansion and contraction (Ansuini et al., 2019; Doimo et al., 2020a; Pope
118   et al., 2021). Data manifolds created by internal representations in deep networks have been also
119   explored from the perspective of neuroscience and statistical mechanics (Chung et al., 2018; Cohen
120   et al., 2020). In LLMs, a geometric analysis of representations has uncovered a rich set of phenom-
121   ena. Geometric properties, such as intrinsic dimension and the composition of nearest neighbors,
122   evolve throughout the network’s sequence of internal layers. These changes mark distinct phases in
123
      the model’s operation, signal the localization of semantic information (Valeriani et al., 2023; Cheng
      et al., 2023). While the aforementioned works analyze internal representations in linguistic process-
124
      ing, the geometry of context embeddings has been linked to language statistics (Zhao et al., 2024)
125
      and used to highlight differences between real and artificial data (Tulchinskii et al., 2023).

247   Evaluating geometric observables at the token level directly probes the model’s internal dynamics.
248   As a way of quantifying geometric changes, we compare in-distribution data to various levels of
249   token shuffling. By progressively disrupting the syntactic and semantic structure while preserving
250   unigram frequency distribution, we observe the incremental effects on our observables across layers.

252   tokens, X = {xi }i∈[N ] , we split the sequence into nBlocks blocks of size B such that nBlocks ×
253   B = N and take one random permutation of the blocks, as schematically presented in Figure 1.
254   Note that the shuffle index for the fully shuffled case (Ŝ) corresponds to the value of S when the
255   number of tokens N = 4Ŝ . In Figure 1, we have Ŝ = 2 since we consider 16 tokens, whereas in the
256   experiments, we have Ŝ = 5 because we have 1024 = 45 tokens.

      4.3   C OMPARING TOKEN GEOMETRY OF PROMPTS IN DIFFERENT MODELS
401
402   In the previous section, we noted that the geometry of internal representations is highly sensitive
403   to shuffled inputs. Having focused on the representations from L LAMA model, we now extend our
404   analysis to include two additional models: M ISTRAL and P YTHIA. As described in Section 4.1,
405   we note that P YTHIA was trained entirely on the Pile dataset. Hence, the dataset we consider for
406   experiments, Pile-10K, is a subset of the same dataset on which P YTHIA was trained. While we do
407   not know on which datasets L LAMA and M ISTRAL were trained, we can assume that, if present,
408   Pile was not the only dataset used. Therefore, we might expect P YTHIA to have a mildly different

507   5   C ONCLUSIONS
508
509
      The primary aim of this study was to connect different approaches to the interpretability of LLMs.
510
      Our strategy towards this goal was to examine the geometric structure of token-level representations
511   across the layers of these models and to connect it to the probability distribution of the next token
512   prediction. We employed three key metrics: cosine similarity, intrinsic dimension, and neighbor-
513   hood overlap, to capture different aspects of this geometric structure. Our findings revealed that the
514   intrinsic dimension of token representations peaks in the early to middle layers, with higher peaks
515   in shuffled data, i.e. when syntactic and semantic structures are disrupted. Additionally, cosine
516   similarity among tokens increases with shuffling, suggesting greater alignment of token vectors.
517   The neighborhood overlap metric showed that structured data maintains more coherent token neigh-
518   borhoods across layers, while increased shuffling reduces this consistency, reflecting the model’s
519
      sensitivity to the input structure. We observe these features consistently across different models. All
      these analyses converge into the key finding of this paper, which is the correlation of the ID of token
520
      representations to the model’s loss, implying that ID could be an important metric for evaluating
521
      model performance across different models.
522
523   This correlation should be notably significant during the training process. As demonstrated at the
524   prompt level in previous research Cheng et al. (2024), and confirmed by our findings at the token
525
      level, see Appendix E, ID remains largely constant and low in the early training stages and is not
      correlated with loss, but it increases as training progresses. At the token level, we observe that the
526
      ID tends to rise due to enhanced model expressivity, while there is also a tendency for ID to decline
527
      as the minimization of loss improves. Indeed, as seen in Figure 19, ID initially rises and then shows
528   a slight decrease after checkpoint 64K. We believe it would be intriguing to explore these aspects in
529   greater depth, but we defer this investigation to future work.
530
531
      Experiments could be improved in several directions: first, we computed our observables at low
      ranges of nearest neighbors. For a more holistic approach, a multiscale analysis can reveal further
532
      relations among these observables. Secondly, the differences in distribution patterns for structured
533
      versus shuffled data, as suggested by cosine similarity and ID studies, might encode essential infor-
534   mation on how tokens are distributed in space in the two cases. It is interesting to consider other
535   geometric observables and understand their relation to the next token probabilities. These targeted
536   explorations could provide practical applications for the design and training of LLMs, potentially
537   leading to more interpretable and efficient models. While we show that the geometry of tokens en-
538   codes the next token prediction loss, we also potentially provide an unsupervised tool to interpret
539   how the model processes a given prompt. An interesting avenue in this regard can be a more in-depth
      analysis of the lower ID peak in P YTHIA which we reserve for future work.

742   A    H OW IS TOKEN GEOMETRY DIFFERENT FROM PROMPT GEOMETRY ?
743
744   Previous work Ansuini et al. (2019); Doimo et al. (2020a); Pope et al. (2021); Valeriani et al. (2023);
745   Cheng et al. (2023; 2024); Cheng & Antonello (2024) have studied internal representations from a
746   geometric point of view by considering point clouds of last token representations as observable.
747   While the approach is similar in spirit, token-level and prompt-level measures of intrinsic dimension
748   probe different manifolds and thus different features of LLMs.
749
      While prompt-level and token-level ID profiles exhibit similar behavior qualitatively, e.g. they peak
750   in early-middle layers, there is a notable difference in the shuffled and unshuffled prompts. At
751   the prompt level, we see that the unshuffled ID has a more prominent peak than the shuffled ID,
752   whereas it is the other way round at the token level. This difference between token and prompt level
753   ID curves offers a window to understand the difference between the token and prompt geometries.
754   The core reason for this diverging behavior is that we are looking at different manifolds and thus
755   observing two distinct behaviors: token level ID is correlated to the input perplexity (measured using
      the cross-entropy loss) and the prompt level ID is a measure of the semantic information Valeriani


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2024). Given a dataset of the prompts with a high perplexity at the token
757   level such as in the shuffled case, we can expect the last token representations are less likely to share
758   semantic content, leading to a lower intrinsic dimension at the prompt level. At the token level, the
759   lesser prominence of the peak of the unshuffled case can be explained using the ID loss correlation.
760   Since the loss is expected to be lower for the unshuffled prompts, we can expect their ID peak to be
761   less prominent than that of the shuffled prompts. In the following section, we examine the prompt-
762   level and token-level geometries for both shuffled and unshuffled cases at the peak layer, where the
763   differences are most pronounced.
764   Token and prompt geometries of shuffled and unshuffled prompts at layer 11
765
