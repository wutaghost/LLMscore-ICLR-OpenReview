009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The advancement of autonomous driving is increasingly reliant on high-quality
012            annotated datasets, especially in the task of 3D occupancy prediction, where the
013            occupancy labels require dense 3D annotation with significant human effort. In
014            this paper, we propose SytheOcc, which denotes a diffusion model that Synthesize
015            photorealistic and geometric-controlled images by conditioning Occupancy labels
016            in driving scenarios. This yields an unlimited amount of diverse, annotated, and
017            controllable datasets for applications like training perception models and simula-
018            tion. SyntheOcc addresses the critical challenge of how to efficiently encode 3D
019
               geometric information as conditional input to a 2D diffusion model. Our approach
               innovatively incorporates 3D semantic multi-plane images (MPIs) to provide com-
020
               prehensive and spatially aligned 3D scene descriptions for conditioning. As a
021
               result, SyntheOcc can generate photorealistic multi-view images and videos that
022            faithfully align with the given geometric labels (semantics in 3D voxel space).
023            Extensive qualitative and quantitative evaluations of SyntheOcc on the nuScenes
024            dataset prove its effectiveness in generating controllable occupancy datasets that
025            serve as an effective data augmentation to perception models.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      With the rapid development of generative models, they have shown realistic image synthesis and
031   diverse controllability. This progress has opened up new avenues for dataset generation in autonomous
032   driving (Gao et al., 2024; Swerdlow et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a). The task of dataset
033   generation is usually modeled as controllable image generation, where the ground truth (e.g. 3D Box)
034   is employed to control the generation of new datasets in downstream tasks (e.g. 3D detection). This
035   approach helps to mitigate the data collection and annotation effort as it can generate labeled data for
036   free. However, a novel task of vital importance, occupancy prediction (Wang et al., 2023b; Tian et al.,
037   2024), poses new challenges for dataset generation compared with 3D detection. It requires finer and
038   more nuanced geometry controllability, which refers to use the occupancy state and semantics of
039
      voxels in the whole 3D space to control the image generation. We argue that solving this problem not
      only allows us to synthesize occupancy datasets, but also empowers valuable applications such as
040
      editing geometry to generate rare data for corner case evaluation, as shown in Fig. 1. In the following,
041
      we first illustrate why prior work struggles to achieve the above objective, and then demonstrate how
042   we address these challenges.
043
044   In the area of diffusion models, several representative works have displayed high-quality image
045
      synthesis; however, they are constrained by limited 3D controllability: they are incapable of editing
      3D voxels for precise control. For example, BEVGen (Swerdlow et al., 2024) generates street
046
      view images by conditioning BEV layouts using diffusion models. MagicDrive (Gao et al., 2024)
047
      extend BEVGen and additionally converts the 3D box parameters into text embedding through
048   Fourier mapping that is similar to NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020), and uses cross-attention to learn
049   conditional generation. Although these methods achieve satisfactory results in image generation, their
050   3D controllability is inherently limited. These approaches are restricted to manipulating the scene in
051   types of 3D boxes and BEV layouts, and hardly adapt to finer geometry control such as editing the
052   shape of objects and scenes. Meanwhile, they usually convert conditional input into 1D embedding
053   that aligns with prompt embedding, which is less effective in 3D-aware generation due to lack of
      spatial alignment with the generated images. This limitation hinders their utility in downstream

105   2.1      3D O CCUPANCY P REDICTION
106
107   The task of 3D occupancy prediction aims to predict the occupancy status of each voxel in 3D
      space, as well as its semantic label if occupied. Compared with previous perception methods like


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      3D object detection, occupancy prediction offers a more detailed and nuanced understanding of the
109   environment, as it provides finer geometric details, is capable of handling general, out-of-vocabulary
110   objects, and finally, enriches the planning stack with comprehensive 3D information. Early methods
111   exploited LiDAR as inputs to complete the 3D occupancy of the entire 3D scene (Yan et al., 2021;
112   Mei et al., 2023). Recent methods began to explore the more challenging vision-based 3D occupancy
113   prediction (Wang et al., 2023b; Tian et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023). By predicting
114   the geometric and semantic properties of both dynamic and static elements, 3D occupancy prediction
115   offers a more comprehensive understanding of the surrounding environment.
116
117   2.2   D IFFUSION - BASED I MAGE G ENERATION
118
      Recent advancements in diffusion models (DMs) have achieved remarkable progress in image
119
      generation. In particular, Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022) employs DMs within the
120
      latent space of autoencoders, striking a balance between computational efficiency and high image
121   quality. Beyond text control, there is also the introduction of additional control signals. A noteworthy
122   work is ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), which incorporates a trainable copy of the SD encoder to
123   extract the feature of conditional images and adds it to the UNet feature. It significantly enhances
124   the controllability and unlocking pathways for advanced applications. We refer readers to recent
125   survey (Yang et al., 2023b) for more details.
126
127   2.3   I MAGE G ENERATION IN AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
128
129   As training neural networks relies heavily on labeled data, numerous studies are delving into dataset
130
      generation to boost training. Lift3D (Li et al., 2023a) designs generative NeRF to synthesize labeled
      datasets for 3D detection for the first time. Several other works employ BEV layouts to synthesize
131
      image data, proving beneficial for perception models. For example, BEVGen (Swerdlow et al., 2024)
132
      conditions BEV layouts to generate multi-view street images, while BEVControl (Yang et al., 2023a)
133   separately generates foregrounds and backgrounds from BEV layouts. MagicDrive (Gao et al., 2024)
134   generates images with 3D geometry controls by independently encoding objects and maps through a
135   text encoder or map encoder. Compared with MagicDrive, our geometry control is characterized by a
136   more detailed and lossless representation of 3D scenes for control, which poses significant challenges
137   than projected layout or box embedding.
138
      Recently, DriveDreamer (Wang et al., 2023a), DrivingDiffusion (Li et al., 2023b), Drive-WM (Wang
139   et al., 2023c) and Panacea (Wen et al., 2023) use a ControlNet framework, which involves projecting
140   bounding boxes and road maps onto 2D FoV images as a conditioning input. This approach has
141   proven to be effective for geometric control. However, it is limited in that it only achieves alignment
142   at the 2D-pixel level. Consequently, this method falls short in capturing the depth hierarchy and
143   fails to account for the occlusion relationships present in the 3D real world. Besides, adding a depth
144   channel like Panacea (Wen et al., 2023) may address the limitations of depth order, but it discards
145   the occluded part and only contains partial observation. UrbanGiraffe (Yang et al., 2023d) train a
146   generative NeRF to perform image generation. WoVoGen (Lu et al., 2023) creates a 4D world volume
147
      feature using occupancy to guide the generation, but seems to rely on object mask guidance.
148   As described above, most of the prior work is restricted by only modeling a projected primitive of 3D
149   boxes and road maps as conditions. They suffer from ill-posed un-projection ambiguity. In contrast,
150   we model 3D occupancy labels as conditions, as they provide finer geometric details and semantic
151   information. However, designing an input representation of 3D occupancy labels into a 2D diffusion
152   model is challenging. In this paper, we propose a novel representation: 3D semantic Multi-Plane
153
      Images (MPIs) as conditional inputs, which not only provide spatial alignment that improves visual
      consistency, but also encode comprehensive 3D geometric information including occluded parts.

      3.1    R EPRESENTATION OF C ONDITION : L OCAL C ONTROL A LIGNS B ETTER THAN G LOBAL
             C ONTROL
185
186   One of the key challenges is how to represent our conditional occupancy input. A straightforward
187   method (Gao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023) is to convert the 3D occupancy voxel to 1D global
188   embedding that is similar to text embedding, and then use cross-attention to learn controllable
189   generation. However, these global methods can be less effective when dealing with dense or irregular
190   data due to the following reasons: (i) They perform controllable generation through hard encoding the
191   spatial relationship between 1D global embedding and 2D UNet features. (ii) Ignore the underlying
192   geometry alignment between the conditional input and the generated image. In contrast, local methods
193   like ControlNet, directly add spatial features to the UNet features, providing 2D local control with
194
      pixel-level spatial alignment. They are better than the global method (see Tab. 1), but suffer from 3D
      ambiguity (see Fig. 6 (a)). Consequently, this comparison motivates us to seek a more compact and
195
      efficient manner to encode and condition our 3D occupancy labels.
196

      3.2    R EPRESENT O CCUPANCY AS 3D S EMANTIC M ULTIPLANE I MAGES
198
199   It is non-trivial to design a 3D representation for conditioning. To efficiently store both the semantic
200   and geometric information of the irregular occupancy input, we propose to use multiplane images
201   (MPIs) (Zhou et al., 2018) as representation. An MPI is composed of a series of fronto-parallel
202   RGBA layers within the frustum of the source camera with a specific viewpoint. These planes are
203   arranged at varying depths, from dmin to dmax , starting from the nearest to the farthest. Each layer
204   of these images contains both an RGB image and an alpha map, which collectively capture the visual
205   and geometric details of the scene at the respective depth. In our work, instead of storing RGB value
206
      and alpha map in the original MPI, we store our 3D semantic labels. Each layer of MPI represents the
      semantic index at the corresponding depth. We display the colored MPI in the top row of Fig. 2 for
207
      visual clarity, but we actually use the integer index for learning. We obtain our 3D semantic MPI by:

233   3.3   3D S EMANTIC MPI E NCODER
234
235   To enable local control with spatially aligned conditions, we develop a simple but effective MPI
236   encoder that aligns the 3D multi-plane feature to the latent space of the diffusion model. The
237   purpose of the MPI encoder is to obtain features from multi-plane images to perform 3D-aware
238   image synthesis. Unlike the original ControlNet which downsampling conditional input through 3×3
239   convolutions with padding, we design a 1×1 convolutional encoder without downsampling to encode
240
      features. In detail, the 3D multiplane features which have the sample resolution with latent features,
      are transformed by a 1×1 convolution layer and ReLU activation (Agarap, 2018) in the MPI encoder.
241
242   After obtaining the multi-scale feature after the MPI encoder, we add the feature to the decoder of
243   diffusion UNet to provide spatial features. Experimental results in Tab. 2 will show that our 1×1 conv
244   in MPI encoder is more effective than 3×3 conv, as the 1×1 conv with receptive field = 1 provides a
245   spatial align feature to the latent feature in the diffusion UNet. In contrast, 3×3 conv is conducted
246
      in a camera frustum space rather than Euclid space, making an imprecise correspondence between
      3D multiplane features and 2D image features. Moreover, using 3×3 conv to process 3D semantic
247
      MPI will introduce a large computational burden as the channel number increases from 3 channels of
248
      RGB to 256 planes. We display our 3D geometry and semantic control property in Fig. 3.
249
250   In summary, we chose MPIs as the representation because they (i) Incorporate lossless 3D information,
251   including scene geometry rather than 2.5D depth. (ii) Provide spatially aligned conditional features
252
      that naturally extend the ControlNet framework from image level to 3D level. (iii) Capable of
      representing geometry and semantics including occluded elements.

      style. Although the nuScenes dataset doesn’t contain rare weather images like snow and sandstorms,
      our method successfully conveys prior knowledge pretrained from stable diffusion to our scenes.
410
      Compared with visualization results in prior work like Fig. 8 of MagicDrive, our method shows better
411
      alignment with the text prompt, demonstrating the cross-domain generalization ability of our method.
412
413   3D Geometric Control Our flexible framework enables us to create novel scenes by manipulating
414   voxels as displayed in Fig. 1 and Fig. 3. Basically, we can edit the occupied state and semantics of
415   every voxel in our scenes for generation. We highlight that we can create a hinged-articulated truck
416
      and an excavator as shown in Fig. 6 (d,e). The generated excavator image exhibits a remarkable
      alignment with the input occupancy that is delineated by a black outline.
417
418   Long-tailed Scene Generation The flexibility of 3D semantic MPI has conferred significant
419   advantages upon our approach. In the following, we create long-tail scenes that rarely occur in our
420   real world for evaluation. In Fig. 1, we show that we manually add parallel traffic cones in front of the
421   ego vehicle. This scene has never happened in the training dataset, but our geometric controllability
422   provides us the capability to create such data. We then use the created scene to test autonomous
423   driving systems such as end-to-end planner VAD (Jiang et al., 2023) to validate its effectiveness.
424   In this case, VAD successfully predicts correct waypoints with the high-level command ‘turn left’.
425
      Moreover, in appendix Sec. B, we generate long-tailed scenes with extreme weather such as snow
      and sandstorms, and evaluate perception model on it to examine its generalizability of rare weather.
426
427   Comparison with Baselines In Fig. 6 (a), we visualize a comparison with ControlNet. We find
428   that ControlNet struggles to distinguish the overlapping instances in 2D-pixel space. This leads to the
429   two parked cars being merged into a single car with incorrect pose. In contrast, our 3D semantic MPIs
430   contain more than 2D semantic mask, but also account for complete scene geometry with occluded
431   parts. Together with our proposed MPI encoder and reweighing strategy, our framework yields a
      realistic image generation with high-quality label alignment. More comparison is provided in Sec. D.

      Layout Genereation Our method is restricted in a conditional generation framework that should
499   have a conditional input at first. Our condition signal is from the original dataset annotation. Thus
500   most of the augmented data is generated using the same occupancy layout, or with minimal human
501   editing. Future research can incorporate the recent research (Lee et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Lu
502   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) that generates occupancy and traffic descriptions of the
503   scenes to synthesize images with novel occupancy or traffic layouts.
504
505
      Extend to Native Video Diffusion Model As our work primarily focus on precise 3D controllabil-
      ity for image generation, we do not specifically tailor for video generation. We have a preliminary
506
      attempt to implement a plug-and-play module of cross-view and cross-frame attention to learn
507
      view-consistent or frame-consistent generation. This experiment serves as a proof of concept, demon-
508   strating the adaptability of our framework. It shows a promising result of temporal consistency across
509   frames, as compared with prior work in Sec. F. However, we argue that this methodology can be
510   inevitably less significant than native 3D convolution and 3D attention in the video diffusion model.
511   In the future, we plan to extend our controllability into native video diffusion using CogVideoX (Yang
512   et al., 2024) or Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024), to further provide controllable video generation.
513
514
      Long-tailed Scene Generation In this paper, we investigate a series of long-tailed scene generation
      and corner case evaluations such as rare layout in Fig. 1 and extreme weather in Sec. B. Future work
515
      can extend our framework to (i) Synthesize more samples for tail classes to boost performance. (ii)
516
      Generate or replicate large-scale databases of corner cases (Li et al., 2022) for robust perception.
517
518   Closed-loop Simulation Given the underlying diverse and controllable image generation of our
519   method, it would be advantageous and valuable to extend our work to a broader domain such as closed-
520   loop simulation (Ljungbergh et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023e), to enable high-fidelity autonomous
521   systems testing. This line of work can be conducted by utilizing motion conditions to generate
522   future frames as in world model (Yang et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2023c; Lu et al., 2023), or by
523
      explicitly modeling scene graph as in the case of UniSim (Yang et al., 2023e; Ost et al., 2021) and
      NeuroNCAP (Ljungbergh et al., 2024).

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we propose SytheOcc, an innovative image generation framework that is empowered
529   with geometry-controlled capabilities using occupancy. We introduce a novel 3D representation,
530   3D semantic MPIs, to address the critical challenge of how to efficiently encode occupancy. This
531   representation not only preserves the authentic and complete 3D geometry details with semantics, but
532
      also provides a spatial-align feature representation for 2D diffusion models. With this property, our
      method enjoys photorealistic appearances and fine-grained 3D controllability, serves as a generative
533
      data engine to enable a broad range of applications. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
534
      synthetic data facilitate the training for perception models on occupancy prediction, and provide
535   valuable corner case evaluation in a simulated world.

713   A    P OTENTIAL D ISCUSSION
714
715   To help a comprehensive understanding of our paper, we discuss intuitive questions that might be
716   raised.
717
718   How to define geometric control? In our paper, we refer the geometric controllable generation as
719
      using a voxel grid in 3D space to control the image generation. Although the voxel is a quantized
      representation of the 3D world, when the resolution goes larger, it can already faithfully represent
720
      the geometry detail of scenes. Currently, we are limited by the precision of ground truth labels. The
721
      0.2m occupancy grid is a tensor of 500×500×40 that cover a space in x-axis spanning [−50m, 50m],
722   y-axis spanning [−50m, 50m], z-axis spanning [−5m, 3m]. In the future, we plan to explore a higher
723   resolution of geometric control to refine our generation.
724
725   Can 3D semantic MPI extend to other representations beyond occupancy? Except for occu-
726   pancy, several other 3D representations can be expressed by 3D semantic MPI, such as mesh, dense
727   point clouds, and even 3D boxes or HD maps. The underlying mechanism is to cast several slices of
728
      multi-plane images at different depths to retrieve geometric information. Our application scope is
      wide, and we left them for future work. As a result, our 3D semantic MPI can be regarded as a general
729
      3D conditioning representation to benefit a wide spectrum of practical systems. These encompass
730
      but are not limited to 3D generation such as text2room (Höllein et al., 2023), RoomDreamer (Song
731   et al., 2023), WonderJourney (Yu et al., 2023), and LucidDreamer (Chung et al., 2023), each of which
732   stands to benefit from the rich geometric context provided by our approach.
733
734   Occupancy is complex. How to edit occupancy for controllable generation? We agree that
735   occupancy is more complex than the 3D box, but it provides a more nuanced scene description. To
736   ease the editing, we provide a strategy that disentangles the foreground control and background
737
      control in occupancy data. If we want to edit a car’s trajectory, we can keep the background occupancy
      unchanged and select the car’s first frame occupancy using the 3D box. During the following frames,
738
      we remove foreground occupancy and simply place our foreground target’s occupancy in a certain
739
      location using trajectory. By doing so, we only add minor steps by using occupancy but provide more
740   precise 3D control, which makes it a favorable choice for conditioning.
