009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative Flow Networks, or GFlowNets, formulate generative modelling in dis-
012            crete spaces as a sequential decision-making problem. Sampling plays a key role
013            in GFlowNet training, as most algorithms use the learned policy to sample tra-
014            jectories from the environment. Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a planning
015            algorithm that has successfully been applied to train sequential decision-making
016            models with reinforcement learning (RL). In this work, we leverage known con-
017            nections between GFlowNets and maximum-entropy RL to adapt MCTS for
018
               GFlowNet training. We prove that standard MCTS tree construction processes
               can be modified to calculate the optimal flows for a GFlowNet, given sufficient
019
               samples from the environment. Our results extend to multiple cases of GFN mod-
020
               elling, including terminating-energy and intermediate-energy environments. We
021            investigate practical strategies for employing MCTS as a sampling tool and apply
022            it to different GFN parameterizations and training objectives. Through extensive
023            experiments in a variety of discrete domains, including a language-based reason-
024            ing task, we show that our proposed method offers an improvement over standard
025            on-policy sampling.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Generative Flow Networks, or GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021a;b), are generative models that can
031   sequentially generate objects based on their energy (or reward). GFlowNets act as energy sam-
032   plers such that they learn and match the underlying energy function and can sample from multiple
033
      modes. The GFlowNet policy generates a sample sequentially, by taking one action at a time, and
      from this perspective, is similar to a Reinforcement Learning (RL) policy. However, unlike the RL
034
      training objective that mainly focuses on reward maximization, the GFlowNet training objective
035
      aims to learn a policy that can match the reward or energy distribution and sample proportionally
036   to it. While Monte-Carlo Markov chains (MCMC) methods can also sample from an unnormalized
037   energy function, they are computationally expensive and slow to achieve mode-mixing. However,
038   GFlowNets amortize the expensive computation in a single trained generative pass, making it possi-
039   ble to leverage the generalization capabilities of machine learning and learn structure in the energy
040   distribution.
041
      The training objective of GFlowNets is usually formulated in the form of a flow objective such
042   that the incoming and outgoing flows are matched (Bengio et al., 2021a;b; Malkin et al., 2022;
043   Madan et al., 2023). The data to compute these objectives is commonly collected using the current
044   GFlowNet policy and the quality of these collected samples can affect the training efficiency of
045   GFlowNets. However, on-policy approaches face several limitations. They can fail to explore the
046   environment by overfitting to high reward (low energy) trajectories that were sampled recently. This
047   is of particular concern in low-entropy environments, where the reward landscape is quite sparse.
048   On-policy approaches also suffer from poor sample efficiency, as each sampled trajectory is only
049   used for a single gradient update. A number of works have taken inspiration from RL methods to
050
      improve sampling for GFlowNet training, including ϵ-uniform exploration, replay buffers, and local
      search (Kim et al., 2024). In this work, we propose a novel and flexible way of improving data
051
      sampling and training efficiency of GFlowNets.
052
053   Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a widely used planning algorithm in Reinforcement Learning
      that has been successfully applied to a number of settings (Browne et al., 2012; Silver et al., 2017;


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Kajita et al., 2020). Since GFlowNets can be trained in an off-policy manner and usually operate
055   in a deterministic environment dynamics, we demonstrate how integrating MCTS with GFlowNets
056   can improve data sampling, leading to a more efficient learning algorithm. Through extensive ex-
057   periments over a wide range of tasks, including an LLM based reasoning benchmark, we show how
058   combining MCTS with GFlowNets can enable efficient training throughout the different default
059   training objectives and GFlowNet parameterizations.
060
      The main contributions of our work are the following:
061
062         1. We introduce Monte Carlo DAG Search (MCDS), an adaptation of MCTS that can be used
063            to calculate optimal flows in a GFlowNet environment.
064         2. We provide a method for using the proposed MCDS to influence trajectory sampling in a
065            way that improves GFlowNet training.
066         3. We empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of our method with different GFlowNet pa-
067            rameterizations and environment structures.

      Consider a Directed Acyclic Graph, or DAG, G = (S, A) where S and A represent the state and
088
      action spaces. Given any two states s ∈ S and s′ ∈ S, an edge is represented as (s, s′ ). The
089
      action space A consists of directed edges S × S and is thus made up of transitions between any
090   two states. A trajectory τ is represented as a sequence of states: τ = (s1 → s2 → · · · → sn ) =
091   (s1 , s2 , . . . , sn ) such that the corresponding actions are (si → si+1 ) ∈ A that iteratively build this
092   trajectory one action, or one time step, at a time. Since G is a DAG, there exists no trajectory with
093   sn = sm ; ∀n > m. Given a transition st → st+1 , the state st is called the parent of st+1 and
094   st+1 is called the child of st . A state s′ is said to be a descendent of s if there exists a trajectory
095   τ ∈ T such that s′ appears after s: we denote this relationship as s ≺ s′ . A special initial state,
096   called source state, s0 , is defined such that s0 ≺ s for all s ∈ S\{s0 }. Similarly, a final state called
097   a sink state sf is defined such that s ≺ sf for all s ∈ S\{sf }. The parent of a sink state sf is
098
      called a terminating state: the set of terminating states is denoted X ⊆ S. A complete trajectory is
      represented as τ = (s0 , s1 , . . . , sn , sf ) and the set of all complete trajectories is denoted by T .
099
100   An environment is a combination of a state graph G and an energy function E(s) : S → R+ .
101   The energy can also be expressed as the reward function R(x) = exp −E(x)/α, where α is a
102   temperature parameter (typically we set α = 1). The reward function can be normalized to define a
103   distribution over terminating states P (x) ∝ R(x). The goal of GFlowNet training is learn a model
104
      P (x; θ) that approximates P (x). The environment is described as having terminating energy (or
      terminating reward) if E(s) = ∞ for all s ∈ S/X , otherwise it is referred to as intermediate energy
105
      (intermediate reward).

123   Many training objectives have been defined for GFlowNets, such as Flow Matching objective (Ben-
124   gio et al., 2021a), Detailed Balance objective (Bengio et al., 2021b), Trajectory Balance objective
125   (Malkin et al., 2022) and SubTB(λ) objective (Madan et al., 2023), and these operate on the level of
126   the state, edge, full length (complete) trajectories and sub-trajectories of any lengths, respectively.
127
      These training objectives are obtained by setting up a set of flow-matching constraints with the
      property that when all these constraints are satisfied, the GFlowNet sampling policy has the desired
128
      property that generates terminating states with probability proportional to R(x). Each constraint can
129
      be turned into a loss, typically by taking the square of the logarithm of the ratio of the right-hand
130   side to the left-hand side of the equality constraint. Each loss term thus corresponds to an amount
131   of constraint violation. Training consists in sampling trajectories and measuring these constraint
132   violations (the loss) and its gradient on the parameters of interest.
133
      The Flow Matching (FM) (Bengio et al., 2021a) objective parameterizes GFlowNets through edge
134
      flows F (s → s′ ; θ) on states s. The Trajectory Balance (TB) (Malkin et al., 2022) objective works
135
      with complete trajectories, and parameterizes the GFlowNet through an initial state flow Zθ , and
136   forward and backward policies, PF (s′ |s; θ) and PB (s|s′ ; θ), respectively. The Detailed Balance
137   (DB) (Bengio et al., 2021b) and the SubTB(λ) (Madan et al., 2023) objectives parameterize the
138   state flow F (s; θ), forward policy PF (s′ |s; θ), and backward policy PB (s|s′ ; θ) on actions s → s′
139   to define a GFlowNet. The flow-matching constraints represented by these parameterized quantities
140   are converted into a loss function by equating the left and right hand sides of the constraint equations
141   as a squared loss. The flow matching equation for the DB loss is described by Equation 1:

145   2.2   M ONTE -C ARLO T REE S EARCH (MCTS)
146
147   Although MCTS can in principle be applied to a variety of environments, for simplicity we consider
148   only environments with DAG-structured discrete state spaces, as described in Section 2.1. For a
149   more comprehensive treatment of the subject, we refer the reader to (Browne et al., 2012).
150   Let the search tree T be a DAG 1 consisting of a set of nodes ST ⊆ S¬f and edges AT ⊆ A, where
151   S¬f = S/sf and A¬f = A/{(x, sf ) : x ∈ X }. Let QT (s, s′ ) be the search value function for
152   edge (s, s′ ) ∈ AT . The MCTS algorithm iteratively builds T through repeated application of three
153   construction steps: Select, Expand, and Backup.
154   The search tree is initialized without any information: ST = {s0 }, AT = {}. In the Select step, a
155   new node s′ ∈    / ST is visited by sampling a trajectory through the search tree (s0 → · · · → s → s′ )
156   where each action is selected using a tree policy PT (s′ |s). In the Expand step, the edge values
157   QT (s′ , s′′ ) from the new node s′ to each of its children s′′ ∈ Ch(s′ ) are initialized using a heuristic
158   approximation. Finally, in the Backup step information from the new state s′ is propagated back up
159   along the path in reverse order, starting from s and moving towards s0 .
160

      However, in many applications the search DAG is sparse and tree-like.

207   3     R ELATED W ORK

209
      MCTS (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006; Kocsis et al., 2006) has long been used for planning in Markov
210   Decision Processes Browne et al. (2012). Early approaches employed simple methods for estimating
211   state values, such as performing Monte Carlo rollout with a heuristic policy. More recent approaches
212   (Anthony et al., 2017; Silver et al., 2017) have replaced these heuristics with neural networks, which
213   are faster and can in principle generalize to regions of the space that are unseen. Remarkably, it
214   has been demonstrated that these networks can effectively be trained with the samples that they
215   themselves generate through sampling, even in cases where the dynamics of the environment are
      unknown (Schrittwieser et al., 2019).


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Maximum Entropy Reinforcement Learning (MaxEnt RL, Fox et al. (2015)) differs from standard
217   RL by seeking a balance between maximizing rewards and maintaining diversity. This approach
218   can improve exploration and make control more robust when the model is imperfect. It can also
219   be viewed as a form of probabilistic inference, where the optimal policy samples trajectories pro-
220   portion to their reward Levine (2018). More recently, MaxEnt RL has been linked to GFlowNets
221   through reward shaping Tiapkin et al. (2024); Deleu et al. (2024); Mohammadpour et al. (2024).
222   This provides a framework for converting existing maximum-entropy RL algorithms into equiva-
223   lent GFlowNet algorithms: for example, the Detailed Balance GFlowNet algorithm (Bengio et al.,
224   2021b) can be expressed as Soft Q-Learning (Haarnoja et al., 2017) with a particular type of reward
225
      shaping. MCTS can also be modified to work with MaxEnt RL (Xiao et al., 2019) and perform
      inference in tree-structured environments (Buesing et al., 2019).
226
227   Our work also shares some similarities with a concurrent work (Morozov et al., 2024): however,
228   our contributions differ in several ways. Our work is more general in the sense that it is applicable
229   to multiple GFlowNet parameterizations (DB, SubTB, and FM) and works with both terminating-
230   reward and intermediate-reward environments, while theirs is limited to Soft Q-Learning (i.e. DB) in
231
      terminating-reward environments. Additionally, the manner in which their tree is constructed differs
      considerably from our own approach: theirs is similar to AlphaZero in the sense that it evolves
232
      several independent search trees simultaneously, while ours is more like TreeSample (Buesing et al.,
233
      2019) in that it builds one large search DAG (in parallel) from which trajectories can be sampled
234   during training, somewhat like a replay buffer. Finally, the way they use search to influence training
235   is distinct: their approach relies on taking the flow estimates from the search tree directly as training
236   targets, while our approach only constructs the tree for the purposes of sampling.

320   4.2   A PPLYING MCDS FOR GF LOW N ET T RAINING
321
322   Most GFlowNet algorithms involve sampling trajectories from the environment and minimizing a
323   differentiable loss on these samples with gradient descent. The precise form of the loss function
      depends on the particular GFlowNet parameterization and training objective. However, regardless

324
      of parameterization, the sampling strategy is a critical part of the optimization can have a large
325   impact on overall performance.
326
327   Let PM (s′ |s) denote the sampling policy. In principle, the only requirement of the sampling policy
328
      is that it has full support over the set of trajectories T . The most basic strategy therefore is to sample
      trajectories on-policy using the current model’s parameters. In the case of DB, SubTB, and TB,
329
      the learned forward policy can be used PM (s′ |s) = PF (s′ |s; θ). In the case of FM, which does
330
      not parameterize a forward policy directly, the sampling policy can be defined using the edge flow
331   function: PM (s′ |s) ∝ F (s, s′ ; θ).
332
333   Our method involves constructing a search DAG D with MCDS and drawing samples with
334
      PM (s′ |s) = PD (s′ |s). Inspired by previous works combining MCTS with RL (Silver et al.,
      2017; Buesing et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2019), we can guide construction of D by using the cur-
335
      rent GFlowNet flow estimates in the Expand step. First, let us consider the DB objective (Bengio
336
      et al., 2021b), which requires parameterizing a forward policy PF (s′ |s; θ), a state flow function
337   F (s; θ), and (optionally) a backward policy PB (s|s′ ; θ). In this case we can apply the flow identity
338   F (s, s′ ; θ) = F (s; θ)PF (s′ |s; θ) and initialize tree flows for new nodes using Equation 7:
339
340                                       log FD (s, s′ ) ← log F (s, s′ ; θ)                                (7)
341

342

343

344

346   combination with the intermediate energy E(s), as described in Equation 8 (see Appendix 7.2 for

353   DAG distribution PD (x) perfectly models the distribution over terminating states P (x). In practice,

359   Empirically we find that mixing samples from PM (s′ |s) and PF (s′ |s; θ) in a 1:1 ratio produces
360   the best results. We can observe that sampling trajectories from the optimal distribution PF (s′ |s)
361   does not necessarily lead to superior optimization: empirically, simple on-policy training can re-
362   sult in faster convergence under certain conditions (Atanackovic & Bengio, 2024). Intuitively, it is
363   important for the sampling policy to capture regions of the space where the current model and the
364   optimal distribution differ. Focusing exclusively on the modes of the distribution might not be the
365   best strategy for finding such states.
366   Building D every iteration can be quite inefficient, since the GFlowNet policy PF (s′ |s; θ) does not
367   change much after a single gradient update. It also slows down training dramatically, since each time
368   D is constructed the model F (s′ |s; θ) and energy function E(s) are queried several times. In our
369   experiments D is built every few iterations, and the construction operations are executed in parallel
370   (see Algorithm 1 for full details).

474   5.3     FACTOR G RAPHS
475
476   The Factor Graphs benchmark, originally proposed in (Buesing et al., 2019) but reformulated for
477   GFlowNets in (Deleu et al., 2024), is a challenging discrete inference task. Each Factor Graph
478   environment corresponds to a factorizable distribution over N categorical variables (each with sup-
479   port size K). Notably, since each factor only depends on a subset of the N variables, intermediate
480   rewards can be given once those variables have been assigned. Each action in the environment cor-
481   responds to the assignment of one of the N variables, resulting in a total of (K +1)N states of which
482
      K N are terminating.
483   We consider two environments: the Permuted Chain environment and the Factorgraphs1 environ-
484   ment (see Section 7.3 for more details). As described in Section 4.1, MCDS construction can pro-
485   ceed with or without intermediate rewards. The forward-looking (FL) variants that use intermediate
      rewards for both tree construction and loss calculation clearly outperform those that do not, as shown

      6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this work, we propose Monte Carlo DAG Search (MCDS), a novel adaptation of MCTS to the
524
      GFlowNet problem. Our method employs reward shaping to modify the Backup step in maximum
525   entropy MCTS so that it can apply to GFlowNets. We show that our approach can be used to
526   calculate optimal flows in both terminating and intermediate reward environments. By employing
527   MCDS as a tool for sampling the environment, we demonstrate how it can improve GFlowNet
528   training. Through a series of experiments covering different state spaces, reward structures, neural
529   network architectures, and GFlowNet parameterizations, we demonstrate the broad applicability and
530   effectiveness of our method for GFlowNet training.
531   There are several promising directions for future work. Our current MCDS formulation requires
532   parameterizing a state flow F (s) or state-action flow F (s, s′ ), which limits its applicability to the
533   DB, SubTB, and FM parameterizations. However, it may be possible to develop a strategy that works
534   with TB. Furthermore, we have not explored combining MCDS with other successful GFlowNet
535   sampling methods like replay buffers, local search (Kim et al., 2024), and Thompson sampling
536   (Rector-Brooks et al., 2023), which could further improve performance. Finally, it would be valuable
537   to explore different formulations of the DAG policy PD (s′ |s) that is used in tree construction. Our
538   approach is most similar to MENTS (Xiao et al., 2019), but it may be possible to consider other
539
      variants (Buesing et al., 2019) which offer a different balance of exploration and exploitation.
