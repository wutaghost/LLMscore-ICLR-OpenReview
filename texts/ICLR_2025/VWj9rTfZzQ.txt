009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Electromagnetic (EM) simulation plays a crucial role in analyzing and designing
012            devices with sub-wavelength scale structures such as semiconductor devices and
013            future displays. Specifically, optics problems such as estimating semiconductor
014            device structures and designing nanophotonic devices provide intriguing research
015            topics with far-reaching real world impact. Traditional algorithms for such tasks
016            require iteratively refining parameters through simulations, which often yield sub-
017            optimal results due to the high computational cost of both the algorithms and
018            EM simulations. Machine learning (ML) emerged as a promising candidate to
019
               mitigate these challenges, and optics research community has increasingly adopted
               ML algorithms to obtain results surpassing classical methods across various tasks.
020
               To foster a synergistic collaboration between the optics and ML communities,
021
               it is essential to have an EM simulation software that is user-friendly for both
022            research communities. To this end, we present meent, an EM simulation software
023            that employs rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA). Developed in Python and
024            equipped with automatic differentiation (AD) capabilities, meent serves as a
025            versatile platform for integrating ML into optics research and vice versa. To
026            demonstrate its utility as a research platform, we present three applications of
027            meent: 1) generating a dataset for training neural operator, 2) serving as an
028            environment for the reinforcement learning of nanophotonic device optimization,
029            and 3) providing a solution for inverse problems with gradient-based optimizers.
030
               These applications highlight meent’s potential to advance both EM simulation
               and ML methodologies. The code is available on our Github repository with the
031
               MIT license to promote the cross-polinations of ideas among academic researchers
032
               and industry practitioners.

037
038   Harnessing light-matter interaction to design or analyze a device with sub-wavelength scale structure
039

      2019; Snaith, 2013), ultra-thin metalenses and displays (Zhang et al., 2018; Aieta et al., 2012),
040
      optical metrology for semiconductor fabrication (Timoney et al., 2020; Den Boef, 2013), X-ray
041

044   computation. Electromagnetic (EM) simulation plays a crucial role in such applications, which also
045   poses a challenging problem due to its time-consuming nature for precise calculation (Burger et al.,
046   2008) and iterative characteristic of optimization.
047

050   optimization algorithms (So & Rho, 2019; Seo et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2020; Colburn & Majumdar,
051   2021; Kim & Lee, 2023) and faster electromagnetic simulators (Raissi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020a;
052   Lu et al., 2021). These researches show significant potential of ML for uncovering new insights
053   and expediting scientific discoveries. On the other hand, from the viewpoint of ML, designing or
      analyzing such devices are ideal environments for developing new ML algorithms, as they provide

089   However, the integration of ML into computational optics presents several challenges. Traditional
090   EM simulation software are often written in languages like C or MATLAB and therefore cannot
091   be seamlessly integrated into ML packages mostly developed for Python ecosystem. This results
092   in the loss of the computational graph needed for automatic differentiation (AD), which is useful
093
      for gradient-based optimization. Furthermore, the scarcity of public data in certain domains, such
      as semiconductor fabrication industry, compounded by stringent intellectual property regulations,
094
      poses significant obstacles, especially to ML researchers lacking domain expertise. Overcoming these
095
      barriers requires innovative approaches in generating and sharing data to enable ML researchers to
096   explore new frontiers in computational optics.
097
098   In response to these challenges, we introduce meent, a Python-native differentiable EM simulator.
099
      meent is based on rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA) (Moharam & Gaylord, 1981; Moharam
      et al., 1995a;b; Lalanne & Morris, 1996; Granet & Guizal, 1996; Li, 1996; Rumpf, 2006; Li,
100
      2014), a high-throughput, deterministic EM simulation algorithm that is widely adopted in optics
101
      across academia and industries. Key features of meent include its compatibility with automatic
102   differentiation (AD) (Baydin et al., 2018; Moses & Churavy, 2020) for modeling and optimizing
103   devices in a continuous space. AD compatibility in ML toolchain is pivotal, and while some existing
104   tools support vector modeling and others support AD, none offer both functionalities simultaneously.
105   For developer ergonomics, meent is developed to be compatible with three different backend
106   frameworks: NumPy (Harris et al., 2020), JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018), and PyTorch (Paszke et al.,
107   2019). By supporting multiple backends, meent facilitates easy adoption among researchers with
      varying levels of domain expertise and different backend preferences.

      EM simulation algorithms. There exist several methods for full-wave1 EM simulation, each
129
      offering distinct advantages. Here, we review finite difference time domain (FDTD) and rigorous
130   coupled wave analysis (RCWA). FDTD operates within the real space and time domain, employing
131   the finite difference method. It discretizes space into grids and iteratively solves the function at
132   these grid points over successive time steps (Taflove, 1980). RCWA operates in reciprocal space
133   and frequency domain, which requires two conditions for Fourier analysis: time-harmonic field2 and
134   periodicity of a structure.
135

139                     FDTD          real space           time         numerical             low
140                     RCWA       reciprocal space     frequency     semi-analytical        high

      Table 1 shows a comparative analysis of FDTD and RCWA. Both methods solve Maxwell’s equations,
143   but they operate in different domains, as explained. FDTD is a fully numerical method, whereas
144   RCWA is considered semi-analytic, as it allows for analytical solutions of the fields in the propagation
145   direction. FDTD is general but RCWA is applicable to specific cases where the fields are time-
146   harmonic and the structure has periodicity. By losing the generality, RCWA can show much faster
147   simulations compared to FDTD for many practical cases.

      ML applications in optics. Assisted with physical simulators, ML is being actively embraced
172   across scientific domains to substitute heavy simulations with deep models that serve as surrogate
173   solvers, offering high throughput and increased robustness to hidden noise. Seminal works such as
174   physics-informed neural network (PINN) (Raissi et al., 2017a;b; 2019) and neural operators (Li et al.,
175   2020a; Cai et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020b; 2023; Lu et al., 2021; 2022; Jin et al., 2022) showed their
176   potential as surrogate EM solvers (Pestourie et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021). Reinforcement learning
177   (RL) also showed its efficacy in the scientific domains, such as magnetic control of tokamak plasmas
178   (Degrave et al., 2022) and classical mechanics (Lillicrap et al., 2015; Todorov et al., 2012).
179   Representative examples of surrogate EM solver include MaxwellNet (Lim & Psaltis, 2022), an
180   instance of PINN. Fourier neural operator was used in (Augenstein et al., 2023), where optimization
181   of nanophotonic device (Park et al., 2022) is also conducted. Deep generative model was used in
182   (So & Rho, 2019) to reduce computational cost compared to traditional optimization algorithm,
183   and the feasibility of using model-free RL was demonstrated in (Sajedian et al., 2019; Seo et al.,
184   2021; Park et al., 2024). Our example explores the possibility of applying model-based RL to device
185   optimization, rooted on RNN-based world model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019b;a;
186
      2020; 2023).

      Electromagnetic simulation algorithm. meent uses rigorous coupled wave analysis (RCWA)
      (Moharam & Gaylord, 1981; Moharam et al., 1995a;b; Lalanne & Morris, 1996; Granet & Guizal,
197
      1996; Li, 1996; Rumpf, 2006; Li, 2014), which is based on Faraday’s law and Ampére’s law of
198
      Maxwell’s equations (Kim et al., 2012),
199
200                               ∇ × E = −jωµ0 H,            ∇ × H = jωε0 εr E,                            (1)
201
      where E and H are electric and magnetic field in real space, j denotes the imaginary unit number, i.e.
202   j 2 = −1, ω denotes the angular frequency, µ0 is vacuum permeability, ε0 is vacuum permittivity,
203   and εr is relative permittivity. As illustrated in Figure 1, it is a technique used to solve PDEs in
204   Fourier space, aiming to estimate optical properties such as diffraction efficiency or field distribution.
205   We reserve the detail of RCWA for Appendices A and G for readers interested in delving into the
206   fundamentals of RCWA.
207
208   Geometry modeling. meent offers support for two modeling types: raster and vector. Analogous
209   to the image file format, raster-type represents data as an array, while vector-type utilizes a set of
210   objects, with each object comprising vertices and edges, as shown in Figure 2a. Due to their distinct
211   formats, each method employs different algorithms for space transformation, resulting in different
212
      types of geometry derivatives, including topological and shape derivatives, as depicted in Figure 2b.
      The topological derivative yields the gradient with respect to the permittivity changes of every cell in
213
      the grid, while the shape derivative provides the gradient with respect to the deformations of a shape.
214
215   These two modeling methods offer distinct advantages and are suited to different applications: raster
      modeling is ideal for freeform metasurface design, where pixel-wise operations are utilized, while

      4.2     M ODEL - BASED REINFORCEMENT LEARNING : METASURFACE DESIGN
401
      Here we demonstrate that meent can be used as an environment to train a model-based reinforcement
402   learning (RL) agent, whose model learns how EM field evolves according to the change of the
403   metagrating structure. The objective of an RL agent here is to find the metagrating structure that
404   yields high deflection efficiency, by flipping the material of a cell between silicon and air. Here, MFS
405   is set to 1, i.e., there is no MFS constraint.
406
407   Problem setup. Here an RL agent undergoes fully-observable Markov decision process described
408   as sequence of tuples {st , at , rt , st+1 }Tt=1 , where the next state st+1 is determined by the dynamics
409   model p(st+1 | st , at ) and the action is the index of cell to flip, at ∈ {0, 1, ..., k − 1}. The state st and
410   the reward rt depend on which RL algorithm is used, and will be defined shortly after. Throughout the
411   decision process, the agent learns to flip cells that maximizes the deflection efficiency ηt . For training
412   purpose, we implemented Gymnasium (Towers et al., 2023) environment called deflector-gym,
413
      which is built on top of meent. Given an input action at , the environment modifies current structure
      gt and outputs FoMs such as deflection efficiency ηt or electric field vt in deterministic manner.
414
415
416   Model-based RL vs Model-free RL. One way to categorize
417
      RL algorithms is whether it has an explicit dynamics model
      p ≈ pθ (st+1 | st , at ), where pθ is some neural network. Model-
418
      based RL (MBRL) agent utilizes the model pθ to produce sim-
419
      ulated experiences, from which the policy is improved (Sutton,
420   1991; Sutton & Barto, 2018). Therefore, MBRL agent is con-
421   sidered to be more sample efficient than model-free agent, i.e.,
422   requires less interactions with actual environment to train.
423
      For an MBRL algorithm, we chose DreamerV3 (Hafner et al.,
424
      2023), the first algorithm that solved ObtainDiamond task of
425
      MineRL (Guss et al., 2019). DreamerV3 is based on recurrent
426   state-space model (RSSM) (Hafner et al., 2019b) that models Figure 6: Learning curve across
427   dynamics in the latent space. Developed to be robust across 3 random seeds. Historical max-

430   set of hyperparameters, most of which were reused here as well.
431   We compare this with Deep Q Network (DQN) (Mnih et al., 2015), a model-free algorithm, adapted
      from (Park et al., 2024).

      4.3   I NVERSE PROBLEM : OCD METROLOGY
474
      A semiconductor device is a three-dimensional stack of layers, rendering direct measurement of
475   parameters beneath the surface unfeasible without causing damage. OCD offers a solution to
476   this challenge by redirecting the observation target from the dimensions of physical device to its
477   spectral characteristics (spectrum). Consequently, OCD becomes an inverse problem: we deduce
478   the dimensions of the structure in real space, which are the causal factors of spectrum shape, from
479   observations. The solution involves a probabilistic and iterative process known as spectrum fitting.
480   This necessitates optimization in continuous space, which can be achieved using meent with vector
481   modeling.
482
483   Spectrum fitting. Figure 8 shows the process of finding solution using spectrum fitting. The goal
484   is to estimate P using S, as direct observation of P is impossible. To achieve this, we initially create a
485   virtual structure with limited prior knowledge provided by domain experts, and sample the initial
      parameters P̂0 from a suitable distribution. Subsequently, we generate Ŝ0 from P̂0 through simulation.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486

495
496   Figure 8: Schematic diagram of spectrum fitting. This diagram illustrates the key components of
497   the approach, including the vector of design parameters (P) from the real device, the set of spectra (S)
498   derived from these parameters, the synthesized spectra at iteration i (Ŝi ), and the estimated design
499   parameters at the corresponding iteration (P̂i ). The methodology involves assessing the distance
500   between Ŝi and S using a loss function, followed by a update of P̂i to minimize this distance during
501   each iteration.
502
503
504
505
506
507

      Figure 9: Loss curves of various gradient-based algorithms for spectrum fitting. Each plot
511   illustrates the change of loss over iterations, with the y axis represented in logarithmic scale.

513
514   We then employ a distance metric as a loss function to quantify the discrepancy between S and Ŝ0 to
515   compare P and P̂0 . The process is followed by backpropagation, which computes gradients of the
516   distance with respect to each element of P̂0 . Following that, P̂0 is updated to P̂1 . This iterative process
517   can be generalized using P̂i and Ŝi , where i denotes the iteration number. As iterations progress, Ŝi
518   gradually converges towards S, and it is expected that P̂i will similarly approach the parameter set we
519   seek to obtain, P.
520
521   Demonstration. We now pivot our approach to meent. Rather than seeking P, we utilize meent
522   to observe the behavior of optimization algorithms, a subject of keen interest for ML researchers. As
523   an example, we introduce a case study involving eight design parameters with the details provided in
524   Appendix F. Employing spectrum fitting, we present the optimization curve of five distinct gradient-
525   based algorithms: Momentum, Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011), RMSProp (Hinton et al.), Adam
526   (Kingma & Ba, 2017) and RAdam (Liu et al., 2020). All algorithms share identical P̂0 to ensure
527   consistency, and evaluated repeatedly with 10 different initial conditions to mitigate the randomness
528   associated with initial point location, a critical factor in local optimization algorithms. The purpose
529   of this section is to demonstrate the capabilities of meent. The introduction of novel algorithms or
530
      the achievement of precise predictions is beyond the scope of this case study.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In our work, we introduce meent, a full-wave, differentiable electromagnetic simulation framework.
535   Through its capability for vector-type modeling and automatic differentiation, meent operates
536   seamlessly within a continuous space, overcoming the limitations inherent in raster-type modeling
537   for geometry representation. We demonstrate with examples of applications how to use meent as
538   a valuable tool to generate data for ML as well as a comprehensive solver for inverse problems,
539   expanding its role beyond that of a simple electromagnetic simulator. This versatility makes meent
      an invaluable framework to both machine learning and optics.

       A     A BRIEF INTRODUCTION TO RCWA
973
974    RCWA is based on Faraday’s law and Ampére’s law of Maxwell’s equations (Kim et al., 2012),
975
976                                     ∇ × E = −jωµ0 H,              ∇ × H = jωε0 εr E,                                   (5)
977                                                                                                                  2
       where E and H are electric and magnetic field in real space, j denotes the imaginary unit number, i.e. j = −1,
978    ω denotes the angular frequency, µ0 is vacuum permeability, ε0 is vacuum permittivity, and εr is relative
979    permittivity. After Fourier transform, E and H in real space become S and U in Fourier space, respectively,
       and Equation 5 then turns into
980
981                                       (−j k̃z )S = ΩL U,          (−j k̃z )U = ΩR S,                                   (6)
982
       where the matrices ΩL and ΩR are composed of wavevector matrices and convolution matrices that lie in
983
       Fourier space, k̃z is a normalized wavevector in the z-direction. We can find S by merging Equation 6 in a
984    single matrix equation as
985
986                                                   (−j k̃z )2 S = Ω2LR S,                                               (7)
987
       where the matrix Ω2LR is a matrix product of ΩL and ΩR . As the form implies, this equation can be solved by
988
       eigendecomposition of Ω2LR to obtain the eigenvectors S and the eigenvalues (−j k̃z ). Then by substituting the
989    eigenvectors for S in Equation 6, the corresponding solution of U can be obtained.
990
       S and U, which we have just computed, represent the set of electromagnetic modes (field representation in
991
       Fourier space) within a given medium. To understand their interaction with incoming and outgoing light, we
992    employ boundary conditions to ascertain their respective weights of the modes, or in other words, the coefficients
993    in linear combination of the modes. These coefficients describe the extent of each mode’s influence on the overall
994    field distribution. Notably, coefficients at the input and output interfaces are designated as diffraction efficiencies,
995    also called the reflectance and transmittance, serving as the primary purpose of RCWA. Subsequently, the inverse
       transformation from Fourier space to real space enables the reconstruction of the field distribution.

       D.2     A BLATION STUDY
1189
1190   We train FNO on various losses shown in Table 8, and name it as {Model}-{Training loss}, e.g. FNO-L2 is a
1191   FNO trained with L2 loss. A model is trained specifically for single physical condition.
1192   On metagrating, more intense and complex interactions occur around the grating area. What makes this area
1193   more important is that, theoretically, the deflection efficiency can be calculated just with the field profile of
1194   grating area. Take a look at the supplementary of (Chen et al., 2022).
1195   Therefore, we derive a simple loss coined as region-wise (RW) L2 loss, which puts more weight on the grating
1196   area, s.t. c1 + c2 = 1. We set c1 = 0.7 and c2 = 0.3. Lastly, H1 loss is a norm in Sobolev space which
1197   integrates the norm of first derivative of the target. Training with H1 loss promotes smoother function (Son et al.,
1198   2021).

       G     BACKGROUND THEORY
1567
1568   RCWA is the sequence of the following processes: solving the Maxwell’s equations, finding the eigenmodes of a
1569   layer and connecting these layers including the superstrate and substrate to calculate the diffraction efficiencies.
1570   Precisely, the electromagnetic field and permittivity geometry are transformed from the real space to the
1571   Fourier space (also called the reciprocal space or k-space) by Fourier analysis. Maxwell’s equations are then
       solved per layer through convolution operation, and a general solution of the field in each direction can be
1572
       obtained. This general solution can be represented in terms of eigenmodes (eigenvectors) and eigenvalues
1573   with eigendecomposition, and used to calculate diffraction efficiencies by applying boundary conditions and
1574   connecting to adjacent layers.
1575
       This chapter provides a comprehensive explanation of the theories, formulations and implementations of meent
1576   in the following sections:
1577
1578          1. Structure design: the device geometry is defined and modeled within meent framework.
1579          2. Fourier analysis of geometry: the device geometry is transformed into the Fourier space, allowing the
1580             decomposition of the structure into its corresponding spatial frequency components.
1581          3. Eigenmodes identification: RCWA identifies the eigenmodes that present within each layer of the
1582             periodic structure. These eigenmodes represent the possible electromagnetic field solutions that can
1583             exist within the system.
1584          4. Connecting layers: Rayleigh coefficients and diffraction efficiencies are determined using the trans-
1585             fer matrix method by connecting the layers. This step enables the determination of the overall
1586             electromagnetic response of the entire system.
1587          5. Enhanced transmittance matrix method: the implementation technique that avoids the inversion of
1588             some matrices which are possibly ill-conditioned.
1589          6. Topological derivative vs Shape derivative: two types of derivatives that meent supports are explained.

1606   meent supports two distinct types of geometry modeling: the raster modeling and the vector modeling. In

1613   limitations of grid-based representations. One of the key advantages provided by vector modeling is that the
1614
       minimum feature size is not constrained by the grid size. This flexibility allows for more accurate and detailed
       representation of complex structures, making vector modeling essential for accurate simulation.

       G.6     T OPOLOGICAL DERIVATIVE VS S HAPE DERIVATIVE
2041
2042   AD enables the calculation of the gradient of the figure of merit (FoM) with respect to the design parameters
2043   of the device. AD in meent can handle both modeling type - raster and vector - with two different forms:
2044
       topological derivative and shape derivative. If the raster modeling is utilized to obtain the geometry of the device,
       the gradients with respect to the refractive index of every pixel can be obtained through AD. This type of AD is
2045   known as the topological derivative, as the device design is updated pixel-wise and the topology is not conserved
2046   (Figure 17a). On the contrary, a shape derivative is effective for vector modeling; the FoM derivative with respect
2047   to input dimensions is obtained as depicted in Figure 17b. The shape derivative is expected to be useful for cases
2048   where the device topology is known, but dimensions of specific structures, such as the radius of a cylinder in a
2049
       layer or width and length of a cuboid, are to be found by optimization.

2736   In Figure 28, calculation time of 32bit case is normalized by 64bit case with the same condition. With some

2737
       From our understanding, the eigendecomposition (Eig) in NumPy operates in 64bit regardless of the input type -
2738   even though the input is 32bit data (float32 or complex64), the matrix operations inside Eig are done in 64bit but
2739   returns the results in 32bit data type. This is different from JAX and PyTorch - they provides Eig in 32bit as well
2740   as 64bit. Hence the 32bit NumPy cases in the figure approach to 1 as FTO increases because the calculation time
2741   for Eig is the same and it is the most time-consuming step. (2) Keep in mind that 32bit data type can handle only
       8 digits. This means that 1000 + 0.00001 becomes 1000 without any warnings or error raises. For such a reason,
2742
       the accuracy of 32bit cases in the figures are not guaranteed - we only consider the calculation time. (3) Eig in
2743   PyTorch shows interesting behavior: as FTO increases, calculation time in 32bit overtakes 64bit - see A8/A7,
2744   B8/B7 and C8/C7. This is counter-intuitive and we don’t have good explanation but cautiously guess that this
2745   might be related to the accuracy and precision in Eig or an optimization issue of PyTorch.

2772   meent is expected to be useful for solving inverse design and optimization problems in OCD and metasurface

       J.1    I NVERSE DESIGN OF 1D DIFFRACTION GRATING
2778   In this example, we will optimize 1D beam deflector that was used for benchmark in Chapter I using AD with
2779   these options - 256 cells, FTO = 100, λ0 = 900 nm and the deflection angle = 50◦ . During the optimization,
2780   each cell can have non-binary refractive index values, leading to a gray-scale optimization. To obtain the final
2781   structure consisting of only silicon/air binary structures, an additional binary-push process is required. The initial
       structure for optimization is randomly generated so the each cell can have the refractive index value between
2782
       of air and silicon under uniform distribution. The Figure of Merit for this optimization process is set to the
2783   +1st order diffraction efficiency, and the gradient is calculated by AD. The refractive indices are updated over
2784   multiple epochs using the ADAM optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017) with the learning rate of 0.5.
2785
       Figure 29a shows the deflection efficiency change by iteration. Two solid lines are averaged value of all the
2786   samples at the same iteration step. Shaded area is marked with ± standard deviation from the average. The blue
2787   line (Before binarization) is the result of device with any real number between two refractive indices (silicon and
2788   air), which is non-practical, and the orange line (After binarization) is the final device composed of silicon and
2789   air. The best result we found is 89.4%.

2791   J.2    I NVERSE DESIGN OF 2D DIFFRACTION GRATING
2792
       Here, we demonstrate optimization of a 2D diffraction metagrating as shown in Figure 30a. Similar to the
2793   previous 1D diffraction metagrating, the 2D diffraction metagrating also consists of silicon pillars located on top
2794   of a silicon dioxide substrate. TM polarized wave with λ = 1000 nm is normally incident from the bottom of
2795   the substrate and the device is designed to deflect the incident light with deflection angle θ = 60◦ in X-direction.
2796   The device has a rectangular unit cell of period λ/ sin θ ≈ 1150 nm and λ/2 = 500nm for the x and y-axis,
       respectively. Moreover, the unit cell is gridded into 256 × 128 cells which is either filled by air or silicon.

       The convergence of RCWA simulation for different number of Fourier harmonics are plotted in Figure 30b.
