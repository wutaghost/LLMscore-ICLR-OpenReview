                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Bootstrap aggregation, commonly referred to as bagging, is a fundamental tech-
012
               nique in ensemble learning designed to enhance the performance of predictive
013            models. It is well-established that the effectiveness of bagging is strongly in-
014            fluenced by the management of correlations among the aggregated models. For
015            instance, random forests, a widely-used ensemble method, address this issue by
016            randomly selecting features to reduce the correlation between individual tree mod-
017            els. In this study, we propose a method called Deep Bootstrap Aggregation for
018            regression tasks, which combines deep network architectures with least squares
019            estimation to improve the predictive accuracy of bagging models. Both theoreti-
020            cal analysis and empirical experiments support the effectiveness of the proposed
021
               approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Regression problems are frequently encountered in real-world scenarios, as they seek to uncover
027
      the complex relationships between a set of input variables and a continuous output variable. A
      conventional approach to addressing these challenges involves the use of statistical models, such as
028
      linear models or nonparametric regression techniques. Among the various nonparametric methods,
029
      regression trees are particularly popular for explaining the relationships between inputs and outputs.
030
031   Bishop (1995, chapter 9) and Breiman (1996) demonstrated the benefits of combining predictions
032   from multiple models to enhance predictive performance. Bühlmann & Yu (2002) provided a theo-
033
      retical analysis of bagging, introducing the concept of subbagging. Later, Breiman (2001) proposed
      the use of nearly uncorrelated models to further improve predictive accuracy. One notable example
034
      of this approach is the Random Forests algorithm. Introduced by Breiman (2001), random forests
035
      employ a strategy that aggregates predictions from multiple individual regression trees. This is ac-
036   complished through two primary techniques: bootstrapping the training data and randomly selecting
037   subsets of input variables within each bootstrap. Specifically, each tree in the random forest is built
038   using a bootstrapped dataset and a randomly chosen subset of input variables. The final prediction
039   is computed as the (unweighted) average of the predictions from all individual regression trees. For
040   a more in-depth understanding of random forests and their implementation, see Hastie et al. (2009),
041   Biau (2012), and James et al. (2021).
042   In random forests, the random selection of input variables aims to reduce correlations among the
043   bootstrapped regression trees, as lower correlations typically lead to improved predictive perfor-
044   mance. However, it is important to note that random input variable selection does not always achieve
045   this goal, making the unweighted average of predictions from individual regression trees suboptimal.
046   Evidence supporting this is presented in Figure 1 in Section 2.
047
      As emphasized by Ulaş et al. (2012), the development of a robust ensemble learning methodology
048   hinges on four critical components: strategic selection of prediction models, precise hyperparam-
049   eter tuning, careful use of data sampling techniques, and deliberate selection of input variables.
050   Extensive efforts have been made to reduce correlations between prediction models, with a focus
051   on increasing model diversity (Rosen, 1996; Ho, 1998; Liu & Yao, 1999; Derbeko et al., 2002;
052   Kuncheva & Whitaker, 2003; Bacauskiene et al., 2009; Hsu & Srivastava, 2012) and refining en-
053   semble weighting schemes (Acar & Rais-Rohani, 2009; Kim et al., 2011; Shahhosseini et al., 2020;
      Mao et al., 2021). Despite substantial research aimed at enhancing ensemble learning through these


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      components, as comprehensively reviewed by Tuysuzoglu & Birant (2020), there remains a signifi-
055   cant gap in addressing the key issue of reducing correlations among prediction models.
056
057   To address this challenge, we begin by interpreting the unweighted sample mean in the bagging
058
      procedure as the ordinary least squares (OLS) estimator within a linear model that includes only
      an intercept term. To account for correlation among prediction models, it is advisable to replace
059
      OLS with generalized least squares (GLS) estimation, which leverages model correlations to im-
060
      prove efficiency. We propose a framework that incorporates least squares methodology within a
061   deep network architecture. This framework effectively manages correlations across models by inte-
062   grating hidden layers, leading to substantial improvements in predictive accuracy. Additionally, this
063   approach extends beyond enhancing random forests, making it applicable to a wide range of models
064   that handle continuous outputs. We demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method through
065   theoretical analysis and empirical validation.
066
      The structure of this article is as follows. Section 2 formalizes our aggregation method as a least
067   squares estimation within the context of linear models. In Section 3, we introduce our framework and
068   provide a detailed exposition of the corresponding theoretical results. The framework is extended
069   to a deep network structure in Section 4. Sections 5 and 6 present the results of numerical studies,
070   including both simulated and real-world examples, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed
071   approach. Finally, conclusions are drawn in Section 7.

      Before introducing the proposed method, we first reformulate the bagging procedure by framing
076   it as an estimation problem in linear models. Let the training dataset be D = {yi , xi }ni=1 , where
077   xi = (xi1 , xi2 , . . . , xip )⊤ represents the vector of p input variables for the ith data point, and
078   yi = y(xi ) is the observed response value corresponding to xi . The goal is to predict the output
079   variable y(x) at an unobserved x, using the training dataset D.
080
      Conventionally, the bagging procedure constructs T predictors, C1 , . . . , CT , each trained on a boot-
081
      strapped dataset from D under a chosen prediction model (e.g., regression tree, K-NN regression).
082
      We refer to C1 , . . . , CT as the base predictors in this article. The aggregated prediction of y(x) is
083   given by:
084                                                          T
                                                         1X
085                                             ŷ(x) =         Cj (x).                                    (1)
086

087   In Equation (1), each base predictor Cj (x) is assigned an equal weight of 1/T , meaning that all
088   base predictors contribute equally to ŷ(x). The use of Equation (1) is supported by Bishop (1995,
089   Chapter 9) and Breiman (1996), who show that the mean square error (MSE) of ŷ(x) is lower than
090   that of any single predictor Cj (x). This property makes bagging a widely adopted machine learning
091   algorithm.
092
      Although equally weighted aggregation, as described in Equation (1), is computationally efficient
093   and easy to understand, it is important to critically reassess the assumptions underlying this ap-
094   proach. In the following discussion, we reformulate the aggregation step as a least squares problem,
095   providing an alternative perspective on equally weighted aggregation.

      4.2   M ULTI - STAGE GLSA
271
272
273   A deep network consists of several layers. In this article, we treat each layer as a stage in the
274
      aggregation procedure. M-GLSA aggregates a subset of predictors using GLSA at each stage and
      produces a final predictor after the last stage. Let C = (C1 , . . . , CT ) represent the set of base
275
      predictors to be aggregated. At stage 1, we divide C into T1 subsets, where each subset contains
276
      T1,1 , . . . , T1,T1 base predictors. We use Ti,j to denote the number of predictors in the jth subset at
277   the ith stage. At stage 1, we aggregate the predictors in each subset using GLSA to obtain the stage 1
278   predictors C1 = (C1,1 , C1,2 , . . . , C1,T1 ), where C1,j denotes the jth predictor at stage 1. Similarly,
279   we divide C1 into T2 subsets, with each subset containing T2,1 , . . . , T2,T2 predictors. GLSA is then
280   applied to obtain the stage 2 predictors C2 = (C2,1 , . . . , C2,T2 ). Recursively, this process continues,
281   with GLSA being performed at each stage using appropriately sized subsets, until the final predictors
282   are obtained.
283   Note that each stage consists of multiple aggregation steps, with each step involving only a subset
284   of the predictors from the previous stage. This property reduces the dimensionality of the variance-
285   covariance matrix Σ in each step and provides a more accurate estimate of Σ . However, M-GLSA
286   has several hyperparameters, including the number of predictors T , the number of stages G, the
287   subset sizes at each stage T1,1 , . . . , Ti,Ti for i = 1, . . . , G, and the number of subsets at each stage
288   T1 , . . . , TG . In the following, we discuss the selection of suitable hyperparameters, taking into
289   account computational time, performance, and numerical stability.
290   Recall that when selecting the number of base predictors T in bagging trees and random forests, the
291   goal is to choose a value of T large enough to reach a stable MSE. We adopt the same approach in
292   M-GLSA. After completing the kth stage, the predictors Ck are more accurate compared to those
293   in Ci , i = 1, . . . , k − 1. This is evident because the number of base predictors used to construct
294   each predictor at stage k is greater than that in the preceding stages. However, if the number of
295   stages G is chosen to be large, the predictors in the final stages may potentially lead to a singular
296   variance-covariance matrix. In our √  experience, two stages (G = 2) are sufficient to reduce the subset
297   size at each step to approximately T , assuming a suitable choice of T . Based on this observation,
298
      we focus on two-stage GLSA, referred to as 2-GLSA. Figure 4 illustrates the comparison between
      GLSA and 2-GLSA.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
511
      Previous research primarily focused on mitigating predictor correlations through algorithmic mod-
512
      ifications or the development of sampling strategies. In contrast, the primary innovation of this
513
      study lies in directly addressing predictor correlations within the context of ensemble learning. Our
514   approach leverages the training data not only to build predictors but also to extract valuable infor-
515   mation for estimating predictor correlations. Empirical evidence from both theoretical analysis and
516   numerical experiments confirms that our method significantly reduces MSE in prediction. One par-
517   ticularly noteworthy outcome is the substantial improvement observed in the random-forest models
518   when applying Generalized Least Squares-based Aggregation, paving the way for new applications
519   of random forests. Importantly, our methodology is simple to implement and does not require pa-
520   rameter tuning. Additionally, we introduce a computationally efficient statistic that facilitates the
521   assessment of expected improvements when using Generalized Least Squares-based Aggregation.
522   Correlations manifest across various aspects of ensemble learning, and these phenomena extend
523   to other applications as well. For example, in cross-validation, the average loss is computed from
524   models constructed using overlapping subsets of training data. Similarly, in stochastic gradient
525   descent, random sampling of subsets of observed data determines the direction sequentially. When
526   the selected subsets overlap, the directions tend to align, leading to high correlations. A potential
527   avenue for future research involves addressing correlations in other application domains beyond
528
      ensemble learning. It is also worth noting that the correlation patterns observed in this study vary
      depending on the datasets and base predictors used. Therefore, another promising direction for
529
      future research is to explore the origins of these correlations and deepen our understanding of the
530
      underlying mechanisms.
