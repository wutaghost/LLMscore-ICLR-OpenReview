009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This work characterizes equivariant polynomial functions from tuples of tensor
012            inputs to tensor outputs. Loosely motivated by physics, we focus on equivariant
013            functions with respect to the diagonal action of the orthogonal group on tensors. We
014            show how to extend this characterization to other linear algebraic groups, including
015            the Lorentz and symplectic groups.
016            Our goal behind these characterizations is to define equivariant machine learning
017            models. In particular, we focus on the sparse vector estimation problem. This
018            problem has been broadly studied in the theoretical computer science literature,
019
               and explicit spectral methods, derived by techniques from sum-of-squares, can be
               shown to recover sparse vectors under certain assumptions. Our numerical results
020
               show that the proposed equivariant machine learning models can learn spectral
021
               methods that outperform the best theoretically known spectral methods in some
022            regimes. The experiments also suggest that learned spectral methods can solve the
023            problem in settings that have yet to be theoretically analyzed.
024            This is an example of a promising direction in which theory can inform machine
025            learning models and machine learning models can inform theory.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Many recent theoretical and applied efforts have focused on the implementation of symmetries and
031   other structural constraints in the design of machine learning models. This is the case of graph
032   neural networks Scarselli et al. (2008); Maron et al. (2019), geometric deep learning Bronstein
033
      et al. (2021); Weiler et al. (2021), and AI for science Zhang et al. (2023). The goal is to design a
      hypothesis class of functions with good inductive bias that is aligned with the theoretical framework
034
      of the physical, mathematical, or algorithmic objects it aims to represent. This includes respecting
035
      coordinate freedoms Villar et al. (2023a), conservation laws Alet et al. (2021), or internal symmetries
036   (e.g. in the implicit neural representations framework Lim et al. (2023)). Symmetries have also been
037   used to provide interpretability to learned data representations Suau et al. (2023); Gupta et al. (2023).
038   Mathematically, it has been shown that imposing symmetries can improve the generalization error
039   and sample complexity of machine learning models Elesedy (2021b); Wang et al. (2021b); Elesedy
040   (2021a); Bietti et al. (2021); Petrache & Trivedi (2024); Tahmasebi & Jegelka (2023); Huang et al.
041   (2024).
042   In this work, we focus on implementing equivariant functions of tensor inputs and tensor outputs.
043   Tensors are the subjects of study in many theoretical computer science and applied mathematics
044   problems, including tensor factorization or decomposition Rabanser et al. (2017), and planted tensor
045   models Hopkins et al. (2016). Many of the algorithms to address these problems have underlying
046   symmetries like the ones we study here. Tensors also have a broad set of uses in the natural sciences,
047   where tensor-valued data appears as polarizations Melrose & Stoneham (1977), permeabilities
048   Durlofsky (1991), and stresses Levitas et al. (2019), for instance. Finally, tensors are the preferred
049   way to represent data for machine learning, where both models and multidimensional data with
050
      batches, channels, etc, are implemented as tensors. Sometimes, these tensor objects have underlying
      symmetries, like in implicit neural representations Li et al. (2022).
051
052   In this paper, we consider classical Lie groups acting diagonally on tensors. The groups we study
053   arise naturally in physics and other settings, including the orthogonal group O(d) (which typically
      appears in coordinate transformations), the indefinite orthogonal group O(s, k − s) (which includes

      as a particular case the Lorentz group, a fundamental group for special relativity), and the symplectic
055   group Sp(d) (the underlying group in much of classical and quantum mechanics).

057   A variety of methods can be used for implementing invariances or equivariances, including group
058
      convolutions Cohen & Welling (2016; 2017); Wang et al. (2021a), irreducible representations Fuchs
      et al. (2020); Kondor (2018); Weiler et al. (2018); Cohen et al. (2018); Weiler & Cesa (2019),
059
      constraints on optimization Finzi et al. (2021), canonicalization Kaba et al. (2023), and invariant
060
      theory Gripaios et al. (2021); Haddadin (2022); Villar et al. (2021); Blum-Smith & Villar (2023);
061   Villar et al. (2023b). This work is closer to the line of research that constructs explicit equivariant
062   functions from invariant features, and it generalizes results from Villar et al. (2021) to tensors.
063
064
      Closest to us is the concurrent work of Kunisky, Moore, and Wein on tensor cumulants Kunisky
      et al. (2024). Their focus is primarily on symmetric tensors, although they show that O(d)-invariant
065
      polynomials on symmetric tensors can be turned into O(d)-invariant polynomials over general tensors
066
      by symmetrizing over O(d). Our results for equivariant tensor polynomials are slightly more general
067   for inputs of tensors of different orders and parities as well as handling the indefinite orthogonal
068   group and the symplectic group.
069
070
      As an application, we consider the problem of sparse vector estimation. Let there be a sparse vector
      v0 and vectors v1 , . . . , vd−1 sampled independently at random from some prior distribution. If we
071
      are given an orthonormal basis w0 , . . . , wd−1 of span({v0 , · · · , vd−1 }), can we recover the sparse
072
      vector v0 ? The problem has roots in tensor PCA, also known as the spiked tensor model Montanari
073   & Richard (2014), as well as dictionary learning Spielman et al. (2012). Solutions for this problem
074   using sum-of-squares and spectral methods were investigated in Barak et al. (2014) and further
075   improvements were made in Hopkins et al. (2015; 2016); Ge & Ma (2015); Mao & Wein (2022).
076
      In Hopkins et al. (2016), the authors propose an algorithm that constructs a d × d matrix A, then
077
      uses the top eigenvector of A to estimate v0 . They prove that this method recovers the planted vector
078
      under certain assumptions. A crucial observation is that the input to the problem is any orthonormal
079   basis w0 , . . . , wd−1 of span({v0 , . . . , vd−1 }). This implies that a function that estimates v0 should
080   be invariant to certain transformations of the input basis. In this paper, we learn algorithms to recover
081   planted sparse vectors from data. To this end, we use a machine learning model that learns an
082   equivariant 2-tensor Â from data, and the estimator for the planted sparse vector v̂ is obtained using
083   Â’s top eigenvector following the same procedure as Hopkins et al. (2016). We empirically show that
084   this approach can learn spectral methods for sparse vector recovery that can work under more general
085   assumptions than the state-of-the-art.
086
      The approach employed here can be seen as a particular case of the more ambitious research program
087
      of algorithmic alignment or algorithmic reasoning Veličković & Blundell (2021); Gavranović et al.
088   (2024). In algorithmic alignment contexts, researchers design machine learning models that match
089   known algorithmic strategies that are known to succeed at solving certain problems, such as dynamic
090   programming Xu et al. (2020); Dudzik & Veličković (2022). Here, the machine learning approach
091   is structurally aligned with equivariant spectral methods arising from sum-of-squares. We believe
092   that the connection between sum-of-squares and machine learning (in particular equivariant machine
093   learning and graph neural networks) is a promising direction to be explored.
094
095   1.1   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
096
097   We provide a generic recipe to define equivariant machine learning models mapping from tensors
098   to tensors. To this end, we give explicit parameterizations for polynomials (Sec. 3) and analytic
099   functions with globally convergent Taylor series (Sec. 4) from tuples of tensor inputs to tensor outputs
100   that are equivariant with respect to the orthogonal (Sec. 3), indefinite orthogonal (which includes
101   Lorentz), and symplectic groups (Sec. 4). This generalizes the existing results of Villar et al. (2021)
102   and leverages the tensor equivariant theory Appleby et al. (1987); Jeffreys (1973); Roe Goodman
103   (2009) into a format useful for machine learning frameworks. On first reading and for those primarily
104
      interested in practical applications, we suggest focusing on Corollary 1 in Section 3 from which all
      our experiments follow.
105
106   As a proof of concept, we applied the resulting models to learning algorithms for the sparse vector
107   recovery problem (Section 5). Our learned algorithm outperforms state-of-the-art sum-of-squares
      methods for this problem in several regimes with both synthetic data and an image denoising problem.

      5     N UMERICAL E XPERIMENTS
363
      With the preceding theory in place, we can define a machine learning model to learn a new algorithm
364
      of interest. The sum of squares solution to the sparse vector recovery problem reduces to finding
365
      the optimal O(d)-equivariant function with vector inputs and a 2(+) -tensor output, and the form of
366   this function is given by Corollary 1. The learned algorithms outperform state-of-the-art methods
367   for this problem in several regimes, and operate in settings where theoretical guarantees have yet
368   to be developed. As such, they may point towards conjectures for more general classes of spectral
369   algorithms. The code is open-source and will be released after anonymous review.

373   We consider the problem of finding a planted sparse vector in a linear subspace. This problem was

      orthonormal basis of span{v0 , . . . , vd−1 }. The problem is to recover v from S.

      6    D ISCUSSION
524
525
526   This paper provides a characterization of polynomial functions from multiple tensor inputs to tensor
527   outputs that are equivariant with respect to the diagonal action by classical Lie groups, including the
528   orthogonal group, the symplectic group, and the Lorentz group.
529   Our main goal behind this characterization is to define equivariant machine learning models. We
530   applied the resulting models to learning algorithms for the sparse vector recovery problem. In the
531   spirit of the neural algorithmic reasoning framework Veličković & Blundell (2021), the proposed
532   machine learning methods are “aligned” to known algorithms with provable performance guarantees.
533   The learned algorithms outperform state-of-the-art algorithms for this problem in several regimes,
534   including those where there are no known algorithms with theoretical guarantees.
535   The application of our characterization is especially useful when the input consists of 1(+) -tensors,
536   and the output is a k(+) -tensor. For these problems, a parameterization based on invariant scalars,
537   similar to Villar et al. (2021), is available and easy to implement. For problems where the input
538   tensors have higher order, the implementation would be less efficient. We also assume no additional
539   structure on the relationship between the input tensors; in those situations, other techniques may be
      required.

1227   F     G ENERALIZATION TO OTHER LINEAR ALGEBRAIC GROUPS
1228
1229   In this section, we will show how Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 can be extended to the indefinite
1230   orthogonal and the symplectic group as Theorem 2 and Corollary 2.
1231   The main idea to extend Theorem 1 to other groups is to use some form of averaging. On O(d), the
1232   compactness guarantees the existence of a Haar probability measure. However, to apply the same
1233   trick over non-compact groups such as O(s, d − s) and Sp(d), we need to use technical machinery to
1234   imitate the averaging strategy.
1235
       First, we introduce some definitions and examples regarding complex and real linear algebraic groups.
1236   The main point will be to establish how to get a compact subgroup over which to average. Basically
1237   the results will generalize to real linear algebraic groups such that their complexifications have a
1238   Zariski-dense compact subgroup. For instance reductive connected complex algebraic groups satisfy
1239   this assumption. Second, we prove a generalization of Theorem 1 for complex linear algebraic groups
1240   with a Zariski-dense compact subgroup acting on complex tensors. Third, we prove a generalization
1241   of Theorem 1 for real linear algebraic groups that are compact or such that their complexification has
       a Zariski-dense compact subgroup. Finally, we prove Corollary 2.
