011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate mathematical reasoning with Large Language Models (LLMs) is crucial
014            in revolutionizing domains that heavily rely on such reasoning. However, LLMs
015
               often encounter difficulties in certain aspects of mathematical reasoning, leading to
               flawed reasoning and erroneous results. To mitigate these issues, we introduce a
016
               novel mechanism, the Chain of Self-Correction (CoSC), specifically designed to
017
               embed self-correction as an inherent ability in LLMs, enabling them to validate
018            and rectify their own results. The CoSC mechanism operates through a sequence
019            of self-correction stages. In each stage, the LLMs generate a program to address
020            a given problem, execute this program using program-based tools to obtain an
021            output, subsequently verify this output. Based on the verification, the LLMs
022            either proceed to the next correction stage or finalize the answer. This iterative
023            self-correction process allows the LLMs to refine its reasoning steps and improve
024            the accuracy of its mathematical reasoning. To enable the CoSC mechanism
025            at a low cost, we employ a two-phase finetuning approach. In the first phase,
026
               the LLMs are trained with a relatively small volume of seeding data generated
               from GPT-4, establishing an initial CoSC capability. In the second phase, the
027
               CoSC capability is further enhanced by training with a larger volume of self-
028
               generated data using the trained model in the first phase, without relying on the
029            paid GPT-4. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate that CoSC significantly
030            improves performance on traditional mathematical datasets among existing open-
031            source LLMs. Notably, our CoSC-Code-34B model achieved a 53.5% score on
032            MATH, the most challenging mathematical reasoning dataset in the public domain,
033            surpassing the performance of well-established models such as ChatGPT, GPT-4,
034            and even multi-modal LLMs like GPT-4V, Gemini-1.0 Pro, and Gemini-1.0 Ultra.
035            It’s important to note that, unlike these proprietary models, our CoSC performs
036            inference in a zero-shot manner, without the need for demonstrations. The code
037
               and data for this work will be released once this paper is accepted.

151   2.1   LLM S FOR M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
152
153
      Mathematical reasoning (Liu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2024b; Toshniwal
      et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024) is a challenging reasoning task for LLMs, which
154
      requires the ability to understand mathematical concepts, computation and multi-round reasoning.
155
      Existing mathematical reasoning approaches can be broadly classified into three categories: (1)
156   Prompting methods (Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b) focus on extracting
157   the inherent mathematical reasoning skills of LLMs by utilizing well-crafted prompting strategies
158   during inference. Notably, they leverage the existing knowledge in LLMs without the need for
159   parameter updates. (2) Pretraining methods (Azerbayev et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024)
160   pre-train LLMs on large-scale corpora containing mathematical problems and related content with
161   language modeling objectives. The goal is to train a base foundation language model as a platform
      for mathematical domain. (3) Finetuning methods (Luo et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023;


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Gou et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2024; Mitra et al., 2024) refine the mathematical
163   reasoning capabilities of LLMs by offering more targeted training, which is inspired by distillation
164   techniques (Furlanello et al., 2018; Hsieh et al., 2023). Central to this approach is the generation
165   of high-quality question-response pair datasets, which needs the assistance of complexity-based
166   chain-of-thoughts prompting or tools-based augmentation. In this paper, we propose a Chain of
167   Self-Correction (CoSC) along this direction that incorporates an iterative self-correction process into
168   datasets generation.
169
170   2.2   E XISTING METHODS RELATED TO S ELF -C ORRECTION IN LLM S
171
172   Mathematical reasoning poses a significant challenge due to its demand for precise multi-round
173   logical reasoning to solve problems. The potential for errors increases with each reasoning step,
174
      making it crucial for LLMs to have the ability to self-correct in order to produce accurate results.
      There are some recent studies (Chen et al., 2023b; Gou et al., 2023a; Lightman et al., 2023; Huang
175
      et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024b) attempt to enable large language models to perform self-correction
176
      by either prompting methods or fine-tuning methods. For prompting methods, they can correct their
177   responses when interacting with external tools (Gou et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023b), such as search
178   engines and calculators, or designing complex prompts (Chen et al., 2024b). Notably, prompting
179   methods can be orthogonally combined with finetuning methods. For finetuning methods, previous
180   works (Yu et al., 2024; An et al., 2023) only model single-round correction during the training stage,
181   and perform verification in a straightforward manner during the inference stage.
182
183   2.3   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN THE PROPOSED METHOD AND EXISTING SELF - CORRECTION
184         TECHNIQUES
185
186   The existing self-correction research can be broadly classified into two categories: prompt-based
187
      methods (Gou et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023b; 2024b) and SFT-based methods (Yu et al., 2024;
      An et al., 2023). Our approach falls under the SFT-based category and fundamentally differs from
188
      prompt-based methods. Specifically, prompt-based methods leverage the intrinsic capabilities of
189
      large language models (LLMs) for mathematical reasoning. In contrast, our method embeds the chain
190   of self-correction, a strong reasoning mechanism, as an inherent capability in the LLMs through
191   parameter fine-tuning. Furthermore, compared to existing SFT-based methods, our approach models
192   mathematical reasoning as a multi-round procedure during the training stage. In addition, these SFT-
193   based works (Yu et al., 2024; An et al., 2023) perform the verification process in a straightforward
194   manner, whereas our work conducts verification in a step-by-step manner by generating intermediate
195   verification steps. Similar to the essence of the CoT approach, our model first verifies whether the
196   generated code aligns with the problem description and then verifies whether the obtained result
197   is consistent with the problem description. This step-by-step verification strategy significantly
198
      improves the verification process in self-correction. The excellent performance of our method on
      the challenging mathematical problem tasks, such as MATH and GSM8k, clearly demonstrates its
199
      effectiveness compared to the existing SFT-based methods (Yu et al., 2024; An et al., 2023).

204   3.1   OVERVIEW
205
206   We propose a Chain of Self-Correction (CoSC) to address mathematical questions in a self-correction
207
      manner. Given a mathematical question denoted as q, the CoSC mechanism initiates the process
      by generating a program p that comprises a Python function. The program p is then executed
208
      using a Python interpreter to produce an output o. However, the program p may contain bugs or
209
      incorrect intermediate code due to potential misinterpretations of the question q, leading to erroneous
210   outputs. Unlike existing methods that either base their reasoning on incorrect intermediate content
211   or halt reasoning upon encountering an error, the CoSC mechanism introduces a self-correction
212   rationale during the reasoning process. After the generation of program p and its output o, the
213   CoSC model analyzes them and generates a verification v. This verification includes suggestions
214   for improvements to ensure the consistency of the program p and its output o with the mathematical
215   question q, respectively. Following this, the CoSC mechanism draws a conclusion c based on these
      suggestions, which is used to either refine the program p or generate the final answer. This process is

343   GPT-4 for annotating them is expensive. Therefore, we use the seed model after CoSC foundational
344   learning to resample the questions in the datasets multiple times, which further improves the model
345
      generalization ability in answering questions. Specifically, we use the CodeLLaMA-34B model
      which has the best performance after CoSC foundational learning to perform dense nucleus-sampling
346
      on 16k training data questions. Each question in the MATH and GSM8K training datasets is sampled
347
      64 times. We filter out the correct answers based on whether they match the ground-truth.
348
349   Dense question sampling. Similarly, there are multiple ways to ask a mathematical question. To
350   improve the model generalization ability of question understanding, we use the data from Meta-
351
      Math (Yu et al., 2023) to generalize the questions. Each question in MetaMATH is rewritten by simply
      rephrasing it, self-verifying the conditions in the question, adding if-then questions to reverse the
352
      question conditions, etc. Specifically, we use the CodeLLaMA-34B model with CoSC foundational
353
      learning to perform nucleus-sampling on the questions, sampling each question once, and filtering
354   out data points based on whether they match the ground-truth.
355
356   In the end, we obtain a total of 339k data points, including 37k seeding data generated from GPT-4
357
      and 302k generated from the CodeLLaMA-34B model with CoSC foundational learning. Then, we
      adopt them to train models from scratch to obtain our final CoSC model.
358

360   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
361
      By using 339k data points, including 37k seeding data and 302k self-generated data, we fine-tune
362
      the base models of LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023) and CodeLLaMA (Roziere et al., 2023) to
363   obtain our CoSC and CoSC-Code, respectively. They have different parameter sizes, such as 7B,
364   13B, and 34B. All models use full-scale fine-tuning. We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning
365   rate of 2e-5 for all models, with a batch size set to 128, training for 1 epoch. To enable training,
366   we use DeepSpeed ZeRO stage 3 (Rajbhandari et al., 2021) and Flash-Attention 2 (Dao, 2023) to
367   optimize the model’s memory usage. During inference, we set a maximum of 3 calls to the Python
368   interpreter and a maximum token length of 2048. The GPT-4 version for CoSC data generation is
369   gpt-4-0613. Our experiments train the models in 7B size with 8 NVIDIA A800 80GB GPUs and
370   train the models in 13B and 34B with 16 NVIDIA A800 80GB GPUs.

      first phase to generate additional data without relying on GPT-4. This approach leads to further

      improvements in performance on the MATH dataset from 42.3% to 47.6% and 47.0% to 50.3%, for

      to embed self-correction as an inherent capability in LLMs, leading to significant improvements in

      mathematical reasoning performance.

      5     C ONCLUSION
523
      In conclusion, our Chain of Self-Correction (CoSC) mechanism equips Large Language Models
524
      (LLMs) with the ability to autonomously validate and refine their outputs. This mechanism facilitates
525
      a sequence of self-correction stages that progressively refine the reasoning process, leading to
526   enhanced accuracy in mathematical reasoning. Our two-phase finetuning approach, involving initial
527   training with proprietary model-generated seeding data and subsequent training with self-generated
528   data, effectively enables the implementation of CoSC at a low cost. Through extensive experiments,
529   we have demonstrated the remarkable performance improvement that CoSC brings to traditional
530   mathematical datasets when compared to existing open-source LLMs, even advanced close-sourced
531   non-multi-modal LLMs, such as ChatGPT, GPT-4 and multi-modal LLMs like GPT-4V, Gemini-1.0
532   Pro, and Gemini-1.0 Ultra. We believe that our CoSC mechanism can provide valuable insights for
533   future research and contribute to the ongoing advancement of LLMs across various domains.
534   Ethics statement. We experiment on two mathematical datasets, including GSM8K and MATH, both
535   of which use MIT License code. The prompts used in these experiments are listed in Appendix A,
536   and we want to emphasize that none of the prompts contain any words that discriminate against any
537   individual or group. Furthermore, prompts would not negatively impact anyone’s safety in this work.
538   Reproducibility statement. We provide part of the codes and some seeding data. We will provide
539   the remaining codes and data of CoSC for reproducibility upon the acceptance of the paper.

1315   Our CoSC framework comprises two main components: verification and correction. The verification
1316   component identifies potential erroneous reasoning, while the correction component generates im-
1317   proved reasoning to address the issues identified in the verification step. To evaluate the effectiveness
1318   of these components, we conducted ablation studies. We provide a detailed analysis of the benefits
1319
       derived from both modules, reporting the accuracy of the verification module and the error reduction
       achieved by the correction module. The accuracy of the verification module reflects how precisely it
1320
       identifies errors, whereas the error reduction of the correction module measures the rate at which
1321
       errors are corrected from one round to the next. Specifically, it examines the proportion of solutions
1322   deemed incorrect in the i-th round that are successfully corrected in the (i + 1)-th round.
1323

1326
       our CoSC-Code is still capable of successfully reducing errors by over 25%. This confirms the

1379

1382   tuning, achieves superior results on both datasets. These findings suggest that for open-source LLMs,
1383   few-shot prompting alone is insufficient to effectively enable self-correction. The lack of significant
1384
       gains from prompting further underscores the limitations of relying solely on in-context examples.
       Therefore, we argue that embedding self-correction as an inherent capability through fine-tuning is
1385
       essential for truly endowing LLMs with robust self-correction abilities.
1386
1387   Moreover, by integrating self-correction directly into the training process, our approach allows models
1388   to perform self-correction autonomously in a zero-shot setting during inference, eliminating the need
1389   for external feedback or few-shot demonstrations. This self-correction mechanism enables even
1390
       weaker LLMs to achieve significant improvements in mathematical reasoning—enhancements that are
       unattainable through prompting methods alone. Additionally, our CoSC framework is open-source,
1391
       making these advancements accessible to the broader research community. We believe this represents
1392
       a pivotal step toward democratizing advanced reasoning capabilities and fostering further innovation.
