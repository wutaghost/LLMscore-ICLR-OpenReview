009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) methods are emerging as
012            a way to fine-tune diffusion models (DMs) for visual generation. However, com-
013            monly used on-policy strategies are limited by the generalization capability of the
014            reward model, while off-policy approaches require large amounts of difficult-to-
015            obtain paired human-annotated data, particularly in visual generation tasks. To
016            address the limitations of both on- and off-policy RLHF, we propose a preference
017            optimization method that aligns DMs with preferences without relying on reward
018
               models or paired human-annotated data. Specifically, we introduce a Semi-Policy
               Preference Optimization (SePPO) method. SePPO leverages previous checkpoints
019
               as reference models while using them to generate on-policy reference samples,
020
               which replace вАЬlosing imagesвАЭ in preference pairs. This approach allows us to opti-
021            mize using only off-policy вАЬwinning images.вАЭ Furthermore, we design a strategy for
022            reference model selection that expands the exploration in the policy space. Notably,
023            we do not simply treat reference samples as negative examples for learning. Instead,
024            we design an anchor-based criterion to assess whether the reference samples are
025            likely to be winning or losing images, allowing the model to selectively learn from
026            the generated reference samples. This approach mitigates performance degradation
027            caused by the uncertainty in reference sample quality. We validate SePPO across
028            both text-to-image and text-to-video benchmarks. SePPO surpasses all previous
029
               approaches on the text-to-image benchmarks and also demonstrates outstanding
               performance on the text-to-video benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Text-to-visual models have become a crucial component of the AIGC (AI-generated content) industry,
035
      with the denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020; Kingma et al., 2021) being
036   the most widely used technology. However, current pre-trained text-to-visual models often fail to
037   adequately align with human requirements. As a result, recent works (Liang et al., 2024a; Black et al.,
038   2024; Wallace et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) adopt reinforcement learning (RL)-based approaches as
039   the post-training process to better satisfy human needs, namely reinforcement learning from human
040   feedback (RLHF).
041
      These methods can generally be divided into two categories: on-policy methods and off-policy
042   methods. Similar to large language models (LLMs), in the post-training of diffusion models, on-
043   policy models use a reward model (RM) to score output and then backpropagate the policy gradients
044   based on the scoring results. A typical approach is DDPO (Black et al., 2024), which utilizes vision
045   language models as the reward model to improve the prompt-image alignment. Even though on-policy
046   method is approved to be helpful in the Natural Language Processing (NLP) domain (Dong et al.,
047   2024). However, aside from the existing issues with on-policy methods, such as reward hacking, which
048   can lead to model collapse (Denison et al., 2024), in the text-to-visual task, despite the availability of
049   numerous evaluation models, it is difficult to find a solution that can provide comprehensive feedback
050
      on all aspects of the visual content (Kim et al., 2024). Additionally, constructing an effective and
      efficient reward model is extremely challenging and heavily dependent on the collection of costly
051
      paired feedback data.
052
053   Another approach is to utilize a fixed set of preference data (generated by human or other models) as
      training data, which is called off-policy method. This method allows the trained model to achieve

054
      a distribution similar to that of the preference data. An example is Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al.,
055   2024), which uses the Pick-a-Pic (Kirstain et al., 2023) dataset that contains paired preference image
056   data generated by various models with human ratings. Apparently, off-policy methods also depend
057   on human feedback data with positive and negative sample pairs, which requires additional efforts,
058   and their results are usually inferior to those of on-policy methods (Tang et al., 2024). Thus, in this
059   paper, our aim is to build a preference optimization method that can mitigate the issues of on-policy
060   and off-policy, allowing the diffusion model to align with preferences without using a reward model
061   or paired human-annotated data.
062   To enable models to learn preferences from human feedback, the construction of positive and negative
063   examples is crucial. However, in most datasets, there is usually only one sample in each data point,
064   which typically serves as a positive example. Therefore, in the absence of a reward model, our initial
065   consideration is how to construct appropriate negative samples. Several previous works, such as
066   SPIN-Diffusion (Yuan et al., 2024a) and DITTO (Shaikh et al., 2024), utilize the previous checkpoints
067   to generate the so-called вАЬlosingвАЭ samples and then use preference optimization for model training.
068   However, these methods cannot guarantee that the samples generated from the previous checkpoints
069
      are necessarily вАЬlosingвАЭ samples relative to the current model.
070   To address this issue, we propose a method called Semi-Policy Preference Optimization (SePPO). In
071   our method, the positive samples are sampled from the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset. In order
072   to obtain sufficiently good negative samples that are not too far from the current model distribution,
073   the вАЬlosingвАЭ samples are generated by the reference model. Unlike SPIN-Diffusion, where the
074   reference model is set to use the latest checkpoint, and DITTO, where the reference model is the
075
      initial model. In our SePPO, the reference models are sampled from all the previous checkpoints. To
      be specific, we first study the selection strategies for the reference model, conducting experiments
076
      using three different approaches: (1) always selecting the initial checkpoint as the reference model,
077
      (2) selecting the checkpoint saved from the previous iteration, and (3) randomly selecting from all
078   previous checkpoints. We found that as the number of training steps increases, compared to always
079   using the initial checkpoint, selecting the checkpoint from the previous iteration makes the reference
080   model less prone to overfitting and yields better results. Moreover, randomly selecting from all
081   previous checkpoints produces similar results to selecting the checkpoint from the previous iteration
082   and leads to a more stable training process overall.
083
      In addition, to determine whether the samples generated from the reference model are genuinely
084   вАЬlosingвАЭ images or вАЬwinningвАЭ images relative to the current model. We further design a strategy to
085   determine whether the sampled examples are positive or negative samples, namely Anchor-based
086   Adaptive Flipper (AAF). If we have a winning data point for the model to learn from and the reference
087   model has a higher probability than the current model to generate this winning data point, then the
088   probability of sampling a winning data point from the reference model distribution will be greater
089   than that of generating a losing data point. In other words, in this case, the вАЬlosing imagesвАЭ are not
090   truly negative samples for the current model. This could negatively affect the model if we continue
091   to use the direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) or SPIN (Yuan et al., 2024a)
092
      loss functions. Therefore, we design a strategy where, if the reference model has a higher probability
      than the current model of generating the winning data point, the model will learn from both the
093
      winning data point and the samples generated by the reference model. This not only helps avoid the
094
      negative effects of incorrectly judging sample quality but also increases the chances of the model
095   outperforming the results of SFT to some extent.
096
097   In summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
098
099         1. We design an iterative preference optimization method called Semi-Policy Preference Opti-
100
               mization (SePPO). Our model can achieve preference alignment without human annotation
               and a reward model, which reduces labor cost and infrastructure burden;
101
102         2. We develop a strategy that first samples the reference model in each iteration, which enables
103            to expand the space of policy exploration. We then design a criterion to evaluate the
104            quality of generated responses and adjust the preference optimization based on this quality
105            information, a process termed Anchor-based Adaptive Flipper (AAF);
106
107         3. SePPO exceeds all previous optimization methods on the text-to-image benchmark, and its
               effectiveness has also been validated on the text-to-video datasets.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Self-Improvement. Self-improvement methods use iterative sampling to improve pre-trained models,
111   which has the potential to eliminate the need for an expert annotator such as a human or more
112   advanced models. A recent work INPO (Zhang et al., 2024) directly approximates the Nash policy
113   (the optimal solution) of a two-player game on a human-annotated preference dataset. Concurrently,
114   the demonstrated self-improvement methods (Shaikh et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) align pre-trained
115   models only using supervised learning dataset (one demonstrated response for each prompt) without
116   using reward models and human-annotated data pairs. Specifically, it takes the demonstrated response
117   as the winner and generates the loser by reference models. DITTO (Shaikh et al., 2024) fixes the
118
      reference model as the initial model and works well on small datasets. SPIN (Chen et al., 2024)
      takes the latest checkpoint as the reference model and uses it to generate responses in each iteration.
119
      However, these approaches have the following shortcomings. First, they only focus on transformer-
120
      based models for the text modality. Second, the selection of reference models is fixed, which has
121   limited space for policy exploration. Third, either a human-annotated preference dataset is necessarily
122   required or they are built on a strong assumption that the demonstrated responses are always preferred
123   to the generated responses. In fact, the responses generated by the models are not necessarily bad and
124   do not always need to be rejected.
125
      RM-Free PO for Diffusion Model Alignment. DPO-style methods (Rafailov et al., 2024) use
126
      a closed-form expression between RMs and the optimal policy to align pre-trained models with
127   human preference. Thus, no RM is needed during the training process. Diffusion-DPO is first
128   proposed in Wallace et al. (2024). Based on offline datasets with labeled human preference, it shows
129   promising results on text-to-image diffusion generation tasks. Diffusion-RPO (Gu et al., 2024)
130   considers semantic relationships between responses for contrastive weighting. MaPO (Hong et al.,
131   2024) uses margin-aware information, and removes the reference model. However, only off-policy
132   data is considered and human-annotated data pairs are necessarily required with high labor costs.
133   To involve on-policy data and minimize human annotation costs, self-improvement methods are being
134   explored. SPIN-Diffusion (Yuan et al., 2024a) adapts the SPIN method to text-to-image generation
135   tasks with diffusion models. It shows high efficiency in data utilization. However, first, it has the
136   issues mentioned above as a self-improvement method. Second, only the text-to-image generation
137   task is considered in all previous works.

524   6.1       L IMITATIONS
525
      First, in diffusion models, the theoretical analysis of exploration in policy space constrained by
526
      reference models is an open problem. Second, the performance may be further improved if the pixel

530   6.2       C ONCLUSION
531
      Without using reward models or human-annotated paired data, we have developed a Semi-Policy
532
      Preference Optimization (SePPO) method, which takes previous checkpoints as reference models
533
      and uses them to generate on-policy reference samples, which replace вАЬlosing imagesвАЭ in preference
534   pairs. In addition, we design a strategy for reference model selection that expands the exploration in
535   the policy space. Furthermore, instead of directly taking reference samples as negative examples, we
536   propose an Anchor-based Adaptive Flipper to determine whether the reference samples are likely to
537   be winning or losing images, which allows the model to selectively learn from the generated reference
538   samples. In text-to-image benchmarks, SePPO achieved a 21.57 PickScore, exceeding all previous
539   approaches on the SD-1.5 model. In addition, SePPO performs better than SFT on text-to-video
      benchmarks.
