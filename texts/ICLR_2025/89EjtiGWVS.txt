009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Off-policy learning and evaluation scenarios leverage logged bandit feedback
012            datasets, which contain context, action, propensity score, and feedback for each
013            data point. These scenarios face significant challenges due to high variance and
014            poor performance with low-quality propensity scores and heavy-tailed reward
015            distributions. We address these issues by introducing a novel estimator based on
016            the log-sum-exponential (LSE) operator, which outperforms traditional inverse
017            propensity score estimators. our LSE estimator demonstrates variance reduction
018
               and robustness under heavy-tailed conditions. For off-policy evaluation, we derive
               upper bounds on the estimator’s bias and variance. In the off-policy learning
019
               scenario, we establish bounds on the regret—the performance gap between our
020
               LSE estimator and the optimal policy—assuming bounded (1 + ϵ)-th moment
021
               of weighted reward. Notably, we achieve a convergence rate of O(n−ϵ/(1+ϵ) ),
022            where n is the number of training samples for the regret bounds and ϵ ∈ [0, 1].
023            Theoretical analysis is complemented by comprehensive empirical evaluations
024            in both off-policy learning and evaluation scenarios, confirming the practical
025            advantages of our approach.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Off-policy learning and evaluation from logged data are important problems in Reinforcement
031   Learning (RL) theory and practice. The logged bandit feedback (LBF) dataset represents interaction
032   logs of a system with its environment, recording context, action, propensity score (i.e., the probability
033
      of action selection for a given context under the logging policy), and feedback (reward). It is used in
      many real applications, e.g., recommendation systems (Aggarwal, 2016; Li et al., 2011), personalized
034
      medical treatments (Kosorok & Laber, 2019; Bertsimas et al., 2017), and personalized advertising
035
      campaigns (Tang et al., 2013; Bottou et al., 2013). The literature has considered this setting from two
036   perspectives, off-policy evaluation (OPE) and off-policy learning (OPL). In off-policy evaluation, we
037   utilize the LBF dataset from a logging (behavioural) policy and an estimator, e.g., Inverse Propensity
038   Score (IPS), to evaluate (or estimate) the performance of a different target policy. In off-policy
039   learning we leverage the estimator and LBF dataset to learn an improved policy with respect to
040   logging policy.
041
      In both scenarios, OPL and OPE, the IPS estimator is proposed (Thomas et al., 2015; Swaminathan
042   & Joachims, 2015a). However, this estimator suffers from significant variance in many cases
043   (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983). To address this, some improved importance sampling estimators
044   have been proposed, such as the IPS estimator with the truncated ratio of policy and logging policy
045   (Ionides, 2008b), IPS estimator with truncated propensity score (Strehl et al., 2010), self-normalizing
046   estimator (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015b), exponential smoothing (ES) estimator (Aouali et al.,
047   2023), implicit exploration (IX) estimator (Gabbianelli et al., 2023) and power-mean (PM) estimator
048   (Metelli et al., 2021).
049   In addition to the significant variance issue of IPS estimators, there are two more challenges in real
050   problems: estimated propensity scores and heavy-tailed behaviour of weighted reward due to noise
051   or outliers. Previous works such as Swaminathan & Joachims (2015a), Metelli et al. (2021), and
052   Aouali et al. (2023) have made assumptions when dealing with LBF datasets. Specifically, these
053   works assume that rewards are not subject to perturbation (noise) and that true propensity scores are
      available. However, these assumptions may not hold in real-world scenarios.

      Noisy or heavy-tailed reward: three primary sources of noise in reward of LBF datasets can be
055   identified as (Wang et al., 2020): (1) inherent noise, arising from physical conditions during feedback
056   collection; (2) application noise, stemming from uncertainty in human feedback; and (3) adversarial
057   noise, resulting from adversarial perturbations in the feedback process. Furthermore, In addition to
058   noisy (perturbed) reward, a heavy-tailed reward can be observed in many real life applications, e.g.,
059   financial markets (Cont & Bouchaud, 2000) and web advertising (Park et al., 2013), the rewards do
060   not behave bounded and follows heavy-tailed distributions.1
061   Noisy (estimated) propensity scores: The access to the exact values of the propensity scores may not
062   be possible, for example, when human agents annotate the LBF dataset. In this situation, one may
063   settle for training a model to estimate the propensity scores. Then, the propensity score stored in the
064   LBF dataset can be considered a noisy version of the true propensity score.
065
      Therefore, there is a need for an estimator that can effectively manage the heavy-tailed condition and
066
      noisy rewards or propensity scores in the LBF dataset.

069   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
070
      In this work, we propose a novel estimator for off-policy learning and evaluation from the LBF dataset
071
      that outperforms existing estimators when dealing with estimated propensity scores and heavy-tailed
072
      or noisy weighted rewards. The contribution of our work is three-fold.
073
074   First, we propose a novel non-linear estimator based on the Log-Sum-Exponential (LSE) operator
075   which can be applied to both OPE and OPL scenarios. This LSE estimator effectively reduces variance
076
      and is applicable to, noisy propensity scores, heavy-tailed reward and noisy reward scenarios.
077   Second, we provide comprehensive theoretical guarantees for the LSE estimator’s performance in
078   OPE and OPL setup. In particular, we first provide bounds on the regret, i.e. the difference between
079   the LSE estimator performance and the true average reward, under mild assumptions. In particular,
080   our theoretical results hold under the heavy-tailed assumption on weighted reward. Furthermore, we
081   studied the convergence rate of regret under heavy-tailed assumption which also holds for unbounded
082
      reward. Then, we studied bias and variance analysis for the LSE estimator and the robustness of the
      LSE estimator under noisy and heavy-tailed reward scenarios.
083
084   Finally, we conducted a set of experiments on different datasets to show the performance of the LSE in
085   scenarios with true, estimated propensity scores and noisy reward in comparison with other estimators.
086   We observed an improvement in the performance of learning policy using LSE in comparison with
087   other state-of-the-art algorithms under different scenarios.

      We categorize the estimators based on their approach to reward estimation. Estimators that incorporate
143   reward estimation techniques are classified as model-based estimators. In contrast, those that
144   work without reward estimation are termed model-free estimators. Below, we review model-based
145   estimators, and model-free estimators. Furthermore, we study the estimators which are designed for
146   unbounded reward (heavy-tailed) scenarios in general RL scenarios.
147
      Model-free Estimators: In model-free estimators, e.g., IPS estimators, we have many challenges,
148
      including, high variance and heavy-tailed scenarios. Recently, many model-free estimators have
149   been proposed for high variance problems in model-free estimators (Strehl et al., 2010; Ionides,
150   2008b; Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015b; Aouali et al., 2023; Metelli et al., 2021; Neu, 2015; Aouali
151   et al., 2023; Metelli et al., 2021; Sakhi et al., 2024). However, under heavy-tailed or unbounded
152   reward scenario, the performance of these estimators degrade. In this work, our proposed LSE esti-
153   mator demonstrates robust performance even under heavy-tailed assumptions, backed by theoretical
154   guarantees.
155   Model-based Estimators: The direct method for off-policy learning from the LBF datasets is
156   based on the estimation of the reward function, followed by the application of a supervised learning
157   algorithm to the problem. However, this approach does not generalize well, as shown by Beygelzimer
158   & Langford (2009). A different approach where the direct method and the IPS estimator are
159   combined, i.e., doubly-robust, is introduced by Dudík et al. (2014). A different approach based
160   on policy optimization and boosted base learner is proposed to improve the performance in direct

524   7 C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
525   In this work, inspired by the log-sum-exponential operator, we proposed a novel estimator for
526   off-policy learning application. Subsequently, we conduct a comprehensive theoretical analysis of
527   the LSE estimator, including a study of bias and variance, along with an upper bound on regret
528   under heavy-tailed assumption. Furthermore, we explore the performance of our estimator in
529   scenarios involving estimated propensity scores or heavy-tailed weighted reward. Results from
530
      our experimental evaluation demonstrate that our estimator, guided by our theoretical framework,
      performs competitively compared to most of baseline estimators in off-policy learning and evaluation.
531
      In future work, we plan to study the effect of regularization on the LSE estimator from both theoretical
532
      and practical perspectives. Moreover, we envision extending the application of our estimator to more
533   challenging RL setups, (Chen & Jiang, 2022; Zanette et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2019a). Inspired by the
534   application of LSE operator in supervised learning for positive tilt (Li et al., 2023), we can explore
535   the performance of the LSE estimator for positive λ as future work.

       proposed by Kallus (2018). Other extensions of batch learning as a scenario for off-policy learning
979    have been studied, Papini et al. (2019) consider samples from different policies and Sugiyama et al.
980    (2007) propose Direct Importance Estimation, which estimates weights directly by sampling from
981    contexts and actions. Chen et al. (2019) introduced a convex surrogate for the regularized value
982    function based on the entropy of the target policy.
983
       Pessimism Method and Off-policy RL: The pessimism concept originally, introduced in offline
984
       RL(Buckman et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2021), aims to derive an optimal policy within Markov decision
985    processes (MDPs) by utilizing pre-existing datasets (Rashidinejad et al., 2022; 2021; Yin & Wang,
986    2021; Yan et al., 2023). This concept has also been adapted to contextual bandits, viewed as a specific
987    MDP instance. Recently, a ‘design-based’ version of the pessimism principle was proposed by Jin
988    et al. (2022) who propose a data-dependent and policy-dependent regularization inspired by a lower
989    confidence bound (LCB) on the estimation uncertainty of the augmented-inverse-propensity-weighted
990    (AIPW)-type estimators which also includes IPS estimators. Our work differs from that of Jin et al.
991    (2022) as our estimator is non-linear estimator. Note that for our theoretical analysis, we consider
992    heavy-tailed assumption for (1 + ϵ)-th moment for some ϵ ∈ [0, 1]. However, (Jin et al., 2022) also
993
       considers 3rd and 4th moments of weights bounded.
994    Action Embedding and Clustering: Due to the extreme bias and variance of IPS and doubly-robust
995    (DR) estimators in large action spaces, Saito & Joachims (2022) proposed using action embeddings
996    to leverage marginalized importance weights and address these issues. Subsequent studies, including
997    (Saito et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Sachdeva et al., 2023), have introduced alternative methods
998    to tackle the challenge of large action spaces. Our work can be integrated with these approaches
999
       to further mitigate the effects associated with large action spaces. We consider this combination as
       future work.
1000
1001   Individualized Treatment Effects: The individual treatment effect aims to estimate the expected
1002   values of the squared difference between outcomes (reward or feedback) for control and treated
1003   contexts (Shalit et al., 2017). In the individual treatment effect scenario, the actions are limited to two
1004   actions (treated/not treated) and the propensity scores are unknown (Shalit et al., 2017; Johansson
1005
       et al., 2016; Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Athey et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2019; Kennedy, 2020; Nie &
       Wager, 2021). Our work differs from this line of works by considering multiple action scenario and
1006
       assuming the access to propensity scores in the LBF dataset.
1007
1008   Noisy/Corrupted Rewards: Agnihotri et al. (2024) utilized offline data with noisy preference
1009   feedback as a warm-up step for online bandit learning. In linear bandits, Kveton et al. (2019)
1010   estimated a set of pseudo-rewards for each perturbed reward in the history and used it for reward
1011
       parameter estimation. Lee & Lim (2022) assumes a heavy-tailed noise variable on the observed
       rewards and proposes two exploration strategies that provide minimax regret guarantees for the
1012
       multi-arm bandit problem under the heavy-tailed reward noise. In the linear bandits, Kang et al.
1013
       (2024) Huang et al. (2024) tackles the issue of heavy-tailed noise on cost function by modifying the
1014   reward parameter estimation objective. The former one uses Huber loss for reward function parameter
1015   estimation and the latter one truncates the rewards. Zhong et al. (2021) and Xue et al. (2024) propose
1016   the median of means and truncation to handle the heavy-tailed noise in the observed rewards. In
1017   this work, we study the performance of our proposed estimator, the LSE estimator, under noisy and
1018   heavy-tailed reward.
1019
       Estimation of Propensity Scores: We can estimate the propensity score using different methods,
1020   e.g., logistic regression (D’Agostino Jr, 1998; Weitzen et al., 2004), generalized boosted models
1021   (McCaffrey et al., 2004), neural networks (Setoguchi et al., 2008), parametric modeling (Xie et al.,
1022   2019b) or classification and regression trees (Lee et al., 2010; 2011). Note that, as discussed in
1023   (Tsiatis, 2006; Shi et al., 2016), under the estimated propensity scores (noisy propensity score), the
1024   variance of the IPS estimator is reduced. In this work, we consider both true and estimated propensity
1025   scores, where the estimated propensity scores are modeled via Gamma noise. Our work differs from
       the line of works on the estimation methods of propensity scores.

1026
        Bandit and Reinforcement Learning under Heavy-tailed Distributions: Some works discussed
1027   the heavy-tailed reward in bandit learning (Medina & Yang, 2016; Bubeck et al., 2013; Shao et al.,
1028   2018; Lu et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2021). Furthermore, some works also discussed the heavy-tailed
1029   rewards in RL (Zhuang & Sui, 2021; Zhu et al., 2024). However, off-policy learning with LBF dataset
1030   under a heavy-tailed distribution of weighted reward is overlooked.
1031
       Mean-estimation under Heavy-tailed Distributions: In (Lugosi & Mendelson, 2019; 2021; Hop-
1032
       kins, 2018), the performance of median-of-means and trimmed mean estimators have been studied
1033   and the sub-Gaussian behavior of these estimators are studied. However, median-of-means estimator
1034   presents practical challenges in implementation: it requires additional computational resources for
1035   data partitioning and mean calculations, while also introducing discontinuities that can prevent
1036   gradient-based optimization methods.
1037
       Generalization Error under Heavy-tailed Assumption: There are also some works studied the
1038
       generalization error of supervised learning under unbounded loss functions, in particular, under
1039   heavy-tailed assumption via the PAC-Bayesian approach. Losses with heavier tails are studied by
1040   Alquier & Guedj (2018) where probability bounds (non-high probability) are developed. Using a
1041   different estimator than empirical risk, PAC-Bayes bounds for losses with bounded second and third
1042   moments are developed by Holland (2019). Notably, their bounds include a term that can increase
1043   with the number of samples n. Kuzborskij & Szepesvári (2019) and Haddouche & Guedj (2022) also
1044   provide bounds for losses with a bounded second moment. The bounds in (Haddouche & Guedj,
1045   2022) rely on a parameter that must be selected before the training data is drawn. Information-
1046   theoretic bounds based on the second moment of loss function suph∈H |ℓ(h, Z) − E[ℓ(h, Z̃)]| are
1047   also derived in (Lugosi & Neu, 2022). Furthermore, in (Lugosi & Mendelson, 2019, Section 4), the
1048   uniform bound via Rademacher complexity analysis over the L2 bounded function space is studied
1049
       for median-of-means estimator. In our work, we focus on generalization error and regret analysis of
       the LSE estimator as a non-linear estimator in OPL and OPE scenarios.
1050
1051   Heavy-tailed rewards in Bandits: Bandit learning with heavy-tailed reward distributions has been
1052   extensively studied. Bubeck et al. (2013) proposed Robust UCB, and Vakili et al. (2013) introduced
1053   DSEE as bandit algorithms with theoretical regret guarantees. Yu et al. (2018) proposed a bandit
1054   algorithm based on pure exploration with heavy-tailed reward distributions. Heavy-tailed reward
1055
       distributions are also studied in the context of linear bandits (Shao et al., 2018; Medina & Yang,
       2016). Dubey et al. (2020) proposed a decentralized algorithm for cooperative multi-agent bandits
1056
       when the reward distribution is heavy-tailed. Our work differs from this line of works by considering
1057
       heavy-tailed assumption on weighted reward.
1058
1059    Heavy-tailed rewards in RL: The challenge of heavy-tailed distributions in decision making has
1060   been studied for more than two decades (Georgiou et al., 1999; Hamza & Krim, 2001; Huang &
1061
       Zhang, 2017; Ruotsalainen et al., 2018). There is a significant amount of study in RL dealing
       with heavy tailed reward distribution (Zhu et al., 2023; Zhuang & Sui, 2021; Huang et al., 2024).
1062
       Moreover, big sparse rewards are a prominent issue in reinforcement learning (Park et al., 2022;
1063
       Agarwal et al., 2021; Dawood et al., 2023). In such scenarios, there is a far-reaching goal, possibly
1064   accompanied by sparse failure states in which the agent attains big positive and negative rewards
1065   respectively. For example in safe autonomous driving, accidents are so costly and, hence are assigned
1066   large negative rewards. They are also delayed and sparse, which means that they are observed after
1067   many steps with a lot of exploration in the environment (Kiran et al., 2021; Amini et al., 2020). This
1068   hinders the training and leads to an infeasible slow learning curve. A common approach to tackle
1069   this issue is reward shaping which inserts new engineered reward functions alongside the agent’s
1070   trajectory to provide guidelines for the agent (Kiran et al., 2021). This strategy may fail because it
1071   biases the model into the strategy hinted by the new rewards, which may not be the optimal solution
1072
       for the original problem. Moreover, the method of reward shaping will not necessarily avoid the
       low-probability high-value rewards, because the imputed rewards are mostly small and high-value
1073
       rewards still happen with low probability. Therefore, handling low-probability large reward is one of
1074
       the challenges in this field, which can be modeled by heavy-tailed distributions as discussed with
1075   more details in App. G.12.

4032
       We illustrate how heavy-tailed distributions can model outlier samples. Consider two sets of observa-
       tions, the first one from a normal distribution N (0, 2) which has an exponential tail, and the second
4033
       from a Lomax distribution L(1.5), which is heavy-tailed with ϵ = 0.5. Figure 9 depicts the histogram
4034
       of observed 10K samples from each distribution. We can observe that the Lomax distribution contains
4035   large, low-probability values (values around 400), but the total range for Gaussian observations is less
4036   than 10. The occurrence of sparse very low probability outlier values is possible by sampling from a
4037   heavy-tailed distribution like Lomax distribution. However, it’s does not hold for an exponential-tailed
4038   distribution like Gaussian. Hence, heavy-tailed distributions seem to be able to model scenarios with
4039   sparse large rewards or outliers, which is not possible using an exponential-tailed distribution. In the
4040   following, we discuss the heavy-tailed reward scenario in RL applications.
