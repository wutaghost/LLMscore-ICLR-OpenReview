011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The expansion of 5G networks has led to remarkable data volume and complex-
014            ity, introducing significant security challenges that require the implementation of
015            robust and scalable anomaly detection mechanisms. Traditional centralized ap-
016
               proaches pose privacy risks and scalability challenges due to the distributed nature
               of 5G infrastructures. Federated Learning (FL) offers a decentralized solution but
017
               often overlooks the importance of feature relevance and privacy preservation dur-
018
               ing model aggregation. This paper introduces a novel Feature-Aware Federated
019            framework that integrates feature importance into the aggregation process while
020            ensuring differential privacy. We employ integrated gradients to compute fea-
021            ture importance for each client, aggregate them globally with differential privacy
022            noise, and use these insights to weight model parameters during aggregation. Ad-
023            ditionally, we propose Dynamic Feature Importance Adaptation (DFIA) to update
024            feature importance occasionally, enhancing the model’s adaptability to evolving
025            data distributions. Experimental results demonstrate that our framework outper-
026            forms traditional federated approaches like FedAvg and FedProx in unsupervised
027
               anomaly detection tasks within 5G networks, achieving higher accuracy and ro-
               bustness while preserving data privacy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The emergence of fifth-generation (5G) networks marks a significant advancement in communica-
      tion technology, enabling high-speed, low-latency connectivity for diverse applications ranging from
034
      Internet of Things (IoT) devices to autonomous vehicles Farooqui et al. (2021); Storck & Duarte-
035
      Figueiredo (2020). This evolution brings about a massive influx of data generated at the network
036   edge, making centralized data processing impractical and raising significant privacy concerns Zhang
037   et al. (2021). Anomaly detection in distributed environments is critical for maintaining network se-
038   curity and performance. However, it has unique challenges due to the sensitive nature of the data
039   and the need for real-time analysis.
040
      In practical scenarios, obtaining labeled data for anomaly detection in 5G networks is challenging
041
      due to the dynamic and complex nature of network behaviors. Manual labeling is time-consuming
042   and often infeasible, leading to an increased reliance on unsupervised methods that can detect
043   anomalies without explicit labels (Yan et al., 2016). Unsupervised anomaly detection becomes es-
044   sential for identifying novel or emerging threats that were not present in the training data.
045
      Traditional centralized anomaly detection methods require aggregating all data at a central server,
046
      which is not feasible in 5G networks due to bandwidth limitations and privacy regulations such as
047
      the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promis-
048   ing solution, allowing multiple clients to collaboratively train a global model without sharing raw
049   data (McMahan et al., 2017). However, standard FL approaches like Federated Averaging (FedAvg)
050   (McMahan et al., 2017) and Federated Proximal (FedProx) (Li et al., 2020) assume homogeneous
051   data distributions and treat all features equally during model aggregation. This assumption over-
052   looks the heterogeneity inherent in client data and the varying importance of different features for
053   anomaly detection, especially in unsupervised settings where the model must discern patterns with-
      out guidance from labels.

054
      Moreover, in unsupervised anomaly detection, certain features may be more indicative of anomalies
055   than others. Ignoring feature relevance can reduce the model’s ability to detect anomalies effectively.
056   Additionally, while FL addresses data privacy at the data level, it does not inherently protect against
057   information leakage through model updates. Clients may inadvertently share sensitive information
058   through gradients or model parameters (Melis et al., 2019). Incorporating Differential Privacy (DP)
059   (Dwork et al., 2014) into FL can mitigate this risk by adding carefully calibrated noise to the shared
060   updates, balancing the trade-off between privacy and utility.
061   In this study, we propose a Feature-Aware Federated (FAFL) framework for unsupervised anomaly
062   detection in 5G networks. The key contributions of this paper are as follows:
063
064         1. Feature Importance-Aware Aggregation: We introduce a mechanism that computes fea-
065            ture importance using Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) at each client and
066            incorporates this information into the model aggregation process through attention weights.
067         2. Differential Privacy in Feature Aggregation: To preserve client privacy, we apply differ-
068            ential privacy when aggregating feature importance across clients, preventing the leakage
069            of sensitive information.
070
            3. Dynamic Feature Importance Adaptation (DFIA): We propose an adaptive strategy to
071            update feature importance periodically, allowing the global model to adjust to changing
072            data distributions in dynamic network environments.
073
074   Experiments conducted on real-world 5G network datasets demonstrate that the proposed FAFL
075   framework significantly outperforms traditional FL methods in terms of anomaly detection accuracy
076   and robustness, while also ensuring data privacy and scalability.
077   The structure of this paper is as follows: Section 2 reviews the relevant literature and prior studies.
078   Section 3 outlines the proposed methodology in detail. Section 4 describes the experimental setup
079   and configurations used in the study. Section 5 presents the findings, analysis, and discussion.
080   Section 6 provides a broader discussion of the findings. Finally, Section 7 concludes the paper and
081   suggests directions for future research.

      2.1   A NOMALY D ETECTION IN 5G N ETWORKS
086
087   Anomaly detection is crucial in network security to identify unusual patterns that may indicate ma-
088   licious activities or system faults. In the context of 5G networks, the distributed and heterogeneous
089   nature of the data poses significant challenges Sheikhi & Kostakos (2024). Various machine learn-
090   ing approaches have been proposed for anomaly detection, including supervised, unsupervised, and
091   semi-supervised methods (Chandola et al., 2009). Autoencoders have been widely used for unsu-
092   pervised anomaly detection due to their ability to learn data representations and detect deviations
093   (Said Elsayed et al., 2020).
094
095   2.2   F EDERATED L EARNING
096
      Federated Learning allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model while keeping
097
      the training data decentralized (McMahan et al., 2017). FedAvg is the seminal work in FL, where
098
      clients train local models and send their updates to a central server for aggregation. FedProx extends
099   FedAvg by introducing a proximal term to address data heterogeneity among clients (Li et al., 2020).
100   However, both methods treat all features equally and do not consider feature importance during
101   aggregation.
102
103   2.3   F EATURE I MPORTANCE AND I NTERPRETABILITY
104
105   Feature importance techniques play a pivotal role in interpreting machine learning models, particu-
106   larly in Federated Learning, where data privacy and decentralized data sources present unique chal-
107   lenges. Integrated Gradients (IG) is a gradient-based method that attributes changes in model output
      to variations in input features, enhancing the understanding of feature contributions (Sundararajan


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2017). In FL, focusing on relevant features can improve model performance, especially given
109   the heterogeneous nature of client data Nakanishi (2024). However, integrating feature importance
110   into the aggregation process remains a challenge due to the distributed and privacy-preserving nature
111   of FL.
112
113   2.4     D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY IN F EDERATED L EARNING
114
115   Differential Privacy provides a formal framework for quantifying privacy guarantees when releas-
116   ing information derived from sensitive data (Dwork et al., 2014). In FL, applying DP can prevent
117   information leakage through model updates (Geyer et al., 2017; Abadi et al., 2016). Techniques like
118
      adding Gaussian noise to model updates have been proposed, but balancing privacy and utility is
      non-trivial.
119
120
121
      2.5     DYNAMIC M ODEL A DAPTATION
122
      Adapting models to changing data distributions is essential in dynamic environments like 5G net-
123   works. Techniques such as continual learning and adaptive optimization have been explored (Parisi
124   et al., 2019). However, dynamically updating feature importance within FL frameworks has not
125   been extensively studied.
126
      Therefore, In response to the challenges associated with unsupervised anomaly detection in 5G
127
      networks, such as data heterogeneity, lack of labels, privacy concerns, and dynamic environments,
128
      the Feature-Aware Federated Learning framework provides a solution by:
129
130           • Utilizing IG to compute feature importance in an unsupervised manner, enabling the model
131             to focus on relevant features without labels.
132           • Incorporating differential privacy to protect sensitive information during feature importance
133             aggregation.
134
              • Implementing DFIA to adapt to changing data distributions, which is crucial for detecting
135             new anomalies.
136
137   The framework combines these components to enhance anomaly detection performance while en-
138   suring privacy and adaptability, addressing key limitations of existing methods.

      6   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      The proposed Feature-Aware Federated Learning framework demonstrates significant advantages
489
      over traditional federated learning methods in unsupervised anomaly detection. By incorporating
490   feature importance into the aggregation process, the global model focuses on the most relevant fea-
491   tures, enhancing detection capabilities. This attention mechanism ensures that critical information is
492   not diluted during aggregation, addressing a key limitation of standard FL methods. The enhanced
493   performance is evident from the ROC-AUC score improvement from 0.974 (FedAvg) and 0.975
494   (FedProx) to 0.981 with our framework, which is significant in the context of anomaly detection
495   where even slight improvements can have substantial practical implications. The confusion matrix
496   analysis revealed that our framework had fewer false positives and false negatives compared to the
497   baselines, indicating a more reliable identification of anomalies without mistakenly flagging normal
498
      instances. Furthermore, the feature importance visualization provided valuable insights into which
      features contribute most to anomaly detection, allowing network administrators to better understand
499
      and monitor critical aspects of network traffic.
500
501   In addition to performance improvements, our framework effectively balances privacy and utility.
502   By applying differential privacy to the aggregated feature importance, we prevent potential leakage
503   of sensitive information while maintaining high model utility. The experimental results show that
504
      the model remains effective even under strict privacy budgets. The Dynamic Feature Importance
      Adaptation (DFIA) enables the model to adjust to evolving data distributions, which is essential in
505
      the context of 5G networks where traffic patterns can change rapidly. Regular updates to feature
506
      importance help maintain model relevance over time. Despite these advancements, the framework
507   assumes that clients are honest and follow the protocol. Future work will focus on improving ro-
508   bustness against adversarial clients, extending the framework to handle non-IID data distributions,
509   and optimizing the trade-off between privacy and utility. Additionally, we plan to deploy FAFL in
510   real-world environments to further validate its performance and explore scalability. Extending the
511   framework to other neural network architectures and advancing privacy-preserving techniques are
512   also key areas for future research.

      7   C ONCLUSION
515
516
      We introduced a novel Feature-Aware Attention Federated (FAFL) framework for unsupervised
517
      anomaly detection in 5G networks, addressing key challenges in privacy-preserving and efficient
518   model training. The FAFL integrates feature importance into the federated learning process and ap-
519   plies differential privacy, and our framework enhances anomaly detection performance while safe-
520   guarding client data. The inclusion of Dynamic Feature Importance Adaptation allows the model
521   to remain effective in dynamic environments. Experimental results validate the efficiency of our
522   approach over traditional federated learning methods. Future research will focus on extending the
523   framework to other neural network architectures and exploring advanced privacy-preserving tech-
524   niques.
