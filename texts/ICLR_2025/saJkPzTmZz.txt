009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Ensuring AI models align with human values is essential for their safety and
012            functionality. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) leverages
013            human preferences to achieve this alignment. However, when preferences are
014            sourced from diverse populations, point estimates of reward can result in subop-
015            timal performance or be unfair to specific groups. We propose Pareto Optimal
016            Preference Learning (POPL), which enables pluralistic alignment by framing dis-
017            crepant group preferences as objectives with potential trade-offs, aiming for poli-
018
               cies that are Pareto-optimal on the preference dataset. POPL utilizes lexicase se-
               lection, an iterative process that selects diverse and Pareto-optimal solutions. Our
019
               theoretical and empirical evaluations demonstrate that POPL surpasses baseline
020
               methods in learning sets of reward functions and policies, effectively catering to
021            distinct groups without access to group numbers or membership labels. We verify
022            the performance of POPL on a stateless preference learning setting, a Minigrid
023            RL domain, Metaworld robotics benchmarks, as well as large language model
024            (LLM) fine-tuning. We illustrate that POPL can also serve as a foundation for
025            techniques optimizing specific notions of group fairness, ensuring safe and equi-
026            table AI model alignment.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   For both safety and functionality, it is critical for AI models to align with the values of human users
032   and stakeholders. Recently, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al.,
033
      2017) has emerged as an effective mechanism for model alignment, using preferences to capture
      human values. However, when preferences are sourced from large groups of potentially diverse
034
      people, methods that rely on point estimates of human values are bound to either be suboptimal for
035
      all groups or unfair to certain groups, both of which are problematic in their own ways.
036
037   In this work, we build upon the notion of hidden context proposed by Siththaranjan et al. (2023)
038   and focus on the problem of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback with Hidden Context
039
      (RLHF-HC). Hidden context refers to information that is unavailable to a preference learning system
      yet affects the preferences given. For example, a person’s dominant hand might determine on which
040
      side they would prefer a robotic assistant to hand them an object. Under this formulation, our goal
041
      is to build a set of policies that contains the optimal policy under the reward function for each group
042   of people. In practice, we see two clear use cases of such a set of policies. First, they can be
043   selected from at test time to find an optimal policy for a given user without in a few-shot manner.
044   Second, this set can be used to measure and ensure fairness between groups. Minimizing risk with
045   respect to this diverse distribution of policies ensures that no specific group is disregarded in the risk
046   measurement–thus enhancing safety.
047
      Preferences with hidden context may be contradictory i.e., not mutually satisfiable by a well-
048   regularized policy or reward function. So, we propose to frame these preferences as objectives
049   with potential trade-offs between each other. With this re-framing, the optimal policy for each indi-
050   vidual hidden context group would be Pareto-optimal (non-dominated) on the dataset of preferences.
051   With this in mind, we propose Pareto Optimal Preference Learning (POPL), where we learn a set of
052   reward functions or policies (directly) that are optimized towards being Pareto-optimal with respect
053   to the set of preferences given by a potentially diverse set of human annotators. To do this, we use
      an iterative selection process known as lexicase selection (Spector, 2012), which has been shown

      Diversity in Human Preferences Data used for RLHF systems often comes from multiple people,
      who are diverse in their preferences and values (Bobu et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Biyik & Sadigh,
100
      2018; Santurkar et al., 2023). This data, when considered in its aggregated form, can not be captured
101
      perfectly by a decision-making model that relies on a point estimate of utility (Casper et al., 2023).
102   These models try to find a single reward function that is most likely, which is often not the optimal
103   reward function for any one single person. When the groups are not perfectly balanced, the minority
104   groups might be underrepresented in the inferred reward function (Siththaranjan et al., 2023; Feffer
105   et al., 2023; Kirk et al., 2023; Myers et al., 2021) or simply treated as noise (Baumler et al., 2023).
106   There have been attempts at explicitly modeling different people with different levels of expertise
107   (Gordon et al., 2021; Daniels-Koch & Freedman, 2022; Gordon et al., 2022; Barnett et al., 2023),
      but these methods generally rely on concrete ways to distinguish between groups.


                                                        2
108
      Dealing with this Diversity In the context of RL, Myers et al. (2021) outlines an approach to
109   learning a multi-modal reward function from online interaction between a human expert and a pref-
110   erence learning system. Ramé et al. (2024) learn a set of reward models by optimizing for diversity
111   amongst the outputs. While similar to our approach, we also aim to align our reward models with
112   hidden context groups through optimizing for Pareto-optimality. For generative AI models and
113   LLMs, there have been a variety of studies attempting to align large models with diverse human
114   preferences. Chakraborty et al. (2024) and Siththaranjan et al. (2023) learn a mixture of preference
115   distributions or a parameterized reward distribution, respectively. However, both these techniques
116   operate under a contextual bandit setting which results in sub-optimal performance when used in
117
      the more general RL setting (discussed further in Section 3). Bradley et al. (2024) and Ding et al.
      (2024) leverage fine-tuning to improve the diversity of model responses for better alignment and
118
      creativity, which do not directly address the ambiguity and hidden context in human preferences.
119
      Jang et al. (2023) and Dai et al. (2023) elicit preferences specifically along different dimensions in
120   order to cater custom reward functions for users in test time, and to be safe with respect to con-
121   flicting objectives, respectively. Whilst we also aim to cater reward functions in test time as well
122   as optimize fairness between groups, we do not have access to labels regarding the context of the
123   preferences generated. Finally, Rame et al. (2024) also generates a set of Pareto-optimal reward
124   functions. However, in their setting, the system has access to ground truth reward functions for each
125   group, and the Pareto-front is generated through weight interpolation between these functions.
126
127   Bayesian Reward Inference Bayesian Reward Extrapolation (B-REx) (Brown et al., 2020b) in-
128   stead learns a distribution of reward models from pairwise human preferences. B-REx is then able to
129   perform Bayesian inference using MCMC (MacKay, 1992) to sample from the posterior of reward
130
      functions. With this distribution, a practitioner can establish high confidence performance bounds
      that can be used to assess risk in evaluated policies as well as detect reward hacking behaviors.
131
      However, B-REx and other reward inference methods often rely on a faulty assumption that humans
132
      provide preferences in a Boltzmann-rational way.

137   Learning from Human Preferences Reinforcement learning from human feedback considers hu-
138
      man preferences over trajectories (or more generally, outputs of a model) in order to learn a reward
      model or policy that respects the preferences (Brown & Niekum, 2019; Rafailov et al., 2024; Hejna
139
      et al., 2024; Casper et al., 2023; Finn et al., 2016).
140
141   In order to learn meaningfully from human preferences, one must characterize how preferences are
142   generated from some parameterized preferences model P (σi ≺ σj ). Usually, this preference model
143   is based on the notion of Boltzmann-rationality, where humans generate preferences in accordance
144
      to the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952). The probability of pairwise preference
      (σi ≻ σj ) between two trajectories segments given some utility function f (σ) can be written as

151
      fields, such as psychology (Baker et al., 2009; Goodman et al., 2011; Goodman & Stuhlmüller,
152   2013). However, this model does not perfectly capture the mechanisms driving these preferences
153   (Ghosal et al., 2023; Jeon et al., 2020; Knox et al., 2022; Bobu et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021).

155   Hidden Context Siththaranjan et al. (2023) introduce the problem of preference learning with
156   hidden context. This is the idea that preferences are generated not only based on the exponential
157   utility (partial return or regret), but also on some latent hidden context variable z. This variable is
158   not accessible to preference learning systems and poses a challenge as it is often the case that this
159   variable results in breaking the assumption that preferences are generated Boltzmann-rationally.
160

      preferences learned, Siththaranjan et al. (2023) introduce Distributional Preference Learning, which

199   Contrastive Preference Learning Contrastive Preference Learning (CPL) (Hejna et al., 2024)
200   learns a policy directly from preferences without needing to learn an intermediate reward function.
201   This method uses a regret-based model of preferences rather than the standard partial return inter-
202
      pretation. The probability of a preference under a candidate policy can be written as the ratio of the
      exponentiated sum of log-likelihoods of the chosen segment to the disregarded segment. We choose
203
      CPL over Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Rafailov et al. (2024) as DPO can be derived as a
204
      special case of CPL with trajectories of length 1, starting from the same state. Furthermore, POPL is
205   designed to be used in a variety of sequential, time-based domanis, but DPO and other contemporary
206   RLHF methods restrict themselves to contextual bandit settings (such as in large language models).
207   CPL, on the other hand, overcomes these limitations Hejna et al. (2024).

212   We operate in a common RLHF setting in which, given a dataset D = {σ1 , · · · , σm } of trajectory
213   segments and a set P = {(i, j) : σi ≻ σj } of pairwise preferences over these segments, we wish
214   to infer an unknown reward function r : S 7→ R that respects the preferences. This reward function
215   represents an assignment of utility r(s) to each state s in the state space S. r can then be used as the
      reward function to train a policy π(a|s) : S × A 7→ [0, 1] with RL.

      In light of our discussion in section 3, we re-frame the problem of preference learning with hidden
217   context as follows. The goal is to learn a set Π = {π1 , π2 · · · , πn } of policies such that, for the
218   hidden context group represented by a variable z ∈ Z, there is a policy πz ∈ Π that is the optimal
219   policy for the ground truth reward function rz for the group. Note that this can be accomplished
220   by standard (reward-based) RLHF (experiments in sections 6.1 and 6.4) or direct (reward-free)
221   RLHF (experiments in sections 6.2 and 6.3). For the standard approach, a series of reward functions
222   R = {r1 , r2 · · · , rn } are first learned from preferences, then used to train n optimal policies. In
223   the direct approach, the policies are learned directly from preferences such as done by Hejna et al.
224   (2024) and Rafailov et al. (2024).
225   We now show that optimal policies for hidden context groups are Pareto-optimal with respect to the
226   set of preferences given by all annotators. Therefore, recovering the set of pareto-optimal policies is
227   a viable way to solve the RLHF-HC problem formatted above.

245   Definition 5 (Contradictory preferences). A pair of preferences (σi ≻ σj ), (σx ≻ σy ) are con-
246   tradictory under a specific policy regularization scheme if the likelihood of any policy that satisfies
247   both is lower than the likelihood of a policy that satisfies either.
248   With no regularization, a policy that satisfies two preferences should have a higher likelihood than
249   one that satisfies one but not the other. This is because the likelihood of a certain policy is related to
250   the total number of preferences passed by it. Passing two preferences would therefore elicit a higher
251   likelihood than passing just one of them. However, if by satisfying both preferences, the models
252   would need to incur a much greater regularization loss, it is likely that these two preferences came
253   from individuals with differing hidden contexts.

265   In this section, we outline an algorithm that can be used to generate a set of polices or reward func-
266   tions that align with the preferences of different groups of people. We introduce lexicase selection,
267   a method that can select candidate hypotheses that lie on the Pareto front. Our population represents
268   a belief distribution that is updated based on observed evidence. Lexicase selection continually nar-
269   rows the hypothesis space based on selection criteria, effectively ‘learning’ which policies or reward
      functions hold promise given the current (hidden-context-laden) data.

288   Lexicase Selection for Pluralistic Outcomes To obtain a set of Pareto optimal policies, we adopt
289   the idea of lexicase selection (Spector, 2012; Helmuth et al., 2015), which uses a random ordering
290
      of metrics for each selection event, with only the candidates that perform the best on each successive
      metric retained for filtering by the remaining metrics. This process is repeated until all metrics are
291
      exhausted or a single individual remains. Through this process lexicase selection prioritizes, over
292
      multiple selection events, each particular metric and each particular combination of metrics to the
293   exclusion of all others. We can consider this to be a “particularity” approach for achieving pluralistic
294   outcomes (Spector et al., 2024).
295
296
      In the setting of preference learning, each metric corresponds to a preference sourced from a human
      with hidden context. The preference is ‘passed’ if the candidate policy correctly ranks the pair
297
      of segments in the preference corresponding to that metric (formally defined in Section 4). If no
298
      individuals in the current pool pass the preference, all individuals make it through this selection step.
299   With this feature, contradictory preferences are addressed by giving priority to the first preference
300   in the shuffle. Each random shuffle of preferences therefore results in a diverse profile of reward
301   functions being selected for. Figure 3 shows an example of a single selection event.
302
      A key property of lexicase selection is that it selects candidates that are Pareto-optimal relative to a
303
      starting set of candidates (as opposed to all possible candidates). These individuals tend to spread
304
      the corners of the Pareto front and thus be diverse (La Cava et al., 2019). Lexicase selection also
305   gives individuals that are good at more subsets of things greater weight. This idea has been utilized
306   in many machine learning optimization problems for improving generalization, as shown in recent
307   work (Ding et al., 2022; 2023; Ni et al., 2024; Boldi et al., 2023; Ding & Spector, 2022).
308

321   In this section, we detail our experimental results to validate the proposed POPL method. To verify
322   that POPL can work in a large variety of settings at different scales, as well as for generating both re-
323   ward functions and policies, we perform four sets of experiments. A synthetic, stateless experiment
      (reward inference), a Minigrid RL environment (policy inference), a Metaworld robotics environ-

324
      ment (policy inference) and LLM finetuning from human preferences (reward inference). Further
325   implementation details are provided in Appendix C.
326
327   Baselines Throughout our experiments, we will use 3 main baselines. In the experiments on re-
328   ward function inference, we use Bayesian Reward Extrapolation (B-REx) (Brown et al., 2020b) as
329   a baseline, as it generates a large set of reward function hypotheses (i.e., candidate models) based
330   on a Boltzmann-rational likelihood function, and has demonstrated efficacy in RL domains. For our
331   policy inference experiments, we compare to Contrastive Preference Learning (Hejna et al., 2024)
332   as it is a leading RLHF algorithm for sequential tasks. We also use a naive method of learning a set
333   of policies based on CPL that we call Multi-CPL. In this approach, after pretraining, we fine-tune
334   the last layer using the CPL objective multiple times to generate a large set of policies. Although
335
      we could do full network fine-tuning, we wanted to hold constant the trainable parameters available
      to each approach to ensure a fair comparison to POPL, which uses last-layer fine-tuning. Policy
336
      learning settings in this work model human preferences as being generated based on regret, as op-
337
      posed to partial return (Knox et al., 2022). Including the policy inference experiments allows us
338   to ensure our method is not sensitive to assumptions regarding how the preferences are generated.
339   For our language model (contextual bandit) experiments, we compare to both B-REx and Distribu-
340   tional Preference Learning (DPL) (Siththaranjan et al., 2023), as well as standard the standard RLHF
341   paradigm (Christiano et al., 2017), as these present a variety of approaches for generating reward
342   models that can be used to ensure fairness across groups.
343
344   Metrics Given a set of reward functions or policies, we can verify how well they perform on the
345   two downstream tasks we have identified for this work: personalization and fairness. For personal-
346   ization, we inspect the content of the personalized policies or reward functions to verify their align-
347   ment with each hidden context group’s preferences. For fairness, we ensure that no single group is
348
      having its values undermined (by taking low-probability actions) in an attempt to satisfy a different
      group. Although this is a relatively simple notion of fairness, this method could be extended to be
349
      compatible with other fairness optimization approaches Mehrabi et al. (2021).

      6.4   L ANGUAGE M ODEL E XPERIMENTS
456
457   In this section, we test the ability of POPL to scale to domains involving human annotations. We
458   investigate whether POPL can be sensitive to hidden context in whether annotators prefer harmless
459   or helpful responses (Bai et al., 2022). When reward models are trained on the entire set of prefer-
460   ences, whether they were generated based on helpfulness or harmlessness is hidden context, as this
461   information affects preferences but is unavailable to a reward inference system.
462   Importantly, preferences based on helpfulness and harmlessness can often be contradictory. In fact,
463   Wei et al. (2024) find examples of user prompts that directly pit these objectives against each other,
464   leading a language model to output harmful outputs, a phenomenon known as jailbreaking. An
465   RLHF system built with hidden context in mind would help detect jailbreaking before a harmful
466   output would be given to a user. In the context of this work, a model that is susceptible to jailbreaking
467   would be unfair to certain groups (compromising its efficacy for the harmlessness group in order to
468   optimize for the helpfulness group).

508   7   C ONCLUSION
509
510   When learning from human preferences for the sake of aligning to human values, systems often
511   rely on point estimates of return or regret, limiting them to aligning to a single group of humans.
512   Preferences, however, often come from distinct groups with diverse preferences. We have formalized
513
      this as the problem of preference learning with hidden context. Under this conception, a set of
      policies must be generated that contains the optimal policy for each distinct group.
514
515   To solve this problem, we relied on the concept of Pareto-optimality to generate a series of reward
516   functions and/or policies that are optimal with respect to unique sub-sets of preferences. To optimize
517   towards Pareto-optimality, we used a technique known as lexicase selection, that selects individuals
518   from a large set based on a randomized (lexicographic) prioritization of the training data.
519   We verified that lexicase selection can be used to generate diverse distributions of either reward
520   functions or policies that align with the diverse preferences that human annotators have. We evalu-
521   ated and verified the performance of POPL in a variety of domains, including a synthetic stateless
522   domain, a Minigrid RL domain, a Metaworld Robotics benchmark, and even language model jail-
523   break detection. Across these domains, we have demonstrated POPL’s efficacy when compared to
524   contemporary algorithms in dealing with hidden context in the preferences. Without modifications
525
      to the framework, POPL can be used to optimize for diverse reward functions or policies, and can
      work in stateless and sequential domains at a variety of scales.
526
527   One limitation of this work is the lack of use of gradients in training policies. The optimization
528   procedure used after lexicase selection relies on random variations and repeated selections, which
529   allows for effective trade-offs between exploration and exploitation of the preference landscape.
530   Although empirically verified to work well, it may be possible to augment the core idea in future
531
      work to allow it to utilize gradients. Furthermore, a study into the conditions required for the output
      of the procedure to be globally Pareto-optimal could be instrumental.

      In the LLM experiments, we assess the performance of reward learning by examining preference
858   accuracy on the test set. To investigate vulnerabilities to jailbreak, we analyze pairs of responses to
859   jailbreak prompts designed by Wei et al. (2024) to deceive the model into giving a harmful response.
860   We calculate the percentage of prompts where it assigns a higher reward to the jailbroken response
861   (“jailbreak rate”). Additionally, we evaluate the reward function’s ability to assess helpfulness on
862   non-harmful prompts, i.e., the reward function predicts higher rewards on the more helpful response.
863   We compare our method to normal RLHF with an LLM-based preference model, Bayesian Reward
      Extrapolation (B-REx), and distributional preference learning (DPL). DPL methods predict parame-

864
      ters of the distribution of reward values for each response, rather than a single reward value, in order
865   to better account for hidden context in human preferences.

867   For standard RLHF, we use the pre-trained L LAMA-2-7b (Touvron et al., 2023) preference model
868
      by Siththaranjan et al. (2023), which is fine-tuned on the HH-RLHF dataset using LoRA (Hu et al.,

870

871   a skip every 20 samples to reduce auto-correlation. For POPL, we run lexicase selection for 100
872   generations with a population size of 1000, and randomly sample 100 reward functions in the last
873   generation.
874
      Because the ranking likelihood is invariant to affine transformations of the rewards, we normalize
875
      the rewards by subtracting the median reward calculated on the training set over all the responses.

      This ensures that the reward values are comparable when calculating the lower quantile of rewards

880   D    B ROADER S OCIETAL I MPACTS
881
882   The proposed work on Pareto Optimal Preference Learning (POPL) aims to enhance the alignment
883
      of AI systems with diverse human values, thereby addressing critical issues of fairness and repre-
      sentation. By focusing on learning from human preferences with hidden context, our method seeks
884
      to ensure that AI models do not disproportionately favor or disadvantage specific groups, making
885
      them more equitable and just. This has the potential to significantly improve the societal acceptance
886   and trust in AI systems, particularly in sensitive applications such as healthcare, education, and law
887   enforcement, where fairness and inclusivity are critical.
888
889
      However, there are potential negative societal impacts to consider. The deployment of AI systems
      that can cater to specific groups might inadvertently reinforce existing biases if the hidden context re-
890
      flects social prejudices or discriminatory practices. Therefore, it is crucial to incorporate safeguards
891
      and robust validation mechanisms to detect and mitigate any biased outcomes. As researchers and
892   developers, we must be vigilant about the sources of our training data and continually audit AI
893   systems for unintended consequences.
894
895
      Moreover, the computational work required for training these models can have environmental im-
      pacts, given the high-energy consumption associated with large-scale AI computations. Researchers
896
      should consider optimizing algorithms to be more efficient and exploring the use of renewable en-
897
      ergy sources to mitigate this impact.
898
899   By considering these factors, we aim to advance AI technologies in a direction that promotes fair-
900   ness, inclusiveness, and sustainability, ensuring that they serve the broader interests of society.
