008
009
010
               Operator networks are designed to approximate nonlinear operators, which pro-
               vide mappings between infinite-dimensional spaces such as function spaces.
011
               These networks are playing an increasingly important role in machine learning,
012
               with their most notable contributions in the field of scientific computing. Their
013            significance stems from their ability to handle the type of data often encountered in
014            scientific applications. For instance, in climate modeling or fluid dynamics, input
015            data typically consists of discretized continuous fields (like temperature distribu-
016            tions or velocity fields). We introduce the radial basis operator network (RBON),
017            which represents a significant advancement as the first operator network capable of
018            learning an operator in both the time domain and frequency domain when adjusted
019            to accept complex-valued inputs. Despite the small, single hidden-layer structure,
020            the RBON boasts small L2 relative test error for both in- and out-of-distribution
021
               data (OOD) of less than 1×10−7 in some benchmark cases. Moreover, the RBON
               maintains small error on OOD data from entirely different function classes from
022
               the training data.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
027
      1.1   BACKGROUND
028
029
      Traditional feedforward neural networks (FNNs) and radial basis function (RBF) networks have
030   been shown to be universal approximators of functions (Hornik et al., 1989; Park & Sandberg,
031   1991), meaning they are capable of representing the mapping between finite dimensional spaces.
032   Thus, these networks are limited in their design to predicting a measurement acting on a subspace
033   of Rd for some d ∈ Z+ . Operator networks, however, are designed to learn the mapping between
034   infinite dimensional spaces; they receive functions as input and produce the corresponding output
035   function. Scientific computing has benefited from using operator networks to enhance or replace
036   numerical computation for the purpose of simulation and forecasting on a wide array of applications
037   to include computational fluid dynamics and weather forecasting (Azizzadenesheli et al., 2024).
038   The two primary neural operators that demonstrated immediate success are the deep operator net-
039   work (DeepONet) (Lu et al., 2021) based on the universal approximation theorem in Chen and Chen
040   (1995a), and the Fourier neural operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021). The basic DeepONet approximates
041   the operator by applying a weighted sum to the product of each of the transformed outputs from two
042   FNN sub-networks. The upper sub-network, or branch net, is applied to the input functions while
043   the lower trunk net is applied to the querying locations of the output function.
044   In contrast, the FNO is a particular type of Neural Operator network (Kovachki et al., 2023), which
045   accepts only input functions (not querying locations for the output) and applies a global transforma-
046   tion on the function input via a more intricate architecture. Motivated by fundamental solutions to
047   partial differential equations (PDEs), the FNO network sums the output of an integral kernel trans-
048   formation to the input function with the output of a linear transformation. The sum is then passed
049   through a non-linear activation function. To accelerate the integral kernel transformation, the FNO
050
      applies a Fourier transform (FT) to the input data, with the FT of the integral kernel assumed as
      trainable parameters.
051
052   Following their initial introduction, several extensions and modifications to FNO and DeepONet
053   were introduced to improve performance in specific contexts. Examples include the Fourier-
      enhanced DeepONet (Zhu et al., 2023) to improve DeepONet’s robustness against Gaussian noise,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      U-FNO (Wen et al., 2022) and MIONet (Jiang et al., 2024) introduce U-Net paths into the Fourier
055   layer of the FNO architecture to improve accuracy for multi-phase flow applications, and model-
056   parallel FNOs (Grady et al., 2023) parallelise the structure of FNO to reduce computation load for
057   high-dimensional data. However, many of these situational improvements did not result in clear
058   error reductions across a variety of contexts, at least not enough to justify the additional complex-
059   ity in architecture contained in some of the proposed methods. This has changed with the recent
060   introduction of a new neural operator.
061   The Laplace Neural Operator (LNO) (Cao et al., 2024) has recently become a benchmark standard
062   for operator networks due to its improved handling of transient responses and non-periodic signals,
063   limitations inherent in the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO). LNO achieves this by leveraging the
064   pole-residue method to represent both transient and steady-state responses in the Laplace domain,
065   leading to better test performance on out-of-distribution (OOD) data in most contexts. Additionally,
066   LNO boasts a reduced training cost and a simpler network architecture. For these reasons, we
067   have selected LNO as the primary comparison for our new operator network, alongside FNO and
068   DeepONet. To thoroughly evaluate performance, we include a problem scenario from (Cao et al.,
069
      2024) that highlights LNO’s small OOD error in predictions.

072
      We propose the radial basis operator network (RBON) based on the universal approximation theorem
073
      in Chen and Chen (1995b); a novel operator network that is, to the best of our knowledge, the first
074
      to be entirely represented with radial basis functions.
075
076          • The universal approximation result in Chen and Chen (1995b) is extended to normalised
077            RBONSs (NRBONs).
078
             • The RBON is the first network to successfully learn an operator entirely in both the time

080
081
             • Despite the simple single-hidden-layer structure, the particular implementation of the

      While operator networks are usually tested only using data generated from known systems, such
089   as in systems of partial differential equations (PDEs), we include a scientific application where the

097   2     M ETHODOLOGY
098
099   The RBON is a numerical representation, G† , for an operator, G : U → V, where U and V are
100   infinite dimensional spaces, using radial basis functions. Following the work as shown in Chen and
101   Chen (1995b), we present, without proof, the universal approximation theorem for such a represen-
102   tation as well as extending the theorem to include NRBONs. The subsequent section details the
103   precise implementation used for the experimental results.

      2.3   L EARNING IN THE FREQUENCY DOMAIN
244
245   The RBON is designed to learn the operator in the frequency domain as well as in the time domain.
246   The frequency domain is a representation of signals or functions in terms of their frequency compo-
247   nents, rather than time. It allows analysis of how signals vary with frequency, providing insight into
248   characteristics like energy, power, and periodicity. The frequency domain is often used to examine
249   cyclic behavior, separate overlapping signals, and simplify certain mathematical operations on sig-
250   nals. Considering that functions have a global representation in the frequency domain, this can have
251   benefits in reducing the variability on the RBONs predictions for OOD data.
252   Thus, the RBON can be trained on functions in the time domain to approximate the operator G
253   shown in Figure 2, or the Fourier transform, F, can be used to convert functions to the frequency
254   domain, in which case the RBON is learning the approximation for what is labeled as G    b in figure
255   2. This is especially beneficial for applications where the data is stored in the frequency domain
256   representation.
257
      While algorithmically the RBON follows the same process whether training in the time domain or
258
      frequency domain, we denote the application of the RBON to the frequency domain as F-RBON. Nu-
259   merically, the coded functions for F-RBON accept complex-valued input since functions defined on
260   the frequency domain are stored numerically using complex valued arrays. This allows the F-RBON
261   to capture both magnitude and phase information inherent in frequency domain representations.

397   the average global temperature as published in Our World in Data (2023). The function T loc is
398   local temperature readings at the same site location where the CO2 data was collected. Specifically,
399   atmospheric CO2 concentrations (ppm) derived from in situ air measurements at the well known
400   Mauna Loa, Observatory, Hawaii (Keeling et al., 2001). The local temperature readings are much
401   more variable than the global average and hence less easily predicted.
402   The nature of the operators Gavg and Gloc is expected to evolve in time due to fluctuations in other
403   contributing factors, however, when continuously updating the RBON with new data, the predictions
404   become quite accurate. While, it is possible to feed the CO2 readings into the network as one
405   function, the centers for the RBFs must be set manually as the K-means algorithm requires at least
406   two function inputs. Instead, it is preferable to parameterize the functions across the years such that
407   t ∈ {1, 2, . . . , 12} with each number corresponding to the month of the year the measurement was
408   taken. Then operators are thus more accurately represented as
409                                         Gavg : un (t) → Tnavg (t),
410
411
                                             Gloc : un (t) → Tnloc (t)
412   where n corresponds to a specific year. Training on the historical data, omitting years with incom-
413   plete data, yields remarkable accuracy in the RBON predictions as shown in Table 2.

526   4   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
527
528   The RBON and its variants offer a simple yet powerful network architecture with prediction capa-
529   bilities that yield errors smaller than the current leading operator network. The network’s compact
530   size provides opportunities for enhanced interpretability and reduced computational load, allowing
531   for exact solutions of network parameters. Most variation across training cycles arises from the
532   location and scale parameters of the RBFs, largely due to K-means’ tendency to converge on local
533   extrema. This variability can lead to errors differing by several orders of magnitude between runs
534   of the K-means algorithm. A practical solution is to run K-means multiple times and select the
535   configuration that minimizes the overall within-cluster distances. Furthermore, the RBON serves as
536
      an excellent tool for scientific computing, where recent advancements have only begun to explore
      the potential of operator networks in various fields. Finally, the RBON’s ability to train on both real
537
      and complex-valued inputs, combined with its other strengths, makes it a promising candidate for
538
      applications in signal processing and computer vision tasks.
