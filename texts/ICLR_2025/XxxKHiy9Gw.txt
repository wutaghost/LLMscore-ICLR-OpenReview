                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               We focus on designing and solving the neutral inclusion problem via neural net-
015            works. The neutral inclusion problem has a long history in the theory of com-
016            posite materials, and it is exceedingly challenging to identify the precise condi-
017            tion that precipitates a general-shaped inclusion into a neutral inclusion. Physics-
018            informed neural networks (PINNs) have recently become a highly successful ap-
019            proach to addressing both forward and inverse problems associated with partial
020            differential equations. We found that traditional PINNs perform inadequately
021            when applied to the inverse problem of designing neutral inclusions with arbi-
022            trary shapes. In this study, we introduce a novel approach, Conformal mapping
023
               Coordinates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (CoCo-PINNs), which integrates
               complex analysis techniques into PINNs. This method exhibits strong perfor-
024
               mance in solving forward-inverse problems to construct neutral inclusions of ar-
025
               bitrary shapes in two dimensions, where the imperfect interface condition on the
026            inclusion’s boundary is modeled by training neural networks. Notably, we math-
027            ematically prove that training with a single linear field is sufficient to achieve
028            neutrality for untrained linear fields in arbitrary directions, given a minor assump-
029            tion. We demonstrate that CoCo-PINNs offer enhanced performances in terms of
030            credibility, consistency, and stability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
      Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)(Raissi et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021) are spe-
035
      cialized neural networks designed to solve partial differential equations (PDEs). Since their intro-
036
      duction, PINNs have been successfully applied to a wide range of PDE-related problems (Cuomo
037   et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). A significant advantage of using PINNs is their
038   versatile applicability to different types of PDEs and their ability to deal with PDE parameters or
039   initial/boundary constraints while solving forward problems (Akhound-Sadegh et al., 2023; Cho
040   et al., 2023; Rathore et al., 2024; Lau et al., 2024). The conventional approach to solving inverse
041   problems with PINNs involves designing neural networks that converge to the parameters or con-
042   straints to be reconstructed, which are typically modeled as constants or functions. We refer to this
043   methodology as classical PINNs. Numerous successful outcomes in solving inverse problems using
044   PINNs have been reported. See, for example, Chen et al. (2020); Jagtap et al. (2022); Haghighat
045
      et al. (2021). However, as the complexity of the PDE-based inverse problem increases, the neu-
      ral networks may require additional design to represent the parameters or constraints accurately.
046
      For instance, Pokkunuru et al. (2023) utilized Bayesian approach to design the loss function, Guo
047
      et al. (2022) used Monte Carlo approximation to compute the fractional derivatives, Xu et al. (2023)
048   adopted multi-task learning method to weight losses and also presented the forward-inverse problem
049   combined neural networks, and Yuan et al. (2022) propose the auxiliary-PINNs to solve the forward
050   and inverse problems of integro-differential equations. This increase in network complexity can sig-
051   nificantly escalate computational difficulties and the volume of data necessary for training PINNs.
052   Moreover, the direct approach to approximate the reconstructing parameters by neural networks en-
053   ables too much fluent representation ability. This alludes to the fact that the conventional approach
      is inadequate depending on the problems due to the intrinsic ill-posedness of inverse problems.

      In this paper, we apply the PINNs’ framework to address the inverse problem of designing neutral
055   inclusions, a topic that will be elaborated below. The challenge of designing neutral inclusions falls
056   within the scope where traditional PINNs tend to perform inadequately. To overcome this limita-
057   tion, we propose improvements to the PINNs approach by incorporating mathematical analytical
058   methods.
059
      Inclusions with different material features from the back-
060
      ground medium commonly cause perturbations in applied
061   fields when they are inserted into the medium. Problems
062   analyzing and manipulating the effects of inclusions have
063   gained significant attention due to their fundamental im-
064   portance in the modeling of composite materials, partic-
065   ularly in light of rapid advancements and diverse appli-
066   cations of these materials. Specific inclusions, referred
067   to as neutral inclusions, do not disturb linear fields (see
068   Figure 2). The neutral inclusion problem has a long and
069
      established history in the theory of composite materials Figure 1: Inclusion makes perturbation.
      (Milton, 2002). Some of the most well-known examples
070
      include coated disks and spheres (Hashin, 1962; Hashin & Shtrikman, 1962), as well as coated
071
      ellipses and ellipsoids (Grabovsky & Kohn, 1995; Kerker, 1975; Sihvola, 1997). The primary mo-
072   tivation for studying neutral inclusions is to design reinforced or embedded composite materials
073   in such a way that the stress field remains unchanged from that of the material without inclusions
074   and avoids stress concentration. Extensive research has been conducted on neutral inclusions and
075   related concepts, such as invisibility cloaking involving wave propagation, in fields including acous-
076   tics, elasticity, electromagnetic waves within the microwave range Alù & Engheta (2005); Ammari
077   et al. (2013); Landy & Smith (2013); Liu et al. (2017); Zhou & Hu (2006; 2007); Zhou et al. (2008);
078   Yuste et al. (2018).

093   Designing neutral inclusions with general shapes presents an inherent challenge. In the context of the
094   conductivity problem, which is the focus of this paper, mathematical theory shows that only coated
095   ellipses and ellipsoids can maintain neutral properties for linear fields in all directions (Kang & Lee,
096   2014; Kang et al., 2016; Milton & Serkov, 2001). In contrast, non-elliptical shapes can remain neu-
097   tral for just a single linear field direction (Jarczyk & Mityushev, 2012; Milton & Serkov, 2001). To
098   address the difficulty of designing general shaped neutral inclusions, relaxed versions of the prob-
099
      lem have been studied (Choi et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2022; Lim & Milton, 2020). Differently from
      the above examples, where perfectly bonding boundaries are assumed, imperfect interfaces intro-
100
      duce discontinuities in either the flux or potential boundary conditions in PDEs. Ru (1998) found
101
      interface parameters for typical inclusion shapes in two-dimensional elasticity for typical inclusion
102   shapes. Benveniste & Miloh (1999) found neutral inclusions under a single linear field. The in-
103   terface parameters, which characterize these discontinuities, may be non-constant functions defined
104   along the boundaries of the inclusions so that, theoretically, the degree of freedom of the interface
105   parameters is infinite. Hence, we expect to overcome the inherent challenge of designing neutral in-
106   clusions with general shapes by considering neutral inclusions with imperfect interfaces. A powerful
107   technique for dealing with planar inclusion problems of general shapes has been to use conformal
      mappings and to define orthogonal curvilinear coordinates (Movchan & Serkov, 1997; Cherkaev


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2022; Ammari & Kang, 2004; Jung & Lim, 2021), where the existence of the conformal map-
109   ping for a simply connected bounded domain is mathematically guaranteed by the Riemann mapping
110   theory. Using these coordinates, Kang & Li (2019); Choi & Lim (2024) constructed weakly neutral
111   inclusions that yield zero coefficients for leading-order terms in PDEs solution expansions (also re-
112   fer to Milton (2002); Choi et al. (2023); Lim & Milton (2020) for neutral inclusion problems using
113   the conformal mapping technique). However, such asymptotic approaches cannot achieve complete
114   neutrality within this framework. Moreover, the requirement for analytic asymptotic expressions
115   poses limitations on the generalizability of this approach.
116   In this paper, by adopting a deep learning approach approach, we focus on precise values of the
117   solution rather than asymptotic ones. Unlike the asymptotic approaches in Kang & Li (2019); Choi
118   & Lim (2024), the proposed method does not rely on an analytical expansion formula and incorpo-
119   rates the actual solution values directly into the loss function design. More precisely, we introduce
120   a novel forward-inverse PINN framework by combining complex analysis techniques into PINNs,
121   namely Conformal mapping Coordinates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (CoCo-PINNs). We
122   define the loss function to include the evaluations of the solutions at sample points exterior of inclu-
123
      sions. Furthermore, we leverage the conformal mapping to effectively sample collocation points for
      PDEs involving general shaped inclusions. We found that classical PINNs–which treat the interface
124
      parameters as functions approximated by neural networks–perform inadequately when applied to
125
      designing imperfect parameters for achieving neutrality. Instead, we propose training the Fourier
126   series coefficients of the imperfect parameters, rather than approximating the function. We test the
127   performance of our proposed method in finding the forward solution by using the analytical math-
128   ematical results for the forward solution presented in Choi & Lim (2024), where theoretical direct
129   solutions are expressed as products of infinite dimensional matrices whose entries depends on the
130   expansion coefficients of interface parameter. Additionally, we leverage these analytical results to
131   explain that why it is possible to train the PINN for the neutral inclusion using only a single applied
132   field (see Subsection 3.2).
133   Many PINNs approaches to solving PDEs focus primarily on the forward problem and are typically
134   validated through comparisons with numerical methods such as Finite Element Methods (FEM) and
135   Boundary Element Methods (BEM), and others. In contrast, our problem addresses both forward and
136   inverse problems simultaneously, adding complexity, especially in cases involving complex-shaped
137   inclusions, and making it more challenging to achieve accurate forward solutions. Consequently,
138   “reliability” becomes a critical factor when applying PINNs to our problem. The proposed CoCo-
139   PINNs provide more accurate forward solutions, along with improved identification of the inverse
140
      parameters, compared to classical PINNs. They demonstrate greater consistency in repeated exper-
      iments and exhibit improved stability with respect to different conductivity values σc . We conduct
141
      experiments to ensure the “reliability” of CoCo-PINNs by assessing the credibility, consistency, and
142
      stability.
143
144   It is noteworthy that by utilizing Fourier series expansions to represent the inverse parameters,
145   CoCo-PINNs offer deeper analytic insights into these parameters, making the solutions not only
146
      more accurate but also more explainable. Furthermore, our method requires no training data for the
      neutral inclusion problem due to its unique structure, where constraints at exterior points effectively
147
      serve as data. An additional remarkable feature is that our proposed method has proven effective
148
      in identifying optimal inverse parameters that are valid for general first-order background fields in-
149   cluding those not previously trained. This impressive result is supported by a rigorous mathematical
150   analysis.

      In summary, this paper contains the following contributions:
153

             • We developed a novel approach to PINNs, namely CoCo-PINNs, which have been shown
               to offer enhanced credibility, consistency, and stability compared to classical PINNs.

      In this section, we briefly explain the reason that the CoCo-PINNs can yield the neutral inclusion
      effect for untrained background fields. Since the governing equation Eq. (1) is linear with respect to
314
      H, the following trivially holds by the properties of linear PDEs:
315
316
      Theorem 2. Consider a domain, denoted by Ω, that is of arbitrary shape and whose boundary is
      given by an exterior conformal mapping Ψ(w). If there exists an interface function p(x) that makes
317
      Ω a neutral inclusion for the background field H(x) = x1 and x2 , simultaneously, then Ω is neutral
318
      also for all linear fields H(x) = ax1 + bx2 of arbitrary directions (a, b) ∈ R2 .
319
320   By Theorem 2, one can expect to find a function p(x) such that Ω is neutral to all linear fields H by
321   training with only two background fields, assuming such a p(x) exists. Although the existence of
322   this function has not yet been theoretically verified, experiments in this paper with various shapes
323   demonstrate that, for given Ω, there exists a p(x) that produces the neutral inclusion effect, meaning
      that the perturbation u − H is negligible for all directions of H.

      (uNN − H) has small values in Ωext by the definition of the loss function LNeutral . In other words, if
354   the interface function provided by neural networks makes both ∥up − uNN ∥ and LNeutral small, then
355   this interface function is the desired one. However, if ∥up − uNN ∥ is not small, it becomes unclear
356   whether the neural networks have failed to solve the inverse problem or the forward problem.
357
      The field of AI research is currently facing significant challenges regarding the efficacy and explain-
358
      ability of solutions generated by neural networks. Moreover, there is a pressing need to establish
359
      “reliability” credibility in these solutions. It is noteworthy that the trained forward solution uNN
360   deviates from the analytic solution up defined with pNN in several examples, particularly in cases
361   involving complicated-shaped inclusions (see Section 4.2). This discrepancy raises concerns about
362   the “reliability” credibility of neural networks.

554   5   CONCLUSION
555
556   We focus on the inverse problem of identifying an imperfect function that makes a given simply
557   connected inclusion a neutral inclusion. We introduce a novel approach of Conformal mapping Co-
558   ordinates Physics-Informed Neural Networks (CoCo-PINNs) based on complex analysis and PDEs.
559   Our proposed approach of CoCo-PINNs successively and simultaneously solves the forward and in-
560   verse problem much more effectively than the classical PINNs approach. While the classical PINNs
561
      approach may occasionally demonstrate success in finding an imperfect function with a strong neu-
      tral inclusion effect, the reliability of this performance remains uncertain. In contrast, CoCo-PINNs
562
      present high credibility, consistency, and stability, with the additional advantage of being explain-
563
      able through analytical results. The potential applications of this method extend to analyzing and
564   manipulating the interaction of embedded inhomogeneities and surrounding media, such as finding
565   inclusions having uniform fields in their interiors. Several questions remain, including the general-
566   ization to multiple inclusions and three-dimensional problems, as well as proving the existence of
567   an interface function that achieves neutrality.

833   A connected open set in the complex plane is called a domain. We say that a domain Ω is simply
834   connected if its complement C \ Ω is connected.
835   Theorem 4 (Riemann mapping theorem). If Ω ⊊ C is a nonempty simply connected domain, then
836   there exists a conformal map from the unit ball B = {z ∈ C : |z| < 1} onto Ω.

       D.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP AND PARAMETERS
979
980
       In common settings for experiments, we set σm = 1, σm = 5 and σc = 3, 4, 6, 7 are also used.
981    The conformal radius γ = 1 and L = 5. which determines the domain of the conformal map, and
982    sample points are located in the square box of [−5, 5]2 . We generated collocation points |Ωext | =
983    21, 808, |Ωint | = 6, 000, and |∂Ω| = 6, 000 with a single epoch so that collocation points are all
984    fixed. For the neural networks’ architecture, we use 4 hidden layers with a width of 20 and the
985    Tanh activation due to its smoothness. We use Adam optimizers under 25,000 iterations adjusting
986    learning rates lr pinn: all types of neural networks and lr inv: for interface (inverse) parameters
987    decaying with η per 1000 iterations along the learning rate schedulers. We fix both learning rates
988    as 10−3 and η = 0.7. We use the CoCo-PINNs with p = p(20) which takes a real number p0 and
989    complex numbers {pi }20  i=1 as inverse parameters. To verify the classical PINNs’ result, we use the
990
       Fourier fitting with an order of 20. The Fourier fitting is explained in Appendix D.4. Note that using
       too high order makes singularities, and hence, it represents awful credibility. We set the initial value
991
       of the interface function to 5 for the square and spike shape to make it easier to satisfy the condition
992
       of being a positive function.
993
994    Remark on the environments Traditionally, hyperparameter tuning is important to acquire the
995    PINNs’ performance. Since balancing them is much more complicated due to the difficulty of the
996
       PDEs, we need to choose the appropriate values to enhance PINNs. Here are the brief guidelines
       for our settings: 1) When we balance the number of collocation points, although the exterior do-
997
       main is much larger than the interior, fitting the background fields on the exterior domain has been
998
       designed to be less complex. So we set 4 times of interior points. Also, the boundary effect is
999    essential for neutral inclusion, we put more points on the boundary same with the interior’s points.
1000   2) Re-sampling collocation points can lead to the uncertainty of the interface function. If we sample
1001   again for each epoch, PINNs are hardly adapted for new sample points, especially for the boundary
1002   condition loss, which is quite important for the neutral inclusion effects. It is also from the exper-
1003   imental results that the results of re-sampling are worse than those of fixed-sampling. Hence, we
1004   fix the points and proceed to a single epoch. 3) To control the learning rates adaptively, we use the
1005   Adam optimizers so that we can handle the sensitivity of Fourier coefficients much better. Since
1006   Adam has faster convergence and robustness, they can adjust the learning rates appropriately during
1007
       the training.
