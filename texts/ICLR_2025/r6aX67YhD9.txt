009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We study how to watermark LLM outputs, i.e. embedding algorithmically de-
012            tectable signals into LLM-generated text to track misuse. Unlike the current
013            mainstream methods that work with a fixed LLM, we expand the watermark de-
014            sign space by including the LLM tuning stage in the watermark pipeline. While
015            prior works focus on token-level watermark that embeds signals into the output,
016
               we design a model-level watermark that embeds signals into the LLM weights,
               and such signals can be detected by a paired detector. We propose a co-training
017
               framework based on reinforcement learning that iteratively (1) trains a detector
018
               to detect the generated watermarked text and (2) tunes the LLM to generate text
019            easily detectable by the detector while keeping its normal utility. We empirically
020            show that our watermarks are more accurate, robust, and adaptable (to new attacks)
021            with no generation overhead. It also allows watermarked model open-sourcing. In
022            addition, if used together with alignment, the extra overhead introduced is low –
023            we only need to train an extra reward model (i.e. our detector). We hope our work
024            can bring more effort into studying a broader watermark design that is not limited
025            to working with LLMs with unchanged model weights.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Watermarking LLM (Large Language Model) outputs, i.e., embedding algorithmically detectable sig-
030   nals into LLM-generated text, has recently become a potential solution to track LLM misuse Kirchen-
031   bauer et al. (2023a); Kuditipudi et al. (2023). So far, LLM watermarking methods focus on token-level
032   distortion in the LLM output. This framework has several limitations. (1) Since we still need the
033   watermarked text to be humanly readable, the output distortion induced needs to be minimized. As a
034   result, watermark accuracy might be suboptimal because the watermark signal injected in the output
035   space is constrained by the readability tradeoff. (2) For the same reason, the limited output distortion
036   leads to vulnerability to paraphrasing attacks Kirchenbauer et al. (2023b). (3) The design space of
037   watermark is inflexible – all the practitioners can do is post-processing the generated text from a
038
      fixed LLM, which leads to certain problems, e.g. lack of adaptability to newly discovered adversarial
      attacks. (4) It forbids practitioners from open-sourcing the watermarked LLMs. If they want to do so,
039
      they would also have to release the unwatermarked LLM because the watermarks are added post hoc,
040
      defeating the original purpose of protecting intellectual property.
041
042   In this work, we ask: Can we watermark LLM texts by directly finetuning the LLM, so that we can
043   enlarge the watermark design space? The watermark in our case is injected by model-level changes,
044
      and the resulting LLM outputs carry the signals that can be identified by detection.
045   In other words, we include the LLM tuning stage into the watermark pipeline as opposed to the prior
046   methods that only work with a fixed LLM, and thus expand the design space of watermark. Unlike
047   prior works whose detectors are statistical tests, our detector is a language model that directly predicts
048   whether a text is watermarked or not. Specifically, we tune the LLM to inject the watermark signal
049   while training a paired detector model that detects the signal. The key insight is: by tuning the LLM
050
      to adapt to the detector, we make the detection easier and more accurate.
051   Figure 1 (right) shows the overview of our reinforcement learning-based watermark framework. We
052   iteratively co-train both the LLM and the detector. In each step, we instruction-tune the LLM to
053   distort its weights and therefore its output distribution. Then, we train the detector to detect the signal
      from the distorted outputs.

069
070
      We choose reinforcement learning Ouyang et al. (2022); Arulkumaran et al. (2017) as the co-training
      framework for several reasons. (1) We can adapt the reward model as a detector. (2) We can leverage
071
      the strong generalizability of the RL algorithm Ouyang et al. (2022) to make sure the finetuned
072
      LLM can generate text that is easily detectable by the detector. (3) We still need to preserve the text
073   readability in general, which can be done by RLHF’s utility-preserving objective.
074
075   Our approach has several advantages compared to the prior works. (1) Detection Accuracy: Since
076
      we tune the LLM to fit the detector, we create more space for the detector because we explicitly ask
      the LLM to generate text easily detectable to the detector. (2) Robustness: Because we do not aim
077
      to rely on low-level (e.g. token-level) output distortion for watermark detection, our watermark can
078
      be more robust to adversarial attacks like paraphrasing. (3) Adaptability: Since our framework is
079   data-driven, we can easily iterate the LLM to adapt to new attacks by incorporating adversarially
080   generated text into the training data, in the style of adversarial training. This is not a feature supported
081   by the traditional fixed-model approach. (4) Zero Watermark-Generation Cost: Once the LLM is
082   deployed, we do not need any special operations during text generation to embed watermarks. This
083   zero-cost watermark generation makes our approach appealing when the LLM is deployed to serve at
084   a very large scale. (5) Open-source Feasibility: Since our watermarks are internally embedded into
085   the LLM weights and no special operation is needed in a post-hoc text generation, practitioners can
086   release the watermarked LLM without being forced to release an unwatermarked version.
087   Through the experiments, we show that our framework achieves near-perfect detection rate and
088   outperforms existing token-level approaches. We observe that our watermark is also robust to small
089   perturbations on the watermarked text. If we encounter large perturbations, we can include the
090   perturbed samples in the training stage, following the style of adversarial training, and achieve high
091   detection rate (AUC over 0.99), showing a strong adaptability of our approach unsupported by the
092   token-level watermarks.

      3     S CENARIO AND G OAL
123
      Scenario. We assume we are LLM service providers who aim to track the generated text from
124
      the LLMs we develop through watermarks. In addition, we have the computational resources to
125
      finetune the LLM and the ability to collect relevant finetuning data. The goal is to distinguish the
126   text generated by our LLM from any other sources (e.g. written by humans or generated by different
127   LLMs) as accurately as possible within a reasonable cost while not hurting the utility of the LLMs on
128   normal tasks.
129
130   Goal. Given the original LLM with parameter θo , we want to finetune it into another LLM θw
131   paired with a detector D : V ∗ × V ∗ → R that has the same architecture as an RM, except that it
132   outputs a detection score that quantifies how likely the output y given a prompt x is generated by our
133   watermarked model θw .
134   Let θd denote the parameter of the detector, D(x, y; θd ) denote the predicted score from θd that output
135   y is generated by model θw given prompt x.4 We want θw and θd to satisfy the following properties:
136   (1) Given an output y w := f (x; θw ) generated by the watermarked model θw from prompt x, the
137   detection score D(x, y w ; θd ) is high; (2) Given an output y nw not generated by the watermarked
138   model θw , e.g. written by humans or generated by other LLMs, the detection score D(x, y nw ; θd )
139   is low; (3) Our procedure should distort the output distribution as little as possible, preserving the
140   utility from the original LLM, i.e. f (x; θw ) ≈ f (x; θo ).

174                                        min [D(x, y nw ; θd ) − D(x, y w ; θd )].                       (3)

180   watermarked text y nw and text generated by the original LLM θo (line 1-8). Then we fine-tune
181   the LLM to obtain the watermarked LLM weights θw while simultaneously training the detector

186
      Detection. The detection of watermark is a simple forward pass through the detector. Given prompt
187   x and output y, we calculate the detection score D(x, y; θd ). A high score indicates that the output
188   y is likely to be generated by our LLM. We pick the threshold based on the criteria that the True
189   Positive Rate (TPR) reaches a certain value.

191   4.3   C OMBINING WITH A LIGNMENT
192
193   Since we need to use RL to co-train the LLM and the detector, we have a computationally expensive
194   stage for offline preparation. Therefore, it is best used together with the standard alignment so that
195   the additional overhead induced by our watermarking can be reduced significantly.
196   Given a normal alignment task where the reward model is θRM , we can use the combined reward
197   from both θRM and our detector θd in the PPO objective (1), i.e. replacing the labelled reward in
198   objective (1) with the following:
199
                                     α · rθRM (x, y) + (1 − α) · D(x, y; θd )                              (4)
200
201   where α is the weight balancing the alignment task’s reward and the watermarking task. All other
202   steps, e.g. LLM finetuning, are the same.
203   Compared with the standard RLHF pipeline, the extra cost we introduce is only training an extra
204   reward model (i.e. our detector) and running inference on it (i.e. labeling detection score). Today’s
205   RLHF already tends to use multiple reward models, and our watermarking reward model can be
206   incorporated into the current RLHF pipeline easily.

      5.4   PARAPHRASING ATTACKS
379
380   We evaluate the robustness of our method under paraphrasing attacks. We paraphrase responses by
381   two paraphrasing models: Pegasus Zhang et al. (2019) and DIPPER Krishna et al. (2023). Similarly
382   in Section 5.3, we incorporate the paraphrased responses as the watermarked text into our training in
383   the style of adversarial attack. Paraphrasing strength in Pegasus is quantified by temperature T , and
384   we evaluate at T = 1.0, 1.5, 2.0. Paraphrasing strength in DIPPER is quantified by diversity q for
385   both lexical diversity and order diversity, and we evaluate at q = 20, 40, 60.
386   Figure 3 shows the results w.r.t. Pegasus. The full results are in Table 15 in Appendix E. Unlike
387   substitution attacks, our method can already achieve decent robustness against paraphrasing and
388   outperform the baselines even when the paraphrasing strength is low. It is because token-level
389   methods are known to be vulnerable to paraphrasing while our model-level approach watermarks the
390   response not based on replacing specific tokens, but modifying the response as a whole, therefore
391   the change we induce is at the semantic level, which is less vulnerable to paraphrasing. In addition,
392   similar to substitution attacks, our method can achieve stronger robustness by adversarial training.
393   Figure 4 shows the robustness of the model adversarially trained on Pegasus-paraphrased responses
394   and tested on DIPPER-paraphrased responses. The full results are in Table 16 in Appendix E. We can
395   see that finetuning the LLM with Pegasus attacks can also improve the robustness against DIPPER
396   attacks, showing the flexibility to incorporate new attacks into the watermarks.

398   5.5   RUNTIME OVERHEAD
399
400   There are three types of runtime overhead for a LLM watermark: the offline preparation cost, the
401   generation overhead and the detection overhead. We emphasize that in practice, the generation
402   overhead is the most concerning, followed by the detection overhead and finally one-time preparation
403   cost. This is because the offline preparation needs to be done only once, while the demand for
404   generating texts with LLMs is usually much higher than detecting whether a text is LLM-generated
405
      or not. In practice, only suspected text (e.g. user-flagged) need to be checked by the detector. In
      addition, the latency requirement for generation is much higher than detection. Generation needs to
406
      be fast because users will wait for it to complete in real time. By comparison, it is more acceptable if
407
      it takes a longer time for the text to be detected (which, in some settings, can be done offline).
408
409   Table 2 shows the per-sample generation and detection time on the PKU task. The time cost on the
410   C4 dataset is similar and thus omitted. The time is evaluated by an A100-80GB GPU and a 32-core
411
      CPU. In addition to per-sample time cost, our method also requires the one-time finetuning, which
      takes around 3 hours for the OPT model and around 1.7 days for the Llama-2 model (which can be
412
      accelerated when combined with standard alignment). Our method has the lowest generation time
413
      and the second lowest detection time compared to the baseline methods. This is because the baseline
414   methods require multiple hashing and sampling processes as the generation overhead and multiple
415   hashing and statistical tests for the detection. Note that these are CPU-heavy tasks and cannot be
416   parallelized with GPUs. By comparison, our method has no generation overhead while our detection
417   overhead can be further reduced by GPU parallelization. Considering an LLM is normally deployed
418   on a large scale, we believe our generation time minimization design is a more appealing tradeoff.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
444
445
      LLM Watermark. KGW Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) is the first work to show how to watermark
446   an LLM output by randomly splitting the vocabulary into two parts and setting a higher probability
447   to samples from one. In a follow-up work Kirchenbauer et al. (2023b), researchers further show
448   that the approach works when the watermarked text is long. Many follow-up works follow a similar
449   approach. Lee et al. (2023) adapt KGW to code generation by only focusing on high-entropy tokens.
450   Zhao et al. (2023) uses a fixed vocabulary splitting and shows it can lead to a provable watermark.
451   Fernandez et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2023) proposes better techniques to improve the generation and
452   detection performance. Hou et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023) proposes to sample vocabulary based on
453   the semantic meaning so that the watermark can be robust against paraphrasing attacks.
454   KGW-based approach has certain limitations, e.g. distributional change and inability to be publicly
455   verifiable Ajith et al. (2023). Partially motivated to overcome those limitations, Kuditipudi et al.
456   (2023) proposes a distortion-free watermark schema by pre-sampling a random key for the LLM
457   generation. Christ et al. (2023) uses a private key and proposes the undetectable watermark from
458   the view of cryptography. Fairoze et al. (2023) proposes that the message can be publicly verifiable
459   using rejection sampling. Note that those approaches are inference-time techniques and do not
460
      fine-tune the model. More recently, Gu et al. (2023) proposes to fine-tune an LLM to distill the
      model with inference-time watermark, making it a model-level watermark which is similar to our
461
      approach. However, the detection pipeline is still statistical tests rather than model-based detection.
462
      Furthermore, the method is often underperformed by KGW-based approaches.
463
464
      LLM Text Detection. Another related field is LLM text detection Wu et al. (2023). The problem is
465   to directly detect whether a text is generated by LLMs or not, without changing any model training
466   or text generation procedures. Mitchell et al. (2023) proposes to detect GPT-generated texts with
467   curvature analysis on the text log probability function. Wang et al. (2023b) shows that the previous
468   work can be improved with self-masking prediction. Wang et al. (2023a) propose to do classification
469   based on the prediction logits. These works aim to detect general LLM texts and do not interfere
470   with model’s training or generation. By comparison, our goal is to only detect texts generated by a
471   specific (watermarked) model, and we finetune the LLM model to help us achieve the goal so that the
472   detection is more accurate.
473
474   7     C ONCLUSION
475
476   We propose a model-based watermarking pipeline to track the outputs generated by LLMs. We
477   use a reinforcement learning based framework to co-train a paired watermark detector and LLMs
478   by alternating between (1) finetuning the LLM to generate text easily detectable by the detector
479   and (2) training the detector to accurately detect the generated watermarked text. We empirically
480   show that our watermarks are more accurate, robust, and adaptable to new attacks. It also supports
481   open-sourcing. We hope our work can bring more effort into studying a broader watermark design.
482   Limitation. We point out several limitations. First, the need for finetuning might make our compu-
483   tational cost higher than the fixed-model approach. Second, as we are a data-driven approach, we
484   require relevant training data. Last, our detection is requires a more costly one-time fine-tuning than
485   simple statistical tests in the fixed-model approach. Nevertheless, the first two issues can be mitigated
      when our watermark is integrated into a standard LLM alignment pipeline.
