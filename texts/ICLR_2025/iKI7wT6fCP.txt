                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become essential for learning from graph-
010
               structured data. However, existing GNNs do not consider the conservation law
011            inherent in graphs associated with a flow of physical resources, such as electri-
012            cal current in power grids or traffic in transportation networks. To address this
013            limitation and enhance the performance on tasks where accurate modeling of re-
014            source flows is crucial, we propose Flow Graph Neural Networks (FlowGNNs).
015            This novel GNN framework adapts existing graph attention mechanisms to reflect
016            the conservation of resources by distributing a nodeŌĆÖs message among its out-
017            going edges instead of allowing arbitrary duplication of the nodeŌĆÖs information.
018            We further extend this framework to directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), enabling
019
               discrimination between non-isomorphic flow graphs that would otherwise be in-
               distinguishable for standard GNNs tailored to DAGs. We validate our approach
020
               through extensive experiments on two different flow graph domainsŌĆöelectronic
021
               circuits and power gridsŌĆöand demonstrate that the proposed framework enhances
022            the performance of traditional GNN architectures on both graph-level classifica-
023            tion and regression tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Graph-structured data represents the complex relationships and interactions between entities as a
029   set of nodes and edges and is prevalent across many real-world domains, such as social networks
030   (Fan et al., 2019), recommender systems (Wu et al., 2022), materials science (Reiser et al., 2022)
031   or epidemiology (Liu et al., 2024). Traditional deep learning methods, which are typically designed
032   for Euclidean data such as images (Li et al., 2021) or sequences (Lim & Zohren, 2021), fail to fully
033
      exploit the irregular structure of graphs. To address this, graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli
      et al., 2008; Kipf & Welling, 2017) have emerged as a powerful framework that extends the scope
034
      of deep learning to graph-based data, enabling models to learn both node-level features as well as
035
      the underlying graph topology through iterative message-passing between neighboring nodes. As
036   graph data becomes increasingly common, advancing GNN architectures is crucial for improving
037   performance in tasks such as node classification (Hamilton et al., 2017), graph regression (Gilmer
038   et al., 2017), or link prediction (Zhang & Chen, 2018).
039
      In many important applications of GNNs, graphs are naturally associated with a flow of physical
040
      resources, such as electrical current in electronic circuits (Sa╠ünchez et al., 2023) or power grids (Liao
041
      et al., 2021), traffic in transportation networks (Jiang & Luo, 2022), water in river networks (Sun
042   et al., 2021), or raw materials and goods in supply chains (Kosasih & Brintrup, 2022). In these
043   (resource) flow graphs, all nodes, except for source and target nodes, are subject to KirchhoffŌĆÖs
044   first law, which states that the sum of all incoming and outgoing flows must be zero, reflecting
045   the conservation of resources. By contrast, informational graphsŌĆösuch as computation graphs,
046   social networks, or citation networksŌĆöare not associated with any physical flow but rather represent
047   relationships or information transfer. Information can be arbitrarily duplicated and propagated in
048   these graphs, unlike in flow graphs, where such duplication would violate the conservation law.
049   As a result, two non-isomorphic graphs may be equivalent as informational graphs (e.g., they rep-
050   resent the same computation) but non-equivalent as flow graphs (e.g., they represent different elec-
051   tronic circuits). An example of this is given in Fig. 1. Since the result of the sine operation can
052   be duplicated without constraints and transmitted to arbitrarily many other operations, the two non-
053   isomorphic graph structures represent the same computation. However, the two graph structures may
      also represent electronic circuits, which are governed by KirchhoffŌĆÖs first law. In this case, the two

080   In recent years, many new GNN models have been specifically designed for different graph types
081
      (Thomas et al., 2023). However, despite their fundamental differences, informational graphs and
      flow graphs are still treated by the same basic message-passing layers (MPLs), such as GCN (Kipf
082
      & Welling, 2017), GIN (Xu et al., 2019) or GAT (Velic╠īkovic╠ü et al., 2018). In these models, messages
083
      exchanged between neighboring nodes do not depend on the number of message recipients. Instead,
084   the information is arbitrarily duplicated and passed to all neighbors. Even attention mechanisms,
085   as applied in GAT, GATv2 (Brody et al., 2022) or Graph Transformer (Shi et al., 2021), are only
086   normalizing across incoming messages and therefore cannot overcome this limitation.
087
      Many flow graphs, including the example graphs in Fig. 1, can be naturally expressed as directed
088
      acyclic graphs (DAGs), e.g., operational amplifiers (Dong et al., 2023) or material flow networks
089
      (Perera et al., 2018). In these cases, nodes are typically updated sequentially following the partial or-
090   der of the DAG, and the final target node representation is used as the graph embedding (Zhang et al.,
091   2019; Thost & Chen, 2021). However, since directed acyclic GNNs are utilizing non-conservational
092   message-passing schemes resulting in identical target node representations, they are not capable of
093   distinguishing between non-isomorphic flow graphs such as in Fig. 1.
094
      A possible approach to overcome the problem of indistinguishable flow graphs is to use node in-
095
      dices or random features as input node features (Loukas, 2020; Sato et al., 2021), which makes
096   the model capable of uniquely identifying each node. However, the resulting GNN model is no
097   longer permutation invariant, which reduces its generalization capability. Similar problems arise for
098   Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) such as PACE (Dong et al., 2022), which incorpo-
099   rate the relational inductive bias Battaglia et al. (2018) via positional encodings. A different strategy
100   would be to introduce KirchhoffŌĆÖs first law through an additional physics-informed loss term (Donon
101   et al., 2020), which is useful if the target variable is the resource flow itself. However, introducing
102   additional loss terms considerably increases the training complexity and does not overcome the
103   fundamental limitations of message-passing neural networks in distinguishing non-equivalent flow
104
      graphs.
105   To overcome the above problems that arise when applying message-passing (directed acyclic) GNNs
106   to resource flow graphs, we propose a new GNN framework that builds upon attentional GNNs.
107   Instead of normalizing the attention scores across incoming neighbors, we normalize them across
      outgoing neighbors. This simple but effective modification ensures that the message of a specific

108
      node is distributed among all message recipients and thereby avoids arbitrary message duplication,
109   reflecting the conservation of physical resources in flow graphs.
110
111   Our contributions are the following:
112
            1. GNN framework for flow graphs: We develop a new framework called Flow Graph
113            Neural Network (FlowGNN) which replaces the standard attention mechanism of existing
114            GNNs with a flow attention mechanism that ensures the conservation of physical resources
115            as they traverse through the graph.
116         2. GNN model for directed acyclic flow graphs: We further extend the new framework
117            to DAGs, resulting in a model called Directed Acyclic Flow Graph Neural Network
118            (DAFlowGNN): We show that DAFlowGNN can distinguish non-isomorphic directed
119            acyclic flow graphs which would otherwise be mapped to the same representation by stan-
120            dard DAGNNs.
121         3. Extensive Experiments: We conduct experiments on two different flow graph domains
122            (electronic circuits and power grids), covering both undirected graphs and DAGs, and show
123            that our proposed models outperform their standard counterparts on graph-level classifica-
124            tion and regression tasks across multiple datasets.

      the distinction of non-isomorphic node neighborhoods despite identical rooted subtrees.

363   4.1   DATASETS , TASKS , AND BASELINES
364
      Datasets We perform experiments on two different flow graph datasets. First, we test different
365
      FlowGNNs (FlowGAT, FlowGATv2, and FlowGT) on publicly available power grid data from the
366   PowerGraph dataset (Varbella et al., 2024), which encompasses the IEEE24, IEEE39, IEEE118,
367   and UK transmission systems. The graphs contained in these datasets are undirected and cyclic and
368   represent test power systems with the aim of mirroring real-world power grids. The test systems
369   differ from each other in scale and topology, covering a wide range of relevant parameters. Sec-
370   ondly, we test DAFlowGNN on the Ckt-Bench101 dataset from the publicly available Open Circuit
371   Benchmark (OCB) (Dong et al., 2023), which contains 10,000 operational amplifiers (Op-Amps)
372   represented as DAGs. The dataset further provides circuit specifications for each Op-Amp, e.g.,
373   gain and bandwidth, which were obtained from circuit simulations. Further details on all datasets
374
      can be found in App. A.3.
375   Tasks For the PowerGraph dataset, we train the models to perform cascading failure analysis.
376   Thereby, we utilize the attributed graphs provided by the PowerGraph dataset, each representing
377   unique pre-outage operating conditions along with a set of outages corresponding to the removal of
      a single or multiple branches. An outage may result in demand not served (DNS) by the grid, and


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      a cascading failure may occur, meaning that one or more additional branches trip after the initial
379   outage. In this scenario, we focus on two graph-level tasks: Binary and multiclass classification.
380   For binary classification, the model is supposed to predict whether the grid is stable (DNS = 0 MW)
381   or unstable (DNS > 0 MW) after the outage. For multiclass classification, the model should addi-
382   tionally predict whether a cascading failure occurs, resulting in four distinct categories representing
383   the possible combinations of stable/unstable and cascading failure yes/no. For Ckt-Bench101, we
384   perform graph-level regression to predict the properties of the Op-Amps. For this purpose, we train
385   three separate instances of each model for the prediction of gain, bandwidth, and figure of merit
386   (FoM), respectively. The FoM is a measure of the circuitŌĆÖs overall performance and depends on
387
      gain, bandwidth, and phase margin.
388   Baselines We compare the FlowGNNs on the PowerGraph dataset against their corresponding stan-
389   dard GNN versions GAT, GATv2, and GraphTransformer. Furthermore, we compare them against
390   two more widely adopted non-attentional GNNs from the literature: GCN, and GINe (Hu et al.,
391   2020), a modified version of GIN, which is able to incorporate edge features. In the second ex-

529
530
531
      In this paper, we proposed FlowGNN, a GNN framework based on a flow attention mechanism
      that accounts for the conservation of resources in flow graphs. We also extended this framework
532
      to DAGs, resulting in a model called DAFlowGNN, which can distinguish non-isomorphic directed
533
      acyclic flow graphs which were so far indistinguishable for existing GNNs tailored to DAGs. Fur-
534   thermore, we showed that our models outperform their standard counterparts on graph-level regres-
535   sion and classification tasks across different flow graph datasets. In the future, it could be interesting
536   to analyze how the proposed models scale to larger circuits and power grids. Another interesting
537   direction for future work would be to investigate the performance of the proposed models on node-
538   and edge-level tasks, as well as on other flow graph data, such as traffic networks or supply chains.
539   Additionally, more theoretical work is required to gain a deeper understanding of the expressivity of
      our models compared to standard GNNs.
