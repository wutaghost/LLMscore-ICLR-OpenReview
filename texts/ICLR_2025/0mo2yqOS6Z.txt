011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we investigate the fundamental trade-off regarding accuracy and
014            parameter efficiency in neural network weight parameterization using predic-
015
               tor networks. We present a surprising finding where the predicted model not
               only matches but also surpasses the original model’s performance through the
016
               reconstruction objective (MSE loss) alone. Remarkably this improvement can be
017
               compound incrementally over multiple rounds of reconstruction. Moreover, we
018            extensively explore the underlying factors for improving weight reconstruction
019            under parameter-efficiency constraints and propose a novel training scheme that
020            decouples the reconstruction objective from auxiliary objectives such as knowledge
021            distillation that leads to significant improvements compared to state-of-the-art
022            approaches. Finally, these results pave the way for more practical scenarios, where
023            one needs to achieve improvements in both model accuracy and predictor network
024            parameter-efficiency simultaneously.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Recently, neural network weight space exploration and manipulation have gained an increase in
029
      popularity as an additional step to improve model performance after traditional model training,
030
      fine-tuning or compression. Examples of these methods range from weight manipulation strategies
031   such as weight merging to improve model performance without fine-tuning (Matena & Raffel, 2022)
032   to weight generation approaches that directly predict network parameters like Neural Representation
033   for Neural Networks (NeRN) (Ashkenazi et al., 2022) or diffusion-based D2NWG (Soro et al.,
034   2024). NeRN extends the concept of neural representations popularized in Neural Radiance Fields
035   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) to the domain of neural network weight space learning that has
036   increasingly become an essential foundation for optimizing model performance in various applications.
037   This innovative approach shifts from representing data (e.g., images or videos) to representing neural
038   network parameters as functions, introducing a structured, coordinate-driven framework for weight
039
      generation. Specifically, NeRN employs implicit neural representation (INRs) (Sitzmann et al.,
      2020) to map kernel or layer indices directly to their corresponding weights, offering a compact and
040
      continuous functional encoding of network parameters. By transforming weights into a function,
041
      NeRN opens the door to exciting possibilities such as dynamic parameter generation, model storage,
042   and novel forms of network adaptation. Despite its conceptual appeal, NeRN has practical limitations:
043   reconstructed weights fail to match the original model’s performance, and its potential usability in
044   applications like model compression, fine-tuning, or transfer learning remains under-explored.
045
      In this work, we address these limitations by advancing the understanding and improving NeRN-style
046
      weight parametrization through novel progressive-training and decoupled training strategies, enabling
047
      effective reparametrization. We demonstrate that better-performing models can be obtained through
048   a weight reconstruction-only objective and these improvements can be compounded over multiple
049   repetitions. By iterating over the reconstruction process multiple times, where each round builds upon
050   the previously reconstructed weights, we establish an a recursive setup with nested improvements
051   in each prediction round. NeRN utilizes a multi-objective loss function where the reconstruction
052   loss serves as the primary objective, while distillation losses (feature-level and logit-level) play
053   a critical complementary role in improving reconstruction fidelity. These distillation losses help
      stabilize the learning process and enable the predictor network to better approximate the original

054
      model’s behavior. However, we identified a key challenge: the multi-objective loss function leads
055   to contradictory training signals during the reconstruction process, resulting in limited performance
056   gains. Therefore, we propose a new training scheme that decouples the learning objectives into two
057   phases: a reconstruction phase and a distillation phase, ensuring that each learning objective has the
058   desired impact. Remarkably, our approach results in significant improvements compared to NeRN for
059   reconstruction fidelity and network compression. Moreover, the proposed separation provides greater
060   flexibility in the distillation step by allowing the use of powerful networks to be involved during the
061   weight refinement. All these improvements and insights led to several usage scenarios that were not
062   possible or practical before, for example, obtaining a better-performing model through a predictor
063
      network that is much smaller than the original network, or iterative improving a given model through
      the reconstruction process. The proposed approach provides a unique, even surprising, perspective
064
      for achieving model performance improvement that is different from existing weight manipulation
065
      approaches such as stochastic weight averaging (Guo et al., 2023a; Izmailov et al., 2018). We also
066   achieve storage compression via the predictor network, which is orthogonal and composable with
067   existing model quantization, model pruning (Lee et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2021; Wang
068   et al., 2021; He & Xiao, 2023) and knowledge distillation approaches (Chen et al., 2020; Gou et al.,
069   2021; Chen et al., 2017; Beyer et al., 2022).

073   2    P REDICTING M ODEL W EIGHTS USING N EURAL R EPRESENTATIONS
074
075
      There is a growing interest in predicting the weights of pre-trained models not only to enhance
076   the memory efficiency of model storage but also to improve the throughput of model inference.
077   Existing solutions range from building high-fidelity auxiliary models (Knyazev et al., 2023) to
078   learning parameter-efficient approximators (Guo et al., 2023b). However, in this paper, we focus on
079   neural representations learning based on their flexibility and potential for producing effective, yet
080   parameter-efficient, approximations for deep networks. The NeRN framework first explored the idea
081   of training INRs and demonstrated its utility in compressing CNNs. At its core, NeRN uses a 5-layer
082   multilayer perceptron (MLP) Gϕ with fixed hidden layer size to learn a mapping from an input tuple
083   (layer ℓ, filter f, channel c) to the corresponding k × k kernel in the original CNN model Fθ . Note
084
      that the output size of the MLP also remains fixed at the largest kernel size and smaller kernels in
      the CNN are sampled from the middle while fully-connected or normalization layers are excluded
085
      based on their comparatively negligible parameter size. Since there is no inherent smoothness in the
086
      ordering of filters in a CNN, permutation strategies are introduced to rearrange filters in the original
087   model based on similarity, ensuring stable training of NeRN.
088
089   The central component of NeRN training is the reconstruction loss aimed at quantifying the disparity
090
      between the original network weights and those recovered using the predictor. Regardless of the
      capacity of Gϕ , one might expect that the reconstruction loss should reliably converge to a meaningful
091
      approximation. However, in practice, it has been found that NeRN is prone to training instabilities
092
      when the auxiliary model is smaller than the original network. To mitigate these issues, the authors
093   introduced additional loss terms inspired by existing knowledge distillation methods. Note that, while
094   the reconstruction loss does not require access to training data, the distillation loss does, making it
095   impractical for scenarios where access to the training data is not available. The overall objective
096   function for NeRN can be expressed as

127   In this section, we take a closer look at learning neural representations for pre-trained neural networks.
128   Specifically, we explore the role of different training objectives in realizing an effective parameteri-
129   zation. Based on our findings, we propose a new training scheme that circumvents the undesirable
130   trade-off between accuracy and model compression rate, and simultaneously improve on both aspects.

      3.1   I S THE R ECONSTRUCTION O BJECTIVE A LL YOU N EED ?
133
134   A well-known limitation of existing approaches used for weight prediction is that they trade off
135   accuracy to achieve parameter efficiency. This non-trivial compromise in model performance can
136   be a critical bottleneck for practical applications. While existing approaches attempt to recover the
137   lost performance through the use of additional objectives, e.g., distillation as in NeRN, they lead
138   to increased reconstruction errors, albeit providing improvements in the accuracy. As this seems
139   counter-intuitive, it naturally raises the question: What is the relationship between reconstruction
140   error and expected model performance?
141   If we assume that reconstruction error, e.g., mean-squared error (MSE), is indeed an indication
142   for performance, a straightforward strategy to improve performance would be to increase the ca-
143   pacity of the predictor network, allowing it to overfit to the original model weights. To this end,
144   we first empirically analyze how well we can recover the
145   original network’s performance, as we continually reduce
146   the reconstruction error by increasing the predictor net-
147
      work capacity. Note that in this analysis, we are not con-
      cerned about parameter efficiency, and the neural represen-
148
      tations are trained solely with the reconstruction objective.
149
      Interestingly, by using only the reconstruction loss and
150   increasing the predictor network capacity, we empirically
151   find weight parameterizations with non-zero reconstruc-
152   tion errors that not only recover the true performance but
153   even surpass it as shown in Figure 2.

      3.2   D ISTILLATION I MPROVES C OMPRESSION , B UT O NLY WITH L OSS D ECOUPLING
225
226   So far, we inspected the behavior of the reconstruction loss, and demonstrated its surprising efficacy
227   in enhancing model performance. Despite the observed performance improvement, we did not take
228   parameter-efficiency into account for our analysis. However, in practice, an important motivation
229   for using weight prediction networks is to achieve signification reduction in memory requirements
230   for model storage, while not trading-off the performance unreasonably. While NeRN originally
231   leveraged with such a compression objective, their accuracy trade-off makes them a less preferred
232   choice over other model compression (or reduction) strategies in practice. In this section, we show
233   how the compression capability of neural representations can be enhanced. To this end, we take a
234
      closer look at the widely adopted distillation objective and how it interacts with the reconstruction
      loss. By doing so, we address the critical need to strike a balance between model complexity and
235
      efficiency, paving the way for more practical and resource-efficient neural networks.

260   progressive training, the reconstruction-only training cannot match the true performance. Hence,
261   to recover the lost performance while also enabling parameter-reduction (i.e., CR < 1), NeRN

263   orange bar in Figure 5(c), the predictor network’s performance can be significantly improved with the
264   guidance of this distillation process. This can be attributed to the fact that the arbitrary perturbations
265   in the reconstructed network induced by the distillation losses can make non-trivial changes to the
266
      decision rules, thus impacting the generalization performance of the network. Hence, additional
      guidance in terms of the prediction probabilities provides valuable task-relevant information.
267
268   While the original NeRN approach has proven effective, we observe that the role of the distillation
269   objective is to primarily supplement the reconstruction loss. For instance, Figure 5(b) illustrates
      the layer-wise discrepancies between the reconstructed weights in the Recon-only and the NeRN

278   To address this critical limitation, we propose to employ the two objectives in distinct training stages.
279   Initially, we train the predictor network solely based on the reconstruction objective (Recon-only)
280   and optionally perform progressive training when the predictor network capacity is high. In the
281   second phase, we fine-tune the predictor stage 1 using only the distillation objective, LKD . As
282   shown in Figure 5(b), this allows the predictor to explore solutions that can in principle be different
283   from the original network, but adhere to similar decision boundaries. Interestingly, we find that
284   this two-stage optimization leads to significant differences in the early layers of the network, while
285
      still matching the later layers. This is intuitive, as it is well known that the decision rules typically
      emerge in the later layers of a deep network. While the larger differences in the early layers may
286
      seemingly compromise reconstruction fidelity, this separate training strategy facilitates a more
287
      effective integration of distillation into the network parametrization, as evidenced by significant
288   improvements (red bars in Figure 4(c)). As we show later, this decoupling of the training objectives
289   not only demonstrates greater resilience to variations in the predictor network size, but also recovers
290   or even surpasses the performance of the original network with predictors that are > 40% smaller
291   than the original network, which NeRN cannot achieve.

293   3.3   I MPROVING C OMPRESSION -P ERFORMANCE T RADE - OFF VIA S TRONG T EACHERS
294
295   In Section 3.1, and 3.2, we considered the performance or the compression objective independently.
296   However, a natural next question is whether one can improve the compression vs. performance
297   trade-off, and obtain additional improvements in both aspects. The proposed decoupled training
298   offers flexibility in the second phase after the initial reconstruction objective is accomplished. One
299   particular benefit of such a decoupling is that it enables the use of other high-performing models
300
      for guiding the distillation phase. We argue that by leveraging guidance from a high-performing
      teacher, one can further improve the efficacy of decoupled training, thereby improving on the
301
      performance-compression trade-off. In other words, through the proposed strategies, one can
302
      achieve non-trivial improvements to model accuracy for a fixed predictor network capacity, or easily
303   push past the original network’s performance via progressive reconstruction. This flexibility of our
304   proposed approach goes beyond the decoupled training, as every component we have introduced can
305   be combined with each other or with other methods (e.g., model quantization, pruning, and standard
306   knowledge distillation). To help put everything together, we provide a summary of our findings and
307   components of the proposed approach in Figure 1.

468
      quantization using the ‘fbgemm’ backend (Khudia et al., 2021) with the int8 approach. Note that
469   the core contribution of this study lies in effective reparameterization via progressive and decoupled
470   training strategies. While decoupled training enhances model compression, our objectives differ from
471   quantization methods. We believe that combining reparameterization with quantization could advance
472   both model fine-tuning and efficient inference. To strengthen our hypothesis,we first perform a direct

477
      than the quantized model. Moreover, our predictor can be further compressed to achieve an even
      smaller model size by leveraging quantization techniques, as demonstrated in the last column. This
478
      further strengthens our hypothesis that these two methods can provide complementary benefits.

498   Weight space manipulation provides a direct way to alter model behavior and comes in a variety
499   of flavors. The recent development of ever larger models (Shoeybi et al., 2019) makes fine-tuning
500   existing models increasingly challenging, as a result, post-training model merging (Matena & Raffel,
501   2022; Tam et al., 2023; Ilharco et al., 2022) are becoming increasingly popular that combines existing
502   available models for performance enhancement.
503
      Implicit neural representations (INR) (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Tancik et al., 2020) were initially
504   designed for representing low-dimensional data (e.g., 2D or 3D) with complex and potential high-
505   frequency signals. Recently, INR has then been utilized for a variety of domains, e.g., from uncovering
506   correlation in scientific data (Chitturi et al., 2023) to estimating human pose (Yen-Chen et al., 2021).
507   We use INR for predicting filter weights for model reconstruction.
508
      Knowledge distillation and pruning has been widely adopted for reducing model size while
509
      preserving model performance. Knowledge distillation (Chen et al., 2020; Gou et al., 2021; Chen
510   et al., 2017; Beyer et al., 2022) utilizes a more capable teacher network to transfer prediction behavior
511   to the student network. Pruning techniques (Lee et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2021; Wang
512   et al., 2021; He & Xiao, 2023) aim to remove non-essential or potentially duplicated functionality in
513   the network and reduce the overall parameter counts.
514
      Semantic representation of neural networks encode meaningful, interpretable features aligned
515
      with human-understandable concepts Unterthiner et al. (2020); Schürholt et al. (2021); Peebles et al.
516   (2022); Schürholt et al. (2022); Lim et al. (2023); Navon et al. (2023); Herrmann et al. (2024);
517   Schürholt et al. (2024); Kofinas et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024b;a). Our implicit representations
518   encode information in a distributed and flexible manner, capturing complex patterns and relationships.

522   6     D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524   In this work, we identified effective strategies that significantly improve the accuracy of the recon-
525   structed model and compression ratio for predictor networks through exploring various trade-offs
526   in the parameterization of model weights with neural representation. While effective, one area
527   of the limitations is that the current predictor only works with CNN architectures, restricting its
528   usage. Moreover, despite the flexibility of the proposed protocols that can be combined or re-applied,
529   the necessary additional steps incur more training runs which can lead to a significant increase in
530   computation cost and complexity. However, the increased effort may be worth it to support edge
531
      applications where the benefits are multiplied by the number of deployed instances. Still, to help
      address these challenges, we need methods that can predict weights for diverse architectures and
532
      are ideally more efficient when the target model grows in size and complexity. Another interesting
533
      direction that is worthy of further investigation is the relationship between the weight smoothing and
534   the model’s generalization ability. Could we directly alter the original weights to achieve a similar
535   effect without the need to train a predictor model? Or can we potentially use that insight during
536   training as a regularization that directly improves models’ generalizability? We believe that our
537   research on several key areas—such as how weight parameterization converges, the data requirements,
538   the interplay between different loss components, and the challenges of distillation in reparameterized
539   models—offers valuable insights that can significantly improve model training and deployment
      practices.

      A                           F REQUENCY AND S INGULAR VALUE M ODULATION
757
758
      In Section 3.1, we observed interesting behavior of the weight through the lens of singular value
759   ratio (Sratio ) analysis, where higher Sratio values correlated with improved performance of the
760   reconstructed model, as shown in Figure 3. We hypothesize that the reconstruction process may
761   implicitly induce a weight smoothing effect, which relates to a reduction in high-frequency or noise
762   components, and in turn, improves the model’s generalization. To establish a closer and tangible
763   connection between model performance and frequency or singular value-based modulation, we
764   consider two simple, complementary approaches that directly apply manipulation on model weights:
765   (1) reducing the high-frequency components of the weight through frequency-based modulation,
766   and (2) downscaling the low singular values that only contribute minimally to the overall weight
767
      structure. We find that both procedures can independently lead to improved model performance
      without any additional training, though these heuristics require carefully tuned parameter on test data,
768
      which makes them less feasible for practical application. Our goal for these experiments is to further
769
      investigate why the reconstruction objective alone can improve reconstructed models’ performance.
770
771   Frequency-based weight modulation: First, we simply suppress the high-frequency components
772   using an exponential low-pass filter. Specifically, given a weight tensor w ∈ Rcout ×cin ×k×k , to focus
773
      on frequency patterns across the spatial dimensions, the weight tensor is reshaped to cout ×cin ×(k×k).
      We then apply the FFT along the spatial dimensions, transforming the tensor into its frequency
774
      representation. To reduce the high-frequency components, we leverage a Gaussian low-pass filter
775
      defined as: H(f ) = exp(−0.5(f /D0 )2 ) where f is the frequency index, and D0 represents the
776   cutoff frequency. A smaller D0 leads to greater suppression of high-frequency components. After
777   low-pass filtering, we apply the inverse FFT to return the frequency-modified weights back into the
778   spatial domain. We apply this module to one block of ResNet56 while keeping the other blocks fixed,
779   considering only the convolutional layers and excluding fully connected layers, batch normalization
780   layers, and others. Figure 7 presents the model performance on test set (CIFAR100) using these
781   modified weights (solid blue line) compared to the original model without frequency filtering (dotted
782   red line).
783   Interestingly, suppressing the high-frequency components with an appropriate cutoff frequency (D0 )
784   improves test accuracy. This trend is observed across all blocks of ResNet56, with the first block
785   showing the most significant improvement. Note that, selecting the optimal cutoff frequency for
786   each layer is non-trivial in practice, especially without access to test data. Our proposed progres-
787   sive optimization automatically performs this smoothing effect, eliminating the need for manual
788   hyperparameter tuning in different layers.

976    G.2     T RAINING D ETAILS
977
978    Training of NeRN: We follow the same settings outlined in Ashkenazi et al. (2022). The baseline
979    method employs a Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) with 5 layers as a predictor, with varying hidden
980    sizes. Training is conducted using the ranger optimizer (Wright, 2019) with a learning rate of 5e − 3.
981    The number of epochs for training is 350 for CIFAR-10 and STL-10, 450 for CIFAR-100, and
982
       16 × 104 iterations for ImageNet experiments. Similar to minibatch sampling in standard stochastic
       optimization, during each training step, it predicts all reconstructed weights but optimizes only on a
983
       mini-batch of them. The weights batch method employed is a random weighted batch, using weighted
984
       sampling with a probability of 1 − puni , where puni = 0.8, and a batch size of 4096 for CIFAR-10,
985    CIFAR-100, and STL-10 datasets. For ImageNet, the experiment was conducted with a minibatch size
986    of 216 . Hyperparameters α and β in learning objectives are set to 1e − 5 for CIFAR-100 and STL-10
987    datasets, and to 1e − 4 to CIFAR-10, and 1e − 6 for ImageNet. Based on empirical observations,
988    increasing the hyperparameter values during training to emphasize the distillation process causes the
989    method to experience highly unstable training, often resulting in convergence failure. Therefore, we
990    opted to use the same values as suggested by the authors.
991    When training predictors, there are two types of permutation-based smoothness: In-Filter and Cross-
992    Filter. Both approaches do not show significant difference in terms of accuracy. The order of weights
993    in the original network remains unchanged; this smoothness only affects the order in which the
994    predictor processes the kernels. In all experiments, In-Filter smoothness was used for CIFAR-10,
995    CIFAR-100, and STL-10 datasets, while Cross-Filter smoothness was employed for the ImageNet
996    dataset.
997    Training of Progressive-Reconstruction Training: We adhere to the same settings as full training
998    in the NeRN method, including the number of epochs, batch size, learning rate, and other parameters.
999    To isolate and illustrate the reconstruction’s pure effect, the predictor is trained only with the
1000   reconstruction loss, Lrecon . For the next round of progressive-reconstruction training, we select the
1001   best-performing models from the previously reconstructed network, determined across three trials
1002   with different random seeds, as the target network. If the performance does not surpass that of the
1003   target network, we conclude the round. To elucidate the training protocols, we present the results of
1004   all three trials conducted on the CIFAR-100 dataset. As observed in the trend of improvement, the
1005
       gap in enhancement diminishes as the rounds progress.
