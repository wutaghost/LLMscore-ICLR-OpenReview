011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            While large language models have achieved remarkable success in tasks such as
014            reasoning and question answering, applying LLMs to interactive tasks like web
015            automation remains challenging. In web automation, existing planning-execution
016            workflow often faces limitations due to the infeasible subtasks. We propose VeSX,
017            a framework designed to enhance subtask feasibility through verification, self-
018            correction, and in-context learning. VeSX introduces three key improvements: (1)
019            subgoal-guided verification, which verifies the execution results of subtasks based
020
               on the preset subgoals; (2) hierarchical self-correction, which combines reflection
               and replanning, targeting to self-correct mistakes in both planning and execution
021
               phases; (3) exemplar bank, which improves in-context learning by partitioning
022
               execution trajectories and heuristically generating metadata for exemplars. We
023            evaluate VeSX on WebArena benchmark and achieve the state-of-the-art average
024            success rate of 0.34, which significantly outperforms existing methods without
025            human guidance on all five scenarios.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved notable success in tasks such as logical reasoning,
032   commonsense understanding, and question answering (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023;
033   Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023). Building agents with LLM as backbones to
034   handle tasks in interactive scenarios has been identified as a feasible and promising approach but
035   applying these agents in the real world remains highly challenging (Wang et al., 2023b; Kim et al.,
036   2024; Wang et al., 2024). Web automation is one such scenario where LLM agents autonomously
037   control web browsers to perform online activities such as shopping and posting by executing actions
038   like clicking or typing.
039   When handling web automation tasks, two predominant agent workflow paradigms are empirically
040   employed by current LLM agents. The first involves sequentially generating a series of actions (Koh
041   et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023), as illustrated in Figure 1(a). The second com-
042   mences with a planning phase, breaking down the task into a series of subtasks which are to be
043   tackled one by one(Kim et al., 2024; Sodhi et al., 2024). For complex tasks that require multi-step
044   reasoning, the second workflow is beneficial for that it breaks actions down into easier subtasks like
045
      chain-of-thought instead of a hard final task. However, such workflows are often constrained by the
      lack of guarantees regarding subtask feasibility (Wang et al., 2023b). An effective and commonly-
046
      used remedy is to rely on expertly designed subtask candidates with manually crafted prompts to
047
      decompose the entire task as illustrated in Figure 1(b). However, this method requires human anno-
048   tation for new tasks, which limits the ability of LLM agents to autonomously adapt to more advanced
049   and diverse web environments Sodhi et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2022). Additionally, there is a sig-
050   nificant shortage of data available for learning within these workflows, which collectively restricts
051   the capabilities of LLMs during the planning and execution phases Lai et al. (2024). To overcome
052   these limitations, we propose VeSX, a framework for LLM agents in web automation that seeks to
053   improve subtask feasibility and empower LLM both in planning and execution through Verification,
      Self-correction, and in-conteXt learning.

      VeSX introduces two significant modules to the planning-execution workflow for web automation
      tasks, as illustrated in Figure 1(c). The first involves adopting subgoal-guided verification to as-
082
      certain the successful completion of subtasks. VeSX generates subgoals in the planning phase and
083
      conducts verification to check whether the execution result satisfies the subgoal once execution is
084   finished. We devised two modes for verification: self-verification which makes LLMs do verification
085   by themselves, and external verification which calls for external functions to conduct verification.
086   Verification ensures the completion of the subtasks and avoids the issue of subsequent subtasks
087   becoming infeasible due to errors in the execution of previous subtasks.
088
      The second improvement is hierarchical self-correction, which aims to self-correct both execution
089
      and planning. When verification fails, VeSX first tries to identify and correct errors in the current
090   subtask’s execution trajectory, a process referred to as “reflection”. If reflection is unsuccessful, the
091   subtask is likely infeasible, prompting VeSX to regenerate the remaining incomplete subtasks in a
092   process called “replanning”. This hierarchical self-correction mechanism facilitates error correction
093   during both the execution and planning phases, enabling the framework to effectively assess the
094   feasibility of subtasks and generate new plans as replacements.
095
      Furthermore, VeSX develops an exemplar bank for in-context learning within the planning and ex-
096   ecution phase. Existing research on in-context learning related to web automation primarily focuses
097   on the execution-only workflow and is limited to simple simulated tasks, leading to a dependence
098   on complete execution trajectories as exemplars (Kim et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023). However,
099   complex web automation tasks in the real world involve long action sequences, and using entire tra-
100   jectories as exemplars includes a lot of irrelevant information, which will consume excessive content
101   space. To address these issues, VeSX samples actions and planning strategy from the entire trajec-
102   tory as exemplars for execution and planning, and heuristically generates metadata for retrieval. This
103   method creates a larger and more diverse dataset for the LLM to learn from, and therefore improve
104
      its performance in both the planning and execution phases.
105   We evaluate VeSX on 5 scenarios under the web automation benchmark, WebArena(Zhou et al.,
106   2023b). VeSX outperforms all publicly reported methods without human guidance by a huge margin
107   and pushes the state-of-the-art success rate to 34.0, which even exceeds the performance of the
      method using human guidance.

      2     V E SX
135
136   2.1     OVERVIEW
137
      The VeSX involves innovative design in both process and data aspects. In terms of process, VeSX
138
      implements a highly accurate verification scheme based on subgoals generated in the planning phase,
139
      which we refer to as subgoal-guided verification (Section 2.2). Additionally, when verification fails,
140   we design a hierarchical self-correction scheme that involves local reflection to tackle the problems
141   within one certain subtask and global replanning to address the errors happening in the planning
142   (Section 2.3). In terms of data, to address the low efficiency of in-context-learning in the previous
143   web automation methods, VeSX uses a single step instead of the entire trajectory for in-context-
144   learning, and improves the metadata construction method to efficiently and accurately retrieve ef-
145   fective exemplars (Section 2.4).
146
147   2.2     S UBGOAL -G UIDED V ERIFICATION
148
149   Generate subgoals alongside with subtasks In order to verify whether execution results align
150   with the intentions, VeSX instructs the LLM to generate not only subtasks but also corresponding
151   subgoal pairs during the planning phase, as illustrated in Figure 2. The requirements for tasks in
152   real-world scenarios are often highly diverse, leading to a wide array of corresponding solutions.
153
      However, the methods for assessing whether these requirements can be met are typically simplistic
      and fixed. For example, in web automation, although there may be hundreds of distinct task in-
154
      tents to handle, the evaluation methods are relatively limited. For navigation tasks, the evaluation
155
      may be based on whether a specific webpage has been reached. For information-gathering tasks,
156   the presence of the required information on the final webpage is often the criterion. We observe
157   that evaluating the execution result of a subtask is much simpler and more standardized compared
158   to addressing the subtask itself. From this perspective, we propose the subgoal-guided verification,
159   which evaluates the execution results of the subtasks according to the subgoals. Specifically, in
160   VeSX, we generate pairs of subtasks and subgoals during the planning phase. Subtasks are articu-
161   lated in natural language to capture the diverse task requirements, which in turn guide the generation
      of actions during the execution phase. Meanwhile, subgoals, which are formulated in either natural

      Verification Method We employed two methods to implement verification as illustrated in Fig-
      ure 3. The first method is self-verification, which involves using natural language for subgoals. Self-
197
      verification incorporates all external environmental information and execution processes, making the
198
      LLM itself verify whether the execution results align with the previous intent. Self-verification has
199   the advantage of flexibility, which means it can give the LLM to make decisions according to the
200   current environment rather than totally depending on the predefined scheme.
201
202
      The second method is external verification, which involves presenting subgoals in a structured lan-
      guage akin to function calls. In this approach, subgoals parameterize a set of predefined verification
203
      functions, creating a logical expression that defines the pass/fail criteria. During the verification pro-
204
      cess, external functions will be invoked according to the specifications of the subgoals. The results
205   of these invocations will undergo logical operations to produce the final verification outcome, as
206   illustrated in Figure 3(b). The advantage of this method is that it relies on external functions for ver-
207   ification, which is more stable, controllable, allows for swift adjustments, and reduces dependence
208   on the LLM’s capabilities.

249

      when reflection fails to correct errors. Specifically, when execution can not pass verification and
255
      reflection cannot rectify the issue, we consider the current subtask to potentially be infeasible. The
256
      previous error information and environment feedback are then fed into the LLM, prompting the LLM
257   to regenerate subsequent subtasks and subgoals. Compared to the common framework that relies
258   solely on reflection, the hybrid self-correction approach allows for adaptive adjustment, avoiding
259   infeasible subtasks and thereby enhancing the long-term planning capabilities within the framework.

      2.4     E XEMPLAR BANK
262
263
      Previous research has typically relied on recalling the entire correct execution trajectories to perform
264   in-context learning for execution. However, real-world tasks often require multi-step reasoning and
265   operations, with the underlying logic of these steps varying significantly. This makes it challenging
266   to use the full trajectory as an exemplar to guide the prediction of single-step actions, as it incorpo-
267   rates a significant amount of extraneous information and consumes substantial context. We decom-
268   pose trajectories into individual actions as exemplars and have meticulously designed a heuristic
269   approach to generate their metadata, which will serve as the key for recalling these exemplars later
      there is a lack of research on collecting and recalling planning exemplars.


                                                                               5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Metadata of execution exemplars VeSX utilizes single-step action instead of the entire trajec-
271   tory as an exemplar to avoid the redundancy of the whole trajectory and enables LLM to learn from
272   more diverse action examples. This involves designing metadata to describe the exemplars effec-
273   tively. Specifically, for each action, VeSX will abstract the tackling subtask and concatenate with
274   the action context, which means the sequence of prior actions within that subtask to get the meta-
275   data for the exemplar, as shown in Figure 4. For instance, in the subtask “Find the latest order” the
276   correct execution trajectory constitutes three actions click [sort by], click [time], and
277   click [order 123]. We treat each action as an individual exemplar and generate its metadata.
278   The metadata for the third action would be “Abstract Subtask: find conditional order; Action Con-
279
      text: click sort by, click time”. For each piece of exemplar, we store its embedding of metadata as the
      key and the exemplar as the value to construct the execution exemplar bank. Subsequently, during
280
      execution, VeSX recalls the most relevant examples from the exemplar bank through similarity-
281
      matching embeddings for in-context learning.
282
283
      Collecting planning exemplars Due to the planning phase playing a significant role in the
284
      planning-execution framework, VeSX also collects the planning exemplars from the correct tra-
285
      jectory except for the execution exemplars. The metadata of planning exemplars is generated from
286   its corresponding task query, abstracting the specific concepts in it, and then the planning exemplar
287   bank is constructed with the key-value pairs of embedding of metadata and exemplars. Construct
288   planning exemplar bank enhance models’ reasoning ability in the planning phase and facilitate the
289   generation of more feasible subtasks.

      3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
295
      We evaluate VeSX on 5 different scenarios within the benchmark WebArena. WebArena is a bench-
296   mark featured with complex web tasks across multiple scenarios including Shop (Amazon Shop-
297   ping site), CMS (Amazon Commercial Management System), Red (Reddit), Git (Gitlab), and Map
298   (Open Street Map). It builds several virtual websites from the snapshots of real-world websites like
299   Amazon Shopping, GitLab, and Reddit, and allows agents to take actions freely like humans do in
300   browsers, making it well-suited for evaluating what LLM web agents will do in the real world.
301
      To ensure fair comparisons, we choose gpt-4o the same as previous works without finetuning
302   the LLMs. We use text-embedding-3-small as the embedding model and retrieve the top 3
303   relevant exemplars for in-context learning. We follow the browser system and action space built by
304   Zhou et al. (2023b). We also set the temperature of the LLM to zero to minimize the uncertainty
305   to generate reproducible results. For the data collection for the exemplar, we sampled 60 tasks
306   randomly for each scenario and let the LLM try to solve them but not evaluate them with the correct
307   answer. We heuristically filter out the bad trajectories like repeating actions or reaching the max
308   actions, then make LLM self-evaluate the result by itself. If LLM considers the whole execution
309   trajectory to be reasonable and the final result may be correct, the actions in this trajectory will be
310
      collected as exemplars. We use external verification in default unless a specific verification method
      is explicitly specified.

380   We conducted a comparative experiment to assess the implementation and methodologies of ver-
381   ification. verification will not be performed for the implementation of no verification because it
382   does not have the ability to detect the error. As shown in Table 2, with the implementation of both
383   verification schemes, the success rate has significantly improved. It is because the two verification
384   methods can both enable the framework the error-detection ability. Moreover, using external verifi-
385   cation provides additional support compared to self-verification, resulting in even more pronounced
386
      enhancements.
387
388   3.3.2   A BLATING H IERARCHICAL S ELF -C ORRECTION
389
      We conducted ablation experiments to assess the impact of various components of self-correction on
390
      overall performance. We tested three implementations: using only reflection, using only replanning,
391   and employing reflection without generating expectations during execution.
392
393   As illustrated in Table 3, it is evident that removing any component significantly affects the final
394
      performance. In comparison to replanning, the removal of reflection has a more substantial impact,
      indicating that errors generated during the execution phase are more frequent or potentially easier
395
      to rectify. Nonetheless, the absence of replanning also leads to a rapid decline in the success rate,
396
      underscoring its necessity. Moreover, the success rate decreases when expectations are not utilized,
397   demonstrating that expectations indeed enhance VeSu’s ability to detect discrepancies between en-
398   vironmental changes and action predictions.
399
400
      3.3.3   A BLATING E XEMPLAR BANK
401
402   We performed ablation experiments on the components of the exemplar bank and evaluated vari-
403   ous strategies for generating metadata to determine each element’s contribution to overall system
404   performance. The construction of the exemplar bank follows the same format as the main results.
405   We sampled 60 tasks from the shopping experiment and employed a heuristic approach, allowing
406   the LLM to evaluate its own execution results. Ultimately, we extracted 35 trajectories that were
407   deemed correctly completed, thereby forming the exemplar bank.
408   As demonstrated in Table 5, excluding either the planning or execution exemplars led to a decrease
409   in the success rate. Notably, utilizing only planning exemplars, without the inclusion of execution
410   exemplars, achieved a higher success rate despite relying on fewer data samples. Based on the obser-
411   vations found in Section 3.3.2, this phenomenon suggests that for each subtask, generating accurate
412   execution sequences requires dynamic adjustments. Even with a limited number of samples, the
413   LLM can accomplish tasks through self-correction, indicating a higher dependency on an effective
414
      error-correction mechanism rather than on the volume of data. Conversely, errors in the planning
      phase seem to pose a more complex challenge, requiring data as a reference for effective reasoning.
415
      This also implies that, compared to execution exemplars, planning exemplars are more efficient in
416
      improving performance.
417
418   We conducted an ablation study on two techniques: abstracting subtasks and using action context.
419   Our results indicate that both techniques are essential for enhancing the effectiveness of in-context
420
      learning. Moreover, omitting abstraction results in a more significant loss in accuracy compared to
      omitting action context. This may be attributed to the system’s challenges in retrieving the most
421
      relevant examples from the exemplar bank and learning from them without abstraction.

424   4   R ELATED W ORK
425
426   Web automation tasks. Web automation is a sequential decision process, where agents are given
427   a task that can be carried out on a series of websites, such as buying products, booking a ticket,
428   and summarizing opinions on forums. The earliest research in this area often formulated the task
429   as a Markov Decision Process and employed reinforcement learning methods Liu et al. (2018); Gur
430   et al. (2018); Jia et al. (2018); Humphreys et al. (2022). However, with the increase in the scale of
431   websites and tasks, these methods suffer for they are hard to comprehend and process the long web
      documents and in lack of generalization to different tasks (Gur et al., 2023).


                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Large language model for web automation. Language models have been used as text decoders
433   since the era reinforcement learning is the prevalent solution to web automation tasks (Humphreys
434   et al., 2022). As the ability to reasoning and acting of language models soared, using language
435   models to make decisions in a web environment shows better performance, particularly on the real-
436   world scale in recent years. Most of these methods use in-context learning paradigm (Kim et al.,
437   2024; Zheng et al., 2023) or deal with other difficulties like long document (Zheng et al., 2023;
438   Deng et al., 2024). However, applying this paradigm to multi-step reasoning tasks often leads to
439   suboptimal performance. Other approaches decompose these tasks into predefined subtasks (Sodhi
440   et al., 2024) or sequences of actions (Kim et al., 2024), which require manual design and may
441
      introduce inaccuracies. There also be works using finetuning to improve language models’ ability
      of reasoning or understanding of web pages (Gur et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2024), which is orthogonal
442
      to our method.
443
444   Large language model agent workflow. LLM agents are artificial entities that can sense their envi-
445   ronment, make decisions, and take actions. They leverage the capabilities of large language models
446   (LLMs) to perform tasks autonomously (Xi et al., 2023). The general conceptual framework for
447
      LLM-based agents includes three main components: brain (primarily composed of an LLM), per-
      ception (multimodal inputs), and action (expanding the action space of LLM-based agents). There
448
      are many development in the research of how to construct LLM agent to interact with wild outer
449
      world, such as (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a;b) or construct LLM agent to follow human’s
450   instructions to complete a given task with external tools, such as (Lightman et al., 2023; Hong et al.,
451   2023; Lai et al., 2024; Significant Gravitas; Agashe et al., 2024). Web automation is a task that
452   necessitates LLM the ability to interact with the web environment with the target given by human.
453
      Planning with large language model. How to augment language models with reasoning skills and
454
      ability to act is of great significance when using language models in real world. Recent researches
455
      find that providing a series of intermediate reasoning steps can help LLM perform complex rea-
456   soning (Wei et al., 2022). Previous works augment the reasoning steps with the observations of the
457   external tools (Yao et al., 2023; 2024). These prompt strategies try to decomposing the task into a se-
458   quence of actions. However, when the tasks become complex and need more steps to solve, it will be
459   hard for LLM to generate correct action each step and the reasoning steps are hard to learn through
460   in-context learning. Some other methods try to decompose the tasks into predefined subtasks, which
461   can be guided to finish through in-context learning (Khot et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Sodhi
462   et al., 2024). These methods need to manually define and write prompts for subtasks, and when the
463   tasks become more and more various, it will be time-consuming and costly. Researches in other
464
      areas demonstrated that decomposing the task into the subtasks and using the verification module
      to ensure the effectiveness of the subtasks perform well in complex reasoning tasks (Wang et al.,
465
      2023b; Gou et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023a). Due to the strong interactivity and complexity of web
466
      automation tasks, simply transplanting these solutions is not applicable.
467

469
470   5   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
471
472
      VeSX is an innovative LLM workflow designed for handling web automation tasks, characterized by
473
      subgoal-guided verification, hierarchical self-correction, and an exemplar bank. During our research
474   process, we identified the following limitations of VeSX. First, its performance depends on the
475   accuracy of verification and the success rate of reflection and replanning corrections. Although
476   VeSX has achieved significant improvements by relying on these modules, there is still considerable
477   room for enhancement to fully solve web automation tasks. Second, data in the web automation
478   field is relatively scarce. While VeSX has implemented sophisticated designs for the exemplar bank
479   to increase data utilization, generating high-quality data automatically without human annotation
480   remains a significant challenge.
481   Additionally, the workflow concepts in VeSX may lead to opportunities in other fields. The novel
482   approaches to verification and self-correction could be beneficial for tasks that require long rea-
483   soning steps and facilitate the verification of intermediate results. For instance, this could apply to
484   complex mathematical logic reasoning or coding tasks, as well as long-term tasks in simulation en-
485   vironments like Minecraft. VeSX also represents a technological solution for building LLM agents
      with error-correction capabilities. Our future work will first focus on making new improvements

486
      and contributions in data generation for web automation tasks, and then explore how the VeSX
487   framework can be implemented and utilized in other tasks.

490   6   C ONCLUSION
491
492   In this paper, we introduce VeSX, a framework of LLM agent for web automation tasks, featured
493   with subgoal-guided verification, hierarchical self-correction, and exemplar bank to deal with the
494
      problem about infeasible subtasks and lack of data. Our experiments on the five different scenarios
      show VeSX have achieved state-of-the-art success rate and significantly outperform existing meth-
495
      ods without human guidance. Our work shows the great potential of the workflow paradigm that
496
      armed with the planning-execution workflow with verification and self-correction. Moreover, VeSX
497   proposes a new method to generate exemplars’ descriptive metadata and constructs a memory sys-
498   tem named exemplar bank for in-context learning in planning and execution phase. The ablation
499   studies on the three contributions validate their effectiveness.
