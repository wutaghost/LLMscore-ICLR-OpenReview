009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks (NNs) have been widely studied in complex fields due to their
012            remarkable capacity for nonlinear modeling. However, in the realm of time se-
013            ries analysis, researches indicate that merely stacking NNs does not yield promis-
014            ing nonlinear modeling outputs and hinders model performance. Conventional
015            NN architectures overemphasize homogeneous feature extraction, impeding the
016
               learning of diverse features and diminishing their nonlinear modeling capability.
               To address this gap, we propose the Cross-correlation Enhanced Approximated
017
               Orthogonal Projection Unit (COPU) to quantify and augment the NN’s nonlinear
018
               modeling capacity. COPU efficiently computes the local cross-correlation charac-
019            teristics between features, amplifying heterogeneous components while compress-
020            ing homogeneous ones. By reducing redundant information, COPU facilitates the
021            learning of unique and independent features, thereby enhancing nonlinear mod-
022            eling capability. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves
023            superior performance across two real-world regression applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      A plethora of successful research endeavors featuring modular designs based on stacked structures
      has emerged in complex fields such as Computer Vision (CV) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and Natural
029
      Language Processing (NLP) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020). The effectiveness of these
030
      designs is attributed to their stacked architecture (Ashish et al., 2017). However, cutting-edge deep
031   learning research on time series analysis utilizes fewer stacked layers than that of CV and NLP, typ-
032   ically only 1 to 4 layers (Chong et al., 2023; Haixu et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2022). This phenomenon
033   arises because base modules designed for CV and NLP reach their expressiveness ceiling quickly
034   when applied in time series. For these methods to be effectively applied in this field, it is essen-
035   tial to recognize that time series has more ambiguous discriminative patterns than other forms like
036   images and text (Alec et al., 2021). Such ambiguity hinders the model’s ability to extract diverse
037   features from the input, obstructing its capacity for nonlinear modeling. Specifically, it is relatively
038   straightforward to distinguish images belonging to different categories or texts conveying various
039
      emotions. However, it is more challenging to discern the effect of two input sequences on the output
      of a system, particularly through a nonlinear dynamic system (Elad et al., 2018). Thus, from the
040
      perspective of input, the discriminative patterns among different time series are not only difficult
041
      to express mathematically but also inherently ambiguous. Figure 1 vividly illustrates this process
042   using a simple kernel convolution.
043
044   Rank Ratio (RR) is introduced as a metric to gauge the confidence degree of a matrix’s inverse in
045
      Shaoqi et al. (2024). We would also utilize it to measure the nonlinear modeling capability of NNs.
      In matrix analysis, the rank signifies the number of linearly independent vectors, a characteristic
046
      that embodies unique information not representable through linear combinations of others (Meyer,
047
      2023). RR can be interpreted as the proportion of linear dependencies that have been transformed
048   into independencies through NN. An RR value approaching 1 indicates that the extracted features
049   contain a great amount of unique information. Such features empower NN to capture diverse and
050   informative patterns, thereby enhancing accuracy. Conversely, an RR value approaching 0 suggests
051   that the extracted features have much redundant information. Such features compel NN to focus
052   on more homogeneous characteristics. When a shift occurs, e.g., from source to target (Ido et al.,
053   2024), from training to testing (Olivia et al., 2022), or from offline to online (Yichen et al., 2024), the
      distribution of extracted features may undergo significant changes, increasing the risk of overfitting.

      Existing NN-based methods primarily focus on proposing innovative model structures while ne-
      glecting the analysis of RR (Tian et al., 2022; Yuqi et al., 2023). Specifically, most NN research
072
      centers on constructing local rules at the neuron level to mimic specific biomimetic or mathemat-
073
      ical patterns, assuming that stacking these local rules will extract conducive global features, and
074   validating this assumption experimentally (Kaiming et al., 2016; Ze et al., 2021). For instance,
075   Albert et al. (2020); Gu et al. (2022); Albert et al. (2022) integrate the understanding of Fourier
076   Recurrent Units and Legendre Memory Units to propose a state-space method for dynamic lin-
077   ear encoding with minimal information loss for long sequence data, empirically employing gating
078   mechanisms to enhance nonlinear modeling capabilities (Alberta & Tria, 2024). Similarly, Nikita
079   et al. (2020) and Haoyi et al. (2021) have modified attention mechanisms based on complex block
080   partitioning and entropy sampling principles, introducing the Reformer and Informer for time series
081   forecasting through multi-layer stacking. Haixu et al. (2021) have also proposed Autoformer, which
082
      leverages autocorrelation by rewriting attention mechanisms. Additionally, Zhiding et al. (2024)
      introduce a hierarchical Transformer-based transfer learning structure to capture temporal depen-
083
      dencies within sequences through stacked NNs. Huang et al. (2024)’s generative structure models
084
      scale-invariant temporal features by simulating evolutionary behaviors. Furthermore, Maximilian
085   et al. (2024) have advanced LSTM architectures by integrating attention mechanisms, while Yux-
086   uan et al. (2024) enhance the generalization ability of large language models across various time
087   series analysis tasks by sharing encoders within each patch. These novel methods use stacked NNs
088   to improve their expressive capability. The mainstream of conventional stacked network research
089   involves empirically explore the feasibility of extracting features using base modules that exhibit
090   outstanding performance under mean squared error or entropy loss in end-to-end tasks. However, it
091   remains challenging to quantify and analyze whether these modules are qualified as feature extrac-
092   tors, and whether the features they extract are conducive to and consistent with the overall model.
093
      Furthermore, they struggle to monitor whether such extracted features are beneficial in optimization,
      much less guide directions for model improvement. An index that enables real-time monitoring of
094
      NN nonlinear modeling capability can significantly enhance interpretability and guide directions for
095
      model improvement (Shaoqi et al., 2024). RR fulfills this role and offers a general NN design prin-
096   ciple centered on increasing RR. RR closely parallels the mesa-optimization problems proposed by
097   Evan et al. (2021) and can be regarded as an analysis of prior alignment in deep NN optimization
098   (Collin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
099

100
      for time series data. CEP leverages the structured characteristics of sequential data, simultaneously
101

102   the differentiation between redundant information and innovation, further suppressing homogeneous
103   information among features while amplifying their differences. As a result, linear dependencies are
104   transformed into independencies, facilitating the effective construction of nonlinear patterns. AOPU
105   is the representation method that utilizes RR for the analysis of the approximation of natural gradient
106   in online NN regression (Shaoqi et al., 2024). AOPU’s gradient propagation of the dual parameters
107   places high demands on RR. We introduce COPU by replacing the random gaussian matrix with CEP
      as the augmentation interface for AOPU, ensuring the accuracy of natural gradient calculations. This

144   In time series analysis, it is common to encounter multiple variables that embody a specific pattern at
145   different time lags (Haixu et al., 2021). Figure 2 vividly illustrates this phenomenon. Each subplot’s
146   input comprises four variables: x1 , x2 , and x3 all represent manifestations of the same pattern, while
147   x4 is independent. Such repetition of variables and their lag characteristics are prevalent in time
148   series analysis, in particular, the industrial process (Qingqiang & Zhiqiang, 2021; Yan et al., 2024).
149   Crucial differences between x1 , x2 , and x3 emerge in the heterogeneous fluctuations observed at
150   the beginning of x3 and the end of x2 ; these innovations could harbor key patterns vital for system
151   identification.
152   Common studies employ architectures such as MLP, RNN, and ATTEN to extract features from
153   those data for subsequent modeling, owing to these structures’ excellent input-output mapping ca-
154   pabilities. However, as feature extraction modules, the capacity to model nonlinearity and
155   extract diverse features should be prioritized. In this section, we leverage RR to compare the
156   nonlinearity of various modules’ output. Simultaneously, we document the performance of these
157   modules when trained end-to-end, providing a wealth of valuable insights.
158   To address this challenge, we propose CEP as a nonlinear modeling solution designed to amplify
159   heterogeneous differences among closely related features. CEP first tackles the issue of sequence
160   alignment among features. Without accounting for time lags, the correlation coefficients between
161   features expressing the same nonlinear pattern may be zero, as exemplified by sine and cosine func-
      tions. Subsequently, CEP quantifies the similarity characteristics of the aligned features. CEP

      In this section, we conduct a comprehensive series of experiments to qualitatively and quantita-
309   tively assess RR and COPU. We begin by elucidating the static differences among various methods
310   in enhancing RR during the initialization phase. Subsequently, by tracking the evolution of RR
311   across different models throughout the training process, we analyze their dynamic distinctions. By
312   integrating these observations, we delve into the relationship between RR and model efficacy. Our
313   experimental findings reveal a positive correlation between RR and model performance. These re-
314   sults are substantiated across two real-world datasets: SRU, and Debutanizer.

      in RR. However, the RR remains quite low.

      4.2   A NALYSIS OF RR ON T RAINING
353
      A larger RR indicates that the algorithm can focus on different unique information from the input,
354   thereby aiding the model in learning more diverse and informative representations. Conversely, a
355   lower RR forces the algorithm to concentrate on multiple copies of the same feature, increasing the
356   risk of overfitting and resulting in poorer performance.
357
      We closely monitor the dynamic shifts in RR distributions and performance metrics throughout the
358
      training processes of various foundational NN frameworks. By delving into these evolving patterns,
359
      we aim to unearth the subtle correlations and dependencies that underpin their performance. Figure
360   7 illustrates the dynamic evolution of RR distribution at output of the 4th layer over iterations. The
361   batch size and sequence length are respectively set to 256 and 16, and different NN frameworks
362   are stacked with varying numbers of layers. It can be observed that as the NN continues to learn,
363   the RR of CEP, RNN, and RES initially exhibit an upward trend, reaching a peak within 10 to 20
364   iterations, after which they decline and stabilize. In contrast, the RR of MLP and ATTEN remain
365   relatively unchanged during training, maintaining low values. Upon stabilization, CEP generally
366   attains the highest RR, followed by RNN and RES, which are comparable and rank second, then
367   MLP, and finally ATTEN. Figure 8 depicts the dynamic changes in the model’s loss on the validation

      den dimension at 32. Minor fluctuations in these parameters are observed to exert minimal impact
      on model performance. The learning rate is set to 2E-4 for all NN models. The dual parameters’
412
      learning rate is set to 1E-1. Our experiments meticulously document performance across varying
413
      numbers of stacked layers. All NN configurations undergo 20 independent repetitions; the mean
414   results are denoted by uppercase numerals on the left side of the table, while the standard deviations

      We wish to emphasize that COPU’s performance can be effectively enhanced by stacking more
419
      layers, whereas the comparative methods are ill-suited for multi-layer stacking. Table 1 shows
420   that CEP is more suitable than conventional NN architectures for stacking to extract conducive
421   abstract features in time series context. When the number of stacked layers increases from 2 to 5,
422   COPU demonstrates significant performance enhancements, with improvements of 17.4% (6.94E-

524   5      C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper proposes a new NN component termed CEP that has strong nonlinear representation
527
      performance in time series analysis and serves as the foundation for developing the COPU frame-
      work. An inherent distinction between time series data and image or text data lies in the ambiguity
528
      of its discriminative patterns: two wide different sequences may exert similar influences on system
529
      outputs, while two nearly identical sequences can produce different outcomes. Consequently, con-
530   structing effective latent features from such inputs is exceedingly intricate. Simply applying methods
531   from CV or NLP to time series analysis fails to yield satisfactory results, as empirical studies have
532   demonstrated both qualitatively and quantitatively. By focusing on and leveraging sequential char-
533   acteristics, CEP effectively extracts innovations from similar features, thereby exhibiting enhanced
534   nonlinear modeling prowess. Integrating CEP with AOPU, we propose COPU; CEP augments RR
535   capabilities while resolving the computational precision and expressive power issues inherent in
536   AOPU. Experimental results reveal that COPU significantly outperforms comparative methods, and
537   ablation studies further underscore the critical importance and indispensability of CEP. Although
538
      CEP is not compatible with other modality data due to its specialization, such focused research can
      more effectively propel disciplinary development and advance the field, while also illuminating the
539
      potential of research on domain-wise characterization rather than general solutions.

       B    A DDITIONAL R ESULT
1010
1011
       In the main text, we primarily analyze the characteristics of CEP using SRU as a case study. This
       section supplements our findings with experimental results on Debutanizer to enhance the paper’s
1012
       completeness and credibility. We aim to emphasize two points: first, compared to SRU, Debutanizer
1013
       is more informative and presents a greater challenge for model fitting; second, CEP is not introduced
1014   as an end-to-end NN. These points explain why CEP’s performance on Debutanizer does not fully
1015   align with the previous analyses. Most importantly, CEP successfully compensates for the AOPU’s
1016   heavy reliance on RR, enabling COPU to be applicable across a wider range of batch sizes and
1017   sequence lengths.
1018
       Figure 15 illustrates how the RR distribution of Debutanizer varies with changes in batch size and
1019
       sequence length. We observe that Debutanizer’s RR is significantly higher than that of SRU un-
1020   der equivalent settings. This indicates that Debutanizer inherently contains more heterogeneous
1021   information and is informative even without CEP. Figure 16 further validates this point; when the
1022   sequence length is 8, the RR at each NN layer is notably higher than the results shown in Fig-
1023   ure 6. The small step size for sequence length in Figure 16 is intentionally chosen to prevent the
1024   Debutanizer’s RR from rapidly converging to 1. That would obscure presenting CEP’s advantage
1025   in enhancing RR. Debutanizer is considerably more difficult to fit than the SRU, as reflected not
       only in the MAPE in Tables 1 and 2 but also demonstrated in numerous studies (Luigi et al., 2007).

1026
       CEP functions as an RR enhancement module rather than an end-to-end regression network. Figure
1027   17 shows the dynamic progression of validation loss for each NN, revealing that CEP’s output can
1028   be suboptimal, even bad performing. This can be attributed to CEP’s excessive focus on amplify-
1029   ing heterogeneous information, thereby neglecting the modeling of input-output relationships. We
1030   would like to point out that CEP is introduced as a feature extraction module instead of an
1031   end-to-end input-output mapping module. The nonlinear modeling capability is prioritized in
1032   this context. Figures 18–23 demonstrate that CEP still effectively enhances RR, allowing COPU to
1033   maintain minimal performance loss with larger batch sizes (i.e., smaller RR) as shown in Table 2.

1150   The issue of RR highlights the significant difference between time-series data and CV/NLP modal-
1151   ities, that the time-series data are less informative. The pronounced decrease in RR as batch size
1152   increases indicates that the unique information contained within samples diminishes, making them
1153   more easily represented linearly by other samples. This phenomenon has actually been implicitly
1154   recognized in the field of industrial system identification. For example, when applying Ordinary
1155
       Least Squares (OLS), there is a stringent requirement for the number of samples to vastly exceed
       the dimensionality of features, precisely because it is challenging to ensure the invertibility of xxT .
1156
       Similarly, Partial Least Squares (PLS) is proposed as an identification tool to refine heterogeneous
1157
       information within samples while suppressing homogeneous ones.
1158
1159   It is this characteristic of time-series data that renders the trivial application of models designed for
1160   CV/NLP modalities inappropriate. Modules such as Attention aims to focus on features of interest
1161
       from vast and diverse information. However, in the context of time series, they might extract multiple
       copies of the same pattern from large amounts of homogeneous information. Such modules lack
1162
       the ability to regularize the diversity of the extracted features. Figure 26 and 27 and Table 1 to 4
1163
       illustrate this point both qualitatively and quantitatively. We believe that a deeper understanding
1164   of RR can help transcend the limitations of existing models and ultimately lead to the design of
1165   network architectures specifically tailored for time-series analysis.
1166
1167
       Remark: The issue of RR further reveals a potential question. In the domains of CV/NLP, increasing
       the batch size can reduce gradient variance, thereby aiding the model in optimizing toward a global
1168
       optimum. However, in the field of time series, expanding the batch size may not change the number
1169
       of effective samples, which may provoke a series of questions and considerations.
1170
1171   The impact of label consistency between redundant and effective samples presents an intriguing
1172   question that remains unclear and merits further investigation. Redundant samples refer to the sam-
1173
       ples constituting the difference between the batch size and the number of effective samples. Es-
       sentially, these are linear combinations of effective samples, prompting us to ponder whether their
1174
       labels satisfy such linear relationships. If their labels do satisfy these linear relationships, which
1175
       essentially implies embodying a linear model prior, will this cause networks to tend toward degen-
1176   erating into linear models? Conversely, if their labels do not satisfy such linear relationships, will
1177   this encourage networks to learn the nonlinear dynamic characteristics inherent in the data, or will
1178   it lead to conflicts resulting in unstable updates? We believe that these reflections based on the RR
1179   issue can deepen our understanding of applying NNs in the realm of time-series analysis.
1180
       The ability of CEP to enhance RR suggests its potentially superior nonlinear modeling capabilities.
1181
       RR itself represents the proportion of linearly independent samples within a mini-batch; its vari-
1182   ation with batch size reflects the modality’s informativeness, while its variation with the number
1183   of stacked layers indicates the network’s nonlinear modeling capacity. The essence of nonlinear-
1184   ity is transforming linearly dependent (but not identical) elements into linearly independent ones,
1185   i.e., acti(a + b) ̸= acti(a) + acti(b). Therefore, an increase in RR implies that the NN transforms
1186   originally linearly dependent components into independent ones, implicitly measuring its nonlinear
1187   modeling ability. By utilizing CEP, COPU can maintain a high RR even when stacking multiple
       network layers, thereby achieving better performance, as illustrated in Figures 26 and 27. Another
