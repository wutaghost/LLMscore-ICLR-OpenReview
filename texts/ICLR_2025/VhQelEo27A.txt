                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               In this paper, we introduce the Context-Aware Video Instance Segmentation
               (CAVIS), a novel framework designed to enhance instance association by inte-
011
               grating contextual information adjacent to each object. To efficiently extract and
012
               leverage this information, we propose the Context-Aware Instance Tracker (CAIT),
013            which merges contextual data surrounding the instances with the core instance
014            features to improve tracking accuracy. Additionally, we introduce the Prototypical
015            Cross-frame Contrastive (PCC) loss, which ensures consistency in object-level
016            features across frames, thereby significantly enhancing instance matching accu-
017            racy. CAVIS demonstrates superior performance over state-of-the-art methods on
018            all benchmark datasets in video instance segmentation (VIS) and video panoptic
019            segmentation (VPS). Notably, our method excels on the OVIS dataset, which is
020            known for its particularly challenging videos. Source code: this anonymous URL

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) is a crucial task that involves segmenting and identifying individ-
026   ual objects within video sequences, applicable in a variety of fields including video understanding,
027
      autonomous driving, and video editing (Yang et al., 2019). VIS has seen considerable advancements,
      with developments in both online methods (Yang et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021b;
028
      Huang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022c; Ying et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), which process videos
029
      frame-by-frame to adapt in real-time, and offline methods (Wang et al., 2021; Hwang et al., 2021;
030   Wu et al., 2022b; Cheng et al., 2021a; Heo et al., 2022), which analyze entire videos to understand
031   inter-frame dependencies thoroughly.
032
033
      Recent advances have brought robust query-based segmentation architectures (Cheng et al., 2021b;
      2022), designed to detect instance centers and cluster pixels into instance-specific groups within
034
      images. These modern VIS approaches increasingly employ instance center associations across
035
      frames to improve tracking accuracy. Techniques such as contrastive learning (Wu et al., 2022c;
036   Ying et al., 2023) and transformer-based trackers (Heo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) leverage the
037   similarities between instance centers for consistent identification of objects across frames. However,
038   challenges persist in scenarios with significant occlusions or when multiple similar objects are present,
039   leading to potential tracking inaccuracies as shown in the top row of Fig. 1. While some strategies
040   (Heo et al., 2022; 2023) attempt to use instance features for tracking across segments or entire videos,
041   difficulties remain.
042   To address this issue, we propose Context-Aware Video Instance Segmentation (CAVIS), a novel
043   framework designed to improve object identification by incorporating contextual information sur-
044   rounding each instance into the tracking process. This approach draws from insights in neuroscience
045   and cognitive science (Bar, 2004; Oliva & Torralba, 2007), emphasizing the importance of contextual
046   cues in human perception for deciphering complex scenes and resolving visual ambiguities. An
047   example of the practical application of this principle is shown in Fig. 1, where recognizing a person
048   is riding a bicycle, rather than just identifying the bicycle, greatly enhances the accuracy of object
049   identification.
050   To achieve this, we design the Context-Aware Instance Tracker (CAIT), featuring advanced modules
051   for extracting and matching context-aware instance features. The context-aware instance feature
052   extractor combines the contextual information at the object’s boundary with the core features of
053   each instance. Then, we incorporate these context-aware instance features into a transformer-based
      tracking architecture (Wu et al., 2022b; Heo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a), enhanced by our

      Furthermore, we introduce the Prototypical Cross-frame Contrastive (PCC) loss to ensure temporal
077   consistency across frames on high-level feature maps. Tracking methods in video tasks have predomi-
078   nantly focused on object feature representation learning (Wu et al., 2022c; Fischer et al., 2023; Ying
079   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023c), with recent studies exploring pixel-level representation learning on low-
080   level feature maps (Kim et al., 2025). Building on this context, our PCC loss emphasizes high-level
081   feature maps, inspired by the observation that object features and high-level feature maps are closely
082   linked, as their similarity drives mask predictions. By constructing instance-wise prototypes from
083   high-level feature maps, this loss maintains frame-to-frame consistency, enhancing training efficiency
084   and ensuring robust performance in dynamic environments.
085   Our extensive testing shows that CAVIS significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods
086   across major video segmentation benchmarks, including YTVIS19 (Yang et al., 2019), YTVIS21
087   (Yang et al., 2021a), OVIS (Qi et al., 2022), and VIPSeg (Miao et al., 2021), particularly excelling on
088   OVIS dataset that include complex video sequences. Our contributions to the field are manifold and
089   can be summarized as follows:
090
091
      1. We present Context-Aware Instance Tracker (CAIT), a novel framework designed to extract
         context-aware instance features and utilize them for enhanced instance matching.
092
093   2. We propose a Prototypical Cross-frame Contrative (PCC) loss that enhances the learning of
094      instance matching by ensuring consistency in object-level features across frames.
095   3. Our model demonstrates robustness in challenging videos environments, establishing state-of-the-
096      art performance in Video Instance Segmentation and Video Panoptic Segmentation.

099
100
      Video Instance Segmentation. VIS methods learn to associate features frame-to-frame based on
101
      instance segmentation architectures. The pioneering MaskTrack R-CNN (Yang et al., 2019) integrates
102   a tracking head into Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), utilizing heuristic cues for instance association
103   Following advancements include SipMask (Cao et al., 2020) and CrossVIS (Yang et al., 2021b), which
104   enhance temporal links through cross-frame learning. IDOL(Wu et al., 2022c) a contrastive learning
105   approach with query-based architectures (Zhu et al., 2020), boosting online method performance.
106   Conversely, offline approaches like VisTR (Wang et al., 2021) and Seqformer (Wu et al., 2022b) use
107   the entire video for mask trajectory predictions, with VisTR applying DETR (Carion et al., 2020) at
      the clip level and Seqformer aggregating temporal information via inter-frame queries. Innovations

109   mechanisms within transformer architectures.

111   Advancements in Query-based Networks. Strong query-based segmentation networks have become
112
      prevalent in current VIS methods, with many relying on Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) as their
      foundation. MinVIS (Huang et al., 2022) achieves tracking through simple post-processing based on

114
      associates frame-level queries to find instance prototypes within a video. GenVIS (Heo et al., 2023)
115   adopts object association approach of VITA and designs a tracking network at the sub-clip level.
116   Inspired by SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), CTVIS (Ying et al., 2023) utilizes contrastive learning
117   with a larger number of frames for comprehensive frame association. DVIS (Zhang et al., 2023a)
118   introduces a decoupled framework for VIS, dividing it into segmentation, tracking, and refinement
119   tasks, thereby enabling efficient and effective learning.
120
      Object Tracking with Additional Cues. Tracking methodologies have been developed across various
121   domains, including video object segmentation (VOS) (Xu et al., 2018; Oh et al., 2019; Cheng et al.,
122   2021c), multiple object tracking (MOT) (Milan et al., 2016; Bergmann et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020;
123   Zhang et al., 2021), and VIS. Despite advancements, many challenging cases persist, prompting
124   research into object association with supplementary data. Early approaches leverage spatial-temporal
125   information such as geometric relation between adjacent frames (Tang et al., 2017) and aggregated
126   object features of previous frames (Xu et al., 2019). BeyondPixel (Sharma et al., 2018) improves
127   inter-frame object matching by proposing a new cost that captures 3D pose and shape based on
128   monocular geometry. BATMAN (Yu et al., 2022) combines optical flow and object query features to
129
      encode motion and appearance information into bilateral space. CAROQ (Choudhuri et al., 2023)
      employs a context feature defined as a memory bank of multi-level image features extracted by a
130
      pixel decoder. However, such a full-context-based approach can lead to increased complexity and
131
      memory limitations. In contrast, our method takes a memory-efficient approach by focusing on the
132   surrounding features of each object during tracking, enabling effective object matching.

141   3.1   Q UERY- BASED INSTANCE SEGMENTATION
142
143   Modern VIS methods adopt a query-based instance segmentation pipeline (Cheng et al., 2021b; 2022),
144   including three main components: a backbone encoder, a pixel decoder, and a transformer decoder.
145   The backbone encoder and pixel decoder are responsible for extracting multi-scale feature maps from
146   the input image. The transformer decoder employs object queries—sequences of latent vectors—as
147
      initial guesses for object centers and utilizes these features to generate object-level features. These
      queries undergo refinement through multiple transformer blocks via a cross-attention mechanism
148
      between the object queries and the feature maps. The refined instance features are then used for
149
      classification and segmentation tasks through respective prediction heads. Typically, the number of
150   object queries, N , exceeds the actual number of objects, NGT , present in the image. Traditionally, the
151   process involves finding a permutation of N elements, σ ∈ SN , that optimally assigns the prediction

      7    C ONCLUSION
509
      In this paper, we introduce Context-Aware Video Instance Segmentation (CAVIS), a pioneering
510
      framework designed to enhance the accuracy and reliability of object tracking in complex video
511
      scenarios by integrating contextual information surrounding each instance. The introduction of the
512   Context-Aware Instance Tracker (CAIT) and the innovative Prototypical Cross-frame Contrastive
513   (PCC) loss are central to CAVIS’s effectiveness. CAIT leverages the surrounding context to enrich the
514   core features of each instance, providing a more holistic view that significantly improves instance de-
515   tection and segmentation under challenging conditions. Simultaneously, PCC loss ensures consistency
516   of these enriched features across frames, reinforcing the temporal linkage between instances and
517   enhancing the overall tracking robustness. Our experiments across multiple challenging benchmarks
518   demonstrate that CAVIS significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly
519   excelling in scenarios that demand robust tracking capabilities. The integration of CAIT and PCC
520
      not only addresses the primary challenges of occlusions and motion but also effectively manages the
      presence of visually similar objects that can often mislead traditional VIS approaches.

759   A.1     L IMITATION
760
761   Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) is an advanced technology designed to perform segmentation and
762   tracking concurrently, capturing the trajectories of individual instances within a video. While this
763   technology has significant benefits, it also poses potential risks if misused, particularly in surveillance
764   applications. Such misuse could lead to severe privacy infringements. It is important to note, however,
765
      that the dataset used in this study is a standard one within the VIS community and does not include
      any sensitive or personal information. This precaution helps mitigate the risk of our trained model
766
      being used for harmful purposes. Nonetheless, the potential for negative impacts should not be
767
      underestimated, and ethical considerations must guide the deployment of VIS technologies.
768
769   Potential error in prediction. Our model is designed to improve tracking accuracy by achieving pre-
770   cise object matching across frames rather than focusing on segmentation performance. Consequently,
771
      if the pretrained segmentation network produces inaccurate segmentation results, performance may
      decrease. However, even in scenarios with imprecise mask predictions, our proposed context-aware
772
      modeling can robustly track objects, as demonstrated in Fig. 4.

792   A.2.1    DATASETS
793
794   Youtube-VIS 2019 and 2021 YouTube-VIS was introduced by Yang et al. in their pioneering study on
795   the VIS task (Yang et al., 2019). This dataset comprises high-resolution YouTube videos, categorized
796   into 40 distinct classes. The 2019 version of the dataset includes 2,238 videos for training, 302
797   for validation, and 343 for testing (Yang et al., 2019). The 2021 update expands these numbers to
798   2,985, 421, and 453 videos for training, validation, and testing, respectively (Yang et al., 2021a).
799   YouTube-VIS is utilized across various pixel-level video understanding tasks, including VIS, video
800
      semantic segmentation, and video object detection.
801   OVIS The OVIS dataset (Qi et al., 2022) presents a significant challenge with its frequent occlusions
802   and a realistic representation of common everyday objects. This makes it highly relevant for real-world
803   applications. OVIS videos are longer and contain more objects compared to those in YouTube-VIS,
804   which increases the complexity of segmentation and tracking tasks. The dataset is organized into
805   training, validation, and test sets, with 607, 140, and 154 videos, respectively.
806   VIPSeg VIPSeg (Miao et al., 2022) is a comprehensive Video Panoptic Segmentation dataset that
807   includes 3,536 videos and 84,750 frames, annotated with pixel-level panoptic labels. Unlike earlier
808   VPS datasets that primarily focus on street views, VIPSeg offers a broader range of challenges and
809   practical scenarios. It features 232 diverse settings and is annotated with 58 ‘thing’ classes and 66
      ‘stuff’ classes, making it one of the most diverse and challenging datasets available in the field.

      A.2.2 I MPLEMENTATION
841
842   Our segmentation approach employs the Mask2Former architecture (Cheng et al., 2022), utilizing
843   the officially recommended hyperparameters. For all experimental settings, we follow established
844   practices by incorporating COCO joint training, as adopted in previous methodologies (Wu et al.,
845   2022b; Heo et al., 2022; 2023; Ying et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). The tracking network consists
846   of six transformer blocks. Within the tracking network’s transformer blocks, we innovate by replacing
847   the standard cross-attention layer with the referring cross-attention layer, as introduced in (Zhang
848   et al., 2023a). Additionally, we conduct experiments with the temporal refiner (Zhang et al., 2023a)
849
      over 160k iterations, specifically analyzing sequences of 15 consecutive frames to enhance tracking
      accuracy.
850
851   For efficient training, we adopt a staged approach where the segmentation network is trained first,
852   followed by the tracking network with all other parameters frozen, promoting stability and efficiency
853   in learning, as suggested by previous studies (Zhang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023a). Optimization is
854   carried out using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), with a starting learning rate
855
      of 1e-4 and a weight decay of 5e-2. The training process spans 40k iterations for the segmentation
      network and 160k iterations for the tracking network, with learning rate reductions scheduled at
856
      28k and 112k iterations, respectively. During training, we sample three frames for the segmentation
857
      network and five frames for the tracking network from each of eight batched videos. These frames
858   undergo resizing to ensure the shorter side is between 320 and 640 pixels, while the longer side does
859   not exceed 768 pixels. The loss function weights are set to λcls = 2.0, λbce = 5.0, λdice = 5.0, λctx =
860   2.0, and λpro = 2.0 to balance the contributions of each component during training. For inference, the
861   shorter side of input frames is scaled down to 448 pixels to maintain a consistent aspect ratio across
862   inputs. All experiments are conducted using 8 RTX2080Ti GPUs for the ResNet-50 backbone and 8
863   RTX3090 GPUs for the Swin-L and ViT-L backbones, ensuring adequate computational resources
      are available for the demands of each model configuration.
