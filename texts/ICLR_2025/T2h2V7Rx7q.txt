009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a novel scaling law for general-purpose decoder-only language models
012            (LMs) trained on multilingual data, tackling the problem of balancing languages
013            during multilingual pretraining. A primary challenge in studying multilingual
014            scaling is the difficulty of analyzing individual language performance due to
015            cross-lingual transfer. To address this, we shift the focus from individual languages
016            to language families. We introduce and validate a hypothesis that the test cross-
017            entropy loss for each language family is determined solely by its own sampling
018
               ratio, independent of other languages in the mixture. This insight simplifies the
               complexity of multilingual scaling and make the analysis scalable to an arbitrary
019
               number of languages. Building on this hypothesis, we derive a power-law relation-
020
               ship that links performance with dataset size, model size and sampling ratios. This
021            relationship enables us to predict performance across various combinations of the
022            above three quantities, and derive the optimal sampling ratios at different model
023            scales. To demonstrate the effectiveness and accuracy of our proposed scaling
024            law, we perform a large-scale empirical study, training more than 100 models
025            on 23 languages spanning 5 language families. Our experiments show that the
026            optimal sampling ratios derived from small models (85M parameters) generalize
027            effectively to models that are several orders of magnitude larger (1.2B parameters),
028            offering a resource-efficient approach for multilingual LM training at scale.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Scaling has proven to be a powerful strategy for improving the performance of language models
      (LMs) across a range of tasks (Thoppilan et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023). Due to
034
      the enormous cost associated with training larger models (Rae et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2024), neural
035
      scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Krajewski et al., 2024)
036   have emerged to be an effective approach to a priori quantify and predict the gains from scaling up
037   model size, dataset size, and computational resources. Previous works on scaling laws predominantly
038   focus on monolingual LMs, neglecting the increasingly crucial role of multilinguality for LMs to cater
039   to diverse linguistic populations and global users (Conneau & Lample, 2019; Conneau et al., 2020;
040   Yang et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). With increasing emphasis on inclusion and wider language
041   support (Qin et al., 2024), there is a pressing need to extend these scaling laws to multilingual LMs to
042   address unique challenges, particularly how to balance training across different languages effectively.
043   Although there exist some studies that examine multilingual scaling laws (Gordon et al., 2021;
044   Ghorbani et al., 2022; Fernandes et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), they often focus on the specific
045   problem of neural machine translation (NMT). These studies predominantly utilize encoder-decoder
046   transformer architectures, which are not widely applied to generation tasks. Furthermore, they
047   typically restrict their analysis to bilingual settings without capturing the complexities of language
048   interactions. This omission is critical, as cross-lingual transfer, a key benefit in multilingual training,
049   plays a significant role in improving performance across languages (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Xian
050
      et al., 2022; Patra et al., 2023).
051   To address the aforementioned issues, in this work, we focus on building scaling laws for general
052   purpose decoder-only LMs pretrained on multilingual data. Our contributions are grounded in a
053   realistic and novel hypothesis that unlocks a broader understanding of multilingual scaling, making
      our analysis scalable to an arbitrary number of languages.

172   3.2   H YPOTHESIS
173
      For simplicity, we first consider a setting where model size and dataset size are fixed, and we only
174   study the relationship between losses and sampling ratios, i.e., Li (p). Fitting each individual Li (p)
175   directly is intractable as p is an n-dimensional vector, and it is computationally infeasible to sample
176   sufficiently many p to effectively cover the n-dimensional space. To address this challenge, we
177   propose and validate a realistic hypothesis to reduce this complexity.
178
      Despite previous attempts to study the relationship between performance and sampling ratios (Fer-
179
      nandes et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), a key factor often overlooked is the impact of cross-lingual
180   transfer. In particular, previous works directly assume Li (p) = Li (pi ), implying that the loss of
181   each language depends solely on its own sampling ratio, regardless of the combination of other
182   jointly trained languages. However, this assumption is equivalent to the statement that there exists
183   no knowledge transfer across languages, which does not hold true in general. For instance, due to
184   insufficient training data, low-resource languages (e.g., Catalan) benefit from knowledge transfer
185   from linguistically similar high-resource languages (e.g., Spanish) (Arivazhagan et al., 2019).
186   To tackle the problem, we provide a more realistic hypothesis. Instead of studying the sampling
187   ratios of each individual language, we focus on language groups with the following properties: i)
188   Minimal cross-group transfer: The majority of cross-lingual transfer occurs within a group, with
189   minimal transfer across groups. ii) Data sufficiency: Each group provides a substantial amount
190   of data, reducing the need to model dynamics of low-resource languages. We find language families
191   to be an intuitive and effective grouping, as they are defined based on linguistic similarities, and
192   each language family contains multiple languages, mitigating the low-resource issues associated
193   with individual languages. 3 Then, we have the following hypothesis.
194
      Hypothesis 1 During the pretraining of a multilingual language model, the test cross-entropy loss
195
      of each language family only depends on its own sampling ratio, regardless of the sampling ratios of
196
      other languages jointly trained.
197
198   Hypothesis testing. We use a controlled experiment to test our hypothesis. We train an LM on a
199   data mixture containing three language families: Romance, Germanic and Slavic. In the training,
200   we fix the sampling ratio of Romance to be either 0.2 or 0.5, and vary the ratios of the other two
201   families. At the individual language level, one might expect high-resource languages like English
202   (within the Germanic family) to transfer knowledge to related languages in the Romance family (e.g.,
203   Spanish, French). However, our hypothesis focuses on cross-family transfer instead of cross-lingual
204   transfer among individual languages. In Figure 2 (left), we observe that with a fixed sampling ratio,
205
      the Romance loss does not noticeably change regardless of the combination ratios of Germanic and
      Slavic data. This stability indicates that the primary source of performance variation is the sampling
206
      ratio of the family itself, rather than cross-family interactions. This finding supports our hypothesis
207
      that cross-family transfer is minimal, making it feasible to model each language family’s loss as a
208   function of its own sampling ratio. To strengthen our findings, we conduct the same experiment with
209   three additional family combinations, detailed in Appendix C.
210
211

      a similar experiment with random groupings. In this setup, both Group 1 and Group 2 contain
212
      languages from the Indic family. If our hypothesis about cross-family transfer holds, we would
213
      expect substantial transfer between these groups, since they share linguistic characteristics. As shown

252   The choice of power-law formulation follows naturally from previous studies on scaling laws, which
253   demonstrate that the loss exhibits a power-law behavior with respect to dataset size (Kaplan et al.,
254
      2020). Our proposed form extends this concept to the multilingual setting, where the sampling ratio
      pi can be viewed as analogous to relative dataset size for each family. The power-law form captures
255
      the intuitive notion that increasing the sampling ratio pi leads to diminishing returns in terms of
256
      reducing the test cross-entropy loss. In other words, as the amount of data for a language family
257   increases, its marginal contribution to performance improvement decreases.
258
259   Interpretation of parameters. L⋆i represents the test loss when a language family i constitutes the
260
      entire training dataset (pi = 1). It indicates the baseline difficulty of modeling this family alone. As
      the model size N increases, L⋆i typically decreases due to higher model capacity.
261
262   On the other hand, γi indicates the decay rate of loss for the i-th family given increasing proportion of
263   the i-th family in the training data mixture. In general, a larger γi indicates that the language family
264   benefits more from increasing sampling ratio and should be prioritized in the training mixture for an
265   overall performance improvement.

343

                  tasks, whereas their work is focused on a specialized NMT setting with encoder-decoder

346
                  limiting the applicability of their findings. In contrast, our scaling law is relevant to a
347               broader spectrum of language modeling tasks, which is crucial given the prevalence of

350             • Multilingual scalability: We significantly extend the scope to 23 languages across 5

353               lingual transfer. In contrast, Eq. 7 does not generalize for more than 2 languages, as it lacks
354               a mechanism for incorporating multilingual interactions.
355             • Incorporation of Dataset Size: Our scaling law explicitly incorporates the effect of dataset

358               affecting multilingual model performance.
359             • Simpler and more predictive form: In Eq. 7, the parameter βp,i is dependent on the

361               unseen values of p, additional heuristics or retraining would be required to determine
362               the corresponding βp,i . In contrast, our proposed law decouples the dependency on pi
363               through the power-law exponent γi , which remains constant across different sampling ratios.

                  This makes our model more straightforward and fully predictive without requiring extra

527   5    C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   In this work, we develop a comprehensive and scalable scaling law for multilingual language models,
530   addressing the complexities of training with multiple languages. Through large-scale experiments
531
      involving over 100 models across 23 languages from 5 language families, we demonstrate that the test
      cross-entropy loss for each language family follows a predictable power-law relationship with model
532
      size, dataset size and sampling ratios. Importantly, we introduce and validate a novel hypothesis
533
      that decouples the interaction between language families, allowing us to model the performance
534   of each family independently of others in the training mixture. This insight enables us to derive
535   the optimal sampling ratios that minimize the overall multilingual loss, providing a practical data
536   mixing strategy. Our results show that the optimal sampling ratios, computed from small models,
537   generalize effectively to models that are several orders of magnitude larger, significantly reducing the
538   need for resource-intensive data mixture selection in large-scale training. This offers a scalable and
539   cost-effective method for optimizing multilingual LM training and opens up new avenues for future
      research in multilingual scaling laws and data optimization strategies.

1073   G     L IMITATIONS
1074
1075   One limitation of our work is that while the scaling law predicts the performance well in most cases,
1076   its utility may diminish when dealing with very small N , D and p values. For instance, using the
1077   formulation in Eq. 2, setting pi = 0 results in an infinite loss. However, in reality, even if a training
1078   mixture contains no data for a particular family, its test cross-entropy loss is likely to be non-vacuous,
1079   as some general language information can still be transferable across families. In such cases, the
       resulting loss will be entirely dependent on cross-family transfer, which is challenging to quantify. For


                                                                      20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       example, intuitively, if the test language family is Germanic, the Romance mono-family model might
1081   perform noticeably better than the Sino-Tibetan mono-family model, due to linguistic similarities.
1082   Consequently, there may not be a fixed value associated with Li (pi = 0) in our formulation.
1083
1084
       Similarly, for small D values, the risk of overfitting may need to be explicitly modeled, as suggested
       in Chen et al. (2024). Note that this issue is more pronounced when modeling individual languages,
1085
       as low-resource languages suffer more from limited data. In contrast, language families are less
1086
       affected due to the abundance of data within each family.
1087
1088   On the other hand, small N values are less problematic, as scaling laws are primarily intended to
1089   predict performance for larger models. We have demonstrated that our formulation works well even
1090
       for relatively small models, such as those with 85M parameters.
