009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            LLM-generated datasets have been recently leveraged as training data to mitigate
012            data scarcity in specific domains. However, these LLM-generated datasets ex-
013            hibit limitations on training models due to a lack of diversity, which underscores
014            the need for effective diversity evaluation. Despite the growing demand, the di-
015            versity evaluation of LLM-generated datasets remains under-explored. To this
016
               end, we propose a diversity evaluation method for LLM-generated datasets from
               a classification perspective, namely, DCScore. Specifically, DCScore treats the
017
               diversity evaluation as a sample classification task, considering mutual relation-
018
               ships among samples. We further provide theoretical verification of the diversity-
019            related axioms satisfied by DCScore, demonstrating it as a principled diversity
020            evaluation method. Additionally, we show that existing methods can be incor-
021            porated into our proposed method in a unified manner. Meanwhile, DCScore
022            enjoys much lower computational costs compared to existing methods. Finally,
023            we conduct experiments on LLM-generated datasets to validate the effectiveness
024            of DCScore. The experimental results indicate that DCScore correlates bet-
025            ter with various diversity pseudo-truths of evaluated datasets, thereby verifying
026            its effectiveness. Our code is available via: https://anonymous.4open.
027
               science/r/DCScore-ICLR/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have shown exceptional performance across a range of fields, such
032   as chatbots (Achiam et al., 2023), computer programming (Gu, 2023), and reasoning (Yuan et al.,
033   2024). Inspired by their remarkable capacities, some research (Ye et al., 2022; Abdullin et al., 2024;
034   Ding et al., 2024) employs LLMs as dataset generators to mitigate the shortage of training data.
035   Although generated data facilitates model optimization, recent studies (Yu et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
036   2023) suggest that a lack of diversity within the dataset—measured by the variation between sam-
037   ples (Long et al., 2024)—may lead to performance degradation in some scenarios. To ensure a highly
038   diverse LLM-generated dataset, previous studies have focused on improving the diversity of LLM-
039
      generated datasets through prompt design (Yu et al., 2024) and multiple topics (Wang et al., 2022).
      However, these studies neglect a critical aspect, i.e., evaluating the diversity of LLM-generated
040
      datasets in a reasonable manner. A principled diversity evaluation metric can provide feedback to
041
      LLM generators and guide them to generate data with higher diversity. Beyond evaluating the di-
042   versity of LLM-generated datasets, these methods can also be used for data selection (Cao et al.,
043   2023), quantifying augmentation performance (Yang et al., 2024b; Gontijo-Lopes et al., 2020), and
044   assessing mode collapse (Dan Friedman & Dieng, 2023). In summary, a diversity evaluation method
045   for LLM-generated datasets is becoming increasingly crucial.
046
      Since the diversity evaluation of LLM-generated datasets remains under-explored, a natural solution
047
      is to directly employ diversity evaluation methods from the fields of natural language processing
048   (NLP) (Khurana et al., 2023) and machine learning (ML) (Jordan & Mitchell, 2015). Specifically,
049   efforts to measure diversity within these domains can be summarized into three categories: N-gram-
050   based method (Zhu et al., 2018; Mishra et al., 2020), Reference-based method (Heusel et al., 2017;
051   Cı́fka et al., 2018), and Transformation-based method (Du & Black, 2019; Zhang et al., 2024).
052   The n-gram-based method evaluates diversity through n-gram statistics, e.g., the distinct n-grams
053   metric (Li et al., 2015) calculates the proportion of unique n-grams out of the total number of n-
      grams. This approach primarily focuses on the form difference of evaluated texts, often overlooking

054
      semantic aspects and offering limited flexibility for evaluators. To align the diversity criteria with
055   human judgment, the reference-based method has emerged as a promising alternative. This approach
056   employs a reference distribution or data as an approximation of human judgment and calculates the
057   similarity between the evaluated data and the reference data (Holtzman et al., 2019). However, the
058   process of collecting reference data can be both time-consuming and potentially bias-inducing.
059
      Drawing inspiration from deep representation learning (Butepage et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021),
060
      the transformation-based method evaluates diversity by first mapping the data into the representation
061   space and then performing diversity summarization (Tevet & Berant, 2020). This summarization of-
062   ten employs high-complexity algorithms, such as eigenvalue computation. Here, various embedding
063   functions can be employed to facilitate the transformation. A popular way is to leverage a sentence
064   transformer, e.g., Sentence-Bert (Reimers, 2019) and SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021), to generate rep-
065   resentations of the evaluated data and subsequently calculate the average similarity between these
066   representations. Owing to the versatility of embedding functions, transformation-based methods can
067   simultaneously consider extensive aspects, such as semantics, form, and style, to encode data repre-
068   sentations, thereby providing a more comprehensive evaluation of diversity. However, this type of
069
      method struggles to offer a holistic evaluation of a dataset due to its reliance on complex diversity
      summarization.
070
071   In a nutshell, existing diversity evaluation methods in NLP and ML suffer from inherent limitations
072   in the LLM-generated dataset evaluation scenario. To effectively evaluate the diversity of LLM-
073   generated datasets, the following challenges must be tackled: (1) Holistic Analysis. The diversity
074   evaluation of LLM-generated datasets is a holistic analysis task, necessitating consideration of the
075
      impact of each sample on the final evaluation results. If a new sample is added, the diversity evalu-
      ation of the newly augmented dataset should completely account for the relationship between each
076
      original sample and the new one. (2) Axiomatic Requirements. Prior research has suggested sev-
077
      eral axioms that diversity metrics should ideally satisfy. To align with these intuitive principles, a
078   well-established diversity evaluation should exhibit properties corresponding to these axioms.
079
080   To sum up, the essence of diversity is associated with the identification of differences between
081
      samples, and the ability to distinguish these differences is a key element in the classification pro-
      cess (Quine, 1969). Motivated by this, we propose an LLM-generated dataset diversity evaluation
082
      method from a classification perspective, namely, DCScore. On the one hand, DCScore treats the
083
      evaluation of each sample in the LLM-generated dataset as a distinct classification task, effectively
084   addressing the need for a holistic analysis. On the other hand, we provide theoretical verification
085   that DCScore satisfies four axioms outlined in Leinster & Cobbold (2012), including effective
086   number, identical samples, symmetry, and monotonicity. Meanwhile, we also observe that existing
087   methods can be reformulated from the view of DCScore by leveraging different embedding and
088   classification functions.
089
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
090
091
             • We propose DCScore, a classification-based diversity evaluation method for LLM-
092
               generated datasets. The core idea behind DCScore is to treat diversity evaluation as a
093
               sample classification task, enabling the capture of mutual relationships among samples.
094
095
             • We theoretically validate that DCScore adheres to several intuitive axioms suggested
096            by Leinster & Cobbold (2012), evidencing the superiority of DCScore. Additionally,
097            we integrate existing methods into the DCScore framework in a unified manner.
098
099          • Extensive experiments show that DCScore exhibits a stronger correlation with diversity
100            parameters and human judgment compared to baseline metrics. We also perform a compu-
101            tational cost experiment to confirm the lower computational expense of DCScore.

      2.1   D IVERSITY E VALUATION M ETHODS
109
110   N-gram-based Methods. With the development of LLMs as dataset generators, the diversity eval-
111   uation of LLM-generated datasets has become a challenging task and remains under-explored in
112   recent evaluation studies (Liang et al., 2022; tatsu lab, 2023). The most comparable diversity evalu-
113   ation research can be traced back to studies in NLP and ML, which can be summarized into the
114   n-gram-based method (Mishra et al., 2020), reference-based method (Heusel et al., 2017), and
115   transformation-based method (Lai et al., 2020). The n-gram-based method is the most popular
116
      lexical diversity evaluation method, leveraging n-grams to capture differences in sentence form (Yu
      et al., 2024). Commonly used n-gram-based diversity metrics include distinct n-grams (distinct-
117
      n) (Song et al., 2024), self-BLEU (Shu et al., 2019), and ROUGE-L (Wang et al., 2022; Padmaku-
118
      mar & He, 2023). However, this method has limitations, as it overlooks differences in other aspects
119   such as semantics and style.
120
121   Reference-based Methods. Diversity evaluation is inherently subjective, leading to a reliance on
122
      human judgment. Consequently, the reference-based method evaluates diversity by comparing the
      distribution of the evaluated data to that of a reference dataset (Heusel et al., 2017). MAUVE (Pil-
123
      lutla et al., 2021) exemplifies this idea by employing a divergence-based metric to capture corre-
124
      lations with human judgment. Regarding the natural language inference (NLI) training set as the
125   reference dataset, (Stasaski & Hearst, 2022) first trains an NLI model to infer the relationship be-
126   tween pairs of generated texts and then calculates diversity based on these inference results. Due
127   to the challenges in collecting reference datasets, a recent study (Le Bronnec et al., 2024) proposes
128   evaluating diversity through precision and recall. Despite these advancements, the reference-based
129   method remains significantly constrained because of the need for reference datasets.
130
      Transformation-based Methods. The transformation-based (Lee et al., 2023) method leverages
131   well-designed models to generate representations of the evaluated data. Then, the diversity of these
132   representations is summarized using techniques such as eigenvalue computation (Dan Friedman &
133   Dieng, 2023) and clustering (Du & Black, 2019). Owing to the superior performance of representa-
134   tion learning, this method considers various aspects of the evaluated data, including semantics, form,
135   and style, offering greater flexibility compared to the other two methods. However, its dependence
136   on high-complexity summarization techniques, such as eigenvalue computation, limits its scalability
137   for comprehensive evaluation tasks, such as evaluating the diversity of LLM-generated datasets.
138   In summary, existing methods primarily focus on NLP and ML fields and are challenging to apply
139   directly to overall dataset diversity evaluation. Different from the above-mentioned studies, our
140   work is dedicated to the holistic diversity evaluation of LLM-generated datasets. Additionally, to
141   ensure flexible evaluation, our work aims to evaluate diversity-sensitive components that impact the
142   performance of trained models in terms of diversity.

      3.2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
190
191
      The diversity evaluation of the dataset is a sample richness evaluation problem. Based on the gener-
192   ation scenarios presented in Table 1, we find that in certain downstream tasks, the diversity of some
193   components in the LLM-generated dataset does not influence the performance of the trained models.
194   Conversely, the diversity of other components significantly impacts model performance. We refer to
195   these components, whose diversity influences performance, as diversity-sensitive components, de-
196   noted as Ti . For example, the input text xi in sentiment classification tasks is the diversity-sensitive
197   component. Conversely, the output text yi , which represents the sentiment label of the sample and
198   is typically a numerical label (e.g., 0 or 1), does not influence model performance in terms of di-
199   versity. Therefore, the output text cannot be considered as the diversity-sensitive component. It
200
      should be underscored that diversity-sensitive components vary across downstream tasks. As such,
      the diversity evaluation of LLM-generated datasets can be transformed into the diversity evaluation
201
      of diversity-sensitive components.
202
203   Given an LLM-generated dataset D = {Ti }ni=1 , we define {T̃i }ni=1 as a collection of diversity-
204   sensitive components. Hence, the problem of diversity evaluation of D can be formulated as follows:
205
206                                     DiversityScore ← Eval({T̃i }ni=1 )                                 (2)
207
208   where Eval is the diversity evaluation function, which takes {T̃i }ni=1 as input and outputs the diver-
209   sity score DiversityScore of D.

230   4.1   DCS C O R E : D IVERSITY C ALCULATION FROM A C LASSIFICATION P ERSPECTIVE
231
232   To tackle the holistic analysis and axiomatic requirements challenges, we propose DCScore to eval-
233
      uate diversity from a classification perspective. According to the problem formulation in section 3.2,
      DCScore aims to evaluate LLM-generated datasets by evaluating the diversity of diversity-sensitive
234
      components. As shown in Figure 1, DCScore can treat diversity evaluation as a classification task
235
      due to the inherent sample difference measurement of diversity evaluation. The difference between
236   samples can be measured by a n-classification task where the evaluation of n sample datasets in-
237   volves n n-classification tasks, with each sample corresponding to a category.
238
239
      Let D = {Ti }ni=1 = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 denote an LLM-generated dataset comprising n input-output sam-
240   ples, and {T̃i }ni=1 represents the diversity-sensitive components. DCScore adheres to the paradigm
241   of the transformation-based method to evaluate the diversity of D. Specifically, given T̃i , DCScore
242   first applies an embedding function Φ to extract the sample representation hi = Φ(T̃i ). For all
243   samples in D, we obtain the sample representation matrix H ∈ Rn×d across all samples, where d
244
      denotes the dimension of sample representations. Subsequently, DCScore utilizes a kernel func-
      tion Kernel to calculate a kernel matrix K, where K ∈ Rn×n and entry K[i, j] represents similarity
245
246
      between T̃i and T̃j . From a classification perspective, K[i, j] can be considered as the logit of T̃i
247   being classified into category cj . where cj corresponds to T̃j . Formally, the aforementioned process
248
      can be formulated as follows:
249                                          H = Φ({T̃i }ni=1 ), K = Kernel(H),                                             (3)

251   kernel. For Φ, a more expressive embedding function can be employed, such as one trained using a
252   well-designed framework like Sentence-Bert (Reimers, 2019).

      5.2.2    C ORRELATION WITH H UMAN J UDGMENT
453
454   Diversity evaluation is a subjective task, and an ideal method should align well with human judg-
455   ment. Therefore, we investigate the correlation between DCScore and human judgment. To miti-
456   gate evaluators’ bias, we enlist three individuals to perform pairwise diversity comparisons among
457   datasets with varying τg values and report the diversity ranking by averaging the win rate across eval-
458   uators. We conduct all evaluations five times to report average results. Moreover, we exclusively
459   use the 70B model for dataset generation to ensure better content creation.
460   Table 4 presents pairwise correlation between human judgment, τg , and DCScore. Table 4 indi-
461   cates a strong correlation between human judgment and τg , supporting the use of human judgment
462   as a diversity pseudo-truth. Based on this observation, DCScore performs better in two settings:
463   Story-Few (story completion task generation under the few-shot setting) and Text-Zero (text classi-
464   fication task generation under the zero-shot setting). This is confirmed by higher human-DCScore
465   correlation in these two settings. In contrast, for Story-Zero and Text-Few settings, we observe
466
      more identical content in the initial portions of the diversity-sensitive components within an evalu-
      ation batch. In these cases, human evaluators tend to disregard the identical content and base their
467
      judgments on the latter sections. However, DCScore is affected by the identical content, resulting
468
      in a lower pairwise correlation. Despite this, the correlation remains strong, as demonstrated by
469   previous studies (Akoglu, 2018).

528   6          C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we investigate the diversity evaluation of LLM-generated datasets, a topic systemati-
531
      cally under-explored in existing research. To this end, we present DCScore, a diversity evaluation
      method from a classification perspective. DCScore regards the holistic diversity evaluation as the
532
      classification task at the sample level, thereby facilitating the capture of mutual relationships be-
533
      tween samples. We provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that DCScore meets the axiom
534   requirements (Leinster & Cobbold, 2012) for a principled diversity evaluation method. Further-
535   more, we show that existing methods can be unified within the DCScore framework. Experiments
536   on LLM-generated datasets reveal that DCScore exhibits better correlations with various diver-
537   sity pseudo-truths, such as τg and human judgment. Meanwhile, DCScore exhibits significantly
538   lower computational cost compared to transformation-based counterparts. Finally, we hope our
539   work encourages future research to pay more attention to the diversity of LLM-generated datasets
      and promotes the wider application of these datasets.

      of diversity evaluation methods as follows.

870
      A.1   LLM DATASET G ENERATOR
871
      Prompt-guided and Dataset-guided Strategies. Recent studies (Ding et al., 2022; Chung et al.,
872
      2023) leverage LLMs to augment existing datasets or generate a dataset from scratch, demonstrat-
873   ing the effectiveness in improving dataset quality and reducing data collection costs. Generally,
874   efforts to employ LLMs as dataset generators can be categorized into three strategies: Prompt-
875   guided (Li et al., 2023), Dataset-guided (Ye et al., 2022), and Instruct-guided (Samuel et al., 2023).
876   The prompt-guided strategy, a prominent data augmentation approach using LLMs, involves design-
877   ing task-specific prompts to guide LLMs to augment data in a few-shot (Yoo et al., 2021) or zero-
878   shot (Mahmoudi et al., 2024) manner. Due to its simplicity and effectiveness, subsequent works
879   extend this strategy to various scenarios, such as medical (Yuan et al., 2023), person retrieval (Li
880   et al., 2024b), and social media scenario (dos Santos et al., 2024). However, simple prompt engi-
881
      neering has limitations in fully exploiting the capabilities of LLMs, leading to the development of
      multi-level prompt designs (Ye et al., 2024) and targeted sample augmentation (Yang et al., 2024a).
882
      To further harness the potential of LLMs, the dataset-guided strategy employs LLMs to generate a
883
      training set and then trains a task-specific model to annotate unlabeled data (Sahu & Laradji, 2024).
884   The dataset-guided strategy aims to approximate the distribution of targeted scenarios, but it is cur-
885   rently only applicable to text classification tasks.
886
887
      Instruct-guided Strategy. Previous studies (White et al., 2023) indicate that the design of prompts
      significantly impacts the performance of LLMs, spurring research into the instruct-guided strat-
888
      egy. Generally speaking, the instruct-guided strategy leverages LLMs to generate instructions that
889
      guide another LLM in dataset generation (Evuru et al., 2024). These instructions typically relate
890   to context (Samuel et al., 2023), criteria (Huang et al., 2023), and tasks (Wang et al., 2022). To
891   further improve the quality of instructions, efforts have been concentrated on selecting optimal in-
892   structions (Li et al., 2024a), integrating soft instructions (Chen et al., 2023), and implementing
893   self-correction mechanisms (Gupta et al., 2023). In a nutshell, LLMs are employed to generate or
894   augment datasets through prompt engineering and multi-step strategies, which encompass various
895   application scenarios and downstream tasks. Meanwhile, the diversity of LLM-generated datasets
896   emerges as a critical factor in measuring data quality. In our work, we focus on the diversity evalu-
897   ation of LLM-generated datasets derived from any dataset generation strategies.
898
899   A.2   A PPLICATION OF D IVERSITY E VALUATION M ETHODS
900
901   Quantifying Augmentation Performance. As data augmentation becomes an essential component
902   in the training of deep neural networks (Zhang, 2017; Park et al., 2019), researchers gradually ex-
903
      plore a better quantification of the quality of data augmentation. Some studies (Cubuk et al., 2019)
      suggest that the effectiveness of data augmentation arises from the increased diversity of the data.
904
      Inspired by this observation, a series of studies have introduced diversity evaluation metrics into the
905
      performance assessment of data augmentation strategies. Specifically, they consider diversity as one
906   aspect of evaluating the quality of augmented data, thereby determining the effectiveness of data
907   augmentation. For instance, Gontijo-Lopes et al. (2020) utilize the fundamental idea that models
908   find it more challenging to fit more diverse data, comparing metrics such as training loss and train-
909   ing time before and after augmentation to assess diversity. Similarly, Yang et al. (2024b) evaluate
910   diversity by examining the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the similarity matrix of samples before
911   and after augmentation.
912   Evaluating Mode Collapse. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020)
913   suffer from a well-known phenomenon called mode collapse, which can result in a lack of diversity
914   in the generated samples (Dieng et al., 2019). Consequently, existing studies assess mode collapse
915   by evaluating the diversity of the generated samples. For instance, a common approach is to train
916   an MNIST classifier and then count the number of unique classes predicted for the generated sam-
917   ples. Following this paradigm, VendiScore (Dan Friedman & Dieng, 2023) compares the generation
      diversity of PresGAN (Dieng et al., 2019) and Self-conditioned GAN (Liu et al., 2020). Addition-


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      ally, some studies (Yu et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2018; Caccia et al., 2018) employ different metrics to
919   evaluate the diversity of generated samples from GANs.
920
921   Other Applications. In addition to the aforementioned applications, diversity evaluation metrics
922
      have valuable applications in various areas, including sample selection for datasets (Cao et al., 2023),
      enhancing robustness (Lee et al., 2022), and eliminating biases within datasets (Huber et al., 2024).

1419   F   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
1420
1421   To enhance our understanding of DCScore, we provide a brief analysis of its time complexity in
1422   Table 13. Denoting Okernel as the complexity associated with general kernels (i.e., kernels other
1423   than linear kernels), we analyze the complexity in the pairwise similarity and summarization stages.
1424
       In the pairwise similarity stage, the computation of pairwise similarities results in a complexity of
       O(n2 ) for DCScore. When combined with the complexity Okernel of the general kernel computa-
1425
       tion, DCScore exhibits a total complexity of O(n2 · Okernel ) in this stage. In the summarization
1426
       stage, DCScore has a complexity of O(n2 ) due to the softmax operation. Consequently, the overall
1427   complexity of DCScore for general kernels is O(n2 · Okernel + n2 ). In contrast, VendiScore has
1428   a total complexity of O(n2 · Okernel + n3 ), where the pairwise similarity stage is identical to that
1429   of DCScore, while the summarization stage incurs a complexity of O(n3 ) due to the eigenvalue
1430   computation. Thus, for general kernels, DCScore demonstrates lower complexity than VendiScore.
1431
       However, when the inner product is employed as the kernel function and n ≫ d, VendiScore can
1432
       significantly reduce the complexity by replacing the pairwise similarity XX T with X T X, where
1433   X ∈ Rn×d . This results in complexities of O(d2 n) for the pairwise similarity stage and O(d3 )
1434   for the summarization stage. In this regard, DCScore has a complexity of O(n2 d + n2 ), which is
1435   slightly worse than that of VendiScore. Fortunately, we can leverage efficient techniques, such as
1436   those proposed in Shim et al. (2017) and Wen et al. (2023), to reduce the computational complexity
1437   of DCScore. Overall, compared to VendiScore, DCScore maintains lower complexity in most
1438   cases, as empirically validated in Section 5.3 and Appendix E.3. Although VendiScore has a lower
1439   complexity when the inner product is used as the kernel, experiments in the Appendix E.3 show
1440   that the computation times of VendiScore and DCScore are quite similar. Additionally, in practical
1441
       applications, different kernels can maintain low computational complexity, which is more useful
       than being restricted to a single kernel.
