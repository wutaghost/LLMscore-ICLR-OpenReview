009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are widely recognized as leading architectures
012            for addressing classification problems involving graphical data. In this paper, we
013            formally define the challenge of effectively constructing edges within a dataset and
014            training a GNN over this graph and introduce SHIKI - a novel method to tackle
015            this task. We provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis demonstrating how
016            graph convolutions can improve expected performance by leveraging edges. Our
017            study focuses on the node classification problem within a non-linearly separable
018            Gaussian mixture model, combined with a stochastic block model, and we visu-
019
               ally demonstrate its applicability to real-world datasets. Specifically, we show that
               a single graph convolution in the second layer can reduce the expected loss when
020
               applying a heuristic for edge creation. We validate our findings through extensive
021
               experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including those related to
022            the entity matching problem and textual review classification. For the synthetic
023            data, we conduct experiments based on the dataset‚Äôs difficulty and various hyper-
024            parameters in our method, drawing connections between the two. Additionally, we
025            perform an ablation study by systematically removing components of our method
026            and testing the resulting degraded approach, which highlights the necessity of our
027            full method. We employ several GNN architectures in the experiments, including
028            GCN, GraphSAGE, and GAT.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for learning from graph-structured
034   data, with applications spanning social networks Ding et al. (2019), molecular biology Gaudelet
035   et al. (2021), recommender systems Wu et al. (2018), and more. Aside from tasks where graph-
036   structure lends itself well to the domain, GNNs were also shown to be useful in tasks where the data
037   is not inherently structured, e.g., entity matching (Genossar et al., 2023b).
038   Graph convolutional models Kipf & Welling (2017) are among the most popular approaches for
039   learning on relational data, leveraging the idea of aggregating the features of a node‚Äôs neighbors
040   rather than just its own. While numerous empirical studies on GCN variants Chen et al. (2020)
041   have demonstrated that graph convolutions can outperform traditional classification methods like
042   multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), there has been little theoretical progress in explaining how graph
043   convolutions enhance node classification in multi-layer networks, especially on non-graphical data.
044   Baranwal et al. recently showed, both theoretically and empirically, that even for applications with-
045   out inherent graph structure, synthetically created edges can boost performance Baranwal et al.
046   (2022). Specifically, they demonstrate an improvement in performance where the data poses a train-
047   ing challenge for a simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP). In their work, edges are created according
048   to prior knowledge of the sample label, and no method for incorporating edge creation into the learn-
049   ing pipeline was proposed. We aim to bridge this gap, proposing a heuristic to create useful edges
050
      on top of an MLP that models non-structural data in a self-supervised manner, followed by training
      a GNN model over it using the generated edges. Our contribution is threefold:
051
052
            ‚ÄĘ We propose and formulate a novel method for adding edges to a non-graphical data.
053
            ‚ÄĘ We show the effectiveness of our method in terms of expected loss.

195   In Section 2 we have demonstrated, using two real-world datasets, an interesting spatial effect. Using
196   MLP, we can construct an embedded vector space in which data items from the same class tend to
197   cluster together. Such a phenomenon provides us with a good starting point when constructing a
198   graph structure that serves in training a GNN to improve the outcome of a classification problem.
199   Our goal in this paper, is therefore, to enhance MLP usage of node features by connecting similar
200   nodes of the same class and use GNN‚Äôs message-propagation to improve the generated embedded
201
      space. We focus our attention on a careful selection of edges to connect nodes of the same class.

      a traditional MLP that does not incorporate graphical information. In particular, we are interested
203
      in answers to the following two questions. First, is it possible to create edges from a non-graphical

264   4.1   MLP T RAINING
265
266
      The first step in the pipeline involves training and MLP on the data. The training yields three
      outcomes that are useful for us. First, it yields a label for each trained dataset. Second, it provides a
267
      confidence in the classification task. Finally, it generates a latent vector space.
268
269   The latent vector space offers a notion of a distance. In general, such a distance measure does
      not have to rely entirely on the outcome of the MLP. For tabular data, we can use the columns as

270
      dimensions in a vector space. For textual data, we can create embeddings using an LLM, capturing
271   the latent vector space of the last layer. Finally, for vector data, we can use the vectors themselves,
272   or alternatively use an MLP‚Äôs hidden-layer embeddings.
273
274
      4.2   C ONFIDENCE OF W EAK L ABELS
275
276   In this step we get the confidence score from the trained MLP, thus using weak labels. Weak labels
277   are noisy and uncertain labels that may differ from the true labels. Using weak labels runs the risk
278   of predicting the wrong labels to nodes in the graph. Wrong labels hurt the training in general and
279   in particular will harm the process of edge creation. In particular, weak labels, when used in edge
280   creation are likely to increase q, the probability of connecting mismatched nodes, which leads to
281
      deteriorated performance.
282   For each instance x, the MLP first calculates a score |M LP (x)|‚ąą (‚ąí‚ąě, ‚ąě), on which we apply the
283   sigmoid function to generate a distribution in [0, 1]. Nodes with high absolute values are assumed to
284   be nodes the model are confident in their prediction, and we call them confident nodes. We assume
285   (and later prove) that more confident nodes tend to produce more accurate predictions.
286
287   4.3   C ONFIDENT N ODES S ELECTION
288
289   In this step we determine the set of confident nodes to be labeled and participate in edge creation
290
      with respect to these weak labels, using the method in (Baranwal et al., 2022). We consider two
      possible ways to select confident nodes, as follows.
291
292
      Top percentile: We order the nodes according to the scores the MLP assigns with them, and define
293           any node x that is within the top percent of the scores, where percent is some hyper-
294           parameter to be a confident node.
295
      Confidence threshold: Given a threshold ŌĄ > 0, a node x is defined to be a confident node if
296
             |M LP (x)|> ŌĄ .
297
298   We note that highly parameterized models, including transformer-based pre-trained language mod-
299   els, often generate uncalibrated confidence scores (Guo et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2021; Genossar
300   et al., 2023a). These scores tend to be mostly dichotomous, clustering near 0 or 1, making them
301   unreliable. Thus, one needs to be careful directly using a LLM (instead of a simple MLP) as part of
302   our model.
303
304   4.4   E DGE C REATION
305
306   Edge creation is done in the same manner as described in Section 2, taking into account only confi-
307   dent nodes. This method, which we refer to as main strategy, is based solely on node labels.
308   We observe that in real-world graphs, edges tend to connect nodes that are in close proximity. Thus,
309   we propose a strategy that takes into account also node distances. We propose two strategies, as
310   follows.
311
312   Proximity Strategy: Following the observation above, closer nodes will be more likely to be con-
313
              nected.
314   Diversity Strategy: Nodes that are further away are assigned with higher probability to be con-
315           nected. This strategy aims at diversity to increase the information gain.

499   6   R ELATED W ORK
500
501
502   There is a significant body of theoretical work on unsupervised learning for random graph models
503   where node features are absent, and only relational information is available (Decelle et al., 2011;
504
      MassoulieŐĀ, 2014; Mossel et al., 2018; 2015; Abbe & Sandon, 2015; Abbe et al., 2015; Bordenave
      et al., 2015; Deshpande et al., 2015; Montanari & Sen, 2016; Banks et al., 2016; Abbe & Sandon,
505
      2018; Li et al., 2019; Kloumann et al., 2017; Gaudio et al., 2022). In contrast, for data models that
506
      include both node features and relational information, numerous studies have addressed the semi-
507   supervised node classification problem, such as (Scarselli et al., 2009; Cheng et al., 2011; Gilbert
508   et al., 2012; Dang & Viennet, 2012; GuŐąnnemann et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2013; Jin et al., 2019;
509   Mehta et al., 2019; Chien et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2021). These works offer valuable empirical
510   insights into the benefits of incorporating graph structure. Our study addresses a slightly different
511   settings, where node features are available, yet relational information is missing.
512
      In Deshpande et al. (2018); Lu & Sen (2020), the authors investigate the fundamental thresholds
513   for classifying a significant portion of nodes with linear sample complexity and large, but finite, de-
514   grees. In Fountoulakis et al. (2022), the authors present a theoretical analysis of the graph attention
515   mechanism (GAT), identifying the conditions under which the attention mechanism is effective (or
516   not) for node classification tasks. Our research, however, focuses on graph convolutions rather than
517   attention-based methods. While several studies examine the expressive power, extrapolation, and
518   the oversmoothing phenomenon in GNNs (see, e.g., Balcilar et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2021); Oono &
519   Suzuki (2020); Li et al. (2018)), we aim to compare the strengths and limitations of graph convolu-
520   tions with those of traditional MLPs when both do not leverage built-in relational information.
521   In Li et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023), the authors also utilize artificial edges using standard k-
522   nearest-neighbors procedure in their node-classification process. However, their setting still requires
523   an existing built-in graph, while we focus on constructing the graph.

526   7   C ONCLUSION AND OPEN QUESTIONS
527
528
529   In this work, we defined the challenge of effectively constructing edges within a dataset for improved
530
      training using GNNs and introduced a novel method to tackle this task. We formally shown and em-
      pirically demonstrated how graph convolutions could improve expected performance by leveraging
531
      these created edges. The results were empirically confirmed through extensive experiments on both
532
      synthetic and real-world datasets, including those involving the entity matching problem and text
533   prediction.
534
535   Our analysis is limited to the SHIKI heuristic. Other heuristics will require a new analysis. Fur-
536
      thermore, we did not solve or claimed to solve the optimality problem (Problem 2). Thus, in future
      work we intend to investigate effective solutions to this problem by either finding the optimal graph,
537
      or showing a way to optimize the task directly.
538
539   Finally, our analysis is limited to graph convolution. A possible future direction is to test whether
      our theoretical insights also apply to graph attention networks (GAT).

       E.4   N UMBER OF EDGES
2619
2620   Here, we discuss the number of edges in each edge creation method. As we can see from the tables,
2621   the number of edges in the SHIKI model is much bigger than in the KNN baseline.
2622
       In easier datasets such as the Walmart-Amazon dataset, the number of edges in SHIKI is about 40
2623
       times larger than in KNN.
2624
2625   In tougher datasets, naturally, the percentage of confident nodes is smaller than in easier datasets,
2626   thus we can potentially have fewer nodes. Even in this case, the number of edges in SHIKI is about
2627   4 times larger than in KNN.
2628   This may indicate that in order to effectively utilize artificial edges, we need to create many edges.
