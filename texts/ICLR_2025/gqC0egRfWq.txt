011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph-based semi-supervised learning is a powerful paradigm in machine learn-
014            ing for modeling and exploiting the underlying graph structure that captures the
015
               relationship between labeled and unlabeled data. A large number of classical
               as well as modern deep learning based algorithms have been proposed for this
016
               problem, often having tunable hyperparameters. We initiate a formal study of
017
               tuning algorithm hyperparameters from parameterized algorithm families for this
018            problem. We obtain novel O(log n) pseudo-dimension upper bounds for hyper-
019            parameter selection in three classical label propagation-based algorithm families,
020            where n is the number of nodes, implying bounds on the amount of data needed
021            for learning provably good parameters. We further provide matching Ω(log n)
022            pseudo-dimension lower bounds, thus asymptotically characterizing the learning-
023            theoretic complexity of the parameter tuning problem. We extend our study to
024            selecting architectural hyperparameters in modern graph neural networks. We
025            bound the Rademacher complexity for tuning the self-loop weighting in recently
026
               proposed Simplified Graph Convolution (SGC) networks. We further propose a
               tunable architecture that interpolates graph convolutional neural networks (GCN)
027
               and graph attention networks (GAT) in every layer, and provide Rademacher com-
028
               plexity bounds for tuning the interpolation coefficient.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Semi-supervised learning is a powerful paradigm in machine learning which reduces the dependence
034   on expensive and hard-to-obtain labeled data, by using a combination of labeled and unlabeled data.
035   This has become increasingly relevant in the era of large language models, where an extremely
036   large amount of labeled training data is needed. A large number of techniques have been proposed
037   in the literature to exploit the structure of unlabeled data, including popularly used graph-based
038   semi-supervised learning algorithms (Blum & Mitchell, 1998; Zhu et al., 2003; Zhou et al., 2003;
039
      Delalleau et al., 2005; Chapelle et al., 2009). More recently, there has been an increasing interest in
      developing effective neural network architectures for graph-based learning (Kipf & Welling, 2017;
040
      Veličković, Petar et al., 2018; Iscen et al., 2019). However, different algorithms, architectures, and
041
      values of hyperparameters perform well on different datasets (Dwivedi et al., 2023), and there is
042   no principled way of selecting the best approach for the data at hand. In this work, we initiate
043   the study of theoretically principled techniques for learning hyperparameters from infinitely large
044   semi-supervised learning algorithm families.
045
      In graph-based semi-supervised learning, the graph nodes consist of labeled and unlabeled data
046
      points, and the graph edges denote feature similarity between the nodes. There are several classical
047
      ways of defining a graph-based regularization objective that depend on the available and predicted
048   labels as well as the graph structure. Optimizing this objective yields the predicted labels and the
049   accuracy of the predictions depends on the chosen objective. The performance of the same objective
050   may vary across datasets. By studying parameterized families of objectives, we can learn to design
051   the objective that works best on a given domain-specific data. Similarly, modern deep learning based
052   techniques often have several candidate architectures and choices for hyperparameters, often man-
053   ually optimized for each application domain. Recent work has considered the problem of learning
      the graph hyperparameter used in semi-supervised learning (Balcan & Sharma (2021); Fatemi et al.

054
      (2021)) but leaves the problem of selecting the algorithm hyperparameter wide open. In this pa-
055   per, we take important initial steps to build the theoretical foundations of algorithm hyperparameter
056   selection in graph-based semi-supervised learning. Note that we focus specifically on algorithm hy-
057   perparameters, such as self-loop weights, leaving optimization hyperparameters like learning rates
058   outside the scope of this study.

060   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
061
             • We study hyperparameter tuning in three canonical label propagation-based semi-
062
               supervised learning algorithms: the local and global consistency (Zhou et al., 2003), the
063
               smoothing-based (Delalleau et al., 2005), and a novel normalized adjacency matrix-based
064            algorithm. We prove new O (log n) pseudo-dimension upper bounds for all three families,
065            where n is the number of graph nodes. Our proofs rely on a unified template based on
066            determinant evaluation and root-counting, which may be of independent interest.

073
             • We propose an architecture (GCAN) where a hyperparameter η is introduced to interpolate

076
             • We conducted experiments to empirically validate our theoretical findings and demonstrate

081   Graph Based Semi-supervised Learning Semi-supervised Learning is a popular machine learn-
082   ing paradigm with significant theoretical interest (Zhou et al., 2003; Delalleau et al., 2005; Garg
083   et al., 2020). Classical algorithms focus on label-propagation based techniques, such as Zhou
084   et al. (2003), Zhu et al. (2003), and many more. In recent years, graph neural networks (GNNs)
085   have become increasingly popular in a wide range of application domains (Kipf & Welling, 2017;
086
      Veličković, Petar et al., 2018; Iscen et al., 2019). A large number of different architectures have
      been proposed, including graph convolution networks, graph attention networks, message passing,
087
      and so on (Dwivedi et al., 2023). Both label propagation-based algorithms and neural network-based
088
      algorithms are useful in real life and perform equally well. For example, although GNN-based algo-
089   rithms are more predominant in applications, Huang et al. (2020) shows that modifications to label
090   propagation-based algorithms can outperform GNN. For node classification in GNN, many work
091   study generalization guarantees for tuning network weights in GNNs (Oono & Suzuki, 2021; Esser
092   et al., 2021; Tang & Liu, 2023). In contrast, we study the tuning of hyperparameters.
093
094   Hyperparameter Selection Hyper-parameters, such as the weight for self-loop, play important
095   roles in the performance of both classical methods and GNNs. In general, hyperparameter tuning is
096   performed on a validation dataset, and follows the same procedure: determine which hyperparame-
097   ters to tune and then search within their domain for the combination of parameter values with best
098   performance Yu & Zhu (2020). Many methods are proposed to efficiently search within the parame-
099
      ter space, such as grid search, random search Bergstra & Bengio (2012), and Bayesian optimization
      (Mockus (1974); Mockus et al. (1978); Jones et al. (1998)). A few existing works investigate the
100
      theoretical aspects of these methods, such as through generalization guarantees and complexities of
101
      the algorithms. In particular, Balcan et al. (2024) studies the regularization hyperparameter in Ridge
102   regression, LASSO, and ElasticNet in statistical settings and provides generalization guarantees. For
103   self-supervised learning, Balcan et al. (2019) propose a parameterized algorithm family of cluster-
104   ing algorithms and study the sample and computational complexity of learning the parameters. For
105   semi-supervised learning, a recent line of work (Balcan & Sharma (2021); Sharma & Jones (2023))
106   considers the problem of learning the best graph hyperparameter from a set of problem instances
107   drawn from a data distribution. However, no existing work theoretically studies the algorithm hy-
      perparameter in semi-supervised learning, or investigates deep semi-supervised learning algorithms

224   In this section, we consider three parametric families of label propagation-based algorithms, the
225   classical type of algorithms for semi-supervised learning. Label propagation algorithms output a
226   soft-label score F ∗ ∈ Rn×c , where the (i, j)-th entry of F ∗ represents the score of class j for the i-th
227   sample. The prediction for the i-th sample is the class with the highest score, i.e. arg maxj∈[c] Fij∗ .
228   Below we describe each family that we considered and their corresponding pseudo-dimension
229   bounds. Notably, the bounds for all three families of algorithms are Θ(log n), which implies the
230   existence of efficient algorithms with robust generalization guarantees in this setting.

272   We study the generalization behavior of the three families through pseudo-dimension. The following
273   theorems indicate that all three families have pseudo-dimension O(log n), where n is the number
274   of data in each problem instance. This result suggests that, all three families of algorithms require
275   m = O (log n/ϵ) problem instances to learn a ϵ-optimal algorithmic parameter. We also complement
276   our upper bounds with matching pseudo-dimension lower bound Ω(log n), which indicates that we
277   cannot always learn a near-optimal parameter if the number of problem instances is further reduced.
278   Theorem 3.1. The pseudo-dimension of the Local and Global Consistency Algorithmic Family, Fα ,
279   is P DIM(Hα ) = Θ(log n), where n is the total number of labeled and unlabeled data points.
280   Theorem 3.2. The pseudo-dimension of the Smoothing-Based Algorithmic Family, Fλ , is
281   P DIM(Hλ ) = Θ(log n), where n is the total number of labeled and unlabeled data points.

      Theorem 3.3. The pseudo-dimension of the Normalized Adjacency Matrix-Based Algorithmic Fam-

356
357
      In this section, we study hyperparameter selection for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf &
358
      Welling, 2017; Veličković, Petar et al., 2018; Iscen et al., 2019), which excel in tasks involving
359   graph-structured data like social networks, recommendation systems, and citation networks. To un-
360   derstand generalization in hyperparameter selection for GNNs, we analyze Rademacher complexity.
361
362
      To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to provide generalization guarantees for hyperpa-
      rameter selection. Prior work (Garg et al., 2020) focused on Rademacher complexity for graph
363
      classification with fixed hyperparameters, whereas we address node classification across multiple
364
      instances, optimizing hyperparameters.
365
366   In Section 4.1, we examine the Rademacher complexity bound of a basic Simplified Graph Convo-
367   lutional Network (Wu et al., 2019) family, as a foundation for the more complex family.
368   In Section 4.2, we introduce a novel architecture, which we call GCAN, that uses a hyperparameter
369   η ∈ [0, 1] to interpolate two popular GNNS: the graph convolutional neural networks (GCN) and
370   graph attention neural networks (GAT). GCAN selects the optimal model for specific datasets: η = 0
371   corresponds to GCN, η = 1 to GAT, and intermediate values explore hybrid architectures that may
372   outperform both. We also establish a Rademacher complexity bound for the GCAN family.
373   Our proofs for SGC and GCAN share a common strategy: modeling the 0-1 loss of each problem
374   instance as an aggregation of single-node losses, reducing the problem to bounding the Rademacher
375   complexity of computation trees for individual nodes. Specifically, we upper bound the 0-1 loss
376   with a margin loss, then relate the complexity of problem instances to the computation trees of
377   nodes. Using a covering argument, we bound the complexity of these trees by analyzing margin loss
      changes due to parameter variations.

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We study algorithm selection in graph-based semi-supervised learning, by tuning real-valued hyper-
527
      parameters that define algorithmic families. Our approach can improve the accuracy of prediction in
528   semi-supervised learning by selecting the most effective data-specific algorithmic hyperparameter
529   automatically. We do this by leveraging access to multiple instances of data from a given domain
530   and providing formal guarantees on the number of data samples needed to learn the best algorithm
531   for several classical parameterized families as well as a novel family that interpolates convolution
532   (GCN) and attention (GAT) in graph neural networks.
533
      Our work also opens up several interesting directions for future research. For the graph neural
534   network hyperparameter tuning, we only upper bound the Rademacher Complexity, and it would be
535   interesting to obtain lower bounds and determine the tightness of our bounds. We also expect our
536   techniques to be applicable to other GNN architectures and graph-based semi-supervised learning
537   families in the literature. Moreover, we only consider single real-valued hyperparameter in our work,
538   and it would be interesting to investigate the generalizability of learning multiple hyperparamerters
539   with our approach. Lastly, we limit ourselves on the sample complexity, it is an interesting question
      to develop effective and computationally efficient implementations for learning the hyperparameters.

       In Table 2, we show the mean accuracy across 30 runs of each η value and the 90% confidence
       interval associated with each experiment. It is interesting to note that for various datasets we see
1428
       varying optimal η values for best performance. More often than not, the best model is interpolated
1429
       between GCN and GAT, showing that we can achieve an improvement on both baselines simply by
1430   interpolating between the two. For example, GCN achieves the best accuracy among all interpola-
1431   tions in Citeseer, but in other datasets such as CIFAR 10 or Wisconsin, we see higher final accuracies
1432   when the η parameter is closer to 1.0 (more like GAT). The interpolation between the two points
1433   also does not increase or decrease monotonically for many of the datasets. The optimal η value for
1434   each dataset can be any value between 0.0 and 1.0. This suggests that one should be able to learn
1435   the best η parameter for each specific dataset. By learning the optimal η value, we can outperform
1436   both GAT and GCN.
