009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reasoning problems with deeply nested formal statements are challenging for
012            humans and machines alike. We investigate how next-token predictors learn such
013            structures, and whether they extrapolate to more deeply nested cases, within a
014            single inference pass. A case study of Boolean logic simplification demonstrates
015            that a specialized decoder Transformer seems to perform well when it overfits, but
016
               fails at extrapolating. To understand if this limitation is universal, we propose a
               theoretical grounding of memorization in a self-attention head. We apply this the-
017
               ory to a simpler problem: completion of a bounded stack of parentheses. From the
018
               theoretical construction we derive a closed-form model that perfectly fits a single
019            sequence training set. We prove that it also completes any out-of-sample paren-
020            theses prefix, regardless of the context depth. In contrast, we observe that decoder
021            Transformers trained with gradient descent on this task fail at depth extrapolation.
022            Gradient-trained decoders demand large samples and a high-dimensional embed-
023            ding space to achieve high accuracy on test sets nearly as deep as the training set.
024            However, when the gap between training and test depths widens, gradient-trained
025            models fail.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Decoder Transformers Radford et al. (2018) exhibit emerging reasoning capabilities (Wei et al., 2022a;
030
      Polu and Sutskever, 2020; Bubeck et al., 2023; Trinh et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024). Extrapolating
031   reasoning capabilities to tasks that are harder than already solved problems would unblock major
032   scientific breakthroughs. Measuring whether a machine can reason on problems of unbounded
033   difficulty raises fundamental questions about fair assessment of models’ performance. Measurements
034   of basic capabilities of neural networks has historically followed the methodology of evaluations on
035   independently and identically distributed unseen data (Vapnik, 1998). However, the internet-extracted
036   massive training sets cover most of human knowledge. Therefore, many reported evaluations of
037   generalization possibly use non-independent test data, raising questions about the validity of the
038   reported performances Mialon et al. (2023); Schaeffer et al. (2024). In a parallel stream of work,
039
      (Delétang et al., 2023; Kazemnejad et al., 2024) have studied Transformers’ extrapolation to larger
      context windows. To study extrapolation to harder-than-observed reasoning problems, we focus on
040
      decoder Transformers’ handling of nested structures (e.g. Boolean expressions), where we use depth
041
      as a measure of complexity. We ask: do decoder Transformers trained on data nested up to depth q
042   perform well on deeper test data?
043
044   In a motivating case study in Section 2, we experiment on Boolean expression simplification (Mc-
045
      Cluskey, 1956). The synthetic data we generate are minimal examples of reasoning with nested
      structures. We observe that general purpose Large Language Models (LLMs) struggle with such
046
      nested data as the test data gets deeper, especially if they use a single pass of generation. Our
047
      experiments on a controlled dataset suggest that memorization is a primary driver of performance:
048   in-sample performance far exceeds out-of-sample’s. But overfit models fail at depth extrapolation.
049   These findings raise multiple questions: is memorization the only mechanism to handle nested struc-
050   tures? We know that generalization in the standard sense is not in conflict with memorization (Belkin,
051   2021; Stephenson et al., 2021; Tirumala et al., 2022; Feldman, 2020), but what about extrapolation:
052   can a Transformer that fits the data perfectly also reliably extrapolate to more deeply nested problems?
053   The theoretical results presented in Section 3 pinpoint a regime where models perfectly fit the training
      data and are also stable in unseen data.

067   2     T RANSFORMERS SIMPLIFY NESTED B OOLEAN EXPRESSIONS WHEN THEY
068         OVERFIT. D O THEY ALSO EXTRAPOLATE ?
069
070   Simplifying entangled information into simpler logic is a critical capability for reasoning on complex
071   tasks. Simple expressions reconcile conflicts in complex logic. They are easier to understand and
072   verify by humans, and require lower effort for human or a machine to answer questions about the
073   underlying data. To simplify Boolean expressions, McCluskey (1956) relies on the truth table of the
074   expression, and performs searches in the exponentially large (in the variable count) stored table for
075   prime implicants. The more practical ‘simplifying inside-out’ method suffers exponential computation
076
      in the expression’s depth. State-of-the-art LLMs fail at this task when the expression’s depth is large
      in a single inference step. If multiple inferences are allowed, they articulate a chain-of-thought which
077
      follows the inside-out logic, but small errors often propagate throughout the chain, leading to incorrect
078
      conclusions. Here we examine the outcome of a single inference pass of a (pre-)trained decoder
079   Transformer on data of known and bounded depth. We compare in-sample versus out-of-sample
080   performance, when data is stratified by depth. We emphasize that our goal is not to build the best
081   inference logic to solve the problem efficiently. Our goal is to show-case depth extrapolation (or lack
082   thereof) of a next-token predictor.
083

474   5   D ISCUSSION
475
476   To study reasoning on harder than training set tasks we conducted a controlled experiment on
477
      simplifying Boolean expressions. We stratified the data by depth of expressions. Evaluations on
      deep expressions show that a specialized model can overfit the data but struggles at extrapolating
478
      to deeper nested expressions. We showed on a simpler problem (Dyck completion) that there exists
479
      Transformers capable of depth generalization. Yet, gradient-based pre-training seems to fail at
480   identifying them. Our findings raise further research questions. First, can the closed-form solution be
481   expanded and its generalization power discussed when it interpolates multiple sequences of bounded
482   lengths? Can it cover other bracket types, Boolean logic, propositional, fuzzy and temporal logic?
483   Second, the characterization of the more stable solutions which generalize in addition to interpolating
484   can hold keys to biasing gradient trained models to reach such solutions. Finally, if extrapolation in
485   a single pass appears to be out-of reach, how do we optimize cost of training models on larger and
      deeper synthetic training sets, versus calling inference routines multiple times?
