               Global warming from greenhouse gas emissions is humanity’s largest environ-
               mental hazard. Greenhouse gases, like CO2 emissions from transportation, no-
013
               tably cars, contribute to the greenhouse effect. Effective CO2 emission monitoring
014
               is needed to regulate vehicle emissions. Few studies have predicted automobile
015            CO2 emissions using OBD port data. For precise and effective prediction, the
016            system must capture the underlying cause-effect structure between vehicular pa-
017            rameters that may contribute to the emission of CO2 in the transportation sector.
018            Thus, we present a causal RNN-based generative deep learning architecture that
019            predicts vehicle CO2 emissions using OBD-II data while keeping the underlying
020            causal structure. Most widely used real-life datasets lack causal relationships be-
021            tween features or components, so we use our proposed architecture to discover and
022            learn the underlying causal structure as an adjacency matrix during training and
023
               employ that during forecasting. Our framework learns a sparse adjacency matrix
               by imposing a sparsity-encouraging penalty on model weights and allowing some
024
               weights to be zero. This matrix is capable of capturing the causal relationships
025
               between all variable pairs. In this work, we first train the model with widely used
026            synthetic datasets with known causal structure among variables, then we apply it
027            to the state-of-the-art OBD-II dataset to find the internal causal structure among
028            the vehicular parameters and perform causal inference to predict CO2 emission.
029            Experimental results reveal that our causal discovery and forecasting method sur-
030            passes state-of-the-art methods for the tasks of causal discovery in terms of AU-
031            ROC, forecasting on multivariate causal time series data, and OBD-II dataset in
032            terms of MMD, RMSE, and MAE. After successful completion, we will release
033            the code (Code for review - https://anonymous.4open.science/r/causal-obd-co2-

               0A0C).

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   In real-world scenarios, multivariate time series data used are generally ubiquitous in nature, for
040   example, electroencephalogram signals (Isaksson et al., 1981), climate records (Runge et al., 2019),
041   stellar light curves in astronomy (Huijse et al., 2012), stock prices from the stock market (Zhang
042   et al., 2017) and so on. Common data-driven decision making tasks for time series data encompass
043   discovering anomalies, forecasting, classification, and so on. One of the most prevalent methods in
044   forecasting or predicting future values is time series forecasting, which utilizes observations from
045
      the past (Li et al., 2023). In the past few years, temporal forecasting models have been applied in
      different applications of vehicular technology in the transportation sector. One such application is
046
      the emission monitoring and prediction of carbon dioxide (CO2 ) and other greenhouse gases such
047
      as nitrous oxide and methane (Melo et al., 2022; Bai & Sun, 2024). Carbon dioxide (CO2 ) is the
048   primary driver of the greenhouse effect, and it is the main emission produced by the electricity,
049   transportation, and manufacturing industries. Among which, transportation has long been a signif-
050   icant factor in the generation of CO2 emissions (Yoro & Daramola, 2020). An effective emission
051   monitoring system is necessary to regulate and restrict emissions. Monitoring vehicular emissions
052   is challenging due to the vast quantity and diverse range of vehicles. Furthermore, conducting ad-
053   ditional individual testing on such a vast number of vehicles is impractical. Previous works (Zeng
      et al., 2016; Grote et al., 2018; Oduro et al., 2013; Zeng et al., 2015) have demonstrated that a


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      data-driven model is effective for estimating CO2 levels. However, these models lack the ability to
055   deduce hidden characteristics that are not directly seen from the available data.
056
057   An effective generative forecasting model for time series should be capable of modeling both the
058
      transition model p(xt |x1:t−1 ) and the joint distribution p(x1:t ) for each given t. While popular pre-
      dictive frameworks like kernel adaptive filters (KAF) (Liu et al., 2008), deep state-space models
059
      (SSMs) (Rangapuram et al., 2018) and the fundamental autoregressive integrated moving average
060
      (ARIMA) offer various approaches to capture p(xt |x1:t−1 ) or p(xt |xt−τ :t−1 ) within a window of
061   length τ , it is important to note that these models are deterministic mappers and not generative. Put
062   simply, these models cannot produce new values for time series by randomly selecting from a man-
063   ageable hidden distribution. In recent times, there has been a growing interest in finding causality
064   from time series data. For instance, in the fMRI data, it is crucial to determine the causal domi-
065   nations between activated areas of the brain (Deshpande et al., 2009). The causal graph presented
066   in this study may potentially offer valuable insights into the diagnosis of psychological illnesses
067   based on brain network analysis (Wang et al., 2020). Unlike purely statistical correlations, causality
068   provides a deeper understanding of how variables influence one another over time. In the context
069
      of vehicular CO2 emissions, understanding that “engine load” causes “CO2 emissions” allows us to
      identify and prioritize variables for emission reduction interventions. This is particularly crucial in
070
      vehicular systems, where spurious correlations may lead to suboptimal decision-making.
071
072   Therefore, the need for an efficient data-driven CO2 emission prediction system from the envi-
073   ronmental perspective, as well as the need for a dependable generative forecasting model and the
074   contemporary inclination towards causal inference, is inevitable, especially for temporal data. Un-
075
      derstanding causal links between different vehicular parameters (e.g., engine load → CO2 emis-
      sions) enables identification of actionable factors contributing to emissions, thereby aiding decision-
076
      making for emission control strategies. Through this work, we propose a novel temporal causal dis-
077
      covery and generative forecasting framework, which itself is responsible for finding the underlying
078   causal structure among different pairs of variables, learning them in the form of an adjacency matrix,
079   and employing the knowledge during inference. To the best of our knowledge, our proposed work
080   is the first endeavor that uses a recurrent variational autoencoder (VAE), consisting of a single-head
081   encoder and multihead decoder, which integrates temporal causal discovery and generative fore-
082   casting of vehicular CO2 emissions, a unique combination not addressed in recent state-of-the-art
083   works. Formally, given an instance from a M-dimensional time series xt = {xdt | ∀d ∈ [1, M ]}, our
084   framework comprises a recurrent encoder and a multi-head recurrent decoder, where each head is
085   dedicated for generating a certain dimension of xt (i.e., xdt , whered ∈ [1, M ]). In the decoder, we
086
      apply a sparsity penalty to the input to the hidden state weight matrix. This penalty encourages the
      model to come up with a matrix A ∈ RM ×M that represents the underlying Granger causal relations
087
      between the dimensions x in a sparse manner. In addition, we present an error-compensation module
088
      that considers the immediate impact ϵt of one process without considering its history.
089
090   We conduct two-stage experiments. At first, we perform experiments with synthetic sequences.
091   Regarding the forecasting of time series, we assess the similarity between the distribution of past
092
      observations and the distribution of predicted data using both qualitative and quantitative methods.
      Regarding causal discovery, we evaluate our identified causal network by comparing it to state-of-
093
      the-art methods that also seek to detect causality. In the next phase, we apply our model on the
094
      widely used open-source OBD-II dataset (Rettore et al., 2016; 2018) to discover the causal structure
095   and enhance the prediction of CO2 emissions from vehicles in real-time. As compared to other
096   recent state-of-the-art techniques, our model has outperformed in all the tasks.

138   2.2   C AUSAL D ISCOVERY OF T IME S ERIES
139
140   Various causal graphs may be explored for time series analysis (Assaad et al., 2022). In this context,
141   we examine the process of recovering a Granger causal graph. This graph distinguishes between
142   previous observations and current values of each variable and seeks to identify all potential causal
143   relationships from the past to the present. Recent works have shown notable advancements in causal
144   discovery and inference, following the principles of Granger causality (Granger, 1969).
145   The concept of ‘causation’ in time series was first introduced by Wiener (1956) in which, a time
146   series or variable x is said to be the cause of another variable y if, from an analytical point of
147   view, the value of y is enhanced by adding knowledge about x. However, Granger (1969) defined
148   causality within the framework of linear multivariate auto-regression (MVAR) by contrasting the
149   variances of the residual errors when x is included vs when it is excluded in the prediction of y.
150   Later, the fundamental concept of Granger causality was introduced for non-linear scenarios by
151
      the use of the kernel technique (Marinazzo et al., 2008) and through the fitting of locally linear
      models in the reconstructed phase space (Chen et al., 2004). Recently, the first work leveraging
152
      deep neural networks to identify Granger causality has been carried out by (Tank et al., 2021), in
153
      which the causal graph is learned by the implementation of sparsity restrictions on the autoregressive
154   network weights. The Temporal Causal Discovery Framework (Nauta et al., 2019) employs an
155   attention mechanism that is embedded inside dilated depthwise convolutional networks in order to
156   learn complicated non-linear causal links and, in certain instances, hidden confounders.

206
      during inference to ensure that each variable in the generated instance should have a potential effect

524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      The study proposes a temporal causal recurrent framework that incorporates the principles of
527
      Granger causality from the data during training and incorporates the knowledge during forecast-
528   ing. We have also shown how this model can be used for CO2 emission prediction for vehicles using
529   the open source real-life OBD-II dataset. We have performed experiments using two causal syn-
530   thetic dynamic systems, namely Hénon and Lorenz-96, widely used for temporal causal discovery
531   research. Our model has shown the lowest maximum mean discrepancy value for Hénon, the second
532   lowest value for Lorenz-96 and outperformed all the chosen baselines in terms of prediction root
533   mean square error. Furthermore, we trained all the baselines on OBD-II dataset and compared their
534   forecasting performance with the proposed model. Again, we have achieved lowest value of root
535   mean square error and mean average error.
536   Future temporal Granger causal discovery research should include unmeasured confounders affect-
537   ing known variables. Traffic and road conditions affect OBD sensor readings and the model’s CO2
538   emission predictions, subject to vehicle environment changes. Thus, to better understand predic-
539   tion algorithm outcomes from vehicular data, the recommended technique must be evaluated across
      different vehicle types and conditions.

      D.1   C AUSAL D ISCOVERY
865
866   For the task of causal discovery, we have chosen four popular methods : Kernel Granger Causality
867   (KGC) (Marinazzo et al., 2008), which utilizes the kernel trick to extend Granger causality from
868   linear to non-linear scenarios; Transfer Entropy (TE) (Schreiber, 2000), estimated using the matrix-
869   based Rényi’s α-order entropy functional (Giraldo et al., 2014); Temporal Causal Discovery Frame-
870   work (TCDF) (Nauta et al., 2019) incorporates an attention mechanism into a neural network. KGC
871   and TE utilize information-theoretic measures, that is, focusing on independence or conditional in-
872   dependence, and employ postprocessing techniques such as hypothesis testing. On the other hand,
873   TCDF and our proposed approach are neural network-based methods that actively and automatically
874
      extract causal relationships during the learning process.
875   The quantitative comparison has been done in terms of AUROC, which has been shown in the
876   first part of Table 4. Figure 7 represents a qualitative comparison on Hénon maps in terms of the
877   estimated causal adjacency matrix. It is to be noted that Neural network-based methods significantly
878   outperform classical methods KGC and TE. This is because conventional methods lack the ability
879   to identify self-cause.

950   E     IMPLEMENTATION SETUP
951
952   The whole system has been implemented in PyTorch v2.4.0 with support of CUDA v12.5 and trained
953   on a single NVIDIA V100 GPU with 32GB VRAM. We have chosen single-layer LSTM (Schmid-
954   huber et al., 1997) for implementing the main causal recurrent model and the error compensation
955   model. We have set the value of λ in PGD as 0.1 and kept the default values of β1 and β2 in Adam,
956   which are 0.9 and 0.999, respectively. The maximum number of epochs is 2,000 and the learn-
957   ing rate γ has been set at 0.03. Additionally, we have incorporated Early stopping regularization
958   based on the validation loss, which stops the training if the validation loss does not decrease for 200
959   consecutive epochs.
