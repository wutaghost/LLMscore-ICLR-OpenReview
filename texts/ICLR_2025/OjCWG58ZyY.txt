009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Goal-conditioned reinforcement learning often employs a technique known as
012               Hindsight Experience Replay (HER) for data augmentation by relabeling goals.
013               However, HER limits goal relabeling to a single trajectory, which hinders the
014               utilization of experiences from diverse trajectories. To address this issue, we
015               present a curriculum learning method to construct virtual experiences, incorpo-
016               rating actual state transitions and virtual goals selected from the replay buffer.
017               Considering that virtual experiences may contain a lot of noise, we also propose a
018
                  self-supervised subgoal planning method that guides the learning of virtual expe-
                  riences by imitating the subgoal-conditioned policy. Our intuition is that achiev-
019
                  ing a virtual goal may be challenging for the goal-conditioned policy, whereas
020
                  simplified subgoals can provide effective guidance. We empirically show that
021               the virtual experiences from diverse historical trajectories significantly boost the
022               sample-efficiency compared to the existing goal-conditioned reinforcement learn-
023               ing and hierarchical reinforcement learning methods, even enabling the agent to
024               learn tasks it has never experienced.1

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) is an advanced form of deep reinforcement learn-
030   ing (RL) that aims to find an optimal solution for tasks involving multiple goals simultaneously and
031   requiring long-term decision-making. For instance, it can effectively guide a multi-legged robot to
032   a predetermined location on a map (Paul et al., 2019) or skillfully manipulate a robotic arm to grasp
033   objects on a platform (Zhang et al., 2020). Unlike many existing methods that necessitate intricate
034
      reward functions tailored to each task (Ng et al., 1999; Brys et al., 2014; Devidze et al., 2022),
      GCRL simplifies the problem’s complexity by relying solely on binary reward signals that indicate
035
      goal achievement (Nair et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2022). Nevertheless, binary rewards introduce the
036
      challenge of reward sparsity.
037
038   Humans possess the ability to synthesize past experiences and utilize this knowledge to efficiently
039   adapt to new tasks. Building upon this concept, HER (Andrychowicz et al., 2017) introduces a
040
      method to reconstruct data from unsuccessful past experiences, partially mitigates the challenge of
      sparse rewards by selecting the actual reached states in the trajectory as the relabeled goals. How-
041
      ever, HER requires limiting the relabeled goals and state transitions within a single task trajectory,
042
      which is not conducive to integrating experience from different tasks. For example, once the skills
043   of ”grabbing apples” and ”opening a drawer” are mastered, there is no need to relearn them from
044   scratch when performing the task of ”putting apples into a drawer”; instead, these skills can be
045   combined.
046
      As demonstrated in HER, merely broadening the selection scope of relabeled goals to the entire
047
      replay buffer yields no benefit. An intuitive explanation for this is that, without real trajectory sup-
048
      port, the connection between actual state transitions and virtual goals remains tenuous, injecting
049   substantial noise into the reconstructed data. During policy learning, such data not only fail to pro-
050   vide valuable experience but also undermine learning stability. To address this issue, we introduce
051   curriculum learning when selecting virtual goals and use subgoals to simplify these goals. Inspired
052   by prior work (Pitis et al., 2020), we propose a method for selecting virtual goals based on task

090

091

092   et al., 2012). The experimental results indicate that the proper use of virtual experience can signifi-
093   cantly accelerate the learning of multiple goals. Moreover, when combined with subgoal planning,
094   it provides valuable guidance for achieving complex long-term goals. This approach can even assist
095   agents in accomplishing tasks they have never encountered before.

      2   R ELATED WORK
099
      Goal relabeling. HER demonstrates that off-policy deep RL algorithms can reuse data in the replay
100
      buffer by relabeling goals in episodic trajectories. It is widely utilized as a core component in
101
      many related studies (Levy et al., 2017; Ren et al., 2019; Pitis et al., 2020; Durugkar et al., 2021;
102   Kim et al., 2023). A growing consensus suggests that HER can be viewed as implicit curriculum
103   learning, structuring curricula within historical trajectories by using transition data. CHER (Fang
104   et al., 2019) optimizes the curriculum by considering the similarity and diversity of relabeled goals,
105   but still does not overcome the limitations of a single trajectory. (Kuang et al., 2020) and (Pong
106   et al., 2019; Pitis et al., 2020) also use the density estimation model to aid in goal selection. By
107   prioritizing low-density goals based on the density estimation of the achieved goals, they increase
      the likelihood of these goals becoming candidates, thus facilitating curriculum learning.

108
      These approaches are similar to ours, but they assume that, under the premise of a fixed initial
109   state, a visited state is regarded as the achieved goal. However, when the initial state distribution
110   encompasses the entire state space, a visited state cannot reliably indicate that the goal has been
111   achieved, as a complete task is defined by both the initial state and the goal (see Figure 1 (a)). Our
112   idea is to estimate the task probability density to track the learning progress of the agent, which is
113   also applicable to the case of a fixed initial state.
114
      Subgoal planning in GCRL. The curriculum learning mentioned above can be regarded as plan-
115   ning within exploration or learning process. Planning is also a crucial concept in hierarchical RL.
116   When confronted with a long-horizon problem, high-level policies plan with temporally extended
117   actions, known as abstract actions, directing the low-level policies to execute primitive actions to
118   complete the interaction (Bacon et al., 2017; Park et al., 2024). In the context of GCRL, these ab-
119   stract actions can be considered as subgoals employed as targets for the low-level policies. Current
120   approaches to subgoal planning can primarily be categorized into learning-based and search-based
121   methods. (Badrinath et al., 2024) treats the midpoint of the path as a subgoal in the context of
122   offline RL and trains a subgoal prediction module using a transformer. (Jurgenson et al., 2020) es-
123
      tablishes a connection between subgoal planning and the dynamic programming equation, which
      inherently benefits navigation and obstacle avoidance tasks. However, extending this approach to
124
      high-dimensional goal spaces remains challenging. These learning-based methods generally require
125
      only a single forward inference to accomplish subgoal planning, making them more efficient than
126   search-based methods. But they also encounter challenges of learning. In contrast, VE can accu-
127   rately predict subgoals based only on sampled self-supervised signals. Additionally, it is a common
128   practice to apply search-based subgoal planning in graphs (Huang et al., 2019; Eysenbach et al.,
129   2019; Hoang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; 2023). Specifically, these methods
130   construct a graph where nodes and edges correspond to states and inter-state distances respectively,
131   and various historical experiences are linked together by the graph. The purpose of planning is to
132   find the shortest path consisting of nodes to reach the goal, where the nodes can be regarded as the
133   subgoals. To apply graph-based planning to complex environments, some work focuses on learning
134
      robust representations that cover the entire state space. Under the constraint of a limited number of
      nodes, (Hoang et al., 2021) measures the similarity between subsequent features to ensure that the
135
      nodes cover as large an area as possible. (Zhang et al., 2021) learns a latent space and obtains graph
136
      nodes through clustering. The adjacent nodes reflect the temporal reachability. Others consider
137   combining traditional path planning methods, such as farthest point sampling (Huang et al., 2019),
138   to find shortest paths based on the graph (Kim et al., 2021; 2023).
139
140
      Although graphs reducing the complexity of planning compared to state space, they still face chal-
      lenges related to computational complexity and suboptimality. As PIG (Kim et al., 2023) demon-
141
      strates, searching for shortest paths for tasks in each batch of training data significantly increases
142
      computational overhead, limiting the batch size to a small value. Moreover, a finite number of nodes
143   cannot plan optimal paths for all tasks. In contrast, VE is not subject to this limitation. It’s similar in
144   form to the high-level policy in hierarchical RL, but it does not control agent exploration; it is solely
145   used to guide policy learning.

202   4     L EARNING GOAL - CONDITIONED POLICY WITH VIRTUAL EXPERIENCES
203
204   In this section, we introduce a new framework named VE, which consists of three main parts: (1)
205   filtering relabeled goals by task probability density for building a step-by-step curriculum; (2) a
206   subgoal planning method based on self-supervised learning for simplifying the relabeled goals; and
207   (3) taking subgoal-conditioned policies as imitation targets to accelerate policy improvement. We
208   provide an illustration of our framework base on AntMaze-U in Figure 2.
209
210   4.1    R ELABELING GOALS TO CONSTRUCT VIRTUAL EXPERIENCE
211

      goal can be used for relabeling when reconstructing data, such as the goal sampled from replay
213
      buffer or designed by a human. We term this approach generalized goal relabeling and present it as
214
      a more generalized form compared to Formula (4):

      In this paper, we discuss the relabeled goal g j sampled from the states of replay buffer. If j = i,
217   it is equivalent to HER, and we refer to these goals as actual goals. Otherwise, if j ̸= i, these are
218   termed virtual goals. Intuitively, a curriculum that gradually increases in difficulty can help an agent
219   complete tasks faster. As shown in the Figure 2 (Left), given a state s, we hope that the virtual goal
220   g in the virtual experience is on the boundary of the reachable state from s. In order to effectively
221   evaluate the reachability of the task (s, g), we propose estimating the task probability density e in
222   curriculum, acting as a filter to screen suitable virtual goals from random sampling.
223   Specifically, we establish a dedicated buffer Bl to record the learned data. Inspired by previous
224   work (Pitis et al., 2020), we approximate the learning frequency of tasks during training as their
225   probability density. When screening the virtual goal for a state s, our initial step is to sample a
226   set of states from the replay buffer B which serve as candidate virtual goals g. Subsequently, we
227   compute their task probability density e(s, g) and select one that satisfies conditions (e.g. 0.8ē <
228   e(s, g) < 1.2ē, with ē denoting the mean) as the virtual goal. It is crucial to note that our method of
229   employing a fixed range to filter out virtual goals on the boundary is empirical and performs well in
230   simple environments (see Figure 5). This process will benefit from future improvements. For details
231
      on the learning process of task probability densities, please refer to Appendix A.
232   A significant advantage of virtual experience is that it broadens the distribution of tasks in the re-
233   constructed data, particularly for tasks that are difficult to achieve through traditional exploration.
234   Learning these tasks through virtual experience, however, is inherently challenging. Firstly, from a
235   policy perspective, there is a lack of connection between the original state transitions (sit , ait , sit+1 )
236   and the relabeled goals g j . This reduces the accuracy of policy evaluation. Secondly, policy im-
237   provements based on virtual experience are also inefficient. So we simplify the virtual goal through
238   the subgoal planning method in Section 4.2.

472
473
      Limitations and Future Work. While VE has shown promising results, it is essential to acknowl-
474   edge certain limitations. For instance, the hyperparameter k is related to the task’s scale. For more
475   complex environments, accurate subgoals with different decision horizons can help further improve
476   the quality of subgoal-conditioned policies. At the same time, complex environments also introduce
477   challenges in estimating task probability density. Future research can consider applying subgoals
478   to both agent exploration and policy learning. Moreover, filtering low-quality subgoal-conditioned
479   policies or considering safety constraints in subgoal planning (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015; Gu et al.,
480   2022) represents potential directions for future investigation.
481   We propose a goal-conditioned reinforcement learning method, VE, that integrates knowledge from
482   different historical tasks by constructing virtual experiences through a curriculum. We highlight that
483   simply using virtual experiences does not effectively aid policy learning, whereas the self-supervised
484   subgoal planning we propose significantly reduces the difficulty of achieving virtual goals. By
485   imitating subgoal-conditioned policies, the agent is expected to overcome the exploration dilemma
      and accomplish tasks that require complex long-term decisions.

651   In order to track the agent’s progress in learning different tasks, we approximate the training fre-
652   quency of the task as the task probability density e. This is based on the simple intuition that the
653
      more frequently a task is trained, the higher its probability of being sampled. Given an initial state,
      we then select one from candidate virtual goals that aligns with the current learning progress frontier
654
      to guide the learning process. Specifically, we compare two commonly used probability density esti-
655
      mation methods, KDE (Kim & Scott, 2012) and Flow (Papamakarios et al., 2021). Our experiments
656   found that the Flow model had better performance in high-dimensional space. At the same time, the
657   Flow model based on RealNVP (Dinh et al., 2016) was more efficient in the calculation, and its time
658   consumption was significantly reduced compared to the KDE method. Therefore, we implemented
659   the Flow model based on RealNVP to estimate task probability density. We designate a dedicated
660   replay buffer Bl for recording recent training data. The Flow model is updated in parallel with the
661   policy learning process to effectively track learning progress.

      E     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
865
866   Determining an appropriate goal for the agent remains an ongoing challenge. (Florensa et al., 2018)
867   define a reward to quantify the agent’s proficiency during the testing phase. This reward, informed
868   by the most recent test results, serves as a measure throughout the training process to identify areas
869   where the agent excels or needs improvement. This evaluative information is then used to set goals
870   for the subsequent phase. As the agent’s capabilities improve, the goals gradually become more
871   challenging until the desired objectives are achieved. (Hu et al., 2023) introduce PEG, an approach
872   based on a world model that uses the Go-Explore framework to facilitate exploration. To reduce
873   the computational burden associated with evaluating the agent’s capabilities, PEG extrapolates the
874
      process within the world model instead of directly interacting with the real environment. Skew-Fit
      (Pong et al., 2019) samples states from a replay buffer and assigns more weight to rare states. It
875
      then trains a generative model with these weighted samples. By sampling new states with goals
876
      proposed from this generative model, a higher entropy state distribution is obtained in the next
877   iteration. (Nair & Finn, 2019) train a subgoal generative model and a transfer prediction model.
878   For a given goal, a series of subgoals are generated from the subgoal generative model, and the
879   transfer prediction model is used to plan a sequence of actions to achieve these subgoals. In contrast,
880   our work focuses on learning strategies from virtual experiences. Our method can be enhanced
881   by selecting suitable virtual goals through curriculum learning or alternative approaches, and by
882   utilizing improved subgoal planning techniques.

      The choice of task probability density estimation model mainly considers computational efficiency
912   and support for high-dimensional data (e.g., the state space in AntMaze has 31 dimensions). We
913   compare the results of the kernel density estimation (KDE) (Rosenblatt, 1956) and Flow (Papa-
914   makarios et al., 2021), and found that the KDE method only supports low-dimensional samples
915   (e.g., x and y coordinates), so we finally adopt the Flow model based on RealNVP (Dinh et al.,

917   goals need to be processed, the calculation speed of the Flow model is significantly better than that
      of the KDE model.
