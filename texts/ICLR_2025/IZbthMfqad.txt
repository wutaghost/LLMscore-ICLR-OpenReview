009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose deep Koopman-layered models with learnable parameters in the form
012            of Toeplitz matrices for analyzing the dynamics of time-series data. The proposed
013            model has both theoretical solidness and flexibility. By virtue of the universal
014            property of Toeplitz matrices and the reproducing property underlined in the model,
015            we can show its universality and the generalization property. In addition, the
016
               flexibility of the proposed model enables the model to fit time-series data coming
               from nonautonomous dynamical systems. When training the model, we apply
017
               Krylov subspace methods for efficient computations. In addition, the proposed
018
               model can be regarded as a neural ODE-based model. In this sense, the proposed
019            model establishes a new connection among Koopman operators, neural ODEs, and
020            numerical linear algebraic methods.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Koopman operator has been one of the important tools in machine learning (Kawahara, 2016;
026   Ishikawa et al., 2018; Lusch et al., 2017; Brunton & Kutz, 2019; Hashimoto et al., 2020). Koopman
027   operators are linear operators that describe the composition of functions and are applied to analyzing
028
      time-series data generated by nonlinear dynamical systems (Koopman, 1931; Budišić et al., 2012;
      Klus et al., 2020; Giannakis & Das, 2020; Mezić, 2022). For systems with discrete Koopman spectra,
029
      by computing the eigenvalues of Koopman operators, we can understand the long-term behavior
030
      of the undelined dynamical systems. An important feature of Applying Koopman operators is that
031   we can estimate them with given time-series data through fundamental linear algebraic tools such
032   as projection. A typical approach to estimate Koopman operators is extended dynamical mode
033   decomposition (EDMD) (Williams et al., 2015). For EDMD, we need to choose the dictionary
034   functions to determine the representation space of the Koopman operator, and what choice of them
035   gives us a better estimation is far from trivial. In addition, since we construct the estimation in an
036   analytical way, the model is not flexible enough to incorporate additional information about dynamical
037   systems. With EDMD as a starting point, many DMD-based methods are proposed (Kawahara, 2016;
038   Colbrook & Townsend, 2024; Schmid, 2022). For autonomous systems, we need to estimate a
039
      single Koopman operator. In this case, Ishikawa et al. (2024) proposed to choose derivatives of
      kernel functions as dictionary functions based on the theory of Jet spaces. Several works deal with
040
      nonautonomous systems. Maćešić et al. (2018) applied EDMD to estimate a time-dependent Koopman
041
      operator for each time window. Peitz & Klus (2019) applied EDMD for switching dynamical systems
042   for solving optimal control problems. However, as far as we know, no existing works show proper
043   choices of dictionary functions for nonautonomous systems based on theoretical analysis. In addition,
044   in the above approaches for nonautonomous systems, since each Koopman operator for a time window
045   is estimated individually, we cannot take the information of other Koopman operators into account.
046
      To find a proper representation space and gain the flexibility of the model, neural network-based
047
      Koopman methods have been proposed (Lusch et al., 2017; Azencot et al., 2020; Shi & Meng,
048   2022). These methods set the encoder from the data space to the representation space where the
049   Koopman operator is defined, and the decoder from the representation space to the data space,
050   as deep neural networks. Then, we train them. Neural network-based Koopman methods for
051   nonautonomous systems have also been proposed. Liu et al. (2023) proposed to decompose the
052   Koopman operator into a time-invariant part and a time-variant part. The time-variant part of the
053   Koopman operator is constructed individually for each time window using EDMD. Xiong et al.
      (2024) assumed the ergodicity of the dynamical system and considered time-averaged Koopman

054
      operators for nonautonomous dynamical systems. However, their theoretical properties have not been
055   fully understood, and since the representation space changes as the learning process proceeds, their
056   theoretical analysis is challenging.
057
058
      In this work, we propose a framework that estimates multiple Koopman operators over time with the
      Fourier basis representation space and learnable Toeplitz matrices. Using our framework, we can
059
      estimate multiple Koopman operators simultaneously and can capture the transition of properties of
060
      data along time via multiple Koopman operators. We call each Koopman operator the Koopman-layer,
061   and the whole model the deep Koopman-layered model. The proposed model has both theoretical
062   solidness and flexibility. We show that the Fourier basis is a proper basis for constructing the
063   representation space even for nonautonomous dynamical systems in the sense that we can show its
064   theoretical properties such as universality and generalization bound. In addition, the proposed model
065   has learnable parameters, which makes the model more flexible to fit nonautonomous dynamical
066   systems than the analytical methods such as EDMD. The proposed model resolves the issue of
067   theoretical analysis for the neutral network-based methods and that of the flexibility for the analytical
068   methods simultaneously.
069   We show that each Koopman operator is represented by the exponential of a matrix constructed with
070   Toeplitz matrices and diagonal matrices. This allows us to apply Krylov subspace methods (Gallopou-
071   los & Saad, 1992; Güttel, 2013; Hashimoto & Nodera, 2016) to compute the estimation of Koopman
072   operators with low computational costs. By virtue of the universal property of Toeplitz matrices (Ye
073   & Lim, 2016), we can show the universality of the proposed model with a linear algebraic approach.
074   We also show a generalization bound of the proposed model using a reproducing kernel Hilbert
075
      space (RKHS) associated with the Fourier functions. We can analyze both the universality and
      generalization error with the same framework.
076
077   The proposed model can also be regarded as a neural ODE-based model (Chen et al., 2018; Teshima
078   et al., 2020a; Li et al., 2023). While in the existing method, we train the models with numerical
079   analysis approaches, in the proposed method, we train the models with a numerical linear algebraic
080   approach. The universality and generalization results of the proposed model can also be seen as those
081
      for the neural ODE-based models. Our method sheds light on a new linear algebraic approach to the
      design of neural ODEs.
082
083   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
084   • We propose a model for analyzing nonautonomous dynamical systems that has both theoretical
085     solidness and flexibility. We show that the Fourier basis provides us with a proper representation
086     space, in the sense that we can show the universality and the generalization bound regarding the
087
        model. As for the flexibility, we can learn multiple Koopman operators simultaneously, which
        enables us to extract the transition of properties of dynamical systems along time.
088
      • We apply Krylov subspace methods to compute the estimation of Koopman operators. This
089
        establishes a new connection between Koopman operator theoretic approaches and Krylov subspace
090     methods, which opens up future directions for extracting further information about dynamical
091     systems using numerical linear algebraic approaches.
092   • We provide a new implementation method for neural ODEs purely with numerical linear algebraic
093     approaches, not with numerical analysis approaches.

515   In the framework of neural network-based Koopman approaches, we train an encoder ϕ and a decoder
516   ψ that minimizes ∥xt+1 − ψ(Kϕ(xt ))∥ for the given time-series x0 , x1 , . . . (Lusch et al., 2017; Li

519
      of neural network-based Koopman approaches for incorporating the knowledge of dynamics have
      also been proposed (Liu et al., 2024). For neural network-based Koopman approaches, since the
520
      encoder ϕ changes along the learning process, the representation space of the operator K also
521
      changes. Thus, the theoretical analysis of these approaches is challenging. On the other hand, our
522   deep Koopman-layered approach fixes the representation space using the Fourier functions and learns
523   only the linear operators corresponding to Koopman generators by restricting the linear operator to a
524   form based on the Koopman operator.

527   8     C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
528
529   In this paper, we proposed deep Koopman-layered models based on the Koopman operator theory
530   combined with Fourier functions and Toeplitz matrices. We showed that the Fourier basis forms a
531   proper representation space of the Koopman operators in the sense of the universal and generalization
532   property of the model. In addition to the theoretical solidness, the flexibility of the proposed model
533   allows us to train the model to fit time-series data coming from nonautonomous dynamical systems.
534   According to Lemma 4.7 and Theorem 4.1, to represent any function, we need more than one
535   Koopman layer. Investigating how many layers we need and how the representation power grows
536   as the number of layers increases theoretically remains for future work. In addition, we applied
537   Krylov subspace methods to approximate the actions of the Koopman operators to vectors. Since the
538   Krylov subspace methods are iterative methods, we can control the accuracy of the approximation
539   by controlling the iteration number. How to decide and change the iteration number throughout the
      learning process for more efficient computations is also future work.

      B.2    G ENERALIZATION OF Td TO MORE GENERAL DOMAIN
820
821   Indeed, Td is the simplest example of locally compact groups, and the Fourier functions are general-
822   ized to the irreducible representations (Fulton & Harris, 2004). For a group G, a representation ρ
823   is a map ρ : G → GL(V ) for some vector space V that satisfies ρ(x)ρ(y) = ρ(x · y) for x, y ∈ G.
824   Here, GL(V ) is the space of all bijective linear transformations from V to V . We note that if G is
825   abelian, then V is always one-dimensional. For example, for Rd , the irreducible representations are
826   ρξ (x) := eiξ·x for x ∈ Rd and ξ ∈ Rd . As we will see in Section 3.2, a crucial property of the Fourier
827
      function qn is the product of two Fourier functions are also a Fourier function, i.e., qn · qm = qm+n .
      This property is valid also for general irreducible representations since we have ρ(x)ρ(y) = ρ(x · y).
828
      Thus, we can consider a generalized version of Toeplitz matrices by using ρ (Rieffel, 2004). Although
829
      showing the universal property of the generalized Toeplitz matrices is challenging and future work,
830   this type of argument gives us a promising way of generalizing our framework.

1003   G     D ETERMINING AN OPTIMAL NUMBER J OF LAYERS
1004
1005   Although providing thorough discussion of determining an optimal number J of layers is future work,
1006   we provide examples of heuristic approaches to determining J. Heuristically, we can use validation
1007
       data to determine an optimal number of layers. For example, we begin by one layer and compute the
       validation loss. Then, we set two layers and compute the validation loss, and continue with more
1008
       layers. We can set the number of layers as the number that achieves the minimal validation loss.
1009
       Another way is to set a sufficiently large number of layers and train the model with the validation
1010   data. As we discussed in Section 6.2, we can add a regularization term to the loss function so that the
1011   Koopman layers next to each other become close. After the training, if there are Koopman layers next
1012   to each other and sufficiently close, then we can regard them as one Koopman layer and determine an
1013   optimal number of layers.
