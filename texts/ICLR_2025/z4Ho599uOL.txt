009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Job Shop Scheduling Problem (JSSP) presents a significant challenge in opti-
012            mizing production processes. This problem requires efficient allocation of jobs to
013            a limited number of machines while minimizing total processing time (makespan).
014            Although recent advancements in artificial intelligence have produced promising
015            solutions, such as reinforcement learning and graph neural networks, this paper
016            investigates the potential of Large Language Models (LLMs) for addressing JSSP.
017            We introduce the first supervised 120k dataset called Starjob specifically designed
018            to train LLMs for JSSP and we subsequently fintune the LLaMA 8B model on
019
               this dataset using Lora. We compare the average makespan gap of our end-to-
               end LLM-based scheduling method with that of the most widely used priority
020
               dispatching rules (PDRs) and neural methods such as L2D. Surprisingly, our find-
021
               ings indicate that LLM-based scheduling not only surpasses traditional PDRs but
022            also achieves on average 11.28% on DMU and 3.29% gap improvement on the
023            Tailard benchmarks compared to the state-of-the-art L2D method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The job shop scheduling problem (JSSP) remains a well-studied and computationally challenging
029
      problem in the field of production scheduling and optimization. It entails the efficient allocation of a
030
      set of N jobs, each with heterogeneous processing times, to a limited number of M machines. The
031   primary objective is to optimize a performance metric, such as minimizing the total completion time
032   (makespan, denoted by Cmax ) or reducing the flow time (average completion time) of individual
033   jobs. JSSP finds application in diverse manufacturing and service environments, impacting factors
034   like production throughput, resource utilization, and ultimately, customer service levels. Traditional
035   approaches to JSSP have primarily relied on mathematical programming techniques and heuristic
036   algorithms Chaudhry & Khan (2015). However, these methods often exhibit limitations in scala-
037   bility and effectiveness, particularly for large-scale problems, or those with complex job-machine
038   precedence relationships. This has motivated the exploration of alternative approaches, particularly
039
      with the recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI). Techniques like reinforcement learning
      and graph neural networks have shown promise in addressing JSSP, offering data-driven solutions
040
      to this problemZhang et al. (2020)Corsini et al. (2024).
041
042   Huang et al. (2022) explored the graph reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs)
043   through natural language on tasks like connectivity, shortest paths, and more complex challenges
044   such as maximum flow and Hamilton path. LLMs represent a class of AI models trained on massive
045
      datasets of text data. While LLMs demonstrate some preliminary graph reasoning abilities, their per-
      formance declines with increasing problem complexity, and they often rely on spurious correlations.
046
      To enhance performance, Huang et al. (2022) proposed new prompting strategies. Valmeekam et al.
047
      (2022) introduce a benchmark to test for evaluating the planning/reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
048   Recently, Chen et al. (2024b) investigate the application of LLMs to the task of graph node classi-
049   fication. Collectively, these studies highlight the growing use of LLMs for tasks involving implicit
050   graphs and structures, though the application of LLMs on scheduling problems still remains largely
051   unexplored. These studies motivated further investigation into testing LLMs capabilities in JSSP.
052   This paper explores the potential of LLMs in tackling the complexities of the JSSP. To the best of
053   our knowledge, we are the first to utilize LLMs for end-to-end scheduling in JSSP problems. We
      posit that LLMs, with their inherent ability to process and reason over complex information, can be

079   JSSP with more than two machines is proven to be NP-hard Garey et al. (1976). As a result, find-
080   ing exact solutions for JSSP is generally infeasible, leading to the widespread use of heuristic and
081   approximate methods for practical efficiency Cebi et al. (2020). Traditional approaches to solving
082   JSSP have primarily relied on search and inference techniques developed by the constraint program-
083   ming community Beck et al. (2010). These techniques effectively leverage constraints to define
084
      the relationships and limitations between jobs and resources, enabling efficient exploration of feasi-
      ble solution spaces and the identification of optimal or near-optimal schedules Nowicki & Smutnicki
085
      (2005). A widely used heuristic method in real-world scheduling systems is the Priority Dispatching
086
      Rule (PDR) Zahmani et al. (2015). PDRs are simple and effective, although designing an efficient
087   PDR is time-consuming and requires extensive domain knowledge.
088
089   Recently, approaches utilizing Deep Learning and Neural Networks have gained attention for finding
090
      promising solutions to the JSSP Bonetta et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2020); Corsini et al. (2024).
      These methods can be broadly categorized into supervised learning and reinforcement learning (RL).
091
      Current research in deep reinforcement learning (DRL) is actively focused on developing advanced
092
      methods to tackle JSSP. Existing DRL methods typically represent JSSP as a Markov Decision
093   Process (MDP) and learn a policy network based on DRL techniquesZhang et al. (2020).
094
095   Large language models (LLMs) are now being applied to a wider range of tasks beyond language
096
      processing. In areas like robotics and planning, LLMs have been employed to direct agents through
      structured environments Huang et al. (2022).
097
098   While there are currently no papers that directly address the scheduling of Job Shop Scheduling
099   Problems (JSSP) using LLMs, some notable works explore the potential of LLMs in mathematical

101   (2023). Optimization using large language models (LLMs) has gained significant interest in recent
102
      years, with several works exploring their capabilities across various domains Yang et al. (2023).
      The ability of LLMs to understand and generate natural language has opened new possibilities for
103
      optimization tasks that were traditionally solved using derivative-based algorithms or heuristic meth-
104
      odsYang et al. (2023). Notably, Chen et al. (2023) have done a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs,
105   incorporating an examination of their performance in mathematical problem-solving. Chen et al.
106   (2023) introduces a novel approach called ”Program of Thoughts” (PoT) prompting. Unlike the

140       3   P RELIMINARY
141
142       JSSP is formally defined as a problem involving a set of jobs J and a set of machines M . The size of
143       the JSSP problem instance is described as NJ × NM , where NJ represents the number of jobs and
144       NM the number of machines. For each job Ji ∈ J, it must be processed through ni machines (where
145       ni is the number of operations for job Ji ) in a specified order Oi1 → . . . → Oini , where each Oij
146       (for 1 ≤ j ≤ ni ) represents an operation of Ji with a processing time pij ∈ N. This sequence also
147       includes a precedence constraint. Each machine can process only one job at a time, and switching
148       jobs mid-operation is not allowed. The objective of solving a JSSP is to determine a schedule, that is,
149
          a start time Sij for each operation Oij , to minimize the makespan Cmax = maxi,j {Cij = Sij +pij }
          while meeting all constraints. The complexity of a JSSP instance is given by NJ × NM .

          6.1   OVERVIEW OF JSSP S OLUTION PARSING AND VALIDATION
254
255       Following inference, we employ regular expressions to parse the output string generated by the
256       LLM. This process extracts job number, operation number, machine number, start time, duration,
257       end time for each operation, and the makespan value (if present). The validation process involves
258       the following key steps:
259
260             1. Parsing Inputs: The function parses the problem data and solution string to extract
261                jobs, operations, and declared makespan.
262             2. Initial Checks: It verifies the integrity of the inputs, checking for empty solutions and the
263                presence of all required jobs.
264             3. Operation Validation: Confirms that each operation’s machine and duration in the LLM
265                output match the expected values from the problem data.
266
267
                4. Machine Conflict Check: Ensures no overlapping operations on the same machine by
                   sorting operations by start time and checking for overlaps.
268
269             5. Job Precedence Check: Verifies that the end time of one operation is before the start time
                   of the next within the same job, ensuring correct operation order.

380
      This paper demonstrates the potential of Large Language Models (LLMs) in addressing the JSSP.
381   We introduced a novel supervised dataset called Starjob for solving JSSP tailored for LLM training.
382   Our results on well known benchmark problemsTaillard (1993), Demirkol et al. (1998) indicate that

      9   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
387
388
      Our exploration of using LLMs for the JSSP marks an important first step in adapting these large
389
      models to the domain of scheduling. While the findings are encouraging, they also reveal several
390   challenges and potential directions for future research. The key objective was to experiment with
391   LLMs on scheduling problems like JSSP and demonstrate their initial potential in this domain.
392
393
      A primary limitation is the significant computational burden associated with fine-tuning and infer-
      ence of LLMs, which can be quite resource-intensive. Large Language Models (LLMs) are still
394
      significantly oversized and resource-intensive when applied to specialized or narrow domain tasks,
395
      where a smaller, more efficient model could potentially be more suitable. Furthermore, due to con-
396   straints in computational resources, the generalizability of our findings to larger JSSP instances
397   remains uncertain. Consequently, additional research is warranted to assess LLMs on larger prob-
398   lem sizes. It is also crucial to compare the performance of various LLMs and different fine-tuning
399   techniques on the proposed Starjob dataset.
400
      Another challenge lies in the interpretability of schedules generated by LLMs, given their black-
401
      box nature. While we employed a basic sampling method to enhance performance, investigating
402   alternative sampling strategies could further improve the quality of LLM-generated schedules.
403
404   Looking ahead, future research should consider the integration of LLMs with other artificial intel-
