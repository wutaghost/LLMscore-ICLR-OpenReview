009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image diffusion-based generative models have the stunning ability to generate
012            photo-realistic images and achieve state-of-the-art low FID scores on challenging image
013            generation benchmarks. However, one of the primary failure modes of these text-to-image
014            generative models is in composing attributes, objects, and their associated relationships
015            accurately into an image. In our paper, we investigate this compositionality-based failure
016            mode and highlight that imperfect text conditioning with CLIP text-encoder is one of the
017            primary reasons behind the inability of these models to generate high-fidelity compositional
018            scenes. In particular, we show that (i) there exists an optimal text-embedding space that can
019
               generate highly coherent compositional scenes showing that the output space of the CLIP
               text-encoder is sub-optimal, and (ii) the final token embeddings in CLIP are erroneous as
020
               they often include attention contributions from unrelated tokens in compositional prompts.
021
               Our main finding shows that the best compositional improvements can be achieved (without
022            harming the model’s FID score) by fine-tuning only a simple and parameter-efficient linear
023            projection on CLIP’s representation space in Stable-Diffusion variants using a small set
024            of compositional image-text pairs. This result demonstrates that the sub-optimality of the
025            CLIP’s output space is a major error source. We also show that re-weighting the erroneous
026            attention contributions in CLIP can lead to slightly improved compositional performances.

030
031   Text-to-image diffusion-based generative models (Rombach et al., 2021; Podell et al., 2023; Ramesh et al.,
032   2021; Saharia et al., 2022) have achieved photo-realistic image generation capabilities on user-defined text
033   prompts. However recent works (Huang et al., 2023) have designed compositionality benchmarks to show
034   that these text-to-image models have low fidelity to simple compositionality prompts such as those consisting
035   of attributes, objects, and their associated relations (e.g., “a red book and a yellow vase”). This hinders the
036   use of these generative models in various creative scenarios where the end-user wants to generate a scene
037   where the composition is derived from words (and their relationships) in the prompt.
038   Existing works (Chefer et al., 2023a; Feng et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) propose
039   various ways to improve compositionality in text-to-image models. These works primarily rely on modifying

041   the latent space during inference. Recent methods based on fine-tuning (Huang et al., 2023) the UNet also
042   lead to improved compositonality. Despite the progress, the core reasons behind compositionality failures in
043   text-to-image models remain unclear. Understanding these reasons helps designing effective methods that can
044
      augment text-to-image models with improved compositional capabilities.

046   models. We identify two sources of errors: (i) We observe that output token embeddings in CLIP have

      2   BACKGROUND
130
      Compositionality in Text-to-Image Generative Models. A recent work Huang et al. (2023) introduces a
131
      benchmark for testing compositionality in text-to-image models showing the susceptibility of open-source text-
132
      to-image models on simple compositional prompts. In addition, the authors also propose a fine-tuning baseline
133   to augment text-to-image models with improved compositionality. The compositionality issue can also be
134   addressed at inference time by modifying the cross-attention maps leveraging hand-crafted loss functions and
135   bounding boxes generated from a language model (Chefer et al., 2023a; Feng et al., 2023; Agarwal et al.,
136   2023; Wang et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2024; Lian et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a). However, Huang et al. (2023)
137   show that a data-driven and fine-tuning approach is more suitable towards improving compositionality.
138
139   Interpretability of Text-to-Image Generative Models. There have been recent efforts to interpret text-
140   to-image models like Stable Diffusion. DAAM (Tang et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022) studies the generation

      process in diffusion models by analyzing cross-attention maps between text tokens and image pixels, high-
142
      lighting their semantic precision. Basu et al. (2023) use causal tracing to understand how knowledge is stored

149   In diffusion models, noise is added to the data following a Markov chain across multiple time-steps t ∈ [0, T ].
150   Starting from an initial random real imagepx0 along with its caption c, (x0 , c) ∼ D, the noisy image at
                                       √
151   time-step t is defined as xt = αt x0 + (1 − αt )ϵ. The denoising network denoted by ϵθ (xt , c, t) is
152   pre-trained to denoise the noisy image xt to obtain xt−1 . For better training efficiency, the noising along
153   with the denoising operation occurs in a latent space defined by z = E(x), where E is an encoder such as
154   VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017). Usually, the conditional input c to the denoising network ϵθ (.) is a
155
      text-embedding of the caption c through a text-encoder c = vγ (c). The pre-training objective for diffusion
      models can be defined as follows:

164
165   We focus on the disentangled BLIP-Visual Question Answering (referred to as VQA for simplicity) score
166   proposed by Huang et al. (2023) as a key metric for evaluating image quality. The VQA score measures how

170   2.3   DATASET C OLLECTION
171
      We utilize the T2I-CompBench dataset (Huang et al., 2023), focusing on three key categories: color, texture,
172
      and shape, with a total of 1,000 prompts across both training and evaluation sets. T2I-CompBench is a well-
173   established and widely recognized dataset (Esser et al., 2024). This dataset provides distinct training and
174   evaluation splits for each category, enabling a structured approach to assessing performance. To generate
175   high-quality images, we use three generative models: SD 1.4 (Rombach et al., 2021), DeepFloyd, and SynGen
176   (Rassin et al., 2024), creating 100 samples per prompt with SD 1.4, 60 with DeepFloyd, and 50 with SynGen.
177   This ensures a wide variety of generated images, leveraging each model’s strengths. For each prompt, we
178   combined all 210 samples from the three models and selected the top 30 with the highest VQA scores,
179   ensuring the final dataset consisted of images that most accurately reflected the prompts.

393   5.3   S WITCH -O FF : T RADE - OFF BETWEEN C OMPOSITIONALITY AND C LEAN ACCURACY
394
395   Fine-tuning models or adding modules to a base model often results in a degradation of image quality
396   and an increase in the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) score. To balance the trade-off between improved
397   compositionality and the quality of generated images for clean prompts – an important issue in existing work
398   – inspired by Hertz et al. (2022), we adopt S WITCH -O FF, where we apply the linear projection only during
399   the initial steps of inference. Specifically, given a time-step threshold τ , for t ≥ τ , we use WiCLPW ∗ ,b∗ (c),
400   while for t < τ , we use the unchanged embedding c as the input to the cross-attention layers.
401   Figure 7 illustrates the trade-off between VQA score and FID on a randomly sampled subset of MS-COCO (Lin
402   et al., 2014) for different choices of τ . As shown, even a large value of τ suffices for obtaining high-quality
403   compositional scenes as the composition of final generated image is primarily formed at early steps. Thus,
404   choosing a large τ preserves the model’s improved compositionality while maintaining its clean accuracy.
405   Setting τ = 800 offers a competitive VQA score compared to the model where projection is applied at all
406   time steps, and achieves a competitive FID similar to that of the clean model. Figure 6 depicts a few images
407   generated using different choices of τ . We refer to Appendix D.5 for more visualizations.

461
462
      Our paper examines potential error sources in text-to-image models for generating images from compositional
463
      prompts. We identify two error sources: (i) A minor error source, where the token embeddings in the
464
      CLIP text-encoder have erroneous attention contributions and (ii) A major error source, where we find the
465   output space of the CLIP text-encoder to be sub-optimally aligned to the UNet for compositional prompts.
466   Leveraging our observations, we propose a simple and strong baseline WiCLP which involves fine-tuning a
467   linear projection on CLIP’s representation space. WiCLP though inherently simple and parameter efficient,
468   outperforms existing methods on compositional image generation benchmarks and maintains a low FID score
469   on a broader range of clean prompts. We discuss limitations in Appendix A.
