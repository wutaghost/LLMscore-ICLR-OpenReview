009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Empirical risk minimization is a fundamental paradigm in the optimization pro-
012            cess of machine learning (ML) models. Several techniques extend this idea by in-
013            troducing parameters which further regularize this strategy in training these mod-
014            els. One of these paradigms is the so-called tilted empirical risk minimization
015            (TERM), which uses a tilted hyperparameter to penalize the presence of outliers,
016            which represent data samples that differ significantly from the rest of the dataset.
017            Quantum machine learning (QML) models have been studied and benchmarked
018
               across various criteria stemming from classical ML, including their training via
               the parameter-shift rule. Therefore, it is natural to extend the concept of TERM
019
               in training QML models, namely the type of models known as quantum neural
020
               networks (QNNs). In this work, we examine the impact of a tilted loss function in
021            training a class of QNNs, specifically for binary classification tasks involving two
022            different datasets with induced class imbalance. In the first dataset, the Iris dataset,
023            we show that varying the value of the tilted hyperparameter modifies the decision
024            boundary leading to reduced importance of outliers and better training accuracy
025            — highlighting the importance of using tilted risk minimization. Additionally, in a
026            synthetic dataset we validate that the training accuracy can be improved using the
027            tilted parameter. Analytically, we extend the parameter-shift training method to
028            accommodate weighted inputs by introducing the tilted hyperparameter for train-
029
               ing QNNs. These results highlight the significance of incorporating regularization
               techniques from ML models into QML models.

039   be variational quantum algorithms (VQAs), or parameterized quantum circuits (PQCs) due to their
040   parameterized nature, which allows them to be trained on classical data using optimization tech-

042   challenges, particularly in their training processes, as they are prone to the barren plateaus problem,
043   where the training landscape resembles a flat structure with gradient values leading to zero (Larocca

      2.1   T ILTED E MPIRICAL R ISK M INIMIZATION
143
      Empirical risk minimization is widely used in machine learning where the goal is to optimize model
144   parameters by minimizing the average loss over the training data (Vapnik, 1998). The key idea
145   behind ERM is that, because we do not know the true distribution that generated the data, we use
146   the available training data to estimate the risk by averaging the loss over the dataset. However, ERM
147   has notable limitations, particularly when dealing with outliers or imbalanced data, and generalizing
148   to unseen data. For example, in situations where certain data subgroups are underrepresented or
149   contain outliers, the model may overfit to noisy data or produce unfair solutions, especially if the
150   outliers belong to subgroups that we aim to serve better.
151   To mitigate some of the challenges of ERM, tilted losses are used for the generalization of this tra-
152   ditional technique. Motivated in large by exponential tilting in deviation theory, works in Ref (Li
153   et al., 2020; 2023) introduce TERM, an extension of ERM with an additional tilt hyperparameter t.
154   The flexibility of tilted hyperparameter allows the model to continuously adjust decision boundaries
155   based on the problem settings, offering robustness against outliers, fairness towards underrepre-
156   sented subgroups, or a balance between both. This approach is especially useful in classification
157   tasks where different groups or data distributions require varying levels of emphasis.

181   2.2   Q UANTUM N EURAL N ETWORKS AND T RAINING
182
183   QNNs are a key class of QML models, which essentially combine concepts from classical neural net-
184
      works and parameterized quantum circuits into hybrid architectures. Demonstrations of advantages
      of QNNs over modern deep-learning architectures remain open, given also the current hardware lim-
185
      itations . QNNs fundamentally provide input-output relationships via an exponentially-sized Hilbert
186
      space, which may allow advantages over classical counterparts in handling complex data and com-
187   plex input-output relationships for specific scenarios. QNNs share a conceptually similar structure
188   to classical neural networks, where a task is encoded into a parameterized loss function that is evalu-
189   ated using a quantum computer, and a classical optimizer trains the parameters in the parameterized
190   circuit.
191
      A QNN is a function f : Cn → Cm that maps classical input data x ∈ Cn to output data y ∈ Cm
192
      through a parameterized quantum circuit U (θ), where θ ∈ Rp represents the parameters of the
193   circuit. The process can be described as follows.
194
195          • State preparation: Consists of a feature map that encodes input data x into quantum states
196            |ψx ⟩. Here we assume the initial state to be the computational zero state |0̄⟩.
197
198
             • Circuit with a sequence of quantum gates: This sequence of gates depends on input
               x and trainable parameters θ (weights) optimized during the training process. The final
199
               state is obtained by applying a total unitary U (x; θ) of the circuit onto the input state, i.e.,
200
               |ψ(θ)⟩ = U (x; θ)|0̄⟩.
201
202          • Measurement: This step determines the output of the quantum circuit by evaluating
203            the model’s performance. The prediction y is obtained through m measurement op-
204
               erators Mi , i ∈ [m] on the corresponding qubits to derive expectation values yi =
               ⟨ψ(θ)|Mi |ψ(θ)⟩.
205
206          • Optimizer: The measured values are used as a feedback to learn the best parameters θ.
207            The optimization typically involves minimizing a loss function, which can be formulated
208            as different forms of risk, such as ERM or TERM, as used in this work.
209
210   QNNs are you often explored in conjunction with implementations in near-term quantum computing
211   architectures (Preskill, 2018).
212   Efficient QNN training remains an open question. To evaluate the gradients of PQCs on quantum
213   hardware, several methods have been proposed. The parameter-shift rule is one of these methods, as
214   introduced by (Mitarai et al., 2018; Schuld et al., 2019). This method efficiently computes the gradi-
215   ent by evaluating the circuit at two shifted parameter values, enabling the optimization of quantum
      models with low computational overhead.

234
      Datasets. We focus on binary classification tasks. For this purpose, we adopt two datasets: the Iris
235
      flower dataset (Anderson, 1936; Fisher, 1936), as well as a synthetic dataset from Ref. (Li et al.,
236
      2020). After loading the dataset, data padding was adopted to make the data dimension same as
237   the size of the quantum state vector. The final step of data pre-processing is the normalization, in
238   order to encode the data into rotational angles of the rotational unitary gates. In this case, we need
239   to encode the classical data of two real value points into the QNN for each use. Characteristics of
240   each dataset are described below.
241
242          • Iris dataset. From the Iris, the two classes Setosa and Virsicolour were chosen. For the 2
243            dimensions, we used a scaled sepal width against sepal length. In the initial dataset, there
244            are 50 data points in each class. To better show the effect of TERM, we want to create
245            class imbalance and an outlier. Class Setosa was selected as the majority class and kept
246            as is. To create imbalanced classes, we specifically select 5 data points randomly from the
247            Virsicolour dataset, to make it the minority class. Furthermore, one of the 5 data points was
248            selected as the outlier and edited to behave like a Setosa data point. Originally, four features
249
               were measured from each sample: the length and the width of the sepals and petals. We
               selected only 2 features — the length and the width of the sepals.
250
251
             • Synthetic dataset. This dataset is manually randomly generated. There are 56 data points
252
               in the majority class, and 6 data points in the minority class (including 1 outlier), adding
253
               up to a total of 62 data points.
254
255
      Angle Encoding. To train a QNN using classical data, we must first convert that data into a quantum
256
      representation. This conversion is accomplished through an encoding process, which we include in
257
      our numerical results. The encoding transforms classical data points into quantum states. Here we
258   discuss angle encoding which is a widely used technique in representing classical data in on a quan-
259   tum computer in the context of machine learning (LaRose & Coyle, 2020; Schuld & Petruccione,
260   2021). Angle encoding uses a sequence of controlled NOT (CNOT) gates and uniformly controlled
261   rotations (Mottonen et al., 2004).
262
      Specifically, in angle encoding, classical data is encoded into quantum gates using a rotation operator
263
      such as Rx (θ), Ry (θ) or Rz (θ), where θ represents the feature value of input data. For example,
264   a 2-dimensional data point x = [x1 , x2 ] can be mapped into a quantum state by applying rotations
265   on qubits, where θ = f (x) is some function of x (e.g., direct or normalized values of x1 , x2 ).
266   Additionally, CNOT gates are used alongside with rotation gates to create entanglement between
267   qubits. By applying these gates, the qubits are prepared in a state where their amplitudes encode
268   the classical data features. These quantum states representing the encoded classical data, can then
269   be processed by the variational quantum circuit for tasks such as classification or optimization. The
      circuit used to encode the data is shown in Fig. 2(a).

281   To demonstrate the effect of the tilted hyperparameter in QML models, we adopted a simple unitary
282   model circuit with relatively few trainable parameters, inspired by Ref. (Schuld et al., 2020). This
283   2-qubit model is composed of three parts. Firstly, the data is encoded into quantum state using the
284
      angle encoding technique as shown above. Secondly, trainable parameters are embedded into the
      circuit. The circuit contains single-qubit rotation gates and fixed two-qubit CNOT gates. A layer
285
      in this quantum circuit consists of a parameterized unitary on each qubit followed by a entangling
286
      CNOT gate. In our model, 6 of these layers were used for the circuit. Randomly generated parame-
287   ters will be substituted into the rotation gates initially, and will be updated by the classical optimizer
288   to minimize the loss function. The number of trainable parameters is 6 × 2 × 3 (number of layers
289   × number of qubits × number of parameters in each unitary). The circuit we used is shown in
290   Fig. 2(b). Lastly, in the read-out module, the expectation value of the computational basis measure-

293   an optimization method that improves the convergence rate for convex optimization problems from
294   the traditional gradient descent’s rate of O(1/k) to O(1/k 2 ) (Nesterov, 1983). Batch gradient de-
295
      scent was used with a batch size of 5, and the optimization process is halted when no improvement
      in accuracy is seen over 10 iterations. Square loss is used with the TERM function in Definition 2.

354   4.1                 T ILTED PARAMETER -S HIFT RULE
355

356

357

499   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
500
501   This work marks a first step in understanding the feasibility of the tilted term in training quantum
502   learning models. Here, we have demonstrated the analytical performance of a loss function con-
503   taining the tilted parameter, under the parameter-shift rule. More specifically, we define gradient
504   training for parameter-shift rule using the tilted parameter. On the numerics front, we have pre-
505   sented the performance of the loss function with tilt on two different datasets, namely the Iris and
506   a synthetics dataset from the original TERM work (Li et al., 2023). Our results have evidenced the
507   role of TERM in training a class of QNNs for two class imbalance induced datasets. For the Iris,
508   positive tilted hyperparameters mitigate the effect of imbalanced data class. While for the synthetic
509
      dataset the non-tilted case, which correspond to ERM, has the worst performance compared to tilted
      cases, thus both positive and negative tilted parameters boost the performance of QNN classification
510
      tasks.
511
512   In future work, we propose benchmarking our numerical analysis further against more complex
513   datasets and additional sophisticated models. From a theoretical perspective, it would also be inter-
514   esting to see the effect of titled loss in quantum models with respect to the generalization property
515
      of these models (Caro et al., 2022). Heuristically, the addition of regularizers terms to the training
      process should, in principle, aid generalization. Moreover, tilted parameter-shift rule inspires us
516
      to define the quantum version of TERM (QTERM) by introducing objective function of quantum
517
      circuits. The necessity for QTERM arises from quantum alternative of the loss function. This could
518   lead to interesting future research directions on quantum risk akin to the ERM principle.
