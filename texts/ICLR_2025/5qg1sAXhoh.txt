                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Neural network supported tree-search has shown strong results in a variety of
012            perfect information multi-agent tasks. However, the performance of these methods
013            on partial information games has generally been below competing approaches.
014            Here we study the class of simultaneous-move games, which are a subclass of
015            partial information games which are most similar to perfect information games:
016            both agents know the game state with the exception of the opponent’s move, which
017            is revealed only after each agent makes its own move. Simultaneous move games
018            include popular benchmarks such as Google Research Football and Starcraft.
019            In this study we answer the question: can we take tree search algorithms trained
020            through self-play from perfect information settings and adapt them to simultane-
021            ous move games without significant loss of performance? We answer this question
022            by deriving a practical method that attempts to approximate a coarse correlated
023
               equilibrium as a subroutine within a tree search. Our algorithm works on coop-
               erative, competitive, and mixed tasks. Our results are better than the current best
024
               MARL algorithms on a wide range of accepted baselines.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithms train multiple agents interacting in a shared
030   environment. The challenge compared to single-player reinforcement learning is that, for each agent,
031   the actions of the other agents are influencing the received rewards, and therefore the evolution of
032   other agents’ policies during training causes the environment to appear non-stationary from the
033   perspective of a single agent. While multi-agent reinforcement learning methods have achieved
034   great successes for tasks where agents receive full information (see e.g., the survey Zhang et al.
035   (2021)), successes in partially observable settings have been more muted.
036   Our study focuses on a subset of partial information tasks most similar to perfect information tasks,
037   namely simultaneous move tasks; the only information withheld in simultaneous move tasks is the
038   actions of the other players. Contemporary simultaneous-move algorithms are largely designed
039   for either cooperative Yu et al. (2021) or competitive tasks Schrittwieser et al. (2019). Most con-
040   temporary algorithms take advantage of assumptions present when all agents are working together
041   (cooperative tasks) or agents are competing against one another (competitive tasks) in their design.
042   The algorithm we develop and present in this study is one of a small group Lowe et al. (2017) that
043   can be applied to both competitive and cooperative tasks.
044   Our new MARL algorithm combines a popular method for competitive tasks, namely deep-Monte
045   Carlo Tree Search (d-MCTS), with online no-regret learning to approximate a coarse correlated equi-
046   librium (CCE), a concept from game-theory. As we explain later, playing according to a CCE gives
047   you performance guarantees against any opponent in competitive tasks. Therefore even though our
048   method is trained purely through self-play, we demonstrate strong performance against all contem-
049   porary algorithms in the competitive setting, even against algorithms trained with human-injected
050
      knowledge such hand-coded opponents.
051   Specifically, we demonstrate that our method surpasses several leading multi-agent reinforcement
052   learning (MARL) algorithms—including Policy Space Response Oracles (PSRO), Multi-Agent
053   Proximal Policy Optimization (MAPPO), and Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient
      (MADDPG)—as well as numerous other competing approaches. Notably, MAPPO and MADDPG

055   (2021); Lowe et al. (2017). Our agents learn superior policies in both cooperative and competitive

119   2.3   W HY C OARSE C ORRELATED E QULIBRIUM ?
120
121   Standard MCTS is tailored to finding min-max solutions. This approach works well for zero-sum,
122   perfect information games like chess or Go, but when we move into the realm of partial information
123   games, the min-max paradigm becomes inappropriate. In these games, players don’t have complete
124   information about the game state or their opponents’ actions, and the optimal strategy often involves
125
      probabilistic decision-making to account for this uncertainty. Moreover, in multi-player or general-
      sum games, the strict adversarial assumption of min-max doesn’t hold. Therefore, to effectively use
126
      MCTS for partial information games, we need to modify the algorithm to converge to a different
127
      solution concept, one that is more appropriate for the game-theoretic nature of these scenarios.
128
129   The computational limitations of Nash Equilibrium Nisan et al. (2007) suggest Correlated Equilib-
130   rium (CE) and Coarse Correlated Equilibrium (CCE).
131   Among these, CCE stands out as a particularly natural choice: the key advantage of CCE lies in
132   its compatibility with no-regret learning dynamics where players only need to observe their own
133   payoffs, not the entire game structure or other players’ actions.

183
184
      Another method for branching into competitive environments is to freeze the policies of certain
      agents during training. This way, the environment becomes stationary with respect to a single agent
185
      Vinyals et al. (2019). A common implementation of this concept is neural fictitious self-play Hein-
186
      rich & Silver (2016), where an agent plays against frozen past iterations of themselves and the pool
187   of past policies grows during training.
188
189   Next, there is a variation of policy freezing where agents either have explicit access to, or maintain
190
      their own approximation of, other agent policies. A popular example of such an algorithm is Multi
      Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (MADDPG)Lowe et al. (2017). This directly addresses
191
      the problem of non-stationary and allows for training on cooperative, competitive, and mixed envi-
192
      ronments. We focus our performance comparisons against MADDPG as it applies to many of the
193   problem to which our proposed method applies.
194
195   Another approach some studies take is to attempt to approximate an equilibrium. Counterfactual
196
      regret minimization Zinkevich et al. (2008); Neller & Lanctot (2013) provides a powerful algorithm
      for approximating a Nash-equilibrium in 2p0s tasks. The algorithm aims to minimize regret. Zinke-
197
      vich et al. (2008); Neller & Lanctot (2013) demonstrate that by attempting to minimize the regret,
198
      they also minimize the exploitability of their policy thus approximating a Nash-equilibrium. Policy
199   Space Response Oracles (PSRO) Lanctot et al. (2017) addresses poor convergence in multi-agent
200   settings due to other agents’ policies. At each iteration, a new policy is added that approximates the
201   best response so the meta-strategy of the other players. It has several variations such as joint PSRO
202   (jPSRO) that are improvements upon the base algorithm. The limitation for equilibrium approxima-
203   tion algorithms is that they are not easily applied to tasks with larger state and action spaces. The
204   majority of testing regarding such algorithms have been on small tasks.
205   Finally there is the method that is most akin to ours, deep Monte Carlo Tree Search (d-MCTS)
206   and its variants Schrittwieser et al. (2019), Silver et al. (2017). D-MCTS utilizes neural network
207   guided simulations at every encountered state to estimate a policy and value, which in turn become
208   training data for future iterations of the neural networks. We provide explicit comparisons for how
209   our methodology differs from d-MCTS methods.

      Before we discuss the benefits we gain through limiting simulation in this way, we first justify why
      this trade-off is necessary. Contrasted to deep MCTS, which typically uses up to 800 time steps per
299
      node Schrittwieser et al. (2019), no-regret learning requires significantly more.
300
301   Using the regret bound provided by Neu (2015), it becomes clear that 800 time steps is insufficient
302   for this method. At 800 time steps, the theoretically guarantees provided by EXP3-IX are very poor:
303   we have not yet found the best action.
304   Let us consider an example state that has an action space of 125. According to the regret bounds
305   from Neu (2015), we would need to iterate at least 20,000 times to reliably determine a best action
306   for each player (and there for CCE). A direct example of this can be in Figure 3. Here, we can see
307   that the action probabilities for both players at the same state,sampled from MPE, stabilizes past
308   25,000 iterations.
309   For this reason, we truncate the immediate simulation depth to 1. Doing this allows us to greatly
310   speed up the value estimation process. Firstly, we remove the need to access the environment, as we
311   solely rely on value network evaluation feed back and do not use rewards obtained during simulation.
312   This is an improvement in terms of speed over model-free deep MCTS, but not model-based deep
313   MCTS.
314   Second, we parallelized the learning process across time. All deep MCTS methods use information
315   from their simulations to choose their next action and begin simulating on the resulting state. Our
316   method instead relies on a policy network to quickly traverse through a trajectory (shown in Figure
317   2), then processes all nodes simultaneously. As shown in Figure 4, both our method and MCTS are
318   able to gather and process nodes (states) using multiple workers. Our method, however, processes
319   all nodes in a single worker simultaneously, speeding up learning greatly.

515

658   A.2   I N DEPTH METHODOLOGY
659
660   Our approach is based on two core ideas. The first idea is to train a separate neural network πi
661   for each agent: this eliminates the curse of dimensionality. Indeed, although there are |A|n joint
662   actions, by training πi : S → Ai for all i = 1, . . . , n, we avoid an exponential growth of the action
663   space. However, this approach comes with a tradeoff: we have effectively made agent decisions
664   independent of each other – which is clearly highly sub-optimal. Indeed, agents could potentially
665   gain from correlating their actions, which they cannot do under this strategy.
666   Out second idea is to mitigate the loss from this by using update rules which approximate a coarse
667   correlated equilibrium (CCE); we view this as a ”second best” solution to making correlated deci-
668   sions across agents. Recall that a CCE is similar to a standard mixed Nash equilibrium, except that
669   there is a joint distribution taken by all the agents which makes deviations gainless. By training agent
670   policies to learn a CCE, we effectively bypass a major limitation of training independent policies.
671   The idea is that even though the individual decisions are made separately, the agents are replying to
672   each other, so that, in the limit, their time-averaged policies converge to a correlated action profile
673   which is good in the sense of being a CCE.
674   This leads to the question of how to build dynamics that attain CCE in our setting. We build on
675   recent work Daskalakis et al. (2022) which shows that EXP-IX, a standard algorithm in online
676   learning with asymptotically vanishing regret, can learn CCEs. We thus replace the standard value
677   estimation methods in MCTS based on UCB estimates with EXP-IV based estimates. Details are
678   given below.
679   We call our algorithm NN-CCE. It is trained by iterating through three main steps: (1) gathering
680   trajectories D, (2) processing trajectory information, (3) training a model on D.
681
      We first gather K trajectories in a given environment, each of length H. Next, we process our
682
      trajectories in reverse order. For the set of states in time step H − 1 over all trajectories, we train a
683
      Q-value network, QH−1 : sH−1 × A → R to output Q-value estimates of state-action pairs. Using
684   QH−1 , we perform Multi-Agent EXP-IX to approximate a CCE policy and value estimate for every
685   state over all trajectories at time step H − 1, {v(sH−1 )}i and {p(sH−1 )}i ∀i ∈ [K] (Algorithm 3).
686
687
      After, we use {v(sH−1 )}i ∀i ∈ [K], to train a new Q-value network QH−2 : sH−2 × A →
      v(sH−1 ) ∈ R, and repeat CCE approximation and Q-value training until time step h = 0. In
688
      total, we would train H Q-value networks per agent. Each Q-network takes as data the trajectory
689
      info for its respective time-step.
690
691   Finally, we train a policy model on all CCE policies calculated during our trajectory processing
692   phase to form a final model that outputs a policy distribution.
693   A detailed algorithm is provided in Algorithms 1, 2, and 3.
694

794
795   In this section we provide a series of ablation studies to demonstrate the factors that affect the
796   performance of our NN-CCE algorithm. Results are given comparing different versions of our agent
797   against a fully random algorithm and MADDPG in small test scenarios within the multi particle
798   environment. The specific scenario is “simple” with 2 adversarial agents and 1 good agent. Results
799   are given in terms of the total reward accumulated during a testing episode. Where either trained
800   MADDPG or CCE agents control the adversarial players, and the other controls the good player.
801   These results guided our development and implementation, contributing to the overall success for

      stricted the number of trajectories sampled to at most half of that of MADDPG.

809   We then trained MADDPG on a three small test scenarios in MPE and recorded its performance after

841   A.4.3    S TRATEGIC D OMINANCE ACTION P RUNING
842
843   The goal of no-regret learning is to grow the regret with respect to the best action in hindsight sub-
844   linearly. The algorithm will converge on to what it evaluates as the best action. If all players utilize
845   no-regret learning, then their learned policies converge to the set of CCEs.
846   Objectively speaking at a given state with N-players each having K strategies, there are multiple
847   CCEs that exist, and our agent would converge to one of them. In fact we also found that successful
848   MADDPG algorithms would converge onto one CCE for a given state as well.
849
      In classical game theory, having two or more competing equilibrium’s for a state is not a problem as
850
      if equilibrium A was strictly better than equilibrium B, equilibrium B would not be an equilibrium
851   by definition, but in MARL it can be an issue.
852
853   This is because the very definition of equilibrium assumes that all other players follow that recom-
854
      mended equilibrium. But in some cases of MARL, such as when our agent controls the 1 good
      agent, and MADDPG controls the 2 adversarial agents. Suddenly that assumption is violated, and
855
      the performance of our agent is due to random chance on how well our equilibrium compares to the
856
      opponent equilibrium; in scenarios like this, more than one player is not following the equilibrium
857   we learned.
858
859   Therefore it is important not only to learn how to play one equilibrium, but also learn to adapt to
860
      different equilibrium’s for a given state.
861   One way we found to improve performance around this problem is to prune the dominated strategies
862   for all players before no-regret learning. Strategies that were deemed to be dominated by any other
863   strategy were masked and not allowed for selection and their weights were ignored when converting
      weights to policy, thereby receiving a probability of selection of 0.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      We found that this optimization did not increase the maximum performance of our agent against
865   MADDPG across any test scenario, but it did increase the mean performance against MADDPG by
866   23% from an average score of 15.3 to 18.8 over many repeated test episodes and 10 random seeds.

869   A.4.4    J OINT VS INDIVIDUAL POLICY OPTIMIZATION
870
871   Joint policy optimization has poor scaling to larger tasks but it allows for much higher express-
872   ability of policies especially for competitive tasks, or tasks with competitive elements compared to
873   optimizing solo policies. The problem is that in team based competitive tasks, if we view the player
874   policies is random variables, the players should not be viewed as independent variables. Instead,
875   they should be viewed as dependent variables (all players on the same team). A joint distribution
876   created by multiplying the player marginal distributions cannot come close to the complexity of a
877
      joint distribution over all possible actions. It does not allow for coordination amongst the players
      and never will allow for such coordination. This is a problem for exploitability of a strategy in
878
      competitive settings.
879
880   In cooperative tasks, this lack of express-ability isn’t actually that much of a problem. Because we
881   are just looking for the best joint action as a needle in the haystack, we don’t need to consider All
882   joint action futures individually, but can consider each players policy and form a joint distribution
883
      by multiplying the the marginals together (treating the players as independent).

      A.4.5    I MBALANCED DATA SETS FOR EQUILIBRIUM VALUE PREDICTION
886
887
      One of the highest impacting aspects of predicting unknown states’ equilibrium values is the im-
888
      balance in value estimates accrued during simulation. This is particularly true in the first iteration
889   where the policy sampling the states is effectively a random policy.
890
891   We can see here for the MPE environment allowing a random policy to sample leads to the following
892
      value distributions. It is heavily skewed in favor of the value 0, as it is very unlikely for random
      policies to collide leading to non-0 rewards for any given trajectory.
893
894   When we attempted to train a Q-value network on this heavily biased data set, we found that, as
895   expected, it primarily predicted a value of 0 for testing data; the errors for non-zero testing data was
896   extremely high and variable.
897   Therefore we applied up-sampling to minority values. Initially we separate the continuous value
898   training data, (X,y) into K classes. Each class is defined as a non-overlapping range of size
899   range(y)/K. In order to prevent over representation of a small set of data points during sampling,
900   we maintain a rule that each class except one, ks must contain at least 1000 data points, where ks
901   is defined as the class with the least data points. We therefore recursively combine the two smallest
902   classes until the condition is met.

      A.4.7    NN SUPPORTED POLICY AND VALUE ESTIMATION
919
920   At the core of our algorithm is the estimation of non-stationary policies and values for any state
921   and time pairing. For any given state, s, at time step h, we run a neural network supported bandit
922   algorithm for a set number of iterations. During the bandit algorithm, we accumulate an average
923   value for s, and after the bandit algorithm we obtain our policy for s. Once all states within time
924   step h have been processed, we train a NN, Nh , on the states of time-step h, and repeat the process
925   for nodes in time-step h − 1 using Nh as our supplementary network.
926   Compared to other MARL algorithms, such as MADDPG, PPO, or deep-MCTS, we trade-off a
927   higher volume of data for attempting to get higher quality data. We can directly compare the effects
928   of this layer by layer approach by comparing our results to those of stationary policy estimation on
929   multiple tasks within the MPE environment.
930
      In stationary policy estimation, we no longer take a layer by layer approach, but instead accumulate
931   value estimation by back propagating visited leaf nodes in the tree through the reverse trajectory used
932   to reach them. Policy estimations, rather than using bandit algorithms at each state, are accumulated
933   by visit count to each successor state. This approach is very similar to the approach in deep-MCTS.
934
      By normalizing the average and standard error of the stationary estimation performance to 1 ± 0.56,
935
      we find that our layer by layer method yields more robust and higher quality results with an average
936
      performance of 2 ± 0.23.

1042   as applicable. Sampling uniformly across layers provides better value and policy estimates in future

1050   A.5.3    N EURAL N ETWORK T RAINING
1051
1052   Neural network architecture follows a standard DQN structure. It takes as input a batch of local
1053   observations and outputs a value estimate as well as policy estimate. There is a representation
1054
       network that is malleable depending on the input type. If the observations are images then it is
       generated as a convolutional NN, otherwise it is a deep fully connected NN. The number of layers
1055
       in the representation network are flexible to the performance needs, but we find 8 layers each of size
1056
       256 to be sufficient for all tasks in this study.
1057
1058   After the data is passed through the representation network it is then passed through two separate
1059   networks, the value and policy network. The value network takes the representation output and
1060
       produces a vector of variable size (support size) which is then converted to a scalar value. This
       method of estimating a value using a vector operation was popularized in Schrittwieser et al., 2019.
1061
       It causes outputs to be between 0 and 1 and thus aids in numeric stability. The policy network also
1062
       takes as input the representation output and produces a vector the size of the max action space of all
1063   agents.
1064
1065   During training, the network predictions for each observation are measured against their stored node
1066
       information counterparts stored in the replay buffer. We use cross entropy loss with stochastic
       gradient descent to optimize the network. Further implementation parameter details can be found in
1067
       the appendix.
1068
1069   The total procedure involves iterating between tree generation and neural network training. In order
1070   to measure intermediary progress, we measure the performance of our network against a random
1071   agent after each training session. We use the score against the random agent as both a validation
1072
       procedure and termination procedure. If the agent scores worse against the random agent on an iter-
       ation, the training is undone and the iteration repeats itself. This is common procedure for iterating
1073
       algorithms such as deep MCTS. If the network does not significantly improve its score after a series
1074
       of iterations, the training process is terminated, and the last updated model is output.
