                                                     A BSTRACT
030             We present Magic Insert, a method for dragging-and-dropping subjects from a
031             user-provided image into a target image of a different style in a physically plausible
032             manner while matching the style of the target image. This work formalizes the
033             problem of style-aware drag-and-drop and presents a method for tackling it by
034             addressing two sub-problems: style-aware personalization and realistic object
035
                insertion in stylized images. For style-aware personalization, our method first
                fine-tunes a pretrained text-to-image diffusion model using LoRA and learned
036
                text tokens on the subject image, and then infuses it with a CLIP representation
037
                of the target style. For object insertion, we use Bootstrapped Domain Adaption
038             to adapt a domain-specific photorealistic object insertion model to the domain of
039             diverse artistic styles. Overall, the method significantly outperforms traditional
040             approaches such as inpainting. Finally, we present a dataset, SubjectPlop, to
041             facilitate evaluation and future progress in this area.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Large text-to-image models have recently made significant progress in generating high-quality images.
046   However, to make these models truly useful, controllability is essential. Users have diverse needs and
047   want to interact with these models in different ways depending on their specific use case. Influential
048   work has been done to enable controllability in these networks, robustly addressing foundational
049   applications and controls such as subject personalization, style learning, layout controls, and semantic
050
      controls. Despite this progress, the full potential of these powerful large models has not been fully
      realized. Some applications that seemed clearly out of reach just a couple of years ago are now
051
      possible with careful approaches.
052
053   We present one such application: style-aware drag-and-drop. We formalize this problem and
      introduce Magic Insert, our method to tackle it, which shows strong performance compared to current

054
      baselines. One might initially consider addressing style-aware drag-and-drop by trying to inpaint
055   using a stylized subject, for example by combining Dreambooth Ruiz et al. (2023a), StyleDrop Sohn
056   et al. (2023), and inpainting. We find that approaches of this type are very expensive and achieve
057   subpar results.
058
      In developing Magic Insert, we address two interesting sub-problems: style-aware personalization
059
      and realistic object insertion in stylized images. For style-aware personalization, there have been
060
      attempts on adjacent problems, such as learning a style and then representing a specific subject in
061   that style Sohn et al. (2023); Hertz et al. (2023), or combining pre-trained custom style and subject
062   models Shah et al. (2023); Frenkel et al. (2024). Recent style work suggests that fast style learning
063   is possible, but fast learning of a subject, including all the intricacies of identity, is a much harder
064   problem that has arguably not been solved yet Ye et al. (2023); Ruiz et al. (2023b); Gal et al. (2023);
065   Wang et al. (2024b). We propose leveraging learnings from both domains and settle on a solution that
066   uses adapter injection of style paired with subject-learning in the embedding and weight space of a
067   diffusion model.
068   One key idea we propose is to not attempt inpainting directly into an image after achieving style-aware
069   personalization. Instead, for best results, we first generate a high-quality subject and then insert that
070   subject into the target image. To achieve our results, we introduce an innovation called Bootstrap
071   Domain Adaptation, that allows progressive retargeting of a model’s initial distribution to a target
072   distribution. We apply this idea to adapt a subject insertion network that has been trained on real
073   images to perform well on the stylized image domain, enabling the insertion of our generated stylized
074   subject into the background image.
075   Our method allows the generated output to exhibit strong adherence to the target style while preserving
076   the essence and identity of the subject, and for realistic insertion of the stylized subject into the
077   generated image. The method also provides flexibility in terms of the degree of stylization desired
078   and how closely to adhere to the original subject’s specific details and pose (or allow more novelty in
079   the generation).
080   In summary, we propose the following contributions:
081
082          • We propose and formalize the problem of style-aware drag-and-drop, where a subject (a
083            character or object) is dragged from one image into another. Specifically, in our problem
084            formulation the subject reference image and the target image may be in vastly different
085            styles, and the plausibility and realism of the subject insertion is important.
086          • In order to encourage exploration into this new problem, we present SubjectPlop, a dataset
087            of subjects and backgrounds that span widely different styles and overall semantics. We will
088            release this dataset for public use, as well as our evaluation suite.
089
             • We propose Magic Insert, a method to tackle the style-aware drag-and-drop problem. Our
090
               method is composed of a style-aware personalization component and a style-consistent
091
               drag-and-drop component.
092
093          • For style-aware personalization, we demonstrate strong and consistent results using subject-
094            learning in the embedding and weight space of a pre-trained diffusion models, along with
095            adapter injection of style.
096          • For drag-and-drop, we propose Bootstrapped Domain Adaptation, a method that allows
097            for progressive retargeting of a model’s initial distribution unto a target distribution. We
098            use this to adapt an object insertion network trained on real images to perform well on the
099            stylized image domain.

151

152

      3.1   S TYLE -AWARE D RAG - AND -D ROP P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
188
189   We formalize the style-aware drag-and-drop problem as follows. Let Is and It denote the space of
190   subject and target images, respectively. The space of subject images consists of images of solely the
191   subject in front of plain backgrounds. Given a subject image xs ∈ Is and a target image xt ∈ It , our
192   goal is to generate a new image x̂t ∈ It such that:
193
194         1. The subject from xs is inserted into x̂t in a semantically consistent and realistic manner,
195
               accounting for factors such as occlusion, shadows, and reflections.
196         2. The inserted subject in x̂t adopts the style characteristics of the target image xt while
197            preserving its essential identity and attributes from xs .
198
199
      Formally, we aim to learn a function h : Is × It → It that satisfies:
200                                  h(xs , xt ) = x̂t   s.t.   x̂t ∼ p(x̂t |xt , xs )                         (1)
201
202   where p(x̂t |xt , xs ) represents the conditional distribution of the output image given the subject
203   and target images. This distribution encapsulates the desired properties of semantic consistency,
204
      realistic insertion, and style adaptation. To learn the function h, we decompose the problem into
      two sub-tasks: style-aware personalization and realistic object insertion in stylized images. Style-
205
      aware personalization focuses on generating a subject that adheres to the target image’s style while
206
      maintaining its identity. Realistic object insertion aims to seamlessly integrate the stylized subject
207   into the target image, accounting for the scene’s geometry and lighting conditions. By addressing
208   these sub-tasks, we can effectively solve the style-aware drag-and-drop problem and generate visually
209   coherent and compelling results. In the following sections, we present our dataset and the components
210   of our proposed method.

289   3.4   B OOTSTRAPPED D OMAIN A DAPTATION FOR S UBJECT I NSERTION
290
291   In this section, we address the problem of subject insertion and propose a novel solution using
292
      bootstrapped domain adaptation. We formalize the concept of bootstrapped domain adaptation and
      describe the dataset used for this purpose. Subject insertion is a crucial component of the style-
293
      aware drag-and-drop problem, as it involves seamlessly integrating a stylized subject into a target
294
      background image. While diffusion-based inpainting approaches Meng et al. (2021a); Saharia et al.
295   (2022b); Rombach et al. (2022) can be used for this, they still face challenges such as generating
296   content in smooth regions, producing incomplete figures, erasing objects behind inserted subjects, and
297   having problems with boundary harmonization. We take a simpler and stronger approach, which is to
298   insert the subject by copying and pasting it into the target image, and then subsequently generating
299   contextual cues such as shadows and reflections Winter et al. (2024) in a second step. Unfortunately,
300   existing subject insertion models are trained on data captured in the real world, severely limiting their
301   ability to generalize to images with diverse artistic styles.
302   Let Dr denote the distribution of real-world images and Ds denote the distribution of stylized images.
303   Existing subject insertion models are trained on samples from Dr , but our goal is to adapt them to
304   perform well on samples from Ds . To overcome this limitation, we introduce bootstrapped domain
305   adaptation, a technique that enables a model to adapt its effective domain by leveraging a subset of its
306   own outputs. As illustrated in Figure 4 (left), we employ a subject removal/insertion model gθ trained
307   on real-data (Winter et al. (2024) in our case) to first remove subjects and shadows from a dataset
308   S ∼ Ds belonging to our target domain. Subsequently, we filter out flawed outputs and obtain a
309
      filtered set of images S ′ ⊆ S, which we use to retrain the subject removal/insertion model. Filtering
      can be done using human feedback or automatically given a quality evaluation module.
310
311   The bootstrapped domain adaptation process can be formalized as follows:
312
                                        ω = arg min E(x,y)∼S ′ L(gω (x), y)                                   (4)
313                                                ω
314   where ω denotes the adapted model parameters, L is the diffusion denoising loss, and (x, y) are pairs
315   of input images and corresponding subject removal/insertion ground truths from the filtered set Sf .
316   The concept of bootstrapped domain adaptation is based on the surprising observation that a diffusion
317   model trained for subject insertion/removal on real-world data can generalize to a wider stylistic
318   domain to a limited extent. By retraining the model on its own filtered outputs, we can effectively
319   adapt its domain to better handle stylized images.
