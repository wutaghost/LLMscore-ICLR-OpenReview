                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) is an effective technique for compressing Large
015
               Language Models (LLMs). While many studies focus on quantizing both weights
016
               and activations, it is still a challenge to maintain the accuracy of LLM after acti-
017            vating quantization. To investigate the primary cause, we extend the concept of
018            kernel from linear algebra to quantization functions to define a new term, “quanti-
019            zation kernel”, which refers to the set of elements in activations that are quantized
020            to zero. Through quantitative analysis of the quantization kernel, we find that
021            these elements are crucial for maintaining the accuracy of quantized LLMs. With
022            the decrease of quantization kernel, the precision of quantized LLMs increases. If
023            the quantization kernel proportion is kept below 19% for OPT models and below
024            1% for LLaMA models, the precision loss from quantizing activations to INT8
025
               becomes negligible. Motivated by the goal of developing a quantization method
               with small quantization kernel, we propose CrossQuant—a simple yet effective
026
               method for quantizing activations. CrossQuant cross-quantizes elements using
027
               row and column-wise absolute maximum vectors, achieving a quantization kernel
028            of approximately 16% for OPT models and less than 0.1% for LLaMA models.
029            Experimental results on LLMs (LLaMA, OPT) ranging from 6.7B to 70B param-
030            eters demonstrate that CrossQuant improves or maintains perplexity and accuracy
031            in language modeling, zero-shot, and few-shot tasks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani
037   et al., 2017) have achieved remarkable success across various domains (He et al., 2024; Dubey et al.,
038   2024; GLM et al., 2024), with model sizes reaching billions and even tens of billions of parame-
039
      ters. However, these LLMs require substantial computational resources for inference. For instance,
      running the LLaMA3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) model demands at leas t 140GB of RAM in high-
040
      precision (FP16). As the size of LLMs continues to grow, reducing the computational resources
041
      required for LLMs inference has become a critical challenge. Quantization, a compression tech-
042   nique, addresses this by reducing model parameters from high-precision floating points (FP16) to
043   low-precision integers (e.g., 8-bit integers, INT8), significantly reducing GPU requirements. To ef-
044   fectively scale larger models on a limited number of devices, it is essential to quantize both weights
045   and activations while utilizing the fewest possible bits, all without compromising accuracy.
046
      Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) compresses LLMs directly without the need for retraining, and
047
      can be further divided into two subgroups based on whether activations are quantized: weight-
048   only quantization (Lin et al., 2024; Frantar et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023) and weight-activation
049   quantization (Xiao et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2022). There are two widely used
050   methods for quantizing both weights and activations: Per-channel quantization (Liu et al., 2023)
051   and Per-token quantization (Yao et al., 2022). Per-channel quantization has demonstrated superior
052   performance for quantizing weights to INT4/INT8, denoted as W4/W8. Per-token quantization
053   is used in activations. However, Per-token quantization results in significant accuracy degradation
      when quantizing activations to INT8, denoted as A8. Many studies argue that Per-token quantization

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
140
141
      Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) of LLM can be divided into two categories: weight-only quan-
142
      tization and weight-activation quantization, based on whether activations are quantized or not (Zhu
143
      et al., 2024).
144
145   Weight-only Quantization, is a method only quantize weights into low-bit integers like INT3
146   or INT4 with keeping activations in FP16, denoted as W3A16 or W4A16. GPTQ (Frantar et al.,
147
      2022) quantizes each channel through iterative refinement, concurrently optimizing the unquantified
      weights to mitigate and compensate for the difference introduced by the quantization process. AWQ
148
      (Lin et al., 2024) focuses on identifying and protecting a small fraction of salient weights based
149
      on activation importance. SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2023) preserves sensitive weights through a
150   sensitivity-based non-uniform quantization and Dense-and-Sparse decomposition. On devices with
151   limited computing resources, such as mobile devices, quantizing weights alone is insufficient; acti-
152   vation also needs to be quantized.
153
      Weight-activation Quantization, quantizes both weights and activations into low-bit values, such
154
      as W8A8 (INT8 for both weights and activations). ZeroQuant (Yao et al., 2022) employs both
155
      group-wise and token-wise quantization strategies to quantize weights and activations to INT8,
156   marking it as the first deployment of a weight-activation quantization method. Most subsequent
157   works attribute the decrease in quantization accuracy of activations to outliers (Wei et al. (2022);
158   Zou et al. (2024)), which have large-magnitude values and emerge in the activations of models with
159   over 6.7B parameters. LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022) utilizes a mixed-precision quantiza-
160   tion strategy, maintaining outliers and their corresponding weights at FP16, while quantizing the
161   remaining weights and activations to INT8. Outlier Suppression (Wei et al., 2023) and OmniQuant
      (Shao et al., 2024) focus on identifying and reducing the influence of outlier values in activations

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to enhance quantization compatibility. SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) addresses the ouliers by
163   equivalently transferring the quantization challenge from activations to weights. We find that the
164   quantization kernel is the direct cause of quantization loss, while outliers indirectly affect the size of
165   the quantization kernel (discussed in detail in Appendix A). Our work also aligns with the scope of
166   weight-activation quantization.

      3     BACKGROUND
169
170
      Given a linear layer in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), it computes Y = X · W , where
171
      X ∈ RT ×I and W ∈ RI×O . {T, I, O} indicates {number of tokens, input channels, output
172   channels} respectively. Initial X and W are composed of elements of FP16, and the quantized
173   Q(X) and Q(W ) are composed of elements of INT4 or INT8.
174
175
      Quantizing activations optimizes model performance by lowering the precision of activation values,
      which accelerates inference for real-time applications. This reduction decreases the memory foot-
176
      print required for activations, improving computational efficiency (Shen et al., 2023). Quantizing
177
      weights also minimize memory usage, allowing larger models to fit within the constraints of edge
178   devices, and decreases data transfer, further reducing latency and energy consumption (Lin et al.,
179   2024). Together, weight and activation quantization enable the deployment of sophisticated models
180   in resource-constrained environments, enhancing the practicality of LLMs applications.
181

514   6   LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
515
516   Our work has the following limitations, which are also our future research directions:
517
518         • Although we have identified that the decrease in quantization accuracy is caused by quan-
519           tization kernels, this conclusion is based on experimental results. We have not yet fully
520           explored the specific information contained in undershoots that necessitates their preser-
521           vation during quantization. In future work, we will continue to investigate the missing
522           features during quantization inference.
523
            • The OPT-2.3B model exhibits a high proportion of kernels yet still performs well after
524
              quantization (see Figure 4). We hypothesize that this is related to the scale of the model’s
525
              parameters. In future research, we will examine why smaller models can tolerate a high
526           percentage (30%) of quantization kernels.

529   7   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce the concept of “quantization kernel”, defined as the set of elements in
532
      activations that are quantized to zero. Investigating the primary reason for the decline in precision
533
      in quantized LLMs, we find that this decline is attributable to the quantization kernel being mapped
534   to zero. Through quantitative experiments, we establish that the quantization kernels need to be
535   reduced to a certain proportion to maintain the accuracy of quantized LLMs, specifically 19% for
536   OPT models and 1% for LLaMA models. CrossQuant, a weight-activation method designed to
537   minimize the quantization kernels, outperforms the baselines in language modeling, zero-shot, and
538   few-shot tasks, with its quantization kernels of 16% for OPT models and <1% for LLaMA models.
539   Our method provides a new analytical perspective to better understand and deconstruct quantization
      loss, and we hope our findings inspire further valuable research in the field of quantization.

689
