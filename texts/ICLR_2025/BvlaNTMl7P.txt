009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have revolutionized a wide range of industries,
012            from healthcare and finance to automotive, by offering unparalleled capabilities
013            in data analysis and decision-making. Despite their transforming impact, DNNs
014            face two critical challenges: the vulnerability to adversarial attacks and the in-
015            creasing computational costs associated with more complex and larger models. In
016
               this paper, we introduce an effective method designed to simultaneously enhance
               adversarial robustness and execution efficiency. Unlike prior studies that enhance
017
               robustness via uniformly injecting noise, we introduce a non-uniform noise in-
018
               jection algorithm, strategically applied at each DNN layer to disrupt adversarial
019            perturbations introduced in attacks. By employing approximation techniques, our
020            approach identifies and protects essential neurons while strategically introducing
021            noise into non-essential neurons. Our experimental results demonstrate that our
022            method successfully enhances both robustness and efficiency across several attack
023            scenarios, model architectures, and datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are at the forefront of technological advancements, powering a mul-
028
      titude of intelligent applications across various sectors (Al-Qizwini et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2018;
029
      Fujiyoshi et al., 2019). Yet, as DNNs become deeply integrated into mission-critical systems, two
030   challenges emerge in DNN deployment. First, when DNNs are used in vital decision-making tasks,
031   their vulnerability to adversarial attacks becomes a serious concern. From a self-driving car mis-
032   interpreting a traffic sign due to subtle, maliciously introduced perturbations, to defense systems
033   being deceived into false detection, the outcomes could be catastrophic (Carlini & Wagner, 2017;
034   Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kurakin et al., 2016; Madry et al., 2017; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016).
035   Second, as DNNs become more complex and sophisticated, their computational demands increase
036   correspondingly, calling for advanced optimization strategies to ensure that these powerful neural
037   network models can operate efficiently even with constrained computational resources (Han et al.,
038
      2015; Niu et al., 2020; Roy et al., 2021; Zaheer et al., 2020).
039   Recent studies suggest that introducing noise not only enhances the robustness of DNN models
040   but also provides a controllable means to achieve such improvement, as evidenced by recent re-
041   search (Liu et al., 2018; He et al., 2019; Pinot et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Jeddi
042   et al., 2020). Noise can be sampled from various probability distributions, including Gaussian (Liu
043   et al., 2018; Lecuyer et al., 2019; He et al., 2019), Laplace (Lecuyer et al., 2019), Uniform (Xie
044
      et al., 2017), and Multinomial (Dhillon et al., 2018) distributions. Introducing random noise into
      the model, whether during training or inference phases, can be characterized as a non-deterministic
045
      querying process. Detailed theoretical proofs have shown that this approach leads to a significant
046
      improvement in adversarial robustness (Pinot et al., 2019).
047
048   However, these methods apply noise injections uniformly to all neurons, and such aggressive strate-
049   gies inevitably compromise model accuracy (Pinot et al., 2019). Moreover, injecting noise into the
050
      model often requires additional computations (Liu et al., 2018; He et al., 2019; Jeddi et al., 2020),
      presenting a significant challenge to the model’s efficiency, especially as the model size continues
051
      to grow.
052
053   The limitations raise a question: Can we design a method to retain the benefits of noise injection
      while enhancing its execution efficiency? To address this, we draw inspiration from techniques to

117   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
118
119   Adversarial Attacks & Defense with Noise. Adversarial attacks, as discussed before (Croce &
120   Hein, 2020), pose a significant threat to the deployment of machine learning (ML) models. Without
121   protective measures, ML models can experience a significant drop in accuracy, often exceeding 20%,
122   even under basic attacks. To defend against adversarial attacks, various methods have been proposed.
123   Noise Injection (Liu et al., 2018; He et al., 2019; Pinot et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020;
124   Jeddi et al., 2020; Lecuyer et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2017; Dhillon et al., 2018) is an effective method
125   where models are trained by introducing random sampled noise to the original weights. Introducing
126   random noise into the model can be characterized as a non-deterministic querying process, which
127
      acts as a shield, enhancing the model’s robustness.
128   For example, RSE (Liu et al., 2018) proposes enhancing robustness by introducing a ‘Noise’ layer
129   at the inception of the convolution block. This ‘Noise’ layer injects random noise into the activation
130   values of the preceding layer, thereby fortifying the model’s robustness. Similarly, PNI (He et al.,
131   2019) adds a learnable Gaussian noise to each weight matrix/input/activation value, further enhanc-
132   ing robustness and probing for the granularity of noise injection. Based on PNI, Learn2Perturb
133
      (Jeddi et al., 2020) presents a regularizer for estimating distribution parameters and progressively
      enhances noise distributions. It is evident that noise injection-based approaches often introduce ad-
134
      ditional computational overhead, which poses challenges in the current era of increasingly larger
135
      models. A recent development, Random Projection Filters (RPF) (Dong & Xu, 2023) proposes a
136   noise injection technique based on random projection. This method employs random projection to
137   introduce noise into a randomized subset of filters. While the RPF approach bears similarities to
138   ours, our method distinguishes itself by selecting neurons for noise injection based on their acti-
139   vation values. Moreover, we note that the RPF approach comes with some drawbacks, including
140   the introduction of extra computational overhead, the requirement for training from scratch, and the
141   inability to select the specific filters for noise injection precisely.
142   Robustness and Efficiency. Researchers explore several methods to improve the performance of
143   deep neural networks (DNNs), including reducing their size and enhancing their resilience to at-
144   tacks. In the domain of sparsity and robustness, a study (Guo et al., 2018) investigates how intro-
145   ducing sparsity in DNN architectures can bolster their resilience against adversarial attacks. A more
146   comprehensive analysis (Ye et al., 2019) examines various techniques such as adversarial training,
147   robust regularization, and model compression methods including pruning, quantization, and knowl-
148   edge distillation to enhance adversarial robustness. Some studies, inspired by the lottery hypothesis
149   (Frankle & Carbin, 2018) and adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017; Shafahi et al., 2019; Wong
150
      et al., 2020), combine pruning or sparse masking with adversarial training to obtain a sparse sub-
      network with robustness (Sehwag et al., 2020; Madaan et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2021b). Nevertheless,
151
      these methods offer only modest enhancements in robustness and present challenges in precisely
152
      controlling and further improving the model’s robustness (Guo et al., 2018).
153

155   3    A PPROACH
156
157   Building on the hypothesis that only a subset of neurons are essential for model inference, we can
158   introduce noise injection to the non-essential neurons as a defense against adversarial attacks. Our
159   challenge is to identify precisely these essential neurons. Drawing from approximation studies
160   (Achlioptas, 2001; Ailon & Chazelle, 2009; Vu, 2016), we propose a learning-based method to
161   identify essential neurons and inject noise into the non-essential neurons. Our approach not only
      retains model accuracy but also enhances adversarial robustness.

198   3.2   S ELECTION OF E SSENTIAL N EURONS AND N OISE I NJECTION
199
200   After we obtain optimized approximation parameters, we can use the approximate results z̃ to es-
201   timate the importance of individual neurons and select those with higher magnitude among z̃. We
202   can select essential neurons by comparing the approximate results with a threshold, which is the
203   smallest one in the Top-K values. A neuron is regarded as essential if its approximation from z̃ is
204   larger than the threshold. Specifically, we can generate a binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}n , work as a map
205   of essential neurons to keep and non-essential neurons to inject noise, where mi equals 1 when the
206
      neurons are essential while it switches to 0 when the neurons are non-essential. In this way, we can
      estimate which neurons are essential without actual computations.
207
208   Once we identify the non-essential neurons, we can inject noise as a kind of perturbation. Instead
209   of adding a noise term drawn from a normal or uniform distribution to the outputs, we propose to
210   directly populate the non-essential neuron with approximate values drawn from z̃ that are computed
211   in the selection step. Details can be found in Algorithm 2. Overall, the outputs of a DNN layer under
212
      our method can be formulated as

313   3.5   I MPLICATIONS FOR E FFICIENT E XECUTION
314
315   As indicated in Eq. 2, the computed pattern is a mixture of precise computation and approximate
316   computation in the form of non-essential neurons noise injection. For precise computation, only es-
317   sential neurons need to be computed and non-essential neurons are from approximation. Hence, we
318   can skip precise computations of non-essential neurons, leading to potential performance speedup
319   and energy saving in the similar spirit of accelerated execution of activation sparsity. In addition,
320
      our approximate method incurs a small amount of low-precision operations.
321   On noise injection granularity. While selective noise injection can improve execution efficiency
322   from computation skipping of essential neurons, the unconstrained and unstructured noise injection
323   patterns would increase the hardware design complexity and execution overheads from irregular data
      access and low data reuse.

361
      accuracy loss. Our detailed evaluation methodology is in Appendix D. Additionally, we also design

      adaptive attack and examine our method for gradient obfuscation (Athalye et al., 2018a). For further

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we introduce a novel approach: noise injection on non-essential neurons, effectively
535   bridging the gap between adversarial robustness and execution efficiency. Inspired by the approaches
536   of noise injection and activation sparsity, our data-dependent method can precisely identify and
537   keep the critical neurons that are contributing more to model accuracy, while injecting noise to the
538   remaining trivial neurons with approximate values to improve robust accuracy. We believe that our
539   findings highlight the importance of fine-grained noise injection, providing valuable insights into
      improving adversarial robustness and advancing the field of machine learning.

      Experimental Setup. We tested the effectiveness of our algorithm on a co-designed hardware
849
      implementation via an in-house cycle-accurate simulator. The simulator tracks key performance
850
      metrics and maps them to specific power values. SRAM power/area is estimated using CACTI, and
851   all other components are synthesized using Synopsys Design Compiler on the FreePDK 45nm PDK.
852
853   To get an accurate reference for an optimized accelerator, we design our own hardware. The general
854
      hierarchy is simple, with a central Matrix-Vector Unit (MVU), core-specific SRAM, and registers.
      The MVU is purpose-designed to match the algorithm requirements as efficiently as possible. It
855
      which uses 4-bit multipliers as a precision-scaling element, offering both 4- and 16-bit multiplication
856
      while limiting additional hardware.
857
858   Hardware Efficiency. By matching the hardware capabilities to the algorithm requirements, we can
859   achieve better hardware efficiency. A major factor is using relatively lower cost of low-precision
860
      arithmetic vs. high-precision. Because multiplication characterizes each bit against each other, scal-
      ing from 4- to 16-bits increases the intensity by 16x, not 4x. As a result, the cost of approximation
861
      is low compared to full-precision calculation.

890   First, with the relative intensity of full-precision calculation, increasing perturbation alone effec-
891   tively improves speed and efficiency. This is because fewer precise values must be calculated.
892   Because high precision execution dominates performance, a reduction in the high precision stage
893   therefore has a significant effect on performance.
894
      Second, when using a structured perturbation scheme, speed and efficiency are improved relative to
895   an equivalent unstructured scheme. This is because with constrained, predictable execution patterns,
896   we can expand the MVU without significant overhead, allowing for a higher throughput and faster
897   execution despite a worse mapping efficiency. Additionally, with structured scheme, there is more
898   data reuse, reducing the SRAM energy. Going further, with structured sparsity, we see that the
899   enhanced parallelism allows for a greater reduction in execution time, similar energy, and higher
900   efficiency between sparsities of the same degree.
901   From these two points, we can see structured dynamic perturbation is an effective means to vastly
902   improve inference performance. This is because it capitalizes on the efficiency gains of sparse
903   execution on high-cost operations while minimizing the energy & hardware costs which often limit
904   the efficiency of sparsity.
905
      A nuance to structured execution is that the optimal execution pattern is not always met. As seen in
906
      5, structured does not always achieve as much of an EDP reduction in ResNet18 under high sparsity.
907   This is due to the alignment and the reliance on minimum pipeline depth, which isn’t consistently
908   achieved under that sparsity. As such, VPUs will not be entirely full, leading to some wasted cycles.
909   This can be avoided with some additional hardware, but that itself could outweigh the cost of occa-
910   sionally reduced efficiency. As such, we perform our analysis with no such hardware. Regardless,
911   we see that it does not significantly effect performance, as it accounts for a lower overhead relative
912   to the savings.

949   Our approach to crafting an adaptive attack is guided by the principle of ‘T3:Adapt the objective to
950   simplify the attack’, as outlined in Tramer et al. (2020). This involves a thorough analysis of the
951   defense mechanisms and design new objective functions, leading us to propose an adaptive attack
952   specifically tailored to the injection of noise. To attack effectively, the attacker should be aware of
953
      the noise injected to the neurons. We estimate the noise impact on the output activations and define
      the final loss expression as:
954
955                           min (LC (x + δ, yt ) − L(A(N oisy(x + δ)), A(x + δ)))
                                δ
956
      where:LC is the classifier’s loss, N oisy is the function that applies noise to the neurons, A is the
957
      activation.
