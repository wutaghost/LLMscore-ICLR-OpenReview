009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many modeling tasks from disparate domains can be framed the same way, com-
012            puting spherical signals from geometric inputs, for example, computing the radar
013            response or aerodynamics drag of different objects, or navigating through an envi-
014            ronment. This paper introduces G2Sphere, a general method for mapping object
015            geometries to spherical signals. G2Sphere operates entirely in Fourier space, en-
016            coding geometric structure into latent Fourier features using equivariant neural
017            networks and then outputting the Fourier coefficients of the output signal. Com-
018
               bining these coefficients with spherical harmonics enables the simultaneous pre-
               diction of all values of the continuous spherical signal at any resolution. We per-
019
               form experiments on various challenging domains including radar response mod-
020
               eling, aerodynamics drag prediction, and policy learning for manipulation and
021            navigation. We find that G2Sphere significantly outperforms competitive base-
022            lines in terms of accuracy and inference time. We also demonstrate that equivari-
023            ance and Fourier features lead to improved sample efficiency and generalization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Many different problems in 3D modeling require mapping detailed local geometric information to
029
      global spherical functions. In this paper, we consider several examples, such as simulating the radar
030   response of an object, predicting the drag characteristics of a vehicle, or modeling an optimal control
031   policy for manipulation. Modeling these types of signals is challenging because it requires very fine
032   discretization of the domain in order to accurately capture high frequency information, such as sharp
033   specular reflections in radar. This can make traditional numerical solvers and simulators expensive
034   (Andersh et al., 2000). Deep learning methods represent an attractive, potentially more efficient
035   approach.
036   One method of using neural networks to model spherical output signals is to output a tensor of
037   regularly sampled values across the sphere using a spatial grid such as Healpix (Gorski et al., 2005)
038   (Fig. 1 a). However, a large number of samples is necessary to attain a high spatial resolution over
039   the sphere and even then, the model can easily overfit to the specific sample locations. Additionally,

055   and computational costs (Vijayanarasimhan et al., 2014). An alternate strategy is to use implicit
056   models which represent functions in a continuous manner and can be queried at any coordinate rather
057   than outputting values at predefined coordinates (Fig. 1b). Implicit models have the advantages of

059   and time to train in order to achieve good performance(Florence et al., 2022). Additionally, implicit
060   models have been shown to be prone to overfitting to the training coordinates (Decugis et al., 2024)
061   and, of particular interest to this work, they lose the geometric relationship between the input and
062   output spaces.
063   In this work, we seek to address these challenges by introducing a new approach to mapping object
064   geometries to spherical functions, where we model the output signal in Fourier space as a linear
065   combination of spherical harmonic (SH) basis functions, enabling simultaneous prediction of the
066   continuous output function at any desired resolution (Fig. 1c). While previous works have modeled
067   mappings from geometric inputs to spherical functions using the spherical harmonics, they have
068   relied on pre-defined mappings to convert the geometry to a spherical input (Esteves et al., 2018;
069
      Ha & Lyu, 2022). We propose G2Sphere, a novel approach to learn mappings between complex
      geometric inputs and spherical systems. G2Sphere maintains the geometric structure between input
070
      geometry and output signal by operating entirely in Fourier space and enforcing end-to-end SO(3)-
071
      equivariance. G2Sphere leverages equivariant graph convolutions (Geiger et al., 2022; Liao et al.,
072   2023) to encode the geometry, spherical convolutions (Cohen et al., 2018) to render the spherical
073   signal, and pointwise MLP non-linearities to model high-frequency information in the output space.
074   This combination alleviates issues with existing equivariant GNN approaches which have been lim-
075   ited to lower frequency modeling, primarily due to computational constraints.
076

080            G2Sphere uses learned spherical embeddings for geometric inputs which are more general
081            and flexible than prior works using engineered features.
082          • Empirical demonstration of the improved accuracy and efficiency of G2Sphere versus sev-

092   Fourier Transform in Machine Learning. The Fourier transform (FT) is an important tool across a
093
      wide range of mathematical and engineering applications ranging from solving differential equations
      (Cooley et al., 1969) to quantum mechanics and signal processing (Arfken et al., 2011). Fourier
094
      transforms have also been heavily utilized in machine learning. In image processing and pattern
095
      recognition the FT has been used to speed up convolutional neural networks (Mathieu et al., 2013),
096   to improve feature representation (Oyallon et al., 2018), and to increase the resolution of generative
097   adversarial networks (Karras et al., 2021). Lee-Thorp et al. (2021) use the FT in the more modern
098   transformer architecture to improve tokenization and improve performance. Li et al. (2020) combine
099   neural operators with the Fourier transform to create the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) to solve a
100   number of challenging PDE systems and Bonev et al. (2023) extend this work to the Spherical FNO
101   which they use to model weather systems. G2Sphere and Spherical FNO have a number of notable
102   differences. First, G2Sphere operates entirely in Fourier space, whereas FNO moves back and forth
103   between real and Fourier spaces. Additionally, FNOs are typically used to model dynamical systems
104
      which have similar continuous input and output spaces, while G2Sphere maps discrete geometric
      inputs to continuous outputs.
105

107   formations under a symmetry group (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Geiger & Smidt, 2022; Weiler &
      Cesa, 2019). Neural networks equivariant to the 3D rotation group SO(3) are used for classifying

135   3   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT.
136
137   In this paper we consider the problem of mapping 3D geometric data, such as object meshes or
138   points clouds, to spherical signals representing physical systems, such as radar response or drag.
139   We consider spherical signals as continuous functions f : S d → Rc where S d is the 1-sphere or
140   2-sphere and c is the number of channels in the signal. Given some input geometry X ⊂ R3 , the
141   objective is to learn a functional mapping F : X 7→ fX . Since full observations of fX are rare
142
      in practice, we assume a dataset consisting of only partially observed outputs. That is, a dataset
      D = {(Xi , ui , yi )}N                                                                           d

      and the value of the spherical signal yi = fXi (ui ) ∈ Rc at ui . Since the output signals are only
144
      partially observed, we consider generalization to new coordinates ui and new geometries Xi .

      Equivariance. The value fX (u) may be considered as a property of the geometry X defined with
147   respect to viewing direction u ∈ S 2 . We assume that both the geometry X and direction u are
148   defined with respect to the same coordinate frame. Thus if the coordinate frame is rotated, both X
149   and u are rotated, and fX (u) = fRX (Ru) should be invariant. This property is equivalent to the
150   equivariance of the functional mapping F ; if X is rotated to RX, the spherical signal fX is rotated
151   to fRX = RfX , preserving the relationship between the input geometry and the output signal.
152   That is, F (RX) = RF (X). This property can be enforced in the model architecture to ensure the
153   learned model respects this property. Since the composition of equivariant functions is equivariant,
154   it is sufficient to ensure that each component of the architecture is equivariant.

156   4   G2S PHERE
157
158   A G2Sphere model is comprised of two key components: (1) an encoder which compresses the input
159   data into a compact latent representation and (2) a decoder which decomposes this latent representa-
160   tion into the spherical output utilizing spherical harmonics (Fig. 2). Encoder-decoder architectures
161   such as U-Net (Zhou et al., 2018) and Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are attractive methods
      for prediction tasks with high-dimensional inputs, such as 3D geometric data, due to latent space


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      compression. The encoder-decoder design is a good fit for our problem since we map between two
163   different types of geometric data, e.g. mesh to spherical signals, and the encoder-decoder archetype
164   allows us to match the geometry of the input and output spaces to the architecture of the encoder
165   and decoder. By leveraging these learned and generalizable mappings from geometry to spherical
166   signals, G2Sphere can be applied to tasks that were previously unattainable or computationally pro-
167   hibitive under previous methods (Esteves et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2023). We
168   detail the concrete implementations utilized in our experiments in Appendix D.
169
170   4.1   E NCODER
171
172   In a G2Sphere model, the first step is to encode the local and global information of the 3D input
173   into Fourier space, thereby ensuring Euclidean and permutation symmetries are correctly respected
174   within our model. This is accomplished by utilizing an equivariant neural network encoder, but the
175   exact architecture can vary depending on the type of input data. Broadly speaking, we define 3D
176
      geometric data as information that describes the shape, position, and spatial properties of objects in
      three-dimensional space. This can include various types of data including point clouds, voxel grids,
177
      object meshes, and RGB-D images. When dealing witth dense inputs, such as point clouds or object
178
      meshes, equivariant transformers such as Equiformer V2 (Liao et al., 2023) are used. However,
179   when dealing with voxel grids or RGB-D images, E(n)-equivariant Steerable CNNs (Weiler &
180   Cesa, 2019; Cesa et al., 2022) are a more natural choice. These types of models are constrained
181   to use low-frequency spherical features, i.e. lmax ≤ 10, due to the computational costs of higher-
182   frequency features scaling significantly when applied on a per node basis. In each case, the input
183   signal is initially spatially dispersed in R3 and at the end of the encoder, the signal is aggregated into
184   a single feature vector representing the multi-channel spherical signal in Fourier space.
185   In Figure 2, we illustrate a G2Sphere model with mesh inputs which uses Equiformer V2 Liao et al.
186   (2023), a SO(3)-equivariant transformer commonly used on 3D geometric data, as the encoder. The
187   mesh is embedded as a geometric graph with node and edge features. The node feature for node i
188   is the position xi ∈ R3 and the edge features are the spherical harmonic embedding of the relative
189   positions eij = Y l (xj − xi ), where Y l : R3 → R2l+1 are the spherical harmonics and i, j are
190   vertices connected by an edge. Then a set of node-wise spherical features are learned over the graph
191   which are mean pooled to form a multi-channel spherical latent space.

      One drawback to using harmonics to approximate functions in this manner is that if the maximum
210   frequency Lmax is low, then their resolution will also be low. The SH excel at capturing smooth,
211   low-frequency components, but struggle with sharp features or discontinuous behavior unless a large
212   number of harmonics are used, i.e. high L. As a result traditional equivariant decoder architectures,
213   which operate with a relatively small number of harmonics (L ≤ 10), struggle in domains where
214   higher output fidelity is required. G2Sphere uses two techniques to improve output fidelity: fre-
215   quency up-sampling where we gradually increase the frequency from the encoder’s frequency Lenc
      to the output frequency Ldec and trainable spherical non-linearities (TSNL) (Bonev et al., 2023).

      5     E XPERIMENTS
238
239   We systematically evaluate G2Sphere across three different domains: two supervised learning do-
240   mains, where we learn a mapping from object meshes to spherical radar and drag signals, and a
241   policy learning domain, where we learn a mapping between states and state-action values. These
242   evaluations benchmark the performance of G2Sphere on dense (e.g., radar response) and sparse (e.g.,
243   drag coefficient) spherical signals and with complex (object mesh) and simple (object keypoint) in-
244   put geometries. G2Sphere consistently outperforms the prior state-of-the-art in all domains. In
245   the following, we provide an overview of each domain, our evaluation methodology, and our key
246   findings.

248   5.1    R ADAR AND D RAG MODELING FROM M ESHES
249
      Many modeling tasks take an object mesh as input and predict a physical property of that object. If
250
      the property depends on a direction vector, that is, a spherical coordinate (θ, ϕ), then the output may
251
      be represented as a continuous function defined on the 2-sphere. We evaluate the performance of
252   G2Sphere against baselines on two such tasks, predicting radar response and aerodynamic drag.
253
254   Radar Prediction. Due to the expense of collecting real-world radar data, we simulate radar re-
255
      sponses for a variety of different meshes using a physical optics method to approximate the elec-
      tromagnetic waves (Balanis, 2012). Specifically, we generate two mesh datasets, Asym and Frusta.
256
      See Fig. 11 for examples. The Asym dataset is a collection of randomly scaled basic shapes (cubes,
257
      cylinders, and spheres), with randomly generated protrusions to make the radar response asym-
258   metric. The Frusta dataset represents a collection of complex 3D shapes, where each mesh is a
259   combination of several basic components stacked together to form roll-symmetric objects. Due
260   to this symmetry, we evaluate each frusta mesh over 360 orientations on the non-symmetric axis,
261   θ ∈ [0, 2π] to generate the associated radar responses. The Asym meshes are evaluated over both
262   axes, θ ∈ [0, 2π], ϕ ∈ [0, π], where θ is discretized over 60 values and ϕ over 20.

      5.2   P OLICY L EARNING
392
393   In order to demonstrate the flexibility of G2Sphere for high-frequency continuous spherical signal
394   learning, we apply G2Sphere on four policy learning tasks from two benchmarks (Florence et al.,
395   2022; Panerati et al., 2021). Although there are many potential policy learning algorithms to which
396   we could apply G2Sphere, in this work we explore the application of G2Sphere to behavioral cloning
397   (BC) (Pomerleau, 1988). BC casts the policy learning task as a supervised learning problem where
398   the agent learns to imitate a set of expert demonstrations. Following Florence et al. (2022), we
399   formulate BC as a conditional energy-based modeling (EBM) LeCun et al. (2006) problem. EMBs
400   define a policy through an energy function which assigns an energy value to each state-action pair.

521   6   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
522
523   In this work, we present G2Sphere, a novel approach to learning mappings from complex geometric
524   inputs to continuous spherical signals. We evaluated G2Sphere across a wide range of challenging
525
      domains including radar response prediction, drag modeling, and policy learning for navigation
      and demonstrated improved performance on all tasks. Additionally, we demonstrated G2Sphere
526
      is capable of zero-shot super-resolution, full-resolution generalization to unseen geometries, the
527
      natural ability to model multimodality, and vastly improved inference times.
528
529   There are a number of limitations which future work could improve upon. First, in regards to the
530   radar and drag domains, the output fidelity of G2Sphere is heavily dependent on the maximum har-
531
      monic frequency of the model. Unfortunately, current implementations of the spherical harmonics
      in equivariant network architectures result in an cubic increase in computational requirements as the
532
      harmonic frequency increases, thereby limiting the expressivity of our model. However, there do ex-
533
      ist more efficient methods for calculating the spherical harmonics which would allow for increased
534   maximum frequencies in future work (Wang et al., 2018; Schaeffer, 2013). Similarly, in this work
535   we model the radial component of the spherical coordinates by either outputting a discrete set of
536   spheres for different radii or by including it as an implicit variable. An alternate method would be to
537   incorporate the radial component into the Fourier basis functions allowing for efficient computation
538   of the all spheres continuously within some defined radial bounds (Wang et al., 2009). Finally, in
539   this work we only explore 2D and 3D positional control policies and have omitted full 6DoF pose
      control. The model can be extended to 6DoF by applying inverse FT over SO(3) instead of S 2 .

724   Equivariance. A function is equivariant if it respects the symmetries of its input and output spaces.
725   Specifically, a function F : X → Y is equivariant with respect to a symmetry group G, if it com-
726
      mutes with all transformations g ∈ G, F (ρx (g)x) = ρy (g)F (x), where ρx and ρy are the repre-
      sentations of the group G that define how the group element g ∈ G acts on x ∈ X and y ∈ Y
727
      respectively. An equivariant function is a mathematical way of expressing that F is symmetric with
728
      respect to G; if we evaluate F for various transformed versions of the same input, we should obtain
729   transformed versions of the same output.
730

732
      dinate frame is arbitrary, many task functions f should be SO(3)-equivariant. For neural networks
      to incorporate this sort of geometric reasoning, it is necessary to parameterize signals f : X → R,
733
      where X = S 2 or X = SO(3) in a way that is both computationally efficient and easy to apply
734
      rotations to. Cohen et al. (2018) provide an effective solution using the truncated basis of spherical
735   harmonics Yml for signals defined over S 2 and Wigner D-matrix coefficients Dmn   l

      In this section, we describe the details of our PyTorch harmonics packages which implements the
759
      Polar and Spherical Harmonics used in this work. While there are a number of existing spherical
760   harmonics implementations (Bonev et al., 2023), they are tailored to the tasks that they solve, e.g.
761   PDEs, and therefore do not integrate well with the equivariant neural networks used in this work (e.g.
762   e3nn or escnn). Additionally, there are, to the best of our knowledge, no PyTorch implementations
763   of the Polar Harmonics (detailed in Appendix C). To address these shortcomings, we developed
764   our own differentiable implementations of the Polar and Spherical harmonics in PyTorch which
765   we utilize throughout this work. The design goals of this package were two fold: (1) allow for
766   the efficient integration of the harmonics with the output of e3nn or escnn and (2) enable the
767   calculation of the harmonics on both a pre-defined grid of coordinates and at specific coordinates.
768   There are two considerations to make when tailoring our package to fit together with e3nn and
769   escnn. First, these equivariant neural networks model the Fourier coefficients as the irreducible
770   representations of the symmetry group and, therefor, our implementation needs to use the same
771   representations. We note that this restriction makes a number of previous implementations such
772   as Bonev et al. (2023) a poor fit as they use different derivations of the harmonics with different
773   Fourier coefficient specifications. Secondly, because these equivariant models typically operate in
774
      real space, we need the harmonics to be in real space as opposed to complex. For example, we use
      the sin and cos form of the circular harmonics (Appendix C) to match the dimensionality of the
775
      SO(2) irreducible representations.
776
777   The second goal of our package is enable efficient batch computation of the harmonics over many
778   values. We require the ability to both compute the harmonics at specific coordinates (commonly used
779   during training to match the partial views u in our dataset) and to pre-compute a grid of harmonics
780
      values at pre-defined coordinates (typically used at inference time to decrease computation time). In
      order to achieve this, when instantiating the harmonics, a grid of harmonics values are pre-computed
781
      at the desired resolution and a number of intermediate variables are saved during this process. These
782
      intermediate variables are specific to the type of harmonics but, in general, they are components of
783   the harmonics independent to the coordinates such as the normalization constants and Fourier mode
784   components. This pre-computation allows for the efficient evaluation of the harmonics and reduces
785   training times.
