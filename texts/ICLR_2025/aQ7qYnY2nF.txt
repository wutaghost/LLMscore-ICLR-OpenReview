011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Video encoders optimize compression for human perception by minimizing re-
014            construction error under bit-rate constraints. In many modern applications such
015            as autonomous driving, an overwhelming majority of videos serve as input for AI
016
               systems performing tasks like object recognition or segmentation, rather than be-
               ing watched by humans. It is therefore useful to optimize the encoder for a down-
017
               stream task instead of for perceptual image quality. However, a major challenge
018
               is how to combine such downstream optimization with existing standard video
019            encoders, which are highly efficient and popular. Here, we address this challenge
020            by controlling the Quantization Parameters (QPs) at the macro-block level to op-
021            timize the downstream task. This granular control allows us to prioritize encoding
022            for task-relevant regions within each frame. We formulate this optimization prob-
023            lem as a Reinforcement Learning (RL) task, where the agent learns to balance
024            long-term implications of choosing QPs on both task performance and bit-rate
025            constraints. Notably, our policy does not require the downstream task as an input
026            during inference, making it suitable for streaming applications and edge devices
027
               such as vehicles. We demonstrate significant improvements in two tasks, car de-
               tection, and ROI (saliency) encoding. Our approach improves task performance
028
               for a given bit rate compared to traditional task agnostic encoding methods, paving
029
               the way for more efficient task-aware video compression.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Video compression is an essential and widely studied problem (Bhaskaran & Konstantinides, 1997;
035
      Wenger, 2003; Sullivan et al., 2012; Bross et al., 2021; Kufa & Kratochvil, 2017). Most video
036   compression algorithms are designed for preserving how a video is perceived by people. With
037   the success of computer vision applications, many videos are used in automated systems, from
038   autonomous drones and cars, to security cameras, and in downstream tasks, like object detection or
039   recognition. In these cases, compression must prioritize regions relevant to the task at hand (e.g.,
040   allocating more bits to objects than to the background). We illustrate the need for a downstream task
041   aware compression scheme in Figure 1. Basically, raw data is too expensive and existing encoders
042   are geared to optimize video PSNR, which may “waste” bits on task-irrelevant parts.
043   Real-world deployment of compression systems complicates matters further. Video data must be
044   collected in real time from devices, using low computational resources, and be usable for training
045   various models across multiple tasks, not just for immediate inference. Furthermore, due to compu-
046   tational and hardware constraints, compression must be done without access to the ground truth for
047   the downstream tasks during the encoding process. Our goal is to tackle these challenges by pro-
048   viding a general video compression method that can be adapted to any task, operates in real-time,
049   imposes low computational demands on the encoding side, and requires no ground-truth labels.
050   Many existing approaches for task-aware compression rely on deep encoding (Lu et al., 2019).
051   This makes them computationally expensive and unsuitable for real-time applications or resource-
052   constrained environments. In contrast, standardized video encoders such as x264 (Merritt & Vanam,
053   2006), are highly efficient but are not designed for adapting compression to specific tasks in real-
      time. Some previous research proposed to use standardised video encoders for downstream tasks, but


                                                       1

054
      usually for a specific task, and commonly employ big models before encodingShi & Chen (2020). As
055   one example, Xie et al. (2022) perform semantic compression by applying a heavy feature extractor
056   before encoding using a ground-truth segmentation maps. While performing well at this setup, their
057   method requires large computation resources before encoding, can not be used for various tasks, and
058   is not suitable for data collection.
059
      In this paper, we propose RL-RC-DoT, a novel solution to the problem of tuning an efficient real-
060
      time video compression system to a downstream task without access to its ground truth labels during
061   inference. Our approach integrates a lightweight network on the video encoder side of the a x264
062   encoder, trained to control the encoding process such that the decoded output is ideal for the task
063   at hand. By leveraging standardized codecs, we ensure that our method is both computationally
064   efficient and easily deployable across a range of devices. The solution allows for real-time video
065   compression without requiring ground truth for downstream tasks.
066
      Coping with these challenges is hard. Standardized encoders are not differentiable, making it dif-
067   ficult to optimize bit allocation for specific tasks. To overcome this, we introduce a reinforcement
068   learning (RL) mechanism that controls the Quantization Parameter (QP) at the macro-block level,
069   adjusting the bit allocation for each block of the frame dynamically. This allows us to efficiently
070   manage the bit-rate budget while optimizing task performance over an entire sequence of video
071   frames. Our experiments demonstrate that this approach yields significant improvements in rate-
072   distortion trade-offs, not just for the task the encoder was trained on, but also for other related
073   tasks, showcasing the robustness of our method. Furthermore, we demonstrate its generalizabil-
074   ity by showing how an encoder trained on one model can improve performance for other models
075
      without additional tuning.
076   In summary, this paper makes the following contributions. (1) We design the first task-aware video
077   compression method that builds on top of existing encoders and does not require solving the task
078   during inference. (2) We show how to optimize the rate parameter of every macro-block in the frame
079   while optimizing the performance of a downstream task on the reconstructed video under bit-rate
080   constraints. (3) We design an architecture that outputs multiple actions, a tailored reward for this
081
      problem, and a task-prediction loss term. (4) We show improved rate-distortion trade-off for our
      agent on two tasks, car detection and ROI encoding with only small interference to image quality,
082
      and further show robustness to task shift, when tested on a related-but-different task than used for
083
      training.

086   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
087
      Task-aware video compression with unrestricted compute. Several previous studies proposed
088
      video compression methods that are aware of a downstream task. Zhang et al. (2024) explored
089
      content-specific filters to improve post-processing in video codecs, optimizing them for machine
090   vision tasks like object detection and segmentation. Ge et al. (2024) introduced an encoder con-
091   trol for deep video compression that adapts to multiple tasks using a single pre-trained decoder,
092   showing significant bit-rate improvement for object detection and tracking. Shor & Johnston (2022)
093   highlighted the limitations of classical codecs in medical videos, proposing learned compression
094   models to allocate more bits to medically relevant regions. Elgamal et al. (2020) presented a se-
095   mantic video encoding system that enhances object detection by selectively decompressing frames
096   in surveillance streams. Li et al. (2024) developed a distributed compression framework that adjusts
097   to varying bandwidth in multi-sensor networks to optimize task performance. Windsheimer et al.
098
      (2024) introduced an annotation-free optimization strategy that aligns video coding with machine
      tasks, improving rate savings without relying on ground truth data. Additionally, While Wu et al.
099
      (2024) focused on real-time, quality-scalable video decoding, it also evaluated the codec perfor-
100
      mance on machine-based tasks.
101
102   All these approaches share a common limitation, they do not use the existing highly optimized
103   and widely prevalent existing video compression ecosystem like the open-source x264 (Merritt &
104
      Vanam, 2006). The challenge therefore remains to design video compression systems that build on
      top of existing technology, but can be tuned in a-content adaptive way to a set of downstream tasks.
105
106   Task-aware video compression with standard encoders Another body of works does employ
107   standardized encoders, but does not consider the inter-frame dependencies. Singh et al. (2022) and
      Fischer et al. (2020) optimize the CTU partitioning to improve the compression for a downstream

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      task. Galteri et al. (2018) uses a threshold on the saliency map to allocate more bits to important
109   regions, while Cai et al. (2021) optimizes over the modelled relation between each block parameter
110   and the task performance. Li et al. (2021) uses RL for optimizing macro-block QPs, but does so
111   in each frame separately, where the sequence is defined over the sequence of macro-blocks in the
112   same frame. In our work we output all macro-block QPs with one policy and the sequence is defined

141   2.1   V IDEO C OMPRESSION
142
      Video compression is a process of reducing the size of digital video files while maintaining accept-
143
      able visual quality. It is a crucial technology in the modern digital age, enabling efficient storage,
144   transmission, and streaming of video content across various platforms and devices. The primary goal
145   of video compression is to eliminate redundant and less perceptible information from the video data
146   according to constraints such as bit-rate of the target video, while maintaining good visual quality.
147
      One key aspect of video compression is the use of Quantization Parameters (QP). QP values control
148
      the level of compression applied to the video data, with higher values resulting in more compression
149
      but lower quality, and lower values preserving more detail but producing larger file sizes. In video
150   encoding, QP can be applied at different levels of granularity. Frame QP refers to setting a single QP
151   value for an entire frame, which is useful for maintaining consistent quality across the frame but may
152   not be optimal for all areas. Per-macro-block (MB) QP, on the other hand, allows for finer control
153   by assigning different QP values to individual MBs within a frame, usually in small perturbations
154   from a pre-assigned frame QP. This approach enables the encoder to apply more compression to
155   less important or visually complex areas while preserving quality in critical regions. Per-MB QP
156   can lead to more efficient compression and better overall visual quality, as it adapts to the local
157   characteristics of the video content. It is especially suitable for task-aware optimization since most
158
      tasks target specific areas in the picture (for instance object detection and segmentation).
159   The effectiveness of video compression is typically measured by comparing the compressed video’s
160   file size and visual quality to the original. Metrics like Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) and
161   Structural Similarity Index (SSIM; Wang et al. (2004)) are often used to objectively assess quality.
      When comparing two encoders the compression efficiency is usually considered. To do so, a video


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is encoded in several desired bit-rates with each encoder to form a rate-distortion (RD) curve, where
163   the y axis is the quality measure, usually PSNR. If one encoder’s curve is higher than the other,
164   it means it suffers less distortion for the same bit-rate rendering it more efficient. If we integrate
165   over the entire curve, and average the result over multiple videos, we obtain a quantity specifying
166   how much more efficient one encoder than the other, a quantity referred to as Bjontegaard delta rate
167   (BD-rate) (Wiegand et al., 2003).
168
      With the increasing usages of videos for machine vision, many researchers have recognized the
169   need for task-aware compression and proposed a suitable evaluation metric (Kong et al., 2016; Shi
170   & Chen, 2020). The most straightforward metric which we also use in this paper is obtained by
171   replacing the PSNR in the RD-curve (the y-axis) with a task-specific loss measure such as mIOU or
172   detection precision and calculating the BD-rate with respect to the adjusted curves.
173
      One may wonder, if a downstream task is given, why is video compression needed at all? For in-
174
      stance, in the autonomous vehicle example, if a car detector is available, why not run that detector
175   on the vehicle, and save only its decision instead of the compressed video. There are several strong
176   reasons not to take this approach: (1) Many downstream tasks require resource-heavy networks
177   that cannot run efficiently on-device, making it impractical to process the data locally. (2) Sending
178   only task-specific features limits human interpretability, as there would be no watchable video for
179   explainability. (3) This also confines the data to a single task, preventing its reuse for other ap-
180   plications or analyses. (4) Large-scale data collection, such as in autonomous driving, depends on
181   compressed video storage; using features alone would limit future training and fine-tuning oppor-
182   tunities. (5) Task-specific features are often tied to a particular model, making them incompatible
183
      with new models, while compressed video remains adaptable across different systems. We show
      that our method allows different models to achieve high performance using the same compressed
184
      data. This is also the reason why we aim to develop a method that still preserves a video that would
185
      be meaningful to a person.

      3.1   M ACRO -B LOCK R EWARD I NFORMATION
263
264   In most RL problems, the reward is a black-box directly mapping the state to a continuous score. Re-
265   cent literature (Ye et al., 2021) has demonstrated that predictive modeling of rewards – implemented
266   as auxiliary heads alongside policy or value networks can significantly enhance agent performance.
267   In our setup, the reward presents a unique characteristic: the reward signals for various downstream
268   tasks are often compositional, derived from aggregating scores across granular components of the
269   input frame. For example, in the case of saliency-weighted PSNR, the reward is computed by
      aggregating per-pixel reconstruction errors. Leveraging this decomposable nature of rewards, we


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      propose augmenting the learning process with an auxiliary prediction loss for these sub-scores dur-
271   ing backpropagation. Specifically, we introduce a block-wise prediction loss that aims to predict
272   the individual block reward information that contribute to the overall task score. This approach
273   of incorporating auxiliary prediction loss for macro-block level reward information is expected to
274   enhance the agent’s performance. Firstly, it provides a more granular learning signal, allowing the
275   agent to understand the impact of its actions on individual components of the reward. Secondly, by
276   learning to predict these sub-scores, the agent develops a richer internal representation of the task
277   structure. Lastly, this method aligns the agent’s learning more closely with the actual composition of
278   the reward, potentially leading to faster convergence and more stable learning. We show the effect
279
      of this improvement in Section 5.4.

288   4.1   DATASET
289
290   We trained and evaluated RL-RC-DoT using a subset of video streams from the BDD100K dataset
291   (Yu et al., 2020), a large-scale driving video dataset, with multi-task annotations. We reconstructed
292
      the raw data from the videos and to allow faster training time, we resized them to a smaller resolution

293
      curves with respect to the downstream tasks of car detection precision, when encoded with the
294
      standard x264 codec (Wiegand et al., 2003). We specifically excluded streams that showed zero
295   precision across most target bit-rates. This approach ensured that our dataset presented meaningful
296   challenges for compression optimization. Our final dataset comprised of 172 streams in total, with
297   65 streams used for training our agent, 7 streams used for evaluation on different hyper-parameters
298   and 100 streams reserved for testing. For reproducibility, we provided a detailed list of the specific
299   stream used in our experiments in appendix A.2 of this paper.

301   4.2   E VALUATION METRICS : RD- CURVE AND BD- RATE
302
303   Since compression is a constraint optimization problem, it is standard to depict results using a Rate-
304   Distortion (RD) curve. An RD-curve illustrates the trade-off between bit-rate constraint and quality
305   in video compression (see examples in Figure 3). RD-curves are traditionally used with PSNR, but
306
      are equally applicable to task-specific metrics like precision/recall for a detection task or saliency-
      weighted PSNR for ROI-based encoding. These RD-curves allow us to evaluate compression effi-
307
      ciency for any downstream tasks on reconstructed videos.
308
309   BD-rate (Bjøntegaard Delta rate; (Bjøntegaard, 2001)) is a widely used metric in video compres-
310   sion to compare the efficiency of different encoding methods. This method calculates the average
311   difference in bit-rate between two rate-distortion (RD) curves at the same quality level. The BD-
312
      rate represents the percentage of bit-rate savings that one encoding method achieves over another
      while maintaining equivalent video quality performance. Therefore, a negative BD-rate indicates
313
      that the test method requires less bits than the reference method to achieve the same quality / task
314
      performance.

527   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   Machine learning for videos understanding became prevalent in numerous applications, but impose
530   high costs of storing, making fast encoding and low bit-rate critical. Task-aware compression has
531   huge potential, but existing methods have critical limitations, like heavy compute or dependency on
532   ground truth task data for compression. We develop an efficient RL solution which encodes every
533   frame in real time while optimizing the future bit-rate and task performance on the reconstructed
534   video. Our learned policy is robust against changes in the downstream models for the same task
535   and to closely related tasks, showing large important potential for data collection for autonomous
536
      vehicle, patient monitoring and robotics.
537   Limitations: Training our models involves encoding and performing the downstream task per
538   frame, and this may slow down converge depending on the complexity of the downstream task.
539   Also, generalizing across video resolutions may be hard because it affects the size of action space
      and the complexity of the learning problem.

840
841   To train the policy, we use the PPO algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017), where the architecture of the
842
      policy is as follows: The per-block statistics are processed through a compact convolutional neural
      network (CNN) comprising three convolutional layers. These layers employ kernel sizes of 3x3 or
843
      4x4 with a stride of 1. The resulting features are subsequently flattened and concatenated with the
844
      global statistics. A fully connected layer then derives a latent representation of dimension 64. This
845   latent representation serves as input to three distinct fully connected networks: the value network
846   (critic), the policy network (actor), and the reward prediction network described in the following
847   subsection. A diagram of the full system is given in Figure 2.
