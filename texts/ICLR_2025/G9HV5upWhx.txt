009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            After training on the source domain, deep learning models often struggle to gen-
012            eralize effectively to unknown target domains with differing data distributions.
013            This is an even more severe challenge when the target domain is not available.
014            In this paper, we tackle the problem of domain-generalized medical image seg-
015            mentation by introducing a novel semantic-guided contrastive generalization al-
016
               gorithm, termed SgCG. The method aligns different multi-source domains based
               on semantic distributions to learn domain-invariant features. Specifically, we im-
017
               plement a novel contrastive generalization loss at the pixel level that incorporates
018
               semantic distributions from the source domains. This approach facilitates the clus-
019            tering of pixel representations from the same category while effectively separating
020            those from different categories, thereby improving the model’s segmentation per-
021            formance while learning domain-invariant features. Furthermore, we establish an
022            upper bound estimation for the SgCG approach by integrating a contrastive gen-
023            eralization loss which includes an infinite number of both similar and dissimilar
024            pixel pairs. Despite the simplicity and straightforwardness of the approach, our
025            empirical analysis reveals mechanisms that can maximize the potential of SgCG.
026            We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach using two public benchmarks
027
               for generalizable segmentation in medical images, where it achieves state-of-the-
               art performance.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Image segmentation Wu et al. (2024), a long-standing research focus in computer vision, poses a
033   core challenge in medical image analysis. Within this domain, tasks may involve various imaging
034   techniques, including microscopic examination Schoch & Maywald (1956), Computed Tomogra-
035   phy (CT) Buzug (2011), X-rays Hessenbruch (2002) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) Katti
036   et al. (2011). They can span different biomedical areas, such as retinal imaging, brain, thoracic, ab-
037   dominal, or even individual cells, and may target diverse structures like cardiac valves or ventricles.
038   This variety has led to the development of numerous specialized segmentation tools, each optimized
039
      for specific tasks or closely related sets of tasks. In recent years, deep learning models have emerged
      as the dominant approach for medical image segmentation Ouyang et al. (2022); Azad et al. (2024);
040
      Song et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023), driving significant advancements in the field.
041
042   In the field of image segmentation Minaee et al. (2021), a major challenge is how to overcome the
043   performance drop of models when faced with out-of-distribution samples. This issue is particularly
044   prominent in the medical domain, as clinical researchers and other scientists continuously define
045
      new segmentation tasks based on evolving population characteristics, scientific advancements, and
      clinical objectives. However, domain adaptation Ben-David et al. (2006); You et al. (2019) re-
046
      quires access to target domain data, which is often difficult to obtain in real-world scenarios due to
047
      privacy concerns associated with medical data. Compared to domain adaptation, domain general-
048   ization Wang et al. (2021b) is a more general task that only requires training a labeled segmentation
049   model on the source domain, allowing it to generalize to unseen target domain data. The difficulty
050   of domain generalization in image segmentation tasks lies in the fact that, during training, target
051   domain data is typically unavailable, making it challenging for the model to learn the characteris-
052   tics of the target domain. A common solution is to enhance the diversity of training data through
053   image-level Luo et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2017) data augmentation. In simple terms, by expand-
      ing the source domain dataset to include more representative diverse samples, the model’s ability to

055   is available. However, current methods for increasing diversity mainly focus on transformations in
056   the image space, making it more complex to generate images in new domains, as it is difficult to
057   specify or learn effective image synthesis strategies.

078
      Inspired by implicit semantic data augmentation algorithms Wang et al. (2021a), we propose a
079
      new perspective for addressing the domain generalization problem in semantic segmentation. Our
080   method learns domain-invariant features by attracting similar pixels and repelling dissimilar pix-
081   els in a pixel-wise representation, thereby reducing domain shift. We first estimate the covariance
082   matrix online for each category, capturing intra-class variation. Then, we sample directions from
083   a zero-mean multivariate normal distribution with the estimated covariance as variance, applying
084   these directions to the features of training samples from that category (see Figure. 1). This approach
085   generates diverse samples from the estimated distributions, enhancing source domain data. Sec-
086   ondly, we observe that enhancing the intra-class compactness and inter-class separability of pixel
087   representations can significantly improve the performance of dense pixel classifiers. Therefore, we
088
      separate pixel-wise representations in both the source and target domains, implicitly defining an
      infinite number of positive sample pairs for each pixel by sampling from the estimated distribu-
089
      tions of the same category. Based on this, we design a pixel-level special contrastive loss function
090
      for contrastive adaptation. We also derive an upper bound for this loss function, ensuring its ef-
091   fectiveness in practical applications. In essence, this represents a novel and robust alternative loss
092   function. Since explicit data samples do not need to be generated, we term our algorithm semantic-
093   guided contrastive generalization (SgCG). We recommend using the popular framework RAM Zhou
094   et al. (2022b) as a baseline to validate the effectiveness of our method SgCG. Experimental results
095   demonstrate that aligning source and target pixel representations with semantic distributions through
096   contrastive learning can effectively reduce domain discrepancies and enhance generalization capa-
097   bility in the target domain. In summary, our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
098
099          • We propose a novel semantic-guided contrastive generalization (SgCG) for medical image
100            segmentation, which encourages the source feature augmentations in an implicit manner.
101
             • By deriving the upper bound of the expected contrastive loss using statistical data from the
102
               distribution of each category, we enable the learning of invariant and distinctive pixel-wise
103
               representations to be both straightforward and effective.
104
105          • Extensive empirical evaluations on several competitive benchmarks, including Fun-
106            dus Wang et al. (2020) and Prostate Liu et al. (2020), demonstrate that SgCG significantly
107            improves the baseline model. Additionally, analytical evidence is provided to validate its
               effectiveness.

294     3.1   E XPERIMENT DATASETS
295
        We evaluated our method on two public domain generalization medical image segmentation datasets
296
        including Fundus Wang et al. (2020) and Prostate Liu et al. (2020).
297
298     Fundus dataset contains retinal fundus images from four different medical centers, primarily used
299     for optic cup and disc segmentation. Each domain has been divided into training and test sets.
300     During the preprocessing stage, we followed previous studies Zhou et al. (2022b) to center-crop all
301
        images in the Fundus dataset, using a bounding box of 800 × 800. Subsequently, we randomly
        resized and cropped each image to obtain a 256 × 256 region as input for the network. We will train
302
        our model on the training set of the source domain and evaluate it on the testing set of the target
303
        domain.
304
305     Prostate dataset collected T2-weighted MRI prostate images from six different data sources, specif-
306     ically for prostate segmentation. All images were cropped to the 3D prostate region, and the 2D
307
        slices in the axial plane were resized to 384 × 384. During model training, we fed the 2D slices of
        prostate images into our model and normalized the data for both datasets individually to intensity
308
        values in the range of [−1, 1].

312
        For evaluation, we employ commonly used metrics: the Dice coefficient (Dice) and average surface
313
        distance (ASD) to quantitatively assess the segmentation results for both the overall area and surface
314     shape. A higher Dice coefficient indicates better performance, while ASD has the opposite impli-
315     cation. To mitigate randomness, we conducted three repeated experiments and report the average
316     performance.

318     3.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
319
320     We chose RAM Zhou et al. (2022b) as our baseline model and have adopted a U-Net-based encoder-
321     decoder architecture for our segmentation framework. Our segmentation decoder is designed to learn
322     domain-invaraint feature, closely mirroring the RAM decoder. The model train for 400 epochs on the
323     Fundus dataset and 200 epochs on the prostate dataset, utilizing a batch size of 8 for each dataset.
        To optimize our model, we employed the Adam optimizer, initiating the learning rate at 0.001.

532
533
      In this paper, we present a novel semantic-guided contrastive generalization for medical image
      segmentation. To improve the generalization in DG segmentation, we introduce a particular con-
534
      trastive loss at pixel label, which implicitly involves the joint learning of an infinite number of
535
      similar/dissimilar pixel pairs for each pixel-wise representation of the source domains. Finally, we
536   get an error bound on this formulation which can assess the extent of our approach to the practi-
537   cal applications. Our method can successfully adapt the segmentation model to the unseen target
538   domain through pixel-wise alignment guided by semantic distributions. The experimental results
539   demonstrate the superiority of SgCG on various benchmarks.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
652
      In the area of medical image segmentation Elnakib et al. (2011); Shen et al. (2010), domain gener-
653
      alization (DG) Zhou et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2017; 2021); Qiao et al. (2020); Muandet et al. (2013)
654
      has emerged as a critical area of research, focusing on developing models that can generalize well
655   across different datasets and imaging environments. For a new segmentation problem, models are
656   typically from scratch, requiring substantial design and tuning. Existing DG methods mainly include
657   meta-learning methods Liu et al. (2021b;a; 2020), feature-based methods Wang et al. (2020); Song
658   et al. (2022) and data-based methods Zhao et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2023); You et al. (2024).
659
      Meta-learning divides a set of source domains into meta-train and meta-test subsets, employing
660
      meta-optimization to iteratively update model parameters, thereby enhancing performance on the
661   meta-test subset and simulating the scenario of inferring on unseen domains. Liu et al.Liu et al.
662   (2021b) incorporate designed constraints into the gradient-based meta-learning approach, enabling
663   the model to extract robust anatomical features useful for predicting segmentation masks in a semi-
664   supervised manner. FedDG Liu et al. (2021a) introduces a novel problem setting for federated
665   domain generalization and presents an innovative approach that utilizes continuous frequency space
666   interpolation alongside a boundary-oriented episodic learning scheme. SAML Liu et al. (2020)
667   employs a shape-aware meta-learning strategy to enhance model generalization in prostate MRI
668   segmentation. However, the meta-optimization process is highly time-consuming, as it requires
669
      considering all potential splitting results of the meta-train and meta-test subsets during training.
670   Feature-based approaches utilize domain-adaptive feature calibration or learn domain-invariant fea-
671   tures to address domain generalization. DoFE Wang et al. (2020) introduces a novel domain code
672   prediction branch and learning strategy to measure the similarities between input test images and
673   various source-domain data, facilitating domain-oriented feature embedding. GLFRNet Song et al.
674   (2022) proposes two innovative modules: a global feature reconstruction module and a local feature
675
      reconstruction module, aimed at addressing the issues of insufficient global context feature extrac-
      tion and spatial information restoration within encoder-decoder networks. However, these methods
676
      do not explicitly obtain domain-invariant features for domain generalization, nor do they effectively
677
      separate features into purely domain-specific and domain-invariant representations, which limits
678   their performance in this area.
679
680   Data-based approaches typically employ various data augmentation strategies to enhance the
681
      model’s generalizability. Zhao et al.Zhao et al. (2019) introduced a learning-based method for data
      augmentation, demonstrating its effectiveness in one-shot medical image segmentation. Zhang et
682
      al.Zhang et al. (2023) utilized the Segment Anything model to augment image inputs for commonly
683
      used medical image segmentation models. MONA You et al. (2024) established a set of objectives
684   that significantly enhance segmentation quality. However, the effectiveness of data augmentation
685   largely depends on its ability to cover the data distribution in unseen domains, necessitating empiri-
686   cal settings and potentially data-specific modifications.
