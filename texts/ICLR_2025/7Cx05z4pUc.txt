                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Grokking is a delayed transition from memorisation to generalisation in neural
010            networks. It poses challenges for efficient learning, particularly in structured tasks
011            and small-data regimes. This paper explores grokking in modular arithmetic, ex-
012            plicitly focusing on modular division with a modulus of 97. We introduce a novel
013            learning method called Decomposed Learning, which leverages Singular Value
014            Decomposition (SVD) to modify the weight matrices of neural networks. Decom-
015            posed learning reduces or avoids grokking by changing the representation of the
016            weight matrix, A, into the product of three matrices U , Σ and V T , promoting
017            the discovery of compact, generalisable representations early in the learning pro-
018
               cess. Through empirical evaluations on the modular division task, we show that
               Decomposed Learning significantly reduces the effect of grokking and, in some
019
               cases, eliminates it. Moreover, Decomposed Learning can reduce the parame-
020
               ters required for practical training, enhancing model efficiency and generalisation.
021            These results suggest that our SVD-based method provides a practical and scalable
022            solution for mitigating grokking, with implications for broader transformer-based
023            learning tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Understanding the learning dynamics of over-parameterised deep learning models is a critical area of
029
      research, especially as these models are increasingly deployed in the real world. Although significant
      advances have been made in optimising training and improving performance, the finer details of
030
      how neural networks generalise remain a significant challenge with problems such as the capacity to
031
      memorise randomly labelled data (Zhang et al., 2017), deep double-decent (Nakkiran et al., 2019)
032   and grokking (Power et al., 2022). The grokking phenomenon introduced by Power et al. (2022) is
033   where models experience a significant delay between generalisation and the point at which a model
034   achieves perfect training accuracy. This phenomenon suggests inefficiencies in how neural networks
035   learn and training setups, which supports Liu et al. (2023), who show that the grokking phenomena
036   can be induced on MNIST LeCun et al. (2010), IMDB dataset for sentiment analysis Maas et al.
037   (2011). Although grokking can easily avoided for MNIST and IBMD datasets with correct training
038   hyperparameters.
039   In this paper, we investigate the original grokking problem using the division mod 97 task, using a
040   two layer transformer, aiming to understand better the underlying mechanisms that give rise to this
041   delay in generalisation. We propose a novel approach that leverages Singular Value Decomposition
042   (SVD) to change the representation of the weight matrices of the layers within the transformer
043   model, aiming to mitigate and avoid delayed generalisation. By applying SVD, we decompose
044   the weight matrix, A, into three matrices, Uk , Σk and VkT , where k is the rank, and explore how
045   this representation, with different layers, ranks, and amounts of training data, affects the learning
046
      process, specifically delayed generalisation.
047   In this paper, we ask:
048
049          • How does the decomposed representation of the weight matrix, A, affect training?
050          • What is the relationship between the rank of a weight matrix and the amount of training
051            data?
052          • How are different layers affected by the decomposition and rank?
053
      The questions are explored in-depth, and our contributions are:

064   2     R ELATED W ORK
065
066   2.1   G ROKKING
067
068   Grokking is the name given to a phenomenon discovered by Power et al. (2022) when training
069   a transformer architecture on binary operations beyond the point where full training accuracy is
070   reached. Effectively, the training data is memorised (training accuracy is perfect or near-perfect),
071   while test accuracy is at the random accuracy baseline; after significantly more training, the training
072   performance is reached on the test set. Power et al. (2022) showed that increasing the training data
073
      can reduce the steps for grokking to occur. Liu et al. (2022) identified four learning phases that
      can occur during training, with grokking being one of them and could be avoided with specific
074
      hyperparameter tuning. Liu et al. (2023) showed that grokking can also occur on more complicated
075
      datasets such as image classification on MNIST. They attributed grokking to a discrepancy between
076   training and test losses at a high model weight norm, referred to as the ” LU” mechanism. Kumar
077   et al. (2024) suggests that grokking can occur as the neural network transitions from lazy (linear)
078   to rich (feature) learning. Miller et al. (2024) highlighted that grokking is not limited to neural
079   networks and showed that it can occur with Gaussian processes, linear regression and Bayesian
080   neural networks, suggesting that grokking may happen in any model where the solution is guided
081   by complexity and error.
082
083   2.2   M ATRIX D ECOMPOSITION AND D EEP L EARNING
084
085   Despite neural networks typically being trained with all their parameters, they have been shown to
086   have an intrinsic dimensionality (Li et al., 2018), allowing fewer parameters to be used to reach
087   the same performance. Aghajanyan et al. (2020) showed that pre-trained language models have a
088
      low intrinsic dimensionality. Hu et al. (2022) introduced Low-Rank Domain Adaptation (LoRA)
      as a method for finetuning the self-attention module of large language models. This method trains
089
      a weight matrix as a rank-reduced composition of two matrices. Training on this decomposition
090
      allowed finetuning to use significantly fewer training parameters while achieving the same or better
091   performance than conventional finetuning. Since methods based on similar approaches have been
092   introduced, such as LoHa (Hyeon-Woo et al., 2023), LoKa (Edalati et al., 2022), OFT (Qiu et al.,
093   2023). In addition to fine-tuning, matrix decompositions, specifically SVD, have been used to make
094   training more efficient by performing low-rank projections on the gradient updates (Zhao et al.,
095   2024; Zhang et al., 2024), compressing models with little performance degradation (Swaminathan
096   et al., 2020; Liebenwein et al., 2021). SVD has also been used dynamically through training by Paul
097   & Nelson (2021), who proposed a learning method using SVD on dense linear layers to reduce
098   the rank progressively and, by extension, the dimensionality of the network during training. It
099
      introduces two hyperparameters, the threshold to remove singular values, and how often to perform
      this operation. This method reduced the training times up to 50% with minimal impact of accuracy
100
      on audio classification problems.

103   3     D ECOMPOSED L EARNING
104
105   Neural network layers are represented by a weight matrix, A, that can represent a linear transfor-
106   mation such as rotation, scaling, shearing, reflection, or a combination. In this paper, instead of
107   learning A for each layer directly, we propose learning it in its decomposed or factorised form. To
      do this, we use the Reduced Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) outlined below.

      SVD algorithmically decomposes a m × n matrix A as the product of 3 matrices, U , Σ and V T i.e.
109
110                                                     A = U ΣV T                                       (1)
111   where U is m × r, Σ is r × r and V     T
                                                 is r × n.
112
113   Each of these has specific properties: U is comprised of r orthonormal columns that span the
114
      columns space of A, V is comprised of r orthonormal columns that span the row space of A and Σ
      is diagonal - its entries are known as Singular Values, σi (Strang, 2000).
115
116   We can further re-write A as a sum of simple matrices (known as rank one matrices) as
117                                                       r
                                                          X
118                                                A=            ui σi viT                               (2)
119                                                       i=1
120
      where r is the true rank of matrix A(r ≤ min(m, n)) and ui , vi are the columns of U and V ,
121   respectively. The importance of SVD lies in the fact that it provides a way to approximate A, such
122   that the computation of the layer activations can be reduced with a minimal loss in accuracy and
123   representational power. This is known as reducing the rank of A. This is achieved by limiting the
124   summation in (2) to k < r. Thus, the low-rank approximation is
125
                                                    A˜k = Uk Σk VkT                                      (3)
126
127   where Uk is m × k, Σk is an k × k diagonal matrix containing only the top k largest singular values
128   and VkT is k × n. k is known as the rank.
129   In this paper, we propose and explore the effects of decomposing A using SVD and training the
130   matrix product in Eq (1) as well as using the decomposition in Eq (3) for various values of k. Thus,
131   Decomposed Learning initialises the weight matrix, A, using standard methods, such as Xavier
132   Uniform and Xavier Normal (Glorot & Bengio, 2010). The weight matrix, A, is then decomposed
133   using SVD, providing initial values for U , Σ and V T and the rank is reduced to create Uk , Σk and
134   VkT . Training proceeds without retaining the above-mentioned SVD properties of orthonormality
135   and diagonality. Hence, for example, the trained Σk can be non-diagonal. After training, Ao is
136   recomposed as

      The impact of decomposed learning on the token embedding layer at rank 12, 25, 50, 74 and full
      rank 99, compared to the normally trained model (baseline), is shown in Figure 3. This Figure,
250
      specifically Figure 3a, clearly shows the grokking phenomena with the training (dotted lines) in-
251
      creasing to a near-perfect accuracy between steps 102 and 103 , while the test accuracy (solid lines)
252   is still at random accuracy. Then significantly later, between steps 104 and approximately 5 × 105 ,
253   the test accuracy sharply increases from random to perfect or near-perfect. Figure 3 shows that in-
254   creasing the training dataset size reduces the number of optimisation steps required before the model
255   can generalise, reducing the steps to grokk. This observation is in line with the original paper that
256   introduced the phenomenon.
257
      It’s important to note that Figure 3 shows that the size of the dataset affects the rank that can be
258   used in decomposed learning. This is clear when comparing the decomposed learning test accuracy
259   with rank 12 (blue) with 50%, Figure 3a, and 80%, Figure 3c of the training data. With 50% of
260   the training data, rank 12 starts to generalise later than the baseline and does not reach the same
261   test accuracy with 106 training steps. Whereas with 80% of the training data, rank 12 starts to grok
262   before the baseline. Irrespective of the training dataset size, decomposed learning performs better
263   with higher ranks. In the case of training with 80% of the dataset, Figure 3c, decomposed learning
264   on the token embedding can eradicate the phenomenon. It is also heavily reduced when using 65%
265   of the training data. This result suggests that higher ranks are needed when less representative data is
266
      available, and as the dataset becomes more representative, the layer weight matrix can be represented
      in a low-rank form.
267
268   Notably, although decomposed learning on the token embedding reduces the number of steps re-
269   quired for the test accuracy to reach the same accuracy as the train, it does not significantly reduce
      the number of steps required for the model to reach perfect or near-perfect training accuracy.

      6   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      Decomposed Learning Encourages the Learning of More Generalisable Representations
489   This method can prevent grokking even with fewer parameters (as seen in the token embedding
490   at rank 25). This structured initialisation, with orthonormality and diagonalization of the matrices
491   that are not maintained through training, introduces an implicit regularisation effect, that encourages
492   the reduction of the stable rank through training more rapidly, see Appendix D, which allows the
493   model to generalise faster when a sufficiently high rank is used.
494
495   More Data Fewer Ranks Our results show that within this setup, fewer ranks are required for
496   the model to generalise as the dataset increases and becomes more representative of the learning
497   problem. Further highlighting the need for extensive and representative datasets for efficient deep-
498   learning training. This suggests that when finetuning with methods such as LoRA, if the data does
499
      not adequately represent the solution space, more ranks should be used; however, if the data does,
      then fewer ranks can be used.
500
501
      Different Layers Can Learn With Varying Levels of Rank Reduction While Maintaining Per-
502
      formance and Mitigating or Preventing Grokking Our results show that different layers require
503   more ranks than other layers to maintain performance and reduce or avoid grokking specifically,
504   in order of the layers that require the fewest ranks to maintain performance, the token embedding,
505   output layer, feed-forward blocks, multi-head attention and position embedding. However, precisely
506   what this means regarding the learning process of neural networks is unclear and requires further
507   study.
508
509   Free Lunch for Compression These results show that often using the full-rank representation of
510   the weight matrix in U ΣV T results in a model that can avoid and mitigate the grokking phenomena
511   the most, especially in reduced data regimes such as 50% training data. This, therefore, allows for
512   a form of pre-training compression, where using decomposed learning, one can specify the size of
513
      the model required for inference, then train in the full decomposed rank representation to allow
      the model to achieve better performance and then recompose this matrix at the end, such that the
514
      inference cost does not change, see Figure 1. Of course, if the model is trained with less than k
515
      ranks from equation 5, then the model can naturally be compressed after being recomposed into A
516   and subsequently decomposed with SVD to the rank selected without harming the performance.
517
518
519   7   C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we have explored the grokking phenomenon in neural networks, the behaviour where
522
      models generalise to the test dataset significantly after overfitting the training dataset. We proposed
      a novel method rooted in linear algebra to mitigate or eliminate this issue. By using SVD to change
523
      the representation of the weight matrix, A, into the product of three matrices U , Σ and V T and
524
      training on this representation.
525
526   Our results show that this method reduces the complexity of the learning task, allowing the model
527   to generalise more effectively with fewer parameters, especially as the dataset becomes more rep-
528
      resentative. Moreover, the flexibility of applying varying levels of rank reduction across different
      layers shows promise in maintaining performance while avoiding the grokking phenomenon.
529
530   These findings further contribute to the growing body of work demonstrating that grokking is an
531   artefact of poor training setups instead of being a natural or regularly occurring phenomenon within
532   deep learning. They also open up new possibilities for more efficient training and inference of neural
533   networks, primarily in transformer-based models, where parameter efficiency and generalisation are
534
      critical. This novel approach contributes to a deeper understanding of grokking and offers a practical
      solution for overcoming its challenges in machine learning.

       This section explores how the layers’ normalised stable rank (Rudelson & Vershynin, 2007) changes
975    through training. To calculate the normalised stable rank of the decomposed layer, Uk , Σk and Vk
976    are recomposed (Uk Σk VkT ) to create the matrix, A, which then is analysed.
977
978
       The stable rank equation 6 is the Frobenius norm squared divided by the squared spectral norm of
       the matrix; it is normalised by dividing the result by the full rank, min(m, n), where m is the rows,
979
       and n is the columns of A.
980

984
       The effect of decomposed learning, reducing the number of steps required before reaching near-
985
       perfect or perfect accuracy, is most dramatic when all layers except the position embedding are
986    decomposed. Therefore, we explore how the stable ranks of layers change through training, with
987    50% of the training data, and decompose learning on all layers except the position embedding by
988    analysing the stable rank and the accuracy at 200 points through training, Figure 20.
989
       For the baseline (normally trained model), top left in Figure 20, there is a slow transition from a high
990
       stable rank to a low stable rank throughout training. As the stable rank decreases, the test accuracy
991
       of the model increases. For decomposed learning with 100% of the ranks for all layers except for the
992    position embedding, top centre in Figure 20, the start of training follows the baseline, with a slow
993    progression from high stable rank to low stable rank and then at approximately point 50, the stable
994    ranks of the layers dramatically reduce close to zero which corresponds with a dramatic increase
995    in accuracy and the model achieving perfect or near-perfect test accuracy. This finding follows
996    for decomposing learning with 75% and 50% of the ranks for all layers except for the position
997    embedding. However, for decomposed learning with 25% and 12.5% of the ranks for all layers
998    except for the position embedding, the model makes the same transition from high to low, stable
999    rank through training across layers, albeit not as high initially and achieves a similar but slightly
1000
       lower stable rank at the end of training, with this the 25% case generalises at approximately the
       same time as the baseline model. In contrast, the 12.5% case cannot generalise to the same level as
1001
       the baseline.
1002
1003   Observing the difference between the baseline and decomposed learning, Figure 21, on 25% and
1004   12.5% of the ranks for all layers except for the position embedding, the baseline starts with a higher
1005   stable rank than the decomposed learning on 25% and 12.5%. This indicates that it is not simply get-
1006
       ting a low, stable rank that is important for generalisation. Instead, transitioning from a sufficiently
       high to low, stable rank is important for generalisation, or at least an indicator.
1007
1008   These results provide an explanation as to why decomposed learning works. It shows that decom-
1009   posed learning can speed up the process of transitioning from a sufficiently high, stable rank to a
1010   low, stable rank if a high enough initial rank is used, which is dependent on the amount of data. This
1011   suggests that it helps the implicit regularisation process in learning to reduce the stable rank more
1012
       effectively and thus can reduce the steps required for grokking.
1013   This general finding extends when exploring decomposed learning with 100% of the ranks for all
1014   layers except for the position embedding with 65%, Figure 22, and 80%, Figure 23, of the training
1015   data. These results suggest that learning can start from a lower-dimensional manifold as the data
1016   becomes more representative of the solution space. In the context of the current grokking theory,
1017   this analysis of the stable rank suggests that lazy (linear) and rich (feature) learning as proposed by
1018
       Kumar et al. (2024) happen in different dimensions with lazy (linear) learning happening in higher
       dimensions and rich (feature) learning happening in lower dimensions.

       This section explores how decomposed learning affects learning on real-world tasks. It explores a
       Transformer on the Tiny Shakepreare dataset and a ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) on the CIFAR10
1356
       dataset. The general finding is that decomposed learning can improve performance marginally, with
1357
       the most significant increase for the Shakespeare dataset being 0.2468%, and reduce the model’s
1358   size while maintaining similar performance. Using 50% of the ranks for all layers resulted in a com-
1359   pression ratio of 0.7215 and a reduced performance difference of 0.1448% while having a smaller
1360   generalisation gap than the baseline model. For CIFAR 10, decomposed learning improved the
1361   model’s performance by 2.97% if all layers were decomposed 100% and could achieve a compres-
1362   sion ratio of 0.4394 with a performance degradation of 1.68% if all layers expect the output and
1363   feed-forward block fc1 where decomposed to 12.5% of the full rank for the respective layer. This
1364   section highlights that decomposed learning can improve model performance and reduce model size
1365   on real-world tasks with limited affect on the performance.
