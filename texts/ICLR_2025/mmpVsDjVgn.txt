009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Varying annotation costs among data points and budget constraints can hinder the
012            adoption of active learning strategies in real-world applications. This work in-
013            troduces two Bayesian active learning strategies for batch acquisition under con-
014            straints (ConBatch-BAL), one based on dynamic thresholding and one following
015            greedy acquisition. Both select samples using uncertainty metrics computed via
016            Bayesian neural networks. The dynamic thresholding strategy redistributes the
017            budget across the batch, while the greedy one selects the top-ranked sample at
018
               each step, limited by the remaining budget. Focusing on scenarios with costly
               data annotation and geospatial constraints, we also release two new real-world
019
               datasets containing geolocated aerial images of buildings, annotated with energy
020
               efficiency or typology classes. The ConBatch-BAL strategies are benchmarked
021            against a random acquisition baseline on these datasets under various budget and
022            cost scenarios. The results show that the developed ConBatch-BAL strategies can
023            reduce active learning iterations and data acquisition costs in real-world settings,
024            and even outperform the unconstrained baseline solutions.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Bayesian active learning (BAL) is a method suitable for scenarios where data annotation is costly
030   or time-consuming (Budd et al., 2021; Desai & Ghose, 2022; Moustapha et al., 2022). Guided
031   by uncertainty metrics computed from a pool of candidate samples, BAL aims to improve model
032   accuracy with fewer samples by iteratively selecting data points for annotation (Gal et al., 2017).
033
      Supported by key developments in Bayesian neural networks (MacKay, 1995; Neal, 2012), BAL
      has proven successful in practical scenarios, from medical applications (Kadota et al., 2024) and
034
      remote sensing (Haut et al., 2018) to natural language processing (Siddhant & Lipton, 2018).
035
036   Although Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) are based on solid mathematical principles (Neal,
037   2012), performing exact Bayesian inference on model parameters given the training data becomes a
038   challenge in practical applications because the posterior distribution is usually complex and does not
039
      typically have a closed-form solution. To overcome this issue, approximate inference methods for
      BNNs have been proposed in the literature, from sampling approaches such as Monte Carlo Markov
040
      Chain (MCMC) (Papamarkou et al., 2022) and stochastic gradient MCMC variants (Ma et al., 2015)
041
      to variational methods like Bayes by backpropagation (MacKay, 1991) and Monte Carlo dropout
042   (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). While MCMC methods can infer more representative and accurate
043   posterior distributions, variational inference methods, particularly Monte Carlo dropout, are more
044   efficient and scalable at the expense of lower-precision uncertainty estimates. In practice, the choice
045   of an approximate BNN is case-specific (Mohamadi & Amindavar, 2020; Wang & Yeung, 2020;
046   Abdar et al., 2021), and depends on available computational resources and the required precision of
047   model uncertainty estimates.
048   In BAL, uncertainty metrics are key for selecting data points for annotation. A pool of unlabelled
049   candidate samples is ranked at each active learning iteration using an acquisition function. Various
050   acquisition functions have been proposed (Gal, 2016), typically corresponding to a model uncer-
051   tainty metric, such as predictive entropy (Shannon, 1948), variation ratios (Freeman, 1975), or mu-
052   tual information (Gal et al., 2017). The latter relies on information-theoretic principles, distilling the
053   model from total uncertainty by computing the mutual information of candidate sample predictions
      and BNN model parameters (Gal et al., 2017).

077   BAL methods often assume a uniform annotation cost across all candidate unlabelled samples. How-
078
      ever, in many real-world applications, annotation costs can vary, and the total budget for a batch may
      be limited. For example, annotating images with a drone in a geographical area is constrained by the
079
      drone’s battery. To facilitate the adoption of BAL in real-world applications, we propose two active
080
      learning strategies for batch acquisition under budget constraints. Both strategies use an acquisition
081   function that maximizes an uncertainty metric, e.g., mutual information of candidate sample pre-
082   dictions and model parameters. As depicted in Figure 1, adaptive thresholding ConBatch-BAL sets
083   a cost threshold at each batch step to manage the budget, while greedy ConBatch-BAL selects the
084   top-ranked candidate sample at each batch step, limited by the remaining budget. We also publicly
085   release two real-world building datasets, build6k and nieman17k, where data annotation is costly
086   and geospatial constraints are present. The datasets include geolocated building aerial images with
087   their corresponding energy efficiency or typology classes. The proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies
088   are tested under various budget constraints and scenarios on the building datasets and a modified
089
      version of the MNIST dataset, where each digit is geolocated.
090   To summarize, our contributions are listed below:
091
092          • Dynamic thresholding ConBatch-BAL and greedy ConBatch-BAL are introduced, two
093            Bayesian active learning strategies for batch acquisition under budget constraints.
094          • Two curated real-world datasets are publicly released containing geolocated building aerial
095            imagery and their corresponding building energy efficiency or typology classes.
096          • A benchmark study is conducted, testing the developed ConBatch-BAL strategies on real-
097            world building datasets, and drawing insights for similar and broader practical applications.

      2   R ELATED WORK
100
101
      Batch active learning. Batch active learning allows the annotation of multiple points simultane-
102   ously, reducing the number of model retraining iterations required. In Batch-BALD (Kirsch et al.,
103   2019), samples are acquired based on the mutual information between a batch of points and model
104   parameters, while its computationally efficient variant, k-BALD, computes k-wise mutual informa-
105   tion terms (Kirsch, 2023). Other methods like BADGE (Ash et al., 2019) and ACS-FW (Pinsler
106   et al., 2019) use loss metrics and loss gradient scores for batch selection. In Kirsch et al. (2021), a
107   stochastic strategy is proposed for adapting single-point acquisition methods to batch active learning.
      Unlike our work, these methods do not consider varying data annotation costs or budget constraints.

108
      Cost-sensitive active learning. Algorithms for cost-sensitive multi-class classification, where mis-
109   classification costs vary across classes, are proposed in Krishnamurthy et al. (2019), Greiner et al.
110   (2002), and Krempl et al. (2015). In Li et al. (2022), a batch active learning algorithm is introduced
111   for multi-fidelity physics and engineering applications, where the cost of requesting a model evalua-
112   tion depends on the fidelity of the chosen model. In contrast to these approaches, our ConBatch-BAL
113   strategies are designed for practical scenarios with varying annotation costs across data points, in-
114   cluding cases where the acquisition cost depends on the previous set of selected points in the batch.
115   Real-world aerial imagery datasets. The AID dataset (Xia et al., 2017) contains Google Earth
116   images for scene classification annotated by remote sensing specialists. X-View (Lam et al., 2018)
117   and DOTA (Xia et al., 2018) are large-scale real-world datasets focused on object detection from
118   aerial imagery, while the dataset SPAGRI-AI (Jonak et al., 2024) applies aerial imagery for bench-
119   marking computer vision methods in crop and weed detection. In Mayer et al. (2023), a method is
120   proposed for predicting building energy efficiency using aerial, street-view, and surface temperature
121   data. In our work, we release two open real-world datasets containing aerial images of buildings in
122   Rotterdam, annotated with their geolocation, energy efficiency classes, and typologies. Our goal is
123
      to motivate the advancement of active learning methods in scenarios where data annotation is costly.

      3.2   BATCH -BALD: BATCH BAYESIAN LEARNING BY DISAGREEMENT
146
147   To rank a batch of candidate samples, {x1 , . . . , xn }, Batch-BALD proposes an acquisition function
148   that relies on the mutual information between sample predictions, {y1 , . . . , yn }, and model param-
149   eters, p(ω | Dtrain ) (Kirsch et al., 2019). In Batch-BALD, the acquisition function is defined as:
150
151
                 aBatchBALD ({x1 , . . . , xn }, p(ω|Dtrain )) := I(y1 , . . . , yn ; ω | x1 , . . . , xn , Dtrain ).   (3)
152
153   To facilitate the selection of a diverse batch of samples, Batch-BALD does not assume independence
154   among candidate sample predictions (Kirsch et al., 2019), and instead, the mutual information is
155   quantified based on joint entropy metrics as:
156
157          I(y1:n ; ω | x1:n , Dtrain ) := H(y1:n | x1:n , Dtrain ) − Ep(ω|Dtrain ) H(y1:n | x1:n , ω, Dtrain ), (4)
158   where the term on the left corresponds to the joint predictive entropy, constituting a total uncertainty
159   metric, whereas the term on the right refers to the expectation of conditional entropy given the
160   model parameters, thereby reflecting aleatoric uncertainty. By discerning aleatoric uncertainty from
161   joint entropy, the mutual information of a batch of samples and the model parameters becomes an
      epistemic model uncertainty metric, indicating the samples where model parameters most disagree.

      3.3     M ONTE C ARLO DROPOUT
163
164   While the proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies can be generally applied to other probabilistic mod-
165   els, we adopt Monte Carlo dropout BNNs in this work because they scale well to high-dimensional
166   data, are more computationally efficient than other BNN approaches, and have a straightforward
167   training process (Kirsch et al., 2019). As demonstrated by Gal & Ghahramani (2016), minimizing
168   the dropout loss is equivalent to performing approximate Bayesian inference, assuming the varia-
169   tional distribution is a mixture of two Gaussians, with a mean set equal to zero for one of them. At
170
      test time, uncertainty estimates can be obtained by computing a number of forward passes, T , with
      dropout activated. For instance, the output class probability vector for a classification task with input
171                                                  PT
172
      x can be estimated as p(y | x, Dtrain ) ≈ 1/T t=1 Softmax(f ωt (x)).

174   4       C ON BATCH -BAL STRATEGIES
175
176   Building on methods that rely on Bayesian active learning (BAL) by disagreement (Gal et al.,
177   2017), we introduce two heuristic strategies to handle batch acquisition under budget constraints,
178   enabling the application of active learning methods in real-world scenarios where annotations are
179
      costly or time-consuming. Both strategies select a batch, {x∗1 , . . . , x∗n }, based on a specified
      uncertainty metric, such as the mutual information between predictions and model parameters,
180
      I(y1:n ; ω | x1:n , Dtrain ), but differ in how they manage the remaining budget throughout the batch.
181
      The proposed strategies are named ConBatch-BAL because acquisition functions other than mutual
182   information can be defined in the selection process. Sample selection under constraints is a complex
183   combinatorial optimization problem because (i) selecting a diverse batch requires computing joint
184   uncertainty metrics, and (ii) the acquisition cost of sample xi at batch step i, may depend on the
185   previous set of selected points, introducing another sequential effect to the problem. Although the
186   definition of the cost model is case-specific, the formulation of the active learning problem under
187   budget constraints is general. One can define the cost model and constraints in terms of monetary
188   units, distance, or any other quantity of interest. While greedy selection based on mutual infor-
189   mation satisfies submodularity and yields a 1 − 1/e approximation guarantee (Kirsch et al., 2019),
190
      introducing cost-dependent samples creates a knapsack constraint that is computationally NP-hard
      and may cause submodularity to no longer hold.
191
192
      4.1     DYNAMIC THRESHOLDING C ON BATCH -BAL
193
194   To manage the budget, cmax , over a batch, dynamic thresholding initially sets a threshold cth,1 =
195   cmax,1 /nmax , where nmax is the maximum allowed number of batch steps1 and cmax,1 = cmax . At
196   each subsequent batch step i, the remaining available budget is recalculated as cmax,i = cmax,i−1 −
197   c(x∗i−1 ), based on the acquisition cost incurred by the previously selected point x∗i−1 , and the batch
198   threshold is adjusted as cth,i = cmax,i /(nmax − (i − 1)). Under the set budget threshold, cth,i ,
199   a sample is selected at each batch step i based on the defined acquisition function and specified
200   uncertainty metric. The batch acquisition process2 is summarized in Algorithm 1 and a graphical
201   representation of two batch steps is shown in Figure 1 (left).
202
203   4.2     G REEDY C ON BATCH -BAL
204
      Derived from Batch-BALD (Kirsch et al., 2019), greedy ConBatch-BAL selects points based on
205
      the specified uncertainty metric, limited only by the remaining budget, operating greedily without
206
      considering the entire batch horizon. The only constraint is that the cost of the selected sample,
207   c(x∗i ) must not exceed the remaining budget cmax . This approach encourages the acquisition of
208   costly but highly informative samples, even if it results in a batch with fewer points, n, than the
209   maximum possible, nmax . The steps for implementing this active learning strategy2 are outlined in
210   Algorithm 2, and the process over two batch steps is illustrated in Figure 1 (right).

252
253
254   BAL allows the acquisition of multiple samples simultaneously within an active learning iteration.
255
      In practical applications, this acquisition cost may vary among samples and batch selection may
      be constrained by a budget. For example, a drone annotator operates under a finite battery range,
256
      or a medical expert may need to travel between labs across various regions. Focusing on practical
257
      applications where data annotation is typically geospatially constrained and costly, we introduce
258   two real-world datasets, build6k and nieman17k. These datasets consist of building aerial imagery
259   and their corresponding building energy labels or typologies, which are key factors for assessing
260   energy efficiency and intervention needs within a city. A few sample images from each dataset are
261   shown in Figure 2a. The build6k dataset includes approximately 6,000 aerial images of buildings in
262   Rotterdam, each classified as {efficient (energy labels A-E), inefficient (energy labels F-G)}. Cate-
263   gorizing building energy efficiency at scale supports recent energy performance directives aimed at
264   progressively phasing out inefficient buildings (Economidou et al., 2020).
265   We also release the nieman17k dataset, containing approximately 17,000 building aerial images
266   with their typology class: {upstairs apartment (<1945), terraced house (<1945), terraced house
267   (>1945), porch house (<1945), porch house (1945-1975), porch house (>1975), detached house}.
268   Building typologies can be used in energy performance simulations (Koezjakov et al., 2018), where
269   thermal properties can be inferred as a function of building typology. Besides energy performance,
      typologies can also be used for architectural analyses focusing on building historical evolution.

309   Hiring auditors to inspect or classify all buildings in a city is prohibitively expensive. Carefully
310   selecting buildings for inspection can significantly reduce costs. To motivate our experiments, we
311   introduce three cost configurations, shown in Figure 2b, adaptable to other similar real-world ap-
312   plications. The first configuration, distance, allows free choice of the first building in a batch but

315   tion, distance return, imposes a stricter constraint by requiring the total traveled distance to include
316
      the return trip to the first building in the batch, accounting for the extra resources needed to complete

317

318
      to 100 cost units. Crowded areas in this configuration are more expensive than sparsely populated
319   ones. We benchmark the proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies on the three cost configurations, pre-
320   senting results for distance and area cost in the main text, with results for distance return provided
321   in Appendix C.1, as they show minor variations compared to the distance configuration.

      6        E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      In this section, we benchmark the proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies against a random selection
327   baseline. The strategies are tested on three datasets, build6k, mnist6k, and nieman17k under various
328   budget constraints. For each dataset, configuration, budget constraint, and strategy, 5 random seeds
329   are evaluated over 800 active learning iterations. We evaluate the number of active learning iterations
330   required to achieve a specified model accuracy on the test set, as this is equivalent to counting the
331   number of completed tours in the tested practical scenarios. Additionally, we showcase the results as
332   a function of acquired samples and cost in Appendix C.3. Note that in the limit of an infinite budget,
333   dynamic thresholding ConBatch-BAL, greedy ConBatch-BAL, and greedy Batch-BALD become
334   equivalent, as all acquire samples based on mutual information.
335   In all experiments, the Bayesian neural network classifier receives embedding vectors generated
336   from the input aerial images using the pre-trained DINOv2 foundation model (Oquab et al., 2023).
337   Further details are provided in Appendix B.1. A preliminary analysis showed that fine-tuning the
338   last two layers of DINOv2 improves accuracy only by 1-2%, agreeing with reported experiments
339   (Oquab et al., 2023). We thus restrict model re-training to the Bayesian neural network classifier,
340   which is modeled with two hidden layers, and hyperparameters listed in Table 2 in Appendix B.2.
341
      In this setup, the specified acquisition functions correspond to the mutual information of the BNN
      classifier parameters and sample output predictions.
342

346   findings (Kirsch et al., 2019), we observe that greedy Batch-BALD outperforms random selection

      In the distance configuration under budget constraints, dynamic thresholding ConBatch-BAL and
379   greedy ConBatch-BAL achieve the highest model accuracy targets with 20–43% fewer active learn-
380   ing iterations on the build6k dataset, and 50–80% fewer on the nieman17k dataset, compared to the
381   random selection baseline. Under the 100-m batch constraint, the baseline fails to reach the target of
382   71% model accuracy on the nieman17k dataset within 800 active learning iterations. With fewer than
383   400 active iterations, both ConBatch-BAL strategies achieve an accuracy of 97% on mnist6k under
384   a 100-m budget constraint, while the random strategy requires over 600 active iterations to reach the
385   same accuracy. Naturally, more active learning iterations are needed in settings with stricter budget
386   constraints, as opportunities to select highly informative samples become more limited.
387   Among the datasets, the performance gap between the proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies and ran-
388   dom selection is larger in mnist6k compared to build6k and nieman17k. We attribute this result to
389   the fact that build6k and nieman17k are noisier, making it more difficult to distinguish model uncer-
390   tainty from aleatoric noise. Nevertheless, the observed trends hold across datasets, with ConBatch-
391   BAL strategies outperforming random selection, especially for higher classification accuracy targets.

      7                           D ISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
454
455
      The experiments indicate that the proposed ConBatch-BAL strategies can reduce the number of
456
      active learning iterations needed to reach satisfactory model accuracy in real-world scenarios with
457   batch acquisition under budget constraints. While the average distance traveled without budget
458   constraints is approximately 25 km per batch, as shown in Appendix C.4, similar accuracy can be
459   achieved under the 2-km constraint across all datasets on the distance configuration. This finding is
460   important for the practical adoption of active learning methods. As shown in Figure 5, informative
461   samples are generally found within a 2 km distance of the reference sample, though fewer samples
462   are naturally available under the stricter 100-m distance constraint. While sample informativeness
463   depends on the data distribution and is case-specific, the effectiveness of active learning methods
464   should not significantly reduce as long as enough informative samples can be found within the
465
      constrained region. To examine the samples collected by the tested strategies over active learning
      iterations, Figure 6 represents the mutual information from acquired batches of samples. More
466
      informative batches are generally collected after a few learning iterations as the training set becomes
467
      less sparse, peaking at the point where highly informative samples start becoming less available. As
468   expected, constraints hinder the acquisition of informative samples, particularly under the 100-m
469   batch distance limit, where the number of samples per batch is significantly compromised.

513   7.1                L IMITATIONS
514
515   Approximate uncertainty estimates. In this work, uncertainty metrics are computed through
516   Monte Carlo dropout BNNs. While these provide a framework for efficiently capturing the un-
517   certainty in model predictions, the resulting uncertainty estimates might not be as informative due
518   to the noise introduced by the MC-dropout variational approximation. Future research efforts could
519
      apply ConBatch-BAL strategies using uncertainty metrics computed through other BNN variants,
      such as stochastic gradient MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2019).
520
521   Model re-training. While the pre-trained foundation model DINOv2 is used to compute feature
522   embeddings from aerial imagery, we do not fine-tune DINOv2 in our experiments and only re-train
523   the BNN classifier, thereby reducing computational demands. Although only marginal gains are
524   reported in the literature when fine-tuning the internal layers of DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), this
525
      research path could be explored further.
526   Aerial imagery dataset. In the introduced real-world building datasets, energy efficiency categories
527   and typologies are informed by building aerial imagery. Complementing the dataset with street-view
528   images or other informative features may lead to more accurate predictions (Mayer et al., 2023).
529
530
      8             C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      We introduce two batch active learning strategies for acquiring informative samples under budget
533
      constraints and showcase their effectiveness on real-world datasets for building energy efficiency
534   prediction and typology classification. While solving exactly the combinatorial problem of acquir-
535   ing the most informative batch under budget constraints is often intractable due to system-level sta-
536   tistical and cost effects, heuristic strategies like dynamic thresholding or greedy selection can offer
537   satisfactory performance in practice. Our results show that the developed ConBatch-BAL strategies
538   can reduce active learning iterations and cost in real-world settings with expensive data annotation
539   and geospatial constraints, even surpassing the performance of the unconstrained random selection
      baseline.

      We leverage open data sources and web services available in the Netherlands to create the released

711   with rectangular bounding boxes that enclose the building footprints, with an added buffer to capture
712   the surrounding context. Each aerial image is then linked to its corresponding energy efficiency or
713   typology class by cross-referencing tabular building data and footprint through the unique building

723   typologies correspond to Nieman categories, commonly used for building energy performance anal-

       The effectiveness of ConBatch-BAL strategies relative to the baseline remains consistent across
1243   batch sizes, outperforming random selection in all tested settings. While more active learning itera-
1244   tions are required to reach the accuracy target for smaller batch sizes, this is naturally caused by the
1245   fewer number of samples that can be acquired per tour. To compare the strategies across different
1246   batch sizes, we examine the number of acquired samples required to achieve the accuracy target.
1247   The results indicate that, in most cases, the number of acquired samples increases with higher batch
1248   sizes. We attribute this result to the fact that the overall available budget per sample decreases for
1249   higher batch sizes. However, dynamic thresholding ConBatch-BAL experiences less variation over
1250   batch sizes, as it balances the budget across the batch.
