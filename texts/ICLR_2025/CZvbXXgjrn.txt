009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Active learning (AL) reduces data annotation costs by querying labels from human
012            annotators for the most informative unlabeled data points during model training.
013            Existing AL methods generally assume the availability of a large amount of un-
014            labeled samples for query selection. However, collecting raw data in practice can
015            be expensive, even without considering the cost of labeling. Membership query
016            synthesis circumvents the need for an unlabeled data pool by directly generating
017            informative queries from the input space. Nevertheless, existing approaches often
018
               generate instances lacking semantic meaning, thereby increasing the difficulty of
               labeling. In this paper, we propose the Generative Membership Query Descriptor
019
               (GenMQD) method for AL to mitigate the risk of generating unrecognizable in-
020
               stances. The key idea is to generate textual descriptions of the desired data, instead
021            of the data samples themselves. Then a pre-trained multi-modal alignment model
022            (e.g., CLIP) can be leveraged to transform these features into natural language
023            texts for data gathering purposes. Extensive experiments on image classification
024            benchmark datasets against query synthesis state-of-the-art methods demonstrate
025            that, on average, GenMQD can improve model accuracy by 2.43% when gathering
026            and labeling 500 examples. A large-scale user study verifies that human oracles
027            prefer GenMQD generated queries over generated image-based queries.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Active learning (AL) (Settles, 2009) aims to reduce the cost of data annotation by selectively query-
033
      ing the most informative unlabeled data from an oracle, typically a human annotator. By focusing on
      the most valuable data points, AL methods can achieve the better model performance with fewer la-
034
      beled samples (Hanneke, 2014). Most of current AL methods are designed for either the pool-based
035
      setting or the stream-based setting. For pool-based AL approaches, a large pool of unlabeled data
036   is generally assumed to be available, from which a subset is selected for human annotation (Kirsch
037   et al., 2019; Sener & Savarese, 2018; Tang & Huang, 2022). In contrast, the stream-based AL
038   approaches receive data incrementally, requiring the AL algorithms to decide in real-time whether
039   to query the oracle for the label of each received instance (Huang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022;
040   Cacciarelli & Kulahci, 2024).
041
      In practice, data collection can be prohibitively expensive in turns of effort and costs involved, and
042   this can be further exacerbated by the cost of data annotation (Cohen et al., 2020; Jia et al., 2020;
043   Aizawa et al., 2020). For example, acquiring high-quality medical imaging scans or gathering sensor
044   data from remote locations in environmental monitoring, can be extremely resource-intensive. In
045   addition, certain types of raw data, such as music concert recordings or artificially created manga
046   images, require significant human effort to produce. In such scenarios, AL strategies requiring an
047   existing pool of unlabelled samples can lead to considerable waste, as the majority of the collected
048   data remains unlabeled, thereby resulting in inefficient utilization of resources and increased costs.
049   The sub-field of Membership Query Synthesis (Schumann & Rehbein, 2019; Guo et al., 2021; Tran
050   et al., 2019), which belongs to AL methods that do not rely on pre-existing unlabeled pool of data,
051   has emerged in recent years, but only receiving relatively less attention in the AL relevant literature.
052   These methods either generate informative synthetic data (e.g., using Generative Adversarial Net-
053   works (GANs)), or produce feature vectors through optimization in the feature space, which are in
      turn used for querying the oracle. Although these approaches have demonstrated effectiveness in

054
      simple scenarios (e.g., finite problem domains (Angluin, 2004)), they face a significant limitation:
055   the generated synthetic data often lacks semantic meaning and might not be easily recognizable by
056   human annotators (Lang & Baum, 1992). Furthermore, although existing AL methods are start-
057   ing to adopt trained models as oracles to provide labels for the synthetic data, they might be less
058   tractable in practice.
059
      To address these challenges, we propose the Generative Membership Query Descriptor (GenMQD)
060
      method for AL. It generates textual descriptions of the desired data with rich semantic context,
061   thereby providing transparent interaction between AL and the human oracle. Specifically, GenMQD
062   first syntheses the desired data feature such that the model learned by the generated feature can
063   maximize the model performance gain on the validation set. Mathematical analysis shows that this
064   problem can be approximately solved by the influence function of perturbing of the training point
065   (Koh & Liang, 2017). To further obtain descriptive text of the desired data, GenMQD is designed to
066   leverage a pre-trained multi-modal alignment model (e.g., CLIP in Radford et al. (2021)) to describe
067   the desired instance in natural language terms. With CLIP as an example, we demonstrate that the
068   text embedding of the desired data is exactly its image embedding. Therefore, existing methods
069
      that map the CLIP image embedding or text embedding into the text descriptions can be adopted to
      generate the required information. Finally, the generated text descriptions can be used for querying
070
      the oracle to obtain a batch of instances that match the descriptions.
071
072   We have conducted extensive experiments on CIFAR-10 and iNaturalist 2021 benchmark datasets
073   against membership query synthesis state-of-the-art approaches to evaluate the effectiveness of
074   GenMQD. On average, it improves model performance by 2.43%, when labeling the same amount
075
      of data with the compared methods. A large-scale user study with 340 responses from human an-
      notators verifies that human oracles prefer GenMQD generated queries over generated image-based
076
      queries. Compared to existing query synthesis methods, GenMQD offers a more human-friendly way
077
      to data gathering and mitigates the risk of generating instances which are difficult to annotate. The
078   generated descriptions, even if do not correspond directly to real-world data, can still convey useful
079   information to the oracle. These descriptions are designed to reveal the uncertainties in the knowl-
080   edge within a target model, thereby guiding human oracles toward more actionable data gathering
081   and annotation decisions.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
085
086   AL aims to reduce the cost of data labeling by selectively querying oracles for the labels of the most
087
      valuable data points. Common selection notions in AL include informativeness and representative-
      ness (Settles, 2009; Ren et al., 2021). Informativeness refers to selecting data points for which the
088
      model is the most uncertain. Techniques for achieving this notion include estimating the distance
089
      between data and the decision boundary (Zhu & Bento, 2017); estimating the expected loss of un-
090   labeled data (Yoo & Kweon, 2019); and estimating the prediction variance of the same instance
091   with slight disturbing (Gal et al., 2017), etc. Representativeness, on the other hand, seeks to ensure
092   that the selected data points reflect the broader distribution of the entire dataset, thereby mitigating
093   bias and improving generalization. Techniques to achieve this goal include maximum mean discrep-
094   ancy (Du et al., 2015), Coreset (Sener & Savarese, 2018), and variational autoencoder (VAE) (Sinha
095   et al., 2019), etc.
096   Recently, there has been significant interest in combining multiple selection criteria, which usually
097   lead to better performances. For example, Du et al. (2015) leverages informativeness and repre-
098   sentativeness simultaneously to improve performance. Tang & Huang (2019) further incorporates
099   ease of data evaluation to avoid querying overly-hard samples facing the learning model. Shui et al.
100   (2020) proposes a principle to design deep AL algorithms, which combines both informativeness
101   and representativeness.
102   Another AL paradigm, membership query synthesis, circumvents the need for selecting from a pool
103   of unlabeled data. Instead, it queries oracles with arbitrary instances from the input space for label-
104   ing. This is usually achieved via data synthesis/generation (Zhu & Bento, 2017; Kong et al., 2019;
105   Yan et al., 2020). Existing membership query synthesis methods can be broadly divided into two
106   categories: 1) feature-based methods, and 2) generative methods. Feature-based methods (Angluin,
107   2004; Cohn et al., 1996; Yan et al., 2020) leverage predefined rules or criteria (e.g., uncertainty) to
      generate new data features from the input space based on the model’s current state or predictions.

128   One significant drawback of these methods is the limited range of application, since they are hard
129
      to generalize to tasks in various domains (Angluin, 2004). Generative methods (e.g., VAE, GAN)
      can synthesize realistic data points that are likely to improve the model. A diverse range of meth-
130
      ods have been proposed to generate desired data for annotation (Zhu & Bento, 2017; Tran et al.,
131
      2019). However, training an effective generation model requires plenty of data, which is usually
132   unavailable in AL. Moreover, the generated instances either need to be annotated by a human or-
133   acle (Zhu & Bento, 2017; Schumann & Rehbein, 2019) or to be assigned pseudo-labels (e.g., by
134   a class-conditional GAN (Kong et al., 2019)). They are prone to unrecognizable queries and un-
135   certain noises. The proposed GenMQD method can address such limitation of existing generative
136   membership query synthesis methods in AL area.

258   3.4   E FFICIENT I MPLEMENTATION
259
260   To improve the practicality of GenMQD, we introduce an efficient implementation in this section.
261
      To simplify computations, we leverage a pre-trained CLIP model with “ViT-B/32” backbone as the
      feature extractor. Specifically, the image encoder pre-processes the images. Subsequently, a 3-layer
262
      neural network f (·; θ) is trained on the extracted features for classification. In this way, the input
263
      space aligns with the embedding space defined by the CLIP image encoder (i.e., x = vx ), thereby
264   eliminating the need to map the generated features into the CLIP embedding space. In addition,
265   using a small neural network for prediction task facilitates the calculation of the influence function
266   in query synthesis step. Furthermore, we initialize x∗ using the class center of the target class y ∗
267   to reduce the risk of the synthesized query being distant from the desired class and to expedite the
268   query synthesis convergence. Finally, a query is generated for each class in each iteration for class
269   balancing and batch-mode querying in our implementation. We summarize the main procedures of
      GenMQD for active data gathering by generating text description in Algorithm 1.

274   Datasets. We employ a commonly used image classification dataset - CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009)
275   - and a large dataset - iNaturalist 2021 (Grant Van Horn, 2021) - to study the effectiveness of the pro-
276   posed GenMQD method. CIFAR-10 is a 10-class image classification dataset, consisting of 50,000
277   training images and 10,000 test images, with 6,000 images per class in total. It has been widely
278   adopted in query synthesis studies (Kong et al., 2019; Zhu & Bento, 2017). iNaturalist 2021 con-
279   tains about 2.7 million training images. Since the generative methods often struggle to generalize
280
      well to the datasets with a large number of classes, we use the super categories as the prediction
      target in this dataset. Specifically, there are 11 super categories in iNaturalist 2021. The validation
281
      set with 100,000 images is used as the test set in our experiments.
282
283   Comparison Methods. Since there are few methods for active text description generation, we
284   compare our GenMQD method with state-of-the-art data synthesis methods and a text generation
285   baseline. Specifically, the following approaches are included in our experiments:
286
               1. RandomText: Querying fixed class-specific prompts for data gathering and labeling.
287
288            2. GAAL (Zhu & Bento, 2017): Generating images using GAN and queries the oracle about
289               the ones near the decision boundary.
290            3. ACGAN (Odena et al., 2017): Using a class-conditional GAN to generate images with
291               pseudo-labels.
292            4. ActiveGAN (Kong et al., 2019): Training a class-conditional GAN to generate uncertain
293               images. The data generated by the trained GAN will be added to the labeled set.
294
               5. GenMQD-Fea: A variant of the proposed method, which only uses the synthesized data
295
                  point z ∗ for model updating.
296
297            6. GenMQD: The proposed method. Generating textual descriptions of uncertain data for ac-
298               tive data gathering and labeling.

      GAN train a class-conditional GAN over the labeled set for data synthesis to update the model; and

341   4.2              R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
342
343   The learning curves of different methods compared on CIFAR-10 and iNaturalist 2021 datasets are
344
      presented in Fig. 2. The results on CIFAR-10 show that the proposed GenMQD method achieves the
      best accuracy. It consistently outperforms RandomText, indicating that the our generated texts are
345
      informative to the model. GenMQD-Fea achieves positive effect on model accuracy. However, the
346
      improvement is not as significant as GenMQD. This can be attributed to the fact that the pre-trained
347   Stable Diffusion2 model introduces additional knowledge to facilitate model learning, thereby fur-
348   ther improving model accuracy. GAAL, ActiveGAN and ACGAN are less effective in improving
349   model accuracy. This can be atributed to the fact that the size of the initial labeled dataset is small,
350   which might be insufficient to train an effective GAN model. Note that there is a common assump-
351   tion in AL setting that the initial labeled set is small. ACGAN achieves the worst performance on
352   CIFAR-10, as it does not filter any generated image, which might introduce noise. GenMQD-Fea
353   surpasses all the image generation methods, suggesting that our proposed query synthesis method is
354   capable of generating more informative data.
355   For iNaturalist 2021 dataset, our GenMQD-Fea method achieves the highest model accuracy perfor-
356   mance. However, GenMQD does not perform well. This can be attributed to the incompatibility of
357   the pre-trained text decoding model with the dataset, as the superiority of GenMQD-Fea suggests
358   that the synthesized data point z ∗ is informative and the performance of RandomText implies that
359   the Text-to-Image model performs well. In our experiment, we employ an off-the-shelf method,
360   DeCap (Li et al., 2023), to decode the CLIP embedding into text. More effective decoders can be
361
      selected according to the dataset to further improve the performance. We leave this matter for future
      study. GAAL, ActiveGAN and ACGAN effectively improve model accuracy with the generated
362
      images. However, their improvement is not significant compared to our proposed data synthesis
363
      method, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed GenMQD method.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel active querying method, GenMQD, which aims to generate the tex-
532
      tual descriptions of desired data for AL data gathering and labeling. Unlike traditional methods, it
533
      does not rely on an unlabeled data pool for instance selection, making it more applicable in practice.
534   GenMQD first synthesizes the desired data by optimizing model performance on a validation set. We
535   show that this problem can be efficiently solved using influence functions. Subsequently, it gener-
536   ates textual descriptions of the synthesized data with a pre-trained multi-modal alignment model.
537   Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed GenMQD method effectively generates infor-
538   mative data and semantically aligned textual descriptions for querying. Furthermore, a large-scale
539   user study reveals that this active query type is more comprehensible to human oracles. In future,
      we plan to investigate alternative text decoding techniques to produce more detailed descriptions.
