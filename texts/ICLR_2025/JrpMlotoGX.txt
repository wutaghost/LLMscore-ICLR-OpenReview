009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Language models (LMs) are widely used by an increasing number of users, un-
012            derscoring the challenge of maintaining factual accuracy across a broad range of
013            topics. We present VERIFY (Verification and Evidence RetrIeval for FactualitY
014            evaluation), a pipeline to evaluate LMs’ factual accuracy in real-world user inter-
015            actions. VERIFY considers the verifiability of LM-generated content and cate-
016            gorizes content units as supported, unsupported, or undecidable based
017            on the retrieved web evidence. Importantly, VERIFY ’s factuality judgments cor-
018            relate better with human evaluations than existing methods. Using VERIFY, we
019
               identify “hallucination prompts” across diverse topics–those eliciting the high-
               est rates of incorrect or unverifiable LM responses. These prompts form FACT-
020
               B ENCH, a dataset of 985 prompts across 213 fine-grained topics. Our dataset cap-
021
               tures emerging factuality challenges in real-world LM interactions and is regularly
022            updated with new prompts. We benchmark widely-used LMs from GPT, Gem-
023            ini, and Llama3.1 family on FACT B ENCH, yielding the following key findings:
024            (i) Proprietary models exhibit better factuality, improving from Hard to Easy
025            hallucination prompts. (ii) Llama3.1-405B-Instruct shows comparable or lower
026            factual accuracy than Llama3.1-70B-Instruct across all evaluation methods due to
027            its higher subjectivity that leads to more undecidable content. (iii) Gemini1.5-Pro
028            shows a significantly higher refusal rate, with over-refusal in 25% of cases.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Despite ongoing efforts to enhance their factuality, language models (LMs) continue to generate
034   inaccurate or arbitrary content, known as hallucinations (Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). The
035   widespread use of LMs and the evolving nature of information demand a dynamic factuality evalu-
036   ation benchmark to identify the challenges LMs face in real-world applications. Current long-form
037   factuality evaluation benchmarks (Min et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024b; Malaviya et al., 2024) are
038   limited by their static nature and limited coverage of usage scenarios. The static design makes these
039
      benchmarks susceptible to overfitting and potential information leakage (Magar & Schwartz, 2022),
      rendering them unsuitable for capturing factuality challenges in daily LM usages. Moreover, ex-
040
      isting benchmarks often focus on a limited subset of tasks. For instance, data used in developing
041
      FactScore Min et al. (2023) primarily addresses biographical queries, while ExpertQA (Malaviya
042   et al., 2024) recruits human experts to curate domain-specific questions. Other benchmarks (Chen
043   et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024b) cover a limited number of domains and are either LM-generated or
044   human-curated, further limiting their applicability to in-the-wild scenarios.
045
      In this work, we make two primary contributions: (i) a dynamic and diverse factuality evaluation
046
      benchmark curated from real-world LM usage and (ii) a factuality evaluation framework that mea-
047
      sures the appropriateness of in-the-wild prompts for inclusion in our benchmark by estimating how
048   frequent strong LMs generate unverifiable and incorrect responses. Concretely, we introduce FACT-
049   B ENCH, an updatable benchmark grounded in the real-world usage of LMs. FACT B ENCH comprises
050   985 diverse information-seeking prompts, across 213 topics, that tend to cause LMs to produce un-
051   verifiable and incorrect responses. Using clustering methods, we begin by identifying 382 unique
052   tasks within the LMSYS-Chat-1M dataset Zheng et al. (2024). Prompts within each task cluster are
053   then labeled for verifiability, indicating whether the prompt’s response can be verified using Google
      search results. We assess the usefulness of prompts by considering factors such as clarity, inter-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      est, and relevance to a broad audience. Finally, verifiable prompts that meet a specified usefulness
055   threshold are considered to be included in FACT B ENCH.
056
057   We further define the “hallucination prompts” as the ones that tend to elicit incorrect or unverifi-
058
      able content from a selected group of strong LMs. To identify such prompts, we design VERIFY,
      a Verification and Evidence RetrIeval for FactualitY evaluation pipeline to detect nonfactual con-
059
      tent in LM responses. VERIFY first extracts content units from model responses and identifies their
060
      type. It then evaluates only the verifiable units against web-based evidence using an interactive query
061   generation and evidence retrieval technique. Finally, VERIFY categorizes units as supported,
062   unsupported, or undecidable based on the evidence. We also propose a hallucination score
063   based on the number of unsupported and undecidable labels to quantify the degree of hal-
064   lucination in model responses. This score helps measure the appropriateness of the corresponding
065   user prompts for our final benchmark. To further refine our selection process, we categorize prompts
066   into three tiers (Hard, Moderate, and Easy) based on the overall performance of the evaluated
067   LMs. We then select prompts with the highest hallucination scores within each tier, resulting in a
068   final benchmark of 985 prompts after manual inspection.
069   To assess the performance of widely-used LMs on FACT B ENCH, we evaluate two proprietary, GPT4-
070   o (OpenAI, 2024b), Gemini1.5-Pro (Team et al., 2024), and two open-weight models from Llama3.1
071   family, i.e., Llama3.1-70B-Instruct and Llama3.1-405B-Instruct (Meta, 2024), across all tiers of
072   FACT B ENCH. The results show that LM performance significantly increases across tiers, aligning
073   with our curation strategy. To compare the effectiveness of different factuality evaluation methods,
074   we use VERIFY units as a common basis and feed them into factuality evaluation baselines for
075
      verification. Our results reveal that VERIFY achieves the highest correlation with human judgments
      compared to state-of-the-art methods. This finding underscores the effectiveness of our approach in
076
      benchmark creation and factuality assessment.
077
078   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
079
      • We introduce FACT B ENCH, a new benchmark grounded in the real-world usage of LMs. FACT-
080
        B ENCH is designed to be adaptable by continuously incorporating newly collected hallucination
081
        prompts that cause LMs to generate incorrect content. This dynamic approach ensures that the
082     benchmark remains relevant, addressing the evolving challenges in factual generation.
083
      • We design VERIFY, a factuality evaluation pipeline that considers the verifiability of generated
084
        content and categorizes units into supported, unsupported, or undecidable according
085
        to retrieval results. VERIFY addresses the limitations of prior work that only makes binary
086     decisions on supportedness and archives the highest average correlation with human evaluations.
087
      • We release our human factuality annotations on 5,519 content units, with each unit independently
088
        evaluated by two annotators. Each annotator evaluates the independence of extracted units and
089     their factuality using existing online resources. This human-annotated data provides quantifiable
090     evaluation resources for assessing future factuality evaluation techniques.

135   2.2      FACTUALITY E VALUATION M ETHODS AND V ERIFIABILITY
136
137   The challenge of distinguishing verifiable from non-verifiable claims is central to fact-checking.
138   AFaCTA (Ni et al., 2024) stresses that claims are verifiable when they provide sufficient specificity
139   for evidence retrieval. The subjective nature of check-worthiness, shaped by political and social
140   contexts (Konstantinovskiy et al., 2020; Nakov et al., 2022), complicates this, particularly in LM-
141
      generated content where fact-opinion lines blur (Vosoughi et al., 2018). To address this, VERIFY
      introduces an undecidable label for claims with ambiguous factuality, accommodating both
142
      objective and context-dependent claims (see details in Section 4.1).
143
144   Long-form content evaluation presents unique challenges due to its complexity and the numerous
145   claims it typically contains. To address these challenges, SAFE (Wei et al., 2024b) and FactScore
146   (Wang et al., 2024b) decompose content into individual facts for granular verification. Our method,
147
      VERIFY, builds upon this approach by not only decomposing LM-generated content into units
      but also distinguishing between verifiable and non-verifiable elements that appear in user-model
148
      interactions. VERIFY focuses exclusively on verifiable units for further verification. It classifies
149
      these units as supported or unsupported only when confident evidence is found, labeling
150   them undecidable otherwise. This approach introduces a more robust framework for evaluating
151   the factual precision of LM-generated content. In contrast, Factcheck-GPT extracts coarser-grained
152   units and heavily relies on parametric knowledge, limiting its reliability for factual evaluation.
153

      3      H ARVESTING H ALLUCINATION P ROMPTS F ROM LM I NTERACTIONS
156
157   Our current understanding of LM performance on verifiable tasks is limited, and existing factuality
158
      evaluation benchmarks cover only a narrow range of verifiable use cases.
159   To address this gap, we collect English prompts from the first turn of conversations in the LMSYS-
160   Chat-1M dataset (Zheng et al., 2024), which is a large-scale, in-the-wild LM conversations dataset.
161   Our objective is to identify prompts that are verifiable, diverse, and relevant for evaluating model
      responses through a multi-step process. Figure 1 (left) outlines our collection process.

162
      • Data Clustering: After cleaning the data (see details in Appendix 11.1), we get 294,333 distinct
163     prompts and cluster them into various topics. We use BERTopic (Grootendorst, 2022), a dynamic
164     topic-modeling pipeline that (1) embeds prompts using OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small model
165     (OpenAI, 2024a), (2) applies UMAP for dimensionality reduction, and (3) employs HDBSCAN,
166     a hierarchical density-based clustering algorithm. HDBSCAN identifies 142,702 (48.5%) of the
167     prompts as outliers, which we exclude to remove overly-specific prompts. Finally, we use a class-
168     based TF-IDF method to select the top 100 most representative prompts from each cluster and
169     summarize them into concise topics (up to 10 words) using GPT-4 Turbo (OpenAI, 2024c).
170   • Verifiability Classification: We focus on prompts that ask for varying degrees of verifiable re-
171     sponses. To identify these, we employ Llama3-70B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024) to distinguish
172     between verifiable and non-verifiable prompts (see classification prompt and the portion of veri-
173     fiable prompts within each cluster in Appendix 11.9.2 and Figure 7). Overall, verifiable prompts
174     constitute 45.8% of total prompts from the previous step.
175   • Usefulness Evaluation. The remaining collection contains around 70K prompts, too large for
176     manual or automated fact-checking Randomly selecting a subset for evaluation would be subop-
177     timal, as it may include unclear or over-specific requests. Therefore, we propose a set of criteria
178     to identify useful prompts. A useful prompt needs to be (i) clear and understandable, (ii) general-
179     izable to various users or scenarios, (iii) has potential interest or value to a broader audience, and
180     (iv) is within the capabilities of LMs (e.g., excludes real-time data). To reduce single-model bias,
181
        we use two language models, GPT-4-Turbo and Llama3-70B-Instruct, as annotators. Each model
        scores prompts on a scale of 1 (low) to 5 (high) for each criterion. The scoring prompt is provided
182
        in Appendix 11.9.3. The final usefulness score for each prompt is calculated as the average score
183
        across all criteria, summing the score from two models. The usefulness scores are then used to
184     select an initial set of prompts, as we describe in Section 5.

187   4     VERIFY: V ERIFICATION AND E VIDENCE R ETRIEVAL FOR FACTUALITY
188         E VALUATION
189
190   In this section, we design an automatic factuality evaluation pipeline called VERIFY to measure the
191   appropriateness of these prompts, i.e., based on whether they tend to elicit unfactual responses from
192   LMs, for inclusion into the FACT B ENCH. Our goal is to verify model responses in natural user-LM
193   settings, where responses may include a mixture of verifiable and non-verifiable statements. To eval-
194
      uate model responses efficiently, we first establish the key criteria for determining the verifiability
      of statements.
195
196
197   4.1   FACTUAL E VALUATION THROUGH THE L ENS OF V ERIFIABILITY
198
      A statement is verifiable if it provides sufficient information to guide fact-checkers in verification
199
      (Ni et al., 2024). We classify verifiable statements into two categories:
200
201   Context-independent Statements: These are objective assertions that can be directly verified
202   against knowledge sources. For example, “RTX 3060 has a memory bandwidth of 360 Gbps.”.
203   Context-dependent Statements: These statements require additional information for verification.
204   For instance, to verify the statement “The difference in memory bandwidth between the RTX 3060
205   and RTX 3060 Ti is relatively small.” one needs knowledge of both GPUs’ bandwidths and an
206   understanding of what qualifies as relatively small in this context.
207   By focusing only on verifiable statements during user-LM interactions, we can assess the factuality
208   of the response in a more efficient and accurate manner, as we will describe next.
209
210
      4.2   U NIT E XTRACTION AND L ABELING
211
212   User requests span a wide range of domains (see the Figure in 2), and model responses contain a
213   variety of content types. To evaluate verifiable statements, we first decompose the model response
214   into independent content units. A content unit can represent a Fact, a Claim, an Instruction,
215   a Disclaimer, a Question, or other relevant content that appears during user-model interaction.
      Many of these content units are not verifiable, meaning they cannot be fact-checked. Examples


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      include questions and disclaimers, which often pertain to the context of the conversation or the
217   capabilities of the model itself rather than presenting factual information.
218
219   We label each unit by its type to identify those suitable for verification. We use the Llama3-70B-
220
      instruct model, which serves as the backbone LM for all tasks in this pipeline, to extract and label
      content units. A carefully crafted prompt with examples (see Appendix 11.9.4) guides the model in
221
      this task. We classify objective statements as Fact and potentially subjective, context-dependent
222
      statements as Claim. Only units labeled as Fact or Claim are passed to next step.
223
224
      4.3      U NIT D ECONTEXTUALIZATION
225
226   Gunjal & Durrett (2024) highlights the importance of “molecular units”—units that contain suffi-
227   cient information to be uniquely identifiable in factuality assessment. Inspired by that, we implement
228   a unit decontextualization component in our pipeline to minimally revise verifiable units and make
229   them self-contained. The prompt is provided in Appendix 11.9.5.
230
231   4.4      Q UERY G ENERATION AND E VIDENCE R ETRIEVAL
232
233   In order to verify the self-contained units, we need to retrieve relevant evidence from knowledge
234
      sources. We utilize SerperAPI1 for Google Search and web-evidence retrieval.
235   To enhance search quality and ensure the retrieval of relevant evidence that best assists in veri-
236   fication, we implement an interactive query refinement technique. The query generator operates
237   iteratively within an interactive feedback loop. It first produces a query for the target unit, which is
238   then used in Google Search to return relevant snippets. In subsequent rounds, the query generator
239   evaluates the relevance of the retrieved snippets for verifying the target unit and refines the query ac-
240
      cordingly. This iterative process continues for up to five rounds, progressively improving the query’s
      quality and relevance. The final set of queries and associated search results are then passed to the
241
      next step for final judgment. The prompt is provided in Appendix 11.9.6.
242
243
      4.5      F INAL A NSWER G ENERATION
244
245   In this step, the model (Llama3-70B-Instruct) is tasked with making a final decision on the units’ ac-
246   curacy by evaluating multiple rounds of retrieved evidence using Chain-of-Thought prompting Wei
247   et al. (2024a). For each unit, the model (i) summarizes the relevant knowledge points, (ii) assesses
248   their relationship to the unit, and (iii) determines whether the evidence supports (supported),
249   refutes (unsupported), or is inconclusive (undecidable). The prompt is provided in Ap-
250   pendix 11.9.7. This results in annotation labels for all verifiable units in the original model response.
251   An overview of our evaluation pipeline is provided in the right part of Figure 1.
252

287   5.1   T HREE T IERS OF P ROMPTS
288
      Using the Hallucination score, we can measure the appropriateness of the collected prompts for in-
289
      clusion in our final dataset. However, weaker LMs have a higher tendency to hallucinate. To prevent
290
      prompts queried to such LMs from populating the whole dataset, we categorize the prompts into
291   three tiers: Tier 1: Hard, Tier 2: Moderate, and Tier 3: Easy based on the overall performance
292   of the model used to generate responses. We rely on the LM ranking provided by the Chatbot Arena
293   Leaderboard website2 to do this categorization. Models and the statistics of each tier can be found
294   in Appendix Table 5. Heuristically, we selected prompts with a usefulness score of 4 or higher from
295   Hard, 4.5 or higher from Moderate and exactly 5 from Easy. This approach assumes that Hard
296   responses can best approximate prompt appropriateness, allowing for a lower usefulness threshold
297   to capture more prompts, with higher standards for subsequent tiers. Our initial prompt set includes
298   4.2K prompts, 53% from Hard, 34% from Moderate, and 13% from Easy.
299
300   5.2   B ENCHMARK C URATION
301
      We measure prompt appropriateness in each tier using the hallucination score (Equation 1) of their
302
      corresponding LM responses. We select 1.1K prompts with the highest scores, maintaining the
303
      original tier proportions (64% Hard, 32% Moderate, 14% Easy). After manual inspection,
304   we excluded 115 instances (details in Appendix 11.2), resulting in a benchmark of 985 prompts
305   called FACT B ENCH. Table 2 illustrates the benchmark statistics and compares it to other long-
306   form factuality evaluation benchmarks. Our work introduces the first dynamic, real-world factuality
307   evaluation benchmark comprising hallucination prompts across diverse topics.

      7.1   FACTUALITY I MPROVES WITH P ROPERITARY LM S AND E ASIER P ROMPTS

347
      To compare model performance on FACT B ENCH, we use the factual precision metric proposed
348   by Min et al. (2023). This metric quantifies an LM’s factuality by calculating the proportion of
349   supported units among all extracted units in a response, averaged across all responses (detailed
350   in Appendix 11.4).
351
      Table 1 compares the factual precision of LMs on FACT B ENCH as measured by different evaluation
352
      methods. Despite all baselines verifying responses at the finest granularity (standalone units), we
353
      observe varying factual precision ranges across different pipelines. We observe that VERIFY con-
354   sistently maintains the same ranking of models across all three tiers, unlike other evaluation meth-

362   undecidable units across all LMs. This primarily stems from stronger subjectivity in Llama3.1-
363   405B-Instruct, with more frequent use of subjective adjectives such as “solid,” “exclusive,” and
364   “well-known.” The rigorous reasoning logic enforced in our pipeline typically classifies these sub-
365   jective elements as undecidable, thereby reducing the factual precision. Detailed analysis and
366   examples are provided in Appendix 11.8.
367   Another significant observation is the consistent improvement in factuality precision across LMs
368   from the Hard to Easy tiers, as shown by all evaluation methods. This aligns with our benchmark
369   curation approach, where hallucination prompts are ranked based on their tendency to provoke in-
370   correct or unverifiable responses. Easy prompts are less likely to trigger hallucinations in strong

503   8                 C ONCLUSION
504
505   We presented VERIFY, a factuality evaluation pipeline that automatically annotates the factual-
506   ity of LM responses in real-world settings. VERIFY breaks down a response into content units,
507   identifies verifiable ones, verifies them using retrieved web evidence, and categorizes each unit as
508
      supported, unsupported, or undecidable. Our method correlates strongly with human
      evaluations compared to existing state-of-the-art methods and is more robust in handling flawed
509
      units. Using VERIFY, we curated FACT B ENCH, a benchmark of 985 prompts across 213 fine-
510
      grained topics that are grouped into three tiers: Hard, Moderate, and Easy. Prompts in each tier
511   are sorted by appropriateness, i.e. their tendency to elicit hallucinated content from LMs. FACT-
512   B ENCH is the first real-world and diverse factuality evaluation benchmark designed to be regularly
513   updatable to capture evolving factuality challenges faced by language models. Finally, we used our
514   dataset to evaluate frontier LMs in proprietary and open-source categories. Our findings show that
515   the proprietary model exhibits better factually, improving factuality from Hard to Easy tiers. Inter-
516   estingly, Llama3.1-405B-Instruct shows lower factuality mainly due to its frequent use of subjective
517   terms that are hard to verify.
518   Current factuality-evaluating metrics oversimplify the evaluation and do not consider model refusals
519   and low factual recall, which is an important direction for future research in this field. Additionally,
520   future work can explore more sophisticated evaluation pipelines that account for individual factual
521   support as well as logical inter-unit connections. Such approaches would verify not only the factual
522   accuracy of each content unit but also the logical coherence within the overall response, further
523   improving the reliability of LM evaluation methods.

704   11.1   DATA C LEANING
705
706   We begin by collecting prompts from the first turn of conversations in the LMSYS-Chat-1M dataset,
707   which is a large-scale, in-the-wild LM conversations dataset. Since the existing language labels
708   are unreliable, we employ the Llama3-70B-Instruct model (AI@Meta, 2024) to identify the lan-
709   guage of each conversation using the prompt in Appendix 11.9.1. This gives us 516,771 distinct
710   English prompts with at least 32 characters. Next, we remove anonymized (30.9%) and duplicated
711
      (12.1%) prompts. Meanwhile, we observed that some users queried LMs with thousands of identical
      prompts. To mitigate this issue’s impact on subsequent clusters, we filter out prompts with a Jaccard
712
      similarity score greater than 0.9. Our cleaned data contains 294,333 distinct prompts.

      11.2   M ANUAL C HECK ON P ROMPT V ERIFIABILITY
715
716   In order to ensure the verifiability specified in Section 3, three authors have conducted multiple
717   rounds of human inspection and validation to exclude all non-verifiable prompts like problem-
718   solving (e.g., “A suit manufacturer has 14 suits for men and 4 suits for women. How many suits
719   are available overall?”) and faithfulness-related (e.g., “Translate the given text”) tasks. More unver-
720   ifiable examples are available in our prompt at Appendix 11.9.2. We excluded 115 prompts in the
721   manual check process.

723   11.3   BASELINE D ESCRIPTION
724
      We use gpt-3.5-turbo-0613 (Brown et al., 2020) as a backbone LM when running all base-
725
      lines.
726
727   • FactScore (Min et al., 2023): FactScore evaluates the factual precision of LMs by breaking text
728     into atomic facts and assessing the percentage of facts supported by Wikipedia articles. The
729     original FactScore method is provided with Wikipedia pages with relevant information. However,
730     the extracted units from in-the-wild requests are not associated with a Wikipedia page and might
731     not even be found in Wikipedia articles. To make a fair comparison, we use the Wikipedia API
732
        (Goldsmith, 2014) to map these atomic units to the 5 closest Wikipedia topics in the Wiki database
        for retrieval.
733
734   • Search-Augmented Factuality Evaluator (SAFE) (Wei et al., 2024b): SAFE evaluates long-
735     form factuality by decomposing text into atomic facts adopting the same FactScore fact extraction
736
        component and checking each fact’s relevancy to the original query. For relevant facts, SAFE
        queries Google search engine for evidence retrieval, and label each fact as either supported or
737
        refuted accordingly.
738
739
      • Factcheck-GPT (Wang et al., 2024a): Factcheck-GPT is a hallucination detection and mitigation
        framework. In the annotation phase, it assesses the factuality of LM-generated content using
740
        a multi-step annotation pipeline that includes the decomposition of claims, decontextualization,
741
        evidence retrieval through Google Search, evidence snippets generation, final factuality decision,
742     and revision of non-factual elements. For this study, the final revision step is excluded from the
743     baseline methodology.

       11.8   C ASE S TUDY ON LL A MA3.1-405B-I NSTRUCT AND LL A MA3.1-70B-I NSTRUCT
973           PERFORMANCE GAP
974
975    As mentioned in the main text, the performance of Llama3.1-405B-Instruct on VERIFY is close
976    to or even lower than that of Llama3.1-70B-Instruct. We conduct an in-depth investigation into
977    the possible reasons. Based on results in Figure 4, we find that Llama3.1-405B-Instruct has the
978    highest proportion of undecidable labels among all the models, which directly results in its
979    lower factual precision. After manually reviewing 30 cases with the largest factual precision gap
980
       between the 405B and 70B models, we observed a common phenomenon: the 405B model tends to
       use more subjective modifiers such as “solid”, “exclusive”, and “well-known”, while the 70B model
981
       usually prefer plain descriptions. For example, consider the following case when both 405B and
982
       70B model respond with the same Debian guidebook:
983
984      Llama3.1-405B-Instruct
985      Atomic Unit: “Debian GNU/Linux: Guide to Installation and Usage” by John Goerzen and
986      Ossama Othman is a solid foundation for understanding Debian’s basics.
987
988
         Reasoning: One KNOWLEDGE point states that the book is a “nice book and CD combo”
         that can get someone started with Debian, but notes that the book lacks in-depth information,
989
         While the book is mentioned as a “nice” resource for getting started with Debian, it is also
990
         criticized for lacking in-depth information. Therefore, the evidence is insufficient to confirm
991      or deny that the book is a solid foundation.
992
993      Label: undecidable
994
995      Llama3.1-70B-Instruct:
996      Atomic Unit: “Debian GNU/Linux: Guide to Installation and Usage” by John Goerzen and
997      Ossama Othman is a guidebook of Debian basics.
998
         Reasoning: The KNOWLEDGE points strongly imply that the book is a guide that covers
999
         basics of Debian.
1000
1001     Label: suppported
1002
1003   Based on the case above, we find that due to the introduction of a detailed CoT reasoning mecha-
1004   nism and the undecidable label, VERIFY is very rigorous in verifying atomic units, especially
1005   when subjective adjectives are involved. We use a pre-trained subjectivity classification model
1006   GroNLP/mdebertav3-subjectivity-multilingual (Ruggeri et al., 2023) to classify
1007
       the output units of both models. The classification results show that 9.4% of the 405B model’s
       response units are classified as subjective, while only 7.5% of the 70B model’s response units are
1008
       subjective. This difference explains the reason why the atomic units of 405B model are more likely
1009
       to be undecidable. Overall, this finding confirms the rigor of our approach and provides a new
1010   perspective for future research on subjectivity in model factuality evaluation.
