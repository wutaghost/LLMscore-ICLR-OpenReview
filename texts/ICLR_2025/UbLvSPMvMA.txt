009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While binary vectorization and sparse representations have recently emerged as
012            promising strategies for efficient vector storage and mechanistic interpretability,
013            the integration of these two paradigms has until now remained largely unexplored.
014            In this paper, we introduce an exciting approach for sparse binary representations,
015            leveraging a soft TopK Cosine Loss to facilitate the transition from dense to sparse
016
               latent spaces. Unlike traditional TopK methods which impose rigid sparsity con-
               straints, our approach naturally yields a more flexible distribution of activations,
017
               effectively capturing the varying degrees of conceptual depth present in the data.
018
               Furthermore, our cosine loss formulation inherently mitigates the emergence of
019            inactive features, thereby eliminating the need for complex re-activation strate-
020            gies prevalent in other recent works. We validate our method on a large dataset
021            of biomedical concept embeddings, demonstrating enhanced interpretability and
022            significant reductions in storage overhead. Our present findings highlight the clear
023            potential of cosine-based Binary Sparsity Alignment for developing interpretable
024            and efficient concept representations, positioning our approach as a compelling so-
025            lution for applications in decision-making systems and compact vector databases.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The rapid growth of data in various domains has necessitated the development of efficient represen-
030   tation techniques that not only reduce storage requirements but also enhance interpretability. Sparse
031   representations have emerged as a promising solution, enabling the encoding of information using
032   only a subset of active features. This sparsity leads to significant reductions in storage overhead and
033   computational complexity, making it particularly advantageous for big data applications.
034
      Binary representations, characterized by their compactness and efficiency, have also gained traction
035
      in recent years. By representing data with binary 1-bit values, models can achieve faster inference
036   times and lower memory footprints, which are crucial for deployment in resource-constrained envi-
037   ronments. However, the integration of sparsity and binary representation remains unexplored.
038
039
      Current strategies for producing binary representations rely on quantization techniques that con-
      vert continuous values into discrete levels. While quantization can yield compact representations, it
040
      struggles to effectively capture the nuanced activation patterns inherent in many linear autoencoders
041
      used for sparsification. Indeed, features of sparse representations trained with existing techniques
042   typically exhibit varying degrees of activation, reflecting the complexity of the underlying data,
043   as detailed in section 2. This variability poses a significant challenge for traditional quantization
044   methods, which tend to impose rigid thresholds that fail to accommodate gradual transitions in fea-
045   ture activations, as well as the varying information needs of different concepts to be embedded.
046   Consequently, the resulting binary representations often lack the fidelity necessary for accurate
047   reconstruction and interpretation.
048   Despite the advantages of both sparse and binary representations, there is currently no established
049   methodology for generating sparse binary embeddings that leverage the strengths of both paradigms.
050   In this paper, we introduce a novel approach—a soft cosine alignment loss for Binary Sparsity Align-
051   ment (BSA)—that addresses this gap by enabling the effective transition from dense to sparse binary
052   latent spaces. Our method preserves the interpretability and efficiency of sparse representations,
053   without encouraging intensity variations in feature activation by applying binarization at training
      time, thereby paving the way for future advancements in representation learning.

      2.1   S PARSE E MBEDDINGS
057
058   Sparse embeddings are a form of vector data representation where the majority of the components
059   are zero, significantly reducing the dimensionality of the data while retaining essential information.
060
      Sparse embeddings offer several key advantages, particularly in terms of computational efficiency
061   and scalability. These embeddings can help reduce the effective dimensionality of data, leading to
062   faster computations and lower storage requirements, which is especially beneficial in large-scale
063   applications (Nguyen et al., 2012; Liang et al., 2021). Sparse embeddings also allow for efficient
064   data engineering and transfer learning, as they combine the strengths of high-dimensional linear
065   models with the benefits of latent factor representations (Van Balen & Goethals, 2021). Moreover,
066   the efficiency of sparse embeddings is further enhanced by the reduced number of floating-point
067   operations due to sparse matrix multiplications, leading to quicker retrieval tasks (Paria et al., 2020).
068   In addition to computational benefits, sparse embeddings improve model interpretability and ro-
069   bustness. They provide better interpretability compared to dense embeddings, as seen in various
070   applications from word embeddings to retrieval tasks (Sun et al., 2016; Kong et al., 2023). The
071   sparse structure also enhances robustness to noise and interference, maintaining competitive accu-
072   racy while offering advantages in hyperparameter tuning and learning speed (Ahmad & Scheinkman,
073   2019). Furthermore, sparse embeddings demonstrate superior performance in capturing meaningful
074   data structures and achieving high accuracy in tasks like signal recovery and object classification, of-
075
      ten outperforming other competitive algorithms (Nguyen et al., 2012; Medini et al., 2021). Overall,
      these attributes make sparse embeddings a valuable tool in machine learning and data processing,
076
      providing both practical and theoretical benefits.
077
078   Sparse representations in machine learning have been extensively studied, with various methods
079   proposed to enforce sparsity constraints. A notable technique is top-k sparsity, which has been suc-
080   cessfully applied in k-sparse autoencoders. These autoencoders retain only the k highest activations
081
      in the hidden layers, leading to improved classification performance, while maintaining simplic-
      ity and fast encoding stages (Makhzani & Frey, 2014). Autoencoders compress input data into a
082
      compact internal representation and then reconstruct it, aiming to minimize the difference between
083
      the input and output in order to learn efficient data representations through unsupervised learning
084   (Bisong, 2019; Bank et al., 2021). Modern autoencoders often consist in shallow linear systems,
085   even in advanced mechanistic interpertability use cases (Gao et al., 2024; Templeton et al., 2024).
086
087
      Similarly to k-sparse autoencoders, winner-take-all autoencoders employ a competitive mechanism
      that ensures only the most significant activations are preserved, facilitating the learning of hierarchi-
088
      cal and deep sparse representations in an unsupervised manner (Makhzani & Frey, 2015).
089
090   Another prominent approach involves L1 and L2 regularization. Older techniques, such as Sparse
091   LSA, leverage L1 regularization to enforce sparsity on the projection matrix, yielding compact
092   and interpretable topic-word representations (Chen et al., 2011). More recently, L1-regularized
093
      autoencoders have demonstrated the ability to achieve high compression ratios with minimal arti-
      facts (Chung et al., 2024). For topic modeling, L2 regularization has been employed to balance the
094
      sparsity of topic and word distributions, optimizing the trade-off between sparse representation and
095
      model accuracy (Anon, 2018). Additionally, non-smooth L1 regularization has been shown to be
096   more effective than smooth approximations in training sparse autoencoders, directly targeting the
097   sparsity objective (Amini et al., 2022).
098
099
      Anchor-based transformation methods have also gained traction. These projection methods intro-
      duce a small set of anchor embeddings and a sparse transformation matrix to efficiently represent
100
      large vocabularies, enhancing scalability and performance in tasks such as text classification and
101
      language modeling (Liang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2019; Medini et al., 2021). These methods over-
102   come the scalability issues of embedding large vocabularies by capturing the underlying structure
103   and similarities between objects more efficiently than traditional independent embedding techniques.
104
105
      However, existing sparse methods generated real-valued embeddings, which might not be sufficient
      to enable efficient search and storage, and are less suitable as input to neurosymbolic approaches
106
      than binary embeddings.

      2.1.1   B INARY E MBEDDINGS
109
110   Binary embeddings are a nonlinear dimension reduction technique that transforms high-dimensional
111   data into binary strings while preserving the structure of the original space (Yi et al., 2015). They
112   can for instance be used to convert real-valued embeddings into binary representations (Sherki et al.,
113   2021). Binary embeddings significantly reduce the size of real-valued embeddings. Tissier et al.
114
      (2019) note that they can achieve a 97% reduction in vector size compared to real-valued embed-
      dings, and they enable much faster vector operations compared to real-valued embeddings. Indeed,
115
      vector operations can be performed using bitwise operators instead of floating-point arithmetic, Top-
116
      K queries can be executed up to 30 times faster with binary vectors compared to real-valued vectors,
117   and loading binary vectors from storage is also much quicker. Despite the significant reduction in
118   size, binary embeddings can retain most of the semantic information present in the real-valued ones.
119
120
      Recent research has explored various approaches to train binary embeddings for efficient repre-
      sentation and retrieval. Huynh & Saab (2020) proposed fast binary embeddings using quantized
121
      Johnson-Lindenstrauss transforms, achieving polynomial and exponential error decay rates. Zhuang
122
      et al. (2016) developed a triplet-based deep binary embedding network, formulating the problem as
123   multi-label classification and significantly reducing training time. Mostard et al. (2022) presented a
124   semantic-preserving siamese autoencoder for quantizing word embeddings, preserving semantic in-
125   formation while reducing dimensionality. Their approach outperformed baselines on word similarity
126   and sentence classification tasks, with individual bits holding interpretable semantic information.
127
      However, these methods do not attempt to produce sparse binary representations, and their applica-
128
      tion to already-sparse embeddings offers no guarantee of perserved sparsity. The aim of this work
129   is to propose a method which enables simultaneous sparsification and binarization of the input data,
130   with stable and intuitive learning dynamics.

161
      embedding. The training loss is the sum of the reconstruction and sparsity alignment losses.

      3.1   B INARY S PARSITY A LIGNMENT (BSA)
165
166   Our method is applicable to the binary sparsification of latent vectors derived from models trained
167   under a contrastive learning objective, and whose relatedness can be measured using the cosine
168   similarity metric. Contrastive learning and the cosine similarity metric in particular have become
169   the standard unsupervised representation learning toolbox for years, with few challengers so far, and
170   they are thus well-studied at this point (Oord et al., 2018).
171   Our approach can generate sparse embeddings of size N and average feature activation count K,
172   capable of reconstructing the initial normalized embeddings of size E using a single linear decoder.
173
      Similarly to the previously-mentioned sparsification approaches, we train our sparse binary encoder
174
      using an autoencoder pipeline (E → N → E). To achieve binary activations, we apply the sigmoid
175   activation function after the linear layer of the encoder, to bring activations in the 0-to-1 range.
176
177             SparseEmbedding := Sigmoid(LinearEncoder(InitialEmbedding))
178
179   By using small initial weights, and because of the isotropic nature of contrastive embedding spaces,
180   our latent embeddings at initialization consist mostly of values just above or below the natural center
181   of 0.5 after application of the sigmoid activation, with roughly half of them above the threshold.
182   Rounding is subsequently applied to binarize the features during training, before passing them to
183   the decoder layer. As rounding is not a differentiable operation, we define a passthrough rounding
184   function in PyTorch for this purpose, which we describe in subsection 3.3.
185
186             SparseBinaryEmbedding := PassthroughRound(SparseEmbedding)
187
188   The key intuition behind our approach is our framing of the desire for sparsity not as a constraint
189   (e.g. TopK) nor as a general regularization (e.g. L1), but instead as an alignment problem. Sparsity
190
      alignment can be measured as the cosine similarity between the currently-produced latent vectors
      and their corresponding ideal sparse vectors, with their K most activated features all positive and
191
      with values close to 1 and their N − K unactivated features close to 0.1
192
193             SparsityAlignmentLoss := CosineDistance(
194
                                               SparseEmbedding,
195
                                               TopK(SparseEmbedding)
196
197                                      )
198
199
      Framing the sparsity desire as cosine alignment plays very well with our autoencoder reconstruction
      loss, also framed as a cosine distance minimization between initial and reconstructed embeddings.
200
201             ReconstructionLoss := CosineDistance(
202
                                            LinearDecoder(SparseBinaryEmbedding),
203
204
                                            InitialEmbedding
205                                   )
206
207   The bounded nature of the cosine distance metric (0-2) ensures that both losses evolve in similar
208
      value ranges, and can meaningfully be combined, unlike MSE distances which suffer from explosion
      effects at large N values. The sum of these two losses (possibly weighted as desired by modulating
209
      hyperparameter α) forms the total loss of the system, with no need for any other regularization or
210
      feature re-activation term.
211
212             TrainingLoss := ReconstructionLoss + SparsityAlignmentLoss ∗ α

      3.3   PASS - THROUGH ROUND F UNCTION
236
      As noted previously, our reconstruction loss is calculated on the basis of the rounded (binarized)
237   sparse embedding. This poses a significant challenge as the Round function produces no gradient
238   for its input, which means that the sparse embedding would receive no training signal from the
239   reconstruction loss, which cannot possibly lead to good outcomes.
240
      To overcome this problem, we define a pass-through Round(x) function which propagates its gradi-
241
      ent to x. This is a strategy similar to the one used in quantization/codebook strategies already used
242
      in other works (Baevski et al., 2020; Esser et al., 2021).
243
244            PassthroughRound(x) := Round(NoGrad(x)) + (x − NoGrad(x)))
245
246   This function behaves like Round(x) at inference, but like x during gradient descent. The effect of
247   this pass-through will be to send a positive signal to features of x whose activation would positively
248   benefit the final reconstruction, and a negative signal to features whose activation has negatively
249   affected the reconstruction (something that is further substantiated below).
250

252   3.4   T RAINING DYNAMICS
253
      This section presents an analysis of the training dynamics of our approach, to build up an intuitive
254   understanding of its underlying mechanisms. Its aim is not to depict in a completely accurate way
255   the derivatives, but to outline the main components and their impact. We also compare the resulting
256   dynamics with those of the popular TopK and L1 approaches, to highlight perceived improvements
257   over the state of the art.
258
      Let us first examine the reconstruction loss. Because rounding is already applied during training,
259
      our approach does not suffer from any mismatch between inference and training times. The value
260   vectors from the decoder do not have the possibility to be modulated, leading to a lesser risk of
261   ”weak activation” of the latent features.
262
263
      Unlike the frequently used TopK clipping method, our approach also propagates the gradient to the
      entire feature space, and not only its first K components; this clipping is a key weakness of TopK
264
      training which give no chance to weakly activated features to reactivate by receiving a positive signal
265
      once they fall outside the top K activated features.
266
267   Finally, the optional TopK loss term can be used to favor a representation of acceptable accuracy
268   even for concepts activating more than K features, ensuring that our method can be used as a drop-in
269
      replacement in existing TopK pipelines.



                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Let us now consider the sparsity alignment loss. Ignoring the effects of normalization for a
271   moment, the general dynamic of cosine similarity is that it increases when features have the same
272   polarity (sign) in both compared vectors, and decreases sharply when features have opposite polarity
273   in the compared vectors. Given our feature activations can only be positive and between 0 and 1,
274   the dynamic is better understood in the setting where these vectors are shifted downwards by the ε
275   term, with values between −ε and 1 − ε (although the dynamic applies identically for ε = 0).
276
      In this setting, the top K features of the sparse vector will receive a positive gradient towards their
277   maximal value (1 − ε), while the remaining N − K features will receive a negative gradient to-
278   wards their minimal value (−ε). This gradient will then be counter-balanced by the reconstruction
279   loss, where features contributing positively to the reconstruction might or might not get deactivated
280   depending on the weight α assigned to the alignment loss.
281
      This approach possess a significant advantage over a classical L1 loss, because only the bottom
282
      N − K features will receive a downward push, while the top K features will be boosted. This
283   makes the training significantly more stable and less prone to total collapse (where significantly
284   fewer than K features remain activated on average).
285
286
      Compared to TopK clipping, this strategy has the additional benefit of encouraging sparsity towards a
      specific sparsity goal without enforcing an identical number of activation for every concept. Indeed,
287
      concepts whose representation requires more than K active features to be accurate will likely end up
288
      with a larger decrease in reconstruction loss when these features remain activated, than the increase
289   of sparsity alignment loss that they cause. By modulating α, it is possible to enforce more or less
290   the sparsity constraint, for instance by increasing its importance as the training progresses.

      4     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
294
295
      We validate our approach on a dataset of biomedical concept embeddings produced using a semantic
      model trained contrastively for the biomedical domain, called BioLORD-2023-C (Remy et al.,
296
      2024). Our concept list orginates from UMLS (Bodenreider, 2004) and contains more than 4M
297
      unique concepts, referenced by their canonical name (synonyms not being used in this setup).
298
299   Note that the embedding model is not finetuned in this experiment and considered as a gold standard,
300   since our objective is to sparsify already-existing concept embeddings while increasing their inter-
301
      pretability. We leave as future work whether our losses can be used to train sparse binary features in
      an end-to-end manner.
302
303   We aim to answer four research questions about Binary Sparsity Alignment (BSA) in this paper:
304
305       RQ1: Can dense embeddings be mapped to sparse binary embeddings without significant
306
               information loss using BSA? Here, we define significant information loss as an average
               reconstruction loss above the cosine margin between different concepts used while training
307
               the biomedical semantic model (µ = 0.15), as this would lead to frequent concept mix-ups.
308
309       RQ2: Which role play hyperparameters N and K in the reconstruction capabilities of BSA?
310
               In particular, it it possible to reduce the reconstruction loss to an arbitrary low value by
               increasing N , at which rate, and how should K be adapted as N increases.
311
312       RQ3: Is BSA sufficient to achieve any desired degree of sparsity? Here, we define achieving a
313            desired degree of sparsity as producing on average K activated features per concept, with
314
               a standard deviation significantly inferior to K itself (for any reasonable value of K).
315       RQ4: What does the activation pattern of the latent features look like after BSA training?
316            In particular, can we detect the presence of a significant number of “dead” latent features
317            which do not activate for any known concept? Are all features activating a roughly equal
318
               number of times, or are there features shared among more concepts than others?
319
      To answer these research questions, we train 16 autoencoders with BSAs of varying hyperparameters
320   (N ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32} ∗ 1024, K ∈ {32, 64, 128, 256}, α = 1, ε = 0.125) and measure their ability to
321   sparsify the latent space with minimal reconstruction loss.
322
323
      We also analyze the hyperparameter sensitivity of the approach.

418   5.3   ACTIVATION PATTERNS
419
420   A common failure mode of sparse autoencoders is their tendency to under-use the feature space by
421
      leaving encoder features which never activate, sometime referred to as “dead latents”. Most papers
      cited in the Related Works section resort to re-activation strategies to mitigate this issue, encouraging
422
      the activation of features whose activation was not witnessed recently.
423
424   These strategies are not necessary for avoiding permanently-inactive features with our approach. To
425   demonstrate this, we observe the distribution of feature activations in one our trained autoencoders
426   on a test set of 775k concept names extracted from SnomedCT (Schulz & Klein, 2008).
427   Figure 2 demonstrates that not only the activation count per concept remains in distribution (left), but
428   also that all features were activated a reasonable amount of time (right). 99% of all features activated
429   between 205 and 165673 times (between 0.03% of concepts and 23.4% of embedded concepts).
430
431

      This figure is not sensitive to the 0.5 threshold used for rounding feature activations, as the vast
433   majority of feature activations offer a pretty clear signal. An astonishing 99.95% of non-binary ac-
434   tivation intensities are beyond the central range between 0.1 and 0.9 (although this is partly because
435   of the high sparsity of the embeddings), and 86% of activated features have an non-binary activation
436   intensity above 0.6 (see Figure 3).

441   6    C ONCLUSION
442
443   In this work, we introduced our soft cosine-based Binary Sparsity Alignment approach, a novel 1-bit
444   sparsification method capable of bridging the gap between dense and sparse binary latent spaces.
445
      Our experiments demonstrated that BSA transforms dense embeddings into interpretable sparse bi-
      nary representations with minimal information loss, thanks to its soft cosine sparsity alignment loss.
446
      Unlike existing approaches that impose rigid sparsity constraints or suffer from unstable training
447
      dynamics, sparsity alignment-based training is stable and flexible by design. Its framework not only
448   provides practical benefits but also establishes a theoretical foundation for understanding binary
449   sparsification in the context of contrastive learning. Overall, our findings underscore the effective-
450   ness and versatility of soft sparsity alignment, paving the way for future advancements in efficient
451   representation storage and more interpretable decision-support systems.
