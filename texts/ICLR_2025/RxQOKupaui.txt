009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Parameter-efficient transfer learning (PETL) aims to adapt pre-trained models to
012            new downstream tasks while minimizing the number of fine-tuned parameters.
013            Adapters, a popular approach in PETL, inject additional capacity into existing net-
014            works by incorporating low-rank projections, achieving performance comparable
015            to full fine-tuning with significantly fewer parameters. This paper investigates the
016
               relationship between the placement of an adapter and its performance. We observe
               that adapter location within a network significantly impacts its effectiveness, and
017
               that the optimal placement is task-dependent. To exploit this observation, we
018
               introduce an extended search space of adapter connections, including long-range
019            and recurrent adapters. We demonstrate that even randomly selected adapter place-
020            ments from this expanded space yield improved results, and that high-performing
021            placements often correlate with high gradient rank. Our findings reveal that a small
022            number of strategically placed adapters can match or exceed the performance of
023            the common baseline of adding adapters in every block, opening a new avenue for
024            research into optimal adapter placement strategies.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Transfer learning is one of the key techniques in modern deep learning, frequently used to reduce the
029
      cost of training and to provide better generalization in the low-data regime (Zhai et al., 2019; Liu
030   et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2020). In transfer learning, pre-trained neural networks are fine-tuned to
031   solve a new, often related, task. However, fine-tuning the entire model is costly in terms of compute,
032   memory, and storage, especially considering the size of modern deep learning networks (Dehghani
033   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Chowdhery et al., 2023). This lead to the
034   proliferation of parameter efficient transfer learning (PETL) methods (Pan et al., 2022; Edalati et al.,
035   2022; Hu et al., 2022; Mercea et al., 2024; Pfeiffer et al., 2020b; Hao et al., 2024; Jie & Deng, 2022),
036   that aim to reduce the memory and storage cost by approximating the full fine-tuning solution using
037   significantly less parameters.
038   One of the most popular approaches in PETL are adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019; Rebuffi et al.,
039   2017) – small modules based on low-rank projections which are inserted into pre-trained models
040   to facilitate transfer learning while minimizing parameter overhead. While both the original and
041   new parameters contribute to the output calculation, only the parameters of the adapters are updated
042   during the optimization process. This results in a reduced memory footprint, making it practical to
043   train large networks with limited resources.
044   In essence, adapters may be interpreted as a way to mimic the fine-tuning of a group of layers
045   using smaller modules. Therefore they are often added parallel to or in-between existing layers. At
046   the same time, existing work on transfer learning shows that layers in a pre-trained network have
047   different importance during fine-tuning and that their parameters affect the performance in different
048   amounts (Chatterji et al., 2020). Despite this observation, however, adapters are typically added
049   uniformly across the whole network, irrespective of these layer-specific differences. Consequently,
050
      we suspect such a practice to be sub-optimal and hypothesise that task specific adapter placement
      could further improve the fine-tuning performance and efficiency.
051
052   Motivated by the above, we investigate the effectiveness of adding adapters at different locations
053   within a neural network. Our work highlights the importance of adapter placement and its influence
      on transfer performance. Our main contributions are:

      2.1   PARAMETER E FFICIENT T RANSFER L EARNING
072
073   The goal of Parameter Efficient Transfer Learning is to adapt a pre-trained model to a downstream
074   task while minimizing the number of trainable parameters (Han et al., 2024). A common approach
075   in PETL are adapters – small trainable modules inserted into the pre-trained network (Rebuffi
076   et al., 2017; Houlsby et al., 2019; Bapna et al., 2019), which have proven to be effective in various
077   applications, including multi-task learning (Pfeiffer et al., 2020a;b; Stickland & Murray, 2019),
078   knowledge injection (Wang et al., 2020) or continual learning (Lin et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2024). A
079   related PETL approach is LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) and its numerous extensions (Karimi Mahabadi
080
      et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024; Zhang
      et al., 2023), which model the update of the parameters using linear low-rank projections, merging the
081
      new weights with the pre-trained ones at inference. Other alternatives include Prompt Tuning (Lester
082
      et al., 2021; Shi & Lipani, 2023) and Prefix Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021; Jia et al., 2022) that append a
083   trainable vector of tokens to the layer input. Solutions based on Side-Tuning (Zhang et al., 2020)
084   allow to bypass the gradient propagation through the backbone, accelerating the training (Mercea
085   et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Munkhdalai et al., 2024). These various PETL
086   methods can be applied together using algorithms designed to select their optimal configurations (Mao
087   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2021). In this work, instead of introducing another PETL
088   approach, we focus on the adapters, examining how their performance is impacted by their placement.
089
090   2.2   I MPORTANCE OF A DAPTER ’ S P LACEMENT
091
092   A key design choice in PETL is determining where to introduce additional computational capacity to
093   the model. For adapters, common placement strategies include the sequential and parallel approaches.
094   Sequential placement inserts the adapters between consecutive layers (Houlsby et al., 2019; Mahabadi
095   et al., 2021; Lauscher et al., 2020), while the parallel placement adds them using a separate residual
096   connection within a layer (He et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Jie & Deng, 2022).
097   Although adapters are usually inserted in every module of a transformer, multiple works find it
098   sufficient to apply them only after the Feed Forward blocks (Luo et al., 2023; Bapna et al., 2019;
099
      Pfeiffer et al., 2020a). Notably, Houlsby et al. (2019) demonstrated that adapters in lower network
      layers can be removed without a significant performance drop. Rücklé et al. (2020) confirmed this
100
      observation, proposing an iterative approach that removes the adapters during the fine-tuning from
101
      selected layers. In the context of Mixture of Experts, higher layers were observed to require more
102   LoRA adapters (Gao et al., 2024). At the same time, a study by Chen et al. (2023a) on parameter
103   allocation policies in PETL concluded that uniform parameter distribution generally performs best on
104   the GLUE benchmark. These findings suggest a non-trivial relationship between adapter placement
105   and performance, motivating our investigation. While previous studies focused on adapting the layers
106   locally, we extend the adapter architecture to include long-range and recurrent low-rank projections.
107   Our goal is to understand how the location, by itself, impacts the adapter performance, and whether
      this influence can be predicted a priori to the training.

      2.3   R ANK -BASED M ETRICS IN A SSESSING L EARNING C APABILITIES
109
110   Measures based on the rank, especially estimates of effective dimensionality of the activations,
111   have been studied in previous research to assess the learning capacity of models. For instance, in
112   Reinforcement Learning, a decline in the feature rank of a value network can be associated with a
113   decreased performance (Kumar et al., 2020). Similarly, Lyle et al. (2022) suggest that a high feature
114   rank is a necessary condition for learning progress. In transfer learning, a significant change in the
115   rank can serve as an indicator for neuron growth or pruning within a layer during fine-tuning (Maile
116
      et al., 2023). Moreover, activation ranks tend to diminish with increasing network depth (Feng
      et al., 2022), which has been linked to a degraded performance of linear probe on out-of-distribution
117
      data (Masarczyk et al., 2024). Those observations underscore the value of exploring rank-based
118
      metrics that estimate the importance of the placement of new computational capacity in the network.

123   3.1   A DAPTERS
124
      An adapter, Aφ , is a small network with parameters φ that is injected into a pre-trained model (Houlsby
125
      et al., 2019; Rebuffi et al., 2017). Adapters are often used in transfer-learning, where the parameters of
126   the pre-trained backbone remain frozen, and only the adapters weights are updated during fine-tuning.
127
128   We focus on the most common adapter architecture, which consists of a low-rank projection followed
129
      by a non-linear transformation in the bottleneck dimension (He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022):

130                                 Aφ (x) = α σ(LN (x)Wdown )Wup ,                                    (1)
131
132   where Wdown ∈ Rdmodel ×r , Wup ∈ Rr×dmodel , dmodel is the hidden dimension of the network, σ
133
      represents the non-linear activation function, and the parameter α is a trainable scalar that adjusts
      the scale of the output of the adapter. The function LN (·) indicates an optional layer normalization
134
      performed on the inputs of the adapter. For all types of adapters used in this work, we include this
135
      Layer-Norm operation, as we observe that it increases the stability of the learning. The bottleneck
136   dimension r of the down projection is called the rank of an adapter, and usually satisfies r   dmodel .
137
138   Consider a block of layers Fθ (x) of the pre-trained model (e.g. the Multi-Head Attention or the Feed
139
      Forward module of a transformer). An adapter can be placed in parallel to the computational flow of
      the network by applying:
140
                                         xi+1 = xi + Fθ (xi ) + Aφ (xi ),                               (2)
141
142   where xi + Fθ (xi ) represents the standard residual connection of the model. Such an approach is
143   known as the parallel adapter, and was reported to perform better than the alternatives based on
144   sequential addition (He et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022). Adapters are predominately
145   used together with transformer-based architectures where they are injected at every block.

445
446   6.2      P LACEMENT S ELECTION A LGORITHM
447
448   In the previous section we demonstrated that the rank score allows a priori identification of the
449   optimal single adapter placement for selected datasets. However, in practice, we aim to choose an
450   arbitrary number N of locations. To this end, we propose a simple algorithm that uses a scoring
451   function, such as that described in Equation 7, to rank locations and select the top performers.
452   Note that greedily picking the top-N indices from the score matrix would predominantly return
453   adapters that are placed within a localized neighborhood. Consequently, multiple adapters would
454   modify the same input or contribute to the same output, introducing unnecessary redundancy and
455   limiting exploration of the search space. To mitigate this, we propose the Gradient Gradient Adapters
456   (GGA) algorithm, which discounts the scores for subsequent placements proportionally to the distance
457   from the previously selected location.
458
459   Gradient Guided Adapters (GGA) Given a desired number N of adapters, the algorithm be-
460   gins by calculating the scores for each possible location, creating the score matrix s(i, j). Af-
461   ter selecting the top position (k, l), the score matrix is updated by discounting the scores of all
462   entries proportionally to their distance from the chosen position, using s(i, j) dk,l (i, j), with
463   dk,l (i, j) = 1 − γ d1 ((i,j),(l,k)) , where γ ∈ (0, 1), d1 (·, ·) is the L1 distance, and denotes the
464   element-wise multiplication. The role of the discounting is to discourage the algorithm from placing
465   further capacity within the close proximity of previously selected adapters. See Appendix H for
466
      details on the Algorithm.
467   We compare the placements found by GGA with discounting (γ = 0.6) and without discounting
468   (γ = 0, resulting in choosing the top-k best scoring placements) to the random selection from
469   Section 5.2 and two other baselines: the last-k selection, and the first-k selection. The last-k baseline,
470   introduced by Rücklé et al. (2020), places parallel adapters only in the final k layers of the model.
471   Conversely, the first-k selection applies parallel adapters only to the k layers closest to the input.
472
      We evaluate all methods on the iNaturalist18 and Places365 datasets across different adapter counts.
      Results are presented in Figure 6a and Figure 6b, respectively.
473
474
475   GGA is Effective with Few Adapters We observe that GGA is especially effective when the
476   number of added adapters is low. In particular, we can match the performance of 24 parallel adapters
477
      (PA) already with 3 adapters for iNaturalist18 and 12 for Places365. As the number of adapters
      increases, the effect of GGA diminishes, ultimately matching the performance of random selection for
478
      24 placements. Moreover, we note that GGA with discounting surpasses the purely greedy algorithm
479
      (top-k) up to the point of approximately N = 12 adapters, after which both approaches perform
480   similarly to random selection. In addition, we also find that the first-k baseline is a better choice than
481   last-k when placing only a few adapters in the model, but this difference disappears when the number
482   of adapters increases.

517   7    C ONCLUSIONS
518
519   This study examined the effect of adapter placement on transfer performance. We expanded the
520   search space beyond the conventional parallel and sequential configurations by introducing long-range
521   and recurrent adapters, allowing information to be updated along paths between any two blocks
522
      in a model. We tested this approach on tasks of varying modalities and difficulty, demonstrating
      that even random sampling from the extended search space yields adapter placements that surpass
523
      baseline methods. Furthermore, we found that the rank of a linear adapter’s gradient is strongly
524
      correlated with its performance on most transfer tasks. This insight led to the development of a simple
525   greedy algorithm for optimal adapter placement, which outperforms baselines in parameter-efficient
526   fine-tuning. Our results underscore the importance of understanding how the placement of additional
527   computational capacity influences fine-tuning performance and open new avenues for research into
528   efficient identification of such placements.
529
530   Limitations and Future Work This study focused on adapter placement in transfer learning.
531   Future research could investigate whether our findings extend to other PETL strategies, such as LoRA,
532   Prefix-Tuning, and Side-Tuning. Recurrent adapters emerged as the most effective single-adapter
533   type, suggesting further exploration of their potential and ways to adjust their computational cost.
534   Additionally, we observed a strong correlation between adapter gradient rank and performance,
535
      though there were some exceptions. Studying the factors that influence this relationship could
      enhance our understanding of training dynamics in fine-tuned models and lead to more reliable
536
      performance metrics. Lastly, future work could aim to develop iterative placement algorithms, where
537
      gradients, ranks, and scores are recalculated after a set number of steps or each time a new adapter is
538   added to the network.

897   the later stages of the model leads to better test accuracy. In contrast, the same blocks in the dSpr-Loc
898   task per-from better with an MHA adapter. That being said, neither strategy emerges as universally
899   predominately better over the other. In consequence, selecting the best block is more impactful than

      choosing between locations within that block.
