                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have achieved impressive performance
012            across various vision-language tasks. However, hallucinations, i.e., generating
013            counterfactual responses, remain a significant challenge. Although recent models
014            have mitigated hallucinations in tasks such as object existence and image descrip-
015            tion, they primarily address hallucinations in response generation while overlook-
016            ing the task question itself. This paper highlights the vulnerability of LVLMs in
017            solving fictitious presupposition questions (FPQs), where the models are prone
018
               to accept the presuppositions of non-existent objects and produce severe halluci-
               natory responses. To this end, we first introduce a novel benchmark, VFP-Bench,
019
               to evaluate LVLMs’ capability to discriminate fictitious presuppositions and gen-
020
               erate factual responses. Moreover, we introduce Antidote, a universal, synthetic
021            data-driven self-correction solution for alleviating hallucination in FPQs and con-
022            ventional tasks. It leverages synthetic data to incorporate factual priors into ques-
023            tions/queries to achieve self-correction, decoupling hallucination alleviation into
024            a preference optimization problem. Applied to the LLaVA series, it enhances per-
025            formance on VFP-Bench by over 50%, POPE by 1.8–3.3%, and CHAIR & SHR
026            by 30–50%, without relying on external supervision from stronger LVLMs or hu-
027            man feedback and introducing noticeable catastrophic forgetting issues.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have achieved significant advancements, manifesting re-
032   markable performance across various tasks, including image caption, visual question answering, and
033   visual dialogues (Liu et al., 2024d; Chen et al., 2024b; Yao et al., 2024). Despite their impressive
034   capabilities and versatility, the hallucination of LVLMs, characterized by the model generating
035   counterfactual information, remains a significant challenge. This issue undermines their reliability
036   and limits their application in sensitive domains like healthcare and autonomous systems. Recently,
037   many studies have focused on the hallucinations related to “object existence” and “image descrip-
038   tion”, commonly referred to as “object hallucinations” (Zhao et al., 2023; Leng et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
039
      2024). “Object existence” involves determining whether an object is present in an image, while “im-
      age descriptions” further evaluate whether the model exhibits hallucinations regarding attributes or
040
      relationships. To address these issues, common practices in recent models are to introduce cleaner
041
      and abundant negative data samples in the instruction tuning stage (Liu et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2024;
042   Liu et al., 2024b). They have manifested effectiveness in alleviating object hallucinations on popular
043   benchmarks, such as POPE (Li et al., 2023) and CHAIR (Rohrbach et al., 2018).
044
045
      As illustrated in Figure 1a, for a straightforward POPE-type question about the existence of a “car”,
      the recently advanced model (Chen et al., 2024b) can easily confirm its absence. However, a surpris-
046
      ing phenomenon is that: when we implicitly presuppose its existence and pose a relevant question
047
      “What is the brand of the car?”, the model suddenly outputs hallucinatory responses. This issue
048   often occurs when asking about an object that is absent in the current image but frequently ap-
049   pears in similar scenes. We call this type of question “Fictitious Presupposition Question (FPQ)”.
050   Compared to the typical hallucination evaluations focusing on response generation, FPQ further
051   requires the model’s judgment of presuppositions grounded by images. Obviously, it is more
052   challenging and can better evaluate the severity of LVLM hallucinations in practical question-answer
053   (QA) applications, especially in scenarios where the validity of the question’s presupposition cannot
      be guaranteed. To address the FPQ challenge, we make attempts towards two aspects:

071   On the one hand, we introduce VFP-Bench (Visual Fictitious Presupposition Benchmark), the
072   first high-quality benchmark designed to evaluate LVLMs’ ability to discern fictitious presupposi-
073   tions and generate factual responses (Figure 2a). The results in Figure 1b demonstrate that recent
074   open-source LVLMs have achieved advancements in general capabilities (MMBench) and halluci-
075
      nation mitigation (POPE). Some models perform on par with, or even surpass, their closed-source
      counterparts, such as GPT-4o and Claude-3.5. However, when faced with fictitious presupposition
076
      questions, they frequently fail to discriminate the implicit presupposition’s correctness, leading
077
      them to follow incorrect presuppositions and generate substantial hallucinations.
078
079   On the other hand, we develop a universal, synthetic data-driven self-correction method called
080   “Antidote”, aiming at alleviating both FPQ and conventional object hallucinations. We argue that
081
      a primary cause of the above hallucinations is the object co-occurrence and corresponding QA bias
      during training. Hence, we aim to obtain images where the statistically co-occurring objects are
082
      decoupled (e.g., a car without wheels) and construct QA pairs targeted on the decoupled, non-
083
      existent objects (e.g., wheel), as presented in Figure 2b. We develop an automated data synthesis
084   pipeline, comprising steps of image caption curation, visual scene understanding, factual verifica-
085   tion, and sample construction. It allows us to derive factual priors for each sample, and then
086   incorporate them into the prompt for self-correction. This process reformulates hallucination
087   mitigation as a preference optimization problem, where the original response is treated as a “re-
088   jected” sample, and the corrected response as a “preferred” sample. By employing Antidote, LVLMs
089   learn a preference constraint during training, enabling them to discriminate fictitious presuppositions
090   and generate factual content well. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
091   · A novel hallucination benchmark towards fictitious presupposition questions: VFP-Bench.
092   We introduce VFP-Bench, a benchmark that challenges LVLMs with questions that presuppose the
093   existence of objects not present in the image. It highlights the critical gap in current models’ ability
094   to discern implicit presuppositions, providing new insight into hallucinations of LVLMs.
095
      · A versatile hallucination mitigating post-training method: Antidote. We propose Antidote, a
096   synthetic data-driven self-correction method that injects factual knowledge into the model’s queries,
097   enabling the model to learn a preference constraint of LVLMs. It is not only applicable to the
098   proposed VFP-Bench but also can be adapted to conventional object hallucination tasks.
099
      · Effectiveness of Antidote. Our experiments demonstrate Antidote can significantly mitigate hal-
100
      lucinations in LLaVA series when confronted with FPQs, object existence recognition, and image
101
      description (such as POPE, CHAIR, and SHR). Importantly, evaluation on general LVLM bench-
102   marks further demonstrates that Antidote does not introduce noticeable catastrophic forgetting.

      Hallucination Mitigation. Previous works addressing hallucinations of LVLMs primarily focus on
      object existence and image descriptions, i.e., object hallucination. Three mainstream approaches
141
      have emerged for mitigating these hallucinations: supervised fine-tuning (SFT), post-calibration,
142
      and post-training. SFT aims to fine-tune with the hallucination-free data (Yu et al., 2024), such as
143   LRV (Liu et al., 2023a) and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023). Post-calibration conducts additional
144   post-processing techniques to model outputs, such as contrastive decoding strategies (Leng et al.,
145   2024; Wang et al., 2024b) and leveraging existing tools or expert models (Yin et al., 2023). Post-
146   training focuses on improving the hallucination of off-the-shelf LVLMs, which commonly employ
147   retraining or preference optimization to alleviate hallucination (Zhao et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024;
148   Zhu et al., 2024). Our proposed method, Antidote, follows a preference optimization paradigm but
149   differs in that it does not rely on any expert models (e.g., GPT-4V) (Zhao et al., 2023) to gen-
150   erate preference samples or exclusively utilize dis-preferred data (Zhu et al., 2024). Instead, we
151
      fully leverage the advantages of our synthetic data pipeline, seamlessly utilizing factual information
      without additional cost to enable the model to self-correct its responses.

      As illustrated in Figure 2a, two key issues can be observed: (1) LVLMs tend to blindly follow the
204   instruction in the task query (Image #1 and Image #2). When asking “what does the label on the
205   beer show?” for Image #1, the model ignores the existence of the subject in the question (i.e., the
206   beer) and directly follows the instruction to identify the text on the label. (2) LVLMs overfit to
207   similar scene-based QA patterns (Image #3 and Image #4). When asking “what is the player’s
208   number?” in a scenario where the player has no number on the uniform, the model generates a
209   hallucinated answer that is usually in the VQA tasks with similar scenes.
210   To this end, we obtain the images where statistically co-occurring objects are decoupled (e.g.,
211   a car without wheels) and construct queries targeting these decoupled, non-existent objects
212   (e.g., wheel), aiming at calibrating the bias of LVLMs. Thanks to the advancements in image
213   generation models (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Esser et al., 2024) and LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Yang
214   et al., 2024), we can synthesize the images and corresponding questions in a controlled manner
215   (Figure 3). Then, with the factual prior during the above process, we incorporate them into the
      models’ prompt for self-correction. Finally, the self-correction process reformulates hallucination

242
243
      Step 1: Construction of Caption Pool. The caption pool is critical for enhancing the diversity and
      richness of the training set for Antidote. Captions can either be sourced either from web-crawled
244
      datasets or generated by LLMs. In this paper, we collect the captions from CC3M (Sharma et al.,
245
      2018) to build our pool. Since CC3M contains many noisy or unsuitable captions for image and
246   question generation, DeepSeek-V2 (Liu et al., 2024a) is adopted to perform a re-captioning and
247   filtering process. To further enhance the pool’s diversity, we employ a fuzzy deduplication strategy
248   through MinHash and LSH algorithms (Jafari et al., 2021), motivated by Yang et al. (2024).
249
      Step 2: Visual Scene Understanding. First, we prompt DeepSeek-V2 with instructions, such
250
      as “remove abstract concepts and specific terms” and “limit to less than 15 words”, to rewrite cap-
251
      tions Cimg for image generation. Second, we use DeepSeek-V2’s comprehension and reason-
252   ing capability to identify the objects Opre within the scenes described by captions. Third, we use
253   DeepSeek-V2’s world knowledge to generate objects Ohallu that typically occur in similar scenes.
254   These objects serve as hallucination candidates that will not be present in the generated images. The
255   prompt template P2 in this step is detailed in Appendix A.5. Inspired by self-reflection strategies (Ji
256   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), each triplet <Cimg , Opre , Ohallu > is fed back into DeepSeek-V2 to
257   verify whether it conforms to the rules in P2, such as “the number of generated objects”, “generating
258   objects with visible entities”, and “avoiding conflicts in Opre and Ohallu ”.
259   Step 3: Data Synthesis. For image generation process, Cimg serves as the prompt and Ohallu as
260   the negative prompt. Benefiting recent image generation models (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Esser et al.,
261   2024), their generated images exhibit a high degree of photorealism and diverse content. Here, we
262   adopt Stable-Diffusion-3 (Esser et al., 2024) as the generator. However, it cannot ensure
263   that the generated content fully aligns with Opre while suppressing the existence of Ohallu . Thus,
264   we introduce “Factual Assessor” driven by a open-set grounding model, Grounding-DINO (Liu
265   et al., 2023b). It checks the presence of Opre and Ohallu in the generated images. If an object in
266
      Opre is not detected, it will be removed. Similarly, detected objects in Ohallu will also be removed.
      If either Opre or Ohallu is ∅, the corresponding triplet will be discarded. Finally, the remaining
267
      triplets are sent to DeepSeek-V2 to generate task queries related to Ohallu .
268
269   In early experiments, we observe that the LLM tends to generate similar presupposition questions
      when facing the same objects (e.g., frequently asking about the “color” or “brand” when select-

270
      ing “car” in Ohallu ), limiting the diversity of questions in the generated dataset. To address this,
271   we update a key-value memory bank to save used captions and corresponding FPQs. The captions
272   are extracted to sentence embedding using BGE-m3 (Chen et al., 2024a) as the key of the memory
273   bank. For each selected <Cimg , Opre , Ohallu >, we retrieve questions whose captions are semanti-
274   cally close to Cimg through the memory bank. These are then integrated as part of the prompt (e.g.,
275   “Do not generate questions similar to the following: ...”) to mitigate redundancy in generation. The
276   prompt template P3 for generating TPQs / FPQs is detailed in Appendix A.5.

      4.3   S ELF -C ORRECTION VIA P REFERENCE A LIGNMENT
279
280   Antidote is a universal method for alleviating hallucination in FPQs, object existence, and image
281   description. Through the data synthetic pipeline, we construct the three tasks for post-training:
282
283
        1) Presupposition Questions: We prompt DeepSeek-V2 to generate FPQs based on Ohallu
        using P3. In early experiments, we observed that only post-training with FPQs will make the
284
        baseline model overly “cautious” in responding to questions. Thus, we also build a TPQ set based
285
        on Opre . For the Antidote for presupposition questions, we prompt the baseline model with “Given
286     the fact that there is {factual information}, please answer: {TPQ/FPQ}” to self-correct
287     the original answer. For these TPQs and FPQs, their self-corrected answer will be used as the
288     negative response yneg , while the original answer will be used as preferred response ypos .
289
        2) Object Existence: We randomly choose objects in Opre and Ohallu to build the training set
290
        of the object existence. The task prompts generated by DeepSeek-V2, such as “Is / Are there
291
        {object candidate} in the image?” and “Can you see {object candidate} in the im-
292     age?”. For the Antidote for object existence, we prompt the LVLM with “Given the fact that there
293     is {object candidate}, please answer: {prompt}” to self-correct its response.
294
295
        3) Image Description: We generate task queries of image description by DeepSeek-V2, such
        as “Please describe the image in detail.” and “Can you describe what you see in the image thor-
296
        oughly?”. For the Antidote of image description, we integrate <Cimg , Opre , Ohallu > into the
297
        query to self-correct its response: “Given the hint of the image: the image caption: {Cimg }, the
298     object(s) you can see: {Opre }, the object(s) you cannot see: {Ohallu }, please {query}”.
299
300   Response Filtering: Since not all model responses contain hallucinations, especially in object ex-
301
      istence queries, such samples are unhelpful for hallucination mitigation. Therefore, we check both
      the original and self-corrected responses and filter out samples with similar answers. In our ex-
302
      periments, we extract the embeddings of both responses using BGE-m3 (Chen et al., 2024a) and
303
      calculate their cosine similarity to perform the filtering.
304
305   Preference Optimization: Through direct preference alignment (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), we
306   encourage the model to favor corrected positive response and reject hallucinatory negative response
307

      preference pairs D, the policy model πθ (i.e., the post-trained LVLMs with Antidote) is optimized
308
      by maximizing the log-likelihood of the preferred response ypos while minimizing the likelihood of
309
      the hallucinated response yneg . Our training objective function is given by:

      5.2    M AIN R ESULTS
396
397   Does Antidote improve LVLMs’ ability to discriminate the correctness of presupposition? In
398   Table 1, we compare the performance of closed-sourced models, open-sourced models, and baseline
399   models post-trained with Antidote on VFP-Bench. The results reveal that current closed-source
400   LVLMs substantially outperform open-source models in distinguishing FPQ and outputting factual
401   responses. The optimal performance is achieved by Claude-3.5-Sonnet and GPT-4o, which resists
402   nearly 90% of FPQ hallucination induction. Among open-source models, LLaVA-Next-Vicuna-
403   13B stands out, achieving an F1-score of 56.0% and a recall of 41.6%. Notably, Antidote brings
404   substantial improvements to LVLMs. For LLaVA-1.5 7B and 13B models, Antidote post-training
405
      boosts their F1-scores from 5.7 and 17.3 to 78.4 and 83.5, respectively. For LLaVA-Next-Mistral-
      7B, we improved the original F1-score from 43.6% to 84.4%, with recall increasing from 16.5%
406
      to 74.3%. As illustrated in Figure 5, we can observe that these models after Antidote can produce
407
      factual responses such as “I cannot answer {...} as there is no {...} in the image”. These results
408   highlight Antidote’s efficacy in enhancing model accuracy in recognizing hallucinations, pushing
409   open-source models closer to closed-source counterparts on this challenging task.
410
411
      Does model size affect the ability to discriminate the correctness of presuppositions? (1) With
      identical architectures and training data, larger model sizes enhance the judgment of presupposition
412
      correctness. For instance, as the Qwen2-VL scales from 7B to 72B parameters, the recall increases
413
      from 13.2% to 33.3%, with a similar trend observed in the InternVL2 series. (2) Across different
414   models, however, model size is not a decisive factor. Notably, MiniCPM-V2.5, with only 8B parame-
415   ters, achieves a recall that is 8.5% higher than Qwen2-VL-72B, demonstrating superior performance
416   in recognizing FPQs. Moreover, InternVL2 and Qwen2-VL, which surpass closed-source models in
417   general performance, do not perform well on the VPF-Bench. Both models have utilized large-scale
418   instruction fine-tuning datasets to enhance visual capabilities. We believe that their performance on
419   the VPF-Bench is strongly correlated with over-learning of instruction tuning.
420   How do LVLMs with Antidote perform on popular hallucination benchmarks? Here, we com-
421   pare various types of mitigation approaches, including contrastive decoding (e.g., VCD (Liu et al.,
422   2024d) and VDD (Zhang et al., 2024)), auxiliary learning (e.g., HACL (Jiang et al., 2024)), and
423   post-training (e.g., Volcano (Lee et al., 2023) and SeVA (Zhu et al., 2024)).
424
        1) For object existence, we assess POPE, where the results (Table 2) are averaged across three
425
        evaluation sets: the random, popular, and adversarial sets (the results for each set can be found
426     in Appendix A.4). On LLaVA 1.5-7B, we improved its original F1-score from 86.07 to 87.89
427     (+1.82%), with an even greater improvement on its 13B version, from 85.67 to 88.99 (+3.32%).
428     Notably, we observed significant improvements on the adversarial subset, where objects are first
429     ranked based on co-occurrence frequencies, and the top-k frequent objects are sampled. On the
430     original 7B and 13B versions, Antidote improves by 2.58% and 4.12%, respectively. This
431     demonstrates that Antidote can effectively mitigate the statistical biases inherent in LVLMs, which
        substantially contribute to object hallucination issues (Li et al., 2023).

      5.3     A NALYSIS
467
468   Catastrophic forgetting. Since Antidote is a post-training method that fine-tunes the baseline mod-
469   els’ parameters, we evaluated whether Antidote causes catastrophic forgetting by assessing the gen-
470   eral capability of the post-trained LLaVA-1.5 series. From Table 4, it is evident that performance on
471   these benchmarks did not significantly degrade and even improved on some benchmarks, such as a
472   2.8% and 2.6% increase on Science-QA. This suggests that suppressing object hallucinations and
473   enhancing FPQ discrimination can generalize to improvements in overall capabilities. There
474   was a slight decrease in performance on LLaVA-Wild, where we observed that the post-trained
475   version was “cautious” when answering uncertain/challenging questions compared to the baseline
476
      model, which is not preferred by its GPT-4 evaluator.
477   Attention visualization. We further empirically investigate how attention from visual tokens con-
478   tributes to important object-related text tokens before and after applying the proposed Antidote. In
479   Figure 6, we visualize some representative instances during training. For example, when asked the
480   FPQ, that is, “What is the fork made of in the image?”, we observe that the original LLaVA-1.5,
481   while outputting “fork”, does not significantly focus on visual tokens, and incorrectly attends to
482
      visual token information when outputting “metal”. However, after training with Antidote, the
      model’s attention to visual tokens becomes more accurate, focusing on the exact areas of the image
483
      corresponding to object-related text tokens, such as “shrimp” and “vegetables”.
484
485   Compare with SFT. A straightforward alternative to Antidote’s preference optimization is contin-
      ual supervised fine-tuning (SFT) using the self-corrected responses constructed in Section 4.3. As

529   6       C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper discusses the issue of hallucinations in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), par-
532
      ticularly in the context of Fictitious Presupposition Questions (FPQs), where models often fail to
533
      discern the correctness of implied presupposition and output hallucinatory responses. Our contri-
534   butions include the introduction of VFP-Bench, a novel benchmark designed to challenge LVLMs
535   with FPQs, along with Antidote, a synthetic data-driven self-correction method that significantly
536   reduces hallucinations. Our experimental results demonstrate that Antidote effectively enhances the
537   accuracy of LVLMs in discriminating fictitious presuppositions and improves performance across
538   multiple hallucination-related benchmarks, such as POPE, CHAIR, and SHR, without causing ob-
539   vious catastrophic forgetting issues. These extensive experiments demonstrate that Antidote is a
      promising method for improving LVLM reliability across various vision-language tasks.

      A.1   D ETAILS OF VFP-B ENCH
705
706   To evaluate model performance on both fictitious presupposition questions (FPQs) and true presup-
707   position questions (TPQs), VFP-Bench presents a carefully curated set of 1,000 samples. These
708   samples are evenly split between 500 FPQs and 500 TPQs. A key element of this benchmark lies
709   in the diversity and structure of the questions, which were designed to explore various dimensions
710   of the model’s understanding of presuppositions within everyday contexts. We categories them into
711   four types: item, knowledge, scene, and activity. As shown in Figure 7, the most frequent question
712   types focus on identifying object attributes, with “what color” questions forming the largest portion
713
      (26.8% of the benchmark). Other common patterns include “what is” (14.4%) and “what material”
      (11.9%). This question composition aligns with the benchmark’s goal to challenge VLM in rec-
714
      ognizing the correctness of question presuppositions instead of blindly responding to fine-grained
715
      attributes asked in FPQs.
716
717   The detailed question categorization reinforces the benchmark’s complexity in two ways. First, the
718   prevalence of specific object-related questions (e.g., colors and materials) introduces a layer of dif-
719
      ficulty in distinguishing between objects that share similar contextual environments. Second, the
      use of fictitious objects in FPQs (e.g., asking about a ”railroad” in a train-related scene) pushes
720
      the boundaries of model reasoning, requiring not just object recognition but a deeper understand-
721
      ing of plausible relationships in the visual context. By incorporating diverse question types and
722   presupposition structures, VFP-Bench ensures comprehensive coverage across multiple dimensions
723   of LVLMs’ language and vision capabilities. This diverse set of queries challenges models to go
724   beyond surface-level statistical biases and engage with more nuanced aspects of visual and semantic
725   understanding.

      As discussed in Section 4.3, the preference optimization we introduce for Antidote can be likened
      to contrastive learning. Specifically, the way Antidote encourages the model to prefer self-corrected
836
      responses over hallucinatory ones shares a similar paradigm with the contrastive learning approach.
837
      In contrastive learning, as shown in Eq. 3, we optimize the InfoNCE loss:

842   where q is the query embedding, k + represents the positive embedding while k − represents negative
843   embeddings. It trains the model to distinguish between positive and negative samples by increasing
844   the similarity of q and k + while reducing the similarity between q and k − . If we simplify the
845   equation by considering only one negative sample, the InfoNCE loss can be reformulated as:
846
847                                                      exp(f (q, k + ))

850   corrected responses act as positive samples (k + ), while hallucinatory responses are treated as neg-

852   relative to the hallucinatory ones, similar to how contrastive learning seeks to maximize the similar-
