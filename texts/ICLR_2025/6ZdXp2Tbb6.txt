                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep learning models perform poorly when domain shifts exist between training
010
               and test data. Test-time adaptation (TTA) is a paradigm to mitigate this issue by
011            adapting pre-trained models using only unlabeled test samples. However, exist-
012            ing TTA methods can fail under severe domain shifts, while recent active TTA
013            approaches requiring full-class labels are impractical due to high labeling costs.
014            To address this issue, we introduce a Binary-feedback Active Test-Time Adap-
015            tation (BATTA) setting, which uses a few binary feedbacks from annotators to
016            indicate whether model predictions are correct, thereby significantly reducing the
017            labeling burden of annotators. Under the setting, we propose BATTA-RL, a novel
018            dual-path optimization framework that leverages reinforcement learning to balance
019
               binary feedback-guided adaptation on uncertain samples with agreement-based
               self-adaptation on confident predictions. Experiments show BATTA-RL achieves
020
               substantial accuracy improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating
021
               its effectiveness in handling severe distribution shifts with minimal labeling effort.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep learning has revolutionized various fields, including computer vision (Deng et al., 2009), speech
027   recognition (Gulati et al., 2020), and natural language processing (Brown et al., 2020). However,
028   deep models often suffer from domain shifts, where discrepancies between training and test data
029   distributions lead to significant performance degradation. For example, autonomous driving systems
030   might struggle with new types of vehicles or unexpected weather conditions that differ from the
031   training data (Sakaridis et al., 2018).
032   Test-time adaptation (TTA) (Wang et al., 2021) is a viable solution to domain shifts by dynamically
033   adopting the pre-trained models in real-time using only unlabeled test samples. However, without
034   ground-truth labels, most TTA methods are vulnerable to adaptation failures (Gong et al., 2022; Niu
035   et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2024b). Recent studies showed that TTA failures are
036   inevitable when there is significant divergence between test and training data (Press et al., 2024),
037   especially in lifelong continual adaptation (Press et al., 2023).
038   To mitigate the issue, the paradigm of active TTA (Gui et al., 2024) was proposed, where an
039   oracle (e.g., an annotator) provides ground-truth labels for a few selected samples during adaptation.
040   However, obtaining such labels in real-world applications is often impractical due to its high cost
041   and interaction bottlenecks, particularly when the number of classes is large. For example, full-class
042   labeling by human annotators suffers a high labeling overhead (e.g., 11.7 sec per image) and a high
043   labeling error rate (e.g., 12.7%) (Joshi et al., 2010). This necessitates a lightweight labeling approach
044
      to reduce the annotators’ burden for TTA.
045   We introduce Binary-feedback Active TTA (BATTA) setting (Figure 1) where an annotator provides
046   simple binary feedback on the model’s predictions, indicating whether they are correct or incorrect.
047   This approach only requires minimal label information, thereby significantly reducing labeling costs
048   and mitigating interaction bottlenecks compared with full-label active TTA (Gui et al., 2024), making
049   our framework more attractive for real-world TTA applications.
050   As a solution, we propose BATTA-RL, a dual-path optimization for BATTA that incorporates both
051   binary feedback and unlabeled samples. Inspired by the recent reinforcement learning studies that
052   show effectiveness in incorporating human feedback in the optimization process (e.g., RLHF, Ouyang
053   et al. (2022)), BATTA-RL leverages reinforcement learning to effectively balance two complementary
      adaptation strategies: Binary Feedback-guided Adaptation (BFA) on uncertain samples and Agreement-

      2     B INARY- FEEDBACK ACTIVE TEST- TIME ADAPTATION
086
      We propose Binary-feedback Active Test-Time Adaptation (BATTA), a test-time adaptation (TTA)
087
      setting where an oracle provides a few binary feedback (correct/incorrect) on the model prediction.
088
      BATTA addresses the critical challenge of adapting pre-trained models to domain shifts with minimal
089   labeling effort. Unlike methods that require full-class labels, BATTA leverages simple binary feedback
090   to guide the adaptation process. Specifically, full-class labeling is as expensive as log(num_class)
091   times trial of binary-feedback labeling regarding the Shannon information gain (MacKay, 2003). Also,
092   the human experiment of full-class labeling on 50-class showed 11.7 seconds of response time with a
093   12.7% error rate while comparing two images (analogous to our binary feedback approach) took only
094   1.6 seconds with 0.8% error rate (Joshi et al., 2010). These results demonstrate that object comparison
095   (e.g., comparing an image with a model prediction in BATTA) requires a lower labeling overhead
096   than full-class labeling, making BATTA more efficient and practical for real-world applications.
097   Feedback mechanism. In BATTA, an oracle provides a few binary feedbacks indicating whether
098   the model’s prediction is correct. This real-time feedback is integrated into the system, enabling
099   continuous model adaptation. The binary feedback mechanism is illustrated in Figure 1, where the
100   oracle evaluates the model’s predictions, and the feedback memory is updated accordingly.
101   Notation. Let x denote a test sample selected for active labeling at time t, and y ∗ =
102   arg maxy fθ (y|x) be the model’s prediction with parameters θ. The binary feedback B(x, y) is
103   given by:                                (

125   3     BATTA-RL: D UAL - PATH OPTIMIZATION FRAMEWORK
126   Motivation. Recent advancements in reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF, Ouyang
127   et al. (2022)) have demonstrated the effectiveness of incorporating sparse feedback signals in large
128   language model training. Inspired by this, we propose BATTA-RL, a reinforcement learning (RL)
129   based approach for binary-feedback active test-time adaptation (BATTA) that effectively adapts
130   to distribution shifts using minimal labeling effort. BATTA-RL leverages binary feedback as a
131   reinforcement signal, offering several key advantages for test-time adaptation (TTA). (1) Binary
132   feedback can be seamlessly incorporated as non-differentiable rewards in the RL framework, enabling
133   the model to learn from minimal supervision (Zoph & Le, 2017; Yoon et al., 2020). (2) The RL
134
      framework allows for integrating binary feedback and unlabeled samples into a single objective
      function optimized through policy gradient methods. By combining sparse binary-feedback samples
135
      with unlabeled data, BATTA-RL provides a robust framework with minimal labeling effort, making
136
      TTA more feasible for real-world applications.
137
138   Policy gradient modeling. Given a batch of test samples B = {x1 , . . . , xn }, our goal is to adapt
139   the model parameters θ to improve performance on the test distribution. We formulate the test-time
140   adaptation process as an RL problem by assigning test-time input x ∼ B as a state, the model
141
      prediction y ∗ = fθ (x) as an action, and the corresponding prediction probability πθ (y|x) as a policy,
      which objective is maximizing the expected reward, defined as:
142
143
                                                  J(θ) = Ex∼B,y∼πθ (y|x) [R(x, y)],                                                                      (1)
144
145
      where R(x, y) represents the reward function defined later. This optimization is performed for each
146
      test batch, allowing continuous adaptation to the evolving test distribution.
147
148   As binary feedback is a non-differentiable function, we employ the R EINFORCE algorithm (Williams,
149   1992), also known as the “log-derivative trick”. This method allows us to estimate the gradient of the
150
      expected reward with respect to the model parameters:
151
                                    ∇θ J(θ) = Ex∼B,y∼πθ (y|x) [R(x, y)∇θ log πθ (y|x)].                                                                  (2)
152
153
      By using this gradient estimator, we can effectively optimize our model parameters using stochastic
154
      gradient ascent.
155
156   To estimate the policy πθ , we leverage Monte Carlo (MC) dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016).
157   MC-dropout approximates Bayesian inference by applying dropout at test time and performing
158
      multiple forward passes. This approach allows us to estimate the model’s prediction probability
      without modifying the model architecture. Specifically, we approximate the policy πθ (y|x) as:

164
165   With the proposed RL framework, BATTA-RL addresses the challenge of utilizing (1) few samples
166
      with ground-truth binary feedback and (2) many unlabeled samples with potentially noisy predictions
      through two complementary strategies:
167
168
            1. Binary Feedback-guided Adaptation on uncertain samples (BFA, Section 3.1): This strategy
169
               focuses on enhancing the model’s areas of uncertainty. By selecting samples where the
170            model is least confident and obtaining binary feedback on these, BATTA-RL efficiently
171            probes the boundaries of the model’s current knowledge.
172
            2. Agreement-Based self-Adaptation on confident samples (ABA, Section 3.2): To complement
173
               the guided adaptation strategy, BATTA-RL also leverages the model’s existing knowledge
174
               through self-adaptation on confidently predicted samples. Without requiring additional
175            feedback, ABA identifies confident samples by the agreement between the model’s standard
176            predictions and those obtained via MC-dropout.
177
178   The synergy between Binary Feedback-guided Adaptation (BFA) and Agreement-Based self-
179   Adaptation (ABA) enables BATTA-RL to effectively utilize both labeled and unlabeled samples. BFA
180   drives exploration and adaptation to new patterns in the test distribution through binary feedback
181   on uncertain samples. Concurrently, ABA maintains and refines existing knowledge through self-
182   supervised adaptation on confident predictions. This dual-path optimization allows BATTA-RL to
183
      adapt effectively across diverse challenging conditions.

185   3.1   B INARY FEEDBACK - GUIDED ADAPTATION ON UNCERTAIN SAMPLES
186
      In BATTA settings where binary feedback is limited and costly, selecting samples for querying
187
      becomes crucial for effective model adaptation. To address this challenge, we propose Binary
188
      Feedback-guided Adaptation on uncertain samples (BFA). This approach refines the model’s decision
189   boundaries and improves its understanding of challenging data points through binary feedback
190   guidance, enabling robust and efficient adaptation in test-time distribution shifts.
191
192   Sample selection. To guide the adaptation, we focus on the most uncertain samples, often the most
193
      informative for model improvement (Settles, 2009). We quantify the samplewise (un)certainty using
      MC-dropout, which we previously employed for policy estimation (Equation 3). MC-dropout offers a
194
      robust uncertainty estimate, while the standard softmax probabilities often exhibit overconfidence on
195
      out-of-distribution samples; a phenomenon observed in recent test-time adaptation studies (Gong
196   et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2024b). Therefore, we define the sample-wise certainty C(x) as:
197
198                                            C(x) = πθ (y ∗ |x),                                      (4)
199
200   where y ∗ = arg maxy fθ (y|x) is the current model prediction and πθ (y|x) is MC-dropout softmax
201   confidence.
202   To implement binary feedback-guided adaptation, we select the set of k samples with the lowest
203   certainty (i.e., highest uncertainty), noted as SBFA :
204
205                                      SBFA = argsortx (C(x))[: k].                                   (5)
206
207   Reward function design. For these selected samples, we query the binary feedback B(x, y)
208   (correct/incorrect) and define the reward function RBFA for active samples as:
209                                                (

211                                                  −1 if the prediction is incorrect.

213   This binary-feedback reward scheme provides a clear signal for model adaptation, encouraging the
214   prediction probability of correct predictions and penalizing incorrect ones. By selectively applying

       3.2                   AGREEMENT- BASED SELF - ADAPTATION ON CONFIDENT SAMPLES
232
233    To complement the binary feedback-guided adaptation on uncertain samples, we propose leveraging
234    the model’s confident predictions on the remaining many unlabeled samples. This approach, which
235    we call Agreement-Based self-Adaptation (ABA), aims to reinforce the model’s current knowledge
236    without requiring additional oracle feedback.
237
       Sample selection. The key idea behind ABA is to identify samples where the model’s standard
238    prediction agrees with its MC dropout prediction. We consider these samples “confident” and use
239    them for self-adaptation. Formally, we define the set of confident samples SABA as:
240
241                                       SABA = {x ∈ B \ SBFA | arg max fθ (y|x) = arg max πθ (y|x)},                                                       (7)

       fθ (y|x) is the standard model prediction, and πθ (y|x) is the MC-dropout prediction.
245
246    Unlike existing test-time adaptation (TTA) methods that rely on fixed confidence thresholds (Niu
247    et al., 2022; 2023; Gong et al., 2023b), our approach can dynamically select confident samples based
248    on the agreement between standard and MC-dropout predictions. Figure 4a illustrates the dynamic
249    nature of prediction confidences during distribution shifts—necessitating the need for dynamic
250
       sample selection. To demonstrate the effectiveness of ABA further, we compare our agreement-based
       approach with various thresholding strategies in Figure 8 in Appendix B. The results support the
251
       superiority of our dynamic selection method over confidence thresholding.
252
253    Furthermore, our method effectively identifies confident samples for self-adaptation. Figure 4b
254    demonstrates the stable accuracies in samples with agreement, while samples with disagreement show
255    unstable and low accuracies. This originates from the prediction agreement of indicating robustness
256
       and reliability via the consistency in model outputs across different dropout masks. By leveraging
       this consistency, ABA can reliably select confident samples for effective self-adaptation.

      3.3    BATTA-RL A LGORITHM
274
275   Our proposed BATTA-RL algorithm integrates binary feedback-guided adaptation (BFA, Section 3.1)
276   and agreement-based self-adaptation (ABA, Section 3.2) into a unified dual-path optimization frame-
277   work, enabling effective adaptation to distribution shifts while maintaining model stability.
278
      To achieve this, we formulate a combined objective function that balances the rewards from both
279   uncertain samples (guided by binary feedback) and confident samples (identified through prediction
280   agreement). Formally, we define our total objective function Jtotal () as:
281
282                       Jtotal (θ) = αEx∈SBFA [RBFA (x, y)] + βEx∈B\SBFA [RABA (x, y)],                (9)
283
284   where α, β are hyperparameters to control the relative contributions of BFA and ABA.
285   Following the R EINFORCE algorithm, the gradient of our total objective is given by:
286
287
          ∇θ Jtotal (θ) = αEx∈SBFA [RBFA (x, y)∇θ log πθ (y|x)] + βEx∈B\SBFA [RABA (x, y)∇θ log πθ (y|x)].

291   model’s performance on the evolving test distribution. We utilize a single value of α = 2 and β = 1

294   4     E XPERIMENTS
295
296   We present our experimental setup and the results across various scenarios in BATTA setting. We
297   evaluate the performance of BATTA-RL against state-of-the-art baselines, ensuring fairness by
298   providing an equal amount of binary-feedback active samples. Additional experiments, additional
299   results, and experiment details are provided in Appendices B, C, and D.
300   Baselines. We evaluated BATTA-RL against a comprehensive set of baselines, including source vali-
301   dation (SrcValid) and seven state-of-the-art TTA methods: BN-Stats (Nado et al., 2020), TENT (Wang
302   et al., 2021), EATA (Niu et al., 2022), SAR (Niu et al., 2023), CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022),
303   RoTTA (Yuan et al., 2023), and SoTTA (Gong et al., 2023b). To ensure a fair comparison, we
304   incorporate an equal number of random binary-feedback data into TTA baselines by adding correct-
305   sample loss (cross-entropy) and incorrect-sample loss (complementary label loss from Kim et al.
306   (2019)). Additionally, we included SimATTA (Gui et al., 2024) as an active TTA baseline, adapting
307   it to use binary-feedback data by incorporating a complementary loss for negative samples. The
308
      non-active TTA and active TTA method accuracies are reported in Appendix C for comparison.
309   Dataset. To evaluate the robustness of BATTA-RL across various domain shifts, we utilized
310   standard image corruption datasets CIFAR10-C, CIFAR100-C, and Tiny-ImageNet-C (Hendrycks
311   & Dietterich, 2019). Additionally, we conducted experiments on the PACS dataset (Li et al., 2017),
312   which is commonly used for domain adaptation tasks. For most experiments, we pre-trained the
313   source model on the source domain while adapting and evaluating the model on the test-time domains.
314
      To more closely simulate real-world scenarios with evolving distribution shifts, we implemented a
      continual TTA setting (Wang et al., 2022) where corruption continuously changes.
315
316   Settings and hyperparameters. We configured BATTA-RL to operate with minimal labeling
317   effort, using only 3 binary feedbacks within each 64-sample test batch, accounting for less than
318   5%. We utilize a single value of balancing hyperparameters α = 2 and β = 1 for BATTA-RL in all
319   experiments. A comprehensive list of settings and hyperparameters is provided in Appendix D.

473   5       R ELATED WORK
474
475   Test-time adaptation. Test-time adaptation (TTA) improves model accuracy on distribution shift
476   on the pre-trained model with only unlabeled test samples (Wang et al., 2021). Existing TTA focused
477
      on robust adaptation (Niu et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Boudiaf
      et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2023b; Park et al., 2024) across various types of distribution
478
      shifts (Niu et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2023b; Press et al., 2023).
479
      However, existing TTA methods suffer from adaptation failures during lifelong adaptation (Press et al.,
480   2023), stressing the need for a few-sample guide for robust adaptation. Active test-time adaptation
481   (ATTA) (Gui et al., 2024) introduced a foundational analysis of active TTA setting. It proposed a
482   supervised learning scheme (SimATTA) using low-entropy source-like sample pseudo-labeling and
483   active labeling from an incremental clustering algorithm. However, SimATTA is sensitive to the
484   pre-trained model and selected active samples, as it does not leverage most unlabeled samples and
485   only utilizes a few labeled samples. In contrast, BATTA utilizes a large set of unlabeled samples
      while guiding adaptation with binary-feedback samples, performing more stable than SimATTA.


                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Active learning. Active learning (Cohn et al., 1994; Settles, 2009) involves an oracle (e.g., human
487   annotator) in the machine learning process to develop efficient annotation and training procedures.
488   Active learning framework has been widely studied in active (source-free) domain adaptation (Ash
489   et al., 2019; Prabhu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Du & Li, 2024; Kothandaraman
490   et al., 2023; Ning et al., 2021) and active TTA (Gui et al., 2024). Compared with active domain
491   adaptation, active TTA focuses on the non-regrettable active sample selection on the continuously
492   changing data stream without access to source data. Using binary feedback is related to the active
493   learning with partial feedback (ALPF) problem (Hu et al., 2019), which seeks to recursively obtain
494   partial labels until a definitive label is identified. Joshi et al. (2010) proposed a binary feedback active
495
      learning setup where users compare two images and report whether they belong to the same category.
      In contrast, our approach leverages single-step binary feedback on the model’s current batch sample
496
      output without requiring additional data. This simplifies the process and reduces the labeling effort.
497
498   Reinforcement learning for model tuning. Reinforcement learning (RL) has been successfully
499   applied in various domains to incorporate non-differentiable rewards in the optimization process (Zoph
500   & Le, 2017; Yoon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023; Black et al., 2024). For
501   example, Zoph & Le (2017) and Yoon et al. (2020) employ the R EINFORCE algorithm to use the
502
      accuracy of the validation dataset as a (non-differentiable) reward in neural architecture search or
      data valuation. In the domain of the natural language process, reinforcement learning with human
503
      feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) has gained prominence for fine-tuning large language models.
504
      Such a recipe has been extended to the domain of computer vision such as fine-tuning text-to-image
505   diffusion models using human feedback (Fan et al., 2023; Black et al., 2024). Similar approaches
506   have been explored in vision and multi-modal research (Le et al., 2022; Pinto et al., 2023). Recently,
507   Reinforcement Learning with CLIP Feedback (RLCF, Zhao et al. (2023)) has been proposed for
508   test-time adaptation of vision-language models. RLCF relies on the pre-trained CLIP model as a
509   reward function, which may not be available or suitable for all domains or tasks. In contrast, our
510   approach provides a more general and flexible approach for test-time adaptation by effectively guiding
511   the adaptation without relying on specific pre-trained models.
512
513
      6    C ONCLUSION
514
515
      We proposed binary-feedback active test-time adaptation (BATTA) to address the challenge of adapt-
516
      ing pre-trained models to new domains with minimal labeling effort. Our approach leverages binary
517   feedback on the model predictions (correct or incorrect) from an oracle to guide the adaptation
518   process, significantly reducing the labeling cost than existing methods requiring full-class labels. Our
519   method, BATTA-RL, uniquely combines binary feedback-guided adaptation on uncertain samples
520   with agreement-based self-adaptation on confident samples in a reinforcement learning framework,
521   balancing between a few labeled samples and many unlabeled samples. Through extensive experi-
522   ments on distribution shift datasets, we demonstrated that BATTA-RL outperforms state-of-the-art
523   test-time adaptation methods, showcasing its effectiveness in handling continuous distribution shifts.
524   Overall, BATTA represents a significant step forward in test-time adaptation, offering a practical
525
      balance between performance and labeling efficiency.

761   A     D ISCUSSION
762
763   A.1     L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
764
765   Despite the promising results, BATTA and BATTA-RL have limitations. First, the reliance on binary
766   feedback, while reducing labeling effort, may still require substantial oracle involvement in scenarios
767   with high data variability or rapid domain shifts. Future work will explore reducing oracle involvement
768
      by developing more advanced and dynamic sample selection strategies. Second, the computational
      overhead introduced by Monte Carlo dropout, although manageable, could be significant in resource-
769
      constrained environments. This overhead could be reduced by efficient TTA (Hong et al., 2023;
770
      Song et al., 2023) and on-device machine learning (Liberis & Lane, 2023; Rusci et al., 2020; Gong
771   et al., 2023a). Finally, our method assumes that the oracle feedback is always accurate, which might
772   not hold in some real-world scenarios. Although our algorithm showed robustness compared to the
773   baseline, designing a method for handling noisy or incorrect feedback remains an area for future
774   research.
775
776   A.2     S OCIETAL IMPACTS
777
778   A.2.1    P OSITIVE IMPACTS
779   • Reduced labeling costs. For Binary-feedback Active TTA (BATTA), the use of binary feedback
780     significantly reduces the need for extensive labeling, lowering costs and making advanced machine
781     learning techniques more accessible to smaller organizations and underfunded research projects.
782
      • Improved adaptability in real-world applications. By enabling models to adapt in real-time with
783     minimal labeling, BATTA can enhance the performance of applications like autonomous driving,
784     healthcare diagnostics, and personalized recommendations, improving safety, efficiency, and user
785     experience.
786
      • Enhanced robustness and accuracy. BATTA-RL’s robust adaptation mechanism can improve the
787
        accuracy of models in diverse and changing environments, leading to more reliable and trustworthy
788     AI systems in critical applications such as medical imaging and environmental monitoring.
789
790   A.2.2    N EGATIVE IMPACTS
791
792   • Dependence on oracle feedback. The reliance on binary feedback from oracles could still be a
793
        bottleneck in some applications, particularly if the feedback is not accurate or timely, potentially
        limiting the method’s effectiveness in highly dynamic environments.
794
795   • Potential for misuse. Like any advanced AI technology, BATTA-RL could be misused in appli-
796     cations that require constant adaptation to new data, such as surveillance or targeted advertising,
797     potentially leading to privacy concerns or biased decision-making.
798   • Computational overhead. The use of Monte Carlo dropout and other advanced techniques might
799     increase computational requirements, potentially limiting the method’s applicability in resource-
800     constrained environments or contributing to higher energy consumption. Recent advances in
801     efficient TTA (Hong et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023) and on-device learning (Liberis & Lane, 2023;
802
        Rusci et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2023a) could be integrated to reduce the computational overhead
        and enhance the applicability in resource-constrained environments.

1137   update batch norm statistics with the unlabeled test batch before active labeling and freeze the

1139   blocks, following the previous work on TTA accuracy estimation (Lee et al., 2024b), with a dropout
1140   rate of 0.3, except for 0.1 on Tiny-ImageNet-C. Additionally, we introduce a memory mechanism to

1142   and incorrect) with a memory size equal to the batch size. Then, we calculate the mean gradient from
1143
       each correct and incorrect sample memory and sum them up. This ensures the balancing between
       correct and incorrect samples in the early stage, where the number of each sample is imbalanced.
