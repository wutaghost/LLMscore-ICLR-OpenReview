009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative AI systems like foundation models (FMs) must align well with human
012            values to ensure their behavior is helpful and trustworthy. While Reinforcement
013            Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has shown promise for optimizing model
014            performance using human judgments, existing RLHF pipelines predominantly rely
015            on immediate feedback, which can fail to reflect the true downstream impact of
016
               an interaction on users’ utility. We demonstrate that this shortsighted feedback
               can, by itself, result in misaligned behaviors like sycophancy and deception, and
017
               we propose to alleviate this by refocusing RLHF on downstream consequences.
018
               Our theoretical analysis reveals that the hindsight gained by simply delaying hu-
019            man feedback mitigates misalignment and improves expected human utility. To
020            leverage this insight in a practical alignment algorithm, we introduce Reinforce-
021            ment Learning from Hindsight Simulation (RLHS), which first simulates plausible
022            consequences and then elicits feedback to assess what behaviors were genuinely
023            beneficial in hindsight. We apply RLHS to two widely-employed online and of-
024            fline preference optimization methods—Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and
025            Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)—and show empirically that misalignment
026            is significantly reduced with both methods. Through an online human user study,
027
               we show that RLHS consistently outperforms RLHF in helping users achieve their
               goals and earns higher satisfaction ratings, despite being trained solely with simu-
028
               lated hindsight feedback. These results underscore the importance of focusing on
029
               long-term consequences, even simulated ones, to mitigate misalignment in RLHF.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Aligning artificial intelligence (AI) systems with human values and intentions is crucial to ensuring
035   they behave in ways that are helpful, honest, and trustworthy. A widely-deployed method for achiev-
036   ing this alignment is through human feedback (Leike et al., 2018), with successful applications to, e.g.,
037   training AI assistants (Glaese et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Achiam et al., 2023).
038   In particular, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler
039
      et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020) leverages human feedback to fine-tune and
      align foundation models (FMs). While RLHF has shown promise in aligning models with human pref-
040
      erences, it often relies heavily on human perceptions during interactions, which may not accurately
041
      reflect the downstream consequences of the service provided. Such myopic feedback can misguide
042   the model’s behavior and limit its effectiveness in aligning with human values. For example, human
043   evaluators could misjudge an interaction on the spot, due to limited resources (e.g., partial observ-
044   ability; Casper et al. 2023; Lang et al. 2024) or limited bandwidth (e.g., constraints on time, attention,
045   or care; Pandey et al. 2022; Chmielewski & Kucker 2020), leading to incomplete or misinformed
046   feedback. A recent study has theorized that partial observability of an AI assistant’s task execution
047   during human–AI interaction can lead RLHF to learn deceptive behaviors (Lang et al., 2024).
048   In this work, we focus on the challenges caused by human misprediction of future outcomes. In many
049   settings, the utility provided by an AI system to a human user (and similarly its “helpfulness” and
050   “harmlessness”, which RLHF evaluators are typically asked to assess), is not an intrinsic property of
051   the outputs that it generates, but rather a function of their real-world consequences, brought about by
052   the user’s real-world decisions upon consuming said outputs. We hypothesize that relying on human
053   users’ prediction of the helpfulness of an interaction right after it takes place creates a pernicious
      Goodhart’s law dynamic: incentivizing the AI system to increase users’ subjective foresight value will

093

095

096

098   simulated using imperfect models—can significantly reduce model misalignment with the evaluator’s
099

100
      ence optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) and proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman
101

102   We also present results from human user studies, in which RLHS consistently improves both users’
103   ground-truth utility and subjective satisfaction, despite being trained with only simulated hindsight
104   feedback. Our comparative findings demonstrate that RLHS significantly outperforms non-hindsight
105   methods—specifically Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF), which similarly uses
106   AI generation as a proxy for real human feedback, and has been shown to produce results closely
107   resembling that of RLHF (Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2023). We provide more discussion of our
      statement of contributions in Appendix E.

228   3.2   I MPLEMENTATION : H INDSIGHT S IMULATION WITH AI F EEDBACK
229
      Hindsight Simulation. While we have demonstrated theoretically that providing hindsight can
230
      mitigate misalignment in RLHF, exposing humans to real consequences can circumvent material and
231
      ethical difficulties. To address this, we introduce the concept of hindsight simulation—the namesake
232   of our core contribution, RLHS—which allows evaluators, whether human or AI, to make more
233   informed decisions based on simulated outcomes. In practice, hindsight simulation can involve
234   collecting feedback from human evaluators or employ another language model as a proxy to simulate
235   the feedback process. After an evaluator makes a decision based on their interaction with the AI
236   (e.g., purchasing an item), the system provides groundtruth information about the outcome, i.e., the
237   hindsight (e.g., whether the purchased item meets the desired criteria). The evaluator then provides
238   feedback informed by both the decision’s outcome and their prior interaction with the model.
239   This feedback typically takes the form of a rating or binary preference, similar to the feedback used in
240   conventional RLHF. However, unlike the immediate feedback provided solely during an interaction
241   without access to the decision’s consequences, feedback obtained through hindsight simulation is
242   more informed as it incorporates long-term outcomes. This aligns with the reasoning presented in
243   Section 3.1 and demonstrates the potential for improving alignment through simulated hindsight.
244   We implement this approach with two subroutines: (i) partial hindsight, where only a limited set
245   of hindsight information is available to the agent, in a way that more closely matches real-world
246   scenarios, and (ii) oracle hindsight, where the agent has access to full set of hindsight information.
247   We hope that through our subsequent empirical study employing both partial and oracle hindsight, we
248   can gain insights into how extending the hindsight step (i.e., revealing additional outcome information
249   to the agent) can improve the alignment performance of the model.
250
      Illustrative Example: Marketplace Chatbot. We demonstrate the practical impact of RLHS by
251
      applying it to a marketplace AI chatbot. The chatbot’s goal is to assist customers in making purchasing
252   decisions by providing recommendations based on available product information. We assume that
253   both customers and the chatbot have access to some public information, such as a list of items and
254   their prices, but customers have their internal preferences, e.g., wanting a TV with 8K resolution,
255   that are unknown to the chatbot. To the best of our knowledge, existing RLHF schemes deployed
256   for training marketplace chatbots (e.g., Amazon, 2024) use customer feedback solely based on the
257   interaction (i.e., if they feel happy about the chatbot’s service) but not on the outcome (i.e., if the
258   purchased item has actually met their preferences), potentially causing misalignment.
259   Our proposed hindsight simulation approach aims to mitigate this issue by deferring the humans’
260   feedback until they have been informed of the outcome of their decisions, e.g., they have received
261   the product and verified that their expectations are (not) met. In hindsight simulation, the simulated
262   customer interacts with the chatbot, makes a purchasing decision, checks the outcome (hindsight)
263   provided by the system, and provides feedback on the customer’s satisfaction with the service.

323   item either has or lacks a specific feature (e.g., a TV with HDR vs. without HDR), and (2) The feature
      can vary in types (e.g., 8K resolution vs. 4K resolution). While in most cases the chatbot has access


                                                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      to this information, there are instances where it is uncertain about a particular feature (e.g., resolution
325   not specified). We will examine these scenarios and investigate when and how the AI acts deceptively.
326
327   In our setting, the feature is always hidden from the customer, requiring them to interact with the
328
      chatbot to gather information. We explore scenarios where the price is either visible to the customer or
      hidden, allowing us to evaluate how restricting observability affects the feedback and, consequently,
329
      the AI’s behavior. We also consider scenarios when the customer prioritizes price by adding a
330
      constraint regarding their price requirements in the prompt.
331
332   Metrics. We use two primary metrics: true utility and satisfaction rating. The true utility metric U
333   reflects both the customer’s requirements and the item they purchase. We define U as follows: if the
334
      customer makes no purchase, the utility is U = 0. If the purchased item lacks the required feature,
      U = −1. If the item contains the required feature and the customer has no price constraints, U = 1.
335
      When price is a priority and the item contains the required feature, the utility is defined as the ratio of
336
      the price of the cheapest item with the feature to the price the customer actually paid.
337
338   The satisfaction rating reflects the user’s evaluation of the chatbot’s service, measured on a 5-point
339   Likert scale ranging from 1 (very dissatisfied) to 5 (very satisfied). For the experimental results
340
      shown in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4, these ratings were normalized to a scale between -1 and 1, which ensure
      that the true utility and satisfaction ratings are on the same scale for clearer comparison. Additional
341
      results using the original Likert scale are provided in Appendix A. Furthermore, we quantified two
342
      metrics in the human study: regret rate, which measures how often users regret their decisions, and
343   hallucination rate, which measures how truthful the language model is.
344
345   Training algorithms. We explored both online and offline preference optimization methods to align
346
      our language model with human preferences. In our online approach, we trained a reward model on
      the preference data. The language model then interacted with the environment by generating responses
347
      and receiving reward signals from this reward model. We utilized Proximal Policy Optimization
348
      (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to fine-tuned the model iteratively to maximize these rewards. For the
349   offline approach, we experimented with Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
350   2024), which aligns language models with human preferences without the need for an explicit reward
351   model. We apply LoRA fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021) for both algorithms to efficiently update model
352   parameters. Further details of these methods are included in the Appendix B.

357   Misalignment between satisfaction rating and real utility. When using standard RLHF (Ouyang
358   et al., 2022), we observe significant misalignment between user satisfaction ratings and true utility
359   as training progresses (left plot in Figs. 3 and 4). While the satisfaction rating steadily increases,
360   indicating that the language model is learning to deliver responses that receive higher immediate user
361   approval, the true utility shows a sharp decline. This suggests that while the chatbot’s responses may
362   appear more polished or helpful in the short term, they are in fact becoming less aligned with the
363
      user’s true needs or long-term goals. As a result, while users may initially perceive the chatbot’s
      responses as helpful, they are frequently misled and ultimately dissatisfied with their final outcomes.
364
      This highlights a fundamental flaw in using standard RLHF with immediate feedback, as it risks
365
      optimizing for superficial satisfaction at the expense of true utility.

      Our human study had three goals: (Goal 1) evaluate the performance of models trained with immediate
418
      feedback vs. hindsight simulation, (Goal 2) assess how hindsight information affects user satisfaction.
419
      To achieve these goals, we designed two similar human experiments. Both experiments used Llama-
420   3-8b (Dubey et al., 2024) trained with DPO using either immediate feedback or partial hindsight. We
421   conducted online human experiments via Prolific (Palan & Schitter, 2018), involving 200 participants
422   across 10 scenarios, randomly sampled from a test set of 1,200. For each scenario, 20 participants
423   took part: 10 interacting with each of the RLHF model and the RLHS model. We report specific
424   details for participant recruitment, compensation, and IRB approval in Appendix D.2.

430   gather information about the products. At each step, they can choose one of the following actions:

      responses are provided for inquiries. In the second round of interaction, participants may ask about

      Statistical Hypothesis Testing. We conducted experiments to test four hypotheses, using one-tailed
455   and standard t-tests for the first three hypotheses (Fisher, 1970), and Pearson’s correlation coefficient
456   for the fourth (Sedgwick, 2012). The one-tailed t-test framework used in Hypotheses 1, 2, and 3 is
457   outlined below. The null hypothesis (H0 ) and the alternative hypothesis (H1 ) are defined as:
458
459                H0 : µ1 ≤ µ2                     (Group 1 satisfaction is less than or equal to Group 2)
460                H1 : µ1 > µ2                     (Group 1 satisfaction is significantly higher than Group 2)
461
462
      Here, µ1 and µ2 represents the mean satisfaction of Group 1 and Group 2, respectively. The two-tailed
      t-test follows a similar format but tests for any significant difference between the group means.
463
464   Hypothesis 1: Models trained with RLHS lead to a higher long-term user satisfaction rate and lower
465   regret rate than those trained with RLHF using immediate feedback.
466   We evaluated hindsight ratings for two models: Group 1 (RLHS) and Group 2 (RLHF). The hypothesis
467   test resulted in p = 4 × 10−8 , well below the significance threshold of 0.001. When reversing the
468   groups for regret rates, the test yielded p = 5 × 10−5 again below 0.001.
469
      Hypothesis 2: Models trained with RLHF using immediate feedback often experience a notable
470
      decline in user satisfaction once future outcomes are revealed, while RLHS mitigate this decline.
471
472   Group 1 consisted of users interacting with RLHF without hindsight feedback, and Group 2 received
473   hindsight feedback. The hypothesis test gave p = 4 × 10−9 , confirming a significant decline.
474   To demonstrate RLHS mitigates this decline, we ran a two-tailed t-test comparing immediate and
475
      hindsight ratings. The result, p = 0.90, showed no significant difference.
476   Hypothesis 3: RLHS lead to significantly higher true utility than RLHF.
477
      We assessed the objective performance of the two models by comparing true utility scores for Group
478
      1 (RLHS) and Group 2 (RLHF). The hypothesis test yielded p = 4 × 10−8 .
479
480   Hypothesis 4: Models trained with RLHS are more truthful, presenting a strong correlation between
481   their high immediate user satisfaction rate (subjective) and high true utility (objective).
482   To evaluate the correlation, we used Pearson’s correlation coefficient and tested whether this co-
483   efficient was significantly different from zero. The null hypothesis (H0 ) assumed no correlation
484   (i.e., r = 0) while the alternative hypothesis (H1 ) assumed a non-zero correlation. The test found a
485   significant correlation between immediate ratings and true utility for RLHS (p = 5 × 10−4 ), while
      no significant correlation was observed for RLHF (p = 0.47).

503   7    R ELATED W ORK
504
505   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. RLHF is widely used for training language
506   models to align with human preferences and values (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019;
507   Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a). The classical RLHF pipeline typically involves three stages:
508   supervised fine-tuning (Chen et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024) reward
509   modeling (Gao et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2023; Lambert et al.,
510
      2024), and policy optimization (Schulman et al., 2017). PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) is commonly
      used in the policy optimization phase. However, due to the complexity and optimization challenges
511
      of online preference optimization algorithms (Zheng et al., 2023; Santacroce et al., 2023), researchers
512
      have been exploring more efficient and simpler offline alternatives without learning the reward model
513   (Rafailov et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023). Our approach
514   using hindsight simulation can be applied to both online PPO and offline (DPO) learning algorithms.
515
516
      Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback. Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022b) uses an LLM to
      provide feedback and refine responses, producing data used to train a fixed reward model. This reward
517
      model is then applied in reinforcement learning, a process referred to as RLAIF. The technique of
518
      using LLM-as-a-Judge has become a standard method for evaluating model outputs (Dubois et al.,
519   2024; Li et al., 2023b; Fernandes et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024; Saha et al., 2023) and curating data
520   to train reward model (Lee et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a). Recent studies have
521   shown that RLAIF performs similarly to RLHF (Lee et al., 2023). Our approach also utilizes LLMs
522   to provide feedback and uses the preference data to fine-tune our model.
523
      Challenges of Learning from Human Feedback. Learning from human feedback presents chal-
524
      lenges (Casper et al., 2023). Human evaluators are imperfect (Saunders et al., 2022; Gudibande
525   et al., 2023), making mistakes due to limited time (Chmielewski & Kucker, 2020) or cognitive
526   biases (Pandey et al., 2022). Evaluators may also have conflicting preferences (Bakker et al., 2022).
527   Modeling human preferences is difficult (Zhao et al., 2016; Hong et al., 2022; Lindner & El-Assady,
528   2022), with models being prone to overoptimization (Gao et al., 2023).
529
530
      8    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we introduced Reinforcement Learning from Hindsight Simulation (RLHS), an algorith-
533
      mic framework that mitigates misalignment in RLHF by providing evaluators with future outcome
534   information. We demonstrated that RLHS can significantly improve utility compared to existing
535   RLHF pipelines that rely only on immediate feedback, while maintaining a high user satisfaction
536   rate throughout the human–AI interaction. While our study focused on simulated hindsight with an
537   application to marketplace chatbot, future work should explore incorporating hindsight in RLHF for
538   additional real-world applications with real human evaluators. Further, we see an open opportunity
539   to equip RLHS with other feedback modalities, such as visual cues, which could further enrich the
      feedback process and improve alignment.

       D.2    PARTICIPANTS AND DATA COLLECTION
1027
1028   The human subjects were chosen from a high quality Prolific participant pool, where participants were
1029   pre-screened to have an approval rate of 95-100 over at least 100 previous submissions. Participants
1030   were located in the USA. To assign subjects to experimental conditions, we used random assignment,
1031   and each participant was only assigned to one shopping scenario (either one purchasing decision or
1032   comparing between two AI shopping assistants). As a negative experience could bias participants’
1033   perceptions of AI chatbots, we ensured that they were not able to retake the study.
1034   The expected duration of the study was 5 minutes, and actually completed the study at a median
1035   time of 4:54. Subjects were compensated $1.10 for their participation, resulting in a hourly wage
1036   of $13.47/hour, which was substantially higher than minimum wage. In addition to participant
1037   satisfaction ratings or preferences, participants were asked to provide a brief 2-sentence explanation
1038   to explain their ratings or preferences. We manually reviewed these explanations for all participants,
1039   and participants that did not provide a reasonable 2-sentence explanation had their data removed from
1040
       the study. We also removed participants that finished the study in an unreasonably short time (<1:30
       out of the estimated 5 minutes). Other than this, no data was removed.

1044   D.3    A DDITIONAL HUMAN STUDY
1045
1046   We conducted an additional human study to assess how closely the feedback and actions of our AI
1047   proxy (Llama-3.1-70B) align with those of human participants. In the study, participants interacted
1048   with chatbots from two different stores, taking actions such as purchasing items or leaving the store
1049
       based on the conversations. After engaging with both stores, participants were asked to choose which
       store they preferred. We randomly selected 10 scenarios from our training set, with 30 different
1050
       participants evaluating each scenario. To determine the human preference for each scenario, we
1051
       employed majority voting. This method was used to ensure that the aggregated choice reflected the
1052   consensus among participants, minimizing the impact of individual variability and providing a more
1053   robust measure of overall preference. Our analysis revealed that the matching accuracy between
1054   LLM-generated feedback and human feedback reached 100%. Furthermore, the actions taken by
1055   the LLM matched those of human participants with 95% accuracy. These findings suggest that our
1056   simulated feedback and actions align strongly with real human behavior.

       E.1   R ELATED W ORK
1074
1075   Statement of Contributions. Our key insight is that the true value of AI outputs lies in their
1076   downstream consequences, especially in how they influence real-world human behavior. While the
1077   importance of long-term outcomes is a fundamental aspect of dynamic decision theory, our work is
1078   the first to address this within the context of RLHF by (1) exploring the negative effects of learning
1079   from immediate foresight human feedback, and (2) proposing a general mitigation strategy that
       evaluates real-world downstream harm caused by inaccurate information.


                                                        20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Comparison with Related Work: One of the recent works cited in comparison is by Lang et al.
1081   (2024), which focuses on the problem of partial observability. This is distinct from the problem
1082   of human misprediction we address. In their setting, user utility is confined to the immediate time
1083   frame of the interaction and does not consider the long-term repercussions on the user’s behavior
1084   or well-being after the interaction concludes. Their analysis primarily highlights scenarios where
1085   an AI system is incentivized to withhold information to avoid negative feedback scores but does
1086   not delve into the real-world impact such deception has on user utility. In contrast, our approach
1087   specifically examines the human user’s decision-making process after interacting with the AI system,
1088   emphasizing how misalignment or deceptive behavior directly affects their realized utility.
1089   Recent studies have investigated sycophantic behavior in language models (Sharma et al., 2023; Wei
1090   et al., 2023; Perez et al., 2022), where the models are optimized to generate responses that align with
1091   user beliefs rather than the truth. Our empirical results also reveal such tendencies. In this paper,
1092   we analyze the underlying factors contributing to this behavior and demonstrate how incorporating
1093   hindsight can be effective in preventing sycophancy.
1094   Theoretical Contributions: We extend the RLHF formulation by mathematically capturing this
1095   dynamic interplay between AI and human decision-making, something that has not been explored in
1096   prior work, including Lang et al. (2024) Our theoretical analysis not only highlights why deceptive
1097   behavior is problematic but also quantifies its repercussions by modeling the "closed-loop" evolution
1098   of the sociotechnical system formed by the human and the AI.
1099   Mitigation Strategies: Importantly, we propose and evaluate a novel mitigation method: Rein-
1100   forcement Learning from Hindsight Simulation (RLHS) that significantly reduces misalignment and
1101   deceptive behavior. While Lang et al. (2024) note that deception is undesirable, they do not provide
1102   solutions or a theoretical basis for understanding its downstream damage. Our work, therefore,
1103   not only identifies and analyzes the issue but also offers a practical, effective mitigation strategy.
1104   Additionally, our partial hindsight approach still operates within a partially observable setting. The
1105   minimal difference between partial and oracle hindsight suggests that the fundamental issue in the
1106   class of misalignment we study is not primarily linked to partial observability, but rather to the human
1107
       misprediction of the downstream consequences.

1109   E.2   B ROADER I MPACT
1110
1111   Human evaluators do not always know the full truth when providing feedback. Without explicit
1112   information about its future consequences, evaluators must implicitly estimate them during their
1113   assessment. This limitation poses significant challenges for real-world applications of AI, particularly
1114   within the RLHF framework we studied. In the following sections, we discussed these limitations and
1115   how our proposed hindsight feedback approach can help overcome them to enhance AI alignment.
1116   Limited Access in Real-World Applications: In real-world scenarios, users and human labelers
1117   frequently interact with black-box or closed-prompt AI systems where internal prompts and decision-
1118   making processes remain opaque. Notable examples include commercial systems such as OpenAI’s
1119   ChatGPT and Amazon’s Rufus. Our proposed techniques (hindsight feedback), and the experimental
1120   settings we used can be applied directly to these systems where full internal access is unavailable. In
1121   such cases, assessing the consistency of responses alone is insufficient, as external context might not
1122   capture the complete implications of an AI’s output. Hindsight feedback allows evaluators to provide
1123   more reliable feedback by considering outcomes, improving alignment in these constrained settings.
1124   Limitations of Human Judgment and Information Access: Even when human evaluators have full
1125   access to models and their prompts (e.g., in open-source systems), perfect judgment is not guaranteed.
1126   Evaluators may miss deeper implications or fail to predict the long-term impact of responses, whether
1127   due to lack of expertise or cognitive limitations. These challenges are relevant to both open-source
1128   and closed-source models. Below, we outline two practical examples illustrating these limitations
1129   and how hindsight feedback can address them:
1130   Code Generation Scenario: Imagine a user asking a language model for code to fit a polynomial curve
1131   to a set of data points. One solution may fit the data perfectly, while another shows some deviation. A
1132   human evaluator might prefer the model with the perfect fit, not realizing that it overfits and performs
1133   poorly on new data. Immediate feedback in this case could lead to misalignment, as it prioritizes
       surface-level satisfaction over long-term utility. By providing feedback after testing the code on


                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       new data (hindsight), evaluators can offer more informed input, reducing misalignment. Hindsight
1135   simulation can automate part of this process by allowing models to test outcomes on unseen data and
1136   report the results to human evaluators for better feedback. One extra benefit of hindsight simulation
1137   is that humans do not need to be domain experts to provide truthful feedback.
1138
       AI4Science Proof Construction: When constructing mathematical proofs for scientific problems,
1139
       model may generate results that are correct only under conditions or assumptions specified by the
1140
       user. Human evaluators, constrained by time or limited expertise, may overlook these limitations
