011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The Log-Periodic Power Law Singularity (LPPLS) model offers a general frame-
014            work for capturing dynamics and predicting transition points in diverse natural
015            and social systems. In this work, we present two calibration techniques for the
016
               LPPLS model using deep learning. First, we introduce the Mono-LPPLS-NN
               (M-LNN) model; for any given empirical time series, a unique M-LNN model
017
               is trained and shown to outperform state-of-the-art techniques in estimating the
018
               nonlinear parameters (tc , m, ω) of the LPPLS model as evidenced by the compre-
019            hensive distribution of parameter errors. Second, we extend the M-LNN model
020            to a more general model architecture, the Poly-LPPLS-NN (P-LNN), which is
021            able to quickly estimate the nonlinear parameters of the LPPLS model for any
022            given time-series of a fixed length, including previously unseen time-series during
023            training. The Poly class of models train on many synthetic LPPLS time-series
024            augmented with various noise structures in a supervised manner. Given enough
025            training examples, the P-LNN models also outperform state-of-the-art techniques
026            for estimating the parameters of the LPPLS model as evidenced by the comprehen-
027
               sive distribution of parameter errors. Additionally, this class of models is shown
               to substantially reduce the time to obtain parameter estimates. Finally, we present
028
               applications to the diagnostic and prediction of two financial bubble peaks (fol-
029
               lowed by their crash) and of a famous rockslide. These contributions provide a
030            bridge between deep learning and the study of the prediction of transition times in
031            complex time series.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The Log-Periodic Power Law Singularity (LPPLS) model provides a flexible and effective mathe-
037   matical framework for representing the dynamics and for predicting temporal transition points which
038   occur in a variety of natural and social systems (Sornette, 1998). Examples include the formation
039   of financial bubbles followed by crashes in financial markets, the catastrophic material failure of
040   systems subjected to constant or increasing stress loads (Anifrani et al., 1995), transient seismic and
041   other geophysical activities preceding large earthquakes (Sornette & Sammis, 1995; Sornette et al.,
042
      2021; Mearns & Sornette, 2021), the formation of cusps in the surface curvature on the free surface
      of a conducting fluid in an electric field (Zubarev, 1998), vortex collapse of systems of point vortices
043
      (Leoncini et al., 2000), the finite-time formation of black holes according to the equations of General
044
      Relativity of the field metric coupled to a mass field (Choptuik, 1999a;b), the finite-time formation
045   of fruiting bodies in models of aggregating micro-organisms (Rascle & Ziti, 1995), or in the more
046   prosaic rotating coin (Euler’s disk) (Moffatt, 2000).
047
048
      LPPLS contains five words that combine three concepts, (i) singularity, (ii) power law and (iii)
      log-periodic, in order of importance.
049
050   The term “singularity” refers to the phenomenon where solutions to a large set of (ordinary or
051   partial) differential equations exist only until a certain “critical” time, tc . Consider the proportional
052   growth equation dp dt = rp. Here, p can represent the population of a species, the size of a financial
053   investment, or the GDP of a country, and r is the growth rate. For a constant r, the solution is
      an exponentially growing function, p(t) = p(0)ert . To understand where a singularity can arise,

054
      assume that the growth rate increases with p as r(p) = ηp, where η > 0 is a positive constant. In fact,
055   this model reflects the growth of Earth’s human population from around 1800 to the 1960s (Johansen
056   & Sornette, 2001; Korotayev et al., 2006). This embodies a positive feedback of population on the
057   growth rate. The solution to the new equation dp          2             1
                                                       dt = ηp is p(t) = tc −t , where the critical time tc
058
      is determined from the initial condition p(0) = t1c . The solution exists for all times less than tc , at
059
      which point a singularity occurs, i.e., p(t) diverges as t → tc . In this case, the mathematical term
060
      for tc is a “movable singularity” (Bender & Orszag, 1999) because its value depends on the initial
061   conditions (and on the structure of the equation). For t > tc , there is no solution. The equation
062   dp        2
       dt = ηp is said to exhibit a finite-time singularity, a divergence in finite time. For more general
063   equations, p(t) can remain finite at tc and it is its first derivative that diverges, or higher order
064   derivatives.
065
066
      The presence of positive feedback (also known as pro-cyclicality in economics) in many dynamical
      systems leads to behaviors that transiently follow finite-time singular trajectories. A particularly
067
      attractive aspect of such mathematical descriptions is that the model contains the prediction of its
068
      own demise, the point where it approaches and passes tc . This characteristic allows these models
069   to naturally predict “regime changes” expected around tc . Thus, the estimation of tc is crucial;
070   having a framework to estimate it well could facilitate targeted interventions to either achieve desired
071   outcomes or avoid undesirable ones.
072
      The second concept “power law” is also illustrated by the above example in which p(t) diverges
073
      according to a power law singularity function, here with exponent equal to 1. Other finite time
074                                                                                   1

075
      singularities can be logarithm like with − ln[tc − t], essential like with e tc −t or oscillatory with
      sin[ tc1−t ] and so on. One should not confuse this power law singularity with power law distributions.
076
077   The third concept “log-periodic” refers to the observable signature of the symmetry of discrete scale
078   invariance (Sornette, 1998), which corresponds to a partial breaking of the symmetry of continuous
079   scale invariance. Log-periodicity simply means that there is a periodicity in the logarithm of the
080   distance to the critical point and, as a consequence a discrete hierarchy of times to tc which appears
081   on top of the smooth power law structure. The periodicity in log-scale provides an anchor that can
082
      be used to improve prediction of tc , similarly to frequency modulation in radio transmission where
      a tiny signal can be extracted by locking-in on a specific frequency. The simplest expression of a
083
      log-periodic function is cos[ω ln(tc − t)] where ω does not have the meaning or dimension of an
084
      angular frequency. It rather represents the value of the ratio λ of the discrete hierarchy of times to tc
085   according to ω = ln2πλ (Sornette, 1998).
086
087   The simplest LPPLS model that combines the three concepts reads
088                      O(t) = A + B(tc − t)m + C(tc − t)m cos(ω ln (tc − t) − ϕ)                         (1)
089
      where O(t) is the observable (for instance, the logarithm of the price for the diagnostic of a financial
090   bubble). Among its 7 parameters, the three nonlinear parameters tc , m and ω play special roles. In
091   the regime of interest here where 0 < m < 1, we have O(tc ) = A and the singularity occurs in the
092   first-order derivative of O(t). The sharpness of this singularity is controlled by the exponent m.
093
      In standard calibration methods, one minimises the sum over all data points of the difference be-
094
      tween the observable and the LPPLS model (1). Calculating the corresponding Hessian matrix and
095
      diagonalising it, one finds that parameters tc , ω and m in descending order are the most sloppy in a
096   technical sense of the term (Brée et al., 2013; Filimonov et al., 2017): the log-likelihood function
097   is the most flat along the direction tc and, unsurprisingly, the determination of the critical time is
098   difficult (Demos & Sornette, 2017), if not unstable.
099
      Given these limitations of standard calibration methods, our objective is to investigate the capabil-
100
      ities of neural networks (NNs) as universal function approximators, as highlighted by Hornik et al.
101
      (1989), in estimating the parameters of the LPPLS model. In this study, we present two NN ar-
102   chitectures that are specifically designed for this purpose. The first architecture processes a single
103   time-series to solve the inverse problem of optimally identifying the LPPLS parameters for that
104   given time-series. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), introduced by Raissi et al. (2019)
105   and further developed by Faroughi et al. (2024), have proven effective in addressing inverse prob-
106   lems. They work by incorporating differential equations representing physical laws directly into
107   the architecture of the NN, enabling them to learn solutions that inherently comply with the laws
      governing the systems they model. Our architecture, largely inspired by PINNs, incorporates the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      LPPLS functional form in its learning process. Because this model operates on a single time-series,
109   we term it the Mono-LPPLS-NN (M-LNN).
110
111   The second architecture we introduce is designed to operate in a conventional supervised manner,
112
      necessitating a large dataset of time-series (features) and their corresponding LPPLS parameters
      (labels). As no such empirical dataset exists, we generate a large corpus of synthetic time-series
113
      with known LPPLS parameters and introduce varying degrees of noise to obfuscate the LPPLS
114
      signal. We show that training on the synthetic dataset augmented by noise is sufficient to produce
115   viable parameter estimates for unseen time-series and that its learning transfers to empirical datasets.
116   Further, this class of models has the desirable property of any pre-trained NN model in that it can
117   be deployed for near real-time inference. In our experimentation, we found that this class of models
118   achieves estimations several orders of magnitude faster than existing state-of-the-art techniques on
119   similar hardware configurations (see Table 2 in Appendix A.4 for details). Because this model is
120   presented with many time-series, we term it the Poly-LPPLS-NN (P-LNN).
121   In the following sections, we describe the analyzed calibration techniques, which include the
122   Levenberg-Marquardt (LM) method, which stands as the state-of-the-art for LPPLS parameter es-
123   timation, and our NN models, the M-LNN and P-LNN architectures. Our experimental design is
124   then outlined, focusing on assessing the effectiveness of these models against the incumbent LM
125   method by testing on a set of synthetic LPPLS series augmented by white noise and/or AR(1) noise.
126   We highlight the performance of each model against both synthetic and real-world datasets. We
127   conclude by discussing the broader implications of our results and proposing directions for future
128   research.

130   2     M ETHODOLOGY
131
132   The state-of-the-art approach for estimating the tc , m, and ω parameters of the LPPLS model is
133   the LM method, which we detail in Appendix A.1. In this section, we focus on introducing two
134   alternative NN calibration methods.
135
136   2.1     M-LNN M ODEL
137
      The M-LNN model is a feed-forward NN that estimates the nonlinear parameters of the LPPLS
138
      model from a specific time-series of arbitrary length. The M-LNN is a unique application where a
139
      new NN is trained for each empirical time series. This bespoke training approach has the potential
140   to ensure that the model is adjusted to the characteristics of each unique dataset, perhaps offering
141   more reliable parameter estimations. However, the trade-off here is that such an approach requires
142   more computational resources.
143
144   2.1.1    M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
145
146   The architecture of the M-LNN is defined as follows (we also provide a comprehensive architectural
147
      diagram in Figure 1).
148            h1 = ReLU(W1 · X + b1 ), h2 = ReLU(W2 · h1 + b2 ), Y = Wo · h2 + bo ,                 (2)

150

152   layers. The ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) function introduces non-linearity, enabling the network to
153   capture and learn complex data patterns.

173   2.1.2    T RAINING PROCEDURE
174
      Training of the M-LNN model (Eq. 2) shown in Figure 1 is conducted through a gradient descent
175
      optimization loop, where the objective is to minimize the combined loss (LM SE + LP enalty ) over
176   a specified number of epochs. It processes the input data to estimate the LPPLS parameters tc , m,
177   and ω, which are subsequently used to calculate the linear parameters using Eq. 8 n Appendix A.1
178   and finally the estimated LPPLS time-series. The learning rate is set to 10−2 . Prior to training,
179   the dataset is min-max scaled to normalize the values and ensure consistent training dynamics. The
180   training loop implements backpropagation to adjust the model parameters based on the total loss
181   using the Adam optimizer. The model state is preserved at the epoch where the lowest total loss
182   is achieved, indicative of the most accurate parameter predictions. This state is chosen as the best
183   model fit.

212   2.2.1    T RAINING SETS
213
214   Due to the absence of real-world datasets containing labeled LPPLS parameters, synthetic time-
215   series data must be created to facilitate the training of the P-LNN models. Each variant within the
      P-LNN family of models undergoes training with a specific noise configuration and a set of randomly

217   of the P-LNN model using differently augmented synthetic datasets. The first model, referred to
218   as P-LNN-100K, is trained on synthetic data augmented with white noise. The second model, P-
219   LNN-100K-AR1, utilizes synthetic data with AR(1) (autoregressive model of order 1) noise. Lastly,

      2.2.4    T RAINING PROCEDURE
251
252   The training process of the P-LNN model focuses on directly optimizing its ability to estimate
253   LPPLS parameters, rather than comparing the original time-series with the estimated one derived
254   from these parameters. This means that the loss function is chosen to be the sum of the squares of
255   the three differences between the true and estimated LPPLS nonlinear parameters tc .m, ω. Initially,
256   the NN is configured with specific architectural dimensions tailored to the input time-series data.
257   Here, we use 252 nodes corresponding to time windows of n = 252 time points. Training occurs
258
      over 20 epochs, determined empirically to allow ample iterations for learning and weight updates to
      achieve satisfactory loss. Each epoch processes data in batches of 8 randomly selected noisy LPPLS
259
      time series. This value of 8 is chosen to balance memory resources and gradient descent efficiency.
260
      Batch processing introduces stochasticity into training, enhancing adaptability. The learning rate is
261   set to 10−5 . Before training, the dataset undergoes min-max scaling to normalize values and ensure
262   consistent training dynamics. Loss curves for each model are included in Figures [8, 9, 10] in the
263   appendix.

453   4   L IMITATIONS
454
455   A limitation of our study arises from the methodology used to generate both the training and testing
456   datasets. We employed the same synthetic data generation technique for creating both sets of data.
457   While this approach ensures consistency across training and testing phases, it also introduces poten-
458   tial biases towards the models’ performance, as they are evaluated on data that may not adequately
459   represent real-world variability and complexity. However, the good performance on the three empir-
460   ical data sets, which are both unseen during the training and containing unknown noise structures,
461   augurs well for the robustness of our NN models.
462   A second limitation is the relatively simple nature of the noise added to the LPPLS trajectories in
463   the synthetic datasets used for the supervised training of P-LNN models. The residuals of LPPLS
464   calibrations to real data are likely to be more structured than a white noise or even an AR(1) process
465   as shown e.g. in Zhou & Sornette (2002b; 2003); Lin et al. (2014). Future works will need to extend
466   the present methodology to noises that possess both long-range correlations and non-Gaussian fat
467   tailed properties in the spirit of Ref. (Zhou & Sornette, 2002b). Indeed, one should always remember
468
      that the complete specification of a model includes both the model itself and the noise. Noise
      specification is often an afterthought of modellers while it should be an integral part of the model.
469
470   We have tested the performance of the trained NN models on three empirical datasets, with very
471   encouraging results. However, this small sample size does not fully capture the diversity and com-
472   plexity of real-world phenomena that could be modeled by NNs. A broader array of empirical
473   datasets encompassing a more varied set of critical events may reveal novel properties of both the
474
      LPPLS structure and its residuals. This could inspire the development of more sophisticated LPPLS
      NN models, including generalised LPPLS formalisms in the spirit of Gluzman & Sornette (2002)
475
      and more powerful NN architectures.

478   5   C ONCLUSION
479
480   Our research confirms the capability of NN models, specifically Mono-LPPLS-NN (M-LNN) and
481   Poly-LPPLS-NN (P-LNN) models in estimating the parameters of the LPPLS model. This is demon-
482   strated by our finding that they exhibit first-order stochastic dominance over the standard Levenberg-
483   Marquardt (LM) method. Notably, the P-LNN-100K model trained with just white noise excelled
484   in minimizing small errors, whereas the P-LNN-100K-BOTH model, trained with a blend of white
485   noise and AR(1) noise, showed proficiency in reducing large errors, thus illustrating a balance in
      noise training methodologies.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      The insights from our analysis underscore the need for a sophisticated approach in training these
487   Poly NN models, particularly emphasizing the role of noise diversity in enhancing robustness and
488   error. The observed variations in performance across different noise configurations indicate that tai-
489   lored training strategies could significantly improve model adaptability and the accuracy of param-
490   eter estimation. One promising research path is the exploration of curriculum learning, as proposed
491   by Bengio et al. (2009). This methodology posits that NNs may achieve superior generalization
492   and accelerated convergence if the training examples are presented in an increasingly complex se-
493   quence. This strategy resonates with the idea of progressively intensifying noise complexity, which
494   could potentially improve learning efficiency and model’s ability to generalize.
495   Another research prospect stemming from this study involve the use of a larger variety of noise di-
496   versity and how that may impact parameter estimation error, especially by extending AR(1) noise
497   with more sophisticated long-memory processes representing the rich dynamics of financial volatil-
498   ity. This could provide further insight into model behavior. Next, particularly for the Poly class
499   of models, there exists a considerable scope for architectural innovation. Adopting advanced ar-
500   chitectures like recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers could mitigate the constraints
501
      associated with the fixed size of the input time-series data.
502   Finally, developing more intricate calibration techniques, potentially by embedding penalties for
503   specific types of errors, could improve the precision in parameter estimation. Further empirical
504   validation also emerges as an essential area of further research, necessitating the application of
505   trained models to real-world datasets to corroborate their efficacy and utility in forecasting finite-
506   time singularities across diverse domains.
507   Our research bridges the gap between NN methodologies and the analysis of temporal critical phe-
508   nomena, offering promising directions for future investigations. The potential to predict finite-time
509   singularities signalling regime shifts and transitions with improved accuracy has important conse-
510   quences for both theoretical research and practical applications in natural, engineering and social
511   sciences.
