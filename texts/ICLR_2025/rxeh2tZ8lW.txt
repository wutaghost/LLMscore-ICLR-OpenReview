009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            General purpose agents need to be able to execute large skillsets in stochastic set-
012            tings. Given that the mutual information between skills and states measures the
013            number of distinct skills in a skillset, a compelling objective for learning a diverse
014            skillset is to find the skillset with the largest mutual information between skills and
015            states. The problem is that the two main unsupervised approaches for maximizing
016
               this mutual information objective, Empowerment-based skill learning and Unsu-
               pervised Goal-Conditioned Reinforcement Learning, only maximize loose lower
017
               bounds on the mutual information, which can impede diverse skillset learning. We
018
               propose a new empowerment objective, Skillset Empowerment, that maximizes a
019            tighter bound on the mutual information between skills and states. For any pro-
020            posed skillset, the tighter bound on mutual information is formed by replacing the
021            posterior distribution of the proposed skillset with a variational distribution that
022            is conditioned on the proposed skillset and trained to match the posterior of the
023            proposed skillset. Maximizing our mutual information lower bound objective is a
024            bandit problem in which actions are skillsets and the rewards are our mutual in-
025            formation objective, and we optimize this bandit problem with a new actor-critic
026            architecture. We show empirically that our approach is able to learn large abstract
027
               skillsets in stochastic domains, including ones with high-dimensional observa-
               tions, in contrast to existing approaches.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   General purpose agents that operate in the real world will need to be able to execute a large set
033   of skills in highly stochastic settings. A futuristic household robot, for instance, will need to ex-
034   ecute the vast number of skills involved in household chores like cooking and cleaning while the
035   human members of the household may be moving as well as conversing with each other and the
036   robot in seemingly random ways. Even the simple act of the robot moving its head to look in dif-
037   ferent directions will produce unpredictable outcomes as relevant objects to the robot may appear in
038   unexpected places. An appealing approach for learning diverse skillsets, regardless of the level of
039
      randomness in the domain, is to find the skillset with the largest mutual information between skills
      and skill-terminating states because this mutual information measures the number of distinct skills
040
      in a skillset.
041
042   The problem is that the two most popular approaches for optimizing the mutual information between
043   skills and states, Empowerment-based skill learning (Gregor et al., 2016; Eysenbach et al., 2018;
044   Achiam et al., 2018; Choi et al., 2021; Sharma et al., 2019) and Unsupervised Goal-Conditioned
045
      Reinforcement Learning (GCRL) (Ecoffet et al., 2019; Mendonca et al., 2021; Nair et al., 2018;
      Pong et al., 2019; Campos et al., 2020; Pitis et al., 2020; Held et al., 2017; McClinton et al., 2021;
046
      Held et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2023), only maximize a loose lower bound of the mutual information
047
      between skills and states. The loose bound means agents are not able to accurately measure the di-
048   versity of candidate skillets, which in turn makes it difficult to find a diverse skillset. In both existing
049   empowerment and unsupervised GCRL approaches, this loose lower bound on mutual information
050   for a candidate skillset is formed by replacing the true posterior probability that computes the prob-
051   ability of a skill given the skill-terminating state and the candidate skillset with a potentially very
052   different distribution. Existing empowerment approaches replace the true posterior of the candidate
053   skillset with another distribution trained to match the true posterior of the current skillset, which
      may have significant differences with the candidate skillset. This can create a loose lower bound on

114   2.1   S KILLSET M ODEL AND E MPOWERMENT
115
      We model an agent’s skillset using a probabilistic graphical model defined by the tuple (S, A,
116   Z, T , ϕ, π). S is the space of states; A is the space of actions; Z is the space of skills;
117   T is the environment transition dynamics T (st+1 |st , at ) that provides the probability of a state
118   given the prior state and action. The transition dynamics are assumed to be conditionally in-
119   dependent of the history of states and actions (i.e., T (st+1 |st , at ) = T (st+1 |s0 , a0 , . . . , st , at )).
120   The remaining distributions ϕ and π are the skillset distributions that can be learned. ϕ rep-
121   resents the distribution over skills ϕ(z|s0 ) given a skill start state s0 . π represents the skill-
122   conditioned policy π(at |st , z) that provides the distribution over primitive actions given a state
123   st and skill z. Assuming each skill consists of n primitive actions, the full joint distribution
124
      of a skill and a trajectory of actions and states (z, a0 , s1 , . . . , an−1 , sn ) conditioned on a par-
      ticular start state s0 and skillset defined ϕ and π is given by p(z, a0 , s1 , . . . , an1 , sn |s0 , ϕ, π) =
125
      ϕ(z|s0 )π(a0 |s0 , z)p(s1 |s0 , a0 ) . . . π(an−1 |sn−1 , z)p(sn |sn−1 , an−1 ).
126
127   In this paper, we measure the diversity of a skillset defined by ϕ and θ and executed from some start
128   state s0 using the mutual information between the skill random variable Z and the skill-terminating
129   state random variable Sn , I(Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π). This mutual information provides a way to measure
130
      the number of distinct skills in a skillset, in which a skill is distinct if it terminates in a set of states
      not targeted by other skills in the skillset. I(Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π) is defined
131
132           I(Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π) = H(Z|s0 , ϕ, π) − H(Z|s0 , ϕ, π, Sn )                                           (1)
133                               = Ez∼ϕ(z|s0 ),sn ∼p(sn |s0 ,π,z) [log p(z|s0 , ϕ, π, sn ) − log p(z|s0 , ϕ)].    (2)
134
135   Per equation 1, the diversity of a skillset grows when there are more skills in a skillset (i.e., higher
136
      skill distribution entropy H(Z|s0 , ϕ, π)) and/or the skills become more distinct (i.e., the conditional
      entropy H(Z|s0 , ϕ, π, Sn ) shrinks).
137
138   The empowerment of a state is the maximum mutual information with respect to all possible (ϕ, π)
139   skillsets:
140                                         E(s) = max I(Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π).                                        (3)

142   That is, the empowerment of a state measures the size of the most diverse skillset in that state.
143   Note that this use of empowerment, in which mutual information is maximized to find the most
144   diverse skillset, is different than maximizing an empowerment reward function, in which agents are
145   encouraged to take actions that lead them to high empowerment states (Klyubin et al., 2008; Jung
146   et al., 2012; Mohamed & Rezende, 2015). Indeed, in order to enable this other use of empowerment
147   that is popular in the literature, there needs to be a better way to measure the empowerment of a
148   state, which is what our work is focused on.

      3.2   P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
271
272
273   Next, we provide a practical implementation that optimizes the objective with deep learning. We
274   first discuss how we optimize the skillset distributions ϕ and π because that informs how we will
275   train the variational parameters ψ. Before discussing how the skillset distributions are updated up-
276   dated, note that the Skillset Empowerment objective is not a typical skill-conditioned Reinforcement
277   Learning (RL) problem in which rewards are only functions of states, skills, and actions. If Skillset
278   Empowerment were to be converted to an RL problem, the reward function for the final action
279
      would be log qψ (z|s0 , ϕ, π, sn ), which depends in part on the skill-conditioned policy π. Optimiz-
      ing Skillset Empowerment with a typical skill-conditioned RL approach, in which the agent learns a
280
      policy mapping states and skills to primitive actions, would be subject to potentially significant non-
281
      stationary rewards because each time the skill-conditioned policy is updated, the final step reward
282   could change.
283
284   Instead we optimize the Skillset Empowerment with respect to the skillset distributions as a bandit
285
      problem, in which actions are (ϕ, π) skillsets and rewards are the diversity of those skillsets as
286   measured by I˜SE (Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π). In order for the skillset distributions ϕ and π to serve as actions,
287
      we first describe how Skillset Empowerment vectorizes these distributions. In our implementation
      of Skillset Empowerment, we represent the skill-conditioned policy π as a vector containing the the
288
      weights and biases of a neural network fπ that forms the skill-conditioned policy. fπ : S × Z → A
289
      takes as input a state s and skill z outputs the mean of a gaussian skill-conditioned policy. That
290   is, π(a|s, z) = N (a; µ = fπ (s, z), σ = σ0 ), in which σ0 is a small fixed standard deviation.
291   We represent ϕ(z|s0 ) using a scalar that represents a uniform distribution that takes the shape of
292   a d-dimensional cube with side length ϕ. For instance, in our tasks in which the skills are two-
293   dimensional, skills are sampled from a square with side length ϕ centered at the origin. Figure 3
294   in the Appendix provides an illustration of a two-dimensional skill distribution ϕ. The probability
295   density ϕ(z|s0 ) = (1/ϕ)d .
296   We optimize both ϕ and π using their own bandit problem. In the ϕ bandit problem, the bandit policy
297   is fµ : S → ϕ, which takes as input the skill start state s0 and outputs ϕ (i.e., a scalar representing the
298   size of the uniform skill space). In the π bandit problem, the bandit policy fλ : S × ϕ → π takes as
299   input the skill start state s0 and a ϕ value and outputs π, the vector of parameters that define the skill-
300   conditioned policy. In the π bandit problem, the reward for an π action is the Skillset Empowerment
301   variational lower bound on mutual information I˜SE (Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π) (equation 8) (i.e., the reward
302   for proposing the skillset (ϕ, π) is is the diversity of the skillset). Similarly, the reward in the ϕ
303   bandit problem for a ϕ action is I˜SE (Z; Sn |s0 , ϕ, π = fλ (s0 , ϕ)), in which the skill-conditioned
304   policy π is the greedy output from the π bandit policy. Both bandit policies are optimized using an
305   actor-critic structure. That is, to determine the gradient for the bandit policy fµ , a critic Qρ (s0 , ϕ) is
306   trained to approximate I˜SE (Z; Sn )(s0 , ϕ, fλ (s0 , ϕ)) for ϕ values around the current greedy output
307   fη (s0 ). Similarly, a critic Qω (s0 , ϕ, π) is used to approximate I˜SE for sets of parameters π that are
308   near the current greedy vector fλ (s0 , ϕ). Figure 4 in the Appendix provides a visual overview of the
309   two bandit problems.
310   The current setup is not yet practical because trying to learn the critic Qω (s0 , ϕ, π) for the π actor
311   when the vector of parameters π may be thousands of dimensions is infeasible. Qω would need to
312   be able to discern the difference in mutual information when small changes are made to numerous
313   parameters in π. Instead, because the gradient with respect to the π bandit policy fλ only needs to
314   know how the I˜SE reward responds to small changes in each of the individual parameters in π, we
315   instead train |π| critics, Qω0 (s0 , ϕ, π0 ), . . . , Qω|π|−1 (s0 , ϕ, π|π|−1 ). Each of these critics only takes
316   a scalar, πi , as input, in which πi represents the i-th parameter of π (e.g., could be a noisy value
317   of the current greedy i-th parameter fλ (s0 , ϕ)[i]). The remaining parameters of π are assumed to
318
      take on their current greedy values from fλ (s0 , ϕ). Thus, each critic Qi (s0 , ϕ, πi ) only needs to
      approximate how the mutual information changes from small changes to a weight or bias parameter
319
      in π. All of these critics are updated in parallel. In Figure 5 in the Appendix we show how each of
320
      these |π| critics attach to the bandit policy fλ that outputs π.

322   Prior to updating the critics for each parameter of the skill-conditioned policy π, the variational

344   3.3   L IMITATIONS
345
      The major limitation of Skillset Empowerment is that it requires a model of the transition dynamics
346
      p(st+1 |st , at ) (i.e., access to simulator of the environment) in order to generate the large number
347   of (skill z, skill-terminating state sn ) tuples needed to train all of the parameter-specific critics.
348   However, subsequent work by Author (2024) (included as an anonymous supplementary file), which
349   builds on Skillset Empowerment, shows how large skillsets can be learned without a simulator.
350   Instead of maximizing the mutual information between skills and states, Author (2024) maximize
351   a different objective that has the same optimal skillset as I(Z; Sn ) but only requires learning a
352   latent-predictive model, which is significantly easier to learn than a simulator as it operates in a
353   lower-dimensional latent space.

      4.2   BASELINES
412
413   We compare our approach, Skillset Empowerment, to both an empowerment-based skill-learning
414   method and unsupervised GCRL. For the prior empowerment-based method, we selected Variational
415   Intrinsic Control (VIC) (Gregor et al., 2016). VIC, like other approaches to measuring empower-
416   ment including DIAYN (Eysenbach et al., 2018) and VALOR (Achiam et al., 2018), maximizes a
417   loose lower bound on the mutual information between skills and states because the variational pos-
418   terior is trained to match the posterior of the current greedy skillset and not the candidate skillset.
419   For the unsupervised GCRL comparison, our focus was solely on whether goal-conditioned skills
420
      can be learned in stochastic domains and not on exploration which is the primary focus of recent
      unsupervised GCRL algorithms. Thus, we assist the unsupervised GCRL algorithm and provide it
421
      with the distribution of reachable states to use as the distribution of goal states and thereby are just
422
      comparing our algorithm to supervised GCRL. We compare to a GCRL objective in which the vari-
423   ational posterior used is the tight diagonal gaussian centered at the skill-terminating state discussed
424   in section 2.2. For the higher dimensional QR code tasks, we implemented Reinforcement learn-
425   ing with Imagined Goals (RIG) (Nair et al., 2018). RIG performs GCRL in a latent space learned
426   separately by a VAE. See section F for details on how the baselines were implemented.

497   5   R ELATED W ORK
498
499   There have been many prior works that have tried to use empowerment to learn large skillsets.
500   Early empowerment methods showed how mutual information between actions and states could be
501   optimized in small settings with discrete state and/or action spaces (Klyubin et al., 2008; Salge
502   et al., 2013a; Jung et al., 2012). Several later works integrated variational inference techniques that
503   enable empowerment-based skill learning to be applied to larger continuous domains (Mohamed &
504   Rezende, 2015; Karl et al., 2017; Gregor et al., 2016; Eysenbach et al., 2018; Sharma et al., 2019;
505
      Li et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2020). However, these methods were limited in the size of skillsets
      they were able to learn as they only maximize a loose lower bound on mutual information, making
506
      it difficult to accurately measure the diversity of a skillset.
507
508   Complementary to our work are the methods that use empowerment for downstream applications,
509   such as using empowerment as a state utility function (Klyubin et al., 2008; Salge et al., 2013b; Jung
510   et al., 2012; Mohamed & Rezende, 2015; Karl et al., 2015; 2017), as a temporally extended action
511
      space for hierarchical RL (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2023), as an evolutionary signal to
      evolve sensors and actuators (Klyubin et al., 2005), as an objective for learning a state representation
512
      (Capdepuy, 2011; Bharadhwaj et al., 2022), as an intrinsic motivation reward (Oudeyer & Kaplan,
513
      2007; Bharadhwaj et al., 2022), and as a way to measure human empowerment (Du et al., 2020;
514   Myers et al., 2024).
515
516   Also related to our work are the methods that learn abstract skills in settings with random envi-
517
      ronment distractors (Bharadhwaj et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020;
      Rudolph et al., 2024; Zou & Suzuki, 2024). However, these approaches only learn a single skill with
518
      the help of a reward function or require supervision in the form of a hand-crafted goal space. To our
519
      knowledge, our approach is the first unsupervised skill learning method to successfully learn large
520   skillsets in stochastic settings.
521
522
523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   Agents need to be able to execute large skillsets in settings with significant randomness and empow-
526   erment should be able to help. We show a major reason why previous empowerment methods have
527   been unable to learn large skillsets is that they have been maximizing a loose lower bound on the
528
      mutual information between skills and states. To overcome this problem, we introduced the Skillset
      Empowerment algorithm, which maximizes a tighter bound on the mutual information between
529
      skills and states using a new actor-critic architecture. We show empirically that Skillset Empow-
530
      erment enables agents to learn large skillsets in a variety of settings, includes ones with stochastic
531   transition dynamics and high-dimensional state observations.

      Although the number of skill-conditioned policy parameters may be large, the |π| sets of variational
851
      posterior and π critic parameters can be trained efficiently using the parallelization capabilities of
852   modern deep learning frameworks (e.g., JAX) and multiple accelerators. For instance, the update
853   step for each set variational posterior parameters ψi requires (skill z, state sn ) tuples from skillsets
854   (ϕ, πi ). Assuming access to multiple accelerators, this update step can occur in parallel for all |π|
855   sets of variational posterior parameters using the pmap and vmap functions in JAX. For instance, if
856   the skill-conditioned policy π has 1000 dimensions and there are 4 GPUs available, each GPU can
857   process the updates to 250 sets of variational posterior parameters in parallel.
858   Table 2 provides some data on the parallel computation demands of our approach for each of our

860   the time (in seconds) required to complete one whole update step (i.e., one iteration of the Repeat
861   loop in Algorithm 1). Note that the update times shown for the four rooms tasks were when using a
862   single A100 40GB device, while the update times for the RGB QR tasks reflect 8 A100 80GB SXM
863   GPUs. When we used multiple GPUs, the update times were roughly 1/Num GPUs of the original
      time with a single GPU.

931   E    I MPLEMENTING H IERARCHICAL AGENTS WITH S KILLSET
932        E MPOWERMENT
933
934   Coding hierarchical agents that use the (ϕ, π) Skillset Empowerment skillsets as a temporally ex-
935   tended action space is simple. For the higher level policy π : S → Z that outputs a skill z from the
936   learned (ϕ, π) skillset given some state, attach a tanh activation function to this policy, which bounds
937
      the output to [−1, 1], and then multiply that output by ϕ, which will bound the skill action space to
      [−ϕ, ϕ] in every dimension, which has the same shape as d-dimensional cubic distribution that ϕ
938
      represents. (Note that in our implementation, ϕ is technically the log of the half length of each side
939
      of the d-dimensional cubic uniform distribution so the output of the tanh activation function should
940   be multiplied by eϕ . We have ϕ represent the log of the half length of each side so the ϕ actor fµ
941   can output negative numbers.) Then once a skill z has been sampled, the skill can be passed to
942   the Skillset Empowerment skill-conditioned policy π(a|s0 , z) which will then execute a closed loop
943   policy in the environment.

      For the GCRL comparison, in the low-dimensional stochastic four rooms domains, we compared
948
      against the variant of GCRL that is a lower bound to Empowerment, in which the variational poste-
949   rior consists of a tight diagonal gaussian centered at the skill-terminating state. The goal distribution
950   is set to the distribution of all reachable state (e.g., all possible agent (x, y) positions in the stochastic
951   four rooms navigation task). For the higher dimensional QR code tasks, we implemented Reinforce-
952   ment learning with Imagined Goals (RIG) Nair et al. (2018). RIG is an unsupervised GCRL method
953   that combines representation learning and GCRL. RIG uses a VAE to separately learn an encoder
954   that maps states to distributions over skills and a decoder that maps latent states to distributions over
955   observations. RIG then performs GCRL in the learned embedding space (i.e, the agent learns skills
956   that target specific latent states). Because the focus of this paper is not exploration, we make it eas-
957
      ier on the representation learning component of RIG and provide it with a large dataset of reachable
      observations (e.g., images of the agent and object in a large variety of positions in the pick-and-place
958
      QR code environment.) The goal distribution for the GCRL phase is the prior distribution p(z) from
959
      the VAE component.
