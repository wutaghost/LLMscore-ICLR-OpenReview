009                                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011               Recent advancements in generative AI have introduced novel prospects and prac-
012               tical implementations. Especially diffusion models show their strength in gener-
013               ating diverse and, at the same time, realistic features, positioning them well for
014               generating counterfactual explanations for computer vision models. Answering
015               “what if” questions of what needs to change to make an image classifier change
016               its prediction, counterfactual explanations align well with human understanding
017               and consequently help in making model behavior more comprehensible. Current
018
                  methods succeed in generating authentic counterfactuals, but lack transparency as
                  feature changes are not directly perceivable. To address this limitation, we intro-
019
                  duce Concept-guided Latent Diffusion Counterfactual Explanations (CoLa-DCE).
020
                  CoLa-DCE generates concept-guided counterfactuals for any classifier with a high
021               degree of control regarding concept selection and spatial conditioning. The coun-
022               terfactuals comprise an increased granularity through minimal feature changes.
023               The reference feature visualization ensures better comprehensibility, while the
024               feature localization provides increased transparency of “where” changed “what”.
025               We demonstrate the advantages of our approach in minimality and comprehen-
026               sibility extensively across multiple datasets, classification models, and diffusion
027               models and provide insights into how our CoLa-DCE explanations help compre-
028               hend model errors like misclassification cases.

051   1     I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Recent advancements in generative models have sparked new interest in counterfactual explanations
      for computer vision tasks Augustin et al. (2022); Jeanneret et al. (2023a); Farid et al. (2023). By


                                                                             1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      answering what would need to change to induce a different outcome, counterfactual explanations
055   are motivated by research in psychology and the social sciences highlighting the alignment of coun-
056   terfactuals to human reasoning Lewis (1973); Byrne (2007). While current development efforts in
057   eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (xAI) often focus on technical feasibility rather than on the align-
058   ment with human understanding of a Deep Neural Network (DNN) model, counterfactuals provide
059   an opportunity for the user to contemplate alternative model outputs Miller et al. (2017). In the
060   image domain, a human inspector can directly compare an original image with its counterfactual to
061   derive which differences induce a prediction change in the model under test. Key requirements for
062   the counterfactual to be deemed a plausible alternative are the consistency with the user’s beliefs,
063
      as being realistic, and a minimal effort for changing towards the counterfactual Byrne (2007). The
      minimality constraint expresses a more likely transition due to a smaller image alteration, while
064
      additionally, the decision boundary between both classes can be better estimated.
065
066   Specifically for image manipulations, diffusion models have demonstrated their strengths in gen-
067   erating realistic high-resolution images with diverse features within the data distribution Ho et al.
068   (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Rombach et al. (2022); Ho & Salimans (2022). Thus, they serve
069
      as an ideal tool for generating counterfactual images Augustin et al. (2022); Farid et al. (2023);
      Jeanneret et al. (2023a). While previous works on diffusion-based image counterfactuals find opti-
070
      mizations regarding all features in an image or a local area inside an image, it is often unclear which
071
      features precisely change toward the counterfactual and how they relate to the model prediction.
072   Especially with many slight feature changes in an image, tracking the changes and comprehending
073   the decision boundary based on these features becomes unfeasible. Considering the example of the
074   “hen” in Figure 1, every part of the animal, e.g., head, feathers, and color, as well as background
075   features like the flooring, could yield significant changes towards the counterfactual class without
076   being recognizable to the user.
077
      In comparison, humans tend to perceive the minimal differences in counterfactuals rather in semantic
078   than in pixel space and prefer representative differences Delaney et al. (2023). This motivates the
079   yet missing strategy of defining minimality semantically in the number of semantic features changed
080   and further encourages a concept-based approach enforcing understandable semantic alterations.
081
      With our Concept-guided Latent Diffusion Counterfactual Explanations (CoLa-DCE), we solve both
082
      problems: We guide the counterfactual generation with a restricted number of semantic concepts,
083
      further enabling a high level of control by concept selection. We additionally include feature visu-
084   alization capabilities, allowing for direct comprehensibility of features that represent the difference
085   between the original and the counterfactual class. Hereby, CoLa-DCE provides semantic as well
086   as spatial guidance and visualization, simultaneously enabling control and better transparency. Our
087   contributions are:
088
089         1. We introduce CoLa-DCE for the diffusion-based generation of counterfactuals using a se-
090            mantic concept-guidance. We show how local counterfactual targets and concept-guided
091
               feature changes derived from the classifier’s perception increase the quality of the counter-
               factuals.
092
093         2. We extend our concept guidance with spatial conditioning and reveal the semantic and
094            localized image changes with transferring methods for concept visualization and concept
095            localization maps, resulting in more transparent and more comprehensible counterfactuals
096
               highlighting the image changes.
097         3. We show how our CoLa-DCE samples help in model debugging by making cases of mis-
098            classification more understandable. The semantic concept visualization provides strategies
099            for feature-based model and/or dataset adaptations.
100
      The source code will be published at github.com/anonimous-url/cola-dce.

140   3     R ELATED W ORK
141
142   3.1   C OUNTERFACTUAL I MAGE G ENERATION
143
144   Generating counterfactuals in the image domain requires the capability to modify existing or gen-
145   erate new features in an image. While approaches like Filandrianos et al. (2022) compare images
146   by the set of assigned attributes and define the counterfactual to be the near miss from the used
147
      reference dataset, most approaches directly modify the base image itself. Counterfactual Visual
      Explanations (CVE) Goyal et al. (2019) replaces feature regions in an image with matching image
148
      patches from a distractor image of the counterfactual class. Other works directly optimize an in-
149
      put image by minimizing a loss, shifting the classification towards the counterfactual class while
150   keeping the image changes minimal Santurkar et al. (2019); Augustin et al. (2020). SVCE Boreiko
151   et al. (2022) yields further improvements to the optimization by combining the L1- and L2-norm to
152   acquire a balance between non-sparse and too-sparse feature changes. However, directly optimizing
153   the image requires a robust classification model. Another group of methods is based on autoencoder
154   architectures to control the optimization in a disentangled latent space Rodrı́guez et al. (2021) or
155   to apply modifications in a simplified interpretable space Zemni et al. (2023). Yet, with diffusion
156   models, better possibilities for high-quality feature generation exist.
157   DiME Jeanneret et al. (2023a) introduces diffusion models for generating counterfactuals, where
158   the classification model guides the diffusion process. However, DiME is limited to robust classifiers
159   explicitly trained on noisy images. ACE Jeanneret et al. (2023b) is a two-step process consisting of
160   computing pre-explanations and refining them. A localization mask for the most probable feature
161   change is computed before repainting the image by combining the generated counterfactual within
      the mask with the original image outside.


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Diffusion Visual Counterfactual Explanations (DVCE) Augustin et al. (2022) relaxes the constraint
163   for the classifier to be robust by including an additional adversarially robust classifier. Aligning the
164   gradients of both models with a cone projection robustifies the diffusion guidance. However, gen-
165   erated features might be induced by the robust classifier rather than the original classifier, decreas-
166   ing the validity in explaining the original classifier. Latent Diffusion Counterfactual Explanations
167   (LDCE) Farid et al. (2023) overcomes the requirement of having a robust classifier by constructing
168   a consensus mechanism for aligning the gradient of the external classifier with the gradient of the
169   implicit classifier of the diffusion model directly. However, feature changes are hard to track due to
170   the optimization on all features.
171   Although the previous works are able to generate realistic counterfactual images, the resulting coun-
172   terfactuals lack transparency regarding which features have been changed and how the change is
173   reflected in the parameters of the target model. To our knowledge, the image domain has not consid-
174   ered a concept-based approach that guides feature changes on a semantic concept level and enforces
175   minimality by restricting the number of feature changes. Concept-based counterfactuals yield the
176   opportunity to improve transparency and comprehensibility for the user while being semantically
177
      more similar to the original image.
178
179   3.2   L OCAL C ONCEPT ATTRIBUTION
180
      Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) Bach et al. (2015) describes a local attribution method
181
      that backpropagates a modified gradient to assign pixel-wise importance scores for an input based
182   on a selected target class. Concept-wise Relevance Propagation (CRP) Achtibat et al. (2023) extends
183   LRP to the concept space by defining the encoding of every single neuron or channel in the latent
184   space as a concept. During the attribution backward pass, a concept mask is applied, which filters
185   the attribution for a single channel so that only the attribution for the selected channel is retained.
186   When inspecting the channel-constrained explanations for multiple samples, denoted as Relevance
187   Maximization Achtibat et al. (2023), a semantic meaning describing a concept can be assigned to
188   the channel. Our approach utilizes the generalization of the latent space masking for a gradient
189   manipulation and applies Relevance Maximization to visualize the determining concepts.

244   4.2   C ONCEPT S ELECTION
245
      For a selected counterfactual target class y, the gradient ∇x p(x|y) of a sample x can be extracted
246
      in each network layer. For the selected layer l, the intermediate gradient is summed over the spatial
247
      dimensions to obtain a one-dimensional representation over the channels, which are expected to
248   encode a particular concept each Achtibat et al. (2023). Taking the absolute value of the summed
249   gradients, the top-k concepts with k ∈ N (1, K), and K denoting the overall number of channels,
250   are selected, which are per gradient most likely to induce a change towards the counterfactual class.
251   The concepts can be visualized using a feature visualization method like CRP Achtibat et al. (2023).
252
253   4.3   C ONCEPT C ONDITIONING
254

258   leads to changing concepts in each step.

      4.4   S PATIAL C ONDITIONING
271
272   While the introduced concept conditioning focuses on semantic features that need to change, the
273   spatial dimensions in the feature layer of choice state where the selected features are most likely
274   to change. We assume that each feature should be only changed at a single location or that the
275   gradient towards these features is approximately equal in equivalent locations. Therefore, we add
276   binary masking to the spatial dimensions similar to Equation 7 based on the gradient for the selected
277   features, which sets gradients below a threshold η to zero. The binary mask can additionally be
278
      upscaled to the input scale like in Net2Vec Fong & Vedaldi (2018), yielding additional information
      about where a specific concept is expected to change towards the counterfactual. The spatial condi-
279
      tioning minimizes the feature change by restricting it locally while contributing to comprehensibility
280
      by providing feature localization.

285
      We test our approach on the ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) validation dataset using multiple pre-
      trained models provided by Torchvision: a VGG16 Simonyan & Zisserman (2015) with and without
286
      batch normalization, a ResNet18 He et al. (2016), and a ViT model Dosovitskiy et al. (2021). For
287
      deriving appropriate targets, 90% of the validation data is used as a reference dataset, while coun-
288   terfactuals for the evaluation are generated on the remaining 1000 samples, including all ImageNet
289   classes. We inherit the parametrization parameters from LDCE Farid et al. (2023). Showing the
290   applicability to different datasets, additional counterfactuals for Oxford Pets Parkhi et al. (2012) and
291   Flowers Nilsback & Zisserman (2008) can be found in Appendix A.3.
292
      As there exists no ground truth for counterfactual examples, a rough estimate regarding the quality
293
      can only be assessed via quantifying desired properties as the minimality and the accuracy. We align
294   our evaluation with Farid et al. (2023) and compute the FID score Heusel et al. (2017) as well as the
295   L1 and L2 norm between the original and counterfactual image to measure their semantic and pixel-
296   based distance, denoting the minimality. The flip ratio (FR) determines the accuracy by measuring
297   how often the classifier predicts the counterfactual class for the generated sample.
298
      As an additional optimization measure, we suspend the concept conditioning for the last 50 gener-
299
      ation steps of the diffusion process. While coarse semantic features are expected to be generated
300   within the first steps of the diffusion process, the last steps incorporate an image refinement, e.g., by
301   completing and connecting edges. When suspending the conditioning towards the end of the gener-
302   ation, visible semantic changes are not perceivable, but the image is classified more accurately. This
303   can also be seen in a consistent FID score and an improved flip ratio.
304
305   5.1   S ELECTING A LOCAL TARGET RESULTS IN IMPROVED COUNTERFACTUALS
306
307   While LDCE Farid et al. (2023) uses WordNet Miller (1995) to derive counterfactual targets based
308   on the semantic similarity between labels, we suggest using the classifier’s perception of the local
309
      input. Selecting a target layer, the classifier-internal representation of a data point can be extracted
      via the activation or the attribution using a local xAI method. Based on the encodings of a reference
310
      dataset, the sample with minimal distance and differing class prediction to the encoded target sample
311
      is extracted. It’s prediction is chosen as counterfactual target. The approach is related to the concept
312   of near misses Rabold et al. (2022).
313

315
      mance metrics. Choosing a local (sample-based) counterfactual target on a near-miss basis leads to

316
      more superficial change between the original and target class. However, retrieving the target via the
317

      that the concept-based gradient can be set to zero in all other regions. Figure 5 shows the difference

      in the generated counterfactuals for the spatial conditioning and basic CoLa-DCE compared to the

      5.5       VALIDITY: D O THE CONCEPTS ALIGN WITH THE IMAGE MODIFICATIONS TOWARDS THE
462
                COUNTERFACTUAL ?
463
464   Testing the validity of our approach considering the selected concepts, we review whether the change
465   from the original to the counterfactual image targets the selected concepts. The difference in the
466   intermediate attributions of both original and counterfactual images signifies the difference in the
467   importance of the concepts for the respective predictions. We assume the channels with the highest
468   difference to align with the k selected concepts. For estimating the relative alignment, we compute
469   the ratio of the difference |attrcounterf actual − attroriginal | for the selected concepts to the top-
470   k values. The same ratio with k randomly selected concepts is computed for comparison. The
471   results in Figure 7 clearly validate the concept-based approach, as the meaningful change towards
472
      the counterfactual can evidently be assigned to the selected concepts for both the VGG16bn and the
      ResNet18. Due to the redundancy of similar feature encodings in computer vision models, a change
473
      in one feature is expected to influence multiple channels in the latent space. Thus, it is reasonable
474
      that the selected features do not perfectly align with the top-k concepts with the highest attribution
475   difference.

480   As ground truth information of an optimal counterfactual image does not exist, only heuristics con-
481   taining desired properties can be optimized. However, the right balance between minimally deviat-
482   ing the image while maximizing the flip ratio depends on a rough estimate of the user’s preferences.
483   Like in LDCE, the influence of the external gradient and the reconstruction accuracy need to be fine-
484   tuned. Including the influence of the diffusion model, we acknowledge that the diffusion’s ability to
485   accurately reconstruct an image and generate similar concept information as the external classifier
      highly influences the counterfactual quality.

504   7   C ONCLUSION
505
506   Our CoLa-DCE method generating concept-guided counterfactuals successfully tackles the lack of
507   transparency and fine-grained control in current diffusion-based counterfactual generation methods.
508
      Starting from an improved target selection incorporating the models’ perception, we show how our
      concept-based approach yields semantically smaller image changes qualitatively and quantitatively,
509
      enforcing the minimality requirement. With the additional level of control by selecting concepts
510
      and adding spatial constraints per concept, the counterfactual generation is more focused on small,
511   localized feature perturbations in the image. At the same time, the image alterations are more locally
512   confined and comprehensible due to the concept grounding. From our CoLa-DCE explanations, it
513   is directly deducible which feature changes at which location cause the prediction change of the
514   classifier, strongly improving the transparency and understandability to a human user. With the high
515   degree of control in generating images with CoLa-DCE, we are confident to induce further work
516   using fine-grained concept guidance for image alteration tasks.
