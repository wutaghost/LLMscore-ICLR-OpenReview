                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Efficiency of neural network inference is undeniably important in a time where
010
               commercial use of AI models increases daily. Node pruning is the art of removing
011            computational units such as neurons, filters, attention heads, or even entire layers
012            to significantly reduce inference time while retaining network performance. In
013            this work, we propose the projection of unit activations to an orthogonal subspace
014            in which there is no redundant activity and within which we may prune nodes
015            while simultaneously recovering the impact of lost units via linear least squares.
016            We identify that, for effective node pruning, this subspace must be constructed
017            using a triangular transformation matrix, a transformation which is equivalent to
018            and unnormalized Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization. We furthermore show that
019
               the order in which units are orthogonalized can be optimised to maximally reduce
               node activations in our subspace and thereby form a more optimal ranking of
020
               nodes. Finally, we leverage these orthogonal subspaces to automatically determine
021
               layer-wise pruning ratios based upon the relative scale of node activations in our
022            subspace, equivalent to cumulative variance. Our proposed method reaches state
023            of the art when pruning ImageNet trained VGG-16 and rivals more complex state
024            of the art methods when pruning ResNet-50 networks across a range of pruning
025            ratios.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   With significant progress in the development of neural networks by the research community, com-
031
      mercial interest has recently taken off. Evermore capable models spark private and public interest,
      excitement, and engagement. However, the computational resources required to train and run these
032
      models are immense and neural networks have long exceeded the computational capacity of general
033
      purpose hardware.
034
035   A variety of approaches have been developed to reduce the computational footprint of models without
036   changing their structure. These range from low-level hardware optimizations (Choquette et al., 2021;
037
      Jouppi et al., 2018) to high-level software developments (Paszke et al., 2019; Abadi et al., 2015;
      Bradbury et al., 2018). Additionally, the representations of models in software have been made more
038
      compact with quantization methods (Krishnamoorthi, 2018; Gholami et al., 2022).
039
040   More promising are, however, methods which modify and compress neural network models to reduce
041   computational cost while maintaining accuracy. Network compression is possible due to the fact that
042   deep neural networks are found to be significantly over-parameterized in practice, with sometimes
043
      orders of magnitude more parameters than should be necessary for computations (Frankle & Carbin,
      2018). In this work, we focus on the sub-field of network pruning and develop a new state-of-the-art
044
      method within this domain.
045
046
      P RUNING NEURAL NETWORKS
047
048   The goal of neural network pruning is to reduce the computational execution (inference) time of a
049   model while maintaining its performance. Unstructured approaches which prune the weights of a
050   model result in arbitrarily sparse weight matrices whose multiplication cannot easily be accelerated at
051   compute time – i.e. without translation to real-world inference efficiency. It is therefore desirable to
052   prune whole nodes, convolutional filters, transformer heads, or other structured groups of parameters.
053   Herein, we refer to any of these sub-parts of networks as network ‘units’. Thus, the question is posed:
      how should one choose which units of a network to prune first? Two different approaches emerged


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to address this question. First, pruning of pre-trained networks and, second, pruning iteratively
055   while training. While we limit our investigation to the former class that assumes starting out with a
056   well-performing model, the latter class holds great potential for also reducing the cost of training
057   next to the cost of inference.
058
      Importance scores When choosing which units of a network to prune, there must first be an attribution
059
      of the relative importance of each network unit. The score computed to measure this is known as
060
      the importance score. Methods which relate the importance score to the magnitude of weights are
061   prevalent, and these approaches try to minimize the impact of pruning on the network’s computation.
062   Li et al. (2016) used the total absolute sum (or squared sum) of incident weights to a convolutional
063   filter as an importance score for that filter. Methods of greater complexity are ‘data-driven’, making
064   use of training data for forward (and in some cases backward) passes to measure importance scores.
065   Molchanov et al. (2016; 2019) found that the square summed weight-gradient multiplication of
066   weights incident to a node can be used as a theoretically justified importance score under a linear
067   approximation of a network via a 1st -order Taylor-expansion (and similarly for the 2nd -order case).
068   Theis et al. (2018) extend this notion to using the Fisher information instead, while Yu et al. (2018)
069
      proposed a scaling of the weight-magnitude with downstream importance scores to estimate the
      importance of a unit that minimizes the change in loss induced by pruning.
070
071   The above methods aim to assign importance scores such that the smallest importance is assigned to
072   network units which are thought to minimally disturb the network’s computation. Other approaches
073   deviate from this assumption. For example, Liu et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2022) look at feature
074   maps to determine filters which have greatest task-relevant information. Alternatively, a number
075
      of methods also make use of correlation measures of feature maps or filter/node outputs within or
      between layers, with greater correlations equated to lower importance scores due to the redundancy
076
      of these representations (Zhang et al., 2022; Ayinde et al., 2019; Cuadros et al., 2020; Mariet & Sra,
077
      2015; Kim et al., 2020; Goldberg et al., 2022) or a variation thereof (He et al., 2019).
078
079   One-step reconstruction Some methods go beyond the step of simply removing nodes when pruning
080   and additionally carry out a form of ‘one-step reconstruction’. This reconstruction modifies the
081
      parameters of a pruned network such that its nodes approximately output the same values which they
      did prior to pruning. This is unlike retraining or finetuning as it is a single step of modification of
082
      the pruned parameters for reconstruction of the pre-pruned outputs. Mariet & Sra (2015) identified
083
      redundant nodes by using determinantal point processes, pruned these nodes, and recovered their
084   impact on a network by linear least squares (LLS) approximation between the original and pruned
085   pre-activations. Similarly, He et al. (2017) used LLS to approximate pruned nodes, while selecting
086   nodes based on LASSO regression.
087
      In contemporary work, Luo et al. (2017) estimated a single scalar value per node to best reconstruct
088
      the lost activity, a method with lower expressiveness than the aforementioned methods. Kim et al.
089
      (2020); Goldberg et al. (2022) chose yet a different, data-free, approach to reconstructing unit activity
090   by moving weight parameters between layers, however this was only applicable to networks with
091   rectified linear unit (ReLU) non-linearities. Chin et al. (2018) went further still, into a non-linear
092   least squares solution attempt using evolutionary algorithms. Similar methods for reconstructing unit
093   activity for large language models have recently been presented (Li et al., 2024; Frantar & Alistarh,
094   2023).
095
      Global importance Despite the importance of local scoring (i.e. scoring of units within a layer),
096   there needs to be a notion of unit importance across layers, a so called ‘global importance’. While
097   for some approaches such global ranking is a natural consequence of the local importance estima-
098   tion (Molchanov et al., 2016; 2019; Yu et al., 2018), several methods only provided local importances
099   and rely on expert knowledge, manual exploration(Wang et al., 2021), or simple assumptions such
100   as the equivalence of pruning at any layers Li et al. (2016). The most advanced methods rely on
101   measurement of some form of network ‘sensitivity’ to achieve peak performance(You et al., 2019).
102   This work We propose and demonstrate the efficacy of three novel ideas for improved node pruning.
103   First, a one-step reconstruction method which relies upon the construction of a subspace in which
104   unit activations are factorized and made orthogonal. In this subspace, nodes can be pruned with
105   immediate reconstruction of layer outputs by LLS. Second, a novel importance scoring method based
106   only upon non-redundant unit activities. Third, the measurement of global unit importance based

132   Consider a typical deep neural network (DNN) architecture, in which the outputs at each layer,
133   l ∈ {1, . . . , L}, are defined as Xl = fl (Yl ) = fl (Wl−1 Xl−1 ), where Xl ∈ Rnl ×s is a tensor of

135   upon a matrix multiplication of the previous layer outputs, from weights Wl ∈ Rnl+1 ×nl , and by
136   an element-wise transfer function fl (·). We consider these fully-connected deep neural networks to
137   introduce our approach, however, we shall also treat convolution in the results that follow.
138   In a pruning pipeline, assuming that importance scores were already available, the next step would be
139   to prune m input units and their associated weight vectors, starting with the unit of lowest importance
140   score. Here we instead propose an intermediate step. We propose that one might remove as much
141   redundant activity from units in a given layer to ensure that pruning has the least possible impact on
142   network dynamics.

      2.2   I MPORTANCE SCORING : R EORDERING UNITS PRIOR TO FACTORIZATION
220
221   As mentioned previously, our method of factorising nodes in the GS subspace holds promise for
222   ensuring that the units removed (in the subspace) have minimal activations and that all redundant
223   information is automatically reconstructed. However, we so far have not addressed the question of
224   how one should choose the order in which units are orthogonalized. In fact, we so far considered a
225   GS subspace transformation based upon the default ordering of units in a layer, an ordering which
226   could be much improved.
227   Generally, the choice of unit ordering is free for a practitioner since it simply changes the order
228   of units from which we compute the GS subspace (consider that one could permute the matrix
229   Ml so long as you also unpermute via matrix M−1    l ). See Appendix B for the pseudo-code of this
230   permutation for pruning individual layers in a network. Some orderings are, however, evidently better
231   than others. Importance scores, as computed by alternative existing work, are good first candidates for
232   a reordering process, allowing the combination of our subspace method with any existing importance
233   scoring method. However, as noted before, these methods include recoverable unit activity in their
234
      estimations. In the following, we propose a method to solve this problem at any individual layer.
235   First, remembering that our subspace construction method aims to orthogonalize units in an ordered
236   fashion, one can ask: which units are best orthogonalized by all other units in a layer? To calculate
237   this, we can attempt to measure how much of each unit’s activity variance can be reduced based upon
238   a linear readout from all other units in a layer.
239   Assuming that one wishes to find a dense matrix, Rl , which computes the amount of redundant
240   activity that each unit has relative to each other unit, we are looking to measure X̂l = Xl − Rl Xl
241   where the desired outcome is that X̂l X̂⊤ l is some diagonal matrix of variances, and the diagonal of
242   the matrix Rl is zeros (i.e. there is no boosting up of a unit’s variance).

268   In the previous section, we described the measurement of an importance score based upon the
269   remaining norm of a unit’s activity after it has been orthogonalized by all other units within a layer.
      However, measuring a local layer-wise importance score is only sufficient for determining how much

      one might prune a single layer. When pruning a whole network, one must then make a determination
271   of how much to prune individual layers.

273   To address this, we build on the fact that our pruning method yields the activity variances of the
274
      latent variables (subspace units) without any extra computation (the diagonal matrix Dl from LDL-

275
      cumulative variance of a unit and all succeeding units, normalized by the total variance of the layer.
276

      We demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed method by application to VGG-11, 16, and 19 as well
288   as ResNet-50 architectures. We use the networks from PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) pre-trained on
289   the ILSVRC (ImageNet) dataset. The dataset contains 1,281,167 labeled training images and 50,000
290   labeled validation images. They are split into 1000 object categories that the models try to predict.
291   When measuring the cross-correlation matrix of every layer’s activations (inputs), we make use of the
292   full training set images transformed via the test-transforms detailed in Appendix D.
293
      To evaluate model performance, we show results in two cases. The first case considers VGG networks
294   before any retraining. We compare our method against reimplementations of the method of Li
295   et al. (2016), which uses the sum of absolute weights (SAW), as well as the unstructured absolute
296   weight magnitude pruning method (Han et al., 2015). Further, we reimplement and compare against
297   ThiNet (Luo et al., 2017) and PFA-EN (Cuadros et al., 2020). Due to limited computational resources
298   and the cost of performing pruning across these baselines, we make re-implemented comparisons
299   of these baseline method on VGG networks only. The SAW, AW, and ThiNet methods provide
300   no guidance on the global ranking of units, instead assuming that a practitioner might uniformly
301   prune all layers by the same amount. Therefore we employ a uniform pruning ratio across layers
302
      when implementing these methods. This means that all layers are pruned by the same ratio of units,
      referred to as a uniform pruning (uni) in all relevant figures. In contrast, PFA-EN performs PCA
303
      to decide a global ranking of units on top of their local importance structure. Notably we apply
304
      PFA-EN’s PCA measure at input nodes, rather than output activations, finding that this produces best
305   performance. We re-implemented all baselines due to unavailable code or outdated packages and
306   have code available for reproduction of all experiments at <SEE ATTACHED ZIP>.

494   method first prunes and then re-trains with no further pruning during retraining.
495   Table 2 shows the results of applying our proposed pruning method to the ILSVRC (ImageNet) trained
496   ResNet-50 architecture. As can be seen, our method is highly competitive with existing methods
497   in the range of 2-3× FLOP reduction, consistently in second place and only being outperformed
498   by TPP (Wang & Fu, 2023). We see the performances achieved as a significant accomplishment
499   considering that almost all competitive methods not only prune networks but also carry out some
500   pruning during retraining in order to more carefully remove nodes (including TPP). Furthermore,
501   most alternative methods have fixed layer-wise pruning ratios, determined heuristically or otherwise,
502
      unlike ours which has a single global variance-based cutoff which automatically determines unique
      layer-wise pruning ratios.

505   5   D ISCUSSION
506
507
      In this work, we introduced a novel, often state of the art, method for structured pruning of pre-trained
508
      deep networks with one-step reconstruction and focus upon the non-redundant variance of each unit’s
509
      activation. Research into such approaches holds promise for the potential future of pruning without
510   considering redundant activity within a neural network. However, a number of additional areas of
511   exploration remain open, including the conditions in which retraining can be ignored, how to best
512   treat non-redundant unit activations, as well as how to apply such methods during training.
513
      On the first note, we find our proposed method to be somewhat successful when pruning networks
514
      without retraining. However, to reach competitive performance, retraining is nonetheless essential
515
      when significantly pruning networks. This finding is in contrast with recent work which attempted a
516   related reconstruction method for LLMs (Li et al., 2024), where it was claimed that retraining could
517   be unnecessary. Future work should consider determining how significantly our findings apply to
518   such alternative model types.
519
      Second, we make use of unit activities within our subspace to compute layer-wise pruning ratios
520
      based upon a global variance-based cutoff. However, this does not at all consider the downstream
521
      sensitivity of a network to these activations, implicitly assuming that all unit variances are equally
522   useful. Combining our measure with unit-wise sensitivity measures has the potential to further boost
523   performance. Furthermore, alternative importance measures which focus on non-redundant neural
524   contributions (within our proposed subspace) may hold significant potential, by avoiding allocation
525   of importance to redundant components unit activities.
526
      Finally, we restricted ourselves in this work to examining the pruning of pre-trained networks. A
527
      promising extension of this work would be to additionally prune networks during training and
528   retraining. The most competitive methods which we could identify all make use of such a during
529   (re)training adjustment of pruned units. Our method could also benefit from such a treatment by
530   maintenance of an orthogonalizaed subspace during training and a more gradual pruning arrangement.
531   This could force a network to encode information in a ranked fashion during training and potentially
532   enable far greater final performance.
533
      In conclusion, we see the subspace node pruning method described herein as a new perspective on
534   the problem of pruning. This method can be combined with any existing method for node selection
535   and importance scoring and has the potential to significantly improve existing node pruning methods.
536   However, there also remain some wide open areas to be explored, including the application to
537   alternative models and during training of models from scratch. Beyond the models investigated
538   here, it also remains to be seen how and whether pruning approaches can truly impact the most
539   energy-expensive of models, such as generative pre-trained transformer models which are currently
      in vogue.

      C    R ECONSTRUCTION - AWARE IMPORTANCE SCORING
724
725
      In the main text, we have discussed a reordering strategy based on the diagonal elements of the un-
726
      normalized ZCA matrix that describe the latent variances in an orthogonal subspace. The orthogonal
727   subspace ensures that we only estimate importance from information that we cannot recover via LLS
728   and our reconstruction method. Here, we show that instead of a ranking based on the variances, we
729   can use a wider range of importance scoring methods while disregarding recoverable information. In
730   particular, we demonstrate how the simple summed absolute weights (SAW) importance measure
731   possibly benefits from this idea.
732
      First, we need to note that we cannot reconstruct any activity from unit inputs that are already
733
      orthogonal. If all inputs were orthogonal, Xl = X̂l and subsequently Ml = Il . Therefore, if we
734   assumed all inputs to be orthogonal, we could prune with minimal impact on the subsequent layer

824   F    R ES N ET PRUNING
825
826   To prune networks with skip connections, we adapt the Dependency Graph (DepGraph) framework
827   (Fang et al., 2023). DepGraph groups layers so that when a node is pruned in one layer, the
828   corresponding nodes in related layers are pruned as well. In ResNets, information flows through
829
      two parallel branches: the residual branch and the main branch. These branches merge by summing
      their outputs elementwise, meaning both pathways must have the same output dimensionality to fully
830
      take advantage of pruning a node. DepGraph ensures that when a node is pruned, the corresponding
831
      input and output connections are also removed in computationally adjacent layers. Consequently,
832   it is not clear which layer of a group to use for importance scoring. In this work, we prune nodes
833   based upon the input activity. Naturally, this extends to scoring group-wise input activities for our
834   unnormalized-ZCA importance and the global variance-based pruning cutoffs.
