009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Describing a scene in terms of primitives – geometrically simple shapes that offer
012            a parsimonious but accurate abstraction of structure – is an established and difficult
013            fitting problem. Different scenes require different numbers of primitives, and these
014            primitives interact strongly; however, any proposed solution can be evaluated at
015            inference time. The state of the art method involves a learned regression procedure
016            to predict a start point consisting of a fixed number of primitives, followed by a
017            descent method to refine the geometry and remove redundant primitives. Methods
018            are evaluated by accuracy in depth and normal prediction and in scene segmentation.
019            This paper shows that very significant improvements in accuracy can be obtained
020
               by (a) incorporating a small number of negative primitives and (b) ensembling
               over a number of different regression procedures. Ensembling is by refining each
021
               predicted start point, then choosing the best by fitting loss. Extensive experiments
022
               on the standard NYUv2 dataset confirm that negative primitives are useful, and
023            that our refine-then-choose strategy outperforms choose-then-refine, confirming
024            that the fitting problem is very difficult. Our ensembling with boolean primitives
025            approach strongly outperforms existing methods; additionally we present several
026            improvements to the underlying primitive generation process enabling us to obtain
027            better decompositions with fewer primitives. Code will be released upon acceptance
028            of the paper.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Geometric representations of scenes and objects as primitives – simple geometries that expose
034
      structure while suppressing detail – should allow simpler, more general reasoning. It is easier to
035
      plan moving a cuboid through stylized free space than moving a specific chair through a particular
036   living room. As another example, an effective primitive representation should simplify selecting and
037   manipulating objects in scenes as in image-based scene editing (Bhat et al., 2023; Vavilala et al.,
038   2023). But obtaining primitive representations that abstract usefully and accurately has been hard
039   (review Sec. 2).
040
      There are two main types of method. A descent method chooses primitives for a given geometry
041
      by minimizing a cost function. Important obstacles include: different geometries require different
042   numbers of primitives; the choice of primitive appears to be important in ways that are opaque; the
043   fitting problem has large numbers of local minima; and finding a good start point is difficult. In
044   particular, incremental fitting procedures are often defeated by interactions between primitives. A
045   regression method uses a learned predictor to map geometry to primitives and their parameters.
046   These methods can pool examples to avoid local minima, but may not get the best prediction for a
047   given input.
048   The SOTA method (Vavilala & Forsyth, 2023) for parsing indoor scenes uses a regression method
049   to predict a start point consisting of a fixed set of primitives. An important feature of this class of
050   problem is that, at run time, one can evaluate a predicted solution efficiently and accurately. The start
051   point is polished using a descent method on a fitting loss, comparing the prediction with depth and
052   segmentation maps from a suitable pretrained network, with backward selection to remove redundant
053   primitives. Finally, evaluation is by comparing the primitive geometry to reference depth, normal and
      segmentation.

078   This paper shows two procedures that yield significant (over 40% relative error) improvements in
079   accuracy. First, we allow a small number of negative primitives in the sense of constructive solid
080   geometry (CSG). Second, we show that an appropriately constructed ensembling method produces
081   very strong improvements in accuracy.
082
      For negative primitives, the predicted geometry is the set difference between the union of positive
083
      primitives and the union of negative primitives. As our ablation experiments show, this significantly
084   expands the geometries we can encode and significantly complicates the fitting problem. On their
085   own, negative primitives produce small improvements in accuracy. With ensembling we obtain
086   significant improvements in accuracy. We ensemble by using multiple predictors, each trained
087   to predict a start point with a different number of primitives; some predictors use only positive
088   primitives, others use both positive and negative primitives. Each predicted start point is then polished
089   by minimizing a fitting loss, and the best resulting set of primitives by fitting loss is reported. This
090   polish-then-choose strategy yields very strong improvements in accuracy. Notably, for some scenes
091   only positive primitives are used, whereas for others both positive and negative primitives are used.
092   Our contributions are:
093
094                      1. We believe our method is the only one that can fit CSG with a set differencing operator to
095                         indoor scenes.
096                      2. Our novel ensembling method results in large improvements in accuracy and allows the user
097                         to control the level of abstraction. We are unaware of another method using ensembling to
098                         improve primitive generation.
099                      3. Our primitive decomposition method for indoor scenes is an effective procedure that sub-
100                         stantially outperforms SOTA on established metrics on the benchmark NYUv2 dataset.

105   Primitives date to the origins of computer vision. Roberts worked with blocks (Roberts, 1963);
106   Binford with generalized cylinders (Binford, 1971); Biederman with geons (Biederman, 1987).
107   Ideally, complex objects might be handled with simple primitives (Chen et al., 2019) where each
      primitive is a semantic part (Biederman, 1987; Binford, 1971; van den Hengel et al., 2015). Primitives

108
      can be recovered from image data (Nevatia & Binford, 1977; Shafer & Kanade, 1983), and allow
109   simplified geometric reasoning (Ponce & Hebert, 1982).
110
111   For individual objects, neural methods could predict the right set of primitives by predicting solutions
112
      for test data that are “like” those that worked for training data. Tulsiani et al. parse 3D shapes into
      cuboids, trained without ground truth segmentations (Tulsiani et al., 2017). Zou et al. parse with
113
      a recurrent architecture (Zou et al., 2018). Liu et al. produce detailed reconstructions of objects in
114
      indoor scenes, but do not attempt parsimonious abstraction (Liu et al., 2022). Worryingly, 3D recon-
115   struction networks might rely on object semantics (Tatarchenko et al., 2019). Deng et al. (CVXNet)
116   represent objects as a union of convexes, again training without ground truth segmentations (Deng
117   et al., 2020). An early variant of CVXNet can recover 3D representations of poses from single
118   images, with reasonable parses into parts (Deng et al., 2019). Meshes can be decomposed into
119   near convex primitives, by a form of search (Wei et al., 2022). Part decompositions have attractive
120   editability (Hertz et al., 2022). Regression methods face some difficulty producing different numbers
121   of primitives per scene (CVXNet uses a fixed number; (Tulsiani et al., 2017) predicts the probability
122   a primitive is present; one also might use Gumbel softmax (Jang et al., 2017)). Primitives that have
123
      been explored include: cuboids (Calderon & Boubekeur, 2017; Gadelha et al., 2020; Mo et al., 2019;
      Tulsiani et al., 2017; Roberts et al., 2021; Smirnov et al., 2019; Sun & Zou, 2019; Kluger et al., 2021);
124
      superquadrics (Barr, 1981; Jaklič et al., 2000; Paschalidou et al., 2019); planes (Chen et al., 2019;
125
      Liu et al., 2018a); and generalized cylinders (Nevatia & Binford, 1977; Zou et al., 2017a; Li et al.,
126   2018). There is a recent review in (Fu et al., 2021).
127
128   Neural Parts (Paschalidou et al., 2021) decomposes an object given by an image into a set of non-
129
      convex shapes. CAPRI-Net (Yu et al., 2022) decomposes 3D objects given as point clouds or voxel
      grids into assemblies of quadric surfaces. DeepCAD (Wu et al., 2021) decomposes an object into
130
      a sequence of commands describing a CAD model, but requires appropriately annotated data for
131
      training. Point2Cyl (Uy et al., 2022) is similar, but predicts the 2D shapes in form of an SDF. Notably,
132   Yu et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2021); Uy et al. (2022) also utilise CSG with negative primitives or parts
133   but, unlike our work, focus on CAD models of single objects instead of complex real-world scenes.
134
135
      Hoiem et al parse outdoor scenes into vertical and horizontal surfaces (Hoiem et al., 2005; 2007);
      Gupta et al demonstrate a parse into blocks (Gupta et al., 2010). Indoor scenes can be parsed into: a
136
      cuboid (Hedau et al., 2009; Vavilala & Forsyth, 2023); beds and some furniture as boxes (Hedau et al.,
137
      2010); free space (Hedau et al., 2012); and plane layouts (Stekovic et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2018b). If
138   RGBD is available, one can recover layout in detail (Zou et al., 2017b). Patch-like primitives can be
139   imputed from data (Fouhey et al., 2013). Jiang demonstrates parsing RGBD images into primitives
140   by solving a 0-1 quadratic program (Jiang, 2014). Like that work, we evaluate segmentation by
141   primitives (see Jiang (2014), p. 12), but we use original NYUv2 labels instead of the drastically
142   simplified ones in the prior work. Also, our primitives are truly convex. Monnier et al and Alaniz
143   et al decompose scenes into sets of superquadrics using differentiable rendering, which requires
144   calibrated multi-view images as input (Monnier et al., 2023; Alaniz et al., 2023). Most similar to
145   our work is that of Kluger et al, who identify cuboids sequentially with a RANSAC-like greedy
146
      algorithm (Fischler & Bolles, 1981; Kluger et al., 2020; 2021; 2024; Kluger & Rosenhahn, 2024).
147   The success of a descent method depends critically on the start point, typically dealt with using
148   greedy algorithms (rooted in RANSAC (Fischler & Bolles, 1981); note the prevalence of RANSAC
149   in a recent review (Kang et al., 2020)); randomized search (Ramamonjisoa et al., 2022; Hampali
150   et al., 2021); or multiple starts. Regression methods must minimize loss over all training data, so
151   at inference time do not necessarily produce the best representation for the particular scene. The
152
      prediction is biased by the need to get other scenes right, too. To manage this difficulty, we use a
      mixed reconstruction strategy – first, predict primitives using a network, then polish using descent.

158
      Our work is based on the architecture and losses of Vavilala & Forsyth (2023) and maintains its basic
159
      inference procedure:
160
161         1. Predict initial convex parameters from an RGBD image via a convolutional neural network.
            2. Refine the fit by directly optimizing convex parameters against the training losses.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      We note that generally, GT primitive decompositions are not available and instead a variety of losses
163   supervise the fitting process. Unlike prior primitive generation work, we employ an ensemble of
164   networks that predict varying numbers of convexes, and select the prediction which yields the lowest
165   error after refinement (Sec. 3.1). This allows us to abandon the pruning heuristic used by Vavilala
166   & Forsyth (2023) to control the number of convexes for each scene. We furthermore introduce
167   negative boolean primitives for scene decomposition (Sec. 3.2). As visualised in Fig. 2, boolean
168   primitives allow for a more parsimonious description of complex geometry. An additional biasing
169   loss, annealing schedule, data augmentation, and thorough hyperparameter search yield further
170   accuracy gains (Sec. 3.3). Fig. 3 provides an overview of our inference pipeline.
171   Our method is RGBD input. Our losses require a point cloud that is extracted from the depth image
172   via the heuristic described in Vavilala & Forsyth (2023). Our method works both when GT depth
173   is and is not available, and we evaluate both scenarios, using MIDAS (Ranftl et al., 2022) to obtain
174   inferred depth maps.

176   3.1   E NSEMBLING
177
178   We remark that much of the literature on primitive decomposition fits a fixed number of primi-
179   tives Deng et al. (2020). Other work starts from a fixed number of primitives and removes excess
180
      primitives according to a greedy algorithm (Vavilala & Forsyth, 2023). The problem with these
      approaches is that it is difficult to know a priori what initial settings are best for a given test image.
181
      For example, post-training refinement could get stuck in a local minimum if the start point isn’t good.
182
183   A solution we employ in this work is ensembling the prediction from multiple networks, and selecting
184   the best one. Naturally, the more members of the ensemble, the better the final quality since we can
185   evaluate each method independently and select the best one. Our aim is simply to show one avenue
186
      of creating a usefully rich ensemble: varying the number of positive and negative primitives. Because
      primitive decomposition networks typically have several primary and regularizing losses, training
187
      networks with diverse hyperparameters would be another way of generating an ensemble, though due
188
      to limited compute we do not show this sort of ensemble.
189
190   Additionally, use-cases where stochasticity is desired (analogous to image generation literature)
191   benefit from ensembling because multiple primitive decompositions will be available for a given test
192
      image. Prior work does not propose a method to generate and select from an ensemble if a user wants
      diverse representations of a scene.
193
194   For a given test image, we can select the best method by running it through each network, evaluating
195   the generated primitive depth map against an inferred or GT depth, and use the best network for
196   subsequent refinement. In practice, we observed refine-then-choose to perform better, whereby we
197   refine each method first then choose the one with the best error metrics. Even though this involves
198
      more compute, the quality gains are substantial (see Table 1).

200   3.2   B OOLEAN PRIMITIVES
201
      A traditional collection of primitives is represented by an indicator function O : R → [0, 1], with
202
      O(x) = 0 indicating free space, and O(x) = 1 indicating a query point x ∈ R3 is inside the volume.
203   When introducing negative primitives, the final indicator can be composed of a CSG operation
204   between the union of positive primitives and union of negative primitives. Let O+ (x) be the indicator
205   of positive primitives only, and O− (x) be the indicator of negative primitives only. The final indicator
206   for our representation is simply
207
208
                                         O(x) = relu(O+ (x) − O− (x))                                       (1)
209
210   Our modified representation allows re-using the existing sample loss, unique parametrization loss,
211   and Manhattan World loss Deng et al. (2020); Vavilala & Forsyth (2023) for both O+ (x) and O− (x).
212   However, for negative primitives only, we must modify the samples on which the overlap loss,
213   guidance loss, and localization loss are applied. During each training iteration, we select samples
214   for which the ground truth label for a point is outside, x = 0, but the indicator function is positive,
215   O(x) = 1. Thus if a negative primitive moves to such a sample, its classification will become
      O(x) = 0, matching ground truth.

      3.3   P ERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENTS
240
241   Biasing sample loss The primary loss for training a convex decomposition network is
242
243
244                                    Lapprox = Ex∼R3 ||Ô(x) − O(x)||2 .                                 (2)
245
      We postulate that negative primitives would be most useful in regions that the positive primitives
246
      over-explain certain geometry, i.e. they explain more inside samples correctly than outside samples.
247   In effect, if the positive primitives are “too big", then negative primitives will help the network carve
248   away unnecessary geometry. In other words, there will be more useful regions that negative primitives
249   can exist. We can achieve this bias by simply introducing an additional sample loss but only apply it
250   to points where the GT label is inside, O(x) = 1
251
252                                      Linside = Ex∼R3 ||Ô(x) − 1||2 .                                  (3)
253
254
      We weight Linside by 0.1, and ablate that choice in Fig. 8.
255   Annealing loss weights Further, we found more stable training by annealing the weight of the overlap
256   loss and alignment loss, starting from 0 at the beginning of training, up to the target weight midway
257   through training. We preserve the annealing of the surface sample weight, whereby early in training
258   free space samples are prioritized in the losses, and by midway of training, all samples have an equal
259   weight. These performance improvements are intended to aid the network in predicting high-level
260
      geometric structure of the scene early in training, then getting the details right towards the end.
261   Data augmentation Prior art did not successfully implement data augmentations in the form of
262   horizontal flips. A correct implementation needs to take into account the effect of camera calibration
263   parameters on the point cloud. We do so here and in practice, we observe substantial improvements –
264   see Fig. 10.
265   Augmentations are especially valuable given that the NYUv2 dataset is relatively small - though
266   clearly sufficient for getting good results. Our procedure uses the standard 795/654 train/test NYUv2
267   split Nathan Silberman & Fergus (2012). We hold out 5% of training images for validation. We use
268   this dataset primarily to maintain consistency in evaluating against prior art. We do not consider the
269   volume loss or segmentation loss from Vavilala & Forsyth (2023) in our experimentation, as they
      were shown to have an approximately neutral effect.

      3.4     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
291
292   Our neural architecture is a ResNet-18 encoder (accepting RGBD input), followed by a decoder
293   consisting of three linear layers of sizes [1048, 1048, 2048] and LeakyRelu activations. We do not
294   freeze any layers during training. The dimensionality of the final output varies based on the number
295   of primitives the model is trained to produce (as we train different models for different numbers of
296   primitives in this work). We implement our procedure in PyTorch and train all networks with AdamW
297   optimizer, learning rate 4 × 10−4 , batch size 128, mixed-precision training, for 5000 iterations, on a
298   single A40 GPU. It takes 26 mins to train a 8 primitive model and 67 mins to train a 40 primitive
299   model. Our inference procedure requires around 5 seconds for an 8 primitive model and up to 20
300
      seconds for a 40 primitive model. We halve the learning rate after 50% and 75% of the steps during
      both training and refinement. During refinement, we optimize for 250 steps, AdamW optimizer, and
301
      learning rate 0.01. Again, we find LR decay helpful during refinement with the same schedule as
302
      during training.

305   3.5     E VALUATION OF P RIMITIVES
306
307   Geometric primitive abstraction is a longstanding interest in computer vision ( Marr & Nishihara
308   (1978), Sec. 2) but finding broad applications of them is ongoing. We are aware of recent efforts to
309
      condition image synthesis on primitives - see Vavilala et al. (2023); Bhat et al. (2023). Our work can
      be a useful building block for such use-cases.
310
311   To that end, we’d like to evaluate primitives appropriately for these tasks. If a user is assembling
312   primitives (possibly extracted from a real image) and editing their position, it is critical that the
313   generated image matches the requested depth. Thus, our evaluation must measure the geometric
314   accuracy between the generated primitives and the source depth. If we are allowed a large number
315
      of primitives e.g. 1 per pixel, we could perfectly match the GT depth at the cost of coarse scene
      abstraction; because we are instead dealing with few primitives (8-40 in this work), depth error
316
      metrics will give a true indication of geometric scene decomposition quality. Similarly, evaluating
317
      per-pixel normals offers a measure of geometric reconstruction quality. GT depth and normals are
318   available at inference time (and they can be inferred by high quality estimators like Ranftl et al.
319   (2020) if not). Predicted depth and normals can be obtained by ray marching the generated primitives
320   from the original viewpoint, obtaining a dense per-pixel estimate. Similarly, downstream use-cases
321   of primitives may require object-level control. We can evaluate how well our primitives enable this
322   capability by assigning each primitive’s face the most common GT segmentation label in its support,
323   and then measuring the dense per-pixel segmentation accuracy across the whole image. A high score
      means that primitives map to objects quite well.

324
      We note that we evaluate these metrics against existing primitive generation works from RGB images
325   (specifically Vavilala & Forsyth (2023); Kluger et al. (2021)), not against methods that predict
326   segmentation, depth, or normals from RGB like Yang et al. (2024); Kirillov et al. (2023). We argue
327   that we can evaluate primitives by looking at their predicted depth/normals/segmentation. But we’re
328   not trying to predict depth/normals/segmentation from RGB using primitives, for which there are
329   well-developed methods. Our evaluation procedure is consistent with prior art. Better metrics should
330   mean better primitives. This allows us to use detailed quantitative evaluations in an area that has
331   traditionally lacked them.

      4    E XPERIMENTS
334
335
      We perform extensive quantitative and qualitative evaluation of our method. To do so, we use
336   established evaluation procedures on the depth, normals, and segmentation inferred from the generated
337   primitives.
338
339
      Any individual network we train beats baselines. Without ensembling, with or without negative
      primitives, our method beats all baselines on nearly every metric - see Tables 1, 2. Here, we present
340
      several networks with different numbers of positive and negative primitives. We apply refinement at
341
      test time. Individually, each procedure performs quite well across a range of initial primitives. In
342   some cases, introducing negative primitives helps on average (we test K − ∈ [0, 1, 2]). When we
343   ensemble the five networks without negative primitives, we get substantially better error metrics,
344   particularly as measured by AbsRel of the depth map. To select the best method, we simply compare
345   the depth of generated primitives against GT. Ensembling with negative primitives can boost
346   quality. Further, when we enrich the vocabulary of operations with negative primitives, depth metrics
347   get better (pos+neg R->S).
348   Refinement improves all methods. In Fig. 7, we apply our refinement procedure on all test images
349   using the GT depth map. Consistent with previous work, refining is essential to getting the best results.
350   Observe how all error metrics, particularly AbsRel, get better with refinement. In particular, the
351   negative primitives we introduced get better with refinement. While we get strong results across all
352   numbers of primitives, the introduction of negative primitives only occasionally helps on average, in
353   some cases slightly hurting metrics, which indicates that our test scenes are quite diverse and different
354   settings are optimal for each scene. Refine-then-select performs better than select-then-refine.
355   When we ensemble the five positive-only networks, rows pos, all error metrics get better than any
356
      method alone. However, the fact that we get better numbers when we select after refining indicates
      that this is an extremely difficult fitting problem whereby what appears to be the best start point
357
      may not necessarily yield the best endpoint. When comparing ensembles with negative primitives
358
      (pos+neg), we again observe that we are better off refining then selecting. Further, on average
359   the network picks 0.30 negative primitives in our best ensemble - which means they are genuinely
360   helpful on some scenes. In Fig 4 we present histograms showing how many total and negative
361   primitives were chosen on our test set. In practice, (left) our procedure is able to handle larger
362   numbers of primitives better than prior work, observing that more primitives is generally better, and
363   right, negative primitives can be quite helpful, noting that they are selected from the ensemble for
364   several scenes.
365   A biased sampling loss should be part of the ensemble. We ablate our decision to bias the
366   sampling loss to favor classifying “inside” points correctly via Linside . In Fig. 8, we test winside ∈
367   [0.0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8] with K − ∈ [1, 2]. Turning this loss on is generally preferred over not having
368   it. We thus let winside = 0.1 in our experimentation as a reasonable mid-ground for networks with
369   negative primitives.
370
      Data augmentation yields more accurate decompositions. As Fig. 10 shows, augmenting RGBD
371   input data with horizontal flips during training reduces the AbsRel depth error and increases the
372   segmentation accuracy measurably, with more modest effects on normal accuracy.

        Figure 11: We perform an ablation on the number of negative primitives, K − as well as the primitive
1052
        vocabulary. By default, in this work we generate parallelepipeds (a more general form of a cuboid)
1053    to maintain consistency in evaluation against prior work Vavilala & Forsyth (2023); Kluger et al.
1054    (2021). To do so, our model predicts three normals and offsets per primitive, and the other three
1055    are implied. Thus the primitives are centrally symmetric. Our experimentation shows that fitting
1056    CSG with parallelepipeds is very difficult, as indicated by the AbsRel getting worse as we increase
1057    the number of boolean primitives (red line). However downstream use-cases may not require the
1058    centrality constraint and good reconstruction quality might be paramount. To that end, we try two
1059    more ablations. First, we remove the centrality constraint and Manhattan World loss (green line).
1060    Notice how all numbers get better and in particular primitive decompositions get better with more
1061
        boolean primitives. We then increase the number of halfplanes to 12, (blue line) and the quality is
        generally better across the board. The implication is that fitting CSG is easier if we fit primitives with
1062
        a more flexible parametrization (convex polytopes) as opposed to more rigid primitives (e.g. cuboids).

1065    supports this richer primitive vocabulary by simply tuning hyperparameters. Experiments conducted
