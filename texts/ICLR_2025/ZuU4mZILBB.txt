009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The effects of ligand binding on protein structures and their in vivo functions carry
012            numerous implications for modern biomedical research and biotechnology de-
013            velopment efforts such as drug discovery. Although several deep learning (DL)
014            methods and benchmarks designed for protein-ligand docking have recently been
015            introduced, to date no prior works have systematically studied the behavior of
016            docking methods within the broadly applicable context of (1) using predicted
017            (apo) protein structures for docking (e.g., for applicability to unknown structures);
018            (2) docking multiple ligands concurrently to a given target protein (e.g., for en-
019
               zyme design); and (3) having no prior knowledge of binding pockets (e.g., for
               unknown pocket generalization). To enable a deeper understanding of docking
020
               methods’ real-world utility, we introduce P OSE B ENCH, the first comprehensive
021
               benchmark for broadly applicable protein-ligand docking. P OSE B ENCH enables
022            researchers to rigorously and systematically evaluate DL docking methods for
023            apo-to-holo protein-ligand docking and protein-ligand structure generation using
024            both single and multi-ligand benchmark datasets, the latter of which we intro-
025            duce for the first time to the DL community. Empirically, using P OSE B ENCH,
026            we find that (1) DL methods consistently outperform conventional docking al-
027            gorithms; (2) most recent DL docking methods fail to generalize to multi-ligand
028            protein targets; and (3) training DL methods with physics-informed loss func-
029            tions on diverse clusters of protein-ligand complexes is a promising direction
030
               for future work. Code, data, tutorials, and benchmark results are available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PoseBench-2CD8.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The field of drug discovery has long been challenged with a critical task: determining the structure
037   of ligand molecules in complex with proteins and other key macromolecules (Warren et al., 2012).
038   As accurately identifying such complex structures (in particular multi-ligand structures) can yield
039
      advanced insights into the binding dynamics and functional characteristics (and thereby, the medic-
      inal potential) of numerous protein complexes in vivo, in recent years, significant resources have
040
      been spent developing new experimental and computational techniques for protein-ligand structure
041
      determination (Du et al., 2016). Over the last decade, machine learning (ML) methods for structure
042   prediction have become indispensable components of modern structure determination at scale, with
043   AlphaFold 2 for protein structure prediction being a hallmark example (Jumper et al., 2021).
044
045
      As the field has gradually begun to investigate whether proteins in complex with other types of
      molecules can faithfully be modeled with ML (and particularly deep learning (DL)) techniques
046
      (Dhakal et al., 2022; Harris et al., 2023; Krishna et al., 2024), several new works in this direction
047
      have suggested the promising potential of such approaches to protein-ligand structure determination
048   (Corso et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2024; Qiao et al., 2024; Abramson et al., 2024). Nonetheless, to date,
049   it remains to be shown whether such DL methods can adequately generalize in the context of apo
050   (i.e., unbound) protein structures and multiple interacting ligand molecules (e.g., which can alter
051   the chemical functions of various enzymes) as well as whether such methods are more accurate
052   than traditional techniques for protein-ligand structure determination (for brevity hereafter referred
053   to interchangeably as structure generation or docking) such as template-based (Pang et al., 2023) or
      molecular docking software tools (Xu et al., 2023).

081          • Our newly proposed benchmark, P OSE B ENCH, enables specific insights into necessary

084
               multi-ligand docking targets.
085          • Our benchmark’s results also highlight the importance of considering rigorous (e.g.,
086            structure-based) dataset splits when training future DL docking methods and measuring
087            their ability to recapitulate the ground-truth distributions of protein-ligand interactions
088            within benchmark datasets.

      2   R ELATED WORK
092
093
      Structure prediction of protein-ligand complexes. The field of DL-driven protein-ligand structure
      determination was largely sparked with the development of geometric deep learning methods such
094
      as EquiBind (Stärk et al., 2022) and TANKBind (Lu et al., 2022) for direct (i.e., regression-based)
095
      prediction of bound ligand structures in protein complexes. Notably, these predictive methods could
096   estimate localized ligand structures in complex with multiple protein chains as well as the associated
097   complexes’ binding affinities. However, in addition to their limited predictive accuracy, they have
098   more recently been found to frequently produce steric clashes between protein and ligand atoms,
099   notably hindering their widespread adoption in modern drug discovery pipelines.
100
      Protein-ligand structure generation and docking. Shortly following the first wave of predictive
101
      methods for protein-ligand structure determination, DL methods such as DiffDock (Corso et al.,
102   2022) demonstrated the utility of a new approach to this problem by reframing protein-ligand dock-
103   ing as a generative modeling task, whereby multiple ligand conformations can be generated for a
104   particular protein target and rank-ordered using a predicted confidence score. This approach has
105   inspired many follow-up works offering alternative formulations of this generative approach to the
106   problem (Lu et al., 2024; Plainer et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024), with some of such follow-up works
107   also being capable of accurately modeling protein flexibility upon ligand binding or predicting bind-
      ing affinities to a high degree of accuracy.

108
      Benchmarking efforts for protein-ligand complexes. In response to the large number of new
109   methods that have been developed for protein-ligand structure generation, recent works have intro-
110   duced several new datasets and metrics with which to evaluate newly developed methods, with some
111   of such benchmarking efforts focusing on modeling single-ligand protein interactions (Buttenschoen
112   et al., 2024; Durairaj et al., 2024) and with others specializing in the assessment of multi-ligand pro-
113   tein interactions (Robin et al., 2023). One of the primary aims of this work is to bridge this gap by
114   systematically assessing a selection of the latest (pocket-blind) structure generation methods within
115   both interaction regimes in the context of unbound protein structures and ab initio complex structure
116   prediction, efforts we describe in greater detail in the following section.
117
118   3     P OSE B ENCH
119
120
      The overall goal of P OSE B ENCH, our newly proposed benchmark for protein-ligand structure gener-
121   ation, is to provide the ML research community with a centralized resource with which one can sys-
122   tematically measure, in a variety of macromolecular contexts, the methodological advancements of
123   new DL methods proposed for this problem. In the remaining sections, we describe P OSE B ENCH’s
124   design and composition (as illustrated in Figure 1), how we have used P OSE B ENCH to evaluate sev-
125   eral recent DL methods (as well as conventional algorithms) for protein-ligand structure modeling,
126   and what actionable insights we can derive from P OSE B ENCH’s benchmark results with these latest
127   DL methods.
128
129   3.1    P REPROCESSED DATASETS
130
131   P OSE B ENCH provides users with four datasets with which to evaluate existing or new protein-ligand
132   structure generation methods, the Astex Diverse and PoseBusters Benchmark (DockGen) datasets
133   previously curated by Buttenschoen et al. (2024) ((Corso et al., 2024a)) as well as the CASP15
134
      protein-ligand interaction (PLI) dataset that we have manually curated in this work.
135   Astex Diverse dataset. The Astex Diverse dataset (Hartshorn et al., 2007) is a collection of 85
136   protein-ligand complexes composed of various drug-like molecules known to be of pharmaceuti-
137   cal or agrochemical interest, where a single representative ligand is present in each complex. This
138   dataset can be considered an easy benchmarking set for many DL-based docking methods in that
139   several of its proteins are known to overlap with the commonly used PDBBind (time-split) training
140
      dataset. Nonetheless, including this dataset for benchmarking allows one to determine the perfor-
      mance ”upper bound” of each method’s docking capabilities for single-ligand protein complexes.
141
142   To perform apo docking with this dataset, we used AlphaFold 3 (Abramson et al., 2024) to predict
143   the complex structure of each of its proteins, where 5 of these 85 complexes were excluded from
144   the effective benchmarking set due to being too large for docking with certain baseline methods on
145   an 80GB NVIDIA A100 GPU. For the remaining 80 complexes, we then optimally aligned their
146
      predicted protein structures to the corresponding ground-truth (holo) protein-ligand structures using
      the PLI-weighted root mean square deviation (RMSD) alignment algorithm originally proposed by
147
      Corso et al. (2022).
148
149   PoseBusters Benchmark dataset. The PoseBusters Benchmark dataset (Buttenschoen et al., 2024)
150   contains 308 recent protein-ligand complexes released from 2021 onwards. Like the Astex Diverse
151   set, each complex in this dataset contains a single ligand for prediction. In contrast to Astex Diverse,
152
      this dataset can be considered a harder benchmark set since its proteins do not directly overlap with
      the commonly used PDBBind (time-split) training dataset composed of protein-ligand complexes
153
      with release dates up to 2019.
154
155   Likewise to Astex Diverse, for the PoseBusters Benchmark set, we used AlphaFold 3 to predict the
156   apo complex structures of each of its proteins. After filtering out 28 complexes that certain baseline
157   methods could not fit on an 80GB A100 GPU, we RMSD-aligned the remaining 280 predicted pro-
158
      tein structures while optimally weighting each complex’s protein-ligand interface in the alignment.
      For the DockGen dataset, we refer readers to Appendix H.1.
159
160   CASP15 dataset. To assess the multi-ligand modeling capabilities of recent methods for protein-
161   ligand structure generation, in this work, we introduce a curated version of the CASP15 PLI dataset
      introduced as a first-of-its-kind prediction category in the 15th Critical Assessment of Structure

199   3.2   F ORMULATED TASKS
200
201   In this work, we have developed P OSE B ENCH to focus our analysis on the behavior of different DL
202   methods for protein-ligand docking in a variety of macromolecular contexts (e.g., with or without
203   inorganic cofactors present). With this goal in mind, below we formulate the structure generation
204   tasks currently available in P OSE B ENCH.
205   Single-ligand blind docking. For single-ligand blind docking, each benchmark method is provided
206   with a (multi-chain) protein sequence and an optional apo (predicted) protein structure as input
207   along with a corresponding ligand SMILES string for each complex. In particular, no knowledge
208   of the complex’s protein-ligand binding pocket is provided to evaluate how well each method can
209   (1) identify the correct binding pockets and (2) propose the correct ligand conformation within each
210   predicted pocket.
211   Multi-ligand blind docking. For multi-ligand blind docking, each benchmark method is provided
212   with a (multi-chain) protein sequence and an optional apo (predicted) protein structure as input
213   along with the corresponding (fragment) ligand SMILES strings. As in single-ligand blind docking,
214   no knowledge of the protein-ligand binding pocket is provided, which offers the opportunity to not
215   only evaluate binding pocket and conformation prediction precision but also multimeric steric clash
      rates.

      4   M ETHODS AND EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
217
218
219   Overview. Our benchmark is designed to explore answers to specific modeling questions for
220
      protein-ligand docking such as (1) which types of methods are best able to identify the correct
      binding pocket(s) in target proteins and (2) which types of methods most accurately produce multi-
221
      ligand structures without steric clashes? In the following sections, we describe in detail which types
222
      of methods we evaluate in our benchmark, what the input and output formats look like for each
223   method, and how we evaluate each method’s predictions for particular protein complex targets.
224
225   Method categories. As illustrated in Figure 1, we divide the benchmark methods included
226
      in P OSE B ENCH into one of three categories: (1) conventional algorithms, (2) predictive (i.e.,
      regression-based) ML algorithms, and (3) generative (i.e., distributional) ML algorithms.
227
228   As representative algorithms for conventional protein-ligand docking, we include AutoDock Vina
229   (v1.2.5) (Trott & Olson, 2010) as well as a template-based modeling method for ligand-protein
230   complex structure prediction (TULIP) that we incorporate in this work to compare modern DL
231   docking methods to the most common types of traditional docking algorithms (e.g., in the CASP15
232
      competition (Xu et al., 2023)). For completeness, in Appendix G, we include a detailed description
      of the TULIP algorithm to provide interested readers with historical context regarding how such
233
      traditional docking techniques have typically been designed.
234
235   To represent predictive ML docking algorithms, we include FABind (Pei et al., 2024) as well as the
236   recently released version of RoseTTAFold 2 for all-atom structural modeling (i.e., RoseTTAFold-
237   All-Atom) (Krishna et al., 2024). Lastly, for generative ML docking algorithms, we include Dy-
238
      namicBind (Lu et al., 2024), NeuralPLexer (Qiao et al., 2024), Chai-1 (Chai-Discovery, 2024), and
      DiffDock-L (Corso et al., 2024a), the latest version of DiffDock, which is designed with pocket
239
      generalization as a key aim (n.b., through its use of ECOD (Cheng et al., 2014) structure-based
240
      cluster sampling). Notably, AlphaFold 3 (Abramson et al., 2024) does not currently support generic
241   SMILES string inputs, so we cannot benchmark it.
242
243   Additionally, we provide a method ensembling baseline (Ensemble) that uses (multi-)ligand struc-
244
      tural consensus ranking (Con) (Roy et al., 2023) to rank its ligand structure predictions selected from
      the (intrinsically method-ranked) top-3 ligand conformations produced by a subset of the DL base-
245
      line methods of this work (i.e., DiffDock-L, DynamicBind, NeuralPLexer, and RoseTTAFold-AA).
246
      This ensembling baseline is included to answer the question, ”Which of these DL methods produces
247   the most consistent conformations in interaction with a protein complex?”.
248
249   Input and output formats.
250
251         1. Formats for conventional methods are as follows:
252
253              a) Template-based (protein-fixed) methods such as TULIP are provided with an apo
254                 (predicted) protein structure and (fragment) ligand SMILES strings and are tasked
255
                    with retrieving (PDB template (Bank, 1971)) ligand conformations residing in the
                    same coordinate system as the given (predicted) protein structure following optimal
256
                    molecular and structural alignment (Hu et al., 2018) with corresponding RDKit con-
257
                    formers of the input (query) ligand SMILES strings, where molecular similarity with
258                 the query ligands is used to rank-order the selected (PDB template) conformations.
259
260              b) Molecular docking (protein-fixed) tools such as AutoDock Vina, which require spec-
261
                    ification of protein binding sites, are provided with not only a predicted protein struc-
                    ture but also the centroid coordinates of each (DiffDock-L-)predicted protein-ligand
262
                    binding site residue. Such binding site residues are classified using a 4 Å protein-
263
                    ligand heavy atom interaction threshold and using a 25 Å ligand-ligand heavy atom
264                 interaction threshold to define a ”group” of ligands belonging to the same binding site
265                 and therefore residing in the same 25 Å3 -sized binding site input voxel for AutoDock
266                 Vina. For interested readers, for all four benchmark datasets, we also report results
267                 using P2Rank (Krivák & Hoksza, 2018) to predict AutoDock Vina’s binding site cen-
268                 troid inputs.
269
            2. Formats for predictive methods are as follows:

                a) FABind is provided with a predicted protein structure as well as a ligand SMILES
271                string, and it is then tasked with producing a (single) ligand conformation in complex
272                with the given (fixed-structure) protein.
273
                b) RoseTTAFold-All-Atom (AA) is provided with a (multi-chain) protein sequence as

279             a) DiffDock-L is provided with a predicted protein structure and (fragment) ligand

282                DiffDock-L does not natively support multi-ligand SMILES string inputs, so in this
283                work, we propose a modified inference procedure for DiffDock-L which autoregres-

                   ing for each complex the average confidence score over all iterations). Notably, as
                   an inference-time modification, this sampling formulation permits multi-ligand sam-
287

289
                b) As a single-ligand generative (flexible) docking method, DynamicBind adopts the
290
                   same input and output formats as DiffDock-L with the following exceptions: (1) the
291
                   predicted input protein structure is now flexible in response to (fragment) ligand dock-
292                ing; (2) the autoregressive inference procedure we adapted from that of DiffDock-L
293                now provides DynamicBind with its own most recently generated protein structure
294                in each (fragment) ligand inference iteration, thereby providing the model with par-
295                tial multi-ligand interaction context; and (3) iteration-averaged confidence scores and
296                predicted affinities are reported for each complex. Nonetheless, for both DiffDock-L
297                and DynamicBind, such modified inference procedures highlight the importance in
298                future work of retraining such generative methods directly on multi-ligand complexes
299                to address such inference-time compromises.
300             c) As a natively multi-ligand structure generation model trained using 3D molecular
301                and protein data sources and a physics-informed (Van der Waals) clash loss, Neu-
302                ralPLexer receives as its inputs a (multi-chain) protein sequence, a predicted protein
303
                   (template) structure, as well as (fragment) ligand SMILES strings. The method is then
                   tasked with producing multiple rank-ordered (flexible) protein-ligand structure con-
304
                   formations for each input complex, using the method’s average predicted per-ligand
305
                   heavy atom local Distance Difference Test (lDDT) score (Mariani et al., 2013) for
306                rank-ordering.
307
                d) Lastly, Chai-1 serves as a multi-ligand structure generation model (akin to AlphaFold
308
                   3) trained on diverse sequence-based PDB clusters and AlphaFold 2-predicted struc-
309
                   tures along with AlphaFold 3-based training losses. Following its default settings for
310                inference, the model receives as its inputs a (multi-chain) protein sequence and (frag-
311                ment) ligand SMILES strings, with no template structures or multiple sequence align-
312                ments provided. The method is then tasked with producing multiple rank-ordered
313                (flexible) protein-ligand structure conformations for each input complex, using the
314                method’s intrinsic ranking score (Abramson et al., 2024) for rank-ordering.

409   5.1   T RAINING ON DIVERSE CLUSTERS SUPPORTS SINGLE - LIGAND DOCKING PERFORMANCE
410
411
      We begin our investigations by evaluating the performance of each baseline method for single-ligand
      docking using the Astex Diverse and PoseBusters Benchmark datasets. Notably, for results on the
412
      PoseBusters Benchmark dataset (and subsequent datasets), we perform an additional analysis where
413
      we apply post-prediction (fixed-protein) relaxation to each method’s generated ligand conformations
414   using molecular dynamics simulations (Eastman & Pande, 2010), as originally proposed by Butten-
415   schoen et al. (2024). Additionally, for interested readers, in Appendix H.1 we include DockGen
416   benchmark results for flexible-protein relaxation as implemented by Lu et al. (2024).
417
      As shown in Figures 2 and 3, Chai-1 and DiffDock-L (in particular, the version of DiffDock em-
418
      ploying structural cluster training (SCT)) achieve the best overall performance across both of these
419
      single-ligand datasets in terms of its percentage of correct and valid generated ligand poses (i.e.,
420   RMSD ≤ 2 Å & PB-Valid). To better understand this finding, in Appendix H.1, we find an even
421   more striking instance where ablating SCT from DiffDock leads to considerably degraded docking
422   performance for novel single-ligand protein targets. Furthermore, in the context of DockGen bench-

464   5.2     P HYSICS - INFORMED CLASH PENALIZATION IMPROVES MULTI - LIGAND DOCKING
465
466   We now turn to investigating the performance of various deep learning and conventional methods
467   for multi-ligand docking. In contrast to the single-ligand docking results presented in Section 5.1, in
468   Figure 4, we see a particular DL method, NeuralPLexer, stand out from all other methods in terms its
469   multi-ligand docking performance. To better understand the factors contributing to its success, we
470   also report results with a version of NeuralPLexer fine-tuned without its (original) van der Waals-
471
      based inter-ligand clash loss (ILCL) function (i.e., NeuralPLexer w/o ILCL), where these (ablation)
      results suggest that training NeuralPLexer with physics-based clash penalties has provided it with
472
      useful knowledge for successful multi-ligand docking. In contrast, all other baseline methods appear
473
      to produce only a handful of correctly docked multi-ligand poses. To more concretely understand
474   why, in Appendix F.2, we plot the distribution of protein-ligand interactions produced by each base-
475   line method for the CASP15 dataset, and we find that most methods struggle to correctly capture e.g.,
476   the distribution of hydrophobic interactions or Van der Waals contacts this dataset presents. Using
477   CASP15 target T1188 as a case study, in Figure 6, we illustrate how this distributional mismatch of-
478   ten leads to methods such as DiffDock-L producing top-ranked predictions with multi-ligand steric
479   clashes that must be (unoptimally) resolved using structural relaxation. To summarize, we find that
480   these interaction-level distribution mismatches translate to poor multi-ligand docking performance
481   for most baseline methods and that NeuralPLexer’s inter-ligand clash loss has improved its ability to
482
      match the ground-truth distribution of CASP15 protein-ligand interactions for multi-ligand docking.

484   Figure 5 we report each method’s percentage of predicted protein-ligand complexes (whether correct
485   or not) for which all ligand conformations in the complex are jointly considered valid according to

497   C ONCLUSIONS
498
499   In this work, we introduced P OSE B ENCH, the first deep learning (DL) benchmark for broadly appli-
500   cable protein-ligand docking. Benchmark results with P OSE B ENCH currently suggest a negative an-
501   swer to the question ”Are we there yet (for structural drug discovery) with DL-based protein-ligand
502   docking?”. In this work, we have observed that while DL methods such as Chai-1 and DiffDock-L
503   can identify the correct binding pockets in many single-ligand protein targets, most DL methods
504   struggle to generalize to multi-ligand docking targets. Based on these results, for the development
505
      of future DL docking methods, we recommend researchers train new docking methods directly (1)
      on structurally clustered multi-ligand protein complexes available in new DL-ready biomolecular
506
      datasets (Abramson et al., 2024; Wang & Morehead, 2024) (2) using physics-informed inter-ligand
507
      steric clash penalties (Qiao et al., 2024). Key limitations of this study include its reliance on the
508   accuracy of its predicted protein structures, its (currently) limited number of multi-ligand prediction
509   targets available for benchmarking, and its inclusion of only a subset of all available protein-ligand
510   docking baselines to focus on the most recent deep learning algorithms designed specifically for
511   docking and structure generation. In future work, we aim to expand not only the number of baseline
512   methods but also the number of available (CASP) multi-ligand targets while maintaining a diverse
513   composition of heterogeneous (ionic) complexes. As a publicly available resource, P OSE B ENCH is
514   flexible to accommodate new datasets and methods for protein-ligand structure generation.
515   Availability. The P OSE B ENCH codebase, documentation, and tutorial notebooks are available at
516   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PoseBench-2CD8 under a permissive MIT license, with further
517   licensing discussed in Appendix A.

       F.1   DATASET PROTEIN - LIGAND INTERACTION DISTRIBUTIONS
1245
1246   Inspired by a similar analysis presented in the PoseCheck benchmark (Harris et al., 2023), in this
1247   section, we study the frequency of different types of protein-ligand (pocket-level) interactions such
1248   as Van der Waals contacts and hydrophobic interactions occurring natively within (n.b., a size-1000
1249   random subset of) the commonly-used PDBBind 2020 docking training set (i.e., PDBBind 2020
1250   (1000)) as well as the Astex Diverse, PoseBusters Benchmark, DockGen, and CASP15 benchmark
1251
       datasets, respectively. In particular, these measures allow us to better understand the diversity of
       interactions each baseline method within the P OSE B ENCH benchmark is tasked to model, within
1252
       the context of each test dataset. Furthermore, these measures directly indicate which benchmark
1253
       datasets are most dissimilar from commonly used training data for docking methods. Figure 11
1254   displays the results of this analysis.
1255
1256   Overall, we find that the PDBBind 2020, Astex Diverse, and PoseBusters Benchmark datasets con-
1257
       tain similar types and frequencies of interactions, with the PoseBusters Benchmark dataset contain-
       ing slightly more hydrogen bond acceptors (∼3 vs 1) and fewer Van der Waals contacts (∼5 vs 8)
1258
       on average compared to the PDBBind 2020 dataset. However, we observe a more notable differ-
1259
       ence in interaction types and frequencies between the DockGen and CASP15 datasets and the three
1260   other datasets. Specifically, we find these two benchmark datasets contain a notably different quan-
1261   tity of hydrogen bond acceptors and donors (n.b., ∼40 for CASP15), Van der Waals contact (∼200
1262   for CASP15), and hydrophobic interactions (∼2 for DockGen) on average. As we will see in the
1263   DockGen benchmarking results reported in Appendix H.1, this latter observation supports our first
1264   key insight of this work, that training new docking methods on structure-based dataset clusters is a
1265   promising direction for future work on developing new pocket-generalizing docking methods.
1266   Also particularly interesting to note is the CASP15 dataset’s bimodal distribution of hydrophobic
1267   interactions, suggesting that the dataset contains two primary classes of interacting ligands giving
1268   rise to hydrophobic interactions. One possible explanation for this phenomenon is that the CASP
1269   targets, in contrast to the PDBBind, Astex Diverse, PoseBusters Benchmark, and DockGen targets,
1270   consist of a variety of both organic (e.g., drug-like molecules) and inorganic (e.g., metal) cofactors.

1287
1288   To better contextualize the benchmark’s results comparing DL docking methods to conventional
1289   docking algorithms, in this section, we provide further details regarding how certain traditional dock-
1290   ing methods in the benchmark leverage different sources of biomolecular data to predict protein-
1291   ligand interactions for given protein targets.
1292
1293   G.1    TULIP
1294
1295   TULIP is a template-based modeling pipeline for predicting protein-ligand interactions that we
       present in the benchmark as a historical reference point to better contextualize the advances of


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       the latest DL methods for docking, as in the recent CASP15 competition template-based methods
1297   outperformed the DL docking methods that were available at the time (Xu et al., 2023). TULIP
1298   takes the target ligand’s 3D initial conformer structure (Landrum et al., 2013), the predicted re-
1299   ceptor protein structure, and identified template structures from MULTICOM (Liu et al., 2023) as
1300   inputs. TULIP first aligns the template structures containing ligands into the same geometric space
1301   as the predicted receptor structure using UCSF Chimera’s matchmaker (Pettersen et al., 2004) in
1302   non-interactive mode. It then saves the superimposed template structures and their ligands relative
1303   to the predicted receptor structure in an output PDB file that is processed by PyRosetta’s is ligand
1304   function (Chaudhury et al., 2010) to identify template ligands by checking each residue against the
1305
       Chemical Component Dictionary of the Protein Data Bank (PDB) (Westbrook et al., 2015). The
       extracted unique ligands from each template and the target ligand are converted into Morgan fin-
1306
       gerprints (Zhou & Skolnick, 2024) to compute their Tanimoto molecular similarity (Bajusz et al.,
1307
       2015) (n.b., a [0, 1] metric of increasing similarity). This step provides the initial binding location
1308   of the target ligand with respect to the receptor protein structure. Furthermore, to adjust the target
1309   ligand’s binding pose and orientation by rotation and translation, TULIP uses LS-align (Hu et al.,
1310   2018) to align the target ligand with the template ligands of higher similarity through both flexible
1311   and rigid-body alignments. Between the flexible and rigid-body alignment outputs, TULIP selects
1312   the alignment with the lowest RMSD between the template and target ligands to obtain the predicted
1313   coordinates of the target ligand. Ligands with a distance greater than 6 Å from the protein surface
1314   are discarded. To handle multiple ligands with the same SMILES string, the identified ligands are
1315   grouped into n clusters, where n is the number of ligands with the same SMILES string. To compute
1316
       the clusters, pairwise distances between the ligands are generated, and agglomerative clustering is
       used.
