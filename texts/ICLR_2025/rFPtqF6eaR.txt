011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Physics-driven deep learning (PD-DL) approaches have become popular for im-
014            proved reconstruction of fast magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans. Even
015            though PD-DL offers higher acceleration rates compared to existing clinical fast
016
               MRI techniques, their use has been limited outside specialized MRI centers. One
               impediment for their deployment is the difficulties with generalization to patholo-
017
               gies or population groups that are not well-represented in training sets. This has
018
               been noted in several studies, and fine-tuning on target populations to improve re-
019            construction has been suggested. However, current approaches for PD-DL training
020            require access to raw k-space measurements, which is typically only available at
021            specialized MRI centers that have research agreements for such data access. This
022            is especially an issue for rural and underserved areas, where commercial MRI
023            scanners only provide access to a final reconstructed image. To tackle these chal-
024            lenges, we propose Compressibility-inspired Unsupervised Learning via Parallel
025            Imaging Fidelity (CUPID) for high-quality PD-DL training using only routine
026            clinical reconstructed images exported from an MRI scanner. CUPID evaluates
027
               the goodness of the output with a compressibility-based approach, while ensuring
               that the output stays consistent with the clinical parallel imaging reconstruction
028
               through well-designed perturbations. Our results show that CUPID achieves sim-
029
               ilar quality compared to well-established PD-DL training strategies that require
030            raw k-space data access, while outperforming conventional compressed sensing
031            (CS) and state-of-the-art generative methods. We also demonstrate its effec-
032            tiveness in a zero-shot training setup for retrospectively and prospectively sub-
033            sampled acquisitions, attesting to its minimal training burden. As an approach
034            that radically deviates from existing strategies, CUPID presents an opportunity to
035            provide equitable access to fast MRI for underserved populations in an attempt to
036            reduce the inequalities associated with this expensive imaging modality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a central tool in modern medicine, offering multiple soft tissue
041
      contrasts and high diagnostic sensitivity for numerous diseases. However, MRI is among the most
042   expensive medical imaging modalities, in part due to its long scan times. Demand for MRI scans
043   has shown an annual growth rate of 2.5%, while the number of MRI units per capita has increased
044   by 1.8% in a similar time frame (Martella et al., 2023). This mismatch has further increased the
045   wait times for MRI exams (Bartsch et al., 2024; Hofmann et al., 2023), particularly in rural areas
046   and underserved communities (Burdorf, 2022), as depicted in Fig. 1. Thus, techniques for fast MRI
047   scanning that can reduce overall scan times without compromising diagnostic quality (Akçakaya
048   et al., 2022) are critical for improving the throughput of MRI.
049   Computational MRI approaches, including partial Fourier imaging (McGibney et al., 1993), parallel
050   imaging (Pruessmann et al., 1999; Griswold et al., 2002), compressed sensing (Lustig et al., 2007),
051   and more recently deep learning (DL) (Hammernik et al., 2018; Schlemper et al., 2018) have been
052   developed for accelerating MRI. In most MRI centers, parallel imaging remains the most widely
053   used approach for the reconstruction of routine clinical images. The acceleration rates afforded by
      these methods, however, are limited due to noise amplification and aliasing artifacts. DL-based

087

088   trains PD-DL reconstruction from routine clinical images, for instance in Digital Imaging and Com-
089   munications in Medicine (DICOM) format. Succinctly, CUPID uses a compressibility-inspired term
090   to evaluate the goodness of the output, while ensuring the output is consistent with parallel imag-
091   ing via well-designed input perturbations. CUPID can be used both with database-training and in a
092   subject-specific/zero-shot manner, attesting to its minimal fine-tuning burden.
093
      Our key contributions include:
094
095         •   We introduce CUPID a novel method that enables high-quality training of PD-DL recon-
096             struction in unsupervised and zero-shot/subject-specific settings using only routine clinical
097             reconstructed MRI images, eliminating the need for access to raw k-space measurements.
098             To the best of our knowledge, our method is the first attempt to train PD-DL networks using
099             only these images that are exported from the scanner.
100
101
            •   CUPID trains on DICOM images acquired at the target acceleration rate, which often have

102

103
                a likelihood or score function, such as generative models.

105         •   CUPID uses a novel unsupervised loss formulation that enforces fidelity with using parallel
106             imaging algorithms via carefully designed perturbations, in addition to evaluating the com-
107             pressibility of the output image. This parallel imaging fidelity ensures the network does

121   MRI raw data is acquired in the frequency domain of the image, referred to as k-space. For fast
122   MRI, data is acquired in the sub-Nyquist regime by undersampling the acquisition in k-space. In
123   this case, the forward acquisition model relating the image x ∈ Cn to these raw MRI data (or
124   k-space) measurements is given as:

127   |Ω| = m < n. EΩ denotes the multi-coil encoding operator that includes information from nc

129   When the acceleration rate R = n/m is less than nc , this system of equations is over-determined

      2.3   S ELF -S UPERVISED AND U NSUPERVISED M ETHODS
175
      Obtaining fully-sampled reference data in MRI can be infeasible due to prolonged scan durations,
176   organ movement in acquisitions such as real-time cardiac imaging or myocardial perfusion (Rajiah
177   et al., 2023), or signal decay in acquisitions like diffusion MRI with EPI (Uğurbil et al., 2013). To
178   enable training of PD-DL networks without fully sampled raw MRI data, a variety of unsupervised
179   learning methodologies have emerged (Akçakaya et al., 2022), including self-supervised learning
180   techniques (Yaman et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021) and generative modeling approaches (Jalal et al.,
181   2021; Chung & Ye, 2022; Chung et al., 2023).
182   Self-supervised methods use a masking approach to generate supervisory labels from the undersam-
183   pled data (Yaman et al., 2020; Millard & Chiew, 2023; Hu et al., 2024). A pioneering method in
184   this field, self-supervision via data undersampling (SSDU) (Yaman et al., 2020; 2022a), involves
185   partitioning the acquired measurement Ω into two disjoint subsets (Ω = Λ ∪ Θ) to train the network
186   in a self-supervised manner:
187
                                      min E [L (yΛ , EΛ (f (yΘ , EΘ ; θ)))]                              (7)
188                                     θ
189
      Even though these self-supervision based approaches demonstrate exceptional performance across
190
      various tasks, they lack the ability to train the model without access to undersampled raw data, as
191
      they cannot operate solely using images that are exported from the scanner.
192
193   Conversely, generative methods learn the prior distribution of the given dataset, which is then lever-
194   aged in conjunction with a log-likelihood data term during the testing phase. Although recent meth-
195
      ods based on diffusion/score-based models have shown substantial promise, these methods require
      large amounts of high-quality images either reconstructed from raw data (Jalal et al., 2021; Luo
196
      et al., 2023) or as DICOMs (Chung & Ye, 2022), as well as computational resources to perform the
197
      training, both of which may not be feasible in the setups we are focused on.

200   3     U NSUPERVISED T RAINING FOR PD-DL WITHOUT R AW K - SPACE DATA
201
202   In this study, we introduce a novel framework to train PD-DL models, utilizing only routinely avail-
203   able clinical images exported directly from MRI scanners. Recently, inspired by the connections
204   between PD-DL and compressibility-based processing (Gu et al., 2022), a compressibility-inspired
205
      loss was proposed to evaluate the goodness of unsupervised PD-DL training (Alçalar et al., 2024).
      However, this approach still requires access to raw k-space data to stabilize training, making it
206
      unsuitable for our goals. Here, we adapt the compressibility idea and augment it with a parallel
207
      imaging fidelity to successfully reconstruct clinical images in DICOM format without needing any
208   raw k-space data.

304   MRI scanner was acquired. In this experiment, we replicate the practical pipeline for CUPID, where

      4.5   A BLATION S TUDIES
459
460
      We carried out two ablation studies to explore key factors influencing the performance of our algo-
461
      rithm. The first study explored the effect of λ parameter to the final reconstruction, by training 5
462
      distinct PD-DL networks using λ ∈ {0, 50, 100, 200, ∞}. We note that using λ = 0 corresponds to
463   using only the compressibility term (Lcomp in (8)), whereas using λ → ∞ translates to using solely
464   the parallel imaging fidelity term (Lpif in (9)). Fig. 6 shows the corresponding reconstruction results
465   for each case. As outlined in Sec. 3, only using Lcomp leads to overly-smooth reconstructions due to
466   network forcing the wavelet coefficients towards zero without maintaining consistency with the data.
467   On the other hand, solely using Lpif results in DIP-like reconstructions (Ulyanov et al., 2018), where
468   the network overfits the data without any regularization, resulting in noise amplification. CUPID
469   with λ ∈ {50, 100, 200} integrates both loss terms to attain high-fidelity reconstructions. Thus, we
470   conclude that CUPID demonstrates robust performance across a wide range of λ values. Our second
471
      ablation study focuses on the effect of the number of perturbation patterns used in the training, and
      is provided in Appendix B.1.

      4.6   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488   Filtering on Routine Clinical Images. MR scans may include filtering operations applied by
                                                                −1 H
489   some vendors that affect the assumption x̂PI = (EH  Ω EΩ )    EΩ yΩ . This was discussed extensively
490   in Shimron et al. (2022), in the context of using retrospective undersampling of DICOM images to
491   train DL reconstruction, especially highlighting the use of zero-padding, which improves the dis-
492   play resolution compared to the acquisition resolution. It was shown that training of models from
493   retrospective undersampling of databases of DICOM images for PD-DL training using zero-padding
494   may lead to biases and inaccuracies. Conversely, our approach is physics-driven in nature, and the
495
      sampling pattern Ω naturally accounts for the zero-padding operation. However, our method is not
      immune to other types of filtering/processing, such as implicit intensity correction (Han et al., 2001)
496
      or deidentification methods (Van Essen et al., 2013), in which case the filtered xPI would need to be
497
      treated as the parallel imaging solution corresponding to a filtered version of y.
498
499   Resources for Fine-Tuning of PD-DL Reconstruction. While our method is aimed to improve
500   equitable access to fast MRI in low-resource settings, we do acknowledge that such low-resource
501
      MRI centers may lack the necessary hardware to fine-tune PD-DL models. We note that, similar to
      what has been shown in Yaman et al. (2022b), transfer learning along with zero-shot fine-tuning may
502
      be beneficial in decreasing training time and resources. Furthermore, since only DICOM images are
503
      needed for CUPID, standard anonymization techniques can be used (Van Essen et al., 2013), and
504   data can be transferred to potential offsite locations with more computational resources. Since no
505   raw data storage or transfer is necessary, the burden for transmission of DICOMs is low, and privacy
506   can be preserved thorough anonymization, though specifics would need to be implemented with
507   proper protocols.
508
      Magnitude-only DICOMs. In some cases, MR vendors only give access to the magnitude of xPI
509
      due to limitations in the reconstruction pipelines, for instance when using partial Fourier imaging
510   in some vendors. Additionally, if a reference calibration scan is not prospectively acquired at exam
511   time, coil sensitivities, and thus EΩ would be unknown as well, which is the case for most existing
512   DICOM databases. In such cases, the phase of xPI and coil sensitivities need to be estimated jointly.
513   There has been work on the latter in the standard PD-DL setup with access to raw k-space data (Hu
514   et al., 2024; Arvinte et al., 2021). However, this is a modification on the PD-DL objective function,
515   and not the learning procedure considered here. Thus, it is not the focus of our study and it will be
516   investigated in future studies.
517   Reinforcement Learning for Optimal Perturbations. Finally, a promising direction for future
518   work may involve using reinforcement learning to simultaneously learn the optimal perturbations.
519   This approach may enable the model to adapt and optimize perturbation strategies so that the sample
520   mean estimate in (9) can be better approximated with a few perturbation examples.

      5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this study, we presented a novel training strategy, Compressibility-inspired Unsupervised Learn-
      ing via Parallel Imaging Fidelity (CUPID), for PD-DL MRI reconstruction without access to raw
526
      k-space data. This approach leverages the compressibility of output images along with strategically
527
      designed perturbations that remain intact post-parallel imaging, thereby enhancing image quality in
528   clinically accessible images in a physics-driven manner without the need for any raw k-space data.
529   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt that does not rely on raw data and uses these
530   clinical images to train PD-DL networks, which is known for their high-fidelity reconstructions.
531   CUPID also alleviates the training burden of generative methods, which requires a large number
532   of data during training to capture the prior well. Quantitative and qualitative assessments of our
533   method, conducted on both retrospectively and prospectively accelerated acquisitions, show its ef-
534   fectiveness in delivering high-quality performance across a diverse range of MRI scans and learning
535   settings. Our source code will be released upon the acceptance of the paper.

      E    C OMPATIBILITY WITH VARIOUS PARALLEL I MAGING R ECONSTRUCTIONS
919
920
      Vendor reconstructions typically use different parallel imaging techniques. For our retrospective
921   studies, we used CG-SENSE (or equivalently SENSE) (Pruessmann et al., 1999) because it natu-
922   rally fits with the DF units in the unrolled network, and it is commonly used in clinical settings,
923   alongside GRAPPA (Griswold et al., 2002). However, we emphasize that our method does not make
924   assumptions about the specific reconstruction method used by the vendor; instead, it assumes that
925   parallel imaging can resolve the perturbations, which is ensured by designing them in a manner that
926   prevents fold-over aliasing artifacts from overlapping.
927   To further validate this, we include representative CUPID reconstruction results in Fig. 11 where
928   xP I is generated via GRAPPA (Griswold et al., 2002), demonstrating that CUPID is compatible
929   with different types of parallel imaging reconstructions as input. We further note that the prospective
930   study also used GRAPPA reconstruction as input, as this is the reconstruction provided by the vendor
931   used in our institution.
