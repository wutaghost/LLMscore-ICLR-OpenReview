009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning from human feedback has enabled the alignment of language models
012            (LMs) with human preferences. However, directly collecting human preferences
013            can be expensive, time-consuming, and can have high variance. An appealing
014            alternative is to distill preferences from LMs as a source of synthetic annotations
015            as they are more consistent, cheaper, and scale better than human annotation;
016
               however, they are also prone to biases and errors. In this work, we introduce a
               routing framework that combines inputs from humans and LMs to achieve better
017
               annotation quality, while reducing the total cost of human annotation. The crux
018
               of our approach is to identify preference instances that will benefit from human
019            annotations. We formulate this as an optimization problem: given a preference
020            dataset and an evaluation metric, we train a performance prediction model to
021            predict a reward model’s performance on an arbitrary combination of human and
022            LM annotations and employ a routing strategy that selects a combination that
023            maximizes predicted performance. We train the performance prediction model on
024            M ULTI P REF, a new preference dataset with 10K instances paired with human and
025            LM labels. We show that the selected hybrid mixture of LM and direct human
026            preferences using our routing framework achieves better reward model performance
027
               compared to using either one exclusively. We simulate selective human preference
               collection on three other datasets and show that our method generalizes well to
028
               all three. We analyze features from the routing model to identify characteristics
029
               of instances that can benefit from human feedback, e.g., prompts with a moderate
030            safety concern or moderate intent complexity. We release the dataset, annotation
031            platform, and source code used in this study to foster more efficient and accurate
032            preference collection in the future.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Reinforcement learning from human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017) has been integral to the
038   alignment of large language models (LMs) with human objectives and values (Ouyang et al., 2022;
039
      Bai et al., 2022a, inter alia). Central to this process are preference datasets, i.e., instances of inputs to
      language models paired with candidate model outputs and human judgment annotations indicating
040
      the preferred output. Collecting preference data involves several key design decisions, and one
041
      important consideration is determining the source of preference annotations (Kirk et al., 2023; 2024).
042   This choice impacts not only the cost and effort required to procure these annotations, but also the
043   performance of models trained on them.
044
045
      There are two major approaches to obtaining preference annotations. One approach is to solicit
      preferences directly from humans. Although this setup leads to generally high-quality data (Wang
046
      et al., 2024b), the annotation process itself is expensive and time-consuming. Moreover, human
047
      annotators can make mistakes, especially when faced with complex examples or when the content
048   extends beyond their expertise (Jiang & de Marneffe, 2022; Sandri et al., 2023). As an alternative,
049   preference annotation can be synthesized from LMs (Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2023; Cui et al.,
050   2023). This approach is scalable, as it only requires prompting an off-the-shelf LM for preference
051   annotations. However, LMs do not always reflect the nuances of human annotators and can be prone
052   to certain biases or errors in judgment (Singhal et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Hence, we posit that
053   obtaining high-quality and cost-efficient preference data involves finding the right combination of
      direct human preferences and synthetic preferences from LMs.

075
076
077   In this work, we present a routing framework that allocates preference instances to human or
078   LM annotators, resulting in a set of hybrid annotations (§2). The crux of our approach is to
079   identify specific instances that will benefit from direct human annotations, while the rest are routed
080   to the LM. We ground this decision in the performance of reward models trained on the resulting
081
      preference datasets, measured by RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). Our framework consists of a
      performance prediction model (PPM, §2.2) and a routing strategy (§2.3) as illustrated in Figure 1.
082
      The PPM learns to predict the performance of a preference dataset based on the statistics of the subset
083
      being routed to human annotators. We then use our trained model to predict the performance of
084   arbitrary simulated hybrid datasets, to recommend the potentially best-performing one.
085
086   To put this framework into practice, we first construct M ULTI P REF, a preference dataset containing
087
      10k instances paired with both human and LM preference annotations that follow the same carefully
      designed annotation guidelines (§3). Then, we train the PPM on this dataset and use the routing
088
      strategy to obtain hybrid annotations from either LMs or humans. We also evaluate the trained PPM
089
      on other existing preference datasets, including Helpsteer2 (Wang et al., 2024b), AlpacaFarm (Dubois
090   et al., 2023), and Chatbot Arena Conversations (ChatArena, Zheng et al. 2023a)on RewardBench
091   and other common LM benchmarks through best-of-N reranking. To obtain synthetic annotations
092   for other human preference datasets, we prompt an LM on the same annotation guidelines used for
093   human annotation. For instances that our routing framework designated for human annotation, we
094   use the original human annotations from these datasets.
095
      Our experiments show that hybrid annotations constructed from the router’s predictions result in better
096   reward models than those trained (a) entirely on direct human preferences, (b) entirely on synthetic
097   preferences, and (c) a random combination of direct human and synthetic preferences given the
098   same human annotation budget (§4), supporting our hypothesis that there exist optimal combinations
099   of annotations that are not exclusively direct human or synthetic. The superior performance of
100   reward models also generalizes beyond RewardBench and achieves better performance on common
101   LM benchmarks through best-of-N reranking (§4.3). The resulting hybrid preference datasets
102   outperform the corresponding original ones by a large margin, with 7–13% (absolute) improvement
103   on RewardBench and up to 3% (absolute) improvement on downstream evaluations on average,
104
      demonstrating the generalization of our routing framework. We then present an analysis of factors
      that render a preference instance to benefit from direct human annotations (§5).
105
106   We plan to publicly release all data and code associated with this work after the review period. We
107   hope that this work contributes to a more cost-effective approach to preference data collection while
      providing actionable, data-centric insights on preference learning.

110   2.1       P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
111
                                                                                 (i)   (i)
112   We first formulate the preference routing problem. Let D = {⟨x(i) , y1 , y2 ⟩}ni=1 be a dataset of
113   n unlabeled preference instances, where each instance can be assigned a label from either of the
114   two sources: one provided by a human annotator, or one generated by an LM. We introduce a
115   binary decision variable zi ∈ {0, 1} for each instance, where zi = 0 corresponds to selecting the
116   human-provided label and zi = 1 corresponds to selecting the LM-generated label. Note that zi
117
      denotes the source of the labels, and not the identity of the labels—when the humans and the LM
      agree, the chosen label is the same irrespective of the value of zi .
118
119   The goal for routing is to optimize the selection of binary decision variables zi for the dataset in order
120   to maximize a performance metric. This optimization problem can be expressed as:
121
122                                            max P ERF(R(D(z))),                                           (1)
123                                          z∈{0,1}n

124
      where P ERF(R(D(z))) is the performance of the RM trained on D(z). Here, z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zn } is
125   the routing configuration, representing the vector of binary label choices for all instances.
126
127   Maximizing Equation 1 is difficult as there is no closed-form solution. In addition, finding the
128
      best routing configuration is computationally heavy, as brute force search would entail training
      and evaluating a reward model for 2n configurations. So instead, we construct candidate labeled
129
130
      datasets D̂(z) with different routing configurations z which we use to train reward models, denoted
131
      as R̂(D̂(z)).1 We use these candidates to train a performance prediction model that approximates
132   P ERF(R̂(∗)) (§2.2). After training the model, we use a simulation-based routing strategy that aims
133
      to find the optimal z to maximize the predicted performance (§2.3).
134
135   2.2       P ERFORMANCE P REDICTION M ODEL (PPM)
136
      PPM is a regression model that provides an estimate of the performance of a reward model trained on
137
      a candidate preference dataset D̂. The PPM takes as input a feature vector representing the routing
138
      configuration of D̂ and outputs a scalar value as the predicted performance. Training the PPM requires
139
      a seed preference dataset D with both human and LM labels, and multiple samples of candidate
140
      datasets {D̂i } with different routing configurations and their actual evaluation performance.
141
142
      Step 1: Defining a feature space for the feature vector. Instead of directly operating on individual
143
      preference instances, we define a feature space for the PPM so that we can make routing decisions
144   about groups of instances that share features, allowing our routing procedure to generalize to other
145   datasets where these features might be present. We construct a feature space based on textual and
146   descriptive information (or tags T ) of a preference instance’s prompt-response triples. The full list of
147   tags can be found in Appendix A.3.
148
      • Textual tags characterize textual information such as the cosine similarity of the encoded repre-
149
        sentation2 of the responses y1 and y2 , the length of the prompt x, or the token length difference
150     between two responses. We discretize the textual tags to convert them into categorical bins.
151
      • Descriptive tags include metadata about the prompt or instruction such as the subject of expertise
152     needed to answer the prompt, or the complexity of user intent in the prompt based on the number of
153     goals or requirements among many others. We obtain these descriptors from a multilabel classifier
154     (or meta-analyzer) trained on a human-validated dataset of instructions and their corresponding
155     tags. More information about this meta-analyzer can be found in Appendix A.4.
156
      These tags are obtained at the instance level. We then represent the routing configuration of a
157
      candidate dataset as a vector v = {Ctj , human | tj ∈ T }, where Ctj , human denotes the count of
158
      instances routed to human annotations with the j th tag.

209   2.3   ROUTING STRATEGY BASED ON PPM
210
211   The goal of routing is to find the best routing configuration z ∗ = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zn } that will maximize
212   reward model performance P ERF(D̂(z ∗ )). We approach this by simulating many candidate datasets
213   and predict their performance using the PPM (Algorithm 1). Since the PPM allows us to approximate
214   the expected performance of any D̂i , we can simulate a large number of candidates and obtain their
215   performance without training actual reward models. Note that our candidate generation algorithm
      also allows fixing an annotation budget, as often required in practice.

      After predicting the performance of all simulated candidates, we take the candidate with highest
217   predicted RM performance and use its configuration z ∗ for routing. For each preference instance di
218   in D, we take the decision zi and route the instance to humans if zi = 0 and to LMs if zi = 1. In
219   practice we generate 500 samples from which we select the best routing configuration.

      4.1        D ETAILS OF THE PERFORMANCE PREDICTION MODEL
255
256   In order to train the PPM, we generate 200 candidates from M ULTI P REF and train reward models
257   using Tülu 2 13B (Ivison et al., 2023) as base. To test the PPM’s fit, we generate 16 held-out datasets
258   and compare the PPM’s predicted performance to the performance of an RM on RewardBench.
259   We measure the comparison using the root-mean-square error (RMSE) and the Spearman ρ correlation.
260
261   We train three types of regressors: a linear model, a quadratic model, and a tree-based model called
262
      LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017). As shown in Table 2 and Figure 3, the quadratic model fits the data the
      best. Hence, we use the quadratic model as our PPM for subsequent experiments.

426
427   In this section, we investigate the features learned by the PPM in order to understand characteristics
428   that render a preference instance a better fit for direct human annotation. To quantify the effect of
429   routing an instance to human annotators, we compute its expected performance gain. We define
430   gain by measuring the improvement in RM performance if a particular instance is routed to humans
431   for annotation. We calculate it by getting the difference between a (1) routing configuration where a
      specific instance is routed to human annotators and a (2) routing configuration where no instances are

433   shows the gain distribution in M ULTI P REF when routing each preference instance individually to

      tag’s condition (e.g., “BERTScore between two responses is ∈ [0.33, 0.67]”) and compute the gain

450   6    R ELATED W ORK
451
452
453
      Preference feedback for model training Modern LMs go through an RLHF (Reinforcement
      Learning from Human Feedback) training stage before deployment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al.,
454
      2022a, inter alia). This approach of preference feedback simplifies the annotation efforts for
455
      finetuning LMs and, meanwhile, can better capture the complex and model-dependent nuances that
456   may not be fully represented in supervised finetuning. Typically, such preference data is incorporated
457   into model training via either PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) that uses the preference data to train
458   a reward model (RM), which later is used to score model generations in an online RL setup, or
459   DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) that directly trains models based on the preferences. In this work, we
460   mainly focus on the RM part by directly evaluating RMs on RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) and
461   Best-of-N reranking performance (Ivison et al., 2024).
462
463
464   Data mixing and selection in LM training. Data mixing and selection have emerged as critical
465   components in the large language model (LM) training pipeline (Albalak et al., 2024). Various
466   studies have addressed these challenges in different stages of the LM training process, particularly
467   in pretraining (Xie et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024, inter alia) and supervised fine-tuning (Wang et al.,
468   2023a; Lu et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024, inter alia). A notable contribution by Ivison et al. (2024)
469   evaluates the impact of different preference datasets during the RLHF training stage and finds that
470
      synthetic preference data (Cui et al., 2023) outperforms human preference datasets available at the
      time. However, their study relied on existing datasets that vary significantly in aspects such as prompt
471
      distribution and response generation models. Our work introduces a novel routing framework aimed
472
      at optimizing in the preference label space, featuring an automated algorithm to select the appropriate
473   annotation source, utilizing human input only when necessary. In this regard, our approach aligns
474   with the active learning paradigm, which seeks to achieve comparable or superior model performance
475   with fewer human labeled examples (Cohn et al., 1994; Settles, 2009).
476
477
478
      Performance Prediction Our routing framework relies on a performance prediction model (PPM)
479
      to predict the performance metric given a dataset. This problem has been studied before based on
480   various factors (Birch et al., 2008; Xia et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2021). Our work has a special focus
481   on the data perspective, particularly in the label space. Our approach to predicting model behavior
482   based on the underlying dataset it is trained on shares similar thoughts to datamodels (Ilyas et al.,
483   2022; Engstrom et al., 2024), but we use a denser tag-based feature vector to represent the data
484   and our objective is to predict the performance metric rather than the direct model outputs. Our
485   simulation-based routing strategy, given the PPM, is inspired by Liu et al. (2024), which studies
      domain mixing in the pretraining stage.

      7    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We propose a routing framework for preference learning that allocates instances to human annotators
489   or an LM by identifying a subset that benefits from human annotation. Our results suggest that the
490   hybrid mix from our routing framework outperforms both 100% human and 100% LM annotations
491   on RewardBench and achieves better performance on common LM benchmarks through best-of-N
492   reranking for unseen preference datasets. Moreover, our routing framework also outperforms random
493   sampling for a given set of human annotation budgets. We also leverage the routing framework
494   to identify key characteristics that render an instance benefit more from human annotations: high
495   similarity between responses, prompts that require human expertise and knowledge, and prompts that
496   fall under select subject areas to name a few. We plan to release the routing model, datasets, code,
497
      and annotation platform used in this study after the review period and hope that our work contributes
      to data-centric approaches in understanding human preferences.
498
499
500   8    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
501
502   Grounding of preference feedback quality. Quality control is critical for human data annotation,
503   especially in the modern era of building LMs. Typically, researchers use agreement as a metric for
504   quality. However, for preference annotation, early works all ended up with relatively low agreement
505   between annotators or even between annotators and researchers (Bai et al., 2022a; Touvron et al.,
506
      2023; Dubois et al., 2023). This is largely due to the complexity of the tasks (e.g., many facts to
      verify, the expertise required, etc.), as well as the subjectivity in many cases (e.g., style preference,
507
      sensitive topics, safety threshold, etc.). This poses challenges for the data annotation process, as there
508
      is no ground truth for measuring the quality. In this work, we decide to ground the data quality into
509   the model training performance (i.e., the utility of the data), and our framework can optimize towards
510   this goal. Future work can explore other downstream utility metrics for optimization.
511
512   Scaling the size of preference annotation. Although we show the successful generalization of our
513   router when applying it to other preference datasets (§4.2, this set of experiments is done at the same
514   size (7K after removing ties). It remains unclear how well our performance prediction model can
515   extrapolate beyond the training data size and predict what instances can add performance gain after
516   7K, so that we can keep growing our preference data to a larger size. We believe our current results
517   and the patterns we find (§5) can provide insights on how to save human efforts, but a systematic
518
      scaling of our framework may require future work.
519
520
      Feedback beyond pairwise comparisons. We focus on pairwise preferences which compare overall
      model responses. However, several formulations of preference feedback exist such as fine-grained
521
      preferences (Wu et al., 2024), aspect-based preferences (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024b, also available in
522
      M ULTI P REF) and preferences for process-reward models (Lightman et al., 2023; Uesato et al., 2022).
523   These annotations are more time consuming, hence, even more expensive, thus providing more room
524   for leveraging LM annotation when possible. We leave this exploration for future work.
525
526   Generalization to downstream DPO / policy model performance. While hybrid preference anno-
527   tations improve direct RM evaluation performance, it’s unclear if these gains extend to downstream
528   tasks when training a DPO model or a policy model using PPO with the reward models. Ivison et al.
529   (2024) found that improvements in reward models do not necessarily translate to improved down-
530   stream performance in PPO, as there are many confounding factors (e.g., the unlabeled prompts in
531   PPO, the KL penalty, etc) that impact the PPO training. We tried testing the preference datasets using
532   DPO (Appendix A.11) but only found small differences when switching datasets or the preference
533
      mixes. We hypothesize that downstream task performance is hard to measure (and still is an open
      research problem), and requires data collection at a larger scale to see significant effects.

       A.6    I NFERENCE - TIME SELECTION STRATEGIES
1567
1568   After training the regressor, we experimented with several selection strategies to obtain the final subset
1569   to route to human annotators during inference. Tables 10 and 11 show the results for each selection
1570   strategy for different human preference datasets. In general, we find that simulated sampling
1571   consistently leads to better RewardBench performance than top-k sampling for both models.
1572
1573          • Top-k gain: for each instance, we compute the gain and take the top-k instances based on a
1574
                given annotation budget. The gain computation depends on the model. For linear models,
                we perform a dot product between the linear regressor weights and a binary representation
1575
                of an instances’s features. For quadratic models, we compute the predicted performance
1576
                difference between routing a single instance to humans and swapping no instance.
1577
1578
              • Simulated: we simulate unseen subsets similar to how we generated candidate datasets
                during training. Then, we predict the performance of each simulated dataset using the
1579
                trained regressor. We take the dataset with the highest predicted performance and then use
1580
                that as the final subset.

       A.8                      B EST- OF -N EVALUATION DETAILS
1675
1676   Best-of-N evaluation converts existing LM benchmarks into a reranking format by using a model
1677   to generate multiple completions for each instance in the original benchmark, and testing whether
1678   reward models can identify the completion that, if selected, will improve the performance according
1679   to the original benchmark metrics.
1680   We mainly follow the setup introduced in Ivison et al. (2024), and we adopt the following benchmarks
1681   to cover a wide variety of capabilities.
1682
1683                            • GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) for math reasoning. We report the “exact match” metric.
1684                            • BIG-Bench Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022) for various types of reasoning. We report
1685                              the “exact match” metric.
1686
                                • IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023) for precise instruction following. We report their “prompt-level
1687
                                  loose accuracy” metric.
1688
1689
                                • Codex HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) for coding. We report the “pass@1” metric.
1690                            • AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023a) for general chat capabilities. We use their version 1 and report
1691                              the “win_rate” metric, judged by GPT4.
1692
1693
       To accelerate the evaluation, for BBH, we randomly sample 50 instances for each subtask, resulting
       in a final set of 1350 instances. For other benchmarks, we capped the number of instances at 1K.
1694
       We sample 16 responses from TÜLU 2 13B with a TEMPERATURE of 0.7 and a TOP _ P of 1 for
1695
       each evaluation task we examine. We then pass these responses (along with the prompt used for
1696   generation) into the a given reward model, and use the top-scoring response as the final output to
1697   compute the corresponding metrics.

       A.11        D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION RESULTS
1783
1784   Other than evaluating different preference datasets in terms of their reward modeling performance,
1785   we also tried training models using direct preference optimization (DPO, Rafailov et al. (2023)) and
1786   see if they the final LM can be improved.
1787
       Our DPO experiments are based off a Llama-3 8B model (Dubey et al., 2024) finetuned with T ÜLU-2
1788   SFT data (Ivison et al., 2023) to get a reasonable initial policy. We use the same set of hyperparameters
1789   as is used in (Ivison et al., 2024). We report the performance on a few benchmarks that benefit from
1790   DPO training, following the setups in (Ivison et al., 2024).
1791
       Table 17 shows the results for our best hybrid preference mix, random mix baselines with different
1792
       fractions of human data, and the base SFT model. Although we see that our best hybrid mix generally
1793
       remains within the high-rank range, but the differences between different mixes are relatively small.
1794   We suspect this is because in DPO training, the learning rate is quite low (LR = 5e − 07), and the
1795   KL regularization prevents the policy from moving away from the base SFT weights. This, combined
1796   with our relatively small data size, may not lead to significant changes in terms of the final model
1797   performance. Therefore, we use reward model performance in the main paper to evaluate preference
1798   datasets.
