009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            How can we harness the collective capabilities of multiple Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) to create an even more powerful model? This question forms the foundation
013            of our research, where we propose an innovative approach to weak-to-strong (w2s)
014            generalization—a critical problem in AI alignment. Our work introduces an easy-
015            to-hard (e2h) framework for studying the feasibility of w2s generalization, where
016
               weak models trained on simpler tasks collaboratively supervise stronger models
               on more complex tasks. This setup mirrors real-world challenges, where direct
017
               human supervision is limited. To achieve this, we develop a novel AdaBoost-
018
               inspired ensemble method, demonstrating that an ensemble of weak supervisors
019            can enhance the performance of stronger LLMs across classification and generative
020            tasks on difficult QA datasets. In several cases, our ensemble approach matches the
021            performance of models trained on ground-truth data, establishing a new benchmark
022            for w2s generalization. We observe an improvement of up to 14% over existing
023            baselines and average improvements of 5% and 4% for binary classification and
024            generative tasks, respectively. This research points to a promising direction for
025            enhancing AI through collective supervision, especially in scenarios where labeled
026            data is sparse or insufficient.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), continue to surpass human performance
031   in various domains, a pressing challenge arises: how do we effectively supervise models that exceed
032   our capabilities? This problem, known as super-alignment, is exacerbated by the scarcity of high-
033
      quality labeled data, which limits direct human oversight. The key question driving our work is
      whether weak models, trained on simpler tasks, can be leveraged to instruct and improve stronger
034
      models in complex settings—a problem known as weak-to-strong (w2s) generalization.
035
036   The concept of w2s generalization was introduced by Burns et al. (2023), where weak models are
037   used to align stronger models in the absence of sufficient ground-truth supervision. However, while
038   this work laid the groundwork, it left several critical challenges unresolved. (C1) Single Weak
039
      Supervisor Limitation. Prior studies (Burns et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024; Charikar et al., 2024; Lang
      et al., 2024) tend to rely on a single weak supervisor, limiting the diversity and robustness of the
040
      supervision. A single model’s perspective often falls short when attempting to instruct stronger
041
      models in more complex tasks, highlighting the need for a more diversified supervisory approach.
042   (C2) Lack of Focus on Weak Model Enhancement. Another limitation is that previous research
043   (Burns et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024; Charikar et al., 2024; Lang et al., 2024) has focused predominantly
044   on improving knowledge transfer from weak to strong models without addressing how to enhance the
045   weak models themselves. This oversight leaves weak models under-optimized, thereby restricting
046   their utility in complex problem settings. (C3) Overlooking Task Complexity. Furthermore, while
047   task complexity plays a crucial role in determining how well weak models can supervise stronger
048   ones, most prior work (Sun et al., 2024) has not adequately addressed this issue. For instance, Burns
049   et al. (2023) briefly explored the impact of task complexity using chess data, but a more structured
050
      and systematic approach is needed to differentiate between easy and hard tasks and study their effects
      on supervision.
051
052   To address these challenges, we propose a novel ensemble-based method designed to improve w2s
053   generalization. Central to our approach is an easy-to-hard (e2h) framework, which extends w2s
      generalization by focusing on the progression from simpler tasks (easy) to more complex tasks (hard).

      This mirrors practical scenarios, where human oversight is more feasible for simpler tasks, and weak
055   models must step in to guide stronger models in tackling harder tasks. In this setting, weak models
056   trained on easy data supervise stronger models working on more difficult problems, creating a more
057   pragmatic approach to w2s generalization.
058
      To further enhance the capabilities of weak models, we develop a novel AdaBoost-inspired ensemble
059
      method for generation tasks, in addition to classification tasks. By combining the supervision of
060
      multiple weak models, we create a more robust and effective supervisory system for stronger LLMs.
061   This ensemble approach overcomes the limitations of single-supervisor systems and introduces a
062   mechanism to refine the weak models themselves, ensuring they can provide meaningful guidance
063   even in complex tasks. Our experiments demonstrate that this ensemble method not only improves
064   the weak models’ generalization capabilities but also enables stronger models to achieve performance
065   on par with oracle models trained on high-quality data.
066
      The main contributions of this paper are the following:
067   (1) We introduce an ensemble method inspired by AdaBoost, combining weak LLMs to provide
068   stronger supervision for training stronger models. Our approach is validated through experiments
069   on binary classification tasks, where we observe improvements of up to 14% over baselines and an
070   average improvement of 7% across all model pairs, showcasing the feasibility of w2s generalization.
071   (2) We extend this framework to supervised fine-tuning tasks for autoregressive LLMs, where
072   our novel algorithm combines weak LLMs via a voting mechanism that adjusts token probabilities.
073   In several cases, we observe our strong model trained using weak labels to outperform the strong
074   model trained on ground truth, thus enabling effective supervision, even on complex tasks.
075
      (3) We propose a practical easy-to-hard (e2h) framework for w2s generalization, where models
      trained on easy data provide supervision for harder tasks. This setup emphasizes the importance of
076
      task complexity and demonstrates significant improvements when weak models guide strong LLMs.
077
      For our EnsemW2S method, along with observing w2s-trained student models outperforming the
078   strong student oracle in several e2h generalization scenarios, we also observe accuracy improvements
079   of up to 10% over baselines and an average improvement of 3.34% and 4.4% for Quartz and ARC
080   data respectively.

      2.1   T HE E ASY- TO -H ARD F RAMEWORK
115
116
      Weak Model hθ as the Teacher. A state-of-the-art LLM hθ is trained on a set of ‘easy data’ that we
117   currently have access to labels, i.e., (xe , y e ). For example, this could be Go games, math problems,
118   or common sense reasoning questions that we have solutions for. This ‘weak teacher’ is trained on the
119   labeled easy data (xe , y e ). Although we refer to this model as a “weak teacher”, it is only relatively
120   weak compared to the strong model we aim to obtain. Moreover, the “easy data” is only relatively
121   easy compared to the hard data for which we currently lack solutions. Thus, the easy data may not be
122   simple but slightly easier than the hard data, which are currently unsolvable using existing models.
123   Strong Model uϕ as the Upper Bound. As an important part of our thought experiment, we establish
124   an upper bound, which is not attainable in practice. Specifically, we assume access to the ground-truth
125   labels of the hard data (xh , y h ), which is impractical but establishes an upper bound for this thought
126   experiment. A model uϕ , larger than the weak teacher hθ , is trained on the labeled hard data (xh , y h ).
127   The reason why uϕ is larger than hθ is that we believe a model strong enough to solve hard questions
128   that no existing models can solve will require high capacity.
129   Weak-to-Strong Model fϕ Obtained in Practice. To test the weak-to-strong generalization, we
130   will train a weak-to-strong transfer model fϕ that has the same capacity as the strong model, i.e.,
131   the same model size as uϕ , but is not trained under the unrealistic assumption of oracle access to
132   hard labels. Rather, it is trained using weak teacher’s feedback. Specifically, we consider using the
133   pseudo-labeled (xh , hθ (xh ) as training data for training the weak-to-strong transfer model fϕ .

135   2.2 E ASY AND H ARD DATA
136   Dataset and Setup. We use the SciQ dataset (Welbl et al., 2017) for the binary classification task. It
137   is a multiple-choice science question-answer dataset and is also used as one of the NLP classification
138   datasets by Burns et al. (2023). We convert it into binary labels following (Burns et al., 2023). For
139
      the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) task on the Q/A dataset, we use ARC (Clark et al., 2018) and Quartz
      (Tafjord et al., 2019) datasets, which are also multiple-choice question-answer datasets, allowing
140
      us to generate multiple-choice pseudo labels. Ding et al. (2024) provide difficulty levels for some
141
      common mathematics and programming problems, chess puzzles, and reasoning question datasets,
142   which can be further utilized to expand this work.
143
144   Easy (xe , y e ) and Hard (xh , y h ) Data Split. To generate difficulty ratings for our datasets, we
145
      employ the n-fold cross-validation method. We train the model on the (n − 1) out of n splits of the
      data and test on the remaining split. We repeat the process n times with different splits for testing
146
      each time and aggregate the errors. We use this error value for each sample as its difficulty rating.
147
      We split the low difficulty-rated data for weak model training and use the high difficulty-rated data to
148   generate strong model training data and testing data randomly. We follow the same cross-validation
149   method, with different training protocols, for generating difficulty for both binary classification and
150   generation tasks. More details and our difficulty rating plots can be seen in Figures 8, 9, and 10 in the
151   Appendix.

      2.3   A N E NSEMBLE OF T EACHERS
154
155
      In a practical situation, we may face a dearth of strong supervisors but have an abundance of weak
156   supervisors. Previous works (Burns et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024) have used only one weak supervisor.
157   Our work aims to combine the power of multiple weak supervisors to provide stronger supervision
158   for better weak-to-strong (w2s) generalization. However, combining multiple weak supervisors to
159   improve w2s generalization is challenging. In the following section, we detail how to combine a
160   collection of weak teachers with diverse skill sets to obtain a competitive weak-to-strong model that
161   is better than the weak model and ideally reaches or even surpasses the strong model, i.e., the upper
      bound of performance.

193
      ensemble of feedback in the form of scores. However, LLMs are generative AI models known
194   for their remarkable ability to generate coherent, free-form text. Applying the vanilla AdaBoost
195   algorithm directly to generation tasks is challenging because (1) the output is not just a single class
196   label but a sequence of text with no fixed length, and (2) different teachers may generate answers in
197   various formats, making it non-trivial to combine their responses.

      By combining the outputs of multiple teachers, each trained in different EnsemW2S rounds, the
      ensemble approach leverages diverse perspectives from the weak models. Each teacher contributes
279
      its learned strengths, and through weighted aggregation, we diminish the influence of models that
280
      are less confident or less effective on certain tokens. This helps reduce variance in the generation
281   process, ensuring that errors from individual weak models are mitigated. The result is a more robust
282   pseudo-labeling system that is better aligned with the true distribution of the hard data, often yielding
283   a performance improvement over any single weak model.
284
      Unlike classification, where scores are combined over a fixed set of classes, generation tasks involve
285
      predicting sequences of tokens, where each prediction affects future ones. This makes combining
286
      generation probabilities more complex, as errors in early token predictions can propagate throughout
287   the sequence. Additionally, we are aggregating probability distributions over large vocabularies,
288   which introduces computational overhead and potential numerical instability.
289
290
      Our method addresses these challenges by using a weighted combination of teacher models’ token
      probabilities, ensuring that weaker predictions from individual rounds are minimized. By normalizing
291
      the aggregated distribution for each token, we maintain valid probability distributions across the
292
      vocabulary, effectively reducing the risk of cascading errors during autoregressive generation. This
293   ensemble approach results in a more stable and accurate generation process, mitigating the issues
294   inherent in sequence modeling.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
320   Weak-to-Strong (Burns et al., 2023) was the first to introduce the problem of weak-to-strong
321   generalization for the super-alignment problem, where the ultimate aim is to elicit the full capabilities
322   of the strong model using supervision only from weak models. (Charikar et al., 2024) provides a
323   theoretical framework for the same with insights on how much w2s improvement can occur, though
      their work is limited to a few layer neural networks. Similarly, (Lang et al., 2024) provides bounds


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      on expansion properties using finite data distributions for when w2s generalization will happen, but
325   only for simple binary classification tasks. (Zhang et al., 2024) proves that transcendence (exceeding
326   the capability of the model that generates the training data) is possible for low-temperature sampling.
327   Although this setting is not exactly w2s, it sheds light on this direction.
328
      Several works have attempted to solve w2s generalization in LLMs. (Sang et al., 2024) tries to
329
      improve this supervision using ensemble learning and scalable oversight for binary classification NLP
330
      tasks but cannot observe significant improvement. (Ji et al., 2024) introduces a model that enhances
331   the alignment of LLMs with human intentions by correcting the residual differences between aligned
332   and unaligned answers by training on a query-answer correction dataset. This method boosts w2s
333   generalization using supervisory signal from smaller models to improve the performance of complex
334   systems. In (Sun et al., 2024), the authors propose a scalable approach for e2h generalization which
335   involves training reward models on easier tasks and using them to evaluate performance on harder
336   tasks. (Liu & Alahi, 2024) introduces a method similar to the classical hierarchical mixture of experts,
337   where multiple specialized weak supervisors are used for weak-to-strong generalization instead of a
338   single generalist model. (Bansal et al., 2024) compares large LLM training from data generated using
339
      weak (cheap) vs strong (expensive) model in a compute matching way and finds larger data from
      weaker model to provide better w2s.
340
341   Ensemble Learning Binary Classification Boosting (Freund & Schapire, 1997) and multi-
342   classfication boosting (Hastie et al., 2009) are common ensemble learning algorithms. In (Verga et al.,
343   2024), they use a voting mechanism to combine multiple small LLMs instead of a single large LLM
344   to evaluate another LLM and show it performs better than large LLMs. An extended related work
345
      section is present in Appendix A.

349   training data randomly into train-weak and train-strong. Train-weak is used to train the weak model.

528     6                     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
529    Conclusion: This paper aims to stimulate discussion on the more holistic problem of weak-to-strong
530    generalization by emphasizing easy-to-hard generalization. We develop a new AdaBoost-inspired
531    algorithm and conduct a thought experiment on how to combine the "wisdom of the crowd" to
532    improve w2s generalization. We are first to focus on the idea of making the weaks less weak using
533    an ensemble, and test our method for binary classification and Q/A-based SFT task. Our method in
534    some cases recovers full strong model capability.
535    Limitation and Future Work: This work only explores the supervised fine-tuning phase. While SFT
536    is an important part of the LLM learning pipeline, our future work will focus on developing weak
537    supervision in the reward modeling phase. Another interesting future direction would be to improve
538    the combination of tokens in the decoding phase by replacing the classical AdaBoost algorithm
539    with more adaptive ensemble learning methods. We hope this work sparks discussion on combining
       multiple LLMs to improve weak-to-strong generalization.

653   Weak-to-Strong Generalisation: (Continue from the main manuscript) Guo et al. (2024) in-
654   troduces an dynamic adjustable loss function for weak-to-strong supervision. Hase et al. (2024)
655   demonstrates that current language models can achieve high performance on difficult tasks by training
656   on simpler, cleanly labeled data, thus avoiding the high costs and noise associated with hard data
657   labeling. None of these works focused on making the weak teachers, less weak but only focus on
658   improving transfer learning and correction of weak labels. Thus, our method can be combined with
659   all ideas focused on improving transfer learning.

      Multi-LLM learning: There are numerous works involving the collaboration of multiple LLMs.
      Chang et al. (2023) proposes Reinforcement Learning with Guided Feedback (RLGF), where a
667
      dynamic black-box guide like GPT-3 is used to fine-tune large language models. Rosset et al. (2024)
668
      introduces Direct Nash Optimization (DNO), a scalable algorithm that combines contrastive learning
669   with general preference optimization. Cai et al. (2024) presents MEDUSA, an innovative framework
670   designed to accelerate inference in large language models by introducing multiple decoding heads,
671   enabling simultaneous prediction of several tokens, and enhancing efficiency through reduced decod-
672   ing steps and parallel processing capabilities. Shen et al. (2024) proposes Co-LLM, a collaborative
673   decoding framework that interleaves token-level generations from multiple models. This method
674   optimizes the latent variable model for marginal likelihood, allowing a base model to decide when to
675   generate tokens itself or utilize an assistant model, thereby improving performance across various
676   specialized tasks without direct supervision. Jin et al. (2024) introduces a novel collaborative decod-
677
      ing framework aimed at improving the factuality of large language models by employing a critical
      token classifier. This approach strategically uses both pre-trained and aligned models to selectively
678
      generate critical tokens, significantly enhancing the model’s ability to maintain factual accuracy
679
      without compromising the diversity of the generated content.
680
681   Additionally, Mudgal et al. (2023) introduces Controlled Decoding (CD), a method for aligning
682   language model outputs with desired outcomes using a separate prefix scorer module. This approach
683
      allows multi-objective RL without additional training and performs well on benchmarks, bridging the
      gap between token-level control and sequence-level best-of sampling strategies.

      The calculation of α cannot rely solely on error, ϵ, as the traditional Adaboost method is valid only
      when ϵ < 0.5. Applying the same equation in our context could yield negative α values. We introduce
847
      a prior term, log( 1−ϵ1pre − 1), inspired from multi-class classification Adaboost works Hastie et al.
848
      (2009), to address this issue.
849
850   Existing works on multi-class classification Adaboost Hastie et al. (2009) suggest using 1c (where c is
851   the number of classes) in the prior term, log(c − 1), as 1c represents the random performance of the
852   model. However, when c (the number of classes) becomes very large, the log(c − 1) term also grows
853   significantly, causing the α parameters of Adaboost to become nearly identical and, consequently,
854   less useful. To address this, we introduce a pre-training error term, ϵpre , which represents an upper
855   bound on the sample error. We then use 1 − ϵpre (a lower bound on accuracy) as a replacement for
856   the 1c term, as our model’s lowest possible accuracy is 1 − ϵpre , not 1c .

1440   Training Cost of Weak Learners: Each weak learner is trained sequentially, as its performance is
1441   contingent upon the outputs of the preceding weak learner. Consequently, while the GPU load may
1442   be lower, the overall training time is directly proportional to the number of weak learners utilized.
1443
       This is because the input and output token count for each weak learner during training remains
1444
       approximately constant, as suggested by Adaboost. Only the frequency of samples are adjusted based
1445
       on weights. In EnsembleW2S we sample the tokens by token-weights but eventually combine the
1446   sampled tokens while masking the ones not sampled, thus keeping the total tokens approximately
1447   similar and training time for each weak-learner independent of the tokens sampled. In the practical
1448   superalignment case, pre-trained weak learners will be used, which may mitigate concerns regarding
1449   training time.
1450
1451
1452
1453   Inference Cost of Weak Learners: The generation process can be executed in parallel as well as
1454
       sequentially, resulting in a GPU load for generation or clock time for generation respectively, that
       scales linearly with the number of weak learners. For decoding, once the token-level distributions
1455
       generated by the weak learners are combined using EmsemW2S algorithm, efficient decoding
1456
       algorithms can be employed to produce the final response. However, this is not the focus of this work.

1488   models (LLMs) has significant implications across various domains. By demonstrating that multiple

1490   challenge of superalignment, potentially transforming how advanced AI systems are developed and
1491   supervised. This approach could democratize access to powerful AI technologies by reducing reliance
1492   on scarce, high-quality labeled data and enabling more inclusive participation in AI development.
