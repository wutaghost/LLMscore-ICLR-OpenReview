011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI), especially Large Language Models
014            (LLMs), into the clinical diagnosis process offers significant potential to improve
015            the efficiency and accessibility of medical care. While LLMs have shown some
016
               promise in the medical domain, their application in clinical diagnosis remains un-
               derexplored, especially in real-world clinical practice, where highly sophisticated,
017
               patient-specific decisions need to be made. Current evaluations of LLMs in this
018
               field are often narrow in scope, focusing on specific diseases or specialties and em-
019            ploying simplified diagnostic tasks. To bridge this gap, we introduce C LI B ENCH,
020            a novel benchmark developed from the MIMIC IV dataset, offering a compre-
021            hensive and realistic assessment of LLMs’ capabilities in clinical diagnosis. This
022            benchmark not only covers diagnoses from a diverse range of medical cases across
023            various specialties but also incorporates tasks of clinical significance: treatment
024            procedure identification, lab test ordering and medication prescriptions. Supported
025            by structured output ontologies, C LI B ENCH enables a precise and multi-granular
026            evaluation, offering an in-depth understanding of LLM’s capability on diverse
027
               clinical tasks of desired granularity. We conduct a zero-shot evaluation of leading
               LLMs to assess their proficiency in clinical decision-making. Our preliminary re-
028
               sults shed light on the potential and limitations of current LLMs in clinical settings,
029
               providing valuable insights for future advancements in LLM-powered healthcare.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      An accurate diagnosis is pivotal for delivering effective medical care, involving the identification of
035
      diseases and therapeutic management based on a comprehensive analysis of patient demographics,
036   symptoms, medical history, and diagnostic test results. It is a complex cognitive process that requires
037   extensive medical knowledge, reasoning, and experience. In the era of digital healthcare, building
038   AI systems that can automate or assist clinicians in this process with high accuracy has profound
039   implications for reducing healthcare costs and enhancing the accessibility of medical expertise.
040
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Anil et al., 2023) have
041
      opened up new possibilities. They have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in text understanding,
042   reasoning, and generating proper responses in various domains (Ma et al., 2024b), including the
043   medical field (Singhal et al., 2023a;b; Ma et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Specifically, LLMs have
044   been thoroughly evaluated and shown to excel in medical licensing examinations (Nori et al., 2023b;
045   Gilson et al., 2023) and medical knowledge QA benchmarks (Singhal et al., 2023b; Nori et al., 2023a;
046   Toma et al., 2023). However, the application of LLMs in clinical diagnosis represents a more nuanced
047   and realistic challenge, requiring not only the understanding of medical knowledge but also the ability
048   to make complex clinical decisions based on real-life patient-specific scenarios (McDuff et al., 2023;
049   Tu et al., 2024).
050   Nevertheless, prior evaluations of LLMs about clinical diagnosis capabilities reveal significant
051   constraints. Most research has either concentrated on diagnosing singular diseases (Kwon et al.,
052   2023; Hager et al., 2024) or restricted its focus to certain medical specialties (Allahqoli et al., 2023;
053   Krusche et al., 2024), lacking the breadth necessary for general clinical practice. With the small
      coverage of disease types, such studies often employ simplified task formats, requiring the model to

054
      choose a diagnosis from binary or multiple choices of diagnostic candidates, reducing the intricacy of
055   real-world clinical decision-making. Moreover, most studies predominantly target at evaluating the
056   performance of LLM in diagnostic predictions (Takita et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024c), while omitting
057   the other critical clinical decisions for thorough patient care, such as the ordering of various lab tests
058   and the arrangement of all follow-up treatment procedures. Table 1 shows a comprehensive list of
059   current studies of LLMs in clinical diagnosis and their limitations as mentioned earlier.
060
      To address these gaps, we introduce a novel benchmark, C LI B ENCH, aimed at a more accurate and
061   inclusive assessment of LLMs’ capabilities within the realm of clinical diagnosis. Meticulously
062   curated from the MIMIC IV dataset (Johnson et al., 2023), our benchmark spans a broad spectrum of
063   cases across various specialties, enriched by a connection to a structured expert-curated diagnosis
064   ontology, the ICD-10-CM coding (icd, 2023a) for precise and hierarchical evaluation. Moreover,
065   our benchmark extends beyond mere diagnostic capabilities, challenging LLMs to also recommend
066   treatment procedures, formulate lab test orders and prescribe medications adhered to the ICD-10-
067   PCS (icd, 2023b) ontology, LOINC coding system and ATC classification system. Furthermore, we
068   provide a dataset construction pipeline that is designed to support not only evaluation but also the
069
      generation of training data, facilitating ongoing training-based improvements in model performance.
070   We conduct experiments using zero-shot configurations of prominent open-sourced and close-sourced
071   LLMs, covering both general-purpose and medical-domain models, to assess their clinical decision-
072   making performance. Our evaluation is hierarchical, reflecting the complexity and multi-faceted
073   nature of clinical diagnosis. Preliminary results from these experiments highlight the strengths and
074   weaknesses of current LLMs in making clinical decisions, offering insights into areas for further
075
      research and development.

077   2    R ELATED WORKS
078
079   Clinical capabilities benchmarks for LLMs. One of the primary benchmarks for evaluating
080   LLMs in clinical settings is their mastery of medical knowledge, particularly in the task format of
081
      question-answering. The widely-used MultiMedQA (Singhal et al., 2022) benchmark suite comprises
      seven medical QA benchmark datasets: MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) and MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022)
082
      consists of multiple choice questions from medical exams, PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) sources
083
      research questions from PubMed literature, subsets of MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) covers some
084   medically relevant topics, and LiveQA (Abacha et al.), MedicationQA (Abacha et al., 2019), and
085   HealthSearchQA (Singhal et al., 2022) was curated from commonly asked consumer questions.
086   These benchmarks from medical exams, research papers, and common questions, though effective in
087   assessing the general medical knowledge of LLMs, significantly differ from the daily complexities
088   and importance of clinical decision-making faced by clinicians in real-life cases.
089
      Evaluation of LLMs on clinical diagnosis. A growing body of studies has explored the capabilities
090   of LLMs in clinical diagnosis, each with diverse target scopes and task configurations. In Table 1, we
091   comprehensively compare the current evaluation studies of LLM’s diagnostic performance, listing
092   the heterogeneity and limitations across studies.
093
      The literature predominantly centers on evaluations with the Focus of Medical Specialties on either
094
      a single disease such as Alzheimer’s Disease (Kwon et al., 2023), Appendicitis, Cholecystitis, Diver-
095

096   et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Knebel et al., 2023; Rojas-Carabali et al., 2023; Madadi et al., 2023;
097   Sorin et al., 2023), Neurology (Horiuchi et al., 2024; Galetta & Meltzer, 2023; Koga et al., 2023), and
098   Dermatology (Stoneham et al., 2023; Rundle et al., 2024; Ravipati et al., 2023). A few works conduct
099   their evaluation containing cases from multiple specialties, but the Dataset Size (# Test Cases) of
100   these works is often less than 100, hardly reaching 500, resulting in limited coverage of disease types.

102   disease spectrum and fail to mirror the wide breadth needed for general clinical practice.

106   et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023). These task settings largely reduce the complexity of the real-life

      responses, but they mainly require extensive manual checking at the expert level to evaluate the

142   3         C LI B ENCH : MULTIFACETED BENCHMARK FOR CLINICAL DECISIONS
143
      We introduce C LI B ENCH, a multi-specialty clinical decision evaluation benchmark covering diagnosis,
144
      procedure, lab test order, and prescription on real clinical cases (Section 3.1). We perform cross-
145
      dataset cross-table data extraction with NLP pipeline and human verification (Section 3.2), diversity-
146   assured evaluation set sampling (Section 3.3), task-specific prompt construction (Section 3.4), flexible
147   natural language output to label matching (Section 3.5) while scoring models under multi-granular
148   settings of various difficulties (Section 3.6).
149
150   3.1          R EALISTIC CLINICAL DECISION TASKS WITH EXPERT- DEFINED OUTPUT SPACES
151   We introduce four clinical decision tasks: deciding diagnoses, identifying procedures, ordering lab
152   tests and prescribing medications. To reconstruct clinicians’ decision processes, different sets of
153   information are provided as input for each task, and the target output space is also task-specific. We
154   show a table laying out the comparisons and examples in Appendix B.1.
155
      Task 1: discharge diagnoses. Diagnosis is defined as the identification of a disease, condition, or
156   injury based on a patient’s health evidence. The task aims to provide a set of diagnoses according
157   to the patient profile, medical record at admission, lab test results within the admission, radiology
158   results within the admission and history diagnoses. Each diagnosis is represented in the International
159   Classification of Diseases, tenth Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-10-CM) code or equivalent
160   concepts(icd, 2023a), which is a coding system used by healthcare providers to classify all diagnoses
161   for claims processing. The history diagnoses are necessary for completeness because diagnoses made
      in previous admissions or other service departments might be inherited.


                                                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Task 2: procedures. Procedures are specific courses of action, to be implemented to intervene in the
163   patient’s health status. The task aims to identify the first batch (defined in Appendix B.6) of procedure
164   decisions after the patient is admitted. The input contains patient profile and medical record at admis-
165   sion. The expected output is a set of ICD-10-Procedure Coding System or equivalent concepts (icd,
166   2023b). Within a certain admission, procedure decisions, lab test orders and prescriptions can be
167   made at any time, where the later decisions are made while the clinician is aware of outcomes and
168   results of previous procedures or lab tests. It is hard to obtain ground-truth non-initial decisions since
169   the actions can be taken in different temporal orders while only the outcomes of the factual action
170   order are available, which motivates us to predict only the first batch of decisions in terms of time.
171   Task 3: lab test orders. With the same input as procedure decisions, the task aims to produce a set
172   of initial lab items after the patient is admitted to facilitate downstream diagnosis and treatment. Each
173   lab item is a unique Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes (LOINC) code.
174
      Task 4: prescriptions. Given the same input as the procedure decisions, the prescription task yields
175   a set of initial medications to be prescribed for the patient after being admitted. Each medication is
176   coded in the Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical (ATC) classification system.
177
178   3.2   DATA PROCESSING AND CLINICAL DATA ELEMENTS EXTRACTION
179
      We extract clinical data elements as the foundational information units by cross-referencing multiple
180
      tables of the hospital and note modules of the MIMIC-IV dataset (Johnson et al., 2023), which
181   contains hospital-wide Electronic Health Records (EHR) from 2008 to 2019 at the Beth Israel
182   Deaconess Medical Center in Boston. First, we obtain patient profile, including gender, race, age,
183   insurance category, language and marital status. Second, we obtain the medical record at discharge,
184   including major complaint, history of present illness, past medical history, social history, family
185   history, physical exam data, allergies, and current medications at the hospital admission time. These
186   patient status records are represented in natural language in discharge notes. We then induce the
187   medical record at admission by removing the sections that describe information only available after
188   the clinical decisions (diagnoses, procedures, lab tests, and prescriptions) are made from the medical
189
      record at discharge. Examples of those sections include discharge physical exam data, updated
      medication lists, and doctor’s notes/comments. The induction is done by a rule-based NLP pipeline
190
      involving section identification, title extraction and keyword-matching. A manual screening process
191
      on 1000 sampled records iteratively updates the pipeline rules for coverage. The correctness of all
192   medical records at admission in the evaluation set is manually verified by a clinical NLP expert.
193   Third, lab test results within the admission includes lab test item, result value, units, normal range
194   and interpretation flags. Fourth, we identify radiology results within the admission including
195   indications and findings of patient radiology scans. Finally, history diagnoses are extracted from
196   discharge diagnoses of the last admission of the same patient and represented in ICD-10-CM codes,
197   comprehended with natural language medical history within the medical record at admission.
198   For ground-truth labels, we use billing diagnoses in ICD-10-CM codes as an estimation of discharge
199   diagnoses, and we extract procedures, lab tests and prescriptions ordered by a clinician at the first
200   timestamp after admission from structured records in ICD-10-PCS, LOINC and ATC codes at the
201   hospital encounter. All decisions are unordered sets of codes.
202
203   3.3   BALANCED AND DIVERSE EVALUATION INSTANCES SAMPLING
204   We sample a representative evaluation set from the extracted admission instances for each task, and
205   the remaining instances can be used for training. There are no overlapped admissions or patients
206   between split sets. We filter out the admission instances where there is no records for the target
207   element (i.e. diagnoses, procedures, lab test orders, or prescriptions).
208
      We sample the evaluation set by complying with the following distributional requirements. First,
209
      we expect the evaluation set to cover a diverse and broad range of output space. To do so, we
210   perform balanced sampling for different chapters of ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes (e.g. E00-E89 for
211   “endocrine, nutritional and metabolic diseases”), top-level categories for ICD-10-PCS procedure
212   codes (e.g. “imaging” procedures), third-level categories for LOINC lab items (e.g. “drug toxicology
213   laboratory” under LP7790-1), and ATC 1st level (e.g. chemicals for the respiratory system) for
214   diagnosis, procedure, lab test order, and prescription tasks, respectively. Second, we require scenarios
215   of various service and care units to be included in the evaluation set. Each admission record
      is associated with corresponding service departments, which cared for the patient during their


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      hospitalization, and care units, the type of unit or ward in which the patient is physically located.
217   There are 21 service departments ranging from cross-specialty departments like “surgical” to more
218   specialty-specific departments like “cardiac surgery”, and 37 care units such as “Medical ICU”
219   and “Coronary Care Unit”. After sampling by output space distribution, we additionally sample a
220   minimum number of instances for each service or care unit to comprehend if the per-category data
221   count is not sufficient.
222
223   3.4   P ROMPT CONSTRUCTION
224   After clinical data elements are extracted and cleaned, we create an input prompt incorporating the
225   system profile (e.g. “You are a professional clinician in a hospital with expert knowledge in medical
226   and clinical domains.”), task instruction (e.g. “The task is to make a list of diagnoses for this patient
227   based on the provided information of the patient...” for the diagnosis task), and verbalized patient
228   record indicated in Section 3.1 for each task. We provide complete prompt examples for each task in
229   Appendix B.2. For long prompts, we truncate the patient record segment by cutting the end of each
230
      prompt segment following the same ratio (detailed implementation in Appendix B.7).
231
      3.5   M ATCHING FROM FREE TEXT GENERATION TO MEDICAL CODE CANDIDATE
232
233   We design a flexible mechanism to convert LLM output to the modality required by tasks. In clinical
234   operations, clinicians have to select from a pool of candidate codes. To avoid severe performance
235   punishment for lack of understanding of the professional coding system, C LI B ENCH accepts natural
236   language decisions along with codes. If the response is in codes, we use the parsed and normalized
237   codes as predictions. If the response is in natural language, we find the most semantically similar
238
      code by calculating cosine similarity between sentence embeddings of definitions of all codes and the
      response produced by a BERT model trained on 1B sentence pairs (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019).
239
240
      3.6   M ULTI - GRANULAR EVALUATION METRICS
241
242   We compare the predicted decision set with the factual decisions and report micro precision, recall,
243   and F1 scores across all evaluation admission instances. To reflect the LLMs’ performance in different
244   granularities, we map each code in the predicted and ground-truth decision list to its ancestors and
245   report the scores from coarse-grained high-level choices to fine-grained capability of distinguishing
246
      similar candidates. The task is more difficult for finer-grained granularities with a larger candidate
      pool. LLMs might be able to predict the correct disease chapters out of 21 chapters correctly but
247
      struggle to decide the specific disease category groups with 283 possibilities.
248
249   For diagnosis decision, we report scores for chapter-level (e.g. “endocrine, nutritional and metabolic
250   diseases”), category group-level (e.g. “diabetes mellitus”), category-level (e.g. “diabetes mellitus due
251   to underlying condition”), sub-category level (e.g. “Diabetes mellitus due to underlying condition
252
      with hyperosmolarity”) and full code matching (e.g. “Diabetes mellitus due to underlying condition
      with hyperosmolarity with coma”) following the hierarchy of ICD-10-CM. For procedure decisions,
253
      level 1 (e.g. “nuclear medicine”), level 2 (e.g. “heart-related nuclear medicine”), level 3 (e.g. “Planar
254
      Nuclear Medicine Imaging”), and full code (e.g. “Planar Nuclear Medicine Imaging of Right and Left
255   Heart using Technetium 99m”) matching are reported. The level 1 to 3 of a full code is defined as the
256   first 1/2/3 character(s) of the ICD-10-PCS code. For lab tests, we use levels 1 to 3 and the leaf code
257   following LOINC hierarchy. For prescriptions, we report levels 1 to 4 of ATC classification system
258   defined as anatomical main group, therapeutic subgroup, pharmacological subgroup and chemical
259   subgroup. The statistics of the candidate decision space sizes of all levels shown in Appendix B.3
260   demonstrate the detail level and difficulty of each granularity.

      5.1    D ISCHARGE DIAGNOSES
379
380   We present the evaluation of diagnosis decisions in different diagnosis abstraction levels in Table 3
381   and the full result in Appendix C.1. We obtain the following observations.
382   1) State-of-the-art LLMs do not perform well for diagnosis decision-making. The low F1 scores
383   for fine-grained levels indicate the difficulty of the diagnosis decision task and confirm that the
384   leading LLMs do not perform well.
385
      2) Generalizable instruction tuning is crucial. Across all three LLM families, instruction-tuned
386
      models achieve much better performance than the ones without (rows 5 vs 2, 9 vs 8, 13 vs 12). The
387
      models without instruction tuning struggle to follow the instruction, considering potential diseases
388   and reasoning (shown in qualitative analysis in Appendix C.5). This indicates the diagnosis task
389   requires in-depth reasoning and long-input contextualization to perform it.
390
391
      3) GPT-4o is better than open models. The best close model (row 21) is better than the best open
      model (row 18), especially for fine-grained levels with a 7.37-point gap for full code. LLaMA3
392
      Instruct 70B outperforms GPT-4 turbo.
393
394   4) Domain-specialized models do not work. Models pretrained or fine-tuned on medical or clinical
395   corpus or tasks do not outperform (rows 6 vs 3) or slightly improve (rows 10/11 vs 8, rows 17 vs
396   12) its base model, but they are falling behind the instruction-tuned model in general domain. This
397
      suggests that domain adaptation compromises models’ general capabilities and calls for improved
      instruction tuning strategies for biomedical and clinical domains.
398
399   5) Tradeoff between precision and recall. We observe that GPT-4 turbo tends to generate more
400   decisions despite the risk of hallucination, while GPT-3.5 turbo is more conservative. LLaMA3
401   Instruct 70B also favors recall over precision compared with 7B. However, Mixtral Instruct is more
402   conservative than the smaller Mistral Instruct model.
403   6) Limited improvement by fine-tuning. We observe that SFT does lead to a better performance
404   compared to its base model. However, the improvement is limited, indicating capabilities beyond
405   memorizing the patterns are required for the diagnosis task. Other fine-tuning strategies, such as full
406   parameter supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization, would be worth investigating.
407   7) Flan-T5 fails to follow instruction. We observe Flan-T5 XL (row 1) yields great precision but
408   unsatisfactory recall. From qualitative analysis of its outputs, we observe the model does not follow
409   the instruction of generating multiple predictions, instead it only produces one predictions in many
410   cases. The conservative behavior explains its low recall and demonstrates its incapability of following
411   instructions.

      5.2        P ROCEDURE , LAB ORDERS AND PRESCRIPTIONS
433
434   We show performance on the other clinical decision tasks in Table 4 and full results in Appendix C.
435   We induce the following interpretations.
436   1) Models are less familiar with procedures and lab orders. The performance on procedures
437   and lab orders is much worse than on diagnoses and prescriptions, potentially due to more presence
438   of disease or medication information in training data. All models yield near-zero performance for
439   lowest-level procedure decisions.
440   2) No emergent capabilities while scaling up for procedures and lab test orders. For diagnoses
441   and prescriptions, we observe better or larger general domain models produce better performance
442   (row 21 vs 19, 18 vs 13, 7 vs 3). Increasing the LLaMA3 Instruct size from 8B to 70B leads to 11.82
443   and 5.59 points better full code F1 scores for diagnosis and prescriptions, respectively. However, for
444   procedures and lab test orders, additional general domain capabilities are not reasonably utilized and
445   do not lead to performance gain.

    529         7                     C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
    530
    531
                In conclusion, our study introduces C LI B ENCH, a comprehensive benchmark from the MIMIC-IV
                dataset, aimed at enhancing the evaluation of LLMs in clinical decision capabilities. This benchmark
    532
                addresses the existing limitations by providing a broad spectrum of medical cases across various
    533
                specialties, including complex clinical tasks such as lab test ordering and treatment procedure
    534         identification, and providing training data support for further model improvements. Our zero-shot
    535         evaluation of both open-sourced and closed-sourced LLMs reveals their potential and limitations in
    536         clinical decision-making. The findings underscore the necessity for continued refinement of LLMs to
    537         better cater to the intricate demands of real cases clinical diagnosis. Future research should focus
    538         on improving the accuracy, reliability, and applicability of LLMs in real-world clinical settings,
    539         leveraging C LI B ENCH as a robust tool for comprehensive assessment and iterative enhancement of
                LLM-powered healthcare solutions.

1067
       A.1    TASK FORMULATIONS AND BENCHMARK DESIGN
1068
1069   Difference compared to existing MIMIC-IV benchmarks. As pointed out in Table 1, we uniquely
1070   position our benchmark design to focus on a comprehensive set of specialties covering different
1071   categories of clinical decisions, using large expert ontologies as decision space instead of multiple
1072   choices with limited answer options to simulate real-world scenarios where the selected options are
1073   not provided in advance, covering a wide range of clinical tasks including diagnosis and procedure
1074   decisions, lab test ordering and prescriptions.
1075   Data from a single medical center may limit the generalizability of the findings. 1) To create a
1076   publicly accessible representative clinical benchmark with reasonable size, we have to make tradeoff
1077   choices. Given the limited test set size (to avoid the evaluation being too heavy), we prioritize
1078   the diversity of output spaces (such as 21 diagnosis chapters), 21 service departments and 37 care
1079   units. This ensures the evaluation covers a wide range of clinical scenarios and outcomes. We
       deprioritized sampling from different medical centers from additional data sources. 2) Though having


                                                           20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       more medical centers would make C LI B ENCH even more diverse, we would like to reiterate that
1081   C LI B ENCH already demonstrates a much stronger generalizability containing patient cases from
1082   multiple specialties with a much larger expert ontology as output space, and supports four tasks
1083   spanning the clinical operation lifecycles as we thoroughly compared in Table 1. 3) We acknowledge
1084   that our proposed benchmark construction method can be applied to an even more comprehensive
1085   range of data sources.
1086
       Around 1000 testing instances for comprehensive targets might not be enough. The 1000 target
1087   evaluation set size is decided following popular LLM evaluation benchmarks. It is designed to
1088   balance the coverage of target decision categories and the ease of use of the benchmark. To achieve a
1089   good balance, we sample the evaluation set considering the distribution of target decision categories,
1090   service departments, and care units as described in Section 3.3. With the provided scripts, we envision
1091   interested practitioners could sample their own testing set to benchmark LLM’s performance for a
1092   more focused group of diseases or other clinical decisions.
1093   Why not sample evaluation set by patient profile info? The four clinical tasks we considered in
1094   the benchmark are admission-based, which focus on understanding the medical records of a specific
1095   admission. We thus split the data to train and test set by admissions, instead of patients.
1096
       Data leak during pre-training might compromise the fairness of the benchmark. 1) The
1097
       license of the data source used for C LI B ENCH requires that models trained on the data should be
1098
       treated as containing sensitive information and thus have to inherit the agreement of MIMIC-IV. This
1099   requirement ensures that models trained on the data have to disclose the source, making data leakage
1100   easy to recognize. This also means that all models that are open to download without signing an
1101   agreement (such as most models on Huggingface Hub) should not use MIMIC data for training.
1102
       2) The challenging nature of C LI B ENCH makes it more robust against data leaks. Unlike many
1103
       existing benchmarks on factual questions with simpler reasoning processes, we show that even if the
1104
       model is fine-tuned with the clinical notes of the same distribution, simply remembering the patterns
1105   without in-depth reasoning does not perform well (as shown by the SFT performance in Table 3).
1106
1107   3) We acknowledge in the limitation section (Appendix F) that clinical notes used for C LI B ENCH
1108
       might be used for training LLMs and the risk of data leakage. This issue is inevitable for LLM
       benchmarks using public-accessible data sources.
1109
1110   Generalizability of the training data might be limited. 1) We do not claim that any of our evaluated
1111   methods or the training data has a strong generalizability toward novel medical conditions. We do
1112   not focus on training a model for this task. We claim that we provide a more realistic and inclusive
1113   benchmark of LLM’s clinical decision capabilities than existing benchmarks. As we mentioned
1114
       in the “Limitationn” section in Appendix F, better generalizability with even more data or method
       design is possible. 2) In our SFT performance shown in Table 10, we observed that fine-tuning on the
1115
       training data can only provide marginal improvement compared with zero-shot. However, this does
1116
       not undermine the value of the training data. Instead, it calls for better method design and utilization
1117   strategy for the training data.
1118
1119   Heavy dependence on extensive manual checking. 1) We would like to clarify that C LI B ENCH
1120
       does NOT manually check the model responses to produce the evaluation scores. Instead, we develop
       an automatic evaluation strategy to produce evaluation metrics from LM’s free-text response in a
1121
       scalable way, as introduced in Section 3.5 and Section 3.6. 2) We use manual checking only when
1122
       creating the data processing pipeline and constructing the input prompt of evaluation test cases (as
1123   discussed in Section 3.2).
1124
1125   Why not provide the decision of a doctor as the upper bound performance? 1) We use the clinical
1126
       decisions recorded in the raw EHR data as the ground truth for the four clinical decision tasks. These
       decisions are made by different specialized clinicians in the corresponding service department or unit.
1127
       We then consider these recorded decisions as ground truth, representing the “collective knowledge”
1128
       of a group of clinicians.
1129
1130   2) As we acknowledge in the “Limitations” section in Appendix F, there could be miscoded decisions,
1131   however, the recorded diagnoses/procedures/lab test orders/prescriptions are the best resources for
1132
       clinical decision labels we can get from existing available data.
1133



                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       3) Since C LI B ENCH covers patient cases from many different units, annotations from a small group
1135   of doctors are not sufficient to have reasonable coverage. Obtaining doctors’ performance from
1136   various units has a non-trivial cost.

       A.2    I MPLICATIONS AND IMPACT
1140
1141
       Potential usage of the benchmark. We envision the proposed C LI B ENCH can be used to evaluate
1142
       and compare the capabilities of practical clinical knowledge of LLMs and LLM-based agent sys-
1143
       tems. As many LLMs achieve close-to-human performance in popular clinical/medical benchmarks,
1144   C LI B ENCH presents a challenging set of tasks, which require domain knowledge, reasoning, general-
1145   izability, and output expert-ontology understanding, to motivate and benchmark the development of
1146   future LLMs.
1147
       Real-world usage of the tasks. C LI B ENCH aims to simulate the real-world clinical decision
1148
       environment with accessible clinical data. Proposing a clinical benchmark without public access
1149
       significantly limits the fairness and value of those benchmarks. The diagnosis decision task aims
1150   to examine the ability to identify a diagnosis based on the patient’s medical records throughout the
1151   patient’s stay. The procedure decisions, lab test orders, and prescriptions aim to simulate the scenarios
1152   for clinicians to make the initial decisions after observing the admission-time patient information.
1153
1154
1155
       A.3    C OMPARING METHODS SELECTION
1156
1157
       Why are few-shot experiments not performed? One of the key challenging aspects of C LI B ENCH
1158
       is its comprehensiveness of the input patient records; the limited context length of LLMs does
1159
       not support the few-shot experiments as feeding information of a single admission takes most of
1160   the context length. The diversity of the clinical cases makes the performance variance of sampling
1161   different demonstration examples quite large. Thus, we focus on the zero-shot setting for the proposed
1162   clinical decision tasks.
1163
       Why not include temporal predictive models as comparing models? We emphasize that the
1164
       clinical decision tasks proposed in C LI B ENCH use a different setting than temporal predictive models,
1165
       and we do not include patient history information in the input of the tasks except last-admission
1166   diagnosis codes for the diagnosis decision task. Instead, the tasks in C LI B ENCH use realistic clinical
1167   settings that focus on the patient records of the current admission.
1168
1169
       Why not evaluate Retrieval-Augmented Generation and tool-use methods? RAG and using func-
       tion calls to provide output space information to an agent-based system are imaginable improvements
1170
       upon performing zero-shot inference. We consider those methods potential solutions for the proposed
1171
       benchmark, and it is not feasible to apply them directly without carefully designing the retrieval
1172   methods, API calls, and tool functions. In this work, we focus on proposing the clinical decision
1173   benchmark tasks and resources, and we leave the development of advanced methods for these tasks
1174   to future works.

1309   B.6    D EFINITION OF FIRST BATCH DECISIONS
1310
1311
       Among all the procedures, lab test orders, and prescriptions included in the structured database, we
       apply a filter and keep only the first batch of decisions as our target prediction ground truth. We
1312
       define the “first batch” as the decision made at the first timestamp, appearing within 24 hours after
1313
       the admission time for each kind of decision (procedures, lab test orders, prescriptions). Full lab test
1314   results are used as input for the diagnosis task, while only the first batch decisions are used as ground
1315   truth for the tasks except for diagnosis decisions.

1317   B.7    T RUNCATION RULES
1318
1319   When we truncate the input prompt, we first make sure the system prompt, task instruction, and
1320   questions are kept in their original complete form. We also keep the full sequence of patient profile
1321   and history diagnoses. We only truncate the remaining clinical data elements, i.e. , medical records
1322   at admission, lab test results within the admission, and radiology results within the admission. We
1323   calculate a truncation radio, which is defined as the remaining token count after keeping the untouched
1324
       segment complete and leaving certain token contexts for output, divided by the token count of the
       to-be-truncated segments. We then apply the same ratio of truncation to all remaining segments to
1325
       make sure partial information on all aspects is kept. The output token context length has to be larger
1326
       than 80% of the token count of complete decoding of the selected model to allow sufficient output
1327   context.

       F        L IMITATIONS AND POTENTIAL NEGATIVE IMPACT
1549
1550
       We would like to raise awareness that there might be miscoded diagnosis codes in the patient records.
1551   The billing ICD diagnosis codes are used as “ground-truth” diagnosis decisions to train our model and
1552   evaluate the performance for diagnosis prediction. The billing diagnosis codes do not exactly match
1553   the clinician’s diagnosis decisions, and they are input after the diagnoses are made. We acknowledge
1554   that the diagnosis code extracted from the EHR dataset should not be considered the best/perfect
1555   diagnosis decision. We also raise the potential data distribution issue as the training and evaluation
1556   data used in this work is largely collected for patients with ICU stay history. Thus, the evaluation
1557   result does not represent the generalized diagnosis prediction capability, and the trained model may
1558   yield compromised performance when different kinds of patient records are queried. There are
1559
       potential mapping errors from the model response to a candidate code using the sentence BERT
       model, which could introduce noise in the evaluation results. Those seeking to use this benchmark
1560
       should be cautioned that it is not suitable for picking up subtle changes in performance as evidence
1561
       of a model’s superiority.
