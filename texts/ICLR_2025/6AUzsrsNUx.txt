011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013                 Utilizing tools with Large Language Models (LLMs) is essential for
014                 grounding AI agents in real-world applications. The prevailing ap-
015
                    proach involves few-shot prompting with demonstrations or fine-tuning
                    with expert annotations. However, mere in-context demonstrations may
016
                    fail to cover sufficient knowledge for complex tools and tasks. Training
017
                    on solution paths is also hindered by the high cost of expert annotations
018                 and generalizing to new tools. A core challenge of generalizable tool
019                 use lies in understanding the “meta”, or fundamental natures of tools
020                 that are transferable across tasks, such as causality and constraints. In
021                 this paper, we present MetaTool, a novel tool learning methodology de-
022                 signed to generalize across any reusable toolset. Our approach incor-
023                 porates a self-supervised augmentation technique derived from a series
024                 of meta-tasks. This involves predicting masked elements in the tool
025                 execution process. The self-supervised procedure enables scalable gen-
026
                    eration of high-quality QA data, which is handy for supervising tool
                    understanding. By incorporating meta-task data into task-oriented train-
027
                    ing, our method significantly enhances the performance of open-source
028
                    LLMs, achieving results comparable to ChatGPT in both tool-based
029                 planning and chatting scenarios. Through large-scale instruction tuning,
030                 the MetaTool model demonstrates impressive zero-shot generalizability
031                 on new tasks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Distinguished from other species, a unique characteristic of human advanced intelligence is using
037   complex tools, which expands the frontiers neural intelligence can reach. With the advent of power-
038   ful foundation models, AI has the potential to solve complex tasks with these external mechanisms.
039
      LLMs have been majorly oriented towards either tool-augmented chatbots equipped with retrievers
      and search engines, or tool-oriented agents (e.g. web navigation Rawles et al. (2023); Hong et al.
040
      (2024), embodied manipulation Chi et al. (2023)) that achieve task objectives through tool output
041
      Qin et al. (2023b). While the former emphasizes generalizing to various tools, the latter focuses on
042   complex tools and scenarios.
043
044   To efficiently integrate LLMs with tools, a mainstream way relies on in-context learning (ICL). The
045
      model is provided with the “cookbook” of tools in zero-shot prompting or demonstrations in few-
      shot prompting Xu et al. (2023); Paranjape et al. (2023); Brown et al. (2020). It may work well
046
      on simple tools with frameworks like LangChain Chase (2022). However, for complex tasks with
047
      sophisticated tools, in-context learning is limited that demonstrations can not exhaust all scenarios,
048   and manuals are also limited in length. Ultimately, it’s impractical to expect LLMs to be intelligent
049   enough to master any tool without the experience of using it. On the other side, training-based
050   methods Qin et al. (2023c); Patil et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024) mainly adopt supervised fine-
051   tuning with annotated expert solutions. Despite the difficulties in scaling up the optimal annotation,
052   supervision with task solutions has limitations. Task-agnostic knowledge of tools can be neglected,
053   which hinders the generalization to diverse scenarios or new tools. Self-play training methods like
      Toolformer Schick et al. (2024) and TALM Parisi et al. (2022) integrate the inference process with

104
105   In this section, we first formalize the task of using a close toolset and define 6 general meta-tasks that
106   are key to tool understanding. Then we show how to construct datasets in an integral self-supervised
107   way covering different scenarios. In the end, we describe several schemes to augment tool learning
      with meta-tasks.

      2.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED M ETA - TASKS FOR T OOL U NDERSTANDING
128
129   Problem formalization. A tool-use task can be generally defined as a Markovian tuple ⟨S, A, T , g⟩,
130   where S, A, T is the state space, action space, and toolset, and g is the goal state of the task. Toolset
131   T = {t}N consists of N tools, each as a state transition function s′ = t(s, θ) that formalizes the
132   outcome of state change when feeding the input parameters θ into the tool. An action a = ⟨t, θ⟩ ∈ A
133   specifies the tool and its input. As an autonomous agent, an LLM should iteratively respond with
134   tool calling and inputs according to the state until it reaches the goal. A solution path leading to the
135
      goal can be defined as a sequence of actions and states p = {s1 , a1 , ..., sT , aT } ∈ P . Broadly, when
      the tools can not alter any external state, tool output like retrieval results can be regarded as the state,
136
      and the desired information is the goal g.
137
138   We enhance the tool understanding of the model with self-supervised surrogate (pretext) tasks in-
139   stead of in-context descriptions or demonstrations. Formally, we regard tools as external systems
140   that implement state transition mappings. Tool understanding, therefore, involves comprehending
141
      the perception-action process of these systems (referred to as tool execution) and should be general-
      izable to various task objectives.
142
143   Meta-task definition. We begin with single-step tool execution D = {s, a, s′ }, peeling off the task
144   goal g context, which results as unsupervised data. Six surrogate tasks (meta-tasks) are designed
145   based on the dataset D. Basically, the model is required to predict masked elements of the execution
146   process. It’s similar with the idea of masked language models Devlin (2018) in a broader and
147
      structural granularity to learn the lurking knowledge beneath the unsupervised data. We define the
      meta-tasks as below (Figure 2):
148
149          • Effect: The model predicts the outcome state P(s′ |a, s) given the initial state and the ac-
150            tion.
151
             • Decision-making: The model decides a feasible action P(a|s, s′ ) given the initial and
152            outcome state.
153
             • Reversion: The model deduces the initial state P(s|a, s′ ) given the action and the outcome
154
               state.
155
156
             • Input Boundary: The model determines whether an action can be successfully exe-
               cuted, namely whether the action falls in the valid action space, given the current state:
               P(1s′ ̸=s |a, s).
157
158
159
             • Output Boundary: The model determines whether a state can be reached with any action
               given the current state: P(1∃(t,θ),s′ =t(s,θ) |s, s′ ).
160
161          • Counterfact: The model predicts the new outcome state P(s′′ |a, s′ , a′ ) if a new action a′
               were executed given that the current action a results in the current outcome s′ .

180   Effect, decision-making, reversion meta-tasks emphasize the causality of a tool, regarding the action
181   as the intervention to the state Pearl (2009); Pearl & Mackenzie (2018) and the outcome as the causal
182   effect is determined by the tool mechanism. On top of that, counterfact task is the composition of

184
      Bareinboim (2016), an advanced form of causal reasoning that humans use to contemplate ’what if’.

      Moreover, tools implemented as APIs may receive non-executable inputs and result in ineffective
186   outcomes. Thus the input and output domains are also unique features of a tool as a function. We
187   consider input boundary meta-task that emphasizes the tool affordance that refers to what actions can
188   be executed considering the situation and the precondition. output boundary meta-task emphasizes
189   the functionality of tools, that is, what goals can and cannot be achieved given the current state.

      2.3     T OOL L EARNING AUGMENTED WITH M ETA - TASKS
221
222   By incorporating meta-task data, we explore several approaches to augment the tool learning for the
223   purpose of achieving task objectives: a) In-context learning: We randomly sample several demon-
224   strations of each meta-task and add them to the system prompt to facilitate tool understanding in
225   a training-free manner. Such task-agnostic knowledge includes the interpretation of rules, supple-
226   menting the solution demonstrations. b) 2-stage learning: Since we aim to build the model’s tool
227   understanding as the foundation of tool-oriented learning, an intuitive idea is to train the LLM first
228   on the metasets as the surrogate tasks and then on the solution data P . In order to maintain the
229
      general ability of the model in the first stage, only the parameters of the query and value projection
      layers of the Transformer are updated instead of targeting all modules. c) Data augmentation: In
230
      this approach, we utilize the metaset as the augmented data of conventional instruction tuning meth-
231
      ods that the metasets are mixed with solution data and the model is trained uniformly. The model
232   trained on the mixed data is referred to as MetaTool.

244   3.1.1    TASK SETUP
245
      We adopt 3 tool-oriented tasks that emphasize complex tool execution and sequential plan-
246   ning. Among them SAW is newly designed while the other two are introduced from PlanBench
247   Valmeekam et al. (2024). The key challenge of these tasks is to understand the rules (preconditions)
248   and the environmental dynamics caused by actions. The task definition and dataset construction are
249   elaborated below.
250
      SpellAnyWord (SAW). In this task, the agent needs to sequentially construct a string that contains
251
      the target string as a continuous substring. The initial state of the task is a void string. Two non-
252   degradable tools (functions) are avaliable: Add: to add two adjacent letters in the alphabet to the
253   end of the current string. The tool input θ should be the preceding letter (e.g. passing ’a’ to Add
254   on current string ” will result in ’ab’). Swap: to swap the position of two adjacent letters in the
255   current string. The input should be the preceding letter (e.g. passing ’a’ to Swap on ’ab’ will
256   result in ’ba’). An example task: The target string is ’any’. A successful action sequence can
257   be [Add(’a’), Add(’n’), Add(’y’), Swap(’a’), Add(’o’)], which will result in a state sequence [’ab’,
258   ’abno’, ’abnoyz’, ’banoyz’, ’banyoz’] and the final string ’banyoz’ has ’any’ as a substring. To
259   eliminate the basis from tokenization, we format each string as a list of letters in prompts throughout
260
      the task.
261   BlocksWolrd (BW). In this scenario, the agent needs to stack several blocks on the table into a target
262   state with one hand. Only one block can be moved at a time. Two tools (functions) are avaliable:
263   Pick: to pick a block in the hand. The tool input should be the target block indicated by its color
264   (e.g. Pick(’yellow’)). Blocks cannot be picked if there are blocks on top of them or there’s already
265   a block in the hand. Stack: to stack the block in the hand onto the target block or table. The input
266
      should be the color of the target block or ’table’ (e.g. Stack(’white’), Stack(’table’)). Blocks cannot
      be stacked on a block with another block already on top of it or there’s no block in the hand.
267
268   Logistics (LOG). The agent needs to solve a logistics problem by arranging trucks and airplanes to
269   transport the package to the target location. Locations are grouped by cities. Trucks can be used to
      move packages between locations in the same city and planes can be used to move packages between

      3.1.2   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
300   Our model is fine-tuned based on LLaMA3-8b-instruct AI@Meta (2024) with parameter-efficient
301   fine-tuning method Qlora Dettmers et al. (2024) on 8 A100 GPUs. We utilize the instruction tuning
302   version of LLaMA3 since comprehending tool-oriented tasks with specific objectives is the basis
303   of tool understanding and use. For each task, we train the model on 10k meta-task data and 10k
304   solution data for 3 epochs with AdamW optimizer and the learning rate of 2e-4. The models are
305   tested in a simulated environment that receives the action of using a tool and returns the outcome
306   and current state. We evaluated the model performance on 100 unseen cases of each three tasks.
307   Baselines. We evaluate several baselines to study the effect of different training approaches (il-
308   lustrated in Figure 1 and described in Section 2.3).(1) LLaMA3-solution: training the base model
309   LLaMA3-8B-instruct merely on the solution data P .(2) LLaMA3-ICL: prompting the base model
310   with few-shot meta-task demonstrations (examples shown in Figure 3 and Figure 4).(3) LLaMA3-
311   2-stage: training the base model first on meta-tasks data M then on solution data P .

339   Among the various tool/function calling benchmarks, we study our method on two of the most

344   3.2.1    TASK SETUP
345
      ToolBench contains diverse user requests with a massive amount (over 16k) of real-world API
346
      tools, which are publicly available on the RapidAPI website. The testset is categorized into six
347   distinct groups and contains 1200 instructions (200 each): I1-inst., I1-tool, I1-cat., I2-inst, I2-cat.,
348   and I3-inst. Groups labeled with I1, I2, I3 include single-tool tasks, intra-category multi-tool tasks,
349   and extra-category multi-tool tasks, respectively. Groups labeled with “inst.”, ”tool”, ”cat.” include
350   unseen user instructions, unseen tools, and unseen categories (e.g. sports, entertainment) of tools,
351   respectively. Two evaluation metrics are designed based on ChatGPT: (1) Pass Rate, calculated
352   by the proportion of instructions successfully completed within a limited budget; (2) Win Rate
353   measured by asking a ChatGPT evaluator to select its preference for two solution paths. For each
354   user instruction (e.g. “Can you recommend some popular restaurants within 5km to hold a party?”),
355
      the model calls an API and responds to the query based on the tool output.
356   ToolBench also provides 126k instruction-solution pairs for training, which are generated with GPT4
357   and depth-first tree search (DFS). GPT-4 gains access to different reasoning paths by choosing ei-
358   ther to continue the current node or give up and expand a new node. On top of that, we extract
359   unsupervised tool execution data and generate 650k self-supervised data of meta-tasks following the
360   procedure in Figure 3. We then conduct instruction tuning based on the mixed data and LLaMA3-
361
      8B-instruct model, trained for 1 epoch to avoid overfitting. LLaMA3-solution is trained for 2 epochs
      following the original configuration in ToolBench. Both the solution data and the meta-task data
362
      share the same loss setting as we construct the metaset as QA pairs. The context-aware states are
363
      generated with the open-sourced LLaMA3-70B-instruct model. All evaluated LLMs are prompted
364   in the ReAct manner to leverage their reasoning ability. Other model implementation details are in
365   line with that described in section 4.1.2.
366
367
      BFCL benchmark is established mainly for the purpose of zero-shot evaluation without holistic
      training data. The benchmark contains 4251 testing cases in total and is categorized into non-live
368
      (self-designed), live (user-contributed), multi-turn, and Hallucination (relevance or irrelevance de-
369
      termination) groups. The model performance is measured by action accuracy with Abstract Syntax
370   Tree (AST) Patil et al. (2023). We test the MetaTool model trained with ToolBench data on BFCL
371   to evaluate the zero-shot ICL ability and generality of our methodology.

      4.1   T OOL LEARNING
468
469   Recent studies have shed light on the potential of utilizing tools to augment LLMs with external fac-
470   tual knowledge Qin et al. (2023a); Nakano et al. (2021); Song et al. (2023); Hao et al. (2024); Shen
471   et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Zhuang et al. (2024); Schick
472   et al. (2024) which is targeted at tool-augmented question-answering scenarios, towards the ‘tools
473   for AI’ purpose in general. On the other side, With the burgeoning intelligence in reasoning and
474   perception, LLMs’ tool-use capability can be widely applied in the automation of various domains
475
      including Embodied AI Wang et al. (2024c;b), web manipulation Rawles et al. (2023); Hong et al.
      (2024); Yang et al. (2023); Deng et al. (2024); He et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2023), and image/video
476
      editing Wang et al. (2024a); Argaw et al. (2022); Hang et al. (2024); Fu et al. (2023).This line of
477
      work is intended for tool-oriented planning scenarios for the ‘AI for tools’ purpose. Effectively mas-
478   tering complex tools challenges the model to comprehend the precondition and potential outcome
479   of using tools. In this paper, we aim to facilitate LLMs for both tool-oriented and tool-augmented
480   tasks by learning robust tool understanding.

      5   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we introduced MetaTool, an efficient and generalizable method that facilitates tool
536   learning with task-agnostic comprehension. This is achieved by deriving self-supervised meta-task
537   data from tool execution actions. Augmented the meta-tasks into either complex toolset fine-tuning
538   or large-scale instruction tuning, our model exhibits sophisticated tool mastery as well as generality
539   in in-context learning. Evaluated on multiple tool use benchmarks, MetaTool outperforms models
      trained on expert solutions and showcases comparable performance with ChatGPT in a size of 8B.

790   We present several examples of context generation (mentioned in Section 2.2) and the prompt in
791   Figure 7 and Figure 5. The LLM worker is only asked to complete the contextual information for
792   the results returned by the tool. In the Effect meta-task, the model learns to predict the contextual
793   results given the input parameters, which helps it better understand the tool mechanism. Otherwise
794   asking the model to predict merely the retrieval results (e.g. 2023 European Tour, Yellowstone
795
      National Park) is impractical and not beneficial. No other information or prior knowledge from the
      LLM worker are needed and provided to MetaTool.
796
797   As showcased in Figure 8, the agent is required to construct stacks containing a green block on top
798   of a yellow block from a pile of 4 blocks. With mere descriptions of tools in the prompts, LLaMA3
799   fails to understand the precondition of using tools resulting in invalid actions. Training on tool-
800   oriented solution data, LLaMA3-solution attempts to lift the yellow block successfully but fails to
801
      sequentially achieve the task goal and falls into repetitive loops. The proposed MetaTool model
      achieves the target state with an effective action sequence (although still not the optimal efficiency)
802
      and corresponding reasoning. These 3 models correspond to the 3 paradigms illustrated in Figure 1.
803
      The results show that LLMs can learn tool use better on the basis of robust tool understanding.
