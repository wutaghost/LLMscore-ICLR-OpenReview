009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning models excel at capturing complex representations through sequen-
012            tial layers of linear and non-linear transformations, yet their inherent black-box
013            nature and multi-modal training landscape raise critical concerns about reliabil-
014            ity, robustness, and safety, particularly in high-stakes applications. To address
015            these challenges, we introduce YES training bounds, a novel framework for real-
016            time, data-aware certification and monitoring of neural network training. The YES
017            bounds evaluate the efficiency of data utilization and optimization dynamics, pro-
018
               viding an effective tool for assessing progress and detecting suboptimal behavior
               during training. Our experiments show that the YES bounds offer insights beyond
019
               conventional local optimization perspectives, such as identifying when training
020
               losses plateau in suboptimal regions. Validated on both synthetic and real data,
021            including image denoising tasks, the bounds prove effective in certifying train-
022            ing quality and guiding adjustments to enhance model performance. By integrat-
023            ing these bounds into a color-coded cloud-based monitoring system, we offer a
024            powerful tool for real-time evaluation, setting a new standard for training quality
025            assurance in deep learning.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep learning models have become crucial for tackling complex computational problems, owing to
031   the rich representations they develop through their multi-layered structures and non-linear transfor-
032   mations LeCun et al. (2015); Goodfellow et al. (2016). Despite their remarkable effectiveness, these
033
      models are often perceived as black boxes, raising concerns related to their robustness, reliability,
      and safety. As neural networks become increasingly integral to critical applications, ensuring that
034
      they are properly trained and perform as intended is paramount.
035
036   To evaluate the training performance and a network’s ability to store a model after training (i.e.,
037   achieve zero loss), one approach is to statistically analyze neural networks under certain assump-
038   tions. This has been done for networks with thresholding activation functions like ReLU, where
039
      researchers have determined the number of parameters needed to achieve full memory capacity
      Vershynin (2020). It is well-known that for ReLU-based neural networks (NNs), once a sufficient
040
      number of weights is reached, the network can achieve full memory capacity or even zero loss in
041
      some cases. In Oymak & Soltanolkotabi (2019), the authors theoretically demonstrate that in the
042   over-parameterization regime, the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm can converge to the
043   global minimum. However, these methods are statistical in nature and rely on specific assumptions
044   about the input data and the model, which may limit their applicability.
045
      The most widely encountered scenario in mathematical optimization, including for training deep
046
      neural networks, is that the true optimum value of the objective function is not known a priori—
047
      i.e., it is unclear to what extent the minimization objective can be reduced. Therefore, finding a
048   way to evaluate the effectiveness of optimizers in minimizing the training objectives is an intriguing
049   pursuit. In this paper, we introduce a novel framework for data-aware certification and monitoring
050   of deep neural network training, which we refer to as the YES training bounds. These bounds aim
051   to provide a qualified answer to the question: Is the neural network being properly trained by the
052   data and the optimizer (YES or NO)? The certificates are data-aware in the sense that they take
053   into account the specific structure and properties of the training data, allowing for more precise
      and tailored bounds on the training performance. This means that rather than relying on generic or


                                                        1

054
      overly conservative estimates, the guarantees reflect the actual dataset being used, providing more
055   relevant insights into the training process. They are also real-time, fulfilling a long-standing desire
056   in the deep learning community for tools that can evaluate and certify the progress made in training
057   a neural network as it unfolds. By offering concrete, data-specific evaluations during training, these
058   guarantees allow practitioners to confidently assess when sufficient learning has taken place and
059   whether the model is converging toward optimal training, all without the need for extensive post-
060   hoc analysis or probabilistic approximations.
061   To facilitate the practical application of the YES bounds, we offer an intuitive cloud-based mon-
062   itoring system that visually represents the training progress. This system uses a color-coded
063   scheme—red (ineffective training), yellow (caution, training non-optimal), and green (effective
064   training in progress)—to certify the training status in real time; see Fig. 2. As the field of artificial
065   intelligence (AI) continues to evolve, there is a growing emphasis on AI safety and regulation to
066   ensure that AI systems meet the highest standards of reliability and effectiveness Howar & Hun-
067   gar; Tran et al. (2023); Aird—Affiliate (2023); Bengio et al. (2024); Balagurunathan et al. (2021);
068   Brundage et al. (2020); Hendrycks & Mazeika (2022); Marques-Silva & Ignatiev (2022); Qi et al.
069
      (2024); Pinto et al. (2015); Zhong et al. (2023); Wing (2021); Li et al. (2023). The proposed YES
      training bounds and the associated training cloud system present a promising pathway toward es-
070
      tablishing a benchmark for the AI industry, regulators, and users alike. We envision a future where
071
      these bounds are widely adopted, serving as a standardized measure to evaluate and ensure the opti-
072   mal training performance of AI systems across diverse applications. This standardization could play
073   a crucial role in fostering trust and accountability within the AI ecosystem.
074
075
      The YES bounds and their associated cloud system primarily serve as a sanity check for the op-
      timizer—the mechanism driving the training process. This system continuously challenges the
076
      optimizer with intelligently crafted examples grounded in mathematically rigorous heuristics and
077
      tailored to the layer-wise architecture of neural networks. By doing so, the bounds lay the proper
078   groundwork for standardization of training practices in deep learning. Two key observations support
079   this assertion:
080
081         1. The Bounds Go Beyond Local Optimization Perspectives: Traditional optimization ap-
082            proaches in neural networks predominantly focus on local information—examining the
083            immediate vicinity of the optimization landscape, such as attraction domains and local op-
084            tima. However, numerical examples demonstrate that training losses can often plateau in
085            regions where the YES bounds and the associated cloud unequivocally indicate subopti-
086            mality. This phenomenon underscores the bounds’ ability to transcend local optimization
087
               insights, providing a more global perspective on training efficacy.
088         2. Deterministic Certification Without Randomization: Unlike methods that rely on random-
089            ization around the current training state to certify local or neighborhood optimality, the
090            YES bounds operate deterministically. They do not produce varying certification results
091            across different training realizations, even when initialized identically or following similar
092            optimization paths. This determinism is particularly advantageous for standardization, as
093            it ensures consistent and reproducible determinations of training quality. By eliminating
094            the variability introduced by randomization, the YES bounds present a robust and reliable
095            candidate for establishing standardized training benchmarks in deep learning.

171   So, what is the case for depth and nonlinearity? In scenarios where a single-layer is insufficient,
172   neural learning models employ multiple layers with nonlinear activation functions such as ReLU to
173   progressively refine input mappings (leading to deep learning). The necessity for multiple layers
174
      can be attributed to the complexity of the mapping task in question. The necessity for nonlinear
      transformations in multi-layer mappings, however, comes from the fact that multiple consecutive
175
      linear mappings are in effect equivalent to a single-layer mapping (with a weight matrix equal to the
176
      product of linear mapping matrices).

      whether a neural network is being properly trained by the data: YES or NO?

      6   C ONCLUSION
520
521
      One may wonder why the YES bounds work as they do. The reason is simple: heuristics outper-
522   form random, and optimal beats heuristic. In mathematics, many effective bounds emerge from
523   insightful heuristics. The YES training bounds are similarly grounded in solid mathematical princi-
524   ples—specifically, that neural networks, with their non-linearities, should outperform linear projec-
525   tions. These bounds provide a useful heuristic that separates the wheat from the chaff, the random
526   from the meaningful. This is particularly evident with the YES-0 bound. But even with the more
527   sophisticated YES bounds and the associated training cloud, the principle holds: they expose the
528   randomness (in the sense of not sufficiently taking the shape of the optimal) in the solution by of-
529   fering a better real-time heuristic. Ultimately, as the process nears optimality, we witness the final
530
      convergence—optimal surpasses heuristic. This transition is marked by entering the green zone
      beneath the training cloud.
531
532   It is worthwhile to note that the YES bounds and their certification should be regarded as a necessary
533   condition for optimality, not a sufficient one. They provide a certificate of potential optimality—a
534   certificate one must certainly have to assert quality in training. They are, however, a concrete step
535   toward bridge the gap between the opaque nature of neural networks and the pressing need for
536
      transparency and reliability in high-stakes applications.
537   Future work may explore the extension of the YES bounds to various network architectures and
538   training paradigms, as well as their integration into automated training systems. Developing more
539   sophisticated bounds and understanding their impact on the AI training ecosystem are promising
      avenues for research.

017   Theorem 1. Let Ω be an activation function in a deep neural network. If Ω is applied in an element-
018

026

027

029

037                                       Ek = Y − Ω(Ak Yk ).                                     (6)

      G    B EYOND C ERTIFICATION : C AN W E G UIDE THE T RAINING P ROCESS ?
433
434
      It is our understanding that the YES bounds and their associated clouds provide not only a mathe-
435   matical framework for certifying AI training but may also aid practitioners as a method to guide the
436   training process. To harness this dual utility without compromising the integrity of the certification
437   process, it is imperative to maintain a clear information barrier between training and certification.
438   This separation ensures that the training algorithm does not gain access to the YES bound data or
439   the certification network weights. Allowing such access would undermine the certification’s pur-
440   pose by enabling the training process to exploit the bound information, leading to several adverse
441   consequences:
442
             • Loss of Randomization Benefits: Randomization, particularly during initialization and
443
               throughout training, plays a crucial role in escaping local minima and ensuring robust con-
444
               vergence. If the training process can access YES bound data and network weights, it may
445            inadvertently eliminate these randomization benefits, resulting in deterministic and poten-
446            tially suboptimal training trajectories.
447
             • Faulty Optimization Directions: The training algorithm might adopt step directions that
448
               do not align with the true optimization landscape. Since there is no guarantee that the
449            certification network weights resemble the optimum, leveraging these weights could steer
450            the training process in misleading directions, ultimately degrading the quality of the trained
451            model.
452
             • Obsolescence of the Certification Test: The primary purpose of the certification test is to
453            provide a reliable bound on the network’s training performance. If the training process can
454            consistently operate at or below this bound by utilizing certification data, the test will be
455            rendered ineffective.
456
457   To mitigate the risks associated with direct access to certification data, it is essential to devise mech-
458   anisms that allow the training process to benefit from the YES bounds without exposing the certifica-
459   tion test itself. One effective strategy is to share only the test results, such as those visualized through
460
      the YES cloud, rather than the underlying certification data or network weights. This approach pro-
      vides the training algorithm with a lower bound on the distance between the current loss and the
461
      optimal loss without revealing any specific information about the certification criteria. Specifically,
462
      the distance of the current loss from the bottom of the YES cloud serves as a valuable indicator for
463   adjusting the learning rate:
464
465                                       dk = max{Lk − LYES , 0},                                           (10)
466   where Lk is the current loss at epoch k, and LYES represents the lower bound provided by the
467   YES cloud. This distance dk may inform the training process on how large of a step size could be
468   chosen to make meaningful progress, ensuring that the learning rate adapts dynamically based on
469   the proximity to the optimal loss. A natural implementation of this guidance mechanism involves
470   defining an adaptive learning rate that incorporates both the traditional vanishing component and an
471   additional term based on the distance dk to the YES bounds.
